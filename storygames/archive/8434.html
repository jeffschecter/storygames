\n In David Artmans game FMA I found this paragraph:

The Number One rule of FMA is best-summarized by the phrase \u201cI Will Not Abandon You.\u201d Originally coined by Meguey Baker (see sidebar), it is one approach to serious, difficult role playing; its opposite is \u201cNobody Gets Hurt.\u201d In FMA, you will get hurt. If you don\u2019t feel angst, sadness, frustration, anger, or emotional distress then you have missed the point of play.

Cool!

Of course this game is all about "angst, sadness, frustration, anger and emotional distress". So I am not complaining that Davids game is something it is not intended to be. I am also truly thankful to Meguey Baker for giving us this phrase. It is very helpful, for a lot of players, gm's and designers.

But the paragraph in FMA got me thinking. It set up two opposites:
- I will not abandon you
vs
- Nobody gets hurt

How about not abandoning players who aims for happiness, love in relationships, strong family-ties, etc. Will they be met with understanding and supportive play, or will they be laughed at for being so naiive?

I'm asking for two reasons:

1 - The "cool" stuff seems to be delving into brutality, cynicism, violence and crime. Why?

2 - I've experienced and seen other become laughingstock for trying to create tender scenes. Why?

    \n Jesse: thanks! To have players being romantic idealistic poetic characters all the time is one solution to what I perceive to be a problem in a lot of classical role-playing; the tendency to shy away from any tenderness or romance.

    Eero: most classical rpg's may be said to focus on "misery for it's own sake"; Vampire, D&D, Warhammer, etc. The focus of these games are violence, and the players growing up with this focus is the ones most inclined to consider you a "softie" if you propose other themes in your play.

    I do know that the slogan "I will not abandon you" is not directed at the typical classical rpg in the first place, but I am of a mind to discuss it in that context. In my view it has as much merit there as anywhere.

    Mind you: I do not blame Warhammer for having a violent focus, or players for playing up on it. None of these games are at fault for doing what they do. None are at fault for this. It is just a phenomenon with consequences. We as a community are cheated and left with less than we could have had, due to this being such a predominant phenomenon in our gaming.

    What i am talking about is how the predominance of violence in games lead some/many* players to abandon each other in scenes of more tender qualities. This problem has been with me in one form or other since the 80s, when I made the naive, poetic rpg Muu in answer to it. Even though much have changed since then, most is still the same for the majority of players. The predominance of games like D&D and Vampire gives the typical players little leeway from harsh brutality. They are "educated" to play up on cynisism, brutality and violence. In my view this manifest in abandonment (amongst other things) towards players that dare to introduce anything soft and serious in these game-groups.

    * how widespread this problem is, is of course something we may disagree on. We really have no way of knowing without proper staticstical analysis. So we may leave that debate dead. Let us for the sake of discussion accept that the problem is real, and look for explanations and/or solutions.

    \n Kaare: any initiative to play on tenderness, within these games or by proposing new games that includes it, has been and will continue to be met with frowns by a lot of players. In some situations I propose (and have experienced) that this reluctance translate into abandonment of the player in question. That is: the player is met with possible hurtful and cynical reactions not warranted by the initiative itself.

    Possible line of play: Roger plays a warrior in a fantasy rpg. He visits his hometown and seeks up his mum. He initiates a really tender moment in his approach to the scene, and the GM introduces his brutal father to end the "dangerous" tendency, to the cheers of the other players. Roger had not realized up until this point that he has really missed such opportunities in this "you may do anything"-game, and are really surprised by the hurt he feels over being "snubbed" like this.

    Of course we may explain this as a bunch of young men with brutal behavior, but there is always the possibility that they are educated by the game to react this way. At last we may observe that in our subculture the opposite is much more rare; that a player is snubbed due to him choosing a brutal/cynical/violent approach to the scenes. If, for instance, a player where to play the character meeting his mother in a cynical way, it may well be cheered as \xfcber-cool by the same group. Again: this may be due to the group being immature, but I have a suspicion it is as much induced by the game itself.

    My claim is that the dominating consensus of a brutal/cynical/violent narrative in the most popular games, leads to a lot of abandonment of players who wish to express other sensibilities in their characters. Players are susceptible to the games they are playing. The principles for our immersion in the interactive system is laid in the game-rules, and as such I believe it will influence the social behavior of immature players around the table.

    This would have been less of a problem if the brutal/cynical/violent-games where not so dominant. If the field where softened by popular games with other themes, even the gamers in the brutal games would be less inclined to abandon a fellow player on such grounds. It would help players to accept that indeed any notion or initiative may be fruitful in a role-playing game, if treated with respect.

    \n You've got a lot of strong cultural forces working against genuine emotional engagement with games... especially vulnerable/"soft" emotion.

    1. RPG culture is overwhelmingly masculine, so you have gender constructions that don't permit expression of emotions
    2. RPG players are often young adults or adolescents, so emotional expression is often difficult due to a limited palette of experiences
    3. RPG play groups are rarely durable, robust social environments in which there is or can be an expectation of emotional support and "safe space"
    4. Related to 3, one of the most common social contract elements that evolves in lasting groups is a variation on "no politics, no religion, nothing controversial or emotional"
    5. Fiction-making in RPGs tends to work best when it's not subtle or nuanced. Fiction construction relies on tropes and cliche, and thus it can be difficult to present genuine emotional content without it looking forced or fake.

    I've always felt that "I Will Not Abandon You" works best as an explicit commitment made by a group of players, rather than a broad aesthetic, design principle, or manifesto. It may be that certain games only work well when that commitment is made, though, just as there are others where it is actively destructive.

    Emotion is hard to communicate in art in general. Representation is easier. The risk of failure and misunderstanding is much more personal and much more severe when working with content that is meaningful and resonant to players on a real-world level than it is when playing with genre trappings and flash-bang-smash. Real Nazis and Dinosaurs are a whole different ball of wax than pulp Nazis and Dinosaurs.

    \n Hi, folks; I wish I'd gotten here sooner (you Euros need to switch to US time and start posting late night!).

    First, the game link directly to FMA is here: http://davidartman.com/games/for-mature-audiences.

    To clarify one thing about the game (for what it's worth) that folks might need to realize for MY treatment of IWNAY: you are, in effect, playing yourself. Your "PC" is an idealized representation of what you want to be, envisioned in a plush doll or stuffed animal or similar. Your "angst and pain" will come in because you Scar this cute ideal with a Sharpie pen, drawing what look like tattoos that symbolize the real-life issue(s) with which you want to struggle in the narrative. Mechanically, those are the points where your Plushie is stabbed with a knitting needle to be introduced into a scene (and from which stuffing is ripped by a crochet hook, to represent the Damage of disengaging from a scene with the issue unresolved--you gotta pick at the scab SOME, or it gets ripped open by someone else).

    Anyway, I don't want to derail the real thread point (to which I reply below), but I think recognizing the intentional push on players to prod what hurts and shames them most is KEY to recognizing how seriously I take IWNAY as The Prime Directive of the game (I repeat it ad nauseum, according to one reviewer). But does that mean FMA is all and only angst and pain. Fuck no, not if it's played fully. Quite the opposite: I imagine moments of serious empathy, sorrow for others, joy at seeing someone break through a painful issue and become stronger--all KINDS of hippie, softtie, crying jag stuff (I get a bit misty just writing about it here). If all one does in FMA is grind and gripe and face-stab, one is totally missing the point and, frankly, being abusive, regardless of the IWNAY Prime Directive. It's a valley, not a plateau: you slide and fall and sometimes trudge down IN ORDER TO get through it, climb out, and stand taller at the other side; you don't crest to some height of masochism and see how long you can hold that feeling in the thin air.


    Second, Meguey's article is quoted in toto (with some... edits... for clarity), with her permission, in the aforementioned sidebar. Of course, direct discussion of it in context as a standalone article could well be done via comments to her blog. But, LO, here she is.

    My understanding (and how I use it in FMA) is that IWNAY is a social contract, an agreement between players that lines WILL be crossed, veils WILL be pulled back, and yet no one will back off emotionally or react with stigma and judgment to other's doing so. Conversely, NOGH play is when you have those lines and veils conversations prior to play and, nope, no one will cross them or unveil them and, thus, no one gets hurt by having their noses rubbed in something they abhor (and can justly leave or react with righteous judgment when that happens). To directly answer Thomas's first line of inquiry: if you're playing IWNAY, you DO NOT GET ABANDONED, period, no matter whether you're raping puppies or composing an impromptu ode to the beauty of an NPC beau (or another PC!). And, conversely, the freedom to push, by others, doesn't equate to the freedom to be abusive: to mock is to abandon, in my opinion, because it perforce requires a "me versus you" stance, so I can start pointing my cruel fingers at your soft underbelly.

    But neither is a One True Way, nor a manifesto, nor a design element (excepting in my own usage of IWNAY as the keystone to support the rest of play). ANY game can be played IWNAY or NOGH (except FMA and others of similar seriousness and intentional risk-taking), even though most games are played NOGH by tradition or due to the players being strangers or due to one or two assholes who can't tolerate being pushed at all (the second is what makes con games rather staid; the first and third are what typically prevent local groups from pushing the envelope). We've had Lines and Veils discussions prior to playing DitV, for goodness sake--is there any game which has as much in the rules about pushing players' actual morals and beliefs, through conflicts and the mechanics of escalation? But, yep, we had the L&V talk:I said no child rape--softie that I am--and, of course, the GM threatened children in every way possible OTTHER than such horror, because I showed him the doorway to pushing me. Had we gone IWNAY, he coulda thrown me right into an Urutsigadoji-on-kids scene and I'd have had to stay in it (shooting the shit out of everything in sight, probably) and not abandon him. (There's some AP to come down from theory clouds.)

    \n Third, I agree with Meguey that the proclivity in gaming (and stories) is to circle around then finally engage in CONFLICT, and conflict is typically recognized when (in fiction) a character is hurt, attacked, denied, shunned. The resolution of conflict could well be the "softie" results that folks seem to find so rare (me, not so much--a LOT of my adult play has explored love, commitment, honor, joy). But the coda is never the climax, and an intrinsically negative conflict source has an intrinsically negative climax. It's rare that the violent villain is "talked down" from his doomsday device by his loving mother or wife. He's shot down by the rugged hero (who then gets the loving girl and a nice smooch--there's yer LOVE and JOY, wimp! In the coda.).

    But, no, I do not agree whatsoever that softie play is not possible in RPGs. Now, many RPGs don't seem to encourage it with tone or mechanics; others do quite a bit (see "Ardor" in Pendragon). I'd even argue that FMA is MUCH more about the loving results, but only because trusting and loyal friends gather to help each other to that place, by exposing and (hopefully) purging things which bring them significant sorrow. If you don't love your fellow players in FMA, you're going to be VERY hard pressed not to abandon them at some trying point. This is all to say, yet again, that the game text and artifact will set the tone and point of play; and drifting amongst NOGH players is done at one's own risk. We sit down to play Champions, the book has already "programmed" us for biff-bam-boom action... and then here you come wanting to have a smooch scene or cry on my cape about how your father's coldness pushed you into crime fighting? Dude, Seg Twelve!


    Finally, to catch up to the drift of the thread, I think the adolescent desire to explore the dark is part and parcel with their difficulty expressing emotions (I, for one, don't think that's male-exclusive, though females have different means of learning how to do so, and do so quicker). Look at the transitions these poor fuckers have to go through:
    * They no longer can cry and moan if some minor slight occurs--that's been stigmatized out of them years earlier. "Stop acting like a baby!"
    * And yet, hormonally, they are awash in the most powerful emotions--some of them BRAND NEW--they've ever felt. "Girls are weird!"
    * Intergender (of, if you prefer, inter-attractional) relations are going through a complete sea change, literally flipping on their head. "Mmmm... boobies."
    * And yet, they're STILL not "old enough" to really get off--to face, address, express, and release these crazy new desires and urges. "Wait until you're older; don't be a slut."

    Given that, is it any wonder the poor bastards try to find channels of expression where they can vent and then back off and say, "Oh, it's just a game." Is it any wonder this demographic is what makes most horror flicks profitable? Is it any wonder they don't have the tools to explore a sensitive and mature treatment of themes of love and honor and sharing... hell, even simple sensuality free of raw sexuality? As Mark points out, it's hard enough for adults, so the "representation" is the cheap and quick solution to get through a weekly session of entertaining gaming.

    Sure, that's a bit of armchair psychology, but it seems to answer Thomas's queries (in their various permutations) to me.
