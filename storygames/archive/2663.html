\n Had our first Spycraft 2.0 session last night, with 2 guys who play in my DnD 3.5 game but only started RPGs in the last year, one guy who hasn't played anything in decades, and one guy who had never played before at all. We've all worked together, and we had lots of fun.

That said, the system is killing me - I consider myself a fairly well-versed d20 player, but the level of detail and mechanics they've piled on top of d20 is DAUNTING. Everyone was fine with me saying "Ok, we don't have time to do things their way, so how about we do this part this way..." and making something up. Everyone was helpful and contributing to eachothers' fun, so it all worked, but I just thought I'd ask anyone out there running it - how do you cope with the system complexity? I feel like it'll take some seriously dedicated study to take it all in.

    \n andy - the awesome stuff exists, but my prep time limitations kept me from incorporating it. The 'awesome stuff' that I've seen talked about:

    - Dramatic Conflict cards - potential for much coolness; but like M:tG Cards, each is really introducing new rules that add mechanics. They'd be just as cool with just the titles that let you conceal your maneuver until both you and your opponent have decided maneuvers, then just incorporate both into the actions that get described that round.

    - NPC creation system is streamlined from standard d20, and the chapter includes advice like 'start with a motivation' that shouldn't be news to anyone here. I didn't use this though, just made up stats for my mooks on the fly, by fiat.

    - There's TONS of gear, and so for gear-oriented people that's Big Fun. For my players, it was overwhelming. The game has an 'intel phase' after a mission has been given but before its start, where there's intricate rules about picking gear for your characters based on 'Mission Caliber' and stuff like that. We did it by me laying out the mission and asking them "What do you want?" then saying Yes or No to their various requests (I think the only one I said no to was explosives).

    \n The gear system is enhanced greatly if you put gear on index cards with the cost. Then you can just pass them out for each mission. Don't try to write up all the cards at once, just have people fill them out with the guns and whatnot they're taking this time, then it goes back into the pile. That will also help with people finding "guns they like" which is, naturally, the way James Bond does things.

    "That looks like a woman's gun."
    "Do you know a lot about guns, Mr. Bond?"
    "No, but I know a little about women."
