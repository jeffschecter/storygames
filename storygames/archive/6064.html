\n Here's an example of a vanilla setting: stock D&D Tolkein-esqe fantasy. As a product of years of D&D play, I can run a fantasy oneshot and take a lot of setup for granted.

(To be clear: there's a *lot* wrong with only having access to such settings, or being limited to them. That said, they have a utility, and are an interesting cultural artifact besides!)

So: what other "vanilla" settings are there or almost exists, the sort that you could sit down with almost any set of players and cobble it together?

Myself, I'm wondering if there's a good sci-fi equivalent.

    I'm not sure if you are looking for Vanilla rules or Vanilla settings.

    \n

    Whilst I might agree that Greyhawk or Forgotten Realms are fairly Vanilla setting I would have to say that Eberron, RavenLoft, Planescape and DragonLance fall a little short of being vanilla settings. However there is a bigger problem in that stock D&D rules imply a lot of 'setting' within them. You will probably find that reason you can run quick one shots isn't that its a vanilla environment, but due to the fact that such a high proportion of roleplayers are familiar with D&D.

    \n

    In fact for one offs I think what you are looking for is familiarity. In fact its possible that when you said 'vanilla' you were actually ment familiar rather than plain.

    \n


    As such it you are looking for rules systems suitable for quick one offs, which can be picked up quickly and played quickly I'd suggest you want:

    \n
      \n
    • Something well known/popular, so there is a high probility that many of your players will be familiar with the rules
      • \n
    • Something with simple rules
      • \n
    • Something with a familiar background, or a background similar to something they will be familiar with
    \n

    A quick off the cuff short list might include:

    \n
      \n
    • Generic High Fantasy - I'd use D&D or True 20
      • \n
    • Modern Dark Horror - Call of Cthulhu - Everyone knows something about the 1930s (you don't need to introduce the background)
      • \n
    • Space Opera - I'd use Classic Traveller even though its probably more Hard Sci-Fi than Space Opera - Simple rules, Easy to understand background and well known
      • \n
    • 1920/1930 Pulp - Spirit of the Century, Adventure!, or Hollow Earth
      • \n
    • Superhero - I'd go with Mutants and Masterminds 2nd Edition, Truth & Justice or Wild Talents. If you can get your hands on it Golden Heroes works well too.
    \n


    The other thing perfect for running one-offs is franchise spin-offs. If you run a Buffy game 9 chances out of 10 all your players will have seen atleast one episode and need no introduction.

    \n

    Good games here include Buffy, Angel, BattleStar Galactica, Dr Who (FASA, Timelord or the new one coming out this year), Stargate, Star Trek and Star Wars etc. None are even remotely 'Vanilla' but they are well enough known that the settings could be considered clich\xe9s.

    \n


    For one offs I often find the best settings are real world history, famous novels or films. You can actually evoke an entire setting by saying something a simple as: "You are Musketeers defending Louis XIII", "You are late 19th Century London-based detectives", "You are Surgeons work at a Mobile Army Surgical Hospital during the Korean War", "Its November 4, 1872. Tomorrow you set sail from Staten Island, New York to Genoa, Italy. Your ship is the Mary Celeste." or "You are Texas National Guard in Waco Texas. Its May 3rd 1993 - two weeks after the seige". No need for any setting books - just a simple sentence that evokes it all.

    \n

    Then pick a rule system that works:\xa0

    \n
      \n
    • I'll use Amber, Risus or something similarly simple for pretty much anything.\xa0
      • \n
    • Spirit of the Century works well for anything pulpy or swashbuckling.\xa0
      • \n
    • Mutants and Mastermind works well for low-grit settings with high end powers.
      • \n
    • Wild Talents works well for gritty games with low end powers.
      • \n
    • Call of Cthulhu work well for modern games with normal protagonist.
      • \n
    • Buffy work well for modern horror games with powered protagonist.
      • \n
    • True 20\xa0is a standby if I cant find a system thats better suited
    \n

    And now I wonder how far I've wondered from the original subject..sorry

    \nSurely it depends on the players. The only reason that you can sit down with "almost any" set of players and cobble together a stock D&D Tolkien-esque fantasy is that most RPG players are quite familiar with D&D. That wouldn't work at all with the players from my current SotC group, since most of them haven't played D&D (or two played it decades ago).

    A vanilla setting in how you define it is any well-tread genre that the players are familiar with. So nearly all of my group were fine with Buffy the Vampire Slayer because almost everyone (except Liz) had watched the series. Liz would be much better with a Western setting rather than a D&D-ish setting, though she does know Tolkien.
