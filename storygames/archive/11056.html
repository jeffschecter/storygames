    \n Most of the Eighth Sea games I've run have ended up working toward a story like this...

    Players go back in time to disrupt reality in the past in order to change the timestream to their advantage, once they claim a prize from an alternate timeline they have to go back and rework the events back to their former "correct" versions for fear of wiping themselves out in a quantum paradox.

    Or...

    Players are forced to correct an issue in the timeline that threatens to wi[e out the version of existence that they know and love. They must then correct the events without inserting themselves into historical events too heavily. For example, a common convention demo of the game involves assassinating Abraham Lincoln when an incident in the past prevents his assassination. The character must find out why he wasn't assassinated, ensure he does get assassinated, and ensure someone looking like John Wilkes Booth does the job.

    \n It seems to me that D&D is only circular in the same sense an anthology-style game is circular, not in the more extreme (old-form) sitcom sense. There is a continuity in a game of D&D or In a Wicked Age: even if the start condition is more or less the same (or made up of elements randomly selected by the same process as last time) there is still a shared history of previous sessions. In some anthology-style games building that shared history, rather than the individual stories, may even be the point.

    Most TV shows today do not start with blank-slate characters each week, even sitcoms. Take Scrubs, as one example: while each episode can be taken atomically and enjoyed as such, there is an additional benefit for the loyal viewers as they see characters morph in slight but noticeable ways.

    From what I gather, RPGA games are more "blank slate" by necessity since the players do not necessarily share memories of past adventures.

    \n So you'll want a game in which
    * There is no concept of "advancement" or "experience" or "fallout" or ANY means of changing what a given PC is or can do.
    * There is either no reoccurring source of conflict OR there is such a source but it can never be thwarted.
    * Non-reoccurring sources of conflict can not have significant potential impact on PCs OR it is assured that they won't succeed if they could have such impact.

    That doesn't leave many vanilla RPGs, because so many of them springboard off the general concepts of advancement, playing a hero's quest, or taking risk for reward. take away advancement, a defeatable enemy, and meaningful consequences from significant risks... what games are left? Toon and similar ilk, maybe?

    \n When reading "Sorcerer & Sword", one of the major take-aways for me was the Destiny choice "Unchanging Star":\n
    The character has a role or identity in the scheme of things that will never change, regardless of the conflicts thrown at him. He provides catalysis and commentary in the face of surrounding events, and moves on.
    \nPrior to that point I'd always thought that the point of stories in role-playing games was, not plot exposition, but character transformation. Playing with players who did not appear interested in having their characters be challenged to change was very frustrating to me (still is, in a lot of cases). But after pondering why Sorcerer, of all games, would include "Unchanging Star" as a Destiny choice I think I grasp this better - some characters in some stories are just not meant to change. These are typically not protagonists, as perfectly static characters are more difficult to empathize with. I imagine comedy is a special case, as the purpose is not necessarily to emotionally engage the audience but rather to entertain them, often on a purposely shallow level.

    Don't know exactly how this relates, but if all you want is integrity of character, I think most games could support this as long as they do not place emphasis on character advancement. Spirit of the Century easy; Dogs in the Vineyard hard. In a Wicked Age decent; DnD 4E tricky.

    PCs (the cops) are presented with a mystery.
