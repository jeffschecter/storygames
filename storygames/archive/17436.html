\n A friend of mine is the only girl in an all guy group. That's not important here, but what is is that during one session she had her character flirt with a guard to try and get past him, or something similar. The group told her she did a terrible job flirting, which wasn't nice, but the dice said quite definitively that she had achieved her objective.

Now there is a lot of discussion out there about whether the primary element to determine outcomes should be game mechanics or player ability, which I will for this discussion ignore. Some games tell the GM to give bonuses for good roleplaying too, but I don't like that either, because what IS good roleplaying? And I don't like games where all the roleplaying can be replaced with a die roll + token description.

The thing is that if you consider BOTH types of input equally, something interesting emerges: It creates fiction and complications! For example, IF the guard let her pass, AND she did a terrible job of flirting, then WHY did he let her pass? Was it because he found the clumsy flirting cute? Is it because he's happy that any woman at all is bothering to talk to him? Is it because he's on to her and leading the team into a trap? The intersection between what the dice say and what the player does is actually a rich reservoir of ideas I rarely see tapped.

The resistance to this idea comes entirely from players who don't like the idea of character actions creating 'facts'. Personally, I prefer to think of it as 'revealing' facts nobody knew, including the GM. And this idea doesn't exclude the possibility of having established and/or secret facts only the GM has say over either, though this does little to sway the resistance's staunchest supporters.

The problem I have with it is that it doesn't allow for tactics. A player can't take facts into consideration to gain leverage because those facts don't 'exist' yet. But perhaps this is also something that does not exclude the possibility of such advantages. In fact, perhaps establishing facts can be part of the game and used to make things more or less possible.

But the core problem though is how is the WHY revealed in the first place? If the guard is afraid of women, then they may act a certain way, but how does the character know that being afraid of women is the reason for the action? This has stumped me for literally the past three years, and I'm dearly hoping Metatopia provides the lateral thinking I need to finally resolve it. And it's a curious thing, but I'd say a significant portion of the facts in the fiction are communicated between the Player and GM, and not through the character's observations, even in 'immersive' games.

Finally, a system like this needs a clear division between action and intent. In other words, the player must be able to express the intent separately from their character's action. I find this troublesome because the difference between action and intent in RPGs is entirely arbitrary. Is there a more or less effective way to divide them? Most RPGs for example divide an attack into a 'take your shot' action and 'hit your target' intent, but you could just as easily treat 'taking your shot' as the intent, and 'readying your weapon' as the action. I think how you choose to divide things goes a long way to defining how your game feels and what it's about.

And if any of these ideas weren't clear, join the club. This is still very much a "I know its there because I see its impact/potential but I haven't quite figured out how to put it in a bottle yet" for me too. I will also end on a completely unrelated note by saying that I find it surprising yet not shocking that Jared Sorensen is friends with people I'm friends with outside of gaming. Why it's surprising is probably a sad testament to my inability to believe that game designers such as myself do anything BUT game design, in a small underground office, where they will be undistracted by things such as sunlight or oxygen.

    Finally, a system like this needs a clear division between action and intent. In other words, the player must be able to express the intent separately from their character's action.
    \nWhat you are talking about is actually how the game mechanic is expressed in the world. There are normally two ways to handle this.

    intent --> description --> mechanic --> consequence

    The player give away her intent through a description, then she use the mechanic to see if it has any effect (success or failure) and lastly how it turns out. Usually the GM tells the consequence, no matter if the action is successful or a failure. This is usually how doll house games works. Another way to handle it could be:

    intent --> mechanic --> description

    The player declare her intent, then use the mechanic and after that describe how the intent and mechanic works together to create an effect in the world. With this method, it's the outcome of the mechanic that choose who gets to do the description of the action. If it fails, the GM describes and if it's a success, the player describes. This is more of a collaborate storytelling.

    ---

    So at least two things are something you have to consider:
    - Your game style. Doll house or collaborate?
    - The game style affects how you use the mechanics: should the description be before (doll house) the mechanic or after (collaborate)?

    It's a matter of taste of what you use, but you shouldn't mix it together.

    \n Very interesting topic. This is one of the reasons why I removed the following things from my game (mostly)\u2026

    Social Attributes
    Mental Attributes

    well\u2026\xa0to hell\u2026\xa0I removed all the classic attributes.

    I still have tangible actions to affect the world, but I always thought social/mental skills were a very problematic point for player characters. I have played in groups that have allowed unskilled characters do all the talking, because the player was a brilliant speaker. And I've seen socially skilled characters run into one faux pas after another because the players were incapable of the finer nuances in socialisation.

    There are some systems out there that try to remove those aspects of the game, so players are free to play however they want. I'm not saying this is an elegant approach, but it tries to be fair. Players invest points in skills, and they should get something out of it. If you build a socializing character, but are clumsy in your player skill, I think it shouldn't matter too much. It has been said by others too: no one expects the player of a fighter to take fencing classes.

    Why are we comfortable with combat rules, and unwilling to apply similar abstractions in other cases?

    If planning (problem solving, mental skills) and dialogue are suddenly a matter of skill points, they better be worth it. Others (lacking those skills) should not get their way because they have a player skill.

    After all, that's not great storytelling either.

    Hope my thoughts aren't too confusing.
