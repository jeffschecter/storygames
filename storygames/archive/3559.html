\n I'm reading Beast Hunters at the moment: an interesting game that, as part of the rules, suggest you shake hands before and after the game. It says this is an "integral part of the game" and is not optional.

    \n It's not a case of "more important" really, more a case of "what I'm willing to be told by a game text".

    A game's text consists of rules and instructions. Both are important, but not the same.

    Rules explain the games mechanics, and I'm willing to be told anything about the game's mechanics in plain old instructional terms: Roll a die, Draw a card, Add a bonus etc etc.

    Instructions tell me how to play the game. It's the extra stuff that moves it from a set of rules which govern play into a guide for how to actually re-create the play experience the author had in mind when he wrote that text.

    When I read the rules for Settlers Of Catan, it tells me to put all the cards off to one side and let one player act as banker. When we play, we tend to put all the cards where everyone can get them and let everyone take their own cards. That works just fine for us. It's an instruction and we decided to do things differently. It changes the experience of playing the game, but not by much.

    My play experience does not/will not involve doing a funny handshake. So I won't do one. That means that my play experience will be different from the one Christian and Lisa intended when they wrote the rules, but that's OK, I know that.

    (I have other issues with the game too, but this isn't really the place to discuss them and I haven't really read the text thoroughly enough to really comment on the other stuff.)

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarOK, sounds you know an objectionable "you must" statement in a body of rules when you see it. Fair enough.
    \nYeah, it's difficult to come up with a definition of what's acceptable and what's not. The more I think about it, the more I'm coming to the conclusion that whenever you come up with a mechanic or instruction which is outside the norm, you run the risk that someone (in this case, me) will say "No thanks, that's not what it's all about". For example, a lot of gamers consider character stance immersion to be pretty much the be all and end all of roleplaying. For them, any system or mechanic which forces them out of that state is completely unacceptable. Another school of thought give the GM absolute authority over everything and will not, under any circumstances, entertain the idea that the GM is not the final word of law. For them, the idea that a player might be able to exert any authority over the game world that the GM cannot veto is unacceptable, and so they have no interest in playing games where that is possible (or when they do, they drift them or ignore rules so that it's not).

    For me, restrictions on what character I can play are mostly fine, they're usually pretty much the point of the game. Like you can't play a Samurai in Agon or a Greek warrior in The Mountain Witch. No sweat. When the Aliens RPG (all those years ago) lacked rules for creating anything except Colonial Marine characters, that was pants because that wasn't how the game was advertised. (That was also very far from the only problem with that game though)

    Off the top of my head, I think that it's reasonable for a game to tell me to:

    Roll dice
    Add up numbers
    Draw cards
    Write things down
    Assign points to things
    Move counters around
    Speak

    There are other things I'm sure I've overlooked, but you get the gist of what I mean. Anything outside of that list is novel and will be considered on it's own merits. So Dread's Jenga mechanics are taken on their merits and would probably be OK. Graham's joke game about punching people wouldn't be OK. I also wouldn't be up for a game where the player's physical skill made any difference to the outcome of anything. So, while I like playing Jungle Speed, I don't really get invested in it like I need to to make an RPG work (because I don't really care if I win at Jungle Speed, I'm just there for the chaos).

    \n Hi

    There are a couple of reasons why we wrote that the salute is mandatory, and some of them have already been discussed above. Let me quickly go through them.

    For one, it's an "unrule" action, as Robin Laws calls it. Gamers are used to treating mechanical rules as somewhat set in stone, at least for the first couple of sessions until they almost inevitably start houseruling and converting stuff. They don't feel the same way about "rituals" or other parts of the rules that are not as familiar. Hence, a reminder that this isn't just a gimmick, but an actual rule. I felt like I needed to make something explicit that, otherwise, would be viewed through the lens of already established practices.

    Second, yes, I think it adds something important to the experience. The reasons, as was mentioned, are outlined in the text. You may think the salute is one of the minor rules in the game. I think it's one of the major ones. Like Jake indicated, it establishes a certain line of communication as well as style of play. Everything else in the game follows from communication and play style. If your group already has those firmly, explicitly established, then this rule becomes much less important. Most groups that I've been in didn't.

    Third, most indie games aim to be Narrativist, while this one's a profoundly Gamist design. That means the explicit establishing of play styles is not only aimed at traditional gamers, but also at indie gamers who are used to Narrativist games. They need to know what to expect and what not to expect.

    Fourth, one of the reasons it says that in there is because I myself sometimes forgot it in the heat of playtesting or demoing. When I ran the game at the first Go Play PDX at Jake's house, for example, I totally didn't do it. (And the game still was great fun! In part that's because I as the designer instinctively communicate and run the game according to its playstyle.) So this is a way of making sure people think about it and keep it in their minds. Because it's the game opener, it's not like a rule you can just look up during the game, like most others. By putting in a strong statement, at least you're going to think and talk about it and therefore not overlook it

    That all said: you can certainly have fun with the game without it. Plenty of people do. And I'm not going to say you're doing anything wrong. If you end up not liking your play experience, however, I'm going to look for that as one of the things that could have gone wrong, exactly because of what I said in above about establishing communication, shared play style, and expectations.

    I also totally support people altering the game to fit their own needs. On RPG.net, The Eye has already changed the Strike rule to make it more deadly. More power to him. Shawn is converting it to a pirate setting and changing all kinds of rules, and I hope it works out and he enjoys it. I just won't be able to guarantee that all the parts work together like they do now.

    And that's all there is to it, really. I hope you enjoy the game, and feel free to do with it whatever you want, armed with the knowledge of why we created it the way we did.

    Oh, and I'm definitely interested in more comments--and Rich, if you find the time to voice your other issues somewhere, I'd be happy to talk about those too.
