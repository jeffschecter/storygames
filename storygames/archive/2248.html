Enjoying your own PCs internal conflicts

jhkim
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
So in the thread Meshing external conflict to multiple internal conflicts, we're looking at the goal of meshing conflicts -- i.e. creating a separate external conflict to mesh with the internal one. I'd like to discuss a bit about more generally the place of internal conflicts. I think this cuts to the heart of what I talk about in Story and Narrative Paradigms in RPGs.

Here's the short form: Within a play, internal conflicts are only visible by externalization (or rarely by voice-over / soliloquy). Within a book, internal conflicts are visible by both externalization and narration (i.e. authorial voice). Within an RPG, internal conflicts are potentially visible by externalization, narration, and direct understanding. i.e. If I'm playing a character engaged in a struggle, I can see the internal conflict.

Now, by imitation of plays and films, we can try to bring out the internal to external action as much as possible.

However, there's another level to internal conflicts -- i.e. the ones which you can directly see, the ones which your PC experiences. My question is, what most enhances enjoyment of these? Note that this is different than externalization. I can enjoy a conflict without it being externalized -- but that doesn't mean that it's automatic. It is still enhanced by external play.

For example, in one game, my PC fell in love. He was conflicted over how to approach the woman, though, because she was an immortal goddess who had suddenly become a mortal human -- and was in some senses an innocent whom the PCs were protecting. Now, he was fairly clear in his attraction, but I found out later that the other players completely misread him -- thinking that he was just lusting after her, when really he had romantic ambitions and was waiting for the right time to propose to her.

The thing is, that I enjoyed the conflict right then. It was interesting to me regardless of what happened later. My question (which I don't know the answer to) is what enhances that enjoyment.

I know that believability issues can kill it, for example, but that's only a negative answer.

    Actually, John, I see Josh's reaction as a contrasting experience to the one you're describing. From the right perspective it might help throw your topic into relief.

    Josh is describing, I think, the experience of playing with someone else who invents and dwells on their internal conflict, doesn't share it at all with the table, and is possibly concealing his contribution from the GM for fear that it'll be stomped on. In a lot of ways, he is describing maladaptive behavior. This one player is, in essence, playing his own little game with the assistance of everyone else. Taking but not giving.

    I presume that it's also an assessment made from an author stance. When you expect everyone to be describing their characters motivations, and riffing off one another, it's almost a betrayal to conceal part of that presentation.

    The phenomenon John is after, I think, is the experience of playing a character with a strong internal conflict - usually unexpected. For instance, a character with an explicit stand on a moral question presented with a dilemma that brings the stand into doubt. (I'm thinking of Luke's advice on running Burning Stuff: "Yeah, well, do you believe that now?") There's a decided enjoyment to chewing over that conflict and acting on it, and there can be a secondary enjoyment to being at the table with a player who's doing that, since they tend to have their characters behave in consistent but surprising ways.

    I tend to think that the enjoyment of an internal conflict this way relies on more of an actor's stance. As the internal conflict of an author's character, it's more enjoyable to say "hey guys, check this out!" But for an actor's character there's enjoyment to wrestling with the conflict for a little while as a result. Between sessions or between scenes it might be worthwhile to step out and say "hey, here's what I'm dealing with," but even that would depend on the table.

    I don't know if this will help anyone else, but I am a bit confused by the "direct understanding" category. How am I achieving this direct understanding, if not by observing my character's external actions or through an ongoing internal narration on behalf of my character? It seems to me that at the beginning of a game all players in an RPG should have the same level of access to a character's internal conflicts -- none of them share a psychological space with the character by default, and so they all have just as much of an ability to extrapolate the character's internal state. What one player (the player playing that character) has is authority to determine what internal narrative is correct for the character, and every time the player makes such a determination without informing the other players they are that much further from being able to create their own equally legitimate internal narration for that character.

    Maybe this is just me chasing the usual 'what is immersion' trail, but that's not my intention. It's worth pointing out that, following from the above, I do -- as a player -- come up with internal narrative for any and all characters that I am interested in during a play session. This includes other people's PCs. And, as mentioned, my version of what their character is feeling is just as accurate as theirs is, until such a point as they externalise their version either through action or through narration. The more often they do that, the easier it is for me to play along -- but if they never narrate any specific internal states, I still have a fairly easy time of it up until they have their character perform an action that clearly shows how differently we're understanding the character. At this point I am surprised, and possibly either disappointed or delighted -- or just confused, since I can't figure out where our two versions of the character diverged.

    So I guess my feeling is that all players at the table have access to this 'direct apprehension,' and furthermore that it is not limited to RPGs in the least -- we can do the same thing when reading a story, simply by thinking about the characters and empathising with them outside of whatever the author is specifically narrating. Same goes for any sort of fiction. The author may have his own internal model based on facts that do not yet exist in the fiction, but that doesn't seem all that relevant to the legitimacy of everyone else's opinions about what is really going on inside the character.

Inasmuch as the group cares about characters having internal states and psychological motivations, it's my job to 'run' that part of my character. Part of what I was pointing out is just that anyone could run it just as well as me, but maybe this is just a case of definition: part of what makes it 'my character' is that I am the one paying attention to those extra bits and pieces.

    But as for 'what's happening in the game,' sometimes my character isn't in a scene, or doesn't really have much going on in a session even. In that case it's quite likely that I am spending more time imagining what's going on in other PC's heads than I am in my PC's head. Pointing out that anyone can do this 'work' and anyone can enjoy it equally, 'directly', what-have-you is just to say that there is a mode of play where everyone has only the one PC, or where everyone has every PC in the scene -- from the point of view of this enjoyment. If you disassociate authority over a character's internal states with knowledge of those states, it becomes clear that everyone can get the enjoyment of this 'direct apprehension', of mulling over a character's conflicts and reactions and motivations and enjoying their own personal take on the character.

    Which doesn't mean that everyone should do this, or whatever. Annoying chess analogy aside, I agree with Corley that it's not somehow cruel or unusual to keep your take on your character to yourself. I just think it's entirely possible for everyone to participate in every character's internal conflicts and narrative, even if only one of them has the authority to decide how it ultimately plays out.

    Personally, I like to share my character's internal narrative as much as possible, because whenever I make something cool I want to show it off to other people. So if something really interesting happens 'in my character's head' I want to make sure that gets across somehow. If I'm feeling restrained or ambitious I definitely will consider trying to show, rather than tell -- finding a way to demonstrate my character's unique state of mind or take on the situation -- but just as often I might just spell it out. And in any case I will definitely want to share it at some point outside the game.

    But, to tie it back in, I also like to talk to other players outside the game about what I think their characters were feeling, or how I understood their actions, or what direction they seemed to be going in emotionally. And I want other players to tell me the same thing about my character -- in part because they might have some insight, and in part because that will tell me how effectively I am getting across what I want to with my portrayal of the character.
