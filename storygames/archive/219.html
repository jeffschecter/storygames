Okay, tried running PTA for the first time last night. And although it didnt entirely explode? I am not convinced it was really a quality beginning.

Our First Session.

Well, we were only able to get 4 hours set aside for the game. Which was already pushing it in my opinion. On top of that buy the time we got there late, got food taken care of. etc. we only had about 2.5 hours. I Decided we would focus solely on series and protagonist creation that night. Seeing as how we would not have time for the pilot anyways. I think this was mistake #1

First let me detail the players.

Sarah 29 Years old. Minored in Art in college. Never RP'd asside from stuff like Diablo.

Billie 25 years old. A few credits shy of a film degree and an aspiring director/script writer. Never RP'd aside from sessions of DnD when she was 12.

Tracy 28 years old. My Girlfriend, a professional artist. I have managed to drag her to a few games of Witchcraft RPG that she claims she enjoyed.

Del 30 years old. This is me, I am the producer. Im a tech geek.

Only one idea was put forth and it was not really "discussed" at all. And although it wasnt a bad idea, it didnt come up at all during our actual session. This did not bode well, but maybe they werent just the "email discussion" types?

    I'd second what Adam said.

    FWIW, my PTA pitch sessions have worked best when we've started with actual pitches, i.e. "It's a show about a haunted cruise ship - the main characters are the crew and there's a rotating cast of guests: think 'Lost' meets 'The Love Boat'." We'll toss one out, talk it over, dismiss it, and move onto the next pitch. After the fourth or fifth try, we're usually on the same wavelength, based on what we've liked about the rejected pitches and on the reasons why we ended up rejected them. I've had worse luck when we've started by simply listing things we did or did not want to see, without thinking about those things in the context of a specific show idea. (I know this has worked for other groups, but I find it a lot less organic).

    First thing:

    No, not a dismal failure; more likely a stumble. You're talking to people and willing to start anew -- those two things are huge positive signs for a successful game. (As opposed to the traditional "talking about what we're doing, let alone changing it, is a sign of weakness.")

    Second thing:

    "My instinct was to have her compromise...I should have looked to the other players to compromise."

    Compromise? Why? Ideally you want everyone on board instead of one person saying, "nah, not this one" and dropping out -- though even that's hardly the end of the world -- but if you try to satisfy everybody with a compromise nobody likes, you'll just lose everyone sooner or later. Get people to say outright what they like -- and then make it safe to say "no" to things that aren't compatible with each other:

    "Hey, I know you want to do X, but I don't see how it works with Y, and I really, really want Y -- so can you give up on X for this game? C'mon, it's only, what, five sessions."
