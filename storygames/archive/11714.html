\n I had an idea for a forum hack (not here, by the way--just an idea, maybe for my own GLASS forums someday):

Change the PHP code so that it doesn't show ANY identifying information about posters EXCEPT to the Moderators and Administrators.
* The whole "anonymity breeds asshattery" issue would be circumvented, because any abuse could still be directly addressed.
* Issues of social currency would diminish, hopefully allowing every post to be judged on its own merits, not by the poster (i.e. no more cults of personality or Karma scoring).
* It would become impossible to ignore someone (at least, using name-recognition scripts). Unless you, you know, read enough of a post to decide to ignore the rest (or scroll past Walls of Text).

Thoughts?

    \n Your idea puts the moderators into the position of policemen.

    Social currency is important for group survival. It curbs most problems already. Remove identity and I think you'll see more asshattery, not less, even if the moderators can see a person's identity.

    People would post ideas without any accountability. If they stay within the safe harbor of the rules of the forum, they can say any damned thing they want without fear of social repercussions. You seem to think this is a good thing, but I think it's awful.

    Anonymity creates a barrier to friendship. Friendship glues communities together.

    Your idea creates a forum of ideas disconnected from people, which is like some kind of autistic dream but it doesn't work well when you're trying to, you know, create a community of people.

    Posted By: Neko Ewen
    Having looked at /tg/ and 4chan in general off and on for a number of years, that's pretty much my attitude towards it. I don't know how much is the particular board's culture and how much isthe anonymity itself, but I don't see much going on in /tg/ that wouldn't happen here or on RPG.net except for lots of swearing and threadcrapping for me to filter out.
    Disclaimer: This is my personal theory, YMMV, IANAL, etc.

    IMO, the specific type of forum culture we see in /tg/ (As much as there is one) isn't something that would have evolved that way without annymity, and it's, I think, because of lack of accountability. 4chan is a place where you can post your bullshit without fear of (much) consequence, both from moderators and other users, at least as long as you stay withing VERY broad rules. While this makes for an ... "excentric" community, it also means the "Hmmm, is this really a good idea" barrier is much lower, so there's a whole load of bullshit there. Horrible, horrible bullshit. "The Abyss" style bullshit. But as 4chan plunges into the depths of human creativity, it also goes towards the other extreme, producing some of the most awesome stuff on the internet.\xb4, whether its weird pics involving cats or the genius that is 4chans 40k. (Thousand monkey, thousand typewriters kinda thing.)

    Another thing this lack of accountability engenders is lack of respect. Lack of respect towards each other and the rest of humanity in parts, which makes it a rough ride, but also lack of respect towards simple stuff like canon, established settings and so on. As with a lot of things in life, respect is a double edged sword. It's great to have, but it can also be another barrier.

    All in all, I doubt that 4chan could be the creative, free-for-all orgy/massacre it is without the default anonymity. And the world would be a more boring place without 4chan.

    That said, I'm not sure the theoretical forum established here would actually work. For one, I agree with other people that moderating it to actually be "ideas without authors" would be a nightmare. People really want to know who're the talking to and want other people to know them.

    But if you build it, you can be sure I'd join, because I'd love to try it.

      \n As long as Adam has dragged me into this, let me clarify something about what I was saying about the dissidents, in particular.

      First, my original point to Adam in the supposed privacy of my living room was that there's a distinction between anonymity and something like the non-attributability that you're talking about here. People like Iranian dissidents certainly want some degree of *anonymity*. But they may want to be able to build credibility as "AyatollahAssahollah666" or whatever.

      Second, there's an asymmetry in the situation. People using the internet to pass information about what's going on in, say, China, need personal credibility because you're *not* just assessing their ideas. My ideas about the quality of the Chinese political system might be just as good as a Chinese democracy activist's. But the activist needs you to believe that s/he is presenting accurate information about events - which has a lot to do with you believing that she is well-placed to observe them, and that's tied to having a particular identity and a track record of being right/truthful, even if not to his or her real name. So I'm not sure it's applicable here. The best analogy would be something like wanting to know that when someone says, "I played Apocalypse World and it suxxored," that they're not just making stuff up.

      That said, as a general point, I think it's easy to overestimate how well "ideas" can be divorced from things about the context and actual world. A paper of mine went through blind review at a journal, presumably so the ideas could be judged on their own. But in order to make the case for those ideas, I need to talk about things like what counterinsurgency warfare is like. Sure, I cite sources - but is it really totally irrelevant to, say, your judgment of how well I assess those empirical sources to know that I've never served in combat myself? I dunno - I think it might be relevant and something blind peer review would miss.

      \n So, Daniel, it would seem that credibility is an issue, the loss of which, with pure non-attribution, would lead to difficulty in judging statements presented?

      Again, I think that's true for many objective endeavors; but I do not believe that it is true for the largely subjective work that we do, both in design and through AP (play analysis). Furthermore, you very example of empirical sources actually helps problems of comprehension: if such sources are quoted, while posting some idea, then not only can they be reviewed but your use of them can be, too. And that will be non-biased--I won't need to know whether you've served in the armed forces, because I can read your attributed expert(s), decide on the degree to which I trust *them*, and then do rather basic logical processes to see if your use of their premises or conclusions is sound or applicable.

      Yet, if all I ever see is, "I served three tours in Afghanistan," then I face only an Argument from Authority--you might have been a base cook, for all I *know*. But any assertions you make from experience--true OR false!--I can judge based on their applicability to the ideas being presented. In a sense, any "truth" one conveys through non-contextual presentation (of authority or of experience) has to stand on its own and, as such, I think would be far more powerful than one which, ultimately, relies on "trust me, I know!" to carry any weight.

      Maybe I'm misinterpreting you? Or maybe I am too enamored of the notion of pure ideas and debate freed of the influence of reputation (good or bad).

      David: A few things:

      Yeah, the main part of the reason I clarified the comment of mine that Adam was paraphrasing was because I *don't* think the situation of a dissident is directly analogous; dissidents have different reasons for wanting attributability.

      That said, no that's not an Argument from Authority - that's reporting experiences, when/if they're relevant. An Argument from Authority is something like, "Daniel Levine said it, and he flaunts his degree around, so it must be true." "I was in Afghanistan and saw XYZ" is reporting an observation. Trusting observation reports requires credibility of the person doing the reporting, or at least no reason to think that the person isn't credible (most of the time, when someone says something mundane happened to them, we just believe them).

      Subjective/objective isn't the distinction you want. If we were just talking about subjective things, then there'd be no question even of assessing ideas. We'd just be having conversations that went "I love Burning Empires." "Sweet, so do I." "I don't, I prefer Heavenly Hash." You seem to mean something more like abstract/empirical.

      That said, a huge amount of discussion of games will not be totally, or often even mostly, on the "abstract" side of that divide. Actual Play seems to be relevant from time to time. Sure, you can assess every play report as a hypothetical - "If everyone really did start bleeding from their eyes when they played Mutant City Blues, then it probably is a poorly designed game," but that's... kinda limiting.

      Yes, you can run down every source to check someone's use of it. But jeez, who has the time? Citations are there so that people can check if they want to know more or if they suspect your use is off. If you start from the assumption that all citations must be verified, you're never going to get anywhere. Human beings developed the practice of trusting people so that we could build on their knowledge instead of each of us having to start from scratch.

      \n I'm reading this thread and all I get is a memory of that nice Abalone forum LARP we had some time ago. Anyone remembers it?

      It was a fun experiment, and even while it collapsed in the end, I learned several cool things from it.

      One of them was the effect of anonymity. You had to post things in character, and even while you knew the people there were people you had seen at S-G, you didn't know who was who. So you based your interactions more in the roleplaying and characterization. And then people told whose character they were playing. And then I got to go all "Ohhhhhh" when matching my previous knowledge (or social currency, if you may) and the way I kept thinking about the character. And I do believe that previous knowledge would have affected my interactions with some of them.

      Funny thing is that same anonymity might have had a severe impact on the forum's collapse. (Although I think the engagement rate had an even bigger hand in that.)

      (I'd totally do it again. So would I participate in a forum as suggested by the OP.)
