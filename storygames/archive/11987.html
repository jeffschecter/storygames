Posted By: komradebobEero,
Anyway, my real point was that, well, I'm sick of character-play being the primary point of discussion or emphasis in design.

Not that I hate that style of play. I actually like it. But I like GMing more, and the kind of fun I associate with that doesn't nearly match up with stuff I associate with character play.

I mean, as GM, I playwithcharacters, not playascharacters, if that makes sense. And I like it. And I get to play with other stuff too, the stuff that surrounds and interacts with the characters.
\nSo did anyone already try and make a game that takes this supposed controversy between player/GM and, rather than try to "solve" it, take it to the extreme? This would be a game where, one player is the GM and he has an agenda of designing a setting/story: period. And all the other players have the agenda of using techniques to play their characters: period. Almost two different games going on at the same time, but designed to work out that way and still be cohesive and playable without learned habits or conventions.

I mean a game that reealllly stretches the extreme by acknowledging this and adding even more rules to facilitate this as transparently as possible? This is interesting to me, because the more I've learned about RPGs in the past 2 years, I've realized that my old gaming group has different gaming goals than I do from RPGs (which has led to confusion in the past, and has now led us to not really wanting to game together any more) and it would be an interesting challenge to design a game that caters to us, despite that fact.

I am also interested in this, because it seems to me that there may be many RPG groups out there who are sort of divided like this but still want to game. And I don't think it's coincidental that I was almost always the GM in my group, and my friends were almost always the players.

-- John M.

    \n I'm gonna agree with Hans on this one.

    The funny thing is, in that quote, I was really trying to get at was that I like games where the GM/Player thing is broken down, and that I enjoy some seperation between player and character.

    I may have phrased it badly, but I was trying to contrast what I like in gaming with one fairly popular style I see talked about a lot around S-G ( and other places) where a big part of the fun is for as direct as possible identification with character. I already see it with a lot of traditional gaming, and even more so with certain kinds of story-games ( where the identification-fun seems even more pronounced).

    Basically, I like Stephen King's Childhood Ant Farm style gaming, and I don't see too much of that designwise. And, since that's a pretty obscure reference, SKCAF gaming is named (by me) for an old Far Side cartoon titled something pretty close to that. Since I can't find a copy of the cartoon online anywhere, the rub of it is that a young Stephen King is peering at his ant farm where he's put a cutout of a picnicking family at the surface level as ants are emerging up to the top level.

    That's how I like my gaming. I don't particularly want to feel some first person emotions or even get into a character's specific mind set. I want to be able to create some quirky and/or appropriate characters and put them in a bizarro situation and see where it goes. And I want, as a participant, to be able to manipulate whatever fictional stuff is necessary to do that, not just a single character.

    \n David Berg posts here (and on the Forge) about his game Delve sometimes. In that design, he very consciously embraces many aspects of the "traditional" GM/player divide and takes them on to the max. For instance, the players don't/can't apply any rules knowledge to manipulate their odds of success--they can only interact with the game by having their characters do stuff in the fiction. They also don't get to decide much about their characters--for instance, saying, "My character's mother died a few years ago" would be controversial. However, Delve does NOT place the "story" solely in the hands of the GM (although it lies pretty heavily there), so it may not qualify on your meter in that respect.

    \n I about the only thing I've heard of that comes clsoe to what I think you're talking about are various shots people have taken at ad-hoc game design, or the sorts of things Professor Barker does with his personal Tekumel campaign ( as I've gleened from some 2nd and 3rd hand stories).

    In those cases ( including my own forays into some ad-hoc gaming), the GM tends to use some sort of very basic die-rollling system, with the results being mostly circumstance-dependant on what die results mean. Generally, again IME, this tends to be a simple High roll good/low roll bad, with how good or how bad being determined by the situation. Slightly more advanced forms might include some foreshadowing of possible bad outcomes which the character would be able to assess prior to the roll.

    IME, this sort of thing tends to work pretty much fine in a relatively high trust environment. It also seems like a fair number of people played in at some point in a similar way to judge from the "it was more fun when we played wrong" thread on the current front page.

    Going along with that general concept, characters seem to be described much more basically, at least initially, perhaps not having much in the way of stats at all.

    It does seem like these sorts of systems eventually grow in complexity upon regular replay, especially after players have gotten ahold of "proper" rpg rules.

    My personal feeling about why this form didn't end up more popular has mostly to do with the difficulty in turning this approach into a commodity. I mean, there's nothing there to really sell anyone, if that makes sense.

    (Despite that, I've considered how one might go about doing just that on many occasions)

    So, Delve is certainly an interesting example. Another game I actually like to point to is Riddle of Steel. (Ignoring the optional "Dramatic editing" rules from Drama points introduced later.) Yes, there is explicit flagging, which might be too much for some players. But here's the thing\u2014it's all in-character. It's what your character believes, loves, hates, and wants (and is Destined to do or be, that one's a bit more problematic from the "no-meta" point of view I'll admit, but works fine in play, and it's totally ok to have some old witch-lady read your character's lifeline when he's a kid so he knows what his destiny is). I mean, the player doesn't have any direct input into the fiction except by simply, well, roleplaying his character well.

    This whole topic, which has now been mentioned in several threads, is very interesting to me because I do think real character-play, complete with funny voices, is necessary to get the most out of almost all RPGs\u2014and this *especially* includes most of the more "out there" Indie games.

    \n Hey Big J Money,

    I think that as long as the players are psyched about the GM's contributions and the GM is psyched about the player's contributions, those two contribution types can be as different as you want.

    Challenge play: GM makes a puzzle / dungeon, players try to solve / beat it.

    Genre emulation play: GM runs aptly-colored setting, players perform aptly-colored character actions, and these combine for aptly-colored situations and outcomes.

    Hell, you can even have a GM tell a full story, with the player characters there to witness it and react to it, if everyone's into that. (Once the players want the characters to influence the story, though, then either the GM's in a position to be psyched about their contributions, which is cool, or he isn't, which means something needs to change.)

    Sorry if that was so broad as to be useless. I just find that symbiosis to be the key.
