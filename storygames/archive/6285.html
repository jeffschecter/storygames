    \n I second Paul's post. The choice should be driven by enthusiasm, not frustration.

    Talking about GM-less games, I think people here, used to years of games where they give adversity to each other, sell very short the learning curve needed to master (as in "learn", not "GM") them. Both Polaris and Shock could fall very "flat" if the people at the table don't understand (or are not used and forgot to do it during the game) to "hit" each other in the right way. Before trying games like these, where the players have to do all the work of giving adversity to each other, I would advice some kind of game where there is both GM-given adversity and inter-character conflict, like for example In a Wicked Age.

    As an aside, Ajax, not as "the" next game you will play with your usual group (it depends of the answer to Paul's question, and on what you and your friends like), but as a very, very good text that explain a lot of this "dirty hippy way of playing" in a language understandable by long-time gamers, my advice is to read Dogs in the Vineyard. Read it, lend your copy to other people to read, then play it with the people who did get excited reading it. Play it as an aside, in a different day, to avoid forcing it to people who would be not so excited (if you are not so lucky that all the group is excited by the book, I mean. In that case go play it, you have found the game to play) and avoid making the rest of the group "menaced" by this new game. Play it like a experiment with like-minded people. It doesn't teach all the techniques used in these games, but it's the best teaching text for many of them, and, most of all, for a "way of playing" very different from the one most gamers are used.

    Also, think about bringing Fan Mail (from Primetime Adventures) and Keys (from The Shadow of Yesterday) into your game.

    \n PrimeTime Adventures is a great "transition" game. You have the GM even though I and someothers here had good experience of playing it GMless. Still the play is driven by the players as in his/her turn the player requests a scene defining its place, overall goal and focus. Other PCs can still participate in other PCs scenes.     \nY\u2019all, thanks for your answers and excuse me for my silence. Family duties kept me away from the internet more than I anticipated.

    Paul C., it\u2019s difficult to focus on only one memorable play experience\u2026 There are so many to choose from. That\u2019s a good new in itself. [I fear my last post has made me seem too bitter.] It\u2019s difficult to choose one that would be relevant to choosing a story game. And it\u2019s very difficult to summarize one. Summarized, they get really lame [or maybe it is I that can\u2019t condense them correctly], and they\u2019re really too long in their full length. [Also, I know how boring and uninteresting other players\u2019 memories can be.] However, I\u2019ll try.

    First, a bit of context. There is this really powerful mage (gm\u2019s character), that had tried to reduce my character in a kind of magical slavery, and failed. Since, they\u2019ve been enemies. PowerfulMage isn\u2019t evil, isn\u2019t a villain, but neither is he good. He has is own goals. PowerfulMage is too strong for my character to defeat and they both know it. They have some common enemies. PowerfulMage is also very attached to being discreet and staying incognito (he really like magical disguises and amnesia spells) and has enough hold over my character to ensure he doesn\u2019t \u201cbetray\u201d him. This goes on for a long time. New players join the gaming group, their characters integrate the setting.

    Now, it happened to pass that another player got her character in a situation that neutralized her for a long time, so she started creating a new one to play meanwhile. She chooses a name, and a description, and a profession that matched my character\u2019s sister\u2019s, so we decide her new character is indeed my character\u2019s sister. Soon after, my character is caught in a side plot that also makes him go his own way for a time. In the intervening time, Sister meets PowerfulMage (neither the player nor the character know who this is) at a party, and she falls for him (player decision), and him for her. Some months go by. My character came back. He is very happy for his sister but does not meet her suitor immediately.

    (Here start the session that contains the singular enjoyable memory.) Sister throws a biig birthday party in their new magnificent house, with many friends. It is very merry. My character is late, he has so many duties, but finally manages to come mid-party. He is so glad to have an evening to relax. There are so many friends here. What a magnificent celebration! And then, his beloved sister introduces him to her fianc\xe9\u2026 Surprised, incredulous, they recognize each other. A glacial conversation ensues. Nobody understands what is happening. The ambiance is crashing. Sister flees in tears, PowerfulMage and my character both try to pursue and appease her, in vain. That was a hell of a session. Good roleplay all around. Climax of a looong tension. The relationships between Sister, PowerfulMage and my character were forever changed in ways nobody had predicted.

    I hope you can do something with this memory. Please do tell if you need more details or another kind of experience.

    Posted By: Ajax Aldwyne
    Hituro, could you quickly detail what makes Sorcerer interesting, Shock fantastic and Burning Empires too much work for you?
    \nI can try

    Sorcerer interests me because of the elegance of it's currency system, the way that mechanical consequences are flexible and open to a vast array of interpretations but still perfectly safe from the sort of abuse that freeform resolution can give you. There are other interesting bits, but it is the system that really grabs me.

    Shock is fantastic because it draws you into compelling and emotive situation so easily, and can tackle issues which I would shy away from in most games, and which you really care about. Playing Shock I was catapulted so quickly into dilemmas and moral confrontations that I hardly had time to breathe, and the game created a very high level of passion and engagement. It doesn't push all my buttons, because I would struggle with long-term play, but it is very good at what it does.

    Burning Empires did the opposite, and really did not push my buttons. There seemed to be so many rules and sub systems, all with their own complexities and nuances, that I was at sea trying to play without a detailed reading of the rules. Worse the rules that did get applied seemed horribly abstract and limiting in a way that the rules of Shock, which do not pretend realism, did not.

    Posted By: Ajax AldwyneRobert, I just believe I would not enjoy playing this role. From what I gather, Dogs behave in the worst religious-fanatic manner and rule the live and morality of other peoples. I would prefer to play people resisting these tyrants.
    Heh, I've been there, myself.

    When I first read Dogs, I had a really bad reaction to it. I'm an agnostic with Atheist leanings and the first reading pushed my buttons in a bad way. But after that, I realized that I had completely missed the point of the game!

    Dogs is not about playing fundamentalist Dogs "faithfully" (As in "True to the setting".) the way you're often pushed into playing Paladins in other games. In Dogs, the setting is just there as a setup for the PLAYERS to judge and make hard decisions. The biggest thing in Dogs are that the characters (and thus by extension the players) are the ones defining scripture. It's their power to say stuff like "That rule there? In the Book? Fuck it, it's a bad rule and it's changed as of now. So speaks the King of Life.". And chances are the people around the would disagree and then you have awesome conflict and the players making statements about how far they are willing to go to stand in for their convictions and making statements left and right. So play an atheist Dog, play a fundamentalist Dog, whatever, 's all cool. But the game is not forcing you to do that or even rewarding you for it. The setting in Dogs only matters insofar as that your characters are in position of power but also responsibility that puts them in hard places and makes them make hard decisions.

    Now, you're not me, so maybe you have different reasons for not enjoying the setup. Put, personally, I'd still recommend Dogs. It's a great game and IMO a great introduction into many ideas you see in dirty hippy games. It's also a great text in itself.
