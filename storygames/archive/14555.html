\nAustralian Convention Glossary

I figured it might be interesting for anyone who is interested in Roleplaying from a cultural/anthropological standpoint. (or anyone who might be looking for insight about RPGs in other parts of the world).

    Mexican Goblin
    \n(a) Generic roleplaying accent. The result of a roleplaying Tower of Babel. In a module where the characters are Irish, Scottish, French, German and Texan, you can guarantee that in five minutes everyone's accent will be Mexican Goblin .
    \n(b) A cutsie cutsie type of module involving funny accents and not much else.
    \n
    \n
    \nI finally have a word for that accent I do...although in my case its more French, Italian, Russian, and cartoon Mexican.

    \n Most Melbourne Cons I went to in the 1990's I ended up playing a lesbian pyromaniac.
    \nA mate almost always got stuck playing the computer geek who was secretly romancing his dog.
    \n
    \nIt was almost parody in its own way.
    \n
    \nA good era for gaming I thought. As for the lexicon? It is interesting if just a little cliquey in parts.
    \nStill, more often than not gaming can be once you get a sizeable core of regular gamers and often it is symptomatic of a healthy gaming scene rather than a sparse and inactive one.
    \n
    \nFootnote: I still refer to freeform gaming as such. "LARP" sounds pretty damned awkward to my own ears.

    \n In my ongoing research for an article on Australian Freeform gaming, I'm finding evidence that the "massive scale live play" genre stretches back to 1982 in these parts of the world. Besides the Braunstein games, if anyone has evidence that live games with 30+ players occured in other parts of the world around this time (or earlier), I'd love to hear it.

    Burgonet, I still refer to "real" live gaming as a freeform as well. LARP is what happened when White Wolf tried to destroy the Live action gaming scene with it's "Minds Eye Theatre" and global "Camarilla" organisation. As for "Boffer" LARPing. I don't think it ever really took off in this part of the world...except among certain medieval weapon groups.

    I'm not sure if it counts, but in Donald Featherstone's Skirmish Wargaming, there's an example scenario set in the Old West that involves 22 characters, a fairly convoluted web of relationshios and 5 five different factions.
