    I haven't had a chance to really play 2nd Ed, yet, so my comments are mostly about 1st ed Exalted.

    Exalted is an interesting beast from a gamist perspective. First off, there is a lot of semi-incoherent stuff in the system that will not help you with your gamism, while D&D tends to be more focused (at least in the core books). Secondly, Exalted doesn't use the same kind of tactical and measured challanges that that D&D does -- there are no ECLs, for example, to help you decide if this challange is a good challange for the group. You have to do most of that by gut and guesswork, and that makes it much more difficult to come up with good gamist challanges that aren't GM whammy.

    Similarly, you have to have your own focus on how you want the game to run, as the core story of the game doesn't tell you inherently how to run it as a good gamist game. If you mostly focus it in a semi-Feng Shui way, however, and work it so that you use information dumps, vista scenes, and then big set-piece combats that chew up most of the focus of the game, you can start to focus on the parts of gamism that Exalted is good at.

    Exalted does, however, have some good gamist elements. They tend to be less tactical in the "movement, AoO, cover" sense than D&D is, and are closer to the tactical elements of a CCG. Making an Exalt who rules in the big set-pieces is about collecting the right charms and building them into the right combos and mastering the timing and resource managment of their use. Because of the dice-pool based system there isn't the same kind of control of stacked modifiers as there is in D&D or Spycraft 2.0, so you have to have charms in order to play the high control game. Everything will come down to who has the better build, the better timing, and the better control of their essence flow. Because of this, as with a CCG, you need an influx of new charms into the game -- thus making Exalted a great method of selling more product as we gobble up the new splat books to get new cards for our charm-decks.

    Part of charm selection is, as with D&D, about watching out for the bunk choices. As with D&D feats there are some charms that just aren't worth it, and should be avoided when possible. This can make the game very frustrating for newbs -- as it isn't always obvious at start which choices are the optimal ones.

    So, with all that in mind, I'd say that Exalted can do a decent gamist game. Not as good as Iron Heroes, and probably not as good as D&D. However, it can also be drifted to sim fairly easily, and gives (just moderatly) better support for that kind of play than D&D does.

    As combat-heavy unfocused wonkiness goes, Exalted's pretty much my current fave.     Exalted is great when you play, and for exactly the same reason it's a bitch to GM.

    Imagine you're playing Speed Chess against 200 people, while they're only playing against you, and for them there's ample time to make decisions.
    It is hard to make tactical choices when you don't have time for tactics, whereas the opposition does.
    I think such games build upon the assumption that both parties will have access to the same tools, because otherwise what use is there for opposition, and the rules for them?

    It fails in that regard, IMO.

    They're what you use to be cool.
