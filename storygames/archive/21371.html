    \n I'm working on a game called The Itinerants for now. It's about people who wander from village to village in a sort of medieval fantasy setting. Skilled trades, merchants, servants of the King, vagabonds and so forth. They go places and solve problems and cause trouble. It's explicitly a game about helping people and violence isn't a part of it on any level. Playtests so far have been sort of warm and vaguely ridiculous. The most recent game was all about a scandalous butter churn. One thing I love about it is that your character has a Time stat, and when your Time runs out, you're stuck - no more wandering, you put down roots right in that village.

    \n I recently playtested my Monsterhearts hack/mutation/scenario, called either "Sunflower" or "The Diary of Professor Rudwick". It's kind of a Depression-era Arkansas version of Michael Crichton's Sphere.

    It's playable and fun; I've excised Skins, changed some of the basic moves, and hacked how harm works, how advancement works, and how monstrosity accrues in play instead of being part of the character's identity from the get-go. The characters are not teens (although they could be) but normal adults who have an opportunity to interact with the otherworldly and to be changed by it.

    It's totally playable in terms of design; to publish all that remains to be done is a good writeup/overview of how to present the game and perhaps some GM advice/guidelines.

    It could work for any kind of "descent into madness" horror/sci-fi supernatural story of finite duration (like The Long Orbit). It's definitely a horror story or a tragedy, not the potentially uplifting or redemptive arc of a long-term Monsterhearts game.

    \n I put some things on the back burner, and now I'm revising Union to be a standalone game instead of a Microscope variant. It was always very different than core Microscope and probably should have been it's own game from the start. Digging into relationships of family and parenting strikes a chord every time I play it. It naturally draws players into more human and real stories, which I love.

    There's also something magical about shifting perspective and looking at different people's lives. Instead of just being a piece of a larger puzzle -- which is exactly what you'd think they'd be as parts of the family tree -- they become the main characters of their own stories. I've seen time and again where an ancestor is introduced who feels kind of like a shallow stereotype, but as we explore their life they feel like a real person and everyone at the table becomes sympathetic to them.

    There's something about seeing people change their opinions about a fictional stranger that gives me hope for humanity.

    \n Thank you for the opportunity. I had a hard time reading the previous posts as my mind was so all into my disclaimer. So I'll just relax and let it go now :

    "Start lightweight
    Launch
    Let your character steer the wheel
    Feel the story flow
    Set your own goals
    Experiment
    Learn to fly"

    The engine is goal auctions. They provide closure and a clear indication of who says what happens. The controls are traits and keys. They induce creativity, yet providing direction. There's XP, victory points, dozens of optional flavours, all that in under 20K characters translated for you in Shakespeare's idiom.

    The experience : because you empathize with your characters, you find yourself competing with your friends to get a grip on the story, and the harder you do it, the more complex the world gets. Roll with the blow if you tried it on your own. Chin up and breathe : what's the next goal on the table ?

    Jason: I like that timer idea too!

    Me? I'm trying to finish testable versions of a few games for the new Seattle area playtest group "RPG Workshop" Any in the area who are interested should look us up on meetup... anyway...

    The one game I have that is already testable is called "The Palimpsest Device" It's a looping and loopey time travel game. Kinda different. It's more about the exploration of character and world reification. It's not about creating a story really... You start playing precisely yourself, but with each loop, each player and their shared world becomes differenter and differenter.

    Others that need more work before they are presentable include the following:

    One is called "Ancestral Spirits". We play members of a tribal community. It's GmLess and I am attempting to use communal shamanic visionary experiences and subsequent tribal counsels as a way to handle some GM duties without breaking immersion as much...

    Another is called "The Party Game". We play party goers celebrating something or possibly commiserating at a social gathering. The mechanics steals the character pool from "The Final Girl". It steals "The Tilt" from Fiasco... cleverly disguised as "The Toast". It all happens in one setting although flashbacks are likely.

    Another I'm calling "Tarnation". It's an Old West setting game that happens around the poker table. The gathered players are all here in a go for broke effort to get the money they so desperately need to maybe just possibly get themselves out of the fixes they each are in... The playing of a hand of cards is utilized to settle upon questions to answer (scenes to frame). The role playing comes in flashback scenes after each hand of poker is played. The amount of chips won in the poker phase is mostly utilized to determine what kind of epilogue characters can have.

    Another: "Dying Words" We construct the final sentence of a character on his/her deathbed. Most of the game is scenes from that characters end of life review. Uses Erikson's developmental psychology model to scaffold the creation of scenes. Explores the many ways that a person's life impacts others.

    Another: Dungeons and Dating: A speed dating game in which we play monsters from the AD&D monster Manual. Other bestiaries could be used, but that's the one that is most dear to my heart... Nuf Said.

    Now I better stop dilly dallying and start grading biology exams. Ugh.

    \n All of these things are started to some degree. I might finish some of them.

    Hacking Elves is a short document that walks through a deconstruction process I use to create new spins on fantasy races for games like D&D. The book also has three or four mechanics examples for use with The Black Hack.

    This is mechanically geared for TBH, but the ideas are generic and easily portable to any game at all.

    I'm excited about it because it shares my mental process, not just my creative output. It's for people who like to build their own settings and need help doing something off the beaten path.

    I hope to do a series of these, one per thing I'm Hacking. "Hacking Humans," "Hacking Wizards," "Hacking Magic," and so on.

    --

    Mirrorrim: The City of Brass is my D&D 5e urban fantasy setting. I already released a free player's guide with no art on DriveThruRPG. I have plans to go back and flesh out that a bit more for the GM side, with a keyed megadungeon and some city adventures.

    I love this setting. It's not a traditional Western feudal D&D setting at all. It's not all white-skinned characters, nor male-dominated culture, nor even human-dominated. It's got hints of Imperial China, Arabic architecture, and cyberpunk sensibilities.

    --

    Looking for Group will be a brief PDF that explains how to organize groups online, using common virtual tabletop tools, social media, wikis, and the like.

    --

    Independence 2776 is an alternate universe for Cepheus Engine (Traveller SRD). It is a retelling of events of the American Revolution in a fictional science-fiction setting. I've been running one adventure at cons for a couple years (an adaptation of the story of Henry Knox and the cannons of Ft. Ticonderoga).

    --

    Main Sequence is another Cepheus Engine setting. I took all the tropes and themes of Traveller's "imperium" and turned many of them on their head. It's very cyberpunk, and a little bit Expanse or Dark Matter.

    --

    Towerlands is another setting I have on the back-burner. It's traditional fantasy stuff but has lots of cool ideas in it. It's the culmination of a lot of ideas and themes that have been bubbling in the pot for a decade or more, and I'm excited that I finally have overcome some of my roadblocks. It'll be a while before I have anything real to publish there.

    It balances novice characters with veterans by giving the former Karma points they can exchange for having the GM twist the situation somewhat in their favor, while veteran ones have to rely more on their own skills and resources to do so.

    Leveling is completely vitual, so the narration changes and things get easier, but character stats are otherwise unaffected. You can redistribute the points used to create your character to change your build, and even make a veteran character become older and lose skills, by exchanging back skills into karma points, which keeps the character balanced as described above.

    Players don't have moves, though their skills will have narrative limitations and activation requirements. This is to let them focus on the fiction instead of the game mechanics, but it doesn't mean they don't have rules and mechanics to deal with to establish things, it means these are more integrated with the fiction than with the game system.

    The GM has a limited amount of moves, but mostly these will list a reference of ways to handle different situations instead of stating specific ways of handling things according to the dice roll, so no need to keep a lot of move lists at hand, just grasp the philosophy behind them.

    It will feature a procedure for the game conversation with ritual phrases and their proper use explained in comic book format, along with a representation of what's happening both at the table and inside the fiction. The GM section will include a copy of it that explains instead how the GM is handling her role and what's on her mind at that time.

    The GM section should feature resources for both the impro and prepped parts of the session. The idea is to make the load on the GM quite light. There's a good amount of things that are best left to improvisation and to the players hands, like plot hooks/motivations, and others that are best handled by the GM, like the detail of the content that will be discovered by the players.

    \n Hey there! My game is called "Prompt." It focusses on people and how they are affected by their experiences.

    A big part of the mechanics is the idea of the system providing randomly-generated prompts, based on the characters and their histories. The world, the story, and the scenes are all generated this way. Not clear on how unwieldy this all is, but more playtesting will help me figure things out.

    This came from my conclusion that RPGs are really all about the game giving you a prompt (eg. success/fail) and the GM interpreting it and narrating the results. I've just taken this idea a step further such that the whole thing is interpreting prompts set by the game, once the characters have been created.

    Instead of being defined by modes of world interaction (Strength, Dexterity) or how a character does things (Sneaky, Flashy), a character's stats are dictated by who they are as people. Each stat (called a Frame, as in "frame of mind") starts as a number between 1 and 6. Low numbers affect the character poorly, high numbers positively. The player then describes a specific experience, a pivotal inciting event in their past, that gave them a core belief and shaped them as a person. They then write down the belief and a single word, used as a label for that "stat."\n
    +6 Peacekeeper \u2014 Mother and Father used to argue all the time about their little freak. Until Mother shot him. She cried every day for years, gave up her captaincy over it. Cyrus now believes everything should be done to protect family from without as well as within.
    \nOnce each character has 3 Frames, some are randomly selected and used to build a world and story throughout the game.

    I'm a writer, so there's a number of writing structure principles used in the game's design. So I guess this game would appeal more to writers? I've used it to write some decent beginnings to some stories playing this solo--with shifts, revelations, and twists I never saw coming.

    And I'll be playing this pretty soon with some RPG friends and a relative for whom this will be their first RPG.

    If you're interested in finding out more or discussing things in detail, check out the link at the top of the comment. ^^

      \n I am writing an OSR fanzine - aimed specifically at LotFP but compatible with most of the OSR games out there.
      It is called Black Dogs and the first issue is available on RpgNow as PWYW, so you can check it out for free.

      The fanzine has a few "goals", if we can call them that...
      - Make a group that sticks together for more than just gold/XP... somehow, put some sort of "mission" or "morality" into the setting design, to keep characters together and make it easier for the GM to frame adventures. This is done mostly in terms of the setting
      - Make NPCs and in general the "social" part of the adventures more relevant (or if not the social part, the "social adventures" which take place mostly in villages or small towns... those that in AD&D would become political intrigues in the city...). This is done trying to present to the GM guidelines and rules which are heavily influenced by games like Dogs in the Vineyard or Circle of Hands (although this is an OSR, and it plays as such)
      - Make the wilderness scary and the monsters relevant, and somehow connected to the "social" part of the adventures (again, guidelines for the GM and random tables to design villages/towns connected to a monster or something in the wilderness)
      - Play with house-rules: everyone doing something in the OSR does this, and perhaps starts with this, and cannot avoid doing this, at least a little bit :-) As many others, I have my own house-rules for certain topics (chargen, encumbrance, combat, morale, skills, experience, classes, etc...). What I tried to do is to keep each house-rule self-contained and modular, so each single rule can be inserted in another OSR game, without the need to adopt all of my house-rules together
      - Present and develop a setting and an adventure-to-campaign style which is very much what I like to have in my games and how I play them, keeping prep reasonably low, and designing as you go instead of in advance. This part (setting and GM advice) is also a lot of fun to write, which I consider it a plus, at least from my personal point of view :-)

      I chose the format of a fanzine so that people can get on board little by little. I hope it's going to be easier to grow a decent audience this way instead of presenting yet another huge OSR tome. This also gives me the chance to receive feedback as I design and adjust my style as I write...

      So, well, if you're into OSR, this might be interesting for you. I am of course available to answer questions (if anyone has specific questions or wants to know more, we can open a dedicated thread).
