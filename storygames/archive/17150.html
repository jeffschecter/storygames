    \n I'm designing a Doctor Who style game called Getting There in Time. It very much embraces the "uncover the mystery" style of story structure that was the foundation of classic Doctor Who stories, and I definitely felt it was important to never orient the mechanics so that information was conditional on die rolls. I've never played any Gumshoe games myself, but I think the "don't roll for clues" thing does make sense for a Doctor Who style game. (I'm not sure the rest of Gumshoe really does, though -- as I understand it, the "party" in a Gumshoe game represents a team of experts who showcase their talents by highlighting who notices things or gets information in a particular way, which I wouldn't think maps well to the "omni-competent Doctor and fish-out-of-water Companion" model).

    \n Fear Itself (another Gumshoe game) has a group of untrained, ordinary people and 1 or 2 experts. These probably arent "the Doctor" but could be a starting point for a hack. AFAIR these experts can buy skills the others arent allowed to buy - and have more points.

    Gumshoe has a separated skill list for investigative and other skills. You automatically succeed in investigative skills when you're gathering necessary clues. You can pay from a pool to get additional clues or shine above the other players. For those other skills like fighting or driving you roll a die and can spend from a pool for a better chance to succeed. IMHO Gumshoe is less suited to action adventures, but Dr Who isnt so much action, so it might fit.

    So ... I think you could make it work, but I dont see much in Gumshoe to support that special Dr Who feeling. Whether that would be easy to add or not I cannot say.

    \n The main issue I have with Gumshoe games - and I've played a lot - is that the mechanics are very hard to get right. Either the players have way too many points to throw at investigational options and the game becomes about running around trying to find the right combo to obtain the clues to the mystery without even the chance of failing at an attempt, or the players don't have enough points (or enough time) and since the game is about discovering these clues, there isn't a good way for a player to strategize how and where to go and investigate within the limited resources they have available.

    It has a good concept at its core - and if you can reduce Dr. Who episodes of the sort you want to do to a procedure (in the same way that many police/law/medical shows are procedurals) then Gumshoe can be a good fit, it's the economy that gets very fiddly/difficult.

    C7 handles mysteries with a GM and skills, more or less.

    \nSorry to double post, but this is a well-reasoned argument that I thought deserved my unworthy response.'

    I think the disconnect here is that the GUMSHOE rules are meant to make the investigation part of the scenario easy, but they don't do a lot to address the consequences of the discoveries.

    There are, in fact, two different economies in a Gumshoe game.
    The economy most players focus on is the clue-distribution economy, although it is not really the most important to the outcome of the story. It's meant simply as a set of guidelines to handle the distribution of needful information to the players.
    A Gumshoe game will not work if you use that economy to restrict the distribution of clues and hints any more than just using it as a guide to see who gets what piece of information.
    It cannot, therefore, be a restrictive economy. It must allow for an extreme budget.

    The second economy, the non-investigative skills, are where trouble sometimes arises.
    Since you can spend from the non-investigative skills to modify your rolls, those are the ones you want to have limited.
    Foresight/Preparedness is a prime example. You don't want someone prepared for every situation if you're trying to create tension. It's okay to have someone who is FREQUENTLY prepared, but the restriction that I think you're talking about JD has to come in there. If you want to create a sense of tension by limiting resources, players need pools that run out quickly and are hard to refresh... but only in NON-INVESTIGATIVE areas!

    Taking this back toward Doctor Who:
    We want to see the neat monsters and weird space stuff, so it should be fairly easy to follow clues. You should have way more clue points than you'll ever need.

    But those monsters have to be overpowering and scary, so you need a lot fewer non-clue-gathering points than you'd need to handle problems face on. We're talking miniscule pools for things like gunplay and preparedness and spaceship piloting. Barely enough to handle one major enemy, let alone the full scale invasion you're going to face...

    Oh, hey, that gives me an idea!

    What if you do have absolutely pathetic non-clue-gathering skill pools (action and problem-solving skills) that you can only amp up by: running away and/or getting captured! You want a pool refresh? RUN!

    \n So how does GUMSHOE work? There are several things going on, at the level of the player and the character.

    Looking at the abilities first, General abilities are about action. These are the areas in which failure is interesting or threatening. Players have enough points to guarantee success a few times in their best abilities and to look good, and most of the rest of the time, they'll have a 50/50 chance to win. It's risky, it's exciting. Excitement for the players is a key part of the roleplaying experience.

    Investigative points serve several purposes. They give you core clues for free, the give you supplementary information about clues and they give you narratively loose benefits (loose in that they're not associated with clues and can be requested by a player or proposed by a GM, for example, in my three page demo, you can spend a point of Evidence Collection to put together an Arabic/English tome giving a pool of 2 points of Occult and 1 pt of Cthulhu Mythos).

    But that's not all. Investigative spends bring the awesome. If you have to spend a point for something, it's going to be something good. If there's nothing to be had you can't even spend a point. So Investigative points equate to spotlight time.

    And on top of that, Investigative points that the characters have are an indicator to the GM as to the kind of things players want to see in the game. If you've got 8 points in Occult, you're going to be seeing lots of occultists, trying to understand the workings of mystical rituals, divining the correspondences between lines on the landscape and the tarot.

    What Investigative abilities do is generate information and the reward for this information is danger and madness, which are both exciting, as well as story, narrative continuity and meaning. The instinct of the player often is to avoid danger and madness, after all these threaten their involvement in the game in the most direct way possible, by threatening the character. That's where the Drive comes in. At first it might seem as a punishment (or as Ken Hite puts it, a dubious stunted carrot) for the engaging with or avoiding the danger, but in practice what it does is create player buy-in and gives them permission to do awful things to their character. So if a player for example, chooses Curiosity as their drive, they can narratively justify their character investigating the awful screams in the basement. Without that justification, the narrative doesn't make any sense, just as it wouldn't in fiction.

    Sources of stability and pillars of sanity give characters further narrative depth and provide post-hoc justification for their actions, as well as being targets for the GM. A player who says Love of Nature is their Pillar of Sanity is asking the GM to prove that this is not the case.

    So what does the player do? The player plays their character using their character sheet as a guide, the character sheet that they have chosen, for what they would do in a way that satisfies whatever version of the investigative narrative they find themselves in. The game is all set up for this narrative enagement and drives the play towards that genre.
