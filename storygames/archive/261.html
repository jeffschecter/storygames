    Dave, regarding the issue Jason brought up:

    Looking at the games that use 'bonuses for decription' rules, I find that the most effective of these games provide very specific directions for applying those bonuses.

    Exalted has one of these that is unfortunately throttled by resource and timing effects; it boils down to, "You can get extra dice by describing why you care about this." So you can be like, "Oh no! That girl is drowning! I remember once I was drowning and the way I felt when a stranger saved me..." and based on that add your Compassion to a roll for saving that girl.

    So, as a result this biases players to make characters that care about things.

    This still has a strong interpretive aspect, but, importantly, it tells the players what kind of input you want.

  • shreyas
    March 2006 edited March 2006

    This is why I suggest a more strongly manipulative rule, and why I used Virtue channelling as an example rather than stunts.

    "You get cookies for having fun" is vacuous. They're having fun! What do they need cookies for? In Exalted, you have to assume that every character stunts every turn when calibrating Charms for Essence economy. It's not a reward, just a fact of life.

    Whereas, by rewarding specific, narrow things that you want, you can expect players to reach for these things when they need a mechanical boost, not all the time but enough to make a difference.

    Edit: It also matters that the bonus have some respectabe magnitude, such that players know they can rely on it at need, or it will simply be ignored.
