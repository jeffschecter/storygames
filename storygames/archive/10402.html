    \n Here's what I said in the other thread (which is probably more appropriate here):\n

    A Penny For My Thoughts was at the Ashcan Front booth in 2007. That was an invaluable step in the development process for me, for several reasons:


    * I learned how to explain the game to people. The process of demoing it at GenCon had a direct impact on the final draft.

    * I validated that the basic design was solid, and that people had fun with it, but there were a few details I needed to tweak. More importantly, I learned what those details were.

    * I made a commitment to a group of people that I would finish the game. Only Ryan riding me was more of a motivator for the 18 months it took to finish the book. I had a group of people who were excited to see the final result, and I didn't want to disappoint them.



    On the "other payoffs" front, here's what I experienced:


    * I sold enough copies to break even on my production costs. It didn't pay for my hotel, for my airfare, or for my meals.

    * I wider awareness of the game, which proved valuable for word of mouth when the game was finally published. I could have taken better advantage of this if the game had been done 12 months sooner, but c'est le vie.

    * I felt like I was part of something. I didn't consider myself a game designer, but I suppose I could have.



    I do find it amusing that when people talk about ashcans, Penny (along with Sweet Agatha) isn't usually on the list, despite being in the first batch of games to use the name.
