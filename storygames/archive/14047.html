\n We just wrapped a Dungeon World mini-campaign and I thought I'd share the cool stuff our heroes found. Some of this is reskinned stuff from the game and some of it is original.

Beiskaldi, Hammer of the Mad Regent
The name means \u201cComplainer\u201d, which is apt for this potent magical weapon with a powerful drive to smash and kill.
When you wield Beiskaldi, roll +Wis

10+ No problem, do what you like
7-9 Choose two
You don\u2019t immediately attack the closest living thing
You don\u2019t smash something precious to bits
You don\u2019t go into a blood frenzy, fighting until death or exhaustion overtake you

Beiskaldi can be used one-handed by a fighter, paladin or ranger. It does 1d10+2. The +2 is pure rage.

(This was abandoned as too dangerous after the Paladin picked it up and crushed his hireling's skull like an eggshell)

Ring of Beckoning Shadows
This beautiful ring is affixed with a multi-faceted gem made from crystallized shadow-stuff.
The shadows you target with this ring become a portal for you and your allies. Name a location, describing it with a number of words up to your level. If all goes well, stepping through the portal deposits you and any allies present when you call upon the ring in the location you described. Roll +Int when you use it.

10+ You arrive safely
7-9 choose three:
Your own shadows are not severed
You don\u2019t attract the attention of beings from the plane of darkness
You arrive at the specified destination
The gem is not fractured and ruined

(This was used just once, but awesomely - the wizard used to to transport his invisible assassin-sorcerer rival into the snow cave of the dreaded Samaten beast, dropping him off and then returning to his comrades)

Lumu\u2019s Rope
This rope responds to its wielder\u2019s every wish, tying itself into knots, grabbing on to items (or enemies), coiling tightly. When you are using Lumu's rope to tie, climb, or grab something, you never have to Dodge.

(This got a lot of creative use)

Spear of the Tuchekonen Martyrs
This stout and heavily ornamented tribal spear that does 1d6 damage. When the user calls upon the Tuchekonen Martyrs, a ghostly martyr will appear! Roll +Cha.

On 10+, choose three
On 7-9, choose two
The martyr is happy with you and will not harm you
The martyr obeys one command
The martyr is a ferocious warrior, inflicting an additional 3d6 damage on the spear\u2019s target
The martyr has some useful skill or specialty
The martyr will stick around more than an instant

(Used to kill a villainous bad guy in one shot)


Rod of Selka the Arbiter
This strongly built and beautiful mithril rod once belonged to the legendary Elven shield maiden Selka, who brokered the Treaty of Nau Tuthima between her people and the dwarves. It does 1d8 damage, can be wielded by anyone, and when held while making a peaceful Parley move, the wielder gains one additional hold.

(They totally missed this while looting Selka's turret)

Mantle of the Hero
When you Make a Stand or Defy Danger while wearing the mantle, you can use the stat of your choice.

(Worn by the wizard, pretty handy)

These all worked pretty well in play. I think they could be refined and invite you to rewrite the custom moves to make them better!

    \n Thanks, Jason. I actually haven't had nearly as much high level play as I'd like, especially with the recent changes I've been making. Monsters are now much more dangerous, and damage scales considerably slower.

    The way it's looking, or at least I'm aiming for, is that monster HP and player damage scales more slowly, while monster damage and player HP scales quicker. This means that the difference between, say, a 2nd level and 5th level monster is not so much their HP as their damage. They're more dangerous because they hurt you more, not stick around longer.

    Our model of player damage used to scale much more with level, similar to HP. We're tying to move away from that. With the changes that are going on now, a +1 damage bonus is considerable. In earlier versions, I had kind of defaulted to damage scaling a lot like player HP, which meant either lots of moves that granted more damage, or moves and spells that automatically scaled (+your level damage, for example). Both of those kind of suck. I feel like, in the newer model, taking a move that grants you +1 damage feels more like a similar move in AW: it says something about you, not just a requirement as you level.

    As for treasure... I think I was wrong. Now I'm thinking about treasure moves, and they rock.

    \nI think that phrases pretty much encapsulates what I wish D&D was like, and why I stopped enjoying it somewhere in the 3rd edition area, and totally lost interest to the point I've never even tried 4th edition.

    Magical as power-ups? Not so interesting. Magical items as tools with unusual powers and unusual consequences or costs? That's what I like. They should be weird tools that make you think twice about whether you wish to use them or not, as well as maybe how to use them or what for. I also like the "flesh scroll", for the same reason.

    If I find the "magic hammer +3 of Thor", it's only interesting if allegiance to Thor is a major factor in the game: so it's something meaningful, not just the +3. I want to be wondering whether I should use the hammer or if I should hide it, and who will want to take it from me if they realize I have it. Maybe showing it in public is likely to launch a revolution, or it's the key to marrying the Princess, or it must be melted down in order to cure the dying dragon. Interesting choices, consequences. Screw magic items as pure power-ups.

    Your mileage may vary, of course. This is just me.
