I'm assembling my next RPGnet article, which is looking back to some early articles I wrote on desk-checking RPGs and simulating players. I'm planning to focus this new article on specific theories of players or groups of players - as tools for building simulated players. These tend to be much less common than theories of play - which focus on what happens in play, rather than player motivations. So far I have three:
  • Forgian Creative Agendas - specifically as gestalt aesthetics of a group of players.
  • Mo's Sockets, Goals, and Payoffs
  • My Dynamic Views
I'm wondering if anyone has any suggestions for others. I've been tracking a fair bit of theory development, but I'm a little sparse on Nordic-larp approaches. Let me know if I'm missing something here, I'd rather be able to add it to the article, than to find out about it afterwards.

    John,

    Some, perhaps even many, of the available taxonomies are based on theories of player motivation, but very, very few of these bases are ever presented. I've not read Robin's Laws, but it would only serve this purpose if it contains an explicit theory of how, say, a power gamer would response to a game situation. Of course you can intuit a naive theory of player motivations from such a category, but you're forced to do all of the lifting.

    Most RPG theories don't make motivations explicit, largely because on the face it seems obnoxious. It seems to deny the freedom a player has to make their own decision - they are a powergamer so they will do X in Y situation. In actuality this is a straw man argument, a theory of motivations is always probabilistic - people can always choose a less likely decision. So a powergamer will tend to do X in Y situation, but will tend to do Z much less than another category.

    Taxonomies imply these things all the time, but stop short of actually giving you tools to directly predict motivations. This is a major problem with using those models for game design, rather than in direct play. You can be intuitive with a single player, but with a nebulous space of players who may play your game, our built-in intuition of people becomes unreliable.

    The other problem is that a taxonomy is more resilient to criticism without an explicit model of player motives. In a sense it doesn't make predictions, apply in large scale (design versus play) situations, or delve into fundamentals of why we play. It is a shallow theory, and the arguments usually end up with - you can categorize my way or your way. If you have actual motives, then your theory can be evaluated on several grounds: self-consistency (your motive theory must imply your taxonomy) and empirical observations (your motive theory predicts actual play decisions).

    To focus things a bit more, the theory of views I've presented explicitly says that players make their decisions to keep the content within their views of intermediate complexity - and those terms are well enough defined that you could test the claim that any player is acting within a specific view. If I present a taxonomy of views - which I have in the past - then they become something that can directly be turned into predictions of players.

    So perhaps I should have said that I'm looking for theories that directly predict player decisions.

    Gabor,

    The function idea is right on. Controls are the strategies / heuristics we use to take the content we see and turn that back to our influence on the content. On the other hand, stances are a bit limited. Critically, PCon3 doesn't distinguish "imagined" and "real" content, because there really isn't a bright line distinction. Stance really only makes sense around the context of the small chunk of imagined content which comprises the character. The thing I'm very interested in is how the views change, and that was always something which creeps into the controls. Specifically, you can produce fun either by contributing or changing the content, or my moving your view to a better part of the content.

    This is especially telling in a larp context: I can bring up another topic of conversation to restart interaction here, or I can walk to the next room to find someone else to talk to. Some of that is "imagined" some of that is "real", but it's all play content and play controls. (On a side note, that makes play constraints a superset of lumpley system - its not just about the imagined content, it's about the entire space of content.)

    Gabor,

    Sorry again for the delay. It's difficult to show an example of an ur-game without the game to be analyzed present at first. I know I didn't get into it before, but there are basically two types of ur-games, both of which reduce the complexity of one game by replacing the players with another game. On one hand, you can do that by replacing players with a mathematical game, so you can start finding optimal strategies on the design side. A short example of that was in the original article.

    The alternative is to reduce things to a "roleplaying" problem, setting up immersive or philosophic structures upon the game so as to analyze the game from there. Of course, setting up a context where you can immerse as a Story Now player or Mo's Impassioned Other is requisite on an ability to immerse well and accurately. These types of ur-games are extremely interesting, as they exist to transform one aspect of RPG play to another. But this can make them highly personal, both to the game and the desk checker.

    I've used both approaches, and I find the later type of ur-game useful. Unfortunately, I've not yet been able to put together a procedure that ecapsulates the process of making one. Both rely on theory to drive them, so you can tie motivations and goals of one approach to another.

    As far as defining a specific view, the best description of that happened over at the Forge in my aborted series of posts (aborted because the RPG Theory forum was closed in the middle of them). Here in particular, with here and here preceding. Physical views are certainly important, but there's more too it than that. Different players will look at a character sheet and see different things. Some may see those ability scores, another the picture of the character in the corner. When I DM D&D and glance over the table at character sheets, I usually see the saves and hitpoints most prominently.

    But views are a little more than what I see, they are also how I see it changing - which is were the activity, descriptions and general banter can become many different views. Generally static views are of dynamic things, because if their subject is static, things are intensely unsatisfying. But a dynamic view could be of a static subject, much like a person looking at a painting, seeing different parts and perspectives over time.

    I hope that is of some help,

    - Mendel S.
