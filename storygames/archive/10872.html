\n I play (off and on) a fair amount of Diplomacy online. I used to play just with friends, but sometimes I play with strangers at http://www.playdiplomacy.com (which is a great site if you like the game).

My last game with friends ended with people quitting, and my girlfriend refusing to ever have anything to do with my crazy-gamer friend ("I don't care if he apologized, I don't ever want to meet that guy") . Another friend of mine has sworn off playing with the rest of us forever. And I'm currently in an internet game with people I don't know where I argue with one of the other players because he keeps telling me to "Take it easy" and "Calm down" whenever I send him what I think are perfectly bland, non-upset Diplomacy messages.

But I don't think any of these people are wildly dysfunctional. I think there are such hugely varying ideas of "How You're Supposed To Play" that people come to the table with very different expectations of the game.

Many people feel that in Diplomacy *ANYTHING GOES* ... there is no such thing as cheating, unsportsmanlike behavior, dishonesty, etc. Which sounds weird, but it's very common. And if you are in a group where everyone thinks that, it works great. But when you combine those players with different kinds of players, social mayhem ensues.

With the guy I'm playing with now, he sends almost no messages. Which to me is a crazy way to play the game. But to him, my multiple "Hey, what's up with your move?" messages apparently make me seem insane and histrionic.

I guess I have no real point here, other than to say that Diplomacy (while not a role-playing game per se) illustrates better than anything else the idea that a game is made up of more than the written rules, at least to me.

    \n All of my Diplomacy play has been online, at http://www.dipbounced.com. There are definitely some unwritten rules that will have an effect on play. I originally made note of these while trying to suss out the fine points of what was a somewhat opaque game to me from a social standpoint, but keeping them in mind has helped improved my game.
    \n
    \n1) Don't be a dick. With rare exception the most brash or demanding player gets ganged up on eventually.
    \n
    \n2) Don't get too big too soon. Early success often leads to being ganged up on and eliminated. Russian players seem to have this problem frequently. Probably exacerbated by the large geographical size of Russian territories.
    \n
    \n3) Don't stagnate. If you fail to maken any, or enough, progress in the form of supply center acquisitions, you will likely be used as a buffer by an "allied" neighbor then destroyed when convenient. Italy seems to suffer from this most often. It's probably better to make risky moves than safe and predictable ones if you can't seem to gain any momentum.
    \n
    \n4) Communicate, early and often. Even if every word out of your mouth is a lie, at least you'll be presenting a facade with which the other players can engage. Nothing is more worthy of suspicion then a player that doesn't communicate, or only does so infrequently or in a very clipped and brief form. Besides if you don't communicate you can't form alliances or keep them going. You also can't engage in various forms of diplomatic scheming and skullduggery. The silent player is a losing player.
    \n
    \nAs far as the issue of honesty/dishonesty, most people I've played with seem to understand that scheming and dishonesty are part of the game. But they will also punish those that engage in dishonesty with them if possible. It usually takes the form of doing whatever can be done to hinder the stabbing/lying player's success regardless of the cost to themselves. So I guess the rule there is "Don't get caught in a lie by a player that will not be out of the game next turn." But I've also found that "It's better to ask for forgiveness than for permission." is something to keep firmly in mind. Part of the fun of the game can be exploring that fine line between actions that are forgiven and those that lead to war or annhiliation. If you're otherwise sticking to the four rules above, you can gain a lot of leeway in this regard.
    \n
    \nMost of the games I've played in have been anonymous, which certainly effects the social dynamic during the game. The non-anonymous games have had some interpersonal drama. Mostly expectation clashes leading to a (misplaced?) feeling of betrayal. From some of the vehemence I've seen during play it's not hard to imagine Diplomacy leading to damaged friendships. Some people take games extremely personally. A game like Diplomacy where play is competitive, socially focused to a large degree, and beyond what any rules text can cover is just ripe for expectation clashes and hurt feelings of all sorts. Sort of like High School.

    \n "Diplomacy destroys friendships" is one half a self-fulfilling myth and one half a symptom of people playing out of their depth. The myth part is when people glorify and mystify the dangerous edginess of the game. I encounter this sometimes, usually in the form of wild stories about how "anything goes in Diplomacy", stories that fail to address the simple fact that if you can't play like a decent human being, then I'm going to get offended, and I'm not exactly a Diplomacy virgin of any sort. It's like people hear too many stories of how British postal players used to break into each other's homes to steal their orders, and then for some strange reason fail completely in realizing that this is criminal, crazy and in no way admirable.

    The playing out of their depth part is just like people have said - good play of Diplomacy requires you to realize that personal trust is not a tool of play in this game. People who can't distinguish between what happens in the game from what happens outside it can't play without getting hurt. Almost everybody has to learn this lesson by losing viciously in practical play, and for some people this very lesson is so devastating that they don't want to touch the game anymore afterwards.

    \n My experience, on the other hand, is that when the play elevates to a somewhat proficient level (all players have a full game of experience under their belts, say), the amount of simple betrayal takes a sharp dive. After that Diplomacy becomes an intricate game of strategy in which trust is given not because you believe in the other player but because trust is mandatory if you want to win. When we hold Finnish Championship tournaments and such, there really isn't any hurt emotions involved, because everybody at the table knows that the only reason for another player to betray them is if that player is going to be benefiting from that betrayal himself. At that level of play the trick is to prefer trust that cannot be broken for benefit, and only risk trusting an opponent in position for stabbing you when all other choices are worse. At that point I don't really see trust as much of an issue - there is none, all players realize that they are ultimately playing against each other, everybody knows the score to the game. You can't offend others when they know what the game is about.

    On the other hand, Diplomacy is a very intricate game, and the styles of play are pretty different in different parts of the world. From what I've read about it, Americans play in a much more emotional and static style than we do around here in the Nordic countries. This might be partially because of dated sources (much of Diplomacy literature dates from the '70s and '80s), but many phenomena that are reported from the USA are practically unknown in the Finnish Diplomacy scene. For example, permanent alliances (possibly pre-arranged before the game or nearly so) and intentionally tied games are not much known around here; on Finnish Championship levels the firmest alliances usually last only 2-3 years at most, ending when the underdogs try to concertedly balance the board position (and improve their own chances) by bribing of threatening the alliances into breaking. I could see how you'd view the game in a different light in that sort of environment, as Diplomacy is much more desperate and wargamey if the alliance structure at the board is naturally static. In an environment where alliances are deeper and more static, there is also more trust involved in forming them, and thus more reward for breaking them, which might account for American reports of Diplomacy as nothing more than a game of betrayal.

    Then again, part of the skill of good play in Diplomacy is definitely a capability for gauging the table's social atmosphere and adjusting your own play to it. I've read about local microclimates in which Diplomacy has become unplayable because players precommit to their alliance structures too much and fear loss of credibility if they ever break these alliances to try for solo victory, but the result of that was already mentioned - the game becomes unplayable if the players as a community manage to convince each other to not strive for victory of the single board. In a play environment poisoned by metagaming I can easily see Diplomacy becoming unfeasible for all sorts of reasons. Aside from metagame issues, though, I find Diplomacy beautifully self-regulating for the most part: an outsider coming into a local play culture will almost always benefit (in the sense of winning the game here and now) by playing counter to the local assumptions rather than buying into them, which means that over time people tend to learn to be better in the game instead of reaffirming their local peculiarities.

    I agree with James in that all games have social expectations involved, and should those not match, play will not succeed. I don't consider Diplomacy any worse in this regard than Chess or Go, for example. I'd rather not play Chess with an impatient newbie, for instance, as I'd just get annoyed with them moving the pieces indiscriminately, doing take-backs and so on.

    \n I routinely refer to the "Diplomacy Effect" as it applies to any game you play.
    \n
    \nThe Diplomacy effect is that self balancing mechanism that only works over the course of multiple interactions (games played in this context) with the same people.
    \n
    \nBeing the ultimate "I swear on my mother's soul" guy who then betrays everything he ever promised For the Win...is a FANTASTIC Diplomacy strategy...once. After that you're marked for life. What makes Diplomacy so powerful is that you have to play it such that you can actually play it again with the same people. If you get known as the guy who makes alliances claiming to play for the allied victory, but always break them at the end for the solo win, who's going to believe you.
    \n
    \nThat's why playing with the same people over and over is so crucial. Diplomacy the game...like diplomacy itself...is a game about reputation and leverage. If you playing with a bunch of people who have no reputation and you have no leverage with, then you're blind and essentially just bouncing little wooden blocks off each other on a map of Europe. If you're playing with people where reputations are at stake and leverage is available...THEN, you're playing Diplomacy. And THEN the price of ultimate betrayal to get the win is meaningful to the extent that it damages your reputation for all future games played with that group.

      So would a better design for Diplomacy be one where you can get that experience of several sessions condensed into a single session? Say you win the first round by being a straight bastard, but then you're marked for all subsequent rounds of play.
      \nNope, that'd be a different game - although to my understanding not as different in the American scene compared to how the game is played here. And anyway, it's not so far off to say that the current design of the game already supports crafting long-term relationships. For example, it's pretty easy to stab another player for one center, but that means that the player will probably be suspicious of you for the rest of the game. So it all works out even within the constraints of one game only.

      The problem with trying to play Diplomacy as a metagame is that the game starts rewarding conformance to arbitrary local social morality. If you don't play according to whatever unwritten fair play rules the yokels expect, you're going to be ganged up upon or they'll refuse to play with you altogether. And all this can happen in a metagaming environment even when you're playing scrupulously according to the rules and spirit of the game. I don't have much patience for that sort of thing - in fact, I actively consider it cheating if somebody tries to blackmail others with threats concerning stuff outside the game, such as "if you stab me now, I'll destroy you in the next game we play". If you allow the discipline of the local play culture to get lax on this matter, you'll be playing through an empty ritual before you know it, just voting for the winner.

      \nActually, Ben, there's another solution to the kingmaking problem, one I deviced myself. I'm pretty proud of this game theory result, being that I developed it through plenty of boardgaming and encountering this same problem you have here. I'll lay it out here for general interest.

      The way we play kingmaking situations at my table is that any player may at their convenience declare a situation in the game a kingmaking one; they'll then demonstrate this by abstract calculation to the satisfaction of the other players, naming the kingmaker and the pretenders. The situation is defined as kingmaking if and only if the player can demonstrate that the kingmaker cannot improve his own game result (by whatever metric the game uses, usually placing), but can influence the results of the pretenders. The game is then ended as a tie between the pretenders. If the calculation does not satisfy a player, the game continues (with the kingmaker playing against the disagreeing player), but if that particular player does not win, the tie stands.

      The philosophy of this practice originates in intuition: I once refused to continue playing when I myself ended up in a kingmaking situation, and liked the resulting implicit tie between the pretenders; it seemed fair. The problem of kingmaking might seem to be in that it's annoying to have the victory taken from you by arbitrariness of another player, but the moral price is actually on the kingmaker: I don't think that anybody should be put into the position of having to decide which one of other players wins, that's not fair on him at all.

      The logic in this practice is that it allocates to each player the appropriate responsibility that derives from their role in the game: any player can declare a kingmaking situation because any player who doesn't consider himself likely to win should the game continue will benefit from splitting the victory - thus most players at any given time have a motivation to reveal a kingmaking situation they perceive. The kingmaker himself does not have a say in whether the game continues once a kingmake has been declared, as the whole purpose of the practice is to remove this unconscionable choice from his power. The kingmaking has to be demonstrated to everybody's satisfaction for obvious reasons; furthermore, a player can't contest the kingmaking for anybody's benefit but his own, as doing otherwise would make for another kingmaking situation. Most significantly, kingmaking is never an abstract condition that might be derived afterwards to annul a game result, but a concrete step in playing the game: if nobody notices a kingmaking situation in play or notices it but decides not to declare it, then a kingmaking situation did not in fact happen.

      Different games are more or less prone to kingmaking situations; in some games they are so prominent that I couldn't in fact imagine playing them without a clean, satisfying rule like this. I wouldn't know what to do with the arbitrary choices thrust upon me, and I'd probably start jumping on walls when some idiot on the other side of the table throws the win to some other player at the last moment.

      Whether all this means that legitimate allied victories exist at all is an interesting question; it's too difficult for me to answer off-hand, though. It would seem to me that at least a legitimate three-player allied victory should exist on the Calhamer board, as there are many essentially stable three-player divides of the board that cannot be reasonably unbalanced without causing a solo victory. Some people seem to think that even a seven-player draw is to be considered a legitimate conclusion to a perfect play; I'm suspicious of that myself due to how much stronger the defensive positions of some Powers are, but figuring it out either way would require solving the whole game. Aside from that, I don't quite know how to approach investigating the theoretical issue.

      To a certain extent, the quasi-experimental design begs the question of whether Diplomacy works "as intended"; that is, it assumes that it does. I describe it as a situation characterized by strategic features, and wonder if highlighting those strategic aspects induces people to regard their interactions with others in that situation as fundamentally "strategic" (in the sense of being manipulative, instrumental, and ends-oriented).       \n I thought it was interesting. I am fascinated by what I think of as the "communication dilemma of Diplomacy" in which sincerity and duplicity (and their concomitants, trust and discernment) are so finely balanced and thus so easily upset. In other words, if you "stab" someone, what judgments are you allowed to make about yourself and the other player, and under what circumstances? Is it unreasonable to feel bad about one's own act of betrayal in a game explicitly oriented toward such behavior?

      \n Yeah, a short end-of-game statement is clearly in order just so everybody knows where my tangled web of deception starts and ends.

      At the beginning of the game I got the direct chat info for England and Russia, which means that I communicated with them somewhat more directly than I did with the rest of the board. England liked to chat, so we speculated quite a bit about general Diplomacy philosophy and tactics and such. Meanwhile, Germany sent me a terse message about nobody moving to Burgundy. In hindsight this was obviously a preparation for stabbing, but I didn't have enough background on the players to know it, so I played it cool and kept my promises to both of my neighbours - France has this luxury, as she has SCs in depth in Iberia, so no need to move against anybody in particular on the first turn.

      Later I found out that the Englishman had initiated a plot against myself from the very beginning, and had drafted Germany to be his cats-paw in a hazardous Mun-Bur maneuver. In the fall 1901 I still didn't know about the English part of the plot, so I just did my best to talk the eastern Powers into attacking the German's back to relieve the pressure against me. The German didn't play his part ruthlessly enough here - he chose to be a good boy and support England into Belgium instead of taking a SC from me; at the time I argued that he shouldn't take my SC, as I could always take it back (which was true), but taking one would still likely have been to his benefit for the tempo advantage - he'd have one extra unit for a year while I'd have to concentrate on getting him out of my home SCs.

      All through the first year I pretty much ignored the rest of the board - I had plenty enough to do with my immediate neighbours. In hindsight the Russian decision to go hassle Germany probably directly contributed to his own downfall; Russia is fragile, and his neighbours are keen to react to any seeming weaknesses in his presence.

      In the spring I exorcised Germany from Burgundy, while the Englishman showed his true colors by moving into the Channel. One of my great diplomatic feats in this game was that not only did Russia and Italy move to threaten Munich last year, but in the spring Russia actually supported Italy successfully into Munich! I have to confess that this was pretty gratifying, the German must have regretted attacking me so hazardously then. Of course actually possessing Munich is only worth a tempo advantage for Italy, as he can't leverage the position to do anything useful - Nathan played the position correctly in that he abandoned the SC later to take the army south against Austria.

      I understand that many Powers tried to paint me as the Devil during the game, citing my supposedly superior experience with Diplomacy. The truth, though, is that I did not have particularly smooth sailing with my northern neighbour. In the fall of 1902 I survived with pure tactical brinkmanship, correctly predicting/directing (often the same thing in Diplomacy) that he'd leave Brest alone; relieved from defending it, I moved a risky MAO-Iri and thus balanced the tactical position - I was effectively waging war against both of my neighbours simultaneously and winning, which is no mean feat. Belgium was my reward there.

      Next year I allied with Germany against England long enough to make sure the two wouldn't have enough unity to oppose me - material advantage always counts in the long term, so I had to make sure that the two would hang separately rather than stand together. The poor German apparently expected me to keep my promises, but his position was so vulnerable and mine already prepared for engulfing him, so the choice was easy.

      While the German was harried by his eastern neighbours and England, the English position proved more difficult to break; attacking the island is always a bitch, and in this case the Englishman made few mistakes in the tactical realm. I could advance against him only with diplomatic means, making him doubt his tactics and encouraging him to focus on other opponents. Even then, slow going.

      In 1904 Germany fell to mine and Russian troops - Russia was already in his death throes, so those SCs were as good as mine in many ways. Meanwhile Italy came to his zenith. At this point I'd talked England into disarming our fleet war by moving out of Iri myself (in actuality to protect MAO, but he interpreted it as willingness to turn south). In the fall I took advantage and moved to the Channel while the Englishman went to Nth. England had not been so close to a fall yet, and my plan seemed clear: I'd clear up Germany, build fleets and crush England, while Italy would keep the eastern Powers in check with his impressive empire. Our mutual borders seemed rather safe, as I didn't have troops in position and he didn't have a motivation to attack me.

      The upset to this plan (and my best shot at a solo victory) came in 1905. England performed very well under pressure, not making any tactical mistakes, so there would be no quick victory for me there. But what's worse, the Italian leadership started deteriorating: first the moves became passive, then outright irrational and incomplete. There was even a complete NMR next year. I don't quite understand the thought processes in action here, but faith in my grand plan started to go when assimilating Germany slowed down (the German proved as tenacious as he had been gullible, fighting for Berlin) and the Italian started to make simply stupid moves that gave initiative back to his eastern opposition.

      My English assault might have seemed grand in 1906 when I occupied London, but in actuality the move was mostly designed to intimidate - I'd have an easier time making peace with the tenacious Englishman if he saw me relinquish his capital as a gesture of good will. The eastern forces were clearly on the move at this point, meaning Turkey mostly. His decision to pass on a free SC in StP and the alliance with what remained of Austria after the Italian conquest were just worrisome, but the Italian passivity and the efficient progress of the Turkish fleet were more than that - it was obvious that I would have to get four fleets to the Med as soon as possible, or the Turk would break through and annex the southern half of my own empire. So I had little choice but to seek peace with England at this point, which was also the point at which I lost my realistic hopes of solo victory.

      The last two years of the game were pretty single-minded from my viewpoint: the Turk was in a very strong position, and he would have surely won by a solo victory had somebody less experienced been playing France. As it was, I built an impregnable Mediterranean defense by drawing almost all of my fleet from the north and making some wise tactical calls. I took a good stalemate line, including Tunis, so Turkey had no theoretical long-term chances of a solo victory as long as the English didn't stab me in the back. (There are only 16 SCs on the eastern side of the final stalemate line we ended up occupying with England.) The northern situation was considerably easier, I annexed the Mun-Ber line while the Englishman secured StP against the solidifying Turkish front.

      The endgame was pretty clearcut diplomatically: we had four players on the board, of which none had immediate tactical chances of a solo victory. We had rapidly stagnating military lines that would move only slowly or not at all in any direction. Should anybody wish to significantly change the positions, that would have to come about by starting a new war; as it was, we had Austria and Turkey against France and England. Our alliance with the Englishman was based on the eastern threat, we both expected and considered stabbing the other routinely, and would have done so were there any opportunity. I'm less clear on what happened in the east, to my eyes it seemed that the mongols had an irrational alliance: The Turk could have gotten rid of Austria years ago, but instead he kept the Austrian happy by feeding him any SCs they captured.

      The endgame from my viewpoint was mostly about whether Austria and Turkey could be made to do war on each other. The main outcome of that would have been the destruction of Austria and a much more honorable 3-player draw; a potential secondary outcome would have been a tactical upset to my advantage, although I considered this a rather slim chance - the Englishman had proved an able player, and I didn't doubt that he'd attack me the second it seemed that I would benefit too much from the Austrian reduction. The reduction of the draw was the main goal, though, as that couldn't be achieved in any other way: none of the other three players was in a position to be realistically removed from the game without either me or Turkey achieving solo victory in the process.

      I ultimately decided to offer the draw at the end of 1908 not because it was the best move, but because I hoped that Turkey would refuse it and signal willingness to get rid of Austria. So far my efforts at getting the two to break had been rebuffed in annoying ways (the Austrian especially communicated in a very annoying manner, his words full of thinly disguised lies), and I was severely doubting their rationality. To an outside observer it very much seemed that the two were not in an alliance of mutual benefit, but rather in some sort of American honor entanglement that had no other point but to mess up the game for the rest of us. Maybe the Turk didn't want to destroy Austria because he felt bad about stabbing a faithful ally? No idea, I on my part did my best to leave myself vulnerable to the Englishman, making it obvious that he would have the means to keep me in check while the Austrian reduction took place.

      We discussed the draw in length with the Englishman before he accepted it, going through the various other options. Presuming that nothing would be able to convince Turkey to attack Austria and go for a three-player draw, we even considered the option of giving the whole of Germany to Austria in the hopes of provoking an Austrian independence struggle. This was deemed too hazardous in the end, as the Austrian had shown no sign of manly thirst for victory - the Austrian might have just continued pushing against me blindly while the Turk might have maneuvered into position to actually achieve the full 18 SCs by walking over the Mun-Ber stalemate line.

      In the end the logic was clear: the only player who could have effectively initiated a reduction in Powers was Turkey, and he obviously refused to do so, as signified by his willingness to settle for a four-player draw. So I don't blame the Englishman for the wimpy outcome, he was just reading the writing on the wall when he accepted it.

      \nScoring

      Props to the English and the Austrian, they both showed grit in the game. The English made no major mistakes in the game, and consequently fought himself into the draw against an aggressive neighbour intent on his destruction. The Austrian, on the other hand, managed a feat few have by bringing Austria back from two SCs - this was mostly the Turk missing the ball, it seemed from the western part of the board, but in Diplomacy it's often impossible to tell which impulses are instigated and which are natural; for all I know, the Austrian manipulated the Turk all the way. And in the end-game the Austrian made all the right calls, evidently, as we were forced to accept his part in the new world order.

      Looking at the game as a whole, I can't say that it would have been much of a challenge for our Diplomacy skills. We just played a bit, had all the deadwood drop off, and ended up with the four players who actually cared enough to play to the end. That's a bit of a too nasty characterization for the German game; he played badly in that he gravely misjudged the motivations of his neighbours, but he stuck it out to the end and only gave up when the Englishman helped me remove him from the board. The Russian play was a bit worse than that in some ways (mostly in being non-directed and not forceful enough), but the main prize for weak-ass play in this case has to go to the Italian - he had the keys to the kingdom, but he flubbed it totally. I might be biased in this, as the total Italian failure directly forced me to abandon my own imperium-building dreams, but that was one dramatic reversal of fortunes for a Power that usually has its largest problems in getting from four SCs to five.
