I've been without a RP group for a while. Lately, my best opportunities to play have been with my brother, who I get to see every couple of months at best. It's difficult to do the game prep close enough to the session to matter, and things don't happen often enough to get much in the way of developed character bits to help with improvising good story, so I've been thinking of pick-up play, something that can be run on the spur of the moment. The times I've tried, it's always been a "Okay, where is your character, what do you want to do?" kind of moment, and without the campaign prep, it is always off to a rough start.

It's gotten bad enough that I've been feeling drawn to stock D&D 3.5, which I've never played and still have a pretty foul taste in my mouth from 2e (I know it's gotten better, but still can't get behind it). I know that it's not well suited to spur of the moment play (unless you want to spend most of the session in the monster manual), but I was feeling drawn to it.

After spending the weekend pouring over D&D, and thinking about it from a "what's fun, and why would I think it'd be easier with this than something else" point of view, there are a couple of things that I've noticed.

1. d20 isn't really that bad
2. Most of what the d20 system is isn't a Roleplaying Game at all, it's a miniatures game
3. It has a strong 'default adventure' (the dungeon crawl)

Built into D&D is this tactical board game of going into a dungeon, killing the inhabitants and taking their stuff. You don't need any plot or inspiration or twists to do this. Sure, it'll get old, sure it's pretty lame, but if you just want to throw down, you're covered. I think this is why I've been feeling drawn to play it.

So anyway, I've been thinking about taking that idea (having a strong default adventure, and a fun game that plays to that adventure well) as a good kick-off, saftey net.

I know this post is a little random, and I don't know where I'm going with this myself, but I wanted it out there to hear what others think.

    Hi Daniel!

    I'm reading a bit into your comments and getting this vibe like you're afraid there might maybe be something inherently wrong or worth feeling guilt over in running an unsophisticated dungeon crawl, so let me squash that hang-up for you.

    Nothing you've said should really be controversial. As you may have observed, however, there was at some point around the mid-eighties the introduction of a snobbish attitude into many (if not most) game texts (inc. AD&D2) implying or outright stating that the strategic dungeon-crawling, min-maxing gameplay associated with the early days of the hobby was brainless wrongbadfun only suitable for the uncouth (or "munchkins"), and the "right" way to play was "story-driven" gameplay where the GM should contrive to get his players to freely participate in his elaborately-prepped plotline. (All the while failing to address the contradiction inherent in such a task.) This was certainly what I considered to be the "way to roleplay" when I was a teenager, and hey, it certainly worked well enough in those days when I and my friends didn't really have a lot of other commitments for our time.

    What I'm basically saying is your instincts and observations on this are pretty solid. The 3.5 revision text, in particular, seems to reflect a conscious attempt to stop trying to be the (impossible) be-all, end-all, one-size-fits-all set of roleplaying rules and focus on supporting a particular style of play.

    My own play experience with 3E D&D bears this out. I've sat through a few too many meandering, tedious games that "upgraded" to 3E -- simply extending the same games that were being played with previous editions of AD&D rules, as though the new rules were like a fresh coat of paint that would magically solve the "mysterious" problem of player attrition. Regarding the 3.5 rules as its own game -- one of action-oriented tactical miniatures glued together with a healthy dollop of the other roleplaying stuff -- has proved to be a lot more fun.

    And I'm pretty sure the reason for this has to do with simply establishing clear social expectations about "what kind of game we are playing." The guy who was only interested in deep character roleplay won't feel like he's justified in passive-aggressive feet-dragging behavior when he doesn't get enough time in-character time to work out his brooding half-elf issues. The gal who just wants to be entertained by whatever epic tale the GM has worked out this week won't complain for the eight time about having to actually learn the combat rules. Everyone's more on the same page, excited to see if that slick attack combo he worked out will be able to take down your half-fiend ogre mage blackguard. That, or they'll have to pipe up and suggest they'd rather play something different.

    Like, no blackjack dealer ever worried Hey, maybe this whole thing is a little too simplistic, maybe everyone would really be happier if I worked something more though-provoking into the game, right? Concerns about "what's fun" without first establishing the social consensus amongst the people who will be participating in the activity are similarly absurd. It's gonna be tough for anyone to offer you advice on what will be fun for you without knowing something about your game's social group.

    Everything sounds good. What's keeping you from playing this already? Rounding up people? Hang-up over prep? Who ya planning to play with? Just your bro, or other unmentioned folks? "Experienced gamers" or not?

    I think the key, Larry, is unfortunately a paradigm shift in the expectations of players. You have to go from "show up and experience the GM's story" or "show up and experience this emulation of cool thing X" and move into "show up and develop, together and in real time, the progress of a story the results of which no one knows ahead of time." Until you accept that anything can happen and the story will not be living up to some external criterion, individual player contributions will always be accepted or rejected on the basis of that criterion (ie, the GM's story, or "how the World of Darkness is supposed to work"). You have to come to the table saying, "I'm going to play this game with these creative people and together we're going to make something new." (Admittedly, it may not really be that new, but preconceived notions will screw things up.)

    How to create the paradigm shift? For my money, the best method is short-run games with little to no prep or build-up. When there's no opportunity to preconceive, there are fewer preconceptions.

    Daniel,

    I don't know exactly why it doesn't work, but a whole lot of accumulated, shared experience provides evidence to the effect that it just doesn't work.

    (Watch out, here comes a bunch of jargon.)

    There's a popular misconception that's pretty easy to form about GNS Modes... if a game contains any degree of competition and esteem-staking, it must be Gamist; if it involves any Exploration, it must be Sim; and if it addresses a story premise, it must be Narrativist. But... ALL things we might reasonably describe as RPGs will variously involve those things in some combination or another. Rather, the correct understanding is something like this: when push comes to shove, one of these priorities has got to trump the others. If it can't, if it's not allowed to, then at best gameplay just... gums up... turns into this situation we're maybe all familiar with where everyone is politely hanging out, enjoying one another's company, but no longer really engaged in the shared activity anymore... you've got a stalemate where no agenda can shine without fear of ruining somebody else's fun. Regrettably, this situation can persist for years, until people realize they have better things to do with their time. (And that's at best.)

    In the case of this particular prospective D&D game you're talking about, I suspect your concerns that you'll need to build some kinda split-mode Gamist/Narrativist hybrid are unnecessary. You can have "this game is about throwing down and whomping the monsterz" and still have grand story elements that might superficially remind you of a more Narrativist game. That's what I meant by "glued together by dollops of other stuff." Just, with the caveat that any participant's attempt to Address Premise in a meaningful way is liable to get mowed over in pursuit of the greater goal of "winning."

    And if what you want out of the game changes as you go, don't you think it's rather more important to plainly express that desire to the other people who are already playing and who may or may not share the sentiment, rather than trying to elaborately engineer a strategy for how that shift can automatically occur, maybe without their consent?
