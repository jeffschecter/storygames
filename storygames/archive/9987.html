    \n I assume you mean just for the indie game scene, ya? Hehe... Define "story game". Or do you mean for role playing in general?

    But I DO like this idea. I'll help....

    I'm thinking single-fold landscape 8.5x11 (i.e. four pages of 5.5x8.5)
    Front Cover
    * Title - "Introduction to Story Games [or Role Playing]"... or "GO PLAY!"
    * Paragraph - "What is story gaming [or role playing gaming]?" section with basic overview of the MOST common techniques (acting, narration, randomizerssx and resources).
    * Maybe a bit of background art showing good looking folks at an interesting table full of high quality accessories and brightly colored maps or what-not, in 30% gray. (NOT a table full of scribbles and dry-erase maps and a disarray of drinks and cookie wrappers.)

    Interior Pages
    * Names, brief colorful descriptions, and web sites for iconic or innovative games, perhaps with thumbnails of the games cover (to better recognize it in an online store?).

    Back Cover
    * Links to "support" sites for gaming in general--here, RPG.net, LARP List (you just gonna do TT?), etc.
    * Links to purchase sites - IPR, Lulu, Unstore (potential for sponsorship or ad revenue, here!)
    * Editable PDF, so that a few of those lines of support and purcase sites can be customized for local game group sites and FLGS links/addresses. (I put my foot down FIRM on this--it MUST be an editable PDF so that it can be localized by the downloaders before printing. This means someone must have Full Acrobat, and the font used for the other links must be a fully embedded font (I can do both, BTW).

    Maybe there's more to do for it, but I think a basic overview, followed by specific games with more complete descriptions, followed by "what you do next" info (learn more, buy, get help) is the only logical structure, regardless of how it's eventually formatted. (Hmmm... maybe a trifold 8.5x11 would have a better "pamphlet" feel than a digest one-sheet, providing more of a sense of authority and "professionalism"? I was mainly thinking digest for the column width; but trifold is a definite standard and very readable and digestable without being overwhelming all at once.)

    \n I suppose it could be targeted, but that's just more work. Kids games would be an almost totally different list than those for an adult (18+).

    For that matter, it could be argued that a pamphlet for mom would be different than one for dad (now we got four). Or adult females v adult males (six).

    In the short term, it's probably best to target female and male adults, and be as comprehensive as possible. maybe the games list could be broken into sections by recommended age or maturity level--kind of like the G, PG, PG-13, and R rating systems but with better-tuned breakdowns that eventually convert to genre for "adult":
    Children
    Young Adult
    Relationships
    Adventure
    Historical
    Futuristic
    Mature (or Dark)

    Just brainstorming, though.
    -----
    This idea will need a Champion, to make the final decisions from the morass of input. The Champion needn't be the same person that does the actual layout work (or hosting of the PDF, though I suppose that could be done on the wiki here). The Champion could, of course, resort to polling, etc, to help with decision-making. But no one way is The One True Way, and so someone has to apply a vision and steer the project to conclusion.

    I don't volunteer. :} But I'll be hot and heavy with advice for content (above) and for later layout critiques (of PDF and its functionality).

    \n Who here has read a pamphlet in the past three years?

    One thing is: a pamphlet has too many sides.
    Another thing is: a pamphlet has too much information.

    If you're going to do a hand-out, why not a small flier?
    It could say:

    What's a story game?
    It's a game in which you make up a story with other players, in a structured yet partially-improvised way, usually with some random elements in the form of dice or cards. It's something that people do for fun, over the course of an evening. For more information, you could visit [A MUCH UPDATED STORY GAMES WIKI]

    However, it's easier to say:

    What's Danger Patrol?
    It's a game where you play members of the Danger Patrol, who guard Rocket City against aliens, Jupiter's evil machinations and imminent danger! It's a story game; you improvise stories in a structured way. These are stories about defending Rocket City, and there's a tactical element to playing the game. The game uses dice and lots of index cards. For more information, you could visit [DANGER PATROL WEBSITE].

    what is a story game?

    Some friends get together and make up a story. They come to a decision about the kind of story they\u2019d like to experience, kind of like deciding what movie or television show to watch. Then everyone makes up a character and makes decisions about how that character behaves. Sometimes the players even improvise and act out dialogue for their characters, much like actors in a film.

    Often, one player has the responsibility of creating problems for the major characters, describing settings, and acting out the parts of all of the minor characters.
    what kind of story?

    Any kind of story you want. If you\u2019ve seen it on TV or in a movie, or read it in a novel, there\u2019s probably a story game about it.
    what about rules?

    Story games have rules that determine who can say what and when. It\u2019s a bit like playing music as a group\u2014the structure helps you play together instead of making noise. Different games have different rules to help you make up different kinds of stories.

    Dice and cards add an element of uncertainty and surprise to the story, as well as determine who gets the talking stick. If one player wants something to happen, and another player wants something else to happen, dice or cards are often used to determine who gets their way.

    how do you win?

    There\u2019s no formal winner in a story game, like there isn\u2019t a winner in charades. The goal is simply to have fun creating fiction together; as long as this happens, everyone wins.

    By the end of the game, you\u2019ve experienced a story as entertaining and compelling as any movie or book.

    why haven\u2019t I heard of this?

    Because story games are a relatively new phenomenon, and the worldwide community of story gamers is pretty small.
    The story games community has a lot in common with the punk rock and jazz scene. It\u2019s comprised of creative, passionate, intelligent people who are very serious about their fun. There is a strong D.I.Y. ethos, and game designers tend to focus on making the games they want to play rather than designing games that will appeal to the masses.

    where did they come from?

    Story games have three distinct ancestors: tabletop roleplaying games such as Dungeons & Dragons or Vampire: The Masquerade, improv theater like Whose Line is it Anyway, and creative writing.

    While some story games are very similar to Dungeons & Dragons in terms of theme and setting, the stories usually revolve around character growth and relationships rather than killing monsters and taking their stuff.

      Story games have rules because the limits those rules add help to make it easier to be creative while playing them. And if you were working with others, it was often hard to not talk over each other, or step on toes.

Often the rules will use random elements, like the roll of a die, the draw of a card, or even word association to add surprises and spur on further creativity. In any case, the power of the rules is that they help provide inspiration when there are not enough ideas about what should happen next, or help resolve creative differences when there are too many.

      \nWhat Are Story Games?
      \nStories you play with your friends.
      \n
      \nGrab your friends. Meet someplace comfy. Have some snacks. And get ready for something exciting and new! You don\u2019t need a computer, funny costumes, special toys, or a lot of time and money to play.

      \nPlaying is like acting in a movie with no script. We write the movie as we play it. Using our imaginations we explore new worlds, overcome challenges, and have adventures. Using the game we twist the story in unexpected and thrilling directions.
      \n
      \nFirst we pick what story we want to play. Like choosing what movie or TV show we want to watch. Then we decide who we want to play in that story. And then we play! When we\u2019re done, we will have experienced a story as entertaining as any movie or book. A story we made together.

      \n
      \nSocial: Story games promote collaboration, build confidence, and bring friends closer together.
      \n
      \nSmart: Story games promote reading, problem solving, and strategic thinking.
      \n

      \nStory games provide rules to inspire, fuel creativity, and bring a group's ideas together. The rules help structure and guide you. And different story games have different rules to help you make different kinds of stories.

      \n (Yes, I am a writer/editor--"good" is in the eye of the beholder....)

      I'd try to get game descriptions into place (as you've requested), and I'd definitely pick a broader range of games. For instance, there are no "shared-character(s)" games (e.g. Universalis... or is PTA a shared-characters game?). I see that you want it to be single-sided, but that's a LOT of white space... maybe you could tighten it up on top and provide more space for listing games? Or provide a Big List of Games and Big List of Sites on the back, for folks really interested and seeking a very particular experience or advice on a specific game or on game play techniques in general?

      I'd also make the FLGS info into an editable form field, as mentioned above (I only meant that the maker of the PDF has to have full Acro Pro, to be able to place the fields; yes, Reader can complete fields and save to a new file.) You can, of course, provide default text--but I wouldn't make that default text localized! Make it say something more like "Most Story Game designers encourage you to purchase from your local game store, which can easily be found in the Yellow Pages under "games" or with an online search." You know: some kind of verbiage that take about as much space as the typical four-line address + telephone and web site link (i.e. about six lines at that column width).

      I'm neutral on the verbiage--I like it light and generalized, but what about my emo porn? There's a LOT of SGs that revolve around difficult and emotional situations, and that is also a problem (I feel) with the sample set of games. Where's My Life With Master or Grey Ranks? I'd DEFINITELY list every award-winning or -nominated game, in the "recommended games" lists (along with the award/nomination)--those are far better indicators of quality than individual experience and personal preference (which is all you'll get from a poll here). You also can't go too far wrong with IPR and/or Lulu's top sellers list (filtered for indie/SGs only).

      NITS:
      - Some of your game site URLs don't work as hyperlinks (those missing the "http://").
      - I'd either alphabetize them or list them by publication date (ascending) or popularity (descending). It looks like they're just listed as they occurred to you.
      - Either use one font size multiple or set the page layout to use register-true line spacing, so it all lines up from column to column.
      - I'm fine with dice, but I'd also include glass beads, maybe a few playing cards, and maybe an r-map-like line drawing on a piece of paper. Get folks AWAY from thinking, "Oh, D&D." If you MUST show only polyhedrals, show great, steaming piles of them, in homage to Vincent Baker games.
      - As for your one-liners for the games you will eventually list, I'd just use the game's blurb, assuming its tone doesn't utterly clash with that of the rest of the flier.

      Looking good so far! I know you're not at typography and layout yet (much), but are you thinking of going with the Soviet/Revolution look that's sort of the scene's "trademark?" Or maybe more "arty" to reflect some of the more targeted game experiences? (This is where a generalized audience REALLY bites you in the ass--maybe you could do two style sheets, with the Soviet thing for FLGSes and the arty thing for conventional book stores or handing out to "moms?" Heck, you could do it all as multiple pages in the same file--even with the form field for FLGS being unique for each, if you have multiple outlets and want to try to target. "Mom" might not stroll into the FLGS as readily as into Barnes & Noble.)
