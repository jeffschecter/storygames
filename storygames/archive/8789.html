\n
\n
\n
\n\nJonathan M \n\n\n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n As I touched upon in the recent Hot War/Cold City thread (and lack thereof) , I find it disappointing that so much of online gaming culture is focused upon the point of sale; the cycle of 'what's cool?', 'what shall I spend my money on?', 'what shall I pick up at gencon?' as well as the fact that lots of discussion of new games takes place under the aegis of pre- and immediately post-release hype.

By contrast, the online resources for actual play are frequently catastrophic. Even though people out there might well be playing the games they buy, they do not keep online logs and if you want to find out about games from reviews you're unlikely to make much headway as most reviews are written by people who have read but not played the games (and even then, most online games reviews are of a terrifically low standard).

I'd like to see online gaming focus less on buying and selling stuff and more on actual play.

For example, our group keep wikis for each of our games with details on player characters, sessions and settings. Thereby making it easier to come back to the games after a break and also providing support for the wider community if they want to see what a Buffy, Te Deum pour un Massacre or Space 1889 game looks like.

Given that gamers love swapping war stories, I'm surprised that this trend has not taken off more than it has. How awesome would it be if every group of gamers kept an online campaign log?

Thoughts, feelings?

    Marketing

    A cautionary tale
    Tedious

    So when you say more focus on Actual Play what in particular are you referring to? One of these two things? Both? Some third thing?
    \nAh, I mean "actual play" as in actually playing the game instead of having it gathering dust on the shelf and talking about it online. Not "actual play" as in "actual play" the type of forum post. Though the second is an example of the first.

    I think that forums (and they're all like this to a lesser or greater extent) tend to focus upon buying stuff because it's something that's easy to bond over. Anyone can spend months discussing what they hope is in some Exalted supplement and everyone can get excited about picking the book up and looking at it. That's a lot easier to build a community around than buying the book, reading the book, digesting the book and integrating it into your campaign. This has created, in my eyes, a kind of shadow hobby made up of people who are 'gamers' but who never actually play.

    I think that it would be nice to focus the online gaming community not upon the point of sale, but upon the interesting bit, namely playing the games. Discussing how the games perform in play is one part of that, describing your campaigns is another.

    To my mind, anything that is about play instead of about buying stuff is a good thing and encouraging people to share their play through the use of public logs or AP reports (caveat what I said above) is a way of doing that.

    Hey Judd, let's not have a fight. Sorry about my earlier post, that was not cool of me.

    \n

    To return to the point: I think there're some really sweet ways that we can strengthen our discussion of actually playing games. One of my favorites is to say, "Hey, here's this thing I used in my game, and I've presented it in a way that you can use it too!"

    \n
    \n

    How awesome would it be if every group of gamers kept an online campaign log?

    \n
    \n

    Our six-year ongoing Exalted game, Sol Invictus, has logs reaching back to the very first session. The thing is that these logs really aren't a good way to present the game to others; even the summary of the game, used to bridge the gap between "stuff you know about from Exalted books" and "important plot and cosmology stuff particular to this game," is extremely unwieldy and takes a lot of focus and buy-in to get anything out of.

    \n

    But over the course of the game, we have created a host of supplemental materials that are useful at the kind of low buy-in that makes them accessible: maps, rules for stuff that's missing in the game (like more interesting monsters and nonhuman beings, better rules for gods, new ways to handle governance of NPCs, our own character doohickeys...), theories and experiments about cosmology, etc. We've got discussions about what rules work and which don't, musings about the setting, and so on.

    \n

    That stuff is pretty awesome and I love hearing about it when other folks are doing it. There was a time when I'd have my finger in every custom-Exalted-stuff pie I could find. Every cosmological theory. Every setting reinterpretation.

    \n

    How can that kind of stuff - the artifacts and accessories of play - become more accessible?

    \n I recently read a journal article on a computer program called MEXICA that writes stories. Well, really it plots narratives from simple emotional transaction events labeled with short bits of user-provided flavor text. Oh, I just found a website with two of its stories.

    Anyhow, one of the things it does internally is model all its narratives in an abstract form and stores them in a file. Anytime a new narrative comes to an impasse, it automatically sorts through the file for previous characters who have been through a similar network of emotional transactions. Also, it can judge the novelty of the current narrative based on that file and add something to shake things up if the narrative is getting too cliche.

    I'm not sure exactly where I'm going with this But it grabs at me that MEXICA's models are creatively useful for other users because the models are stored centrally and as abstract forms rather than as detailed artifacts.
