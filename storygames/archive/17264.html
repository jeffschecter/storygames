\n(Usual disclaimer about these only being generalizations and there are many exceptions, blah blah blah)

This topic evolved from a conversation with a woman in my gaming group, who obviously games, and with my wife, who doesn't. The gamer girl population increases every year. In many avenues, females can actually outnumber males. While this is good news, the bad news is how little this is actually changing the games themselves. At a BlizzCon panel discussion an female querist was booed for suggesting that maybe the female characters should be able to wear clothes.

But the problem goes way deeper than that. Even within RPGs where female gamers are not only accepted but delightfully encouraged, the roles they prefer are often within healer, support, or diplomatic archetypes (again, major generalization, but largely true nonetheless). Wish fulfillment seems to be the more desired form of escapism as opposed to power fantasy or strategic simulation.

Tabletop gaming's roots are wargames, and this continues to form the foundations of almost all mainstream RPGs. The point is ultimately combat, no matter how many peripheral rules and options exist within the particular game system. And at the end of the day, many women just aren't interesting in killing things and taking their stuff.

Assuming you accept my premise (and I know that's a big if), this should be informing game design, but it isn't. With the notable exception of Nobilis, even games with female contributors ultimately seem to be about fighting. Beyond the wargame roots, the other obvious explanation is that combat is the most easy and obvious form of conflict, and it's hard to build any kind of play that doesn't revolve around conflict.

The video game world started to figure this out, and we got the Sims, a fantastic series of games (It is; deal with it) that is also more popular than the Bible. A Second Life is still in existence, largely due to its popularity among female gamers.

I also believe the popularity of Game of Thrones lies in its evolution from the Tokienesque war of good vs. evil toward a focus on individual characters striving toward deeply personal goals.

The question is this: If the bedrock of tabletop RPGs were not male-centric wargames, but rather a different female-centric or androgynous premise, how would it look different? How should this change in thinking change the way we're designing games?

    \n Can I just point out that there's a bit of irony present when hobbies (computer games and RPGs) that, 20 years ago, were viewed as bastions of sad outsider antisocial nerd culture have become socially acceptable to the point where they now represent an element of dominant culture and oppression.

    That being said, I think that the problem you've identified is already being addressed as part of modern long-tail economics, the premise of which is that in the contemporary state of reduced production costs (it's comparatively cheap to produce and market a PDF game text, and way cheaper and easier to do decent layout - look at the ugliness of the first 5 years worth of D&D books in comparison) and increased communication and delivery (way easier to set up a website to market a game and make at least a little money off of it than it is to negotiate the old ways of brick and mortar distribution), products will come available to satisfy more niche elements of a market rather than only focusing on the most dominant demographic.

    And that seems to be happening at a reasonable rate.

    And it's a solution that is self-compounding. More games that are available that satisfy more diverse interests results in more diverse demographics becoming involved which results in more market available for more diverse games.

    Yes, the biggest hitters with the highest production values (and therefore the highest production costs and the need for the broadest market appeal) will target the primary demographics more.

    But the long-tail market will continue to grow as long as niche demographics continue to be able to find suitable niche products. And that seems to be happening just fine.

    \n The indie RPG movement was started primarily by a group of guys (yes, guys) who wanted to look at how RPGs could be redesigned in ways that would focus more on how to construct stories and less on how to simulate tactical combat. (Yes, that's an oversimplification - I'm not writing a 7 page Ron Edwards style essay on the subject.)

    That movement lead to more games that do different things in play.

    That lead to more people being able to find games that are a good fit for their interests. Including a closer to even percentage of women than was previously represented in the hobby.

    But also, gaming by women became more popularized in the video game market as well, which enhanced the number of women willing to look at RPGs as a potential activity and reducing the social barriers.

    And, as I said before, more diversity in audience leads to more diverse product production in a reduced production cost environment.

    \n Hi, let me wreck this whole thread. (rubs fingernails on lapels, cracks knuckles)

    The main issue with these kinds of discussions is that in RPGs there is no central organization which can make systemic changes (other than D&D Organized Play, more on which in a minute.) So let me quickly talk about chess.

    I was involved in competitive chess in the late 1980s and early 1990s. I was a bad chessplayer but I loved it, and I ended up the most organized guy in my college chess club, so I was President. This meant I organized some tournaments, and got to interact with all kinds of other tournament organizers, pro chessplayers and officials. In the US, the US Chess Federation was and is the premiere organization for competitive chess.

    Everyone recognized that in the 60s and 70s the game had a huge problem with horrible, misogynist environments at chess tournaments, and outright hostility towards female chessplayers from the bottom of the hobby world to the top of the competitive world. There were trailblazing, brilliant women who pushed their way in, but overall it was just a very ugly situation. In the late 70s, the outcry became too much and chess organizations started to open their doors and shut down sexist actions at tournaments. Codes of conduct, etc. Yet when I came around, competitive chess at your typical local tournament was still around 80-90 percent men.

    A lot of hand wringing ensued. "Why is it like this? We've opened our doors, it's not gross and unfair like in the old days!" When I learned to post on Usenet in college, this was what was heard. (Maybe something really IS wrong with their ladybrains, concluded many morons.) So the USCF decided to go further and entered partnerships with the Girl Scouts and other women youth organizations to teach chess, to organize chess tournaments specifically for them, and to actively seek out and promote women in chess. You would have thought they proposed abolishing the rook. The screams and howls of people saying that this was discriminatory, that this was unfair to men, etc. were amazing. This is the first time I knew that "reverse discrimination" was bullshit - and ever since that time I have always remembered that. ("Did he just say 'reverse discrimination'? Okay. I remember that. That's bullshit.")

    By the time I left competitive chess (after college, like a lot of people), I was still a shit chessplayer, but the stats were coming in and the USCF had doubled, in a very short amount of time, the number of women players in competitive tournaments, even in regular old local tournaments. Doubled.

    So start from this position: you're in a hobby dominated by white dudes. How does this change? It changes with actively seeking out women to join the hobby, not just making sure that the hobby isn't gross and horrible to them. Women are capable of pursuing their interests just fine, but nobody, women or men, will slog through a horrorshow of harassment, rape threats, and garbage just for some leisure pretendy funtime.

    Now let's look at the RPG hobby itself. In America, the majority of roleplayers start by playing The Current Edition Of D&D and never play any other game unless it's The Next Edition Of D&D. If they end up not liking D&D (anymore) then they don't find another game, they leave the hobby. This means that the key element, the gatekeeper of the hobby is organized play of D&D. This puts the RPGA in the position of the USCF in my earlier story.

    Thus I reach the conclusion: if you want more RPGs that "appeal to women", then you need more women RPG designers and players, and if you want more women to design RPGs, you need more women to play RPGs, and overwhelmingly, in America, that means you need them to play D&D. (There was a time when you could substitute a White Wolf LARP for the RPGA, but that time's past.)

    This is all as prologue to my suggestion: organize an RPGA chapter and do active outreach with your community's women's organizations.

      \n So, there are a lot of really interesting comments about encouraging inclusiveness in a community, and most of what I have to say on that would be "me-tooing" what someone else has put in. I will say that when I was younger, I was very much in the minority in geeky type hobbies (and at University and my professional life as an engineer), and the comment that Rob made about new girls being sniffed over by the unattached males in the group definitely applies (it should be flattering, but actually can get pretty tedious); as well as getting a bigger share of the 'emotional work' that goes into maintaining a community ("There's a thing that's bothering me - and you're a girl! Talk to me about it." Uh, yeah, I'm not actually very good with people. ;-) ) And these downsides (often not expressed in creepy ways, but still downsides) tend to go down as the gender ratio starts to even out.

      In the community that I'm currently in, the ratio of women doing tabletop roleplaying is about 25% (using convention attendance as a proxy), and larping is about 50% (totally awesome!) For people who are interested in doing outreach in their local communities, that 25% feels like a lot of women around. It's enough that you can have women in your game without having to make an effort, it's no big deal to be female and in a leadership position (running cons, writing high profile games, pushing for community wide changes), there are some kinds of games (like riffing off Austen or PG Wodehouse or teenage girl social dynamics) that get good attendance - men as well as women, that I suspect just wouldn't happen without a certain critical mass of XX chromosomes around. I've also read texts about encouraging behaviour change in the workplace that put the tipping point ratio at about 30-33% - if you can get that proportion of people to change what they're doing to what you want, to everyone else it starts to feel inevitable and like it's everywhere already, so they feel they should fall in with the change as well. So if you want to change your gender ratio, as Jason Corley said about chess tournaments, I'd say that it's important to push hard in the early days, but once you're up to around 1/4 or 1/3 you'll have a self sustaining density - people will just turn up through their social networks or because they perceive your hobby as a 'thing that people like they are' do.\n
      The question is this: If the bedrock of tabletop RPGs were not male-centric wargames, but rather a different female-centric or androgynous premise, how would it look different? How should this change in thinking change the way we're designing games?
      \nTo go back to the original question, as a thought experiment about if roleplaying the hobby hadn't come out of military enthusiasts playing Braunsteins, but maybe from a Bronte-esque group of women putting some formality onto their shared story telling, some stuff that you might get:
      (Can people assume lots of disclaimers about how I'm proposing tendencies rather than any particular individual or group?)

      - Lots of crafting tie ins. You can already see that in the ways that many women engage with roleplaying and related hobbies - it's common for women I know to turn up to their gaming group with knitting or embroidery or something to fidget with while they talk; women-oriented publications like Cerise magazine and Gaming As Women both put in regular features about crafting or sharing food with one's gaming group; anecdotes from my friends who are into wargaming is that they know very few women playing at a highly competitive level, but they're used to seeing women doing really well in the painting competitions; craft days and prop making are a great community builder in my local larp scene. Lots of stuff like that. Things that aren't exclusively female or pursued by every female, but do show up as a trend.

      - Different aesthetics. This is kinda linked in with the crafting thing. I think that a lot of hobbies that are stereotypically 'female' have got a lot of focus on micromanaging choices around colour and texture - so textile arts, bead work, scrapbooking, being interested in fashion, that whole class of activity can look kinda boring if you're not into it, but if you are into it, it's really engaging. So, I think that a reason that games like The Sims did well with female audiences is because they enabled a large amount of customisation and aesthetic choices. I've also read accounts of women engaging with video games where they found it really rewarding being able to customise their character images in a lot of detail, body shape, faces, outfits, all that stuff. That's only part of the game experience for them - these are the same women who talked a lot about strategy and enjoying going out and blowing stuff up, but it's one that they appreciated. (Please note, when I say that I think a lot of women are interested in aesthetics, I don't mean "Make it all pink." That pretty much strips out all the creative customisation features that are appealing.) Conversely, I've watched men I know half-hypnotise themselves doing stuff like micromanaging inventory layouts in video games, or poring over treasure lists, or keeping a database of every book they've read ever. It's not an interest that I shared, but it certainly looked like they were getting a dopamine hit out of it - maybe that's something that affected the design of D&D style games?

      I also think that the artwork would be different. I'm happy for my opinion on this to be challenged by an Arts or Design major, but my general impression of art in the bulk of roleplaying, video gaming and fantasy/SF fields is that it kind of has a tunnel vision effect - the images are designed to funnel your attention to a few key parts of the image, particularly art that gets labelled as catering to the 'male gaze'. It's not just the content of these pictures, it's how the images direct your attention strongly to a few focal points. I suspect that if fantasy and roleplaying were more of a 'for women' thing, that the artwork might tend to come with a more diffuse focal ground, possibly with different base palettes (less bold and/or murky), and be more textured.

      I think there'd still be lots of action-adventure stories, but there's a But. I think there would be less focus on the specific details of how a conflict plays out, like spending an hour and a half crunching out the numbers to resolve a fight, and more attention paid to inbetween scenes. I think that games that spend a reasonable amount of time negotiating the relationships between the characters (sometimes romance, but also friendships and family relationships), would be much closer to the default than 'unusual games worthy of note'. I think that violence would be treated differently: less about keeping score of hitpoints followed by healing back to the status quo, much more about recovery from violence and how to deal with it emotionally. I'm going to expand on that last point some - in the women-oriented fiction I cited above, Hurt/Comfort is a recurring Thing. It's not the only way that acts of violence are handled, but it turns up over and over and over again - where the act of violence itself (maybe someone's lost their family to a tragic accident, or been beaten, or sexually assaulted) gets dealt with very briefly in the text, often with depersonalised attackers or even happening before the story starts, and the rest of the story is 'about' how someone goes about getting their life back, dealing with PTSD, controlling the information of who knows what about what happened, who helps who recover, how to reintegrate into one's community... It's the opposite of refrigerating a character, and it's something that I generally only see in fiction that's got women in the intended audience. Fiction that seems aimed predominately at guys, like big action movies, seems to be more interested in playing out the precursors to violence (like when heroes and villains trade threats at each other) and the details of the violence (like who punches who), but seems to move over the recovery phase pretty quickly - or move straight into the revenge scene. Anyway, it's something I've been thinking about. (And getting depressed about, but what the hell.)

      The weird thing about women in gaming is how we have always been here, yet there seems to be an continuous, deep-rooted impression that we are suddenly just arriving on the scene. I've been playing RPGs for over 30 years. Very nearly all my regular gaming groups have had at least one other girl or woman in them. I know I'm not the only one.
      \nI think this part of a narrative that there used to be sexism in the past in gaming, but now it's being phased out. That narrative implies that we shouldn't be concerned about it so much anymore, because bias is a thing of the past.

      By my estimate, tabletop RPGs early on had roughly 5% - 10% women. In the nineties this went up to around 10% - 20% women, and has stayed at roughly that level. Larps have a significantly higher percentage of women (30% - 40%, varying depending on genre). As far as I have seen, the newer style story games have about the same ratio of women as other tabletop RPGs. \n
      Part of the answer here may be to look at wargames as less male-centric? As is anecdotaly pointed out several times in the thread, there are plenty of womenfolk who enjoy a good fierce fight. Throughout history, women have fought. Who was defending the homefront every time the menfolk marched off to the battlefield? The manner of fighting might differ some, might be more devious and political and ruthless and less about moving tight groups of bodies from one place to the next, but women have a sense for warfare and fighting and strategy and competition just the same. This points back to Emily's comments about conflict over combat. Conflict is gender-free, combat is more gendered. Design for interesting conflict, calibrate the blood to where you want it for your game design, and play games with women. That way more women will be playing games.
      \nIn practice, conflict mechanics often mean that we use abstract zero-sum mechanics (i.e. win-vs-lose) for social interactions as well as for physical combat, as opposed to the traditional case of using zero-sum mechanics specific to physical combat and not using explicit mechanics for social interaction. I think combat-like social conflict can be gendered. For example, White Wolf's larp system (MET) allowed social conflicts - but my observation was that they were rarely used - and when they were used it was generally by men rather than women. I feel like that is also true for my tabletop experience as well.

      \n Amongst all of its other appeals, I think MLP has value for it's non-intended audience in that it also allows them to enjoy something they don't normally get to. It's almost cool to like MLP and gives one an almost iconoclastic status. I'm in my 30's, big bushy beard, former military, I mountain climb and go camping in very remote places... oh and I watch MLP. Its underground popularity allows me to express and acknowledge something about myself that is very different from a lot of the other (somewhat) masculine things about me.

      Not everyone is interested in exploring that "feminine" side, but there is a desire to do so within the existing gaming community, and people not currently gamers (like women). I put it in quotes, because it's not accurate, but for my limited vocabulary it works as a shorthand. Looking at the feminine side of our culture, how issues arise and are dealt with, how non-gender problems are solved and relationships are built won't just open up gaming to new audiences, it has the potential to give us new ways to game as well.

      In FATE, you can get someone to cooperate with you by causing them stress. Insults, intimidation, threats, being overbearing are a few examples and they're all pretty aggressive. In a game where you can create scene Aspects for your friends to use, you can try to influence them by creating rewarding opportunities, but that means they have to follow your lead.

      This doesn't necessarily reflect cooperation and compromise terribly well, or at least not in a mechanical sense. If there is compromise, it happens between the players or characters on level of interaction outside of the rules. I'm not terribly familiar with LARPs, but the little bit that I know, I'm guessing a lot of this can happen outside of the mechanics of the game as well, but there the game is primarily made up of social interaction. A table-top is a much smaller setting, so we need mechanics that reinforce a broader array of social dynamics, not just dominance.
