\n I saw the words "narrative style" and immediate thought, I wonder if you could make a game that uses this as a technique.



What do you think? It might not be an actual portal, it could be a narrative heavy TORG style game of multiple genres that switches based on the character in control. Or whatever.

(for the record, I don't have an interest in pursuing the idea fully)

    \n We're sort of doing this with our Galactic game.

    Each scene shifts to a new Captain and crew, and each ship has its own style. One is gritty and brutal, one is professional and brisk, one is comic and homey, etc. Trollbabe games sometimes have this quality, too, as you shift around to each 'babe's storyline.

    You could do something super crazy with PTA and introduce the ability to change channels while you play. That hurts my brain just typing it.

    \n On 'Whose Line is it Anyway' and in other improv venues I've seen, they play a game where the same scene is played as the referee occasionally shouts style changes. I can't think of a lot of reasons I'd want to do this in most games, but I can see it being funny or interesting if done just so.

    \n I love self-referential genre manipulations. There was a Zippy comic once where the entire comic was drawn in a gritty Frank-Miller like style, with Zippy thinking stuff like "Here I am again, wearing polka dots and ready for trouble." Apparently, the public's tolerance for this is a lot lower, since it's apparently a big taboo in comic strips (at least until Webcomics came along and cut out the middleman).

    I read about a game once on RPG.Net where each session, they'd do a different genre with the same characters, usually through giving them a themed missions. So one week they're in film noir territory exploring a mysterious death, next week it's helping out some aristocrats with their wedding plans in a drawing-room farce. I envy people with skills to run/play stuff like that.

    \n "Itras By", a Norwegian RPG, does this with its chance cards. Each session, each player may draw one chance card; they have all sorts of surrealistic effects, and some of the ones my group tested changed the narrative style. One called for a scene to be played as a musical, for example; we were all standing up and singing our lines.
