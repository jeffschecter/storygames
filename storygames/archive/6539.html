\n Played In a Wicked Age with three others last night using \u201cthe Unquiet Past" Oracle. I'd played an off-genre game at Dreamation, but this was otherwise our first attempt. We stumbled over the rules in a few places, especially in the three-way conflicts, but muddled through and had a good time. We\u2019re currently trying to reconcile some differences we see about the nature of conflict as described in the rules and as described by Vincent in forum posts - they seem to be very different in some instances. I really enjoy the negotiation part of the game. I\u2019m just not sure we played it as intended.

But my comment is more about face-stabbing.

There were two instances where my character, an honored emissary named Daol, was in a scene that started with dialogue but moved almost immediately to my adversaries launching themselves at my face with sharp, pointy objects.

Our group has become fairly adept at identifying scenes and actors that are likely to lead to entertaining play and likely to lead to conflict.

The first ISYITF scene was initiated by our GM, Colin. My honored emissary, Daol, is interviewing youths as prospective candidates for a situation at a prestigious academy in the capital of the Thelan Empire. His intent is to single out Tremellion, the twelve-year old \u201cChosen One\u201d who\u2019s prophesied to be a dangerous adversary someday. He intends to separate him from his home, and ultimately to see him dead (orders from Emperor Adad). Tremellion is being played by David. Daol opens with some innocuous comments, Tremellion insults him but Daol pretends not to notice. When Daol asks Tremellion to name the seven principles of metallurgy, Tremellion pulls a long metal hairpin from hiding, makes an awesome quip about its metallurgical properties and \u201cdrives it into Daol\u2019s eye.\u201d

I think the penultimate scene was arrived at by consensus. We were getting close to winding up the evening/chapter. The scholar Belleran is shackled to a pillar in the throne room. He has been framed by the high priest Shahkam for the crime of assassinating King Melak. Christian is playing Shahkam. Daol wants to see the scholar ascend to the throne and has been using his network of spies for some months to spread rumors of the king\u2019s frailty and the scholar\u2019s worthiness of rule. Shahkam gives a speech about the worthiness of the old gods, once forbidden, and that the crown should belong to him. Daol has spies planted throughout the throne room, who are starting to shout things like, \u201cBelleran is innocent!\u201d \u201cBelleran should lead us!\u201d Daol starts to work the crowd and give his own speech about how Shahkam was the assassin (true), and how only through the scholarly leadership of Belleran and the friendship of the Thelan Empire can the kingdom of Goloqaat hope to prevail against its enemies. Before I could make my statement that I knew Christian would object to strongly, leading to conflict, Shahkam drew his dagger and plunged it into Daol\u2019s face.

Both were situations where my character seemed to have the upper-hand narratively (scenes that suggested rushing up to a conflict), but in both scenes my character\u2019s opposition were able to make the conflict about whether Daol had a sharp object through his cranium, by stabbing him in the face before I made a noxious enough statement-of-intent.

So Daol and Shahkam are rolling around on the throne room floor and their players are negotiating back and forth as the dice and their decisions dictate. This is pretty hilarious and improbable on reflection; they are in mortal struggle but having to whisper to each other about what each of them want for the negotiated outcome to make any sense. Shahkam finally relents of his attack, lets Daol up and announces that a horrible mistake has been made, that Belleran is indeed innocent and should take the throne. Daol makes a secret signal to his spy network who begin to shout things like, \u201cYes, Belleran our King!\u201d \u201cDown with the monkey god, up with the gods of old as Shahkam has told us!\u201d All per our negotiated outcome.

Please understand that all are having a good time, and no one feels like anyone\u2019s toes are being stepped on while they\u2019re being stabbed in the face (if you take my meaning).

I\u2019m just wondering whether we\u2019re rushing up to conflicts too quickly, and if we are, whether the rules as we understand them aren\u2019t *strongly* suggesting that be the mode? And if we aren\u2019t, then is it an interesting feature or an interesting bug that characters that seem to be in a situation where they\u2019re headed towards a favorable end, can potentially so suddenly have things reversed on them, simply by the opposing character\u2019s player stabbing that character in the face? Is that the rules, our group, or something else? Have we missed the point of the game entirely?

    \n I've been thinking about "social conflicts" since the game, particularly the Shakham (SHAKHAAAAM!) vs. Daol conflict.

    As I understand it, any character can do anything they can reasonably be expected to have a chance of doing, including persuading "extras" (by which I mean, NPCs who have no dice). It's only when the action is something that someone else with dice, be it PC or NPC, is around and doesn't want it to happen that we roll.

    Say Daol wasn't around in that scene, of *course* Shakham (SHAKHAAAAM!) could sway the crowd, he's the high priest of Goloqaat! Or the reverse with only Daol around, of *course* Daol could sway the crowd, he's a respected emmisary from the Thalen empire *and* he's got spies hidden throughout the crowd to praise his name.

    It's when they're both around and one doesn't want the other to succeed that we go to dice.

    Cases of one PC directly trying to persuade another would seem to break down into one of the following:\n
      \n
    • Character A offers some attractive deal to Character B, probably something that lets him achieve his character's best interest. The players agree. Negotiation concludes without dice.
      • \n
    • Player A says "I persuade character B to (do disagreeable thing), and *he does it*" (per JDs explanation above), but Player B doesn't like this and they go to dice. Player B may end up doing whatever it is he didn't want to do, just to avoid getting exhausted or injured, or he may choose to get exhausted or injured and not do the thing.
      • \n
    \nSo, if I'm processing this right, saying that you persuade someone is fine, and it will work against inconsequential people, but you can never *make* an PC/NPC *do* something if the person running them would rather that they die than do it. Which is ok with me, it makes things clearer in some ways.

    \nThis points up the confusion I (we?) have with conflict resolution.

    We played it the way you describe above. There is an implicit setting-of-stakes in your example: "If I win, you give me the milkshake." We play out three rounds of conflict, and at the end we negotiate the result - "Hand over the milkshake, or become Injured/Exhausted."

    In a IAWA thread on the Forge forums, Vincent cautions against setting stakes and states unequivocally that the only stakes in the conflict are the consequences - Injury/Exhaustion (and/or a negotiated alternative). Everything else follows the narrative:

    Me: I convince you to give me your milkshake.

    You: Like hell! It's pure creamy goodness!

    [Conflict Round One: I gain advantage]

    Me: I shame you for your selfishness, and you sheepishly hand over the milkshake. Grinning, I start to slurp it down...

    You: [shakes fist]

    [Conflict Round Two: You steal advantage]

    You: Ha Ha! I smack your elbow, so the milkshake spills all over your new silk shirt. If I can't have the milkshake, NO ONE CAN!

    Me: Aaargh!

    [Conflict Round Three: You win]

    You: Your clothes are ruined, and you are Humiliated! Take a die size off of "For Others" and "With Love."

    Me: Curse you! I will avenge the milkshake! But I'll take my lumps...

    It's unclear from the rules-as-written, but quite clear from Vincent's posts on the matter, that actions resolve in the middle of conflicts rather than being suspended until the end. At least it seems clear...

    \n A big piece of this confusion is confusion between actions and intended consequences. The proper response to "I convince you to give me your milkshake" isn't "like hell," it's "oh really? How?" Thusly:

    Me: I convince you to give me your milkshake.
    Mitch: Oh really? How?
    Me: Um, I say, "give me your milkshake. You know you want to."
    Mitch: "Yeah, no, not so much." I'm like, slurp, slurp.
    Me: Like hell! I snatch it before you can slurp. No slurping for you.

    NOW we can roll dice.

    When you roll dice in response to someone saying something, that means your character is going to try to prevent them saying it. Thusly:

    Me: I convince you to give me your milkshake.
    Mitch: Oh really? How?
    Me: I say, "hey, give me your milkshake."
    Mitch: The hell you do. I punch you in the throat before "hey, give -" is out of your mouth.

    \n Technically, by the printed rules:

    The player of the character who tops the Owe list chooses the Oracle for the next Chapter, and chooses one item from that Oracle to be included in the next Chapter. The character who tops the Owe list is automatically included. The rules seem to presume that the character will be played by the player who played him or her during the current Chapter.

    When buying a character into a Chapter by striking a name off the Owe list, a strict reading of the text implies that the player of a character chooses an entry of that character's name on the Owe list to strike, and that character is then included in the next Chapter. (This is different from striking a name to get a bonus die -- strict reading of that text implies that you can strike the name of any of your characters in order to get a die for your current character.)

    The third way to bring back a character is to identify a new Oracle element as a previously-existing character (probably accepting some random Oracular development of the character in order to bring him or her back into play). The text strongly implies that only the player who played the character originally is allowed to bring the character back in this way.

    All that being said, it appears to be a fairly common rules-drift to allow some flexibility with those rules, particularly from groups that have a lot of indie-gaming experience, and may not feel the strong ties of character-ownership that many trad-gaming groups do:

    If the player of the recurring character will be absent from the next session, some groups go down the list until they find a character who's player will be present, and go forward from there, leaving the skipped names for later sessions. Some groups give a characters original player 'first dibs' on playing the character, but if they choose not to, the character is up for grabs. (This may become problematic when a character shifts back-and-forth between being a PC and being a GM-run NPC, especially after Chapter 3 when the GM position can rotate, but anybody drifting the rules this way should be able to figure out how to handle it.)

    Hope this clarifies things, a bit.

Our emotions swell up unbidden in reaction to the world around us, and we are swayed and persuaded by others' words. Sometimes we do things against our own best interests, and sometimes we will even rationalize our actions afterward as being entirely our own ideas. Communication can be used, even without deceit or the threat of force, to change our minds without us realizing it.

    Having that kind of control over our characters' heads, then, presents a problem. It means that our characters can't be goaded or convinced of anything we don't want them to. Which makes their hearts, minds, and souls far safer than their bodies, which can be threatened with injury much more directly. Words are weapons because of the way they make us act in spite of ourselves.

    In a game I played this weekend, I fell victim to this problem. One character was goading me into foolish action using fairly reasonable tactics, but I was used the inviolable mind of the character to choose resonses that were reasonable but other than her intent. A thumbnail recreation of the scene. The prince and princess are in a functionally private conversation, and the kennel has become a deathtrap. The princess wants the prince to go there and be trapped in death:\n
    GM: She says, "You are a coward."
    Me: "A woman knows nothing of honor or cowardice."
    GM: "I know that a woman would not fear the kennel."
    Me: "A prince does not deign to spend time with dogs."
    [Etc., etc.]
    GM: You are so enraged that you get up and rush to the kennel.
    Me: Hell no.
    She was clearly using her words as weapons, but I hid behind my control over my character's emotions to deflect those weapons (or, more accurately, to deny that the weapons even existed at all).

    Strangely, I think my biggest problem with narrating "You are so enraged that you leave the banquet hall and rush to the kennel" is not that it is impossible (it is not), but that it is bad storytelling. It just sounds terrible; that kind of thing never gets said in stories because the author just starts describing a character with a changed mind. But it's a player's call to the game for help with the story. It's a player saying, "What I want can only be handled by us as authors, so I'm going to risk my character in the story to put your character in similar jeopardy, in the hopes that I'll be able to negotiate that your character is persuaded." Because we aren't setting stakes, the characters' conversation may instead turn to previously unexpected but very dangerous places in the struggle.

    I guess that one way is to make sure players know to narrate emotions in other players' characters. This may be counterintuitive, but it's more like the way the world works, where we can't control whether we feel emotions, but we can choose how to react when feeling them. So a player can narrate, "She says, 'You are a coward,' and you are filled with rage." I might say no, because I don't want my character filled with rage, or I might say okay, and continue my own narration having accepted that my character is filled with rage. Another good example is probably, "I remove my robe, and she is filled with lust." It doesn't necessarily mean she'll go to dice or succumb to my charms, but it'll complicate the story.

    But that doesn't fully address persuasion. The threat of force is always easy to identify, but the threat of influence and manipulation are, by their nature, camouflaged. What I don't yet see, and what I'm looking for, is a way to say, "Look, this playful exchange of words we've been having? That wasn't 'just words'; it was a psychological assault to convince your character to do this thing. So either you start narrating your character as convinced of it, or I'm going to pick up these dice, throw them on the table, and possibly crush your character's soul instead." I'd like to be able to do that without breaking the tone of the story using hacky summations of complex psychological issues. I don't know if it's possible.

Let's just say that it looks tasty and that I'm hungry. First, I'd ask how you were doing. I imagine you'll say something like "Fine," or "Pretty Good," or "Can't complain." Maybe you say something like, "This milkshake is so yummy, how can anything go wrong!" Then I'd explain that I'm not feeling too good just then. You see, I skipped breakfast thinking I'd grab a meal later, but when I was long out of the house, I realized I didn't even have my wallet. Now, I'm starting to get cranky because I haven't eaten, and it'll be a long time before I can get some real food.

      Then I'd ask if you could spot me for lunch. If you said yes, well, then I've gotten a lot more out of you than a milkshake, I've gotten a whole meal. The meal may even come with a yummy milkshake of my own, in a flavor of my choice. But if you said no, I might remind you of the time I brought snacks to a game when you happened to have missed lunch yourself. If you still demurred, I'd be understanding. Then, I'd ask, "Hey, do you think I could finish off the rest of that milkshake, to tide me over for a while?"

      Now, I haven't presented a rationally convincing argument or presented a clear negotiation of valued items. If you see that in what I've done, it's purely coincidental. I've spent all that time creating a situation where you feel obligated to give me something. First, I get you to say how pleasant your situation was, to provide a contrast to my own. Even if you don't actually feel as pleasant as you said you were, you feel a pressure to be generous to live up to the emotional state that you attested too, lest you appear inconsistent or deceitful. By bringing up similar gifts in the past, I remind you of the implicit rule of reciprocity in our society, that gifts freely given should be freely repaid. Finally, I give you another gift, a concession, by changing my request from a full meal to a mere unfinished milkshake. By that time, having rejected so many seemingly reasonable offers of reciprocity, you'd feel responsible for my situation, since it would have been your refusals keeping me hungry. By the end, part of a milkshake would appear rationally reasonable, and you'd also be feeling a great emotional pressure to give me something, anything.

      Is it possible that you wouldn't be effected in that way? Of course, but intense study has demonstrated that it is far from likely. In the same way, it's possible that if I take out a gun, point it at you, and pull the trigger, a bullet won't rip through your body injuring you; but it's not likely. It's so reasonable, in fact, that in IAWA, I can say, "I take out a gun, point it at you, and pull the trigger; and the bullet rips through your chest puncturing a lung." So it doesn't seem logical to me not to be able to narrate, "And after I say all of these things, you feel a sense of responsibility for my situation and an obligation to give me something in return."

In the Wicked Age, you should roleplay persuasion out. No character is more persuasive than her player. Instead, every character and player has recourse to force and violence when persuasion isn't working.
      From what I've seen, it's true that no character is more persuasive than her player. But what I've also seen is that each character is actually much less persuasive than her player.

      Because the effects of psychological threats can't be narrated in the same was as the effects of physical threats, characters can't persuade other characters in the way that a player can persuade another player. If the GM had stopped the game (without dice), the deathtrap transcript may have gone like this:
      GM: Hey, tell me why your character is so calm right now.
      Me: Well, he's a veteran warrior, and he doesn't set much stock by his sister.
      GM: I see. It just seemed that, from the way you've been playing your character, that he should be more hot-tempered. He usually isn't this good with words.
      Me: I guess, but I know that you're just trying to get him into the kennel, and I really don't want that.
      GM: No, that's fine. How about you just let the prince react to the princess's words--get angry or embarassed or whatever--and you can decide what to do from there.
      Me: Um, sure, okay.
      Sound reasonable? Care to identify the emotional manipulation used on me, who doesn't have an inviolable mind? Maybe I wouldn't have actually given in. I mean, there's no reason in the rules that I should--the GM can't dictate what my character feels, there's no game-system stick, and he can't technically go to dice until he switches to force (where my character will regain an advantage over his). Still, I'd feel the pressure.

      But what happened instead was that GM had to convince me to agree to narrate that my character feels a particular emotion, using only the narration of actions of his character. That's incredibly difficult, far more so than persuading me directly.

      In my understanding of the game, the desire of a player to protect her character from harm is part of the driving force of the system. But when I want to protect my character from emotional harm, all I need do is deny the existence of a threat and have my character act accordingly. Essentially, I roleplay, "I am rubber you are glue," until the other character switches to some other sort of threat. (This other threat shouldn't be called "escalation," which implies that the threat is necessarily greater. In your model, knocking the milkshake out of your hand is an "escalation," even though the danger is far less than me, perhaps, beginning to undermine your self-confidence. "I'm glad that you can feel comfortable having a milkshake out in public.") So this aspect of the system prevents a player from modeling character emotions naturally. This, in turn, ensures that persuasion will never be modeled accurately, and thus emotional manipulation will almost always fail, necessitating force. And now we've gone full circle to the "I stab you in the face" game.

      As it stands, I guess there needs to be a disclaimer that, no, you can't create a character who will manipulate others' minds and emotions if you're playing a non-modified version of the game. That character's subtle persuasion will almost never work, because his emotional weapons will be ignored while other physical weapons are always respected. Your master manipulator must be a manipulator because of these kinds of physical actions, a manipulator who stabs people in the face fairly often. The character concept for the princess was flawed from creation.

      \n Maybe this is another example IAWA drifitng slightly out of the tone it was intended for. I agree that Conan is never Shamed, and I certainly can't recall him succumbing to emotional maniuplation. But when I look at Nest of Vipers, I frankly don't see Conan. I see Hamlet and Othello. And when I look at Blood and Sex, I see Les Liaisons dangereuses. Those protagonists must be psychologically manipulable for their stories to work.

      Perhaps the inviolability of the mind corresponds to the inability to shame. And consequently, in a game without Injured, the body should be inviolable to another's narration. In that game, I can pull out a gun, point it at you, and pull the trigger; but I can't puncture your lung, only scare you senseless.

      Why am I so insistent on psychological persuasion? Because it's my favorite part of the game as written. During and after action sequences, the negotiation that occurs so exciting for me. And I'm somebody who usually hates it; I can't stand the deal-making aspects of Monopoly, Settelers, or Diplomacy. But taking a stick and seeing what I can get with it? Facing down a stick and seeing if I can turn it to my advantage? It's exhilarating. So I'm surprised that the same interplay can't exist on the story level.

      \n Three things!

      Thing one: the milkshake thing, what you're doing is you're playing my best interests against each other. My drinking the milkshake vs my fulfilling my social obligations to you. Characters in the Wicked Age don't have the complex mix of best interests that I do, but they still have some - more than are written on their sheets - and you can still play them against each other. If you can't figure out how to do it to this particular character just now, that's how it goes. After all, I'm not always going to fall for this obligation you're manufacturing either.

      Thing two: it should be pretty damn easy to match action to words, to force the issue. ("Force and violence" means forcing the issue, not crude bodily attacks.) "She puts your sword into your hand and leads you to the door."

      Thing three: ask the other player what her character does. This corresponds to the moment when you stop wheedling about my milkshake and look at me like it's a done deal, maybe with your hand out. "She's looking at you like you're the most shameful worm any woman ever had for a brother. What do you do?" This is a setup. When you say what your character does, you're committing to which dice you'll roll when she tries to stop you, which of course she will. "Screw this. I walk out." "Great! So that's, what, Directly and For Yourself? She stops you. She'll use With Love and Covertly. She catches your arm. 'Wait, brother, I relent...'"

      The specific details of the mindfuck matter in cases two and three too, of course, not just in case one. The setup, the manufactured obligation, the undermined confidence - that conversation is what's going to get me to stop your action stupidly and to take stupid action for you to stop.

      So, no, you absolutely can play a character as persuasive as you are, whose strength is manipulation. You don't do it by saying "your character becomes enraged, so you're obliged to play him that way now, the dice say so." You do it by playing his best interests opportunistically against each other, by deftly and subtly forcing the issue, and by setting him up to take actions that make him vulnerable to you.
