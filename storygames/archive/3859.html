\n Maybe this has already been suggested and I missed it.

Instead ofthe setting book (which I'm inclined to veiw as a dinosaur), the situation book.

Instead of write ups for the "important" characters in the setting, a gallery of NPCs, each packaged with the unstable situation that they create if they appear in your game.

Factions, groups, and organizations are packaged with an outcome. If this faction exists in your game, here's what unacceptable thing happens if you ignore them.
Each situation element included is tied to something actionable that the characters can do: a situation to become entangled in, an ability or secret to acquire, a bang to resolve.

Instead of providing the GM with a bunch of things for the players to find, provide the players with things that they can pick up and say "This person, place, orthing is in our game." Maybe these need to be printed on cards that can be left out on the table for players to call upon when needed.

Nothing from the situation book matters unless a character engages it. Instead of saying "there's this cool cult", and having everyone nod, you have a cult that only appears in the game when a PC decides to join it, or when it makes sense for a NPC to be member.

Maybe a Clinton-style oracle should be included, custom designed for this setting.

A library of images: pictures of buildings, monster, characters, and items that any player can pick up and say "this is what this person, place, or thing looks like."

For example, in a historical game, the book could include a bunch of historical figures that can be brought into the game.
In a game with factions and intriue, each faction might have a set of setting aspects that hold true as long as that faction is ascendant, like "Dwarves are hunted", "the city is in a perpetual state of military readiness", or "no one dares go out at night."
How about a planetary exploration game with a library of hostile environments and the circumstances they create for exploration and conflict?

This is an idea I'm thinking of exploiting for my alternate-history Renaissance game Principia. The game purposely leaves setting vague. But I also want to give people access to some of the awesome historical story gaming meat I've discovered in my research. Would you use this? What would make this idea awesome for you?

    \n Well, I'm no theory guru, but I like what Vincent says about situation.

    I boil it down to situation = character + elements arranged into a structure that won't stay standing as it is.

    Elements can be broken down into locations and threats (and maybe some other peices too, I'm not clear on this).

    You can also have static situations that stand up just fine, but I don't think those are what we're insterested in here.

    The structure can't stand up as it is because it contains a conflict of interest. Even if the situation is ignored, something is going to happen that some character's don't want.

    Situation is driven by reward, i.e. resolving situation makes some kind of reward happen through the game system. I think a situations book can be fairly abstract about the reward. It probably doesn't need to be system-specific.

    So a situation book could probably contain:\n
      \n
    • Character, who must have interests that can conflict with other interests.

    • Locations, possible ones that create an atmosphere of conflict, danger, or evoke a strong feeling of interest (like a happy home you want to protect). I think there was an SG thread last year about cool places for fight scenes. This might be a place to look.
      • \n
    • Threats that can damage or destroy interests.
      • \n
    • Maybe there's a place for a list of cool interests.
      • \n
    • Maybe there''s a place for a list of cool consequences.
      • \n
    \nQuestion: do you have a bunch of categorized lists, or do you create something more like the Clinton oracle?

    \n This is what I'm striving for (and haven't yet achieved) with Underground Railroad.

    I'm planning to post more about this when I actually do it, but I have an idea for a "Coolness Map" that would fit with this idea. It's like a relationship map, but it shows the linkages between elements of the setting and cool stuff you bring into play if you include that element. For example, if you want Racism, Gadgets and Wealth in your game, all three of those link to Dwarves, so stick one or more dwarves in and you're set for those themes.

    The other aspect to it is that it's a map. The boxes have page references, so you don't need to read the whole book in order to get at the coolness you want. "OK, my coolness is on pages 17, 28 and 32. I'll read those and any cross-references, plus the quick overview in the front; I'm now set to play. That's all the setting I need to know - we'll be making up the rest as we go in any case."

    \n Just to clarify a few things on community publishing projects, from my experience:

    1. If you are doing it for charity, sell ads up front, while you are soliciting content and before you lay it out. Get that taken care of and never worry about it again.
    2. Somebody needs to take a strong, central role to keep it alive and encourage submission. This person will also be the one to organize and format submissions, probably.
    3. You need to have pretty rigid submission formats (for your own sanity) and a clear licensing policy (so people know what's going to be done with their work and can make an educated choice about participation).
    4. You can find other kind souls to take on other tasks, like laying the beast out and actually publishing it. I'd be glad to jump through the Lulu hoops and put it up along side the Name Project, for example.

    \n The difference between genres is really one of nouns and adjectives as far as I can tell.

    "Monster Terrorizes Countryside" (Fantasy)

    "Bandits Terrorize Countryside" )Historical)

    "Aliens Terrorize Planet" (SciFi)

    "Killer Terrorizes City" (Detective)

    It's all the same plot, but feels very different depending on what content you hook in.

    This is made a *lot* easier with web-based generators. But you could pull this off in a book by having a section of plot/situation hooks that were designed to have genre-based elements plugged into them. And then separate lists of elements by genre.

    I am thinking of this book almost as a list of bangs that a GM (or players can flip through at the table or before play and say, "Whoa, this would totally fit in with John's Barbarian character! He totally wants to hunt down and kill his brother!"

    \n Speaking personally, I find a lot more inspiration from things that are concrete and interestingly detailed instead of filled with blanks or written in the abstract. It seems like anyone with half a brain can change "So there's this king, right, who rules a lonely tower in the midst of a vast wasteland..." to "So there's this vampire lord, right, who rules from a crumbling office spire in the old, now-bankrupt financial district..."

    Good situations, in my mind, are made of colorful and surprising details and complexities, not cliches or universals. I would be much more interested in a book of fantastic story seeds in all sorts of genres, ones that would spark my own imagination or make me think, "Wow, this situation would be a great premise for a 3-session arc of pirate Agon!"

    \n Late to the thread, and I'm hammered this week at work, so I won't be in here, but let me echo what Jason said about Abulafia, and provide a couple of examples of community-created situation fun that are already there.

    Basically everything on the Plot Kickers & Oracles page is related to this, but in particular I'd like to point out:

    * Fantasy Scene which has the format of "You're with (npc) at (cool-sounding location). Situation: (steal from Vincent's AG&G oracles or pose a moral question)"

    * Random Fantasy Plot, which has the format of "A (bad) (villain) (menacingverb) (good) (victim) but (hero-adjective) (hero) (mediation)."

    *Spycraft Triggering Event This is just some kind of badness that espionage-types might get called in for a pre-action briefing on, set modernly. Format: "(some time recently), (badpeople) (did something bad) (and a chance they had help from more bad people)"

    *Champion of Darkness, which gives you a fantasy villain, what they're after, and where they're going about it.

    *Spycraft Scene, which is like Fantasy Scene - "You're with (npc) (at a particular location doing something), when (something exciting happens)"

    If nothing else, these might give whoever's organizing said project some ways to think about how you want to organize the phrasing of your situations for maximum versatility and effectiveness. Anyone who has an account on Abulafia (they're free, and only necessary for my benefit to prevent wiki-spam) can look under the hood at how any of those generators works by clicking 'edit' - if you do, feel free to add a few choices. If you just want to see more results, refresh the page.

    Also, if whoever does this wants to mirror the work as a set of random generators on Abulafia, I'd be delighted to assist in that process. The Names Project is already partially implemented there, and may someday be completely done.

    edit: x-posted with John H. - I concur that 2-page spreads of awesome situations would be a great book to have, and don't want to sound like I'm pimping Abulafia for this if that's not the direction it should be headed. Actually, in D&D sourcebooks of late, they've often been including 100 ideas for adventure with X! type tables, which are a less-fleshed-out version of this idea.
