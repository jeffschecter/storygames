    \n Some of my work deals very explicitly with this. In "ScenePlay" the group selects a template to follow while playing, but in practice may diverge from it as long as the key beats are hit somewhere in the proper act. In "DayTrippers" the "vertical control" of the GM is a conscious mimicry of standard television four-act structure, and each session is designed to produce one "episode", ending with the PCs' return to Earth.

    What are the implications? Well, diehard storygamers report that they find such approaches overly rigid or GM-controlled, while diehard trad gamers find them overly vague or Player-controlled. Somewhere in the middle, people find a mix of freedom and structure in it: basically we are working within a well-known narrative model and filling it with the emergent actions of the characters at the table, under the conscious facilitation of a strong GM.

    It is a compromise, in a sense. The key concern is the idea of the "one-shot", which was a strong design motivator for me in both cases. Busy people, hard to get the same group together every time, etc. This forces the designer/GM to consider models in which climax and denouement (narrative elements) are guaranteed to occur within a single session (real-world limitation). TV episodes are a natural fit under such conditions.

    The RPG parallel would be a long campaign that goes where it will before eventually arriving at a good stopping point. I agree with Tod (AsIf) -- if you want a familiar arc in a one-shot, you can't be too hands-off about it.     \n Excellent thoughts - what a fun thread!

    I think it's always worth exploring how rules are broken and how that can lend unpredictability to storytelling.

    If you consider films like "The Usual Suspects" or "Memento", you can see examples of storytelling which break expectations not (or not just!) by breaking the conventional arc of narrative, but by messing with the audience's perception of the story in presenting it in a different way.

    Cortazar's novel "Hopscotch" is another good example: it's a novel where each chapter is numbered, but not in the right order. You have a choice when you start reading: you can open the cover and read straight through the book, or you can find the first chapter and then read #2, then #3, and so on - read them in the order they are numbered. You get a two different stories out of the same words in the same book.

    I am often bored by media which doesn't break any rules at all - whether rules of narrative expectation (what happens when) or of dramatic delivery (what we see/read and when). Those are definitely two very interesting things to think about as a designer, I think.

    Consider also, however, that very strong media, however exciting and novel, creates its own "rules" and expectations. (And you may find, like George Lucas, that you can create a new thing and have everyone tell that you didn't do it right because you didn't follow the rules...

    \nYes, but\u2026

    It sometimes seems to me that you have feint to narrative expectations in order to create the \u201cbuy-in\u201d that makes the narrative subversion meaningful to the viewers/players.

    Cortazar\u2019s Hopscotch, for instance \u2013 in my opinion, a masterpiece \u2013 is one of very few 20th century novels considered successful in its narrative experimentation even by canonical (ie trad) literary critics. One of the ways in which it does this is by offering two (or more) ways to read the novel \u2013 one linear, one a \u201cchoose your own adventure\u201d-style jumping around to create surrealistic connotation. People can be very resistant to not knowing what they\u2019re going to get in a one-shot, and yet at the same time resistant to someone else (eg a GM or another player) supplying the \u201clogic of sense\u201d to the story.

    My experience with roleplaying games has been that *more* formulaic games generally do create better stories. Generally, mind you - there are always exceptions. Simply breaking the rules of storytelling doesn't make things more engaging.

    I believe there's a "sweet spot" that's going to be a bit different for each person playing, based on their expectations and experiences. You need enough structure for familiarity and buy-in, and enough freedom and surprise for a sense of novelty and to bring 'the unexpected'.

    An 8-yr-old kid playing an RPG for the first time will be *thrilled* and possibly very surprised if a typical, predictable story arc results. A long-time D&D player looking for story development will often be blown away by a "traditional" conflict resolution-based Forge game from 2005. Rickard, however, finds that boring, because he's done it a bunch and he's looking for something else.

    I think what a lot of my favourite games (and certainly AW) do well is that they focus on creating tools for the players to enact change in the story. Whether it's change in character, change in situations, or changes in the setting, transformation is what we measure time by, and that's extremely apparent in games which give us stories we like.

    Dormer's comments may be applicable to television, where shows were once extremely formulaic, and now they are looking for ways to surprise the viewers. However, as gamers, we have our own (quite different) sets of formulas and expectations (as, it seems, @omnifray found in his or her Con scenario).

    \n It's very interesting to consider exactly what it means to imagine that audiences are "wising up". As @Paul_T mentions, an 8-year-old playing an RPG has a totally different experience of it than a grizzled veteran roleplayer does. Likewise it may be that to a 12-year-old many tired TV tropes (as adults might see them) seem fresh and exciting. Perhaps there is an age by which on average most members of society have "wised up" to most of the common TV tropes and the fun of them is spoilt for those individuals. Remember, though, that this is the audience's loss - if the audience is harder to please, the audience has less fun. The self-satisfaction some may take at being too "sophisticated" for certain forms of entertainment is more than matched by the resulting dissatisfaction that those who are "sophisticated" suffer with entertainment. At a more basic level, whilst I would not wish on anyone (and certainly not on myself) the brain injuries that would take away their memories of watching Friends, the fact of having those memories means I can derive only minimal enjoyment from watching a show that used to have me yearning for more.

    Another interesting question is, if audiences are wising up, how has this happened now, if it didn't happen before? Is it just that we consume a lot more passive entertainment now than we used to? That may no longer be the trend, as more interactive forms of Internet-based and gaming-based entertainment (sadly, I do not mean tabletop RPGs) slowly catch up and even surpass TV/cinema as a form of entertainment. I hardly think it right to imagine that we are vastly more sophisticated on the whole than our forebears. Then again, perhaps the answer could be this:- that TV has become more and more formulaic, and since viewers have seen a more and more limited number of formulae while watching more and more TV (subject now though to the effect of the Internet and interactive entertainment), viewers have become very specialised at spotting those particular formulae at work.

    Of course this is all rather speculative, without hard data.

    He said that commercial forced the TV to use a certain formula to keep their audiences.
    Absolutely. The hallmark of commercial TV is the "turning point", where the situation changes and the audience asks, "Now what?" Turning points are present in almost all narrative media of significant length, but in commercial TV you can basically cue them up before every commercial break.

    I think this kind of scripting culminated in "24". 24 has the most obvious and over-the-top sequence of "So NOW what?!" non-stop crises I've ever seen, scene after scene, episode after episode. Over the course of many seasons, the show supported this frantic threat-offering by escalating how far they'd push it. Just about every ad for the show focused on "this time there's treason!" or "this time there's torture!" or some version of "we're one-upping what we've done before!"

    I have no idea if audiences are "wising up", but if they are, it might be because 24 and its imitators pushed the standard TV model as far as it could possibly go and now everyone's burned out on it.

    Separately, I think Modern Family represents a new development in comedy arcs. At some point, the show writers realized that the compelling nature of turning points and the fun of seeing the cast react to them really is the whole deal, and very little resolution is actually required. Every episode ends with some cozy reconciliation of one crisis, but often the episode will have introduced 3 of them, and the audience is simply expected to not worry about what what happened with the other 2. So the end result feels like a familiar arc, but two thirds of what happens in the middle is just jokes which don't contribute to that arc's plot. When the 3 crises have anything to do with each other, it's often thematic rather than literal/causal/logistical, so I guess there's some "poetry" in that.

    Some episodes of Louie go even farther, letting the jokes linger without any resolution. I think that's more intermittent, though.
