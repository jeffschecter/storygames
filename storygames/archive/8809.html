    \n In the playtest, I played "Machiavelli," a cunning Italian prince (yeah, I know) with a sword as quick as his wit, and a horse who could leap over walls. Machiavelli could see into the spirit realm, talk to the spirits there and command them. When our little boat was assailed by storms on the high seas, I looked into the spirit world and saw that the ocean roiled with the ghosts of a million dead sailors.

    Seriously, this game rocks already, and will rock at 11 when Ryan actually writes the fucking rules down for us.

    Edited because none of that makes it clear that it was totally my idea to have my horse go leaping over walls, carried by spirits, or that it was my idea to see an ocean full of angry sailor ghosts when I used my spirit sight power (written on my sheet in a vague form), and that nothing in the rules gave Ryan the power to go, "Nuh-uh, fool, you don't do that." That is what makes the game rock.

    The rules give you a little bit of structure to create an almost-mythic character (if you were truly mythic, your buddies would have to END you), and then you're rolling handfuls of dice and making up the craziest, gonzo shit you can think of. To use the one really mythic power you have, you need only grab that Myth Die and roll it with the rest. Of course, doing that puts you at all sorts of "risk" (Oh no! said Br'er Rabbit). Sometimes, nothing bad happens. Sometimes, you climb the Myth scale (whatever it's called) and that makes you more awesome but at the same time brings you one step closer to becoming a myth yourself (and then your buddies have to END you).

    Can I save the world from monsters without becoming a monster myself?

    One thing I'd like to see is something in the rules that leads high-scale mythic characters towards acts of hubris and abuse. Otherwise, mythic characters are just really powerful good guys and it's hard to see why mythenders should be ending those myths. Perhaps the GM should be empowered to show how the very presence and use of mythic power corrupts the world in some terrible way.

    \n Actually, it's the opposite: Mythenders are looking to end the world of myth, so to become a Myth is actually worse (and yet, more awesome) than death. The things they desire and the changes they want to have happen become corrupted if they fade into myth. Their grand accomplishments are undone or inverted, and they give new life to the dying world. But, changing aspects of the setting is a big part of the ongoing play of the game rather than an endcap to the story. In my home game, the Mythenders are doing this because they see the mortal world as flawed and want to:
    * remove the stains of lust from the hearts of men
    * remove the idea of treachery
    * make all people follow Islam devoutly

    As the GM, I want to see these guys actually achieve this and continue playing in the world, to react to what they've done.

    On the flip side, Jason (khelek) became a Myth at the end of the final battle of the second Dreamation playtest, and while I can't remember the specifics, I remember feeling like he totally understood the premise when he talked about how the other PCs would later hear of a ghostly being (him) terrorizing the children.

    If it makes any sense, Don't Rest Your Head is a spiritual grandfather of Mythender.

    Does that make any sense?

    \n Regarding how our end game went down:
    \n
    \nI played in the second session, but also as the Italian Prince (Adam Dray played him in an earlier session). Each character did want different things out of killing myths. Some were pretty aligned, but you could see how in longer term play (or at least in a non-con atmosphere) the various pcs would want to address different things, and may not want to kill each myth.
    \n
    \nRyan gave us sorcerers killing orphans... it is hard to turn that around and defend the sorcerers. though now that I type that it does seem possible. Our pcs mostly worked together. with myself talking some smack and calling O'Sullian a kill stealer.
    \n
    \nQ: Is there any benefit/danger for being the hand that ends the myth?
    \nQ: Are there nay rules for how to resolve conflicts between the PCs? or just bashing heads? (this never came up).
    \n
    \nAt the end of the conflict against the great Maw, a fragment of Loki, which was devousing orphans three characters were at the precipice of becoming myths. two players made their roll and left the cave, i failed and became a myth. Ryan had done a good job of letting us know that becoming myth is a bad thing.
    \n
    \nThought: Maybe by becoming myth the player should rewrite the character's belief to be at odds with what they wanted before.
    \n
    \nThe spirit talking/commanding prince took all of the orphans' souls and used them to terrorize the village and surrounding country as my spirit army of dead children. I saw this as his betrayal (the character believed that the mythic world gave man the ability to be treacherous, and wanted to end all treachery, not violence, just betrayal), which fulfilled my vision of becoming "evil."
    \n
    \nI definitely felt the connections to Agon and a whiff of the Nobilis mythic level of events.
