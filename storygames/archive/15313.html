\nD&D4E, buffed with the Hard Boiled Armies rules. Our fortieth session! We have two paragons and a third on the cusp.

Following their most recent triumph\u2014this time restoring the Empire's weather and preventing a horrendous famine\u2014the Crimson Blades have begun the long odyssey back to their home city of Greyhawk. WIth them: a motley army of hobbits, ski archers, minotaurs, kenku, and mageblades, all eager for adventure and treasure.

At the dwarven gate-fortress of Hamarkand, the Blades demand unhindered passage through the Ossen Mutark thoroughfare that pierces the barrier peaks. But they will offer nothing in tribute or service to the paranoid Fang Thane, and the dwarven commander orders them driven off.

Bolts fly from the dwarven garrison! Casualties are high on both sides.

It becomes a real tabletop freakout when a trio of enormous aboleths burst from the stony ground, seeking not to attack the combatants but instead to steal the fallen as "beautiful fuel for the dreaming pits." The Blades do not hesitate to descend into the slimy aboleth tunnels and fight viciously to recover their dead.

(Hard Boiled Armies is a seriously cool product. Ever since I read Knights of the Dinner Table, I have wanted to do epic KODT-style wars as part of my tabletop games, and HBA does exactly that.)

    \n Steppenes Desennier (Centuries of the Steppes): This is a game of the rise and fall of civilizations, tribes and other phenomena - on a huge steppe.

    The boat people, having sent flaming rocks to crush the comb tribe, have for generations labored under the curses of their ancestral Tree of Life. The last of them go upriver to seek a new life. They find a quiet lake - and face extinction, years later.

    The dragon, now centuries old, sends her children out to explore. They find the graves of the boat people, and dig them up to play with the bones. And the shiny things.

    The knife king has led his people into the subterranean houses of the long-gone singing elfs. There, they become increasingly paranoid and incestuous.

    \n Ran Lady Blackbird four moderate-to-intermediate players and one total RPG n00b. Went very well, although we didn't get very far in the story - they basically escaped the Hand of Sorrow (with many distractions and some daring stunts) and dove into the depths, headed for Haven, when they heard an ominous blip on the sonar... At one point it became apparent that she had been playing the game competitively "against" the other players, which was really interesting, as I hadn't thought to point out that RPGs are generally cooperative.

    \n Microscope at Story Games Seattle. A secret society is the invisible hand that steers civilization, from medieval monks all the way to stellar empires.

    Raven & Pendulum

    Possibly my favorite scene was when the secret society had rigged the trial of a 16 year old girl who'd been in a crippling car accident and had her consciousness transplanted into an experimental cyber-brain. Both lawyers, the judge -- everybody involved -- were secretly taking orders from the society to decide the outcome and make sure that cyber-brained individuals were not granted basic human rights. We had a bitter discussion in chambers about whether we should be frickin' ashamed of ourselves for using this poor girl as a pawn.

    Sanctimonious Lawyer: "It's a tragedy about the girl, but this technology will be critical one day for making sure the ideals of our Order survive."

    Pissed-off Judge: "We have a method for that. We've had it for thousands of years. It's called raising children."

    Oh, and the girl was suing for the right to die. She couldn't stand her new monstrous existence. And we wouldn't let her.

    \n Mouse Guard - Summer 1153

    Copperwood bigwig Chesterton, an adventurous and wealthy mouse has recently been building a "hunting lodge" in frontier-town Wolfpointe. Since then, there have been increased disappearances in the crops and foodstuffs of the town that are being blamed on the local Wild Mice. The patrol investigates and learns that members of the Blue Feather have been disappearing, seemingly murdered. The tribe's best trackers know that the culprit is somewhere in Wolfpointe and have been taking their retribution in the form of crops and food, as is their way.

    Evidence gathered with the Blue Feather points to Chesterton and through a two-pronged approach of breaking-and-entering and guile, the patrol discovers Chesterton's grizzly secret trophy room, which features several Wild Mouse skins. Everyone is outraged, including the Wolfpointe village leader, who would deal frontier justice immediately. The judicious patrol leader Kynes knows that executing the politically powerful Chesterton would have vast consequences. He considers delivering the magnate to the Wild Mice but thinks this will be a death blow for the tribes if they decide to execute him.

    In the end, the patrol leader sends the rest of the patrol to the Wild Mice with peace offerings pilfered from Chesterton's vast stores. With the patrol away (and thus, absolved) Kynes hands Chesterton over to the Wolfpointe leader. Chesterton's head on the block and the whole village out to witness his execution, Kynes asks if he has any last words.

    "None that are worthy for you lot." Chesterton sneers and then spits on the dusty earth, his head hitting the ground shortly afterwards.

    \n I've been playing a lot of Microscope-on-demand at Story Games Seattle recently (oh no, woe is me!). Last night's game had a particularly good history concept: Earth picks up an alien radio signal from deep space and the mere knowledge that "they are out there" changes civilization.

    The Signal (Microscope)

    No, the aliens never show up. No, the message is never understood. We banned those things on the Palette at the outset. The history is all about how people react to the knowledge:\n
    Cue scientific debate, messianic fervor, and a collapse of human progress as we know it. Why bother solving the world's problems when you can sit around and spend decades trying to unlock the secrets of the alien message\u2026 because THEY must have the answer.

    \n Experimenting with a game where a bunch of typical RPG/story questions are written out on cards, playtesting it by myself as a trial run, came up with this, thought it was pretty cool:

    He hears the ticking of the cuckoo clock in the hall [the very first card was "What do I hear?" and it drove the rest of the storylet] - he\u2019s in an isolated part of the house, otherwise he would hear more ticking, for he is a clockmaker, and his house is full of his clocks.
    \u201cI cannot stand the ticking - once I notice it, I can\u2019t ignore it,\u201d the boy says.
    \u201cI love clocks more than people,\u201d he says.
    \u201cI know,\u201d the boy says, \u201cthat\u2019s why you made me.\u201d
    The boy is a clockwork boy.
    The clockmaker prepares to leave; the boy asks, \u201cWhere are you going?\u201d
    \u201cI don\u2019t know. I have to get away. Start over. The loneliness.\u201d

    Outside, a girl in a white dress watches the house. She shivers in the cold. She sees the mechanical boy look out of the one lit window. She sees the old man leave, and he is going around the house \u2026 with wires \u2026 and dynamite. He has a detonator. He means to blow up the house? Get a fresh start that way?
    She steps out. He notices her. Fate! He\u2019s decided to end his loneliness and here, before him, a beautiful woman.
    \u201cWhat are you doing?\u201d she asks.
    \u201cI\u2019m burning my bridges,\u201d he says.
    \u201cHow can I get you to stop? Don\u2019t do this.\u201d
    \u201cIf I knew \u2026 there was someone for me \u2026 I wouldn\u2019t have to.\u201d And he kisses her.
    And she lets him.

    Inside, the boy does his rounds, winding up the clocks. When he gets to the special machine that winds him up, he discovers it has been smashed and broken. He feels himself winding down...

    The girl is allowing the clockmaker to have his way with her. As he comes, she feels for a rock, lifts it, smashes him in the head. And she is up, running, running to the house.
    Walking out of the house, the boy. Holding a stick of dynamite in his clockwork hand.
    She runs to him, throws herself around him, kisses him. She is crying.
    The wetness of the kiss, her tears, shocks him. Electricity arcs. \u201cI told you to be careful \u2026 kissing \u2026 me,\u201d he says, as he shuts down.
    She winds him back up, as the clockmaker approaches, holding the detonator.
    They stare at him.
    He tenses over the plunger.
    But no, he cannot do it.
    \u201cWhat\u2019s your name?\u201d he asks the girl.
    \u201cSkyla,\u201d she says. \u201cI work at the shop. That\u2019s where we met.\u201d She looks at the clockwork boy.
    He nods. \u201cI was looking for a lover. But maybe. Maybe I can be content with a daughter-in-law.\u201d

    \n Played a pilot session of PTA, the series name is The Company. The game is a post war sci-fi setting where super powered humans and normal humans clashed (actually there were supers on both sides.) The war ended when the human resorted to orbital bombardment and broke the supers will to fight. In the post war era, most supers gravitate towards the frontier where the shortage in manpower makes them more effective. Our heroes (supers on the losing side of the war) work aboard an eccentric ex-war transport with a goofy AI doing odd jobs for The Company which is the dirty jobs division of The Corporation.

    All three of the players were new to PTA, but since we all played The Trouble with Rose several times, it didn't take us long to get up to speed on the differences between the two games. The fan mail system took a while to warm up to since we didn't have a baseline of what was awesome or not. The net result was that 4 and half hours passed by in a flash. Good times, looking forward to the other sessions in the series (the Producer has played one-shots before but want to see how a weekly series handled.)

    \n My role-playing has always really sporadic these past few years and after weeks and weeks of trying to get a real campaign off the ground, I gave up and instituted "alternative one-shot rpg afternoons." This last Sunday, we got together for our first session of our first game and played In a Wicked Age.
    \n
    \nA soul of a dead wizard kept battling against a malevolent genie (whom he woke up, along with his very, very evil brothers) for control of a pretty evil child. The child, corrupted by the horrible energies around it deceived everyone around himself and tried constantly to sacrifice his guardian. In the end, our scholar/adopted mother of the child, fell to the corruption of one of the genies, the child used his evil powers to goad townsfolk into hunting his guardian, the soul of the wizard defeated his soulless body, escaped the genie brothers and finally possessed the child's body at the exact moment that his guardian put an arrow through the kid's throat. Our next story is set during the rehearsal of a funeral of an old mayor, somewhere nearby.
    \n
    \n(I really need to gm more. I was SO bad at setting scenes, pushing forward and actually tying things together. Despite that everyone had a seriously good time!)

    \n Second session of our WW2 South Pacific hack of "The Regiment" (itself a AW hack). This adventure was called "The Milk Run".

    The crew of the PT-89 barely managed to rescue a downed US Navy pilot who had drifted way too close to a small island heavily-defended by the Japanese. His squadron mates were circling overhead but finally had to turn tail due to low fuel and an incoming storm.

    Damage sustained: two dead crewmen (including the Executive Officer), radio aerial sheared-off, one of their two engines out of commission, two damaged torpedo tubes, destroyed twin .50 machine guns and all their 20mm ammo expended. Oh, and another Navy plane crashed 15 feet away from their boat and almost set it on fire. And there were sharks in the water.

    War is heck.
