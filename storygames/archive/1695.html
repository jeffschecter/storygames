Gender and assumptions about a game's agenda

viktor_haag
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
(This discussion is specifically about gender and it's intertwining with games qua games; if this has been hashed already in an older thread, please just post a link to same and tell me to move along... 8))

I recently read this thread on 20x20 room that started scratching the surface about gender assumptions and agenda in "The Prince's Kingdom". Judd had (at time of writing) the last word in the thread, and had this (amongst other points) to say:

But he wrote a game about growing up a boy and turning into a man. It is what he knew.
Now, I get that TPK narratively "assumes" that all the player roles are "little princes" and that they are strategically directed in their efforts by their "father" who is the "king".

However, I don't at all get that TPK mechanically makes these assumptions at all.

It doesn't seem to me out of the way to make all the player roles "little princesses" (even the sons of the parent are called "princesses"). Neither does it seem out of the way for the parent to be a Queen instead of a King. Or to have the players decide what their title will be, and what the gender of their parent is.

I don't really see that the genders involved have a fundamental effect on the narrative mission of the players, or the mechanical effect of playing out that mission:

What do the player characters do?: Travel their parent's realm to solve problems and learn how to be a ruler themselves.

What do the players do?: Explore themes of conflict resolution and leadership by having their characters placed into situations where their authority and responsibilities may far outstrip their current resources.

Gender may, in fact, play a role in this game -- how do players conceptions of gender get drawn out in their portrayals? What do the players think consitutes conflict and resolution and leadership as informed by gender?

But what I don't see is how "the game is about growing up a boy and turning into a man", necessarily. It could be about that, but by the same token I see no reason why the game (pretty much as written, mechanically) couldn't be about "growing up a girl and turning into a woman", or "growing up a child and turning into a monarch (or, more generically, leader)".

Specifically -- anyone have thoughts on how gender intertwines with the game present in "The Princes' Kingdom"? How does this interaction of gender in TPK differ from Dogs In The Vineyard, its parent game? (From my point of view, DitV brings issues of gender much more to the fore, given that gender roles in the game's default background seem to have much more overt in-game effects with respect to character expecations and what characters "should and should not be doing".)

Generally -- anyone have thoughts about how gender intertwines with the game in a variety of other rolegames? (If TPK is not, or couldn't be, a game about "growing up a girl and turning into a woman", in your opinion, what game is or could be?)

    Caveat: this topic wavers on the very fine line between identity and essentialism, and it's really, really difficult territory to negotiate, so bear with.

    One thing that Dogs and by extension TPK is actually really bad at is building consensus, which is purportedly one of the primary tools in the feminine conflict resolution toolkit. What Dogs dice do allow you to do is to get your own way, which is a very male sort of parallel tool to building consensus. If you wanted a feminine version of TPK supporting consensus-building, you'd have your princesses go out to the various islands, get to know the denizens, and construct a solution that everyone can feel a part of -- and have the mechanics support that. So each island would be a sort of people-collection exercise, with players using the system to put the denizens on their character sheet, making overtures to get them to buy in, and (very importantly) modifying the players' proposed solution so that as many people as possible could buy into it (and buy into it willingly, not coerced by means of authority).

    IMHO you're confused because the game isn't really about that. There's no value of growth the game promotes that I, for example, wouldn't want for my daughter. I'd be surprised if there are parents of girls who would say differently.

    While TPK is derived from DITV, there's no truly useful sense in which "Both Princes' Kingdom and Dogs in the Vineyard do _X_ with gender." DITV provides a setting that consciously, productively shoves gender issues embedded in a culture in the play group's face and says, Go on, deal with it. TPK simply fails to have a text that is welcoming to girls who might read it. There's nothing about the game or the setting that girls would enjoy less than boys, summing over all girls and all boys. Girls might like it better, actually, but that's the sort of thing that only playtesting the game with lots of actual children of both genders would answer definitively.

    The action of TPK is making communities work again when they've stopped working.
