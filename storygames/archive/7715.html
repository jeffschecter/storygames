\n Dear fellow story gamers,

I am having insane troubles getting a stable gaming group together. People will generally respond positively to my game pitches, but then when it comes to finding an evening, taking part in online character generation etc. I get a lot of radio silence. I live in a quite small town which is part of the problem. I suspect it would be quite easy for me to find a 4e group for instance, but that's not what I'd like to play right now.
So, time and time again I find myself thinking about playing with \u201dnon-gamers\u201d. And every time, my plans stop as soon as I start to think about the particulars: which game, how to sell the idea etc.

So, I'd really appreciate if some of you could tell me if you'vr had similar issues. And how you solved them.

In particular, I'd like to find out how much to tell people about the games beforehand: I don't want to bog people down with all sorts of details, but on the other hand, I want to be upfront and honest and thereby, hopefully, get the right kind of people. I'm thinking that describing the atmosphere and the kind of stories these games offer is better than detailing all sorts of rules \u2013 but, otoh, I don't want to end up with players who'll balk at even simple rules.

Also \u2013 what games are great for this purpose?

Thanks in advance, people!

Peter

    \n Maybe you are over-thinking it, Peter. Roleplaying is an activity like anything else - would you invite people over to play a board game? To watch a movie? How would you pitch that?

    "I'd like to have you over to play a board game this Saturday. It'll be fun!"

    I'd make it a one-time deal with no expectations.

    In my experience, people completely new to roleplaying are excellent players. You don't really need to explain how things work too much, because they will understand stuff like author stance and scene framing pretty naturally, and game-specific behaviors just require a little modeling - you go first and they will catch on. I'd offer to run something you love and not worry about complexity - most people's first game is D&D, and that seems to work out all right.

    "I have this game where you make up a TV show and play out episodes, and I'd like to have you over to play this Saturday. It'll be fun!"

    \n I had some really good luck pitching 1001 Nights to a group of co-workers (doctors and technicians at a veterinary clinic). They all like food, so we turned it into a dinner party using the suggestions in the book for things to cook or bring. If you choose to go this route, I'd be happy to send you the recipes I used. I think turning it into a dinner party really greased the wheels and I can't recommend that approach enough. I'll also say that these folks were primed by a recent foray into one of those How To Host A Murder Mystery deals.

    In the past, I've had pretty good experiences with just doing what Jason suggested and inviting people over to try and play. The thing to keep in mind is that you can't really prime someone to be an excellent roleplayer or whatever. It's like anything else, you offer them the opportunity and if they like it, they'll develop into someone you (hopefully) enjoy playing with. If they don't like it, or if they don't enjoy playing the same things/way as you do, that's okay too. Trying to front-load the situation too much won't help.

    Regards,
    Daniel

    \n Ditto to what all these folks are saying.

    For a starting game, I'd pick something based on two criteria:

    1) a game that you know and love, and
    2) a genre that will appeal to your fellow players

    For example, I'm playing Spione with a couple of friends, one of whom was attracted to the game because of the political aspects of the genre. That kind of thing works for him.

    \n I think a lot depends on their interests.
    \n
    \nIf you're inviting over people that are really interested in board games, wow, or any number of other crunchy things, don't be afraid to try a more rules heavy game. On the other hand, if your players are people that don't sit still well, or don't like number, stick with something lighter (Buffy comes to mind).
    \n
    \nI'd also watch the time. Running a 5+ hour marathon game might work for some people, but most, in my experience, like something thats no more than 3-4 hours for their first time trying the game. You can get more time in if you take a meal break though.
    \n
    \nJust my thoughts. Good luck!

    \n try to make it something that can be played to at least a relatively satisfactory conclusion in one session, and a session of probably not more than 3 hours.
    i haven't had as much luck as i'd like getting my non-gamer friends to play rpgs with me, but one thing that i think is probably a stumbling block for a lot of people would be feeling like you're asking them to commit to doing something every week for 4 hours (or whatever schedule you usually like). people are busy. make it a one time thing, and that way if they really enjoy it, they'll probably ask to do it again (and then you can see if they'd want to make it a regular thing with an ongoing game) and if they don't like it, they've only lost a couple hours spent with people they'd probably hang out with anyway.
