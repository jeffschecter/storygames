\n So, I squinted and it struck me that Amber Diceless and Dungeon World have complimentary approaches to resolution.

In Dungeon World, if you attack a really weak opponent (e.g. a kobold youngling), the GM takes this into account when deciding how the narrative evolves. You might be defying danger to avoid getting your boots dirty, for example. When you attack a Giant Mantis Shrimp, the tables are turned completely - you might be defying danger to avoid having your hand shattered along with your sword.

The GM has an opinion about the relative toughness/capability of the opposition, and this opinion affects how the narrative evolves long before the mechanics are engaged. The mechanics are used to explore the margin - so you're totally outmatched, fine, but what exactly happens?

In Amber Diceless, these values are quantified. The Kobold might have Warfare: 1, the PC Warfare: 10, and the Giant Mantis Shrimp has warfare 20. This drives how the narrative unfolds - the shrimp has a massive advantage, and is going to win unless the player thinks of some clever way to reframe the conflict, or makes do with a lesser goal. The range of outcomes, constrained by the karma, is explored with free-form role-play.
