    Josh,

    Because fantasy lets us call upon our romantic illusions, the golden age mythology of every culture in which the residual tags of a fading age are held up as cultural artificats, the innate streak of militantism, neo-luditism, and other grungy things that lurk in the foul rag and bone shop of the heart and pretend -- for a short time -- like we believe in any of them. (Or reiterate to ourselves that we do, in fact, believe in at least some of them. The power of repitition and ritual in storytelling is something a lot of texts on RPGing miss.)

    Also, because a lot of us grew up on this stuff and the conventions of the various genres around fantasy (of which, I'll note, we're already starting to run into problems of when different people say the same word and mean different things) are part of our storytelling heritage. When you grew up reading the Lord of the Rings and the King of Elflands Daughter alongside Beowulf and The Charnel God, it becomes part of your cultural heritage. At that point either confronting it or celebrating it makes a potent tool for telling stories.

    Finally, let us not forget that the kind of fantasy of which you speak is really at this point and in this context not so much Tolkien as it is D&D. As for that, D&D has become a genre of its own. Many RPers these days were not even born when the first Greyhawk setting books came out -- so for longer than they've been alive "game" and "fantasy" and "adventure" have gone together as one word in the mass cultural vocabulary.

    Edit: Also, unlike snobs like you and I not everyone is all like "postmodernism is cool! lets deconstruct the centrality of common narrative and write books like Slaughterhouse 5."

    Okay, I'm back. Elves are one of the things that I like just fine in Tolkien, but which I strongly dislike in nth generation fantasy roleplaying derivatives, even those I otherwise like such as Harn, Runequest, etc. So for me to talk about them in that context is a bit like speculating on the attraction of someone else's religion: it's bound to be patronizing, and it may be flat-out wrong.

    The thing about Elves in D&D is that at least originally, D&D was a pastiche of various fantasy influences. In my opinion, its use of Elves is both a parody and a tribute to Tolkien. I think later games, including possibly later versions of D&D, picked up on many D&D elements but lost the satiric relationship to the original source material. (I see a strong parallel in the development of Star Wars--what started as a loving parody became canonized and smothered by reverence.) Whatever Elves were to Tolkien, I think that present-day FRPG fans are attracted to them through a combination of:

    Romanticized Celtophilia (which might have to be scrutinized in its own right)
    Angsty reflection on a lost Golden Age
    Flight from the mundane and the profane, the commercial and the industrial in favor of high-minded devotion to Art and mystical harmony with Nature (There's a bit of the Noble Savage in Elves, except that they deny the irony of the Noble Savage-Sinful Civilization dichotomy. Elves are a vestige of a Noble Civilization.)
    Possibly, in some cases, a bit of Northern European racism--it's hard to ignore the way that fairhaired, light-skinned, rural/sylvan Elves are being crowded out by rapidly-breeding, crude humans who crowd burgeoning cities--like early 20th-century immigrants from southern and eastern Europe.
    Possibly, in some cases, a bit of orientalist fetish. It's hard to ignore the slight build and almond-shaped eyes.

    One of the few non-tongue-in-cheek, non-Tolkien-derivative depictions of Elves I've seen in gaming that I've enjoyed is that which appears in Albion: Land of Faerie, an obscure wargame produced by SPI back in the early 80's. While there are parallels with Tolkien in that game, the influences are more directly from Irish & Welsh mythology, and medieval romance filtered through English Renaissance poetry/drama. (E.g., the king of Elves is Auberon.) Albion does recycle the usual tropes of a noble Golden Age civilization which is destroyed through a combination of hubris, external evil, and loss of vigor compared to the "new race" of men. But at least it picks a different moment to show: the crisis of the civilization, when Elves (called Sidhe in the game) are still dominant but threatened--not the time long after when the Elves sing whiny lays about their lost glory.

    Tolkien did much the same thing with the Silmarillion, although there the fall of Elvish civilization has a clearer historical parallel (conscious or not) with World War I, a self-inflicted tragedy which basically destroyed the old European social order, put the fortunes of Europe into the hands of "younger, more vigorous, but less cultured" America & Russia, and was in many ways a symbolic culmination of the destructive effects of industrialization on humanity (machine gun, tank, and submarine replacing the chivalrous sword and horse and the picturesque and romantic sailing warship).

    I may have missed this but...

    Sometimes there is no deep answer to the question "why this?" in any medium. It's a combination of (often) hard work by a lot of people and the whim of the world when that work is presented. Umberto Eco's essay on the various uses of the Middle Ages makes the point: they serve lots of different purposes. They're popular now because works of medievaloid fantasy happened to capture imaginations in the past. It could have been something else, but either nobody did it at the right time or something got in the way of the audience appreciating it.

    It's not an entirely random process. There are qualities that a successful milieu is likely to have, in some mix, and some neat ones just don't get there. But there are always going to be ones that could have caught on, or did catch on for a while but haven't stayed with us. (Look at a lot of the pulp genres. Or, for a more interesting case in some ways, the nurse novel, huge for a century and now collapsed almost to nothing, and it collapsed pretty quickly, as changes in law and practice widened the real-world options for working women.)

    So there may sometimes not be a point in drilling too deeply - sometimes it's there because it's there.

    Josh,

    I am telling you why I like fantasy, and why I don't like it. Apparently, because I took something of a hands-off tone you mistook my points for the general.

    I like it and don't like it because of the entire idea of the current vs. the residual and the romanticization of the previous age. It's pretty clearly expressed in "Wind and the Lion" when el-Raisuli gives the talk about how guns are random and ugly weapons, and that real men fight each other with swords so that they can see the eyes of their enemy. (Mirroed in Star Wars with the "a more elegant weapon for a more elegant age" line.)

    I mean, on one hand this is obvious romantic crap. On the other hand, despite that it works well in fiction because it causes a resonance (or dissonance) of ideas. Which hits me harder, the duel of jet pilots at Mach 5 with laser guided weapons or that of sword on sword in a circle of dirt? The latter. Why? Because the latter has a lot of history, a lot of warrior culture nonesense built up around it in literature and song and story that is only now starting to build up around the former.*

    So when I'm looking to confront (or celebrate) issues of violence and the male identity, or anything similar and less pretentious sounding, I'm going to be more drawn to the sword on sword angle. It's deeper wedged into my psyche, for whatever combination of reasons, and so lingers there.

    There is also, I think, probably the still lingering issue (after how many hundreds of years?) of reason vs faith, of the subjective vs objective universe. I think many of us are pretty much of the rational and humanisitic leaning, and despite having an identifiable religion with obvious mystical overtones, I am one such. I see the world as ordered and understandable, I believe in law and civilization and not hitting the idiot who uses their cell phone in the theatre durring Lord of the Rings the Musical. And yet there is this little hobgoblin of my mind, this cultural trap door, that still likes the idea of the universe rotating about the earth (or about myself), that likes the ideas of honor and vengeance and spirit morality being more important than ethical calculus or planned communities. It's a clash of things inside our culture, and inside myself.

    I could also walk through similar issues around individualism vs collectivism, neo-tribalism vs nationalism, and on and on and on. However, at this point I hope I've belabored the point from my earlier post enough to make it clear enough to be seen.

    Fantasy, in the traditional mold you're specifying, points at all of those areas. Those residual, fading or resurficing areas of the Brand-cultural pysche where the idea of "barbarism's innate savage honesty" clash with my aware-mind certainty that barbarism is a bunk concept, and that savagry (what folks like Howard were really talking about) is nothing to be admired and is, in fact, the force that always loses. I'm saying that there is a lot about fantasy that is unexamined, things that contain all the tropes of our civilization and culture as it goes through change that sit there festering or fermenting -- depending on how you feel about each individual issue.

    When I can walk up to issues like that and slap them in the face, I like fantasy. When I can push and pull and use swords and non-rationalism and both escape into and escape from a world that is both what I want and what I fear, I love it.

    Now, I think its worth noting that very little fantasy gives this to me anymore. Or, it simply embraces all the atavistic things that I want to confront with an uncritical acceptance that speaks of the uglier forms of essentialism. Which is why I dislike fantasy and don't do nearly as much of it as I used to.

    Which hits me harder, the duel of jet pilots at Mach 5 with laser guided weapons or that of sword on sword in a circle of dirt? The latter. Why? Because the latter has a lot of history, a lot of warrior culture nonesense built up around it in literature and song and story that is only now starting to build up around the former.

    There are two angles to this, Brand. On one hand, it can't be denied that the choice of traditional fantasy is a matter of familiarity with the idiom. On the other hand, there really is a difference in the suitability of the two concepts for the desired theme. Which is also reflected in how the theme warps depictions. Sword fighting always involves the direct clash of individuals who can see each other. Aerial combat by contrast has tended to be conducted at greater and greater ranges, often being decided by ambush and, in the modern era, without ever visually sighting the enemy. Yet fictional depictions of air combat still tend to emphasize the close-in dogfight.

    This is another way that Star Wars is fantasy, and I think that bears on part of Josh's question. Traditional fantasy has selected those elements of culture that are particularly suited to emphasizing certain themes, also warping them to heighten those themes. Sometimes in real-time: there were attempts to ban the crossbow because it upset the superiority of the knight.

    At the same time, though, I think we're still better off talking about individual fantasy elements or particular constellations of elements rather than fantasy as a whole. E.g., the pseudo-medieval background with kings, castles, and peasants is a common feature of the fantasy genre, but is generally played down in swords & sorcery (such as Leiber) even though the latter does share with fantasy the idea of individual heroism.
