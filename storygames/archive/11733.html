    \n There are a few things I loved about Cyberpunk 2020.

    1. The Lifepath Generator. I never played Traveller, so I don;t know how it compares to the liufepath system n that game, but I've modded the C2020 lifepath system to plenty of other systems and settings over the years. It was (and still is) a great way to add story hooks into characters that are otherwise fairly one dimensional, and it provides some funs twists into characters where the player is willing to let things fall into the hands of fate.

    2. Cyberpsychosis. A great precursor to the "humanity" system found in so many other games. This is the kind of thing that I found a missing factor in later games like RIFTS; full conversion cybernetics, drugs that pushed a body to superhuman levels, genetic exploitation, magical sacrifice to demonic/supernatural beings....none of which had psychological effects on the character except in a few notable character class exceptions (ie. The "crazy"). Cyberpunk actually made you think about the psychology.

    3. A damage system that wasn't really a hit point system. Everyone basically had the same number of hit points, in the same way that everyone's body is basically made up of the same stuff. Differences in damage resilience came through damage reduction armour and innate endurance/ability to withstand the effects of the incoming damage. It's not really that revolutionary now, but I'd never seen anything like it when I first read the book.

    I also thought it was better than Shadowrun because it didn't rely on non-human characters or magic to get it's style across, everything was scientifically plausible for the future (or at least we thought it was at the time).

    \n All right little Billy let's all gather around and listen to crazy ole uncle JDCorley talk about Cyberpunk 2020 and cyberpunk gaming in general.

    I am a huge cyberpunk fan. I feel like one of the biggest losses in American literature is that "The sky was the color of a television tuned to a dead channel" now makes people say "what, the sky was blue?"

    A lot of the stuff in cyberpunk, as with any science fiction, looking back, made bad predictions. The rapid development of the cellphone and the AOL-ization of the Internet into something essentially harmless and neutered makes cyberpunk's anxieties seem outdated. We happily threw our privacy in the garbage for ten percent off on our supermarket discount cards. Japan didn't come over here and acid rain actually turned out to be something people cared enough about to work on. Cyberpunk's anxieties turned out to be partly groundless and partly trivially true. Nevertheless I still love it, for many reasons.

    First of all, I loved what I perceived as its roots in noir. Cyberpunk heroes were outsiders and losers. The system was rigged against them, the deck was stacked, the world was corrupt and innocence and goodness was rare at best and often illusory. I could draw a straight line from Raymond Chandler through to William Gibson. By the by, I was and am a huge Gibson fan.

    Because I'd grown up voraciously reading science fiction, the exact trappings of cyberpunk, or its exact borders, I knew it was futile to ever chase that kind of thing down. Besides, Gibson's short story collection Burning Chrome had so many different varieties of story that I was never as hung up on "oh this novel doesn't have some kind of Net in it, it can't be cyberpunk" as some people undoubtedly were. The cyberpunk "scene" passed me by. I knew it "only" as literature (as if that wasn't enough.)

    The first semi-cyberpunkish roleplaying thing I was aware of was Shadowrun. I have to say I never got it. If there was one thing that didn't seem to fit with the noir, corrupt world of cyberpunk it was elves-and-trolls-and-spellcasting fantasy. I just flat never had any interest in it. I liked the human detective archetype, who even eschewed cyberware and replaced it more or less with a fedora and a typewriter font narration balloon above his head. People I played Shadowrun with even got a little annoyed at me because of how little I would engage with most of the game elements. When it's all about spells and big guns, who wants some asshole to sit in the game saying "It was all because of the girl, wasn't it? You thought you had her under wraps but she wanted a slice of the cake." Bah, nobody.

    Anyway, so Cyberpunk 2020. I first really got involved in the 2020 edition, not in any earlier ones. The main thing that struck me about it as being interesting was the wide variety of roles and the equally wide variety of game situations the roles envisioned. That seemed to me to be a lot more interesting than being a shadowrunner and a shadowrunner only. The GM's advice even suggested things like Trauma Teams and bands and journalists as central elements to organize a campaign around. In fact, one of my first campaigns was about two shadowrunners who were also in a band. Keeping track of their girlfriends became a big part of the campaign.

    More soon, about the challenges of bringing what I loved about cyberpunk literature to the gaming table and why I never felt like I quite nailed the target.

      \n Now let me be clear. I liked the missions of Cyberpunk 2020. I got it. The big boys have a problem, you're a loser with guns and a need for money, they will pay you to figure out and handle the situation and that way they have deniability, because if anyone comes after you, you'll be crushed so fast that nobody will ever figure out who put that money in your pocket. Sweet. And I did several games like that.

      I also did games where you were still a loser with guns and a need for money but you had a plan of your own - either you were a medic on a Trauma Team or you were a crummy no-rent entrepreneur trying to become one of the big boys, or a nomad family, or a journalist, or whatever. This helped out a lot because the characters had goals of their own and were self starting. The plots became more personal, it really mattered if someone stepped on you because it stopped you from getting what you wanted, not the big boys that had hired you.

      But still, I didn't feel like I was really capturing the cyberpunk sf or noir feel. For one thing, none of those were power fantasies, none of them were about characters reaching their goals, the core cyberpunk stories, I felt, were tragedies, often romantic tragedies, but tragedies nonetheless. I already knew I hated when books and comics introduced a character, made you care about them just to kill them off, but I LOVED Shakespearean and classical tragedies in which the natural arc of the character landed them inevitably in a horrendous place. That was the same kind of tragic arc that you would see in noir or cyberpunk, and what distinguished the protagonists from the other characters was that the protagonists knew it would end horrible.

      Now, I wasn't the only one that saw this. CP2020 was the one that went to Walter John Williams and got him to help write a HardWired supplement and to George Alec Effinger and got him to help write a When Gravity Fails supplements. They did what very few genre adaptations have done since then - try to be literary rather than cinematic, and do so by going to the original authors. But even in those supplements, the authors were more concerned with bringing what they had created to the roleplaying experience rather than bringing the experience closer to what they created. (Williams' HardWired was actually a bit more technothriller-y in structure than cyberpunk-y, so it wasn't a long trip.) Particularly the When Gravity Fails supplement was disappointing to me, not because what was presented was bad - the Budayeen is so wonderful I could run 101 Nights worth of games there, hint hint, but because what was in the novel was so exactly cyberpunk, so exactly noir that I was disappointed not to see more of it there, especially in the GM advice section. Audran literally becomes the killer he's chasing (through cybernetic personality implantation) in order to survive, and it destroys him. That's awesome - where is that story in CP2020?

      I also used CP2020 for comedy - as you can see, it's ripe for satire even within the source material, they are making funny commentary on our consumer culture. This was greatly enhanced in Cybergeneration, the underappreciated semi-sequel to Cyberpunk that used a stripped down system and anime stylings to get across a "teen superhero adventure" attitude. The Repo Man campaign I described above is part of this.

      Anyway, I have always wanted to go back to cyberpunk gaming because although its specific anxieties are very 80s, and it was unbelievably prescient in terms of how much and how quickly corporatism has beaten us in terms of privacy and security, the story is still compelling. Noir is a creation of the late 40s and early 50s' anxieties about the Bomb and the horrors of World War 2 still reverberating around the world, yet it remains relevant today because it speaks to feelings and desires that are human and present in every day and age. I see cyberpunk the same way.

      The bad. It was incredibly prone to minmaxing. Far too much so. Also I thought the core book had the right vibe but the supplements soon stopped being about the guy trying to make his mark in a shitty world and became all about the bling. I didn't really care about the bling. Cyberpunk wasn't about style over substance. That's such a fundamental misreading it's pretty much got it backwards.

      Since I'm here, the ugly. The setting. I never used it. I was surprised how the later supplements all assumed you were. I'd always taken the game as generic. Cyberpunk 2020 set in London was cool. Set in Night City it was lame.

      If I could find a way around the minmaxing I'd consider running it again.

      \n The thing I remember the best of all the Cyberpunk 2020 rules and setting stuff is the rule for guns locking up when you fire the damn things.
      \n
      \nSee, we misread the rules. In our initial runs of the game (and when I say initial, I mean about a year's worth of 3 weekly sessions), we operated with a draconic gunlock rule - you had to roll to see if the gun locked EVERY time you fired.
      \n
      \nThis had a couple of effects;
      \n- Mexican stand-offs were a really hardcore, nerve-y thing to engage in.
      \n- Firing your gun became a less important part of the game (alternatives; bluffing, intimidation, etc. Oh, and grenades. Loads of grenades)
      \n- When the forces of the large corporations were after you, hunting you down, tracking you, your gun was unreliable. Even your gear fucked with you, in addition to the fact that your contacts sold you out to the highest bidder, ex girlfriends kicked you out, and you were left in a coffin hotel, sweating and trying to use what little med skills you had to patch up that leg you took a knife in.
      \n
      \nIt all built up around this desolate feel of the setting that I really dig. Nothing goes the way of the PCs, yet by pure luck (at times) and some skill, we usually managed to push through. You know, apart from the occasional shot to the head by random gun-toting NPCs.
      \n
      \nLater we realised that we'd misread the rules, obviously.
