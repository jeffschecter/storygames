\n Hey folks,

Tomorrow night (which actually will be TONIGHT since I see it's after midnight) a bunch of friends are coming over for the first of every-other-weekly game nights I'm starting up. The purpose of the game nights is to play games that most of the gamers in my area don't get a chance to play.

We're starting with Fiasco. Mostly because I'm addicted to it.

The problem, though, is this: I may end up with up to nine or ten people coming over and there's the distinct possibility that none of them have played Fiasco before. I'm not sure what to do about that. I've considered the idea of not playing myself, but splitting them into two groups and then leaping back and forth to try to facilitate both. I've also thought about facilitating one group and shoving the book at a volunteer in the other group and hoping they'll be able to figure it out, but I don't know if that'll work. I've considered saying to heck with it and running a 10-man game of InSpectres, but I'd really prefer it if we did Fiasco because that's what folks will be expecting (besides, InSpectres is coming up on the list shortly anyway).

Any thoughts? Ideas? Evil schemes?

    \n If you are that addicted to it, I'm guessing you would enjoy splitting them up and hopping in-between groups to help move things along. I'm not sure I would shove a book in someone's face just so you can "play". And I mean that in the nicest possible way:)

    But as far as evil schemes go, print out a few playsets (not too many...or else they will spend forever reading them all) and let folks create their own groups.

    If they want to use the same playset, that's cool. Pause the groups at the halfway point and have each table exchange a piece of content with the other table. Just something cool (and new) for them to incorporate into their second half. I did this with the Dallas 1963 playset recently and it was fun!

    \n Facilitating two tables simultaneously is pretty easy, particularly if you can give them the initial rundown as a group and then divide them into tables. Do a little social engineering and make sure the two players most excited to try the game are at different tables. Fiasco is an easy game to understand so as long as you are available for questions, a table that gets the initial orientation and then is left to their own devices will do fine. If you want them to have a good time, just flit back and forth and don't play. A four person game is probably the sweet spot if you can swing it. If you have 10 gamers, I'd be tempted to go 3-3-4 instead of 5-5 if you have the space.

    \n Success!

    It turned out I only had five people show up initially, so we only had one group and I (being the sixth) facilitated for them and played NPCs. (We had a couple of other folks show up about an hour and a half late; they were happy to watch for a bit and play NPCs, themselves, every now and then).

    We played Objective Zebra. Before the night was over the players encountered a friendly Cthulhoid monster scratching on the hull, zombies running around, the mysterious disembodied voice of someone's Dark Master and an inhuman cook with trained attack potatoes. We also came up with about half a dozen different ways for the sub to be destroyed, but somehow it emerged without a scratch.

    It was everyone's first time playing Fiasco - and in one case, first time playing an RPG - and they LOVED it. They're definitely hooked and want to play it again some time.
