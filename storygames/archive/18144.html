\n I read a very interesting argument recently on another rpg forum.

Here's my restatement of the argument in point form:\n
1. The Forgean concept of Creative Agenda says that your game(s) must be focused on a creative goal, and all the parts should be fine-tuned to work towards that goal.

2. D&D, until recently, has not been a game which supported a unified Creative Agenda very well. (Although it clearly worked for some people, and had tremendous commercial success in the RPG market.)

3. With 4th Edition D&D, the designers of the game were influenced by Forge concepts and tried to design the fourth Edition to be a focused, coherent game.

4. Due to this decision, D&D 4E was less successful: the game could no longer do everything its fans wanted it to do. Lots of people gravitated back to older editions, Pathfinder, etc, and many dropped 4th Edition altogether.
\nWhat do you think? Is there a grain of truth here? (Almost all of the points above are pretty controversial, after all.)

No matter what you think about the validity of this argument, what lessons do you think we can we take away from this story?

    \n (Coming at this from someone whose favourite edition of D&D is 4th ed, but felt a little left out when Essentials came around)

    See, 4th ed was a really interesting grab at a very tactical RPG, to an extent that we haven't really seen since Chainmail. It is very much focused around the depth of the mini combat and the action around it. Because it was so focused, it drew a lot of critics and alienated a lot of people. I feel like the argument that there isn't roleplay in 4th is a little odd, in so much as though there is little rules support for it (Beyond some very gorgeous setting background in their 'Points of Light' idea which I think was pretty neat and the 'Playing a...' sections that exist in the various Power supplements. Roleplaying support for PCs in a supplement is not really the most encouraging sign.) but there really needn't be. I think 4th assumes that players will take inspiration from what little they have and run with it in a very freeform way, and there was, to an extent, I feel, a constraint on RP in previous editions in how much roleplay was rules-mediated.

    Overall, of course, these things depend entirely on personal taste.
    Essentials was when the real money problems started occuring, from what I can gather, and I feel this was probably due to the result of muddied the design water. In seeing lost members to Pathfinder, rather than sticking behind their game design vision, they tried to bring them back with a new spin on old school (The Essentials martial classes, for examples, which rely on at will abilities, have much less tactical depth than their Player Handbook cousins) and rebooting the core.
    The result was, really, that they lost more players, as the folks who were playing 4th ed and enjoying it for the tactics didn't like the new classes, the folks who loved the balancing of it compared to previous editions were put off by the seeming imbalance that existed among the Essentials classes (Numerically no, but in diversity yes) and the target audience of players who were playing Pathfinder were happier playing Pathfinder, and all the more power to them.

    Whether 4th ed had Forge influences, I dunno. Nor whether D&D is its own genre (It seems to me to just be a toolkit to run kitchen sink high fantasy, adding and omitting weirdness to taste). What I can say is that the design goal likely alienated people, and the change in goal alienated further. Its a pretty good game design morality tale really.
    I'm super excited for 13th Age, which appears to revise 4th ed with some more indie inspiration.

    \n Excellent discussion, everyone, and lots of great points from all sides. I'm really enjoying reading your reactions to this! (And, so far, I think I agree with pretty much all of what's being said, if anyone is curious about that at all.)


    As a point of clarification:

    What's the deal with the Forge, and Creative Agenda, and design priorities?

    It comes in two flavours I've seen, both rather extremist:

    1. "I like the Forge! I like new games! I'm really excited that the new D&D looks more like a 'focused' game! Those designers have learned things from the Forge, and this is a step forward from 'incoherent' D&D! Finally, a good game, thanks to the Forge. This proves that Forge-ite ideas have value!"

    This was a popular opinion a few years back, when D&D4E was coming out. I don't hear it a whole lot anymore, and haven't for a while.

    2. "I don't like the Forge or the new games. D&D4E was more 'focused' along what I think are Forge-ist lines, and that is a big mistake. The game is a total flop precisely because it attempts to follow Forge-ish ideas. Because those designers bought into Ron Edwards' dogma, the game is terrible, and doesn't sell well. A terrible, terrible game, thanks to the Forge, which ruins roleplaying. This proves that Forge-ite ideas are horrible!"

    I don't know how old this second idea is, but it definitely exists today in certain circles (I've heard it several times recently).

    I find the attempt to equate Forge concepts with the financial success or failure of D&D4E very interesting! I have no idea whether there's any truth to that (on either side) or not.

    As a point of clarification:

    What's the deal with the Forge, and Creative Agenda, and design priorities?

    It comes in two flavours I've seen, both rather extremist:

    1. "I like the Forge! I like new games! I'm really excited that the new D&D looks more like a 'focused' game! Those designers have learned things from the Forge, and this is a step forward from 'incoherent' D&D! Finally, a good game, thanks to the Forge. This proves that Forge-ite ideas have value!"

    This was a popular opinion a few years back, when D&D4E was coming out. I don't hear it a whole lot anymore, and haven't for a while.

    2. "I don't like the Forge or the new games. D&D4E was more 'focused' along what I think are Forge-ist lines, and that is a big mistake. The game is a total flop precisely because it attempts to follow Forge-ish ideas. Because those designers bought into Ron Edwards' dogma, the game is terrible, and doesn't sell well. A terrible, terrible game, thanks to the Forge, which ruins roleplaying. This proves that Forge-ite ideas are horrible!"

    I don't know how old this second idea is, but it definitely exists today in certain circles (I've heard it several times recently).

    I find the attempt to equate Forge concepts with the financial success or failure of D&D4E very interesting! I have no idea whether there's any truth to that (on either side) or not.
    \nI don't doubt that there were some people saying #1, but having wasted entirely too much time following 4e-related forum threads in recent years, to me it seems like that sentiment was nonetheless vanishingly rare. As for #2, it's the product of a very shrill fringe group that doesn't seem to grasp that it's 2013. In the present day Ron is off doing his own thing, no one really talks about GNS except the people who are really angry at the Forge as it was nearly a decade ago, and on Story Games old-school D&D gets discussed quite a bit (certainly more than GNS or, say, Spione). Hell, the last time I saw something from Ron it was a podcast where he was praising the OSR. In my view GNS theory and its ilk helped foster some unique and innovative games, but for the most part everyone's outgrown it except for its haters.

    Thinking about it more, I would say that 4E was also very much a follow-up to 3.5. Although Mearls and company do play other games, they work on D&D professionally and communicate first and foremost with D&D fans. A few years of fans' determined character optimization put the worst of 3.5's balance issues right in the designers' faces, and although other things definitely played a role, I think it's hard to overestimate the influence of 3.5 and its fanbase on what they attempted with 4e. Where they did look elsewhere, it seems like they ultimately did so in service of the goal of fixing perceived problems with 3.5. You wouldn't think it from the way some Pathfinder fans talk about it, but 4e has an awful lot of 3.5's DNA, and it absolutely would not have turned out the same way if it hadn't been made by experienced d20 designers.

    One of the unique things about D&D, and one of the reasons that in many ways I'm glad to not be working on it, is that I don't think any other game of any kind has been so many different things to so many different people. It launched as a barely complete game for which houseruling was basically required, and including basic and advanced there have been around a dozen different official versions, in addition to countless hacks, imitators, tributes, and retroclones. In light of that, narrowing the game's focus may have been a mistake, but something a lot more basic than what theory they might've been listening to.

    On the other hand, I think that it wasn't so much that 4e was too narrow in focus as that the non-combat elements were simply among the more underdeveloped elements of the game. A hypothetical 4.5 could develop Skill Challenges into what they should've been, make rituals more appealing and effective to use, make Utility Powers more plentiful and more non-combat oriented, hugely streamlined combat, made it easier for every class to have non-combat merits (instead of screwing the fighter over for skills), and any number of other things.

      \n Actually, JD, the discussion of "roles", as a term, is very specific and very narrow and very different in the first 4e books.

      That was perhaps the most interesting thing I noticed when I was reading 4e. They almost never used the word roleplay. They just used the word role. And role consistently means "the job you fill in team-based tactical combat". They clearly define the roles that the system supports - striker, defender, leader (which should probably have been called buffer), controller.

      They use the word role to mean that thing very very consistently. They do not use role to mean character. They do not use role to mean a meta group level thing, like caller or GM. They do not use role to mean something social, like the host. They only use it to mean a classification of the type of tactical things a given class build is good at.

      They tell you lots of things about roles. They tell you which 2 roles each class is designed to be good at. They tell you which of the powers are more suited to which of those roles. They tell you that the game works best when you have exactly 4 players, each one built to a different role. They tell you that you that you will have the most success as a team when each of you fulfills your determined role successfully.

      And in doing all that, I think they subtly introduce a different definition of roleplay. They do this by repeatedly and consistently defining the "role" part of roleplay.

      In 4e, roleplay does NOT primarily mean the portrayal of a character other than the self (though, of course, informed by the self), as it does in most other contexts - in most other RPG games and in general English language use.

      In 4e, roleplay means playing your role. It means fulfilling your responsibilities in combat as a striker, defender, leader, or controller.

      Has anyone else noticed this?

      \n 3.x is definitely not the same game as 4E but with less defined procedures. One approach to 3.x might fit that statement, but certainly not all play.

      The best illustrations of this is in the game modes in 4E. You have dialogue, exploration and encounter (I think those were the modes outlined in the DM book). And encounter is a defined period of time in terms of the refresh cycle and level pacing. 10 encounters should give you enough XP to level and when you start or end an encounter, you are interacting with the beginning and end of a time spent in a given game mode.

      Pathfinder/3.x not having that resource refresh period of "encounter" means you can do variable (in terms of XP budget/CR) encounters a lot more easily. You can also spend more time in exploration mode and make the transition to combat more far, far less jarring as you are not entering and exiting a unit of game time called an "encounter." It's easier to stay in the "I describe the fictional situation, you describe what your character's do, then we use the system to resolve it" mode.

      You end up making decisions based on the described fictional situation more in 3.x/PF than in 4E, where you are more likely to make decisions based on game mechanics. However, it is more than possible to play 3.x/PF in the exact same way as 4E and have player decision making to be based on the game mechanics rather than the described situation. 4E really supports that approach better though. 3.x has things like spells that describe what happens in the fiction rather than in terms of squares, conditions, the economy of actions, encounter durations, etc., for example.

      All this said, 3.x was the D&D that made me take a multi-year hiatus from D&D while 4E brought me back in. I just tired of 4E and went back to 3.x/Pathfinder after a few years of playing 4E weekly. After about 6 months of weekly 3.x/Pathfinder, I went back to BECMI, Swords & Wizardry, Mini-6 and OpenQuest for my trad gaming fix. They have even more support for the circuit of description procedure of play than 3.x/Pathfinder does (which in turn has more support for it than 4E).

      If you are looking for play in which the players decide what their character's do based on the described fictional situation, both 3.x/PF and 4E are pretty poor choices, but 3.x will support it better than 4E, which has support for its game modes so well built that it can get in the way of responding to the described fiction, even to the point of making in nonsensical to do so. Or, at the very least, very sub-optimal.

      \n The difference between 3e and 4e is one of clarity and success. The text of 3e makes all kinds of allusions about how to play it but the game mechanics when played as written produce a complex, gamist, slightly-disconnected-from-the-fiction tactical challenges and set pieces. But it's broken as fuck and the right choices mechanically can often be quite jarring in terms of the fiction.

      4e takes the game that 3e's mechanics were trying to achieve and succeeds at it. The text is much clearer, the tactics and so on are much more interesting, the combat is more fun, and the different choices are infinitely more balanced against one another.

      The clarity is the problem. Writing a text that successfully communicates the intended playstyle also successfully communicates that other playstyles are not quite suited. Meaning that people feel more reluctant to drift the game by ignoring/changing great swathes of the mechanics to suit their own playstyle and even the assumed overall intent of the designers ('this must be what they meant'). I think that most of the people who like 3e but hate 4e are those who play 3e but in a heavily drifted approximation of 2e kind of way.

      \n Imagine a guy with a crossbow is hiding behind a pavisse. He's been reloading and shooting for a while now and is well stocked with ammunition. His melee capabilities are quite poor but the combination of his cross bow and his defensive position make staying where he is in a fight ideal.
