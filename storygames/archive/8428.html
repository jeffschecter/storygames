\n We played Cold City the other night, and the post-play feedback threw me for six:
\n
\n"You railroaded us!"
\n
\nOuch. (I'm paraphrasing the comment, and we did talk about it at the time: I'm just opening this up.) Now I can say over and over again that I wasn't railroading, but the feeling was that I was railroading the play. Now why was that?
\n
\nWhen I GM, I like to play too! I'm not there as a facilitator for other folks fun... I wanna play as much as anyone else. This is not controversial: everyone has equal weight at the table. Sure, some folks may have different roles, but the weighting of their play is the same. When I'm a player, I like to engage in a lot of 'table chatter': I refer to the OOC and sometimes IC talk that flies around the table where you suggest stuff, and plans of action, and there's this whole furious cloud of ideas zinging back and forth. I was doing just that when we played Cold City: I'd frame a scene, and then I'd jump into the roles of the NPCs, responding to the PCs and stuff... but I was also encouraging the other players to action and getting into the scene myself, saying "Wouldn't it be cool if you did this...", "That lousy Yank is lying through his teeth!" (was he? In my role as GM I knew, but my NPC guard didn't... I was just playing up his stereotype) and stuff.
\n
\nPost-session, one of the players made the insightful comment that the tradition of RP has generally invested the GM with a lot of authority. So when a GM is saying "Hey, wouldn't it be cool if..." and suchlike, then some folks are going to think that the GM is suggesting that that course of action is the right thing to do... hence the feeling of railroading. Another player said that he does the 'table chatter as GM thing' too, but that he makes sure to preface such chatter with words that make it clear that these are just suggestions or pure roleplay and so don't have any more weight at the scenario-meta-level than that.
\n
\nDoes anyone else table chatter as a GM? And by that I mean specifically chatter about the PCs actions, plans, and stuff. Everything is open right, and since I feel that I'm able to make the distinction between in-character knowledge and player knowledge, the same goes for GM-knowledge and player/PC knowledge. Just because I know the beliefs and such of the NPCs doesn't mean that I will try to metagame it to the NPCs advantage... that would suck.
\n
\nCheers
\nPondering Pete

    \n When a GM player who doesn't have the authority (by the rules) and doesn't claim the authority (explicitly during the game) gets accused of railroading, the players probably need to unlearn some old habits. The GM can help by saying stuff like, "but that's just a suggestion" and "my ideas have the same weight as yours here," but there comes a point where -- assuming they know the rules -- the players need to just own their narrative control.

    \n I do a lot of table chatter, as you put it, as the GM. The onus is on the other players to know the rules of the game and categorize my participation correctly. I'm not responsible for their play - they can ask me to shut up if they find me distracting, and they have, but not saying anything and inventing their own rules for the game, such as "have to do what Eero suggests every time he opens his mouth" is a problem in their heads, not in mine.

    On the other hand, when I say something mandated by the rules, I usually signal this clearly by emphasis or referring to the rule in question directly. I even flag my NPC portrayal based on the rules I'm basing that portrayal on. For example, I might say something like "The NPC is getting difficult, he's willing to fight you for the treasure. I can't let you get this too easily, he's an antagonist after all." So basically any player who is paying attention to the rules and procedures of the game in progress will always know why I'm saying something and whether it has some sort of procedural meaning or not.

    Of course, all this means that my games are never going to give you a safe platform for pure imaginative immersion, as I'm usually not upholding much of an illusion regarding the workings of the game setting. Things happen because people want them to happen in some manner, not because an objective and invisible force makes them. The closest I get to "pure" portrayal is when I base the actions of NPCs on my character vision for them, which is also something I'll usually remark on now and then.

    \n First: railroading is a tool you can use. Railroad parts of the dramatic content to focus on other parts.

    Second: if you go on talking in-character, and invest your npc's with your authority as GM, then eventually the players will start to think for themselves. Let them develop a critical stance to what npc's are telling them. Have patience.

    \n In our game of Polaris (which is GMless, as you likely know) on Saturday, we had a moment where the table chatter started us all pushing the story in a direction where the creepy corrupt Senator took control of the army and was planning to declare himself Emperor. This hadn't happened yet, but everyone though it was a good idea. But then we blew up the city (a second time), which dashed all our plans for the Senator. There was a "You're going off the rails!" moment, where we were like "But the senator's supposed to become Emperor, and now he can't". Which is to say we were all railroading ourselves. We had gotten invested in future events that hadn't been confirmed in the fiction, and we panicked hen they didn't happen that way.

    Ultimately, the city was destroyed. Maybe the Senator will become Emperor of elsewhere, or will rule the rubble. Or maybe that will just be another story instead of this story. I don't know yet. but the important thing was that we were reminded to play the game now, rather than get invested in the idea of what the future parts of the game will be.


    So, uh, perhaps table chatter becoming railroading is not necessarily tied to the GM role. Though that invests you with some authority you might not have otherwise.
