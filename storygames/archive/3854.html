\n Hi. I just watched The Bourne Ultimatum last night, and one recurring device really stuck in mind -- the Improvised Escape. Throughout the film, Bourne is being hunted by enemy agents, and makes use of everyday items to create diversions, improvise weapons, or create escape routes to evade his enemies. Most of the Escapes are largely cleverness on the part of the screen-writer(s), but how great would it be if my friends and I could pull out the same cleverness at the gaming table?
\n
\nI'm envisioning a pattern of play where one player places Obstacles in front of a Fugitive player, and the Fugitive player must quickly improvise an Escape. The goal would be to entertain each other with cleverly described escapes. I personally think it'd be difficult to come up with crazy ideas on the spot for an entire session. Ideally, you'd want rules that would enable players to improvise like Bourne does on-screen.
\n
\nMy solution: A deck of cards, each with one everyday item (flashlight, coat hanger, fire extinguisher, Febreeze bottle, food processor, etc) listed on it. As a Fugitive player, you would keep a small hand of these cards. When confronted with an Obstacle, you play one or more cards from your hand, describing how your character uses each item to Escape. Perhaps you earn a reward of some sort for each item used, with a greater reward for items that would be difficult to work into an Escape (like a pink rubber eraser).
\n
\nFor flavor, you could change the content of the deck depending on the setting of game--add hookah for a game set in Cairo, bicycle for Tokyo, or Hummer for LA. Or maybe players could write down some random items on index cards at the beginning of each session, so each session has a fresh mix of ideas.
\n
\nAny thoughts on how to make this better, or how to do it differently?
\n
\nTo take this a step further:
\n
\nA few other Hollywood thrillers (like The Fugitive, Enemy of the State, Chain Reaction, etc. ) follow the same basic structure as the Bourne movies. In each story, you generally have three characters/sides in conflict:
\n
\n- The Hunted - a person on the run, who is trying to prove his innocence/avenge himself/reveal the truth before the powers that be capture him.
\n
\n- Keeper of the Peace - on the side of the law, she pursues The Hunted because she believes he is guilty of his crimes and must be stopped for the greater good.
\n
\n- The Conspirator - also on the side of the law, but he knows that The Hunted knows too much, and wants to eliminate The Hunted before he reveals the truth.
\n
\nThis could work as a three-player game, where The Hunted player doesn't know which Law player is the Keeper of the Peace and which is the Conspirator.
\n
\nThoughts?

Alternately, you can have the KotP and Conspirator use a separate set of rules, slightly differentiated for their different roles (but not so much that the Hunted could tell the difference easily).     Or perhaps... oh, hey, what if:

    The Hunted wins if the KotP arrests the Conspirator or escapes both.
    The KotP wins if he arrests either the Conspirator (identified by the Hunted) or the Hunted.
    The Conspirator wins if he kills the Hunted or the KotP arrests the Hunted.

    Then allow the Hunted to somehow give the other two players resources -- if the Hunted chooses loyalties wisely, he gives resources to the KotP, who can use the resources to expose the Conspirator. However, if the Hunted chooses poorly and gives the Conspirator resources, the Conspirator can use those resources to help catch up to the Hunted. This would represent all sorts of tips, evidence, or "I'm here, meet me" or similar such interaction that usually populates these movies.

    Yes, if the KotP wins, one of the other players wins. Both other players want the KotP on their side, but make it so the Conspirator can't mechanically help the KotP, only hinder the Hunted. And the Hunted isn't sure which one of the other players is the KotP and which is the Conspirator.

    You'd have three very neat, very different roles that are all bound up to the same situation.

    \n Right, so like: the Hunted sits there, looking at the two Law characters, and watches them put poker chips down in rows on the table, and give significant looks at each other. But he can't quite tell what's actually happening.

    I'd think it would boil down to:
    The Hunted escapes 'trapped' situations and provides evidence to the Law characters.
    The KotP uses evidence to build a case against the Conspirator and provides the Hunted with situations.
    The Conspirator uses evidence to create a smokescreen that combats the KotP's case and provides the Hunted with situations.
    Both the KotP and the Conspirator can use evidence to "get closer" to the Hunted (and/or make the situations harder to escape).

    Oh, and make the Hunted's success at escaping situations give him evidence to bestow to Law characters. The better you do, the more evidence you have -- which may end up with you hanging yourself.
