    /dev/random is still a pseudo-random generator, unless it's talking to some sort of RNG hardware.

    The most entertaining RNG I've heard of was developed at SGI. They fed the computer digitized photos of a lava lamp, and generated random numbers from that.

    Apparently roulette wheels are excellently non-fudgable RNGs. Props to A. Cherry!
  • SeleneTan
    March 2006
    /dev/random forms a pool of bits based on internal hardware/software timings. If it doesn't have as many random bits in its pool as you've asked for, it'll block other processes until it receives enough input to give you the remaining bits.
    You can see this if you do cat /dev/random: it'll spit out a bunch of stuff, then wait a bit and spit out more, wait a bit, spit out more, and so on.

    I recently insisted that if my sisters (and oldest nephew) wanted me to GM for them that they had to have dice. I didn't care WHAT dice, but they were to provide dice, whether it be ransacking the boardgames and ending with a lot of d6s, or begging Dad for some of his old dice bags (which is what happened.) It helped shape the nature of the campaign; we didn't actually really use a particular system, but it gave us some randomizers and tests to see "how good" a character was at accomplishing something. I could have (and often do) run diceless, but I know three things about my younger family members:
    1. They need the math practice.
    2. Dice are great to have something to do with your hands, and they fiddle and have a lot of excess energy to focus.
    3. It makes them feel more like they know what they can do as characters.
    It does mean that I figure I'll be buying personalized dice for them for upcoming birthdays. There are pink sets, after all, for my youngest sister (who numbers amongst her interests, "Ballerinas, horses, pink, zombies, kittens, and pirates.")
