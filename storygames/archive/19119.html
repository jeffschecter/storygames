One of my complaints with many traditional games is the meandering, almost accidental quality to any story that happens to come out of a game. Of the various storytelling structures, television seems the most accessible and easy to convey. Most one-hour dramas have four acts with an optional teaser and/or tag. The act breaks typically follow the commercial breaks and this structure can handle anything from purely episodic (one-shot stories) to a serial narrative. That and there's a lot of games that center around role-playing in TV show universes, so emulating that structure from the ground up seems like a benefit.

    \n I'll start off by throwing a few useful links up for the basics.

    TVTropes: Act Break. Not quite right on the rising action / climax positioning, but that's an older model. Standard "set an alarm" warning as it's TVTropes.

    Scriptfaze. Some more basics of TV scripting.

    Movie Outline on TV script formatting and structure.

    Most one-hour dramas are written in four acts, with an optional teaser and tag. It's Four Acts. There are Four Lights! You may think you see six, or have heard about three, but there are Four Acts. Each act ends on something of a cliffhanger. Long lost heir, end of an act. He's not our guy, end of an act. The monster chasing us for the whole episode is really a brunt up time-shadow of a possible future you, end of an act.

    They breakdown like this:

    Teaser: that grabby, hooky bit of action before the opening credits. Law & Order they find a body. Star Trek a spatial anomaly. Doctor Who the Doctor pops out of the TARDIS and discovers something interesting.

    Act One: that initial bit of investigation and confusion. Law & Order they search for clues and investigate, interview suspects. Star Trek they scan, have some weird happenings on the ship, Data says something about something being offline. Doctor Who the Doctor finds someone in trouble and his companion might be in jeopardy.

    Act Two: the investigation is basically over and they know what's going on. One mystery leads into another. Confusion gives way to conflict and fighting. Law & Order they discover the real killer (the first two are red herrings). Star Trek the Romulans decloak and Worf wants to blast 'em. Picard says no. Doctor Who the Doctor is running.

    Act Three: the group refocuses and takes the fight to the baddies. No longer on the back foot, but still facing complications. Down notes and despair. End of the act things hit the low point. Law & Order they switch from police procedural to legal drama. Star Trek there's a Romulan virus in the ship's computer, Troi says something emo. Doctor Who the Doctor nearly loses his companion and/or the villain says something clearly and irrevocably evil.

    Act Four: the group is on the offensive. Over the course of the act all the baddies are dealt with, and by the end of the act all the problems are solved. Law & Order despite the scumbag defense lawyer, Jack and his team have won the case (most nights). Star Trek the ship is saved by technobabble and Riker's Beard. Doctor Who the Doctor, pick one: asspull cleverness, deus ex machina, or the power of love.

    Tag: the moral or the quip at the end, laugh, and ride into the sunset. Law & Order someone makes a remark about the case, they laugh (or don't), and walk down the hall or get on the elevator. Star Trek everyone beams up, they meet in the conference room, or Picard stares out his office window. Doctor Who the Doctor looks ecstatic or morose, cheers up at the end and grinding wheezes away.

    Acts one and two are generally more passive, the characters are reacting. Acts three and four are generally more active, the characters are pushing back.

    That 4-act breakdown is nicely coherent, thanks, now I know what you're after. My big question is, how do you take a structure composed largely of protagonist action and then reproduce it in a game where the players are free to use their protagonists as they will?

    Here are some solutions I've encountered:

    I've played a few games that have set steps of a dramatic arc that's going on around the characters, informing what they do without mandating exactly what they must be doing. In Montsegur 1244, the characters' castle is besieged in phases, and you play out who's doing what at each phase. The aim is to address the characters' personal issues rather the the siege itself, but the siege provides the pressure cooker for them to do this. Grey Ranks works similarly, but with a larger number of steps, preceded by radio announcements of what's going on in the larger world of 1944 Warsaw. I can talk more about these games if you like.

    Then there's the structure of Sorcerer, which isn't hard-coded, but consists of an initial premise, the directive to end play once that premise is addressed, and various GM prep and improv techniques to help push things along toward a conclusion.

    Other games use mechanics to track progress and endgame conditions, like Dead of Night's Tension Die, which goes up as players take action, and acts as a guideline for the GM re: which phase/act of horror movies to emulate.

    Final Hour of a Storied Age, which I haven't played, seems to have a hard-coded general act structure which is then customized by the group. (Please correct me if I'm wrong, @DanMaruschak )

    Finally, there's the tried and true technique of GM railroading, where my quandary is irrelevant because players are not free to use their protagonists as they will. Please note that I don't mean "railroading" in the socially dysfunctional sense -- I just mean play where the GM controls what goes on. If the players are into that, it can be quite fun.

    Also! We had an interesting conversation on Story Games last February about a similar topic (Help: Basic Formula for Story), with one poster describing how he used Dan Harmon's Story Circle when GMing. Might be fun to skim through.

    Does any of this sound like it's in the direction you want to go in?

    My first recommendation to look into: beats. In TV and movie-speak, a "beat" is a thing where stuff happens. I know, that's terribly vague. Here's an example of a beat in action...

    When the protagonist receives sudden news, one of the following happens: it's a breakthrough in the case; there's a complication with the plan; something terrible just happened.

    Now, wait a second. Doesn't that structure remind you of any games out there? ;-)

    That's right; Apocalypse World et al are actually modeling the way that TV shows and movies work. (It's small wonder that Dungeon World has been called "Indiana Jones for D&D"!) A "move" is a "beat"--it's a space where something happens. Beat propels into beat, like move cascades into move.

    What controls the beats? The tempo of outside pressures. In *World games, that's Fronts. As more fronts come closer to an end, the way that beat propels into beat becomes more intense, because the stakes are higher.

    \n Housekeeping. Beat and scene are practically synonymous, but not quite. They're used interchangeably in all but a few corner cases. As above, a beat is a specific story event, something happens. Jack confronts Jill about her affair. That's a beat. A scene is one location. A single beat often happens at a single location, so beat = scene. But sometimes the beat moves from one location to another. If Jack confronts Jill about her affair at their house and the fight continues in their car that's one beat in two locations. But practically speaking, a beat and a scene are the same thing.

    In general terms the acts are all the same. They open with a reaction to the scene that came before, and end with a cliffhanger, which propels the next act. They're slightly different thematically, act one being initial investigation, act two being confrontation with the problem, etc. The acts are made up of several scenes, while the teaser and tag are usually no more than one scene.

    Starting from a fairly simple system that's close to this, Fiasco, we can extrapolate. In Fiasco there's no GM, each player in order takes a turn framing a scene starring their character. All the characters are tightly connected and there's plenty of interesting bits in place to draw from. Fiasco goes around the table twice, two starring scenes for each player, then has an act break (tilt). Play goes around the table twice more and you have the end (aftermath). Each act in Fiasco follows the same pattern, basically. A series of interconnected scenes that--hopefully--push the story forward, ending in a cliffhanger or twist.

    You could easily have the GM set the first scene (teaser) to kick the story off. Play around the table once or twice and end on the GM again for the cliffhanger scene to kick the story into the next act. The cliffhanger scenes are natural break points for food and session ending as they're high tension, oh shit, what happens next moments. They're designed to bring the audience back through the commercial break. Play through the four acts till you get to the last scene of the fourth act, then end with a table-determined scene (tag).

    This works for more GM-is-in-at-least-basic-control style games, and could easily delve into railroad-land. As long as the group knows there's only a loose story in place and to not venture too far afield, the story that emerges from play would be a lot more coherent, and likely interesting, than the accidental/boring story that comes out of pure sandbox or more traditional, D&D style games.

    Most scenes, especially the cliffhangers, will have rather obvious reactions from the characters. GM: There's a dead body. Players: We investigate. GM: You find definitive proof Suspect A is not the killer. Players: We investigate further. I think if the GM and players can separate the next generally logical thing to do, story-wise, from the next GM-enforced you must do X then you can have the best of both worlds. By that I mean that as long as the GM has a rough idea for the outline of the story--there's a murder, NPC A is the killer, okay go--instead of pixel bitching or having a very specific story in mind, then even this basic framework can get something like a coherent story going without venturing into railroading.

    \n Fiasco plus a GM doing some teasers and cliffhangers is a neat idea. I'd rather see a cliffhanger at the end of each act (i.e. turn around the table) than just once as the Tilt.

    Some system to create different contexts for each act, so that the within-act routine can repeat itself while still progressing dramatically from act to act, might be good.

    Perhaps these two could be combined? At the end of Act One, the GM introduces a cliffhanger which ushers in the Act Two content that hadn't seen play until now -- wrapping up the initial mysteries, an antagonist appears, danger threatens, conflict begins. At the end of Act Two, the GM wraps up loose threads and establishes where the bad guys are and what they're up to, so the players can plan their approach for Act Three. The Act Three cliffhanger would then be the mother of all cliffhangers, with imminent death or loss staring the players in the face.

    As far as counting acts by player turns, I think there are some pros and cons. An agenda to reach a certain story point by a certain turn can help add focus and direction, but it can also be something of a burden on character play and organic surprise. Total freedom, on the other hand, can lead to play that doesn't particularly build, making the act-end cliffhangers arrive more out of nowhere. An in-between, such as act ends triggered by fictional events or player choices (like in Dead of Night), might be a nice balance between unconstrained fiction and fiction-independent rules.

    Another thought: character moves with Act values. If a player is tempted to use an Act 4 move in Act 1, they might wanna try another way; if a player does use an Act 4 move in Act 1, that might be a signal to the group to slow down a bit; if a player uses an Act 1 move in Act 3, that might be a sign to ramp things up; etc.

    Except that Star Trek doesn't have a tag. It has five acts. Sure, Picard is often staring out his office window at the end of the fifth act, but it's also the same act where Riker's beard saves the day.

    Sure, it's pedantic, but it hints at a big point: TV shows use the act structure that works for them. Some use four acts. Some use five. Some have a tag, and some don't.

    In Star Trek, the fifth act gives the show more time to link the day-saving action to the philosophy behind it. Whereas in Law & Order, they want to wrap up the plot, and then have a separate scene where everyone stands around and comments on how justice was served (or wasn't, as the case may be).

    So when you're adopting the formalized act structure of TV for your game, do so by thinking about what that particular game needs. The act structure in an game that cares mostly about solving the investigation is going to look different from the act structure where the investigation is just a backdrop for the interpersonal drama.

    Act 1 of Monsterhearts looks a lot different from act 1 of Sagas of the Icelanders. Figure out what each act is supposed to look like for the game you're running. Figure out how many acts are appropriate for the game you're running. Figure out if you want a tag and/or a teaser for the game you're running.

    \n Yeah, it's a good point. Trek did use Shakespeare's five-act structure with a teaser tacked to the front end. Five-act structure is wonderfully explained here.

    But when trying to explain a concept that people might not be familiar with it's easier to take the standard and detail that. The vast majority of one-hour dramas use the four-act structure above. Many sitcoms use a two-act structure with a teaser and tag. Squash-and-stretch cartoons use a one- or two-act structure. Rather than explaining all of them and their differences, far better to start with the most common format (one-hour dramas) and the most common structure (four acts).

    For gaming purposes though I think the symmetrical four-act structure that dominates TV land is the most useful. If you're trying to replicate an act structure at the table with fairness and equal spotlight time for each player, using four acts of equal length--rather than the unequal act lengths of say five-act structure--is far superior. You could just as easily use two equal length acts as Fiasco did.

    I think the teaser is a great way to kick the game off with the GM setting the scene and presenting the players with what's going on. Sure you could do that without a teaser, but I think it's easier to do it that way. So too with the tag. The GM gets to kick things off, why not let the table wind things down with the tag. I'm not saying any of these structures are better or worse for storytelling, only that this one is the best--I think--for gaming with.
