\n First, I direct y'all to read this over here (or at least skim it, which is what I did):

http://bishi-wannabe.livejournal.com/39675.html#cutid1

Here's my thought: Up until now, we've seen games with extensive character building and advancement over time either have great amounts of front-loading (D&D, Exalted, etc), or none at all.

The idea is to present all the rules in one huge lump up front, You read them all, and figure out something like this:

"In 3 months of gaming, I want to aim for Those Abilities Over There. Which means I have to take This Ability and That Ability now." So you have to do some thought into career-pathing before going further.

I had a possible idea to take some of the weight of front-loading away, and wanted to throw that out into S-G:

Maybe create some kind of "stepped pyramid" levelling/character advancement structure. So, in the example of, say, D&D (with a MODIFIED Feat System), it would look something like this:

Every level you gain a Feat (told ya it was modified)
Levels 1-4, for each class there are a few choosable feats, and maybe a few general feats. Maybe some stack on each other ("Improved Fast Strike requires Fast Strike").
Then, at level 5, you hit a plateau. You no longer get feats from the level 1-4 pool (or, alternately, for something like Exalted, you'd automatically get the rest, or most of the rest). Then there's a new pool of class and general feats from levels 5-8 to choose. NONE of these require feats from levels 1-4, or build off of them. It's like a completely divided space, where only these new feats exist. Same thing from levels 9-12, etc.
Then, you divide the Feat/abilities section into not a laundry list of ALL abilities, but step those, too: Abilities for levels 1-4, 5-8, 9-12, etc. They're each in a new section or chapter.

That way, you can just play in the moment, still have the fun of levelling up, gaining XP, building your dude. And yet, you don't have the overhead of worrying about what you need to take NOW to get X LATER. When you get to that plateau, you can take any ability that you want without worry.

Also, it will be easier to jump into a game where "Level One" (or the basic char build in Exalted) isn't the starting position. If you want to have a game of all Level 12 characters (or "high plateau Exalted dudes"), you don't need to start them from Level 1 and build up from there in steps, you can jump straight to that level and put experienced characters together without a huge amount of downtime.

Now, some people will want to read ALL the data up-front to begin with for shits and giggles. It's not like Front-Loading is an Evil which must be destroyed, but for those folks who want to read all the rules, they're there; and for the people that like character progression but don't want to be worried at Character Generation Time what they need to take to have a decent build in X months down the road (*raises hand*), this might be a good alternative method.

Thoughts?

-Andy

    \n Sort of like "Improved Fast Strike: Requires Fast Strike or Level 5+"?

    I can get behind that as a principle. Seems pretty neat. Although I can see certain people complaining because:

    a) With the stepped progression where having X lets you buy Y later you usually see a pretty consistent progression with a character. So I want to be "Mr Totally Awesome At Stabbing People In The Eye With A Rapier". At level one I buy Rapier Combat, Level 2 I buy Poking, level 3 I get Improved Rapier Combat, Level 4 I get Improved Poking and at Level 5 I get Called Shot: Eye. I've progressed along a single path. If I can just buy Called Shot Eye once I'm level 5, I might have bought Club Combat at level 1, Bashing at level 2 etc etc and then just decided that I'd drop all that and buy my guy a rapier at level 5 and get Called Shot: Eye.

    b) People who're gonzo for minmaxing will end up with very, very diverse characters. That is, they'll get stuff from early levels in one area and then get when they hit the next plato they'll buy Improved versions of something completely different. So I start off as a Bowman, then instead of becoming an Improved Bowman after level 5, I decide that I'll stay being good at Bows and add Improved Swordsman. So I'm now as good a swordsman as the guy who wants to specialise in swords, plus, y'know, I can shoot arrows and shit too.

    If the game's designed well, I can see both problems being solvable, but I personally wouldn't want to tackle getting something like that right.

    \n Honestly, one thing that bugged me about D&D is that the awesome mostly comes into play at higher levels. If I have to wait 4 levels before getting assassin tokens, that reinforces that notion; in fact, it would make me never play characters below L5. One thing that's so cool about IH is that you start out with competent characters and have tokens to play with right away.

    That all said, there's another way to go at this, which is to do away with requirements but allow synergy going into specializations. So Poking would apply to all poking weapons, but Rapier Combat only to rapiers. Their effects stack. It makes more sense to get Poking first, because it has a broader application, but if you want to end up being a rapier expert it doesn't matter which one you take first, you can always take the other one later and gain the synergy. And Improved Whatever then adds +1 instead of +2 of the basic ones, so you could get it first, but it makes more sense to get the basic one first. Still, if you did take it first, once you take the basic one, you're back at the same efficiency level as someone who went the other way. So basically, you can make up for non-optimal choices over time.

    That kind of system is something I've been playing around with for a design in which each ability gives a certain bonus to either the type of tool you're using for it, the type of effect you're trying to achieve, or some other function. So if you've got Melee Combat, Blade Training, and Basic Maneuvers, then you'd have a +3 when doing a melee maneuver with a sword (say, disarm), +2 when trying to do harm with a sword (i.e., a non-maneuver), +2 when doing a maneuver with a non-bladed weapon, +1 trying to do harm with a non-blade weapon, and so on.

    \n Rich, some of the comments you brought up can be avoided by creating an entirely new playing field at each level. Perhaps you retain your Rapier In The Eye skill from 4th level, which will be quite handy against 1st-4th level monsters, but at 5th you pick up Perfect Parry, which disarms anyone who doesn't have a perfect defense (a la Exalted). All your low-level stuff retains utility, but typically against weaker opponents or in very specific situations. The higher-level abilities work in utterly different ways that can be augmented by the lower-level ones.

    Example with too much math:
    Level 1-4 gives a +X bonus to various things.
    Level 5-9 gives a *X (times X) modifier, applied after the addition.
    Level 10-14 gives ^X (raised to the X power) modifier
    Level 15-19 gives a +#\xe2\u02c6\u017e (plus a certain kind of infinity) modifier

    Each plateau outstrips the last, but if you studied rapiers in levels 1-4 and keep with it in 5-9, you still beat out the people who studied bows in 1-4 and switched to rapiers later.

    Christian's idea isn't bad either - "You gain +1 with Fnords, with another +1 for every Fnordner feat you have."

    \n This has give me a different idea -- feats that grow with the character. Instead of taking "Bean Counting" and then "Improved Bean Counting," itself a prereq for "Count Focus," etc., you take the core feat at some point. Next time you can take a feat, your "Bean Counting" feat turns into "Improved," and so on. It wouldn't replace the fact that you get a new feat -- that's still part of this setup. Example:

    Level 1: I take Bean Counting
    Level 3: Bean Counting becomes Improved Bean Counting. I'm also taking Exotic Weapon Proficency (Banjo).
    Level 6: Improved Bean Counting becomes Master Bean Counting, and Exotic Weapon Proficency (Banjo) becomes Weapon Focus (Banjo). I'm also taking Skill Focus (Riverdancing).
    etc.

    This answers the question in a different way. Instead of reducing the influence of prereqs, this assumes you'll keep improving in your skills and will get to that "Awesome thing you can do" point. You could incorporate the idea of the plateaus, and separate gaining new feats and improving existing ones.

    \n I think a powerful factor that would make your kai-model work, Andy, is the social aspect.

    When you were running through Kai0, not only do you learn your stuff, but you watch your friends learning their stuff. This works even better than a videogame, because for an RPG, everything's got to be out there on the table. There's no black-box. The GM can't handle simultaneous player actions, so just about every game has turns, and if you're polite and smart, you watch and listen to the turns of others. You see them run through the mechanics, and you learn vicariously - not with the same fidelity as doing yourself, but it's workable.

    So when you hit Kai1, you get all the Kai0 tier stuff, and you have a reasonable working knowledge of it because you've seen your friends learning to use it. If you're unsure of something about it, hell, you've got an expert sitting right there: "Hey, Rebecca, should I put Kai Cuts the Horizon in this combo? Is it going to be any good against this guy we're fighting?"

    I think you can keep low-level abilities useful throughout the tiers without too much trouble. Videogames do this all the time - the basics are still valuable weapons in your arsenal even in the final boss fight (the Okami boss battle uses every single brush technique, IIRC). Exalted 2 with its excellencies and WotG do it well. D&D? Well, D&D is a big space, but I find myself metamagicing up 2nd to 4th level spells to 9th+ pretty routinely.
