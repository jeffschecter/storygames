\n We, as story gamers, possess a lot of hate.
As evidenced here.

However, some of us also possess mad design chops.
And so, a contest!

If you want to enter 2nd Edition, the contest of fixing seemingly-irreoncilably-broken games that people claim they will hate forever, post here with your extensive and impressive design credentials.

Rules:
You must have already posted to the other thread, bitching about an irreconcilably broken game that you will hate forever.
You must post here claiming to be the author of one of the much-hated games listed in the other thread (this will be part of your extensive and impressive design credentials).

If you pass those two requirements, you will be randomly assigned one of the much-hated games listed in the other thread, and tasked to write a second edition (or, if you are lazy or uninspired, a "patch" for the first edition).

You will have until August 10th to complete the second edition.

You will be judged by two people - the author of the first edition (who might even give you PERMISSION to publish the 2nd Edition!) and the first person to post in the other thread about how much they hate the previous edition of the game.

WHAT'S THE PRIZE, you ask?
Well, I'm currently working on the 2nd edition of Perfect.
I'll send the winner a copy of it, when it's finished. That's pretty cool!

    \n I am Jupiter Starchaser, and I am both a Sagittarius and a game designer. During my last pilgrimage to Burning Man, I became lost in the desert. Wearing nothing more than the suit Mother Gaia saw fit to give me, I communed with the blazing sun overhead. At that moment, I was connected to the spiritual overmind of humankind, and I never felt closer than I have to my brothers and sisters that share this planet.

    It was during that time that the seeds of The Challenge sprouted in my soul.

    I knew then that what my fellow humans wanted was not the same as what they needed. I am not a liar, but I had to deceive my fellow man in order to bring The Challenge into the Zeitgeist of human experience. It was always my intent to bring about a competitive spirit that will smash the opponent's face, but the opponent was never supposed to be the fellow travelers at your table. The real opponent is the forces of darkness that stain the human soul. Things like talking louder and faster in order to win arguments, macho posturing, and trash talking. How else do you fight such a darkness, than with the spirit of collaboration, drama, and narrative beauty?

    I made the book in softcover because if you bent it around your arm, it felt soft and warm, like a hug. And that's what the game is, a big book hug that shields the human heart against the darkness of war, tyranny, and professional sports.

    If you cry, even one tear, as you play The Challenge, then you have won, dear friend. You have won.

    \n Well, looking at the calendar and the massive amount of work before me, I'm not going to get to a playable state with Crunch Dot.

    It makes me sad.

    Honestly, the interpretation I took for the 2nd edition of the game became a little disturbing for me - it's one of those games that paranoid folks would point to as evidence that gaming promotes drug use and anarchy. I also didn't like some of the terminology (pranks, specifically) that I had come up with, but wasn't able to find a good replacement word. Here's some of the text of the game.

    -=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-

    Dear Reader:

    Thanks for finding the new edition of Crunch Dot!

    Those who know the story behind the original version might be surprised at some of the changes here. When I collaborated on the original version, those of us involved weren't... well, let's just say we weren't operating at 100%. Mentally. I mean, we were really fucked in the head. I had done so much meth that I couldn't see straight for weeks at a time. We weren't allowed much in the way of technology (or dangerous items), so the whole thing got banged out on a crappy manual typewriter by someone with Tourette's. Once I got clean, I decided to ask for the rights to the game and redevelop it, keeping some of the core ideas and throwing out what didn't work. You won't see the scattered, twelve-chefs-stirring-the-pot design of the last version. You will definitely see a brutal game about breaking the system both mentally and physically. You'll also see some of our therapist's Zen Buddhist influence, and a unique mandala-like character creation system. Finally, you'll see why the game is named the way it is. I hope you enjoy it.

    -=-=-=-=-=-=-

    Your character is a drug addict. He or she is hooked (psychologically, if not physically) on a particular type of illegal substance. Moreover, this substance has some severe drawbacks that are going to make your character's life hell from time to time. However, unlike in the real world, this drug has opened your character's eyes to a world beyond the one we know. He or she is a boddhisahttva - someone who has achieved enlightenment and returned to our world to help enlighten others.

    The eventual goal of all characters in Crunch Dot is to bring enlightenment to as many people as possible. This doesn't mean that your character will go out and give drugs to as many people as possible - most people aren't ready for it, and the drugs have some pretty heavy side effects. What you're trying to do is dole out kensho, little doses of insight that lead to eventual satori (enlightenment). Many of your character's actions will appear to most people as clever pranks. A certain percentage of the population will realize the meaning behind the pranks and find their minds dwelling on them, some eventually experiencing kensho. Particularly effective pranks can leave whole crowds staring slack-jawed as each of them experiences a tiny slice of nirvana.

    This lofty goal is not easily reached. When it comes to pulling pranks, many things stand in your character's way, from locked doors to armed guards. Materials for these pranks must be obtained as well, legally or not. The greatest challenge your character faces, however, is his or her own addiction. Each drug unlocks a specific part of the character's mind or body, but comes with substantial negative side effects and the dangers of overdose. You'll spend about as much time dealing with your character's addiction as you will attempting to bring enlightenment to the masses.

    In short, here's what's going on in the game:
    Your Character's Goal: Break "the system" and bring enlightenment
    Your Character's Methods: Drug use, self-alteration, mental programming, screwing with people's minds
    Your Character's Obstacles: The difficulty of pulling of enlightenment pranks, the problems that come with drug use
    What Your Character Does: Carry out a prank. Cope with the drug dealer getting busted. Run from the cops. Pull a B&E for money. Sneak into a secure location. Deal with a friend or teammate who overdosed. Distance or try to convert old family members. Have a conflict with another sect of characters.

    -=-=-=-=-=-=-=-

    The specific drugs told you your character type: acid, cocaine, heroin, ketamine, or meth. Drugs had an in-game effect, but also had side-effects listed for during use, long-term use, overdose (or just a really bad instance of use), and withdrawal. You had to go through withdrawal before you could retire a character.

    I was going to try using my old graphical character sheets idea as well. Oh well. Still on the back burner.

    The Illuminatus trilogy was a big inspiration.
