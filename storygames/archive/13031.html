\n So this week, our RP group did a Board Game Week.

So, I'm not an audio learner, I'm slow to read (audio), and when rules are read to me instead of shown through examples, I have to basically stab my leg with a pencil so as not to fall asleep.

So instead of explaining the rules or "cool points" of the game Long Shot with us, he simply whipped out his Macbook and showed us a Youtube video of a sort of play demonstration of the game:
http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uohh0Tp0xUQ

It's got a review tucked in there, but it explains all the basics of how the game works, as well as the cool points. He didn't cover the entire game, but most of it, and by the time the video was done I was ready to bust into the game and review the rest of the rules.

I wish more RPGs did this. Jason is doing it for some of his games, and there's the awesome video for Hell For Leather which shows the die column and all. But those are both done by the creators. I'd like to see more fans try to tackle basic RPG rules explanations over video/youtube for games that they like, even if its just the basics...

Huh. I guess this post is a "I would love to see someone else do this thing", with no real discussion beyond that. :-) But if anyone has any comments (Hell Yeah! No And Fuck You!) I'd like to see them.

-Andy

    \nI read an interestingNY times articlea few weeks ago that suggested a lot of conventional wisdom about the way people learn is wrong. The article mentions a psychologicaljournal articlewhich seems to claim that even though a lot of people believe in "learning styles" there's not a lot of experimental evidence to support it. I thought that was thought provoking, but I'm not sure there's any useful takeaway from it.

    I think on a case by case basis you can sometimes take things away. For instance, I was doing a survey and the result turned out that I was 50% kinesthetic, 25% aural and 25% visual (not exactly, but that's roughly the split). I thought that it was interesting, but that I potentially had a bias towards not sitting down and paying attention and maybe I just answered the questions that produced that result. Then there was an example they had that kinesthetic learners do their best thinking while moving around, it wasn't exactly that, but still something to that effect. I happen to do my best thinking for essays at 3AM pacing back and forth. Not sure what the 3AM has to do with it, but the pacing back and forth certainly fits. There isn't a huge deal to take away from that though, as I can learn pretty well by reading books and listening to lectures, although in both cases it depends on good books and good lectures, so perhaps that's the key - something has to be taught well, and that's more important than how it's taught.
