    hello, internet.

    allow me to introduce you to a little game that i've been working on for the past few years called "the Smoke Dream."

    the Smoke Dream website


    as you may have heard, a pre-final edition was available at GPNW02009. i still have a few copies of that,
    you can arrange to recieve one from me through the mail.
    email me at the.smoke.dream aatt gmail ddott com

    ...


    at this point the Smoke Dream is very intentionally a physical artifact. i have no plans to make a pdf version of the game in the near future.
    (though my partner has pointed out that a hypertexted version of the game would be useful)

    this is a card game that one plays with one's self. draw cards off the top of a normal deck of playing cards and look up their meanings in the
    book. some cards are rooms, some are items. some are people.
    discard is to the bottom of the deck, so encounters are in the same order. you never shuffle.

    you are permitted to read parts of the card write-ups depending on what items you are carrying and who your companion is.
    other parts are initially unexplored.

    tony called this game exploratory; that seems apt.


    if you are interested, reader, i would love your help. more than that, i would love your dedication; but i suppose that will have
    to be won. i am attempting to have the final version finished for next year. if you can spare it, i would ask $10.
    and your mailing address.


    thanks, Matthijs, for being interested, and thanks to my assistants who are already wandering the dream.

    -jackson

    \n I just played a second game a few minutes ago. The first game wasn't actually very interesting, or long, so I decided to play again, and I could not be happier that I did. Jackson, you've really managed to evoke the texture of a dream.

    In the end of my second game, I was coming to believe that The Hollow Lady had taken the Clockwork Heart, and, tragically Her own Heart, while The Witch of Brass and A Thief kept me from accumulating an inventory that would let me deal with The Hollow Lady to get my heart (or Hers) back. I kept running through the rooms, futilely searching for something, anything, to do, running into the unhelpful Faerie of Concord, tumbling down Halls of Mirrors, until... The Merchant King appeared, offering my Heart to me, seemingly out of nowhere (The Jack of All Trades had something to do with it). I had nothing to trade to him, but my companion fleeced him and presented me with my missing heart.

    I am just full of ideas about this game now \u2014 how it relates to Baker's recent explorations of IIEE, how well it conveys the flavor of dreaming, how a moderately complex mechanical device (the ordering of the deck) can provide state to a story game. And I can't wait to play it several more times \u2014 I still want to find out what happens to "that other person", what happens when A Venerable Unicorn is fully marked, and what The Boy with Unruly Hair does. I'm honestly not reading anything I haven't been instructed to read, because the revelation in-game is so enjoyable.

    And more, I can see new design space opening up for single-player story games.

    Posted By: hansel... I can't help but realize that the win condition for the game seems to not really have much at all to do with what I enjoy about play--namely, the discovery. I can try to angle for a bunch of cool interactions with cards and see what happens (this excites me), or I can try to get my heart back (this not as much), and the two don't really seem to have much to do with each other, at least in my play experiences.
    isn't that similar to the role of ending Roach with a high reputation and uninfested sinuses, as opposed to instigating and escalating a gonzo bloodbath? We agree totally, I think, on the relative importance of exploration and revelation vs "winning.". How much does it matter that the game asks you to pretend that you'd like to win and get your heart back? It is an interesting question.

    What I do like about having winning as a goal is the structure it gives to the emerging story, and the way it challenges you to hold off on finding your heart while you explore some facet of the game world. I intentionally got the heart out of the way in my third game, for instance, to develop the relationship with the wolf-blood girl. And I enjoyed the process of trapping the heart in a prism, sending it to the Thicket Edge... and finding it again when I found myself alone in the end. That's story material.
