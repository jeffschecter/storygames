    \n I guess what it's saying is that you shouldn't expect the mechanics as written in the book to 100% reflect everything that's established in the fiction.

    There's no specific mechanic for "When you fail a Defy Danger roll during combat, a monster can dislocate your shoulder. When your shoulder is dislocated, you do d4 damage for Hack and Slash instead of your normal damage, until you receive 6 points of healing and rest for at least one hour."

    Those details aren't spelled out in excruciating detail with mechanics. Instead, you establish fiction and work with what makes sense given the fiction as established.

    Which means that once the GM has established that your arm is dislocated, you can't just say "well, I swing my sword at the orc again" and roll Hack and Slash, because that doesn't make sense.

    Maybe you say "I slam the orc into the cave wall, leading with my shoulder, trying to pop my joint back into the socket and rough the orc up a bit in the process." And when you roll well, maybe the GM determines that you resolve the shoulder thing (but still have, perhaps, Weak for a while cause it's sore) and do 2 points of damage. Because that makes sense, given the fiction as established.

    \n It's not the loss of HP that allows the GM to say that, it's the fiction. If your character goes from 20 hp down to 3 hp after fighting some dudes, the GM can say "you're starting to feel faint from loss of blood. Are you going to do something about that?" And if you don't do something about it, then maybe you have to defy danger to not pass out.

    But this isn't because you are at 3 hp. If you're at 3 hp it's probably because you got stabbed a bunch of times, and if you got stabbed a bunch of times, you're probably bleeding, and if you're bleeding all over the place, well... it makes sense you'd pass out at some point, so that's an obvious hard move for the GM.

    Unless it's not obvious. If nobody remembers what injuries the characters have, then obviously they're not very important. If nobody cares, move on to other stuff. Or make up a new injury that the character didn't notice before because of adrenaline and tell them to deal with it, and consider whatever else happened to be inconsequential wounds.

    \n Zelbinian: First, I agree that HP is weird. It's counterintuitive that there is a number counting down to character death that only has a casual (if any) relationship to the physical condition of the character in the fiction. But, mechanically, that's how it seems to work. In our DW games, we decided that the loss of HP did not equal the loss of "health" or "life force" or "blood" or whatever; loss of HP just meant: "an increased likelihood that the next bad thing will result in death."

    So, in that sense, a character could roll a 6 and lose 8 HP without getting physically stabbed or clubbed or whatever -- the loss of 8 HP could have represented that the character was "shaken" or "off her game," and, therefore, more susceptible to leaving herself open to a deathblow. The converse was true too, if a character lost HP because he "lost an arm" that character might heal the HP, but the arm is still gone . . .

    I think it's useful to distinguish between the loss of HP and the fictional harms. When a GM reduces a character's HP, that means that, according to the mechanics of DW, the GM has raised the difficulty of getting out of the immediate dangerous situation alive, and thus the player might make decisions with the increased possibility of death in mind. If the GM causes a fictional harm, it means that the character has to react to the new fictional situation just like every other fictional situation the GM presents.

    Anyway, that worked for us.

    \n The way Harm works in AW is one of a handful of things that I really wish was better. It's too easy for it to become divorced from the fiction (see also: Barter). DW doesn't really solve this problem and, in some ways, exacerbates it slightly by making it more granular (i.e. you can generally take more HP damage than you can take Harm). In both cases, you can overcome this problem by emphasizing the fiction that goes with HP loss or Harm, but it takes additional effort and mindspace on the part of the GM that I would prefer to spend on something else. There have been a number of proposed custom moves for connecting Harm more closely with the fiction, and I'd love to see more people give that a shot, because I think it might improve AW hacks in general.

    I don't think it's DW's "fault" or anything that it didn't "fix" harm. One of the principles behind DW's design was to have it be understandable and semi-familiar to folks who were used to playing D&D, so having a HP-based system makes sense. Also, if you're playing in a semi-OSR style where the fiction matters some but, really, the characters are mostly avatars through which the players explore hazardous fantasy environments and probably die (see also: World of Dungeons), then measuring things in HP seems fine. But if you really want a gritty Burning Wheel-style thing where being ill or bleeding or injured matters a great deal (and you can be less ill/injured or more ill/injured), I think you may ultimately want mechanics that bring in that fictional context more. So... custom move?

      \n Here's an alternative way to handle hit points:\n

      When you resist harm, the MC will tell you what injury threatens your life. Roll+number of hit points you'd like to spend. One a hit, choose options. On a 13+, all of them. On a 10+, choose 2. On a 7-9, choose 1.
      * You can ignore this injury for now, it doesn't hamper you
      * You got lucky: the injury's not as big a deal as it seemed
      * You maintain your position or advantage, and can react immediately [optional: take +1forward]
      On a miss, you suffer the injury in full, right now.
      \nWhen Frodo gets "run through" by the troll in the Mines of Moria, but we later find out that he had a mithril shirt on and he's actually totally fine, that means he rolled 7-9 and chose "you got lucky" (but loses his position, can't react, and is seriously affected by the blow).

      This shows clearly that hit points are not a representation of how tough or how damaged you are, but something more intangible.

      Hint: If you spend at least 5 hitpoints on a roll, that danger can't kill you.

      \n He and I were talking about this and while he should probably post his actual takeaway, the brief answer was that (in Vincent's clouds-and-arrows terms) taking damage in *World involves the cloud and people and system sections, but that tracking damage is purely a system thing. Specifically that the cloud portion of the harm is different and totally disconnected from the mechanical in-system harm tracking and reduction mechanics. For example, if I cut off Hooch's arm with my machete (3 harm) and he then later heals the harm away, he's still down an arm. This is because while taking harm (me cutting off his arm) is both a cloud and system event, him regaining that harm is purely fiddling around with some numbers in system land and has no real connection to the state his arm is in.

      Similarly in Dungeon World, a blow that does less mechanical damage (removes fewer HP) but more in-ficion trauma can perfectly coexist with another blow that does more mechanical and less fictional damage as descriptive injuries are the in-game consequences of getting hurt and that hitpoints are purely tracking mechanics to death. I'm personally of the opinion that all games that have health tracking that is totally (or at least mostly) disconnected from any other in-game effects (examples: AW, D&D, nWoD, Feng Shui) work in the same way of divorcing fictional injury from system damage tracking and need some in-game description attached to the damage event in order to make the damage mean anything.
