\n This was a topic we received significant feedback about, and it probably needs its own thread for serious discussion on the topic.\n

Topic F: Kids & Adults

\n
F1If there are activities labeled adult it should be honored. That means no kids please.
\n
F2Suggestion: If kids above carrying/toddling age (5-14 yrs?) participate in Nerdly, have a designated track of games/activities for them, with designated (volunteer?) adults willing to supervise. (Adult areas = ADULT AREAS). Maybe Fred Wolke could do the kids' program/supervision, since its important to him? NOT a personal attack on Fred W. - he could be good at it & might feel more included.
\n
F3I love Camp Nerdly, but I wish it was either only for adults, or kids had their own space and were not allowed to play any of the adult games.
\n
F4I'd like the adult-only area to be more enforceable. I would like to suggest that either young toddlers or older (16+) kids be preferred. Grade school and tweenies not be preferred.
\n
F5I was very dissapointed by the lack of enforcement of _adult-only_ space. This was supposed to be (I thought) a grown-up vacation, but often I felt like the presence of children intruded on my play-space. If they could have their own kid-only activities that would help end the friction of younger kids always trying to intrude on adult games. Even if the parent gives them permission, I don't feel it is fair to those adults trying to run &/or enjoy their games. I am not trying to be anti-kid, but their presence made me uncomfortable and it didn't seem like the right kind of event to bring kids to. I would _very much_ prefer this be an age 16+ event (or higher).

      4> "This activity is specifically engineered to attract children and anyone who plays should expect to find them there."

      Should the first class of games be segregated away? Of course.

      I think we need labels for the second. Boffers should have been listed as such (and I should have recognized them as such, and informed the kids thereof).

      The third is, I think, the default, but I think some people would like the first to be the default, or possibly the second. Respond to me here.

      The fourth is necessary for the proper operation of the first two. That is, the best way to have an adults-only space is to pull the kids out of it, not push them out.

      (edit: Pushing them out should be a last resort)

      \n I know I wasn't there, but, as somebody who has thought about this a lot (enough to be involved in the creation of a book on the subject), I have a few things I'd like to share. Fred actually has more time under his belt by far with children than I, so, again, I hope that my conclusions aren't just hot air.
      \n
      \n
      \nFirst, there's a very good reason that Frank and Jason and others, and even myself, do not want to play RPGs with youngsters. Because we're not youngsters.
      \n
      \nNot to lean to much on Ron here, but in the Infamous Five one particular principle of social interaction emerged in particular. That one should play RPGs with those with whom they would socialize in other ways. Fred, would you expect that Jason would want to go out to a movie with your children? Or go out to dinner with them?
      \n
      \nThen why would you expect that he'd want to play with them?
      \n
      \nWhere RPGs go beyond being just tactical games, they are about the transmission of meaning between the participants. It's not just that Jason would be uncomfortable with having a scene in which a Nazi guard rapes a Polish girl... but that he'd have to worry that the child in question would think that he, perhaps, condones rape. With an adult he has the expectation that they are complex enough creatures to understand that, in fact, the rape scene is a condemnation of rape. Or there to look at some other very subtle issue that's not even something we can state clearly outside of play.
      \n
      \nThat's something for adults to discuss.
      \n
      \n
      \nWhich is not to say that people shouldn't play with children.
      \n
      \nBut one feels no trouble playing with their children because, in fact, one does have a social connection to them, as a parent. It's just that nobody else has this relationship to these people. Sans that relationship, it's unreasonable to expect anyone to want to play with the kids.
      \n
      \nOK, that's actually overstating the case. If, in fact, the kids get to know the other player as "Uncle Jason," then there might be the connection that allows there to be the impetus to play. Note that the confering of the honorific "aunt" or "uncle" on a non-family member is perhaps far more important than some people realize. It says that the member should be treated for most intents and purposes as family.
      \n
      \nNow, that said, does this mean that Jason in this case is now the friend of your children? No. No more than you, Fred, are your children's friend. I'm pretty sure, knowing you, that you've not fallen into that trap. And I would hope that Jason would not as well.
      \n
      \nSure, these are sophisticated youngsters. Adolescents, even. You would say, "When I was their age, we had rape in our RPG play." I know that my game did (though for awful reasons). But between youngsters, this is just peer testing, and getting to know the water. It's not the same as what we do when we play.
      \n
      \nSo, should Jason play with your kids? Yes, sure... but if so, if he gets to be Uncle Jason, then he should play as you should (and I believe do), with your kids. Meaning that you do so in a parent/child manner. Meaning that at all times the relationship is not one of peers, but one that is both pedagogical and, more importanly, a matter of being in the position of teaching moral values.
      \n
      \nThat is, between adults, we play to say, "This is how I feel about this value proposition... what do you think?" When we play with children, we should play to say, "This is how I feel about this value... don't you think that you ought to as well?"
      \n
      \nKids, children, youngsters, toddlers, adolescents... it all amounts to "non-adults." Adult, in this context, means "no longer sombody that we have the responsibility to teach, but who's opinions on value matters we respect." Until we can look at somebody, and know that they are an adult, playing with them is simply not the same thing. Should not be.
      \n
      \nNot to put too fine a point on it, we're NOT playing games here. Just because a historical artifact leaves the term "games" in the phrase role-playing games, doesn't mean that they're the same sort of activity as playing baseball. It's all too easy for we adults to forget that we're adults, and act like kids with respect to this.
      \n
      \nThere's one other form of relationship that's functional with kids. I'll get to that below.
      \n
      \n
      \nAll of you non-parents are cute. You want to have the kids do "crafts," make owlbear costumes, and have them play games about fairies. When I was playing D&D; as a youngster, I thought that the Wizard of Oz was crap, because it was all singing and dancing. I wanted to kill things, and listen to AC/DC. My son Alex went to school yesterday for costume day as "Tacky The Penguin" (http://www.amazon.com/Tacky-Penguin-Sandpiper-Books-Lester/dp/0395562333). He got home afterwards and declared himself too embarassed to put on the costume ever again. He's outgrowing "kid stuff."
      \n
      \nDid I mention that he's six years old? He'll be in first grade next year. I shall watch soon with pride as he graduates from Senior Kindergarten.
      \n
      \nLast night he stole out of bed to play Breath of Fire III on the ol' Playstation. He plays Lord of the Rings on his Gameboy, and worries about whether or not when playing Aragorn he should take two swords, or a sword and shield. When playing Warhammer Fantasy (I made the mistake of laying it on my bed where he could see it), he's not satisfied until he's aquired a lot of EXP from killing lots of baddies. And when I read the color descriptions from the book about kills (akin to the Rolemaster crit charts), he crows with delight.
      \n
      \nHe's seen my copy LotR trillogy on DVD about twice as much as I have. When he plays with his friend Josh, they play Lightsabres with the big plastic swords, and try not to hit each other too hard.
      \n
      \nEr... what I'm saying is that, if you want to make an entertaining game for kids... don't pansy it down. Put another way, if you want kids to be engaged, think of what would have engaged you at that age. It's OK to give them cool stuff to do. They want to do the same stuff that the adults are doing...
      \n
      \nWell, to the extent that they understand it, that is. They want to BE adults, you see.
      \n
      \nSo, here's the thing. If you want to run a "Kids Track" game, there's another sort of relationship you can establish with a child on the fly. Just like we can establish short-term relationships at conventions that allow for RPGs to be played fairly successfully between adults, we can form a short-term age-appropriate relationship with kids.
      \n
      \nAnd that's as "Day Care Person/Teacher." In that role, you are the authority, and, moreover, again, the child is not there to judge your values, but for you to teach them. Oh, it doesn't have to be heavy-handed preaching. It can be presenting them with tough moral dilemmas, and then telling the parent the response afterwards so that the parent can discuss it with their child. Or complex problem-solving. Just so long as you're not asking the child to participate in an open dialog on values between the two of you.
      \n
      \nBy the way, it's not nearly as fun as playing with adults. It feels more like work. That's not to say it's non-fun. But just to put it in perspective. If you're playing with kids and your main motive is to be entertained by it, I think you're out of line. The welfare of the kid should be your primary motivator.
      \n
      \nThis is the first thing I wanted to comment upon. I have another, weirder, idea that I'll pitch below.
