    \nThis would be my advice:

    Don't rely on this "just being D&D; everyone knows what that is!"

    Figure out what *your* game is all about (whether as an authoritarian GM, or by asking your friends what they like) and put it above-board, front and centre.

    Once you've got that nailed down, it becomes much easier (as a group) to fine-tune everything you're doing to hit that target.

    Otherwise, your "D&D" becomes the Rorschach Test of RPGs.

    \n I GMed the first session of Horde of the Dragon Queen...and there is a lot to talk about.

    Here is some context: Played with minis. 5 players in the party: Warlock, Cleric, Monk, Barbarian, Rogue. First session was spent making characters, figuring out the rules, introducing the scenario, fighting some battles, and everyone made it to level 2.

    First, I really wish inspiration was written more like it is in DW where you have a "when you do something" you get inspiration, rather than just a statement of belief or philosophy. I'm totally lost in handling it. I'm considering going through all of my player's BIFTs and re-writing them in the "when you do it, do it" fashion. What do you think?

    Second, the new death & dying rules are brilliant. Even in the first session we had an incredibly tense situation where the life of a PC hung in the balance of a single die roll. It was much more tense than usual because the player rolled a 1 on their death saving throw (it's a success based system where you make a DC 10 unmodified save each round you spend dying, if you make it to 3 cumulative successes first you live, if you make it to 3 cumulative fails first you die, on a 20 you can get up with 1 hp, on a 1 you get 2 failures). This system is incredibly simple and makes going down in combat dramatic as hell.

    Third, there was much confusion about charging and flanking. Our group spent at least 20 minutes hunting through the book for rules on these basic maneuvers from 3rd edition. Eventually I (temporarily) house ruled that charging is a +1 attack, -1 AC. With flanking I'm not so sure how I want to handle it. It's definitely something some of the players are interested in and not overly complex since we already use minis, so I'll need to come up with something.

    Fourth, character generation is fast and easy. Loving it. It takes about half the amount of time as 3rd edition and forces people to think about their character's beliefs and values more than just picking an alignment.

    Fifth, I'm enjoying the adventure design of HotDQ. It's highly modular and leaves lots of blanks for the GM to fill in. It doesn't seem to be heavy on railroading (yet, more commentary on this to come as the campaign progresses).

    Overall, it was a great experience and it seemed like my players had a lot of fun as well.

    \n It's really more like TIBFs than BIFTs, but BIFTs is probably easier to say out loud. It stands for (Personality) Traits, Ideals, Bonds, and Flaws. You get 2 traits (one positive, one negative) one ideal, one bond, and one flaw. Unless I missed it, there are no rules so far for changing them in the course of play.

    It really is too bad that (unless I missed it) the Player's Handbook didn't include better advice about writing these traits. In Story Game circles, we have years of accumulated experience and best practices from games like Burning Wheel (BITs) and Dungeon World (bonds). For example, there are no examples in the new Player's Handbook about having your bond be to another PC. Whereas for someone who's played TSOY or Dungeon World, that would be an obvious choice. If everyone creates characters together, as @Deliverator suggested, you can go even further. For example, if my flaw is "I hate it when anyone tries to control me," your flaw could be "I'm a control freak." But making characters together is, in my experience at least, not necessarily a common practice in most D&D groups. In most of the groups I've personally played in, everyone kind of creates characters separately, and the characters come into the game with no relationship with each other whatsoever.

    Also, a lot of the examples in the book are just pretty weak. I don't have it in front of me right now, but having a bond to your father's axe is a lot less interesting than having a bond to your NPC father, which is in turn less interesting than a bond to your PC brother. You'd think that a book called the "Player's Handbook" might include better advice about creating interesting, dynamic characters who work well with the other characters in the group. But, alas. I wonder whether the designers really have a solid grasp of how to make these mechanics work well, and just didn't do a great job of explaining it--or whether they just kind of don't get it. Even the combination of traits, ideal, bond, and flaw seems a little bit arbitrary compared to more tightly designed mechanics like BITs in Burning Wheel (Beliefs, Instincts, and Traits all do different things in BW, whereas TIBFs all do the same thing). In conclusion, I love that D&D now has a story-game-like mechanic, but I worry that it seems kind of weak and poorly implemented.

    I would love to have a more detailed, analytical discussion of the Inspiration mechanics in D&D--perhaps that would merit a separate thread?

      \n As for inspiration.
      I\u2019ve seen a range of interpretations from
      \u201cIf the characteristics lead you or your party into trouble, you get inspiration\u201d
      to
      \u201cIf you are a \u2018good dog\u2019 and does what the DM wants you to do, you get inspiration\u201d

      I prefer the former.
      There is this meme going around my local area about \u201cpoodle point\u201d game mechanics \u2014 I think Rickard (who posts here) originally formulated the critique, or have I confused it with someone else \u2014 game mechanics that feel like a \u201cdog show\u201d. When I heard it, the first example that came to my mind was GURPS 3e, where the GM should give out points \u201cfor good roleplaying\u201d. \u201cJudging\u201d party games mechanics like in Apple to Apple or Say Anything also catch my ire and is part of why I love Dixit so much more.
      Of course, there is still going to be judgement calls. But it\u2019s easier to make judgement calls about the more specific and precise
      \u201cDid you follow a characteristic and it had negative consequences for your character or the group\u201d
      vs a more general superset of
      \u201cDid you stay true to your character\u201d.
      I\u2019m not an acting judge, but I think I can tell (most of the time) when someone is making a choice in spite of so-called \u201cmeta game benefits\u201d.

      When I DM:d it last friday, it was with a group new to me at a small local con. One of the players was a heavy \u201cstory-gamer\u201d (I think they read this forum occasionally) whose favorite games are all powered by the Apocalypse, but who wasn\u2019t familiar with 5e yet. That player looked at their flaw \u201cDespite my best efforts, I\u2019m still unreliable to my friends\u201d and asked me and the group: \u201cAbout playstyle \u2014 how much focus is there on \u2018completing the quest\u2019? That\u2019s to say, is it frowned upon to follow this trait, or should we play on them hard?\u201d

      It felt great to be able to answer: \u201cFirst of all, there is no \u2018quest\u2019. Just explore the world. Second, as for the flaw, play it hard. You\u2019ll get compensated with advantages later\u201d and I explained the inspiration rule and ended up with \u201cThe game rewards you for being true to your character\u201d. Everyone ended up playing hard to their TIBFs/characteristics/BIFTs and the inspiration was flying and I as DM had a great time.

      One of the first times inspiration was awarded, this player shouted \u201cHello\u201d into a dungeon (leading into them getting attacked), claimed it as a use of \u201cDespite my best efforts, I\u2019m still unreliable to my friends\u201d and asked for inspiration, which I awarded. It\u2019s still a judgement call between following that particular flaw vs general stupidity \u2014 even an unreliable person might be cautious in the face of a dangerous and weird looking cave (they had found skulls), and even a usually reliable person might make that mistake for the sake of curiosity \u2014 but it\u2019s clear that it\u2019s a choice made with the purpose of being true to the characteristics rather than the purpose of \u201cquest success\u201d.

      Also I wanted the inspiration to get going, and it worked. If one player is doing it, more will start doing it.
      5e is cementing itself as my favorite game.

      \n Honestly I *hate* the "XP for monsters and for jumping through the GM's hoops" thing. It's the worst part of 5e, hands down. The DMG will have some alternatives but won't be available for a few months.

      Here is my suggestion. Ditch the 5e XP system entirely and replace it with something closer to Dungeon World's.

      At the end of the session, answer the following questions:

      -Did the group defeat a significant threat?
      -Did they find out something new about the world?
      -Did they acquire a worthwhile treasure / magic item?

      For each "yes," everyone gets 1 XP.

      Then, individually:
      -Did you resolve one of your BIFTs? If yes, get 1 XP and rewrite that BIFT to reflect the new fictional situation.

      Level up every... 7 XP or so seems about right, eyeballing it, if you're okay with people leveling up once every two-four sessions.

      There are ways of making this basic framework more interesting. For example, you could make the XP for defeating a threat (which would include outsmarting it, BTW) = its CR, and then have escalating costs for leveling up (probably just 7/8/9...). You could also add, "Did you make a new enemy which will be a persistent threat going forward?" And make that worth CR+1. That incentivizes the PCs not to one-shot the villain. :-)
