    Posted By: Luke Wheel
    Do you think your elitist ignorance is typical of our sect?
    \nWhat does this even mean, seriously Luke?

    Ben was at a huge Portland anime con that has recently been trying to push its tabletop aspect harder. I know about Kumoricon in particular, because the gaming leads hunted me down as well as a bunch of others who produced anime-style RPGs. Jake Richmond ended up going and running Maid as well as his anime-themed games (presumably Panty Explosion, Land of Yeld, etc). In his words, "The response was enormous. If you brought a few dozen copies of your game, you would have sold out by the end of the con, absolutely."

    This is something I've been thinking about for years, but recent movement at local anime cons solidified it: If you have an anime-themed gaming product, and make your target market the existing roleplaying community, rather than the much larger anime community, you're doing yourself a disservice.

    Anime cons are usually filled with people who love the medium and are looking for something new. They're also looking for something to do, which isn't something most anime cons have: You can go to the dealer's hall to look at manga you already have and videos you already watched (at store prices); the big sellers are ones that produce interesting goods you can't normally find in stores. You can go to the anime viewing rooms to watch anime you already have seen. You can go to con panels to watch people talk about what they feel when they do the voice-overs for Panty Princess. You can meet your online friends. You can watch the cosplayers. That's about it. Just observing, not doing.

    The events that feature *actual things to do* tend to get extremely popular and nuts: Karaoke, Guitar Hero/DDR, Video Games, etc. Tabletop RPGs tend to fall into this category as well: The anime cons that feature a TRPG area tend to overflow with anime gamers, or more importantly anime fans who are also interested in gamers.

    From a merchant standpoint, that's a huuuuuuge target market, far more a target than aiming to run anime-themed games at a general gaming con (especially one that appears to be 90% electonic/console-focused). From a social point of view, again if you have an anime-themed game, anime cons will be a far more receptive audience.

    If you're trying to run Sorcerer or carry or Burning Wheel or Polaris or whatever, then yeah probably an event like PAX would be good for that sort of thing (if they don't run out of tickets before the event, yikes). But if you're trying to run Panty Explosion, Exalted, Bliss Stage, Maid or other anime-themed games, it would be ridiculous to not consider existing anime fans as a rich pool of interest in your product (and I don't mean just a "potential sales mark", but rather a huge - HUGE - community that is potentially more interested in your game than the fewer folks interested in playing anime-themed games in a general gaming convention.

    It would be like passing up a small RPG con in the next town over (given the percentage of folks interested in tabletop vs console) in order to run Mouse Guard at Comicon. But then being asked why you're elitist for not going to the "gamer con" instead.

    So how is aiming at a target market/community elitist now?

    \n Luke isn't wrong about PAX. I went to PAX this year specifically to scout it out as an alternative to Gen Con. I only spent a few hours this year because I can't spend much money and time on an unproven convention. What I saw made me very happy.

    The Good:
    Even though the convention floor was stuffed with video games, off the floor I saw board games and RPGs happening everywhere.
    Luke's Beyond D&D session attracted a large crowd (sorry I had to leave early)
    The Dreaming game store booth looked plenty busy and was full of indie RPGs.
    At PAX the convention floor space and the social space are much more closely integrated than at Gen Con, and there's a more generally people-friendly feeling to the spaces outside of the exhibit hall, included places for informal socializing and resting.

    The Bad:
    The convention floor is ridiculously loud. I had to scream to talk to the guy demo-ing his game for me because the next booth over had a full sound stage set up with the volume cranked to 11. WTF?
    It's not particularly kid friendly.
    It's a crowded con and the convention hall is dark and maze-like.

    I wouldn't sell PAX as a replacement for Gen Con. It's its own thing again, and in some ways potentially better than Gen Con. With the exhibit hall and play spaces being so close together, it would be incredibly easy to have a booth selling games in conjunction with a room where you could go play them. In fact, I think that's more or less what the Dreaming folks were doing already. Do I have that right?

      Considering a good third of this site seems to rotate around the question of "Why aren't more people doing this?" or "How do we get more people to do this?"
      \nI think there's a gap Jared.

      When people talk about how to get SG games out there, they do so for different reasons:

      - Those who design games want more people to buy and play their games.
      - Those who design games AND those who invest in the "SG" identity, want the games to be recognized. This is the main issue I believe, the "get the recognition they deserve, and by proxy, so do I, who self-identifies".
      - They think the games are fun, and want to help more people have fun (often assuming that they do not have fun now, even if implicitly).
      - They want there to be more people with whom to discuss the games they enjoy talking about. And for some people, they need more people to play these games with.

      For most people, aside from the recognition issue, things are ok as they stand: They have enough people to talk to about the games (here), they have enough friends to play the games with, and they're not going to get more money or direct pleasure out of it.
      It's often a purely academic question.

      I think a better question would be, "NW designers, why weren't you at PAX?"
      The people who speak otherwise of SG getting recognition and traction, it's as much idle curiousity as it is wishing the success of an endeavour they have vested personality (not personally) in. There are several issues being conflated here.
