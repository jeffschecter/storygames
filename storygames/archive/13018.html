    \n I'd think it would be okay to use a soundtrack, but it's likely to be far less personal. It strikes me as being analogous to naming your wizard Gandalf - it's likely to be superficial fun, but the game repays deeper investment with significantly greater rewards. Also, when you say "Gandalf," you have a whole set of associations that go with that, just as when you hear the soundtrack to a road movie, you think of the events of that road move.

    Summary of the summary: If your friend isn't interested in assembling his own mix, it's probably a poor choice of games for him. But it won't break the game if he does that.

    Posted By: cwilburthe game repays deeper investment with significantly greater rewards.
    \nThis is true. Karina, your friend is being given an sort of rare opportunity. The entire group is going to create art out of their favourite music. That's cool!
    I'd definitely try to push said friend a little bit. Making a 40-minute mix isn't hard, and no one's expecting these mixes to be earth-shatteringly good.

    Making a mix is part of how you buy in, emotionally. It's a step that can be skipped, and using a soundtrack from a movie is just fine, but it's not ideal.\n

    \nYeah, exactly! Go back to a previous CD. If one didn't get lots of playtime (because of a Detour), that one might be the best one to return to. Otherwise, just return to the first one used. Start where you left off, or if you can't remember where you left off, start at the beginning.

    I just want to note, also, in regards to Obstacles: you don't need Obstacles in every scene. I usually encounter 1-5 Obstacles across an entire game of Ribbon Drive. The games with 1-2 Obstacles are very much slow personal dramas. The games with 3-5 Obstacles are a bit more punctuated and upbeat. The games with more than 5 Obstacles tend to be zany and off-the-wall.
