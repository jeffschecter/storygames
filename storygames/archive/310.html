    In the context of Story Games, I'm not sure there's a good reason for task resolution.

    For some forms of "sim" (modulo my understanding and or the legitimacy of the concept), task resolution is useful, as others have said, because it supports the idea of "being there". That is, we can view achieving any complex goal as the product of a series of tasks. Our intentions form the goal, but our intellect guides our manipulation of the external world in an effort to achieve the goal.

    Task resolution represents a viewpoint of emergent reality constrained by the physical world. Conflict resolution by contrast is the direct manipulation of metaphysical ideals.

    Task resolution is Nominalism, conflict resolution Realism.

    I think it all comes down to immersion. Droog hit on it with "Being There" in the first response but I don't think he really made the point, so I'm gonna try.

    Premise 1: When you're playing in an immersive mode, Director stance blows it for you. You want to be "I" and not be playing "my guy" or "that guy."

    Premise 2: Conflict resolution requires Director stance. To think about what you, the player, want out of the situation, you must step outside the character and think at a macro level.

    Conclusion: Conflict resolution kills immersion.

    Conflict resolution does not lend itself to translating the wishes of the player in an immersive Actor stance as well as task resolution. To resolve a situation with conflict resolution, the player first must step back, determine what he (the player, not the character) wants to occur, then roll, then translate back into immersive terms. The back and forth transition derails immersion.

    Task resolution, on the other hand, relates directly to what characters are doing, even if it doesn't matter to the story or the players. There's less translation. You, as immersive player, act through your character in the game-world. Someone else translates the action into dice. You roll. Someone else interprets the dice and translates that into game-world results. You don't break immersion except to toss a D20 or whatever. If the game is like Dogs and it requires you to play poker dice or something very-much-not-related to your character, that blows your immersion, too.

    Really, it requires a certain amount of hubris to say that rolling to see if you get drunk doesn't matter to the players. They obviously think it matters, or they wouldn't do it. Maybe you can't fathom enjoying that game, but as long as they're having fun, cool.

    (I'm actually not an immersive player, but I understand them, I think.)

    OK, I'm going to try answering Shreyas' example here. I think concrete examples are good here, because I think when talking about broad categories (like Task vs Conflict), there's a lot of potential for miscommunication.

    Shreyas Sampat wrote:
    We have two characters, Ethan and Julia, at high school.

    The facts of their social situation are that Julia can openly argue with teachers, and they will engage her in argument. Even if she advances some ridiculously flawed argument, the teacher will respond to it as though they are engaged in a debate - that is, they will pick it apart logically. There aren't disciplinary consequences for her.

    Whereas, Ethan's situation is such that he'll get sent to detention, regardless of the value of the point he makes.

    How do I represent this in a task resolution system?
    Well, the obvious thing that leaps to mind for me is a Reputation modifier. I believe this originally appeared in GURPS, but it has been imitated in many games since then. In GURPS this would be something like "Reputation: trouble-maker -6 (14-) to teachers". The limited category of the reputation reduces it's value. The result is that the game mechanics are that the teacher is hostile to Ethan. (That is, there are a set of relation states like Friendly, Neutral, etc. The reputation downgrades that.) The hostile state is a directive for how the GM should have the NPC behave, and I believe a modifier for social skill rolls.

    Note, I'm not arguing you should like this, but rather I'm answering how I would approach this in a Task Resolution system.

    It's not perfect, but it's not terrible. And to be fair, I'd also be stuck as to how to represent this in many Conflict Resolution systems. For example, The Pool and Dogs don't have negative traits -- a relationship or reputation always helps you succeed. If his player makes a conflict of it, there's a good chance that Ethan won't get sent to detention.

    Instead, let's say "Stakes Resolution." Much easier to define and understand, without getting all confused about stances, scale, scene, and other stuff that has nothing to do with it.

    Stakes Resolution: Before we roll the dice, we come to agreement about what the outcome will mean for the fiction. In other words, "We set stakes, roll, and abide by the outcome." That's all there is to it.

    "My guy climbs the cliff."
    "Okay, make a climbing roll with a +1 bonus from your rope and spikes. If you win, you get to the top without taking a fatigue level. If you lose, you take one fatigue level per failure level."
    "Okay."
    <roll>

    That's Stakes Resolution. So is this:

    "My guy climbs the cliff."
    "Okay, make a climbing roll with a +1 bonus from your rope and spikes. If you win, you make it 20 feet farther up the cliff. If you fail, you fall all the way back to the bottom, and take damage."
    "Okay."
    <roll>

    Notice how SR has nothing to do with stance, scale, immersion, or specific mechanics.

    This is NOT Stakes Resolution:

    "My guy climbs the cliff."
    "Okay, make a climbing roll with a +1 bonus from your rope and spikes."
    <roll>
    "Success!"
    "You climb 20 feet and are blocked by an outcropping. Roll again at -1."

    Some people call the above thing "Task Resolution" which is also problematic. I prefer to simply say, "Not resolving stakes."

    So, to put my own spin on Joshua's question... Is there any reason to NOT resolve stakes?

    So far, for me, the only good answer is Ben's: To provide color. Otherwise, I have to say no.

If I rescue the princess from an abusive argument with her father, and suddenly the king has my lands siezed and me banished then I don't have any sense of whether this is because:

      • Butting into the argument was legitimately a huge, huge source of social damage, or
      • I'd been riding the ragged edge of disaster for some time, and didn't know it until I dropped the straw on the camel's back.

      In fact, in the past, I've been known to argue that the Rolemaster way is the right way to climb a cliff or cross a river, with detailed maneuvers. Because if you're going the detailed route for combat and if crossing the river is at all important in the frist place, then you should go the detailed route for crsossing it as well.

      What I was doing, before I had the tools to do it, is try to promote crossing the river and climbing the cliff into conflict resolution, because they were Really Damned Important.

      > some players get annoyed that they have to roll that stuff

      They do, and again, depending on the (circumstances in the) game, I am one of those players. If it doesn't matter at all, why roll? The GM (if there is one) should just decide what works best or what makes more sense, and go with it, because it's Not Important At All.

      Thing is, there is a relatively high number of circumstances where what happens next falls somewhere between Really Damned Important and Not Important At All. And that's where Task Resolution comes in.

      --

      Additional point: Task Resolution is hard to nail as a mechanic, way harder than Conflict Resolution. And that means, often times, TR comes out broken. And because the stuff being resolved with it is Important, disputes ensue.

      Depending on the game and the group, those disputes and rules discussions should simply be considered part of the game.

      This, of course, irks immersionists no end, and is, I believe, one of the sources of the role-player-vs-roll-player connundrum.

      Cheers,
      J.

      Maneuver Resolution. Yeah, that's pretty nifty.

      However! I don't think MR has a "pre-defined result". Because if you have agreed to the result before the roll... well, you're resolving stakes. :-)

      Remember, this is stakes resolution:
      "If you roll a 25 or better, you hit and do 2d8 damage."

      So, maneuver resolution would be:
      "Roll to hit. DC 25."

      "That would take a climbing roll. +2 for your rope."

      "Make a stealth roll."

      There's no agreement before the roll about what is being resolved (no stakes, in other words). It's just a roll "to see if you do it." The consequence of the action is not explicit and agreed-upon beforehand.

      John, I'm talking about something that pretty much splits TR into two things.
