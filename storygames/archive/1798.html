I finally finished Legends of Alyria!

greatwolf
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Directed Promotion
I will admit to underhanded promotional desires with this post. I will admit that I'm hoping to attract some business from my fellow denizens of Story Games. But, besides that, I just wanted to tapdance happily and note that, after eight years, there's finally a print version of Legends of Alyria available to the world. You can get it from Lulu.

Who might be interested in this game? Folks who are into slightly strange fantasy settings. If you liked The Shadow of Yesterday or Hero Wars, you might be interested in Legends of Alyria.

Also, Legends of Alyria was an inspiration for both Universalis and Polaris. This is a weird fact, but it is true.

But, even if you aren't in the market for another game, I just wanted to share the joy. After such a long time, I'm finally finished.

Seth Ben-Ezra

    How long was this sucker in development, from the first "hey, this is a cool idea" to book?
    Hmm, let's see. If I'm remembering correctly, I remember beginning to do work on the game sometime in fall 1998. So, just counting time elapsed, that's eight years.

    Reality is that development was fitful. The basic rules set was locked down by 2001-2002. and the current rules simply formalize stuff that I was already doing informally. The biggest change was the "tension level", which sets the number of "successes" that you need to win a conflict. I was doing this sort of thing informally during development, but it's better to have it formally established.

    Most of the delay was in writing the manuscript. This is mostly the result of Real Life interfering, but also I was trying to figure out how to explain Narrativism without talking about Narrativism. How do you teach the creation of a storymap without actually being there? Hopefully I succeeded.

    I wasn't just being an artsy flake with the use of symbols, though. Removing the numbers from the game actually has helped people who don't like math to enjoy the game. I know that this sounds silly, but one of my sisters really grooved on the use of symbols for just that reason. You can easily grok "more" or "less" by comparing the symbols without ever thinking about numbers. In addition, this method allowed for a bell curve distribution for dice rolling while still using one die.

    There is also the artsy flake reasons for it. To this day, when I go outside at night and look up at the moon, I think of Alyria.

    And that's why, when I play the game, I'll still be using the original moon phases. I was true to my word and didn't publish with moon phases, but my personal games will still use them.

    You'll have to forgive me for my ignorance - I've head of LoA, but know virtually nothing about it. Care to clue me up a bit more, whilst we've got you here talking about it? What's it about? What does it do?

    What's cool?
    Ha! A "Sell me" request! Can't pass that up.

    Legends of Alyria is a post-apocalyptic science fantasy game. One of the major influences is Gene Wolfe's Book of the New Sun, if you're familiar with that, crossed with Lord of the Rings and Final Fantasy VII. Honest.

    So, first, a thumbnail sketch of the setting. Alyria is a gas giant that was colonized a thousand years ago by refugees from Earth, now known as the Progenitors, who were fleeing an alien menace known as the Outsiders. They built great cities, roads, and other such things, including terraforming machines to create the breathable atmosphere. But the Progenitor society was overthrown. The dragons that live deep in the atmosphere of the planet discovered the Progenitors and appeared to some of them, offering power in exchange for sacrifice. Then, when the moment was right, the dragons and their cultists struck. Along the way, they released a biological weapon known as the ripper plague, which was a powerful mutagen. The overthrow of this primal society is now known as the Rape. This cataclysm shaped the current face of Alyria.

    So now Alyria looks quite different. On the political front, there are two "superpowers" of sorts. The first is the Citadel, which is the archtypical dystopian city, made from iron, and ruled by the five High Lords. The second is the Ark, which Andy already mentioned. On the religious front, there are the Keepers, who worship the god Pheric, who is really the computer that controls the terraforming machines. There are also the dragon cultists, who still worship the dark powers. Also there are the Chosen, who work with the Unicorns to oppose the dragons and their slaves. The ripper plague produced various mutations. Most of them are lumped under the category of Misbegotten. Some have developed raw psionic powers called the Blessing, which enable them to wield tremendous power which also destroys their bodies.

    Oh yes, and recently, the Weeping Moon rose over the horizon. This red moon is the harbinger of doom, for the Outsiders have finally found Alyria.

    There's a lot more going on in the setting. For example, I haven't even touched on the Web (my personal favorite), but it should be enough for you to get a feel for what is going on.

    System-wise, the game is pretty straightforward. Each character has three Attributes (Force, Insight, and Determination) that set his basic abilities. However, during conflict, these can be overridden by a Trait, which are various moral characteristics of the character. These Traits can be used both for and against the character. So, for example, a character with the "Honest" trait might be able to use it in his favor when trying to earn someone's trust, but he might have it turned against him when another character is trying to lie to him. As a result of their choices, particularly in conflicts, characters earn points in Inspiration and Corruption, which are used to buy and modify Traits, attempt "auto-win" in conflicts, and the like.

    Thus, the focus of the game is on the moral choices that the characters must make..

    To play the game, you first have a brainstorming session, similar to the pitch session for PTA. During this session, you construct a storymap, which is a relationship map variation, showing the various characters in the story and defining their connections to each other. This is done before characters are assigned to players. So everyone gets into the act, doing the "prepwork" that a GM normally does. Once this is done, characters are handed out and play begins. To be clear, all the major characters are played by players.

    In a lot of ways, many of the techniques in Legends of Alyria have already entered the world in other forms. I mentioned the pitch session from PTA as being one. The general idea that PvP gaming can actually produce good stories is an idea that has earned a lot of traction in the last several years. The storymap concept is present in the Polaris cosmos (in part). Now, I'm not claiming that anyone stole anything from me. Rather, I'm saying that, when I was doing most of my design work, these ideas were fairly new and radical, even though they may seem fairly normal now.

    I actually do have the text of Alyria online, although I'm not sure exactly what I'm going to do with it, now that I have a print version available. So, if you're wanting to do more investigation, you can have a peek over there. And, of course, I'd be more than happy to answer further questions from folks, either here or in my forum on the Forge.

    Thanks for all the interest!

    Seth Ben-Ezra

    To put some perspective on things, Ralph and I were interested in Alyria, and actually got to play in one of the early playtests at Origins along with Jason Blair (in which I laughed so hard at one point I fell off my chair). We were writing that one of the innovative things about the game was how it had no map.

    Still no map, right Seth? Like Tim says, today that seems like "So what?" At the time the idea of a setting like this having no map was revolutionary.

    So Ralph and I were thinking together, "what if we made a game in which the idea was that the players created the entire map by making skill rolls based on the characters abilities?" That was the birth of what would eventually become Universalis.

    And that's how influential Alyria has been, and how revolutionary it was at the time. No map. Can you imagine that? :-)

    Mike

    Nope! No map! When I recently saw that Dictionary of Mu doesn't have a map, I thought of my early efforts. Ah, memories....

    And, as Mike says, the map discussion is what launched Universalis. You can see that thread here. Notice the discussion between Ralph and Mike. That's where Universalis started. Pretty cool, huh?

    That Origins demo was great. I remember thinking that our session had a distinctly Little Fears vibe, but I think that Little Fears rubbed off on lots of stuff that year. The Bag actually made it into the manuscript, too. For those of you who don't know, The Bag is essentially the One Ring crossed with a Bag of Holding. Reach into the Bag, make a wish, and pull out what you wished for. The problem, of course, is that whatever you wished for will actually harm you. And The Bag has a mind of its own....

    Oh, about being "influential". Recently, I've been reading a lot of detective novels of the noir persuasion. I'm thinking here of Raymond Chandler, a little Dashiell Hammett, and now I'm starting in on Ross MacDonald. I recommended one of these books to my wife, who began reading it. When I asked her what she thought so far, she said that the author seemed to be trying too hard to evoke that classic "noir" atmosphere. I laughed and told her that she was reading the actual classic "noir". Suddenly the book didn't seem cliche anymore. It wasn't an homage to an older time; it was that older time.

    That's what I feel with Legends of Alyria. I look at it now and think, "Man, these ideas are so done by now." But, at the time, they were revolutionary...if I can say that without sounding arrogant.
