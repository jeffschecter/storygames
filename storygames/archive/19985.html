Are Gamers Used Gum?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nwhduryea \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJanuary 2015 \n in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Lately, there have been a buch of attempts to find useful metaphors to describe our experiences as gamers, the different kinds of games we play, the different varieties of gaming cultures, etc.

Personally, I think a gamers are like discarded wads of chewing gum. The games that we play are just whatever filth and detritus happened to be there when we were spit on the floor, while we we still sticky and pliable. Some of us were dropped on the floor of hobby shops after Friday Night Magic and Saturday afternoon Pathfinder was in full swing before someone found us, scraped us up, and drop us in the trash on top of a stack of unsold copies of some third tier X-Man's solo series. Others were dropped on the sidewalk between a used clothing store and an independent theatre showing The Brother from Another Planet and ended up stuck to the sole of a pair of vintage combat boots. I was wadded up inside a newspaper article on the Boston Marathon Bombing and dropped inside a used needle return.

Regardless of where we came from, now most of us are hardened and calcified and nothing new will stick to us, so the only thing left for us to do is to try to make sense of the garbage that is already there. Surely it's there for a reason, right? Our tastes have to be cohesive; they have to point towards meaningful categories or taxonomies, or else shit would just be random, and we would just be sad and gross.

    \n The implicit question, though: what about the pieces of gum that were used for, I don't know, art installations or jewelry? Even if we subscribe to a model of acculturation where human development stultifies early and geeks continue obsessing over their childhood nostalgia forevermore, that doesn't mean that everybody's damage is equal and equally meaningless. We could posit a gamer who was intentionally or accidentally exposed even-handedly to varied, noble and significant influences, causing them to calcify into a pleasing and useful form.

    Of course each gamer (or human in general - surely gaming's no different from any other cultural influences) would be personally unable to judge who's a jewel and who's just a gross mastication product, but as long as we're presupposing that used gum is gross (to some outside observer, presumably), we're clearly also capable of assuming that non-gross things may potentially exist. Thus, some might be more equal in their sad grossness than others, even if we know not which ones.

    I appreciate the humility inherent to the metaphor, though, and agree with the idea that early influences do much to shape aesthetic language - what humans find compelling.
