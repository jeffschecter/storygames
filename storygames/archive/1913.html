CRANIUM RATS (Underkoffler Partial Remix)

WHAT IS THIS GAME?
This game is all conflict, all the time. There is no room for
cuddly feelings, unless you use them to overcome your friends.
There is no room for not owning up when the power to set the
stage is yours.

Gameplay is aimed at duplicating the underlying psychological
motivations behind furious, interwoven storylines that begin to
criss-cross more and more and eventually culminate together, as
seen in movies like Lock, Stock, and Two Smoking Barrels,
Snatch!, Layer Cake, and Pulp Fiction.

In this game, players do NOT play individual characters; they
play the psychological Aspects of several characters: the
â€œCranium Rats.â€ The Cranium Rats control these characters,
pulling and pushing them around in their struggle to achieve
their Goals. The players -- as Cranium Rats -- take turns
narrating the actions and events of each character, focusing on
the particular Aspect and Goal they are embodying for that
player.

With players often taking control of the same character, play is
all about conflict: internal and external. Cranium Rats games
can be deeply psychological, high-octane action, or ironically
macabre.

Cranium Rats works best with at least 3 or 4 players.

THE ASPECTS

[ INSERT triangle of aspects; place "Enlightened" in center of
triangle ]

Each player will play all three Aspects (one Dirt, one Rat and
one Water), but divided over three characters. It will result in
each character having the three Aspects, but a different player
will play each.

A fourth player plays the Enlightened for all the characters in
the game, and serves as a referee, mediator, or arbitrator in
cases of game-stopping conflict or confusion.

ALTERNATELY, each player can play all FOUR Aspects (one Dirt,
one Rat, one Water, and one Enlightened), but divided over FOUR
characters.

Dirt
Dirt is the ant. It is the part that on one hand builds great
monuments, for society, for itself, but on the other hand is a
faceless peon. Dirt is the mother and father. It is that which
care for continuity, for the societyâ€™s well being. You return to
dirt from whence you came; dirt is the â€œhuman experienceâ€. Dirt
is the effort you put into it to draw nourishment from it. Dirt
is the effort you put into building society and its structures
from it. Dirt is where you inter your forebears for safekeeping,
into which you hope to be interred in the future.

Dirt is Society. Dirt is slow and gradual, stable growth.
Dirt is what most humans claim to be or wish to be seen as on
the surface.

Dirt is Organization.
Dirt is Cooperation.
Dirt is Thought.


Rat
Rat is the darkness behind your eyes. It is every animal or gut
instinct that you have and which needs suppression or heed. Rat
is the sex and the violence. It is that which you wish you could
be, the power that guides you, if youâ€™d let it. The urge to
reproduce, yhe urge of survival. saving you and yours. Mindless
actions. The need to prove yourself; your foot on your fallen
enemyâ€™s chest. The way in which fear transmits like a disease in
a crowded rat-den, the pheromone control we exert and that is
exerted upon us. Rat is what most humans draw their strength
from but wish to repress.

Rat is the Individual. Rat is fast and explosive; Rat often
destroys. Rat is what most humans hide from others.

Rat is Division.
Rat is Initiative.
Rat is Urges.

Water
Water is the mirror. Its shape and behaviour change as you act
in different manners toward it. Water is constant. Try to tell
the mighty river to not flow. Put a stone in the path of the
brook and it will simply flow over or to the side. Water changes
and is changed by the environment.
Water is the need to keep on moving, to shape environment and be
shaped by it. It is the need to escape those who will restraint
you, the need to push others into conforming with you.

Water is the Moment. Water is changeable and flowing; Water
creates and destroys. Water is how most humans appear to
others.

Water is Change.
Water is Movement.
Water is Reaction.


The Enlightened
The Enlightened is the world and all within it, the Enlightened
is the Godhead, but he is also the illusion that keeps those who
still sleep within it. The Enlightened is to be impartial; which
is to say, he is equally against all the Aspects. He is rooting
for the characters, for if they reach Enlightenment is gives him
joy, that other throw off the shackles that he did before. He
doesnâ€™t help an Aspect that fell behind, heâ€™s merely hindering
the one ahead.

The Enlightened is the Future. The Enlightened is the ultimate
goal. The Enlighted is the sum of all things. The Enlightened is
unseen and unknown to most humans.


ASPECT RELATIONS & TRAITS
Aspects do not exist in a void; they interact with one another.
As such, certain relationships between them emerge.

These relationships also give rise to the basic Traits of
individual characters.

Dirt and Water give a character their Physical trait (Clay).
Dirt and Rat give a character their Mental trait (Personality).
Rat and Water give a character their Social trait (Imeptus).

As the strength of Aspects in a particular character change,
both their characterization AND their Traits alter slightly.

As Dirt rises in relation to Water, the Clay becomes harder but
also more brittle. Rat is given more protection, but it has a
harder time shifting its position.

As Water rises in relation to Dirt, the Clay becomes more
malleable, but also harder to keep in form. Rat is given less
protection, but finds it harder to move and shape its
surrounding area.

As Rat rises in relation to Dirt, the Personality becomes more
bestial. The person becomes more prone to act based on his urges
and instincts, the body becomes paramount. Water is being
controlled.

As Dirt rises in relation Rat, the personality becomes more
refined. The person becomes more detached and aloof from his
surroundings. The mind becomes paramount. Water is being
channeled.


SETTING UP
Before character creation, a basic genre, setting, and "rating"
for the game should be picked, and game materials need to be
assembled.

    Each Aspect -- and thus each player -- has a different set of
    priorities and responsibilities during character creation. The
    characterâ€™s concept, history, personality and so on will be

    you leave more space for fine-tuning later.

    Each player starts with a blank character sheet in front of
    them. They will all work on the Dirt Aspect of one character,
    then pass the character sheet clockwise for the next Aspect.

    DIRT
    Dirtâ€™s player:
    * Writes his own name under "Dirt" on the character sheet.
    * Comes up with a two to seven word description of the
    character.
    * Assigns four dice between the character's Traits (Clay,
    Personality, and Impetus).
    * Picks one Specialty for the character and assigns one die to

    knowledge, social status, wealth and other temporal things.
    These help the character when they can be brought to bear in
    conflicts. Specialties cannot be personality-based, such as
    friendly or pessimistic,
    since the Aspects cover personality.
    * Pick one Goal for the character that relates to things Dirt
    entails.

    Pass the sheets clockwise.

    RAT
    Rat's player:
    * Writes his own name under "Rat" on the character sheet.
    * Adds one to four words to the character description (if

    * Assigns three dice for Specialites -- can be the old one

    * Pick one Goal for the character that relates to things Rat
    entails.
    * Gives the character a name.

    Pass the sheets clockwise.

    WATER
    Water's player:
    * Writes his own name under "Water" on the character sheet.
    * Adds one word to the character description (if desired).
    * Assigns five dice between the character's existing Traits or

    * Pick one Goal for the character that relates to things Water
    entails.
    * Gives the character a connection to one other character.

    THE ENLIGHTENED
    The Enlightenedâ€™s player:
    * Writes his own name under "The Enlightened" on the character

    * Gives each Aspect of the character 5 dice.
    * Gives the character a connection to one other character (not

    * Tells the players where the character starts within the

    I'll answer to Ewen and hopefully the answer will also provide for Chad's underlying points, rather than specific issues, but first a small answer to Chad.

    Genres/Settings are something I actively don't want as I want the option of stories to mirror one another by not being in the same settings; like a cowboy rising to protect his town with a woman on the doorstep to divorcing her husband(Yes, it can even be that gritty - in fact, that's often deeper-penetrating).

    I want the manual to have a measured voice, a blank voice. My other project(which is still only inside my head), Alone, will hopefully be written in one in-character voice that transmits the feel of the game. Reading games like Polaris always puts me in awe, but I don't see any voice for this game to have. The footnotes are less "The Competitive Voice" and more "Guy Shalev's Voice".

    As for the way in which rules are presented, I took the way I've been using from Sydney Freedberg's Ronnies version of Apocalypse Girl. That's the way I found most suiting to me thus far. Though I admit to being annoyed by it while reading the game, afterwards I accepted internally that I could see no other way to do it.

    The methods for rules-writing that I have noticed thus far are:

    Write about A, if it mentions B, C and D, only explain them in that later section, perhaps not even mention them in section A, and only refer back to it in sections B, C and D.
    Write about A, if it mentions B, C or D, make a break in the rules, explain B, C or D and then return to section A; this breaks the flow of A and drives me mad, most game-texts I see use this method.
    Write about A, mention B, C and D, explain them as best as you can now, and later when you write sections B, C and D, explain them again, in full this time, and refer to section A as appropriate, which is what I did.

    This solves the 12*12=? issue from our prior email discussion Chad, I refer to a rule, I give a small explanation of it, and in the appropriate section explain it again. There is also the part where I explain things numerous times, to ease remembering them. In Dice I explain what you can do with the various Tokens in regards to Dice. In Tokens, I say what you can do with Dice, and again bring up the various ways in which Dice interact with Tokens.

    If I sound defensive, that's not my intent, I just want to explain the why of the how, so our dialogue would be able to progress further.

    Guy, seriously, this isn't a measured or blank voice in the CR manuscript. It's kind of snotty and superior, while being vague and disjointed.

    Game manuscripts are first and foremost technical documentation for a process. That is the core. They need to be as clear and unambiguous as possible. Art and style can -- and should -- inform that core, but they should not overtake it.

    Write your game out as a series of bullet points first (philosophy, descriptions, mechanics). Then turn that into text -- that's where the style comes in.

    At least, that's my process.
    The methods for rules-writing that I have noticed thus far are:
    Write about A, if it mentions B, C and D, only explain them in that later section, perhaps not even mention them in section A, and only refer back to it in sections B, C and D.
    Write about A, if it mentions B, C or D, make a break in the rules, explain B, C or D and then return to section A; this breaks the flow of A and drives me mad, most game-texts I see use this method.
    Write about A, mention B, C and D, explain them as best as you can now, and later when you write sections B, C and D, explain them again, in full this time, and refer to section A as appropriate, which is what I did.
    Or, what I do:
    Write about A. Write about B. Write about C. Write about D. Describe how A, B, C, and D interrelate.

    This differs from your second example in that you need to define your terms before you describe how they work. FIRST tell us what they all are. THEN tell us what they all do.

    You're trying to tell us what they are, and what they do (in detail), without giving us all the pieces.

    If you want, tell us what they are, and then what they are related to and what they do generally, but not the nitty-gritty details of how they do what they do. Save that for later, until you get all the involved terms defined and described.

    IMAO, of course.

    I have already critiqued this game for Guy in a previous edition. On Chad's review, and on Guy asking me to look at it, I went and looked at the current version. In short, I agree with Chad. The game is not as bad as it was previously in terms of being able to absorb it, but I, too, can't get the idea of how play happens from the text.

    At first I thought I was being lazy or something, perhaps, and that I should just try harder. I'm glad Chad has had the same experience, so that I can say that it's not just me.

    Guy, with any text, part of making it readable is grabbing the reader. Even with a textbook, you have to at least present the material so that the reader has a base of understanding to work on to see what it is he's learning. And then you build on that. And the reader follows along, because he feels he's accomplishing something in the act of reading.

    With your game... what's it about? You say it should give output like "Lock, Stock, and Two Smoking Barrels." I love that movie. But then all of a sudden there's these metaphysical passages that have so little to do with the supposed source material from my POV that I can't reconcile the two at all. You'd do better if you just axed them from the text. They are impenetrable.

    As for the A including B, C and D problem...introduce B, C and D first. It's that simple. That is, instead of saying:

    "Roll a d6. We'll explain later what a D6 is."

    Say:

    "A d6 is your standard die. Now you roll it."


    I get a feeling that some of the problem is grammatical. I know that everyone in Israel learns English, but this is not English that I am used to. Like you write:

    "You may offer to buy narration rights from a player whose turn it is by giving
    him a story token (which becomes a mechanics token for him), once he picked
    the character, though you can ask him to do so before he picks the character,
    though as always, he is under no obligation to meet your request."

    That's a run on sentence, and lacks unity of tense and person. I'd write:

    "On a player's turn any player may offer to buy narration rights from him, giving him a story token if he accepts (if so, the Story Token is converted into a Mechanics token). This can be done before or after he picks the character for the scene."

    If the rule above is not correct, it's because I couldn't get the correct rule from the text you wrote.

    Phrases like "he is under no obligation to meet your request" are redundant, and send the reader looking for why you would have to say the same thing twice. Was there some exception I missed in reading, thinks the reader.

    Is there some mechanical point to having to point out that the player can buy narration before or after the character selection? If not, then it should be:

    "On a player's turn you may offer to buy narration rights from him, giving him a story token if he accepts. This Story Token is converted into a Mechanics token. This can at any point until just after the player has selected the character for the scene."

    Or perhaps better for the last sentence something like: "...any point before he begins narration." Because as written the rule doesn't seem to have any purpose. It could, I just can't see it from the text.

    Between grammatical problems, apparent intentional obfuscation in the rules, and reverse presentation of concepts - as Chad says - the text is unreadable.
