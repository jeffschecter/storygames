\n Do players need some sort of character advancement system in order to play a long term game?
\n
\nI'm referring to more of a mechanical advancement rather than a narrative one. (by mechanical I mean gaining new skill points or upgrades in hitting/defending and by narrative I mean changing relationships, goals, and personal philosophies)

    \n Character change can be useful in maintaining interest in a character or a story. In terms of mechanics, people tend to be loss-averse, putting a higher value on something being taken away from them than onto a thing that they're gaining, so mechanical changes that objectively might look like 1-for-1 even exchanges may subjectively feel like losses. Mechanical "advancement" or "growth" can put some inflation in the system so that character change can happen without feeling the sting of loss.

    \n I'll give the same answer I gave the last time this came up: for me, it depends heavily on whether I was able to make exactly the character I wanted right out of the box. Is this character as good/skilled/talented as I want him to be? Can he accomplish the tasks I want him to accomplish? Is the thing I want to do in the game completable as-is, or will the character need to develop/learn/improve in some way to get there?

    If the answer is yes, my character is where I wanted him to be, then I'm not all that concerned with mechanical advancement. But if the system for creating characters doesn't let me make that guy the way I want him to be, then I want a way to get him there. Or if I'm looking to play a character who learns and improves and develops new abilities, then I need there to be mechanical advancement to be happy.

    A secondary, smaller reason why I might want mechanical advancement in a long-term game is to keep from getting bored with the mechanics. D&D is a fun game, but a steady diet of combats where I have only the same selection of powers, spells, abilities, etc., would be a snorefest. Mechanical advancement gives me more tools and toys to play with and lets me explore new ways of dealing with recurring situations.

    \n Keep in mind that even in adventury, tactical games, that bumping up the numbers on the heroes (which usually means bumping up the numbers on the bad guys by a similar amount, making it kind of silly and moot) isn't the only way to "advance".

    I ran a D&D-inspired campaign for a year using a hack of Savage Worlds as the core system.

    I started the characters off broke and used a reasonably realistic-ish game economy, where average people made a reasonable income, but then spent the majority of it on day-to-day expenses. So the characters needed to scrape together money for basic gear. This gave them tons of incentive to engage in adventuring - at the start of the game they had maybe enough cash to live for a week at the inn in town. Because of this, just a successful looting of bad guys counted as character improvement - it would significantly improve their current situation. And they started out under-geared. So improving their armor or weapons, for example, was a really big thing.

    Also, I had a player interested in alchemy, so I whipped up a litle component-based alchemy system. So when she found more components or more recipes for how to use them, those things were a significant boost to her efficacy that didn't have to come through a "level up" process. Plus, it let me work in plot-driven alchemy challenges as interesting story elements - figuring out how to cure someone from a fairy-charm, for example.

    Also, the group recruited some henchmen when they knew they were going to face a big fight (which, in the Savage Worlds rules, are fun and easy to add in). So they improved their combat efficacy that way when needed.

    Over the course of around 20ish sessions, I think we did regular stat advancement maybe 2 times, which seemed to be plenty enough to keep the players happy.

    Of course, the characters started out as being plenty competent at their main areas of expertise right off the bat. I don't think I had anyone boosted their main stats. My son took a perk or two that boosted his combat efficacy a little. I think most of the rest of the players used advancements to round out things they hadn't started out good at but had proved to be useful as the game progressed.

    So yeah, advancement/change can come from several directions. And you don't necessarily need much of it if you start out competent (which is my preference).

    Posted By: UpstartWhat is a world shift?
    \nTake, for example, D&D 3x.

    At first level, a group of 5 players with 1st level characters. They have, on average, 9 hit points and AC 16 (as a rough example based on my foggy memory, don't flog me on details). Have them fight, say, 7 kobolds with spears and they'll take a few hits and get a bit bruised up, but they should be fine. So that's the kind of fight encounter the GM would likely use. The Challenge Rating system helps you design an encounter like that (it's imperfect, perhaps, but it's a reasonably good guide).

    Years of play later. The characters are all level 16. They have, on average, 72 hit points an AC 23. If you were to put those same 7 kobolds against them, the fight would be over in, at most, 2 turns and the heroes would probably not have taken a single hit. So instead, you face them off against 9 Bugbear Captains who have been statted upward with Fighter levels. And now you have a fight that meets an appropriate Challenge Rating and is a good fit for the party.

    That's a world shift.

    As the characters level up (and up and up and up), the world conveniently levels up around them because otherwise things that would have been scary before would now be silly weak.

    And it bugs the hell out of me.

    I prefer systems where the thing that is big and scary at the beginning of the campaign is always something big and scary, but maybe over time you've gotten to the point where, instead of having maybe a 20% chance of being able to succeed against it, you've gotten yourself up to a 65% chance (which is still FAR from a sure thing). Or maybe, though specific questing, you've obtained something that will improve your chances greatly against that one big scary thing, but doesn't help you at all against the other big scary things in the world.

Or 5 orcs, or a bear, or whatever the toughest fight from their low-level days was -- just take the fight that was a serious challenge for them back then and give it back to them with no changes and let them beat it in under two rounds without taking a single hit.

    When I did that, it turned out to be the one fight they talked about from that game more than anything else, the fight that defined the most awesome part of the game for them. It was undeniable proof that their characters had become certifiable badasses, as well as long overdue payback on the monsters that terrorized them at first level. They got nothing tangible in-game from it -- no treasure to compare with what they already had, no experience whatsoever, not even a tactical challenge to overcome -- but man, it was glorious.

    Lots of people talk about throwing challenges that are too tough at a group, and yeah, okay, that works; having to run away from the big scary thing is definitely memorable (even if there aren't many players who would remember it fondly). But I highly recommend doing the opposite, too: give 'em something so far beneath their level that they can just cut loose and show off with it and get a sense that their characters' skills and place in the world have truly advanced.
