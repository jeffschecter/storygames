\n Hello,

So a couple of weeks ago I was struck by inspiration to develop a game that is probably best described as The Three Musketeers as written by Frank Miller and directed by Quentin Tarantino. This idea sort of formed whole sale in my mind and demanded that I write it down. So I did and the result is

Thornes

That's a playable alpha draft of the text but I have not yet play tested it myself. If it captures anyone's imagination enough to try it, I welcome feedback. Almost everyone I've described the game to has had a very visceral reaction.

Design Goals

When it comes to resolution mechanics that promote detailed back-and-forth conflicts my two favorites are Dogs in the Vineyard and Sorcerer. Given the concept I really saw no reason to re-invent the wheel. I think Sorcerer is a little better suited to the visceral forward moving action I wanted out of Thornes so the core resolution system is lifted nearly wholesale from that game.

Given that I was using a pre-existing die mechanic it was my goal to build up around it a bunch of fairly unique systemic components which would serve the design purposes of creating a very visual and emotionally violent game. Here are some of the highlights.

* Character Creation begins with creating a distinct Image for the character before building up any other details.

* The game is narrated entirely in Black and White but each player gets one Color they may use to paint narrative elements that are important to them. However, the color Red has special meaning in the game and has unique rules that impact the GM's ability to influence outcomes.

* Characters build up Motifs over the course of the game which the presence of marks a scene as particularly important to a given character.

* All conflicts result in someone being Injured in some way. If you're picking up the dice your character is trying to harm someone.

* The central stat of the game is called Ego. You gain Ego by having sex, killing someone or crushing someone else's Ego. However, if your Ego gets too high you enter a state called Ego Tripping where you become more vulnerable.

The title of this thread uses the somewhat more polite form of the tag line. However, the text still retains the original tag line that was conceived with the help of Alex Duarte (author of unWritten).

Thornes: A vulgar game about fucking your enemies and murdering your lovers.

I hope some of you find a use for this game. Assuming I can play-test it I will update the text periodically with the results.

Jesse

I realized that like most things in Sorcerer something which seemed like a nod to old-fashioned "realism" thinking had an underlying narrative logic. It's not that a blade does more damage than a fist, it's that it takes more moral conviction to beat a man to death with your bare hands. I liked that effect in Sorcerer and wanted to preserve it for Thornes.

    \n Loving it. Thoughts on first read.

    On the 10 points to distribute: As written you can't start with an Ego higher than 3. Since Ego is used in every roll and is essentially your story hitpoints, I cannot see any reason to set Ego less than 3. If you set Ego at 3, then you must set Dominance and Violence at least at 3...which means essentially...every character starts with 3s in everything with 1 point to add to D or V. I see where the Ego Tripping rules address this, but honestly, those rules don't seem very fun.

    I'm thinking either Ego should be Hitpoints only and not something you ever roll...so there is value to D and V that E doesn't have. Or that E costs twice as much as either V or I so that you get more bang for your buck by buying dice in D or V...but that doesn't help every roll and doesn't provide story hitpoints. With this you'd probably need 20 points to distribute with E being 2 each.

    How are non "Conflicts" adjudicated. Entirely at GM fiat? If the overall goal of the conflict is to inflict harm, does each individual die roll need to be? What if I want to do to set myself up (via chaining) for a major smack down on you...but is not in itself a harm produding action. Legit? If so...am I restricted to only taking the Chaining option?

    Why limit Chaining to just that scene. I always thought Sorcerer's "next roll only" rule to be the weakest part of its dice mechanics. Without that restriction you can use Chaining to model all sorts of really cool stuff...like starting a rumor mill today that will give you beau coup bonuses later on when you find yourself trying to debate that person in front of the king and can use those rumors to humiliate him.

    Drop the "most dice" tie breaker. With it the Interruption won't happen that often and Interruption sounds far more exciting and interesting than just winning by 1 anyway.

    What value are you rolling your Dominance against to stay in the fight once you've suffered wounds > violence? The opponent's roll for the roll you're trying to make? How about making the roll against a number of dice equal to the largest single wound you've suffered instead. That way you aren't doubleing up on the opposing difficulty and can have a neat mechanical differentiator between lots of small wounds vs 1 big one, without any additional rules complexity.

    Similarly, I'd give the opponent bonus dice equal to your single greatest wound rather than current wound total. Have total wounds simply accumulate towards the Violence thresholds.

    I like the injuring relationships rule but it seems weak that the injury is "healed" after the first time it is used to someone elses advantage. Also not a fan of the Relationship Recovery rules. I'm not sure what I'd replace it with, but this seems like an area of HUGE potential that currently is nearly untapped. Making these rules more robust could create a game where manipulating each other's relationships like pawns becomes not only fictionally plausible, but mechanically reinforced as well. I'd like to see a whole relationship map of progressively damaged relationships, perhaps with the ability to Chain bonuses into relationships as a form of earning favor with someone. I'd like to see some kind of "relationship wars" where I can steal your relationships and make them mine, and "capturing" relationships becomes an important game activity. Perhaps each player has a limited number of relationship slots and once relationships have been invented to absorb damage such that all slots are filled, no more relationships can be invented but must be taken from others. Thus characters with expanded webs are more "invulnerable" and characters whose relationships have been stripped away are at risk. Maybe a Player can always shunt Ego damage into a relationship representing mistreating that individual in some way that restores the character's Ego. Lots of potential there but I don't think the current rules really scratch the surface much.

    Not a fan of losing Ego to make an appeal. This seems to run afoul of the game answering its own premise mechanically. If being honest and sincere loses you Ego, and Ego is your story relevance...then the game is automatically programmed to force you to not be honest and sincere. That seems much less interesting than letting someone try to be sincere if they are willing to risk the E vs. E+D disadvantage of doing so. Similarly the restriction on there being no possible way to inflict injury seems unnecessary. I'd rather see this as an open player choice...sure be honest if you like...let's see how long you can get away with it before the lack of effectiveness frustrates you enough to turn to dominance or violence.

    Recovering from wounds: If using the individual wounds method, I'd half each wound rounding down after a scene. That way a character with a bunch of 1 point wounds heals almost instantly, but larger wounds linger for several scenes. Also, this works very well with the Aggressor only getting a bonus equal to the largest wound. Using that bonus halfs just that largest wound, potentially meaning it won't be the largest anymore. That sounds nicely flavorful rather than then the current rules which seem rather sterile.

    Ego Tripping. I think making Ego Tripping simply a mechanical penalty sounds rather boring and unfun (in the same way that whiffing is unfun). Try this on instead, roll the dice E + D or V as normal. Any Victories you earn in excess of 1 on the roll give the GM Consequences. Bad Things that happen to your character (immediately or at some point in the future) as a result of your hubris. For instance, you defeat your opponent in a duel while Ego Tripping...you won the fight but now court
    oppinion of you has fallen...you are seen as boorish and capricious and your defeated opponent has won great sympathy. In other words, use the Consequences to turn their victory into ashes rather than just make victory less likely.

    Audience Pool is mentioned under locations, but I couldn't find the text that says what you do with them...
