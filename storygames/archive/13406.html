    \n That's not an awful position, and beginner games are naturally volatile, so anything can happen yet. Normally a passive Turkey through first year negotiations would be a massive problem, as it'd be very easy for Austria to leverage this into an anti-Turkish alliance with Russia; as things stand, you should be able to talk with both of those players and figure out something other than a Turkish dismantling. Austria would be the main beneficiary if you went caput, so if he knows what's good for him, he'll drive Russia hard to join in attacking you. Russia could go either way, so that's where you need to negotiate.

    My first advice, however, is to always be courteous and good-humoured towards everybody, including prospective enemies. There is no such thing as a permanent enemy in Diplomacy, so don't try to analyze the board in terms of friends and foes so as to know what sort of theatrical mask to wear towards each other player. This is a typical beginner mistake, to confuse the concept of diplomacy with play-acting and boisterous over-signalling; much better to act naturally and friendly towards everybody equally.

    Tactically your position is good; Turkey's greatest strength is the immensely strong defensive position, and your presence in Bla combined with an amiable Italy makes you practically impregnable; my take here would be to sow discontent in between Austria and Russia by demonstrating to them how futile and annoying trying to destroy you would be; they could take Bulgaria with great work, but they would never benefit off the act, for the war with you would never end, and they would have to tie up more forces in fighting you than they'd get from seizing that Bulgaria. This is admittedly a bit of an advanced argument, but if you can understand yourself why it's better to fight wars where the outcome is actually a net increase in free units, then you can try to argue that to your opposition as well; the only way to get free units out of a Turkish war is to destroy Turkey entirely, which is really friggin' hard.

    (If looking into the future does not work as an argument - it sometimes doesn't when beginner players go into the game with the assumption that all communications are a priori lies - then you might potentially have to go through the practical demonstration in which Russia and Austria attack you. It's important to not get disheartened here, as you'll still be willing even if those two manage to make superficial gains. It's just a matter of time before Italy backstabs Austria and Russia realizes that he's not benefiting in the war; in 1904-5 you'll be out of the war with Russia as your ally and Austria near death, yourself dominant in the Balkans.)

    Incidentally, Austria is fucked in this situation if he decides to trust in a Russian alliance against you: the utopic Turkish dismantlement is never going to happen, or if it is, that's going to happen in five years at soonest. Meanwhile Italy will sooner or later realize that attacking Austria is an obvious route to power: you will encourage Italy in this, and perhaps choose his moment. The Italian stab in the Tyr-Tri area will show Austria how very fragile he is. Even should he somehow avoid that, the Russian betrayal you'll concoct will prove to be his doom; once you've lost/relinquished Bulgaria it'll be obvious to Russia that it will be untenable for him to hope to gain anything from the war - anything except an excellent opportunity to seize the Austrian heartland, that is.

    In summation, I wouldn't be overly concerned with Austrian aggression at this stage; you are the wolf and he is the sheep, even if he does not realize it. (It's common for a beginner Austria to self-destruct as long as the neighbours keep a cool head.) The most interesting questions here for you are two-fold: where will Italy jump, and what will you do with that fleet in the Black Sea. The former is a heavy diplomatic and strategic question, I recommend getting into deep talks with Italy soon; beginners as well as veterans react well to concrete, realistic plans, so throw something regarding an Austrian partition on the table and try to find out what Italy plans to do. The latter is a tactical issue, and issue of Russian strategy: it would be entirely realistic for you to play time through the spring and then make a surprise attack on Rumania, for example, if you wanted to put pressure on Russia. If Italy joins you in near future it would even be favourable to shoot for Black Sea domination: take Rumania and Sevastopol and then sue for peace with the trump Russia, encouraging him to accept the fact that lasting peace between you is ensured by a Turkish fleet in Sevastopol. The other tack would be to vacate Black Sea in exchange for peace; a sensible Russia would jump for this, likely, but in a beginner game it might be better to leverage the position offensively; a beginner Russia won't realize how very fucked he is by a Turkish fleet on the Black Sea, which means that you can't leverage the position in negotiation, but only in war.

    \n I suppose that basic idea with a genuinely anonymous venue of communication would be to pretend to be a third party with important information, but unwilling to identify yourself because of distrust. "Russia really is planning to do you in and here I drop some hints that might mean that I'm either England or France, but obviously I don't want to tell because if Russia finds out I told then I'm fucked." It's an interesting idea, but I haven't had much experience with anonymous communication, especially not the sort where you can limit the recipients.

    Regarding immediate tactics, keeping that fleet on the Black Sea is fine for you, but an experienced Russia would consider it an extremely obnoxious and aggressive move. I was Russia in the last SG postal game and Turkey wouldn't budge from the Black Sea - it just started to annoy me so much I had to kill him to get that fleet out. He should have known better.

    Between Greece and Rumania it ultimately depends on playing the players - will you handle Russia or Austria better as an enemy? How about an ally? If you can work well together with Russia, then go for Greece, and if you can do both, then take Rumania. It's notable that taking Greece will thrust you into closer contact with Italy, while taking Rumania puts you into a position where you also have to take Sevastopol in the long run, or relinquish Rumania after a year in exchange for some other concessions; keeping Rumania without either taking Serbia or taking Sevastopol is not really an option, as the place is in between Austria and Russia, easy for them to retake if you stay in position. If you take Rumania, do it in the fall so as to get the build out of it before the opposition adapts.

    That Italian fleet deployment is heavily anti-French - stupid too, but that's a beginner game for you. You'd much prefer to get Italy into a fracas with Austria, of course, so perhaps you'd do well to talk with him to find out what he's planning - if it's an attack on France, you could always broker a peace between them before it gets serious. That German stab probably doesn't mean anything much, Italy is always doing that sort of unexpected stuff - it's Italy's bread and butter. In fact, one of the best ways of attacking Austria is to first go into Germany (with German concent or not) to throw Austria off the scent. If Italy actually has an army in Tyrolia, then you're cooking - just make sure Italy bounces in Munich with the army in the spring (perhaps tell Austria that France promised a support) so as to be in position to sneak into an Austria SC in the fall.

    An Italian fleet in the Ionian is a two-pronged thing for you; as long as Italy does something ineffective in the west at least he's completely out of your hair - could even open you the option of going to the Ionian yourself. The Ionian Sea is an immensely important position, sometimes Turkey spends the entire game just trying to get there; if you can take the position because Italy messes up, it might be worth it to encourage a western strategy for Italy. Depends on whether you believe in your chances in striking a wedge between Austria and Russia; basically you need to get either Italy or Russia to attack Austria, otherwise you're in for that long and annoying defensive war I mentioned earlier. You don't need them both, although I suppose your dream scenario would be a Russian attack on Austria and an Italian land assault/stab with the Italian fleet regardless going west; then you could ally with the desperate Austria, go to the Ionian, take Sevastopol, stab Austria and move to the end-game.

    Looking at that map and assuming hostile Russia and Austria, your best bet would probably be to go to Aeg, support Bul in place and move Bla-Sev to discourage an eastern attack. However, I don't think that you have a sufficiently substantiated Austria/Russia alliance in your hands here - the correct move would be to emphasize diplomacy, get to heavy petting with both and try to score a homerun with one if not both. Consider your diplomatic goal to be to build a viable long-term plan with both players in such a way that it does not require you to act against either immediately in the spring; this way you can gauge their intentions and get some diplomatic capital if one or both decide to betray you despite their promises.

    This being a beginner game, I would only assume a solid anti-Turkish alliance if both players refuse to negotiate or seem to lack initiative and interest in negotiations. Beginners usually signal their intent to betray pretty well; look for over-eagerness in accepting your thoughts, perhaps.

    The main thing in negotiation is to always have a game-plan that is both credible and involves your negotiation partner as an ally. The first thing you do before going into the negotiations with Austria and Russia is to build two plans: one where you attack Russia with Austria and one where you attack Austria with Russia. Bonus points if your own moves in both plans are the same, as then those two won't know which you're allying with from your moves. Your goal in the negotiations is to get both, or at least one, of those two to decide that your plan is better than whatever the third player is offering (if anything - beginners can be pretty pathetic about proactive negotiation now and then). The most likely result of the negotiations is that both say that they agree to your plan, at which point you'll just decide which is more trustworthy and gamble on them doing as they say - this is why you don't want a crazily risky plan. Be ready to attack with utter aggression against one of those two if it seems that only one of them is willing to work with you at this point, but also keep your eyes open for credible arrangements where you can delay making the choice of allies until the fall. And of course always keep in mind that your alliance only lasts as long as you need it, not as long as the opponent wants it.

    \n Bill's right - also, don't stress about it too much, no reason to make sure that everybody agrees with your orders. There's such a large psychological component to this game that there's rarely only one defensible set of orders - and perfect ones only appear in hidsight, in relation to known enemy moves. (The cardinal error that now and then occurs for everybody is that you tell your own moveset to an ally only to have the ally use it against you. For this reason risky moves that leave you open should only be made when an ally who might benefit does not know about the move.)

    An Italian army in Greece is also a good idea. I've played this particular game with Italy several times (shipping into Greece in the first year usually, though), and historically I've often found it a good idea to stab Austria even when both Turkey and Austria are amiable - if you offer Greece to Italy and Austria doesn't immediately counter-bid, then it's a clear case in your advantage diplomatically.

    \n Usually the most effective method of giving your communications an advantageous spin is to work very hard to create a solid, truthful strategic framework that just happens to involve allying with you instead of the other guy. This is at least my own diplomatic approach: emotioning and giving self-serving suggestions to others is not nearly as useful as long as the opposition can gauge you and is not gripped by one of the many Diplomacy-related illusions; as long as the opponent remembers that it's a game and the intent is to win, you simply can't convince them of anything with a spiel that falls short of the truth.     The way I'd approach this, I'd discuss the entire strategic framework with Italy first to make sure he understands what I'm thinking, and then I'd tell him that it seems to me that him taking Greece would be advantageous to us both because of this and that. I'd likely do this as a part of a long-term plan where I discuss the opportunities for a quick stab in the Tyrolian region as well, and hint at possible partitions of Austria and later mutually advantageous developments. In case of Turkey vs. Italy I'd pledge to limit myself to two Mediterranean fleets quickly, almost without the other party asking about it; two is enough for when i want to attack Italy, anyway. (I'd even go to one fleet if that's what it takes, but then I'd require Italy to make some tactical concessions as well.)

    The clear diplomatic advantage here comes from the fact that if Austria does not offer Greece to Italy with a complete strategic plan as a rider, then Italy will basically have the choice of taking the plan that promises him both short- and long-term advantages, or taking the sucky Austrian plan that at best offers vague promises, and possibly not even that if Austria is a green player who thinks that he can be a neighbour to Italy without engaging him fully in diplomacy. It's not even sufficient for Austria to offer Greece if he's not also capable of talking to Italy about the future; Italy will of course take Greece, but as that's what you wanted him to do in the first place, it's OK for you if Austria should offer it. Not that he's going to, with his own units in position to lay claim.

    In summation, offering Greece to Italy is a diplomatic advantage to you because Austria has no other immediate leverage to offer to Italy in their relationship; by offering to help Italy into Greece you take away the only thing Austria could have offered, and thus remove the only concrete motivation Italy had for allying with Austria and not attacking him. If Austria realizes this and aggressively counter-bids by offering Greece and laying out a Lepanto plan of some sort, then the situation is closer to even - he can theoretically match you term by term in both gifting Greece away and laying out a long-term alliance plan, in which case the matter is much less clear-cut. However, as I said in the original quote, this is pretty much the only option for Austria diplomatically: failing to immediately counter-bid makes the choice an easy one for Italy, with the only reason for refusal being that he suspects you to renege on the promised support. (You can formulate your explanation of the plan to Italy in a way that makes it clear that it's your advantage to get Italy into Greece, too; this helps alleviate doubt, as the most suspicious offers of alliance gifts are the ones that are merely offered in hopes of alliance without the act itself being a substantial gain for the courting party. An experienced player would usually go for a promise of this sort, as you wouldn't actually benefit in any way of offering a false support for Italy; you'd have no reason to lie to him this early in the game, which would make any such promise seriously made rather credible.)

    And of course, we have to remember that Austria might not even realize that he has to counter-bid - if he thinks that he can get by in his Italian relationship with homilies and general non-aggression, then he won't necessarily even realize that you're courting Italy before the deed is done. A smart Italy might tell him and ask for a counter-bid, but that has its hazards, too - and as discussed, Austria doesn't really have anything he can offer that you can't give to Italy, too. Diplomacy is interesting in that the same move (Italian convoy to Greece) will have completely different connotations if it's offered by Austria or Turkey than if it's initiated by Italy.

    Looking at the Turkish defense this way, it's not that Austria and Russia can't kill you if they start trying now, but rather that doing that will be counter-productive every step of the way when England is committing everything to seizing the Nordic sphere of SCs and France is ready to seize the Italian peninsula.     All of the above is, as usual, something that your opponents need to understand as much as you do - most of the time it's important for you to impose your own, truthful view of the board dynamics on everybody else as well, as without this view they'll do foolish things that lead to you losing the game.

    Assuming that you get a manageable diplomatic situation where Austria and Russia (separately) acknowledge the necessity of a living Turkey for the time being, then there is plenty that you can do even without directly attacking or provoking them to attack each other.     \n I'll tell you a secret that some have been wondering about in these Story Games Diplomacy games - how come when I decide to attack Eero somebody else always decides to attack my flank the very next turn? It's not actually that difficult, you just need to ask the other guy neighboring your enemy to help you out by attacking them. They will usually do this because, first, the fact that your enemy attacked you means that they'll have a soft flank, and second, it's usually easier and probably better to do what somebody asks you to do rather than doing an arbitrary other thing, especially if the guy doing the asking has a head on his shoulders and can provide you with good advice.

    In practical terms, assuming that Austria and Russia have a solid alliance and have already planned for your demise: you recognize where the allies are sacrificing position, potential, security, dynamic ability and other values so as to be able to attack you, and then you make sure that those risks are realized by informing anybody who'd benefit from exploiting them in as clear terms as you can. I find that being theatrical about this communication is not useful, you're not there to roleplay - just try to explain to others in as clear terms as you possibly can why and how they can benefit from the Austro-Russian decision to attack you. Once you've done this communication you'll then maximise the difficulty of attacking you and wait for the Austro-Russian alliance to shatter; the smarter one of the duo notices that they underestimated the difficulty of killing you and starts answering your biannual suggestions of stabbing the other positively. (You are, of course, talking with both of them every turn to update your plan of how the two of you can do unto the third one most effectively.)

    In Diplomacy, when somebody attacks somebody else, this is always against the interests of any third parties, as a success in such an attack means that the attacker grows in strength and thus comes closer to victory. For this reason a player who can communicate the logic and translate it into concrete suggestions will always have the edge in negotiations; Austria that attacks Turkey is naturally at disadvantage against Turkey on the question of whether Germany should backstab Austria in response, for example; in practice Austria can get one over Turkey anyway on this issue if he has prior long-term agreements with Germany, or Germany is in another war, or if Austria is so strong from other possessions that he can fight on two fronts effectively. Most of the time, however, the basic nature of the game will ensure that other players have a vested interest for coming to the aid of the weaker party in a war.

    (The above doesn't count for players who show a weak diplomatic hand, incidentally: if a player seems untrustworthy or airheaded, others will often deny him aid simply because they don't believe that they can benefit from it themselves. There's also the issue that if a weak player can't protect himself, then it might be better to take a part of his SCs for yourself and protect them that way from the aggressive party. The lesson is, don't seem weak as a player even if your absolute board strength is currently less than your opponent's; don't let others think that the best thing to do is to split your possessions between them.)

    In practice what you do in the current board position to make Austria and Russia pay for attacking you is that you make sure that England has the motivation and the knowhow to execute a devastating attack on Russia, and you make sure that Italy does his level best to defend against Austria (perhaps broker a peace between Italy and France by having the latter attack Germany). Then you ensure that both Austria and Russia are acutely aware of the best moves they can make each turn were they to decide to attack the other; promise to support Austria into Rumania, and Russia into Serbia, for example. You'll find that if you can get those two to attack each other even once, your position will become immeasurably stronger, as then they've proven to one another that they're capable of actually playing the game instead of just agreeing to collude against you.

Splitting Austria-Russia to the point of one betraying the other probably won\u2019t happen until the betrayal has demonstrably more benefits than adhering to the partnership. But splitting Austria-Russia doesn\u2019t have to mean betrayal. If one partner or the other agrees not to continue offensive actions against Turkey that is a result almost as good as an actual betrayal as it makes defending Turkey easier. And, if the decision by the partner is made known to the other partner, he, too, might agree to an armistice. One-on-one fights in the early game are much too fair to be worth fighting.
      I think Russia is a good target for diplomatic efforts along this line. He has taken all he is going to get in the Balkans and will need that Sev army to fight Germany. Plus, if the player is concerned with \u2018honor\u2019, you\u2019re not looking for a betrayal of Austria, just peace with Turkey. He may feel honor bound to keep fighting if Austria keeps fighting, so this would be one instance where talking to Russia and Austria at the same time makes sense. In fact, you can tell them that you were wrong to suggest (before Fall 1902) that the only way you could make progress was if one of them betrayed the other. It\u2019s clear that you can make progress as their partner, with your fleets complementing Austrian armies\u2026while Austria and Russia work together in the center and Russia handles England in the north. You\u2019ll all meet in London.
      Whether France and Italy can work together or France fights Italy, Austria is going to want to look west soon in order to gain some of the Italian territory as well as assist Russia in Germany, and to prepare for what happens once Italy is eliminated and France continues east.
      English pressure on Russia in the north is one of the factors that may convince Russia to make peace with you. Although I don\u2019t see any immediate breakthroughs, Russian forces are fully committed right now, while England (and Germany), have multiple units that are not yet engaged against Russia. As these forces are brought into the fight, Russia will face defensive dilemmas it cannot solve, unless it can pull Sevastopol north (and maybe not even then). Anglo-German cooperation, for the time being, helps Turkey.
      Franco-Italian cooperation directed against Austria is probably too much to hope for. Still, the points to make supporting such a partnership stem from the naval superiority a combined Franco-Italian armada would have over the single existing Austrian fleet, as well as the potential for an Austrian war against Turkey. Failing that, Austria could be convinced that there are easier pickings against Italy than against Turkey. This seems clear without much convincing. Your support for his fleet into the Ionian potentially gives him shot at Naples or Tunis\u2026or at least gives Austria some influence whether France or Italy get Tunis. Austria should want to delay France\u2019s rise as much as possible relative to Austria\u2019s own expansion. Or you can seek Austrian support to move your fleet to Ionian, as you suggested earlier. I think you know what you\u2019re doing!
      So, in conclusion, I think Turkey is not in bad shape. It can defend itself for a while. I think the Russia-Austria alliance is not set on defeating Turkey\u2014you may have unintentionally given them more concern than is real. More importantly, there are other threats and opportunities that should attract Russian and Austrian attention. They should welcome the opportunity to gain your support.
      Finally, as you wrote, you can\u2019t trust anything Russia or Austria tells you at this point. Your orders for Spring should focus on defending Bulgaria. You\u2019ll know by Fall if Russia and Austria are aiming to eliminate you. Defend strongly in Spring, and don\u2019t offer something you won\u2019t deliver. To me, that means don\u2019t get involved in a betrayal of Russia/Austria vs the other until you\u2019ve seen it. That means you can support one in a move that is neutral (such as Aeg support Gre to Ion), but don\u2019t support an attack of one against the other because it might not happen. And don\u2019t offer to support such an attack. For the time being, you\u2019re playing nice.

      \n Sounds like you're playing a good game - the only way I know for convincing newbies to change tack (often a tricky task, as the player finds solace in going all-in and feels pot-committed about their current stance) is to play a hard defense, give them a bloody nose and crow incessantly, loudly, about how they're losing the game just the same as you are by making the choices that they're making - they're not actually playing a winning game even if you are losing, but rather just ensuring that you both go down. Every turn of successful defense or counter-attack, every third-party intervention and every success others have on other parts of the board rubs your erstwhile neighbour's nose on the fact that they're not getting what they wanted out of attacking you. In this particular case Austria and Russia are relatively committed to not cooperating with you, but that doesn't change the basic technique.

      The French play is such that my own reaction to similar is usually to play a very close diplomatic game with the player: stay close, advice often, be sympathetic about their uncertainties, fear and distrust. Usually extreme passivity is because a player feels overwhelmed by the game's tactics and exaggerates the dangers of betrayal in their mind; they're fearing losing and being the victim of a betrayal so much that they're willing to lose by inactivity instead of being made the fool. Often another player who is clearly in an impartial position on the other side of the board can advice such players successfully by arguing from their position: acknowledge the things the player is thinking, and then tell them what to do on the basis of their fears - no matter whether their thinking is realistic or not. In this case you'd probably want to just keep harping on about the necessity of turning north and leaving Italy alone, in as many variations as you can think of; it might take time to take root, but sooner or later the uncertain player will play every option offered to them.

      Obviously enough, make sure that England has the best move-set she can have for decimating Russia; also let Russia know that you're advicing England, make him fear your tactical insight; ensure that it's absolutely clear that you'll fuck up the Czar seven ways to Sunday by proxy as long as he maintains his anti-Turkish attitude. Show a bit of anger, make him realize that lying to you for frivolous reasons like he's been doing is a way of making a dangerous enemy. Be the sort of enemy where he'd want to have a real army at hand, so as to make him realize that he can't actually have one because he's under Anglo-German assault up north.

      Italy is basically non-feasible now, which means that you need to make sure the player doesn't give up: wrack your brain to give him optimistic, solid advice for playing the underdog game. Most gamers are instinctually inclined to go home and cry in Diplomacy when the going gets rough, so you need to counter-act the tendency by showing the player the attitudes and thinking that go with being the underdog. Remind them that the goal is to survive, and victory means being still alive at the end of the game so as to participate in the draw; solo victories are the stuff of fantasy fiction, exceptions to the rule. Italy might have great difficulty getting up and becoming a major Power (not that it can't happen if the player keeps their head), but that's no reason to start playing an even worse game or even NMR'ing in the middle of the game; he should respect the other players and their passion by playing the best game he can even when the situation is dark.

      Aside from those observations, the large-scale situation is basically as I've outlined before - Turkey in your position needs to break up the Russo-Austrian alliance, and while Turkey usually could defend indefinitely against those two with some diplomatic work, an extremely weak Italy is causing you difficulty by being a very impotent ally against Austria and by attracting French attentions. Even then, it'll take time and resources for those two to eliminate you, which means that you have time to talk to them and their western neighbours, engineering more western pressure for your enemies and keeping them apprised of how the strategic situation is developing against them as they blindly attack you.

      (Not all assaults on Turkey are blind, of course, but for the purposes of diplomacy you as Sultan will, of course, hold onto the stance that these particular attacks on Turkey are foolish and liable to cause adverse imbalances in the European situation, imbalances that lead to the destruction of the entire eastern triangle. It's possible that your enemies are acting rationally about killing off Turkey, of course - I myself would greatly prefer to start the game as Austria by killing Turkey. Of course it never happens because attacking Turkey is such a pain in the ass.)

      If the situation seems diplomatically truly desperate, you might choose to employ the extreme gambit: if Russia and/or Austria blatantly disrespect your negotiation and signal that they're not considering a living Turkey in good faith, then the correct play for you is to very loudly let the both of them know that you're willing to throw the game to one of those two players if they do not ease up on you and help you out against the other guy. This is sort of the Diplomacy nuclear option: by tactically throwing your possessions to one of your neighbours while fighting the other with everything you have you could theoretically cause them such a powerful position that the only logical consequence for the other neighbour would be death in the hands of the dominant eastern Power. A player blessed with basic intelligence will thus realize that the only way for them to prevent you from throwing the local game to their other neighbour is by keeping you at least happy enough to not suicide yourself.

      (Make a distinction between threatening the above course and actually committing to it; ideally you'll never actually suicide in Diplomacy, but if and when you're convinced that your neighbours truly are so stupid/colluding that there is basically no way for you to convince them to change their minds, then you might as well do it and not just talk about it; if you're losing the game anyway, then every step you take should be the one that optimizes your survival after all. A biased suicide is ironically a pretty good option in this regard, as often enough your actually starting to commit the act will also trigger that breakdown in the enemy alliance you've been waiting for; you might lose half of your SCs and be left with a barely viable trump state, but if the troop positions are correct, it's possible for that trump state to continue in existence as a balancing linchpin between the two now clearly dominant local Powers. Of course this isn't going to happen if the players have decided in advance in the meta-game that they'll kill you off, but then they're basically cheating - nothing much to be done about it if they're both willing to compromise their own success in the game just to make sure you lose.)

      I'm not suggesting that your situation is desperate enough now to start talking about throwing it to one of your neighbours, but that's just because I'm not there in the field observing the other players. You'll have to decide for yourself if there is actually a person considering your diplomacy there in control of your enemy or if it's one of those players who shut their ears against enemy lies once they've made an alliance at the start of the game. Observing your enemy and deciding how they think will let you know how big a hammer you need to be using to make them actually listen to you; against an experienced player you should theoretically never have to speak of suicide, but as this is a beginner game, it's possible that the players arent' simply calculating the situation up to the very end and are therefore unaware of how, exactly, the killing of Turkey will ultimately go down. They might be talking completely unrealistic stuff among themselves, which is why you need to talk with them constantly and discuss the future with them, including the future where they eat up Turkey and ride into the sunset: you need to make them realize that no, Austria won't be just taking Bul and Con while Russia takes Smy and Ank - all that stuff is optimistic rubbish when you account for the fact that Turkey will be meanwhile colluding with Russia to ensure that Austria gets nothing, and Russia'll be building new armies to start up an anti-Austrian war the very next winter after they start actually gaining builds off Turkey. That's the sort of discussion a truly desperate Power goes into once it's obvious that his neighbours have decided to kill him. Still might not work if it's more important to the other players to kill you than to win the game, or if they're playing basically unfairly by having decided a priori that they'll never betray each other, but it's pretty much your last shot if everything else fails.

      My estimate from the board positions is that you're not in the suicide territory yet, but if the players seem disinterested in your arguments and they routinely and blatantly lie to you only to ignore all agreements the very next turn, then perhaps you are there. Your call. Don't start that sort of thing prematurely; suicide is heady stuff in this game, and difficult to communicate in a way that doesn't make you sound like an irrational crybaby.

      We can talk tactics, too, but it seems to me that you have a basic handle on that stuff. Just keep outguessing your opponents while you make sure to give everybody on the board a suggested, high-quality move-set that does not involve attacking you. This includes England, France, Italy, Austria... everybody deserves to have a slice of your tactical genius. It's so much easier to do what you want somebody to do if they already have at hand a smart, full moving plan they can just follow and prosper with it. (This approach to tactical discussion of course presumes that you're not in the business of trivial betrayal yourself.)

      \n It's important to note that playing for failure is not necessarily useful; if you assume that the enemy will continue relentlessly and nothing will break their alliance, then you're already as good as lost.       One thing you might try with Italy is outright threatening him with the consequences of his actions: if he doesn't restrain himself now that the north is already in control of about half of the board, then to hell with him, and Turkey'll play for the second best thing, which would here be crippling Italy while maintaining his own position, thus allowing Turkey to remain on the board to the end, probably gaining you a prestigious three-way draw. After all, if you can't hope to beat back the northerners, then it seems to me that your advantage is in ensuring that England and Germany become equally powerful (to reduce the likelyhood of soloing), both get plenty of troops into Austria (so that the forces against Turkey will be split in the end) and that they'll overrun Italy quickly (to bring to the game to an end-state quickly). It depends on the player how this sort of direct communication works, though - I've played with people who think that I'm lying out of desperation or strutting for no reason when I declare intent of future hostility in this way, so it doesn't work all the time. If the player cares about their own position on the board, though, and they can follow the logic of why you'd play the way you claim, then this sort of thing might have weight in their thinking. It's certainly true that if Italy and Turkey both go into wrecking mode, Italy with its central position is much more likely to become surrounded and die long before the northerners manage to breach Turkey.

      Basically, what I'm saying is that I don't really see this position as so beneficent that the south can afford internal strife. Either Italy plays on the team or you'll be better off selling everybody (Italy included) down the river to set up your own position in the draw. I'd think that it's a priority for Italy in its exposed central position to keep Turkey happy, but what do I know.

      As for the psychological aspects of the eastern front, you'll need to make the Anglo-Germans change their thinking: if you know that they're thinking aggressively, you need to foil their likely attacks and counter-attack in ways that'll give them a bloody nose. You're in no immediate danger here, so just support the line in place until you get a fleet to Bla, then use this increased strength to do the Rum swap, then start slowly grinding for position. In the final calculation what the Anglo-Germans think about the eastern front will not change the fact that you're the party with the material advantage, it just means that you need to make the approach a bit more carefully while waiting for them to realize the truth as well. If all goes well, you might even be able to cause a tactical upset when they overreach later by attacking when defense would have been warranted. As an arbitrary example, you might consider outright attacking Galicia from Vienna this turn if you think that they might assault Sevastopol. Depends on how good a feel you think you have on their tactics.

Make full use of your sacrifice in not taking control of the central areas (Austrian SCs are mostly what I'm talking about) by encouraging Italy and Germany to fight over the area; don't overdo it and allow England to prosper, but try to keep those two suspicious of each other and focused on the idea of fighting the other for the Austrian SCs. (In practice you might start by allotting one SC from the area to Germany as a fief.) Also encourage Italian fleets to move to the Atlantic. England can't interfere because your flanks in Russia and Iberia are very defensible against the west, and Germany and Italy will find it difficult to join forces to stop you in Austria (they can't stop you in Italy) because you've been fostering resentment between the two. The others likely won't react to your threat too early in this arrangement because the areas I designate as your targets are not particularly ear-marked to anybody else now that France and Russia are both goners.

Just make it clear to Italy that whoever puts their fleets there has the right of conquest as well. If you end up moving more than one fleet to the Atlantic zone, you should take both Iberian SCs and not move any further (think up excuses if you must); if Italy accedes to advancing, encourage him to move to NAO and in general to mess up the English home defense zones. Your goal here is to take firm control of Iberia yourself in preparation for attacking Italy while making disengagement as impossible as possible for England and Italy. Note that you do not need to control anything more than Iberia for your own solo.

      \n Well, often a long game is because either the players are spinning their wheels (this is the case here - there's a history of half-realized wars with no real goals in this game, those slow the game down) or they're all so formidable that they need to go through several cycles of iteration to find out whose vision of European politics reigns supreme.

      England is not realistically taking a solo out of this if you continue resisting him at this level. However, if Turkey makes a decisive move, England might get reneved when the others turn against Turkey. I would prefer having more of a margin of error before making my decisive move, were I the Turk; that's what it comes down to here, how long you can string everybody along before it becomes obvious to the clever ones that you're on the brink of a solo attempt.

      I note that Germany is not doing well at all here, he only has four SCs. You need to either relegate him to the dustbin of strategy with the Austrian and French remains (although there are so many of these minuscule hangers-on left that together they almost matter), or you need to work proactively to restore him. Depends on the diplomacy, but the ideal play would be to somehow prop up Germany, get him into full control of his homeland and perhaps even get him a few fleets. The wet dream scenario would be Germany pushing towards Paris while two German fleets and the StP army contest the Nordic area. This would make an ideal background for an Italian stab on Germany, immediately followed by a Turkish stab of Italy and a wild rush at a solo.

      The above sort of groundwork takes time that you might not have, but you also can't pull the trigger immediately: England is still too strong, and you don't have a good enough handle on Iberia; you need to get into a situation where England is too entangled with Germany and Italy to threaten a solo when you try for it yourself, which basically means that you can't start it with Italy in position to throw the game to England. Then again, waiting too long is not good either, as psychologically these situations almost always work out to a stab of some sort from your allies if you don't move first; you need to act before Italy realizes that you have a shot at a solo, basically.

      In practical terms, it would be ideal if you could get Warsaw for either yourself or Germany. Get Germany to Berlin and Kiel if you can, he's being weak for no good reason here. Get more feet on the ground in Russia, you want to have three armies there when you pull the trigger, and they'll help you in taking Warsaw, too. That fleet in Sev can go back to Bla to move troops, and later it needs to go south to join the anti-Italian moves when the time comes. Keeping armies in Serbia and Rumania is good, but as discussed earlier, I wouldn't myself move to Austria unless it starts lacking in attraction for the other Powers - you want them to fight over a piece of ground that you can take trivially with your Russian forces when the time comes, as that keeps them from uniting against you. Encourage Germany and Italy both to maintain their claims to Austria, and use Budapest as a negotiation token to mislead folks: offer to support either Germany or Italy or perhaps both there, whatever it takes to make them think that you're on their side. (Giving Budapest to Italy has other problems, so I don't really recommend it for realz - if only Germany had an army in the area, it'd be so much easier.)

      Encourage Italy to overreach on the Atlantic any way you can. For instance, MAO-NAO would be a brilliant offensive move here. Your own fleets are in nice positions, don't move to the Atlantic yourself before you get new fleet on the scene; remember that your Atlantic goal is taking Iberia and nothing else (except perhaps Marseilles if the opportunity comes). Try to get the Italian into Marseilles, get him into thinking in terms of conquering France by building armies. If you can start your last war with Italy in a situation where every Italian fleet is on the Atlantic and he only has armies while you can attack him from both east and west, that'll be so perfect it's insane.

      If Germany proves non-viable, you need to move for your own solo very fast, but you probably can't attack Italy to do it then. The problem is that with Germany gone or reduced into scraps the game has basically moved into a three-way end-game, and those are very stable when played correctly: you are hardly in position to fight both England and Italy simultaneously, and you're obligated to defend Italy against England to prevent him from soloing. For this reason you need to get lots of armies into Russia, and when you decide that Germany can't be fed into a fourth Power, you go into a solo rush where you claim Austria, Russia and Germany with Italian alliance, and then hopefully crawl over the finish line by stabbing the naive Italian for the last few SCs. This is far inferior as a plan compared to the "take Iberia, stab Italy while Germany fights England" thing, but it's the best you can get in a couple of turns, and the only one you can try if Germany folds. The plan rests on the premise that you can avoid having Italy turn on you by continuing to play like nothing has happened even as you make your solo push against England and Germany; if that goes on long enough without Italy realizing (due to inexperience) how close you are to your goal, you have a chance.

      \n Well, one Italian fleet probably wouldn't be that bad for you, but two could be a problem of sorts. The main issue with fleets is that if Italy has them in proper positions (at sea, in position to take Tunis, in position to threaten Greece), he has much more flexibility in defense than he would have with armies only.       The main concept that you need to keep an eye on when you consider Italy as a victim is overreaching, a very common problem with less skilled players: you want to attract Italy into a position where his commitments (the positions he needs to occupy to maintain his borders and policies) form a great majority of his available forces. This happy situation (happy for you, that is) reduces the flexibility of the Italian forces and forces Italy to make compromises on where and what to defend when you suddenly double his commitments by turning so far peaceful flanks into warzones.

All of these are matters that require him to commit forces outside his homeland and to build units that are not ideal for waging war with you. One of the big secrets of Diplomacy is that any units that are fortified in enemy territory, busy holding onto SCs, are illusionary force: they cannot be moved without losing the SCs that are supporting them. You want as much as possible of the Italian force to consist of these types of possessions and commitments. The ideal moment to stab is when Italy has moved too many units out of immediate defensive range for some ambitious foreign adventure, as recalling those forces is both slow and hazardous, as the trouble might follow them home.

      \n Couldn't you just leave the game unresolved? Seems to me that changing the victory conditions at this late date lacks legitimacy anyway, and if the group is lacking motivation to play the game through, then surely it's no big deal to not resolve it fully. Instead of saying "I won" you can just say that the game was played close to the finish, you were in the lead, but it was left unfinished due to scheduling trouble. Nobody actually needs a full resolution of the game, after all. The play is the point after all, full resolution is just a cherry on top of the process.

      Another similar option would be to declare a three-way tie: it's a likely outcome anyway, and if the group doesn't have motivation to play, any two of the remaining three great Powers can actually force the draw factually on the board, so just agreeing to it in the abstract is a very minor change to the game's procedures. I'd probably opt for this over either changing the rules or leaving the game unresolved, assuming that both Italy and England want to end the game. It does involve helping them along a bit, as you need to explain to them why and how they can force the game to end in a favourable manner, but at least it's a real draw they'll have achieved then, instead of a artificial sham victory or defeat.

      A variant of the above option would be to play a set number of turns and then declare draw between the remaining Powers regardless of SC count. This would be essentially the same as above, except that Germany would have an opportunity to remain in the draw by tactical play. It'd be relatively pointless from my viewpoint, though, as whoever decided the draw deadline would effectively also determine whether Germany would live or die - his survival would not depend on making himself indispensable to the draw, but merely on hanging on until the end. Still, better than declaring a winner on SC count.

      If you yourself feel very motivated to play to the end, I'd suggest contacting the GM privately and asking him to not rock your boat this close to the end; he's essentially fucking up your endgame by bringing the victory conditions themselves to the table as a negotiation item, which might in the worst case turn the rest of the board against you if everybody else ends up wanting to quit while you insist on continuing the game. The only honorable action for the GM here is to back the agreed-upon rules and leave the game unresolved if the players lack the time to finish it. I might also instruct the players as to legitimate ways of finishing if the game were educational in nature to begin with - this table could be finished within a year as a three-way draw, which should be preferable at least for Italy and, considering the diplomatic situation, also for England. While having the GM remind the other players about the drawing rules would be interference in the diplomatic process, I might prefer having informed players who make objectively good choices, especially if this meant that the game could be concluded fully within the available time-frame.

      (Of course, I've no idea what philosophy your GM has on the victory rules of Diplomacy; for all I know he might think that draws aren't real endings and every game needs to have a victor. Might explain why he suggests ways to artificially introduce a sole victor instead of the much more natural draw solution. Perhaps you might convince him otherwise; at least four in five Diplomacy games ends in a draw anyway, so it seems foolish to try to force a solo victory when you want to end a game prematurely. I'd definitely prefer a solid draw out of this board position rather than an artificially induced victory.)

      As for the optimal solution to playing less than full-length games, I do agree with you that playing a set number of turns is very disruptive to the game. Lowering the SC threshold of solo victory is probably the lesser of the two evils, although it has massive impact on the end-game too, as it directly changes the necessary defensive and offensive lines players need to reach to gain or deny solo quorums to the other players. I probably wouldn't mind this too much if the decision to lower the limit were made at the game start or at least before the end-game; in this case you've been counting SCs for a while, though, so changing the requirement would influence things quite a bit.

      An interesting variant occurs to me: what if the soloing limit started from 34 SCs (full board, that is) at the game start and then dropped by one per year? Perhaps drop by two per year to make for a quicker game. This might be interesting in how it forces the game towards resolution over time. Probably not as interesting as the draw-focused play of standard Diplomacy, but if you needed to have a solo victory, that might be one of the more elegant ways of ensuring one in a reasonable time-frame.

      \n My experience is that we always use a timer in Finland for negotiations, and this time includes order writing as well in all but the first rounds of a beginner game. Typically we'd start with 20 minutes on the first round and go to 10 minutes for the rest of the game, except for later in the game we might go down to five minutes if everybody feels that there's not anything to negotiate. (Can't go below five minutes and still have time to write the order sheet.) In practice this schedule allows you to play something like eight years per evening without stretching, so usually not a full game; my experience is that we do not usually play the game to the finish, but will rather agree to a negotiated winner or a coalition on the basis of SC count or position - a "moral winner", so to speak. No big deal when you're playing with friends and purely for fun, anyway.

      If I had to set up a full game face to face, I probably wouldn't go for a long schedule like you have here, but would rather play the game over a weekend: start on Friday night by playing the first two years, then continue until 1912 or so on Saturday and then finish the game over Sunday. This way there's not too much play on any single day, and the odds are that you'll be able to end the game; most full games tend to be over by 1915 or 1920 at the latest.

      The ending you got here is not too atypical, a Diplomacy game often deteriorates in the real world near the end due to a lack of table discipline among the players, and due to a lack of game-closing skills, so the quality of play falls sharply towards the end. This is sort of a fault in the game as a social enterprise even if I can't acknowledge it as a fault in game design; it's not so much that the game fails to close gracefully, but that it is too beautiful for our imperfect world Probably this could be helped a lot by educating the players on the goals and strategy of the game; Diplomacy is much quicker and above all more sensible in its endgame if all the players are familiar with the preferential endings, the draw theory and so on.
