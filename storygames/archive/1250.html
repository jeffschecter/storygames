The Beginnings of Ludology

J_Walton
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Stuff to Watch
Over at the Terra Nova blog (a regularly awesome source for recent thought on MMORGs and other virtual worlds), Greg L uses a book review to talk about the formation of "ludology" as a new discipline and some of the dangers along the way. These are very important issues for the indie roleplaying community to consider.

http://terranova.blogs.com/terra_nova/2006/09/academic_instin.html

Personally, I worry about trying to think of "ludology" or "games studies" as a new discipline, rather than trying to use the methodologies of existing disciplines (anthropology, sociology, aethetics, narratology, performance studies) to examine games. Games, to me, are better suited to be an object for study, rather than a way of studying. When you look at the "disciplines" of, say, Religion or East Asian Studies (two that I've historically been involved in), half the time people are approaching the same content from very different backgrounds and getting very different results. And that's awesome. But it makes me wonder if these are really disciplines at all and not just groups organized around studying particular subjects. After all, an anthropologist who studies games is likely to have much more in common with other socio-cultural anthropologists than with, say, an economist who studies games.

Anyway, some neat stuff to think about.

    Thanks for linking that. I just ordered the book from my library.

    Personally, I'm rather skeptical of the value of an academic approach to studying games, and here's why:

    Some time ago, I went through a phase of being fascinated by Herman Hesse's Glass Bead Game. The term Ludology alludes to this book's German Title, "Magister Ludi". If you haven't read it, it postulates a future contemplative society who play a game called "The Glass Bead Game". The game is decribed as being not only elegant, but as being an analogue for the contemplation of art, philosophy, and life. There's are multiple online communities dedicated to creating their own Glass Bead Game. I spent quite a bit of time exploring them searching for just that goal. What I found were groups of people whose approach to game design was to start by saying "We will begin by creating an ontology". Invariably, they failed to ever get to actually playing a game. The inevitable conclusion (to me), was that that if you want to do something with gaming, you need to start with play. I am skeptical that academics will do that.

    Game theory would be very useful if games were the sort of product you could completely pre-design and, through enough diligence and forethough, perfect "on paper" according to theoretical truisms. We do not live in that world.

    Instead, as artisans, we do our best when we _iteratively_ construct the product. Our first attempt will be shit, no matter how much forethought went into it. The trick is to find out _why_ that first attempt is shitty, and the only good way of doing that is to _make_ a thing and examine it thoroughly. Then you toss it out and make a second thing based on what you learned from that first thing. Repeat until you run out of time and/or sanity.

    It seems like academics want to be able to "prove" a game design like one would prove a math theorem. It always reminds me of that forward on poetry the class reads in Dead Poet's Society:

    "...If the poem's score for perfection is plotted along the horizontal of a graph, and its importance is plotted on the vertical, then calculating the total area of the poem yields the measure of its greatness...."

    My initial reaction was rather knee jerk. I've still got baggage from my own epxeriences with academia.

    When academics come to study games, some are going to do it well and some aren't. Doing it well would include, IMHO:

    1. Paying attention to play, either as a subject to be studied itself, or at least as the sine qua non of games. A theory of games that doesn't say anything about play, or that chops out kinds of play as irrelevant is immediately suspect.
    2. Attention to the theory that the gaming and game design communities have already created, not as turf war over who gets to define "narrativist", but as a body of work that already exists and doesn't need to be created again.

    Almost anything worthwhile about gaming written by academics that I've seen has approached gaming from the point of view of one discipline. Economics and anthropology come to mind. I'm not sure what a bona fide ludology would look like.

    The amusing and frustrating thing about being an academic studying gaming is the amazing wealth of analysis and study that has already been done within the gaming community. There's so much stuff already out there, as Jonathan and others have indicated, but very little of it seems to be engaging with the rest of it. The Forge stuff is all internally consistent, but I read it and then I read Daniel Mackay's <i>The Fantasy Role-Playing Game: A New Performing Art</i> and I'm lost in Schechner-land, with very little overlap between the emic (in-group-based) theory and the academic stuff.

    Gaming scholars need to start doing a better job of drawing comparisons and the different schools/ideas together so that they can be more than a smattering of varying-size theory/study islands in a freakin' immense sea and become, like, a scholarly archipelago. Ok, that metaphor is officially spent.

    My personal perspective as an academic is as a folklorist, with a strong pop-culture/cultural studies leanings. Therefore, my thesis comes from several specific angles:
    1) My own experience as a gamer of fifteen years, going 'hey, this one part of gaming is really interesting!'
    2) Performance Studies Methodologies -- A focus on process, performance context, and integration of theatre kinds of ideas like varying acting methods and such.
    3) RPGs as a hybrid genre existing in a weird place between miniatures wargames, fiction writing, improv theatre, collaborative storytelling, and so on. Genre analysis, in the sense of formal genres (verbal play vs. fiction writing vs. film, etc.) and literary genres (science fiction, horror, western, etc.)

    I think that gaming studies is already interdisciplinary, and that interdisciplinarity is important for being able to get a broader perspective. I think that while it's cool to bring varying ideas together, if Ludology were to become well-established as a distinct academic field, it might lose the advantage it has as a subject area--in that the wealth of approaches we have with people coming to study gaming from psychology and sociology and folklore and anthropology, and so on.
