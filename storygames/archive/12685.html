\n I recently pulled out my copy of Afghanistan: d20 and had a look through it for the first time in years. I don\u2019t know how many people bought it when it was released in 2002, but I was one of them and I was well aware of the controversy surrounding its release.

The argument against Afghanistan: d20 was that it was somehow disrespectful to the troops fighting there to make a game of their hardships and, yes, their deaths. My perspective was that we have successfully made games out just about any war you care to think of, from WWI to Vietnam. Some have been wargames, some roleplaying games, but they were all still games. Did these efforts, too, qualify as disrespectful?

I guess on the one hand I can kind of see the argument against a roleplaying game. With a wargame things are abstracted. The death of one or a dozen or a hundred is just numbers on a sheet of paper. Properly executed, a roleplaying character has a life of his or her own and that character\u2019s death is going to be felt more acutely as a result. Assuming the player is the empathetic sort. Some aren\u2019t.

In a less touchy-feely but more practical sense, there\u2019s the argument that there is little to do in an Afghanistan: d20 campaign. I believe this comes from a perspective that doesn\u2019t see the United States\u2019 involvement in Afghanistan as a fully fledged war, but as some kind of low-intensity peacekeeping mission. War is full of plot hooks, as anyone who\u2019s run a WWII game or the like can tell you.

At any rate, America\u2019s war in Afghanistan has become our longest. It seemed like the right time to look at Afghanistan: d20 and review not only the controversial origins of the supplement, but what we can do with it now to tell meaningful stories.

It seems to me that the first thing we have to do is look at the changing face of the Afghanistan conflict. What was at the time of Afghanistan: d20\u2019s writing a battle to oust the Taliban from power has now turned into a counterinsurgency effort, with the guerrilla Taliban using the porous border between Afghanistan and Pakistan to strike into areas the US and Afghani militaries cannot defend adequately. Meanwhile, Afghanistan\u2019s part in the global opium trade has expanded dramatically. We\u2019re fighting a drug war, not just a war against a human enemy.

Our primary responsibility would seem to be drawing out the human elements of the war as it\u2019s being fought. US operations are increasingly adrift in a sea of growing responsibility, while in the wilder expanses of Afghanistan the people are torn between the bad old days of Taliban rule and the corrupt regime the United States supports. It seems to me that that there\u2019s plenty of low-hanging fruit to be picked here without even having to delve deeply into the combat part of things.

We also have to acknowledge that Afghanistan is something special. Our longest war, as I said. As we endlessly recycle soldiers in deployment after deployment, what questions arise? Is it possible or even advisable to expand the scope of an Afghanistan: d20 game to include the home front?

And while we\u2019re on the subject, what about the Afghani home front? Afghanistan: d20 provides for homegrown character types that needn\u2019t be as extreme as \u201cTerrorist Cell Leader\u201d (a prestige class). What sorts of stories could we tease out of the situation by taking the view of enemies and bystanders?

Basically I think there\u2019s a lot of life and relevance left in Afghanistan: d20. Clearly we could go on arguing its propriety, but I\u2019d rather talk about the things we\u2019d do with it and the ends to which our efforts are put.

    \n Complex post-colonial politics, an ancient and de-centralized civilization thrown into repeated conflict with different cultures, guerrilla warfare, some of the most challenging and magnificent geography on earth, internecine power struggles and religious extremism. In the abstract, that makes great material for a game.

    But I sure wouldn't want to play a game set in Afghanistan in the present day, because real people are dying there. Not historically, but immediately.

    And, while I'm sure there are meaningful, respectful stories to be told with a game like that, I'm skeptical that Afghanistan d20, featuring the 'Terrorist Cell Leader' prestige class, is the game to do it.

    Which is not to say the text itself trivializes the war (I haven't read it), but I'm not sure the stories best told with the d20 rules are the stories you'd want to tell about the war in Afghanistan.

    \n I think this is enlightening as a thought experiment, if nothing else.

    So, to me, that points up the difficulty of using a game like d20 - rules that are mostly about combat and tactics and physical conflict - to talk about a REAL war.

    Most Taliban jihadis are not cartoon villains (ie - what Nazis have become in popular culture). Most of them, at least the ones carrying rifles in battles, are young men, mostly uneducated, heavily influenced by system that does not actually help them or even consider their well-being. They're not orcs, which in-game heroes can destroy with ethical impunity. They're, you know, real people, even if they are also combat enemies of the United States.

    Again, I can see the possibility of a d20 game that acknowledges these things, but I think it would trend heavily towards picking off guards with sniper rifles and knifing sentries and other bad-ass war-movie stuff. And doing those kinds of things in a game about an ongoing conflict with real people seems like a bad idea to me.

    Given that the objective in D20 is to level, and you level by killing bad guys (and even if you don't, the abilities you gain by whatever method you use to gain XP, gives you new ways to kill bad guys), I'm pretty sure D20 is a terrible way of looking at a conflict made of real people, many of whom you're likely to meet.

    \n

    What's interesting about the war to you? (Not just Sam, but anyone who thinks this is interesting)?

    \n

    To me, I care about the social structures that make this kind of thing happen, and the people who are the force behind those social structures. The greatest weapon a social structure has against other social structures is, ironically, the ignorance of its own people of the humanity of its opposition. The US is a Zionist tool of Satan. Afghanistan is a nest of Terrorists who hate our freedom. I'd like to try operating within those social structures, where the support you get from them feeds into the powers you have even when you know they're wrong. I mean, of course that kid with the rocket launcher has a family and wants this invasion force to stop threatening his lifestyle and killing his people. But if you think of it that way as a soldier or Marine, you can't continue. Even when you know that it's true, because if you don't shoot him, he'll shoot you, and if you die, then what about your own family, what about your buddies, what about Al Qaeda, who will probably strike at their next opportunity?

    \n

    That's the tension I'd want to be represented. I'd want the fundamental humanity of all parties laid bare, even as you kill each other in the hopes that your people will survive.

    \n Well, the supplement actually does a pretty good job of providing a rundown of Afghanistan's war-torn history and there's meat enough there that one could run (just as an example) a Soviet-era game without too much difficulty. The focus in the classes/prestige classes presuppose that you'll take the roles of professional soldiers \u2014 which means Americans, British or Russians, depending on the time frame \u2014 though there's some stuff, like the Clergy and Smuggler classes, that are clearly meant to be occupied by Afghans.

    Probably the best example of how the book approaches the topic of US/Afghan relations is in the sample adventure. The mission is similar to what I've outlined before: small unit with a specific target individual in mind for capture. The PCs run across different sorts of Afghani NPCs as the adventure progresses, from an Afghan who sees the American war effort as an opportunity to improve the lot of his people, another whose field has been mined and wants help with that (but no other interaction with the PCs), and a hero of the Soviet war who sees the Americans as no different than the Russians. There's also a man who supports the Taliban because they brought "peace" of a sort, but isn't a zealot. And we haven't even gotten to the clear bad guys of the scenario yet.

    Clearly Afghanistan: d20 wants to bring moral ambiguity to what might otherwise be a series of interchangeable military adventures no different from Vietnam or WWII Europe.

    \n Whenever I run a war-themed RPG, if I'm going for any sort of realism, my overriding goal is to show how much the situation sucks, and how the participants of whatever side, soldier and civilian, willing or unwilling, simply have to deal with it and try to survive. There are people on both sides who are heroes and assholes, and there is stupidity and brilliance - the whole range of human strengths and failings. Generally, everyone actually involved at the sharp end just wants it to be over and to get the hell out of there. But, as long as they have to be there, they're going to do what they have to do to survive.

    If I ran an Afghanistan game, it would be from that standpoint - the characters could be American troops out in some remote mountain outpost or local farmers dragooned into some Taliban warlord's army. I'll boil it down to the endpoint of all the politics and idealism to the experience of the poor guy on the line with the rifle. How's he going to get through the next day? The next patrol? How's he going to deal with the things he's told to do? How's he going to live with himself when it's over?

    So this is what I present to the players in such games that I run. They'll bring their own take on things, playing their characters as they like - some want to be Rambo, some want to be the misfit, whatever...and what they do and say makes their characters more real, and may even bring them into conflict. But then the bullets start flying and all the politics and personal shit goes out the window and it's just about living through the next battle, and what they have to do to survive.

    It's less about the politics for me than the human experience of War, a hell we create for ourselves, and how we survive it and live with it all afterward.
