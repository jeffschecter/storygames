\n I saw this particular discussion, and I thought I'd start a new thread because I didn't want to derail.

I'm currently involved in some brainstorming and pitching for a book that's doing a ton to overhaul, update, and focus Vampire: The Requiem. My voice is one of a handful that's going to be polishing the game and giving it a stronger sense of identity. We're already adding some more story-centric mechanics, and overhauling a lot of the fiddly bits to offer more narrative control for the players. But I'm interested in pitching some more ideas. We've brought this up to more Vampire-friendly forums, but their answers tended more toward, "MOAR OF THE SAME!" or "YOU ARE LITERALLY HITLER!" I figure this is probably the best place to solicit thoughts and input.

So, if there's one thing you could add or change about the game, what would it be? This could be mechanical, setting, or thematic.

    \n The one time that I played Requiem, here's what I was really into (with apologies for probably forgetting exactly what stuff is called):

    The relationship between your Virtue, your Vice and Willpower. Specifically, there was a great thematic moment for me that came directly out of blowing a willpower roll, I think, when my character went overboard while feeding and killed the guy. That was a really "pure" moment of Vampire play, for me, and it came out of that mechanical relationship.

    The fictional relationship between a character and their sire/childe, and secondarily the relationships between clanmates and what those mean. In our game, we put a lot of that in ourselves - I'd love to see some kind of framework that encourages really getting into those relationships.

    Backgrounds. To this day, my favorite part of a WoD character.

    What I wasn't into: making skill tests, primarily in combat situations. And I'm pretty acclimated to WW-style mechanics, so I didn't find them particularly difficult or anything, just unnecessarily fiddly for what I'm interested in in that game.

    Personally, I would re-prioritize the mechanics with Virtue-Vice-Willpower-Blood Points being the primary reward cycle, then some kind of Kinship mechanics, then Backgrounds, then Attributes. Use skills as binary have-it-or-dont-have it things, and just roll Attribute pools, with (maybe) bonuses for having a relevant Background, Kinship, etc.

    That's where I would start!

    TL;DR: the one thing I would do is prioritize the "parts" of the character sheet like this: Virtue-Vice-Willpower, then Clan, then Backgrounds, then Attributes, then Skills as simple have-it-or-dont-have-it, with attendent rejiggering of what causes you to roll what dice and whatnot.

    \n What I would really like there to be is more specific advise on how to use the game. Whenever I looked at WoD games, I never was quite sure what to do with all the cool ideas the books were tossing around. There's some good advice, but the lack of procedure made it really hard for me to grok before running a lot of the Apocalypse World based games. I don't mean the GM moves and such, but a list of principles would be good. Maybe some really detailed sample Themes and Moods with some examples of how to implement them in play.

    I want a really some kind of way to have players connect with and generate background details that tie into the game. I've noticed a ton of little suggestions about how to do this throughout the nWoD games, but it should be more at the forefront. I'd also like to see more discussion about how to set up scenarios, maybe a big list of seed material for motivations, possibly sorted by clan and covenant.

    Really, I'd just like more really solid tools to get some detail in the fiction without pouring over big giant sourcebooks. Have you seen the bits of Apocalypse World that just have a bunch of elements you can mix and match to create opposition? No real mechanics at all, just tools to create fiction easily.

    Some discussion about what the design goals for the game are at their core actually are would be good too. Why do you only get the power to be invisible at level three of Obfuscate, rather than at lower levels? Why do Vampires risk frenzy when they first meet? Why is Vinculum in the game? There's lots of stuff that I feel like could use a bit of discussion to really get the most use of it.

    Oh, and some official discipline cards would be neat! Just a deck of cards that summarizes the rules for each discipline.

    Mechanically, I would like to see some discussion of how to make a truncated stat block. There's a lot of little numbers to worry about right now, and really just know health willpower, and some numbers to base some dice pools on in a pinch would be great. Also, some non-combat uses of some of the disciplines that don't have them would be cool. I'm thinking about Celerity specifically here, it seems like going really fast should let you do way more things than just make you hard to hit, that sort of thing.

    Virtues and Vices are neat, but I'd like to see them come up a lot more, and avoid some of the problems like how, say, Sloth seems to imply characters won't do anything. Maybe even something more like the old Nature/Demeanor system as an option could be good. I don't know if there's a sourcebook that talked about this, but I'd like to seem so talk of Disciplines above five. I liked the idea of them being something each vampire got to invent, and mechanical backing for that would be really neat.

    Really, I think the base system is pretty solid already. I want tools to use it better, rather than a total overhaul. In looking at it again recently, I've been astounded by how well made the basic mechanics are, and how much easier to grasp than almost any other 'trad' game I've seen. I know this post has been a tad rambly, but I hope it helps.

    \n The original thread got me going back over my oWoD games, and triggered me doing a quick sheet for Requiem. The major changes were: no Clans, no Covenants (this is not something I'd want to argue, for most folks this seems to be the center of the game and for me it's the big problem, after 'metaplot' and poorly integrated superpowers); Humanity is not morality. We've been playing on roll20 a bit now, and I may pitch this after we wrap up the current game. Anyway, here are the rules for Humanity:

    Humanity
    Humanity does not determine or depend on your moral state. It is how close you are to losing to the Beast, and limits daytime activity and relating to mortals as per the rules. Humanity is not lost from doing evil. It is lost from being vampiric, and gained from meaningful human interaction. Tests to lose Humanity are made every time a vampire feeds, commits Diablerie, or enters frenzy, and may be called for by the ST when dramatic failures are rolled for Disciplines or other distinctly vampiric activity. A Humanity test is made on (11-current Humanity) dice. Modifiers for the test are as follows:
    >feeding from animals +2
    >feeding from vampires +1
    >tried to resist frenzy +2
    >tried to ride the wave +1

    Loss of Humanity does not trigger tests for Derangements.

    A vampire may only spend experience to raise Humanity if they have created, cultivated, and maintained mutually rewarding relationships, free of coercion, with human beings, since their last Humanity loss. Some friendships and loves form quicker than others, but at least a month of frequent contact to call the relationship established.

    As you can see, I haven't squared away Virtue/Vice and the morality cycle, which may be subsumed in a different advancement/willpower recovery cycle. But it pushes down, down, and the only way back is through making human relationships and spending XP Thus the advancement cycle needs to be carefully considered, and so I didn't want to pull something off the top of my head.

    \n Been toying with systems a lot in the past year for V20 related reasons - everyone felt like playing again - (half a dozen iterations for now, we never seem to get one that does everything we want* right) in an attempt to do just that. Latest iteration goes something like this:

    - D6 based. Hit on 4-6. Difficulty = number of hits.
    - GM Turn/Player turn cycle. You can win more actions in player turn by playing "beast aspects".
    - Three dice pools: Vampire, Humanity, Beast. Vampire is constant, and only grows as you humanity degrades. Humanity is an expandable resource you can only refresh by setting scenes with whatever it is that maintains you connection to human society during player turn (whether family, craft, job - one Ventrue player started by putting his company as his link; been fairly interesting); relationships degrade over time as they are used, filling the vampire. You get +1 beast die every time you roll a 1 on vampire. If at any time you roll more success on beast than the other two pools, you frenzy. You can reduce beast by using beast successes (which then means you surrender to its sadistic/sociopathic/deranged, your actions are being colored and changed by this).
    - Finally, in case of conflict, victory is negotiated. The winning player gets the upper hand and get to impose the conditions stated before the roll, but has to buy back the hits of the opposing player - either by taking negative statuses (hurt, angered, etc...) or by paying in boons. A player can use his boons to use the resources of another player (for example, buy the right to set a scene with his support cast).


    Totally not satisfied yet, but I think we're moving in the right direction. At least I hope so.

    *: ie, what we want is a political-focused game on self-alienation and the disintegration of identity - with a bit of super-heroing on the side for steam-blowing purpose.

    \n You have your work cut out for you.

    1) The Fog of Eternity really bugs me for some reason. Ancient vampires, products of another time, are excellent tropes of the genre and the fog of eternity really interferes with a lot of interesting character concepts. I would prefer that older vampires go underground because interacting with mortals becomes riskier and riskier; that they can't fit in with humans and need to move farther behind the scenes.

    2) Please add in Amenesis from Amaranthine. Excellent mechanic that screams "Vampire" for me.

    3) Use the willpower resource effectively. Give vampires willpower when they suffer from their signature weaknesses, or their vice, without no virtue. Give them more weaknesses like that which continue to feed them willpower. If willpower is lower than Humanity, give them a hard time resisting the beast. As a default ability, let them spend that willpower to cow the beasts of other vampires. That will force them to either engage in their Vice, or suffer from their weaknesses to replenish their essential willpower and stay in control.

    4) You could have social conflict mechanics that tie into the willpower resource effectively. Win a conflict, steal their willpower. Lose a conflict, lose willpower and possibly lose self control.

    \n Fog of Eternity is a cool concept, but I think it would be best if it was something less universal. Some vampires lose their memories, while others retain them but lose the ability to adapt to modern society.

    Amenesis is a mechanic where two immortals look at eachother for the first time and as players, you create a flashback of events in their shared history. Think of the old Highlander tv show from the 90's, where two enemies remember the good old days when they were best of friends, or vice-versa. It gives a sense of age, or shared history and of relationships. Just what I like to see in my vampire games.

    \n Instead of just virtue/vice, I'd like to see more of character's personality to have mechanical impact. Things like goals, relations, rules one tries to follow and emotional weaknesses. It should be much harder to persuade (or dominate) someone to act against his central values than to do something that helps their goals. It should also be easier to control own instincts when they endanger people one cares for and harder when it's someone one hates anyway.
    Additionally, this kind of description is a big help for the Narrator, as it is a ready-made list of hooks to build a story around.

    And a thing I definitely don't want to see is various fiddly little traits that may create combos and result in either surprising effects or tactical optimization. I really liked the simplicity of basic NWoD system - and then I encountered fighting styles and similar things that threw this simplicity away and replaced it with D&D-like character building. Please, keep them away from Vampire...

    \n I would let any 1 on a roll decrease Humanity (in some way).

    I would let generations add dice to all rolls. The older you are, the more dice you roll.

    Yes, this means that you roll more ones if you're older. See what I'm getting at here?

    I would also decrease to numbers of attributes to just three: physical, mental and social. And let the skills be used in three different ways (Feng Shui style): practical, theoretical and connections. The current numbers of attributes makes it hard to use the skills with different attributes, so you tend to just use the standard one all the time.

    I would probably also decrease how many dice a skill or attribute may have to perhaps three and combine a karma resolution (single rolls) with fortune resolution (opposed rolls). A human should have just one die and another one for each human beyond the first.

    The game is fine world-wise.
