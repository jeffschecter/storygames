\n So a thing that strikes me as odd about setting up a Dirty Secrets game is that you seed the Crime Grid with the Victim's name, and not the Suspect's name. Why is that? The effect of this is that it makes it just *slightly* more likely during Crime Resolution that it turns out that the Victim faked the crime (or committed suicide, etc.). If the Crime Grid was seeded with the Suspect's name, it would make it just *slightly* more likely during Crime Resolution that the initial hypothesis turns out to have been true all along.

That being the case, it seems that designating one of the starting characters as the Suspect has no mechanical effect on the game. It only serves to draw the Investigator's attention to that character at the start of play. You could, it seems to me, go through the whole game without it ever being mechanically possible that the Suspect committed the Crime!

Anyone have any idea why this is? Seth, do you read this board?

    \n Hey, I got this answer from Seth:

    "When I was designing Dirty Secrets, I was mainlining a *lot* of hardboiled detective fiction, and one thing that many of them have in common is that the presenting problem that kicks off the story has little or nothing to do with the *actual* story. There's only one thing that we know: the person *coming to the investigator* is involved. The Suspect isn't even necessarily involved. He could just be a patsy that the Victim/"victim" is using to take the blame. Or, you know, not.

    So, you're actually correct that the only purpose of the Suspect is to draw the investigator's attention at the beginning of play. That entire rules subset exists simply to get things moving."

    Cool!
