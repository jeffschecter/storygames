\n I have a very wierd relationship with tabletop/wargames, ever since I first saw those wierd pewter figures at an older friends place as a kid: They fascinate me terribly, but I never really played them. At least not much. I bought the Epic 40k starter box with my brother and played maybe once. (Playing with the figures itself and destroying them to make wracks for terrain was much more fun at the time.)More recently, I bought into Warmachine, again together with my brother, and haven't played much yet either. (The one match we played was fun, though, even though I was the first to be eliminated. Caster-Kill, go figure...)

And still I'm fascinated. Fascinated enough that I'm scouring the internet for something I've been itching for ever since I really thought about them in any concious form: A generic tabletop game you can use with any genre, with any figures.

I think on some level, this is like the search for the "perfect system": Futile and wierd. On the other hand, looking for "better systems" has led me to system that are actually better for what I want. Even just Wushu hits so many of my buttons, it's scary. (In a very satisfying way...) On another level, this search is driven both by my dislike of being bound to one publisher and my annoyance at many of the "mainstream" tabletop games I investigated. There are many ... "golden cows" that don't seem to have much use but can totally go on my nerves. (Like "No pre-measuring"...)

Anyway, I'm rambling. (Sorry.) The thing I actually want to talk about is my ULTIMATE FAVORITE Tabletop system right now: Mechaton.

When I read the first version of Mechaton, I went "Cool design, cool idea, there could be a good game in this". And then Vincent actually went and made that game. Awesome. So I bought it and it has totally stoked my fascination with tabletop again. (And Lego. Really must get my lego washed and ready again. Cats+Tons of Lego=Bad)

But while I'm thinking about playing a game of Mechaton, there's this tiny voice in my head wondering "Could I use Mechaton for other stuff? Could I make Mechaton into the universal game I've been looking for?"

So, that's what this (terribly winded, rambling) post is actually about: Poking Mechaton in strange places to make it do things Vincent probably didn't intend but which might be fun. Hopefully. To me, anyway...

Stuff that totally works for what I want:

I love how building Mechas (system-wise) works. The balance between various ranges and attachments, wether to go for heavy and hard-hitting or fast and agile, One-Shot Rockets, and so on. Totally works in its simplicity and is pretty much completly what I want. While I have this itch to overcomplicate things, I think this could cover virtually any type of unit I could thing of in a tabletop game. At worst it would be easy to houserule additional details, like different Mech/Unit sizes or new attachment types into the game, as things are abstract enough. (I even saw rules for exactly those things today while searching for Mechaton threads somewhere here, on the Forge or on RPGnet, but I can't find them now. Wierd. Ah, found them! ^_^)

The general system, rolling and assigning dice, cover and basically the whole shebang. Again, Abstract enough to cover virtually anything and still make for good gaming.

Stuff that might not work so well for what I want:

The "points/winning condition/doomsday clock/attacker-defender-thingy rules complex". Sounds stupid but I couldn't find a better way to describe it. The way I see it, this works great for Mechaton as a game, it makes things fast, it sets up the battles and makes for interesting tacticical battles. But I'm not sure if it works for what I want. Maybe this is just the "But I want to simulate stuff" guy inside me making things more complicated then they really are, dunno. But could Mechaton-as-an-engine also work without the strict attacker/defender split? Or without the doomsday clock? Would it work for a deathmatch-style last-man-standing-wins fight without points? Would it work for a fight where there's just one location to attack, with the defenders in the trenches and attackers trying to rush them? For an ambush on a suply train that's got to reach the edge of the playing field in X rounds?

I don't know and here is where my lack of Actual Play really shows. Maybe trying to shoehorn Mechaton into something completly different would break the game in painfull and unfun ways. On the other hand, I can't be the only one thinking about this, can I? Has anybody else here thought about using the Mechaton rules in ways Man Wasn't Meant To Play?

    Posted By: komradebobI don't know squat about the game. What makes it so special?
    Well, I can tell you what I like about it:

    When designing Mechs, you give them "Attachments", which give you dice. So for example a gun attachment might give you a red die for attacking at [some Range]. A booster attachment gives you a green die for movement and so on. Depending on how many and what attachments you give your mecha, you can get other dice for balance, so for example, if you take less then the maximum number of attachments (four), you get additional Initiative dice, which nicely modells a smaller (ie. with less weapons/gear/armor), more fragile (Because attachments also sorta serve as "hitpoints") and also faster mecha. Or if you don't take any Ranged attachments, you get an additional green d8 (normally you only get d6) for movement, so you can potentially be faster then anybody else.
    So already the way you build your units is pretty nifty and covers a lot of ground. (There are also a number of options I didn't describe, like One Shot missiles or multiple weapons at the same range, ...)

    The next cool thing is how you actually use those dice. When it's your turn, you roll all those dice your mecha gets you, plus two white dice all mechas get, and then assign them to Attack, Defense and Movement. (Leaving additional things like Spotting out for now.) Colored dice can only be assigned to their appropriate actions (So red for attack, green for movement, ...), but white dice can be assigned to any action, so there's choice mixed in with the randomness of the roll, too. (Like, do you go for the low dice in defense, reserving the high one for attack? Or maybe leave the attack out and instead concentrate on movement to get into Cover? ...)

    All in all, it's a pretty tight design, pretty rules-light (Which I like) and flexible (Which I like too) and enough abstraction to over much ground while still providing a solid framework. (As an example, it doesn't matter what sort of Defense attachment you have, wether it's shields or Armor or whatever. It gives you those blue dice all the same. Or how One Shot Rockets could also describe grenades or maybe even airstrikes. Well, the last one was a spontaneous idea and I don't really know if it's rules-kosher. Still. it wouldn't be hard to houserule in.)

    That the whole thing works with Lego is a bonus to me, but I think it would work just as well with other miniatures. (Though you couldn't splatter the table with destroyed attachments and shattered mechs...)

    Posted By: komradebobThe fact that folks talk about this game in particular makes me think that it's either some serious big shit advance (which would be awesome) that needs to get looked at more closely or really there is more than a bit of parochialism at play (which would be saddening).
    \nI don't think that it's parochialism at play. Rather. Mechaton is cool because it does a fairly good job of abstracting real-world combat issues. I remember reading about Vincent's design process on his blog, where there were various people (Sydney Freeburg and Ralph Mazza spring to mind) bringing up actual combat issues. So, for example the spotting rules reflect some of this discussion, as do the initial setup rules, where one attacker is placed so as to have been "sighted" by the defender. The rules are simple, but they are not dumbed down.

    There's also the Lego aspect of the game. Like most minis games, there's a "toy" factor involved. But, unlike most minis games, where the visual appeal is the result of painting and whatnot, Mechaton's visual appeal is the result of your skill (or lack thereof) at Lego modeling. I am not a good Lego modeler, by the way.

    Also, I think that the unit activation system is nifty. Essentially, if you shoot at an enemy mech that hasn't gone yet, it takes its turn immediately. So, sometimes you might not want to shoot at a certain unit, simply because you don't want it to get its turn yet.

    Finally, the victory points system balances out armies without appealing to point costs. Essentially, each mech or "station" (aka Take and Hold Point) is worth a certain number of VPs to each player. If you have more mechs or more attachments on your mechs, you gain fewer VPs from capturing stations and get the disadvantaged setup at the beginning of the game. So, there can be an advantage to taking a smaller army, because you are automatically granted a handicap.

    If you have any interest in minis games with quick setup and clever rules, then you should pick it up. The PDF is only $6 at Vincent's website.

    \nIt's funny, but I have been giving a little time to trying to come up with a mod for a spaceship version of mechaton. And I had assumed that I would change the rules, basically because my son has these sets of legos for ships, and I wanted to make the rules work with those. Plus some ideas about things like "Hull" and such that I wanted to put in.
    \n
    \nI hadn't considered for a moment using the system pretty much as written (just creating spaceships, rather than mechs). But I think I should give it a try.
    \n
    \nAs for parochialism... yes and no. Mechaton is, in fact, sorta "indie" in that it's not like most miniatures rules, where they assume that you will want to dedicate a day to playing the game with as many miniatures as you have and can reasonably fit on a table. Like I frequently do with Warhammer 40K. It appeals to us, I think, because it challenges the idea that a miniatures game has to be this huge investment of time and effort, and needs to have endlist lists of equipment and such.
    \n
    \nIt's not the first system to do this, BTW. The urge to go in such directions often rears it's head in minis. Take, for example, the recently popular game "Hordes of the Things" (AKA HOTT). Which uses a generic system to enable people to use whatever miniatures they have on hand, and has one system to cover them all.
    \n
    \nOne difference here, however, is that HOTT isn't really all that well designed (produces fascinating tactics with the flying creatures and their travel patterns, for instance).
    \n
    \nSo, in addition to Mechaton being indie-ish, it's also better than a lot of the systems one can find out there. So, while some may be playing it because it's all they've encountered, I play it because it's really one of the better systems that I've come across.
    \n
    \nTo say nothing of the fact that it uses Legos, and does it better than any other Lego system that I've seen.
    \n
    \nSo it's really a combination of things. Mechaton isn't really spectacularly better than other miniatures games out there, but it's functional, fun, locally available, fast to play, and about legos. Until something else comes along that presents itself as well, I'm not surprised that it'll be popular around these parts.
    \n
    \nThe real test is whether, since it's not all that "dirty hippie," whether or not it'll get any traction in the larger game market. And I think it's got a good shot.

    Posted By: Mike Holmes
    It's funny, but I have been giving a little time to trying to come up with a mod for a spaceship version of mechaton. And I had assumed that I would change the rules, basically because my son has these sets of legos for ships, and I wanted to make the rules work with those. Plus some ideas about things like "Hull" and such that I wanted to put in.

    I hadn't considered for a moment using the system pretty much as written (just creating spaceships, rather than mechs). But I think I should give it a try.
    I think Mechaton works great for other stuff because it, in a way, a pretty abstract game. It doesn't simulate much, at least not in the very concrete way most wargames try to do. So, for example, ranges attachments don't have varous stats, they don't crit, they don't really have varying ranges (At least not beyond the three ranges at attack attachments fit in.) and they don't really have varying damage. Ultimately, they just help you damage other mechs by increasing your chance for a high roll.

    \n Komradebob:

    The cool thing about Mechaton is the white dice. On your mech's turn, you roll all your dice at once and up front: red for attack, green for movement, blue for defense, yellow for surveillance/communication, and white for wild. From your roll you choose one die to be your defense number (a blue or a white), one to be your movement (a green or a white), one to be your attack (a red or a white) and one to be your spotting (a yellow or a white).

    The white dice mean that you get to constantly prioritize and reprioritize. Say you roll a red 4, a blue 1, and a white 6. If you use the white 6 for defense, then that leaves you with a 4 for your attack, which is okay but pretty weak. But if you use the white 6 for your attack, that leaves you with a super crappy defense number.

    Every turn, you get to weigh your mech's immediate objective against the realities of your roll and make real choices about how to follow through.

    That's what I think's cool about the game.

    -Vincent

    Posted By: komradebobNow what's the Doomsday Clock bit? I keep seeing references to that, but don't have a clue. Is it a way to up the tension by making a game more likely to come to a close, but it's voluntarily altered by players rather than being a simple "Turn track"?
    Pretty much. At the start of the game, the Doomsday Clock (a d12 or d20) is set to eleven. Then, at the end of every turn, it gets ticked one down, with every player (in descending order of the current score) having the option to tick it down another point. When it's at zero, the game is over and the player with the highest score wins.

    Uh, that was me in the last quote there, not Vincent.

    \n

    Well, my feeling about the first playable version of Mechaton was that:

    \n
      \n
    • You built your guys to be as awesome as possible, then marched toward each other, shooting. Every game was like that.
      • \n
    • It was a resolution system, but it doesn't resolve anything.
      • \n
    • There was no reason to do one thing over another.
      • \n
    \n

    So I sez to Vincent, I sez, "You've got this mecha game, but I'm gonna make one that's all about taking objectives!"

    \n

    So I made one, and it was boring, and he added objectives to Mechaton in a weekend.

    \n

    And I was, like, "Oh."

    \n

    (Actually, I think it was more than that. It's on the blog. We talked about it a bunch, with Ralph and Sydney giving a lot of input.)

    \n

    See, the thing is, if you're just playing to the death, there's a death spiral problem, which is frustrating and boring \xe2\u20ac\u201d if it's obvious that I'm going to win or I'm going to lose, then why are we still playing?

    \n

    Let's say I've got four guys and you've got three. They're all equally awesome. I'm totally gonna win. With firearms, fatalities go up as the square of the differences in numbers, if I recall (Can someone give me a ref and maybe correction on this? I think Sydney pointed it out). Unless I'm spectacularly unlucky \xe2\u20ac\u201d and, sure that happens \xe2\u20ac\u201d you're just going to crumble. At first, the matchup is 1:1, 1:1, 2:1. Then one of your guys goes down. Then it's 2:1, 2:1. Then another of your guys goes down. Let's say you take one of my guys down, too. Then it's 3:1.

    \n

    If we start with 4:4, it just means that there's a prelimenary phase until someone gets the advantage by dumb luck.

    \n

    With objectives, it gets riskier and riskier to try to win, but it's possible for much longer in the game. You have interesting choices for most of the game and the game gets more and more tense as it goes. I've rarely seen a game of Mechaton where there wasn't some last-turn flailing for points that would totally make a difference in the course of the game \xe2\u20ac\u201d if not in the winner, then at least in the spread.

    Can't tell if that's tongue-in-cheek, but, yeah, it's a wargame for non-wargamers. It has nothing to do with who designed it (that is, i was interested long before i found out who wrote it, and, given my very mixed opinion of DitV, i wouldn't've bought it on the designer's name alone, anyway). Though, in all honesty, i can't tell you how much of it is the mechanics, and how much is that i get to play with Legos. I'll put up with an awful lot if it involves Legos.

    For me, at least, what's so special about the game is that it uses Legos, and it's not terribly fiddly. That is, it's pretty abstracted, at least compared to most wargames i've looked at. There are only 6 sorts of attachments, and mechs have at most 4 attachments, with fairly limited options for duplicate/redundant attachments. So, mechanically speaking, there's a fairly limited pallette of possible mechs. But, since it's Lego, you can have near-infinite variation in the fluff (and the appearance), it just doesn't complex-up the crunch. For some, that would be a bug. For me and mine, it's a feature. It means i can have mechs with a high-intensity laser, an X-47 rifled railgun, and a semi-automatic rapid-fire tungsten-carbide self-reloading howitzer with satellite-assisted targetting--and each one's just an artillery-range weapon attachment. Plus, it uses Legos.

    Likewise, the interactions of the various dice (attack, defense, spotting, etc.) and the few other rules (cover, line-of-sight, movement, etc.) are for the most part straightforward and simple, and the strategic options, mechanically speaking, are fairly limited, once you've got your mechs built. Most of what there is in terms of strategy comes in in building your mechs in the first place. So those of us whose teeth itch at the mere mention of 'wargame' aren't frustrated by Mechaton. It actually reminds me of Settlers of Catan more than most wargames--a similar level of complexity, and, similarly, neither skill/experience nor initial conditions seem to dominate the game. The more-experienced player will tend to do better, but not drastically so. So the less-experienced players still have fun. But there is a point to careful mech construction and strategic play, so the more experienced players don't feel like there's no point to playing well.

    Don't really no what the "hippy gamers" thing has to do with it. I don't think "hippyness" can be much of a measure of crunchiness, seeing as how most of the Forge-inspired games are all mechanics, with everything of import (by intention) tied to the mechanics.

    And, did i mention that Mechaton uses Legos?

    Seriously, if you hate Legos, and enjoy crunchy [war]games, Mechaton is probably not for you.
