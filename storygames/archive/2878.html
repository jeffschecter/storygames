\n With all this talk about the difference between practice game design and publication game design, I figured I ought to offer my view of practicing game design:

Your first dozen completed designs will suck. Your second dozen will suck less. By your third dozen, you might consider asking money for it.

And if you are really good or really lucky, some of your first two dozen designs may be worth publishing - but they will still suck, especially in comparison with what you can do later. To put this in a possibly more positive sense, each time you design you will get better at it, and your designs will be better. Just because your newest design is better than all your previous ones doesn't mean that you've achieved your pinacle, it means you are going in the right direction.

This is why my first and most important piece of advice on how to make your game better is to design a few others first, and go back to your magnum opus later.

- Mendel S.

    A corollary to the dozens rule:

    \n

    Learn to Kill Your Children.

    \n

    In fact, eat them alive.

    \n

    Let's say, in your first dozen games, you came up with an idea that really intrigued you. But it's part of your first dozen, so it sucks. What can you do? You could tweak it, but since it's part of your first dozen, it sucks lots and lots. You will never fix the suckitude by mere tweaking.

    \n

    If you want to save the good stuff, you need to gut the game. Kill your misshapen baby. It's an abomination. It should not live. Just take the one or two sentences that were good and trash the rest. Make a new game using those one or two sentences.

    \n

    Why is this a corollary? Because your first dozen games suck because you are clinging to misshapen ideas. It takes one dozen games to loosen your grip on your hideous abortions a little, and another dozen before you finally let go.

    \n

    Another corollary:

    \n

    Design Early and Often.

    \n

    Design a dozen games. Show them to people. Let them tell you why they suck. If any of them miraculously get good reviews, try to play them and find out the horrible truth. Then design another dozen games. Show them to people. Play them. Learn why your games still suck. Do this all as quickly as possible and get it out of your system.

    \n

    Hmmm. Now I have to go back and count how many games I've designed, to see where I am now...

    Posted By: jhkimIn any case, the counter-argument would be that if you have 24+ complete RPG designs under your belt, you won't know very much about how any of them play compared to that other designer who has two designs that she plays a ton of and develops.
    \nThat dips into one of the major fallacies of RPG design. More effort on designing a game does not necessarilly make the game better. The major problem is that after a very short while, revisions to your game will be random as far as the effectiveness of the game - because even with lots of playtesting you won't know what to look at because everything is seen through the context of that one game. If you spread your development and playtesting across multiple games you begin to see the bigger picture. Which lets you make better decisions in your design.

    To put it another way, one of the most important rules of design is to have reasons behind your design decisions. This means, being able to ask what about your design goals for this game compel you to make these decisions (whether it's mechanical, setting, or even social context). With only a handful of games the main reason to make the decision is "because that's what RPGs do." But when you've encountered that decision before and taken other paths, you become better able to make those judgements.

    Game design is a skill. There is a limit to how good you can become at that skill by working on just one or two instances. You need to diversify and experiment. Certainly reading diverse games and listening to diverse game designers can help. But there is quite a bit that isn't immediately obvious in a particular design or that can't easily be related. And ultimately you can't know if you've understood the concepts until you've tried to use them, and that requires test designs.

    On another note, there are always outliers. Those we often see published. But here's the thought, if someone's 8th game is that good, there is a decent chance that by the time game 28 comes around it will be truly worthy of the term "awesome".


    Ryan, I mean playably (although perhaps not well) completed whole designs. Not individual design decisions or components. One of the really important things is to work with differing design goals.

    I think the first two are both true about different things, John. The actual design side is mostly governed by the first, and the setting/premise side is mostly governed by the second. A particularly brilliant new mechanic might give your game an edge -- if the system as a whole is solid, which only happens if you have experience. Hard work can improve your presentation of your setting or premise -- but the overall power of the setting or presence is determined by inspiration, not perspiration.

    \n I think that the major advantage that experienced developers have over new hands is that they know the mental terrain: When they've got rules that seem 99% done, but then suddenly something comes up in playtesting and they have to break the whole damn thing down and rebuild it from pieces ... well, that's a catastrophe when it happens in your first game. It can convince you that, in a world where these things happen to you, it is impossible for you to complete a game.
    \n
    \nWhen it happens in your second game, it's not a catastrophe, just part of the process.
    \n
    \nThe major advantage that new hands have over experienced developers is that they don't know the mental terrain: They can set out with a jaunty step, not wincing at the distance they know needs to be travelled.
