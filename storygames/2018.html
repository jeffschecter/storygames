When I first came to indie nar games, one of my first impressions was that the majority of people on the scene had substantial GM experience behind their belts, and that this was a powerful influence towards trends in design like redistributed responsibility among the group to share traditional GM roles like setting creation, scene framing, GM authority etc.

My local gaming scene is a people with widely mixed experience, who all came to gaming in different ways, but most of which met each other through LARP (Some of those folks still LARP, but the biggest body of game that happens these days is tabletop). Of this group, about half of the players have little or no GM experience at all. In my experience, this has been one of the biggest obstacles that I've faced in introducing indie nar games to this group: many games take the ability to GM for granted, and require a set of skills or abilities that non GM players have never tried to use.

Until very recently, I was right there with them. My GMing experience can be listed on one hand, with some digits left over: I tried to run a game of Tribe 8 for one, then two, then three players, managed to get about five sessions out before the game fell apart. I ran a game of Tribe 8 for two players, one of which was the focus character that was moderately more successful, and finally, I have been running a solo nar game for Brand using a patched version of Unknown Armies. This last one is the first game I consider successful, and the only one that has run long term. Just last month, we realized that the game has been going for over a year, and that we've had more than 20 sessions in play averaging about 5 hours of game in each session, and Brand is loving it (or so he says) and the feedback from the people I bounce ideas off of it, point to me doing something rightish.

It's only in second half of those that I feel that I'm in a real kind of GM stride, and have begun, in small bits and pieces to have fun while I do it. I've discovered that I kick ass at building and working a relationship map, and much to my amazement, that to some extent, and with some well hones skills, GM's can run immersively.

I think I'm going to start a couple of threads on this, but for this first one, I'm calling out all the people who are in or have been in the same boat that I have. If you have very limited or no GM experience and have never really felt you owned the title of GM, I want to hear from you.

  • Do you play hippy games, and if so, do you think that you have had a different experience with them than others because you have not historically been a GM?
  • Have you played any hippy games where traditional GM responsibilities were shared by all players in lieu of a GM (E.g. Polaris)? If so, do you feel the game supported you with the new functions you had to fulfill, or do you feel it just assumed you had the skills to do it?

Could you unpack it a little more? Have you had any experience with any game that supports this better than others?

    That is, selecting opposition that provides the appropriate amount of resistance, in an interesting way. Like, to make a D&D analogy, you simply do not send a 1st-level party to fight a dragon, of any age. It will be a slaughter in two rounds or less, and the players will be bored.

    Similarly you do not send regular rank-and-file skeletons up against 20th level characters; it'll be a slaughter in the other direction, and not engage the players tactically.

    And, by the same token, you take the characters' abilities into account - you get a lot more tactical interest from flying opponents if you have some characters that can also fly or use ranged attacks.

    D&D in its various versions has done things to support it, but its mechanical complexity makes its calibration tools weak at the knees. They function as weak-voiced suggestions, rather than confident advisors.

    Not to get sidetracked in the conversation, but just as an aside. Brand gave me some guidance on building maps (this may have come from from somewhere else, so nobody dogpile me for mis-crediting) Is to throw out a bunch of characters and then find one of four reasons for them to be involved: by Blood , Violence, Money or Sex. If they can't be connected by any of those things, then they don't get connected at all. Then the characters are given goals that they want in the world and what they want from the PC (kind of like town creation in Dogs), and an obsession a noble and a fear stimulus like in Unknown Armies. The goals are optimally contradictory between the NPC's and the stimuli often are contradictory in the characters, to make demanding, unreasonable, broken, deperate people who will push and pull on the PC as dynamically as possible.

    Then in each game, I take a look at what the PC did last session and look for repercussions along the Blood, Violence, Money or Sex lines that I've connected, and throw the repercussions out at the player, and play the PC's as hard as I can.Optimally I like to have two or three small localities on the Relationship map so that one episode will focus in one area, and that area causes just enough of a ripple to get the next locality involved for the next session. Hope that makes sense.

    What did Agon do to support you in a way you thought was critical?

    I've said this before, but for the purposes of the thread - the Antagonist has a resource, Strife, that he uses to create all the challenges the players face. Simply keeping an eye on my Strife pool helps me know where I am in the game; I generally pre-create some large challenges in the forms of NPCs or extended conflicts, and then I can sort of fill out the interstices with mid-size and smaller conflicts on the fly. Meanwhile, the remaining Strife pool, and the prepped encounters that I haven't used yet, help me to be able to say 'this is about the middle of this adventure' or 'this is the end' or whatever.

    So, that's the pacing element.

    The 'fit opposition' thing comes from the way Strife is calculated and how single encounters are constrained, based on the characters' abilities. This means that I know where the ceiling of challenge should be, and from there it's much easier to eyeball medium and small challenges than if I didn't have any reference points at all.

      I spent most of my 'career' as a gamer on the player side of the equation. The few times I actually ever got round to the other side I, well, was never very satisfied by the results. I felt like I had no real grasp of how to respond to the players' innovation in a substantive way and would get kind of frustrated (important to note that virtually all these efforts happened in middle or high school and that I am a wickedly introverted person). I felt I just wasn't 'creative' enough.

      Fast forward to a D&D campaign in the last five years. The game went through some real spats over gaming agendas and I did my share of negotiating between people involved. Over the course of those negotiations, I started to get a good sense of what people around me liked and didn't like about different GM styles. Slowly, slowly, I began to warm up to the idea that I could run something. Then, I jumped in, taking over that longterm campaign. And I was very satisfied with myself and the game. All that said, when I discovered Story Games, I was overjoyed because I saw a clear direction for what I had cobbled together to be carried a little further. My thinking was very much "wow, if you start getting two or three or four people all doing what I was doing as a GM, then how fun would that be?" Now, clearly, there are some real flaws in that notion when spread too broadly.

      So what was I doing that made me really get into GM'ing and then get really buzzed about 'hippy' games?

      --attending to the interests of the player, not just beforehand, but during the game, modulating events to adjust to what they were responding to, to what excited them. More than once, a player would totally invest in an element that was mere color, but once they did, it became a key element, something they could take hold of. I can keenly remember those occasions where I failed to do this and how it made the game less fun. This skill is clearly all over hippy games, and something that most non-GM's don't intuitively latch onto.
      --prepping beforehand, imagining and toying with different directions for the sessions, different sorts of characters that might ome into play, and then being perfectly happy to let that all go away when some other element came to the fore. That pre-game engagement helps a lot to 'tune in' but can become a real hindrance if you take it as the 'truth' about the world. Some very real failures in the game I did run happened because I decided to run a module--which made to easy for me to just be lazy and take it all at face value, which left me less up to improvisation and response. This is probably less 'all over' the hippy games, but still implicitly present, and intimately related to the adjusting to your fellow players. Most mainstream settings with their emphasis on faux history, actually discourage this, encouraging players and GM's to 'stick to the canon.' First thing I did when I took control of the campaign was to entirely recontextualize the setting so there could be no canon.
      --And recontextualizing is a big, valuable GM skill which pays off in hippy games. Take an action, then think about how that action can be seen in different ways. This is the beginning, I think, of interesting scene framing. Don't just show some action, show the value of the action, and let there be room for the scene to 'pan out' so that value becomes something entirely different. Where an action that looks cruel is actually kind, where secrecy is not evil but careful.

      The funny thing about all these modes of interacting with the players, the game, the setting--I used them as a player but they never got me anywhere because the 'players' weren't given the sort of access to the game required to use them well. I GM'ed because this was the easiest way to access this fun...to date, still may be, but I still have a fairly shallow pool of properly 'hippy' games, so I am still holding out a lot of hope in that regard;).

      What do I like as a GM?

      Constant Direct Participation: When I GM I usually end up involved, deeply and directly, in every single scene of the game. I get to play with every player constantly and without interruption. Now, in most of my games these days even the players stay pretty involved in scenes they aren't in, but its indirect and through table-talk and suggestions and the like. As a GM I get to do those and play hard into everything directly and ICly.

      I am a Cast of Thousands: Related to the above, perhaps, is that as GM I get to play lots and lots of characters. While this does stop me from (usually) getting the directed involvement in a single character that players get to have, it serves my ADD well. I don't have to like any given character or stick with any given thing for any length of time, I can be as many bizzaro folks as I like!

      Hands Direct On Story: In the old Illusionist days the only way to tell a story is as GM. These days this is no longer so. However, even now there is a degree to which the GM in most GMed games still has the most direct hands-on access to the story. The players get to author their characters, and if I'm being a good GM in my current model those choices determine the plot. But all the choices they're going to face (not the results, just the choice) the ways NPCs not controlled by the results of conflicts, the relationship map itself, and large chunks of tone and theme are still well and fully in my hands as GM. And as I'm Story Socket primary...

      I Am the All Singing All Dancing Shit of the Universe: I'm an entertainer and storyteller, both by preference and by training. I've taken classes on oral storytelling, as a Mormon I learned in my young life to enjoy speaking in front of crowds, doing stupid talent shows, and putting on plays about how stealing hurts my spirit. As a result I love to do my song and dance for other folks. In negative ways this lead to illusionism, but even in my modern enlightened ways it still makes me happy being GM. I get to sing and dance and do the juggling act with all the player's themes and flags and be the leader (not the boss, this being the big difference) in helping everyone make them fall together just right. This also ties in with the old saw of "I like helping other people have fun" but it isn't just that I like to help them have their fun, its that I get to do so while dancing like a cat on the stairs.

      Put the Music On and Dream: I love prep. Not the way lots of people describe their prep, with making maps and stats and all of that (though I did used to like that). I love to sit down and think about the PCs and NPCs and their relationships and the whacked out fucked up stuff that could happen. I love to listen to music and dream up bang after bang and situation after situation. (I've even gotten good at not thinking to the "what happens next" part or assuming that even 1/4 of what I dream up is going to make it into the game.)       And for one that isn't something I necessarily like like, but that certainly weighs in...

      Can We Just Fucking Play Please?: In almost any gaming situation where I'm getting together to play and we haven't decided what we're playing yet or whose GMing or what there is a time where we have to sort that shit out. Who is going to GM tends to be a big slow point, and sometimes kills it all together when hesitance or tiredness or whatever keeps anyone from stepping up. So often I just step in. That way that whole thing goes away quickly and we can actually get to playing.

The reason for that is, honestly, I would rather GM than play 90% of the time. I like GMing, I am good at GMing, I take pleasure in GMing. I want to GM in the majority of the games I play. And so the fact that I have to GM in most of the games I play is really an "oh no, don't throw me into the brier patch" issue.

      I do think its worth noting that if you look at some of these things from Ron's excellent recent series of posts about the different types of authority and the difference between authority and leadership, that none of these are inherent to the "GM." If I could be story leader when I'm not GM that may still make me awesomely happy -- but story and aesthetic leadership are still things we tie so tightly to the GM through assumptions (or at least that I do) that the easiest way to get them is to GM. The last point, especially, falls into this category.
