\nThis and that made me think... are GMs and players not really so used to story twists in unexpected moments? Like, designing an encounter and let it eternize because players aren't hitting the right answers or are in a bad dice day? I know most of the time we all perceive this at the table and start pulling our own emergency protocols, like GMs fiat to make it go foward, helpful npcs and clues that pop out of thin air or get gracefully inserted and save the day. Or like players who go a bit out of character to push the story fowards or just plain attempt to breake the world to find their own fun.

Ok, basically I don't mean to rant at poor solutions from any side, nor praise the good solutions and intentions nor enumerate all possible in-game, houseruling and out-of-play solutions at all. I'm aiming right now just for one of those solutions and I would like to know if you have ever used it, to what degree of success and if you ever made an habit of using it.

And that's breaking the fight. Ending the boring combat or dungeon crawling scene with a totally diferent situation, viewpoint, atmosphere. Either as a GM or as a player. Here's my experience with it.

Our campaign had a good start with an scene a la "Hangover", where the PCs awoke in a tabern not knowing what happened... in the past 5 years. They find out their check was payed by an stranger who was drinking with them last night and go to check up their trunk at the stable. They find several strange things, like a wanted poster with their faces on it... as it seems they are now accused of kidnapping a princess. Oh, and on the bottom of the trunk they find the head of said princess. Anyway, they start to run from kingdom to kingdom only to find they have done more bad than good on those five years everywhere and then... well, the campaign started to go somewhat flat. It was kinda my fault as a GM but I still hadn't got a clue about how to solve it.

For the next session I had a random dungeon generated and a couple of events that I was hoping would work, but the dungeon was taking ages to cross... so in the middle of it and without finishing the pursue of an NPC who seemingly had some clues about what were they into in those five years, players make an stop and came up with the most radical solution ever. They started their own cult, took over the entire town, made the dungeon their main base of operations and forged an organization to make an stand against all the kingdoms and powers in the shadows that had used them. Instead of running they prepared a "welcome party" and I found it so amusing I totally rolled with it. We had loads of fun.

Later in that campaign we went into "Microscope" mode to roleplay what had happened in the ancient past on the setting, which also became a really fun way to explain the players an important piece of the setting, with massive battles almost turned into a single roll conflict resolutions. Again, this made for a great change of pace and later we kept changing back from micro to macro management and back again as situations required.

So, why don't we use this more? Why aren't we engraining more such options into games rules? Why is that "conflict" in RPGs has just become a synonymous of combat and competition? I say, let's breake the friggin fight more often!

    \n The key words there are "the story." What story? Whose story?

    I suspect you mean "the GM's story," which suggests that the players are merely along for the ride. As you suggest, this can be tricky to handle. The players don't do the "right" thing. The dice don't support the direction the GM wanted to go. The fun can come to a grinding halt.

    In book advice about this kind of thing, you'll often see topics like:

    * How do I keep the players on track?
    * How do I keep the players engaged?
    * What do I do when the dice ruin the fun?

    There are a couple different philosophies about this. One basically says that the job of the GM is to make things fun, and that the rules aren't perfect and were never meant to be complete, so the GM has to ignore them here and there to keep things on track. Another philosophy says that the game rules should strive to be more complete so that the GM isn't responsible for the fun in the first place.

    Personally, I fall in the middle somewhere. I think some rule systems are just broken and should be improved. On the other hand, I don't discount that the GM is a valuable part of a good (GM-ful) gaming experience.

    I assume, in your example, you were playing D&D or something close. Those games are more or less about fighting things to earn XP, then spending XP to improve so you are better at fighting. In 4E, this was tuned so perfectly that it's fairly difficult to get players to care about anything else. In earlier versions, it was less perfectly tuned for that, so there was more wiggle room, but the reward cycle still strongly rewarded leveling, which encouraged XP-oriented adventures, which discouraged anything that wasn't rewarded with XP. Depending on the GM and campaign, you might get as much XP for being mystery detectives as fighting orcs and looting gold, so there was no incentive to go into the dungeon.

    Now, if your players were having a blast traipsing across the continent and finding out their backhistory and all these (alleged?) misdeeds, then suddenly they are thrust into a dungeon for more than a couple hours, it's no surprise they changed the music. Forget dungeon crawling, limited environments, meaningless encounters, torches and ten-foot poles. Back to social encounters: a new cult, a town takeover, and taking on the world! And "we had loads of fun." Of course you did.

    How much did D&D or whatever you were using really support that kind of freewheeling, continent-crossing play? Probably not a whole lot. I have fallen into the same pitfall as you, throwing meaningless combat encounters or dungeons at players when I run out of ideas. It's because that's where all the Situation support is in the D&D rules. The basic situation of a D&D game is, "You and your mostly-trusted friends are at the entrance to a dungeon, full of monsters guarding the treasure you seek!" If you want to play that kind of game, it will make you supremely happy. If you do not, you will fumble around and make the fun yourself, and the rules won't help all that much.

    Look, at some point you switched to Microscope, which gave you the kind of support you wanted for the game you wanted to play! Enough said.

    Don't get me wrong. I love D&D. I play D&D. I've played just about every version of D&D since Moldvay. I still run 13th Age campaigns, and that's basically D&D. I still struggle with getting the game I want out of it, though, because I don't want to focus on dungeon crawls or set-piece combat encounters.
