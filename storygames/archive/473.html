Setting as Playground

Levi Kornelsen
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
I keep thinking about setting in game books, what it is, and what I like seeing. I see a lot of people saying "hey, I want this, or that", and most of them have really colorful analogies. So here's mine.

I want a playground.

I want everything I see in the playground to make me go "hey, we could play on that - we could do this, or that, or something else". And if it's all matching colors and styles, that's cool; a big chunky blue plastic playground says "hey, space games". It can be detailed - if you want to let me know that the merry-go-round is *not* recommended for games of tag, that's cool; I might do it anyway, but that's my lookout.

But I don't want to ever see a big statue in the middle of the playground a sign that says "do not touch", with the teacher standing over it making sure I don't find something else to do with it. If it's in the playground, it better be fair game for *something*.

And if somebody has a list of cool games we can play on the playground, that's cool. Now I know we can play tag, and hide-and-seek, and sardines, on this playground.

I'm not sure I'm going anywhere with this. Just kind of blathering.

    April 2006
    Okay, first principle:

    If there's anything in your setting that is intended as an "absolute force", I'm not going to find it interesting above and beyond the fact that it does X, and let's not think about that anymore. I don't go to the playground to look at the ground and remark on how cool it is; no more will I be impressed by the "hard barriers" of your setting, though I'd like to be aware of them. That means if I'm gonna meet the undefeatable NPC or super-dude, I don't want to hear more about him than the raw basics. Bob can't be beaten, he's like a raw force of nature, and he wants X. X is what interests me, not Bob; he may be a great MacGuffin for bvringing in plot, but I'm not interested in him past that.

    Second Principle:

    Anything in your setting that you don't expect me to play with - stuff that's just color - should be instead folded around something I can play with. Don't string Christmas lights over my playground and expect me to do anything other than try and play with them; I'll tie my shoes together and throw 'em up there if I have to.

    Third Principle:

    Anything that's in the playground is there to play with *how* the kids want to. Yeah, if we fall off the top of the slides / piss off your mega-corporation / whatever, we're gonna bust something, and that's fine. But don't be telling me that it's impossible to fall off the slides; just tell me how bad it hurts when I do, maybe warn me about it once.

    I disagree, I think there's plenty of things to do in Forgotten Realms that don't require deconstruction. If you look at my adventure hook thread, you'll find all kinds of ideas that nonetheless are right there in the text waiting to be played.

    What it does require is an approach that recognizes the source material is basically juvenile fantasy, to be enjoyed with a grin and a laugh even during its most seemingly oppressive and grim moments. Imagine it as a theater you know well, with 100 different backdrops and 100 different costumes - sure, eventually you'll see them all, but eventually you get familiar enough with them that you can improv a whole show just by yanking ropes at random and sending the hot chicks back into wardrobe to get into a different outfit. The audience has probably seen it all too, but if you're good with formula, you'll keep them coming back.

    It is very possible to work too hard at Forgotten Realms games. There's always going to be something more you can read. (Good call at connecting this feeling to oWoD.) The question in all such highly developed settings is, or ought to be, "what piece do I grab?", not "how can I fit all this on stage at once??"

    What I started to do was to make lifepathing ridiculously simplistic, and try to do more with the other aspects of chargen. So yeah, it was about 3 steps, with:

    A "Born To..." step, which was primarily about social class, with a few weird outliers.
    A childhood step, which corresponded to a D&D attribute: A Strong Childhood, A Dextrous Childhood, A Charismatic Childhood, etc.

    Okay, deviating slightly from the OP here, but still in the vein of 'the setting as something that is an active part of the fun' I have a few thoughts. First off, Apocalypse World gives you some very rough guidelines, and then actively encourages you to not only make up parts of the setting in play, but then to ruthlessly change/discover/tear down these elements as you go, and while there's a heavy emphasis on interpersonal relationships, in a lot of instances, elements of the setting become like characters of their own.

    Archipelago also very consciously sets out to establish some facts about the setting that you play with, and it does it in such a simple, fluid way that I'm really impressed. I'm still trying to internalize its wise lessons (thanks, Matthjis!)

    Shock: Social Science fiction likewise has the potential for the setting to take center stage with what you're doing, considering the 'tagonists are basically vehicles for exploring this funky social situation you've set up.

    Finally, if I may, my own game in development The Book of Threes features an active setting/situation creation element, and my first playtest attempt (which petered out in an informative way) gave me some insights into what you do and don't want there. I found out that a) requiring too much 'setting creation' before actual play doesn't do it for everybody, and b) when players are creating a setting, while they like collaboration, they also like to have some 'ownership' of certain elements (which I should have learned from Archipelago!).

    So, if you are going to go for tinkering with your own stuff to play with a setting, I'd recommend fast, fluid initial prompts, clear ownership of certain elements, and then ongoing detail building through play. That way you for sure don't have any big 'canon' getting in your way, but you still have clear artistic vision to keep things consistent. That's the tack I'll be taking with my game, anyway.
