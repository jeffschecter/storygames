\n Over at enworld a little thread has started up with a very interesting "theory" that I believe ties in well with why some gamers avoid D&D at any cost.

And it got me thinking.

I thought some of you might enjoy the read, despite the rancid responses he's earned as a result of his opinion.

http://www.enworld.org/forum/general-rpg-discussion/251957-why-world-exists.html

In short, the debate is whether or not heroic gaming leads to "entitlement." Is the world here for the characters to explore or are the characters here to explore the world? There's a lot other ways to explore this theory as well\u2026 the risk and reward of discovery vs. getting XP for killing the same orc over and again, but it doesn't appear to be going there (yet).

    \n I think he does touch on quite an interesting and real schism between different players.

    My group has just such a schism. In a recent WHFRP game it was made clear that every niche had been filled and that the government was aggressively bureaucratic. So if you came up with some trick for making money a) the tax collectors would be on to you, b) guilds would ask why you weren't registered and paying your dues and c) people would spring up and try and rip you off or replicate your idea until the market saturated. One player commented that he found this world uncommonly bleak. He effectively sees the game world as a series of problems and challenges that can be solved by quick thinking (usually in the form of a speech and brutal violence).

    Of course, I do not take this view. In fact, I tend to feel somewhat uncomfortable assuming the role of a hero. Partly due to playing too many games in this vein and partly due to personal politics, I would be happiest as a GM and a player to play in a world where the actions of the characters have no repercussions beyond the local level. Any problem that can be solved by a speech and a strong right arm is most likely not a problem I recognise as real and if I don't recognise a problem as real I tend to think that we're just engaging in childish power fantasies.

    I actually think the desire to spend time pretending to be a hero is symptomatic of a cluster of character flaws. I don't think it's particularly healthy.

    \n Jim, I think the argument in the enworld post isn't very well thought out. He creates a binary opposition, "player centered" vs. "world centered," and then attributes to the first a whole bunch of nasty player behaviors that do not necessarily derive from a "World exists for the players" style of play. Whining players demanding loot and XP from GMs might be indicative of immature players, but doesn't not necessarily flow from a game that is tightly focused on the PCs desires and beliefs, rather than being a sandbox. He goes on to attribute to the second a slew of impartial behaviors that have an arguably positive effect. However, there are plenty of negatives that can come from a view that the game should be as indifferent to the PCs as the game world is in the fiction.

    So, I disagree with his conclusion that there are only two ways to look at it, one repugnant and the other benignly impartial.

    \n It can discourage you from taking on easier monsters, since the challenge rating system provides smaller and smaller rewards for fighting things below your level. This will, in turn, slow down the rate at which you gain levels (not necessarily a bad thing for some, but deadly boring for others).

    It also makes the process of evaluating how tough a fight might be much more complicated: now neither the players nor the GM really know if the party can handle a CR7 encounter. Obstacles which might have only been a slight inconvenience to a fully-equipped party (deep chasms, poisons, monsters with damage resistance, etc.) could be game-ending catastrophes.
