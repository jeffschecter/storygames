    \n We held back with it a lot at first, which is saying something since I think at least half our group had INT highlighted. It took us a while to get comfortable with doing it.

    The key for me was having at least some information to play off of so that my Spout Lore felt like I was expanding on what was there rather than making something up whole cloth. For example, the cultists we were fighting had been kidnapping people and showed obvious signs of large snake-bite wounds on their arms. So I spouted lore that the cultists of the snake god used drugs and poisons to cloud the minds of people to make new recruits. That may or may not have been what Adam intended all along, but it felt right, as opposed to some of the earlier times we'd rolled it when it felt like we were just groping around for something to add to the situation.

    If I was playing an ongoing game, I don't think I would tend to use it as much as I do read a person/sitch in AW, but maybe that would change as I got even more comfortable with it. I like player authorship in games a lot, but I still feel some oddness about this particular implementation of it that I can't quite put my finger on.

    \n I think Spout Lore and Discern realities are the two most important moves in the game, so naturally I have some opinions here.

    The core of traditional old-school is that the players ask questions and the GM gives answers, typically starting with \u201cI open the door, what\u2019s in the room?\u201d This conversation is what establishes the fiction and sets the expectations for what the PCs can or cannot accomplish, and how difficult it will be to accomplish. Eero has written eloquently elsewhere, for example, about how we establish what the threat is within a situation and how we go about figuring out the risks involved in resolving that threat. DW shines in providing a set of moves that drive and focus this conversation. Spout and Discern are the core of this. All the other moves do this too in several different ways as well.

    The form of the conversation in ODD and in Spout/Discern is PCs ask and DM answers, but of course this isn't the only way we converse. Players also suggest things and the DM responds. Often the suggestion is in the form of a question like "is there something in this room I could wedge the door with?" or "how much do I know about what carrion crawlers eat?" Sometimes it\u2019s bigger like \u201cwouldn\u2019t it be cool if Vecna was behind this!\u201d or \u201cwhere can I find a +5 Holy Sword?\u201d

    The stuff that\u2019s not covered by \u201cwhat\u2019s in this room\u201d is pretty important. If, for example, a player has an interesting vision of their PC as the latest in a long but disgraced family line, it's extremely useful for the DM to know that. However, if it doesn't come up in the form of the back and forth of question and answer, it can remain hidden for a long time. One way DW addresses this is through its great variety of moves. Many of them involve detailing the character in various ways (like the fighter's ancestral weapon option) that build on established fantasy tropes and tease out the PCs identity and campaign focus in interesting ways (great work on these Adam and Sage!).

    So the new Spout Lore inverts the question and answer form by inviting the player to expound while the DM assents, denies, or fills in detail. This is a highly productive inversion and a potential game changer; even a genre changer. There are some speed bumps though.

    I want to talk about the game I ran at GPNW a bit. The adventure was a one-page outline I put together for GPWN called "Trapped in a Dwarf Hole". The Dwarf Hole is focused on dungeon survival and details a hazardous environment that the PCs must traverse to survive. The environmental threats are lightly detailed, but the more meta questions about the environment are purposely left blank. Through several spout lore rolls, the players, not the DM, established an overarching story that explained why the dungeon existed, why the threats were arranged the way they were, and how to address the issues raised by the answers to these questions. As a group at the table, we invented a horrible curse, a way to remove it, and a set of risks involved in trying to do so. As DM I was pretty well able to weave the elements of my prep into the story in a way that satisfied my principles. That's a pretty huge win for the DM. There was also a great moment where Sage's fighter blew his Lore roll, and spouted that Dwarven Traps were often set off by stepping into moonbeams (that's Elves, dummy!). This led to some hilarious play as Sage went around the dungeon recklessly stepping into rooms because "there aren't any moonbeams."

    There are a couple of issues though. First, the new Spout Lore cuts across the grain of ODD as it is commonly played*. It's not a philosophical issue, but it re-frames the conversation in a way that may be unfamiliar to players, and therefore uncomfortable or disjointed for them. It felt quite natural to me, but mostly because it's a way of playing that my own group uses often in Apocalypse World.

    There's also a mechanical issue. As DM, one of the things I must do is remain true to my prep. This means that some of the lore spouted is going to be wrong. As the DM, it's my job to say "no, that's not true." This puts the character in the position of having spouted something, being wrong about it, and having better information as a result of knowing that they were wrong. There's a fictional weirdness here that feels rather odd in play. Spouting \u201cwrong\u201d feels like failure, and fictionally looks like failure, but gives you better information and thus an advantage moving forward.

    The new Spout Lore really highlights the differences between DW and ODD, and I love that about it. I think it's part of what makes the game new and different. I suspect that if you carefully address player expectations and look at how the move addresses the fiction, you'll be able to smooth it into the rest of the rules pretty well.

    * For my definition of "old school", I look to Matthew Finch's Old School Primer: http://www.lulu.com/product/file-download/quick-primer-for-old-school-gaming/3159558

    \n Excellent assessment, Tony! The inversion you talk about was on my mind when I wrote the move. It's very tricky to construct a player-move that allows some setting authorship, without "crossing the line" of player/DM responsibilities. The new Spout Lore is skating very close to the line, and maybe even tipping over a bit, which is what Lukas encountered, I think.

    My main goal was to make sure Spout Lore was not a lame stand-in for Spot rolls. Spot rolls aren't required in DW (I think they're actually harmful to play, in fact) so I tried to revise the move to make it fail for that use. The DM tells you what's there as the normal course of play -- especially including half-hidden figures in the dark and strange patterns in the floor tiles. Rolling to "spot the adventure" always sucks, and can be tossed right out for DW play.

    But it's a tightrope act. Spout Lore doesn't let the player invent what's in the dungeon, it lets them expound on what they think it means or where it came from or what it might do (as players are wont to do) and then, depending on the roll, get their ideas confirmed, clarified, or revised according to the DM's prep.

    Having said all that, I'm not entirely convinced that it's the right move for the game. Adam wrote a different version which is also good, and hews more closely to standard ODD practice.

    Adam's version:
    When you consult your accumulated knowledge about something, roll+Int. On a 10+ the GM will tell you something interesting and something useful about the subject relevant to your situation. On a 7-9 the GM will only tell you something interesting - it's on you to make it useful. The GM might ask you "How do you know this?" Tell him the truth, now.

    EDIT @Lukas: I've seen that same fictional hiccup. It's an issue, yeah. For me, it's weird to have a move where the PC doesn't actually do something, though. "Remembering" doesn't cut it, for me, as an action taken in play. But maybe I can come around to a new point of view on that.

    I feel like Spout Lore is there to expound on the details. To take what the GM has already given you and fill in the character knowledge where the player knowledge is lacking. Sure, Avon the Magnificent knows all about the Goblin Marches but Adam doesn't. Why would I?

    This is the problem I had, in game - "Spout Lore doesn't let the player invent what's in the dungeon, it lets them expound on what they think it means or where it came from or what it might do (as players are wont to do) and then, depending on the roll, get their ideas confirmed, clarified, or revised according to the DM's prep."

    Emphasis mine. The players are always going to use this version of Spout Lore to say things like "Oh, well, shit, of course these monsters are totally susceptible to Magic Missiles.     I feel like what we're trying to force with the current Spout Lore is better handled by the GM just remembering to Leave Blanks and Ask Questions because if they're doing their job correctly, they're letting the players contribute flavor anyway.

    I think that Tony and I are on the same page on this one, mostly. His feedback seems to fall in line with how I understand the Spout Lore move fits into the rest of the game.

    As for making it a result of a concrete action, well, that's a bit trickier. Really, when it comes down to it, the players are seeing a thing in the game they want to know more about and think that their characters already do. It's the move that bridges the gap between Player and Character knowledge. So, really, what the character is doing IS just searching their memory and saying "Hey! I know about this!" while the player is saying "What do I know about this?"

    \n The actual text of the move is:

    When you take some time to share your knowledge about something, tell the GM a few things you know about it and roll+INT. On a 10+, the GM will clarify your knowledge \u2013 adding details, dispelling untrue facts, and making useful and interesting information clear to you. On a 7-9, the GM will confirm a single true and useful fact about the subject. On a miss, the GM will give you information that is dangerously wrong. If you act on this incorrect knowledge as if it was true, or convince others to do so, mark xp.


    Michael, no matter what, the flow of information is from the GM to the player. The player just gets a chance to suggest what information they want. I'm thinking about this kind of thing, which I recall seeing a lot in d20:

    Player: "Okay, I make my knowledge check about the Red Gibbons of the Dread Mountains."
    GM: "Sure, what do you want to know?"
    Player: "Can we use the unicorn meat to distract them? If we have to run away, are they less likely to follow beyond the tree line?"
    GM: "Yeah, both of those. Red Gibbons are "Red" because they're often drenched in blood, they love meat. I bet unicorn's pretty tasty too, nice and lean. They're also tree-hunters, dropping down on their prey. They're at a disadvantage without trees, so they instinctively stay in them."

    With this Spout Lore, in DW:

    Player: "I tell everyone what I know about Red Gibbons of the Dread Mountains."
    GM: 'Sounds like Spout Lore. Looks like you got a 12, nice!"
    Player: "Well, there's an ancient myth where Helferth descends from on high and drives the Red Gibbons into the trees so that humanity will be safe from their predatory ways. They eat meat and lots of it, so Helferth dies their fur red, so everyone would see them."
    GM: "I'm sure some would disagree with you on the reasons, but that's all true. Red Gibbons live in trees, so you can escape them if you get out of the forest, and they are vicious carnivores who would love that unicorn meat."

    This isn't player authorship, at all! It's the player prompting the GM, but they're prompting them with solid fiction.

    \n Hey guys, how about this. I consider it more or less a rewording of John's move:

    When you consult your accumulated knowledge and experience on a topic say what facts, information, and conjecture you have so far, then roll+Int. On a 10+ the GM will expand on your information, correcting conjecture, pointing out other pertinent facts, and tying it all together. On a 7-9 the GM will correct one conjecture or add one useful bit of knowledge. On a 6-9 the GM will give you a dangerously false conclusion. If you act on it or cause someone else to act on it mark XP.



    Examples:

    Weak Hit
    Player: "Well, we know there's a trap here, since the dead body came from the stairs, and it's gotta be a dwarven trap since this is a dwarf hole. What conclusions can I draw from that about dwarven traps?"
    GM: "Sounds like you're spouting lore?"
    Player: "Yup. 9!"
    GM: "As you take that all into consideration you notice the blood trail, it's not from the stairs, its from the closed door. Whatever killed it is probably over there..."
    Player: "Ah! I say to everyone "get behind me, the trap must be this way!"

    Solid Hit
    Player: "Well, we know there's a trap here, since the dead body came from the stairs, and it's gotta be a dwarven trap since this is a dwarf hole. What conclusions can I draw from that about dwarven traps?"
    GM: "Sounds like you're spouting lore?"
    Player: "Yup. 12!"
    GM: "As you take that all into consideration you notice the blood trail, it's not from the stairs, its from the closed door. Whatever killed it is probably over there. And from your past experiences dwarves prefer sound-based traps, since sound moves well in tunnels."
    Player: "I drop my voice to a loud whisper: "I think we should just take the stairs, traps that way."

    Miss
    Player: "Well, we know there's a trap here, since the dead body came from the stairs, and it's gotta be a dwarven trap since this is a dwarf hole. What conclusions can I draw from that about dwarven traps?"
    GM: "Sounds like you're spouting lore?"
    Player: "Yup. 5!"
    GM: "Well, let is something rare in the dwarven tunnels, so you're pretty sure moonbeams would be the most likely trigger to the trap."
    Player: "Might as well act on that. 'Hey everyone, no moonbeams here, so I think we're safe. Let's just move on.'"

    Conjecture
    Player: "There's Red Gibbons in those hills, and I know of the ancient story where Helferth came down and saved humanity from the Red Gibbons by banishing them to the tress, where they would live out there days. He also marked them red, since they love meat so much, so prey could spot them and stay away. Which means we can use the unicorn steak to distract them and run for a clearing if need be."
    GM: "Wow, that's a lot of conjecture, make your roll"
    Player: "Sure, 10."
    GM: "That behavior certainly matches up with everything you've seen and the villagers stories."
    Player: "So, friends, let's head that way. Better them than the crocodillians, am I right?"

    Action (listing out facts) -> Roll -> Result -> More action (coming to a conclusion)

      \n One way to keep the two apart is:

      GM preps what's happening in the dungeon, here and now

      Player Spouts Lore about what he/she learned else-where and else-when

      GM reconciles the two, matching up where things fit and differentiating where they don't

      So:

      GM: You hear a chittering sound on the other side of the door. (Her prep tells her they are insects.)

      Player: Chittering? That reminds me of the glottal stops in the speech of Kobolds--they say [...]

      GM: Where did you learn that?

      Player: I read it in the Red Book of Gorath... / That's what they taught me in seminary... / I learned that fighting Kobolds in the Iron Hills...

      GM: Ah, well the term Kobold is used to refer to a wide variety of creatures created by Draconic magics [blah, blah]
