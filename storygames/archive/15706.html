\n After our recent trip to Oakland, I was deeply impressed with Endgame, and for good reason. The space is fantastic, the selection of games broad and inspiring, and the staff were knowledgeable, generous, and fun to be around. The store has a mezzanine level upstairs that was filled with gamers all weekend and everybody had a smile on their face. I think I might be ruined on all other gaming stores now!

So please prove me wrong. Tell me about all the other awesome game stores that I hope are out there, possibly in your hometown. I'd like to put together a list of "regional game store champions" that I can give to Bully Pulpit customers when they ask where our games can be found. I'm looking for stores all over the world*, so don't be shy.

* Canada, I'm looking at you. I'm especially interested in where you guys go to buy games because I've had distributors tell me that nobody there buys indie games. Say it ain't so!

    \nIn Vancouver, there are a couple gems.

    Drexoll Games is a family-focused board game store, but it's got a lovely little indie section, charming staff, and a fairly large open gaming section in the back. Darcy is the guy you'd want to talk to, as he's the resident RPG fan.

    Strategies Games is run by Darren, a guy who's both knowledgeable and passionate about games. Their primary things are tactical minis games and board games, but they stock a great indie selection (last I checked), and have always been super friendly with me. Darren wants to see people succeed, which is really exciting.

    The Connection Games has tons of open gaming area, and is a pretty cool store. I went in and ran a demo of Perfect Unrevised there, and they bought several copies.

    \n In Olympia, there is a totally fantastic store called The Danger Room. It's run by a guy named Frank and another guy named Casey.

    The Danger Room is primarily a comic store, but they stock an indie RPG selection. What's always impressed me about these guys is that they are knowledgeable, enthusiastic, they make an effort to get to know everything they stock, and they're just super charming people. They'll ask you to pitch them the game, and they'll ask questions that make it clear they're trying to absorb every detail in order to relay it to their customers. The Danger Room is my favorite place in Olympia to spend money.

    \n I fear very little can compare to Endgame's sheer awesomeness. But that doesn't mean other stores don't have their own unique charm!

    The 20 Sided Store is scrappy, vibrant, in a hip location, with a mix of punk rock sensibilities, and attracts a young crowd. There RPG stock is small but their staff and owners are amazingly approachable and active gamers. They game all the time. Super nice people.

    The Compleat Strategist is like an RPG museum! It's very old school NYC in a convenient location. Looking for an old RPG tome that few people would ever consider keeping on their shelves... there's a good bet The Strat has it!

    Not local but definitely dear to me...

    Guardian Games is huge and every third Thursday, January through September, they have a beer and pizza night that feels like an almost 100 person mini convention.

    Powell's Books is the single best bookstore I have ever been to. Ever. And it's RPG section is insane. They had Shadowrun 1E, 2E, 3E, and 4E plus supplements. And it's not even a gaming store!

    \n In Seattle:

    Gamma Ray Games is located in trendy Capitol Hill. It's small, but it's friendly, lovely, and has an upstairs gaming area. The owner Eric has proven time and again that he's passionate about helping indie story games succeed. There are two weekly drop-in story games meetups that happen in this store, and so it's got a huge story games scene built around it (thanks to the legendary Ben Robbins, who kicked those off and runs many of the meetups).

    \n For the Portland Metro area, I'm particularly fond of Rainy Day Games, which is technically in Aloha, smack-dab between Beaverton and Hillsboro.
    They have an expansive selection of board games, indie board games, weird board games, not-quite board games, party games, puzzle games, mystery party games, puzzles and fun toys (like Ugly Dolls and those weird mini-Lego things).
    They have a good, diverse assortment of the Warhammer minis, and a decent selection of other minis. And of course they have CCGs and such.
    And they recently started carrying graphic novels, taking advantage of all the shelves they've looted from a Borders bookstore that closed down nearby.

    And then there's the RPGs. They have the expected selection of competing D&D4E and Pathfinder, GURPS, Warhammer, and all the other mainstream standards like Savage Worlds and Call of Cthulhu. The main thing, though, is that they are increasingly catering to indie and similar games. Gumshoe, Burning Wheel, Don't Rest Your Head, Fiasco, Happy Birthday Robot, Kobolds Ate My Baby, Hell 4 Leather, Poison'd, etc. It has, in my opinion, the widest selection of indie games of any Portland-area game store that I have personally been to (and there's only two other game stores I haven't been to, yet).

    They also have a good area in back with tables for people to play games, though it is usually taken up with miniature gamers, Magic the Gathering, or D&D Encounters. We try to hold GoPlay PDX West events here, though typically only Chris Goodwin and myself actually show up lately. We're trying to solve that problem.

    \n In the Bay Area we have Game Kastle in Santa Clara. It can't quite compare to Endgame in terms of the quality of events it hosts (there have been some Story Game Days there, but nothing on the level of Endgame's minicons), but it's a big, brightly-lit store with an excellent selection of basically every kind of tabletop game, including independent RPGs. They have a big gaming space that hosts tons of CCG, war game, and D&D stuff, and they periodically have flea markets to boot. The staff is friendly and they really know their stuff.

    The owner Ray is one of the few FLGS owners who contacted us directly about ordering Maid RPG, and in fact I'm going to be stopping by there to drop copies off yet again fairly soon. I get the impression he puts comparable effort into finding other independently done products to sell, which is really cool IMO. I periodically make orders from IPR and spent way too much at Gen Con the two years when I went, but a considerable portion of my indie RPG library came from Game Kastle.

    \n Right near Endgame, just north in Berkeley is the imaginatively named "Games of Berkeley." Not only do they have great and wide selections of board games, puzzles, kids games, wargames, etc, they have an amazing rpg seciton, including a stunning selection of smallpress/indie/storygames.

    Last time I was there, I picked up In A Wicked Age, Poison'd, Lacuna, Inspectres, and 3:16, all games I thought I'd never see on shelf. Amusingly, the clerk looked at my selections, and asked: "I just want to be sure... you have Dogs in the Vineyard already, right?"

    They also have a way to search for stock online. Now, if I could only get them to magically find copies of Shadow of Yesterday, Primetime Adventures, The Mountain Witch, My Life With Master or Faery's Tale....

    \n We've got a few decent game stores in Sydney Australia.

    My nearest local game store is called "Hall of Heroes", and they have a policy to give a chance to any locally designed miniatures, card games or rpgs. If the stuff sells they'll keep stocking it. If it doesn't sell, then at least they've given it a shot. They're more of a miniatures store in general, with a side focus in Magic and other card games, but they're interested in expanding their roleplaying stuff. I know the owner and I've had a few good chats with him. I'm sure I could run a regular indie gaming day in his store if I had the time.

    We've got a growing franchise in the form of "Good Games", who claim on their website that they give game designers and others interesting in gaming a step-up into the markete...but my attempts to cotact them have been left with no response. They seem great when it comes to D&D/Pathfinder, but ignorant of anything indie.

    Then there is the biggest game store in Sydney, probably in terms of sales anyway..."Games Paradise". They have a great range of indie stuff, probably all from IPR. They have been quoted on numerous occasions that there are no games produced in Australia, sometimes followed up by the claim that no-one would wan to buy them anyway. This is a common laughing point among the Aussie design community (most of whom buy directly from overseas authors to avoid the incredible mark-up that Games Paradise puts on their products. The service in this store is another common point of humour among the local game community, most people do their research online and if they need to physically see a copy of a book before buying it, they'll go in to "Games Parasites" on a quick scouting foray to peruse the hardcopy before going home and buying it online for 25-50% less.

    There are a few other stores in Sydney, but they are in other parts of the city and I'm not as familiar with them.

    \n Down here in Orange County, California, I favor DiceHouse Games. It's the spiritual successor to a long-lived store (Game Castle) that I used to patronize as a kid, and that finally closed its doors a few years ago. DiceHouse isn't huge, or a Mecca of indie games, but it does have a great community atmosphere, a buyer rewards program (5% of all purchases goes toward your store credit), a 20% discount on custom orders (and oftentimes, if I talk up a new small-press release and order it through them, they'll order another couple copies and put 'em on the shelf), a friendly, knowledgeable staff (of one, currently), a good selection of used games for cheap, and frequent in-store events. If you're in or near Fullerton, it's well worth checking out.

    There's also The Realm up in Brea, which seems nice and all, but the only time I've gone there was for Free RPG Day, so I really couldn't say more than that.

    \n In Seattle, there's this amazing place. It's called Card Kingdom.

    Card Kingdom has a really big board game room, a mid-sized trad RPG room, a mid-sized CCG room, a puzzles & family games room, a large ccg-duelling room...
    ...and then the really cool stuff: two beautiful private gaming rooms and an in-store gaming bar.

    The gaming bar, Cafe Mox, is (A) the most beautiful bar I have ever been in, possibly, and (B) designed from the ground up to host games and gamers. There is a long wooden table, perfect for a couple Magic: the Gathering duels. There are a few booths with high-backed padded seats, and draw curtains, comfy and secluded for a roleplaying game or longer board game. The tops of those seats feature beautiful, hand-painted murals. And then there are a couple more booths, perfect for grabbing a quick game of some sort. They have a couple good beers on tap, some simple-but-tasty meals, and lovely staff. And since it's right inside the Card Kingdom store, you're allowed to check out a board game from their extensive board game library, for free, and then take it into the bar and play it while having a pint of something delicious.

    Cafe Mox is beautiful. You can have a drink while hearing the clatter of dice and smelling delicious soup being carried across the cafe. You can play a round of Ascension: Chronicle of the Godslayer while eating a gourmet grilled cheese sandwich.


    Card Kingdom sadly doesn't stock indie games, beyond the Burning Wheel line-up, that I could see.

    \n If any of you are ever stuck in the middle of Massachusetts, we do our best to carry a nice selection of Story Games at my shop Modern Myths.

    It helps that we have a ridiculous wealth of riches in terms of game designers in the general area, most of whom are cool enough to sell us some copies of their games to have on the shelf. We support JiffyCon, and have a once-a-month 'Indie RPG Showcase', 2nd Thursday of every month. The shop is pretty recent, and pretty rural, so we don't have the accumulated years of product of places like Powell's and some of the really awesome metro stores, but I like to think we're not bad for a little place tucked away in New England.
