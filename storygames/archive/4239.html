\n This is an off-shoot of the other discussion I just started, I suppose, but it seems to be different enough that it ought to be in it's own thread. Don't worry, I won't go on as much.
My gaming history is pretty traditional, and the same goes for the couple other people I usually play with.
I want to start playing more "indie" and "story" games. We've tried a couple, and they haven't gone as well as I'd hoped. I think one of the problems are the habits we have all gotten in from more "traditional" "mainstream" games, especially regarding the GM/Player divide, and I wanted to know if anyone had any suggestions for trying to transition into more the more story-focused, narrative-focused, player-empowered kinds of story games. Currently my tactic is essentially "keep playing them until it works" and I think this might work, eventually, but it would be cool if I could make it a smoother journey. Fortunately, the two people I do most of my gaming with are almost always willing to try anything, even if it isn't a theme or setting that really grabs them, so I've got that advantage if I do keep on my current path...

    \n Our path was fairly smooth. Burning Wheel was the first step away from traditional games and it's really not that far off. But the little devices for player empowerment force the referee to re-evaluate his role and put the onus on the players to pick up the slack. We used Universalis in a constrained role to develop the setting for that game, and that gave us experience with the GM-less storytelling idea without diving straight in to a story -- we had external constraints and purpose that made it more comfortable for us.

    Since then the barriers are pretty much down. Everyone is champing at the bit to GM some Fate based games and we've run some more daring stuff with Polaris, Shock, Carry, Capes, and so on. We still find that the GM role is important for long running games, but we are comfortable rotating the GM every session in some cases. Most importantly we find that games that diverge from the traditional in really strong ways no longer provide the stilted result that they might otherwise have -- everyone is prepared to grab the narrative authority and run with it. In some cases it has democratised our traditional gaming by side effect -- the old authority balance is no longer accepted unspoken.

    I'd say the order in which we tried these things was simple luck and I don't know (because I only have one test group) whether a different order would be better or worse.

    Posted By: Malcolm SheppardNever understood what was non-traditional about The Burning Wheel. Burning Empires? Hell yes. BW? Not so much.
    \nI largely agree. But the bits that are "non-traditional" turn out to be huge stumbling blocks for some people. I've had more than a few people on the forums for whom Burning Wheel is the first non D&D role-playing game after 20 or 30+ years in the hobby. Their groups are generally very into the game, but struggle with the player-empowerment portions of it -- particularly stuff like the Wise skills or Circles.

    They generally need a fair bit of hand holding through those portions of the game, not because they aren't explained in the text but because those ideas are completely outside their frame of reference. It scares them when powers that are generally held only by the GM are shared out among the players as well.

    I remember one group was made very nervous about the idea that the players got to pick their own goals and were expected to be proactive in choosing how to pursue those goals. For them, role-playing games had always involved the GM presenting his plot and fighting the monsters that he put in front of them when they went where he led them.
