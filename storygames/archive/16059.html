    \n Not game-breaking. All those D&D sacred cows are so much bullshit, I don't even know how this could break the game. Some awkwardly thinking individual sometime in the '70s or whatever thought to start comparing character classes in an armchair theory way, and therein grew a completely unrealistic model of class balance comparison that relied on these types of details as supposed balancing mechanisms. For some reason most people will gladly agree that say the Paladin code or the high ability prerequisites Rangers get are bullshit balance measures, but everybody seems to think that the sky will fall if everybody can use whatever weapons they can lift in the fiction.

    (The above does not consider people who oppose lifting weapon restrictions for some other reason aside from game "balance". For example, it's not an insane argument to say that the various restrictions help differentiate between classes, which supports the game's idea of specialization. I happen to think that this is not needed when you give the classes some nice positive class abilities, but there is an argument there.)

    Of course, later editions of the game have grown around these types of D&D arcana (quick, somebody give this a snazzy name, I want to use it in my book - "Dungeonica"?) like a tree grows around a rock, so I'm sure that 3rd and 4th editions would have real and definite balance issues if you just removed these limitations without doing anything to rebalance. Of course 3rd edition balance is a joke to begin with, so maybe 4th is the only real problem.

    In our campaign the only reason to not use plate mail is if you need to be that much more nimble, light, or comfortable, or if you can't afford it. The world is a cruel place, the guys with the thousand silver guildens that it takes to buy a full panoply are head and shoulders above others, experience level be damned. Even those who can afford it do get into situations where they don't want to use a plate mail, though: wizard types don't want to buy it because they have other money sinks, and they don't want to wear it while doing ritual magic due to the comfort penalties. Nobody wants to wear it while swimming or jumping or climbing or just running. Nobody wants to wear it while sleeping. If what you do often involves those things, or you suspect that those'll be a feature in the next adventure, you leave the plate at home.

    The methodical answer to this whole plate mail thing is to look at the fiction and draw on your understanding of medieval armouring, and push, push, push appropriate mechanics and subgames until the fiction starts to pleasingly approximate your vision. Doesn't take too long, and the longer you stick to it the better your local application will be. For example, I'm considering doubling most of the various armour penalties in our campaign to further emphasize the awkwardness of plate mail in certain situations; I also make a point of the time it takes to don or undress it, so the characters have to actually think about whether to wear it or not. I consider myself successful when armor is no longer a fetishistic identity statement like it is in modern D&D (you pick a type of armor that your character always wears as part of his official ensemble), but rather a tool you use or don't use depending on the situation. Character class only enters into this in that Fighters are usually more adept at using armor.

    \n I think a related but more important issue is to look at the wacky money economy in most editions of D&D, especially the old ones where you have to give buckets of cash out because gold=xp.

    If you tone down the cash haul, tighten up the economy considerably, make wizards spend their cash on research and reagents and such, and make platemail super expensive, then it becomes not really an issue.

    I played a D&D-inspired campaign (but used Savage Worlds for the system) for a year. I started the players off dirt poor and I was somewhat stingy with loot, because I wanted the need for cash to be one of the driving forces of the game. Over the course of several adventures, the players never got to the point where paying for room and board wasn't a meaningful expense. Their armor upgrades came from looting (I'd generally rule that they could grab about half the armor from the defeated enemies - the other half was too broken up by them killing the enemies to be useful), plus I made them pay some cash to get things refit and to maintain them.

    Sure, not everyone wants to do a campaign that way. But if you're playing a game that's "too heroic" to worry about realistic cashflow, then why would you be asking the question of whether wizards should be able to wear armor anyway?

    Oh, and I tweaked the way armor worked in Savage Worlds - I gave it an armor bonus, but also a negative of about half the points that was applied to spellcasting, swimming, climbing, riding, and a couple other things. Better quality - more expensive - armor had a lower negative. The players thought it was balanced and easy to work with.

    \n This is terribly edition-dependent.
    In low-level Mentzer/Moldvay (that is, the Basic set), for example, there is no difference in to-hit ability between a lev. 1-3 Fighther and a lev. 1-3 Magic User: thus, the greater combat attitude of Fighters in such a situation is entirely predicated on having more HPs and broader armament options. In Basic, then, the ability to wear chainmail and plate armor is a distinguishing class feature of Fighters, Clerics and the Demi-humans (also fighter-ish classes) and giving such ability to Magic Users and/or Thieves would significantly dilute the appeal of those other classes. Different flavors of OD&D, AD&D or different tiers in BXCMI may vary on this, though, especially depending on the mix of character classes involved.
    On the other hand, in about 10-yrs playing D&D 3.x I've seen very little PCs use heavy armor at all, because those were only useful to low-dexterity characters, while most fighter-types had a number of incentives to not dump their dexterity score. Medium-weight armors were all the rage, and most armor-using PCs wore a breast-plate (the "best" medium armor in 3.x), with exotic-material breast-plates (mithril, etc.) being highly sought after. Rogues, 3.5 rangers and the like used either masterwork studded-leather suits or mithril chain-shirts; strictly no-armor classes could easily pick up armor proficiency feats, but nobody I've met IRL ever bothered with those.

    \n There's two parts to the argument, right? There's the game balance part, and there's the fictional verisimilitude part.

    Game Balance

    Presumably, wizards have a bunch of other cool things that make up for living with a shitty armor class. There's a trade-off. 3E and 4E designers actually tried to make the classes balanced against each other, so changing this willy-nilly fucks with the order of the universe. So if you're gonna give wizards a better AC, you can either ignore the change in game balance or you can add some kind of cost that makes sense and can't easily be circumvented by clever players.

    Then again, look at Basic D&D where fucking-elves get to wear any armor they want, and cast the same spells magic-users can cast. Somehow, that didn't break the game. Elves advanced much slower than everyone else, though, and you had to roll good ability scores to qualify (remember random ability score generation?).

    \nI think that's a fine course to take. The idea that magic doesn't like armour because metal interferes with the magical energies is just interesting, I remember how it seemed exciting to teenager-me in MERP for how it complicated the life of a wizard who wished to don arms.

    D&D is often very abbreviated and sparse in how it maps things into the fiction. One might easily imagine a game text that gave D&D's armour limitation and mapped it into the fiction by e.g. saying that you can't cast spells in armor or a wizard's dick drops off if he dons metal or wizards are magnetic and thus repulse metal so that you literally physically can't wear metal armor. It's the lack of fictional mapping that's so offensive, it's much easier to take that limitation if it has a reason and is not just an arbitrary mechanical limit. And if the fictional justification rings false and ridiculous to you - well, perhaps that's a sign of something, maybe that original limitation wasn't that good an idea?

    Even further, instead of a limitation we can just put in a penalty. Penalties are better than limitations for many things because they cause conditional limitations: in this situation it is not feasible for me to wear armor, but in this other situation it is, and it's up to my skill to perceive which it is. In this way it's so much more sensible to say that a wizard can wear armor, he just can't cast spells while in it. Or he can cast spells, but he needs to make an extra check of some sort. In general, I'm much in favour of game structures that force players to make choices in the game instead of pre-game: don't choose whether your character is a fighter or a wizard and dress accordingly, but rather learn of the situation at hand and dress accordingly. One of these is a character-building choice, the other is a fictional positioning choice. Entirely different things, and I can't say I care much for the character-building choices.

      Posted By: Eero TuovinenEven further, instead of a limitation we can just put in a penalty. Penalties are better than limitations for many things because they cause conditional limitations: in this situation it is not feasible for me to wear armor, but in this other situation it is, and it's up to my skill to perceive which it is.
      \nDungeon World handles this in an interesting way: by giving each class its own encumbrance limit, combined with how much gear a given class needs to "work", you can steer people gently away from bucking tradition, but in a way that doesn't just limit your options.
      When it comes to wizards and weapon choices, all weapons wielded by a magic-user do 1d4 damage (plus any relevant mods). It basically frees you up to experiment more with your character's Color, and thus get a chance to better "interpret" the character and get to know it better. Maybe wizards just use lighter, more elegant versions of conventional weapons? Maybe they just don't have the training to hit as hard as professional soldiers can? It kind of leaves room for interpretation, since you can mix and match class and weapon as you like.

      Ironically, 3e D&D tried to capture this "interpretive" attitude of mixing and matching character elements, but they went about it incorrectly: if every PC race can be paired up with any class you want, then the theme and color and I guess the "tone" of the PC race choices is diluted. When you put limitations there, at the first stages of character creation, you can guide people into certain genre conventions. Elves can't be clerics, so maybe they don't have organized religion. Dwarves can't be wizards, so I guess they can only do magic if the gods are involved. And for some reason, neither elves nor dwarves can specialize in criminal activity. What's up with that? It kind of implies that only humans and halflings actually live in towns or cities, where theft could be a specialized skill.

      Conversely, when you take limits off what items characters can actually use in play, you can much more effectively customize your character - - rather than trying to "build" your character correctly, which stifles your creative options down the road for fear of "breaking the build" - - by changing how you see your character after a particular experience or the acquisition of a particular item.

      Picture having a wizard who finds a dark, scary runesword with a couple of cool powers. Sure, he can only swing it for d4+2 damage, but it's cool as hell and can cast darkness and lightning bolt, so what's not to love?

      \n (Take this in good humour, I'm just riffing off the few latest comments. Perhaps showing the process of evolution you can take to a character class will be educational.)

      You guys have D&D-itis, and it's making you sell the fighter short. That plate armor is meaningless, it's not where his core competency lies. By saying that you can't give the wizard armor because then he's as good as the fighter you're saying that the fighter is a wimp who's only got more metal on him than the next guy. There is nothing preventing you from stacking the deck in the favour of the fighter to whatever ridiculous degree you want.

      For example, in our campaign:
      • Initiative is important in a fight. You can expend Initiative to take extra actions in a fight, or you can spend it in reactive actions (blocking with shields, dodging, etc.). Have your Initiative lowered below zero and you're confused, which is a dangerous state to be. Fighters get a bonus equal to level to Initiative, and they're eligible for sick specializations that further ensure that they will be acting first and acting often.
      • Attack checks are like you'd expect in D&D, with an attack bonus against AC, with a d20 involved. Roll well enough and you're getting stunts, which can range from your usual disarms and trips to outright save or die with sufficiently high rolls. Only Fighters get experience bonuses to attack rolls.
      • All weapons do d6 damage, unless they're off-scale and cause multiple dice of damage, or they're wimpy (not intended for killing people, basically) and they do something like d3. The feasible way over this is by boosting your attack roll, above, but if you're a Fighter you can acclimatize to special fighting styles (basically, styles intended against inhuman targets that don't reliably go down on that single d6 when hit) that enable you to use a bigger damage die by default.
      • Everybody's hit dice are d6, unless you've got consumption and they're d4. You reroll your hitpoints after every long rest. Most Fighters will be Tough, meaning that they roll their hit points on d8 and retain the better of their roll or their prior points when resting. This means that your typical mid-level Fighter will have well over double the longevity of anybody else in a fight-or-equivalent, provided they've had a few days of rest. They need 10 minutes to reroll their hit points after a fight, by the way, so multiple skirmishes do not exactly blunt the effectiveness.
      • Fighting is a chaotic process, especially in a dungeon; you will often not know what you fight, how many there are or actually anything else, including where you are or where your allies are. Most Fighters will be Tactical, meaning that they do not need to waste combat rounds keeping track of what is happening.
      \nThe above is the result of a completely ordinary process of houseruling on the D&D chassis - it's an entirely legitimate way of running the (old-school version of the) game, in other words. Sticking a mage into a plate mail is basically completely irrelevant next to this series of consistent violence-related supremacy the Fighter is bringing to bear. Heck, if the Fighter really wants, I'll give him a +2 to AC for just plain being more skilled in using his armor. This entire combat-based comparison between character classes is not going to matter, because we are talking about several arenas of conflict, one of which is basically the rightful expertise of this other character class over here. Putting artificial restrictions on a wizard to balance him in a fight is ridiculous: if he want to win the fight, he's going to have to do it with magic or something, as he's not going to be significant on the level the Fighter operates in anyway after a few experience levels.

      In a nutshell: if your fighter is going to be irrelevant because of some armor on the wizard, then you need to be adding some flavourful, sensible mechanical leverage for the fighter. Continue doing that until you're happy with the level of dominance a life dedicated to violence affords to the guy - depends on your aesthetic when you stop. I could see a more minimalistic take where everybody's basically as good in a fight as the next guy, but then I'd remove the Fighter character class altogether, too.

      Note that the above does not comment upon whether the Fighter's many advantages are relevant or balanced against advantages other types of characters have in different venues of conflict. The whole point of the game is to discover whether it matters for shit that your character is the toughest swordsman between here and Naples. Maybe it doesn't matter, because nothing important will be resolved by fighting. (This seems to be the case in 3rd edition D&D by the book, by the way - fighting competence matters for shit because the world is full of magic that trumps violence on a strategic level.) Not up to me as the GM, not up to the game designer - the play will carry out whatever truth about the real nature of power. If you think that the wizard getting plate mail makes the fighter redundant, feel free to play a wizard instead.

      Posted By: RafuPosted By: Paul T.I've never been able to understand why the metal plates are placed *inside* the leather? What's the rationale there?
      \nFor one thing, it makes it harder for your opponent to thrust a blade right in between two scales, as they can't as easily notice where those junctions lay in the heat of fighting.
      That's a big part of it.

      Also, when you put the plates on the outside, you have to attach them in more of a lamalar fashion to keep them from flopping looser and presenting more weaknesses. When they're on the inside, they're being held in place better between the leather and the body - you don't have to be as detailed and fancy about the attaching methods. Consider it the equivilant of a kevlar vest with plate reinforcements - it's easier to contain the plates inside the vest than to attach them to the outside. And it's easier to have the armor attached in a way where it's more flexible and moves better with the body, without needing to come up with super detailed and fancy fastenings of some sort (which are ultimately much harder to create and maintain, and are more fragile in long term use). This reduces expense and lets you put more people in reasonably good armor.

      And also - putting the leather on the outside insulates better than the other way around. Having metal as the outer layer does more to increase heat exchange and make you colder on a cold night. Very useful when you're a guard or some other sort of combat job other than a field combat warrior.

      Also, metal on the outside is noisier. It clanks around. It bangs against walls and cobblestones. It gives away your position in a forest skirmish/ambush. Whenever you're doing anything other than a pitched organized field battle, not having clanky plates of armor all over you is a good thing.

      Also, if the plates are on the inside, the whole suit can be held together by a couple of straps under the armpits that are easy to attach quickly. Once you have stuff on the outside, you start needing fasteners that are more complicated and slower to put on. Donning armor quickly is a real concern. Full plate takes frickin FOREVER to put on.
