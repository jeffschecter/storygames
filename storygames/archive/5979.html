\n OK, take, say, RBH, because my design maps to it a lot. Say characters are made with points instead of level - stats and talents both give points. So the GM might be up against four 20-point heroes. So there's 80 points arrayed against the GM.

By default, the GM puts up 80 points of opposition in his scenario, which he can spend on monsters, mooks, enemy NPCs, and dangers. At the end, only opposition that actually came into a scene (even if it was avoided) counts.

Anything less than 40 points is too easy to deal with, so when the PCs defeat all opposition... they get 40 points to spend on rewards.

I want the players to be able to say "hey, I want X points of opposition" and up it from 40 to X. Bigger threat, bigger reward.

So imagine I can figure out how to price opposition properly (some points for monster abilities, extra points if they have a good chance to surprise the party, etc.)

The problem for me is figuring out how to price rewards. I don't want each reward to be a trinket for better adventuring - I want some of the rewards to be other things the PCs want. But there's this seems really weird to me. Why does taking apart the 100-point dungeon give you more ability to ensure that Mr. Gabban who put the party up for a night doesn't lose the family farm, compared to the 50 point dungeon? Are you just dropping treasure on everything? Is that what gets the princess to marry you too?

Am I trying to solve a problem that doesn't exist?

    \n I think that "story rewards" that are plot developments/resolutions (the farm is saved and the arranged marriage is dissolved in favour of a love match) and "asset" rewards (to use some loopy term some guy game up with for efficient design purposes) that feed back into resolution systems (thus are 'character sheet stuff') should be in "different pools" so to speak.

    Risk story to win story, risk character sheet attributes to win such. Mostly I say that because I don't think a "rate of exchange" between the two that applies consistently across players is feasible.

    They cross over if the saved farm translates into a reduction in living expenses due to free bread and cheese or a +2 bonus on information gathering from friendly NPC's etc.

    \n Yeah, I think you're conflating mechanical rewards (stick of +5 smacking) with story resolution (and we save Mr Gabban's distillery!). I think it's quite possible to, and even preferable to, have a system that outputs both -- however, outputting them interchangeably (stick or distillery?) leads to problems. You're essentially making players choose between a cool story and a cool character, and choosing either sucks if it means not choosing the other -- especially when it's pretty easy to rig the system to make both happen every time.
