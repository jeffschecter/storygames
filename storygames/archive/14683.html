I was asked to GM D&D 3.5 for a bunch of theatre people at the basement of a game shop, in front of a live audience.

\n

Event details

\n

I was nervous at the beginning (I thought it was just being filmed, not necessarily done in front of a live audience). Once we settled in it was like any one-shot of D&D 3.5. We lost a lot of audience at the halfway mark; apparently the players were hard to hear.

\n

Afterward lots of people told me that I was this amazing GM, which was nice.

\n

Next time we'll arrange the room differently so I'm GMing to the whole audience instead of being in profile. It was fun but I felt exhausted afterwards. If you can't tell, I'm not sure what else to say about it.

    \n I would use a different setup from the usual table.
    \nIf the players were positioned further away, for example, they would need to be louder and naturally get more theatrical.
    \n
    \nSome setup (maps, dice, etc) could be recorded and projected on a background screen.
    \n
    \nYou might also want to use hypersimplified rules to not lose your audience. Make rolling dice a dramatic moment, where players have to standup and go to roll the dice in the middle of the audience, or something like that :-)
    \n
    \nFrom doing some live programming sessions in front of an audience we learnt that what you do always have to be modified to pace your audience. More drama, Less thinking, less fiddling with numbers. Systems that never break and always do something.

    \n Very cool, Ryan! If I'd known you were running, I would have come by.

    I *love* the idea of giving the audience secret insider knowledge. Either a display of some sort or a printed program which would describe the dangers they're about to face (in the context of a dungeon crawl) would be great. "Little do the heroes know that in the room behind the altar there is fire demon [...] the main threat is that the demon has a powerful weapon which [...] how will they find a way past the Ogre on the bridge, an opponent far too dangerous for them to fight on even terms?" Etc.

    That would give people who have no clue about roleplaying some sense of what to watch for and what's interesting.

    System-wise and presentation-wise, some new design would definitely add to the drama of the proceedings. For example, some kind of central raised table with dice on it, which players would have to approach in order to roll, would make a big difference. "Ok, Mark, step on up to the dice!"

    I love the idea of doing "confessionals" or similar asides to the audience. Perhaps, like a sporting event, you could have expert commentary, where commentators explain especially smart (or especially stupid) tactical decisions by the players. Or even moments where a commentator pauses play to interview one of the players. "You're in a bit of a bind. You don't have your sword, and there are two dire wolves chasing you. What are you thinking right now?"

    But, fantasy aside, I want to hear more about how it went.

    Ryan, from your side, what were things you found yourself doing differently as a GM because of the setup?

    What were things the players did that might have been different from a "typical" session? It sounds like some of them, at least, were actors or other public persons.

    Did the audience seem engaged? I can't imagine people watching a totally "regular" session of D&D and being all that interested.

    We had a few artists around that were doing little sketches of stuff from our game. Looked kind of like a Tony Dowler thing, but thinner lines. That was pretty sweet. I think that helped.

    \n

    There was definitely tension between the goal of showing what D&D is and making it fun for the audience. The "show" of it was really Aislinn's creature(the co-ordinator), and I was brought in specifically to GM 'authentic' D&D. I know I ran a pretty great one-shot, but I was focused on the players, not the audience.

    \n

    In all I got the impression that the 'show' part wasn't super for the audience... not exactly the right area was lit, not exactly the right seating, certain people couldn't hear me well. That's all stuff that Aislinn will be adjusting for the next time. But I got lots of kudos for doing my job well, and people asking for my contact info and how to get into gaming at the break and after the show, so I'm happy to have done it.
