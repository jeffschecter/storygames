    The only caveat with all of the "Fie on 'The Industry'! What did The Industry ever do for me?!" rhetoric is that, speaking purely from the standpoint of social networks, there is a point beyond which the social cohesion of the hobby will break down, simply because there aren't enough people to create a robust network to withstand shocks.

    Granted, there will always be *someone* playing RPGs somewhere. Hell, there are still people performing the Commedia della Arte, after all. But without a robust social network of some sort, the margins for publication are going to get slim to the point of insolvency. Like it or not, the main reason that the indie movement can thrive is because people find D&D and White Wolf, and through either the social network or the internet, discover the indie scene. There might be some bleed-through from, say, the theatre or other related interests, but D&D remains the gateway drug for all things RPG.

    Personally, I think that RPGs still have a long way to go before extinction. I'd just prefer that they remain healthy, vital, and one the radar screen.

      In a corporate environment, your number one priority is to make money. This isn't because the corporate world is full of greedy pirates who are only out for profits. It's because you're not working for yourself and your sensibilities; you're working for the owner of the company. Increasingly, the "owner of the company" isn't any one person, it's a collection of shareholders, most of whom have little-to-no knowledge of the actual operation of the company. They own shares, they invested their money into the company, they deserve a return. To take it a step further, these shareholders are not necessarily tycoon investors who are only out for profits -- shareholders can just as easily be guys saving up for their kid's college education, pension funds trying to cover medical costs for their greying consituents, banks who turn their dividends around into the interest of their clients, some of whom are just trying to make ends meet. The corporate world is increasingly interdependent and is made up of a whole lot of 'little guys'. And you work for them.

      So when you sit down at your desk, you conceive of things that can turn a profit. Sure, if it's beautiful or clever or whatever as well, that's gravy, but profitability is the first and foremost concern, and whatever you start will be optimized for more profitability by yourself and others before it sees the light of day. A large large portion of that is marketing (making it "look a certain way") but some other bits involve manufacture (making it cost less to produce) and financing (protecting the IP for later uses).

      Contrast that with indie game publishing, where you do stuff cause you want to, and either accept a small loss or merely work to have a project pay for itself rather than trick it out for maximum profitability. The end results will be quite different.

      On the subject of the economics of self-publishing, I can't believe that people have missed the most important part of the formula.

      First off, I think that the profit ratio is far higher than what's being bandied about here. If you look at Ben's numbers, those are more like what I'm used to. So nearly a 10 to 1 ratio of profitability. So you have to sell ten times as many books to make as much money.

      But that's going to vary somewhat depending on the deal. Let's say for argument's sake that the ration was only 2 to 1, and your publisher is promising three times the sales. You'd have to be nuts not to take it, right?

      Wrong.

      There's a finite market out there. Eventually you're going to sell to all of them. This is inevitable. So, sure, with the deal offered, I sell to my 6000 potential fans in two years instead of in 6 years. But in the long run, selling slow and steady for six years, I'm going to make twice the money when all is said and done. Even if because of lack of exposure I only get to 4000 of them, I've still made money.

      And that's with our hypothetical 2:1 ratio. If the ratio is more realistic, you're a fool to sell out. Because, yeah it might take longer, and you might miss a few people. But you're going to make way more money over the life of the product selling it at the higher profit per copy.

      I think that slower discovery of a game by the market is actually beneficial too. But that's a whole nother discussion.

      In any case, the small-scale publishing model is the one that can weather a contraction in the market, should that happen. It's the bigger publishers who feel the contraction, because they depend on volume for profitability.
