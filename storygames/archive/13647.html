    \n Ritual Phrases from where I used to work:

    Hey Bobby (i require the attention of the person in charge)
    Shake and bake (never mind what i was just saying, carry on)
    Do it (i'm done arguing about this, you just do what you were going to do anyway)
    That little guy? Don't worry about that little guy. (that thing/idea/error isn't important enough in the big scheme of things to worry about, move on)
    Barbeque (spin that object around it's horizontal axis)
    Boiling cats! (i don't like your musical choices, and i want you to know that; alt: hey, this is my music that i know you don't like)
    Hold please (stop what you're doing and let me figure out the consequences of this choice of action before you continue; alt: hold on while i get a helpful or necessary tool to make what you're doing easier/possible)
    Appleboxes! (lets celebrate the fact that this endeavor that we thought was useless at the time is actually really helping us out right now)
    Little baby jesus with lazer eyes (i'm just talking to fill up the silence)

    \n You know what you need? A corporate satire game (a la Office Space, The Office and/or Dilbert), wherein the ritual phrases all are near-meaningless corporate buzzwords and "motivational phrases". Like what you'd see on an executive's wall, on a poster under a picture of an eagle. Or hidden inside a press release or the CEO's email to the company. "Maximizing synergy" "externalizing liability" "utilitzing social media to increase brand presence", etc.

    Similarly, a game about political pundits on 24 hour TV networks could use some ritual phrasing as well, for "talking points". Maybe each session they create new ritual phrases?


    A couple of TV shows have heavy ritualized their narratives. A mystery game based off of Monk would need ritual phrases "he's the guy" (which allows Monk's player to decide one NPC did the crime, no matter how implausible) and "here's how it happened." A Phineas and Ferb RPG would need ritual uses for phrases like "hey, where's Perry?" and "aren't you a bit young to be [doing whatever]?" "Yes, yes we are."

    \n Does a character or the player say the phrases? I'm imagining power-word catch-phrases...

    "I've got a bad feeling about this" gets your character into trouble but also gives you a story-advancing clue.

    Instead of "Aren't you a little short to be a stormtrooper?" maybe it's flexible. "Aren't you a little x to be y?" Gives the character you said it to -1 if they try to continue to be/do y.

    "I want that x, not excuses" +1 to manipulate subordinates after they've already failed once.

    "It's clobberin' time" +1 to Go Aggro.

    "Damn it, I'm a (insert your character class here), not a x!" +1 to do x, if you can narrate a way to make it about your character class.

    Maybe characters also come with catch-phrases others can use on them. "Snake Plisskin? I heard you were dead." Um, I don't know, +1 Hx with Snake.

    Hmm, but now I want part of the game to be coming up with the catch phrases in the first place, rather than stealing them from flims...

    \n In Apocalypse World, our Driver set out for a tiny fishing village in the jungle to make a delivery. He joked that the village chieftain was named Ganakagok. It was all downhill from there.

    We decided that once he arrived there, having driven hell for leather through the jungle with burning wheels and the blossoms falling from the trees behind him, hoping not to get poison'd by the jungle creatures, the villagers asked the Driver to sign in, stranger at their mist-robed gate, for he was but a warrior, in a wicked age. Ganakagok had a daughter, a maid named Polaris with whom the Driver was infatuated\u2014he really had her under his skin. He had no trouble breaking the ice with her. He dropped off a transgendered prostitute who wanted to work there, and the villagers were shocked at the human contact with her. Then he traded them some supplies for fuel, but it was the wrong octaNe, so he was stuck there for a while in the dread house of Ganakagok. He was so bored it felt like he was there for 1001 nights, far from the cold city where his garage was. The whole business was a real fiasco.
