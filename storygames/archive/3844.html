\n So, all respect to Graham, I shant shy away from what I think of a game. I am not one of those movie reviewers that is trying to be mean cause it brings ratings or any such non-sense and certainly not because I want to hurt anyone's feelings but because, unlike normal criticism, is actually helps a lot of people.

It helps sell books: bad reviews are good publicity and I write my reviews to say "I didn't like this and why but you might." Not to say every game is a gem but i bring to the table the good and the bad and someone out there likes this game.

It helps others make informed decisions. The market will bear out the rest.

It helps designers target me and all that dig what I dig. Since my reviews are all based on play and my comments include a group of people's perspective on play then I think it would be useful to bring concerns to the table. I was really happy that my comments, although not taken wholesale (which is grand) were addressed by gregor's rerelease of Best Friends. I'm going to play again and do an undate post GenCon including the new information.

Now, let me first say that there are very few games I actually regret buying (and I buy a lot). I have literally over a thousand traditional RPGs that go unused on my bookshelf. I read them and absorbed them into the gaming brain to bring out when useful. The only time I regret buying them is when I see there was something else I bought later that I could have used.

Here is an example: There is ONE book I returned to the store from I bought it from in exchange for another game. years and years ago I bought Immortal. I read it cover to cover and thought "what crap!" and returned it for Legacy (a Highlander wannabe). They tried to rename everything in gaming while retaining all the same shit - hit points were called one thing and AC was called something else obscure and hoity-toity. When I was done I knew very little about the game.

A few years later a buddy of mine bought the abridged version with all the language cleaned up and said "gee, this game is pretty cool." I read it and rebought the game now that I understood what was going on.

So far I have not come across an indy game I didn't like on some level. I have bought games that weren't my style and games that didn't do what I needed them to do but none I could say I regretted buying.

That being said and by no means meant to just be reactionary but I honestly did not like Donjon and in retrospect, Sorcerer. I don't like the die mechanic. I produced some unsatisfying results. First Donjon, to me, is hard to figure out if it's completely tongue-in-cheek or can be played in a fun but serious tone. I had no luck the two times I ran it. I liked the ideas there but not the die mechanic. In Donjon there were too many rolls and those rolls were too random. Additionally what I really wanted was a cool dungeon SIM game where the game was more about the dungeons than about the characters.

I will quote Fred "Calling Dictionary of Mu a Sorcerer supplement is like calling the Sistine Chapel a supplement to the Bible." We played it three times and it fell flat every time. The stories we told and our plans and inclusions in the Dictionary rocked but that was almost always despite the rolls not because of them. I have heard it said that Sorcerer is incomplete without the other Sorcerer books but to be honest I am not interested in that sort of buy in when I have so many games that do stand on their own and Mu would make an excellent port for them.

I know thousands of people have enjoyed Sorcerer and that mechanic but my experiences have not been among them. This is a situation that I don't think Ron needs to do anything about nor can I say I am playing it "right" but it's been my experience, none-the-less.

So what Indie/Story Games have you regretted buying, would rather use something else (other than your game), or would just give the designer a bit of advice that would otherwise make it better for YOU.

My AP Review of the first part of Hero's Banner should be up this week and the second part will be up after GenCon.

Thanks,
- Don

    \n intro: after don's well worded piece i'm going to sound like a barbarian. here i go.


    game i regret buying:

    Faery's Tale. i feel like i've seen this a dozen times before. pick a class. choose from lists. i hate this format. i don't want to play this with kids; i want to play games with kids wherein they tell me (if i'm facilitating) what they are and what they can do and then i balance the group out power-wise by trimming or fluffing and then we throw down. too many numbers (for kids), too much like changeling with the "are you this, or this? one or the other."
    hey, and what the fuck is up with the brownies? so, pixies: adorable flying things with pixie dust. oh, i guess girls are supposed to play pixies. sprites: little knights with thorn swords who ride bees. oh, i guess boys are supposed to play sprites. pooka: can transform into animals. i guess the queer kids are supposed to play pooka, as that these faeries are ugly, change form, and are loners. so, sign up for your gender roles early, kids.
    brownies: their powers are to clean up around the house and be invisible. oh, like wives. fuck off.
    abilities seem skewed and most aren't too interesting. instead of cool magical stuff, it's all like "brave" or "fortunate" or "lucky". yeah, that's right. also, while the good/evil thing i guess makes sense when it comes to source material (fairy stuff, not to be confused with fairy tales) it's still hella boring and teaches kids that people do things "for evil"... like, as if "evil" existed and was something that ANYONE actually strove toward. Disney doesn't need your help in the disinformation department.
    also Expend Hp To Do Magic Stuff is boring. (edit: for kids. can be awesome otherwise, except if point of game is to do magic stuff, then is at cross purposes to "fun")
    Narrator judges when you get to rejuvinate your hp/magic meter is boring.

    all that being said, the art is totally gorgeous, and i'm totally going to play it when i can find folks who want to twist shit around and have crazy (imaginary, but i'm open) faery sex and deal with corruption and loss and expenditure of life force for "magic", which is to say "manifestation of beauty in a dark world". and lots of faery death. shallow little graves.

    if this makes me an asshole, it's better that you know now. i don't know Patrick, Sandy, Christina, or Robin; i hope they are happy with their game and i'll tell them that i don't have a ongoing gaming group, they or anyone else are welcomed to make snide remarks as to why this might be or to call into question how active my sex life is.

    //

    what would make this game good for me: well, i like faeries. if there were some ideas about different kinds of faeries that were non-exclusive... crap, i just wrote and have deleted a list to choose from. there's two directions here - the little kid direction, and the adult gamer direction. instead of making an introductory trad-feeling game, i'd like to see a more free-formy kind of game which wouldn't have kids picking up pencils to cross off essence scores, or looking at character sheets to know what abilities ("gifts") they had.

    you know, this is hard to do without describing a game in particular that i am making.

    i guess there's not really that much i like about it. now on, i'll read reviews before buying things.

    thanks for reading.
    -jackson

    \n I rarely regret games for the reason that, if I'm not satisfied with it, I can always just throw it on RPGNet sales/trade, or eBay and make some money back on it (or trade it for something else).

    I'm really racking my brains here, but I honestly can't think of one I really regret. I can name the ones that I bought, peeked at, went "Nope!" and turned right around on RPGNet Sales, but I was able to pick out a little something cool each time.

    In return, though, I can name a game I regret selling: CONTINUUM. I have bought this game THREE TIMES.

    The first time, I read it, thought the background was awesome, found the game unplayable. When I returned to the US and started selling all my games to clean out my junk and pay for rent (before I ended up finding awesome work), it got sold. I mean, why not? I wasn't going to run it again.

    Later on, I felt nostalgic for it. Bought it on eBay for like $10. Read through the cool background section again. Because the background sections were like 100ccs of pure awesome in the brain. But then in a later culling I sold it again.

    I bought it last year for the Third and Final Time on eBay. It now sits on my shelf in its respectable position next to Blue Planet, the other game that I will never play but proudly own and read. The background and ideas that Continuum spawned were worth reading, and every once in a while I go through the alternative history of the world section when I want some ideas.

    I have Playstation games I regret buying, though. Killer 7. What the fuck? I got it in a bargain bin for $5.00, and I sold it later for $5.00, and yet I still feel like I lost out on the deal somehow because of the 20 minutes I spent playing it. The Kangfu (part of Japan's "Simple 2000 Series"): They took Kung Fu, mixed it with a storyline using real Cantonese voiceovers, and set it in this classic Shaw Brothers style game. And yet, the game wasn't complete for them without making sure It Sucked. You only had two attacks: Punch and Kick; WTF, is this NES?? They took Cantonese Kung Fu/Shaw Brothers, and drained it of all its fun and action. Again, I sold it, net loss of about $5, but I totally regret that purchase. SIREN aka FORBIDDEN SIREN I regret that it was too hard that even Mensa said "fuck you". The second one (Forbidden Siren 2) is perhaps one of my favorite games, and probably one of the best three horror video games ever made. But the first was as fun as sitting naked on broken glass, but you couldn't tell that until after about a day or two of playing.

    Oh, shit, I think I thought of one: GURPS 4th (characters book). After all the work that we put into deciding that this was the system that we were going to use for a campaign run by one of my gaming group members, that campaign never took off. Part of the reason? Rules sucked the life out of us.

    -Andy

But, well, I feel like the Internet talk about it says more than the actual book does. I pick up this thing, read through it carefully, get confused, and just kind of shrug -- I can't isolate the key stuff. Honestly, I need a book that lays it all out on the table, with phrases like "Say yes or roll the dice" and "Use pool refreshment like a rock star." Not having that part was really messing me up and made me very unsure of what would happen if I just picked up the book and muddled through. I feel like I'll have to do a lot of work before I can play it. Not a bad buy, but I'd really rather have shelled out some more cash for something that was less artful but included more guidance as to what to do with it.

    And, speaking of the part that I wanted only being a small part of the actual book (as Sorcerer, above): the Riddle of Steel. The good parts of the game are really underdeveloped in the core book. Through shipping weirdness, I managed to get The Flower of Battle, the weapons and fighting supplement, before the core book, and that greatly inflated my expectations as to what kinds of stuff I would find in the main tome, since I figured there'd be pages and pages about historical fighting styles, the nuances of different weapons, and generally well-explained mechanics. The TROS main book's actual section on combat is pretty short and leaves a lot of nuances completely unexplained, the weapons are kinda generic and have barely any text associated with their entries, and the Spiritual Attributes were sprinkled around strangely in the other stuff -- it felt more like reading someone's notebook than a finished game. All in all, TROS ended up feeling like bits of "fantasy heartbreaker" around a juicy kernel of fighting-ness.

    I also totally regret buying 95% of the D&D (3rd) books I've ever held in my hands, in retrospect. I spent like three years just having that game get more and more and more frustrating every time I touched it.

    -- Alex
