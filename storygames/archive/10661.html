\n In the school playground, this is how we used to get people to play a game. Two of us would form a line, with arms around each other's shoulders. We would then walk around the playground, chanting "Who wants to play It? No girls! Who wants to play It? No girls!".

Obviously, this was in the days when associating with the opposite sex was a bad thing, and likely to lead to accusations that you were, yourself, a girl. Even so, I'm not sure we meant the "no girls" thing. It was just part of the rhyme. I remember that, on one occasion, we changed it to "No girls or Richard", to discourage Richard from playing with us. The girls themselves, of course, did not use this method, forming their groups using conversation or social skills or one of those other abilities we didn't have.

If you wanted to play the game, you joined on the end of the line, placing your arms around the shoulders of someone on the end of the line. Eventually, you would get a line of eleven or twelve boys, all chanting the same thing, at which point we would start playing the game.

Looking back, it was a remarkably efficient way of getting people to play a game. You hung around talking to people until you heard someone chanting something you wanted to play. You joined the line, and chanted, which kept you busy while you were waiting to play. Then, when you were ready to play, you were all together and ready to start.

I wonder if there is a way of adapting this to the con environment? I'm not actually suggesting that we form long lines, chanting "Who wants to play In A Wicked Age? No girls or Simon" with arms around shoulders. We would certainly not exclude female gamers: although the idea of adding riders like "No-one under 18" is appealing. In any case, the idea of calling out game names, then slowly forming groups until you reach critical mass, might work well.

Graham

    \n How did it work at Go Play Northwest?

    I like the idea of a social way of getting games together. However, one problem with social methods is that they can just end up with people playing with people they know. I want to play Dogs, but the game signed up in seconds, mostly with friends of the GM.

    One thing I like about this method is that it's inclusive. You can join the line, whether or not you know the people in it. Well, OK, realistically, you ask "Can I play?" and they probably say "Yes".

    It does rely on people having other things to do while games are being formed, I think.

    What other social ways are there to form games at cons?

    \nSo true.

    Make a point of not playing with your home town crew at conventions. Make a point of playing with more distant friends. Make a point of playing with strangers.

    I think the best way to organize an informal game at a con is to bring one and run it. Stand there, announce you are putting together a game, and grab the first four people who express interest, no matter who they are (they won't be your home town crew, because those people know better). Happily, there are many games that are well suited for convention play that require no prep and no GM, so being the organizer doesn't have to be a huge ordeal. I do this all the time and have made many friends this way.

    My unhappiest pick-up game experiences typically involve trying to engineer this process - committing to play a certain game at a certain time with a certain person.

    \n Oh, that's neat, Dev, thanks.

    One problem with Jason's method is this: I want to play a game. Jason shouts "Who wants to play And My Axe"? Now, I wouldn't mind playing And My Axe, but there's other games I'd really like to play. So what do I do? Play now? Or wait and risk playing nothing?

    That becomes easier if we're doing stuff while we're waiting. So, if I'm in the Embassy Suites having a drink, I think, well, no, I won't play And My Axe, I'll stick around with these guys for a while.

    There's other interesting social things you can do, too. You can have a rolling game that people join in and leave when they've got something more permanent: Inspectres is pretty good for this.

    You can negotiate games openly, with people saying "I'd like to run And My Axe", and someone else saying "OK, you guys go over there and run And My Axe, and we'll do a game of Tripod over here". I think it's the best method, but it only works with small numbers of people. I'd love to find a way to extend that to bigger numbers.

    And there's sign-up sheets, of course. Not a social method, but they get you playing with people you've never met before.

    There won't be one Best Method For Everything, but I'm interested to work out which methods do what well.

    Graham

    \n In my boardgame club, we had a game we called the metagame, for when lots of people want to play a game, but no one can really agree on what game they want to play.

    Everyone picks one game and puts it on the table. Give everyone a number of playing cards equal to the number of games (so if you have 13 games, Ace-King. Everyone should have different cards, so you'll need multiple decks with different backs.)

    Everyone puts one card on each game. Higher card, greater preference.

    Count the highest four (or five, or whatever) cards on each game. Whatever game has the highest total gets played. (Tie breaker is highest-low card. Next tie-breaker is highest low card from other cards not counted.) The people with those cards? They play that game, and remove their cards from all the games.

    Now you've got like 9 people left in the mix. Do that again- count the highest four cards on each game. Those four people play that highest scoring game.

    Now you've got 5 people. Sum up the last games. Play the one with the highest score.

    If you end up playing a game you put your Ace on, you get an extra card next time you play the metagame- if there's 8 games, you get a Ace-9, and may leave one card unplayed.
