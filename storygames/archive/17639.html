\n I haven't been around here in over a year, before this newfangled layout where you can actually private message people, save drafts, follow threads, and all those spiffy features.

After an Apocalypse World game fizzled out in early 2011, my group basically switched wholly to Board gaming. I found it satisfied my gamer ADD quite better (can play new games all the time) and worked well with our rotating members never knew who would show up.

I was in a local gaming store yesterday picking up some deck boxes to sort my Netrunner decks and saw Fiasco sitting there on the shelf for only $13. I had avoided picking it up when it came out because the subject matter didn't really interest me (despite being an enormous Coen brothers fan).

For $13 I couldn't help it. And now I'm thinking about getting some members of my group together to play this and things like Bangs, Kickers, Story Now, have returned from that hazy part of the back of my brain - hurtling the marching armies of Eclipse, Space Alert, and Chaos in the Old World.

No real point to this post yet I couldn't help but share.

    \n In my experience, people who get "burned out" on RPGs either:

    a) Have trouble wrangling a group of people to commit to long-term projects (e.g. "campaigns") which are never realized.

    or

    b) Find themselves putting in a lot of work (e.g. very prep-heavy games, or games with a heavy social footprint), without the same level of return.

    Generally speaking, setting proper expectations can help with both of those issues. Playing a no-prep, one-session game like Fiasco is definitely a good solution!

    \nSo your group formed as a weekly RPG group and the you all dropped it together? Do tell.
    \nYeah, kind of. I've gamed weekly with my younger brother for nearly 15 years. Another member of our group has been around for 10 years. Everyone else has come and gone and come back again due to life/other interests/etc.

    I was running a weekly game from like 99-2011 without missing a week. Occassionally we'd play a boardgame depending on the mood but our current campaign was our main focus.

    We evolved as a group. I found the Forge, was freshly jazzed and felt like a little kid again preparing his first GM session. Started introducing bangs and stuff into traditional games and then we moved onto story games. I guided the group into Indie-RPGs and they were fully willing, loving all of the new games we played.

    Somewhere down the line in the last few years, a couple members of the group started having diverging interests. They weren't looking for as serious of a campaign as I was, they thought it was a lot of work emotionally and mentally to be tied up in a heavy story like that. They all the sudden wanted to just kill shit and take their stuff or just play a board game. We gradually started floating towards board games and never looked back.

    Board games made sense as I have gamer ADD and was always finding that cool new story-game that I wanted to abandon our current campaign and try the new one out (damn this forum). They also support rotating player bases (including 1v1) better than the majority of RPGs. No more worrying about scheduling.
