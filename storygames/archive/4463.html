\n The emergent thread start out of the gate with some awesome stuff that I was interested in, but it was more game design than emergent play, and the thread focus shifted.

I've been looking for design primers. I've found threads on the Forge. The Power 19. And some other methods, but none of them really lay out some core design principles or where to start when you're staring at a blank page (preferably with some notes). The Power 19 seems to work the best for me at first, but then it gets into mechanics, and, well I don't have those yet. All the components I've seen have been pieces, but it seems like for different puzzles. I haven't found anything that tries to pull it altogether, or at the very least, clearly states "This is a place to start" ("a" not "the" I'm not looking for a definitive be-all thing). So, maybe we can make one in the form of this thread.

So, I'm going to start this off by quoting Tony's tidbit of a design principle that I thought was helpful.\n
Posted By: TonyLBOkay, first item: Feedback.

You have some resource (saymoney), in amount X. You have an activity which requires that resource (say playing the stock market).

There is abig differencebetween an activity which costs you X and returns X+1, and an activity which costs you X and returns X-1. Yes? That is, generally speaking, the difference between positive and negative feedback.

Positive feedback will make things explode ... very reliably. People who start rich will become much, much richer at an exponential rate. Fighters who get experience from fighting to plow into more fighting will become very fightworthy.

Negative feedback tends to make things damp to a common ground. People who start rich will become ... well, poor again. Fighters who get experience for searching for peaceful solutions, to plow into fighting skills that make peace more difficult to attain will tend to find a balance point.

Make sense?
\nYes, it does, thanks Tony.

So, I'm wondering. Where do you start? How do you get from brainstorming to game making? Do some people start with mechanics and build story/setting/theme around it, how about the other way around? Do you start with some sort of design doc, or do you play around with mechanics until something strikes a chord? What are other design principles?

    \n What I've found really, really works for me is to decide what the game is about and build from there. When I get an idea for a game it's usually of what I thinkof as the "surface" stufff. Stuff like "Psychic Japanese school girls blowing shit up". Once I have that I try to strip it down to the very essence of the idea. I say "try" because a lot of times I think you can strip and idea down and not realize there are still plenty of otehr layers that you haven't touched yet. But that's fine. I strip my ideas until I get to the part that I really want to focus on. The one thing the game will really be about. Then I try to think of a way to build a game around that. Ususally this is a bunch of false starts followed by a brainstorm and a lot of furious writing. What I've found is that when I have the right idea everything feels really right. If I haven't found the right idea yet I definetly know it.

    But that' s just me maybe.

    \n Alvin,

    I'm a little concerned that your specific questions (which are really good) are going unanswered. And it's difficult to know where to start when you're designing. Would an example help?

    I wrote a game recently for the Sight and Sound competition called kalma.

    First of all, I looked at the ingredients. The picture of an old man reminded me of an idea I had about 12 years ago for a supplement for Wraith. It was sort of 'Buffy, if everyone was old'. So I wrote down 'old people vs the supernatural' (notice how I assume the versus). The 'liminal' ingredient was interesting. What if the old people were close to death? Inspired by things like Insects of God, how about the characters have just one more task they can achieve? And I didn't worry about fitting in the last ingredient, as both the 'freaky choir' and 'heavy breathing' could fit. And let's call it kalma coz it's a word I like.

    So. Old people. What are old people about? Rage, rage against the dying of the light. So characters have one last burst of energy/enthusiasm before they die. The system should involve balancing traits. Semper Fidelis balances Health/Ammo/Time for a Doom-inspired game. So what would old people have to balance? Time left. Health. Enthusiasm. A storygame with no relationships seems wrong, so characters have to maintain connectedness to people.

    The best idea I had at this point was inspired by one of Judd's character sheets with a section 'what do you hate about this character?' So what if in kalma, the character sheet had a question 'What is it you can no longer do?' That ties in to the feeling of regret. There may also be a mechanical aspect to it - perhaps like the negative traits in Cold City or penalty dice in TSOY. But perhaps it just provides color or assistance in scene framing.

    Ah! Inability! How about instead of Health and Energy, you have traits like Frailty and Tiredness? It'd be cool to have the attributes be the opposite of most games - stuff you can't do. This reminded me of a fantasy game where the attributes were Clumsy and Stupid. So maybe the attributes count down somehow.

    Then I wrote a character sheet. At the top, a space for name, a thing you can't do, and three attribute tracks. Then four tracks. Then back to three. I eventually settled on three - Tired, Lonely and Frail. 'Time' didn't make thematic sense, though I considered the possibility that a character running out of time (but not the other three) would fall to accidents.

    Players should be able to fight back. So like Semper Fi, let's include stances: temporary bonuses the characters could buy. Let's match them to attributes. Energised, Connected and Healthy. Healthy's too passive, let's call ir Vigourous. Energised is boring too, let's call it Stubborn. When players use these stances, the word 'stubborn' will inform play - the player is likely going to play the character as stubborn, which is far more interesting than 'energetic'. Cool.

    Now, characters could just buy bonus dice. But buying flavorful stances seems somehow more interesting. There's a chance that everyone would end up similarly stubborn (and the characters all feel like Statler and Waldorf) but let's see where this goes.

    This all took three days.

    How's that?

    \n Everybody's different, right? Find a way that works for you. I know people who start with existing games they like and play their new idea using that system until it breaks, which gives them a starting point for something new. I know people who are obsessed with mechanical interactions and let the game's theme emerge as a gloss on those at first, like German board games. I think I start with the experience of play and try to tease out what sort of things I want that to be like. I often think about things like the length and character of an instance of play, and how to keep handling time to a minimum, while building the game to reinforce the things I want to be important.

    Here's an example - I'm working on a game about people who fly airplanes during a war, and the terror of their missions. So what I really want to focus on is that terror, and also their personal stories. I don't really give a shit about the missions themselves beyond their utility as color and a source of adversity - this is the first important realization I made by looking hard at my source material and inspiration. So the question I was asking myself in this case was "how do I include the content of their missions without having some tedious board gamey mission component?" And the answer I came up with is a debriefing scene. The regimental commander asks you how the mission went, and the pilot and navigator's players answer in character, and both the adversity and the way it was addressed are resolved in real time (in this case, using playing cards). And once I had that piece, I asked myself "once this interaction is concluded, you've got a little stack of eight playing cards. How can I use those to resolve an actual scene relating to the character's stories, and how can the results of the mission impact that?" And so on, iteratively.

    Posted By: Alvin FrewerSo, I'm wondering. Where do you start? How do you get from brainstorming to game making? Do some people start with mechanics and build story/setting/theme around it, how about the other way around? Do you start with some sort of design doc, or do you play around with mechanics until something strikes a chord? What are other design principles?
    I start with a feeling, genre or situation that I want to explore. My goal is to have a series of measurable, objective targets such that I can say "If players do X, Y, Z and A, B, C reliably happen in the game then the game successfully captures the feeling I want," but I never start out with that. The challenge of turning a feeling into a formula is ... a substantial one. I think it's part of what the Power 19 and other "What do you want from the game?" questionnaires try to tease out.

    My method to get from feeling to formula is playtesting. I make a formula that I think will support the feeling, and then I make a game that expresses that formula as an emergent property, and then I play the game. Some things do work, some things don't, and I get a better idea of what formula I should really be designing for. Then I make another game.

    As you might guess from the above: Going from formula to game design is (at least for me) the easiest part of the process. Easy enough that I use it as a tool to address the other parts of the process. Once you have an actual formula you're just shuffling math about in fairly predictable ways. That's why so much design advice addresses the chain from formula to design: it's the part where people can speak with most authority.

    \n I'm one of the oddballs that starts with mechanics. I have tons of text files each with notes on a die mechanic or an idea on how to handle scene progression or whatever.
    However, I don't really consider that game design. That's more scribbling. For me, game design starts with the interface-- when I start thinking about real people picking up those snippets of rules and using them. I try and figure out how they'd feel as they played, what goals they could pursue, and perhaps most importantly, how the players interact with each other through the medium of those rules.
    So I'm not sure if this is helpful to you-- my answer to "how do you design a game" boils down to "take a handful of dice tricks from the quarry, see how they'd work together."

    \n Once I have a basic Idea, I start with thinking about, "who gets to control what?" That is, am I taking the traditional GM-and-players-with-PCs route? Are we going to share in world creation? Play NPCs for each other?

    Posted By: Alvin FrewerI think even if most of us start design another way, we still end up mining there. Could you give some advice on what you put into your quarry? Or if not, maybe share a sample of no more than two or three things in it?
    \nWhat goes into the quarry ranges widely. I have everything from pages-long rambling manifestos to three-line resource trading restrictions. Usually, they are so small or incomplete that they do not make much sense on their own. So like:

    "What if by merely playing the game, you lose resources, and you only gain respurces by winning (something). You must maintain a positive net gain to stay in the game." (That's been incorporated into Agora)

    "Roll howevermany dice. 1, 2, and 3 are successes of increasing power. All others are failures and are given to the GM."

    A "whole" game, by my estimation, needs at least six to ten of pieces like this. A new design may start with only three or four, but before it's done, it needs to pick up more.

    It is well nigh impossible to solo-test. The reason (I think) is that players are as much and as continually motivated by what they don't know as they are by what they know.

    I can firewall information that I know, and say to myself "Okay, I won't act on that information." I'm a gamer, after all. But I find it much harder to firewall information and say to myself "Man, I really need to figure that out!" and act from that passion when, in fact, I already know the thing. I can't merely not act ... I have to figure out what that kind of ACTION that ignorance would drive people to. And I find it really, REALLY hard to firewall information and say to myself "Well ... How can I get the real feeling of gambling on the unknown when it's not actually a gamble?

    No, I totally need people who are actually acting from ignorance of each other's hidden information.

    \n People have already talked a lot about pieces of this, so I'm not claiming to be original or even adding a lot of new ideas here. This is just how I do it.

    When working on a game design, I think about it in different layers.

    There's play experience. What kind of play do I expect this game to produce? I use the aforementioned Structured Design technique (which I find horrendously named, by the way, as it suggests a kind of decomposition mentality used in structured design in computer programming, and it isn't that). Basically, I write out examples of play or even broader outlines of play experience goals.

    There's game flow. This is a refinement of play experience. What is the give and take among players at the table? How does interaction occur and in what places do I want to control it? This is IIEE territory, somewhat.

    There's authority. This takes IIEE and Ron's four types of authority and mingles them a bit. Who can say what, and when? Who gets to decide things? Are there mechanics for shifting authority?

    There's the Big Model or, rather, the deconstruction of my game idea into the layers of the Big Model. I look at the components of Exploration (Character, Setting, Situation, Color, System). I look at support for Creative Agenda. I recall Vincent Baker's rule, or something like it: "Nowhere to go but out" and make sure my game has something that bootstraps Situation in a meaningful way for the players. I don't want players creating characters then going, "Now what?"

    There's mechanics. I want these to be fun and interesting, by themselves, but not be meaningful without the role-playing stuff. I've probably been fiddling with dice and currency and who knows what else in all the other layers of thought, but this step is where I really try to tease out the mechanical subgame and optimize it.

    There's optimization. Here's where I cut out as much as I can and streamline stuff. I try to make different currencies and dice convert 1:1. I try to reduce handling time. I simplify.

    I do all these things at once, really. Not in discrete steps. The actual mental process is nowhere near as clean as the layers above. I am just trying to deconstruct my messy mental process into something you could use. Hope this helps!
