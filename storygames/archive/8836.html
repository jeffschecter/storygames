\n I was reading an interview with Grant Morrison about Final Crisis, and why it's so weird, and he said:\n
I found myself wondering what it would be like if comics\u2019 storytelling stopped aping film or TV and tried a few tricks from opera, for instance. How about dense, allusive, hermetic comics that read more like poetry than prose? How about comics loaded with multiple, prismatic meanings and possibilities? Comics composed like music? In a marketplace dominated by \u2018left brain\u2019 books, I thought it might be refreshing to offer an unashamedly \u2018right brain\u2019 alternative.
\nSo I thought, "why not try to write an rpg that reads like a poem? Or a collection of poems that you can play, or something." Poems are not a good medium for communicating procedures (except very blatantly), but it might be fun. So I did.

It's called Virtue, and it's an rip-off/homage/poem of the Ultima computer games from way back when. It's not finished, but it's a good skeleton so far.

I'd love to have some folks guess at what the game mechanics are. I'm also looking for general feedback, and wondering if anyone's tried something similar before.

    ok, so we have characters who desire to be virtuous.
    They identify virtue with three qualities, and these qualities are the axes (pl. of axis, not ax) on which they conflict with one another.
    No, with the world around them.
    the three are Love, Courage, Truth.

    it is a game about staying true to your own moral code,
    wherein the other players test your character's code by presenting them with difficult choices.

    "we know what is right and wrong", this means that we are able to state what is right or wrong within the fiction.

    characters have three states, the Blessed, Not-So-Blessed & Cursed days. Hmmm. actually the third choice
    is called "dark times", to not be confused with the "Curse" ability, which i'll describe later.
    i don't know how the three states are navigated between. perhaps randomly, or another player chooses for your character?

    anyway, regardless of state, one player creates a scenario. (this could be the player of the protagonist, or another player) two non-protagonist
    players invent choices that the protagonist might make - "right" choices, or "wrong" choices, depending on what state the protagonist is in.
    the right/wrong designation is placed on the choices by the players making up the choices, based on the protagonist's moral code. yes?

    so, the protagonist player makes a choice, and somehow gathers or loses virtue points.

    when dealing with minor characters, protagonists are told their virtue score. virtue is tricky because if you make a lot of
    good choices in life but a few more bad choices, you'll have a low virtue, whereas if you don't really do anything at all then
    your virtue might well be high.
    & it's further complicated by minor characters with, you know, bad judgment in some areas but whom are beloved to other minor
    characters with high virtue. ha!

    the virtue that we gain helps us interact with minor characters, appear trustworthy etc.
    the endgame uses a different mechanic, however, a further extrapolation wherein first you use the Virtue points that you've gathered
    to impart lessons onto your character's children... and depending on how things work out (mechanics gap, here) your kids
    will become virtuous or not, and you will be happy or not as a result.

    the second to last paragraph states that anything of mechanical value in this game hinges on the
    Virtue points that you've gathered. No swords +2 or "i have a good relationship with my father 2d8"


    ok, help me fill in the gaps, people!
