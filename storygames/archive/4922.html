\n I've been thinking a lot about how full of ritual societies are, and I wanted a way to play with more of that in my games. It seems like, at a minimum, it can be just as cool as describing architecture, language, fashion, or weather in a setting. There's tremendous variety in both the reasons a ritual is performed (coming of age, death of a kinsman, christening a ship) and in the rituals themselves (throw a party, dress in black, break a bottle).

So I made big lists of everything I could think of for both reasons and rituals themselves and put them in a Random Ritual Generator on Abulafia. I'm intending to use this both during worldbuilding for fantasy settings and when the characters go "off the map" and I need instant color for a new people they're encountering.

How are you using rituals and ceremony to liven up your settings?

    \n Ritual can certainly be a direct way to establish otherness.

    I recall a Eurocentric fantasy game I ran where the PCs established an alliance with the weird people over the mountains. Right before they all went into battle together, the weird guy priests started dragging ropes through their pierced tongues and anointing people with the blood. It accurately telegraphed some cultural misunderstandings just down the road.

    \nI'm a big fan of odd customs and rituals in games. I once ran a whimsical urban fantasy game that used a lot of real-world superstitions, but gave them all new justifications or rationales that fit the setting. So a 'baker's dozen' was still thirteen cakes, but the thirteenth one was set aside for the urban fairy-folk, the beastlings. The players chipped some in, too - a crossroads where people left unwanted puppies, that was once an old shrine sacred to a forgotten dog-god; stuff like that. It made the city feel a lot more authentic.
