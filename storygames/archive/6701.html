\n I've recently been wanting to set up what I call a "fantastical fantasy" campaign, based on the pre-Tolkien current of the genre best embodied by books like The Once and Future King, Le Morte d'Arthur, and the recent Gene Wolfe novels The Wizard Knight. (Shameless plug and extra information: check out the latest entry on my blog.)

So what ideas would you want to incorporate into such a game, in terms of adventures, foes, the characters...? What new twists on Arthurian legend, Celtic and Norse mythology, and traditional fairy tales would get your blood pumping as a player?

    \n This is one of those situations in which I am required to self-plug.

    Polaris is my homage to a particular pre-Tolkein fantasy strain, particularly the romantics, embodied by Lord Dunsany.

    So: Swords made of starlight, the beautiful destructive power of the dawn, a doomed people dancing to demonic rhythyms, a single knight facing down a horde he cannot defeat, but yet they will not bring him down. A world as fragile and beautiful as frost in the dawn.

    If I were going to do something else, it would be superstitions whispered valley to valley, a place where the language over the next hill is unspeakable and unlearnable, but yet stories pass from place to place, where sorcerers' spells begin with "once upon a time" and, in the case of particularly wicked sorcerers, end with "happily ever after," where the truth is dangerous and brave and unwelcome like a brand to flesh, and where a shadow's breath may be the price of a long bargain.

    In other words, less "the king of elfland's daughter" and more "the charwoman's shadow." Which is to say I'm hung up on Dunsany.

    \n You might like to take a look at my Solar System game Errantry, Jean. It's more William Morris's Well at the World's End than Morte d'Arthur, but I had in mind also Marie de France, the Faerie Queene and more modern treatments like Tamora Pierce's. (I have to say I dislike both The Once and Future King and The Wizard Knight, mainly because of the modern/postmodern feel to them; I like my medievalism to be more medieval.)

    The Errantry setting is much more Arthurian or Breton than Celtic or Norse, but you could move it in those directions easily enough by giving more emphasis to the faerie side of things for Celtic or adding some more monstrous monsters for Norse, and of course emphasizing tragic doomed heroism.

    It has a "magic dial" which you can set high, medium or low - low is basically your standard medieval knightly tale, medium is Marie de France or William Morris and high is Tamora Pierce, roughly.

    Adventures typically consist of episodic encounters with opponents and helpers in the course of a quest.

    Foes are Black Knights, enchanters/enchantresses, beasts both speaking and non-speaking, natural and heraldic.

    And characters are Knights, Ladies (quite a powerful character class), Ecclesiastics, Enchanters, Troubadours and Speaking Beasts, or combinations thereof.

    It's unfinished and unplaytested (though most of it is standard Solar System, so it should play fine), but you might get some inspiration from it.

    \n I like seeing people create stuff and I've seen too many [4e] posts on this site, lately, to have any love for shills of any kind, so, here's a few straight suggestions toward creating your own (or cannibalizing parts from games you'd like to play.) Hope I'm not too late, here.
    \n
    \nIf you say pre-tolkien, I'm guessing you mean Arthurian (as the Hobbit came out in 1936 and T. H. White brought out the "Once and Future King" in the 50s.)
    \nSo, we're looking at the works like the French Romances, right?
    \n
    \nWell, in that case, you could look to Robin Hood, Orlando Furioso, Lord Dunsany and the Songs of Charlemaine, too, perhaps?
    \nWe're talking secret meetings in the Greenwood, maybe? Fantastic Isles and feats of passion and faith? Some combination of these?
    \n
    \nMaybe using the works of Howard Pyle as inspiration might work?
    \n
    \nYou could try capturing that feel of singular moments that define the path of a character up until a certain legend blooms, followed by the final passing of that character.
    \n
    \nLike, for instance, you could have everyone create a legend, first, and then work out, one by one, the vignettes or sub-scenes of how those legends met, how they fell in love, how they triumphed, how they almost fell to their greatest foes and how they died.
    \n
    \nPerhaps you could use a simple index system, where everyone has to fulfill a certain number of these scenes and they must aid others in completing that other players' respective scenes in order to earn chits or dice or cards or some other resource to "glorify" or add significance to their own. Am I on a good track, here?

    \n Well, you could do a campaign where each player picks one major triumph from the life of their legend that they want to play out, knowing that this major scene will shape the eventual demise of that legend sometime in the undefined future.
    \n
    \nThen, as each other player enters the game (on their early or first turns) you could play out vignettes from their past that "ready" or "boost up" certain powers or traits that the players think will be useful in the major event. This kind of jumping back and forth in time and listing attributes was common in the old courtly lays, as I recall... rather dimly, actually. No, that's definitely part of the structure of the Maid Marian poems, anyway.
    \n
    \nThen, once everyone's introduced, you could start throwing out the choices the legend has to make in order to accomplish this major triumph. These should start piling up factors that will lead to the legend's eventual demise (creating dire enemies, revealing weaknesses, making vows, being cruelly betrayed or set up for it, that kind of thing.) I don't know if you'd want to handle that with cards or dice or player suggestions, but it's part of the structure.
    \n
    \nThe main character for the game must have a solid reason to be striving for this major victory and the other players should get some benefits, like increases to their own legends' powers and glory, for introducing tough choices. You'll also need some sort of delimiter or fly wheel mechanic to keep the other players from adding too many tough choices to the game.
    \n
    \nOh, and Howard Pyle was an American children's illustrator, sorry. But he did some really great versions of King Arthur and Robin Hood.
