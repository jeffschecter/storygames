\n I'm starting this thread to offer some advice(and allow others to offer theirs) about graphic design(and possibly art) for game designers. I'm by no means a professional, but I have created a myriad of blog and blog themes, websites and did graphic design for my own and other projects(Mainly my old horror e-zine ''Carnival Macabre'', music industry work and other misc. stuff).
I'm a big believer in good graphic design both in general and when it comes to rpg games. Borders and good typography helps alot to convey themes and atmosphere as well as giving your game a more professional look. So, what can poor talentless(I assume you are, otherwise you wouldn't need any help right?) you do?

Art and Design
iStock Photo is probably the single most easy, relatively cheap and awesome resource there is. Just do a search for '' + vector'' and you're on your way to cheap images. Vector images usually cost 1-4$ each. You can also use normal photographs that can look pretty awesome with a bit of photoshop editing(DRYH did this and I was blown away with the results when I figured it out). You can also search for borders which you can use for character sheets.

DeviantArt is a very popular art community . You can't just take whatever you want, but you can track down artists and ask for permission or strike up a deal to use their work. Also useful for tutorials and stock photos, although the latter may not be free to use for commercial projects. You can always ask though.

Typography\n
    \n
  • DaFont is quite possibly the only font resource you'll need. If you're going to sell your game, make sure the font you choose is public domain. It should state so in the readme file.

  • Blambot is ostensibly a site for selling fonts for comic book lettering, but they also do a series of free fonts. They make great title and header fonts.(by Andrew Kenrick)

  • Smashing Magazine's free fonts category(by Linnaeus)
  • 25 Best Free Quality Fonts(by Linnaeus)
  • Urban Fonts(by Linnaeus)
    • \n
\nIdentifying fonts:\n
    \n
  • Identifont You can not only look up fonts by name, but by description: if you can picture the font you want in your head in detail, but you don't have it on your system and don't know the name (so you can't find it,) Identifont's Q&A system will help you find it.(by talysman)
  • What the Font identifies a font from an image you provide. Very helpful if you can't figure out what kind of font you're looking at in a magazine or book.
    • \n
\nLayout and Book Design
dot-font: Design in a Bind(by talysman)
dot-font: Book Design, Part II(by talysman)
dot-font: The Last Word on Book Design(by talysman)

Books about Design
The Non-Designer's Design Book by Robin Williams(by jim pinto)
Start with a Scan by Janet Ashford and John Odam. (by MikeRM)

Misc Resource Websites
C-Side Media(by MikeRM)
xenoglyph(By Joshua A.C. Newman)

    On the subject of fonts: make sure the one you use is readable. Display fonts should only be used for titles and headings. Pick a nice easy-to-read font for the main text. Serif is better than sans serif for paragraph blocks. And if you want a heading to look handwritten, write it out by hand and scan it in.

    \n

    On the public domain fonts issue, I'm not sure that applies to fonts included with commercial design products like Adobe InDesign. I mean, aren't you paying to use that font commerially when you pay for the product?

    \n I made a page about game design resources, including some of this stuff, off www.silverbranch.co.uk => About.

    * Go dig up Microsoft's font management extension thingy. It lets you lots more properties of fonts, including what level of embedding permission they have set. Maybe other DTP packages will tell you this, but my software didn't. Checking it out at the start when setting the styles for a project saves a lot of layout heartache later when you can't output your book.

    * Choosing fonts - make a page with a paragraph of text in several different fonts, print it out and look at it. You'll find that even fonts that seem quite similar can give a significantly different feel. (I kind of agree about Times being overused, and avoid it like the plague - except when I did unChosen, where that jaded, unambitious feel was what I wanted.)

    \n I get asked that all the time... what's a good body font.
    \n
    \nThere are hundreds of body fonts out there. Hundreds. You need to find one that's readable, not over-exposed, and that matches your design style. Not an easy task.
    \n
    \nAdd to that the fact that fonts need to be legible on screen and in print (damn pdfs) and you have a wicked combination of things to juggle.
    \n
    \nLuckily for INDIE developers, you don't also have BRANDING issues to contend with. I work in advertising and packaging during the day and you can't imagine how annoying it is to hunt down Helvetic Neue 57 Condensed Medium for one client and Helvetic Neue 97 Black Condensed for another.
    \n
    \nAt most, you have two or three fonts to deal with in your book... and in fact, limiting yourself to three fonts will really help your book design. Stick to your STYLE SHEET and you won't have to worry about whether or not the typography on page 17 works. It worked on 22 and it worked on 3. It'll work on 17.
    \n
    \nTo use Matthjs as an example, he used a separate font for his page numbers... which is just more work. Use your head or subheader font for your page numbers. Make sure your KEYS are legible.
    \n
    \nAnd make sure to keep my eye on the page. WHITE SPACE is great, but too much and I'm distracted and looking elsewhere. Know why something is weighted the way it is. Find out what you're missing and find out what you don't need.
    \n
    \nAm I typing in circles now? I hope this is helpful.

    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanI don't know what "embedding permisions" means... I suspect that's Microsoft DRM, yeh?
    \nNo. It's a hidden property of a font, which means you can get a book all nicely laid out as you want it and then be told by Acrobat that you can't output it to PDF. This is not good. There are four levels of setting, to do with whether you can embed the font in a created document at all (which is what happens in PDF so the user can view it without needing to have the same fonts installed), and whether you can edit it or install it on another machine. The font extension thingy lets you see that property. So as Jim said, you might find the version of a font that came with some software has limited embedding permissions, whereas if you buy it explicitly it'd be OK.
