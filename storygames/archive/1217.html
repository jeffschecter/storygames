    I'm on JD's level of experience, and I've spent a LOT of time thinking about this. One thing you need to take into account is why these people play. Here are some of the strongest motivations:

    a) Flirting and sexual relationships
    b) Social networking/making friends
    c) Avatarism (character serves as wish fulfillment for and is thoroughly interwoven with player)
    d) Showing off one's writing skill and other ego boosts

    None of these motivations are helped with additional rules. Note that these motivations are not mutually exclusive and that a player's particular motivation at a particular time differs and can depend on mood and other factors.

    There are other motivations (in the minority) such as expressing creativity and dramatic storytelling, and those might be helped, though I think mostly with techniques rather than rules (such as aggressive scene framing, situation creation, etc.).

    That said, the place where I currently play has a dice roller, people give their character three stats (magic, combat, psionics), and during conflicts they roll to see how well they did. Some people abuse the lack of guidelines for how exactly to use the system by just declaring three attacks and rolling thrice at one time, etc. But overall Lisa, who has never played table top in her life and has played character ownership chat games for as long as I have, has suddenly realized that having a random element and defined stats has actually added something to her play experience.

    This would usually lead me into my diatribe about the issues with freeform chat play, such as players' comfort zones, but I'll save that up for its own thread sometime.

    By no means am I absolutely committed to the idea that there is nothing that can be marketed to this population that they'll buy. On the contrary, I have the gut feeling that they're the Next Big Thing. But I have to recognize that this is only my gut feeling. God help me if I can point to anything that makes that even remotely plausible.

    I strongly agree with xeno's list, although it's not exhaustive.

    The biggest thing I'd add is celebration of the source material. And it has to be a certain kind of source material. There's a reason there's one million Buffy "simming" groups and few or none based on, say, the writings of Mickey Spillane.

    Here are some observations and then some thoughts. I could go on and on. If people have specific questions that would help:

    Observations:

    1. This population skews young. Sometimes VERY young, sometimes much younger than you might think from the level of writing. I have been extremely surprised by the level of writing that a 13 year old can produce. But remember the adage about source material being so important. They have put a lot of energy into the source material which might be more sophisticated than they "naturally" are, this allows them to fly on a higher level.

    2. The primary "game balance" concern of most groups (as gamers would understand it) is fairness, not equality. In other words, it is not necessary that everyone have the same "power level" or skill level or whatnot, so long as there is acknowledgments of all characters' strengths and weaknesses. Groups are typically highly sensitive to one person stealing too much spotlight. Even 13 year old girls these days know what a Mary Sue is and are trying not to put it in too much.

    3. The crossover between fanfic writing and play-by-post, yahoogroups play and "simming" is absolutely enormous. In practice, they're very similar. People often use characters and situations from one in the other.

    4. Players often play the same characters in many different forums, recycling the "application" for the character in the same way.

    Areas where damn near every one of these games could use improvement:

    1. Player dropout is ENORMOUS. Damn near every enterprise of this kind dies due to people just not posting anymore, plotlines falling fallow, etc. My yahoogroups list is full, full to bursting, with games I felt were neat, that I posted on actively for a few months, but then everyone started to drizzle away and it became "talking to the void".

    2. Conflict is avoided assiduously, to the degree that players are in general highly reluctant to introduce conflict even to drive the story further (or at all). I, with my background in pulp fiction and paperbacks, have absolutely no problem sending in four guys with guns to make my character's life hard, but I am an exception. (This may be connected to the very-young thing above? I remember in high school my fics were essentially character interaction with no conflict whatsoever.)

    3. Conversations between characters take forever compared to actions. A conversation can go back and forth tediously and painfully. Actions can be described quickly and extensively. (Conversations bogging down is one thing that leads to #1 having a huge impact, if the other person doesn't reply to your conversational post for like 3 weeks, you may be completely out of the loop.)

    There are also genre/field-specific questions. I have been playing in my first Star Trek sim, and there are all kinds of weird artifacts in that thing that I have been trying to figure out. I made a character more or less along the lines of Dr. Cox from Scrubs or House from the eponymous show, someone who is highly talented but stays more or less at the bottom of the pecking order due to being an insensitive, regulations-ignoring jerk. Yet because I wrote posts regularly, my character got promoted at the end of a mission. It had nothing to do with what the character did, in fact what the character did should have gotten him demerits and scrubber duty or whatever they do in Star Trek, but because I had out-of-character contributed to the mission, my guy went up a rank. I see what they're trying to do - reward people in-character for helping keep the game going out-of-character - but what consequences does that have for the person that wants to play the Really Bad Officer Who Never Should Have Been Assigned Here?

    Gads, I could go on more and more. Specific questions would help.

    I just want to second what JD and Christian said about the motivations that seem to inform this play -- I think "celebrating the material" is a crucial insight, and any system that is going to be successful for these groups will absolutely have to focus on colour and genre to a much greater degree. These people want to produce something exactly like the best parts of these shows/books/etc. -- they want to recreate Buffy with or without the numbers filed off. It's not about coming up with awesome stories in general -- it's about coming up with exactly the right kind of awesome story, with the same balance of elements as the original.

    While my experience is primarily with MUSHing, most of MUSH play is essentially freeform. The desire to perfectly simulate the source material is extremely strong -- to the point that a huge amount of player drama is the result of disagreements about how things would really work, or whether what is happening is consistent with each player's view of said source material. Concerns about whether it makes for a good story are secondary, and any variation from the accepted material is interpreted as an attempt to hog the spotlight. Of course, there is rarely any concensus about what part of the source material is acceptable -- I figure this is less of an issue with games based of TV series, since they have a much clearer canon than say, comic books or the WoD.

    As far as how these groups play, I think they play using a series of conventions that are enforced with extreme social prejudice. However, as mentioned, each group appears to have its own conventions, and the conventions themselves appear to develop organically. There is almost zero self-examination of these conventions or their possible impact on play -- they are simply "the right way to play," and disagreement is grounds for divorce. They can range from extremely specific -- a wide variety of them concern writing style, including first or third person, allowed tenses, etc. -- to very vague ("no metagaming") and are enforced through the usual Internet-social means.

    Edit: I also wanted to add that I don't think it's fair to say these people are all happy and having a perfect little gaming experience. I imagine this is more true of some of the bboard/yahoo groups, but if MU*ing is any indication, they are also full of people totally dissatisfied with what is going on and absolutely no idea how to change things for the better.
