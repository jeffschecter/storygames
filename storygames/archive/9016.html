How to communicate a game's theme

\n
\n
\n
\n\nCalvino \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2009 \n edited March 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Suppose you're writing a game. And suppose this game has some fairly well-defined theme, philosophical, ethical, political or other. Would you...

1) spell out this theme, tell your readers explicitly what the game is about and what they're supposed to attend to whilst playing it, thus risking locking their creativity in prefabricated patterns, or

2) not spell out the theme, but rather try skillfully to design system and setting so as to render it likely that the theme will be adressed during the course of play, or

3) not spell out the theme, but make it clear that there is a theme for the reader to discover, making the game a kind of puzzle or detective story for the reader/player to solve, all the while keeping open, of course, the possibility that there isn't actually no theme at all aside from the one the players are creating themselves (this might be termed the "Lacuna-approach").

What do you think are the pros and cons of these approaches?

    \n Reasons why you might want to explicitly state your well-defined theme:
    - You might be wrong about how well-defined it is. Perhaps you'll notice that when you try to explain it.
    - Explaining it helps you, no matter what effect it might have on your audience. Everyone knows one of the best ways to learn something and study it in depth is to teach it to someone else: it's likely that when you sit down to explain your theme, you'll understand how it relates to your game even better, and can feed that right back into your creative process.
    - Explicitly stating it makes it easy to provide references and other sources for people to consider: if the theme is that important to the game, why not give your audience some additional resources to explore it further?
    - You might be wrong about how cool the theme is. Explicitly stating it gives the reader an open invitation to notice this and call you on it.
    - Explicitly stating the theme makes it easier to deconstruct your game and do other things with it. You might not care about this, you might even HATE this, but it's still a good thing for everyone, I think. It will let your game reach new people (who will rip it apart and use it for their own purposes, yes, but that's show biz, and anyway, a sale is a sale).

    Reasons why you might not want to:
    - The theme might be unappealing, and stating it explicitly could drive people away. "Oh, I hate those games about man subjugating the environment." "Oh boy, another one-note symphony about mankind's alienation from himself."
    - Explaining your theme well can be really hard to do.
    - It can, as you mention, lock some people into prefabricated patterns. Could be good, could be bad; depends on what you like, I guess.


    If it were up to me, I'd say explain it. Don't skimp on #2, you should absolutely still try to skillfully design your system and setting to address the theme or produce the kind of play you want, but I really see more benefits from being up front and clear about your goals than I see drawbacks.

    \n This may sound like a cop-out, but doesn't it depend on the theme? Some themes likely won't be addresed if they aren't explicit and others don't need to be mentioned if the mechanics create them in play. You can create paranoia, desperation, and distrust with the right rules and secrets. If the theme is political allegory or the moral rights of the church or something, you'll probably want to take that head on.

    But, if you can reliably produce the theme in play through the mechanics, I would think that's a better choice - as long as the game is still fun and compelling.

    \n I don't know, I think "Design as if you were dead" could be a viable process for some people, at least if you're not normally in the camp that creates a game, releases it into the wild, and never looks back.

    Start from the position that once your game is out there, you no longer have any stake in it, that no one will care how you thought the game should be played, and that no one will, in fact, play it the way you thought they would. Your only contribution to the groups playing your game is posthumous: you are leaving a memoir, a record of your thoughts on the game that they may or may not pay any attention to.

    So, as with any memoir, your goals are to record your opinions about your game for posterity, to be interesting enough to get the attention of the people who survive you, and to contain useful information that they can identify with so that they don't think you're a boring old crackpot who had absolutely no idea what was good about his own game. You'll still be dead either way, all your contributions are just so that they'll think of you when they play your game.

    \n I think there's some vagueness in this thread that's leading people to talk past one another.

    To begin with, I don't know what's meant by the word "theme". Either people are using the word in a way that it's not usually used, or they are talking about games doing something they don't usually do.

    A game doesn't normally have a "theme", it has a thematic premise. A thematic premise is the generalized dramatic question that a game system asks, like "what's worth dying for?" or "who deserves mercy?". A "theme" is the specific answer that emerges from play, like "love is worth dying for" or "only the innocent deserve mercy". (This is basically how it works in movies and books as well.) Furthermore, it's worth noting that a game's thematic premise is often over-simplified for advertising's sake, and the true premise is much more broad. For example, Dogs is often described as being "about" judgment, while actually there's a whole lot in it about responsibility, coming of age, spirituality, getting along with others, etc. Similarly, emergent theme relies heavily on dynamic content, meaning the stuff that gets invented in play. So taking Dogs as the example again, the emergent theme might actually be something like "proving your worth to a loved one", even though there's nothing inherent to the rules to promote such a theme.

    So when y'all talk about a "game's theme", are you referring to the thematic premise, or do you mean a game that actually presents its own, pre-defined dramatic "answer"? There's more I can say, but I'm going to wait for some clarification.

    Posted By: JDCorley
    I think he's saying he has expectations, which will inevitably and always be proven completely wrong. At least that's been my experience.
    \nIndeed. I think that one can have *pre-conceptions* about a particular game but short of sitting down and playing that adventure with that GM and those players, there's little or no way to make an informed judgment about what a game is likely to be about. Pre-conceptions about games are just examples of lazy thinking and prejudice. there are so many levels of interpretation and creative autonomy in-between Pondsmith writing Cyberpunk and my sitting down to play a given session of Cyberpunk that "I'm playing Cyberpunk" is close to being meaningless.

    Discussions of roleplaying have, over the years, migrated into this form of ultra auteur theory. Auteur theory being a way of talking about a film in terms of the creative decisions of its director alone. The problem with auteur theory is that everyone in the cinematic process has a degree of creative input. From the actors and the DP down to the guys that do the lighting. In most cases the directors don't even have last cut of the films they direct. Auteur theory makes no philosophical sense as even before you get into questions of who is responsible for what effect and what their intentions might have been you have to deal with the text of the film that is hugely open to interpretation. One of the best films I saw in 2008 was a thriller whose real meaning had clearly completely escaped the Director. He'd wanted to make some sleazy reactionary piece aboutthe dangers of technology but watching the film it felt more like an attack on the border-line stalkerish behaviour everyone uses in the run up to asking someone out. You might ask your friends about them, maybe google them, prick up your ears when you hear what their favourite band is... the fact that the film's bad guy used tactics that most people use completely failed to register with the director. Therefore, the film he intended to make was a piece of shit, but the film that somehow got made is genuinely creepy and incredibly cool.

    Most RPGs have even more levels of interpretation and creative input between the author and the end product than films do. Doubly so if you have an RPG written by a load of different people.

    The only reason for talking about authors in relation to RPGs is as a way of doing PR. "Oh it's a such-and-such game! I'll buy that!" In truth who wrote the thing doesn't mean a thing. Games are things that emerge almost by accident... they are not written. Books are written and as any of those people with dozens of shelves full of supplements and core books but no gaming experience this decade can tell you, a book is not a game.

    \n For the games I like, I like the game to present what it's about in the form of a question. The idea being that the game's design is a tool for getting the group to address that question through the created fiction.

    Sorcerer - How far are you willing to go to get what you want?
    Dogs in the Vineyard - Where do the needs of the individual vs. the needs of the community begin and end?
    My Life with Master - Are some monsters worth loving?

    Other games (that I like) don't provide an explicit question but instead have tools built into them that allow the group to arrive at a custom question for that specific play instance.

    In A Wicked Age... -> the cross purpose best interests.
    Primetime Adventures -> The character's Issues
    Dirty Secrets -> The various crimes cross-sectioned with the demographics.

    I don't think, however, that you need to spell the question out either in the game text or even as part of play. I think people do a REALLY good job of extracting relevant life questions from evocative situations. Trollbabe works on this 100%. It just says: TROLLBABE! And you either go, "Yes, let's play!" or "Huh?"

    In fact, that's where my buy in to a game starts. I look at it's basic setup and see if it raises within me questions I want to answer through that setup. Then I look at the rules to see if they provide me with real tools to address those questions. Sadly, the answers are often Yes and No.
