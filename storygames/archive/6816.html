\n Rituals are a bit borked.

Most of the rest of 4e is slick, but rituals have me scratching my head. Even taking aside things like materials costs, which I've never used anyways (I mean, 1000gp for Passwall? WTF.), because they key on a resource that's not mechanically regulated, rituals are amazingly not fun. I think they're basically intended to be for outside-of-combat utility, which is all well and good, but the choices in rituals are odd. Comprehend Languages is a ritual, but Mordenkainen's Mansion is a daily power?

Has anyone else taken a look at rituals, or seen any decent discussions on them?

    \n Could you explain more what's broken about them? They make sense to me. If you're getting more-or-less the right amount of gold per encounter, the component cost is little more than a speed-bump, saying "don't do this all day every day". I don't think the game would fall apart if you removed that though.

    The reasoning for the rituals is pretty straightforward: Given a choice between spells that are fun but rarely useful, and spells that are actually good in combat, people will choose the combat spells every time. Rituals, which benefit the whole group mostly, remove that false choice - now you get both.

    I think the choices of which is which make sense to me too. Mordenkanian's Mansion is a daily power because it's useful in combat - it lets the entire party rest for eight hours and come back to the fight fresh - if they can make it to the door's square. Comprehend Languages, while it could be useful in combat, is a Ritual because most of the time you're not going to use it in combat, and you'd choose almost any power instead of it.

    I've seen complaints about specific rituals that make sense to me - Silence is just a perception penalty, and Knock only unlocks doors, rather than busting them open. I can get used to those differences though, and there's no reason not to create your own rituals that do more powerful things at higher levels. I'm expecting to see a lot of suppliments including extra rituals. That's the biggest advantage of rituals, in my opinion. Because they're never used in combat, I think it's much easier to create new rituals without unbalancing the game. Because rituals could concievably do anything, it makes magic feel much more magical. There's the counterargument that it was cool when every magical effect came from a specific appliction of a known spell, and I'm sympathetic to that, but the suppliment bloat in 3e kind of destroyed that anyway.

    \n To clarify the kind of cost we're talking about, a first level character can cast 36 rituals for the same cost as a magic item of their level. The gap narrows in the mid levels (a tenth level character can use 12.5 rituals for the same cost as a tenth level magic sword), but then widens dramatically at high levls (58 "True Portals" for the cost of a 28th level item). Bear in mind that generally characters are using items above their level, and that you're only going to be using a small number of rituals in the time it takes to go up a level. At first level, you'd get 14 rituals if you spent all of one character's gold income on rituals, not counting magic items - that's more than one ritual per encounter.

    The component cost for rituals is just there to say "don't use these without thinking about it - only use them when it's cool." The actual cost in terms of character effectiveness is negligable.

    \n Sure, Simon - myself, I'm regardless bothered by the solution, if only because it's a choice between meaningless accounting and actually imbalancing the game: either you use the rituals "correctly" and the expenditure of money is just unnecessary fiddling with pennies, or you use them "wrong" and the GM needs to ride to the rescue with extra treasure to ensure that the group doesn't lag behind the sacred development curve. Both are rather rotten outcomes in that they're extra work when the system could have easily been made self-balancing as well. (Of course, a self-balancing ritual system would really just remove a minor part of my own wealth leeching problematics - the modern D&D; system of using WBL guidelines simply breaks down horribly if you allow characters to spend their money on anything but combat effectiveness. I myself seem to need an average of 1.5 sessions before my character gets an idea involving buying an orphanage, tavern or castle with his ill-gotten gains, or something of the sort - might just waste it all on vine and women, retire on the profits or give it away to the people it was stolen from, who knows. So you might say that the money management system in modern D&D;, focused on channeling lucre into new magic items, really isn't doing it for me. The ritual system is just a minor annoyance on top of that.)
    \n
    \nThat being said, I do kinda like an implication the rituals have: with rituals being decoupled from combat powers of character classes, it becomes pretty trivial to take the decoupling to the logical conclusion and remove all implied connections between a character's combat class and his out-of-combat occupation. All that's needed really seems to be to remove class-based skill lists and some minor fiddling for edge cases. Fiction-wise, it seems to me that there'd be some rather interesting possibilities in having a "Fighter" who is actually an arcanist with fighter combat powers, for example - and that's whether you describe it as the fighter just being a scholar of the arcane, or the character being some sort of wizard who just happens to use fighter crunch on top of his fluff.
    \n
    \nAnother interesting question is why rituals are all just dull magic - I'd have kinda expected that if somebody bothered to have this kind of rules-system, he'd include all kinds of fun non-magical "rituals" as well. Having a ritual for putting together a safari with the bargaining skill (whatever it's called) would be interesting and useful, for example. Actually, I think that I'd probably need to go through the ritual list with a pen if I were ever to run an extended campaign of the game - I'd want to get rid of the high-fantasy stuff I don't like so much, adding in the cool stuff that inexplicably didn't make the cut. I'd also make up some guidelines for slotting skill challenges into rituals - it's pretty strange that rituals, which could be rather big deals fiction-wise, are handled without particularly detailed rules-contact.
    \n
    \nI don't remember off-hand whether this already was in the ritual chapter, mind, but one simple thing I'd probably do with it would be to have characters buy the ritual equipment in advance - so instead of just burning abstract gold whenever you wanted a ritual, you'd have to at least go into town and buy whatever components the ritual required. This'd also make the ritual thing a bit more worthwhile as a logistical challenge, as you'd need to try to figure out beforehand which rituals you might want to cast in the dungeon, and how many of each. Make rituals inventory items, in effect, like scrolls used to be.
    \n
    \nThat's my thoughts on rituals, anyway.

    \n The cost issue has no one fix, since it depends a lot upon the GM's approach to money in a particular campaign. That said, in D&D money really _is_ mechanically regulated, at least as written. You can discard that in favor of an economy that makes more sense, and many do, but the existing system puts a lot of thought into how much money players get and how much they have to spend.

    That said, I've considered the following house rules:
    * Players have a kit of supplies of a specific type (Nature, arcana, etc) with a specific GP value. So long as the value of the kit is greater than the cost of the ritual, they can use it. Casting a ritual uses up 10% of its cost from the value of the kit.

    * Magic Items and Ritual Items contribute a permanent value to your ritual pool, effectively providing a discount. Having the shield of St. Cuthbert might count for 400g towards any religious ritual, but not be expended with use. These items might be rare and powerful (in which case they should be storied) or common but weak (resulting in wizards carrying stacks of crap - some people dig that)

    * Allow Skill Challenges to reduce the cost by sending the rest of the party off to scrounge, beg, borrow or steal components. This depends on either a creative ritualist or an amusing table to random needs, but it might be fun to figure out what skill check they'll use to get a virgin's tears. I am personally fond of this one, but have not yet done the math for it.

    * Make ritual spellbooks bigger, much bigger, and require large, expensive accoutrement's. In doing so, you basically force rituals to be something done back in town, with a bag of holding meaning you can bring the apparatus for one or two along. That then makes the price to cast into the price to pay an NPC to cast the ritual. Now add a feat that allows a player to internalize a ritual (I'd say treat them like languages, three per feat) and make internalized rituals take just as much time, but require many fewer ingredients, just chalk and string and bits on hand. The only drawback to internalized feats is that they use a bit of the character's will to smooth over the details, so casting such a ritual costs a healing surge.

    * The rituals in the book are the ones you can find in Rituals for Dummies. Better, cheaper, more potent versions exist, but they tend to be in lich filled libraries and ancient goblin temples. If only there were adventurers around!

    Rituals are a great dial in my mind, so I admit I'm all for fiddling with them.

    -Rob D.

    \n I dig rituals. Essentially, I did the same thing, though with hand waving rather than formal rules, in previous editions of D&D. It's nice to see it formalized now.

    In my last big D&D campaign, it ended with the party sorcerer agreeing to help the arch-villain perform a major ceremony to bind a group of doppelganger assassins that had been making trouble all over the kingdom. The PC knew that the arch-villain was going to pervert the ceremony into one that would make him a lich (and sacrifice the PC in the process) at the last minute. So the PC did the research to figure out how to double-double-cross the villain and change the ritual so HE would be come a lich and bind the doppelgangers at the same time.

    This plot would have been easily handled with 4e rituals and skill challenges. As it was, we kind of hacked it with a bunch of opposed Know (Arcana) checks, but the new rules would have made it much more of a "mental chess game" encounter.

    A ritual is an excuse to let any magical effect into the game without worrying about balance.

    \n Something to remember about rituals is that they don't just cost money, they require components that you have to buy with money. So if you're out in the middle of the woods and you want to cast Animal Messenger, you don't just hand 10 gold pieces to the nearest squirrel, you have to have already bought 10 gp worth of Nature components and brought them with you. So rituals are a way of putting back a little of the resource management/planning ahead aspect of previous editions without totally bogging the game down with it.

    The money that the components cost is really secondary in importance to "how many gp worth of components do we have on hand right now?" and "do I really want to use these components now or save them for later?" Take that away, and the rituals just become at-will powers that happen to take a long time to activate.
