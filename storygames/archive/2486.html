So what do you drink when gaming?

Andy
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
For me, it's mostly water, and sometimes coffee if it's been a long day at work (though my Thursday group, Alan and I have a long, grand tradition of taking cat-naps in the middle of gaming on those rare occasions when we have huge work projects: We actually game around that). Although I came to realize recently that after being involved with Three Gaming Groups, and about 10 local gamers who meet up about once a month, Clinton and I are the only gamers in the area groups that actually drink coffee.

Every once in a while in my weekly group someone brings a case of HOEGAARDEN (weiss beer) or Blue Moon, and I'll have one then. Rarely enough to get a buzz, though, as I find it distracting:



The Official Beer of Thursday Night Gaming

Otherwise, I usually don't drink soda or juice (in general, or while gaming).

When I'm running something (as a GM) and am nervous, or introducing a new game, I usually have a glass of wine or a mixed drink of some kind (Rum and Cola). I find that it takes the edge off.

How about you?

-Andy

      Almost always water. Very rarely I will add a soda. I also drink 12 ounces of tea a day, which could happen at game, usually decaf green tea or herbal apple tea.

      For those folks who don't like to have to deal with a kettle, I drink 'fridge' tea much of the time. I have a one gallon container with a tight screw off lid, this is not a pitcher, you can flip it over and the water remains inside. I put four tea bags in the container, with the strings going over the threaded end. I then screw the lid on. This means tea bags are suspended in the air inside, and the tags are on the outside. I then add water through the snap off portion of the lid, filling up the container. I stick it in the fridge and let it sit for at least about 12 to 14 hours, but typically at least 24 hours. Then I pour it into a frozen beer mug and add splenda to taste. I like it, you might also. I typically reuse the bags by adding more water after use to make 2 or so batches with one set of tea bags.

      I call it fridge tea because all the iced tea recipes I've found call for brewing with hot water and then chilling the tea which makes the tea really bitter to my taste. Fridge tea isn't bitter, so you need less sweet.

      Clinton,

      Not Coke Dark, Coke Zero -- which is normal Coke without sugar. This is as opposed to Diet Coke, which is New Coke without sugar.

      As far as the Mormon thing goes, it's like this. We've got this scripture (in the Doctrine and Covenants) called the Word of Wisdom. It has some dietary prohibitions and general advice, and is sort of an elastic scripture -- it's supposed to be read with a few absolutes but with much room for personal interpretation beyond those absolutes.

      Anyway, the most famous prohibitions are absolute bans on alcohol, tobacco, coffee and tea (in the scripture these are "hot drinks" a point that was clarified by a latter prophet, more on that later), and any kind of hard drug. There are also less famous (and less followed) recommendations about the limitation of meat, especially red meat, in the diet and a focus on whole grains and fruits and such.

      The most contentious point in the scripture in the modern church is over the exact position of "hot drinks." We've been told since that coffee and tea (black and green tea, at any rate) are right out. Some people also won't drink any drink that is hot, such as hot chocolate. Others will drink herbal teas, but nothing made from tea leaves. And, of course, many people take it as a prohibition (or recommendation) against caffeine. This is absolutely fine and in accordance with the spirit of the scripture -- as we're supposed to use it as a basis to decide what is healthy for us and what isn't. The problem is that lots of folks like to make their thing everyone's thing, and some well publicized drives in this area have left many people, in the church and out, with the idea that caffeinated soda is categorically against the Word of Wisdom -- which isn't true.

      So, any more questions?

      (Yes, it would become an acronym as WoW.)
