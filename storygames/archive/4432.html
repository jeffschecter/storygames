    \n\nClint Krause \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nOctober 2007 \n edited October 2007
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Well, the game gives you a loose premise (a "quest" as the case may be) but the content can be totally different each time. The GM is free to create challenges and such so the proceedings are different each time. It's like the dungeon in D&D. Yeah you've done 50 dungeons before . . . but this one's got a different layout, different monsters, and new, exciting challenges to explore.
    \n
    \nAnd then there are the dark fates, which REALLY mix things up. Even two games of MW with roughly the same challenges would play out very differently based on what fates people drew and how they decided to play them.
    \n
    \nFinally, and this is probably the biggest point: The game isn't about killing the Mountain Witch . . . it's about the characters who are trying to kill him and their agendas, trials, and ability to relate to each other. In other words, the thing about the game that SEEMS repetitive (some dudes with swords, uh, climb a mountain) isn't ultimately what the focus of the game is. It's bound to be different every time based on who those dudes are and what they do with their swords.

    Posted By: Lars Ericson

    Can you give some advice on running TMW at a convention in a single session? I've run TMW once as a two session game and it rocked. I'm thinking about running it atOwlCon (Houston, TX; Feb. 8-10, 2008)next year.

    \nDo check out the link I posted in my second post on this thread. But here are some quick tips:

    1. Spend some time getting to know everyone at the table, what they expect from the game, what they know about it and what they're bringing to it.

    2. Tell everyone straight out "By the end of this, some or all of your characters will probably be dead, very likely by each others' hands." Explain how the game is designed to bring tensions between characters to a boiling point. Be sure everyone is totally cool with this. Tell them, the Witch is like the suitcase in Pulp Fiction, or the heist in Resevoir Dogs - resolution by killing the Witch is less important than what happens between the characters.

    3. Tell everyone about how the game is designed for a slow burn, but for this session, you're going to drop the hammer and bust the quarter mile in no time flat, and therefore, every player has got to step up and take their characters' fates in their hands. If the players aren't pushing their Dark Fates and associated agendas at every opportunity, they won't have as much fun. Players must engage in order to make it work. Be sure to stress the importance of player involvement. Tell them your job as the GM isn't to stop the characters from getting up to the top of the mountain, it's to make them want to kill each other when they get there!

    4. Require players to foreshadow their Dark Fates at least once, if not twice or more, during every chapter. "OK, before we start, I want a foreshadowing scene from everyone." "Does anyone want to take this opportunity to foreshadow?" "Jimmy, you're up, give us a foreshadowing scene." Push, push, push. There is no time to be coy about the Dark Fates in a 4 hour con slot. If a player hasn't pretty much revealed their character's Dark Fate by the end or middle of Chapter 3 (or earlier), you should ask the player to give you a scene that if it does not totally reveal the Dark Fate, it nearly does.

    5. Start with a bang, tailored to a specific character if possible. Or start with a general event that makes everyone engage the rules and begin evaluating Trust immediately. A bakemono or oni attack is good.

    6. Nobody rides for free. If someone is not doing anything, start pressing his or her character's buttons.

    7. Keep an eye on the clock and move when you have to. I shoot for 4 chapters with about one chapter every 30 to 45 minutes - 1. Getting through the haunted forest to the base of Fuji-san; 2. Ascending the mountain; 3. Infiltrating the castle; 4. Final confrontation with the Witch.

    8. Constantly remind players of their Trust options in conflicts - aid, betray, and buy narration.

    9. Be prepared. Even if you don't make notes, be prepared to keep them - you need to know how to target bangs at the characters for one thing.

    10. Be enthusiastic! Keep the pace up! If nothing is happening, make something happen, now! Keep reminding players of their Trust options, their responsibility to push the Dark Fates, and encourage them to engage their characters and each other at every opportunity!

    Good luck!
