Hey everybody!

I've got this killer idea for a game, that I think is really going to rock. I'm to the point where I'm about to enter game jam play testing with my crew, and hopefully shake out some real rules in the near future. What I don't have is a name worth calling it. And I'm hoping that the seething creative fury of Story-Games can help out.

The game concept is international espionage, stretched broadly and cynically enough to include terrorist (or revolutionary) groups and major organized crime. Players will control several agents each, in one round planning the life threatening missions that they'll later carry out as the agents in the field. I'm definitely inspired strongly by Greg Rucka's Queen & Country. I want to address both the people in the field risking their lives (hopefully) for their cause, but also that in order to protect their ideals, they have to sacrifice their own expressions of them. The end run is also hitting the idea that relatively small, essentially normal men and women are acting in the arena reserved for sovereign nations, and making decisions at best on the hope that the people they represent would approve of their actions if they could only know.

Current musings include:
  • Matters of State(this is the current working title)
  • Blood of Patriots
  • Peers of the Realm
  • Sovereign Soldiers
  • Hidden Princes
  • World Orders
  • Cover and Seal
  • Under Cover of the General Interest
  • They Died with Their Masks On
I'm not thrilled with any of the above. In fact, some of them are really wretched. Which is why I'm reaching out here. Any ideas?

    Posted By: Graham Walmsley"Ask Not" is too American, but if you want an English version, how about "England Expects" (a reference to a signal sent by Nelson during the Battle of Trafalgar)?
    As much as "England Expects" pushes my buttons, it's as problematic as "Ask Not" for the same reason: I decidedly want each player to be running their own group, and that could mean MI-6, the CIA, SVR, or any one from this list. Mischa keeps using the Secret Masters of the Boy Scouts of America as a funny example when we talk about it. Part of the challenge to this, that I've found, is that the really good names are very specific, and the general names aren't so good.

    On the other hand, I'm also dwelling in Latin, which is bad because I've got no training with it whatsoever. And it technically leaves out the Eastern (and Middle Eastern) world. That said: Patria Specto or Referum Patria? By which I intend: The Fatherland Expects, or To Delver to the Fatherland. Bah.

    Following Mark's suggestion, I've been searching Google Books, and came across Hallek's International Law and Vattel's Law of Nations which both have some choice observations about espionage. One passage that will at least make it to the final text, and might be inspirational:

    "Spies are generally condemned to capital punishment, and not unjustly; there being scarcely any other way of preventing the mischief they may do. For this reason, a man of honour, who would not expose himself to die by the hands of the common executioner, ever declines serving as a spy. He considers it beneath him, as it seldom can be done without some kind of treachery. The sovereign, therefore, cannot lawfully require such a service of subjects, except, perhaps, in some singular case, and that of the last importance."
