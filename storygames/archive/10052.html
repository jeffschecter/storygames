\n Years ago, I stood at a convention booth at ICON, a mixed sci-fi/anime/gaming convention. We were trying to sell indie games across the way from someone who was (it seemed to me) trying desperately to avoid drowning in money as a result of putting stacks of manga books out on a table. It was, to say the least, a humbling experience.
\n
\nI turned to the people next to me and said "Man, if I were a completely mercenary money-grubber, which I am, I would design an RPG where I could make a cover with a boy and a girl gently kissing while cherry blossoms fell around them. That's clearly where the money is."
\n
\nToday I finished the cover art for the wrappers that will go around the tuck boxes of Misery Bubblegum cards:

    \n Translation would seem entirely feasible, as Misery Bubblegum has a nice, tight wordcount (of short text on the cards). But for what it's worth, the reason indie designers don't translate their games is because if they can't speak the foreign language they can't properly interact and represent the game to the foreign gamer internet community, and answer questions from prospective customers. And interaction and enthusiasm is what makes self-publishing work for game designers. I've had several offers to translate My Life with Master to German for very reasonable fees. But the translation offer an indie designer needs is from someone who wants to evangelize and represent for the game in the foreign culture.

    Paul

    \n Hey Lance,

    My only further thoughts are that I disagree with Ewen. If I were to pay for translation and print a German language edition of My Life with Master and put it up for sale on my website, I'd sell fewer than a handful of copies. The Finnish and Italian translations of My Life with Master have been successful because Eero and Michele and Claudia licensed the rights to translate and publish those editions from me, and they represent for the translated editions and interact with the native Finnish and Italian gamers. A designer can't himself create enthusiasm for his game in a culture for which he doesn't speak the language.

    Paul
