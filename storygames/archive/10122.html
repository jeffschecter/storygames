\n Hi all,
\nRather than clog up this post on Birthright, this thread is to ask for help tracking down rpgs (and other games) which cover similar ground.
\n
\nWhat games are you aware of that are multi-layered in that they are able to deal with high, medium and low level situations. By this I mean High level as in the political / grand strategic level, medium level the operational level and low level the tactical.
\n
\nExamples I can think of are Birthright, Reign and Burning Empires which mechanically and thematically support all three levels of play to differing extents and success.
\n
\nWhat others are out there, and how well do they succeed in seamlessly integrating all three (or at least 2 of the 3) levels of play?

    \n One example of a game that tried this is Underground. I only played it briefly, but while the high level stuff fired me up to try the game it never came up while we were playing it.

    A lot of gear heavy science fiction games marry the tactical and operational fairly well, where the marshaling of resources to obtain or create more gear creates tactical subplots. I'm sure people could throw up a million examples - a Shadowrun game I played in came to mind where we took over a section of the Barrens as a hard end of a street gang and built out our underground lab. All the gear creation stuff in all of the books went into the game, and we worked hard to get the customers, materials and money we needed to complete our plans.

    It strikes me that that sort of thing is pretty common in D&D as well (Stronghold Builder's guide, magic items, etc.). A lot of campaigns center on a Lord of the Rings style war, and there is generally quite a bit of operational detail involved. In my experience, most of the games of that sort have only sort of grazed the strategic level, often heavily prodded by the GM.

    Spelljammer involved all three levels, although without much support for the strategic level. Operationally you had a lot to take care of to keep your ship in top condition, and the rules supported it fairly well.

    One of the treasures from a early module was an entire uninhabited planet. The strategic things emanating from that became a big deal in the game, which is interesting because I think I sprang exactly from the module the way the designers intended - precisely by having no support. Only the size, fauna and buildings were detailed. There was no mention of how you could populate it, who might want it, or how much you could sell it for. In reverse order those things induced us to keep it, have recurring villains try to seize it, and populate it from our home nations.

    \n This has actually been one of my specific designs goals in Quincunx. I've been trying to develop a system where character versus character works in a similar but fractally magnified manner when dealing with team versus team play, then fractally magnified the same way for faction versus faction play.

    The levels of game play aren't identical, but once a player has mastered the basics of one play style, they've got the concepts for each level of play.

    I know some people have an aversion to the Forge around here, but THIS is a thread that I started about the notion.
