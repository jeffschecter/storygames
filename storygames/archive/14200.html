    \n The post title 'what freeform means' and the post - which sounds like we're defining the differences between mechanics and procedures - don't jibe for me. Paul seems to be answering the title.

    I'd like an answer to the other thing. I've also been using the word 'techniques' lately. I think I know them when I see them but I'm not sure how to define them.

    "Say 'try a different way'" "Write down an issue." "Everyone gets four scenes." "Move the characters tokens to the stage they're on for the scene" "Decide what hollywood actor would play your character" "Choose whether you escape into the night, recant, or burn at the stake" - procedures

    "Don't narrate another player's character's thoughts." "Make Apocalypse World seem real" "Try to make good things happen for your character." "draw a veil if the subject matter is uncomfortable for you" - techniques

    "Roll 2d6+hard, on a 10+ do 2 harm." "After your fifth experience point, mark improvement." "When you fall below 0 hit points, you're unconscious." "If you lose the roll, get back your pool dice +1" "When you act on your key, gain xp." - mechanics

    But then there's gray areas. What's Archipelago's "Draw a Resolution card and interpret the results" - is that a procedure, mechanic, technique - or a mix?

    One difference is purpose: a mechanic is usually there to softly incent player behavior. ("I better not roll, because I'll probably lose," or "I better roll, because I'll get xp" or "I better act roguish, so I can draw a card." or "I better play to my weaknesses so I can go on the owe list.") Mechanics might take a while to kick in, because the players won't realize until after multiple playing the value of the incentives. And a player might always choose to ignore the mechanic.

    A technique or procedure, otoh, usually demands a certain player behavior. It's the rule. You have to do this. If you don't "Make Apocalypse World seem real", then you're not playing Apocalypse World.

    \n I like the following criteria (and I'm not going to go into my experiences with these forms of play right now, if that's ok\u2014I know you're supposed to in the other thread but it's alright not to here, right?), some of which people mentioned in the other thread, to broadly define what it means for a roleplaying game or activity to be "freeform":

    -characters are not mechanically differentiated; my guy doesn't have a sword skill while yours has a magic skill or whatever

    -relatedly, the game's mechanics don't directly impact characters' "powers," like flying or whatever, though the genre might\u2014so it might be appropriate to narrate flying carpets in an Arabian Nights story but not in a realistic modern-day one

    -also relatedly but even more broadly, there are simply no mechanics to resolve conflicts, short of perhaps "the GM decides what happens"

    -the general improv rule of "say yes" is in effect\u2014that is, players have broad power to introduce facts into the narrative (even if there is a GM-type-role in the game): "say, remember when we had Chinese food last week, and you got all sick when you ate the General Tso's?" (Dave Berg may disagree with me on this one here! I'll certainly stipulate that this particular criterion is heavily limited by genre.)
