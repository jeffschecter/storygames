\n So I was listening to the latest episode of the Canon Puncture podcast and they're interviewing a freelancer with Palladium. The interviewer asks him why he doesn't independently publish, and he gives his reasons, and it reminded me of something I've been wondering for a while.

Is there room in the independent gaming world for a collective of independent designers to get together, publish under an imprint, share one another's expertise on marketing, physical design, experience with publishing, maybe even share pieces of cost and profit?

Such a thing would have to have utter creative control (though that becomes harder with cost and profit sharing), but it strikes me that excellent design skills do not necessarily overlap with excellent marketing and publishing skills, or any of the various things that come with the entrepreneurial skill-set.

    \n We have a kind of informal group here in town that I refer to as the Portland Gang. Matt Schlotte, Travis Brown, Nick Smith, April Brown and myself. We help each other out with writing, art, playtesting, layout and all kinds of other stuff. Sometimes we work on games together (Nick and I are currently working on Sea Dracula while Trav and I are working on Gun Mage), other times we develop games solo. But in either case we have the support and input of the whole group. This extends to sharing resources like advertising, publishing and distribution services (like Lulu and Drivethru RPG account), printers, shipping, convention space and other stuff.

    I'm personally adverse to the idea of banding together the Portland Gang as an actual company, but we do function as a fairly tight group, and we've been talking about ways that we might do things that will make our publishing efforts both easier and more effective.

    Posted By: Robert BohlIs there room in the independent gaming world for a collective of independent designers to get together, publish under an imprint, share one another's expertise on marketing, physical design, experience with publishing, maybe even share pieces of cost and profit?
    \nI'd say that there's not only room for them, but that could easily be split up into two sorts of collectives: design collectives (like the Master Mines group that Mike stealthily linked to) where the group gets together to hash out each other's designs, and publishing collectives where people pool resources to publish.

    But, and here's the thing, there isn't much difference between our ideas of this ("ours" meaning folks like us who are doing this small press thing because it's awesome, not because there's money in it) and just getting a groups of friends together for this, because it's practically the same thing. It's a naturally-created process. I got Paul Tevis to write for me and Fred Hicks to do layout for my book not because we're in some collective but because we're friends. Mike & I got to know each other at an Endgame Minicon, and a couple months later we decided to start up a design group.

    I'm not trying to nay-say, but just pointing out the differences in what we're doing versus what other folks are doing. It looks like we have room for collectives based on friendships and social networking, but is there room for something more structured and, I hate to say it, "business-y"?\n

    \n My brother and I were just talking about this on a roadtrip this weekend. When you see all the indie comics publishers out there that basically serve as an editorial and production staff for independent creators, it seems like doing something like that for small press games would be awesome. Pooling the talents and skills of groups of indie designers is one way of getting at it and a great solution in the short term.

    What we'll also need in the long term -- I suspect -- is people who are not primarily interested in being game designers and are more interested in the printing, marketing, editing, bookkeeping end of things. Initially, in many indie comics, this ends up being handled by the spouse of the comic creator, which is not really optimal. There are, however, a bunch of indie comics publishers that specialize in handling the footwork and leaving the creators more free to focus on developing creative content.

    Indie comics publishers we could look to for inspiration:
    - Fantagraphics
    - Drawn & Quarterly
    - Top Shelf
    - Image
    - Oni
    - Picturebox
