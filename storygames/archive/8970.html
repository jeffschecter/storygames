So, I was thinking about like shuffleboard and Bingo. You know, games to play when you're old.

\n

What kinds of roleplaying games are there for old people? I'm thinking that Shadows would be pretty good. Also PTA. I figure you don't want anything too fragile or taxing, since you want the games to be safe to play for people who need assistance doing basic things like having teeth or finding their glasses (they are on your head), and you want them to be fairly robust to drifting attention spans, bathroom breaks, and so on. So Polaris and Agon are out.

\n

I'd probably steer away from anything with particularly racy subject matter, too. Old people talking about sex is kind of... well, we've all seen Golden Girls. It is not pretty.

\n

Also maybe we should consider arthritis-friendliness. Is all that dice rolling a good idea? I think maybe it isn't.

\n

That eliminates a lot of pretty good games, which makes me think maybe that's why shuffleboard and Bingo are not exactly shining examples of explosive fun (limited design space and all), but I'm sure that there are great games that live in that space already. Where are they?

    \n I don't know that old people have many special needs that younger people don't have. Sure, some of them have failing memory, but that's not the entire group.

    The main factors, I think, are generation gap and the fact that they're not gamers. To address the first, choose subject material to which they can relate. They're not going to get Panty Explosion. They will get Grey Ranks or Spione, though. To address the second, design rules that are simple and don't rely on gamer-fetishism (this advice applies to designing games for non-gamers of any age).

    It seems odd to design a game just for old folks. I mean, sure, the people in the retirement home have a lot of free time and spend most of their time with other older, retired people. I think the vast majority of people over the age of 70, though, live with family or live alone. A lot of 20-somethings have trouble finding four other people to game with; I can't imagine it'd be any easier for a 70-something living alone.
