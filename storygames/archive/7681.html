\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2008 \n edited September 2008 in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I'm currently writing Bleakworlds, a sci-fi fugitive game. To British people, that'll be a Blake's Seven game; to others, it's a Firefly game.

I'm finding it difficult to make failure satisfying. For example, if any of the following things fail:

1. You fail to hack the electronic lock to the military base.
2. You fail to hack the computer.
3. You fail to sneak past the guard.
4. You fail to persuade the merchant to sell you the crystals at a knock-down price.
5. You fail to make the ship take off.

...what would be a satisfying failure?

I'd appreciate some help: either tell me what would be a satisfying way to fail each of those five tasks; or give me a solution to the general problem.

One quick point: I'm trying to avoid the PCs being captured. One problem I have is: if the consequence for failure is "There are guards coming", it often leads to the PCs' capture, which kills the story.

Thanks for any help!

Graham

    \n When there's a failure that happens, I've also seen:

    - you muddle through, but Someone Is Watching / Tipped Off / Sneaks On Board (but, this does lead to Guard Are Coming)
    - someone gets hurt / KO'd / pushed into a sideplot
    - some key piece of gear is borked and needs a sideplot to get working again
    - you have to do it the Hard Way (but the Hard Way has to have its own consequences)

    1. You fail to hack the electronic lock to the military base.
    2. You fail to hack the computer.
    You're given access/crippled-access, but you're "bugged" and now tracked as well.
    You're given bad information.
    The defense mechanism tazes a friend. Now you have to find a medkit!
    You'll have to go the Other Way, which is probably unsavory and/or gross.

    3. You fail to sneak past the guard.
    Alarms activated (no element of surprise).
    You have to fight him.
    Taken to the chief who, being corrupt, will dumbly try to use you for his own ends rather than handing you over to the authorities.

    4. You fail to persuade the merchant to sell you the crystals at a knock-down price.
    The Vice Cop sees what you're doing and breaks up the marketplace.
    You get the gems for far too much money (and need a sideplot to score some more coin).
    OH NO YOU HAVE INSULTED THE MERCHANTS GUILD / RIOT IN THE MARKETPLACE.

    5. You fail to make the ship take off.
    You'll have to abandon the ship and grab a ride with this unsavory band of pirates.
    Or, survive in the wilderness of the planet you're on.
    Or, survive amidst the dangerous population while you fix the ship.



    I think Blake's 7 (maybe other shows) did have capture happen every other episode. Y'know, the guard get blake, they walk him down the hall with guns at his back, maybe some local goon scowls at him, then when he's being walked to the brig he knocks out the goon and is on the loose again. Not more than once per episode, though. So if you want to avoid capture as a consequences, don't roll for such things unless you can think of a satisfying alternate route, or say that they succeeded but very poorly (in an interesting way).

    Hi Graham,

    I'm going to give your question on the skill checks some thought because you've nailed exactly one of the reasons that GDW's Classic Traveller rules failed me -- even as a youth! And the problem still niggles at my brain. I wanted those cool, Hard SF skill checks to matter and to make sense... but what do you do when the PCs fail? After a while, it always just got dull. However, I don't think I ever saw the problem so starkly phrased and understood it so well as I do now having read your post.

    You have nailed one of the reasons I abandoned Task Resolution once I realized I could and have never looked back. (Max: Mea Culpa)

    That all said, it always seemed that in a cool SF game (like Traveller, or yours) you should be able to make such science and tech skill checks cool. But I never figured it out. I'll be starting up a Traveller game Sunday, jettisoning all pre-2001 rules concepts and using the mechanics of Sorcerer (sans demons) in part because I could never figure out a way around this problem.

    The reason I want my brain working on it is because I think it still should be possible to make such things cool. As it stands, I find conflicts between interests to always lead to more interesting results, either in failure or success.



    Off the to of my head I think you'll be heading to a "story-structure-interesting" solution-- like PsiRun, for example (there are others) -- where a roll to do something provokes flashbacks, flashforwards and other bits of narration that let us, as audience members and creators, get a better handle on who the characters are and deepen the conflicts and characters. That wouldn't work for Traveller, but since you're posting this at story-games, it might line up with your agenda more properly.

    So, my first question is, are you trying to find a solution that is old school, hard SF skill check Task Resolution, or are you open to narrative-structure chicanery (like on the TV show Lost, for example), where the stress of avoiding capture triggers background moments of character?

    \n What if they don't fail immediately? For every consequential roll let them bet something that they'll lose in a later scene if the roll fails; if the roll succeeds they keep the bet and get what they want, if the roll fails they succeed but will lose the thing. Tie the bets to the level of risk involved (i.e. higher the risk, worthier the bet), it could include resources, contacts, traits, anything. In this way you separate the mechanics from the story and don't need to change the scene.

    \n1. You fail to hack the electronic lock to the military base.

    Your team leader and love interest loses faith in your abilities.

    2. You fail to hack the computer.

    The computer feels sorry for you and offers you the information you need if you promise to help build a mobile chasis for it and free it from it's oppressors. Oh, it wants a new graphics card too.

    3. You fail to sneak past the guard.

    The guard recognizes you an old army buddy. He won't let you in, but he won't turn you in either. He may become a recurring character as well.

    4. You fail to persuade the merchant to sell you the crystals at a knock-down price.

    It turns out that merchant is one of the few honest ones on this planet and his crystals are genuine and reliable. You might not have gotten a bargain, but you won't have to worry about premature crystal burnout either (automatic success when rolling on the Crystal Failure Table).

    5. You fail to make the ship take off.

    You have to fix a race to win a bet to buy new parts and you end up with a freed slave-boy apprentice as an unexpected bonus!

    Okay, that was mostly under duress, but I'm trying to give good answers to be helpful. I agree with other posters that these circumstances are largely inherent problems with all tasked-based systems. You shouldn't make anyone roll for anything unless the outcome will add to and not detract from the story and the fun. In particular, #5 is very lame. I've played games (Star Wars) where you're supposed to roll for a successful takeoff or starting up the ship and it's almost always a bad idea. Even if you turn it into something good, there's no justifiable reason for starting out with something so lame.

    Another kinda-meta idea is to track failures and have them add up to something big and bad later. In each of the circumstances above, the players can skate by because enforcing the failure result would stiffle the story (or anytime the GM can't think of an interesting consequence for a particular check). But after five failures of this kind, something bad happens story-wise and the party has to deal with it. Maybe up until that point the players were calling the shots and taking turns framing scenes, but after five collective failures the GM gets a scene and gets to advance the villain's plans significantly - and the villain knows what the PCs have done up to that point! That would make each skill check count and when you get to four failed checks it could make a normally boring check very tense as everyone watches to see if this will be the roll the triggers the next bad guy scene.

    \n Thanks for these responses. I meant to get back to them sooner.

    I'm seeing a few general categories of failure, which go something like:

    1. You just fail: You fail. You can try again, if you like.
    2. Locked out: You fail. You can't try again.
    3. You need this: You can't succeed until you do or get something.
    4. Deal with this: You fail and this thing happens. Deal with it.
    5. Deal with this later: You fail and this thing will happen later.
    6. Suffer this: You fail and get a disadvantage.

    Here, 4, 5 and 6 come in a "you succeed" flavour, too. ("You succeed, but this thing happens. Deal with it.")

    With 3, 4 and 5, there's an implication that you need to make a mechanical roll: to deal with the thing or to get the thing you need.

    With 6, there's an implication that you're mechanically hampered in some way.

    That's useful, because I can give the GM specific limits to narration ("You can say the Criminals need something. It'll cost them one die roll to get"). Thank you very much.
