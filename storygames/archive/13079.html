\n I'm GMing our first Mortal Coil game tomorrow, and there's just one thing in the rule book that I don't get. Desperate reactions. I'm hoping someone here gets it.

1) If a character has already committed to 4 actions, does that mean he can't do Desperate Reactions at all? I.e. his defense will be zero against the particular attack that he hadn't scripted any defense against?

2) For the option of committing two action tokens in order to get a Desperate Reaction, I take it that any tokens from the Action Pool have to be used first. The book has an example where the first token is taken from the Pool and the second from another scripted action. But what if the pool is empty to start with? Can both tokens come from another action?

3) There is also an option of spending one Action token to re-allocate tokens. Can you do this without first doing the "commit 2 action tokens" step? Or are these two independent choices? Edit: The significance of this, as I see it, is that if you don't have to first do the "commit 2" step, you could still keep an action token in your Pool, spend a committed one, and therefore not "go all in" and get fatigue. See also question 4

4) In the example on p. 88, would Jason end up with two fatigue: One for the re-allocation, and one for "going all in" (per the fatigue rules)?

    \n I meant to answer this last night, sorry for the brief reply. I'm posting via iPhone.

    I think this is the weakest and most confusing rule in Mortal Coil. In fact, I hate this rule and think it sucks. The purpose of the desperate reaction is to keep you from being boned if you fail to anticipate an enemy's attack. There should be a penalty, fatigue works. I'd charge a fatigue and let the player reallocate.

    \n Ok. So do you think all of these should be possible:

    - The character has gone all in and now wants to re-allocate. He does so, and ends up with 2 fatigue (one for all in, one for re-allocation).
    - The character has not gone all in and now wants to re-allocate. He does so, but keeps his remaining action token unallocated. He ends up with 1 fatigue (for re-allocation, but not for all-in).
    - The character has not gone all in and now wants to re-allocate. He does so, and also allocates his remaining action token. He ends up with 2 fatigue (one for all in, one for re-allocation).

    In all cases, all the action tokens on the new action count (unlike the rules as written in some cases).

    Does the fatigue from re-allocation happen before or after the actions have been resolved? I would vote for before (i.e. one token is moved to the Spent pile immediately), in order to make the cost a bit higher, and I think this is more in line with the philosophy behind the rules in the book. On the other hand, I assume the fatigue from going all in happens *after* the actions have been resolved.

    You didn't comment on what happens if all the action "slots" have been taken (or no appropriate slot is available). I assume one can still use the faculty only for defense, but no aptitude/action tokens?

    -----
    We had our first session last night, and I'm happy to say that no-one attempted to do any of the above

    I did have some trouble with the conflict system, although not the trouble I expected. The trouble I expected was that I thought it would be hard to match actions up against each other and figure out what opposes what. But the actual problem was that it was very difficult to come up with non-obvious actions to script ("side bets"). And without those, the conflicts get kind of predictable and boring. I think part of it is that I need more flesh on my NPCs in order to give them appropriate "side goals", but I also think it requires a lot of fast thinking on your feet, and it's hard to be super creative for a whole session...

    We did have some interesting magical facts created, and some very good free-form role-playing where I just sat back and enjoyed the players playing their characters bantering and arguing with each other in a pretty believable way

    I think I need to write a proper AP at some point...

    \n I'd go with options 1 and 2, for my taste. I also vote for allocation fatigue to be immediate, before reallocation results are calculated.

    If all action slots have been taken, the person would need to change one of his actions for one of the slots, abandoning whatever previous action he had there. I rarely have all four slots fill up in a game, though, so that shouldn't come up too often.

    It's true that the conflict system gets a little bland if people aren't creative with their actions. It takes some time and familiarity with the system to get good side bets going, but one of the tricks I use as a GM is to perform a mental action when the characters are expecting a physical one, and vice versa. That always gets people's attention. There is some thinking on your feet required, which can be difficult and tiring. I'll cop to that, for sure.

    I'd love to see an AP! Thanks for giving it a try.

    \n One thing I'm not sure of is whether a "mystery" (Alice's disappearance) is appropriate for a game like this. Is the game meant to be played with "all cards on the table"? For example, should I as a GM share all the NPC writeups? In some ways it could be considered unfair in conflicts for me to know the PCs abilities but not vice versa. Is it ok to have planned plot elements? For example, should I decide what really happened to Alice ahead of time (and not share it with the players)? I found this game difficult to prep for because the players can change a lot with their magic and power tokens, making any pre-planned plot devices inconsistent... One option is to have the game be a mystery for the characters but not for the players, but I'm not sure this group of players would appreciate that... Just rambling here...
