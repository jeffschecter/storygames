A list of some of the best advice for anyone creating a game in any form. This was originally written by your friends over at Board Game Designers Forum (http://www.bgdf.com, all original credit will go to them) and we think people would do very well to follow these few "Best Practice" rules closely. Please add any additions to the thread about your own personal experiences and best practice principles so that we can build an industry wide important resource together for both budding and professional Game Designers.
\n
\nHere is the list of Best Practices (click the link above for the descriptions):
\n
\n- Do not add a rule to take care of an unusual situation.
\n- Your scoring design is your game design.
\n- Avoid false strategy.
\n- Balance with incentives and costs, not with restrictions.
\n- Pay attention to the rules people forget.
\n- Simplify.
\n- Removal is OK.
\n- Theme and art are very important.
\n- Understand how mathematics and probabilities affect game play.
\n- Try to make your mechanics reflect the theme.
\n- Never assume players will make smart decisions.
\n- Failures are successes too.
\n- Half of building should be breaking.
\n- Include a "fair" setup.
\n- Graphics are meaningful.
\n- Know for whom you're designing the game.

    \n There are some ideas here that I actively disagree with.\n
    Your game will only be as fun as your dullest subsystem
    \nI understand what you mean by this, but it is hyperbole. A whole load of boardgames have some pretty dull subsystems: "move token on the score track", etc. They're typically fine and unobtrusive.

    What you want to avoid are tedious subsystems. Not every mechanic needs to be fun or entertaining in itself -- but if they're not, they should stay out of the way.

    Even more so, most really great boardgames aren't about entertaining mechanics at all. They're about solving problems in an interesting space. Don't confuse mechanics used to build that space with the space itself. The space is what is interesting, the mechanics are there to give you access to that space.

    If those mechanics are tedious, don't work properly, or -- even worse -- are not needed to access the problem space in the first place, then yeah, the game is going to suck.

    Games with entertaining mechanics are fun for a short time. Games with entertaining problem spaces are fun for a long time.\n
    Give players something to do when its not their turn
    \nBoth sort of games have their place.

    One thing I adore are boardgames where you can just relax when it isn't your turn: you can chat with the rest of the players, etc. This works best when the rounds don't take too long, and when the situation doesn't change too drastically during a typical round -- so you don't need to take long to re-orient yourself.

    Sure, games where you're "on" all the time are great too -- but not nearly as social.

    \nIn my experience, this is terrible, and it tends to drag the game out. Maybe in something simple like Ticket to Ride where most turns you just pick up a card or two, and it's easy to watch other players do that while thinking of something else, but in anything with reasonable complexity you need to either be watching other players on their turns, or thinking of what you're going to be doing next.

    Maybe I just have bad memories of really long Twilight Imperium games where people talk on the side, you come to their turn, and then they have to figure out what to do. It's not a reorientation problem, it's that the game gave them something to do during other players' turns (i.e. plan theirs), and they didn't do it.

    So "give players something to do when its not their turn" doesn't have to be something particularly active. It can be planning, it can be giving suggestions (like in cooperative or semi-cooperative games), the ability to interrupt, or just making other players' turns interesting enough to watch.

    I understand what you mean by this, but it is hyperbole. A whole load of boardgames have some pretty dull subsystems: "move token on the score track", etc. They're typically fine and unobtrusive.

    \n
    \n

    I think it's important to make a distinction between "dull/tedious" and "automatic."

    \n

    Sure, counting spaces on a track isn't exactly loads of fun, but it's not particularly tedious, because it's easy. It doesn't take the focus of attention away from the parts of the game that are interesting. Similarly, rolling dice, drawing cards, and other simple gestures are so familiar and efficient that they have become automatic.

    \n

    By contrast, some games have non-interesting mechanics that are also not automatic, and this makes them a lot less fun - for instance, the otherwise excellent game Power Grid has an intricate, context-sensitive resource replenishment mechanic that doesn't involve any decision-making, takes a lot of time, happens every turn cycle, and requires you to look at a table basically every time. It's like someone flushed the toilet while you're taking a shower - a rude shock that interrupts the otherwise excellent flow of that game.

    If there's nothing to do, but I need to keep an eye on the game, and it isn't chess or another game where I can use all the thinking time I get... it gets boring for me pretty quick.

    If there's nothing to do, and I don't need to keep an eye on the game -- great. I'll chat with the guy who I haven't see in months. I'll put a kettle on.

    The funny thing is that this doesn't really apply the first or maybe not even the second time I play. Then I'm engrossed in figuring out the game. It's when the basics have been learned that these things start to matter. I don't mind thinking about tactics, but ... there's a limit.
