\n I've been playing and running games since I was old enough to know that dice had different numbers of sides, and that the Shirt Tails were the best thing on television. I've been to many conventions and played in many convention games, and had a blast. However, I've never before run a game at a convention other than a large-scale LARP, and that was a completely different affair of event micro-management.

This weekend, at GameStorm, I'm running my first ever con game. I'm running three, in fact, and they are sessions of a game that I wrote myself. Needless to say, I'm nervous as all get-out. I've got all the paperwork printed, and even wrote a personal self-only "mission statement" to help psych myself up before each session.

What advice can you offer for a first-time Convention GM who is otherwise accomplished in the home gaming arena?

    \n Be prepared, whatever that means (for me it means a full water bottle and a good night's sleep). Show up a little early. Make sure everybody knows everybody. Shake hands. Be clean and friendly. Project excitement. Assume people will need a pencil, paper and dice Tell people what you want and ask what they want. Make sure everybody is on the same page concerning what's appropriate and what isn't, both in game and out. Start on time. Enjoy yourself. Schedule in a break. End on time. Thank everyone for playing and ask for feedback.

    \n Go into the bathroom before the session, brush your teeth briefly and look in the mirror:

    * Blow a raspberry (that is, stick your tongue out between your lips and blow so it makes a fart sound). Make it big. By the end of it are your lips flapping? Do your cheeks feel loose? If not, repeat until they are.

    * Say "nnnnnnn" on the left side of your mouth. Say it on the right side of your mouth. Do this three times on each side.

    * Say "Mumula" and on the "la" open your eyes and mouth as wide as you possibly can. Do this 20 times.

    * Say "Red Letter, Yellow Letter", starting very very slow, over-articulating, then increase speed to your normal talking speed. Do the same for "teaching ghosts to sing", "unique New York" and the names of the characters in your scenario.

    * Raise your hands above your head as far as you can, standing on your tiptoes, hold it for four seconds. Return to normal posture. Do this five times.

    * Lean on the wall, reach back lift and catch your left ankle, pull gently. Do the same with your right leg. Do this twice for each leg.

    * Stand normally, then reach downward with your right hand, bending only at your waist. Return to standing normally. Do this three times with each hand.

    * Stand and breathe as deeply as you possibly can, slow and relaxed, in through the nose and out through the mouth. About a minute of this.

    This is not some kind of exercise, this is warming up the muscles, if anything starts to hurt, don't do it so much or so hard. At the end you should feel loose and energized. And yes, bring water.

    Edit: And before anyone asks, YES I ACTUALLY DO THIS. I do it before normal gaming sessions too. Thank you high school drama club!

    \n At many conventions, the players will expect you to run the game, in a traditional way, and to entertain them.

    If your game doesn't work like that, be confident and happy, like you expect them to have a good time. Where your game requires player input, in an unusual, non-D&D way, tell them directly: hey, this game works really well if you guys describe the scenery (or whatever).

    That doesn't mean be over-confident, but come to the game with energy, like it'll be fun. Be prepared for a little bit of showmanship, and that players will look to you to find out what to do next.

    Graham

    \n Having done a fair few demo games over the years, I'd advise you to figure out the best way to teach rules WHILE playing, rather than with a rule-dump at the beginning. In a Wicked Age is great for progressive learning because one can talk about Oracles while actually choosing them, then get into character design by making an NPC (using PC rules) from them, than talk about the conflict mechanics when someone first tries to do something to another person, then talk about the Owe List when it comes up mechanically. (Then explain running multiple chapters at the end, though con games almost never do more than one chapter.)

    So break down your rules in that manner. Character creation, in particular, can be a time-suck; have some set of questions to help drive folks to the "class" or whatever they'll most enjoy. Don't worry about giving them every, exhausting option: make them tell you about what they'd like to play, after your Setting Pitch, and then basically build their PC for them. Obviously, have some pre-gens of "common" archetypes for gamers (NOT for just your game; see Bartle) and offer them if someone mentions one as their "favorite" type of character. Just be like, "OK, here's something similar--buy a few more Power with the remaining points and give a name and background" or whatever. Times three, for three sessions--the coolest thing about demoing an RPG is that you can send them home with their character sheet... with your "Buy The Game Now" URL on it!

    On that note: it's YOUR game; have a copy of the rules for EVERYONE, including yourself. (I blow this often, with both my Icehouse games and, lately, with ASCII @HACK.) It's up to you whether you let them keep them or not--I wouldn't mid, for an ashcan, but if you're in print, it's a loss of potential revenue... or maybe it will sell five more books, because they take them home, run it for friends, and some of those firends want their own copy.

    (Good thread topic, there, actually--Giveaway Copies: Lost Profits or Loss-Leaders?)

    And, yeah, do all that other stuff above.

    \n Oh, yes, what David said:\n
    ...figure out the best way to teach rules WHILE playing, rather than with a rule-dump at the beginning
    \nIn particular, I'd suggest that you don't explain the conflict rules until the first conflict (and then explain them through the conflict). And, at the start of the game, explain the background of the game in minimal detail. And don't feel you have take people through everything on the character sheet.

    I have bad memories of GMs who'd explain the setting, for 10 minutes, then how conflicts worked, for 5 minutes, then go through the character sheet, for 10 minutes and I'm not kidding about that, then start the game.

    In short, keep your opening speech as short as possible, preferably below three minutes.

    \n Wow, this thread is full of great advice! I've GMed a lot of con games over a lot of years and the bases are very well covered here. The only thing I have to add is that a four hour con slot is far shorter than you can possibly imagine right now. Focus is critical at a convention game and the players may even hold you accountable if you let them get off track. There is no time for out of game talking, funny stories, movie quotes, or anything else that is not the game.

    Now if your game runs in under four hours normally, you might not have a problem. But most new GMs have a very hard time finishing what they have prepared within the slot. So don't have too much game prepared. Have a watch and check it often and start wrapping up at least a half-hour before the end of the slot. Jason listed "finish on time" above, but I'm foot-stomping it because I think one of the worst sins is running long. Your players may want to eat between games or maybe they'll have their own game to run in the next slot. It's a matter of courtesy to finish on time so you don't force them to "quit" the game before the end. At the very least be aware of the end of the slot and let players go at that point.

    Posted By: scottdunphyHave a watch and check it often and start wrapping up at least a half-hour before the end of the slot
    \nQuote for Truth. I make sure my phone is out and I can see what time it is at any point. But that said, don't be afraid to take breaks. I like to take five at least once an hour, to get up, stretch, let me mind process what's gone on, etc. I don't always do this every hour, but I try to be mindful of it.

    And relax. I like to joke that "players can smell fear," but it's true -- others have said to walk in confident, and that's a big deal.

    It might not apply to you much since it's your game, but avoid rule look-ups like the plague. If you have to make some sort of judgment call on the fly, err on the side of "what would be fun?" rather than "what would be right?" A con game isn't the same sort of beast as even a one-shot home game, since you're having to spend the first little bit of time quickly building a rapport with the other people around the game.

    One thing I do is always take a blank piece of paper and draw boxes on it, corresponding to where the players are sitting. I write their name on top of the box and their character's name & whatever pertinent information I feel is needed for that session in the box. I usually don't have to refer to it afterward, but I do that in order to get my mind to remember this information (as I'm a bit of a kinesthetic learner -- your mileage may vary).

    And design the scenario in your mind has having multiple pieces you can bring in or ditch without breaking the game -- you might find you'll run through your game in 90 minutes because there wasn't enough, or it would have taken an extra 90 minutes because it was too much. Hard to predict when you don't know who you'll have at the table.

    \n Tough question, really. I'm from Denmark and our way of running games sound a bit differently from what I'm used to. You mention, for instance, these "dice", and I can't recall when I last used those at a con.
    \nAnywho, I've been running games at cons for 17 years, and for the past ten or so, that's all I do at cons. usually five or six games.
    \nWhile I agree with (some of) the previous points, here's what I do.
    \nFirst off, talk to the players. Ask what they usually play, what type of characters they usually play. If they respond with "warrior" og "Mage", I sigh, gently, and prepare to be disappointed. If they respond with personalities, that's a different matter.
    \nI warm up the players. I have a series of drama exercises that force the players out of their shell. While I know some prefer simple childrens games, I prefer to run a fast-paced quarrel. What I do is take two players and put them in front of each other, standing. One plays the man, the other the woman. I then add problems to their conversation (she's extatically telling you about the new handbag she just bought, and you just got fired) and then suddenly pulling one player out and pulling in another to take over the part, or suddenly switching roles between the two active players.
    \n
    \nIt works, it gets their heartrate up and and it forces them to think fast. It also allows me to get a measure of their playing skill, and I use it to cats them for their characters. It takes 15-30 minutes, but gets them into character MUCH faster. In my experience it nets me an extra hour of quality playtime.
    \nAfter this bit of sport, I give them the character, give them 60 seconds to skim the sheet (just the description, not the rules if there are any, and if there are, I usually ignore them utterly anyway) and then start with the intro scene.
    \nAfter the intro scene, they get time to realy read their characters, because by then they've already tried playing them and have a good idea about the underlying person of the character.
