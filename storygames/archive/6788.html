    \n We do this in Grey Ranks, inspired by Burning Wheel. Patrick created a font consisting of the two symbols we use, the Kotwica (anchor symbol of the Home Army, for history) and lightning bolt (for play advice).

    As far as pitfalls, I think you run the risk of creating visual clutter if they are not pertinent and used sparingly and appropriately. Also, the differentiation needs to be prominent - in our case, for example, I'm not sure that a reader will skim the book looking for all the Kotwica sidebars, for example, or all the lightning sidebars. I think Burning Wheel is the same way - they use three different symbols, but it is a little subtle and they are all equally interesting. "Dummies" books use large icons that are obvious, so you could go through and just read all the bomb icon entries if you wanted to. It is not subtle. But it is visually cluttered. So you can't win.

    \n I, too, have plans for icon callouts (Toggles, Options, Examples, and Warnings). Having done them SEVERAL times in professional technical publications, I can offer a bit of loose advice:

    * I prefer icons set off in a margin, not inline with text. In other words, the text doesn't flow around them (like the head icons in Burning Empires) but rather the right edge of the icon bounding box is aligned the the left edge of the text block margin (on left pages; reverse for right pages). That serves to both increase the scanability (find the passage with the icon) and the readability of the text block (no "visual clutter" for your eyes to swoop around).

    * Don't use an icon when a paragraph style will do. For instance, in GLASS, my Toggles and Options will be things one could actually fill out (dot the former, check or uncheck the latter), in the online shotGLASS PDF, before printing. However, my Design Notes will probably be in sidebar boxes, perhaps with a different background effect (fill). They could be read or ignored or reviewed after reading the rules, to help grok how the design philosophy all hangs together. So, for your "read this" text, you might do fine with an inline bounding box (a la old D&D modules) or a font change (face or style or even size).

    * If you must use inline icons, be SURE to calculate their size based on the line height and spacing, so that your text wraps cleanly around it. If you want them within sentences, they can be no taller than the baseline height of the text (ignore descenders) so that they don't force additional padding on the line (looks narf) or crowd (or overlap!) the line above.

    * Icons, bounding boxes, and paragraph styles all establish a sort of prioritization of information, based on how they draw the eye if one flips to a random page. An inline icon is a MUCH lower priority than one hanging out, mostly alone, in the margins. A bounding box may or may not trump an icon, depending on a number of factors. A mere paragraph style tends to be even lower, unless it includes a font size change or a radically contrasting color, which can bump it clear to the top of the eye-attraction list (viz major headings). Balancing those influences will go a long way toward removing the "cluttered," For-Dummies look... which I suspect is a factor more of them NOT establishing a clear prioritization, so the eye thinks EVERYTHING on the page is Top Priority, than it is a factor of using many icons, callouts, sidebars, and figures/graphics. For instance, look at a top-notch junior high school text book sometime: you'll see that it's not cluttered--though full color and chock-a-block with iconography and sidebars--but organic, drawing the eye from the teaching points to vivid illuminations of those points, to the higher detail text, to the follow-up activities or questions to ponder.

    * Icons are iconic (another thing I dislike about BE's talking heads). I should be able to glance at it for, oh, a quarter of a second and distinguish it from all other icons in use. This uniqueness across the set has a nice knock-on benefit: when fast-scanning for a particular page, humans' memory of the "look" of a page helps more than memory for the "location" of that page in the work. This is the Main Benefit of using a variety of sidebars, art sizes, art placement... and, yes, margin icons. You want to look up damage and you just REMEMBER that it was on a page with a sidebar just about here, an icon opposite it, and this quarter-page illustration in the corner.

    * Spot color is your friend (in small or web press, NOT POD). If you know you're going to need, say, the corporation's Deep Blue color, and thus you know you're going to at least be two-color (spot + black), then USE that color in the icons. You can shift the transparency a bit (i.e. introduce dot patterns to make for "dimmer" tones) and you can use undercolor to darken a bit (black under the spot color). USE that control, otherwise you're wasting the spot color screen on the boring stuff like headers and footers or heading colors or book art (rules and such).
    ---
    So if you want "readability," kill the inline icons and use margin icons, for scanability ("ease of use"). Make them big and bold. But don't make them the sole proprietors of content designation: use paragraph styles and bounding boxes and all the other typographic tricks to distinguish your content, relying on icons for the serious callout stuff (frex, in tech manuals, they are invariably used to caution or warn against loss of data or danger).

    In your rules, the best use of icons might be to callout Main Rules and important GM actions only, then use bounding boxes for "read this stuff" and normal text for explanations, maybe in italics for examples. You'll have to fiddle a bit for the right emphasis and prioritization, but that's FUN (really, it is--it's the typesetting of 100,000 words that becomes a grind).

    Hope this helps;
    David

    \n About choosing the right image for an icon, here's a good bit of advice taken from the Gnome Human Interface Guidelines: differentiate heavily the icons based on their shape.

    So, if you have 4 icons be sure to have them in 4 different shapes (I'm talking about the outline).

    4 icons inside a circle -> 4 circles -> Bad idea
    4 icons, one triangular, one square, one circular and one with an arrow shape -> 4 differend shapes -> Good idea

    People tend to recognize icons much more easily if they have different shapes.

    See:
    http://library.gnome.org/devel/hig-book/stable/icons.html.en
    and also
    http://tango.freedesktop.org/Tango_Icon_Theme_Guidelines

    (at the bottom of the page there is an example of a "round silhouetted arrow" )

    \n I'll follow Jason--rough impressions and alternative suggestions.
    * Read Aloud could be just a speech bubble (see next).
    * Explain could be just the section mark.
    * I'm not a fan of the cutoff hand, in Hand Out--perhaps just a hand with no wrist, solid black (whatever) I.
