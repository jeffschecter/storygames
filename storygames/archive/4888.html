\n In the recent past, we've seen a lot of games move away from "total authority over your character" and towards "distributed authority over everything" shared among the players.

I've been designing and playing some story games which do not feature any association between players and characters--they're pure collaborative storytelling, no "roleplaying" at all. As it turns out, it's quite a design challenge--it's much easier to tie in reward and/or penalty systems when each player has an entity (their character) that their influence in the game is permanently attached to.

So, the question is:

How does your Fun increase, decrease, or change based on how much character-identification is present in the game, whether for you or for other players?

For instance, maybe you really enjoy games more when you can feel like "you're really there", and sense the thrill of danger to your character vicariously. Or maybe you can't enjoy an RPG if your character could potentially die before the game is over. On the other hand, maybe you have more fun as a GM because the other players have an alter ego, and you can try to get to them by throwing situation at the character?

As a secondary question, has moving along this continuum of "full character identification" to "no character identification" brough you any sort of Cosmic Wisdom about game design?

Finally, I read that Vincent Baker has written somewhere why he strongly prefers games where players portray characters over games which just "tell stories". I'd love to read this (or even a paraphrase), but can't find it. Can anyone help me out?

Thanks,


Paul

    But here's kind of a thing about actual play - the stories we tell when we inhabit our characters seem to be really heartwrenchingly personal, and to an extent, autobiographical; the stories we tell (and by "we" in this post I mean "the folks I play with") when we inhabit them less are more historical and environmental, and their emotional force is lessened.     \n "Or maybe you can't enjoy an RPG if your character could potentially die before the game is over."

    I'll post more about the topic after I've had more time to think about it, but for this specific point I care less about character death in shared-authority, because in pure character-perspective-only play if the character dies, I'm basically out of the game. In high shared authority if "my" character dies, then I'll just play another character, or focus on setting, etc.

    Furthermore, if the only thing I can influence is the character, I'm more likely to invest in the game (i.e. invest in the character) if my character's expected longevity is longer.

    Therefore, unless there's a way to both distribute the authority and the burden away from me and to fulfill the player's need for more character immersion, that spin-off is basically dead in the water. For some, definitely, having the broader view is a deal-breaker.

    \nAnother day, another thought. Personally, I found games that lie in the middle of the continuum awkward. When playing DitV, PtA, and Polaris, there were various strong temptations by myself and other members of my group to abandon any character focus whatsoever. Two people complained in DitV that they had to ground narration for sees and raises in terms of their character. Several of us wondered why there was a producer-player distinction in PtA; why not have everyone be a producer, and have a pool of characters anyone could draw on? We've had repeated problems in Polaris of people ignoring restrictions on "free play" to respect cosmos ownership of the characters. (Moons spontaneously narrating demons, etc.) One player has made multiple requests that we house-rule away those restrictions.

    I personally think this is because I'm used to two ways of playing - as a player and as a GM. As a player it's almost 100% character focus, almost anything (with certain specific exceptions) done "out of character" is extremely frowned upon. As a GM though I have the opposite ethic - all NPCs are pawns to be used or abused in service of entertaining the players and oneself. I have 20 years of experience with these two perspectives. Anything in between strikes me as very unnatural.

    \n Eldir's experience closely matches what I saw in my group -- at least in terms of having players who prefer playing their character to narration, and the excruciating silences/awkward pauses, and probably the players who weren't really having as much fun with PTA as they did with our more traditional games. Losing it was no big loss, considering how many other games we were wanting to take out for a spin, but reconciling myself to the evidence that the majority of our group just plain doesn't like that style of play (not to mention the realization that I don't much care for it, either!) took a while. I spent a few bad months trying to figure out how PTA could be hacked into something more palatable and thinking that there was somehow something wrong with us because we didn't see the fun in it...but now I'm okay with PTA simply not being a good fit for us, and I can look at favorable actual play reports of it with a mixture of amazement and mild curiosity.

    Posted By: Accounting for TasteI spent a few bad months trying to figure out how PTA could be hacked into something more palatable and thinking that there was somehow something wrong with us because we didn't see the fun in it...but now I'm okay with PTA simply not being a good fit for us, and I can look at favorable actual play reports of it with a mixture of amazement and mild curiosity.
    \nYou know, one of the things I find fascinating about PTA in that regard is that really, most gaming groups confer the same authority to the players that PTA confers - it just happens implicitly instead of explicitly, and so doesn't ping on anyone's meta-radar.

    Like, when the GM looks at a player and says, "Hey, what do you want to do next?" and the player says, "Well, I'm going to go question the shopkeeper, 'cause I think he knows something," that's exactly the same thing as a scene frame in PTA, with the same authority and established details. It's not like the GM's going to go, "Nah, I don't want that to happen next. Pick something else." I mean, they might, but if they do, I think that GM needs to be solidly struck with a mackerel across the face a few times.

    Same thing with narrating conflict stuff - I think almost every GM's turned that over to the player at some point and gone, "Okay, so you kill him. How do you do it?" And in a group with good communication, the players might offer up side details like, "Oh, sweet, the king has a magic sword... it should totally be encrusted with gems and just be crazy-looking, like it's a status symbol as well." Not all the time, but sometimes, and usually a GM will take that kind of stuff and say, "Okay, sure, we'll say that's true."

    And I'm not really sure what it is about PTA that makes people tend not to drift the rules when they run into those awkwardnesses, like people do with nearly any game, nearly all the time. I mean, I've played a ton of Vampire where the players had lots of say over story and narration on the meta-level, and I've played some PTA that was basically the most rules-light traditional game ever. It strikes me that often, the character ownership / actor stance thing is more about ignoring other kinds of authority than actually lacking them.

    Charles: What is it about play in which each player has a primary character that you like? You've said that you prefer this style of play (although you'd like to mix it up a bit more) over play in which player and character are less well connected. Why?

    I've been thinking about this.

    I think it's the reverse: I like play in which all the significant characters have their own player responsible for them. That includes the significant NPCs and the GM in Dogs, for instance.

    I like immersion a lot. I also like taking on responsibility for a character or characters and fulfilling it, to everyone else's enjoyment. I like when my fellow players do the same - a big part of the pleasure of participating in Soraya's story, for instance, was participating in Emily's portrayal of Soraya.

    If each character has one player primarily responsible for her, that creates an intimacy between the character and the player that I enjoy a lot, whether I'm that player or that player's audience.
