    First point: mechanics for "giving" and the like are not universal and not necessarily necessary (you like that? that's why they call me the wordsmith!); they are part of the larger thematic question "how much are you willing to give to get what you want?" which is one of many approaches to game design. So, for instance, FLFS has nothing similar because that question is pretty much totally irrelevant to play of that game.

    Secondly: Agora conflicts produce both fallout and burnout. Fallout is going to whip around at the end of the scene and make you re-evaluate and re-write your character's central beliefs. Burnout is already lost resources -- you're never getting those dice back -- but at the end of the scene it will be rolled for a continuing advantage of another sort. So a player looks at both to gauge how much they want their character to change as a result of this, and how many resources they want to lose. However, they may also want to push the character to change, and they may want to grab for that alternate advantage. So while there may be some mechanical pressure to give, the same mechanic can also be exploited, or used as a sort of counter or gauge for the level of import of the conflict.

    Point being, the question of "when to give" is something that's really, really complex and very, very contingent on a host of other bits of data throughout the game. I would go so far as to say, in fact, that a straightforward mechanic guiding the player when to give is going to produce some pretty boring play. "When to give" is in many ways a central thematic decision in the games where it appears, and if it does not take as inputs the vast landscape of the rest of the game, it's short-circuiting that decision.

    JBR has some good general observations, allow me to kick in a little bit on an even more general, strategic level.

    Giving in/retreating is a strategy that is emergent from other rules. There is nothing in the rules of chess that "signals" you when a strategy is getting you into trouble and you need to pull back onto defense. But an experienced player evaluates that kind of decision with every move. As Tal said, "pawns are like buttons, once you lose them, your pants will fall down all by themselves". Similarly in the aforementioned 'hit point' games, there's a gamble involved in every move.

    The heightened impact of losing the last piece of a resource is simply psychological, it is not necessarily part of the game. Sometimes it can be misleading. If you're playing poker, the first $20 you bet is worth just as much as the last $20 in your wallet - still, there's strategy involved in buying pots and trying to whittle down opponents by the psychological difference between the two.

    The question of retreat/giving in is emergent from the rules of many games. Absolutely there are different strategies for retreat in combat in (for example) D&D and WoD games due to the lack of penalties for injury and the presence of attacks of opportunity in D&D. To an experienced player of these games, there might not be a 'flag', but there certainly is strategic evaluation.

    The same is true for Amber social gambits. But that's another story.

    Hey Mark,

    I could be way off base here, since I'm not in your game. But here's a stab at it.

    Maybe the trouble is that Harm has been stripped of impact in the fiction. I mean, sure, "1 point of Harm" in the abstract, isn't a big deal. No one cares. But! That 1 point of Harm was done to your character because of a specific, colorful, event in the fiction. Something happened to the character to really hurt them. So, one question to ask about when to give is, "Can I accept this hurtful thing happening to my character? Is winning worth getting hurt that way?"

    When there's color, things matter. 1 Harm taken because your lover just said you were a soulless thing that desevered to suffer and die -- well, that's painful. Getting over that ("healing the harm" system-wise) will also be given meaning via the color of your fiction. What do you do to feel better about being hated by the person you love? Did you spend Instinct to recover the Harm? If so, what happened? Did you turn to the bottle? Did you pour your heart out to a stranger? You had to do something to "get over it." And that something is another element that can color a decision to give in a conflict. "Can I accept the things I'll have to do to recover from this kind of hurt?"

    Whaddya think? Am I way off base? I've seen this happen in RPGs of all kinds. When color and impact in the fiction fall away, and people start marking down numbers and saying "I spend an Instinct" ... things can lose their bite.

    Well, cool. I'm glad I was on track.

    About sub-stakes:
    I think we're drifting a bit. Harm isn't a "sub stake" at all. Harm is just "getting hurt" -- whatever that means right now.

    Losing the memory of your sister could be Harm (in some kind of psychic duel of memory-erasure), but more probably something like that would be "the thing at stake" in its own conflict resolution. Harm doesn't have to be grand or severe in order to be colorful.

    Take your cue from the actions initiated by the characters. If someone says, "I stab him in the face!" during BDTP, and they hit and do some Harm... then yeah, they totally stabbed that guy in the face. How bad is it? How much Harm did it do? If he's Broken, then, damn... that there is an ugly face-stabbing. Maybe an eye is gone. If he's Bruised, then he has a nasty cut on his cheek and he twitches and snarls and curses from the pain. But he doesn't suddenly lose his best friend or something. The Harm flows from the actions taken, and the severity of the result.

    Try to keep things focused on what the characters are doing right now, and the consequences of that stuff, without reaching out to bigger-scale, "so your mom sleeps with the demon!" kinds of craziness.

    Something else that worked for us was to re-negotiate intentions in the middle of BDTP, to entice a Give. It's not strictly by the rules, but we liked it.

    "Man, I am so screwed here. I don't want to lose an arm!"
    "Okay, how about... you just get mangled? You're all messed up and require major healing, but nothing gets lopped off."
    "Okay, yeah. I can give to that."
    "Done."

    But beware! This escape-hatch is not the ultimate solution or anything. Sometimes it's the wrong thing to do. Generally speaking, the TSOY GM should set up stakes that are dangerous and thrilling, and see if the PCs have the nerve to take their chances. After all, once you hear what's at stake, the PC has the option of not pursuing that line of action at all. This alone can be a powerful statement of what your character cares about, and doesn't.
