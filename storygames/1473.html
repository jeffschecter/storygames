    No, not yet, but only because it's still a little bit of a stumbling step, and it currently has less functionality than the last time the e-book hit the marketplace and raised all the same questions (which would be back around 2000).

    Which is not to say I don't have the love. My rocket e-book (Same kind of device, except thicker, and with less battery life due to an LCD screen) has served me well in numerous games, and I think the potential is limitless. However, at the current price point and functionality, it's a brick. The Irex Iliad, a competing device, actually has the functionality, but it costs more than twice as much.

    The big problem is that e-books are perpetually one of those ideas that are conceptually awesome but that no one has come out with a good business model for. Worse, a lot of the folks who would have been early adopters (both as purchasers and content providers) got burned pretty bad when the whole thing went down the toilet a few years back. Now, I'm optimistic that this will eventually rock - e-paper is a technology I've been panting after since I first read about it, god, like 10 years ago and it's awesome to see it become a reality. What's more, I think that devices like this have a _lot_ to offer, not just to gaming and novel reading, but in all manner of information management. In pure device size, the market has yet to really put together the device that is bigger than a palm, but simple to use and reasonably priced. The new generation of ultra mobile PCs which have this kind of form factor, are terrible, and other mini-tablet ideas (like the nokia 770) get cannibalized by needs of cell phone users.

    And ultimately, that may be the thing that crushes my optimism. The form factor is very appealing only when compared to carrying around a book. Compared to your cell phone or ipod, it's ridiculous. Now, there are some other promising advances that may address this, like roll-up or fold-up screens which take advantage of some of the physical properties of e-paper, and may allow for a more easily portable device that still has a comfortably readable screen.

    Anyway, if it's not obvious, I'm a huge nerd for these things, and I'm really glad the Sony reader has hit American markets if only because it should really get the ball rolling on a market I _want_ to see grow.

    Posted By: misubaUmm... is there some reason this is going to affect RPGs in particular? If we don't see a lot of laptops at gaming tables (yet), why would we see a lot of these?
    Well,let me speak to my use of the Rocket-book, a similar device.

    With a form factor similar to a book, I can hold it in one hand, turn pages with my thumb, and generally speaking use it as a reference for my game notes _more_ efficiently than I could with paper. What's more, because it was electronic, I could take advantage of the medium to cheat a little. As an example, I was running Exalted, so I ran a random number generator and literally filled the left hand margin with d10 rolls. I had a little stylus so I could cross stuff off or highlight it, and it let me walk and talk as I GM'd, just marking off the numbers rather than physically rolling dice. Could I have done the same thing with a clipboard and some printouts? absolutely - in fact, the number thing was something I used to do on paper. But combined with the ease of flipping through my notes without a mess, it was pretty powerfully awesome. And I was directly engaged with my players - unlike with a laptop, this was in my hand, not between me and them. The same thing might be doable with a Palm or similar device, but I've never successfully done so, mostly due to size limits.

    Now, that was purely my notes. Imagine, if you would, if I'd had the (searchable) pdfs of the rules in the same device? Quick search on a charm or rule, and everything goes more easily than a physical lookup might be, and I didn't need to haul my whole collection to the game, something which, with a game like exalted, is a pretty big deal.

    An offshoot of that is that if I have a device like this, I will be _much_ more willing to buy pdfs because I have a comfortable, easy way to read them without the necessity of printing and binding. Pdf publishing is its own thing, but it's cheaper and generally more profitable for publishers, so the possibility that the barrier for publishing get even _lower_ than it is now is intriguing in the extreme.

    Now, the new Sony can't do this yet. It's a pure reader, so it's mostly useful for carrying more books in less space, but it's still a potential publishing platform, and it's (I hope) just the first step. But the bottom line is, both as a tool for play and an avenue of productions, there are lots of potential application to RPGs, some of which I suspect haven't even been thought of yet.

    -Rob D.

    Okay, I'm going to rave about the technology. Why? Because I've been keeping my eye on this particular technology since its inception, and I am utterly, completely thrilled to see it getting a widespread commercial application.

    Each pixel of this is a microcapsule filled with white fluid, and possessing a tiny, magnetic, black ball. Each of these capsules is surrounded by little magnets. The magnets can be individually controlled, causing the ball to either float to the surface or sink to the bottom. Power consumption is very, very, very low by comparison with light emitting media. I could be wrong, but I think that power consumption when the text is static (i.e. the spheres are just locked in position) is literally zero. The battery drain of the display occurs only when updating the content. The Sony site lists battery life in page-flips. Seventy-five HUNDRED page-flips.

    The Sony unit has the paper behind glass. The paper does not need to be behind glass (although I'm sure that it will increase the lifetime of the page). The material is flexible ... comparable with paper, in fact, as a material.

    The Sony unit charges ~$400 for a display unit that uses a single page. Long term, this display is not anything like that kind of expensive. It doesn't have to be assembled by sub-micron laser etchers. The folks at MIT designed the process specifically so that, with enough investment and work, the pages can be driven down to be comparable to the cost of paper. The economy of scale goes down to pennies a page.

    Likewise, contrast, color and density of pixels are all factors that scale down as the process refines. Color is easy, if you want it ... just put little magnetic red, green and blue spheres into the capsules instead.

    So ... the Sony unit? As a product? Eh. But damn am I glad that they're supporting the technology, and I sure as heck hope it takes off. Because I would really, really like the product this paper is designed for ... a virtual book, with say a hundred pages of two-sided e-ink paper, the electronics and battery in a light-weight spine, and the controls on the inside of the cover. You'd read that one just like a normal book ... select "Writers > Asimov, Isaac > Foundation Trilogy", riffle through the book and you can see all the pages at once.

    Oh how I want this technology.

One change could be that designers could use more "look-up table"-oriented designs. Not a look-up table for every step of play, of course, but maybe a specially-designed set of linked look-up tables that cause situation changes at specific moments of play; sort of like random encounter tables, but arranged in an escalating list, with play moving to the next encounter table in the chain whenever a special condition occurrs.
