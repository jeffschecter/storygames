\n So our first game of Apocalypse World has wound down in #playnow and we're repositioning things for the next big story arc. I was playing a Chopper to middling effect and figured this break was the perfect chance to come up with something that would give me more face time and a bigger role in shaping the ongoing plot.

The roles of Hardholder and Operator are taken, and that's fine because I don't want to do that. What I'd like to do is create a character almost straight out of Dogs in the Vineyard: a hellfire-and-brimstone preacher with a gun and a mission from the Lord to make right those things that aren't so. Into the sinful nest of the hardhold he comes, ready to spread death and the Gospel in equal measure.

But how to bring this character to life?

If you're familiar with Apocalypse World you know that characters are drawn from a set of predetermined archetypes. There's some wiggle room in them to account for player taste, but each is meant to occupy a specific role and have abilities distinct from one another. So which best fits the Preacher?

The first and most obvious choice would be a Hocus. Spreading a faith is part and parcel of their role, after all. But they also come saddled with a congregation of followers and that doesn't fit my wandering-figure-of-retribution concept. There's Battlebabe, but despite the name the Battlebabe actually isn't all that tough, so that's out, too.

I figure some combination of archetype and improvement might do the trick. Because Benhimself, the MC, is generous, he's allowing me to port over the improvement slots I earned with my last character so I have two I can take right away. I also have four circles filled so I'm this close to getting a third.

So you can see what I'm after here: a hardened drifter with a head full of the Good Book and a handful of shootin' iron. A wasteland prophet with little time for the sinful save to fill them full of lead. I don't believe it's impossible to realize this idea using the system, but I'm just not fluent enough with Apocalypse World, despite playing in a game of it for weeks, to do it without help.

    \n Some initial thoughts and people can feel free to correct me if I'm totally off the mark:

    After some chat with Benhimself, we figure that the followers option for a Hocus can be tweaked a bit to mean not a group of people that follow him around, but pockets of the faithful that exist here and there in hardholds around the wasteland. Kind of like how a DitV character encounters those folks in each town who believe. This takes one of my concerns out of the equation.

    Anyway, following on the Hocus path, I figure I can start the character process with the following stat block: Cool-1 Hard+1 Hot=0 Sharp+1 Weird+2. Taking Fortunes, as set aside by the playbook, I can follow up with Frenzy and Charismatic. These allow the Preacher \u2014 who I've taken to calling Brother Zedekiah \u2014 the opportunity to seize upon groups of followers and turn them to his (sometimes destructive) will. And Charismatic allows him to bypass his totally average Hot to use Weird instead.

    Because of Benhimself's generosity, I have two improvements to work with. One will offset the Cool-1 I'm saddled with: Spooky Intense, taken from the Savvyhead's playbook. This turns his "acting under fire" disadvantage to a net advantage by linking it to Weird.

    So would the second improvement be Fucking Wacknut? That boosts his Weird to +3, therefore making his Cool and Hot replacements that much more effective. I like the idea of taking more Hard, though, and boosting the utility of his big gun. I do have that other improvement right around the corner, though. Should I wait, or just go with improving Hard now? I mean, Weird+2 is nothing to sneeze at.

    And did I just solve my own problem? I don't know. I'm still not as confident with Apocalypse World as I could be, and that means I could be totally screwing myself with this build.

    \n Honestly, I think going into AW with that strong of an image for a character is going to doom you to disappointment. AW is a hard, cold, uncaring doesn't-give-a-fuck-about-your-character game. You can visualize him being the badass hellfire and brimstone preacher all you want. But what he actually is, is whatever the rolls let him be. Which may wind up being a total schlub that everyone laughs at and who winds up ignobly dead in your first session.

    AW is a game designed to grind up your characters and spit them out. Rather than go in with an image of how badass cool you're going to be, I recommend just playing the role out of the book...you're effectively a nobody, a mook, one more post-apocalyptic statistic who's name is forgotten before your bones are even picked clean...until the rolls you make in the game say otherwise. That picture above...MAYBE you're that guy after a 10 session campaign. Or maybe you're dead, or maybe you're something else entirely.

    AW is not about playing larger than life characters. Its about playing through a slice in the life of people. Its more "documentary" than "heroic fantasy". The game doesn't care if you're the lead with marquee credits, supporting cast, or uncredited "Dead Guy #3". What happens to you depends on what you do, what rolls you decide to make, and what the consequences of those rolls are.

    Posted By: ValamirThe entire purpose of the game is to "see what happens when..."
    \nNo it isn't. The entire purpose of a game without important choices in character generation is to "see what happens when..."

    In AW, the choices you make during character creation and what advances you choose throughout the game have a significant effect on how extraordinary your character will be in actual play. Some of those choices can increase your likelihood of being a bad-ass on any single roll to higher than 50% and some, like "An Arresting Skinner" guarantee effective action.

    Sam is making not just a starting character, but a character starting with several advances. How is he supposed to choose those advances without a character concept? He's already past the point of "just playing the role." If he junks his concept and just goes with a "see what happens when..." attitude, he's going to be more likely to wind up with a schlub. If he focuses on choosing special moves that support his concept, he's going to be far more happy with his guy in play.

    The difference between making a character that is extraordinary 20-30% of the time and 80-90% of the time IS absolutely a player choice.
