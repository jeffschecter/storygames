\n You stumble upon a gate leading into an ancient underground dwarven kingdom now occupied by the undead minions of Orcus. The foul ones have painted an inscription over the entrance in slowly drying entrails. You gradually decipher the demon scrawl to read:\n
(pour) (salt) [plural] [mark subsequent phrase as object] (you) [possessive] (dear) (person) [plural];
(she) [plural] [possessive] (grave) [plural] (negative) (contain) (previous object) (long time)
\nEach encapsulated word or set of words is a single ideograph, making the inscription a rhyming couplet, each line containing nine ideographs. Those in brackets are purely grammatical, altering the meaning of the ideographs that came immediately before it, with the exception of [mark subsequent phrase as object], which changes the meaning of the ideographs that follow. Also, this isn't like Greek, where you can move all the words around and keep the same meaning. Word order matters.

What does the inscription say?

    \nIdeographs are distinct from both syllables and words. Imagine the inscription looks something like this:



    Now, the second ideograph is read "k'plahgtor," which is demon for "salt." That's a word by itself, a noun. But when combined with "atk" which comes after it, the ideograph that denotes that a non-living thing is plural, it becomes "k'plahgtoratk" which means "salts" or "a large quantity of salt." Note that, despite being two ideographs now, it is still one word, one idea, "a bunch of salt." However, the original ideograph, "k'plahgtor," is also pronounced with two syllables.

    \nHm. There's an odd problem with the way your first line translates. Specifically, in your literal translation, the object is "salt," not "your dear people" (which is the object of the preposition that isn't indicated). The way I am understanding your grammar, "(pour) (salt) [plural]" has to represent a single verb, which is a bit confusing. Otherwise, I'd expect something like "(pour) [mark subsequent phrase as direct object] (salt) [plural] [mark subsequent phrase as indirect object] (you) [possessive] (dear) (person) [plural]". The literal interpretation of "salt" as a single verb is more likely to be "preserve" or something like it.

    Another thing is that the absence of a subject doesn't necessarily mean that the sentence is in the imperative mood. In fact, I was beginning to thing that there was a presumed first person subject. (I was working with "I bleed your loved ones.") It might pay to be thorough and indicate the imperative in some way. Combining these two ideas, you might get a first line similar to:\n
    (you) (preserve) [imperative] [mark subsequent phrase as object] (you) [possessive] (dear) (person) [plural]
    \nIn the second line, "(previous object)" is a bit vague in English. I'm assuming it means "object of the previous clause" (which then obviates the need for a grammatical marker that the object phrase is beginning).

    Also, I find it interesting that "(negative)" is considered a word and not a grammatical marker. Is there a reason for this?

    And as for the nongendered pronoun, you can always make the ideogram translation "(she or he)", but I kind of like the implication that the default gendered pronoun in this language is "she."

    \n Tony, I think you're making too many assumptions about the similarity between demonic and English.

    Deomonic grammar might be, in general, (agent) verb topic (object marker) (object), with the imperative mood formed by dropping the agent, who is assumed to be the audience of the utterance. When the topic is also the agent or there is no agent, a declarative sentence might begin with a reflexive pronoun, to distinguish it from a command. Topic can take the role of either subject or object, depending on context; when there's an explicitly marked object, it's always the subject. The structure of the language appears to be generally head-first, with modifiers coming after the word or phrase they modify.

    The verb for "to pour" might be generally intransitive. So, "(bob) (pour) (water)" would be literally translated as something like "As for water, it pours by means of Bob," and "(reflexive ) (pour) (water)" would be "Water pours." When you throw in an object, the object is the thing onto or into which the substance is poured. So "(bob) (pour) (water) [object marker] (cup)" would be "As for water, it pours into a cup by means of Bob," and "(reflexive) (pour) (water) [object marker] (cup)" would be "Water pours into a cup."

    This grammar also accounts for the second line. "As for your loved onces, they are not contained for a long time by means of their graves." Her you see the topic acting as an object in the absence of any explicitly marked object, forming a kind of passive.

    Posted By: JephTony, I think you're making too many assumptions about the similarity between demonic and English.

    Deomonic grammar might be, in general, (agent) verb topic (object marker) (object), with the imperative mood formed by dropping the agent, who is assumed to be the audience of the utterance. When the topic is also the agent or there is no agent, a declarative sentence might begin with a reflexive pronoun, to distinguish it from a command. Topic can take the role of either subject or object, depending on context; when there's an explicitly marked object, it's always the subject. The structure of the language appears to be generally head-first, with modifiers coming after the word or phrase they modify.

    The verb for "to pour" might be generally intransitive. So, "(bob) (pour) (water)" would be literally translated as something like "As for water, it pours by means of Bob," and "(reflexive ) (pour) (water)" would be "Water pours." When you throw in an object, the object is the thing onto or into which the substance is poured. So "(bob) (pour) (water) [object marker] (cup)" would be "As for water, it pours into a cup by means of Bob," and "(reflexive) (pour) (water) [object marker] (cup)" would be "Water pours into a cup."

    \n For added fun, drop even more basic linguistic concepts. Like, make a language that lacks verbs. Maybe it's a colony of alien ants or something, where the particular context of a situation uniquely dictates what any member of the group would do. Thus "vacant low hill at noon near food" is clearly understood (by any member of the group) as an imperative to climb to the top of that hill and scout for threats to the food source. You never need to describe action at all if everyone does the same thing in response to a given stimulus, you just need to describe the stimulus.

    This could also work in reverse if action uniquely correlates with stimulus. So, now you just have verbs and adverbs.
