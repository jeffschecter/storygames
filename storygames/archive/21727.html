    \n Do these games start the characters off with certain prejudices? Things that might distance them? What good is coming together, if there isn't a clear, if only superficial reason, why they're apart?

    In The Breakfast Club, the characters outwardly are different, and more than just different, they contrast one another, superficially. But it's all just outward appearances, I mean it's a bit more than that, but mostly.

    This outward contrast, is what makes them seem so unique. A contrast highlights the original aspect it's contrasting. It's why heroes are only as good as their villains.

    I was thinking about a set-up where you would describe your character by throwing another character under the bus. You'd describe your character by adding what you view or what your character views as negative traits, to another player's character.

    "(insert name of another character here)isn't(insert adjective)like me".
    or
    "(insert name of another character)doesn't have(insert noun)like me".

    Maybe do all this round-robin style? One at a time, like Fiasco.
    Maybe include examples of opposites for players?

    I guess the characters have to start off divided, so maybe the first thing that needs to happen is to separate them, by the superficial, petty and real things that separate us.
