Disclosure: I recently released Mist-Robed Gate, the first edition, under Creative Commons and started distributing the PDF at no charge. (here: http://summerbird.wordpress.com/2012/02/14/mist-robed-gate-pdf/)

\n

I've noticed that, on the heels of this release, I've been getting a good amount of interest in the game; people are running it again and asking for rules clarifications. If you've read it, you know that it is a problematic text and it could be a lot better. I'm looking at this as an opportunity to clarify the gameplay issues that people are having with the game, by explaining the rules more fully and more clearly. That means we'd give a lot of background on how knifework and wirework are supposed to work and what parts of the game they resolve, as well as providing better play aids.

\n

What we want to do is produce the game as a book accompanied by set of character, prop, scene, and play cards. The character/prop/scene cards would include a pre-made scenario as well as blanks for you to roll your own, and the play cards that you can use with knifework and wirework.

\n

I think that creating these tools will make the game a lot smoother in play, but it'll be a significant amount of graphic design and upfront printing cost, so I'm thinking about Kickstarting it, and that's where you guys come in. What would you like to see happen with this? How can I make a new edition of this game that works better for you?

    \n I think that the game is pretty fine as it is - that is, I personally understood it and appreciate it. The extracurricular stuff on food was amusing, the bibliographical material excellent. The explanation of the gameplay and rules was pretty opaque and condensed, but good enough for me. The true hardcore is sufficiently motivated to see through a less appealing text to the core of the game, but I'm sure that the game has lost less motivated people due to the text.

    If a new edition were in the works, I'd most like to see a rewritten text that appeals to people and inspires them to play; I think that the current text doesn't quite get there. Apart from that, I'd lean towards a nicely made reading book with no props, as the nature of the game is such that teaching the game and inspiring the reader through a nice reading experience into actually playing is the largest hurdle.

    If I was productizing seriously, I'd go entirely scenario-full with the game, probably boxed set; ready-made factions on some widely appealing and colorful topic, two dozen ready-made characters on cards or whatever, a board/map depicting key locations, another depicting the complex and unstable relationship map among the characters. Don't do a book, do a rules pamphlet. Remove the generic scenario-construction advice and rename the game into MRG: Whatever. Advice that while the game can take a lot of replay, it's also possible to get more scenarios off the net or write your own, perhaps with the aid of your book. Write the wider general book on the game separately. Or perhaps don't - what I outline here is already a very wide game, people have gotten a lot of mileage out of e.g. Street Fighter, which has about the same amount of storyline potential all without needing to be accompanied by a separate "how to write a different beat-em-up drama with new characters" book. Put in a good relationship map and 1-2 dozen appealing characters, and people can play with different combinations and emphasis for a long time.

    \n Yes to both points. Cool poster attracts attention. Knife attracts attention.
    T-shirt? ASK ME ABOUT type badge/button? Also marginally effective.

    Player take-homes are a must. Business cards, bookmarks, etc for random passers-by, and info on the character sheet is a definite must. On the bottom or on the back is fine, but somewhere.

    My general rules for Con Demos are:
    -> Make it super trope-y up front, and seek out the personal and less cliche once you're settled in.
    -> There's no time for red herrings. This is the fantasy story that outlives the facts.
    -> (Especially in a game like MRG) Don't finish the scenario. Have key facts missing, with a prompt for the players to fill out. For MRG this is probably just "have factions with opposing goals, have proto-characters, Go. Toss in Set Pieces if the group stagnates. Have plenty of Blank Cards that players can win through WireWork."

    Also, if the Prop cards are pretty, players will often fight over them, and it's a cool reward to take it home with you if you end the game with it. Not always, but enough times to make it worthwhile.

    Just my two cents. If this is ready (significantly) before Dragon*Con (Labor Day Weekend) I can test it out there.
