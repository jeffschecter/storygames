\n What if you had a Monster Manual with no statistics for the monsters that the players could freely read?

Lamentations of the Flame Princess does this (except without a book dedicated to Monsters). Monsters are included but instead of statistics, the GM has a system to generate their own unique stats for these monsters to keep the players guessing... to keep even the GM guessing!

Both my favorite RPG podcasts just did shows on LofP: Atomic Array and Jennisodes.


Now lets expand this!


My favorite monster book is The Book of Unremitting Horror. I have both the OGL D20 Horror and Gumshoe versions. I can't read the entire book in one sitting because it's too upsetting. Here's a review.

The Book of Unremitting Horror doesn't just include simple descriptions of each monster. That's not horrific. Seeing the horror engages the rational mind. This book describes what it feels like when the monster ends you, what the monster leaves behind when it's done with you, what your neighbor saw before running away instead of helping you, and the evidence the police bag up when investigating the last moments of your life. The book is filled with eye witness accounts, journal entries, forensic details, police reports, and even the first person perspectives of the victims. It's all unsettling (to me).


So what if you combine these 2 ideas?


The Book of Unremitting Horror minus Stats.

Instead include a system the GM can build monster Stats based on the artwork and descriptions. A system that engages the GM's emotions. Look at the thing, read what you think it does, how does that make you feel, and base mechanics on those feelings. Mix in rumors and legends; information that may or may not be true. Increase the art budget. Maybe instead of monster drawings it's photos of crime scenes, evidence, found video footage, and sketches based on witness descriptions.

Actually, I don't know if I even want this... I just scared myself writing this... I closed my eyes imagining what this would look like and maybe it goes too far.

    \n I love this idea, and have often considered it myself. I'm mostly excited about two aspects. I don't know if they can be combined in one book effectively or not:

    1. A book about monsters that's exactly the way it would appear in the "game world": all the information is presented "in-character". So, reading up about ogres (or whatever), you'd find maybe an account from some famous explorer, some sketches by ogre attack survivors, some first-hand accounts, rumours, stories, legends, etc.

    2. A book that gives the GM all kinds of ideas about how to use a monster, how to make it interesting and exciting. Randomized stats are a great idea! But we can include fictional variations as well, lots of ideas on how to use the monster, how it attacks, what it eats, what its weaknesses might be, where it might appear in the game world, what kinds of stories it might inspire, and so on. Something along the lines of this thread.

    I can see how these functions *could* be coordinated in one volume (with the second function included as myths, legends, and hearsay), but it might not be possible to do so very elegantly.

    \n We almost removed stats from the DW monsters as written, instead leaving it to the GM to use the stat system to make them as needed. The cool part of a goblin isn't some stats, it's the poor sod pushover dim monster that's pushed out front of the orc horde (or whatever cooler description).

    \n Yeah, if someone can create a process for translating "how this makes you feel" into "how it functions mechanically in play", you win.

    Here's an attempt:

    1) List what you're afraid of. What horrible things could happen?

    2) Divide that list into two lists: consequences of engagement and consequences of avoidance. Neither list can be empty. If "avoidance" is empty, think more on how you feel, and why simply running away doesn't solve everything.

    3) Look at your two lists, and figure out which of these consequences it'd be possible and permissible to inflict on player characters in your game.

    4) Declare that that's that your monster wants to do, and give it the stats and powers to enable it to credibly try.


    Alternatively, instead of creating dilemmas between engage/avoid, you could create dilemmas between methods of engagement:

    1) List what you're afraid of. What horrible things could happen?

    2) Figure out which of these consequences it'd be possible and permissible to inflict on player characters in your game. Pick two.

    3) List methods the player characters could use to defeat the monster. Pick two.

    4) Connect one consequence to each method. (Burning the monster allows/encourages it to implant its eggs in you, while hacking it up allows/encourages it to suck you into its maw.)

    5) Make these connections obvious in play; demonstrate how it works.

    \n I'll plug Robin D. Laws' book, GURPS Fantasy II: The Mad Lands

    A short piece of fiction inspired by the setting in that book:
    http://www.sjgames.com/gurps/Roleplayer/Roleplayer30/MadlandsStory.html

    Steve Jackson Games page for the book:
    http://www.sjgames.com/gurps/books/MadLands/

    There are three monstrous selling points.

    First, Robin D. Laws was a much younger man. His voice as a writer was more... pure. Simple, yet experimental at the same time.

    Second, the deities in this setting are all very powerful and tied to the natural world, but still (with one exception) barely aware of the existence of human beings. When they come across people, hilarity ensues.

    Third, all the monsters are just human beings who have lost one essential human quality. Yeah, it's pretty twisted.
