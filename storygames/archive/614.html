Lies, Lies, It's All Lies!

kleenestar
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Play Advice
Is it just me, or do your players have a really hard time wrapping their heads around the idea that NPCs might lie to them? No matter how counterfactual my NPCs are ("No, really, the sky is green!"), the players always get this shocked and surprised look on their face when they eventually figure out that, yes, they were lied to. They often say things like, "Wow, I never would have expected that," even when it's clear to observers that the character in question has been lying their ass off, and the character not lying introduces serious plot inconsistency. Unless I hugely exaggerate the "I'm lying" signals (inability to meet the players' eyes, for example), they just don't consider the possibility that NPCs might lie, no matter what the other evidence for it.

So, is this true in your games? If your players have a hard time accepting NPCs who lie, do they have an easier time accepting lies coming from PCs? Are there techniques you use to make them treat your NPCs more like real people who might deceive and manipulate them?

I've got a couple of theories about why this might happen, but I thought it would be good to see if anyone else has this issue before I rant too much.

    I used to run Amber.

    I would have NPCs tell the PCs something and both preface it with "Bear in mind, everything I'm about to tell you could totally be me lying," and then end it with, "Unless I'm lying to you in order to serve my own agenda."

    Player conclusion? "Well, obviously, if he were actually lying he wouldn't warn us that way, so it must be utter and complete fact, the bedrock word of God."

    You want my opinion? It is a player tactic, sometimes unconscious, sometimes deliberate. In exactly the same way that people who do not want to back down from a fight will leap into conflict daring the GM to kill their character, players who do not want to face difficult thinking will leap to obvious conclusions, daring the GM to go through the effort of educating them about their own stupidity.

    It's one of those things that's self-correcting if you have even one player who gets that NPCs lie. Because then, as GM, you can just say "Oh ... that guy gets it ... so it's not me, it's just that this other guy's being stupid ... cool, I can screw with him!"

    Yeah, I had this problem. And it hit me that the reason it was the case was what Marco's saying, that the players have no senses the the GM doesn't give them in older style games. They have extremely limited narrative power, so they're in a double-bind typical of GMed design: the GM is their only avenue to truth, and can give false information.

    I think this is partially the question of the Players not being allowed to know things their characters don't know. If the players know that the dude's lying but the characters don't ("Roll to see if you know he's lying") then you have this great situation where players conspire to have their characters find stuff out, or stumble into something unknowingly while the players savor the situation. It's like watching a horror movie: you're yellin' at the screen, "Don't go in the basement, you idiot!" but then she does, and you love it.

    It's significant that Dogs' solution to this is "Tell the players then find out if the characters know." It's the same thing as rolling to see if the PCs see the clue. If they don't, they have insufficient information and the game can't continue. It's a problem with its roots in linear pre-planned scenarios, GM fiat, task res systems, and underdeveloped narrative power of the players.

    When the player wants to know something that the character doesn't, that usually happens in OOC social discussion.     When the character knows things that the player doesn't, we usually just discuss in general terms what the character knows, and I remind the player to tell me that they have this undefined knowledge whenever they're making plans or taking actions where they think it might be relevant. Then, when they're actually trying to do something concrete, they can say, "Hey, remember that my character knows about who has recently arrived in Gaul from Rome? I should totally be able to know if the messenger has showed up yet." This is a way of signaling that the character has made a narrative investment, as in the Greek slave example above, and we'll use it accordingly.

    When neither the player or the character knows what's going on, we do nothing until and unless there's a reason to. A reason can be narrative as well as character-based: we'll occasionally say, "Meanwhile, in another part of the city . . . ." when it makes story sense to do so.

With three GMs, six players, and a lot of player responsibility for inventing parts of the world, this happens a lot.     I guess what I'm trying to say is that the rules are there in a way that's more than 'being made up on the fly': they're just not mechanical. But I think this is really not coming across.

    The best way that I can try to describe it is that it's "How do you find out?" - but not asked from the players to the GM, but from the GM to the players. The players decide the kinds of things they're interested in, then signal that to the GMs by questions, suggestions and actions. The GM in return asks, "How do you find out?" They could fail to find out, but only if they can't figure out a narrative reason why they could find out. The "what" they want to know and the "how" they find out both matter. Does that make more sense?

    Again, I'm sorry if I'm not being very clear, but I think that this is quite different from what many other people here do. I don't have a lot of vocabulary to talk about it with, and talking about this is really hard. Nonetheless, it's worked extremely well for our group for the past six years (even if the system we're using may have something to do with the lying-dififculty issue).

    Honestly, I'm still a little nervous about talking so openly about this, because I don't want to be attacked as, "You're just a bad fiatty GM who doesn't allow player autonomy or authority!" In fact, our players have a huge amount of narrative input and authority, and there really is a (mostly diceless) system here. What's hard is deconstructing the ways in which we negotiate that process, because I'm really used to doing this and I don't know too many other people who work the same way.
