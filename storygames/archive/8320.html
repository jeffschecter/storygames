    \n Well, Matthijs, it's nothing new; it's "what does [word x] mean?" Check it:
    in\xb7no\xb7va\xb7tion (\\\u02cci-n\u0259-\u02c8v\u0101-sh\u0259n\\, noun)
    Date: 15th century
    1 : the introduction of something new
    2 : a new idea, method, or device : novelty

    So, for instance, removing the notion of one player = one PC (an innovation from one player plays many units, with no character elements) is novel, a new method of play. By the absolute, denotative standard, if it wasn't done before, and then it was; it's innovative. One can contextualize--frex, rolling dice to determine resources hasn't been innovative since the earliest gambling games, but it was innovative for the RPG world, where prior to that (wargames) resources tended to be static by unit--or one can just say "new or not, period."

    In the pure, non-contextual case, there's probably only been a handful of innovations in the history of RPGs, starting with "we're going to use rules to inform a story, rather than just pass the stick or sit and watch one person tell it." Some have, yep, been done by folks on these boards.

    And to REALLY get your noodle in a knot: is it "innovative" to essentially put a term of negation in front of a common practice, "flipping the mode?" For example, "RPGs are NOT about winning" or "in this RPG, you never control one PC exclusively"?

    I came not to bury this thread, but to praise it--through context. Otherwise, Mirriam-Webster answers it at Post 2.

    \n Actually, there's an interesting question at the core of this thread.

    The concept of "innovation" is the idea of novelty: something new, different, hitherto unseen or not done. And while we can prize innovation, in the sense that we are looking for the next hot newness, innovation is also a potential turn-off or disincentive: it is possible for something to be too new, too different from "how we do it now" to be practicable. Not for nothing is there a whole field of social science interested in "diffusion of innovations" (how new ideas spread within and across communities); concepts from this field like "change agent" and "early adopter" have entered more general usage.

    So the interesting question may have to do with this: what are the RPG innovations that have a chance of widespread adoption, and which are likely to remain relatively rare? What innovations have gained currency, and which haven't over the past several years?

    For example, I think the arrival of IAWA with its oracles for situation generation gives Ganakagok a better shot at adoption because of how it lets gamers try out the innovation of interpreting card meanings to create situation, which Ganakagok incorporates to a greater extent.

    \n I like novelty.

    I like novelty in RPGs that does just a few things, though:

    * Addresses subject matter in an insightful, new way
    * Provides new procedures that are fun in their own way
    * Leads my players to play in a different way then they currently are, just to see if different is fun

    What I don't like about novelty in RPGs:

    * New words for old ideas
    * Lengthier processes that don't add anything substantive to the experience
    * Shorter processes that remove something substantive from the experience
    * New processes that don't clearly lead to new experiences.

    In addition, I used to really enjoy the sense that I'd just uncovered a deliberately engineered emergent property of a game, now it just pisses me off.

    Okay.

    Storygamer Paul v1 picks up The New Hotness (or in my case, slightly old and dusty hotness), pushes through the system and has a game session where something cool and unexpected happens.

    Paul v1 gets wind of cool emergent things that are supposed to naturally occur when he plays other games. He plays those other games. The emergent thing doesn't happen. Then he goes back and plays his successfully emergent-play-making game but with a different group, and again it doesn't happen.

    Storygamer Paul v2 picks up The New Hotness and wants to know why rule X exists, because the rest of the rules are written in an opaque way. I ask a community of gamers (say, story-cunts.com) about the intent of rule X, and get a bunch of coy bullshit. They don't want to spoil the surprise. This pisses me off, because I have yet to see a game that consistently produces an "emergent" property of play.

    Any bit of awesome "emergent" play I've ever gotten out of a game is lightning in a bottle -- so much depends on the players, expectations set in the text, our willingness to study or not study a game. Example: I've had incredibly neat things emerge from Lacuna, and I've had total dud Lacuna sessions.

    At this point, I'd rather just have a straight no-bullshit understanding of a designer's intent. I don't care about having my surprise spoiled. I DO care about drilling down to the good stuff in a game. I don't want to take the chance that I may waste an evening.

    When someone writes a game such that a specific emergent quality of play can be consistently created without revealing its existence in the game text, I'll enjoy the experience. But as of now, the notion of baking a hidden emergent play quality into a game just pisses me off. Nobody can do it consistently, and when it doesn't happen it makes me feel like I've played the game "wrong." (Or the old standby blowoff: Oh, this game isn't for you.)

    Posted By: Bill_White
    But you have to imagine that every new game is in some fashion different from the games that came before it, so to that extent at least there's adegreeof innovation in every game. The issue is how different any given game can be from those predecessors and still be recognizable as the same kind of game (since we suspect that people interested in one sort of game will be interested in other similar games); or, in other words, how similar a game can be to another and still be acknowledged as a game in its own right.
    \nChanging within a kind may be by degree, but changing from one kind of game to another kind of game would be of a sudden. You suddenly take away from the game one essential quality of the former kind, and so suddenly the game becomes representative of a different kind.*

    A game represents a kind of game that is innovative of games if and only if it lacks at least one essential quality of any previously existing kind of game. The game itself is innovative of games if and only if it is the first of its kind.

    If the above statements are true, then:

    Innovation of games may not be purely additive in relation to another kind of game. "D&D Plus Food Fights" is not innovative because it retains all the essential qualities of D&D. The game is not the first of its kind. (Wait, back up. It's not strictly correct to say that "D&D + FF" would not be innovative. It may be an innovation of D&D; it just wouldn't be an innovation of games. See what I did there?)

    Innovation of games may be purely subtractive in relation to another kind of game. Assuming characters are an essential quality of D&D, then "D&D Minus Characters" is innovative so long as there has never been such a game. "D&D - C" may be innovative even if there has previously been a not-purely-subtractive game like "D&D + FF - C". (However, practically speaking, it isn't likely that the latter game would have been created without at least a foray in the former territory.)

    Of course, even assuming I didn't screw up my logic/wording above, this argument assumes that games may be categorized into kinds of games determined by essential qualities. Instead, maybe a game can be modified by degree with no point at which a change from one kind to another may be recognized.


    *Objection: Essential qualities may be possessed to different levels of degree, thereby making the change from possessing them to not possessing them not so sudden. An example may be colors perceived as pure versus colors perceived as mixtures of pure colors (ala the theory behind NCS color codes). From one pure color to another, there may be no clear demarcation. One pure color may provide a clear demarcation between two other pure colors or mixtures of colors, so there may be kinds nevertheless.

    In the context of universally defining innovation of games, it seems like this would require a predefined set of pure kinds of games. Innovation would be either proving the existence of previously undiscovered pure kinds of games, or less dramatically, concocting untried mixtures of the pure kinds of games.
