\n I just finished reading Puppetland, and I was really struck by the narration style. To quote the author's play example:

Puppet Master: "And then, the door burst down! Three nutcrackers stood there, one of them still chomping on the doorknob. 'Stop, you mangy puppets!' cried one."

Actor 1: "Run! Run out the back door! We must escape the nutcrackers!"

Actor 2: "Where is that rock? Here it is! I shall hurl this rock and smash your greedy mouth, nutcracker!"

Puppet Master: "The rock flew across the room and hit the first nutcracker in the jaw. 'Rmmf!' cried the injured nutcracker. 'He cannot bite nor chomp!' cried another. And he was right: the nutcracker's jaw was broken in two."

I've played IAWA and Shock: once each. I think both of those sessions would have been enhanced by this approach, coming out as tales one might write, read, or speak to an audience, bard-style.

    \n Interestingly, I was just talking to Shreyas about playtesting Daniel Solis' upcoming game, Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple, and a related issue came up. Apparently, in the most recent draft, Daniel was trying to get the players to narrate things happening instead of roleplaying the characters doing stuff and some of the players found this distancing and a bit awkward, feeling stuck in director stance without being about to enjoy being the characters.

    But, yeah, there's definitely something interesting that happens when you can have the whole narrative framed as a story that you're reading or telling instead of something that's happening right now, in the present tense. The past-tense thing from Puppetland is definitely an early exploration of that kind of idea. I do think that, like in the Do playtests, finding some way to allow players to shift out of director stance (which does look like it happens in your example, with the Puppet Master mostly being the one focusing on past tense) may be necessary in some cases. Or maybe it would just involve a lot of practice, so you can feel comfortable shifting between stances/tenses with declarations like:

    "Phillipe ran for the door, with the panther right behind him. Suddenly, he remembered his magic amulet. 'I knew this would come in handy!' He turned and pulled it from around his neck..."

    That's not the kind of thing that comes naturally in many roleplaying styles, so it might take a while, if the group really hopes to cultivate it.

    \n In Blood Red Sands, there's a thing called The Voice of the Chronicler. The Chronicler is the rotating faux-GM position, and the conceit is that he's the guy ages after the events of the game whose telling the story of the hero and their many ordeals. So whenever the player who is curently the Chronicler uses one of the Chronicler's GMey powers they have to do it in third person past tense as purple as they can.
    \n
    \nthusly:
    \n
    \n\u201cAnd then did the hero Karn bring forth the Death Viper Spear and, having impaled the vile sorcerer Malethax upon it, hung him by his flesh from the Tree of Bones and the ravens didst devour his eyes.\u201d
    \n
    \nIn the game that's when Malethax's player responds with "That's not how it happened!" as if he was protesting the version of the story being told and then it goes to dice to see what really happened.

    Posted By: shreyasSo you always have to describe what you're doing in your character's voice, as if you were, uh, Steve on Blue's Clues going "Hey, it's Mail Time! Time to check the mail!"
    Let's be clear here, though: that's not a bug, that's a feature.

    I've run Puppetland more than any single game except Call of Cthulhu, probably around twenty sessions by now, and I've grown wily. The most helpful technique I evolved was when I began to show up with a bag of puppets, mostly finger and hand, picked up cheap on random trips to craft shows/IKEA/toy stores/etc, and to have the players each pick one out. We then played in a LARP-ish way. There were not a lot of props, no backgrounds except when someone was moved to sketch one, and some of the GM characters were not represented by appropriate puppets, so it wasn't quite up to the usual LARP level. (I did make important NPCs on the spot from paper lunch sacks, socks, and a few blank finger puppets and hand puppets, sewn from old T-shirts and stuff and then modified with Sharpies.) The most important effect was that the players could, instead of saying "Oh, nasty nutcracker! I shall stuff your mouth with flowers so you can't hurt anyone any more!" or whatever, they could say "Abracadabra!", and have their puppet gesture magically at the mouth of my NPC. They could then make it known via eye contact that they were planning on using their "This puppet can make flowers appear magically wherever she points" ability; upon realizing that, I could more naturally be the one to say "And poof!, a bouquet of bright red roses appeared in the nutcracker's mouth!"

    \n When it gets remembered its very powerful. Most of play occurs in "normal roleplay mode" only the GM-esque bits like "scene framing" or "now this horrible things happens to you" are unique to the Chronicler. Often the player winds up just doing those things in "normal roleplay voice" too. But when they remember to do it in "The Voice" it can be very powerful. The quote above was from actual play (modified to make a better in text example) and was pretty memorable.
    \n
    \nInterestingly, the ritual phrases NEVER get forgotten. Slamming your hand on the table and loudly proclaiming "that's not how it happened!" or meekly submitting with "as you will..." after losing a Challenge is too much fun to get overlooked. The other players will sit and stare at you expectantly until you utter the right phrase.
    \n
    \nThe combination really makes the play experience feel special and unique, much the way the First Person Present Tense requirement for Puppetland does or the ritual phrases in Polaris. There's no mistaking what game you're playing, when the very language you use to play it is different.
    \n
    \nI like that. More so than just different dice or fancy character sheets it really puts a stamp on play.

    \n This is an interesting topic.

    I worked on a number of story-telling games over the last two years: like roleplaying games, except no one plays a role. Everyone narrates.

    And one thing I found interesting was that, despite the insistence of the game text and the players to narrate in past tense, it almost never happened... and when it happened, we could only maintain it for a few minutes at a time. It was as though creating the story in real-time meant it must be narrated in real-time, in present-tense. I can't emphasize enough how difficult this was, and consistently difficult, with different groups of people. I can only think of one instance of a playtest that remained in past tense for a significant period of time.

    Now, the really interesting part is that when I played Polaris, past tense came naturally and easily. Because the premise and the key phrases keep you there; they make that the only thing that could make sense. (I can't comment on Puppetland, other than that I like the idea.)
