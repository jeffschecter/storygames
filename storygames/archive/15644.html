3/ If the plot has already been charted, what are the other fruitful voids? character development?

    \n GMless games often have sharp constraints on the progression of scenes. This has the effect of moving the players creative inputs to other areas. Montsegur 1244 is a great example - you know exactly what's going to happen on the macro level and how the game will end. What you play to discover is how the characters will make a single decision, and what that decision will be. I don't think this is precisely what you are looking at but it's probably a useful cousin to look at.

    1/ First of all, am I interpreting the term correctly?
    \nI think so.

    It's important to remember that these terms arose in a rhetorical context. "Illusionism" isn't just a definition. It's a way of arguing against a particular GMing practice, in which the GM designs the storyline. "Participationism", then, is a way of defending that GMing practice: it's a way of saying, hey, some players like the GM to design the storyline.\n
    2/ How would you design such a game to be compelling to players? any great examples out there?
    \nThe most obvious examples are Dungeons and Dragons and Call of Cthulhu.

    And, then, a game like Trail of Cthulhu has your railroading built in. Cthulhu Dark does a bit of this, too.\n
    3/ If the plot has already been charted, what are the other fruitful voids? character development?
    \nYes, I think so.

    But then it depends what you mean by plot. You can argue that, in Call of Cthulhu, the plot is about what happens between player characters. Similarly, in Montsegur 1244, the plot isn't really charted, even though prescripted events occur, because the plot is what happens between characters.

    \n Really, there's a rich tradition here of games where either the progression of events or the ending of the game are fixed from the outset.

    There's a whole indie games tradition of "countdown" mechanics that drive play towards fixed endgames (though the results of the endgame vary, the fact that the game ends there doesn't). The mechanic itself started with Sorcerer (humanity!), My Life with Master, Dust Devils, Breaking the Ice, and Polaris and has continued from there, including things like Roanoke and even Agon, in a way. These ideas obviously predate indie games, but "endgames" as a thing have sorta become an indie games trademark.

    There's also a slightly different fixed endgame thing in Breaking the Ice (it does both!), The Mountain Witch, It's Complicated, Montsegur, Silver & White, and most of Jason's games (The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach, Grey Ranks, Fiasco) that doesn't have a stat that counts down to the endgame, but there are relatively fixed numbers of scenes or events until the game is over.

    There are games that have a fixed progression because they end when most of the characters are dead or the horror has been revealed. Cthulhu games are partially in this tradition, as are more PVP or survival games like Mist-Robed Gate, Geiger Counter, etc.

    The degree to which the events of the story are set from the outset is something we've been wrestling with from the beginning.

    \n I think the "Participationism" definition is a pretty terrible place to start if you're interested in plots being defined before play. The definition you've posted presupposes both a GM and fudging, neither of which are required for a plot. I'd say that the universe of plot-first games is bigger than just illusionist and participationist play, and getting the concept of plot mixed up with old arguments about illusionism is an easy way to get distracted. My currently-in-beta game Final Hour of a Storied Age is a GM-less game which involves a mechanical procedure for outlining a plot up front, but I'd say it has little to do with the illusionist/participationist play style.

    \n A good participationist game really works when the plot is really cool but what it does is gives the players a chance to showcase their character and what they are good at/what interests the player about the character. Traditionally this has been handled by scene framing and the gm setting up situations where the characters can shine, it's not that difficult for a gm who can read people and give them what they want as long as the players create characters who are clear archtypes. i.e. Player A makes a super combat killing machine, so you put them in situations where they have to kill tons of enemy's it's even better if you can make it look like the odds are impossible but miraculously the killing machine pc manages to pull off amazing maneuvers that save the day (a lot of gm fudging tends to come in to play here)
    \n
    \nD&D; does the above well with the different character niches, most groups tend to have varied classes represented leaving each player an area of specialty. Other mechanics that can work well are things like compels in the fate and fudge based games that get the player to go along with whats happening in exchange for more power to affect the situation down the road.

    \n I think one important question to ask is, "what is truly at stake in this game?" and then, "do the players get to decide how that shakes out, or is preset/decided by a GM-figure ahead of time?"

    There's a big difference between

    a) a Participationist game that's really just all about enjoying the scenery. (An extreme example might be the reading of a radio theatre play: you're still "playing a character" by virtue of speaking their lines, but you have no input at all into how the game turns out. I suppose something fundamentally pretty similar could be designed with a really heavy railroading style of play)

    and

    b) a game where certain elements of the story are set in stone, but the real matter of interest is still up in the air.

    A good fiction example might be the movie Titanic: we all know the ship is going to sink at the end. But we DON'T know what's going to happen to the romantic leads and what decisions they will make, who will survive, etc.

    I wouldn't call that game Illusionist or anything of the sort, just one with certain constraints based on future events. ("The ship WILL sink by the third act. Now, what do you do?")

    If, on the other hand, what we really want to know is which of the player characters can survive the catastrophe, but the GM has secretly decided that they all will, except Bob... then you have some kind Illusionist thing going on. I think it could be fun if done right -- or at least done really well -- but once you take meaningful decisions out of the hands of the players (or the resolution system you've agreed to use), then to me it becomes a different activity: like some kind of performance art.

    \n Ivan,

    I have a slew of current threads on the Forge addressing the very question you asked in your OP. This one's the most directly relevant:
    [Pitfighter] SBP: is there anything better to roll for than success?
    The list of "other fruitful voids" starts here and gets refined over the next bunch of posts.

    More general stuff and more AP:
    decoupling Reward Systems from broad-scale Story Arcs
    [Pitfighter] SBP: the GM's role in resolution

    I think the biggest category, and the easiest one to tie to game incentives, is Character Positioning. The GM's plot can unfold exactly as intended while the character's fates ebb and flow based on the player's choices and the dice. The range of risks and rewards is still vast, it just can't include anything that would ruin the GM's plot (like death). You could look at it like this: "Your character WILL be at the final confrontation with the badguys, but what STATE will they be in for that? Confident, determined, armed with guns and magic, with your loyal legions' hopes riding on you? Or broken, naked and forgotten?"

    Some Participationist/Illusionist GMs are actually happy to relinquish control of the Final Scene's outcome, so those guns and magic might even make a difference!
