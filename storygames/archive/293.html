    Dave,

    Thing about it is you have to ask what your game is doing and why. Why do you have falling and drowning rules?

    Is it because your game is about exploring and surviving the rigours of the outdoors?

    Is it because other games have them and so there is no reason you shouldn't?

    Is it because you're trying to make your system model the physical reality of your world?

    People talk about it being bad design when the game isn't a game in which falling or drowning should be important (how often does someone drown on Friends? or fall off a high place and die on Family Guy?) and you've these excess rules there, it is (at best) clunky. If you have rules you should have a good reason for having them -- they should tell you what the game is about and what you want to do with it.

    As Brand said. And "falling and drowning" is my summary for the gamut of World Dooms: falling, drowning, poisoning, electrocution, rapid decompression (in an SF game), vacuum (SF), blood loss... And further from there, things like "bullet tumbling effects", "blunt weapons with piercing nubs on them", "shock due to internal trauma", etc.

    Some people don't consider a game a game unless it has speicifically defined how these things should work. I think it's a combination of "All Games Have this Stuff" and "All Games Should Model Reality at least a Little".

    I think the litmus test for this is hearing about initiative in the game "FVLMINATA": Slaves and Workers and Soldiers defer to Patricians in rank and all social and economic status, therefore Patricians always "have initiative".
    Some People: Ohh, that's interesting!
    Some People: Ohh, that's fucking retarded!

    People in the second group tend to put a high mark on having the game rules model 'reality' (physics, etc).

    In the end, it's not bad design, unless you include those rules in a game that has nothing to do with drowning or falling, or if you don't include those rules in a game that clearly has lots of those sorts of events.

    The classic case here is the Kevin & Kell RPG, which bizarrely decides to adapt Gold Rush Games' Action! system -- including in full rules for underwater combat, rubble modifiers, and so forth -- for a game based on a humorous comic strip of suburban life with anthropomorphic animals.

    Just to clarify, a game doesn't have to be about drowning and falling for it to include rules for it. There's room for rules-heavy games which sort of by definition are going to have rules which don't individually tell you what the game as a whole is about. But by including a rule, you should at least be able to picture using those rules in a way which makes the game better than ignoring them.
