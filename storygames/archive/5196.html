    \n There are several supplements on that (Temporality, Chronomancy) and my favourite old White Dwarf (become it became only GDW material) article from Feb/Mar 1982 "This Is, Of Course, Impossible" (Marcus L. Rowland).

    He describes different models of time you can use:

    1 Linear Time (immutable) - no change whatsoever can take place, "the moving finger writes and moves on". The only gains in time travel are through observation (e.g., you can see where the treasure was buried so you can go back in present and dig it up). Not much grist for roleplaying. Time travel really amounts to "viewing the past". It does create law enforcement and other implications for an internally consistent setting if it is possible. [E.G. there is a short story in which a time travel inventor is approached by various religious authorities to get him to agree never to visit the time periods in which the "truth" of historical claims to their faiths would be determinable].

    1. Linear Time (Variable) - this is where roleplaying likely happens, three types.

    (a) Avalanche Time - minor actions can snowball, killing the butterfly in the Pleistocene era can mean that dinosaurs still rule the world in present. There is no tendency for events to "revert". Killing dictator stops war. There may be particular "safe zones, paths" etc. where "as long as you stick to this it's safe" (e.g., you cannot leave the road when going through woods, you must not talk to any official in the town and you must not harm any livestock). This kind of "short list of restrictions" can be used to create amusing/challenging scenarios as long as the consquences of "what happens if you do" are acceptable failure.

    (b) Elastic Time - stretchy time, history reverts, question is just how long. Killing dictator at best slows down war, war still happens and ends up much the same.

    (c) Conservative Time - almost immutable, it's like elastic but almost immediately and prophylactically, shoot at the dictator and you miss, try to blow him up and he's called away suddenly, kill him and it turns out that was a body double and the real one is unharmed [TV stories use this a fair bit, essentially you do something to change past but it's futile]. Another version of this is "defensive time" which will eject anyone who would cause a big change (e.g., in the Avalanche restrictions models, step off the path and the "chronovores" show up to gobble you down). In Conservative time, history as you know it was already based on all the time travel that ever takes place. This suggests future also "determined".

    Then there is Branched Time where changes simply create an alternative fork, Parallel Time where all possible forks happened already and there are infinite parallels, Looped Time where past and future are looped and always feeding back to one another.

    Implication is that different models of time work best for different stories. If your timeline is Avalanche, any time travel can create large changes, and if you can travel back any large amount of time it's huge, history rewritten so much it doesn't count when it's made. This works best only if time travel can only go back a few days for "recent history do-overs that will really matter" (TV shows have explored this, both as 'do overs' and 'time cop' 'time war' concepts).

    Conservative time is an interesting model to explore in a time travel story (e.g., you don't know yet which type of timeline is working, can you change history?) but I don't think it would work in ongoing play.

    If it's Elastic, to notably change present you must travel back no more than a few decades (depending on rate of correction). Changes are more relevant at personal level (saving your lost love) than "historical" (the revolution will happen anyway). If it's Elastic with "breakable if event big enough" then you've got Time Cops/Time War stories about defending history and "repairing it" (e.g., intervening to put it back on track, which can be a problem if a new timeline 'post break' is the new 'elastically defended timeline).

    Also, if it's Elastic you may want to ponder a bit "why" - what makes events revert to producing the same long term outcomes? Is there a destiny? Is there an uber time cop force at work protecting a particular timeline? Is it benign or not? Why does the timeline revert could be an underlying "question"/"mystery" in the campaign.

    [I have embroidered Rowland's basic outline with the story implications and you can tweak all of these to work differently but I think they are useful categories to think about in establishing time travel]

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonIs there a way to make that work?
    \nI've been working on something right up that alley over on the Imagination Sweatshop. The initial post only really hints at what's going on (more posts will be up later), but the basic gist of it is: as the characters meddle in time, the more currency they have to spend on doing all those juicy little time travel tricks (like going back in time to help yourself); but the greater the possibility of them causing a paradox that would unravel the universe. So, the future not only can't be changed, but they must actively avoid changing it.

    Mechanically speaking, each time the characters try to affect history, the players must roll dice. From this roll, they will find a number that they must enter into a grid. The more instances of a single number in the grid, the greater the risk of altering history (and ending reality as we know it). If the players can make preset patterns of numbers in the grid, they gain Synchronicities, which are those aforementioned juicy tricks. As the game progresses the more time tricks the characters can perform and the more dangerous their meddling becomes.

    \n Continuum sort of does this. They've got a concept of your "Yet", which is the stuff you've heard about (or indeed even seen) yourself doing, that you haven't done yet, and which will eventually need to be fulfilled.

    Like you walk up to a dude, and he goes "Hey, where's that $20 you owe me?" and you've suddenly got "Borrow $20 from this dude" in your Yet. And occasionally you get Frag which means you need to deal with something in your Yet, and if you can't deal with it, someone else will, and that's generally considered suboptimal to you fixing your own problems.

    I would suggest just stealing that, and trying to avoid the paranoid mindset of altered memories and Thespian body-doubles that results from the vast webs of informational paradoxes that the rest of the game's model of time-travel seems like it would inevitably result in. Unless, y'know, you like that sort of stuff. You could probably get a good "Philip K. Dick writes 12 Monkeys" story out of it.

    \n Have you seen Chrononauts, by Looney Labs? It's a card game, but the neat part is that it ties together several events in history, so that changing one causes a ripple effect across other cards. The cards even track "paradox" and can cause everyone to lose, if there's too much of it.

    The seed notion here being that you could get a lot of game play out of changing history and working out what ripple effects occur on a known timeline--i.e. not just playing PCs but doing world building (or world breaking, same diff). A mix of alt-history adventuring and collaboration on speculative fiction.

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonThese don't quite cover what I'm looking for: I'm looking for something a bit more... quantum.
    \nIf you want quantum, then the infinite universes is really the way to go. Basically, time is branching all the time with each possible event. Time Travel is really travelling to another dimension where you appeared in the past (or future, or whatever). From that event, creates all sort of possibilities based on the actions you take until you leave and either go to a point futhur down one of those branches where you see the effects of your changes, or possibly even back to your own time where none of the changes happened, even in the past.

    The problem storywise, is you can simply skip the changes and instead go to a branch where those changes were made (either by another you or even someone else entirely) to your liking. Not very exciting. So, either there's a reason for them to make changes, or their travel is not that easily fine tuned or even under their control.

    \n Heya Ryan,

    My first game is about time travel. It's called Timestream, and I dunno if things work in it the way you're talking about (in terms if I have a set of "Time Travel Laws" that I personally like, but they're pretty arbitrary in the big picture of things). But the basics are that the mechanical stuff about paradox and such is a dial set by the group, so you could easily say that the big changes in history stuff gives you, say, twice the normal amount of Strain (my term for accumulating amounts of paradox) than in the base rules, or something along those lines.

    \n Hi guys,

    I am writing a time travel game called Stitch and have found the following useful:

    http://www.timetravelinstitute.com/

    http://www.lifesci.sussex.ac.uk/home/John_Gribbin/timetrav.htm

    Stitch is more focused on creating a pressured game where time is an actual part of the mechanics. Basically it is 12 monkeys the roleplaying game. The way I approached time travel was that there is one timeline the players manipulate. When they change something everything else forwards of that point rearranges slightly to accomodate. Once they have rearranged it enough the apocalypse never happens. Sort of elastic time with some twists.
