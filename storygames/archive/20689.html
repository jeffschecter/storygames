\n Since it's so intersectional I decided to just start a new thread and collect ideas into a hypothetical system for better analysis.

Every Character has a set of Assets each rated 1 to 6. These can be anything thematically appropriate.

Every Character and Scene has a set of Events which are visible to everyone and rolled for when encountered. These are the points of uncertainty and the only elements determined by the system.

Each Event has a set of 6 Outcomes. The GM gets to choose one, while the player gets to choose or invalidate (to prevent the GM from picking it) one for every success they roll. These can also be determined randomly.

Any time a player triggers an Event, they roll a number of d6 equal to the associated Asset. Even numbers are successes. Alternatively they roll the same number of Fate Dice where each [-] invalidates an Outcome, each [+] requires choosing one, and each [-][+] pair cancel each other out. Players always have the option to avoid triggering an Event entirely by changing their action.

Ideally, Outcomes should be easy to integrate into the narrative by describing how you achieve and/or avoid certain ends.

Examples forthcoming, as I still need to give this some time to jello.

    \n So one of my goals is eliminating Target Numbers (at least those set arbitrarily by the GM), and one of the ways I'm attempting that is with results that determine discreet states (broken arm) as opposed to degrees of values (Arm HP: -6). Every success should be a single event or transition, not just more of a value like damage, speed, etc.

    In most RPGs, degrees of values end up translated into discreet states in the fiction anyway. For example, jumping far enough (degree) means you leap over the pit (discreet). They're also used to delay game ending outcomes. For example, dying only after running out of HP.

    Situation: Trapped in a Haunted House


    Action: Perform a Seance
    Roll: Curious
    On any result a spirit is contacted
    1. But they possesses one of the characters
    2. But they won't cooperate a second time
    3. But the wrong one is contacted
    4. And they answer 3 questions
    5. And they tell you how they died
    6. And they ask for a favour in return for one
    \n
    Action: Run away in Fear
    Roll: Cautious
    On any result you get away from the thing you were running from
    1. But you become separated from the party
    2. But you run into something worse
    3. But you reach a dead end, staircase that goes nowhere, etc
    4. But you forget the path you took
    5. And you find the body of one of the other characters, even if they aren't dead (or are they?)
    6. And find a hidden space with a journal, puzzle box, etc
    \n
    The considerations in writing these were...

    Thematically relevant results. It didn't matter if these were positive or negative, only that they represented what one would expect to happen in a haunted house.

    Avoiding mutually exclusive outcomes. If one outcome was the ghost tries to protect you, but another was the ghost tries to kill you, then picking one will negate the other, which means 1 success could effectively cause/avoid 2 outcomes.

    A logical consequence of the action taken. It's all too easy to just go full story mode and write things from an author's perspective. For example, you open a door and find a monster, but a monster being behind that door is not a logical consequence of your character's action.

    Squinting closely you'll see that this is basically a more formalized version of the resolution system in 7th Sea 2nd Ed, which suffers from the same problems being addressed here. For example, every success (Raise) theoretically enables/prevents a single action/event, yet in practice doesn't because there's nothing which defines what a single action actually is. Is 'visit your lover's boudoir' a single action, or several like 'sneak past guards', 'climb scaffold', and 'lockpick window'? So a GM can get whatever result they want simply by dividing the action into enough parts (through Action Sequences) and/or setting the target numbers (of Consequences) high enough, and if they could normally do that anyway then why go through all this trouble?\n

    \n Oh, so these aren't "Events" anymore, but are now character "Actions"? Well, then it's much better.

    However, you seem to have re-invented Apocalypse World's "moves" (particularly "custom moves"). Is that your intention?

    We already know that those work great. But what are you trying to add to this?

    The "cause/avoid" dichotomy, however, that jumped out at me. That's a new piece of technology. And potentially interesting - if it's just "here's a list of stuff you'd like, and here's a list of stuff you don't want", it's only slightly interesting. (The dice narrow your choices, that could be fruitful or frustrating, depending on how exactly you're writing the moves.)

    However, if there's some fun thing you can do with "causing" and "avoiding" which adds new information to this, then you have something which builds on the AW "move" concept, and perhaps in a pretty interesting way. Now we're building on this technology to create something new!

    For example, let's say one of the outcomes is something like "You become separated from the party" or "Someone is injured".

    Avoiding this could mean:

    * No one is separated from the party/injured.

    Or:

    * You are not separated from the party/injured.

    Those are quite different!

    Causing this could mean:

    * You get to choose who is separated from the party/injured. (Which could be yourself.)

    Usually, not as good as "avoiding". But sometimes, if you want to get separated or injured (to take the blow for a loved one, or because you led the group into an ambush), or you want to betray a companion, this could be a very attractive option!

    Now that's getting more interesting to me. You could build in risk/control this way: if you take lead on a particular action, like moving into a certain room, starting a fight, or performing a seance, by default you will be the one facing the outcomes, both good and bad. Roll badly, and all the bad stuff is probably happening to you (or at least out of your control). Roll better, though, and perhaps even if you don't *avoid* the bad stuff, you'll have some control over where and how it lands.

    That's getting more juicy!

    You'll also need to distinguish between things which happen by default (if no dice are spent), like "someone gets injured", and things which only happen if dice are spent.

    Logically, those would be the things to avoid vs. the things you could achieve, but there might be a more interesting way of doing that, as well.

    \n In a recent design I noticed that while there are tons of types of fictional and non fictional skills, a lot can be reduced to types and flavors of well, non-fictional ones.

    Like magical invisibility being an elaborated form of Stealth, or darkvision an elaborated form of perception. So I built the skill system so characters have all the same skill list but can further personalize those skills in specific ways I labeled specializations. While the skills do get numerical bonuses that players can increase, specializations don't add numerical bonuses but state fictional rules for their use instead.

    A player with high perception but no darkvision can still roll to discern shapes in poor light, but one with low perception and darkvision rolls to see details or hidden things in the dark.

    A magician may have high magic combat related skills, but he still needs to rise his levels in stealth (invisibility) to go completely unnoticed, like a thief with high stealth and no magic can. Basically this prevents players for investing all their points on a single skill that does everything, so I can let players define any specialization they want on skills already defined.

    Fiction wise, my rule is that nothing in the character sheet can give players an automatic success. They can earn it through their efforts in the fiction, yes, but being inhumanly fast doesn't mean you can beat everyone all the time. But then again, not because your character failed at doing what he trained all his life to do, but because the opponent can still anticipate your next move or be otherwise prepared for it, maybe outperform you by sheer luck or other smart move. So lately, I don't wait for a high roll as a GM to present opponents as fearsome, player's low rolls present a better chance for that.

    The rest is solved by always asking the player "How exactly do you do that?" meaning how do they use the skill they mentioned to get the goal they want. Only then if the asset is applicable and/or makes sense in terms of the fiction to use in the way described, as a GM I can either decide to concede an automatic success, ask for a roll or give an explanation of why it wouldn't work based on the logic of the setting used and conditions presented.

    \nThis is a part of the game I haven't been able to quantify or mechanize properly, nor I've seen a proper satisfactory mechanization.     The chess like approach ends up producing problems like loopholes in the rules that deform the fictional game with the intrusion of mechanics that wouldn't make sense not only on the real world but in any of the settings used as reference (like the peasant railgun) or at least, slows down the game way too much for my tastes, when the GM and/or the players have to waste game time searching for rules.

    Reducing the exchange to the intent means that you will be mostly using the system as a way to add anything interesting to the combat, which in some cases translates to numerical stats and bonuses exchange. It's a valid fun, but personally, not my fun.

    Decreasing system data exchange and trying to make the whole conversation about description potentially increases the risk of turning the game into the most nocive form of forum roleplaying, where players manipulate the narration to obtain advantages by stepping over other's narrative rights and spotlight. In forum it doesn't go past one talented player writing more than others, but in tabletop gaming it translates to a sort of competition for spotlight hoarding and less time/opportunities/worse quality of the experience for less talented players.

    So, my take ended up being a middle point using my judgement in a way that I can't possibly mechanize, so I turned it instead into a procedure. When a player answers the question "what do you do?" in my table, the initial answer is more or less the intent. "I attack him with my axe" if the final intent is to kill, scare or mutilate the opponent, doesn't need to be my concern yet. Then I ask the player "ok, how do you do that?" to get a clearer picture of what is happening. At best, the description will add or change the intent, at worst it will just add color to the scene. Nevertheless this description is the base for me to give a proper feedback either the dice rolled after are high or low, either a hit or a miss.

    If the player's description says "I aim to his neck with an horizontal cut" then the intent is to kill. I can decide that's an aimed attack and add penalties or just take it as a general direction and ask the player for a roll. A critical would meant the opponent is either beheaded instantly, or he is wounded and his armor is broken if it offered neck protection according to the piece of art I'm using for reference.

    For me whatever makes sense given the references, preparation or circumstances may block the final intent despite what the dice say, but only if I find it interesting to offer such outcome to the players, which is the part I can't actually codify into any kind of system. I mean, have we been extending the combat too long? Does it feel like we've been rolling dice and doing repetitive things for the past ten or twenty minutes? Then it's a perfect chance to behead the guy, have his companions run in disarray and wrap up the combat. Has it been 30 mins of actually interesting and satisfying combat and nobody wants to let it end here? Then the armour saved him and he continues fighting. It's all about the energy I'm being able to give and the perception of the energy I'm receiving from the players, not about an exact amount of time or parts of the system used.
