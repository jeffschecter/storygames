There's a gift die mechanic pretty much lifted straight out of The Shadow of Yesterday, see, except you put a little check mark by a stat when someone gives you a gift die for a test of that stat.     \n Players may not react well to "external shaping" of character though an uncertain medium.

    I think a basic bargain of D&D is GM controls everything outside PC's skin, Player controls everything inside it, rules resolve any penetrations across barrier in either direction - successful play as a player means you keep the flow "going out" not "coming in". Without level advancement, feats are now the definition of character development in system and player is not being allowed to define their character except "through a mirror darkly" via others' perceptions of play which itself is subject to the GM's control of scenes and random die variations.

    The uncertainty of this medium for communicating player desires for advancement then becomes an issue. It's not as simple as "you want X, you do Y and if successful you get X".

    Social dynamics and different agendas of players may come into play still as much as ever or more. Much depends on whether IC or OOC "chatter" is expected to factor into matters. The rogue waxes eloquent "in play" about how he would love to hamstring and slow down foes when striking with sneak ~ others get the hint regarding the feat to do that. Another PC ends up with Craft Magic Arms and Armour because the players of other PC's want magic arms.

    Dissonance of play styles etc. may be aggravated (no table really has everyone with same tastes, to some extent you live with fact someone next to you design and player characters differently than you with the thought, "well that's their character, I'll design and play mine my way.").

    I think there are really two issues here:

    - 1st is making feat selection flow from events in play (the long time bugaboo of level based systems being dissonance of play and specific advancement results compared to "skill check" systems like BW or dare I say it old Chaosium systems did - with "advance what you use")'
    - 2nd is putting that "from play events" selection under group authority (I can't think of a PC-based game example of the group authority issue here, I can think of "group veto" approaches [yes BW traits] but not "group authoring").

    It might be useful to consider the issues of each. I would favour "advance what you use" (choose feats that matter) over "group interpretation of what matters for each player". You can make this more immediate by giving the feat slot but not having the feat chosen until it would matter and then letting it be chosen immediatley (e.g., the cleric with empty slot may end up taking Spell Penetration if their spell fails to beat SR by 1 or might instead end up taking Extra Turning if they run out of uses and undead are still coming - in each case effect is immediate, spell did beat SR, cleric has the extra uses of Turn/Rebuke this day). Player can still "have that in mind" but choice isn't locked down until it would matter in play (which means player doesn't lose anything as if it never matters they lost nothing in play).

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton I want "get feat for stuff that happened in game" - so maybe group veto power would be better.
    \nI like "group veto" because it emphasizes "you are not playing to amuse the DM, you are playing to entertain the table", a direction that leads to everyone having more fun. Don't just "show the DM what you've been emailing privately about in terms of character development", show everyone, act it up a bit more when your character is doing the stuff you intend to build up more with feats.

    Can you eloborate "earning it"?

    Do you mean in sense of "you can opt to get Improved Critical if you scored a critical using that weapon or if your blow with that weapon killed a foe" - the narrative play created says "Zamora runs the pirate through with her rapier and he drops dead" (scored a crit and rolled well), now meaning that to facilitate reincorporation of Zamora being effective and deadly with her rapier in a story-game sense (her most notable actions are not a collection of dice flukes, there is pattern), her player can now take any feat for her that makes that likely in future (Weapon Focus, Specialization, Improve Crit etc. assuming other qualifications met).

    Or do you mean "you tried to be effective that way and weren't", that earns the ability to increase your effectiveness (after all narratives are full of tried, tried, tried, then succeeded). "Zamora misses the pirate with her blade and then hits but fails by narrow margin to confirm critical, suffering his blows in return in meantime and being sorely wounded", so now Zamora can say "I learned about handling rapier" and can take any rapier-use enhancing feat?

    If you want to "influence play" you only need to make the qualification work on "try" (2nd option) not "did".

    Combining them "tried but didn't succeed" is something rest of table can veto (it doesn't make sense to table that she would ever be good at that), whereas "success" is something table can't veto (look, she took him down with her sword, as unlikely as it seems, it should be more likely that she can do it again). This creates a "player-only" zone to satisfy those needs as well as encouraging "demonstrating action to table".

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton - because IMO the best feats are the ones that define something about your character rather than giving a new bonus in their combat niche.
    \nI suggested on WOTC boards a while back that all feats can be "dressed up" in "character-relevant" terms and if the dressing not "preset" but rather authored by player then this can respect and enhance character concepts.

    There is something more fun about saying, \u201cI'm using my grandfather's sword, +1 to hit\u201d than \u201cweapon focus - sword\u201d; about, \u201cI belong to the Order of the Arcane Secrets\u201d than \u201cSkill Focus Knowledge Arcana\u201d; about \u201cI always strike to kill\u201d or \u201cStrength is the only thing to respect\u201d than \u201cPower Attack\u201d; about \u201cI am confident enchantment magic is best\u201d rather than Spell Focus - even if all pairs do the same thing mechanically and the first is simply a descriptive element added to the second. The alternatives give more cues to roleplaying and suggest stories and obstacles for GM to create.

    You can impose on Players an obligation to add such scripting to their feats. The feats still exist as the technical substrata. Whenever, in play, the feat matters (e.g., you hit by one) the player declares it, "I would have missed but recall my grandfather's teaching" etc.

    The same weapon focus bonus can come from \u201cI killed my first man with a longsword\u201d, \u201cLongswords are sacred to my god\u201d, \u201cThe longsword is the mark of a true warrior\u201d. These call out of a story that involves that first killing or the consequences of first steps to violence, a character reacting to the treatment of longswords and possession of them by those considered unworthy, a character who treats people differently based on the weapons they bear etc.

    You can do the same for class features. What is the character explanation of your sneak damage, rage or power against undead - they could be \u201cI look for weakness\u201d, \u201cAnger is my power\u201d, and \u201cI hate undead\u201d or \u201cI love to twist the knife\u201d, \u201cThe great bear takes me\u201d, and \u201cUndead killed my love.\u201d

    You can see an example of this for a character I did in a short-lived campaign run by a great GM but alas now on indefinite hiatus http://ebrys.pbwiki.com/Rothe

    \n In our 3.5 game, players can vote for each other's feats based on how they spend their action points. We use an action point system where you can spend an action point to do many things including but not limited to:
    \n
    \n- use a feat you don't have whose prerequisites you meet for an encounter
    \n- use a feat you don't have whose prerequisites you don't meet for one action
    \n
    \nAs you spend action points on these abilities, you mark down the feats acquired in this manner on your character sheet.
    \n
    \nYou acquire feats as normal for leveling up but you may also acquire new feats via feat vote at the conclusion of a storyline.
    \n
    \nThe feat vote works as follows (inspired by Burning Wheel trait votes):
    \n
    \n- in order, we place 1 person's character sheet in the middle of the table
    \n- each person (including the GM and the person who owns the character in question) nominates the character for a new feat
    \n- everyone votes
    \n- if the feat in question was a feat they used an action point on, majority vote awards them the feat
    \n- otherwise a unanimous vote is needed
    \n- if multiple feats are voted in, the player in question chooses the one they want
    \n- people are encouraged to explain why they nominated a specific feat and re-tell all the cool things the player did to earn the nomination (this makes for a fun, let's talk about how cool our game was, and also gives everyone insight on what they like about each other's characters)
    \n
    \nIt works really, really well! Everyone becomes invested in each others' characters, people can use action points to communicate the direction they want their character to head in, and the feat vote provides a feel good moment for the group and can be held in between games on an off night.

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughtonhmmmm...I have an inconsistent player base so everything needs to happen in the same session. In theory I could do that on a forum afterwards, but I'm not sure that's the way to go either.
    \nGood point. If you want to do this every session, it sounds like you need a short in game solution. A vote may not be the way to go. You probably need something easily communicated without an actual discussion. In playstorming Soap Opera Violence, we've been using different group inspired character growth techniques. It's a very different setup than 3.5 but hopefully it may act as fuel for your own inspiration.

    In SOV, we have 2 types of scenes. Getting Over and Fighting. Getting Over means investing the audience (the other players) in your character. To get over, you narrate a scene, place your character sheet in the center of the table, everyone else grabs a d6, counts to 3, and then places a d6 on the trait they felt invested in or place a d6 on an empty trait slot. If placed on an empty trait slot, they fill in a new trait. Multiple d6s can be placed on the same trait.

    You could do something similar with feats. Breakdown feats into general categories that can be represented on a single sheet of paper. After a predetermined interval (scenes, encounters, sessions), every player places a marker on the category they felt best represented by the actions of the character in question. This could generate currency with which the character's player may spend to purchase a feat in those specific categories.
