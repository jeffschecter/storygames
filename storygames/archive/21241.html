\n This post is not about the game design scene.
It is about our extended community, above and beyond the confines of Story-Games, but also including it.

I want to open this thread not to get back at the Mods or the Users that flamed about my design article and closed the previous thread before I could even pitch in with a single reply. It would be petty and useless and a total waste of my time and energy. So please believe me when I say, it is not about that.

I want to open this thread because I see the incident with my article as a symptom of a more widespread behaviour that worries me. Both in this community, and on G+ and FB and across some blogs too.
I don't expect to change minds with this post. Of course. But I believe that positive change starts by giving voice to doubts and criticism, so that they can eventually be worked out. Maybe it is just me. Maybe someone else will find my words worth of consideration.
I hope for this post to start a civil conversation: agree, disagree, what could be done about it, etc.

I will start by taking a brief look at my "scandalous" design article, as a practical and safe example.
"Safe" for reason I'll explain towards the end of this post.

It should come as no surprise to anyone that has at least once attempted to express an idea through an article or blog post, that only through later discussion it will be easy to distil a long article into a clearer and more concise form... a nutshell... the article writer will/should try to do this to begin with, but in the end the whole article IS their way to express the idea as clearly as possible.

In my case, here it is:\n
Paul_T said:


I also think @ValyrianSteelKatana is quite right in that a lot more innovation might take place in the OSR world if people were more willing to experiment with new designs and shake off traditions. There is a lot happening already, but it tends to be small-scale rather than large-scale.

\nThis!
Bold highlights are mine.
The design article that had so many people enraged actually boiled down to THIS. Nothing more.
I expressed this observation, and tried to develop the argument to support such observation, so that readers might understand where I came from and agree or disagree on the merit of the discussion. In addition, I expressed perplexity that in all our indie scene there was not already more going on in this direction.

Now, while I am sure that my communication was far from perfect, I have the distinct feeling that very little charitable reading was applied in reading my article. Rather, effort was put into expressing one's individual rage and consternation is a civilised and proper way. Reading charitably and raging in a urbane way are not the same thing. And I'm seeing this happening more and more often in the widespread "indie" community.

It's a weird feeling.
Everyone talks about inclusiveness, safe spaces, mutual understanding.
And then?
Flaming, raging and rising shields in front of something just because it is presented with an imperfect writing tone/style ... no matter what the honest and genuine contents might be; no matter all the lines of text explicitly saying "Your fun is OK, your games are OK, I'm saying a different thing!".

In the past few years I believe I noticed two concurrent phenomenons feeding each other in having a negative effect on the community.

For one, people seem to be developing thinner skin.
Everyone is much more prone to be offended than before, content is becoming less important than proper tone, being critical of something is automatically seen badly and unless the form of this critique is flawless ... bam.

I get it. Internet has become an incredibly toxic environment over time, with trolls as kings of the hill.
But it's unthinkable to address this problem by reacting with force against anything that causes anyone to somehow feel offended or upset. Which in turn causes its own unique kind of peer pressure, mobbing and abuse. Safety from trolling and ostracising deviant opinions are not the same thing.

On one hand A LOT of honest and intelligent and valuable conversations happen outsides people's comfort zone, or go and poke at someone's beliefs and values.

On the other hand A LOT of shit and abuse can happen easily if people look mainly at the form, rather than at the substance, of things.

And this is the other problem.
People seem to be losing the ability to distinguish between a constructive critique and a trolling, or between a dissonant idea (an idea different than yours, an idea you disagree with) and a trolling, or between an earnest argument and an aggressive attack.

Sure, half of the responsibility lays on the shoulder of the one trying to communicate something. Charitable reading can not be an excuse for being carelessly rude, venomous or destructive.
But the way to face something expressed in a less than optimal way is to point these flaws out AND THEN go on discussing the content, not to read the flawed form AS the content.

This is not what I see happening.
And it's getting worse.
It is my personal perception of something that has been creeping up at the periphery of my senses in the past few years. Unfortunately I can only offer the incident with my aforementioned article as a safe example. I could mention a bunch of other instances, but I choose here not to because I don't want the topic to derail from the matter at hand towards the specific minutiae of those instances. They were hugely inflamed events that split hard the community and were never solved, simply lost momentum after enough time had passed, so I don't think it's worth to rekindle such embers.

Besides, it is not my aim here prove that what I say is right.
If my words make sense to you, good.
If they don't, maybe it's just me misunderstanding stuff and projecting my personal demons outwardly. Fair enough.
Either way I just felt important to express this.
And sorry for the long winded post.

    \n Yeah, this is a wider Internet culture thing. It's real, but probably not something that we can influence in any way except by being aware of it and trying to be better people individually. The sheer width of the open discourse spaces we deal with today does privilege all sorts of shouting, posturing, outrage, snap judgements and so on.

    The quick fix solution is to stop getting involved with Internet 2.0, of course. Would save us all a lot of headache, and perhaps remove one bad habit on the way of developing personal virtue. I already cut down a lot of my own engagement half a decade back, and think that the solution has been a net positive for me. Unfortunately this "brain drain" phenomenon where the people with a whit of sense or sensibility abandon the community does little to improve what remains [grin].

    Utopianism is another approach: start new discussion spaces and develop them into communities that uphold better values than the wider Internet. It's a lot of work, though, and although there is wide agreement that something's wrong (in comparison to our old-fashioned flesh relationships), everybody seems to have their own opinions on the particulars. Even in this practical case study half of the populace seems to think that you were in the wrong in posting a controversial opinion, while the other half thinks that moderation shouldn't be based on avoiding controversy. And that's just the big picture, I'm sure you'd find quite a range of specific opinions on how to run a forum if you went looking.

    \n I was kind of shocked that the prior thread got first a mod warning and was then shut down, without anything untoward actually having occurred. I'm pessimistically used to "tone policing" happening in progressive circles online, but not here. It appears that on a moderated forum there's no need to start a witch-hunt against someone expressing an unpopular opinion, since the mere potential for controversy can influence the mod to pre-emptively stop the discussion.     I am really uncomfortable with "______ is a historically terrible type of conversation" as a rationale for shutting down a conversation before anything terrible occurs. Especially given how subjective a decision this is. But doods, we are talking about fucking tabletop games here. And not even the full games, but the mechanics behind them. If dice-rolling and stat-allocating are widely seen as fraught topics that people could get hurt by, someone is taking this far too seriously. (And yes, I have been online for 35 years and totally understand that people do take things far too seriously. But using that as an excuse to shut down discussion is not the right response.)

    \nI wonder to what extent this "problem" has come into being because of new ways of communicating.

    For example, it's easy to start to think that "in the past, you could say anything you wanted! There was no language police!" However, sit down in a room with your grandparents for a few hours, and tell me that's still the case. Chances are that there are *all kinds* of unwritten rules about what you're allowed to say and what you're not allowed to bring up... it's just that you don't have to think about them very hard.

    It's just that our rules for what kind of communication is appropriate are much less useful in a format where all kinds of people from all over the world are suddenly talking to each other. Now, instead of being able to fairly unconsciously "know" what's inappropriate to say in front of a group of older people from a particular culture, we are often in environments where we have no idea who's listening or what their standards are.

    In places where people are willing to learn from the experience (like here!), I think that's generally a good thing. I am a big believer in freedom of speech and expression, but that doesn't mean there's nothing to be gained by learning how to present those same ideas in ways which are polite, proper, humble, and open-minded.

    I'm looking forward to seeing what the future brings - hopefully we'll all get better at this as time goes by.

    \n I'm glad to read this kind of replies.
    After writing this post I myself started digging around for some kind of info on the phenomenon I noticed and was surprised to find out that it was so widespread and virulent in many facets of our current society. It's comforting to see I'm not imagining things.

    One thing that pushed me to make this post in the first place was a totally unexpected consequence of my article. I made the same post both here on SG and on RPGnet. I expected to have a deeper design discussion here, and something more superficial and rougher on RPGnet.

    Imagine my surprise when here a flame exploded so fast and violent that the thread got closed, while on RPGnet (a bastion of "trad" gaming) people managed to discuss much more reasonably: sure a lot of people basically disagreed with my post (not all, though ) but no one felt offended, no one thought it was an inflammatory article, and actually it turned into a very interesting conversation about games I never heard of and about fringe elements of the OSR that are trying to innovate (although without really breaking the original mold).

    Same happened in other circles, like FB groups, were I actually cashed in positive comments along with the expected "you know nothing of OSR, Hasimir Snow!".

    So I was like: wait, what's happening on SG?!?! o_O

    This mixed bag of results gives me hope that, in the end, my article wasn't after all 99% noise.
    Many of you mention the title as inflammatory.
    But... seriously?
    "OSR design is stuck in the past ... and it's a pity"
    It sure is a strong statement.
    But... asserting that OSR design is not moving past its original roots should at most have people say "dah-ha it's what OSR is all about, what did you expect? Dummy!" which is by far and large what happened on RPGnet.
    But being offended by that?

    The article itself does NOT criticise people that play OSR, nor OSR design.
    It describes what everyone agrees to anyway: some games are just clones of the old originals, some games are more polished and have minor innovations but don't really break the original mold, some games celebrate the look of old dungeoneering but do a totally different thing.
    How this is offensive to people, is beyond me.

    And yes, of course, in so doing I mention WHY I find interesting the idea of pursuing old values through new methods.
    Of course I say that the old games had a primitive design. Because that is true, a fact, the first ever made rpg has to have a less mature, less aware, less polished design than whatever came later.
    Is this an insult to that game? No. It's a critical description.
    It doesn't mean that the game is crap. Or has no value.

    Somehow in a den of traditionalists and grognards this came through clearly enough that a fruitful conversation could be held.
    Somehow in a progressive, open minded, inclusive, safe community such as SG ... this lead to madness. I mean, some of our community wrote that they were barely able to contain their rage in front of the article I wrote.

    Man, who cares about my article anymore!
    This incident worries me. Probably because it is closer to "my reality" than whatever other people can say about the world as a general and vague concept.

    Tone policing, sure... we should all be nicer to each other. But there is a bigger problem here that I don't think can be solved just like this.
    And I don't know... CAN we do something to help the situation at least in this small piece of world that is SG?
    Do we even WANT to try and face/address this problem?
    I would like to, but I'm not in for a useless solo-crusade that nobody else feels matters.

    I don't know. What do you all think?

    This is all very understandable if one takes a cynical view on the nature of Internet discussion: what are we but apes shouting 'yea' or 'nay' in affirmation of tribal politics, amassing social capital? I believe that much of the "thin-skinnedness" present in modern Internet culture is about primal social instincts going on over-drive in the simplified virtual world. Humans are basically hard-wired to seek social capital (it makes us safe and happy), and the main ways you get that is by fawning on the elites and oppressing the outsiders. (The third method would be impressing others with the cut of your jib, but who's got the jib for that...) This means that anybody who makes themselves an "easy target" by saying something that others can confidently predict to be contrary to group-think will be attacked instinctively. The attack doesn't cost you social capital because the target is easy, and you reap the satisfaction of getting to act as part of the group in stoning the infidel. The group will obviously excuse the attack, for it was made in righteous anger, motivated by the desire to belong to the group and fight for it.
