Which other mid-life crisis games are there, apart from Apocalypse World?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2013 \n edited October 2013 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n For fortysomethings like myself, Apocalypse World is the ultimate mid-life crisis game. If you're the Hardholder, you own property and spend your time managing the people who live on it, often with violence. If you're the Chopper, you define yourself by a motorcycle; if you're the Driver, you define yourself by a car; if you're the Gunlugger, you define yourself by a gun. For those who wish they'd done something worthwhile with their lives, the Angel and Touchstone let you devote yourself to healing and a better future respectively. If you're the Quarantine, you get to lecture the young folks on how things used to be.

In any case, you spend the game in an end-of-the-world fantasy straight out of the 1960s, with endless casual sex.

Which other mid-life crisis games are there? Is there anything good I've missed? What, in these games, will feed into my mid-life crisis?

(Note: I trust the subject matter of this thread is sufficiently different from The Stages of Aging: Games for Grumpy Old Folks to warrant a separate thread. I didn't necessarily want to divert the conversation on that thread.)

    \n As someone who currently counts the time to their 40th birthday in weeks, "muddle aged men" is my new favorite phrase.

    Also, crisis is the basis of adventure, right? I prefer to think of it as a mid-life adventure!

    I guess in the realm of live combat boffer action games, the one I just created certainly caters more to mid-lifers than games I've created in the past.

    X-Command (inspired by the video games of similar name) features:
    - short combat missions about 5-7 minutes long,
    - lots of ducking behind cover while shooting Nerf guns (which, while a little hard on the knees, is less hard than 20 minutes of foam sword fighting footwork),
    - hyper-minimalist rules with hardly anything to remember, and
    - a short campaign with no need to attend every event (so it's fine if you miss game 3 because your daughter has a soccer tourney that weekend).

    So yeah, I guess I kind of unconsciously designed a game that is particularly well suited to the older end of the live combat crowd (but also enjoyed by the young'uns).
