    \n Being the player of the "thief with a heart of gold" Ryan mentions above, I'll weigh in.

    First off, I tend toward the good guy types. It's not uncommon I play the dark evil sorts, but as a rule, I tend to play the good guys. Which is why Omid (the thief) decided to help the executioner who was going to kill him, and stayed loyal to the mage who tried to hang him out to dry. When the game started, I thought I was toast, with many best interests turned against Omid. I was determined to go out as the victim of the wicked age, and instead, became a sort of minor hero.

    One thing that I think had a big effect on this was the Best Interests.. Or rather, that they don't appear to have any mechanical support. I'm not sure I like or dislike this. If they were mechanically supported so that we had to play to accomplish them, or had some encouragement to do so, things may have been very different. As it was, the events of the fiction as well as the vision of the character I had made me abandon one of them almost immediately into the game, and I think perhaps the same happened with Jahangir, the executioner. Whether this is a feature or a bug will really depend on whether you like the ability to reverse yourself so easily. Yes, Jahangir wanted to execute Omid, and Omid wanted to frame Jahangir for the burglary. By the end, it was pretty obvious that these had fallen by the wayside. Jahangir had attempted to be merciful and almost kind to the young man he was to execute, so in turn Omid didn't attempt to frame him. When Omid helped the blind Jahangir, his sense of obligation made him follow the young rogue without trying to catch and apprehend him.

    Anyway, brief impression of the game. I look forward to playing again, either as Omid, or as some other figure in the ongoing chronicles of this wicked age.

    \n I played a character who, by many lights, would've been a senior citizen who wanted to get with a 16 year old girl. Yet he had very high "for love" and "for others" and very low "for myself." His Best Interests had to do with protecting a nation and protecting the girl.

    I don't think he was an out-and-out villain, but he wasn't exactly a hero.

    The fact that you can do that kind of thing, that heroism and villainy are up for grabs, is one of the things I love about IAWA.

    \n One thing I noticed from our game is that we all stuck to characters directly implied in the oracle. Not a single character, PC or NPC, came from outside those implied by the oracle. Mind you, given that one of the lines of the oracle involved a "convocation of mages", that made it a little easier. My PC and Brennan's PC both came from the same line, and Ashur, a reoccurring character who came in for free because he was at the top of the Owe List, was fit into one of the implied mages, even. So a mage previously established to be at least a bit of a bastard, a criminal and a paid killer.

    Given that, I don't think we had any real idea of being good guys when we first began, though when I added "with a heart of gold" on to my description, that was the first step toward being good. Ryan also thought it foretold my doom.
