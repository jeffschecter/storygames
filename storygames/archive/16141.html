\n Has anybody translated MG into GM principles? I imagine it might look like:

On the GM turn, in order:
* Thwart the characters' goals
* Thwart the characters' beliefs
* Encourage the characters' instincts to get them into trouble
* Create missions that evolve from previous missions
* Create obstacles and twists involving animals, wilderness, weather, and mice
* Provide opportunities for characters to use their skills and traits
* Beat up the mice
* Use conflicts for important moments

On the player turn:
* Create twists that thwart the characters' beliefs and encourage their instincts to get them into trouble

If those were my principles, am I doing mouseguard right?

    \n A lot of these come off as way more adversarial ("thwart", "beat up") than my understanding of Mouse Guard provides. I also don't think I'd split up the goals, beliefs, and instincts, and I probably wouldn't put them in as principles (i.e. things that guide moment-to-moment play) since I only really worry about that stuff during mission creation. For me, MG is about creating a mission that will be interesting and will be a challenge, and then being a fan of the players if and when they overcome it. My take on the player's turn is that I'm almost completely hands-off as a GM, I just call for rolls when the fiction demands it, setting the Obs that the rules demand, and playing the NPCs as "realistically" as possible given what I know about their personalities, etc. I don't intentionally inject new fiction during the player's turn.

    \n Not exactly principles, but...

    On your turn:
    *Give the patrol a dangerous, urgent or serious mission or an innocent one that turns out to be one of the above.
    *Present obstacles, offer solutions.
    *Play nature, animals, weasels and other mice passionately.
    *Make the players' characters heroes by giving them chances to fight for what they believe.
    *Challenge their Beliefs, and give them opportunities to bring their Instincts and Traits into play.
    *Drive play towards a safe spot or conclusion of the mission.

    On their turn:
    *Sit back, let them do what they want.
    *Portray the NPCs with gusto.
    *Manage rolls where needed.
    *Wrap up when they're done.

    \n Great stuff here. Completely agreed on the importance of writing a good mission ahead of time that challenges beliefs and instincts.

    I would just add that along with being gatekeeper to the fiction, you are also gate keeper to the mechanics. It's important to remind players of how to gain checks, how to properly spend persona, tax their nature and so forth.

    Along those lines, during the GM turn, enforce the rule (through the fiction) for spending checks on player turn actions. Keep up the sense of urgency and immediacy, etc.

    \n It occurred to me I can look through the text for the circle icons - it's basically the same as Gregor+Johnzo -

    * Challenging the players' Goals is your prime job as GM. (p. 46, 55)
    Transform the players' guardmice characters into heroes, by challenging the players with obstacles set in their path (p. 8)
    * Keep with the mood of Mouse Guard as a whole (p. 14)
    * Create missions that develop organically from the situation of the patrol (p. 57) [The ** ones below are mission creation guidelines]
    ** Challenge the patrol with weather, wilderness, animals, and mice (p. 61, 64)
    ** Pick two hazards as known problems and keep the other two hazards in reserve (p. 61)
    ** Challenge beliefs (p. 44, 55, 62)
    ** Put the player in situations that trigger his instinct (p. 48, 55, 63)
    ** Play goals against beliefs (p. 63)
    ** Threaten relationships (p. 63)
    * If you're playing a session that will represent a whole season, use your twists to create long term problems (especially in players' turn) (p. 72, 75)
    * Put the character in situations where the reputation of the Guard is at stake (p. 44)
    * Don't repeat the same situation twice. (p. 91)
    * If you're feeling generous, give the player what he was after but give him a condition (p. 92 ) [I read that as: a twist should be your default and the condition a fallback. Agree?]
    * Arbitrate the use of wises (p. 92)
    * As the action in the game builds to a head, call for a conflict (p. 96)
    * When everyone is excited and shouting, stop the action and ask everyone to be quiet. Then go around the table asking each player what they are doing and why (p. 132)
    * Present a rich, colorful, and consistent world to the players through vivid descriptions and by bringing to life engaging characters, by imagining their belief, goal and instinct and keeping that in mind as you play them. (p. 133)

    On the players turn:

    * If you're playing a session that will represent a whole season, use your twists to create long term problems (especially in players' turn) (p. 72, 75)
    * Prompt the players with the rules (p. 76)
    * Play the Relationships and Characters (p.76)
    * If the players engage each other, use a character to play into the situation. (p. 76)
    * Offer advice on the rules (p. 77)
