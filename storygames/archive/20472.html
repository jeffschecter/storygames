\n So I recently watched the film Into the Heart of the Sea, which is a sort-of Moby Dick like tale. It really piqued my curiosity on how to run such a scenario on the tabletop. I wanted to find a system (or at least a basis for building my own) that could emulate a group of specialists, all maneuvering around a deadly creature, to line up a deadly killing strike.

Some highlighted themes I seem to find were: a need for trust between individuals (and the lack-there-of bringing tension), team-work (everyone has to essentially be at the right place, the right time, and doing their job - all the elements must align), and then threat of things becoming so deadly so quickly.

Any ideas?

    \n So my game Utpost (freely availible but only in Swedish) is about an isolated group of people on an outpost in space. It's full of dysfunctional relationships and people getting on each others' nerves. There's a central gauge called Friction which measures the atmosphere on the outpost. Friction goes up when people are fighting each other, and it goes down when people bury the hatchet, say sorry and open up to each other (which is mechanically scary to do, since it leaves you very vulnerable if the other person doesn't accept your apology). High Friction makes it difficult to cooperate, and there's a growing danger threatening the outpost. As it gets bigger, it becomes impossible for people to win against it by themselves and they need to cooperate, which means solving their differences. Basically, you play to find out if they manage to overcome their issues and stand united or if they fail and are overcome by the threat.

    This isn't necessarily whaling, as the htreat can be anything from zombie hordes to earthquakes, but it does kinda do "a need for trust between individuals (and the lack-there-of bringing tension)" and "team-work (everyone has to essentially be at the right place, the right time, and doing their job - all the elements must align)". I could expand on the mechanics, if you're interested.

    \n Aside from games with specific mechanics to create tension on the relationships between PCs (which I find a cool take on this) I believe the basics of specialists teamwork can be achieved in any game using a more skill-oriented version of the Unique idea from 13th Age. Basically, after players pick their characters but before they assign their skills, state as a requisite that every character should have a clear role in the group recognized by all the PCs, one that is crucial in any battle. Then decide how their usual battle plan would go and the order in which they will take their actions, foregoing rolling initiative order except to decide if the players or the antagonist go first.

    Let's try with D&D. Players decide their battle plan would go as this: the ranger goes first drawing the enemy's attention. Fighter goes second charging to meet the enemy and preventing them to reach the back ranks. Then goes the paladin either protecting the casters, helping the fighter or buffing up to for a big hit the next round. Wizard's turn then to figure out any weakness of the opponent if the ranger hasn't found any, and then apply the proper buff to the right party member for the job.

    So basically players can develop any battle plan, the GM uses this forced initiative order to help them stick to it; but yet anyone can easily see how there will still be many variations on this, how opportunities can change it slightly and bad rolls can wreck it (though you can give one re-roll or advantage to a single roll in the encounter or turn, that any player can use. Of course, that applies only each time the party goes for a battle plan they have practiced enough and are all familiar with)

    Going against many opponents with different initiatives? assign a fixed turn interval between each player. Say, PC with highest initiative rolls a 16. We arbitrarily make the interval to be 4. That means the next player acts in initiative count 12, the next at 8 and the last at 4. You roll for the NPCs and assign them to the right initiative count. You can find a more fair interval by comparing the PCs with the highest and lowest Dex and dividing the difference by the number of players.

    \n I'll speak to the part of the whale-killing scenario that interests me, which is the tactical hurdles, not the trust issues.

    I would be inclined to view the whale-killing as a Project, which has certain conditions which must be met. These conditions could be defined by the GM, or they could emerge from player deduction/investigation/assessment/strategy rolls (e.g. worse roll = more conditions).

    The players would then have to sort out whose job is to fulfill which condition, and then everyone would have to perform their part successfully. If time-sensitivity is an issue, the whale could get rolls to undo established conditions, giving the players an incentive to try to get everything as close to simultaneous as possible (while knowing that if 2 succeed and 1 fails, those 2 have to hold on against the whale's resistance while the other 1 tries again).

    Once all the conditions are met, then the harpoonist either completes the killing blow, or rolls to do so; whichever better fits your vision. Personally, I wouldn't roll for success (the whale is distracted and immobilized! yes, you kill it!) but I might roll for collateral damage or other secondary outcomes.

    The relevant system, then, would be one which establishes good conditions (or helps the GM or players do so) and provides satisfying arbitration (skill rolls, etc.) for the characters' attempts to achieve those conditions, with interesting results for failures (e.g. "try again at greater risk").

    I bet we could build that right here in this thread if you wanted to.

    As for published games, I second BPG's notion of how you could do it with Fate and W-Monk's notion of how you could do it with a specialized-skill party.

    \n First, people working as a team to murder a huge creature (aka the "dragon fight"): why not some iteration of D&D, specifically? Answering this may help you pick from the many suggestions you got already.
    Additionally, one more game which focuses very much on this, and I can't see mentioned in this thread, is Mythender.

    Second, trust between team-members as an issue: most AW-derivatives have mechanics for this, owing to Hx rules in AW, which in turn are based on The Mountain Witch. Malcolm Craig's games (Cold City and Hot War) are also descended from TMW.
    Ultimately, before you choose or design your own system for this, you need to decide how much the issue of trust and betrayal is genuine, central and plot-driving: in TMW it's definitely the core everything else revolves around, and in AW it's pretty close to it, whereas for example in Dungeon World it's reduced to more of a characterization thing \u2014 with friendly banter and a pretense of rivalry being standard, but actual betrayal being off the table.\n
    Definitely take a look at Anima Prime : http://www.animaprimerpg.com/main/
    \nI'd definitely second that if the OP's looking for something on the "DW" end of the trust-and-betrayal spectrum. AP has it built in for the main characters to bicker mid-battle for re-rolls and mechanical goodies (see Traits), but definitely cannot deal with actual sabotage or betrayal except by "splitting the party" or retiring a character. I suggest, though, that AP battles against one powerful individual aren't as interesting as battles against one powerful individual with adequate minions: for a dragon/whale scenario I recommend going with the (by the book) suggestion of statting the monster up as multiple individuals, like your average computer game boss monster of the bitmap era.

    \nI know nothing about fun whale-hunts, but I can make some guesses based on fictional fights with dragons.

    Necessary conditions:
    - Whale exposed. Where it normally resides, it's hard to get at. Force/bait/enrage it into coming to where we can get at it.
    - Whale depleted. Normally, this creature moves quickly and powerfully, and it's tough to do anything against it with any sort of precision. Before we can hope to get in a kill shot, we need to weaken it and slow it down. We could go for exhaustion, poison, blood loss, or something else.
    - Whale in vulnerable momentary position. Sometimes you've got to shoot a big ol' creature in just the right spot, and you can't do that unless the right spot is facing you. Unlike the two previous conditions, which will usually persist for a good chunk of time once achieved, this one could possibly be fleeting.
    - Clear! Anyone who's obstructing the kill shot needs to get out of the way!
    - Steady! The shooter's perch can't be shaking around enough to throw off the shot.

    Those are the first 5 things which come to mind, and I think they imply several potential character skill sets.

    Character skill sets:
    - monster behaviorist/psychologist
    - prey/bait -- run away from monster and tire it out without getting themselves caught and killed in the process
    - damage-dealers -- snipers, slashers, poisoners, etc.
    - rope expert to catch hold of the monster
    - strong people to yank the caught monster into the right position
    - master of pyrotechnics or other distractions/attention-getters
    - kill-shooter
    - fast or steady transportation for anyone else
    - danger-spotter and bailout for everyone else
    - first-aid for everyone else

    Useful specific skills:
    - perceptive, insightful, knowledgable re: monster
    - fast, agile, deceptive
    - fearless
    - dexterous, perfect timing
    - strong
    - alert, great reflexes
    - weapon-trained, rope-trained, vehicle-trained, medic-trained, pyro(etc.)-trained
