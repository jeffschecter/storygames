\n I'm working on creating a new setting, a world that's being invaded by a "glitchworld", a sort of other dimensional nightmares that pop up as both as monsters and locations.

In these newly opened dungeons (but they still can look old because they are old in the "glitchworld") most of the coins you find is from the glitchworld and is recognizable as such.

They don't dissappear or anything like that, it's just that they're recognizable as being part of this corrupting extradimensional presence.

In the setting, I've replaced the nine alignments with three: Nature, The People, and Glitchworld.

I've decided some people, even People-aligned, will accept glitchworld currency because hey, it's money. (It's not that logical from an economical standpoint because the metal is slightly weird and it's not the local tender, but there is like a black market that can use it.) And some will be angry.

So the trigger for the move is "When you offer glitchworld currency, roll [????]. ???"

We don't typically roll for social situations (we're moving more and more to a DramaSystem/D&D 5e mashup). The move is supposed more of a randomizer of the particular merchant's pre-disposed attitude -- and instinctual reaction -- to the glitchworld money. It's like "frowned upon" but it's still money. It should be risky to offer it I think.

    \n Unless you wish to apply some character skills (for persuasion, I'd presume) to the process, it'd be a straight roll, no +, right? Your stats have no bearing on someone else's pre-disposition.

    As far as outcomes, I'd guess a tier system like this (in more concise fashion) would work:

    10+ This person values glitchworld money for its relative scarcity, thinking that once the public loses their fear of it, it'll be worth more than gold! They are unable to hide their zeal, and you negotiate to pay half-price (relative to normal currency).

    7-9 This person would rather be paid in normal currency, but will accept your glitchworld stuff (for a slight markup if you track currency closely; otherwise, never mind). Once the transaction is done, however, they'll take steps to ensure that they or their area aren't seen as a glitchworld-friendly place of business, as they don't wish customers like you to scare away their regulars. They'll rat you out to whoever's most convenient.

    6- This person thinks that using glitchworld stuff makes you an enemy of the people. They will immediately attack if their odds of defeating you are plausible; otherwise, they will immediately seek out someone else who will take you down.

    I dunno how valuable this type of specificity is, though, when you could just use AW's Seduce/Manipulate to resolve whether you get what you want and how costly it is, and then color in the details on the fly as befits the particular glitchworld dynamics you're aware of.

    \n When you offer glitchcoin for a purchase, roll. If you've had a successful transaction with this person before, take +1. If you offer something to sweeten the pot, take +1. If you've got a bad reputation around here, take -1. On a 10+, they take the deal and keep their mouth shut. On a 7-9, the GM will tell you one:
    * The exchange rate they offer is atrocious
    * They'll tell you not to come back with this junk
    * Word's gonna get out you're dealing in it
    * They make you swear to keep it quiet, or else...

    On a 6-, the GM will tell you one or more of the above, or tell you they won't take the deal, or tell you they turn on you.

    How would I as GM choose between those four?
    \nGoing by the typical Powered-by-the-Apocalypse, the general rule is that the MC picks whichever one makes the most sense and conforms to the MC moves.

    For you, given your recent discussion, you may want something more constrained. Here's a procedure to get a random result off a list where some might not be applicable and you don't want to repeat:

    Given a list of random items, roll 1d6. Count down the list, skipping items that don't make sense for these circumstances and crossed off items. Perform the result and cross off the result. When all the items are crossed off (or otherwise invalid) erase the marks and start over from the original list.

    \n I mean you could just go the old "random table" route and make a list of plausible results, then roll.

    When you player barters using glitchcoin and you're not sure of the other party's disposition, roll 1d20. If the result is lower than the lower of the player's wisdom or charisma score, use that value instead.
    1-6: They immediately lash out at you, feeling insulted, betrayed, or angry.
    7-10: They take the deal, but they'll rat you out if they get the chance.
    11-13: They'll take the deal, but they won't keep their mouth shut about it.
    14-16: They'll take the deal and keep quiet, but the exchange rate is horrible.
    17-18: They'll take the deal and keep quiet.
    19-20: They'll take the deal, keep quiet, and offer something extra.
    21+: They'll take the deal, keep quiet, and offer something extra. They'll also get in touch if they hear of a good deal in the future, and they'll come to you first.

    (This assumes a score range of typical D&D, but massage as needed...)

    You can flesh out with more entries but I'm doing this quick so it's just a brainstorm. Sorry mate
