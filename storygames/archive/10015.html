    \n OK, I think one issue is that we have people answering two different questions:

    Some people are answering the question:

    "Can doing something creative -- such as story games -- help you be more creative?"

    Most people answering this question say yes.

    Others are answering the question:

    "Are story games a magical thing that has unique powers to make me creative, which no other pursuit can match?"

    Most people answering this question say no.

    So the real disagreement here is about how to interpret the question, not about the answer to the question. You can't agree *or* disagree about an answer when you can't agree how to interpret the question.

    There is at least one additional issue...

    If you reduced the question to:

    "Can *anything* help you be more creative?" you would find a few people saying "no," for various reasons of substance or interpretation, and those people a fortiori will answer "no" to either of the interpretations above.

    \n If you haven't expressed, you haven't created. Having a lot of new ideas isn't sufficient. Ideas are not something you create, but something you become or experience. You can't create yourself. Anything you create must extend outside of yourself, so there is no "creativity" stat prior to expression. In that sense, a game may help you be more creative by providing methods of expression. And playing the game may help you be more creative by helping you to discover and internalize those methods and by motivating you to express.

    On the other hand, if "creativity" is just a usage meaning the after-the-fact social value of your creations, then whether a game helps you be more creative depends on whether or not it rewards you for appreciating the values of others that you don't already have yourself. Probably most games do this except possibly some solo games designed by yourself for your play only.

    Of course, for both of these usages of "creativity," there is a question of whether or not any particular game session is momentarily the best way to become more creativity. But that's aside from whether or not they do so in an absolute sense.

    If I understand you correctly, then I disagree with this. Have you read Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi? He is one of the most famous scholars in the field of creativity. He wrote the "Flow" books, in which he expresses a similar idea: that creativity only exists externally, in the way that it impacts society, and that therefore it is society which judges whether or not an individual/work is creative. His focus, therefore, is on "Big C" creativity, or people who make a major impact on the world of idea.

    It seems to me that the effects of personal creativity are pervasive and impossible to track. If I live a creative life, even just internally, that will implicitly change how I think, how I speak, how I act, how I vote. It will change the way I interact with others even when I'm not engaging in explicitly creative pursuits. If I "create myself", the results of my changes may have profound effects on the world. It is not difficult to imagine a butterfly-effect type of phenomenon rippling out from such personal creativity. This is not particularly a solution for me, as I am an externally creative person as well as an internally creative person, but this does apply to, for instance, my father. His life and those of his family has been greatly improved by his embrace of internal creativity as he's gotten older, even though he still lacks the skill and confidence to create external product.

    That being said, the book "Creativity: Flow and the Psychology of Discovery of Invention was profoundly personally meaningful to me and I highly recommend it to creators of all kinds.
