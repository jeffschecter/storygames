In Jon's combat, he was hustling for every bonus dice he could find--and as a result, the combat between Nimrote the Hunter and Thak the Four-Armed Albino Primite ended up being a titanic battle of wills as Nimrote was forced to reevaluate his past actions, drawing strength from his sinful pride.     \n I'll vote for Sorverer too. No long lists of maneuvers there -- you build rollovers based on what the player describes. So a Sorcerer exchange might start
    \n
    \n"I charge and try to impale him, I mean real fast. My Stamina descriptor is Vigorous."
    \n
    \nGM: "He's cutting at you. Are we all happy with our declarations? Okay. Let's resolve action, but first, I'm going to say we roll Stamina vs Stamina before the action resolution to see if that gives you an advantage."
    \n
    \nRoll. "Cool, I get two extra dice."
    \n
    \nGM: "You can describe how they carry over into your next roll or hang onto them to adjust the result afterwards."
    \n
    \n"Ok, I charge in and catch him while he's on the upswing. I'll add my two dice now."
    \n
    \nGM: "I'll give you an extra one for coolness."
    \n
    \nThe main roll is then resolved. If the player wins, he can describe how he exploited his advantage. If he fails, the group could describe how the NPC outfoxed his attack--and even set those dice up for carryover!
    \n
    \n==========
    \n
    \nThat's a basic example of rollover in Sorcerer. There's also an example in the 3rd suppliment Sex and Sorcery of creating styles of martial arts with chained maneuvers.

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton
    I'm looking for combat that is about chaining attacks, countering and ripostes, leadership, audacity, willpower, teamwork, distraction, focus, form, skill, mobility, awareness, the rock-paper-scissors of fighting styles, that's sort of thing.
    \nSorcerer is BUILT for this. However, it uses one single unified mechanic. Basically this:

    1) Everyone announces what they want to achieve this exchange be that an attack, a taunt, a distraction, whatever.
    2) Everyone rolls dice simultaneously Stamina for physical actions, Will for social/mental ones.
    3) Actions are resolved from single highest die to lowest.
    4) Targets of an action who have not yet gone have the option of giving up their declared action or defending with just one die. If they've already gone or they give up their action they defend with full dice.
    5) Those that succeed in their actions can carry their victory dice over into their action on the next exchange.

    That's basically it. ALL actions work that way. So you taunt your opponent which gives you victory dice on your sudden lunge but he successfully parries which gives him victory dice on his riposte but you counter with a punch to the face which yeilds penalties to him for when you try to kick him to the ground and so on......

    What actions chain into what other actions are all in how actions are announced and the logistics of the situation at the end of an exchange. No special rules. Just the steps listed above.

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonWhat I was hoping for was something that would encourage players to approach combat creatively and hit those kinds of buttons while doing so.
    \nIn that case, I would say HeroQuest. Or perhaps more accurately, HeroQuest with the chained conflicts from Mystic Russia. Because at default, the simple conflicts are resolved in a single roll, so there's not a whole lot of complexity within a conflict, unless you really play up the augments (using mutliple abilities in a conflict to improve your primary ability) but players can augment each other and with the right abilities, all that flavor you want can be right there. As in, "I manuever to knock him off his feet, giving him a temporary aspect 'Stumbling'" and then the next player can tag that for a bonus. You can really promote teamwork and countering and chained attacks, and it's all on the fly so there's no charts to memorize.

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonI'm still not sure about the merits of BW, Sorcerer, Beast Hunter, Riddle of Steel. ... What I was hoping for was something that would encourage players to approach combat creatively and hit those kinds of buttons while doing so.
    \nThere's my cue

    Beast Hunters does this by rewarding creativity in actions all the time. When a player makes a maneuver against an opponent, she describes tactics, use of environment, passions, cool little details, and so on. The Challenger then evaluates the action and offers a certain number of advantage points. The player can take those and successfully complete the action, or roll the dice for potentially higher gain, but also potentially no gain at all.

    Ryan Macklin just talked about how this promotes a certain style of action combat on his Master Plan podcast, Episode 18.

    Creative tactics is exactly what I was going for with the game. The narrative counts, dice are a fallback mechanism. Stepping up by wowing the other players with your creativity, ingenuity, and handle on what they're into is what the game is all about.

    I know this is going to sound cheesy and self-interested, but for combat I think that base system for Aeternal Legends would work, since it's designed for tactically-involved combat without the rules totally dictating look and feel. Basically I wanted the feel of maneuver-based systems without separate rules for every little thing. The Wits, Will and Narrative Bonus layers suit this nicely.

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonCan Bronze be played GMless?
    \nI don't know. In playtest, the referee's workload was pretty minimal, as I let players create most of their own conflicts, only introducing NPC or environmental conflicts when it seemed necessary to spice things up. It worked out well. But the referee acting as a player (i.e., controlling a single character and having the same rights/controls as all other players)? Uhm. . . I don't know.

    I think the best way to try this might be to expand the role of the referee to encompass all other players, rather than trying to eliminate it. That is, treat all players as referees, rather than trying to strip the referee down to being a standard player. Some of the referee privelages really make the game run. Distributing them equally shouldn't stop that but eliminating the altogether probably would.

    The only danger I see in this approach is that play will ostensibly be much less ordered, to the point that it might fall apart due to confusion about who has control over what and, subsequently, what kind of 'moves' are allowed.
