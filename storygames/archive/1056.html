    three of my favorite games are L5R, Nyambe, and Deadlands. I love these games because they have strikingly rich settings full of flavor, history, and story. I rarel if ever get to play these games, and when i do i'm often dissatisfied. You know why? Because in order for a play group to really utilize these games fully, everyone has to be up to speed on the content. You gotta read the book. Maybe if everyone lived in their moms basement we would all have time to read 250 pages of story, but in real world thats too much of an imposition. This is why i'm not playing the new Vampire, that book is fucking dense, and so tightly nuanced if you don't know it all the game gets boreing fast.

    Now a lot of indie games tackel this by going light on setting, thats fine. But there are other methods. I cite Luke's new Burning Empire's as a startelingly brilliant example of using art and "atmosphere" to convey important game information. The setting of BE is a deep and interesting one with a lot of facets. Rather then spend big chapters discussing it, that information comes out in things like lifepaths and skills and most importantly, the art. Without the art the game wouldn't have any flavor, any color. I attempt to a similar thing with my game Primitive. Luke is very good at this technique, but he is far from the first to attempt it.

    Art/Design is critically important. It doesn't just sell books, it helps you read and understand them. A picture is worth 1000 words right? Well elegant design is worth even more then that. All human language developed from pictographic interpretation, the letters of the major world languages are all basterdized adapted modified pictographic representations. Pictures and ideas are permanently welded together. To seperate them is possable, but doing so hamstrings both sides. There is a reason textbooks have pictures.

    Images, millions of images. Thats what we eat.

    I think there's something else worth questioning here: who are these people who are blowing everything out of proportion, perhaps pretending to be offended, deceiving themselves, etc? Are they a big problem here?

    Just looking at the quotes that Stuart pulled, I don't see anyone particularly up in arms, or acting especially huffy and offended by anything, or blowing things out of proportion. It's more people saying stuff like, "If game companies don't do a woman-friendly production, no wonder women don't buy in much" and "White Wolf showed that they were trying to take women into account. And that got me interested." So I guess the more constructive thing to do is actually address the stuff that's there, rather than the mysterious, hypothetical threat of hysterically overreacting will o' the wisps. I admit I haven't read all the quotes in the dissertation, just the stuff in this thread; so maybe there are some people like that in there. But I think we're wandering into the danger of just constructing an imaginary group and then arguing about why they are or aren't right.

    The concrete quotes, and the earlier reactions to them in the thread, are probably more useful to focus on. And turning back to the issue of appearances, some folks are indicating in the quotes that the visual representations matter to them: that visual representations in RPGs affect how these folks talk about RPGs, how they make purchasing decisions, and perhaps also how they play.

    Should they? Is that a reasonable way to make these decisions?

    I'm with the "visuals matter" folks on this one. Hell, I'll go you one more: I think the visuals are part of the substance of the books. They're not just "gloss" and "style." They're substantive and meaningful parts of how the book represents its subject matter. I think this both in general terms as well as in the particular case of sex, which can be done well and can be done badly. The images on the cover of an RPG, and the images inside, represent the game. The written words don't do that work alone. And what's more, the visual representations didn't simply wander by, trip, and fall onto the book. There was a design process of some kind involved, choices to be made, etc. Visual design and artwork presents and represents the game, and it contributes to the pool of resources available to players when they're actually playing the game. Some folks will make more and some less use of these visual influences in actual gameplay, but I don't think you can simply argue that visuals are (or should be) just irrelevant, or that they're just not substantial. You can argue it -- but I don't think we can just take it for granted. (And this is somewhat orthogonal to Mike's points, which I happen to find pretty convincing as well.)

    I'll stop now and hold back for a bit, because I'm conscious of the danger of this becoming just a big dogpile on you, Thunder_God.
