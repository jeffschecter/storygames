\n Today I ran my first-ever session of IAWA. I only had two players, but they are two of my best. We used the God-Kings of War oracle, and ended up with a basic setting wherein during the aftermath of a major battle, the losing army was left to pick themselves back up, and they were visited by three mystics: from the east, two of the giant female warrior-monks of the red-robed Order of the Eastern Tower, and from the west, the famed Spirit-talker Kalashman-Enlil, keeper of hunting birds.

My PCs were the ppirit-talker and a veteran of the losing side, who, having recovered from a seemingly-fatal wound, was beginning to wonder just how human he was. My major NPCs were the two monks (one older, one younger), a berserker veteran of many wars who had grown tired of battle (and who had killed the old God-Emperor), a captain of the PC veteran's unit, a ghost from that same unit, and the Spirit of Carnage that had infected the berserker.

My major conflicts --arising from best interests -- were: 1) that the spirit-talker wanted to deliver a message to the elder monk, but the younger monk wanted to prevent that; 2) the spirit-talker wanted to put the spirits of the dead to rest; 3) the veteran wanted to kill the berserker for betraying them in battle, and the berserker wanted to kill the veteran for witnessing his shame (whatever that meant, precisely), and the captain wanted to keep the berserker alive; 4) the veteran wanted to find out more about how magic had been responsible for his side's loss; 5) the older monk wanted to kill the "unknowing prince", who I had originally intended on being the veteran soldier, but it got away from me mid-play; 5) the berserker, driven by the carnage demon, wanted to kill the spirit-talker.

So, oracle-consulting and character creation went smoothly, but then once we got down to actual play, I had a hard time framing scenes to result in conflict. Even though many of the characters had opposing best interests, I just found it difficult to put those characters together and shake them up enough to enter conflict. I was also unclear on how to force conflict that wasn't specifically violent. Is it fair game to say "I try to convince you to help me protect these people" and then roll, say, Covertly and For others? Also, can you change forms in the middl eof a conflict, or are you stuck with the ones you picked at the outset? If you lose the conflict, can you "escalate" DitV-style?

Overall, we had a great time with the game, I just felt that it was a lot harder to drive things to conflict, unlike DitV, which tends to drop conflict like leaves in September.

    \n "Convincing" is too broad. Say "Okay, so what do you DO" over and over until someone says "like hell you do that", then it's a conflict.

    "I say 'don't you see, you have to help me protect these people.'"

    "Hm, interesting, my guy says 'nope.'"

    "I say 'It's really important!'"

    "My guy just looks at you."

    "I grab you! 'Don't you get it?!?'"

    "Wait, like hell you grab me!"

    Conflict.

    The main way to get people into conflict is to have opposing best interests and put them in a situation where someone is pursuing it. Also, start with a scene with everyone in one place. And not every scene needs a conflict.

    \n I've had similar frustrations driving towards conflict when running IAWA. My players and I are more used to traditional games where the GM is creating adversity (and a storyline) for PCs that are more or less cooperative. So as characters get created, the players aren't thinking about building in conflict, especially with each other. As a result I find myself creating NPCs who will both create conflict and somehow tie the PC's stories together, which in turns reinforces the PCs cooperate/NPCs create adversity/GM's responsible for the narrative habits. In the end, the game felt a little flat because the PCs didn't have much reason to interact other than having NPC adversaries in common. After we debriefed our last session, one of the players suggested that next time I be a lot more explicit about what kind of PC creation is needed to drive the game.

    While reading the original post, I found myself wondering what brought the spirit-talker and the veteran into conflict, or if not outright conflict, then some sort of tension.

    I find it easier to wrap my head around how conflicts apply to non-physical situations if I parallel physical and non-physical conflicts with physical and emotional abuse. When the verb changes from convince or persuade to browbeat or guilt-trip, the dice come out. So instead of "I convince you to help me protect these people" it becomes "I make you feel like a creep for not protecting these people" or "I publicly shame you for not helping these people".

    \n Hi Adam,

    My first couple IAWA games were the same. Fun times, but not many conflicts.

    One thing that helped me in subsequent games was to be explicit about the conflict requirements with all the players up front. "Hey, remember .. in this game, we ONLY go to the dice when two characters are in a direct, physical confrontation!"

    If everyone is mindful of that, it helps lead you to more conflicts.

    It also leads to a more dramatic, fast-paced game (if that's what you're after.)

    \n When I run IAWA, as a general rule I try to never create more (named) NPCs than there are PCs, and to keep the NPCs' best interests mostly focused toward the PCs. If you have 5 or six players, you may not even need NPCs. When NPCs come into conflict with each other, make sure it happens 'on camera' with the PCs right in the middle.
    \n
    \nDuring character creation with new players, you really have to be explicit and tell them that their best interests should be a *direct attack* against another named character. By direct attack, I mean you want to do something to them, or want something from them, that they cannot ignore. The first time or two someone plays, require every best interest to be an attack on someone else. All of the characters should start within easy walking distance of everyone else.
    \n
    \nBut as others have said, not every scene needs a conflict, especially in the first half of the session.

    \n I think you're on the right track with your NPCs. I've found that I need to:

    1. Make sure my PC has strong feelings toward an NPC
    2. Have another PC or an NPC directly do something to that first NPC that my PC wouldn't want to happen

    Good strong feelings: love, lust, revenge, hatred, fear, jealousy...

    Direct PC-v-PC conflicts are good, too, but I think NPCs that PCs feel very strongly about are necessary story fodder for IAWA. I see you fared better with DITV -- think about how you create strong NPCs there, through town creation, and how the PCs directly and passionately interact with those (and the most intense parts come about when the PCs disagree about how to deal with them, which is partially built to emerge from the tension between duty (judgment) and caring (frex, family relationships--which you create in play using unassigned relationship dice)).

    \n I've found that IaWA seems to work best with everyone being strongly proactive. When the spotlight's on you (whether as a PC who's just been asked "what do you do?", or as a GM describing the actions of an NPC), you've got to jump on it right away and DO SOMETHING.

    I think telling people to remember that you're producing "short stories" is a good thing. Those stories don't have a lot of exposition or character-building moments. They open with the action right away (though action doesn't necessarily mean violence or conflict, just meaningful interactions between the major players in the story). So, if one character wants to marry another character's daughter, the very first scene should be something like that character coming to the father and asking for his daughter's hand in marriage. Or maybe climbing in through the window to kidnap her. Something like that.

    And then "fade out" on the chapter earlier rather than later. Let later chapters fill in the gaps.

    By the way, some people here seem to be saying that they're switching up dice from round to round in an action sequence. I'm no IaWA expert, but I'm pretty sure that the intent of the rules is that you pick up your dice at the beginning and you keep rolling those same dice until it's through. (Advantage dice, of course, will come and go, however.) After the first round, you're not limited to narrate within the forms you chose, but you DO stick with the same dice. That's an important feature of the rules when it comes to how the Owe List works, I think.

    Anyone care to deny or confirm that, just in case I'm misremembering?

    \n I've found Wicked Age a good learning tool for me.

    See, I have this problem as a GM. I call it "reasonable NPC disorder." I often end up making NPCs who, however bad ass they may be, are willing to listen to reason and at least attempt to negotiate. This is fine for some styles of games, but often ends up letting the PCs talk themselves out of drama.

    In IAWA, I found that when I did that the game didn't go so well. So I started making NPCs who would stab you in the face for your shoes. Or, as Christian suggested, who would stab another NPC who you liked in the face for their shoes, and then taunt you about wearing the shoes of your dead friend. And while it isn't all literally face stabbing and shoe stealing, the NPCs are often aggressive, motivated to do harm, and active in going after what they want as often as possible while preventing themselves from being stabbed in the face.

    And that right there was the difference, for me, between dry conflicts that just exist to cause conflicts and conflicts that made shit explode: NPCs who had goals that they were aggressively pursuing, and who telegraphed their pursuit. I often have initial setup scenes with NPCs who I'm going to be stealing shoes with in which they show what sorts of folks they're going to be without necessarily initiating a conflict in that scene. I establish them as aggressive, willing to cause harm (for good reasons or bad), and actively moving towards a goal. Then when later in the game that goal comes up, and the NPC is all with the knife and the "give me your shoes bitch" everyone knows where it is coming from. It isn't a sardoodledom moment of the plot/desire for some dice to roll makes me force a conflict. It is a moment when an aggressive, goal oriented NPC who we've seen being motivated steps up and starts acting like the raging asshole swords and sorcery badass they are.

    Its probably also worth noting that this was a bit of a shift for some of the folks I usually play with, who were more used to being able to be the ones who initiated shit. When all of a sudden they couldn't hang back and bide their time, talk the reasonable NPCs down, and realized that they were going to lose their shoes right fucking now if they didn't get with the face stabby, it caused some shift in the way they played. But it also didn't happen all at once, it took us several games to really get it going, and for them to stop making reasonable PCs and start making unreasonable ones.

    And that's where IAWA cooks, for me -- when unreasonable characters of all sorts decide to get active and take what they want.

    \n The "reasonable NPC syndrome" is a big one, indeed. However, I think it applies just as much to the players as it does to the GM of IaWA, and possibly more so. The couple of games I've seen flounder were because the PCs weren't willing to be all desperate and unreasonable.

    I've had the "reasonable NPC" problem in other games, too, although sometimes it was that they were TOO reasonable and too diplomatic, and convinced the PCs over to their side, which ruined any kind of building conflict. That one's annoying, too. I'm trying to portray the NPC convincingly, and, instead, end up convincing the player that the NPC is right. Whereas I was really hoping to get them riled up and start a fight or something.

    So, that's one I'm taking home: don't be afraid to let your character(s) (whether PC or NPC) get desperate and irrational. The game needs it, more often than not.
