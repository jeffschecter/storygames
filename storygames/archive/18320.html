    \n Whose goal, and what type of goal?

    The let's say Forgean orthodoxy (for the lack of a better term) against pre-planning is an observation about an endemic, counterproductive GMing practice. It is not the same thing as any and all goal-setting, intentional play; in fact, that advice is usually discussed in the context of making your play more intentioned, more focused, more achieving.

    Thus: in most Forgean narrativist games it is definitely a good idea to have clear goals on two levels, while avoiding having goals on one specific level. To wit:
    • It is a good idea to sit down at the table with a clear creative commitment and goal. For example, your shared goal as a group might be to create an unexpected, effortless and delightful tale of derring-do. This goal, by existing and being acknowledged, helps the group coordinate their actions and evaluate how useful their activity is instrumentally.
    • In many games of this type it is a fundamental requirement that so-called protagonist characters - often PCs - have clear, sympathetic goals they strive towards. We as players establish these goals in most games. They are not our goals, but they are goals nevertheless.
    • What the advice against planning and goal-setting means is that most of these games, and the creative agenda behind them, hurt badly if the players pre-plan the plot, the "what is going to happen" of it. A given character might think or want for a certain set of events, but the players do not - the players are there to find out what happens. By pre-planning a player is effectively plotting against the group's desire for spontaneous excitement. Pre-planning also makes them unable to utilize extremely dynamic response techniques that these games often assume; either you abandon your wrong kind of prep to play the game as intended, or you're tied down by your prep, and thus cause the game to work against its intent.
    \nHopefully that clarified something. I certainly agree with you that entirely reactive play is often very chaotic, and obtaining complex structural order requires planning at either time of play or in advance. However, it's the type of planning that matters for the purposes of determining whether it's bad planning or good planning.

    Frodo; Goal - destroy the Ring (Failed). he succumbed to the Ring at the last minute BUT if his character's Goal was listed as "bring the Ring to the Fire" then Success.
    Sam; Goal - aid and support his master (Success) almost to the ruin of Middle-Earth; he really should have pushed Frodo over the edge into the lava (but then Elrond should have pushed Isildur in the movie... oh well)
    Gollum; Goal - retrieve the Ring (Success) however fleeting.

    At any rate, I think the chaotic nature of "play to find out", "don't plan ahead" suggests a lot of interesting twists towards a goal that a planned plot couldn't anticipate.

    \n In-character plans do not have effective traction in many rules systems. Polaris, for example, was mentioned - you can plan to your heart's content, but actually the only thing that matters for your character's story is your decision over what they want, and perhaps the most generic description of how they'll go about it. You don't get bonus success for good planning, except perhaps on the theory that the Mistaken will let you go easier if they fall in love with your narration - but that's just good narration, not good planning.

    This puts planning vs. advocating to a strong contrast: many of these games are intensely interested in hearing you tell us what your characters wants, why (psychological motivations), and what they're willing to do to get there. Meanwhile the game will often just have other players nod along as you speculate about the plan, but there is no step in the play process where your plan is evaluated for effectiveness. It's all just descriptive color, like Star Trek: it simply does not matter whether their plan for "recalibrating the engine core by reversing polarity" or whatever is a good one, the important thing is to show how diligent and brave these officers are in the execution of their duties.

    \n Insofar as this is relevant to Narrativism:

    It's good - essential, even! - for characters to have goals. It's good to have a dude who really wants to become King in the North.

    \n It seems to me that "plan" in the sense you mean it does not mean much to usual rpg theory. This means that the premise of your question is incomprehensible - a straw man, if you will. The answer is that no, nobody advices against planning in that sense of the word, so there is no contradiction between planning and having a goal.

    What we usually mean when we discuss how planning ahead can be harmful to playing is prescriptive, authorial expectations that players develop regarding future events of play: the problem is not that your character wants to be the king, or that you figure out what needs to happen for that come to pass; the problem is when you grow angry at another player whose legitimate game actions ruin your plan, or you break the game's rules to make your plan come to pass. As you can see, it's not planning really that has a problematic reputation in certain types of rpg modeling - it's the associated motivations and techniques that players historically attach to the mental activity of planning.

    In other words: plan all you want, as long as you accept that the game you're playing might not give any weight to your plans, even if you are the GM. Some games are designed to ignore pre-planning specifically to pre-empt the authorial expectations that so commonly come hand in hand with plans. It's a strategy for shaking roleplayers out of their routine modes of thinking, if you will.

    \n You know, the Big Model is not too popular these days, so I even hesitate to bring it up, but concepts like Creative Agenda can really help us talk about this kind of thing meaningfully.

    For example, if you're playing a game like combat-focused D&D, where you know your character will be leveling up and choosing abilities from a known list, it's quite good and smart play to "plan ahead": you could even chart out the feats or abilities or whatever you plan to gain during the next five levels, given a certain goal ("I want to maximize my Armor Class"). This meshes well with the system of play and the way the group is playing it, and there's nothing wrong with it.

    On the other hand, it would be pretty weird to go into the game with the "plan" that you want to see your character defeated and captured by a particular group of "bad guys". This would work well as a character's goal (the way we're using the word in this dicussion), but not too well as a plan put together by the player. (The game will almost certainly be more functional if the player, just like everyone else, is invested in finding out whether the plan works or not.)

    In the weird middle ground might be something like: "I plan for my D&D paladin to level up to level 6, and then switch to the anti-paladin class for the multi-attack bonus." This has strong "story" ramifications--a paladin turning evil--but if the game is all about the combat challenge, this story element is just a fun bit of colour added to the game we're playing.

    On the other hand, playing a straight-up Narrativist game focused on difficult moral decisions (even if it's the same D&D), you probably won't get the most out of it if you go into it with the idea that after three sessions, your character will turn evil. You're working against what the group has agreed to play.

    I think it depends on how your "goals" and "plans" interact with the nature of the game you're playing and the creative direction of the group. For instance, I'm playing a Diplomacy game right now, with some light roleplaying elements. Although we do associate personalities and bits of story colour with our "characters", we're playing to compete with each other on a strategic battlefield. In this game, having an in-character goal (like "I will capture Berlin, no matter what I have to give up to do it, because my grandmother was tortured there!") will ruin the game, whereas a player's plan for how things will go ("I need to move two units into the Atlantic by 1906, or else Joe will not be able to pull off his Lepanto maneuver") is exactly what's needed.

    So the key is aligning these decisions with the creative direction of the group in the game you're playing.

    Some "goals" and "plans" will work towards the purpose of the game you're playing; some against it. If you're playing to find out how the story turns out, a player plan ("My rival will return to tempt me in season three!") takes away from the group's ability to spontaneously create story. If you're playing to re-create a familiar H.P. Lovecraft-type story, then having a player goal like "I hope my cultist goes completely nuts by the end of this one-shot, and I'll push towards that" might be totally the thing you need to do.
