How do you explain why/when to roll dice?

James_Nostack
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
Every once in a while, I try to get friends interested in RPG's if it seems to be the kind of thing they'd dig but just don't know about. Once in a while, I try to write something down, explaining who does what, and why.

Most Forge-y games involve a "conflict resolution mechanic" of some kind. What's the point of having such a thing? When, in general, should it be used?

Like: for me, all this RPG stuff is about collaborating to tell a story. There will be moments, though, when the actual-people-around-the-actual-table can't agree on something--or, we know something should happen but we're not sure how it would shake down in the imaginary world of the story. So, we reach for the dice/cards/coins to settle the issue. In other words, it's really solving a Conflict at the table: either between players who disagree, or an 'internal conflict' caused by uncertainty.

Now, this seems intuitive to me, but I'm not a game designer or theorist. I'm also aware that this can't QUITE be right, because the implication is that if the players are totally grooving on each other's ideas there's no conflict and hence no need for resolution--whatever's suggested happens. And while this may be a valid way to play for some games, I get the impression that few Forge-y games are not meant to be played in this "kumbayah" sort of way.

Pretend you're explaining to someone who's never played an RPG before. What requires rolling dice, what doesn't require rolling dice, and what's a judgment call? And why?

    Well... it depends somewhat on the game, but here's what I say in The Mountain Witch:
    A conflict represents those situations where the characters must overcome opposition or resistance to acheive what they hope to accomplish. This can take a number of forms:

    --Active opposition from a living creature, regardless of whether the creature is directly or indirectly opposing you;
    --Some sort of active resistance from a non-living or static obstacle;
    --Any situation that carries with it an associated cost for failing.

    More subtly, conflicts represent conflicts of interests between characters. One character wants to see one thing happen, and another something else. A Conflict roll determines whose interest is realized...

    In this sense, a Conflict is not about a character's skill. TMW assumes competent characters, and under "normal" circumstances the character should automatically accomplish any reasonable action within their ability. A Conflict roll is only required when the character is confronted with active opposition or something likewise out of the ordinary. The question is not if a character can physically accomplish the task at hand, but rather can they overcome the forces that are working against them.

    ... [I]t is still a good idea for you to roll the dice whenever a player suggests a course of action that has a reasonable chance of failure. Even when everyone everyone at the table wants to see a character succeed (or fail), adding that random element of the die roll will often push the story in unexpected---and intersting---directions.
    The reason you roll dice in story games isn't to solve disagreement---it's to push the story in interesting and unpredictable directions. That's what story games are about, improvising fiction. It's not just about collaborative story creation.

    Oh yeah - one reason to use dice that isn't about creating uncertainty, is to work in degrees smaller than the scale of the game. That's a really awkward sentence. I will explain with an example:

    Everyone in game X has five hit points. You might want a weapon to do less than one but more than zero damage. Half a point of damage would be ideal. But working with halves is inelegant, and you don't want to double all the hit point/damage values. So instead you interpret the fraction as a probability and say that you flip a coin - the attack does 0 damage on a tails and 1 damage on a head.

    You can do the same thing with resource expenditure systems. You don't want to be dealing with pools of more than (say) ten points, but you want people to be spending those points all the time and you also want a fairly wide range of possible costs for things. So you change "number of points you need to spend to do X" to "probability of losing a point when you do X" with the rider that you must have at least one point in order to do X in the first place. Which effectively multiplies the size of the resource pool (when looked at over time) without increasing the fiddliness of record-keeping.

    I keep seeing this argument against the words "player disagreement." I am unconvinced. At the very least, within the scope of games I'm interested in, I don't recognize where player disagreement is not happening.

    Along those lines, I consider the following nonsense, despite Vincent and Ron trying to convince me otherwise:

    Conflict is all about when characters want different things in the fiction.

    It's nonsense to me because the Characters Don't Exist. Characters -- even "fit" ones -- want nothing more than what we put into them. They are, however mutely or indirectly, our real-world-people's desires. Thus, characters desire only that which players desire.

    Therefore, when two (or more) players want different things to happen, we have conflict.

    So, it brings my head back around to my definition: "Conflict is when players have different ideas about 'what happens next' in the story."

    I used to use the word "disagree" instead of "have different ideas." I don't anymore. I think that covers the overly negative connotation of "disagree" much better.

    Why roll the dice?

    I think resolving different views on what should happen next is really secondary.

    In a roleplaying game, specifically one where players are trying to collaborate to produce a story, I started to say that the dice are rolled at what, in a story, would be a pinchpoint in the plot. After the dice rolls, something important will change and be irreversible, potentially unexpected, moving the story forward (this is a concept from screenwriting called the "dramatic event").

    At first when thinking about this, I imagined that the players together must estimate when such a pinchpoint is approaching. Then I thought about how in Trollbabe anyone can declare a conflict (dice rolling) at any time. I suddenly saw that, with the right rules, the very act of calling for a dice roll creates an event that produces irreversible change. Halleluja!

    So my answer is: roll the dice when you want to move the story forward.

    In our TSOY game, we tend to reach for the dice when we really want to "push" at the plot. I mean, obviously, we roll to determine the outcome when we're engaging in a battle or in a test of skill, but we also use the dice almost as a signal to the GM and to each other that we've decided something is important and we're going to poke at it and see what happens.

    AP example: My PC is talking to an NPC and the conversation is going around in circles a bit, so I say, "Ok, I really want to find out what the big bad secret in her past is that she keeps dancing around, so I'm going to do a Counsel check to see if I can get her to open up." GM says, "Cool, she's going to passively resist." We roll, I win, so NPC tells me she ran away from her family because they were criminals.

    I didn't HAVE to go to dice there. I could have danced around the subject for a little longer with the NPC, and then decided she wasn't going to give up the goods, but that it wasn't that important. Or I could have decided that it would be more interesting to push for the information later. Or, I might have totally missed the idea that she was trying to hide something, and the story would have galloped merrily on (albeit in a different direction) with no one any the wiser. It's like Alan says above -- you use the dice to create an event and assign importance to something, prodding the story in a direction you find interesting.

    Well, I think I should make my point with more care.

    1) I see that when and why to roll dice (ie use a formal resolution system) depends on why you are playing.     2) Forgive me, but I must use Forge-speak: when and why to roll the dice depend on what Creative Agenda you're trying to support. The triggers will be different depending on which you choose. A call for dice resolution can be an act of taking a stand and of the player putting guts on the line, or it can be a way to reinforce a model, or it can be a way of declaring the dramatic importance (to the player) of a moment. I imagine that each of the three agenda's has more than one use for dice resolution and that will further complicate things.
