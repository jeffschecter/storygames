    The players should have the choice to play the movers and the shakers in the world not the bottom rung of the Vampire/Werewolf/Mage heirarchy. None of that stuff is nearly as much fun as playing say... The Prince right off the bat.
    No way. No. That's all backwards.

    Here's the great thing about Vampire: you're a neonate, a new Vampire, and you're in a world you don't understand. There are creatures immeasurably superior to you and slowly, but surely, they draw their plans against you.

    This is a great premise. It's great. But then the rulebooks don't follow through on the premise: they give you books with the Elder powers, so that the superior beings aren't inimaginable at all. They've got kewl powerz and they're available to play. It's shite.

    Here's what you can do:

    1. No elder level powers. Elders are just NPCs with unimaginable powers. If you cross them, you die, so you don't cross them.
    2. Come to that, no high level powers at all. Nothing like Majesty or (God forbid) Aegis. Just little, low-level powers like Enchantment and Aura Perception.
    3. Bring in a mechanism like Trascendence in The Shadow Of Yesterday. So, when your character becomes powerful, one day he'll transcend his present existence and become an Elder. That day, he becomes all -powerful; and he stops being a player character.
    4. No rulebooks that explain the rules of the Camarilla. The Camarilla should be a vast, unimaginable organisation that tolerates the PCs, but never accepts.

    And you fix the combat system so combats get resolved with two or three rolls.

    Graham

    I'm not sure Vampire: the New Alleged Hotness needs a revamp (ugh). It's actually pretty cool already. It's essentially gang warfare, with magic powers. I'd strip out the new-skool political factions, or turn them into pretty minor secret societies.

    Vampire, it seems to me, is sort of an addiction story. The question is whether you can get what you need, without alienating yourself from your old life and everything you loved by turning into a crazed junkie. And the trick is that your town is rife with gang politics, and everyone is trying to screw with your shit--not always out of malice, but because that's how things go down. In order to survive, you've got to do horrible, horrible things--like eat other people alive. There's no escape, and you'll eventually lose it completely, so you've got to figure out how to die properly, with all your personal issues and loved ones taken care of, somehow.

    The best-case scenario is to do a Scarface: show up with nothing, rise to the top, have a few good years, and then get skragged once all your loose ends are tied up.

    The thing that gets me about Vampire is how *tame* it is. Has anyone seen "Ichi the Killer"? That, right there, is how Vampire ought to be. Take the most sick, twisted, gross and disturbing stuff you can imagine--and that's your life, right there.

    Here were some thoughts about using SOY--
    This is a thread about using "The Shadow of Yesterday" (SOY) to run a game like "Vampire: the Requiem" (V:tR).
    ===Rough Notes on a Conversion===
    Vampires have magical powers, represented in SOY as Secrets. Mortals cannot have Secrets.

    There are motivations--Keys--associated with each covenant, but these are logically independent. You can belong to a covenant, without having the Key on your character sheet, and vice versa.

    Traditional remedies against vampires, like garlic, running water, seeing yourself in a mirror, etc., impose a penalty die.

    A vampire can only die by Bringing Down the Pain (a sub-system in SOY), and a mortal can only kill a vampire if special vampire-killin' gear is involved (stakes, fire, sunlight, etc).

    A vampire has three damage states under SOY. When bruised, a vampire can heal the damage by suckin' on somebody's neck for a little bit. When bloodied, a vampire can heal the damage by sucking enough blood to kill someone (possibly, drawn from a list of the character's relationships. "How much do you really love me, son? Let's find out"). And if broken, the vampire has to heal the damage by going into torpor for a long time--which, I suppose if it's a historical game, might be used to shift the color and action of the game ahead several years/decades/centuries depending.

    Players can gain a mechanical advantage--Gift Dice--by doing stuff that is creepy, scary, or disturbing to the other players. (This is a special case of the usual Gift Dice rule, which is to reward a player for entertaining you. It's a horror game, so you should reward horrorific stuff.)

    Vampires can refresh their pools (their mechanical oomph) in special ways. A vampire refreshes its Vigor pool by engaging in a frenzy scene. A vampire refreshes its Instinct pool by engaging in a Gothy scene where they are reminded that they're forever set apart from mortal lifestyles. A vampire refreshes its Reason pool by engaging in a scene where they display mental illness and derangement.

    (Shadow of Yesterday is available for free off the CRN Games website, but it's kind of tucked away. I encourage everyone to check it out, because it's an awesomely flexible fantasy game.)

    I'm still wondering how to handle diablerie, and I think relationships need a fancy mechanical thing. (I was originally thinking of using them as weapons or armor--"Bill the Accountant: +2 armor vs. financial attack" but I'm not sure about that. I'd really like to figure out how to add the person as a relationship, mechanically, and then force the player to sacrifice such a character if they end up bloodied. "Bill, I need to audit your blood supply. Just lay still, okay?")

    And obviously there's stuff to work out, relating to specific Secrets, and particularly Keys, which should reflect the various tropes of vampire fiction.

    I already did this, too, but unlike Jared mine's available online: Web of Shadows (Also unlike Jared, mine did not earn me any money or l33t bylinez.)

    But the key thing for Vampire that is never mentioned in a book is that Vampire society is this great, baroque, ossified social structure. Any entertaining game must directly threaten that social structure. "Day in the Life of" stories are dull in Vampire. What's engaging, and what makes it a "Game of Personal Horror" is when the status quo is shaken up, the complacent are shown the actual horror of their day to day lives, the powerful are vulnerable, and the young bloods have an opportunity to change their world. Things have to go to shit, and the players have to decide whether to put things back the way they were or to remake things in a new design. So to make this specific, include a situation creation mechanic that is based off of "What happens that changes everything?"

    (Caveat: Never played any WoD games. Ever! Just read some.)

    One natural balancing mechanism between weak, new vampires and powerful, old vampires -- instead of, or in addition to, having a threshold beyond which you transcend to unfathomable NPC status -- is making the Old Ones frighteningly clueless about the modern world. They're part of this "great, baroque, ossified social structure" (thank you, Joshua) to the point that they are personally ossified, stuck repeating patterns centuries old. I'm not just talking about ancient vendettas, but also simple things that make it impossible for them to interact with the human world directly: No matter how many times you explain it to them, on some fundamental level they can't understand that there are educated black people, or women as powerful executives, or flying machines that can take you from New York to London in less than three months. They just don't get it -- which means younger vampires have to act as their interface with the modern world and carefully twist, ignore, or subvert their master's orders when their master is totally out to lunch:

    "Yes, my prince, I was appalled when that slave refused to take your coat. I will have his master flay her alive shortly." (walking away, muttering:) "How to kill the mayor... kill the mayor... or I could just kill some other 40-something black woman and give him the skin. It's not like the Prince can tell them apart, anyway. I'll just have to censor his newspaper a little more thoroughly until the next citywide election."

    Andy,

    One of the reasons for that is that Vampire was originally targeted at the kind of Sisters of Mercy listening goth-punk who'd go to school (highschool or college) during the day and engage in petty politics because it was the only way to survive and be part of the group that got them laid (or stoned) and then come home at night and turn on the music, off the lights, and feel the inner angst and torment about what a horrible person they had become. Double bonus points for having the politics result in a breakup with an SO that leads you to write the kind of song that Johnny Cash will later cover and make more famous than you did.

    So, in a real way for a lot of the original audience the politics and the self-horror were bound up together in the big ball of angst and self-hatred that is late adolescence. To do one was to lead to the other, in what must have seemed an almost causative way.

    Of course, it wasn't actually causative, and thus the issues started.

      See, I'd keep the power link, because I'd tie it down even more than its tied down.

      As part of that I'd also make clans small, tightly knit, and utterly dysfunctional. Each clan is pretty much an inbreed undead family like something out of a horrific version of a V.C. Andrews novel. There is a good chance that you know every single member of your clan, by face and name if not closely (and many all to intimately) because every single one of them is going to be in your business until the day you don't die. They are the people you hate the most, and the people you need the most, because the members of the other clans will fucking kill you a diablerize you if they get half a chance. If you ain't got family, you ain't got shit, yo.

      At that point the orgs become less like party clubs and more like mutually assured destruction communities, where every meeting of mixed clans is like the mafia getting together for drinks with the triads. You don't get together to hang out and talk about the horror of being undead, you only get together for big shit and even then the real danger is that you're going to kill each other rather than solve anything.

      You could also tie all this in with the "blood of those close to you" and make it a generational game. At first you have to drink the blood of your old mortal family and lovers to gain the strength to stay alive, or else become the bitch-pawn of one of the evil elders of the clan. Either way, eventually your old life is gone and your whole life is your clan and the horrible incest blood-orgy that they represent. As you work your way up the ranks (if you survive at all) you come to be tied more and more powerfully to your clan -- and then as you become an elder you have to start eating them.
