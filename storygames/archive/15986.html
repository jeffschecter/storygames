    \n As written it's a cycle: you write bonds so you can aid/interfere, you resolve them for XP, your character's relation to others is always changing. Your net ability to aid/interfere is static, unless a move grants you a new Bond (I LOVE Marshall's idea), but how that's allocated changes. Maybe at one point you're better at aiding Thelian, at another point its Omar.

    Adding a new Bond at the end of each session doesn't feed that cycle. Now getting XP means turning down an improved ability to help someone (since the resolved Bond won't count towards your aid/interfere, and since you still only gain one Bond per session). Invoking the simple resolve/replace cycle is now a choice between different rewards, so some people won't want to resolve Bonds. That's not our goal.

    Could you expand on why you're not that into that cycle? I'm very into the ongoing play it creates.

    \n The overall ability? Yeah, I don't know that's something we're designing towards. Part of that is the practical nature of Bonds as sentences: you get more than 5 or so and each one starts meaning less, they're harder to keep track of.

    As it stands your ability to help any given person can always be shifting, but your overall ability is capped, which is to my liking. It has the effect of spotlighting player-to-player bonds, instead of an slow growth of overall bonding.

    I'm working on the Hacking chapter for Beta 2 today, I may throw something about this in there, but I don't think the current mechanic will change for this particular reason.

    One more thing to think about: if you always get a new Bond and have the option of resolving one as well (but you still one get one new Bond) then the optimal long-term strategy seems to me to be: don't resolve until you have three bonds with everyone, then start resolving one every session and replacing it. I'm sure there's a way to design around it, but that is not the goal of how Bonds are supposed to work. They're supposed to be shifting ties.
