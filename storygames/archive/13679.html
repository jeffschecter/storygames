\n A neat idea, that a game creator mentioned. I don't know if I'm translating his idea correctly. But the idea I'm coming up with is something I'm curious to have folks that HAVE DONE commission work and writing work - to poke holes in. Figure out the problems. See if some form of this idea is workable and fair.

Okay.

The idea is to have an open call for writing submissions on a project.
Submissions would be geared toward the product description/goal with guidelines, primarily NPC origin/background story and stats.
The creator finishes the idea, submits it to the book project planner.
When the NPC/background is approved, the creator is given the contact info for a (pre-established) artist that will illustrate the character idea.
The creator pays the (pre-set) fee for the character and the image/character idea is submitted to the book project planner.
The project planner organizes all of these submissions and the new art created for them and lays them out in the book to be published.

When the book is published the creators (all) get a free-complimentary copy (perhaps even 2-5 copies to give friends) of the entire collection (either PDF or Print or both). Therefor "paying" them for their submission with a substantial book supplement, as well as giving them writing credits for their idea.

The artist is one set up by the book project planner. Either a single artist or a few artists that are the only options for illustrating for the book, to keep consistency in style. The artist could be set up to either get the full commission or set it up to have the book project planner be a sort of "middle man"... or rather like an Agent for the artist. The artist is getting (potentially) a steady flow of art commissions. As the writing for the project is being created (with approval and direction from the planner). Either the artist getting the full commission or working out a plan 80/20 % commission pay for the Artist and book planner.


Where are the problems, holes, potential downfalls, concerns. Or good thoughts about this idea?

NOTE...
I am not asking or suggesting this in some sort of "break-the-bank" scheme acting as a project planner. I know that much of our niche hobby is about creation and love of the project. But in this I'm seeing the option to create quality products with a unifyied theme that also allows new amateur creators a chance to get published at the same time producing a good deal of material and giving them a "payment" in free product.
Sort of like creating a submission and buying the art for your creation acting as a "pre-order" to the overall project book in the end.

thoughts??

    \n This is a traditional vanity publishing set-up, including the notion that the writer gets to pay for getting published. Historically this has not been a respected practice in writing, and for a reason: vanity publishing generates value for the publisher not by servicing the public but rather by downplaying the value of the creative work and exaggerating the ill-defined publisher services provided by the benefitting party, going to such extremes that the poor victim starts thinking that it's right and proper for him to actually pay for the right to associate with these respectable "professionals" who so kindly are willing to take his money and publish his writing. Although there are many ways you can set up a vanity publishing endeavour, they are simple to recognize as what they are by following the money: if the publisher has a vested interest in publishing your work not because it's good but because the mere act of taking you on is generating them income, then what you have is a vanity publishing operation. Real publishers make their living by selling works to the public, not by selling services to the creator.

    The twist in this particular case is that the direct beneficiary is the artist and not the publisher (except for the free IP he's getting). If this seems like a fair set-up, do consider this: would it still be fair if the artist and the publisher were the same person? Because that's what I would totally expect if I saw this floated seriously somewhere.

    As to why exactly everybody is ragging on this thing, it's because we know from our own experience that the publishing world does not work the way vanity publishing legend says it does: there is no "writing credit" that makes up for lack of skill, and the mere fact of seeing your name on a page does not make you any more valuable as a writer. You can't fake experience, and the reason for why people give weight to writing credit on their CV is that it comes from real, vetted publishing projects, not some arbitrary pay-to-get-published scheme. There is also no magic publishing procedure that is somehow out of reach of the ordinary mortal to such a degree that you actually have to pay somebody else to do a half-assed job at it.
