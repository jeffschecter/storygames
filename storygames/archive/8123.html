\n Today I got together with Robert and Chris P. to play Matthijs Holter's game Archipelago.

This is a game about characters who are moving towards their destinies. We had all read the rules at some point, but only Chris P. had read them recently. So we went over them at the table as we were starting play.

Setting:
The game is really about islands in an archipelago, but we were all into some sci-fi. So we agreed that asteroids in the Kuiper Belt, specifically the sparsely asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. This was taking place sometime in the 22nd century, but the feeling was less Cyberpunk and more gritty. Maybe closer to the movie Outland. Man had been mining valuable minerals from these asteroids for several generations now and shipping them off to Mars, or even Earth.

As per the rules, we drew up a quick map of the region with several asteroids (mostly being mined by a government/corporation group), a small mass of micro asteroids, a few radio relays, a tiny trading post build from the remnants of a spaceship and an asteroid, a small group of religious pilgrims (Unitarians) who carved out their own destiny called Rapture, and a few other things such as the abandoned and contaminated Skylab 8 and a giant dust cloud. Oh and an alien artifact that had just come out of the cloud.

Characters:

Robert created Jorge "Duck" Dobbins, a old first gen belter. He had adapted so much to the low-gee that he can't go back to Earth. Robert also created a connection to Imram Khalil, an Elder of the religious pilgrims at Rapture. The two had little in common as far as Faith went, but they had relied on each other many times over the years and had become as close as they could be.

Chris P. made Matthew, a young third-gen Unitarian who was a grandson to Imram Khalil. He also created a connection to (someone whose name is too long to remember so I'll just say) Laireli, someone who was unknown to him except that he had an upcoming arranged marriage to her. And he didn't know that she was an alien.

I made Xiao-Ching Atkinson, a young man who recently showed up and works for Artemas Gomez, the grumpy old guy who runs the tiny but independent trading post and carries the interesting and rare supplies that the government ships never bring into the belt. Xiao-Ching is secretly in love with a girl named Abigail Zebulon who lives on Rapture. "Dogs in the Kuiper Belt", anyone??

Destiny Points

Each player is supposed to write one destiny point for each of the other players, but that would give each of us only two to choose from, so we doubled this to have more choices. Here are the destiny points that were written for us with the one each player chose in bold.

Robert:
-Duck discovers that Imram Khalil is secretly plotting against Franklin's Landing (the huge government base in the belt that is run by only a few people including Duck).
-Duck finds that the Rapture Unitarians are preparing Skylab 8 as a human-alien breeding ground.
-Duck moves into Rapture's old folks home.
-Duck disappears into the Crylla (dust) Cloud.

Chris P.:
-Laireli bonds with Matthew, fusing with him and destroying his solitary identity.
-Matthew finds that the government is trying to take the alien artifact.
-Finds that his alien bride really does exist.
-Discovers a love that tempts him to leave Rapture and the Unitarians forever.

Chris B.:
-Discovers the horrible alien truth about Abigail.
-Xiao-Ching dies in Skylab 8.
-He marries Abigail and returns to Earth.
-He has an opportunity to steal a starship and keep it.

I listed out all of the possible destiny points here not only to show you the cool possibilities each of us had (that were authored by the others), but also to note that we all used elements of at least one of the other destiny points to add some more interest to their story.

Ownership:

Each player is supposed to take ownership over certain elements of the fiction and gains veto power over that part. This was actually one of my favorite parts of the game since there is no GM and I felt like this provided some weight to keep the fiction from going off the deep edge and gave each player strict creative control over something other than just their character.

After some discussing, Robert chose Social, Chris P. chose Alien Technology and I chose Human Technology.

    \nStory

    Since this isn't a playtest, I'll just hit the highlights of the story.

    -The Unitarians flew a regular supply ship to the trading post to get the things that they needed. And Abigail made sure she was on those runs so she could get to see her secret love, Xiao-Ching. Matthew came along as well to make sure they got the right supplies. Xiao-Ching expressed his love for her and said that he would think of a way to get her out of Rapture so they could be together.

    -Duck was monitoring some signal traffic when he noted that someone from Rapture was sending signals to something in space. It turned out to be some sort of alien device or craft that was heading away from the dust cloud and towards Franklin's Landing and Rapture. Duck ended up flying to Rapture to confront his old friend Imram about this and was told that "something wonderful was going to happen" and was convinced to keep things a secret just until the next regular supply ship from Mars arrived in three months.

    -Matthew was kept from hearing about the conversation between Imram and Duck, and he was nervously awaiting to find out who his bride-to-be was. Turns out it was this totally hot alien woman (who looked like Liv Tyler). He was scared, but also kinda turned on.

    -Long story short, Abigail got "sick" after arriving back in Rapture and was put in confinement to avoid an outbreak while the Eldars asked for medicine to get delivered from the trading post. Xiao-Ching had his spaceship-owning friend Jefferson Cho come by and pick him up in his hooptie shuttle he liked to call "The Cho". They flew from the trading post to Rapture just as Duck was leaving Rapture to go back to Franklin's Landing.

    -While this was happening, Duck got an urgent call from the few other guys at Franklin's Landing saying that something strange was now heading straight for their base on the asteroid! Duck called up Imram to ask him what was going on. But as the five men tried to leave Franklin's in space suits and EVA sleds, they were trapped by the alien device which crashed into the base and started expanding it's crystal latticework all around the asteroid. Creating some sort of mechanical Genesis Device effect.

    -But it gets creepier. Xiao-Ching took the "medicine" to the "sick" Abigail, and ended up shooting her with a tranquilizer to make it look to the Elders like she was doing really had and needed to be taken to a hospital facility to save her life. It was a lame rescue attempt, but then Xiao-Ching was an excitable 20-year old spacer guy. He got her out and they took off in "The Cho", but when he went in the back to consummate things on the shag floor, she showed her true alien self and began to meld with him, blinding Jefferson in the cockpit with white beams of light.

    -And then we switched over to Matthew, who was getting married to Laireli in a solemn ceremony on Rapture. And as the two went behind a dais to consummate their union, she melded into him and the audience stood as they were bathed in the same bright white light.

    -The story ended with Jorge "Duck" Dobbins realizing that Imram had lied to him and that the aliens were going to take over the entire mining area in the asteroids. So he set a course for Mars orbit and used all of the fuel on his shuttle to make a long burn, hoping that he would make it far enough before he died to be able to send a signal of warning. That the aliens were coming.

    Thoughts

    As is usual in these types of games, the first few scenes were a little unwieldy as we tried to feel out the system.

    Here are some other observations and problems we had about the rules:

    1. Some players found the "Do it differently, please" or "Draw a card, please" to be weird or disruptive in play. But we all liked the "More detail, please" as long as it wasn't overused.
    2. Once we got into the meat of the story, we found that our destinies were moving together. And the nexus was likely going to be in or near Rapture. This was probably a conscious choice by us as players to keep the story arcs intertwined and not just have three different stories that didn't really connect. It might be interesting to add this to the rules, unless you didn't intend for their stories to be connected.
    3. The creation of Destiny Points was confusing to us. There is info about creating them that appears later in the rules, and that should probably be referenced on the earlier page that deals with Starting the Session so the players don't learn about it later. We all ended up creating Destiny Points that were quite a bit different in structure, which made us wonder who was doing them wrong. But in the end we all had four things to choose from so it was okay. But definitely more guidance and more/better examples would have helped here.
    4. We had a lot of confusing the first time someone said "Draw a card", as the rules there don't seem to match the example given. We made a call at the table that we would follow the rules and ignore the example, but some clarity would help there.
    5. I felt like the Ownership and possible also the Veto rules should have been placed earlier in the rules as they are very relevant to setting up the setting and characters.
    6. We didn't read over the Important Techniques very much at the table. But in scanning them I think they would be very useful for players who were either new to gaming or to story games in particular.

    Overall I think Matthijs wrote a very elegant game that plays better than I had expected. I wondered how something so seemingly open-ended would play at the table. But the combination of Destiny Points and other techniques like Ownership/Vetos and "What else can you do?" tightened the game up considerable and made it a lot easier to not have an all-powerful GM at the table. I think the rules could take another editing pass to tighten them up, but the game is very playable (and enjoyable) as is.

    My take on Archipelago:

    * The game worked well once we were up and running. It was a little too free-form for my tastes: any conflicts were negotiated outside of the rules or by the 50/50 "draw a card" rule. But that's my personal preference in games.

    * World creation was fun, but I think we invested too much time mapping details that never came up in play. (We only used about 50% of our asteroid map.) If "pre game" world creation is fun (and it was), it could benefit by closer integration into "real" game play.

    * I originally thought 3-4 scenes was waaay too quick to reach our Destiny Points, but it was actually just right. With 4 scenes, we knew the end of our characters' story lines should be ending. We could plan what events or scenes needed to happen to reach our Destiny Points. That worked very well.

    * Like in many of our games, some NPCs began to overshadow some of the PCs.

    \n "More Detail, Please' we used a lot.

    "Do it differently, Please" came into play once early on in my description of Gomez's Trading Post, where Caesar_X also laid down the ownership hammer on technology. The description ended up workig out better, but it threw me for a loop at first, as I had to quickly re-wind and stumbled a bit.

    "Draw a card, Please" was a little odd and we stumbled some. It wasn't clear whether we supposed to talk about what the character wanted or what the player wanted. I was pulling for it to be about what the player wanted, but Los Dos Chrises voted character want was the correct interpretation, so we went with that.

    There was at least one example late in the game where there was a "Draw a card, Please" situation, and I think I managed to muddle through something close to a As the Character I want(Good) vs As a Player I want ( Bad) set up ( which, in keeping with our local traditions, Caesar_X immediately busted me on).

    A couple of times, the active player also called for a card draw and asked the other player for help setting it up, just because we (the active player) wanted some sort of fork in the story and a bit of randomness in direction.

    Overall, I really think Archipelago is a great look at what a co-operatively minded, GMless story game can look like. It's verfy much stripped down to core elements, and gives enough method/structure to get the players off and running. I thought that the Destiny Points worked out excellently, and nicely showcased how challenge has an important place even (perhaps especially) in a co-operative game, but in a way different from what it's often considered to have.

    One minor point that might be worth discussing, and which I realized grated a little with Archipelago and also with Fiasco when we played it last week.

    In GMless games where the players still have a main character, there is a design tendency to always pass off the ball to the next player, in order, throughout the game. I assume this is probably out of a sense of fairness and an attempt to not create an opening for spotlight hogging.

    For me, I think that can make things a bit disruptive in terms of story-flow. I'm wondering if some ( probably optional) alternative structure of scene framing could be cobbled together, so that if one character's story is really jazzing up the group, they can follow it for more than one scene. Any thoughts?

    \n I'd like to talk a little bit about the map and the Destiny points for a second.

    I see them as very central to the game, and why it functions very well.

    For myself, the DPs are a really great creative-constraint, and a very ealy feedback point from the other players. While the open brainstorming session is good ( and I like similar methods in other games), I find it to be helpful to have some bit of time or method where I can step back from simple discussion and write down a bit of my personal vison for the game, initially private from group discussion. I think the DPs work well for that, because rather than focssing that personal vision on my own character, I'm focussing it on the other characters, and it just seems like a natural way to merge my ideas with that of the overall story.

    On the recieving end, I get some undiscussed feedback on what the other players are seeing in my character and the game. It is a form of challenge to work those ideas in with my vision of things ( in a good way: It challenges me as a player). Having four options to choose from in this game meant I could focus on the one that struck me as most interesting, but the other options also helped me modify my ideas and have something more to work with.

    For example, Chris P's DP (Duck retires to the old folks' home on Rapture Base), started me thinking more about what Duck was like, and opened up the possibility for a stronger friendship with Imram Khalil, which turned out to be very important. There was a turning point where I, as a player, honestly didn't know which direction Duck was going to take because of that.

    As for the map, I think having a physical artifact to work with really allowed for the openly discussed situation ideas to develop. Unlike a R-map (or something similar), which I sometimes find intimidating, I was able to both have ( again) some personal, undiscussed creative space to work with, and a way to tie some social relationships around the physical stuff. The literal closeness of Rapture Base to Franklin's Landing, as well as the appearance of the Alien Artifact and its projected physical path directly between the two ended up paralleling some of the clashes in relationships between the characters.


    So, yeah, good stuff!

    \n Hmm. Random event.

    What if there was something like the DP cards, but it was a seperate deck developed during play, perhaps between turns? I really like that a player can choose which of the DPs provided to try to work into play, so maybe drawing two and picking one to work in?

    That might work really well for me, when I start to become interested in the story of the secondary characters. For example, instead of exploring them deeply, when can have a bit of a cut scene that tells us just a bit more about them ( even if just in flashback). I suppose the same could be true about other backdrop stuff, too. Some of our background was a bit vague. For example, we never really explored the founding of Rapture, or how and why the Unitarians went there. I think we were all a bit enamored of Jefferson Cho and his character as well.

    So, here, I'm thinking that at the end of each scene, a player simply writes out a simple one sentence topic and tosses it in this other deck. After a full go around of doing scenes, someone draws two of these cards, and picks one to do an "outside" scene revolving around that topic or secondary character.

    I don't know that this secondary deck should accumulate throughout the game, but perhaps should be created after each set of individual scenes, and then rotates through players in the same sequence as the normal character scenes. In this way, it reminds me a bit of some styles of fiction ( especially SF) that I've seen.

    So, the overall sequece might look like:

    Caesar_X PC scene 1
    K-Bob PC scene 1
    Chris P PC scene 1

    Break: All players create outside scene cards.
    Shuffle. Caesar_X draws two cards then starts a scene based on the one he finds interesting.


    Caesar_X PC scene 2
    K-Bob PC scene 2
    Chris P PC scene 2

    Break: All players create outside new scene cards, or alternately re-usesa previous outside scene card.
    Shuffle. K-Bob draws two cards then starts a scene based on the one he finds interesting.


    Caesar_X PC scene 3
    K-Bob PC scene 3
    Chris P PC scene 3

    Break: All players create outside scene cards (or a re-used one).
    Shuffle. Chris P draws two cards then starts a scene based on the one he finds interesting.

    Caesar_X PC scene 4
    K-Bob PC scene 4
    Chris P PC scene 4

    I don't think there needs to be one after the fourth round of scenes, although I suppose a debrief could use those ideas. That might help with keeping the main story focussed on the core characters and their interactions, while having a release valve for the other interesting but slightly tangental topics and characters, while also giving grist for dveleopments in those stories.

    So, a sort of controlled set of evolving random events/topics directed by the interests of the participants, but not so metagamey?

    \n More thoughts on this game after mulling it over for a few nights.

    Posted By: Caesar_XI wanted to throw in mini scene. Or at least establish a truth about something that my character saw. But there was no option for that in the rules.
    \nIf your characters were in each others' vicinity, you could easily jump in with your character in someone else's scene. That would make your character a secondary character for that scene - which means other people could narrate his/her actions, and go "Do it differently, please" on you. The idea behind this is, of course, to make sure that nobody can steal the narrative control from the current player; if you jump into someone else's scene, you're dancing to their tune.\n
    Matthijs, could you talk a little about your campaign structure?
    \nWe've only played twice so far, but the structure is really very simple. Each session starts with generation of Destiny Points for our characters - we don't create new characters or maps. Then we play.

    We've started to gravitate towards each others' characters; specifically, we seem to play a lot of scenes involving General Katashi (a PC), who's stirring up a big war with the Monkey King (an NPC). It's a central conflict, and the character is a powerful man, heavy with destiny. The game seems to favor that sort of character (which wasn't intentional, but certainly is a bonus - it was meant, after all, to emulate the Earthsea books, where everything revolves around Ged and Tenar).

    The characters and their relationships were originally designed for another system. That means we spent more time than implied in the Archipelago rules talking about our characters, and we set up an R-map before play. I will definitely include this in the next version of the rules; I think it gives the campaign a much more solid base to run from.\n
