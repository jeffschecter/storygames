\n I'm not talking about just wandering through a randomly generated dungeon killing randomly generated monsters; I'm thinking about games where the main characters (and their nemeses) would have clearly defined goals that would allow one player to roleplay each in turn as the game progresses - with a bit of a random element to keep it interesting.

A couple of RPGs that I've just been looking at that I think could have potential as solo games (with just a few modifications and additions) are Wushu, 3:16, and (from a quick glance, since I just got it) Maid.

Wushu because the emphasis of the game is the storytelling aspect of combat - which makes that part very easy to do solo. All you'd need is a strong character-driven story-line to link your combat scenes (and that should be easy to get from the kind of source material the Wushu games are based on).

3:16 because it already has a built-in storyline, as well as well-defined combat scenarios (defined abstractly and just needing description to bring them to life). And, of course, the characters' military roles are already very well defined. All that is needed are some personal goals for the characters.

Maid because it has mechanisms for creating all the main characters, their primary goal is part of the setting, and it includes a number of well-defined scenarios. All that is needed is a way to generate define the opponents and combats in detail.

Has anyone else done much along these lines?

    \n I've found it hard to be enthused by something where all the creative input is my own, it just doesn't do anything for me. 2 player games can be fantastic, 1 player, much less so without an external input.

    Now that external input is usually static, as in a computer game, or a choose-your-own-adventure book, where there is a block of pre-written stuff to interact with, and those can be very satisfying story wise, with believable and hatable antagonists, and genuinely compelling situations, but they are, of course, not usually that repeatable. You get a packaged interactive experience rather than a system.

    Could you do the same with some sort of randomized system, where your input is formalized and then reacted too? I think yes, indeed most computer AIs are doing exactly this when they supply opposition in games. Could you do the same with a plot in such a way that it was still engrossing ... maybe, but I have no good ideas how to go about it!

    \n I prefer to run games than to play in them. To me, running a game is enjoyable because you have a basic premise, but the other players add to it with their input. I always describe it as writing a novel around what the players' characters want to do. Looking at it that way, I think you could have just as enjoyable a game (albeit in a slightly different way) if you had a system to create characters with well-defined goals (as well as attributes), and to provide a basic story line.

    \n I've had good experiences using the Mythic Game Master Emulator (essentially a collection of randomizing rules that use both die-rolls and logic to answer the kind of questions that a GM normally answers) to run solo games.

    As far as the 'feel' of the experience, it flips back and forth from feeling like a player to feeling like a GM, at least for me. There are times when I am making decisions essentially in-character as my protagonist, but also times when the Mythic GME has me using logic to interpret it's random input, which is GM-like thinking.

    @ David, specifically: One of the solo games I'm doing with Mythic has drifted into a kind of 'GM mode', in that I have become as interested in the events and story surrounding a character that was originally just a throw-away NPC. I promoted him to PC status, so I now have essentially two PCs, sometimes cooperating, sometimes competing, and sometimes dealing with completely separate storylines. The though processes that requires feel more like being a GM to me than it does like playing two PCs.

    \n Not to nit pick, but I don't see Dungeon Keeper as "play the GM;" it was just that "evil" became the protagonist--the ones you wanted to win, at least--in an otherwise bog-standard RTS(TBS?) game. You didn't control NPCs, you controlled units that opposed "good" adventurer units. One could play D&D as written, with such a flip of pro/antagonist, with or without other players.

    All that said, DK WAS a cool game--I blew HOURS on it, while answering the tech support line at Interactive Magic....

    No, I'm talking about the common, literal GM roles in (non-computer) RPGs today, with the "generators" providing protagonists, antagonists, supporting characters, extras, and even setting or situation (nar hooks). You have more of an "adjudicate the game in operation" and "react to various character actions and motivations" role than a "portray half of the characters" role.

    But I'm just spit-balling.

    The hard part in solo gaming, it seems to me, is there are not good boundaries between the random shit that happens in your brain, the possibilities, vague ideas, and other neural firings -- and the fiction, or the SIS as the kids call it.

    \n

    In a Tunnels & Trolls solo, what happens is written down on the page, so it's solid in that way. (Though limiting)

    \n

    In a proper roleplaying game, what people mutually agree on and remember as a group is the "in-game reality" which is distinguished from random shit in your head.

    \n

    For solo play to have much to grab onto, I would think you'd want a mechanism for recording what happened, that's not so spare it's unclear but not so dense it's tedious to write down...

    \n

    But then I'm not very good at coming up with stories entirely in my head and following along with them. Maybe some people are better than me at it and for them solo gaming wouldn't have this problem.
