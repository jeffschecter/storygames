    \n Lessons learned:

    Personal attacks are usually in jest. And when they're not, its best just to ignore them.

    Always post your reviews (although perhaps not the scores).

    Know what you want out of the contest before you get into it, but be able to be surprised.

    Take reviews seriously, but with a grain of salt. Don't expect a reviewer to have read all of your game. And don't expect a reviewer to be wrong either.

    - Mendel S.

    \n Give yourself a few minutes to sit back and think before submitting your completed entry. Try to objectively compare it to other indie games. Is it like nothing else? Does it promise a gameplay experience you'd be powerfully enthusiastic about if someone else had designed it? Is it hard for you to not see it capturing an enthusiastic fan-base, or critical recognition? And are you also passionate about playtesting, developing, and publishing it? Then consider actually not submitting it to the contest. You may not win the peer review. And even if you do, the recognition will be less enduring than if you develop and publish it without having submitted it to the contest, and not appreciably contributory to the game's future success. Consider that submitting it makes it publicly available to the curious for free. If it's almost fully formed you'll be undercutting future sales.

    Paul

    \n Wow, Paul, that's like totally opposite to the advice I'd give.

    Put everything you have out there, on the line. Every great idea about roleplaying you've ever had in your life, the ones you've been saving for "that big project" you're gonna do down the road that's going to change roleplaying forever. Because you know what? You'll get more great ideas, and you can only make room for them by spending the ones you already have. Game Chef is an opportunity to put the pedal to the metal and design at the top of your game. It's not about making the most polished thing you've ever done, it's just about making people go "&#@ I want to play that right now, because it has just rearranged the contents of my brain in a new and pleasing way." Rock the fuck out. Push those boundries. Take no prisoners. Bleed your passion out on the page.

    \n The thing that saved me last year:

    If the process of designing and writing the game starts to exhaust you, find a way to play the game (line up playtesters now!) or do a related but completely different creative activity that can refuel you. For me it was creating a funky layout that made me have fun mindlessly re-engaging with the colour of the game so that I was jazzed to get back at the writing. Otherwise, draw, write fiction around it... or something else creative that keeps your mind focused but allows it to relax.

    \n On Mo's note:

    Never switch core concepts in the middle of a contest. You won't have time to finish your game. Never, EVER, switch core concepts more than once, because you won't get anything of any substance finished at all. It's better to take a few days and settle on a concept you feel really strongly about instead of pursuing some half-assed idea and then changing in the middle. By all means, follow your passion, but don't follow your whims.
