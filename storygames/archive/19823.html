\n Although I really like the core of GUMSHOE, I find the skill list of Night's Black Agents confusing. I think I understand the idea of having many specific skills and some overlap between them, so that you have different approaches for the same situation.

However, I don't like that some skills seem to be there just because there was that scene from that movie, like Data Recovery or Traffic Analysis, they look too specific. More importantly, I don't get how some general skills can be used as investigative, like Mechanics, but not others, like Digital Intrusion.

Are people expected to memorize how 60 different skills work, with alll their little nooks and crannies, plus the optional rules?

    Yeah, I like to play fiction-first, but maybe the game works better by poiting at a skill on the sheet and saying "I want to do this"?
    \nYou can do it that way, but there's a risk of that being so context-less that it's hard for the GM to make sense of the skill use (and thus come back with what effect it has) and for the player to understand the connection between what he or she did and what the GM says occurs as a result. There needs to be enough fictional grounds for using the skill.

    Of course, I could just be kidding myself. Here are some attempts at running NBA that my brother and I have recorded. These are complete games, but if you fast-forward through them, you will be able to hear episodes of skill use.

    \n So, like, in the second link above (me running NBA at Gencon), right around the 47 minute mark, you can hear an example of Investigative skill use: the players are fleeing the scene of a gunfight with some unknown hostiles who are trying to mess up their meet with a contact. Thinking a little ahead, one player says, "So, can I use Urban Survival to find a safe house somewhere in the city?" And I say, yes, absolutely, spend a point of Urban Survival and tell me where it is. So that's sort of like pointing at the sheet, but it's still connected to the fictional context and to the player's intention for the character.
