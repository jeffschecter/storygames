    \nB5: Horror on the Hill was the first module I bought, and it remains my favourite old school dungeon. I like the outdoor map with all the trails over the surface of the hill -- and the players can decide to leave the trails and cut cross country if they wish. The NPCs the party encounters in the Dungeon is a nice way of getting players who've lost a character back into the game. Best of all -- it's low level (1-3) and uses the Basic rules (B/X) which is how I like D&D; the best.
    \n
    \nI've run several different groups of players through this module over the years, and there are still areas none of them have discovered. The players who panicked and ran from a random night-time encounter with ghouls were playing in this module.

    \n I played (or GMed) heaps of them. I wonder if I can remember all of them in order.

    B2 Keep on the Borderlands: my intro to GMing. We played it on Boxing Day 1980, after I'd got the blue book boxed set for Christmas. I think we played it all out within a week.

    After that I got AD&D. I can't remember how we began that campaign, because A1 seems to be for levels 4-7, but I remember it as our first game. Maybe not. Or maybe we started at level 4.

    A1 Slave Pits of the Undercity
    C2 The Ghost Tower of Inverness
    S2 White Plume Mountain
    S3 Expedition to the Barrier Peaks
    G1 Steading of the Hill Giant Chief
    G2 Glacial Rift of the Frost Giant Jarl
    G3 Hall of the Fire Giant King
    D1 Descent into the Depths of the Earth
    D2 Shrine of the Kuo-Toa
    D3 Vault of the Drow
    Q1 Queen of the Demonweb Pits


    After all that I got interested in starting again from lower levels, and made up my own setting. We did a lot of city adventuring in that one using Gamelords' Free City of Haven. I also started making up my own adventures, but I did use a couple of modules.

    L1 The Secret of Bone Hill
    U1 The Sinister Secret of Saltmarsh
    X2 Castle Amber


    The modules I remember as most fun for us were C2, S2, the G/D series and X2. Good hacky-choppy fun in all of them. I'd never do it again, but it was fun at the time. We also went through the D series (but not the G as well) in one night; with me hiding the module covers so the players wouldn't know what they were getting themselves into. There were about eight of us sitting on the floor around a low wooden bed we used for a table, and we finished as the chrs entered the demon dimension and the sun came up.

    \n Tony... you got through Tomb of Horrors on the first try, with no casualties?
    \n
    \nYour GM was fudging in your favor.
    \n
    \nHere's my experience with Tomb of Horrors. One party goes in, dies quick. The second one is generated, goes in, avoids the pitfalls (sometimes literally) that killed the first party, only to die a little later. So you generate another party. This one gets a little further. Maybe even makes it to the lich and then dies. You get the idea.
    \n
    \n
    \nWhat's fascinating to me about this is that we at no time questioned that the players were using player knowledge to get further into the dungeon. It's a puzzle for the players to work out. Not the characters... it should kill most characters that come inside, presumably not knowing about the Sphere of Anihillation and such fun stuff, for instance.
    \n
    \nThis sort of play clearly promotes "Pawn Stance" - playing your character like a pawn in a game, and not considering having it *not* act using knowledge that only you as a player has (to say nothing of characterization, etc).
    \n
    \nPeople always think that I'm down on these games for this sort of play. I'm not. It's not what I prefer from a TTRPG these days, no. But it was fun then. And it's still fun for me as World of Warcraft now. Tomb of Horrors is simply unrelenting in terms of providing maximum challenge for players.
    \n
    \nMike

    \n Hope this works. This is from a LJ post I made when they announced Tom Moldvay passed away. \n
    For those unfamiliar with Moldvay's work, he was responsible for some of Dungeons & Dragons earlier modules, including one of my all-time favorite adventures, "The Isle of Dread."

    This module, set on a tropical island filled with monsters and new creatures/races, came with every blue-box expert set, and was one of the first modules I ever played or ran. One of the things I remember most about the setting was the freeform nature of the game. You had this huge island to explore, and it was dangerous to set off without a goal in mind. Many players visited just a small portion of the coastline of the Isle of Dread, never realizing the crazy things that waited further inland. An entire race of monkey-like creatures that could glide like flying squirrels, massive tar pits, giant spiders and fearsome dinosaur-like creatures were just a few of the hazards that could be encountered.

    Speaking of tar pits, the Isle of Dread taught me the valuable practice of map making. A party I controlled once wandered into a huge tar pit, and my inability to tell direction (north, south, east, west) from my brother's description led to the entire group drowning because they continued to venture further and further into the tar rather than trying to escape.

    I also remember some of my terror at having to sail to the island. My brother made it a difficult trek, and a huge monster (possibly a Dragon Turtle; my memory fails me) took a good portion of our ship before we made landfall, stranding some of my favorite characters on the isle.

    It was a massive, dangerous and fascinating place, all contained within a relatively small adventure module. And I loved it.
    Moldvay also was credited with contributing to the Fiend Folio, one of the most fearsome gatherings of monsters in the D&D game, and designing or co-designing several other modules that filled my early D&D days, including "The Lost City," "Palace of the Silver Princess" (which introduced my first recurring villain) and "Castle Amber" (again, one of my all-time favorites). Only a few years ago I picked up Moldvay's sequel to Castle Amber, "Mark of Amber," which was part of the then-TSR's audio-enhanced series. I still listen to the CD in my car when I travel.

    A lot of these old modules set the tone and feel of D&D, and even roleplaying in general, for a lot of us gamers who grew up in the 1980s. Moldvay's writing especially became the template I used in creating my own adventures, and moments playing through his modules are still among my most treasured gaming memories.
    \nME

    \n A few months ago, I ran my wife's solo 3rd edition character through the old-school dungeon White Plume Mountain (Module S2). Even though I was trying to be dungeoncrawly, my mind refused to allow the villain to just build a dungeon and advertise its presence with no motivation whatsoever. So I determined that the dungeon was a challenge, like an obstacle course: the immortal evil wizard Keraptis was looking for someone to grant immortality to, so that he could have someone to hang out with who wouldn't die in a couple decades.

    The dungeon itself was pretty good, in that the challenges were nicely varied. But the magical sword Blackrazor that is acquired in the dungeon is cooler than I can easily express in words.
