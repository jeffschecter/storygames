\n So I have been crazy busy. Aside from work, lack of sleep and exciting news for MouseGuard fans and me, I have also been doing a goodly amount of gaming - two to three nights a week. Namely we (when I say we I include a number of different play groups) finished up our high fantasy DitV meets the Iron Kingdoms game, ended our PTA game based loosely on the ideas in Legends Walk, started a new canon DitV game which I will be taking inspiration from the manga PRIEST to write the towns and running a few D&D sessions using Story games techniques.

Anyway, I am here to talk about my SG D&D sessions (as the title suggests). This in reference to a handful of threads started by me and others to do just that. In a brief nutshell my goal was to run a narrative based session of D&D yet using the lion share of D&D rules. I still plan on trying a mini campaign but I wanted to run a few practice games first to test out some techniques.

Okay, so first the caveats. If you're going to try heavy Story Game techniques with a bunch of hippie gamers then all is well. If you're going to introduce some of these ideas into your game group and they are all completely on board and looking forward to a new style of play, then by all means try it out.

That being said. I have run this twice now and both times were met with some resistance. The story gamers involved took it all in stride and were comfortable with much of what was going on (yet they often noted that the limits of the approach were often attributed to the system itself and not the SG applications). I have a couple of friends that do play a bunch of SG games (namely due to doing half of their gaming with me). However, when they come to the table to play D&D they plan on doing just that and doing it the way they had been doing it before being introduced to hippie games.

The moral of the story... the big problem with running things this way is that I find myself breaking social contract (my own personal inviolate grail) before the players even come to the table. I mean there was no outright dysfunction as we all like each other and trust one another however there was a goodly amount of grumbling or concern afterward. Rob Bohl was privy to the first game and I ran another with some alterations. I will talk about techniques more later but I'd like to hear some thoughts before I do.

So what I am looking for is comments on your thoughts about social contract and where our responsibilities lie. Normally I deal with social contract at the table - here is the game we're playing and here are the ground rules we all agree to. But now I am realizing that there are assumptions folk come to the table with regarding playstyle based on prior experience. It hadn't occured to me yet as I thought about it makes perfect sense - how do you keep up social contract at all when using a game someone else knows? I mean, even if I say "I'm playing DitV," I cannot be certain HOW someone else played Dogs and what their experience is. Concievably I am running into expectation issues even before we sit down to play.

Thoughts?
- Don

    \n I think you've hit the nail on the head by identifying this as a social contract breach. "D&D" is one relatively well-defined thing; "Story Games" is another. Telling your players you are going to play "D&D" and then playing Story Games is something of a bait-and-switch.

    That said, I think you could still do "Greyhawk using Story Games" or even "Dungeon Crawling using Story Games." Just identify the thing that you want to play and say that, rather than use the trademarked product name to refer to something that isn't that product.

    \n One thought I had is that story games (to the extent I can define that term) seem to require active participation from all particpants for the story to unfold. Even story games with a strong GM need players to provide fully fleshed out characters and descriptions of their actions. In a non-story game (at least at the extreme end), as long as someone moves the party from point A to point B, you really don't need everyone's input until its dice rolling time.

    Some people may not feel comfortable engaging in a game where their active participation in story crafting is a requirement for fun.

    \nLuckily, there is no real, solid, binding definition (at least none that I've seen that I'd really point to). It could be a game where the GM sets up a relationship chart at the beginning! Or, it just might be a shoe! Who knows!

    Seriously, the name started as kind of a joke, and it just stuck. There are some things that some of these hippie games do (set up "situation" before play, either through the GM's mouth or through player collaboration; more rules on guiding story, relationships, motivations and other non-physical stuff; scene framing, etc), some things that a very few games do (No GM, high player collaboration, etc), but we (I or whomever) hasn't bothered to draw a line, saying "X Y and Z make a game a Story Game".

    The whole thing developed out of a hubub on RPGNet some two years ago or so. There were these great (as in "big", decidedly not as in "good") debates on various games: "My Life With Master is not a Roleplaying Game, because it has an endgame condition. Oh, it's a game alright, and a game where you Play a Role, but it's not a Real Roleplaying Game". Same with Dogs in the Vineyard, Capes, Polaris, Universalis (well, ok, on that front I agree to a point; even the creator says it's not an RPG :-) ), etc. You play a role. And it's a game. But... it's not a roleplaying game. Because of stuff like relationship rules, or endgame rules, or deciding what happened up front and then playing towards that, etc.

    So what the fuck are these games, then?

    Clinton, back on some blog post he made, said, "I dunno. Maybe we should call them "Story Games"?" (or maybe it was someone else ON Clinton's blog, I forget now)

    It stuck.

    Oh, they're not roleplaying games? Well shit, I guess we'll have to call them Story Games or someshit then.

    Originally, I was going to use this title as the name of my gaming company (Game: Coming Soon! ...Maybe!), but later, when I decided to make a friendly small-press-centric goober fan forum, I decided to use the domain name for that instead.

    Maybe some day people will put together a list of definitive qualities. I hope not. If anything, I hope it doesn't get any more complicated than Jeff Foxworthy's "You Might be a Redneck If..." tongue-in-cheek qualifier list:

    "You May Be a Story Gamer if..." (Insert Own Punchline Here*) and so on.

    Just wanted to explain that bit. Storytime Over

    \nI was, in fact, debating for the sake of debate, that I hadn't changed the rules. no doubt it was a different game they were playing. I was purely speaking to the written word and the literal and not to the spirit of the game itself. I more added "rules" than changed them, I'd say but, again, it was a VERY different game than normally expected out of D&D.

    I don't think it was a matter of unfair. Rather, several folk enlightened me to their expectations and what they got was a different experience. It was hard to quantify at first but I think I figured out part of the issue:

    D&D and games like at are about expectations, specifically. They are about controlling the odds and the action through the mechanics in very finite quantifiable terms. When I played it I used to do the very same thing. I would be assured that the adversary would only have X spells and only so many of them. He could only move X spaces based on his level, class, race and certain feats. He could only jump so high based on his Strength and Jump scores. When the GM acted outside these limits I would call him on it - not to say he can't do that but to let him know that I knew he broke the rules.

    In a Story Game the focus is on good story. I use as much of the general game mechanics as possible focusing on modifiers more than squares and rounds. But I think that the story effect of the scene often works outside those parameters that the players expect.

    For example: I played a game recently for a friend trying to learn more of the mechanics and she wanted me to be "that difficult gamer that knows the rules." So we got into the final battle with a spell caster. I cast Touch of Idiocy to lower her Will save and my cohort cast Hold Person. End of combat. Heck. We didn't even have to kill her. We tied her up and put her under water until she went unconscious. By the time she made her save round to round she was done.

    In another game I played a grappler. We faced some big bad elementalist and his elemental minions. Someone cast flight and enlarge on me and I charged and pinned him. That's all I did. Pin and some damage every round. My grapple check was so high there was such a small chance he could break free. The bard convinced the elementals that they were free to go back to their home planes (we accidentally found some doorways to the planes). Done. The mage got off one fireball that did some damage because he rolled a 20 and I rolled a 1 to break the pin. I reapplied it.

    In a Story Game you just don't ever get those lockdowns unless everyone at the table wants that. More soon on my techniques.

    Posted By: JDCorley
    More importantly, ifyou yourself personallydidn't enjoy your tactics, or the reasonably forseeable results of your tactics, why in the world did you use them?
    \nI used to run into that problem a lot when I was playing D&D. In a game where advancing the plot often depends on killing stuff, you want your character to be really good at killing stuff. At the same time though, when killing stuff is such a big part of the game, you want the process of killing stuff to feel exciting and dangerous. It can be very difficult to achieve the former without sacrificing the latter -- especially for spellcasters, who get all kind of effective but boring "save-or-die" type spells.

    The last time I played a wizard in a D&D game, it struck me that in combat I was faced with a dilemma very similar to the one I faced as a DM -- trying to pull my punches just enough to be effective in combat without spoiling anyone else's fun. Instead of making tactical decisions from a "what's the best way to defeat this opponent" standpoint, it was more of a "hmm, I could cast this save-or-die spell and end the encounter before anyone else even gets to go, or I could cast this insanely damaging spell and leave the fighter feeling totally outclassed, but that's lame too... oh, wait, I know! The rogue hasn't been getting many chances to sneak attack lately, so I'll cast improved invisibility on her to help her out."

    Kind of an interesting tactical exercise, I supposed, but certainly not the one I signed up for when I sat down to roll up a character.

    \n Well, here's the thing: the D&D rules as written make it very likely that a PC will be killed at some point. It's a game where most conflicts boil down to "succeed or die," and since you can't succeed all the time, you can expect that sooner or later a character's going to go toes-up on you.

    Which, incidentally, is exactly why you see people, including yourself, playing their D&D characters as incredibly cautious rather than as daring risk-takers. Unlike a story game, the standard-issue D&D game will kill a PC. It will kill a PC randomly, at an undramatic time, in a manner that does not significantly add to the story. You will eventually get a bad roll at the same time that the GM gets a good roll, and your character will be d-e-a-d. And that blows, for both the player and for any game in which there is a continuing story that the PC is actively involved in. Which is probably why you don't see a lot of story games where someone's character can be removed from the game randomly and without anyone's consent.

    So, yes, I will reiterate: Raise Dead is a safety net. It's a way of taking the default stakes of a D&D conflict (victory or death) and turning them into something smaller and less permanent, providing more options for the GM and players alike.

    You can institute other safety nets, of course: I think their Eberron setting added action dice, which can be spent in ways that will reduce/eliminate the unplanned-death problem. A lot of people have a house rule that says you're not dead until you're at -X or -2X hitpoints, where X is your Constitution score. You can even explicitly state a "I'm not going to kill your characters when I can capture them, maim them, or otherwise make them miserable instead" clause when you're pitching your campaign. All of these are wonderful options and I totally recommend them. But just 'cause you don't like Raise Dead doesn't change the fact that it's there to protect the PCs...and by extension, to protect the story.


    Getting back to the story-games thrust of this topic, I think the single best use of story game mechanics in D&D is using them to set up conflicts other than "succeed or die." Once the random mortality of the system is mitigated in some way, either by changing what most encounters are about or even just by house-ruling an escape clause for PCs, D&D ends up being nearly as good at hippie-game style fun as a system expressly designed for it.

    With sudden permanent character death off the table, everyone's more free to develop deeper, more interesting characters. There's less reason to play the "I search for traps every 10 feet" and "Let's just camp here tonight and heal" cards. There's more room for complicated, PC-driven plots to spring up. You can start to mix in novel-like elements like skipping over boring stuff (because now you know the characters are going to survive to reach the good parts). You can even make the climax of the story bigger and cooler by putting PC death back on the table for it, and it will finally mean something to the players because it will be new and different from all the other encounters that came before.
