So...

For the sake of argument, say I have a Stealth Project.

(I have a Stealth Project)

Now that y'all have gotten that out of your system (pun intended), what would you pay for it, and what would you prefer to pay for?

Over at Wicked Press, I see items for prices I wouldn't pay: $6/16 pages, for example.

On the other hand, I did pay for the novelty of a low-numbered steel-bound Judge Dredd d20. I think it cost me $100 plus postage from the UK to the US. I don't play d20 anymore, but I'm never parting with that book if I have a choice.

Now, I know there are people who would pay for page count. Take that out of the equation. The product is what it is. I won't pad it or trim it. We also have to assume that the game is unique, eminently playable, thoroughly playtested, and something that piques your interest.

I'm looking for ways to attract interest beyond the actual content of the book -- something to draw people who havne't heard of me, or who wouldn't otherwise consider the book for just its content alone. Call it marketing gimmicks if it makes you happy, but I want customers to feel like they're getting something extra for paying a premium.

Here's a few options (feel free to add more if it would increase the price in a way that wouldn't alienate you):

More art: as much as a piece per page/spread, with text designed around the art.
Novelty covers: simple (heavy stock) to complex (special materials) to ridiculous (handmade)
Special paper: see covers above
Special printing: from colored ink only, to specialty inks, to hand calligraphy or something similar
Special supplementals: remember the candy bar from AFMBE? Or the roach from the Roach?

And I guess, most importantly, what's your upper limit? People are already paying $100 for books, and a lot more for stuff that's out of print. If I come out with a very limited edition, what would be the most you would pay? And if I were to release it to a wider audience, as a limited edition owner, would you care whether they had "normal" print copies, or would you prefer they only have access to PDFs?

Now, I realize I'm not all that well known, so feel free to factor that in.
But be sure to put in your upper limit price for the same design by {your favorite author.}

I understand this is very subjective. I expect people to qualify their reactions, but please include dollar values with your comments so I have something to work from.

    Actual play is handy, but I'm more likely to buying because there's something to steal, so I'll start with interesting.

    There are definitely games for which I've "overpaid", which is to say, paid far in excess of the apparent page count and production. With the exception of the Game of Thrones Deluxe edition, which was a whole other set of reasoning, I am more likely to do this at a convention, though the reasons for this are mixed. Having people who can speak about the product definitely helps, but being able to flip through the product also makes a big difference. My FLGS doesn't shrinkwrap (well, at least one of them doesn't) but they're unlikely to have the smaller games on their shelf, so conventions are the only opportunity I have to physically pick this stuff up.

    Two notable examples are both from the Wicked Dead booth: John Wick's "Wilderness of Mirrors" and Annie Rush's "House of Horiku". I paid $8-10 for Horiku (which is 12 pages, staple bound, no color - very minimal) and I think $20 for Mirrors, which is only a little bit bigger, but has one of the most fantastic covers you will ever see. I picked both up at Gencon, and had not planned on getting (or had even heard of) either beforehand.

    Now, I feel like I got a good deal on both. Mirrors has proven itself as awesome several times (plus, it has cachet) and Horiku had a cool, steal-able XP mechanic. But if I were purely crunching the numbers on pages, words and such, both games were ripoffs, so why did I pay the premium?

    First off, Mirrors really looked _that_ good. It makes you _want_ to pick it up. And as soon as you do, that's an opening for the pitch, which is succinct and interesting. It's a game for doing one thing - planning intensive espionage - and it's a thing which both interested me and for which I could not think of a ready alternative. Also, it had John Wick's name on it, which I have faith in. All in all, it seemed expensive, but it had all the hallmarks that reminded me that I wasn't paying for volume, I was paying for the total product, and once you switch over to that thinking, the sale is easier.

    Horiku was an upsell, on fairly spurious grounds. I had mentioned that the Scorpion book had resulted in me laughing my ass off, so Wick pushed a ninja game on me. Since I was already engaged with him, and the upsell had reasonable grounds, I failed to politely dismiss it our of hand, so I picked it up to look at it. Now, in this case, production worked against it. It's clean and professional, but it's production is within the bounds of what can be accomplished with a binding stapler, some cardstock and a laser printer. This did not look bad, but since I can do the same thing at home, spending 8 bucks seems overkill. However, there was definitely a social component, and Annie Rush makes good stuff - her name wasn't as much of a sell as Wick or Sorensen, but it's got legs. Of all things, I was sold by the character sheet, which used some symbology I was unfamiliar with, but which looked interesting. That was enough to tip it into the realm of curiosity purchase, where the under $10 price tag was ok.

    Now, notably, I was pleased enough with Horiku that I later grabbed another one of Rush's games in a similar format, and it was much less to my taste. Bad for me, but nicely illustrative of the fact that I had fully made the transition to buying the product.

    Buying at the FLGS is similar, except with less engagement, so issues of appearance and layout matter more. Appearance will impact whether I pick it up at all. I am unlikely to buy a game for it's layout, but a game with a good layout is likely to make it easier for me to get a grasp of what it's _about_. Beyond that though, FLGS shopping kind of exists in a vacuum, which is kind of a shame. It's worth noting that I'm _less_ likely to buy a funky edition of something at the game store, since it will either be sealed (and thus unavailable to review) or open (and thus probably less pristine than I'd like). Even more, the cost on hand is almost always going to be too high for an impulse buy. If I'm getting a deluxe edition, it's because I ordered it.

    Anyway, online, is a different ball game. Among other things, I have the option of ordering a PDF, and when I purely intend to rummage through a game for ideas, that's not a bad approach. The shorter the game, the more likely I am to just get the PDF, since longer games are harder to read onscreen. I am reasonably likely, if I dig a game, to then pursue a print copy, but if I can get a deal on it because I bought the PDF, all the better.

    I will pay more when I feel the company is treating me well, even if it costs them little or nothing. Matt Snyder made a sale of the revised ed of 9 worlds based pretty much entirely on the classy fashion in which he handled the upgrade from first to revised. In contrast, EOS press is in the middle of alienating me as a customer at the moment as they released the PDF of their WOTG companion this summer, and printing and shipping problems have meant that the book has still not shipped (and I know this only because I check their boards occasionally). I can still buy the PDF at full price, though.

    Anyway, bottom line for me: Gimmicks may sell your first product, or at least catch my eye. Customer Service is going to sell or not sell your subsequent ones, and if proactive enough, might sell me the first one too. I might buy you're game because it's awesome. I'm more likely to buy your game if _you're_ awesome.

    -Rob D.
