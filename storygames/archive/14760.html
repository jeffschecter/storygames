\n So, I'm interested in learning more about games that use tokens as central part of their mechanics. By tokens I mean any physical thing or do-hicky or widget that represents a resource in the game. I'm especially interested in games which involve some sort of economy or exchange or dynamism with respect to the tokens. (If any of you should be so inclined, please don't quibble with the term "token." That's not what this thread is about. Also don't say things like "Hit Points are basically tokens!" Because they aren't. Assume it is a 12 year old using the work "token," not a part-time philosopher, and read sympathetically.).

Tell me the name of the game and give me a simple and quick summary of how tokens/the token economy works in the game. Please focus on games that use tokens as a central mechanic-- Universalis or Do, for example, but not Bennies in Savage Worlds (where Bennies/Tokens are important, but not central to the primary mechanics). And please don't assume that I know much of anything about the game, because chances are that I don't.

    \n I am uncertain what you mean by "central". Ironically, while I've heard of both Universalis and Do, I've not had a chance to play either yet.

    That said, I'll make some assumptions. I presume you mean where the token economy is a core part of the game play, such that removing the token economy breaks some or all of the game. I'll start with Fate - for which the tokens are unarguably a first class mechanic. In this system, you use fate points to do all sorts of crazy stuff, including tagging an aspect (calling on parts of the story in a mechanical way), and compelling (getting people to do stuff that's interesting to the story).

    Next up would be Don't Rest Your Head, in which players end up getting despair coins, which then get converted to hope coins, both times influencing the effects of the game.

    Both of these games are under the auspice of Evil Hat, so there's that.

    I would argue that Prime-Time Adventures should be on your list, in which an economy of fan-mail goes to players, and is spent to make those characters more effective in a scene. While this fails the separability test I mentioned earlier, it's such an important part of the system that I can't imagine playing PTA without fan-mail.

    <self-promotion>And then there's our game, Becoming Heroes, in which Destiny/Doom are used to fuel threads (meta-narrative hacks) and to provide for the kind of world-ripples you see in epic fantasy.</self-promotion>

    Are you looking for just a starting point, or a discussion about how such mechanics work?

    \nBest Friends pretty much only has a token mechanic and nothing else; each character has 5 stats rated from 0 to 3 (or maybe more, but not likely) and when the GM decrees that you are trying to do something beyond the level you have in that stat, you have to give one of your tokens to another player. At the character creation stage, you specify which other player character your tokens will go to for each stat, e.g. for your 'Smart' stat, anytime you have to push it for a success, you must pay a token to Player #2 and so on. Everyone starts with a limited nuber of tokens, so if you've given all yours away, you have to push other player characters into doing things that will send tokens back your way.
    \n
    \nAndrew Kenrick's forthcoming Lost Days of Memories & Madness uses character traits as physical tokens; the PCs battle over the rights to memories, which are recorded on index cards, and each character is defined by the memories they currently have; even once you have a memory, other players can attempt to steal it off you and you may have to sell your memories to pay off your debts, which are incurred in the form of the dice value of each memory.

    There are a couple of rather big ones in my collection that have informed my personal direction in game design over the last bit:

    \n

    Marvel Universe RPG: Surprisingly, despite all the other static that went on around its release, its a legitimately good game. With the vast amount of examples in any and all of the supplements, plus whole tonnes of rules discussion in the setting expansion books ... yet still few enough pages that require really knowing that you can really grasp it on a number of levels quickly, it just works. MURPG is a system that demands trying it blind before you'll really get it, but get it you will. The token economy is the very center of the conflict resolution system, managing them as resources, knowing how much you can afford to spend toom long before you get to the end of your rope. It builds good drama and things resolve right.

    \n

    MSG(tm): Seemed to just get lost off the tracks, but the whole thing is about building up resources which you can then bid secretly on project conflicts and augment them with arguments provided only to generate more resources as a fight ... Where everyone in the group is working their own angle, collaboratively, for mass screwage. Its one of my fave game designs in general.

    \n This might be applicable. Fourpenny Touch is set in Victorian London and the main mechanic uses Victorian pennies. Each player has a stack of 10. When you need to resolve a scene, you pick and toss a number of coins from 1 to 4. If you get any heads, you succede. However heads are discarded so your stack always goes down and it's very significant for the game when anyone runs out of coins.

    \n In Western City there is no Game Master. Narration rights are bidded for with tokens. Everybody starts with 5 Tokens and at the end of the scene they are redistributed in the favors of those that didn't get their say in this scene. It's America, therefore it's American auction style, where you have to place your bet in the pot no matter whether you win or not. The bidding mechanic is used in other places as well: You bid for the extras before the actual game. You can get bonuses when fighting, or even add mooks. (5 / \u0e23\u0e01 chip). Extra coolness bonus applied if you put an earthen bowl in the middle of the table which makes authentic "plock plock" sounds. Just sayin'.

    \n Mortal Coil has nothing but tokens, and three relatively small pools of tokens at that.

    What makes these tokens able to express a wide range of outcomes is that there are three methods for spending them: one in which you get them back at the close of the action, one in which you get them back at the start of the next session, and one in which you burn them permanently.

    With three types of increasingly weighty tokens and three ways to spend them, it makes for some very interesting tactics, though it never gets complicated.

    Good stuff, Mortal Coil, I recommend playing it 1,000 times.

    Again, I've heard of Nobilis, but I haven't read or played it. Could you tell me how tokens are used in the game? (Double points for a short example!)

    \n
    \n

    Here's the very-basic lowdown:

    \n

    Say your character Marsiglio needs to catch the train to Tokyo before the bomb inside it goes off. Only problem is, he's in Munich! He decides to use his Aspect (the ability to do awesome things with miraculously supported human action) to run really fast! He needs to perform a level 6 Aspect miracle (number made up) to run fast enough.

    \n

    Marsiglio has Aspect 4. That means that he can perform Aspect miracles up to level 4 for free. If he wants to do higher-level miracles, he needs to spend tokens (Miracle Points), in chunks of 1, 2, 4, or 8 (I don't know why this is true but it is). So, he needs to spend 2MP to get to the Tokyo bullet train in time.

    \n

    Marsiglio spends 2MP and gets there.

    \n

    On the way, he runs into a friend of his and his friend asks to bum a cigarette. Because Marsiglio has the Cigarette Bond Affliction, he must hand over a cigarette and stop to have a smoke. Precious time is slipping through his fingers! Because of his Affliction, the HG says, "You get there in time, but only just, and you don't have the luxury of concealing your miraculous actions from regular folks. This will attract some attention." Marsiglio gets 1 MP because his Affliction caused trouble for him!

    \n Hi, Stephen!

    I wrote a game called http://storybythethroat.wordpress.com/games/, which runs entirely on tokens.

    How it works: three players portray, respectively: a Heroine with mundane adolescent trauma who goes to Faerie to work out her shit, the Dark Faerie Nemesis who tries to break her or seduce her to his way of being, and a Light Faerie Companion who travels with her and offers material aid and moral support.

    Each player has cards with stones on them: A Gift and Flaw for the Heroine, a Nemesis and two Powers for the Dark Faerie, and a Companion card for the Light Faerie.

    When the Dark Faerie player confronts the Heroine with a Peril, he places stones from the Peril's source (Nemesis or Power) in a bag. Heroine player decides how to react to it (Gift or Flaw), pulls any number of stones from that card, and places them in the bag. She reaches blindly into the bag and pulls one out.

    If it's Light, the Peril goes well for her, and she describes how. If it's Dark, the peril goes badly, and the Dark Faerie describes how.

    The person describing places the stone back on a card (the rest are discarded). They can place it on any of their own cards, OR on the card their opponent used in the Peril. This represents gaining some kind of control over, mastery of, or resistance to that element. So the Dark Faerie can gain some influence over the hero's Gift or Flaw, the Heroine can learn to cope with or even utilize a Power, and so on!

    (The Light Faerie can use their Companion card to interrupt the Heroine and face a Peril in her stead. They then do all the stuff with Stones just like the Heroine would.)

    When you've got stones on an opponent's card, you can use that to create or face Perils. So the Dark Faerie turns the Heroine's Flaw against her, the Heroine subverts or co-opts a Power, and so on.

    And when the all the Stones have dwindled down to all Light or all Dark, the journey is at an end, and the Heroine either retjurns home bearing all the lessons learned, or stays trapped in Faerie for good!

    Hope that helps! What sort of application for Token mechanics are you looking for?
