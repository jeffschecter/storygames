Fear of commitment

TonyLB
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
So, many games I play these days let you make explicit decisions that have massive consequences for the length of your campaign. As a single for-instance: in Primetime adventures you choose an Issue for your character, and that issue massively shapes absolutely everything that follows.

This makes me nervous. What if I do it wrong? Can I really trust myself to get the right Issue? And if I don't, and everyone else builds their characters to interact with my issue, I'm pretty much stuck with playing it and having it suck for ... what ... five sessions? Nine? Depends on the length of the game, of course.

It's a fear of commitment, pure and simple. Do other people feel it too?

    I also think about this. IME, I most often "find" what issue I want to address after play starts.

    Because of this, I'm leery of the current trend of front-loading thematic issues---aka "flagging" issues at the very start of play.

    Awhile ago, someone... Emily Care Boss maybe, commented that freeform often passes through two phases. The first is the experimental stage, where players don't know who their characters are and what direction play should travel. After a while, a certain direction will emerge. At that point, players usually shift modes and start pushing that direction.

    You can see this progression in most RPGs, IME. And I think that's something designers should be aware of and try to design for, that players won't "find themselves" until after playing a little while.

    December 2006 edited December 2006
    Tony & Tim -- the concern is a real one, and the thing that solves the problem for me are self-correcting mechanics. They could be a little more explicit in the text of PTA, but to take it as an example, (a) the pilot allows you to try things out and see if you want to retool things before you start the 'real' season, and this includes your issue; and (b) you can change your issue during or after your spotlight episode, so if you were worried about doing it wrong, you could simply make your spotlight episode the second or third episode, giving you the option of switching it out for something different later.

    Similarly, Keys can be bought off, Thematic Batteries can be swapped out, and BITs can be reshuffled and rewritten. (These also have the added bonus of redundancy; if one isn't working for you, you have a couple more that might do better.) Now, I do think it would be a good idea for any game that front-loads content or themes like these to have a little sidebar or something for the folks who worry about getting it wrong, explicitly telling them that they can change things in play, and pointing out optimal strategies for doing so.

  • Tim C Koppang
    December 2006
    Tony,

    This sort of thing happens all the time when I play Hero's Banner. Of course, in HB your character will be dead in a session or two, so the long-term commitment is minimized. Nevertheless, the game still forces players to choose what they want their characters to be about at the start of the game and that's a big deal.

    In more general terms, I'm not quite so leary of this type of mechanic as I once was. At least I know what I'm getting into when I start a game of HB or whatever. Many games, on the other hand, have a front-loading problem that they don't even telegraph to the player. You can even think about this in terms of D&D. What happens if you choose Ranger class, and realize two sessions later that you hate playing a Ranger and all of the baggage that comes with it? Too bad, you're stuck and you didn't even realize you were making a big commitment to begin with. Ok, sure you can convince the other players in any game to retool things for you. Still, I think there's a difference between a game like PTA, Breaking the Ice, HB, etc., where the making of these important decisions is explicit in the rules, and other games where you have to tease out the fact that you're doing the same thing but without any mechanics to help you out.

    I suppose you can look at any game in terms of (1) how much commitment to theme, etc., you have to make before play starts, (2) to what degree does the game help you or at least formalize the process of making these decisions in the pre-game stage, and finally (3) how much does the game allow you to make changes (things like buying off keys in TSoY) once the game gets rolling. I can't think of any game that doesn't require at least some commitment during the pre-game, but surely the degree to which that commitment affects or even defines the actual play to come can vary greatly. And as with any gaming preference, these sorts of things can be varied from a design point of view to suit different styles and players. I don't, for instance, think that all heavily pre-loaded games should necessarily have heavy-handed mechanics available to the players to facilitate change in the actual play stage. But playing that sort sort of game would definitely require some serious forethought.

    Hi Antti,

    The people who've replied have asked a lot of questions. I'm posting instead to say, it might not be a big deal, and to offer some possible explanations for what you are encountering.

    1. In 1994 or so, I and a few friends had not role-played for two or three years, and decided to start playing together. It was terrible. We spent ages doing the simplest things, just like you describe. For some reason, everyone had 100 opinions but no one was good at getting anything started in actual play. Looking back, I think that all of us were so concerned with how games had not been fun in the past, that we were too hesitant and too uncertain. We did everything to avoid trouble rather than to have fun.

    2. I think you are right about the problems with authority. The real trouble, in my view, is that simply saying "the GM does it" is actually not a very good solution; it puts the pressure for enjoyment onto a single person for too long, for instance. We can talk about this a little bit more later.

    3. I've observed the pressure you've described many times. I think it's based more on fear than reality. If the people are accustomed to playing with one person have total scene-authority - and also the responsibility for it to be "good" - then they get the idea that any input of that kind also has to be masterful, authoritative, prepared, and generally idealized as "good GMing." They can't see it, yet, as just throwing the ball up into the air and not knowing where or how it will land. So there you are, sitting at the table, and bam! You have to do it (make the scene, take "story responsibility," be good, be perfect, "do it right," et cetera) and it seems like everyone is looking at you.

    However, the reality is much easier. In many ways, it is like learning how not to use certain muscles when doing something athletic - rather than having dozens of responsibilities at the moment, you really only have one or two, leaving the rest up to others' input and to the interactions during the scene itself. Instead of concerning yourself with the perfect setup, you begin with the minimum necessary and then only concern yourself with playing within the scene.

    My main point is, have patience. All of what you're experiencing is like shaking the dust and dead bugs out of the bedsheets. It's kind of gross and icky at the moment, but it has to be done, and the end result is much more pleasant.

    Best, Ron    "

    Here's one of many useful words: "...leaving the rest up to others' input and to the interactions during the scene itself."

    In other words: start and trust. You will find a way through the process of character creation, the process of the scene, the process of the session. But you'll do it by trusting the act of creativity and intereaction with others.

    What we're talking about here is peformance anxiety. Trust me, I know all about this. When I was finishing the re-write on the script, my body was flush, I kept pacing, my head was dizzy. I had to force myself to sit down and write, almost line by line, the last four pages. Because I knew I couldn't make it any better and I was going to have to show it to people!

    But I'm suffering through the same thing on a new script I'm writing right now. Same stuff. I'll be thinking, "This idea isn't good enough." Or, "This scene won't work." Whatever. It's the same anxiety a musician or an athlete has before going out to do the job.

    One of the things I find really smart about the talk about people putting story first in their games is exactly this point about performance anxiety. If Gamism is gamig the rules for prestige, then Narrativism is being creative for prestige. You know what is good (even you if actually CAN'T know it ahead of time) and the mind and body begin to freak out beause you want to create well. It is NO different than being in a room of writers working on a script, being on a set in front of a cast and crew while trying to do the best work you can.

    [continued p. 2]

    So, I'd say the following.

    1) From my reading on the matter, understand that this is GOOD. We want to do well, and your body is pumping us up to be ready -- even making us feel, as Attii says, "Write four relationships into the protagonist sheet." Suddenly, it becomes a matter of life and death. It clearly isn't a matter of life or death, but our body doesn't know how to modulate that.

    2) Don't try to get rid of the fear or anxiety. Just do the deed. Focusing on the anxiety will not help. Doing the deed will.

    3) Trust that something good will happen if you focus on the deed. In fact, this is one of the real draws of games for making stories with people -- you're all there to support the process. When I get stuck in a scene these days I STILL get that panic that says, "Oh, no, I picked the wrong scene, in fact, this scene won't work, in fact, the whole idea for what I've done so far sucks and I should go back and start again..."

    But I don't. Cause I'm smarter now. And I think, "In ways I cannot yet see, I've picked all the choices up to this point for a reason. And I picked this scene for a reason. And I'm going to finish this scene, and get to the other side of it, and by doing that, I'll see the next scene, and I'll then see why I picked the scene I'm working on, and I'll be really glad I wrote through it."

    In other words, I'm trusting my creative process now. I'm trusting my imagination. A basketball player doesn't have time to think, "Do I know all the moves I'm going to need to get me through the game I'm about to play?" He can't be thinking about whether he picked he right coach back in high school. He's got to play the game NOW. And only by playing the game, moment by moment, with what has come before -- by BUILDING ON whatever choices have come before and not second-guessing himself, does he have a shot at getting the ball in the net and working effectively with his team.

    I'm offering the same to you. Pick an "Issue" for PtA and trust that from that choice you will make other choices that work. Since the whole point of PtA is to keep moving forward through scenes that spin variation on the Issue, all you need to do is start one scene after another that deal with the Issues. Trust me, if you start, you can finish. And inbetween you'll find a way of addressing the issue in an entertaining way. Especially if you don't start the scene knowing how you'll do that!

    The only way to go wrong is to second guess and decide you already made a mistake before or during the scene. The human imagination is ridiculous -- just ridiculous -- in it's ability to spin gold from simple ideas. You must trust that you will find ideas as needed.

    4) The Issue can be changed. Joshua was explicit about how the rules can help in this. I was blunt and said, "Look, if someone has a better idea, change it." But I wouldn't do it because it's "not working." That's running from the challenge. And it's by walking through the challenge (like stepping onto the basketball court and plaing) that one finds solutions one could never have expected or planned before play.

    Know that simple ideas, like an Issue in PtA, is your ally in the effort to produce scene material, not an enemy.

    This is so vital I reall need you to pay attention.

    When a painter paints, he limits his palet. He does this for two reasons: One, the human eye can handle only so many colors at a time before they go all fuzzy; and Two, it lets him handle what colors he is using better, so he can be more effective in complentary and contrasting colors and such.

    In other words, by limiting his pallet, he becomes more effective as an artist.

    How? Because he has provided Unity to the painting. And we human beings love Unity. We love knowing things "line up" in a story or piece of art even if we are not aware of how it's Unified. One of the things that Unifies the movie Aliens is that it's a struggle between two mothers -- even if many (most?) audience members didn't pick up on that. Same with a limited choice of colors for a painting.

    However, too much Unity means boredom. So we mix the colors and we get Variety. If I'm working with, say Red and Green as my two colors, it looks like I'm really stuck. But I'm not. Because if I mix red and green, they provide variations on red and green. And if I mix them in equal proportions I get a nice neutral gray because they're contrasting colors and cancel each other out. So I get this range of of colors and values I can use to create apples on a green cloth if I want, with the mixed colors providing form shadows and shadows which provide depth and the illusion of form.

    Unity and Variety.

    Okay. We freak beause we want to pick the "right" Issue or whatnot, when that's not the issue at all. The game (the creative game) is to pick an Issue that turns you on and then spin Variety from it in one scene after another.

    But how does one know an Issue will do this? Beause ALL issues provide scene material. How does one know one's brain is the right brain for this issue? Because human beings are fucking awesome and will always provide more material.

    The Value of the Issue (or the Humanity Scale in Sorcerer, or the Dark Fates in Mountain Witch or whatever) is that they focus our creative thinking. They are our allies. They provide Unity so we're not running all over our imagination's inner-landscape, rummaging through this or that to find SOMETHING to focus on.

    By limiting ourselves right off the bat we can move forward with the variety.

    In other words, it's a limitation that's not a limitation. You're never "stuck" with the wrong Issue. What you have is a springboard for countless, entertaining moments.

    If you go into each scene knowing -- and I mean, absolutely knowing -- that if you keep moving forward with the scene you will find the entertaining Variation on that Issue that will reveal something about the character and be entertaining. You may not hit it out of the park on every scene.... But who does that with anything. But you will NEVER be stuck.

    6) Now you might be thinking, "What if it's boring scene material? Or boring to me?" And all I can say is, I believe that if you chose it for genunine interest during character creation it's because it really does speak to you.

    I'll go further -- from my own experience and the work I've done with other writers, usually when we're back peddling (not always, but usually) it's because we know the idea (that Issue or whatever Unifying element turned us on) is so close, so warm that we're afraid of it. We're afraid of what we might discover or what we might reveal. Which is, of course, gold. So move forward.

    ***
    If you thought of an Issue and it tickled you in one way or another, trust it.

    When you get scared of it, trust it.

    When you don't know what to do with it when you're starting a scene, trust it.

    The anxiety isn't ours to control (outside of leaving behind things we care about most -- which I tried for years, by the way, and it really makes your life suck).

    What is our to control is stepping forward with the anxiety.

    That's the best I got.

    Christopher
