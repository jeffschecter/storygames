\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJune 2008 \n edited June 2008 in Actual Play
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n We played Steal Away Jordan last week. I was apprehensive about playing, but just wanted to mention, quickly, that it's bloody good.

Firstly, there's a real sense of theatre about it. Nobody seems to talk about this, but it's great, and not something you see often in indie games. For example, the GM gets to decide your name. That's really cool and slightly weird. Then the GM leaves the room while you decide your goals. That is cool too.

There's the Skull Die, that sits in the middle of the table. You can roll it any time you want, for a bonus, but if it rolls a 1, you die. (We played that, if you touch it, you must roll it. I don't know if that's in the rules, but it's fun.) This is one of the most theatrical, cool things I've seen in a game.

Secondly, the game is not preachy. I feared that, when we played Steal Away Jordan, we'd get self-indulgent: oh, I'm a slave, look how awful my life is. (Not that slavery isn't awful, you understand, but you don't necessarily want to play a game about awfulness.)

It's not like that. You play people with goals: now, admittedly, you're fighting against overwhelming odds for these goals. But, because you're fighting for your goals, it's a very hopeful game.

I had real doubts about Steal Away Jordan before playing. Instinctively, I thought it wouldn't be a game for me, but I was completely wrong. It's fun stuff and one of the most interesting games I've played for a while.

Graham

P.S. Sorry, this post is a bit rambly. I can't find a way to write it that doesn't sound like "Hey, guys, slavery can be fun!". That is not quite what I'm saying, but it is a fun game.

    \n Based on our experience of actual play it is an excellent game, actually upbeat, and I'd happily play it again. I had some problems with what I perceived to be statistical issues with the rules, but it turned out I was wrong. Some of the rules text could do with a little polish, but nothing much. Even if you find the rules preachy (which I don't) the actual play that emerges is about people who are enslaved rather than slaves.
    \n
    \nIt was very strange to see players turn their ideas down to avoid my gaze when we were doing master-slave scenes, and the little things they did just to keep some pride and dignity were gratifying. The slaves' secret agendas were very effective - I tried hard not even to consider what those agendas might be.
    \n
    \nThere were lots of good scenes but one favourite was the death of the overseer, and how the other slaves helped the root doctor cause his downfall. The house slave leant a few dice by polishing the stairs extra shiny, another slave made sure the overseer was in his cups, and the instigator came round late at night mock him. The overseer tumbled down the stairs drunk and smashed his head on the balustrade.

    \n Brian, I think you're bringing up a good point with your comparison to movies we watch that are "good experiences" but "not fun."

    But I think there's there's something else about these games that helps produce "the fun": the act of creation. It's one thing to be an audience member, but it's another thing to be the active creator of dramatic moments -- even if they are sad or dreadful moments. There is a pleasure to be found in the act of creation, the building of patterns, of making choices that are surprising and that "click."

    From everything I've read about Steal Away Jordan (I've yet to play it), I'm sure it delivers these moments for the firing of synapses in our brains that go, "Ah, that was good, here's some pleasure."

    Thus, the subject matter is one thing (and, as Graham points out, to be respected), but the act of play is another thing -- and has its own specific and unique rewards.


    On another point, I think Graham nails it rather plainly. The Characters have goals. The stakes are high. The opposition great. Boom. Good story. Good story = fun. [Edited in light of Julia's post directly below: I think "fun" is often a tricky word. I prefer, when talking about my response to movies, games and books the words "engaging," "engagement," "pleasure" or "pleasing." The feeling of "fun" is one quality or subset of these other words for me. In the example Julia links to, she's right, it wasn't "fun." But I do see "engagement" in spades. And I think this creates "pleasure" in us -- the pleasure of a communal experience, the pleasure of creation, the pleasure of building something that is right even if it's sad.]
