\n I was 16, in my friend's backyard after filming a video for a school project. I tried... stress tried... GMing Shadowrun 2E for 13 people with a small crowd watching including a girl I had a crush on.

I "thought" I knew the rules. After realizing I didn't, it turned into more of an improv LARP. I realized next time I needed to do a little more homework and start with a smaller group. Maybe less people watching!

I'm pretty amazed how patient everyone was with me. I remember feeling a little embarrassed but determined. After my first attempt I tried again a few months later with Star Wars. It took 3 years before I really got the basics down. And a few years more before I felt I nailed it. But it was worth it! I'm glad I didn't give up after my first time.

    \n I had just turned 14, I think. D&D 2nd Edition, I believe: there was still THAC0 and it was still owned by TSR. The guys in my group had already forbidden me from playing female characters (this is a story I tell a lot and I think most of SG has heard it by now), and I was tired of playing dwarven warriors. I think the guys thought I was nuts when I said I wanted to run our next campaign\u2014 in hindsight, I WAS totally nuts. I didn't own any of the books. I had character sheets I made in DOS and printed out on my dot matrix printer, based off of the character sheet my friend Tom let me borrow from the real book.

    I don't think we ever played a game that was in one of the canon settings: I don't even remember knowing such things existed. I threw together a fantasy faux-celtic world where three brothers were fighting over the throne: a wizard, a fighter, and a bard who didn't want to get involved initially. I remember they had a paladin sister that the guys kept trying to bang, until I rolled a natural 20 when she was about to kick one of them in the crotch. The boys hooted and hollered and never bothered my female NPCs again. I remember creating all of this setting and all of these decision points that seem so railroady, looking back on it in indie game terms, but somehow the unthinkable happened and one of the PCs (not the one from the crotch-kicking incident) was crowned king and married the paladin. The general consensus among the boys was that I was a great GM, but still didn't know how to play right.

    I had a blast with that particular game (it was 2-3 hours 2-3 times a week for over a year), but weirdly enough, I don't like GMing any more. Hm.

    \n Oh, man. When I was about 11, an older friend (in his late 20's) introduced me briefly to something vaguely D&D-like (basically, he sat me down, with no preamble, described a situation, and said "what do you do?"). I liked the idea--something in me saw the endless potential of such an activity--and asked him about it. He told me about a game called D&D and gave me a book and some of his notes.

    I immediately went to my group of friends and said, "Want to try this game someone told me about?" I ran the first session for them, but insisted that thereafter we take turns GMing (although in practice I ended up GMing more than 50% of the time). It was odd: we had a continuous "campaign" but with rotating GMs. Of course, pretty much everyone took advantage of their "turn" to give their own PC powerful magical items, which was questionable at best. We had to retire the campaign pretty soon because the inflation of powerful items got out of control extremely quickly.

    I recall that the first adventure consisted of nothing but a carefully sketched dungeon, some imaginative monsters (I remember a giant mechanical brass spider, and a monster that looked like a pretty girl sleeping facedown, but when disturbed would jump up and attack), and my notes listed a "to-hit" number for the monsters--apparently I didn't understand the concept of Armor Class at the time, or at least wasn't interested in applying it to monsters.

    The worst thing about the game was that my best friend really wanted to play a Wizard. Or he did right up until we actually started the game. Then he decided he'd rather play a Ranger. But I was so excited to give him some cool artifact for his Wizard that I didn't let him play a Ranger. I cringe, thinking about that, to this day.

    Anyway, the upshot of it all is that I GMed a lot more often than I played for the first ten years or so of my gaming life. I have to say that the impact on my social skills and personal confidence was HUGE--I was a shy kid, and to be in charge of an evening for a group of friends was a tremendous challenge I learned a lot from.

    It was 1983. I was eleven. It was the first game of D&D I'd ever participated in -- I DMed before I ever played a character. It was a one-on-one game, and we decided I was DMing a few minutes before gametime. I think I made up the dungeon as I went along.

    I remember the ending very vividly. Because I was a nut for ancient Egypt at the time, the dungeon exit looked like Tutankhamun's tomb, an uncovered 10x10x10 room cut down into the rock, with a stairwell emerging up into it, from the depths.

    The PC had one hit point left when he came up out of the tomb, and he was surprised by the goblin sniper lurking above him. But the goblin rolled two misses! The player, desperate, and out of ammunition, threw his sword at the goblin. The die came up 20! I decided that was a good result and the tomb raider won!

    Twenty-seven years later...

    \n I'm having to sort of reconstruct this from partial memories. I'm pretty sure it was 1975 because I think Greyhawk (the supplement, not the setting) had come out. I would have been 16 or 17. Mark Swanson had created a world map for all the MITSGS D&D games to locate themselves. I asked for and got a large island and small islands near the edge of a mostly unoccupied continent, and immediately dubbed the area "The Gothic Islands" (I was thinking of both Gothic literature and the historical Goths). I drew up my own map with a bunch of good guy nations (The Grey Empire, The Seven Cities, the Northern Kingdom, the Republic of Freeport, Seaward Out) and a bad guy nation (The Necromatic Empire (why be subtle?)). I decided the PCs would be based out of the Republic of Freeport (which was ruled by complicated mess of a local power named He-Who-Rules-But-Does-Not-Govern, A Civil Council and a Military Council). Underneath the city of Freeport (I stole the name years before Green Ronin stole it!) there were unmappable catacombs (they were like 4- or 5-D) left by the Si'aric (an extinct, time-travelling race (or as extinct as any race of time travellers ever go). And I built my first dungeon, the 9 towers (the remains of a mage guild that had once occupied a small valley, but the magical equivalent of nukes had reduced it to a sea of green glass with the stubs of several towers sticking up and extensive underground tunnels patrolled by blue-glowing empty suits of armor and various monsters). And I started running with players who had been unable that day to get a slot in one of the better known MITSGS games like Gorree or Hellsgate or Edwyr.
