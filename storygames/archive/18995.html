WHAT IF... the PCs were animal companions of a Wizard or Ranger, in a D&D world able to speak and have certain granted powers - in a world of GAME OF THRONES style story turning into a morally ambiguous world of characters characters, to explore the issues of social hierarchy, religion, loyalty, corruption, sexuality, civil war, crime, and punishment.



I'm looking for ideas on a one-shot game I'm GMing, to take the original premise of a neat-little fun adventure and keep the "fun" energy but shape it into a potential campaign that will be more about satire and depth in role-play and roles of the PCs world.


Below is a bunch of my inspiration/direction...
I'm looking for your advice on anything from:
general plot and overall story themes and allegories (to current world or history)
specific neat/fun "adventure" ideas or encounter/scenarios to be exciting
ideas on "factions" and interactions with PCs and/or NPCs
ideas for varied NPCs


SOURCE - the old FUDGE adventure:

"ANOTHER FINE MESS"

An Animal Companions Adventure!
The Master has gotten himself into another fine mess \u2013 captured by an evil sorcerer! What can you \u2013 mere animals \u2013 do?
Why, lots! Shadow the cat knows where the Master was captured. Reek the ferret is a contortionist and has a magic twig! Penny the falcon is fearless, while Midnight, the master\u2019s steed, has strength and speed aplenty. Isabelle the bloodhound can track a scent anywhere.Barney is a strong, loyal, agile dog for whom most obstacles prove mere annoyances. Bandit the \u2018coon is clever with his paws \u2013 very clever. And Anaxagoras the owl is in training to be a wizard!
The player characters are animals!


I would like to take an approach of a much more SATIRE style that you can see in stories like: Watership Down (book/animation), Plague Dogs (book/animation), We3 (comic), and the classic Animal Farm (book).

Below are some notes on the original adventure I will be using as a base for the campaign start... the short adventure HOOK... But I'd like to use ideas from those sources for a more "ORWELLIAN" style role-play and world approach.


ANIMAL FARM
The original commandments are:
Whatever goes upon two legs is an enemy.
Whatever goes upon four legs, or has wings, is a friend.
No animal shall wear clothes.
No animal shall sleep in a bed. (No animal shall sleep in a bed with sheets.)
No animal shall drink alcohol. (No animal shall drink alcohol to excess.)
No animal shall kill any other animal. (No animal shall kill any other animal without cause.)
All animals are equal.

Eventually, these are replaced with the maxims,
"All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others"
and "Four legs good, two legs better!"
as the pigs become more human. This is an ironic twist to the original purpose of the Seven Commandments, which were supposed to keep order within Animal Farm by uniting the animals together against the humans and preventing animals from following the humans' evil habits. Through the revision of the commandments, Orwell demonstrates how simply political dogma can be turned into malleable propaganda.




a reviewer noted:
Were this written for typical humanoid PCs, it would be a forgettable dungeon crawl: a fine way to spend a gaming session, but hardly a campaign highlight. But with the animal PCs -- a raccoon, a peregrine falcon, a ferret, two dogs, a cat, an owl and a horse -- it becomes something different. Not necessarily superior, but certainly much more challenging and entertaining. After all, what's simple for human hands to accomplish can present a formidable challenge to even the smartest cat. The adventure recommends that the Master's influence allow the animals to speak to each other, which allows them to brainstorm without an added language barrier.

It all starts when the Master, a mage in the service of the Free Rangers of Amberland, leaves camp to apprehend a man who has been doing bad things with magic. An hour or so later, Shadow the cat gets a distress call from the Master. Isabelle the bloodhound picks up his scent trail, and the search and rescue is on.

While logical, the scent trail makes the adventure too linear. The animals don't have to work to find the Master, and they aren't likely to go down an alternate path just to see what else might be around. (OK, so Reek Havoc the ferret did during my playtest. He's an exception.) The challenges come in the form of the encounters on the way.

Most of the encounters are monsters created by the evil sorcerer. Some are hostile, some neutral or potentially friendly. With the hostile creatures, Midnight the horse has the potential to make any combat extremely short, with a 9 damage factor to trample. Nothing in the game can survive a direct hit. He's a big horse with steel shoes, so it's as logical as the scent trail, but the net result was a few lucky rolls ending most of the combats, before any PC got hurt. Combat was so skewed in favor of the PCs that I began to wonder if the dice were loaded. Had it been part of an ongoing campaign, I'd probably have cheated in the monsters' favor, or at least made them more resistant to damage.

The biggest challenges to the animals would be trivial to a group of humans. For example, a locked door. These situations are where Another Fine Mess did its best job pointing out the animals' weaknesses. There are ways around all of these situations, but the bulk of the party's time was spent on them.

Also, each has the Master listed under Gifts, but each has a different description of him from the animal's point of view. Example: the ferret thinks of the Master as "person with pockets," while the owl (who believes himself the Master's apprentice) describes him as "Homo Sapiens Thaumaturgist." If this is the kind of quality we can expect from Animal Companions when it ships, that will be a must-buy.

These notes do admit that the adventure is very linear as it stands.

    \n Have you seen John Wick's game Cat?

    It is similar to the idea of playing an animal companion, in that PCs are cats, out to save their human companions from bad dreams. It's also similar in that things easy for humans tend to be difficult for the cats.

    It has nice rules for the ability of a cat to do magic and while it may not be exactly the scenario you're thinking of, I think it could be easily reskinned to do so. In any case it might be a nice source of ideas.

    \n Golden Sky Stories AP's may also help with ideas, one of the common complaints (in the UK anyway) is that the players will happily try the most convoluted methods possible to solve a problem in animal form that could be fixed in seconds if they just transformed in to a human (i.e. 30 minutes of planning involving kidnap and trail laying to get someone to a place fixed by a brainwave of 'I turn in to a human and tell them X will meet them here...')

    I've run Another Fine Mess a few times and even created two or three follow on scenarios (I'm very bad at writing them up so they don't really exist in any format anyone else could use). The thing to remember is that, as mentioned in the review, the interesting bits are when the players get to act very much like animals and have to use animal skills/reasoning to get past problems. One of the reasons I mention GSS is that not only is this heavily mirrored but also the split within the animals, in Another Fine Mess the ferret, raccoon and dogs are very impetious and young while the other animals are more studied and 'adult' which often leads to a split in play. Similarly in GSS Fox and Cat characters often end up split from Tanuki, Bird or Rabbit characters as they suggest very different methods of characterisation.

    With the less linear stuff that I wrote for AFM (although still quite linear, you need very few encouters to fill a one shot with this style of play satisfactorily and with the need to confront the animals with unique problems a degree of 'railroading' works well) the group very often split in these groupings of maturity.

    I've not tried making it more serious, I don't know if looking at something like Mouseguard might not be the better option there. It takes very little prompting (read, 'none') to get the players to be acting in a quite fun and juvinile fashion in games like this and getting them to treat it as a serious thing may actually be a bit of a task, I'm not sure you'd get more by trying to run Rabbits within a Watership Down setting than if you were just running humans in a Watership Down style set up. Considering we're playing made up people already the books need to use animals to analogue humans may be somewhat redundant.

    \n Depends on what type of animal story. Off the top of my head.

    Animals as action heroes in their own setting-
    Incredible Journey, White Fang, Tailchaser's Song

    Lassie, Benji, Flipper

    Animals as companion who can communicate with their owner-
    A Boy and His Dog, Mr Ed, Francis the Talking Mule, Possibly Alice in Wonderland if you squint and only play attention to the parts with the Cheshire Cat, Scooby Doo

    Anthropomorphic Animals-
    Animal Farm, Redwall
