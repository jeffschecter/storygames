\n I love mysteries on TV, in books and in movies. It's not a genre that translate too well into my games. Most Mystery RPGs will be about a test of character-skill and player-skill. We roll to see if the character finds the clues, then we see if the player connects the dots and solve the case. I have yet to be in a game where that's fun. Though conceivably, it could be.
\n
\nMore fun are games where the players don't actually solve the mystery. They reveal it through their narration. In InSpectres, the GM can say the characters find an old, dusty book in the attic. It's written in an ancient language. One of the PCs roll academics to translate the text. Should they succeed, they get to say what it says. Is it a magic grimoire? A forbidden tome? A vampire autobiography?
\n
\nIn Dirty Secrets, at various times, players can write down a plausible suspect of the crime. In the end chance picks the killer, and the players narrate how it makes sense.
\n
\nDogs in the Vineyard, though it has a mystery, doesn't care to make it hard for the players to solve it. What's interesting is how you'll deal with it. When you find the killer, will you shoot him and give him a medal?
\n
\nHow do you like your mysteries?

    \n I've got a mystery-based game called Hex Rangers. It's based on the Procedural genre. How it works is, you show up, and learn about something that has just happened. This prompts you to investigate (because it's your job). You investigate until you get stumped or run low on resources, at which point you decide to Refresh. Upon Refreshing, something else happens: basically, I take the "the killer just killed another dude" that you see in Procedurals and apply it to the paranormal investigation Color. This, of course, gives you more clues, and prompts more investigation, until you Refresh again. Depending on how quickly you solve the mystery, you can either prevent things from getting really bad, or things are gonna get really bad.

    It works out pretty damn well.

    That said, I do share one thing with you: I also dig mysteries where the main point is not whodunnit but rather what you are going to do about it now. That's awesome.

    \n A fundamental problem in how mysteries are usually handled is that these games present an ostensibly serious challenge, but completely punt the responsibility for providing consequences and meaningful play in case of failure. This is ridiculous.

    Mysteries are difficult to solve, that's true - and even further, most people who enjoy mysteries in literature do so not because they try to solve them, but because they provide a fine framework for dramatic revelations. However, nothing in particular prevents us from playing challenge-based mystery games as long as a) we have means to hone the difficulty level of the mystery play to an enjoyable level, perhaps by iterating gameplay until the correct level for the players is found and b) the game is completely serious about being functional even when and if the players fail in solving the mystery.

    Looking at how mystery games are usually ran, both of those conditions are failed miserably. The GM creates some mystery and decides that now he has enough clues for the players to solve it - how does he know, and how does this system ensure that he will do better the next time around if it proves that he's wrong? Likewise, just look at how these games (and by "these games" I mean Call of Cthulhu, my nemesis and dance partner) deal with failure procedure-wise: first you spend two hours doing nothing because there is no way for the player to know that he's stumped, then your character dies and the game ends. Really fun.

    I'm personally especially mad about point the second up there - I find it a moral failing for a game designer to propose a challenge and then fail to provide the tools, techniques and content for making play meaningful or exiting it gracefully in case of failure. That's a fatal mixup of dramatic concerns with challenge play if something is - the poor scenario-writer can't even imagine that the players would fail at step A and thus never arrive at his great scene at step B, as surely the GM knows enough to fudge the results into success no matter what the players decide to do.

    \n There is a standard Story Games line on mysteries, which goes like this:

    1. Secrets in games are bad.
    2. Secrets kept by the GM are particularly bad.
    3. When the players gradually uncover a mystery, this is railroading.

    These are play preferences, of course, and perfectly valid ones. Some people don't like secrets in games; some people don't like gradually uncovering mysteries. But they aren't failures of game design and they aren't objectively Not Fun.

    Eero makes a good point, above, about frameworks: "most people who enjoy mysteries in literature do so not because they try to solve them, but because they provide a fine framework for dramatic revelations". This is a good point. Many mystery games aren't just about the mystery: they're about how it affects the characters. Murder mysteries work like this; Call of Cthulhu can work like this. This is why I like Trail of Cthulhu: because it ends up being about the characters, not the mystery.

    That's how I like my mysteries.

    Graham

    \n Oh and.

    One thing I hate, in mystery games, is when the fun is postponed. For example, I recently played a game where I was Secretly A Bad Person (they were pregenerated characters). The fun was in knowing I was Secretly A Bad Person and, after the game, telling people I had Secretly Been A Bad Person.

    That was appalling. It's even more appalling if you're not Secretly A Bad Person, because, at the end, someone reveals that they were Secretly A Bad Person and you think: well, what was I meant to do about that?

    So, I don't like mysteries where the fun is in keeping secrets (and maybe revealing them after the game). Games when secrets come out, and they affect people, are great. Games where secrets remain hidden are, in my opinion, not fun.

    \n I agree with Graham that puzzle solving is rarely a Story Games approach though because it tends to force a very linear approach - find the clues, piece them together, solve the mystery. Story Games tend to favour the player gets control of the narration so he gets to define an element of the mystery over there is an existing mystery to reveal.

    I think there is a difference between puzzle solving as a game element, which tends to reward player skills, and mystery though. Some folks like that kind of meta-reward for gaming, some don't. I suspect it parallels with those folk who like to participate in a story, Dragonlance style, and those who want to create it. I don't think folks want to solve mystery in the same way they do a puzzle, but more earn the revelations in game. Mystery is pretty much anathema to story games though, because as Graham points out, what is the point to cool secrets if the players cannot directly use them in play.

    Solving a murder in a game is usually more of a puzzle than a mystery and its worth bearing in mind that for most puzzles you have all the clues, the skill is in using them to solve the puzzle.

    \n Let me know if this is a Story Games approach or not...
    \n
    \nIn "Secrets & Lies," I wanted to emulate the relationship focus of hardboiled detective stories. Crimes aren't solved by following a trail of clues; they're solved by sussing out the motivations of all the suspects and testing them until the guilty reveal themselves. I find this approach more compatible with RPGs, because it asks the players to take a very active hand in digging up clues and resolving conflicts.
    \n
    \nGame prep just requires building a relationship map for the supporting cast: the victim, the suspects, their mistresses, bookies, rivals, relatives, etc. During the first half of the session, the protagonists explore this map by questioning, tailing, and researching their suspects. The second half is all about what they _do_ with that information: blackmail, bribery, alliances of convenience, backstabbing and betrayal.
    \n
    \nIt's not a police procedural, by any means, but it does allow the players to solve and resolve a real, pre-determined mystery.

    \n The fundamental paradox of a "mystery game" that emulates the genre of detective fiction is that if the PCs are the detectives, then the fact that the killer might get away (or, more generally, that the mystery might remain unsolved) is unsatisfying for the players-as-their-own-audience because it breaks genre, but any guarantee that the killer will be caught is unsatisfying for the players-as-advocates-of-their-own-characters because it deprives them of agency in the narrative: if it's all going to work out anyway, then their choices don't matter. Trail of Cthulhu tries to get around this by saying hey, you'll get (discover) the clues you need; the question is what do you do with them. It's not entirely true that the result is a character-focused game, since the gap between player and character is actually pretty narrow. That is, I don't think the game mechanics reliably produce any kind of robust character portraiture. The assumption is that faced with cosmic horror most people will react pretty much in the same way: start gibbering and raving. The game works best when players get into playing out their increasing desperation, confusion, and despair. In Serial Homicide Unit, the solution to mimic a detective sub-genre in which the bad guy getting away is a distinct possibility: the police procedural. Its approach is to say that you'll get (invent) clues, but whether you get enough of them in time to save the next victim, whom you care about because you've been playing them out not as victims but as people with dreams and aspirations that just might get cut short. The investigator "characters" in SHU are functional ciphers; they exist to introduce "clues" that have both a narrative function (guiding the story) and a game-mechanical one (letting you "win" the game), but the real story lies in the lives of the potential victims. In both cases, the effect is to shift the focus from the quote-unquote mystery to something else.

    Of course, that's only half-true, because it implies that the mystery doesn't matter, really, but in fact it really does. But it doesn't matter more than player agency, however that's realized. I ran a Trail of Cthulhu game at Gencon that almost ran aground in the early stages when the players thought it was going to just be about me revealing clues to them -- "this guy reading us his story," as one of the players put it -- but picked up as soon as the players got the chance to do something in the game. It was a good lesson, and it colors how I'll design TOC-style mysteries from here on out.

    \n I should note that I was only discussing mysteries as challenges up above - if you're just using the mystery as a backdrop and framework for dramatic storytelling, then it is obviously quite OK to not build it up as a functional challenge model. I've never seen this sort of game as particularly problematic, however; the same rules and procedures work whether your story has a backdrop of heroic monster-slaying or clever detecting, there's very little difference. Just remember that there's little reason to keep the players in the dark aside from pacing, and remember to not let mandatory success hinge on outdated dicing rituals. The challenging mystery thing is much more difficult as a design case due to how we don't really have many working case studies of the idea on paper as finished games, despite the fact that many people have played or tried to play that sort of games over the years.

    Serial Homicide Unit is an interesting game to be sure. It's not a challenge game but a storytelling one - as Bill says, it's also set in a grim genre wherein it's possible for the serial killer to get away completely. The players don't really have much influence over that aside from a bit of dicing strategy. I also wouldn't consider it much of a mystery game in anything except the most superficial structural part, as the focus of the game definitely is not in unknown information.

I did these games when I was in police administration school. The fun was in the play acting. I learned from these games that the actual mystery had to be painfully obvious for the players to solve it. If there was anyway for them to go astray they would.

    Now I do murder mysteries where players make up the clues as they play (Sherlock Holmes Engle Matrix Games). Only one player is Holmes, the others are potential suspects. The clues made up narrow down who could have done it. The fun of these games is trying to pin the murder on one of the other players. In this way it is a competitive game. There is no problem of clues being worthless because if they were made up it was for a reason. I've heard people say that this approach is unrealistic. Sadly I think it is more realistic than any of us would wish. Police form theories and then find clues to support them. It's not an objective exercise.

    Right now Komrade Bob and I are in a variant of a EMG mystery where the whole game is pre ploted. Each turn people make up answers to the plot point of the moment. The fun is making up the story but also in trying to get your answer through. We count up victory points based on who'se action wins the roll and who built off of which action. As a game it works well in PBEM formats but maybe it's not a real mystery. There is no chance for failure for instance - it's just a question of how you succeed.

    I like the comments about Call of Cthulhu. I love that game. It's a great one shot con game. They are role playing tour de forces (like my old murder mysteries) but like my old games they have to be painfully obvious. I don't mind that. Like reading Lovecraft a lot of the fun is in play acting feeling the horror as the insanity of the plot is unfolded.

    I'm playing around with a new MG format where players place tiles to create a location with encounter tiles on it. The game is divided into two parts. Both parts score victory points in different ways. The first half of the game is about finding clues about what the twist of the game is. The second half involves resolving the twist. When players fail action rolls their character's stress increases (which develops that characters personal matrix). It's all untested but I have a draft of the rules. Not directly a murder mystery but it does involve making up clues.

    \n I have a sketch of a game in my drawer about slice of life police procedural that I invented after watching too much Third Watch and some German detective series late at night. The key idea in it is that the player characters who work as detectives or whatever are constantly being pressured by cases that pile on their desks. The players have great leeway in triaging and priorizing this mass of work (possibly centralize this one by making one of the characters the boss), but the thing is that whenever they fail a case, that has an adverse impact of some sort on the overall health of their community. I figure that this gives the players enough of a motivation for working the cases while taking care of good failure procedures as well - the characters can easily fail just like detectives do in real life, and the game won't end because of it; everybody just feels a bit bad and comes to work the next day just like before. This has many benefits for the design, including the fact that game balance is a non-issue; whatever means I might device for actually resolving cases in this game, it doesn't matter if the given case is too easy or too hard, as play will be rewarding and meaningful regardless. There is no single point of failure like Call of Cthulhu and the like have, where failing only a single case ever will often ruin the whole campaign.

    \n I run mystery games, and I run games with mysteries.

    In a Cthulhu investigative game, the fun for the players is in teasing out information as other stuff happens. They know the other stuff will happen regardless of what they do, but putting together clues might give them ammunition against it. Plus, I always have a plan B in case they fail to find a clue.

    Part of how I do this is I throw out a bunch of clues without having any idea how they link together. I just put out cool things: a book, a stranger, a message, etc. Then the players find them, or glimpse them, and start speculating what might be going on. I take the best ideas and make that the solution. Sometimes they don't out-right tell me what's they think is going on, but you can guess: "Is there a pattern in these killings if I line them up with a map? Can I roll versus Criminology to see that?" "Sure." Then success could let me say there's a pattern, and failure could let me say they haven't got enough information to answer that question, but might if they went and got another clue.

    And in games that aren't investigative, I still like mysteries, so I always have something lurking at the shadows, some hint of somebody not being what they seem, and so on, and let the players who enjoy the long scope of games enjoy the reward when these clues come together. By tossing out a lot of hooks and remembering them, I can come up with what the mystery actually is later on, and then retroactively include those hooks. Players who have been paying attention will feel rewarded for having done so. They may even have figured out chunks of the mystery along the way (again, they came up with a good idea and it fit, or my good idea retroactively fit.) This worked for the X-Files. . . it works for Lacuna too, btw.

    In a game that is more story games oriented, like Dogs, I'll dump the mystery up front, because the mystery isn't the point. It looks like a mystery game, but it's really not.

    And I agree with the "you're the bad guy, don't tell" syndrome being boring. If you're the bad guy, tell. The players will get a lot of enjoyment out of knowing what their characters do not know. This is why we have fun when we see bad guys sneaking up on unsuspecting good guys in movies. In fact, do that in games too. Tell them there's an ambush up ahead that they don't see, and ask them how they are reacting. Or cut away to a two sentence snippet of what the bad guy is doing, then go back to the players.

    That's how I like my mysteries.
