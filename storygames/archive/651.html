    See, I start writing things as expansions, like Vesperteen being a variant of Little Fears and Lions on the Precipice being a variant of Dogs...

    And then I decide that I have to basically re-write the whole game and create something new in order to meet my design goals. So I end up with yet another design project. It's annoying.

    I think something I want to move towards is altering existing systems in dramatic ways for use in specific campaigns. Like Shreyas rewrote the entire Exalted combat system recently, and we'll probably only use it for one IRC game before he rewrites it again.

    Not everything needs to be a completely new game. And if it does, maybe it's something you could just use for a couple one-shots for your home group or you might print 20 copies of it at Kinkos to give away. For example, nobody really desperately needs a copy of Seadog Tuxedo, my pirate penguins game. It's fun and neat, but you probably wouldn't want to play it for more than 3-4 sessions.

    Roleplaying is ephemeral. It's only natural that some design projects and entire games should be ephemeral as well. Not everything should be an opus. But that doesn't mean you shouldn't put all your design chops into it.

    So, yes and no.

    I remember Jared Sorensen gave some good advice once about only adding two or three strange new mechanics or rules subsystems for any given design. If every little thing is freaky and new, then nothing in your design is going to stand out.

    So I have two or three conflict resolution methods and one character creation method that I reuse with minor modifications for every game. Some of my games are going to be "that one core system, plus these rules for reincarnation (or rocketship battles, or folk sorcery.)" Others are going to need stranger rules to cover the complete feeling I'm trying to convey in that game. So most of the time, my decision is to extend. If I have to extend in three ways, that's a completely new system.

  • talysman
    May 2006

    It's not just Jared.

    If you think about it, it's a restatement in RPG terms of T. S. Elliot's commentary on characters in fiction: you can have a bizarre character in an ordinary world, or an ordinary character in a bizarre world, but not a bizarre character in a bizarre world, because there's no point of familiarity to draw the reader in.

    Don't confuse this with being revolutionary. First, because it's not wise to get too hung up on being revolutionary, 'cause then you're just trying too hard. People can smell a poseur a mile off. Second, the really revolutionary stuff is subtle. It's taking something people are familiar with in another context and applying it in a strange new way. You can use a lot more context shifts than totally new concepts in a game because context shifts by their nature are completely comprehensible..

    (Thunder God--I just posted a comment directly to your blog answer, some of which gets repeated here in a different form)

    I don't know if you do know until you have pushed and pulled at it a little. It may be that I just don't have enough experience to know early on, but I don't realize I have a robust enough concept that stands on its own until I have really tried to make it fit with a pre-existing system and it just hasn't worked.

    Even then, I can't be entirely sure that the idea itself is just not screwy, so I have to fidget it with a little using the original system. If it still won't work, then I begin to start building a system around the idea, seeing if that goes somewhere.

    And it may be that it works very well with one system but would just be oh so much cooler with a system of its own to support it...or you build a cool system only to realize there is an entirely different system out there that would support it even better.

    It's not an exciting answer, maybe even bordering on the banal--"you know because when you create a system around it, something cool happens." It's craft, it's experimentation. You know by doing--what is it Linus Pauli said? Genius is just two things: a means for producing lots of ideas and a means for selecting out the good ones.

    Produce, produce, produce, edit, edit, edit. The guts ain't pretty, but oh the joy when the monster breathes ; )
