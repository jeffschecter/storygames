\n Traditionally RPGs possess two separate and inter-related types of resolution: conflict resolution (which resolve an entire situation) and task resolution (which resolves a specific event). These definitions are necessarily a bit vague, but the borders of a conflict situation are often explicit in play. For example, in D&D surprise and initiative mechanics would herald the beginning of a combat conflict.

The problem with this design approach is that the two forms of resolution don't always interact very well. The GM was required to facilitate the translation, and numerous abuses crept their way in through that gap. Which led to design schools (often originating at the Forge) of focusing on conflict resolution, and hence eliminating the discord between resolving conflicts and tasks, by removing tasks. This implied traditional games used pure task resolution, a claim that doesn't hold up to detailed inspection (D&D is one of many counter-examples).

What I've realized recently is that most of the games I've designed in the past 6 years have focused on task resolution, minimizing conflicts in the same way the many indie games minimize everything except conflicts. The result has been fun and highly functional games which barely resemble either traditional or typical indie games.

It is a strange and wondrous place, and I'm determined to continue exploring it. But I wonder whether there is room among story gamers and indie designers for it.

So tell me your thoughts on task-centered design or minimizing explicit conflicts in RPGs.

- Mendel S.

    Hang on, there's like 32 different definitions of conflict vs. task. There's the one you just said, which is the way it's defined in TSOY. Then there's:

    I don't know where this started: Task focuses on whether characters can do stuff, conflict focuses on conflicts between players

    Ron's definition: Task focuses on whether or not a character can complete a task based on character effectiveness; conflict focuses on conflicts of interest between in-game entities, and how, when, why, and to what effect those conflicts resolve.

    Vincent's definition: Task doesn't have mechanics in place to resolve the relevant bits, conflict does.

    As far as design goes, I'm inclined to think that only Vincent's distinction is important (although I stick to Ron's definition of the actual terms). In other words, don't worry about "task" or "conflict," just make sure you have mechanics in place to resolve what's important. Like, lemme paste from my blog:\n
    Player: \u201cI punch him in the face!\u201d
    GM: \u201cTo what purpose?\u201d
    Player: \u201cTo hurt him!\u201d (rolls dice; they say he hits)
    GM: \u201cYou break his nose, and he starts bleeding!\u201d

    The above example is an example of stupid. You know why? Because whether or not you hit the guy was not relevant. All that was relevant was whether or not you hurt the guy.

    If you\u2019re not convinced that the above example was an example of stupid, here\u2019s another one:

    Player: \u201cI punch him in the face!\u201d
    GM: \u201cTo what purpose?\u201d
    Player: \u201cTo hurt him!\u201d (rolls dice; they say he hits)
    GM: \u201cYou hit him, but his jaw\u2019s like a rock. It doesn\u2019t phase him. And your hand hurts now.\u201d

    If you were that player, you\u2019d probably feel pretty hacked off, right? The relevant bit was whether or not you hurt the guy, but the dice (which are representing the mechanics conceptually here) didn\u2019t tell you that; there was no system in place for determining it. The people playing the game had to implement an informal system for it themselves. In this case, the GM decided. That\u2019s called Fiat. If you\u2019re going to resolve the relevant bits by GM Fiat, then why the hell are you rolling dice? The rules tell you that the dice are the \u201cresolution mechanics,\u201d but don\u2019t resolve anything that matters. Why are you using mechanics that lie to you?

    Here\u2019s a thing: GM Fiat can be functional and fun. You\u2019ve probably played in a game where it was. But it\u2019s prone to dysfunction, and you\u2019ve probably played in a game where it went bad. The only reason for that is that it is rarely handled in an above-board fashion. If you\u2019re going to resolve the relevant things by Fiat, then quit screwin\u2019 around with dice and crap, and just say so. You know what happens when you do that? BAM, you\u2019ve got a system in place to resolve the bits that are relevant.

    Player: \u201cI punch him in the face!\u201d
    GM: \u201cTo what purpose?\u201d
    Player: \u201cTo hurt him!\u201d (looks at the GM expectantly)
    GM: \u201cHm. You break his nose! He starts bleeding everywhere! And now your hand hurts.\u201d

    This whole issue is an issue of Effect, as in IIEE (Intent, Initiation, Execution, Effect). If you don\u2019t have a handle on IIEE, don\u2019t worry, because it\u2019s not really important from a design standpoint, and it can go screw with task and conflict resolution. Instead I\u2019ll be saying \u201cBig Effect,\u201d referring to the big fat concept of what the important effects of in-game actions are. The first two examples lack Big Effect mechanics. The third one has one. The original rules for Vincent Baker\u2019s kill puppies for satan do not have Big Effect mechanics, but the annotated ones do. D&D has a curious mix of Big Effect and No Big Effect mechanics.

    D&D\u2019s combat rules have Big Effect, if the effect you\u2019re looking for is hurting someone. You roll to hit then roll damage. That\u2019s inelegant, of course, because whether or not you hit is irrelevant; we only care whether or not you did damage, and how much damage you did. D&D\u2019s designers would have done well to consolidate those into one roll. But it gets fuzzier when you want to give someone a scar. There\u2019s no systems in place for that. D&D\u2019s rules for picking locks have Big Effect, if the effect you\u2019re looking for is simply undoing a lock. But it gets fuzzier when the effect you\u2019re looking for is actually getting a door open (the GM can always say it\u2019s jammed, even if you manage to unlock it).

    \nWithout an example of "it", In this answer I consider "it" as "playing a game without conflict resolution". In this case, the answer is a very big "yes", not only there is room, but this room is already full of games. Spione, It's Complicated, 1001 Nights, Kazekami Kyoko Kills Kublai Khan, Breaking the Ice, etc... there are already a lot of indie games without conflict resolution (and no task resolution, either). Even Jeepform games are without conflict resolution mechanics of any kind. It's a little like asking "seeing rpgs only use dice, do you think there is room for games that use cards?"\n

    So tell me your thoughts on task-centered design or minimizing explicit conflicts in RPGs.
    \nTwo very different things, task-centered design and minimizing explicit conficts in RPGs.

    First, about task-centered design: I think it's broken. Period. It was a leftover from the old wargaming days, and the idea that the rules should have to follow a "realistic" sequence, associated with the idea that no gdr could work without being based on GM's fiat. Both axiom were demonstrated as false.

    About conflicts in RPGs... I thought that it was impossible to have a rpg without conflict. Lately the Jeepen Frederick Berg Olsen did try to confute this axiom by writing the game "Lady & Otto", where every form of conflict was prohibited. His experimental conclusions after having played the game with different people at various times were (citing from memory, but you can find this in the notes about the game) "playing without conflict is possible, but it's mind-numbing boring" (and in some occasions I think the players got really angry at him during the game). From these test and observations, I would suggest that designing a rpg without conflict of any kind is at least unwise.

    This doesn't mean that every conflict has to have people chopping at each other with big swords. Conflicts can be much more "life-like". I have played games where the only conflict was a growing tension between sentimental parters. Not something that you want to settle by "resolving" the conflict and deciding "who won". So it's perfectly possibile to have rpgs where the conflict is part of the "fruitful void", like these, or is of so little importance in the game to be narrated by someone as an little detail (as in "it' complicated"). Notice as these are two polar opposite extremes: in one case that conflict is so important to be put outside of the rules (this is a rare case in indie games. The thesis that they are all about conflicts is patently false. The most important core of a narrativist games is into the fruitful void, so if there are rules about conflict, that core CAN'T be that conflict. Usually is the reason WHY there is that conflict, or the decision of entering in conflict at all. The conflict is mean to a bigger goal), in the other case the conflicts are of so little importance to be considered by the rules in the category "you can narrate it the way you want" as if was the color of the socks of the character. Most Indie games are in the middle of these extremes.

    \n Just to be clear, I'm not using Forge terms incorrectly, I'm using terms in common parlance (among RPG designers) which happens to have several Forge definitions. That doesn't make the Forge definitions correct or even orthodox. (Indeed, the most orthodox definition of conflict resolution I've found is resolving conflicts between people - as it exists far beyond the scope of RPGs.)

    It may help to go back to Sean's example. Here there is an implicit conflict (presumably structured and developed among the players) convincing a crowd to execute your brother. The two examples are two brands of conflict resolution. One piggy-back's directly on task resolution - resolving the conflict as a task. The other uses GM fiat to resolve the conflict, with (perhaps) plausibility constraints due to the tasks resolved during the conflict. Neither of these are very good types of conflict resolution. The former over-simplifies and essentially deflates the drama. The later is prone to serious abuse, although "good" GMs can make it very functional.

    But the task resolution happens beneath that layer. It's task resolution because the implicit or explicit conflict is not being resolved, merely action within a larger context.

    The games I'm talking about resolve the action, but delay or avoid resolving conflicts. In some cases this is because there is one real conflict, which is already resolved before play begins. In others it is because conflicts only resolve when they cease to be important, when the potential for interesting tasks has been exhausted. In others, (like Pure Shoujo) the tasks contribute to resolving relationships, not conflicts. I promise to get to a few more brass tacks on this shortly.

    As far as the whole "what is conflict resolution?" kerfluffle that happened since yesterday:

    I really don't buy any definition of conflict resolution which necessitates the player's intent being known. That gives us weird problems like:

    Conflict Resolution:
    Player: I attack an orc.
    GM: Um, which orc?
    Player: Huh? The left one.
    Player and GM do mechanic.
    GM: You hit the orc. (or GM: You miss the orc.)
    Player thinks: That resolves my burning desire to know if I'll hit the orc.

    Task Resolution:
    (the same until)
    Player and GM do mechanic.
    GM: Purple monkeys fly out of your butt.

    Unless:
    Player: Awesome! Monkeys did fly out of my butt. And. They. Are. Purple!! Are we talking lavender or more a royal purple?

    Which switches them, at least from the perspective of Player. Perhaps from the perspective of the GM (who is also a player) the purple monkeys don't matter at all.

    That's why I consider a conflict to have some beginning and ending communicated at the table. Whether it's hard and fast rules or more guidelines, the design should encapsulate the creation and resolution of a conflict to qualify as conflict resolution. Look at Dogs in the Vineyards, if dice are used it's a conflict. You never have one player thinking it's a conflict and another thinking it isn't. Likewise with D&D combat (the original conflict resolution mechanic) the conflict begins with surprise/initiative and ends with the elimination of a side using task resolution mechanics.

    Matters of fictional interest to one or more players can certainly exist without a conflict being generated and resolved. Indeed, something different in play can be experienced without churning the context of what matters so much. After all, if you only know how to resolve a conflict then you cannot explore what it is like to be within the conflict. Some games and stories are about how conflicts will be resolved. Others are not.

    In a game like Coming of Age, your main conflict is to come of age, and that will happen. It's part of the fictional conceit of these stories. If we resolve that conflict then it's over. Instead we explore and experience that conflict, building, discovering, coming together to see the final ending, not when we force the resolution of the conflict, but when it has arrived as a natural conclusion to the story.

      \n Example 4: Homeworld Project

      Homeworld Project is a strange game. In terms of mechanical and narrative structure it is both precise and highly flexible - each piece acts on each other - making it like a M\xf6bius loop of system. The premise of the game is Astral Space Opera, playing operatic stories in a world more permissive than even space opera tends to be. The model of the game is literary, so it is spaced into chapters, with a rising drama leading to a cliffhanger-type turning point for each. During the course of the design I specifically wanted to move away from the scene-structures which have become so pervasive in RPG design. Thus Homeworld Project has a narrative structure called, the action. The idea is anything worthwhile happens in the action, and characters are not so much killed as removed from the action. Another principle of the action is that anything in the action can, in principle, affect anything else. That's one place where the _astral_ space aspect of the game comes in handy.

      At the core of Homeworld Project are bouts, which take place between two entities in the action. These could be people, ships, space storms, rioting crowds, etc - but always something which a player (including the GM) has imbued with some of their limited resources. All that is required for a bout is how the entities are describe initiating the interaction. Then dice (d12s) are rolled or progressed, with the winner (if any) generally describing a consequence for the loser - rated by the difference of the two rolls. Other mechanics can allow the dice to be progressed further during a bout or alter the rating of a consequence. Still other mechanics (such as "narrative wounds") affect the description of initiating the bout. These bouts likewise affect the pacing of the story (every 1 or 12 brings the chapter closer to an end and ramps up the drama level). The values rolled often indicate what the next value for that entity will be (dice are not cleared after being rolled and as the drama increases dice tend to increase or decrease by 1 rather than being rolled).

      Another core of the system are grains, which belong to characters and ships (of the PCs) and are mirrored in the GMs resources, which are called reflections. Multiple grains can share the same reflection, which links them all. Grains are the mechanical representation of what a player's character is about, while reflections represent how this links with something in the larger world. A reflection is also what a player wants to achieve. Grains are a metaphor for the grain in the center of a pearl, as each layer is added, something beautiful or terrible comes closer to its own existence. At the 13th layer a reflection takes on an existence apart from the players, becoming a fixture rather than a central part of the story. This could mean finding the lost treasure or the success of the slave rebellion. Grain (and reflection) layers are used to bring in manifests (supporting characters and other entities), affect consequence ratings, or progress dice. If a layer progresses a die to 1 one layer is lost from that grain, if it progresses a die to a 12 the grain likewise gains (or regains) a layer.

      The result (from playtests) has been a game of building drama, where turning points may re-envision the core conflicts described by reflections but they remain on-going twisting and turning back on themselves till the 13th layer is reached.
