    \n I'd love to talk at length about it. I'm not sure any of my playtesting crew is involved in this forum (I may be wrong). But very soon, I might be posting the basic rules document draft (sans specific class rules, since I'm not finished with those).

    The basic flow of the game goes as follows:

    1) The GM presents the main conflict. The players determine how they want to tackle it, and why. They compile some dice. The GM compiles some dice. These represent the conflict, and the solution.

    2) Throughout the game, you do adventury stuff with somewhat-traditional pass-fail mechanics. Any given monster/trap/thing is represented by a pool of dice you destroy.

    3) However, while adventuring, if you're in pursuit of your team's revolution, your personal revolution, or fulfilling a personal need, you can take an improvement (think Apocalypse World Advancements, kind of) and risk manipulating the final solution. You might step up or down one of the solution dice, or add or subtract dice from the GM's pool, depending on what you're doing.

    4) At the beginning of the final act of play, the players roll their solution pool, and use it to dramatically reduce the GM's conflict pool. That makes the conflict pool reasonable to face. The players then face the diminished conflict pool using actions that go toward solving the problem. One character might be researching, one might be dropping rumors through the town, one might be slaying the evil baron. Everyone contributes toward the revolution in their own way.

    5) Everyone gets cake.

    \n The background solution comes down to various elements of it. For our specific purposes, they're Precedent, Research, Probability, Conjecture, and Experimentation. Every character class has one thing they're particularly good at contributing, and can develop others with advancement. So hypothetically, if a necromancer is ruling a village through fear, you might have a character that's looking into how to break some wards on the village, one might be looking into past things that have convinced the peasants to rise up, one might be actively hunting the wizard's pupils to find out their weak points, et cetera and so forth.

    It's rather abstracted, because it needs to apply to more possible challenges. Not everything's going to work perfectly within the constraints of the process, so we made some alterations and such in order to better fit things. The important part is, the various characters are contributing various parts to the communal solution, and one approach will not work. If you attempt to slay the dragon without first researching, using past experience, and otherwise thinking the problem through and experimenting, you will die. Sometimes, you need to take little steps, and try various solutions before you find the right one for the job.

    Have you ever played an adventure game where the party spent time buying supplies, planning tactics, researching history, talking to the townsfolk, and so forth in preparation for the adventure? We have too. But in Farewell to Fear, that preparation is the difference between a chance for victory, and utter annihilation.

    \n Well, the game works off "modules" which are kind of like single page playbooks. They're full of seeds, ideas, and all that stuff. Ideally, you either have one, or build your own. But that information is only there to foster improvisation. The players reserve a healthy dose of agency in determining results and consequences. Basically, whether or not something gives you experience is up to your answer to, "Does this mean something important to you?"

    And yes, it'd do an old school D&D style game pretty well. One of my goals was to encourage some of the play style that I always enjoyed with fantasy games, and give mechanical incentives for that style of behavior.
