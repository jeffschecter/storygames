      \n The Forge's long and often inward-facing conversation is not always easy to summarize or enter into. A lot of the work done over there is brilliant and I think the Forge deserves a lot of credit for shaping the online discourse of roleplaying and story games, at least among that audience that is aware of it. For some the Forge defined the lingua franca, for others it was an inscrutable stone around which the discourse flowed. Either way, the influence is there.

      That said, the Forge has a problem as an ongoing discussion, and that is its accessibility. I'm a highly invested, reasonably informed guy when it comes to RPGs, and I've never felt very comfortable or welcome stepping into the discussion over at the Forge. It always felt to me like a ring of smart people having a conversation at a convention, surrounded by rings of onlookers and side conversations. Because I wasn't there early enough to snag all the jargon and subtext at the beginning, and because I'm not one of the (for lack of a better term) cool kids in the inner ring, When I've visited the site, I felt like I'd be interrupting if I were to post something.

      It is, in other words, an entrenched brand. To get involved, you need to a lot of back reading, for good or ill, and often the gems to be read are half-buried in grainy discussion threads. If this winter phase means a new opportunity for folks to get aboard the train, then I celebrate it.

      If it's just going to mean more good things for existing Forge readers, then I applaud it from afar yet do not imagine that this will much affect people like myself.

      So I'm glad to see the Forge undergoing a conscious and controlled renovation, if that's apt, and even hope that I might find a seat in the pews this winter. But I won't be surprised if I don't.

      Edit: I'm sure it's possible to distill the conversation of the Forge's early years down into a book. Easy? Feasible in costs and benefits? I don't know. One difficulty would be making sure that credit went where it was due, I suppose. But the primary expression of Forge thinking seems to be in actual games and ongoing discussion. I certainly won't say that's bad, but it does carry with it an intellectual accumulation, piling up around those works and discussions, that means that much context for a new game or a new post can often be lost on a newcomer. So it goes.

      Posted By: TeataineWell, I'm beginning work on my diploma (one of two) now and it's going to be about RPG theory. My biggest sources are Huizinga, Caillois and Fink (theory of games), Erving Goffman (role theory), Ranciere (modern theater & performance), a few books about the philosophy of sport (tied to theory of games) and "some stuff some guys said on the internet" (ie. Baker, Edwards, Sorensen, Crane etc. etc.), so yeah, there's definitely a big gap there in terms of "official literature"
      \nIt makes me happy when people start talking about the scholarship of games. I have interested motives, to be sure, since it makes it easier for me as an academic to justify putting Ganakagok and Castle Bravo on my CV if I can claim that designing games enhances my ability to address and connect with the audience for scholarly articles about table-top role-playing.

      So my professional ambitions depend on the existence of this admittedly tiny audience--among others, the game studies crowd, the popular culture aca/fen, and our friends at the Scandinavian nodal point. Only among the last is the Forge recognized to any degree as an authoritative source on role-playing games, which is attributable to a bunch of different factors--academics tend to look askance at enthusiastic amateurs, and since the Forge is basically a design community or "expert player tradition" it's not to be taken all that seriously, the games studies folks are primarily albeit not exclusively oriented toward videogames--but the lack of a coherent, cogent, and accessible primer on the Forge's approach to understanding games is one of them. Emily Care Boss's chapter in Playground Worlds--"Key Concepts in Forge Theory"--is a nice review of the "Big Model," but it doesn't show how to listen carefully to an account of actual play and tease out from that account an understanding of how the social and procedural dynamics of the game are working together to create a given experience of play, which is what I think those who are well versed in the Forge approach are in fact particularly good at. Once you realize that "Creative Agenda" is about the dynamics at the table rather than the psychology of the individual, Forge theory becomes very interesting as a way of conceptualizing variations in game design, of exploring the "possibility space" of role-playing games and what they are capable of. And I think that's important, because the recent crop of books on role-playing (e.g., Cover and Bowman) tend to be written by academics using a specific disciplinary apparatus to talk about their own personal engagement with gaming--and we all know how idiosyncratic one's own personal engagement with gaming can be. The result is a partial or incomplete account of tabletop games, which is no good!

      So I agree that some sort of codification of the Forge approach would be a good thing. It may have to take place in pieces, though, the same way it was built. For example, Emily and I are working on a paper with J. Tuomas Harviainen for an academic panel about "immersion" in New Brunswick NJ in April put together by a fellow named Evan Torner; we're going to compare the Forge conversation on immersion with the development of the jeeper concept of "bleed" (in Scandinavia, the characters plays you!) in the service of an argument about how orientations to immersion are conceptual touchstones within communities of play. Check us out:\n
      White, Harviainen, and Boss say:Recognizing that \u201cimmersion\u201d is fundamental to a variety of emerging ludic or quasi-ludic forms characterized by the spatial, temporal, or social extension of the Huizingan \u201cmagic circle\u201d has a double effect. First, it extends the object domain of the academic field of game studies, which often seeks to limit itself to digital media as a way of circumscribing its boundaries. At the same time, it provides the impetus for a broader interdisciplinary engagement with the play element in culture. In other words, because immersion is central to many participants\u2019 understanding of their own experience in these activities, it can serve as a foundational problematic for the study of that experience as a cultural form and enable connections to be made across fields of study and types of play. However, its very centrality makes immersion problematical in a larger sense: it is used to mean different things by different people, some of whom are trying to talk to each other. This paper attempts to address this issue by examining the discourse of immersion in two different communities of play: (1) the U.S.-based \u201cindie [tabletop] RPG design community\u201d centered on an on-line discussion forum called \u201cthe Forge,\u201d on the one hand, and (2) the Scandinavian larp/tabletop hybrid movement known as \u201cjeepform,\u201d on the other. In both cases, the ambiguity of immersion as a concept can be shown to have been recognized by members of those communities, and alternative conceptual schemes that nonetheless valorize some form of immersive experience (while at the same time eliding the language of immersion) to have been adopted. For example, the \u201cBig Model\u201d of Forge theory identifies three different types of \u201cCreative Agenda\u201d that shape the aesthetic experience of play. Since identifying a group-level Creative Agenda is a matter of seeing what play-behaviors are socially reinforced, Forge theory implies an immersive ideal that is related to a mutuality of experience--a game that \u201cclicks\u201d for all participants. In contrast, the jeepform notion of \u201cbleed\u201d centers on an individual experience of play where engaging as a character in a game feeds back (or \u201cbleeds out\u201d) into the player\u2019s psychological reality. This suggests an immersive ideal where the point of play is to create psychologically or emotionally resonant individual experience. The paper concludes by suggesting that understanding the specific kind or quality of immersion that is valued within a particular community of play is an important analytic precursor in discussing the varieties of ludic experience.
      \nIn other words: games and gaming are becoming more pervasive and more diverse in form and function, and so figuring out how to get at the common underpinnings in the experience of play across that variety is probably worth doing. So I can't get too excited about the haters on therpgsite and the jokers on rpg.net. The former are a textbook instance of what political scientist Richard Hofstadter called the paranoid style in politics; the latter, a manifestation of what he called anti-intellectualism in American life. My reaction is to want to study them, possibly as part of a stab at contributing to the intellectual history of the Forge.

      I've read Emily's articles a while back, so I can't comment, I should read them again.

      1. History and context are a big problem, because a lot of it was discussion upon discussion at the Forge and you can't really lay that out chronologically. But if you want context, I think these two posts (one, two) can do a lot to explain the atmosphere in which the Forge is assumed to have flourished.

      Basically: bad experiences with play, dysfunctional, suffering groups and the subsequent investigation why that was so. Why were games sometimes fun and sometimes not?

      To those that never had a dysfunctional group, this means nothing, but it is indeed the "root of the problem". The point of the Forge was that play is supposed to be "personally and socially fulfilling" and it's obviously not being fulfilled if we're playing different games. Most groups or campaigns start with the assertion "Let's roleplay." without actually figuring what that means - often different things to different people.

      Another forge assertion was that "system matters". System is rules+social contract. So, the assertion that
      a) playing with D&D rules was not the same as playing with WW rules, or whatever - rules impact how the game plays
      b) that rules were not something that would necessarily "get in the way" but that it could actually help you get your desired experience
      c) that what people agreed at the table mattered just as much if not more than the rules - what people agree impact how the game plays

      2. Examples of games illustrating concepts in action
      Well, this is a problem, because a big part of Forge Theory rests upon the shoulders of actual play and social dynamics, not game texts. There are games that support certain styles of play, but don't assume that's all there is to it.
      For games that support Step on Up play, I would advise to look at early D&D, D&D 4E, 3:16 and Agon. An extreme example outside the domain of roleplaying is Chess.
      For games that support Story Now play, I'd suggest Sorcerer, Trollbabe, Apocalypse World and Dogs in the Vineyard. And extreme example outside the domain of roleplaying could be Improv/Storyjamming. (but not quite)
      For Right to Dream I don't know any games that were designed to explicitly support it. An extreme example outside roleplaying might be fandom/fanfic internet forum roleplaying.

      3. Glossary, well there is the Foge glossary, but I expect it's not very useful. I personally find the wikipedia article on The Big Model pretty informative and it explains all the core concepts.

      4. To see "how to use this in my own games", the question is: are your games dysfunctional? Or are you agreeing how to play and everything is going fine? In addition to those two blog posts above, here's the third one: click. It helps you get your group on the same page regarding agenda. You might also take a look at this flowchart. [EDITED picture into a link] I find it a little biased and perhaps outdated, but it does a fairly good job of summarizing a lot of discussion at The Forge into a step-by-step investigation of your own game. The three green results are supposed to correspond (more or less) to "functional" agendas that result in a good game. There are ways to play outside those results but it usually ends up in a loop, failed game or tedious experience.

      A thing I often notice is that people, when presented with those choices, say "but we do ALL of those things". Yeah, that's possible, too. I think the term for that was zilchplay or "unfocused" play. It works most of the time. But most of the time, one of those agendas is going to surface. And if the players aren't all onboard, they will be left behind.
      Which very often results in passive-aggressive behaviour at the table, arguments, deception, status games etc.

      To summarize, the only thing the Forge really offers a "regular Joe" is: Talk about your game with the other players for christ's sake and figure out what everyone wants to play.
      If you take another step it also gives you a bunch of tools and some terminology to have that conversation with your players.
      Another step further is design. How to make a game that will support those player choices.
      Another step further is Today. Where we stop talking about how games should be good, and start talking about ways of making them good (not implying that there hadn't been good games before).
      And beyond that lies the future.

      And that's it.

      Tell me if I'm making any sense to you.
