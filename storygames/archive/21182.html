    \n Sensible stuff, I dig.

    I think that the way you prioritize the rewards in terms of lesser and greater ones is somewhat biased in that it presupposes a certain type of player and play. In my experience the priorities you indicate (with social and experiental rewards being overwhelmingly more important than others) are typical of newbies, casuals and freeform gamers. It is not uncommon for long-term practitioners to take the experiental rewards for granted, ignore (or even despise) the social rewards, and have a passionate relationship with some less obvious, "deeper" rewards available in the activity. This is of course possible psychologically speaking because the experienced and competent player is capable of predicting with confidence that a non-obvious and abstract reward will reliably occur.

    For example, in my experience serious wargaming is ruled by the lesser rewards you list. The same goes for serious drama play.

    But then again, that's just vapid bickering over presentation. The base observations are solid, I feel.

    \n Yeah, if Forge stuff is nothing but tears for you, I can try to short-circuit that process, and maybe help you steer clear of situations that made people cry in the Forge years.

    Here's what I think is useful to take away from CA:

    1. It's actually a super simple concept until you start trying to get into specifics about types of CA.

    2. It's really hard to generalize about why people play without talking to all the people. This path of understanding is fraught with "well, I do it this way--doesn't everyone?" folly. If you're just trying to capture what's useful to you, then be careful about stating any of it as a general rule.

    ("Why do rpgs even work at all? Why do we play them?" is very different from "Why do rpgs even work at all for me? Why do I play them?")

    3. It's super hard to look at motives and rewards as a phenomenon in RPGs. There's little data that you can trust.

    4. If you're going to try, anyway, realize that looking at techniques and ephemeral "in the moment" stuff rarely captures the whole picture. You need to look at large-scale structures of play: Creative Agenda is a structure that necessarily (not incidentally) penetrates every layer of the Big Model.

    \n It is Creative Agenda, yeah. I didn't want to say anything because, like, what do we care? It's possible to say something new and interesting, and sometimes it's nice to start from scratch and say things in your own way. Just imagine if Rob opened up by lambasting some old essay from Ron and explaining about how "Simulationism" has been misinterpreted, and so on. And then somebody would've come in to complain about the Big Model, instead of letting Rob's points stand on their own.

    If we wanted to summarise Rob's article in traditional Forge terminology, here are the basic points as I understood him:
    * Roleplaying is rewarding as an activity for reasons outside the substance of play itself; we should not forget that in CA analysis, as human psychology mixes up social satisfaction with creative satisfaction easily and naturally. Rob discusses social reasons to play under the rubric of "social rewards" and compares them with creative reward structures.
    * Simulationism is the dominant creative agenda mode, Rob calls it "game-experiental rewards". It's the creative satisfaction you get out of Exploration in itself. He names three examples of psychological mechanisms that are activated by this creative agenda.
    * Social rewards and Simulationist agenda can and do clash when the players ignore one for the sake of the other.
    * Other creative motivations are considered lesser because Rob thinks that they are either more uncommon or weaker psychologically when compared to Simulationist base psychology - they're not as essential as "reasons to play rpgs", generally speaking. He explicitly identifies three psychological reward mechanisms which Forgite theory traditionally considers either social or fundamental to Creative Agenda, universally present in good play. Interestingly he does not directly name any of the unique psychological reward mechanisms involved in Gamism or Narrativism (competitive desires, need for self-expression, etc.).
    * Psychological mechanisms underlying creative agenda are not simple, because they form complex cycles of enforcement and have mutual dependencies. Ultimately, however, we should be able to pinpoint the psychological root needs that rpg activities serve, and those needs are going to be the same ones that all the other cultural activities tangle on, because that's how humans are.
    * The reason for why people care about creative agenda is that creative experiences - activating reward cycles - are pleasurable. Over a longer time-frame, the best roleplaying is the kind that gives out the most rewarding experiences. Essentially, if one wanted to interpret Rob as giving game design advice, he's saying that the best game is the one with a powerful, coherent Simulationist agenda, that's easy and fun to play with friends.

    I imagine that one might conclude that Rob's talking about something different because he's focusing not on the endlessly popular GNS modal theory, but rather the psychological nuts and bolts: he's asking why people have a creative agenda in the first place. People sometimes think that it's not creative agenda if it's not GNS. It's a good question to ask even in the Forgite theory context, specifically because Ron as the most read Forgite theorist never went into it in any excessive detail (due to his own disinclination towards psychology, I understand).

    Now that I went to the effort of translation, I suppose I might as well pinpoint my precise criticism of Rob's essay: as can be seen in my summary, I understand Rob to claim that Game-Experiental Rewards - or Simulationism - are universally the central psychological reward mechanism for roleplaying. I disagree: in my experience it is relatively common for various types of roleplaying that the intense rewards of immersion are largely ignored or taken for granted while the actual focus of play - and the reward psychology - concerns something else entirely. This is, of course, simply the observation that creative agendas that have nothing to do with Simulationism do exist.

    I often use the holodeck metaphor to explain this point, here's how it goes in this context: Rob's saying that the reason to love the Star Trek holodeck and want to buy one for your home is that it's such an intense experience, and you can get all sorts of kicks out of various programs as you immerse in the holographic entertainment. I remind him that many of us are certainly excited about the holodeck, but it's not necessarily the visceral excitement of immersing in it that intrigues us: it's also possible to play complex games of strategy and intrigue in it, or express your own artistic sensibilities and philosophy by programming it, or use it as a learning tool, and it's simply not true if you claim that the visceral thrill is always gonna be the main factor in turning on the holodeck.

    \nI bring it up because the only way I've ever been able to make sense of CA has been to ruthlessly separate it from player motivation, which is what Rob's essay seems to be digging at. While the two overlap, and realizing a particular creative agenda is very commonly a type of player motivation, I still think that the two can't be conflated.

    Some of the things Rob lists line up fairly well with identified CAs, but others, like social action, meaning rewards, and to some extent status rewards, do not.     Is it possible to play a game with a coherent creative agenda that you have no personal stake in, and still feel "rewarded" at the end of the game? You may get very few GE rewards, but that doesn't have to preclude having any fun at all. And even though the extent to which you experience other rewards may depend on adopting that creative agenda, the desire to experience them is still not the same thing as the creative agenda itself.
