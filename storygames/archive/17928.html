    \n I tend to like recognizing and labeling patterns as a way of understanding things.

    Seems I'm not the only one. I got this book a while back that does pretty much the same thing for computer games.

    So if I was telling someone how to get started in design, I would start with what Jason says - "play lots of different games". But I would add to that the message: "and look for patterns in them and connections between them".

    First, look at patterns and design elements in the core mechanical aspects and how those parts interact with each other.

    Then look at how patterns and design elements start to drive emergent properties that may not be obvious from a pure mechanical interaction standpoint.

    Once you start being able to see how a game does that, then it's mostly just a matter of practice. And I don't mean practice in terms of just acquiring initial proficiency. I mean practice the way one practices meditation or martial arts or yoga or painting or knitting - a state of continuous growth through the thoughtful repeated application of skill and technique.

    \n I tend to think of game design as a more specific branch of something we might call "social interaction design," which includes all sorts of stuff from urban planning to human-computer interfaces. Basically, you have to spend a lot of time examining human behavior and then experiment with structuring it in different ways (through game design) to create interesting interactions. There's lots of ways to approach this! You can study it academically (what do people want? how do they behave?), you can do a lot of trial and error, you can draw on your own personal experiences, etc. I think the most important things are to:

    1. Play a lot of different games. Like, at least a dozen new games every year, if not more.
    2. Play some games a lot. Many games reward long-term play and exploration, revealing new insights.
    3. Make a lot of different games. Work seriously on at least a half-dozen every year, if not more.
    4. Play some of your games a lot. Many of your games will be crap, but others will turn into worthwhile things. Play the crap out of them until you figure out how to get them to do what you want. But don't be afraid to decide something isn't working and abandon it permanently or temporarily.

    I discovered LARP. Time passed and I discovered boffer LARP. I played in two different systems which were "broken D&D copies" and drove me crazy for four or five major design reasons. I went home and thought... and discovered The Forge and thought... and outlined and spreadsheeted and wrote. From that, GLASS was born. It is lightly tested, in Gold Master for system test.
    ---
    Conclusion: I saw niches and filled them; or I saw problems and re-designed from the ground up to avoid them while preserving the intent or mode of play.

    \n I originally started on writing just background and settings, then moved to writing house ruled additions, then onto full module hacks, and am currently designing my first system from scratch, so I lack some of the experience of others in the thread, however:

    I think when looking at patterns, it is important to unpack them. It is not so much seeing the comparisons and being aware of the differences, but being aware of the 'Why'. This will help reinforce design decisions that you choose to make later on. Listen in on people at your gaming table and ask why they liked that particular feature, or what they thought was useful about it.
    For example, I did some work and threw up a Dark Heresy hack to run Shin Megami Tensei: Persona. I chose to turn the light/dark spells into damage dealers, rather than the instakills of the video game series, but also made all the elements effectively the same, with the differentiation being using the DH weapon properties. The resounding table opinion was that it made things easier, and better to remember. They checked the spell level and knew the numbers, which in a very crunchy system like DH is a godsend.

    On the other hand, it comes with noting the differences between games and comparing them.
    Eg:
    Fiasco and D&D sit at very far ends on pretty much all scales (Short form vs long form, Story game vs. Trad and a bushel of others). However, both of them use character differentiation mechanics. We all know that these are things utilised by many RPGs. They stop players at the table from feeling like they are blending into eachother, helps set up a productive narrative space for gaming, helps define how the player interacts with the world and others and encourages particular dynamics at the table during the game.

    D&D has possibly the most traditional of these mechanics, the race and class. It gives the player a 'job', something that they do in the world. It affects their skills and abilities, thereby encouraging them to interact with the world in particular ways. However, outside of particular niche cases (Such as the Paladin in some editions, who had to have a particular alignment considered, to the extent that D&D has roleplaying mechanics, to be the closest thing to it) it mandates very little about background, outlook or relationships. Bob the Fighter is likely going to be good at hitting things with weapons, probably quite strong and/or dextrous and he has little need for smarts, though he might have them in some corner cases. However, he could be a street thug or a nightwatchman or a knight in shining armour. Just knowing he is Bob the Fighter tells you a lot about what he can DO, but not a lot about who he IS. This makes sense given the design goals of D&D.

    Fiasco has a character differentiation mechanic in the form of Relationships and Needs. It is unlikely that players at the table share these, and in essence, they function as the 'race and class' of Fiasco. Within the game design goals here, however, the Relationships and Needs tell you very little about what your character can DO, but a lot about who they ARE. Many relationships may hint at some kind of profession, which would inform that - 'Newly Minted Gangsters' from the Saturday Night 78 playset comes to mind - but are often fairly loose in general. These gangsters could have any kind of background, training or proficiency, much how a D&D street thug could be a fighter, rogue, etc.

    Edit (For clarity): These decisions inform players how then to interact with the game and other players at the table. Within play, the status or ability granted you by the things you receive here affect progression and change the focus of the entire game as a whole, as players exploit the hooks given to them.

    While the two games at a large scale may seem very different beasts, and certainly play very differently, getting down to a microscopic level and really unpicking the stitches to see what bits go where, why, how that lets you accomplish what the game wants to do can be very useful.

    As much as I'm enjoying the "how to build a game from scratch" advice (and making one is totally on my bucketlist) if any of you have heard Luke (@Luke_Wheel) give his "Game Design is Mind Control" talk, my goal here is similar: to learn how to use the elements of gaming and game design to influence human behavior. In other words, I want to apply gamification to my studies and work that involves using more than stupid badges, and my inclination is learning how to analyze what games tick - and how games change the way people tick - is the best way to do that.

    \nA lot of my game design consulting work is in the gamification world. And I work with the Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab on some interesting behavior change work. So I can speak a bit about that.

    If you want to be a game designer, people have already given great advice about playing lots of different games (not just RPGs or card games or board games) and deconstructing them. Every great game can be improved in some way, and every crap game has something interesting in it. Try to squint your eyes a bit at the genre tropes and get at the underlying mechanics in the games.

    But influencing human behavior is something else entirely. You need to start with an end goal in mind. What is your intention? What does success look like? The you need to look at the commonalities of the current interactions and where they are falling short. Look for the possible trigger points where you can have a real effect on behavior, even if its a small one. Set a call-to-action that is easy to do and difficult to say "no" to. Have a way to track the behaviors so you can see what is working and where you need to improve.

    Doing this doesn't require game design or necessarily building a game around it. But understanding the principles of what makes some games tick is very useful. Engagement loops, trigger points, baby steps.

    You might find this interview interesting.

    \n I'd say that a major purpose of playing games to inform game design is to build a vocabulary of tools, learning what tools are out there and developing an eye for when you want to use which ones.

    I play a ton of games but don't always remember the tools they showed me. Accordingly, I've sought out lists of such tools as reminders. I haven't had a lot of luck with that on the RPG internets, but Rules of Play lists a few, and I have retained a few on my own, so here's a list:

    positive feedback loops (victory spiral, death spiral)
    negative feedback loops (losing? game gets easier! winning? game gets harder!)
    perfect information (pure strategy challenge)
    imperfect information (some element of gamble)
    skill*
    luck
    teamwork
    competition
    prisoner's dilemma
    trading
    auction
    secret bid
    make progress concrete (gold stars, points, level ups)
    unlockables
    victory conditions
    loss conditions
    product of play

    *what player skills are tested? players must value being good at these in order to engage
    memory
    logic, deduction
    processing (crunching numbers, calculating odds)
    acting
    lying, convincing
    reflexes
    storytelling
    improv
    writing
    drawing
    humor
    expertise (see Trivial Pursuit categories, niche interests, facts about people in the group)

    Once you get into the nitty-gritty there's probably an infinite number of specific techniques, but these are the first broad ones that come to mind.

    I'm not sure if this belongs in this thread or not; just putting it out there to see if you find it useful and want to pursue this direction, now or later.

    \nWell, my starting place for figuring out how to influence human behavior was to analyze what players want out of a game experience.

    I wrote an article on Analyzing Player Motive to Inform LARP Design, which goes into what sorts of things players want to get out of a game at any given moment, how to recognize what they want, and how to give it to them.

    That's a good way to start looking at how players of games tick and how players interact with the ticking parts of a game.

    After that, you can go all BF Skinner and start incentivizing behaviors you want to see.
