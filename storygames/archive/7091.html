\n OK, so here's the deal: You are trying to revive a dude in full on ventricular fibrillation, trying to convert his heart rhythm.

Each round, you have a default 10% chance of success.

Each round after the first, you may (by increasing personal stress) elect to permanently increase your odds by 10%. So you can (for a cost of one stress) begin round two with a 20% chance of success and round seven, if you've kept on spending, with a 70% chance of success (at a total cost of six stress).

If you have not succeeded by the end of the seventh round, the dude dies - and this has serious implications in a variety of arenas.

The stress you accumulate is mechanically painful to shed, and messes up your guy later on. You want to assiduously avoid it, but you also don't want this dude to die. Mathletes, what is the optimal strategy?

    \n First of all: Spend any stress you're willing to spend right up front. Delaying any spending of stress is clearly sub-optimal, because each stress gets you an additional 10% times the number of rounds left.

    Probabilities, spending...
    0 stress -- 52% chance of saving
    1 stress -- 76% chance
    2 stress -- 87% chance
    3 stress -- 93%
    4 stress -- 96%
    5 stress -- 97.5%
    6 stress -- 98%

    Just looking at the rate of return on your stress there, I'd say that 2 or 3 stress is about optimal. Certainly after 3, the marginal gain for spending more stress is pretty minimal.

    \n Probably the optimal strategy, the first time playing, is to let him die (well, do the default 10% (since it's for free) but otherwise nothing else) and deal with the serious implications when they arrive, stress free and at full capacity. Otherwise your risking impairment to support a resource you might very well be able to shrug off anyway.

    The thing about life is that you only get one go at it. While with games, you can afford to use a strategy which allows the fact that you can always play again and try and save him next time if it's worth it.

    I suppose you could better emulate lifes difficulty (I'm just guessing your aiming there) by having him grant some special sort of award or secret knowledge or something nifty at the end of the game, but if he dies its lost. Either forever - and I'm talking RL forever - you'll never reveal it. Or that there's a RL delay before they get another chance at keeping a guy alive and learning the special secret. That'd shift it more toward 'play it right the first time' like real life is.

    I suppose some people play from the heart normally and try to 'play it right the first time' all the time. But I think there's a subtle yet powerful difference between wanting to play that way, and having no damn choice but to play that way.

    \n Thanks everybody for your thoughts on this.

    Ben, adding stress limits your choices, and the more stress you have, the more negative your set of options becomes. So you'll have goals that can only be accomplished with, say, 2 or less stress, but if you have eight, for example, your options are: mess up because of a self-destructive habit or lose a patient. So those are crappy options. You can halve your stress by choosing a particular scene type, but while you are relaxing the other doctors in the E.D. are stealing your thunder and getting better parking spaces.

    There's no stress penalty associated with losing a patient, but you lose a point of positive currency which hurts a bit. Also, if you lose two patients in a single session, your doctor is fired. So tactically, going with the 10% and allowing a patient to (potentially) die isn't a deal-breaker, but it isn't smart.

    One last point, if it helps - cardiac arrests are pretty noteworthy and unusual. You'll likely see a couple in a session. I just want them to have an interesting stress-related mini-game associated with them, because they are such a staple of medical drama.
