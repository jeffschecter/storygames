I've been really honored and moved by the response Human Contact has gotten. The project is about to go submarine, taking all the backers with it. While in submarine mode, the backers will be getting glimpses of Alex Drummond's new art, playable drafts, and probably a goodie or two that won't make it into the final. If you want to see that stuff as it happens, please back the project and join the Human Contact forum on Xenoglyph so you can get the previews and give feedback about your experiences playing the game.

\n

And speaking of those playable drafts, I'll be looking for committed playtesters from that group. Backers have already committed materially to the project and they'll be the first to get to play with it. Those committed people will give me the kind of feedback I want, and I'm really looking forward to hearing about the worlds, cultures, relationships, and individual people they create.

\n

Periscope down in 26 hours.

    Totally! Do I remember correctly that you're a backer? If so, head over to the closed playtest forum on xenoglyph and sign up! I'm skeptical of the onlineability of any of my games, but an cheerfully enthusiastic about the prospect of being shown a way that works.

    \n

    I should note that Human Contact has an order of operations of the chapters you play. You start out in the Contactor with a bunch of Academics, then go to the colony to see what their culture is like and how it's evolving, and only on the third chapter do you start to mash the cultures together. For one-shots, I recommend using the Black Ops Anthropology rules, so you can at least start with the assumption that the culture is going on with no knowledge of the Academy's presence. It's definitely worked out better some times than others, but at least you've got a chance to have the colonial culture do its own thing that way.

    \n

    I think Kickstarters work best when you can make a pitch about something that's already a thing. What I'm doing right now is making Human Contact coherent and sewing all the notes together. To be sure, it's a lot of work, but the completion of the project is the beginning of the publication, not the end. I'm expecting to burn through my first print run in a couple of months. The point is not just to send books to the backers, but to invest heavily in a print run to sell, which can then pay for future print runs... and so on, until it stops selling.

    \n

    So what, you might ask, am I doing on Story Games? Good question.

    Oh! Huh. For some reason I'd gotten the impression that the final 150-200 page HC book was going to look a lot like a setting supplement. You know, tons of details about character backgrounds, cultures, activities, toys, threats, etc.

    \n
    \n

    That's exactly what it is; it's just stuff that's designed to be questions or creative constraints, not answers. I also don't really care was Academic Earth is like that much; I have some established things that the players will wind up challenging anyway, but I'm taking the Le Guin route: use established facts as convenient, but care more about what's happening here and now than making sure it all matches up. She even has two planets named Werel, completely by accident.

    \n
    \n

    I mean, I know there's game procedures too, like the chapter structure, but that stuff tends not to jack up the page count as much.

    \n
    \n

    In Shock: those things take about 50 pages. I expect it to be about the same for Human Contact. The rest of the book is effectively inspiration and ethical questions, wrapped in a unifying aesthetic that helps to frame them. And as many illustrations as I can afford to license or produce, serving the same functions.

    Since the only people who are going to see it are either people who have lived there for years, or don't speak or read the same language, sigils are often iconographic. When there's writing, it's in Kepho-Rn, or perhaps a language with some symbolic value, the way Latin does in our mottoes.
