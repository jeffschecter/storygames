\n (or: what do you do when some of your players won't play together?)

As you might have read about a month ago, I had some pretty major inter-personal stuff happen between three of my regular players; they're now no longer friends and 2 of them are unwilling to talk to the other (and vice versa) - which, as you might imagine, makes role-playing mighty difficult. I've gathered up a new group of people who are down to play basically whatever I have for them (all new to the hobby), but I don't want to exclude the friends who had the fight - I'm still close to all of them.

What I was thinking of doing was, basically, alternating those guys - have the 3 new guys in for every session (maybe once a fortnight), and have A and B one session, then C in the next session, then A and B again, and so on. It's obviously not ideal, but it means everyone gets to play at least sometimes, and it stops A, B, and C from having to deal with each other.

Has anyone done anything like that in their games? I'm worried for a couple of reasons. Firstly, I'm looking at running Monsterhearts or Leverage; while I think they'll both mechanically be able to handle the "slightly different line-up each week" thing I'm not sure that the fiction will be able to deal with it. (Especially MH - there's no good reason for teenagers to be out of town every second week, is there?) Second, I think it might be tough on the regular players, having to keep track of who was there for important things and who wasn't. The other thought was running two games in alternating weeks (MH for A and B, Leverage for C), but I'm pretty new to GMing and I'm not sure I can take that on just yet.

I'd appreciate any thoughts you might have, especially anything coming from your own experiences dealing with stuff like this.

    \n There are at this moment 22 people in the group I am playing with and we play once or twice a week. Some of the players show up on most playdates but most participate only sporadically. To ensure that the right amount of people show up at the right times we use www.trello.com where we can get a good overview of when people are free to play. Using this kind of scedueling device you can put the responsibility of the two not meeting in their hands instead of you having to juggle them around. So, get trello up and running and just say something like: "Hey everyone, sign up to the dates when you are available and we will play something where enough people sign up!". Of course longer campaigns (or even few session campaigns) are troublesome but not impossible (we have a few running in parallel right now) so I would recommend focusing on one-session games like Fiasco, While the World Ends, The Daughters of Verona, Best Friends, etc etc.

    \n I did this before very successfully.
    In Mutant Chronicles, of all things.

    It began as something simple, where there were two high-paying jobs offered at once, and the characters shuffled themselves into teams. I understand that your set-up is less ideal.

    The groups were in different fictional locations, but they would catch news from one another. When things got terrible on Venus, I sent the group on Luna the headline. They thought it was interesting, and it built into their own tension, but they had no idea it was the other players that were causing the trouble. They affected each other in little ways like this. It made things easier for me.

    Where one group would do a favor for one organization, (say a less-than-legal arms dealer...) the other group would get ambushed by cheap thugs with surprisingly effective weapons (which they could only afford because of the less-than-legal arms dealer).

    Definitely try it. It can be a lot of fun, and you shouldn't have to feel like the groups will do completely unrelated stuff, or completely intertwined stuff. You'll find your own style and maybe everyone will have fun.

    ... but that would require two entirely separate groups each week. I mean, for blending, I would pick up something like Remember Tomorrow?
