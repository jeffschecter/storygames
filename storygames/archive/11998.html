    \n Don't Rest Your Head, despite the high quantity of dice required - it's easy to pitch to new players, quick to play, amusing, weird, fun.

    Polaris, because it has few material components, never gets enough long-form play (I'm thinking there's fellow hitchers to play with...), and it's great.

    Breaking the Ice, to play with cute girls you encounter - also low on material components, very easy to teach, quick, fun.

    Mist-Robed Gate, fairly free-form, easily played around a fire, dramatic and serious. Assumes you have a sheathed knife and a scarf, which if you're hitching, you may well have.



    All told, it's a small stack of books (only a few hundred pages, cumulative), and the material components are: a block of d6's, a bunch of tokens in two colours, a sheathed knife, a scarf, some pencils.

    Those components will also let you play Yahtzee and Fargo, so that's a plus.

    Under the Bed was designed to be stuffed in your back pocket. And it's only twelve bucks. If you're into The Journey West (AKA Monkey or The Monkey King), you probably want a copy of Ben Lehman's Journeying West, which is designed to exact UtB specs so you can wrap a rubber band around all of them together. It takes 7 or 8d8 per player, which, well, it was my first published game, so sometimes you make marketing mistakes.

    \n

    (Edited to add: you can play with d6es, but it makes success really easy. I encourage you to try! D10s make winning conflicts marginally harder, and don't have the bulk price advantage of d6es, but would work fine if you've got a ton of them.)
