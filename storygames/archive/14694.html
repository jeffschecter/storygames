\n I always GM for my group and I always have a strong vision of what I want play to be like (Color, tone, atmosphere). My group is always on board and we see eye to eye generally.

I have this problem though, where I veto shit a little too often. See, when we play my goal is to produce fiction that is comparable to literature.

When we play Dust Devils I want it to be fucking Blood Meridian

When we play Burning Wheel I want it to be Tolkien

When we play IAWA it is Robert E. Howard goddamnit.

And, well this doesn't work all the time. Sometimes it does and play is that damn good and we still talk about those campaigns. I want to recapture that magic each and every time we play.

So yeah I veto shit. We'll come to a discussion point in game about where somethings going to go or I'll pull the Mountain Witch Trick and the answer won't be perfect. I'm picky and I'm not a dick about it, the players don't seem to get too discouraged.

I'll say things like "Yeah, that's pretty good, but..." I clearly know why I do this but I want to stop worrying about making the perfect story. I just don't want things to be lame and not everything that is thought up on the spur of the moment is golden. So how do you reconcile this?

I guess at the heart of the issue that I don't 100% agree with Graham's statement from Play Unsafe which states something like "Don't try to be brilliant, be obvious."

I know from personal experience that sometimes discussing something for 2-4 minutes and digging for the perfect idea sometimes leads to brilliance. Sometimes it drags things on and the first, obvious thing was best.

Does anyone else have these same hangups? How do you deal with and learn to let go?

    \n Dirty Secrets has a great little mechanic. It has a clever name, but I forget what it is. Basically, it works like this: when ever anyone at the table isn't feeling what's going down they can veto it. But, if even one other person at the table speaks up in defense of the thing the veto is over ridden. What that means effectively in play is that everything you say has to be appealing to at least one other person. You don't have to please everybody, you don't have to pause play to take a vote, you don't have to get all Universalis and bust out hard core challenge mechanics...it just flows.
    \n
    \nSo maybe what you could try in your game is that. You find yourself vetoing something, fine, that's not a bad thing. But if even one other player is like "I don't know, I kinda like that thing" then just back off and veto the veto. You may not even need to make this a formal part of your game (although I think it works best when everyone can do it) but it might make for a nice personal training exercise to give you a signal that you can recognize to just let it happen.

    You have a "strong vision" to "produce fiction". There's nothing wrong with that goal. So ignore those who say it's wrongbadfun:\n
    you're saying, "When we engage in a semi-structured group play activity, I want [my memory of play] to be comparable to items of heavily crafted individual art."

    That is a crazy thing to ask for. You have to stop asking for it.

    \n

    I don't want me games to be literature. I want them to remind me of literature but to ask for our table's product to be some kind of edited, re-written text sounds like putting contact poison in everyone's dice bag to me.
    \nInstead, focus on how to achieve your 'produce fiction' goal.



    you say things like "Yeah, that's pretty good, but..."

    Is that even a Veto? A veto is a 'no'. You're prompting players to make a second suggestion, and go with whichever one's better.
    (Which is, if you look closely, less of a Veto than the story-games-approved technique of "Try a different way." Try a different way does veto the first suggestion.)

    David Mamet says the first suggestion offered will be the mind's brown-nosing attempt to be liked, by blurting the Teacher's Pet 'right' answer (pandering for social approval), or by blurting clich\xe9 (repeating what you've seen TV, movies, books do before, rather than answering the question as if it were the first time it had ever been asked.) Mamet advises getting past the initial lazy answers to Produce Good Fiction.

    You want to get-to the point where your & your players' answers aren't about your Selves...
    but about the subconscious tackling the task at-hand: answers which best match the question, rather than suit the answerer.

    So, 'yeah that's pretty good, but...' is actually good technique. It coaxes out a second reply, while putting the ego in as little defensive posture as possible.



    You don't 100% agree with Graham's statement from Play Unsafe which states something like "Don't try to be brilliant, be obvious."

    Mamet gives the same advice to actors... but not to screenwriters or directors. Plays and movies have a division of labor which allows that.

    You could adopt a similar division of labor, where you take responsibility for being brilliant, and the players execute it. But you'll be violating story games sacred cows if you do so,
    so you may not find much help here in figuring out ways to do that well.


    Also, be aware that 'brilliant' and 'obvious' are a false dichotomy. It's the 'trying' [too hard] part, that's the problem.

    I am a broken record about recommending David Mamet's book "On Directing Film", but it does directly address the issues of this thread, minus the conclusion that it's 'impossible'.

    \n For years, I too was interested in the fiction output of gameplay. Story After.

    And when that was my interest, it served me well. It didn't conflict with Narr techniques, because I wasn't walking in with a pre-concieved
    notion of Story Before. I wasn't fighting against Emergent surprises.

    Now that I'm less interested in fiction as an artifact of gameplay, I am passionate about fewer games:

    I've lost my yardstick to measure which games and techniques suit my subjective needs.
    This game is good at This. That game is good at That. Neither one's particularly good or bad for me personally,
    because there's nothing specific I want from the game.



    One advantage of Story After, is that it serves as a good tie-breaker. If I want to do one thing (sink a ship) and you want to
    do another thing (burn the ship); who gets his way?

    If we share a stated-goal of wanting to produce good fiction; I can argue that a sunken ship sets us up for a future story hook
    about searching for the lost treasure. Whereas a burnt ship is gone for good.

    I can win you to my view on a basis other-than my personal gratification at expense of yours, because we agree on the criteria
    by-which we are making decisions.
