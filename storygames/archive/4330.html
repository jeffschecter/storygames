\n So, I was just over on the BW forums and a thread there made a lightbulb go off over my head. I think I finally realized what the crux of the issue or problem or dispute or whatever over social issues actually is, and why it was so hard to come to any satisfactory resolution:

(Here's my hypothesis, anyway)

The argument isn't actually about mechanics for social interaction as opposed to freeforming social interaction; the argument is about the intersection of narration and mechanics. In a lot of Indie games I've played, the narration and the mechanics are very closely tied together, because in order to bring something into the game mechanically, you also have to bring it in through narration of your character's actions\xe2\u20ac\u201dor, significantly, through your in-character speech!

BW and DitV spring to mind immediately, but there are almost certainly other instances of this principle. SotC certainly seems to be in this category, I just know so little about it... :-(

Anyway, that general principle, for all mechanics, applied to social mechanics, is what can make the latter work, make their use interesting and engaging.

And this isn't a principle I've seen in trad games. Yes, in order to use the ability you have to narrate appropriately. You can't describe holding a knife to someone's throat and roll Diplomacy in D&D; that action clearly falls under Intimidation. But interesting narration will get you at best a +2 bonus that's completely up to the whimsy of the GM.

Note, this has nothing to do with stakes setting, or in other words the issue of "my guy would never do that!" That's handled by things like BW's rule that you can't force someone into a Duel of Wits if you can't agree to stakes. What I'm trying to get at here is actually much more basic. It has to do with when you narrate, and in the games we're discussing, you do a lot more narration *before* the roll than in the trad games I've experienced.

Yes? No? Death threats? Poo-flinging?

    \n That's a good insight, noclue, and it's exactly why the conflict-level analysis doesn't ring true for a lot of players. They don't see the mechanic as resolving a conflict between players. Although the player of the dude with the sword may want his guy to hit the orc, he may not (and I submit probably rarely) think the GM wants him to miss. And he is usually right about that, typically GMs don't have that level of granularity of intent. Often times players don't either - they may separate tactical or second-to-second decisions from their intent fairly explicitly, although they have to make tactical decisions, they're not invested in whether any one choice turns out to work or not.

    In other words, if you asked my group "So do you think Steve should hit the orc?" most, including the GM, would shrug. That's why we use the mechanics, not to resolve conflicts between players, but instead to establish narrative at a level of detail where nobody has a particular point of view.

    \nI'm gonna come down on the 'no' side. IME, the difference is precisely the inverse: you do a lot less narration before the roll in the more Narrativist-oriented games. Precisely because of the close tie between mechanics and narration, combined with a frequent emphasis on "cutting to the chase" and emphasizing the 'important' scenes, plus [excellent] ideas like "say yes or roll the dice". These factors combine to deprecate lengthy narratives, because if it doesn't drive to the conflict, or lead to mechanical results, it's devalued.

    A specific example: I played an excellent game of Primetime Adventures at Gen Con. Each scene we set the setting, did a bit of RPing in those cases where we didn't know the conflict right away, resolved the conflict, and RPed the results. So, in most cases, ~5min to get from scene opening to resolving the conflict. Similar scenes from my trad gaming could've easily taken an hour of RPing before we'd get around to worrying about whether or not there even was a conflict, much less resolving it. And, in fact, my trad gaming experience is that probably 90% of the time around the table has nothing to do with conflicts or their resolution. So, always driving to conflict, without any mechanical changes, would cut the narration:mechanics ratio by a factor of 10. And i'm not talking about off-topic discussions and other distractions, i mean of the serious, on-topic, focused play.

    Which may tie into the "20min of fun in 4hrs" meme. That is, for someone who only sees the heart-wrenching, thought-provoking, tough-choice conflicts and decisions as fun, yeah, most of my gaming would fit that definition. But we were having fun with every bit of it--and maybe more fun with the stuff that didn't tie to conflicts or address themes than the stuff that did. Also, for me, the very act of driving to the conflict actually decreases the fun for me. In general, a given conflict will be more engaging for me if we got there through a couple hours (or months) of meandering, low-intensity, RPing; than if we simply describe the conflict, and set the stage as it comes to a head, and then play out the climax of the conflict.

    Which, at least to me, *isn't* a tangent, but directly relevant to your initial question of the interaction of mechanics and narration. The big difference i see with lots of more focused indie games isn't the lack of narrational impact on mechanics (though, you're absolutely right, in a lot of trad games, that is unfortunately true--or at least possible), it's the lack of mechanical necessity for narration. In With Great Power\xe2\u20ac\xa6, i can narrate all i want, but if i don't have the cards or traits to back it up, it's has no impact on the story. So it's kinda pointless. In OtE, the narration is frequently the whole point, with rules just intruding on occasion, and conflicts, at least IME, easily and frequently resolved without ever touching the dice; and narration can even be it's own resource, rather than being tied to or constrained by some other mechanical construct. [i'm thinking specifically of the rules that lack of narration causes your combat rolls to steadily decrease; though narrating bonuses is constrained by your bonus dice, just like escalating in DitV or changing tactics in WGP.]

In fact, when nobody is invested, often nobody wants to make up the narrative, and it's best to leave it to a die roll.     \n

Nat is right that often this goes on far longer than when using systems with a Narrativism bent (really more just games about discerning player intent), because often the players have no recourse other than to just continue arguing.       \n See, that's my point. "Narrate this and you get a cookie" is passive and possibly lazy. It merely looks otherwise because people are moving their jaws to get said cookies. The assumption -- the destructive, vision-limiting assumption -- is that cookie handling is the sole axis of reward, whether it's a cookie for picking good feats for a cookie for pulling the narrative levers for a cookie.

      I've written about this in the past and called in consumerist gaming, because it follows that model instead of the dramatic troupe or competitive wargaming routes often seen in other contexts. You (for the sensitive, you can assume this is not "you personally") approach the table with the assumption that you will be paid in some symbolic fashion for the quality of your participation. In some ways this comes from the failure of other modes to discover and disseminate good craft. What's missing is a coherent plan to entertain (gamers still do not do much thinking about their role as the audience of each other's efforts) or a plan to explore a theme situated within one's personal interests instead of for the sake of the sockets the game offers. I would really like to see a vision of narrative in games that is expressed with more passion than one associates with getting bonus Air Miles or coupons or somesuch.

      Without the expectation of reward, raw commitment isn't safe -- like I said, it's not for everybody. It means you fall on your face when you think you stunt should be cool. It means that your impassioned speech is sometimes no better than saying, "I make a speech." But when we basically trust in this commitment from everyone this tension between our desires and the game exists to provide a dramatic rhythym specific to the form instead of paying the player off to emulate some other form. Naturally, there are degrees to this, where we can mix directed reward with that tension. I prefer this for "genre emulation," because they add a bit of flavour without underminin the unique aspects of RPGs as a form.

      I've been hiding away from the forum for a while just due to time. A lot of good ideas here, but I just can't keep up with all of them.

      Now that I've got to talk to a LOT more people, I think I can clearly state some of the following things, and 'hopefully' get this thread back on topic.

      Ok. I think the OP is on the right track, but one (note ONE) of my problems with rules for social interaction is a question of granularity in the results. A sword makes people bleed in exactly the same way every time. We can argue some subtleties to it, but cutting someone will never make them get better.

      Now, in a social situation, something that hurts in one case, helps in another. Take the knife example for intimidate. It's intimidating to someone who is alone in a warehouse, it's not intimidating when they know they have a friend in the shadows with a gun. For some people being loud and yelling really shakes them up, for others it's just fuel to their inner fire (he he, I got under their skin). Even something as senstive as race/gender, I'm scared of the opposite race/gender, or they're all worthless scum who can't touch me. And the degree to which each person feels about it is different.

      The variables just aren't quantifiable without falling into stereotypes. A little give and take by a human being is necesairy IMHO.

      And, to clear something up D&D wise.

      No, there is no "+2 for using a knife to intimidate" but the GM can make the call, and the player can ask for the call, to either lower the DC or create a modifier for it. If you don't let player decisions and RP affect the mechanics that are there, that's your choice. Not a failing/quality of the system. I personally have stacked lots of modifiers up and down both ways on things based on character action. Both in the conversation, and things they've done elsewhere in the game world. By leaving these rules 'open' I would think you have a lot more rope to run with both as a GM and as a PC.

      On the other hand, if rules are in place for social interaction, and you apply them here and there, but not every where, then you're creating a different issue, directly system related.

      Again, these are just my opinions.

      \n John:

      Yes, I've read the article, and I think it proves the exact opposite of what it's talking about.

      No one 'wins' that scenerio. No one stops moving forward. The situation does change, but people don't loose themselves in the process. They get to react to the new scene. Are they going to change or not isn't really up for grabs. How much and in what ways are the only real questions.

      If you've decided you're only following one story, then yes, two people move into supporting roles for the one story. But if you're doing that, then you're missing out on some of what can make RPG's great. Multiple stories, growing, coliding, seperating, falling, growing coliding, seperating, falling...

      Each of those people are going to have a different reaction to exactly the same scene. They are going to be more, or less, involved with it. I trust the player to handle the new situation over a mechanical system. They will take way more things into account with the new situation than I can. Will they do it in a way that keeps their character persona one they're interested in following? Sure. Is that so bad? Not usually.

      When the player begins to react continually in a way where they're trying to 'win', it's time to decide what the goal of the table is. Are we competing? Or are we working as a team? I don't mean characters, I mean players. Are we trying to defeat the other players, or are we all working together for the best story possible?

      One means staying true to character, even over self preservation, the other means the opposite.

      Every one of my table rifts in 20+ years of gaming comes down to that problem. One person trying to win, while someone else trying to play as a team. The table has to agree. We want to win, or we want to work together. If they do, the social part tends to take care of itself.

      Now, I'm not sure where your going with the jetliner example there. Are you saying that it should have had more roles? Or that because it only had 1, that the system isn't granular?

      My point is that by leaving things as open ended as possible, it gives the players the chance to nudge things in a situation how they think makes sense.

      Before we can get on with that, we need to get into what skills are, why they encompas a series of complicated things (or don't) and what all that means. Frankly, that's a topic in and of itself.

      But if you can accept that 'piloting' means 'flying planes', the amount of deviation from ideal flight conditions covered by that description is up to the table. Does weather count? Run way conditions? People shooting at you? Your health? Age of Plane? Pilot familiarity with class and size?

      If you choose to consider these things, you as humans, can filter them in. If you don't, don't. The books, to my understanding, say as much. If you choose to just make a flat roll, and that's fun for your table, then good. Have fun.

      Now with your plane example, the modifiers are all set. If you wanted them spelled out in rules, I'd have no problem with that because a wet runway is always harder than a dry one.

      Now, spiders scare me a lot less than my wife. If you whip out a house spider, she'll roll before you can say "look what I have here." I, on the other hand, just got really brave, because if that's your opening move, you're obviously new to this.

      If you're just looking to get info on what someone's weak spot might be, and you're using D20, I'd probably allow a sense motive roll to give you some insight. If you're using my 'dream' system, I'd expect you to try things till you figured it out. Just like anyone else would have to do.

      \n This may have been mentioned upthread, but my thought is that it's pretty easy to remove compels (at least the kind you're uncomfortable with) without breaking the game.

      Make any compel be instead an Aspect-related complication or issue to be dealt with. If you don't want people to be able to buy them off, then just hand them a Fate point when it's resolved.

      That keeps the "Here's something keyed to your character, that you have to deal with; enjoy" effect, and I think it's mentioned as an option in the book.

      Of course, the easiest way to avoid Compels of your emotion or personality traits is to not take Aspects that could be compelled that way. If your group prefers stuff like that to be role-played out in dialog anyway, they don't need Aspects relating to that kind of stuff. It doesn't have to be "I invoke your Greed Aspect, act greedy", it can be "I invoke your 'Veteran' Aspect, that guy across the bar looks exactly like someone you shared a foxhole with."
