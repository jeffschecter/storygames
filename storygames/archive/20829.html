    \n Single protagonist, inner monologues, inconsequentiality, fracturing psychies, horrific revelations, inevitable end...

    It's downright Lovecraftian. In fact, this is the most Lovecraftian RPG I've ever encountered. The only fundamental difference are the anxieties addressed. For example, concerns over the other (and its racist implications) are absent, but concerns over issues specifically relevant to women are there in spades, and it has me thinking that perhaps gender is largely defined by a set of concerns, and that horror as a genre is essentially feminine in nature.

    In addition the game's feminine perspective feels authentic rather than forced, the art and layout (on the Kickstarter alone) lickably gorgeous, the writing threateningly sensual, the system supports the premise and gives players permission to explore it, the rules are clear, the prep engaging, and play easy to jumpstart.

    It's not even finished and I think it's a masterpiece.

    My only concern are the politics which I'm certain will get in the way. I've already seen people with otherwise insightful comments refer to potential customers as 'allies'. We don't need that. The work stands on its own, and making it about women in gaming rather than the work of three wonderful individuals who happen to be women will only serve to erase them.

    Because a man is recognized for their individual achievements, while a woman is recognized for the achievements of all women. How is that equality? And the biggest problem in promoting this is figuring out how not to appear as if there's a feminist agenda behind it.

    \n Dave, here's something my friend J. Li said better than I could:

    "Bluebeard's Bride blew me away when I first played it, in no small part because it contains some fascinating technologies.

    First of all, there is an effect that I have a hard time describing that I have come to increasingly value in media, a sort of feminized structure. Historically, the "female-centric" version of something has taken what is perceived to be "feminine content", and stuck it into spaces that "masculine content" occupies, within a design narrative that is fundamentally still based on masculine logic.

    The simplest example of this I've historically used is Copenhagen Beer-- a beer "for women" whose presentation doesn't involve "girly' images, but rather a sort of elegance that rethinks the contexts in which women may want to approach beer-- namely, while feeling haute instead of just casual fun. (Seriously, go image search it, it's very educational.)

    Bluebeard's Bride does the same thing with indy rpgs. When I first played it, I was most struck by a sort of frictionlessness of the experience of immersing in play that I didn't know I had previously felt restricted in.

    It takes the question of "what is gradual horror?" and answers it from scratch from a feminine perspective. It's not a "classic" (read, historically masculine-conceived) horror story about the encroachment of a dangerous man, a creepy house, and unsettling clothing. Instead, it's about a growing unease of reality, and the creeping boundaries between the real self and the performed self, and their eventual dissolution.

    What's lovely about Bluebeard's Bride, in short, is that you could hypothetically take classically masculine content, plug it in, and it would still be a game of feminine horror.

    To get there, Bluebeard's Bride introduces a number of new and essential techniques for onboarding the player to this new but deeply immersive way of thinking. From the way it ritualistically begins with the telling of the story so that there are no surprises, to the unexpected effect of thinking the ring is power when it's really not, to the core all-players-are-the-same-character design, I fully expect it to be profoundly seminal in the this field.

    The fact that the Apocalypse engine is nigh-unrecognizable is a huge advantage-- Bluebeard's Bride does a beautiful job of stripping what it needs from historical options and re-oozing it together in an entirely new way.

    Anybody who wants to produce representative games at a structural rather than purely content level needs to see this game.


    (PS, while we are talking about structure-- structure can be strengthened greatly by gravitas, and gravitas can actually be built with money. If you are an ally and backer, please consider backing at one level higher than you ordinarily would.)"

    \nI have not played but from watching some of the AP's one of the mechanics to encourage denial is healing of trauma.

    Each of the sisters marks trauma as they are injured, but if they take a Token of Faithfulness upon exiting a room (i.e. believing in Bluebeard), they all heal a trauma. If they take a Token of Disloyalty, they mark an additional trauma. Trauma can lead to a sister shattering, which basically moves them over to be an ally of the Groundskeeper (the GM) where they work against their sisters.
