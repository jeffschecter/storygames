\n DISCLAIMER: I ask this not out of hostility or malice, but bewilderment.

I bring this up because another cool game that I am finally getting around to noticing, Microscope, I go to buy, and discover that the PDF tacks on another six bucks to the price of the hard copy. I just don't understand it. The person who is buying the physical book wants the PDF for one, two, or all of three reasons as I see it:

1. In order to have a copy in their hands quicker than UPS/USPS delivers packages, in order to start reading right away.
2. In order to have a copy of several of their favorite games on an e-reader, as opposed to lugging around that many hard copies.
3. Similarly, in order to print out copies of key bits of the book, like condensed instructions, reminders, character sheets or other materials from the book for their players to use at the table.

So yes, there are benefits to a PDF that make the bundle more attractive than a hard copy alone, but it is blatantly obvious to the end user that it is not costing the publisher a dime to drop them an email with a PDF copy attached, so it feels a bit like being gouged.

    \n The PDF you give to customers is not generally going to be the same one you prepared for print- print PDFs are much larger, often including printer and bleed marks. Consumer PDFs will (ideally) be lighter, full of links and bookmarks, and probably including the cover as extra pages (where the printer gets them as separate files).

    As Ben said, a PDF included for free is promotional. In the case of publishers participating in Bits and Mortar, it's value and convenience for the customer and a way to support brick-and-mortar retailers by helping them compete with online retailers. It's an excellent idea and I encourage publishers to do it, but it's not cost-free. Setting up the mechanics that allow people to download files has a cost in time, as does the customer service involved in making sure people get their files. Preparing good PDFs costs time, or contractor fees.

    The cost of a "free" PDF is often built into the cost of the print book (as you suggested) but that's not the only model. as Jason Pitre said, you could make the print book a little cheaper by charging for the PDF.

    \n Market forces!

    For a while, I offered the print and the PDF. Some people bought one, some the other, some both.

    Then, PDF started selling more than print. I added a print+PDF bundle that was a significant discount off of the price of buying both. People seemed to buy either the PDF, or the print+PDF bundle, but not just the print as much.

    PDF still outsold print+PDF, so I decided that it wasn't worth it to me to maintain 3 listings for every game, and moved to a print-includes-PDF or PDF only model. I think the goodwill and customer experience of getting the PDF "for free" with print outweighs the negligible extra money I was making on the print+PDF bundles.

    Also, at this point, all of my production costs have been well paid for for all of my titles (other than occasional print runs to restock), so breaking out the additional cost of making a customer-level PDF isn't really a factor for me, and I build that effort and time into my production process for new stuff anyway.

    So, there's one small-volume publisher datapoint for you!

    \n In order for something to be an economic good, it needs to be scarce. If something can be infinitely reproduced at next to no cost and the very act of accessing it creates a new copy, then any scarcity is going to have to be artificially forced onto it from the outside. I think deep down people know this and thus don't like paying more for a PDF after buying a print copy. If the argument is one of it being a separate product, people see through the artificial scarcity.

    Now if the argument is one based on labour or time spent in producing the work in question, then asking them to pay twice is seen through pretty quickly as well.

    The RPG market is not huge. Many games sell in sufficiently low quantities that the best marketing is often the maker of the game letting people know about it directly. It also allows the game maker and those who enjoy what they make to be part of the same community or have some form of a relationship. It's pretty much the best possible defense against lost revenues due to piracy. Having a relationship with the people who use your product means they are more likely to see you as a person and want to support your efforts directly. But it also means that if you charge them for something that is infinitely reproducible with a click of a mouse after you've already charged them for a print copy, you'll end up with the people who like your product feeling like they are not being treated like a person. The relationship approach works both ways.

      \n Even the most obscure thing seems to pop up on 4/7chans' /tg/ so I don't think you're actually going to reduce piracy. DrivethruRPG uses a watermarking system that puts the customer's information into the file. They do a few watermarkings, the customer's IP and then also put a uniquely generated hash tag into the file as well. The problem with that is that PDF security is a joke, with both linux and Mac OS being able to strip it natively and Windows such a capability is only a download away. Then the pirate will:

      1) Remove the watermark images
      2) do a find & replace on the document for their IP address
      3) strip out all metadata and non-essential data from the file

      If they are extra careful they will also:

      4) print the document and capture the printer spool's output, completely obliterating any data that is not part of how the document is displayed
      5) convert the printer spool info back into a PDF, restoring the text and bookmarks manually as part of the process
      6) be confident that even if they missed their IP address somewhere that they used a VPN to hide their true IP
      7) be confident that even if there's a unique identifier that they missed, they used a convenience store prepaid credit card and a fake name

      One route some publishers have gone is to go with a dedicated app on a smart phone/tablet where you can only read the work after you buy the app and only when connected to the internet so that what you paid for is verified every time you use the file. But the same software used to develop this is available to the pirates and the most locked down app-only publications also appear on pirate sites after the content is captured, cleaned and converted.

      Every time some new approach to stopping piracy comes up, it attracts pirates to the challenge. It's part of the hobby of being a pirate to overcome challenges like new technology.

      \n I've come to this forum as someone who didn't really know the indie RPG scene existed 12 months ago but is very familiar with the very similar debates which rage throughout the film, music and mainstream publishing industry.

      Most serious studies which have looked at the economic effect of piracy have found that the highest users of pirated materials are also the highest consumers of legitimate product. You also have people like Nail Gaiman and Radiohead who have experimented with giving their work away for free and found that, via "name your price" and sales of physical product, it has actually made them money. Of course, whenever examples like that are trotted out it is always counter-argued that that only works for artists who are already well established and it can be argued until the cows come home one way or the other. I merely put it out there because I think it's worth thinking about.

      As someone who has only recently discovered the indie scene, I'm somewhat in love with it. But I've been struck by what I perceive to be the lack of business sense within it. The last ten years has seen a renaissance of RPG design in which so many of the problems inherent in "trad" gaming have been solved, but it's failed to make its mark. I don't think people fully realise the potential gold mine the indie scene is sitting on if it only found a way to infiltrate the mainstream. While it might seem vulgar to talk about it in such capitalist terms, that means thousands, maybe millions, more people getting into the hobby and exploring their ideas and expressing themselves. I think that's a worthy goal to shoot for.

      I've written all that at the risk of veering off topic because I think the bigger picture is worth thinking about when considering issues like print vs PDF. All too often, I fear that people focus on the little picture. They see lots of people getting hold of pirates PDFs and see lost revenue and people (to use the term the music industry has abused) "stealing" from them. What they don't see is the fact that many of those people may be simply getting a PDF of a book they've already bought, might struggle to find a legitimate place to buy the book, or might end up leading to future sales of legitimate product due to word of mouth.

      Personally speaking, I prefer to buy all my books from my FLGS. My particular, FLGS, Leisure Games in London, is heaving every Saturday with young gamers who sit around tables right next to the shop's well stocked and appealing indie games section. It's very clear to me that by helping to keep that shop open I'm helping indie publishers sell product to a new audience they would otherwise lack.

      All too often, I'm frustrated in this because the book is out of print or simply not available for retail. Neither should be happening; POD is now a reality (and whatever the margins are, they're better than no sale at all) and from what I've seen, game store distribution is in a rather healthy state right now.

      I find it incredibly frustrating when I can't get the PDF bundled with the physical book. Nine times out of ten, I go onto buy a separate PDF. But I would remind you of my above statement: I'm in love with the indie scene right now; for me, it's totally worth it. But I won't always feel that way, and I certainly won't necesarily always be in as good a financial position as I am now; both are subject to change. I suspect that anyone not bundling, and certainly not participating in the Bits and Mortar scheme, is missing out on a lot of potential sales.

      I've read a lot in this thread about how producing PDFs is not cost-free. That's all valid, although a) most modern publishing software makes a lot of the processes involved quite simple and b) relatively few indie PDFs I've purchased bother with things like comprehensive indexes, print friendly formats, and so on. But crucially, those are fixed costs; the marginal costs are clearly much lower. Let's not get carried away here.

      In conclusion, I'd urge self-publishers to see PDFs as an opportunity not a threat, but also to recognise that to ensure that it truly is an opportunity you need to ensure your print versions are readily available via retail (with Bits and Mortar) and POD. I'd also encourage you to think about the value added you can get from print and physical product. I got the burlap bag edition of Joe McDaldno's The Quiet Year in the post the other day and I simply adore it; it contains pretty much everything you need (apart from pencils and paper) to start playing the game right away, in package in keeping with the look and feel of the game. All RPGs should be packaged like that.

      Add prominent tip jars on your websites and take a leaf out of Jason Morningstar's book (literally) and include a page in all your PDFs encouraging people to financially support the game. Don't worry about people getting your stuff for free but, equally, don't be embarrassed about asking them to make a contribution. I know Amanda Palmer irritates some peope, but she has a point: http://www.ted.com/talks/amanda_palmer_the_art_of_asking.html

      Sorry for going on for too long; I just think there's a huge amount of untapped potential that won't be realised if people are too precious about "protecting" their intellectual property. Worse, I think it is counterproductive in the longer term and leads to too many brilliant games languishing in a self imposed ghetto.
