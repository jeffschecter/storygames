\n Really quickly; Paul (Paul Talesien) and Rob (Valvorik) and Kate (not forum-active indie player and GM) ended up coming out for In A Wicked Age, with Kate subbing in for the guy who dropped.

The players were all children (plus a devil in the guise of a human boy). The game got REALLY dark really quick, and it spooked me a little afterwards how quickly and effortlessly I dropped into the role of abusive patriarch. But it was really great finally meeting Paul and getting everyone introduced. I lost track of my IAWA games at 20 or so, but once again the game delivered a engaging, visceral chapter, and once again the fiction was dark and brilliant.

We're going to play again in 2 weeks.

    \n A few points, from Rob/Valvorik (player of the child/moon seer possessed by 10 rival spirits)

    (a) The Sex and Sorcery book I gave Ryan at session coincidentally has the lines/veils concept for dealing with things like violence against kids and sexual violence etc. in setting parameters in play. I'm a veil guy for most stuff (hey, I work for the Ministry in charge of film and video game rating in Ontario). Which is to say I'm fine with (paraphrasing stuff in the Ron E's book), "we go into ally and have wild monkey sex with SM edge where she submits to me", but don't want an action by action narration of that at the level I'm okay with combat where "I punch him in face", "hah, no I duck his punch, grab his wrist and pull him off balance and to his knees" level of specificity.

    (b) B~ being such a bastard in the fiction/Paul's playing of Aram.

    Well villains need to do more than wear black, have moustaches and go "bwah hah hah". They can't all be plotting to end the world or other grandoise things. Sometimes they're just the local S.O.B. To me, B~ emerged as a character who the fiction "needs to deal with" in order to have narrative structure (e.g., we see whether he gets his comeuppance or not). My play style for this sort of stuff is to have at least as much eye on "what is this story" as "who is my character". In fiction, particularly TV/movies, there is often an early establishing scene "see just how bad this bad guy is".

    First time I've met/played with Paul, I similarly put his in play actions down to, "well, he was playing a devil - it says "devil" not spirit, ghost, angel - what do you expect a devil's influence in a situation to result in, lollipops for everyone and people making good decisions? The scene he evoked talking to his mother was creepy - presumably when your dead child you don't acknowledge comes back as a devil, creepy is an option. That he finished off showing himself "more a fiend" physically while "a caring person at heart" creates an interesting dichotomy. He was most fiendish when apparently a boy and most human when apparently a fiend.

    (c) To Ryan, do you want any write up of the narrated stuff to track it.

    In a wicked age, in the garden of the noble house of Midas, met the two children, the gardener's son, moon-watching Rab Seen, and the governess' daughter Cajun, wise beyond her years. On this night, Aram the devil child came to the house to seek his parents. On this night of the new moon, an alabaster tower arose unseen from the waters of the bay.
    ....
    Cajun and her distraught mother fled using the money stolen from B~'s lock box as he lay unconscious and bloodied, his favourite cane broken nearby and the cook as witness to their flight.

    Aram freed Rab Seen of his possessing spirits, which were dragged back down under the waves of the bay with the tower's descent. However, as Rab Seen tried to free Aram from the chains binding him to the tower as it bound the spirits, he did this only at cost of one re-entering him and creating such a chain between Rab Seen and Aram.
