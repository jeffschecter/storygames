    \n Apocalypse World, surprisingly our last session. What had been a fairly cooperative relationship among the players devolved into the Chopper taking over the Hocus' town, including a running battle across ther desert between the Chopper's and Gunlugger's gangs. The Gunlugger, badly wounded, his gang scattered across the desert, made it to his mutant medic girlfriend, earned his last advance by facing down one of the Chopper's gang, and retired to safety. He and his woman escaped the rapidly deteriorating situation to start a new hold somewhere over the rainbow, or wherever. (And now I want to play Apocalypse Oz.)

    Meanwhile, the Hocus tried to seize back control of the town by riling up the mob. The Chopper used her Hx to cause the Hocus' roll to bomb, and the crowd tore him limb from limb. The Chopper, as a last "what the hell" kind of act, opened her brain. She failed, and the part of the maelstrom that had been encouraging cannibalism as a spiritual practice poisoned her soul. The last image of the game was of the Chopper thinking the Hocus' brains looked mighty good.

    Things I liked best about the game as a whole: how it would suddenly hit high gear and go spiralling off into crazy directions, where stuff just flowed from one event to another. The twists when I caame up with a really good move for a failed roll. Also the small moments of tenderness that oaccasionally came into play.

    Things I liked least: I don't think I ever became truly comfortable with the Maelstrom. It's a very important element, and it ties into the cause of the Apocalypse, so I felt like I should treat it as something that needed to be pre-defined and revealed, rather than a more organic thing that the players were defining through play. I never got "loose" with it. Also, it "belonged" to just one player. The other two had little interest in opening their brains and such, so I didn't have much room to set up PC-Malestrom-PC triangles, so to speak.

    I'd really like to play again, but preferably not as the MC next time.

    Up next: one shots and pick-up games, probably starting with Shock.

    \n Remember Tomorrow: Session 2.

    We created awesome characters and a kind of ripe world/setting for play (via factions and character details) in the previous session. Played six scenes, had some difficulties.

    Session two: Fell flat. We had problems interacting with the system, one of the players very conceptually. We found a lot to love in the game, and had a really ripe setup that we were all behind, but we couldn't keep it moving through the full session time.

    "(reads) Oh, hey, turns out we were doing that wrong" had become the phrase of the evening, despite three of the four players having read the text a few times.

    A lot of good came out of it, though: Again, the setup and characters. Also, Matthew G was able to put together a 9-page notes doc that summarized/streamlined the game. Matthew is going to put together the thoughts and details of our successes and problems with our run on his blog at some point.

    Also, I'll likely give it another shot, cause I have some ideas of making it work better, likely even next week.

    \n A pretty free-form thing that Elastrius came up with that we're playing over IRC.

    A rabbi (with his golem), a ghost, and a sniper who can turn invisible are part of a US supernatural response team in WW2. Investigating Nazi occult crap in Tunisia.

    And there, the ghost meets the ghost of his lost love, who is somehow tied to a SS officer.

    He has just two doses of some magical salts that can temporarily give corporeal form to a ghost. He gives one to her - she takes shape. And the Germans are alerted. A firefight breaks out. Our sniper kills the SS officer that she is tied to. The rabbi is riddled with bullets. The ghost is torn - help his friends or stay with his love?

    After the shooting stops and the Germans are dead, the ghost uses his last dose of salts to take physical shape and spend just a few corporeal minutes with his love.

    Then the salts wear off and she is gone.

    \n Apocalypse World. The characters live on The Strip, a seedy but relatively secure hardhold in the urban decay of post-apocalypse West Hollywood near the Hacienda Arms apartments.

    The characters venture a few miles SW to make a call on the Ooklahs, a group of bio-mutant cannibals and their Keepers in the ruins of UCLA. They're there to barter for a cure to "The Blight."

    A noxious fog rolls in from the sea, and it's as thick as pea soup, requiring the Chopper's gang to walk their bikes through the campus. They see lights and hear shambling movements here and there. The Maestro D' reads the sitch and misses. That's when one of the gang notices that the Maestro D' and one of the gang members are missing. "We're being picked off one by one in the fog, boss!" The Chopper orders the gang to circle their bikes on the roof of a parking garage and he and The Faceless go off looking for the missing ones. They find them ultimately but they are being held as dinner. The two are completely surrounded by about two dozen feral mutants and going aggro on the ringleader has gone pear-shaped. I'm calculating the Harm these freaks are going to soon be inflicting on the encircled group and thinking "Ugh. Shortest. AW. Campaign. Evar."

    The Faceless tears off his mask and gives a speech about how they are *NOT* food, and for the Ooklahs to bring forth their elixirs that their flesh may be healed. Unmasking stuns them long enough for him to be able to make his mad case; he buys the Hocus move "Frenzy" with the advance he'd just earned. He rolls his Weird+1 for the Frenzy move. Everyone one leans forward to see the result... an eleven.

    The situation is defused and The Faceless is, for now, the freaks' mad prophet.

    \n Almost free-form, home-brewed system. Random Fantasy Agendaless Chaos playing, trad players wanting to have fun and go over the top and a trad GM not wanting to crunch on infinite numbers.

    BUT: the trad GM was talking with me about rpgs for long time, and...

    \n Star Wars d6: The crew of the Wydraz Dragonfly sitting dead in space after being hit by an ion cannon and fighting off boarders, questioning the three rodians they captured from the boarding party. The rodian tech Ona keeps offering to help them repair their ship, but they don't trust her. She warns them that the rodian scavenger ship will come back soon, but they don't free her. She offers up info on a 5K credit bounty on one of her fellow captives if they free her, they refuse to let her go. They then threaten to send her adrift in the rodian shuttle they used to board the Dragonfly with no fuel and only life support. They don't ask for anything, they just threaten her.

Plus, she adds, she can get the shuttle up and going in a day by herself.

    I wasn't frustrated by the players as much as confused by them. They couldn't figure out who was telling the truth and since they hadn't chosen a captain, they never made a decision, just kept going and doing things then coming back and ask questions or talking to the one "friendly" NPC.     \n OK, we are in between campaigns, but after some talking we busted out some Chaos in the Old World: http://www.boardgamegeek.com/boardgame/43111/chaos-in-the-old-world

    We only got two rounds in, just to understand the game for the next throwdown, likely next week. But I noticed:

    * It was really, really interesting. Far more than I expected.
    * It was honestly the best-written rules for a board game of its size that I have ever seen. I literally read the book through our first round, reading right from it and doing what it told us, and there were NO moments of unclarity, misunderstanding, etc. This is incredible, because the last like 10 board games I played were nowhere near as easy to understand as this, and weren't even as complicated. Tons of easy and complicated examples aided in understanding, but we didn't even need them.

    Kind of a dumb thing to get excited over, but man, what a well-written instruction book. And yeah, the game has all sorts of layers and emergent effects, which we only caught glimpses of. Thumbs up.
