\n I broke this out from the thread Women in Gaming Communities.\n
It might not be every group's cup of tea, but as I stated in the post that started this thread at Story Games Seattle we intentionally play GMless no prep games to put everyone at the table on an equal footing.
\nIt's an idea I never really thought of, and it's good thinking, but I think that few GM less games enforce, by themselves, a behaviour that makes everybody speak up and therefore feel like equals.

1. Make sure everybody get the chance to speak
You need some kind of pacing mechanism so even the more silent ones can get their share of the spot light. Fiasco and Polaris achieves this by rotating which character that has the spotlight moment. This Is Pulp does it by giving tokens to those who act, and the ones with the least amount of tokens are the ones who get to act next. It's a GM technique that I built as a game mechanic, where I always look to activate the least active player (a technique that backfires in InSpectres).

2. Make leading roles
There are other things that the GM less games in general don't provide. They don't tell us who is getting the more important parts. I've been talking about this before, with passive, reactive and active relations (agendas) that creates token, supporting or leading roles. It doesn't matter if you got a bunch of relations to the other characters, which tells us that your character probably will be in most of the scenes, if the character is just a token that's moved around by others. The one who is organizing the game must be aware of this and, by either meta game or in-game methods, support the players that are in the risk zone of playing supporting or token parts.

3. Acknowledge each other by praising
One mantra I learned as a soccer coach, and that I brought into my gaming sessions, was that I should have as a goal to praise every player at least once per session. It's important to acknowledge someone else by praising, both to enforce player behaviour but also to make the players feel that they contribute to the session. That they feel important. Very few games has this kind of support - and I'm not talking about giving XP or dice to other people for their ideas. It's not the same thing. I would say that no GM less games to my knowledge practise this.

4. Acknowledge each other by using their ideas
Another way of acknowledging the others around the table is to take their ideas and make it part of the story. Some games does this, like all those games where the GM ask the others questions and make something out of it. But as you can tell, I had to write GM, because I've just seen one GM less game - Bad Family - do this successfully. The questions are actually the best part of the game. A game that only uses "Yes, and" and "Yes, but" without any kind of randomness would also acknowledge the participants' ideas.

---

These are four guidelines that I think is really important in GM less games, when it comes to creating a creative environment so all players feel that they provides something to the session. I would dare to say that few GM less games support them, and the person organizing the games have to take care of this instead. A person who, by explaining all the rules, instantly gets a more important role around the table. That's something to take into consideration as well.

    \n Yes!

    My favourite game mechanics which help engage shy/quiet/uncertain players generally:

    * Give that person unquestioned authority in some way. (E.g. It's your turn to talk, everyone else must listen. Or: you have final say over this decision-making debate.) But they also:
    * Give that person a way to reach out for help, so they don't feel like they're put on the spot. Granting authority is important, but it must be tempered by some tools which allow that person to feel that they're not out there on a limb on their own. All that attention can be frightening!
    * Encourage other players to work with that player in a supporting role. This last one is maybe the trickiest! It's hard to be truly supportive and engaged without taking over the spotlight.

    One example of the last category I really like is when the rules create incentives to interact with a player's role or character in the way that they like. For instance, Judd's game First Quest (unpublished) had "reverse Keys": you could choose a Key which would have criteria for other characters to fulfill.

    A really good one for a shy player might be:

    "Whenever someone follows one of my orders without question, they mark experience."

    \nI usually draw up two guidelines before session for helping a player out: never give examples! Because if you give examples, you're taking one solution away from the player because that player would feel like a looser for using another player's idea. In other words: examples blocks the inspiration. Instead, ask questions. In tough cases, ask leading questions like "Didn't your mum say something about this?". Even if this is the same thing as giving example, the question itself leaves space for the player to say "no". Most people don't, and uses the question as an inspiration to move on.

    I may be misunderstanding, but doesn't A Penny for My Thoughts do this pretty well?
    \nI don't know. Haven't read the game. Can you tell me more about how it works?
    \nIt's a fairly classic amnesia game, GMless. The conceit is each character is taking part in a procedure to regain their memory, they have taken a drug that allows all four characters to share bits of memory and the other players have a better grip on relevant bits of the travelers memories.

    So you all write memory triggers, one player is designated the traveler and pulls a random trigger (say, 'the name Susan carved on an old tree'), the other three players each ask a leading question which is actually a 'yes and' statement about the trigger.

    With the platforming done by the 'yes and' questions the traveler goes on to describe the memory from that point and when they get to an action of consequence, because they can't remember it themselves, they ask two other players to remember it for them. They choose the one that is 'correct' and move on. The memory continues through steps, denoted by how many pennies the traveler has (you pass a penny to the player who gave you the 'correct' memory and add more in as the game continues so each memory will have more steps to it). When the traveler has no pennies left they have come to the end of the memory, take a penny and the next traveler is chosen. The mechanic for choosing a traveler means that it shares it around and almost favours the quieter players as they get to see more unfold while not being left behind.

    In the standard 'skin' all the characters are normal people in a normal world and so the memories rarely link together. But there are options to play as cultists in a world with eldritch monsters or as spies in a Bourne Identity style mission gone wrong debrief within the book which would link the memories together much more (and make the experience less intense). The standard skin is almost always misery porn in the way it plays for some if not all of the stories.

    It was written with a view to adding improv techniques to the core of the game, so is very heavily planted around the concept of 'yes anding' statements but this isn't necessarily a bad thing.

    \nWell, it just has to be something that's meaningful in that particular game, something the players need or want. It might be "when you follow that character's plans, you get a +1 to your roll", or "every time you frame a scene involving this character, you get one Story Token", or more specific connections like, "The Beast may only use his power of Fury when his love interest is in the scene" (and the love interest is the shy player).

    The point is:

    Since the shy player is not super likely to speak up themselves, it's great to have some kind of incentive which makes the other players want to include that player's character or input.

    If I'm playing a game where survival is on the line, and we're in a tough a situation... but you get +1 when you follow the advice of the Wise One... I will never jump into danger before asking her, "Hey, hang on. What do YOU think we should do here?"
