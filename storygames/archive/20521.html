\n So I've had over a half dozen playtests of my (very much a) work in progress game Perseverant. As a designer, I've typically stayed closer to the traditional side of the spectrum - there's a clear GM, characters tend to have special abilities or skills, they're all a group doing a particular thing, etc. All of my Ethos Engine games are this way (Vow of Honor, Hunt the Wicked, Belly of the Beast), and Cornerstone is mostly this way (there's a lot more player creative authority than in EE).

I've struggled for a long time on making a more "narrative" game (I know there's baggage around the word, don't know how else to describe it) that doesn't have a GM, that is designed more for collaborative storytelling, that generally plays out in one session, that has relatively freeform character and narrative control, etc.

Many discarded games and prototypes later, I started from scratch with Perseverant. And, I'm relatively pleased to say, the game works...ish. Because my home group (and core group of playtesters, some 20-odd people) tend to prefer my more trad designs, it is difficult to gauge the "fun" factor of this game. In other words, it isn't something that my normal circle of RPGers would enjoy playing more than once.

We've certainly had a couple of cool stories, and we've had some pretty obvious failures where the system broke down. But, I'm in a bit of a pickle that I don't know how to rectify without asking for outside help. So, that's where you guys come in (hopefully, if you have the time).

A brief on the game

Perseverant is a story game about survival. A group of players creates characters, defines their relationships, defines the Wilderness that they must face, and what their eventual goal is (it could simply be survive, it could be to get to a certain location, or to flee from another group, whatever). It has a pretty rigid scene structure, with a cycle between challenge and social scenes.

Characters face challenges, expend trait cards, and roll dice. Successes return the cards, failures don't. Complete group failure elicits more challenges in the future. There's some random / collaborative / group phases in which you create locations and challenge sources (some of which a few of you helped me to refine in a previous thread).

The problem

I don't know if my game is fun. I don't know if the themes are tied as elegantly as they could be. This is a new headspace for me, and I'm worried that I'm being too influenced by other story games that I like, and by my past designs. I'm not necessarily looking for suggestions on what to create or change, but I'm interested in knowing your opinion on whether or not the game is fun and well executed.

If not, perhaps why.

Thanks again for any time and comments, criticism especially.

Full game doc, v0.7, updated 3/10/16.

    \n If your ideal severity is between half the number of players and twice the number of players, you could do something like this, which would feel familiar based on your existing rules:

    To determine severity, roll two dice per player. Each 4-6 turns into one point of severity.

    Or, if you care more about the lower limit, but no so much the upper:

    Severity starts at half the number of players you have. (You can round up or down as you like.) Now, for the remaining number of players, roll two dice per player. Each 4-6 bumps the severity up by 1.

    E.g. You have five players. You decide to round down, so severity starts at 2. Now roll 6 dice (two for each of the remaining players). You get 4 hits, so your severity is 2+4=6.

    The equilibrium idea is also good. It might not feel as random (since you'll always know the severity of the upcoming challenge), but could be good in other ways (and the simplicity is nice).

    Severity
    Severity is determined by adding the number of players to the result of 1d6 per three players, rounded up. For example, a group with 4 players would start each challenge with a severity of 4 + 2d6, summed.

    If the group rolled a 1 and a 3, the severity for the challenge would be 8 (4+1+3).

    Trait cards
    I will replace Method (or Ideals, still on the fence) with Gear. This is a significant kit or collection of equipment or items that are relevant to the character (either their goal or their history), the nature of the Wilderness, and can not overlap with any other character.

    Betrayal
    At any time during a scene, a character can Betray another character. This automatically reduces the challenge to 0 severity, and all of the Betraying character's spent cards are returned to their hand immediately. Internal conflict rules follow as normal.

    Potential addition
    I'm thinking of adding another "stack" of cards - similar to Locations and Challenge Sources - that are a beneficial resource to the party. They might even be called "Resources."

    What these Resources would do is essentially act as another keyable card for the character - although whether they were successful or not they're always spent and discarded.       As for things "bloating" the game, I don't think that's a concern. You just remove the same amount of other things to keep the balance where you like it. For instance, if you have to deal a bunch of Resources, you shouldn't have BOTH four Traits per character AND several Relationships. Instead, reduce the Traits and/or Relationships, and replace them with Resources, so the ratio of available cards stays the same.

      After that, it's all about implementation.

      "Fitting failure" still sounds awfully vague to me. (e.g. I would have no idea how it apply it in-game.) Clarify further!

      Do you mean that it can be chosen to "stay on the table", just like any other keyed card, and, if so, you don't get it back?

      If so, cool, but why would I ever do that instead of leaving a keyed card (which is effectively a renewable resource)?
