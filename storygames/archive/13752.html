\n OK, so it occurred to me that Kickstarter might be a cool way to get a con started and ensure that the con was self sustaining and that it didn't force its organizers to eat costs. This seemed like a great idea, backing the project would cover your registration and the con wouldn't run if there weren't enough prepaid attendees. Great, then I realized that any area that could support a con probably already had one. So, the question became, how do you get people to back a con, even if they can't attend?

One thing that scientific conferences do is to offer conference proceedings and books of abstracts. That got me thinking - if game materials were produced at a con or published at a con then you would have something to offer your backers, even if they couldn't make the con.

In terms of producing things at a con, many recent games have playbooks, playsets, frames, or other small mandated fan-made elements. A workshop at the con could probably put together some pretty cool stuff that could be put together for attendees and backers.

So, I guess the question remains, how do you get people to back a con, even if they can't attend?

    \n Have the con focus on a topic that cuts across all locales and is underserved. There aren't lots of those left, but nothing helps a Kickstarter out like being able to be a net-wide rallying cry.

    Go Play NW will have a "proceedings document" of sorts this year, in the form of Walton and Macklin's Magic Missile.

    \n You can start fairly small and build without anybody eating anything except piping hot games. Both Camp Nerdly and GPNW are good examples of this. Nerdly rocks a surplus every year that is passed on to the next year as a cushion. It is affordable and includes lodging and five meals.

    I like the idea of proceedings - this has been going on with Knutpunkt/Knudepunkt/Salmukohta for years.

    \n Have a game designer make a game/supplement/adventure at a panel at the con, playtest it throughout the con, and release it to investors after the con is done.

    Or crowdsource an adventure/supplement design: con attendees provide input and vote on what goes into the thingy, then it gets cleaned up and made into a presentable product. (WotC did something recent, with a panel on monster design at Gencon becoming a recent Dragon magazine article on modrons.)

    Have a super cool adventure played as a showcase game at the con, and then release it to the public afterward. Make the coolest adventure you can possibly make, and premiere it at the con.

    Document the creation of the con thoroughly, and make a book or documentary on how to start a con from scratch, which is sold to investors once the con occurs.
