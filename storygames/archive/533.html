    April 2006
    Ooooooooh, that's not at all what I thought you were talking about. I thought you were talking about starting off with The Shadow of Yesterday and then switching over and suddenly playing Shadowrun.

    I would suggest that you present the tourney as a 'tutorial' phase where the players get into the game -- they learn the basic rules (assuming you're using the same mechanics to hit, just dropping damage, and the like) and they get acclimated to setting and character. Then you take off and go.

    But I think you should make it explicit from the start that the game isn't the tourney, the tourney is the prequel to the "real meat" of the game. Otherwise you may have players who really dig on the courtly tourney and get disappointed when they're stuck on a battlefield.

    (Quibble: it sounds like the setting doesn't really change, the rules get elaborated, and the situation is expanded)

    I'd be entirely up front, myself. If you think the jarring surprise will delight them, just tell them the game will be changing mid-stream. I think it would be better to lay out the entire idea, since they can then help you foreshadow and get their characters into interesting, compromising relationships in advance of civil war.
  • Vaxalon
    April 2006
    If the POINT of the first game is to create relationships, and the system rewards and encourages that, then they'll do it.

    If the POINT of the second game is for those relationships to come into conflict, father against brother, husband against wife, and the system rewards and encourages that, then they'll do it.

    What would the point of the third game be? To pick up the pieces afterwards?

    In the first game, reward people for creating, defining, and enhancing relationships.

    In the second game, reward people for "burning" relationships, in whatever way seems best.
