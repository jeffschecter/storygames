\n For my rewrite of Left Coast, I'm trying to simplify a lot of the procedures.

The dice mechanics I'm using are a little fiddly, and Simon Carryer has suggested a way of simplifying them. I'm hoping there might be some probability-calculating experts here who can talk me through whether my current approach and Simon's approach are roughly comparable.


At the moment, the way conflicts are resolved is a variation of the Sorceror dice mechanics: \n
    \n
  • There are 7d6 on the table
  • The player rolls between 1 and 6 of those d6 (the exact number depends on the value of the stat the PC is using)
  • The GM rolls all the remaining dice (eg. if the player rolls 5 dice, the GM rolls 2 dice)
  • The player and GM compare the highest result they got in each of their dice pools: the person with the highest result is the winner
  • Tied highest results are discarded until there's a clear winner
  • The winner gets a number of 'victory points' equal to the number of their dice that beat the loser's highest result.
    • \n
For example, Malcolm rolls four dice, and Steve rolls three dice. They get these results:

Malcolm: 6, 5, 4, 4
Steve: 5, 4, 2

Malcolm wins with one success. He has one die that's higher than Steve's 5.

To illustrate the 'tied dice' rules, say they get these results:

Malcolm: 6, 5, 5, 4
Steve: 6, 4, 2

Both Malcolm and Steve would throw away their tied '6' results. Malcolm would then win with two successes (he has two dice that are higher than Steve's 4).


Simon's suggestion is to simplify all that, and just have the player roll a number of d6 equal to the value of their stat (so, between 1 and 6 dice). Any 4s, 5s, or 6s count as successes and get converted to 'victory points'.

So, if Malcolm has a 'Money' stat of 4, he rolls 4 dice ...

Malcolm: 6, 5, 3, 1

... giving him two successes.


Are these two approaches roughly equivalent?

Is there an alternative approach that's simpler?

My intention with Left Coast's conflict resolution system is that when you're rolling with higher stats, you'll generate quite a few victory points, but still have the possibility of failure. Any suggestions for simplifying my current approach will be gratefully received.

    Posted By: Steve HickeyMy intention with Left Coast's conflict resolution system is that when you're rolling with higher stats, you'll generate quite a few victory points, but still have the possibility of failure.
    \nIn think the system as written may not deliver the first criterion (depending on what you mean by "quite a few" victory points) and the simplified version doesn't deliver the second all that well (depending on what you mean by "possibility").

    The second system is easier to graph. WIth four dice, for example, the player will fail only six times in a hundred. Technically, that is a "possibility" of failing, but not one that matters much.

    Both systems can build as many successes as there are dice, theoretically. I don't know if that counts as "quite a few".

    Any system which uses a pool of dice to hit a certain number, the tool to use is this question: for a target X, what is the chance of not hitting that number on all dice. That is, what are the odds of all dice failing? So, say you are rolling four dice and want 4 or better to succeed. A total failure happens only if all dice roll three or less. For die, there are six possible outcomes, three of which fail. This is 3/6 or 1/2. Since one die does not affect the others, each die is an independent event. The rule for independent events is to multiple the probabilities for each die. You are rolling four dice, so all of them failing is 1/2 * 1/2 * 1/2 * 1/2 = 1/16 = 0.0625 = 6.25%.

    More generally, probability of failing:

    n = number of dice
    t = target number
    d = sides per die
    P(n,t,d) = ((t-1)/d)n

    That is the failure case. But what if you didn't fail? That would mean that at least one of the dice was a four or higher. The probability of the "not fail" case is 1 minus the probability of the fail case, or (1 - 1/16) = 15/16 = 0.9375 = 93.75%.

    The original system, things get more tricky. You need to look at each outcome. As an example:

    n = number of dice for the player
    g = number of dice for the gm = 7 - n

    Will the player win with a six? Well, chances of him at least one six: 1 - (5/6)n. Chances of the GM not getting any 6's: (5/6)g. These are independent, so odds of this happening are:

    (1 - (5/6)n) * (5/6)g

    Will the player win with a five? Player needs at least one five, GM needs none:

    (1 - (4/6)n) * (4/6)g

    Note, however, that this result includes the results where the player gets at at least one six, so those need to be subtracted off. In short, it's a mess.

    Better off simulating it, most likely.

    \n I like the system as is.

    Reminds me of Risk a little.

    Which is such an epic resolution system. The match the highest (and the second highest if both players rolled at least 2d); lower score loses an army (thus up to a maximum of two armies); defender wins ties. Which allows for epic defenses even if the armies are 20 : 1.

    So, I think that depending on your design goals, this could apply to ties. The player should always win ties and you only ever match the least number of dice. That means that the player rolling 1d versus 6d still has a chance to walk away with one victory point, rather than rolling a six and then the GM walking away with five victory points.

    FREX

    GM rolls: 6, 5, 4, 3, 2, 1
    STEVE rolls: 6

    STEVE gets one victory point.

    Whereas,

    GM rolls: 6, 4, 2, 2
    STEVE rolls: 6, 5, 5

    STEVE gets three victory points.

    Whereas,

    GM rolls: 6, 6, 2, 1
    STEVE rolls: 5, 4, 3, 2

    GM gets two victory points; STEVE gets one.

    All that being said, I think the system is more or less fine as is.

    \n Right, so in Risk [and not in Left Coast] you roll usually three dice if you're attacking and two dice if you're defending. You then compare the highest and second highest results from each player respectively. Sometimes, you get situations where both sides lose an army.

    EG

    Attacking Player gets: 6, 2, 2

    and the Defending Player gets: 5, 3

    So, you match the highest respective dice [6] and [5], thus AP wins, and then the second highest respective dice [2] and [3], and the defending player wins. The extra [2] die is disregarded.

    If you were to adopt this for Left Coast, that means that some results would mean that both the GM and the Player might get victory points.

    In the example I gave, the third highest respective dice match, the Player won, because [3] is better than [2].
