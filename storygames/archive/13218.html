\n This thread popped up on RPGNet. It's kinda interesting, although the opinions soon coalesce into a few camps, each camp's members with their own strong feelings about how games should work, etc.
"I Don't Reward Roleplaying!"

It's an interesting discussion to me, because I can see within it about 3-4 different opinions that even I myself had on this topic, and how it changed over the years.

For example, now, I only like to play games where roleplaying is rewarded (!!!). Major games that set me on this weird path to where I am today include:

Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium. (The "Cool Rule")
Primetime Adventures (Fanmail)
The Shadow of Yesterday (Friend dice)
Tenra Bansho (Aiki chits)
etc
etc

In fact, more often than not these days, as my gaming group can attest to, on "long campaign" style games, if a fanmail-like mechanic doesn't exist, I'll find an easy way to introduce one to the game.

Now, by roleplaying I don't simply mean the "act of acting out a role", I really mean "stepping up your (personal) game", "dropping a line - on purpose or inadvertantly - that is a game-changer or jaw-dropper", etc. We'll always do personal feedback ("Woah!", "Cool!", etc), but I like games that actually drop a reward (character power, game-changing stuff) for going above and beyond with that role-playing.

Comparing that to things I used to reward as a GM: "Accomplishing goals" (to get XP, etc) seems kinda hollow: It means that they basically followed my pre-set path without too much complication or variance, not necessarily bringing anything memorable to the table. I have run or played in lots of games where great goals were accomplished (for the setting, the party, the player) but where I can't remember really anything much about those games. And yet, I can very much recall games that were seeped in some poignant role-playing moments (whether they were explicitly awarded or not).

Awards for showing up ("being able to play the game is reward in itself"), bringing food and stuff, I could go either way on. But I've really fallen for systems that put effort into having the players step up their game, acting/role-wise.

What about you? What do you like to see rewarded in your games?

-Andy

    \n Let me get my helmet back on... been a while since I needed it. OK, here goes: The OWoD games rewarded you for portraying your Nature and Demeanor with Willpower, which was vitally important in those systems. That made Mage and Vampire, in particular, into games about Nature and Demeanor, i.e. they rewarded you for roleplaying. That worked for me in a big way, even if it tended to lead to hamming and narrow (or maybe "archetypal") characterizations. I remember those characters.

    Paranoia rewarded players for entertaining the GM, a useful because explicit inversion of the usual expectations. I thought that was a great idea, and it was pretty mind-expanding the first time I encountered it. If you have a GM, and you don't want him to be The Entertainer, give him a fanmail mechanic for the other players!

    A Penny for my Thoughts rewards asking good questions, and giving the other players something to grab on to, narratively. It also helps out players who are having trouble accumulating currency by letting the other players offer more guidance to them when their turns arrive, as a useful side effect; it might be the most mechanically sophisticated game I've ever played.

    \n XP are a pseudo-pacing mechanic for a campaign. They are sorta a poor reward economy.

    Reward mechanics are best if their arbitration is not centralized, if they allow instant feedback, if they allow the optimal game choice to also be the optimal chioce for RP, and if they allow for more input into informing the emphasis on play.

    Fan mail decentralizes reward.
    Aspects/Fate points make it instant.
    Keys, Aspects, and similar mechanics make the optimal choice game-wise be the best RP choice.
    Fan mail and the Debt->Story Token reward cycle allow for player determined point of play.

    All these are really cool takes on reward mechanics that have come out of Indie games.

    Actually, John Harper has a nice list. On it are group created point-of-play and player-created rewards. That was 2005. I agree some with the grognards, XP is a poor choice for RP rewards, but RP rewards are actually a really cool feature in a game.

    \nI don't find his article convincing, myself, though his "kicking the ball" metaphor is clever. But it's just a metaphor. Roleplaying is not kicking a ball. I'd say the topic is still alive and well.

    I brought it up before - http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=12668

    I haven't played enough games like BW or TSOY to deserve an opinion, but I'm going to give it anyway. My limited experience with Keys/Beliefs/Aspects is that our roleplaying feels 'forced' and 'unnatural' and flow is broken as we ask the GM for our cookies. And theoretically, there's a ton of reasons why a mechanical-incentive system (particularly one where the GM is distributing awards), could be a bad idea:
    * don't fix something that ain't broke; perfection is achieved when there is nothing left to take away - if our co-players are bringing it without encouragement, why mess with the system? There's a bunch of great games out there that don't give you cookies for roleplaying and people roleplay with them anyway. (In a Wicked Age, Fiasco, D&D)
    * you can break flow, interrupting the good stuff to hand out cookies or ask for cookies
    * you're just replacing one goal ("I want to play a character who's bent on revenge") with another ("I want to earn XP or narrative power, by playing a character bent on revenge")
    * the rewards themselves are suspect. Why do I want XP? So I can get buffs? Why do I want buffs? So I can get more XP?
    * incentive systems are notoriously hard, maybe impossible, to get right. You inadvertently find yourself incentivizing the wrong thing. (Lady Blackbird incentivizes throwing away your secret romance key so you can get buffs - is that really what Harper intended?) You can even accidentally disincentivize! A couple real-world examples are the day-care where they started charging parents who picked up their kids late (the parents started doing it more) [that one's from *Freakonomics*]; the classroom where kids were paid to draw (the kids drew, but then refused to draw unless paid, whereas the control group drew anyway).
    * even fan-mail can be gamed by saying "You give me all your fan-mail and I'll give you all mine" - although nobody would really take it that far, I bet frequently there's something unspoken, like (I gave Joe fan mail so now he kind of owes me.)
    * so these systems seem to come with an assumption: "We're not going to try to game/exploit this" Which makes you wonder why we made a gamey system out of the thing in the first place, if we're not going to play it to win.


    I like:

    Taking the XP out of Pathfinder and D&D. That's how we've played our last two campaigns. Every two-three sessions, Lisa says "You all go up a level." Now, the decision to kill a monster or let them get away is purely in the fiction. "Banish" is possibly a better spell than "Dimensional Anchor". We've been given permission to roleplay.

    I like clearly stating character's goals: IaWA's best interests, for example. Games need goals. Stories need goals. You don't need to get cookies for pursuing your goals, but you have to have goals.

    I like how in AW you get experience for making rolls on highlighted stats, especially if you treat everything as a stat including Angel Kit and History-With-Joe and whatnot. Flow's not broken because it's not a matter of opinion whether you deserve your cookie or not, and I haven't figured out how to game it. It's not really incentivizing role-playing, though: you could make a roll that works against your character's goals and get XP for it.

    And I don't think fan mail hurts anything - at best, people are getting rewarded for being the kind of players the others want them to be, and at worst people game it but they can't really break the game with it. So why not have it.

    Posted By: Zac in DavisBack on topic: I do think that Sorcerer's "bonus dice for good description;penaltyfor lazy description" is interesting - have you tried that out at all? If so, what did you think? Or what did anybody think, for that matter?
    \nOne of the things that really helped me with this was to take the break down in the book VERY LITERALLY. It's a little more specific than just "good description." This is the exact break down:

    +2 For an usual version of an action.
    +2 For a situation changing action.
    +1 For for particularly compelling dialogue.

    Those are my paraphrases anyway. So that's up to a total of 5 dice and I follow those exact values and break down exactly. It's a lot more "objective" than you would think.

    Example:

    A player was being shot by a nail gun. He said, "I pick up the table and use it like a shield." I thought that was interesting but it was a bar fight with lots of furniture use already so no bonus. He succeeded and then he said this, "Okay, now that the table is filled with nails, I ram him with it." Holy fuck! Two die bonus for "unusual version of the action". He also said it about as blandly as I described it here. The point is that it wasn't the *description* that counted, it was the actions *content* that mattered.

    I've had players give me the most detailed description of the most bog standard "sweep kick" or "upper cut punch" and I always just kind of look at them stoney faced. They seem to expect the dice and they don't get them from me.

    Here's an example of a full FIVE die bonus from one of my games.

    The story involved a property dispute. After being confronted with the antagonist's legal representative the player simply said this, "Let me summon my lawyer." That's it. The WHOLE table new what in a SORCERER game that damn well meant.

    +1 Witty Dialogue
    +2 Unusual version of an action. SUMMON a lawyer?!
    +2 Situation changer. Oh fuck yeah, that was going to change the situation.

    "Let me summon my lawyer" 5 words, 5 die bonus.

    The penalty is actually much, much rarer. I generally use it to remind players not to repeat themselves. So a player might say, "I jump up from behind the bar and shoot him." That's a no adjustment kind of action either way. But then sometimes they'll follow it with, "I shoot him again." And that's when I'll apply the penalty.

    Things can switch from a bonus situation to a penalty situation when you do that to. I was playing in a game where a player tried to jump on a giant spider's back to ride it. I gave him a +2 for unusual action. But it didn't work out (I think he had to abort to defend himself). So the next round he tried it again and I gave him a -1 for repeating himself. He took it because he REALLY, REALLY wanted to give it a go. He actually succeeded.

    \nAs I understand it, the compel mechanic is supposed to be about roleplaying your character in a particular way at a particular time in a way that creates a particular problem. The theory is that most characters are multi-faceted enough that there are multiple ways they could approach situations, and the compel mechanic is a way to see which particular situations are engaging to the player and which are not by shining a spotlight on one particular path to see if that's the one they want to take. If "new problem + 1 FATE point" roughly balances with "-1 FATE point" then it isn't a straight reward. From listening to AP podcasts and from my own FATE experience, compels can sometimes not work as well in practice as the theory predicts. Some people start to treat FATE points like "good roleplaying rewards" and hand them out for rich in-character idle banter, or let players fast-talk their way out of facing any consequences for accepting the compel. The bigger problem is when the compel functions like a double reward: if you as a player are eager to face particular types of problems then the offer of a problem of that type is something you wouldn't want to refuse so there's no real decision with the compel -- you accept the problem and the FATE point and are happy for both. Since FATE scenario generation and "good compels" are somewhat nebulously intertwined with the concept of "good GMing" it's hard to evaluate the compel mechanic in isolation.

    \n I think the rpg.net thread by the way was about a very specific sort of reward mechanism, that is a system which awards xp for various activities (killing monsters, solving puzzles, spending money, whatever) and which includes bonus xp for roleplaying well.

    It's a common thing to see, particularly since the 1990s. Like that rpg.net poster I think it's a terrible idea.

    XP bonuses for roleplaying raise two main issues. Firstly, you're monetising something people have come wanting to do anyway, which diminishes it and may even disincentivise it ultimately. Secondly, you're giving the GM a mechanical means with which to impact how the player portrays their character, which in a traditional rpg context is I think a big no no.

    After all, if GM Bob doesn't like how I play my character Pete the Paladin, his idea of how I should roleplay my character now manifests into an effect on how fast my character progresses. But, that character is all I have, how I roleplay him really should be up to me.

    Now, what does that mean for reward mechanisms in a story games context? Not very much I suspect, it's a problem peculiar to a particular reward mechanic in the context of a particular play paradigm and within that mechanic and paradigm it doesn't work. More sophisticated reward mechanisms in games with other paradigms could potentially work just fine.

    \n I agree with Paul, the arc of Wushu play tends to be that people just filibuster routinely for the maximum dice possible every round, so you get lots of narration that's loosely attached to die results that are pretty much just a pacing mechanism. The thing I really do like about Wushu is the interchangeability of Details, so you can describe your guy getting beat down in loving detail and it's every bit as effective as the guy who uses clever tactics. But really, Wushu in practice doesn't have any reward mechanism at all.

    \n My regular gaming group usually has a majority of players who prefer a good, dramatic narration instead of role play. Roleplaying in the sense of talking in character usually turns up as a kind of curve ball - it's fun to issue as an occasional challenge, both by the GM and by other players. Then it doesn't matter if you then stutter trough it or deliver something brilliant - the fun is in seeing you try. Any mechanical rewards are usually given out by popular demand.

    You might say that I've showed them the carrot and the stick, and then we all decided to go have cake instead?

      Posted By: D-503It's a common thing to see, particularly since the 1990s. Like that rpg.net poster I think it's a terrible idea.

      XP bonuses for roleplaying raise two main issues. Firstly, you're monetising something people have come wanting to do anyway, which diminishes it and may even disincentivise it ultimately.Secondly, you're giving the GM a mechanical means with which to impact how the player portrays their character, which in a traditional rpg context is I think a big no no.
      \nI'm curious how various games stack up against this statement:

      FATE Aspects - These are awarded by the GM when they compel an aspect in order to take the game in an interesting way. It's a recognition by the GM that the player made their job easier by feeding them a juicy plot point. The mechanic is obviously meant to flesh out the story and encourage roleplaying your character, but it doesn't directly award the behavior.

      TSOY Keys - Obviously also meant to encourage bringing the character into the game and driving play with what's on the sheet. However, a player awards oneself.

      PTA Fan Mail - Awarded by the table, excluding the GM, based on the group's subjective idea of what is entertaining.

      Burning Wheel Artha - Awarded specifically for bringing the character's beliefs into play, and for playing instincts and traits when they do not provide a mechanical advantage. Playing beliefs is the character's primary purpose for existing, and challenging those beliefs is the GM's primary role. So, this mechanic is the heart of the game.

      The above mechanics have been factors in some fantastic sessions of gaming for me. So, it would be hard for me to call any of them "a big no no."
