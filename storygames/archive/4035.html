    \n Hi Emily,

    Firstly, as a warning, there's different schools of improv. It's one of those subjects, like LARPs, where people say How It Is Done, when they mean How Their School Of Improv Does It.

    Like Marc, I come from the Keith Johnstone school, so I'll say...

    Ignore the games. They won't tell you anything.

    There's various techniques that might be useful, though. For example:\n
      \n
    • Yes, And...: The idea that you should build on what the other person gives you. If you diminish what the other person says (e.g. they say "You're blinded" and you say "But only for a moment"), that's bad. Always heighten.
      • \n
    • Status: Status differences lead to interesting scenes. If I play a high-status shop owner (relaxed, direct, sure of himself) and you play a low-status customer (nervy, checking himself), the scene will be much different from if I play a low-status shop owner and you play a high-status customer. And then there's all sorts of tricks with reversing status, changing status, etc.
      • \n
    • Reincorporation: If, at the start of the story, we establish you've got a mysterious key in your pocket, it's really impressive if that key is used to open a door at the end of the story
      • \n
    \nAs I remember, there's not really a good place on the web to learn about this stuff. Try reading Impro by Keith Johnstone. It's great. Better still, take an improv course. You'll love it.

    Like I say, that's a Keith Johnstone view of improv. There's also the Del Close school of improv, which is all about finding the game within the scene, etc, etc.

    Graham

    \n Chicago-style improv is terrible at telling stories. That's not its function. It's good at moments, it's good at reflection a, it's good at growth, but its concern is not towards story.     I don't disagree with anything you said, Graham, and I'm a Del Close-style improvisor. I keep getting told there's a big difference between our styles, but I always fail to see it. Am I just not Jedi enough?
    \nWe have this huge discussion on our local improv group boards every few months. Austin is in a neat place, improv-wise, because we have a strong base of Johnstonians, influenced heavily by some killer Chicago-style people, and it's all been incorporated into this wonderful mashup of everything. Naturally, a friendly snark about the Harold or somesuch will spark off a lively debate about the two styles, and it almost always comes back to this: Both of the two main schools of improv are aiming at the same goal, they just get there by different paths. When you see the best players from either side operating at the top of their game, the product can sometimes be indistinguishable - it's just great theater, which happens to be funny along the way. The fact that you come up through the schools in one way or another, concentrating on narrative forms, or thematic ones, or going through a prescribed set of class levels or making your own way, all that doesn't matter. Like Remi said, once you've got the basics down, it's all a matter of style from there - the schools aren't all that different, in the end.

    Graham gives some great examples up there - "yes, and", reincorporation, playing with status, and finding the game in the scene are all pretty essential. But the games - so, here's the thing about improv games: most of them suck. Some of them are great for quick gags and easy laughs, but they lack re-playability, and are essentially empty and meaningless. Many short-form games evolved out of other exercises, and they get a laugh, and they get stuck. Good improv games are priceless, because they not only look good on stage, but they teach valuable lessons to beginning improvisers. If you read interviews with Johnstone in the last while or so, you'll see his utter frustration with the state of things in that regard. These games were originally developed to solve problems that they were actually coming across in rehearsals and performances, and many of them have been twisted or neutered to suck those lessons out of them. You'll find some good examples of these in the Impro books, but if you're just looking at a big list, yeah, it's hard to tell what's worthwhile, and why. Every worthwhile game has a lesson, though, and those are the ones that I love.

    (NB: the "game of the scene" mentioned above is a different thing than a short-form improv game)

    \n Game of the Scene: Looking for a pattern in the scene to exploit for comedic or dramatic effect. This is your 'game'. Finding game and playing game are integral to many styles of improv, best explicated (although not particularly well-explained) in Improvise by Mick Napier of the Annoyance Theater.

    Posted By: Remi
    Currently a minor annoyance for me because I've yet to encounter someone who could explain a good way to find and play game other than tons of experience.
    \nThe way we've been taught most of the time is to either watch for the first "weird" thing that happens in a scene, or listen for the first thing that gets a laugh - that's your game, and then you go about weaving that into the scene and heightening it. Alternately, you just take whatever choices your character makes in the first few moments of the scene - that's who you are, and you hang on to that character like grim death ("sticking to your shit", as they say), and play with the game that arises from the chemistry between them and the others in the scene.

    How you "play the game" really depends on how long your scene is, and what kind of scene you're shooting for. If you're doing 1-2 minute bits in a montage, you jump on that game and ride it hard. If you're playing longer scenes, or more grounded ones, you obviously want to draw it out more, and not heighten as ridiculously as you might otherwise - chew on it a little bit, roll it around some, then put it away for a little while while you work on character or environment building, or a different game, and then when you bring it back out and ride it a little more, that's when some magic happens.

    You're right, though - it's a hard thing to get across in words, and sometimes it takes a really good teacher to draw it out for you. And practice. Lots of practice.

    \n Emily, I'll write a few posts about this on my newly revived blog. It's all too much to talk about here: you get the techniques, but not the background.

    On structure: it's rather a double-edged sword. It can squeeze stories into the shape you want, but it can stop the story spinning off in an exciting, spontaneous direction.

    Our local impro group, The Spontaneity Shop, uses a set structure on their romantic comedy format, Dreamdate. Romantic comedy works well with a rigid structure (boy meets girl, they get along great, there's a problem, they nearly split up, they have an exciting reunion, usually at an airport). They don't use a structure with other stuff.

    (Note: when I say "structure is rather a double-edged sword", I'm using Great British Understatement. What I actually mean is: structure is a bad place to start thinking about improv. Oh, depending what you mean by structure. Aaaargh. I'll blog instead.)

    \n Graham,
    I understand what Emily's saying. By bringing back your Large, Excellent Coat in a Dogs game you reinforce the importance of that item in the story.

    However, I agree that this isn't quite the reincorporation you see in improv. The important thing about improvisational reincorporation is that you're usually recontextualizing whatever it is you're reincorporating. Sometimes this is short-term, such as bringing back a stated belief from earlier in the scene and juxtaposing it with the changes in circumstance that have come about through the scene. Sometimes this is long term, such as reintroducing a character from earlier in the show into a later, different scene. In both you're reincorporating in order to say something about the scene and/or about the thing reincorporated.

    This is not always the case with items on a character sheet. You sometimes use the Coat if it's just a handy way to say you have authority, and can use it in this way without ever reflecting on the situation or the item. It's really fun when you use an item in Dogs and it becomes more important, or something is revealed about it or the person using it. Sometimes a gun is a gun, and a belief is a belief, and using them in the game doesn't say much more than that. To go further, I used the same battle axe for almost a year in a D&D campaign (modifying/powering it up as I went along) and it never gained meaning or weight, it was just a battleaxe. Just reusing something doesn't mean you're reincorporating it in the sense we're talking about.

    It occurs to me it is easier to reincorporate people, as people are defined by their reactions to a situation, whereas some effort has to be made to imbue an object with meaning.
