\n A while back I played D&D; with a DM who had a funky way of rolling for wandering monsters.
\n
\nEvery player in the group rolled a die, and so did he. If any player rolled the same number he did, we had a wandering monster encounter. The size of the die varied depending on the likelihood of encountering monsters, so wicked dangerous areas were a d4 or d6, and towns were a d20 (or a d12 in the sketchy neighborhoods).
\n
\nWhat I noticed right away was that a big group of players meant a lot more random encounters, which seemed to work out okay because more players meant more characters meant easier to chew through bad guys. And also meant more XP. It was also cool because it made the wandering monster roll way more tense and interesting, as we all hoped to roll well and avoid danger... or not, depending on our moods.
\n
\nWhat I haven't been able to work out is the probabilities and whether this system is screwy compared to the old school "roll a 1 on a dWhatever". Can anyone with math skills help me out?

    "Roll a 1 on a dX" has 1 chance in X (a probability of 1/X) of producing a random encounter. I'm sure of that.

    Whereas - does "roll the same as any of Y players on a dX" have a probability of Y(1/X) of producing a random encounter? That would theoretically mean that if you had 4 players and rolled d4s, you would be 100% likely to have a random encounter, but that's not the case, because they could in theory all roll differently from the GM.

    \n You can do it the long way by mapping out the combinations... then figure out the algorithms.

    GM and a player on D4s can roll the following 16 combos:

    1;1
    1;2
    1;3
    1;4
    2;1
    2;2
    2;3
    2;4
    3;1
    3;2
    3;3
    3;4
    4;1
    4;2
    4;3
    4;4

    That's 4 out of 16, or a 25% chance. If you've got GM and 2 players, you'd have 64 combinations, out of which 28 have at least one of the player numbers match the GM's, so that's 28/64, which is 43.75%. And so on.

    \n I just realized that while it matters that you're trying to match with the GM rather than just roll any pairs, it doesn't matter what the GM rolls. The GM is just establishing the target number; that variation does not enter into the calculation. In other words, you could just say "what are the odds of at least one player rolling a 4" rather than "what are the odds of at least one player matching the GM." After all, just changing the target number doesn't change the odds of matching it (since this is a direct match, not a roll-above or roll-below).

    So of course one player with a D4 has a 25% chance. Two players have a 25% + (75% * 25%) = 43.75% chance (that is, out of those remaining 75% where the first player doesn't roll a match, 1/4 are going to be a match). Continue that as needed, always subtracting the existing chance of success by that step from 100 and multiplying it with 0.25, as in 25% + (75% * 25%) + (56.25% * 25%) for the next step (where you get 56.25 through 100 - 43.75). Or, for a D6, replace 1/4 with 1/6; for a D20, with 1/20; and so on.

    This is actually a very straightforward calculation when you think about it.

    \n It gets very easy if you make the calculations with the chance of NOT having an encounter.

    Mathematically you have to raise the power of the chance of not having an encounter to the number of the players.

    So if 7 players are rolling d4s, then the chance of NOT having an encounter is 0.75 on the 7th power. (Remember, the chances are always less then 1, so their higher power will be lower.)

    EDIT: It's even easy to combine different kind of dice (for example if you have a ranger, it has less chance of attracting enemy wild animals in the forest). You only have to multiply the chances of not having an encounter. For example if you have 1 d4 and 3 d6s, then it's (5/6)^3 x 0.75 (x 100 %) chance of not having an encounter. Of course only if the GM rolls with the smallest die or the encounter number is 1 wich would be the easiest.
