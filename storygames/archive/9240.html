    I'm not a math guy. Probably some math guy will chime in shortly and give you what you want. (If not, ask at The Forge.)

    But if I were pursuing your project, here's how I'd be thinking about solving this aspect of it.

    I'd be thinking what I want is a big chart. One player picks a number, which maps to a column of the chart. And the other player picks a number which maps to a row of the chart. The cell at the intersection of that row and column has a number in it, which is the random result. If the result numbers are distributed properly, it's not possible for a player to game the chart for a specific result.

    Results from 1-100 would require a non printably huge chart. But if you wanted to create a chart to generate results between 1 and 9, the simple non mathy way would be to create a grid of multiple solved Sudoku puzzles.

    \n Unless I'm missing something, a slight modification of Paul's solution can get you the random numbers you want without a huge chart and no math.

    Something like:\n

    0\t1\t2\t3\t4\t5\t6\t7\t8\t9
    1\t0\t6\t5\t1\t3\t2\t9\t4\t7
    2\t9\t0\t8\t7\t6\t5\t4\t1\t3
    3\t7\t5\t0\t9\t4\t1\t6\t2\t8
    4\t6\t1\t8\t0\t2\t3\t9\t5\t7
    5\t8\t9\t6\t1\t0\t7\t3\t4\t2
    6\t3\t4\t5\t9\t2\t0\t8\t7\t1
    7\t1\t2\t3\t8\t4\t5\t0\t9\t6
    8\t5\t7\t2\t3\t9\t1\t6\t0\t4
    9\t4\t6\t1\t7\t8\t2\t3\t5\t0
    \n
    Two digits from 0-9 are like percentile dice -- 01 to 00 (100).

    The 10s and 1s digits of the numbers you're given are the look-up values. The result is the matching place.

    Example: Player 1 gives you 45, Player 2 give you 25.

    10s digit = Column 4, Row 2 = 7
    1s digits = Column 5, Row 5 = 0
    Result = 70

    This particular chart has some interesting properties:
    * if a player gives a containing a 0, that place in the solution is equal to their opponent's. (if I give 03 as my number, the 10s digit of the solution is equal to the 10s digits of opponent's value).
    * if both players put the same digit in the same place, the result is 0 (the result from 91 and 92 is 09).
    * if both players give the exact same number, the result is always 100.

    Also, the order you put the numbers into the system matters (column versus row).

    Since the numbers are given secretly, you shouldn't be able to game the system in any case, since you don't know your opponent's value.

    That solution seems to fit what you're looking for. Or am I missing something?

    \n I still suspect the modulo thing is perfect. It's simple, and if at least one person is picking "randomly", it's more or less impossible to game. Regardless of what one person picks, every number result is equally likely to come up.

    Here's an example. Say you're looking for numbers from 1 to 6 instead of 1 to 100. Alice picks "1", wanting a low number. If Bob picks 1 too, the result is 2. If Bob picks 2, the result is 3. If Bob picks 3, the result if 4. If Bob picks 4, the result if 5. If Bob picks 5, the result is 6. If Bob picks 6, the result is 1.

    So despite Alice's "low" choice, every possible result from 1 to 6 is still not only there, but, if Bob is picking randomly, is also equally likely.

    Plus "Each player picks a number from 1 to 100. Add them together, and then if the result is greater than 100, subtract 100 from it" doesn't require any chart whatsoever, which is, I think, a pretty big plus.

    \n Modulo is correct. If you want a random number from 0 to N-1 inclusive, two people choose a number from 0 to N-1 inclusive, add the numbers, subtract N if it's too high, and you're done. Either player can make the final result 100% random by making their own choice 100% random.

    To convert from 0 to N-1 to 1-100: Both people choose a secret number from 1-100 and hand it to the GM. The GM adds them and subtracts 1. If it's over 100, then subtract 100. Done.

    Posted By: timonkeyI think that using a fixed table of some sort just complicates the choosing without addressing the issue I mentioned. If I want a low number, and I think you'll pick the last column, I'll pick the second to last row and get 0.
    You do understand that doesn't work, right, because this is a Vizzini gambit? The best strategy is to pick randomly; your plan to game it offers an illusion of an advantage. The same is true of the sum mod 100 method mentioned above, but I imagine that laziness will be more detrimental to the sum mod 100 method (since if both players pick small numbers, and they will, it seriously affects the uniformity of your distribution) than the magic square (or other hash) method.

    I am sympathetic to the problem that you have to have the magic square in front of you, and you'd rather reduce hardware. What you're looking for, optimally, is an algorithm that takes at least two inputs with the smallest required amount of entropy and hashes them to an output with about seven bits of entropy, and that can be computed in a way that doesn't require a machine whose state can't be readily memorized. This is a rather interesting problem. I think I'm going to spend a little time considering it, in fact.

    By the way, consider reducing your required range. Smaller range = less required entropy in the output to achieve dice-like results.

    \n Sage, I don't remember offhand whether that paper had a discussion of smaller ranges, but for other, evo-psych, first-principles reasons, I expect that everyone in the world can be trusted to pick somewhat uniformly from [1, 10] or so \u2014 at least, more uniformly than from [1, 100]. The assumption is, and it must be, that humans are poor at generating uniform distributions, because we are so astonishingly good at patterns. But if you are willing to do (e.g.) the lookup on the magic square table, we can make that largely irrelevant, and better, we can incentivize attempts at real randomness because if your opponent sees you using a pattern, and he's right, he can harvest that non-randomness.

    While it's true that a hash method doesn't increase the number of the results, it does spread them out better. Consider: if I'm much more likely to pick a number in [1,100] that's less than 20 than one that's greater, and so are you, the sum of our results is likely to cluster in the [2, 40] range. But any reasonable hash of our results will instead give a result of 40ish modal values evenly distributed in [1,100] instead. The end result is thus more dicelike.

    The concern about handling time is relevant. But, you can make a linear feedback shifter that's easy enough to compute on paper, as long as you're not put off by short periodicity. That's why I asked how many numbers might be required in a session.

    Oh, and John: rock-paper-scissors is un-gameable precisely insofar as the players are capable of making random picks. The evolutionarily stable strategy (that is, the one that best resists invasion) is to pick at random.
