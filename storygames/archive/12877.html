      \nElizabeth: I turned you down on hosting forums because I'd just gotten a whisper from Vincent offering to host them on the Forge, which I think is both a better place to have the discussions and means you don't have to go through the hassle of creating an entirely separate forum. If you could give me the benefit of the doubt for now, I'd appreciate it.       For the record, Polaris and Ganakagok were both created in 2004, before there were any real design discussions taking place during the contest. But whatever my personal feelings about whether discussion is critical in designing games in a short amount of time, I'm committed to creating a space for discussion because clearly it's very important to many people. So that's happening: don't doubt it. Because Game Chef is so short (9 days), the cycle of feedback+design is super condensed. It can definitely still be important to get some feedback, to show that people are interested and push you to finish, and to also build a community interested in your game to provide you more support after the contest is over. For all those reasons, I think having one or more discussion forums makes sense.

      Still, my own feelings pretty much mirror what Vincent's said over here, about design really happening in the quiet moments:\n
      Forums are great for brainstorming and getting some feedback, but the real design work happens when you're by yourself, late at night or in the shower or staring grimly at a half-written text file... There's a cycle I've observed, over and over: first there's lots of forum activity, then it falls quiet, then there's lots of forum activity, then it falls quiet again. The quiet phase is when the designer's actually working, and the forum is a distraction to the designer's work.
      \nSo I'm keeping my eye on that as well, because it's important to remember that the discussions are NOT Game Chef (talking != design). The discussions take place in parallel to and in support of game design, not the other way around. Or, at least, I'm going to emphasize that while I'm administering things. Whatever the next Master Chef wants to do is obviously up to them.

      \n Yeah, part of my impulse in trying to set up multiple places for people to post about Game Chef is that the contest deserves to be bigger than any one community or any one set of identity politics or design/play styles. There's plenty of folks -- for whatever reason -- who would have never participated in it on the Forge. So I'm trying to work out a way for GC2010 to have dedicated areas on a few different sites, both to deal with the increase in numbers and to allow people to post in a place where they feel comfortable or already have an account. So, right now, it's looking like we'll have a dedicated forums on the Forge and 1KM1KT, plus Praxis and the RPGnet design forum. In that spirit, Elizabeth, if you are still interested in setting up a Vanilla account somewhere and hosting a Game Chef-related design forum, that sounds cool and I'll definitely link folks to it, in addition to the others.

      Like all previous social components (the forums on 1KM1KT, Andy's GC forum with specific groups, the Artists First! setup, last year's combined blog feed), this is going to be partially an experiment, but that's part of how this goes. Hopefully by having a variety of options, all designers can find a place to be happy, assuming they even decide to be involved in design discussions.

      Anyway, that's how things are looking now. Some things are still up in the air until I confirm them this week. I'm just trying to let folks know how things are developing. Maybe it's a bad idea, since things could still change and people might get upset, but I'm trying to respond to questions as helpfully as I can.

      \n The Forum/Community side of Game Chef should be secondary to the goal of the competition. In the years I've participated in Game Chef, the forum/feedback aspect of Game Chef only ever served to distract me from doing meaningful design work on my game and put unnecessary pressure on me in the process.

      Getting a game design done in a week or two is hard enough as it is; especially with work and family responsibilities. Adding an obligation (if even just a perceived one) to read and participate in untold forum threads to give feedback makes the whole process very onerous for those of us to whom design doesn't come so easily.

      I'm all for making the feedback element completely optional, and merely including a link to various fora and/or blogs relating to GC games in development on the Game Chef site.

      \n No amount of pretty is going to get a game through the initial review stage of the contest if it's not a solid game. I can tell you that right now.

      It's possible that pretty + designer rep will matter slightly in the playoffs, but that depends on how many games make the playoffs and what they're like, I suspect. It's also notable to realize that Game Chef has never been a 100% fair contest: the winner has typically been chosen semi-arbitrarily and very few winners have gone on to be published commercially, while non-winners like Polaris, The Shab-al-Hiri Roach, and etc. have had more significant later success.

      Here's the list of past winners:
      - John Laviolette, Court of Nine Chambers
      - Walt Freitag, Previous Fluid
      - Jack Aidley, Chanter
      - Mischa Krilov, 1984 Prime (published)
      - Moyra Turkington, Crime and Punishment (made it to playtesting)
      - Fred Hicks, Schizonauts (playtest draft, I think)
      - Remi Treuer, Young Adult RPG (still working on it, maybe)
      - Nick Wedig & Dale Horstman, House of Masks
      - Mikael Andersson & TomasHVM, The Dreamer\u2019s Genii

      The only ones there that I've actually seen being played are Crime & Punishment and Schizonauts (I think). So Game Chef REALLY isn't about winning the contest. The contest is just a structure which motivates people to write drafts of fun games, which shows that anybody can write an alpha draft of a game concept, and often leads the way to further development and publication for some games, but that's not the point, even for the winners. I abolished having a winner at all last year for that reason, but people seemed to miss it, so I brought it back this year.

      But don't miss sight of what this is really about, which is YOU having the experience of writing a game, the best game you can write in a week. And now, with the playoffs, it's also about having the opportunity to actually try out some of the best games from the contest, including your own game, if you like.

      \n My feedback group was very helpful. Not a huge number of posts, and dialogue waned at the end when (I assume) we were all hurrying to finish our own games. But there were a few key suggestions early, a few key catches late, and enough general back-and-forth to add to the spirit of the event.

      So, yeah, if you're like me, I recommend forming a feedback group next time.

      I also experienced some nice unplanned reciprocal commenting. "If someone comments on your game, at least look at theirs" might be a good rule of thumb. Maybe it already is.

      "Here's some cool ideas! Do you like them?" was fun.
      "Here's my current plan, here's what I hope it will achieve; do you think it'll work?" was more useful.
