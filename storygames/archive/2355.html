[MB] The power of being wrong

TonyLB
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Play Advice
So there's this moment that is common to most of the stories I'm reading these days. It is the moment in which a character realizes that they have been horribly wrong about themselves and their place in the world.

Ferinstance, in Shaman King, I just read a lovely little piece with Tao Ren. Ren is an abused young man from a horribly dysfunctional shaman family. He believes in strength. He believes that friendship and human association is for the weak, and the weak are to be destroyed. This philosophy works for him for a long time. And then ... it stops working so well. Another shaman defeats him and (worse!) befriends him. Ren starts doubting himself. He goes back to his home in order to confront (and hopefully defeat) his father, to still his doubts. He gets his ass handed to him. His friends come to rescue him from the dungeons, and he tells them to sod off, this isn't their fight, he'll handle it on his own (even beaten and tortured and exhausted). His friends tag along anyway. They get beaten and savaged by Ren's father, and his father hammers him over and over again ... "You're weak!"

And then we have The Moment.
Ren: Yes, I am weak ... but it's because I'm weak that I'm fighting you.
Friends variously get up, go to join Ren
Father: Impossible! Why are you getting up? Don't you fear me? Why do you fight so hard for one another?!
Ren: I can't answer that. But I know that if I were in their place ... I'd do the same for them.
Now there is also the opposite moment ... when someone confronts the fact that they're wrong, and can take no power from it (Faith, in Buffy, turning her failures as a Slayer into a bitter rage that ultimately destroys her). Sometimes their failures crush them. There is something that happens before that moment of crisis, some build-up, that makes one outcome or another natural. By the time he reaches his crisis, Ren couldn't look at weakness and be broken by it. By the time she reaches her crisis, Faith couldn't look at her failings and take strength from them.

So I'm going to be reviewing a lot of my favorite fiction, looking specifically at these moments, what builds up to them, and what correlates with my sense of the inevitable. I'd like to figure out what actually goes along with each moment, so I can build those patterns into my system. But it'll be a lot easier for me if people can give me some first hypotheses ... patterns I can be mindful of as I'm looking through the source material.

What kind of things would you watch for that you'd expect to go along with a story where a character gains strength by being compelled to redefine themselves?

What kind of things would you watch for that you'd expect to go along with a story where a character is destroyed by being compelled to redefine themselves?

    "You will deny me three times"

    A common feature I've noticed is where the character is blatantly confronted by the fact that they're wrong and we can see it, and the people around them can see it, and they can see it, but they decide to ignore it and deliberately make the "wrong" decision.

    That's the turning point. The character may well change later on, but first they actively deny the fact that they should change. Seeing that character make a choice to deny the truth is like a sign to the reader/viewer that this character's conflict is important. And it helps sell the idea too, since you need to see them conflicted before changing their nature feels right.

    They might deny themselves any number of times (depending on pacing), but there's a pattern where eventually they either acknowledge that they've been wrong and change themselves, or they break under the strain. And, having broken, fully embrace the "wrong", changing their own nature rather than changing their actions.

    The straightforward (read: boring) game-translation would be that each time the character denies the relevant truth you (insert conflict resolution system here) and that results in either that realisation, breaking under the pressure, or getting a "notch" toward future (insert conflict resolution here).

    On a related note, don't Shadow of yesterday's keys - the buyoff mechanic in particular - handle this kind of thing pretty well?

    This is an answer to your question, really:

    I'm reminded of an old, bad joke:

    FIRST MAN: Ow! Ow! Ow! AAAUGH!
    SECOND MAN: You nutjob! Why are you hitting yourself in the head with a freaking hammer?
    FIRST MAN: Because it feels so good when I stop!

    It's much easier to realize you're wrong -- to give up an illusion you had about the world or yourself -- when you realize that being wrong was hurting you, and all you have to do to feel better is stop:

    "Hey, I don't have to hit myself on the head with this hammer in order to feel good later! I can just stop, and feel good all the time!"
    "Hey, I don't have to drink myself stupid to get through the day! I can just stop, and be able to drive without crashing, and hold down a job, and talk to my family without getting into hysterical arguments!"
    "Hey, I don't have to meet my father's definition of being strong by doing everything myself and always being alone! I can be weak and have friends!"

    Looked at this way, giving up what you believed about yourself is like setting down a heavy load of baggage. Change becomes liberation.

    The book Radical Honesty has an interesting take on which way the cards are likely to fall.

    Basically, the notion is that we learn about moral correctness in a couple of different ways. The more common (sadly) way is that we are required to absorb someone else's scorn, anger, and judgment about our wrongful behaviour, and are not allowed to emote. The greater the degree of this disjunct (the authority gets to feel but we don't), the more likely that future encounters with similar situations will be accompanied by moralizing. (Thus, someone who is beaten when she is caught stealing, and who is enjoined to take punishment like a grown-up, and who is given no empathy, is more likely to regard thieves as scum, and to beat herself up when she does stealing-like behaviour.)

    I think the missing ingredient (if you want to mechanize this kind of thing beyond the excellent Keys system) is simply neurotic blocks. Even neurotic behaviour is attempting to fulfill some legitimate need; the problem is in sticking to that behaviour in the face of all evidence. ("Insanity is doing the same thing twice and expecting different results," as the saying goes.) Do you love your idea of yourself more, or the truth? Faith expressed that "love," of course, in a deeply hateful way, both to herself and others; but it is worth noting that she was at all times attempting to protect herself from getting hurt. But she was stuck on the notion that exposing herself was too shameful to survive, so she must not do it; a way to ensure she would not do so was to receive that punishment, in advance, from herself, with utmost interior (and often exterior, 'cause hey, this is TV) violence. Thus "helpfully" pre-abused, she would be less likely to take the chance that Buffy or whoever might look at her and destroy her.

    Obviously this cuts to the core of what it is to be a flawed human being, tough stuff to pin down for a game.

    I think I'm going to say that characters are always empowered by change, in a neutral sense. Does the self that interacts as honestly as possible with the world get empowered, or does "the only way I can stay safe is never to let on what a loser I am 6d4" get empowered? For someone who is good and messed up, a generous act on someone else's part can be an opportunity for (potentially gameable) conflict, as can a seemingly low-cost opportunity for generosity or openness of their own. Here's a real-world interior conflict I have witnessed literally dozens of times that leads to minor verbal violence:

    Me (pimping for charity on the street, clipboard in hand): "Hi, got a moment for Amnesty International?"

    Them (internal 1): "I want to help!" (This is one of the most natural reactions in the world.)

         (internal 2): "No way. It's not safe, we can't afford it." (Also natural)

     (internal 1): "Sure we can."

     (internal 2): "Piss off. You don't appreciate how the world really works."

    (At this point, a decision has already been reached--actually it probably was reached on the pre-verbal level.)

        (internal 3) (actually a variant of 2): goddamnit. I suck. I'm so selfish. (Now trying to protect against falling into an overly self-absorbed personality. Ironically.)

    (internal everyone): "Arrrgh!" (A state of pain brought on by conflicting values)

    (internal 2): "We're under attack! Retaliate! Protect the human!"

    Them, outside voice: "Fuck off!"

    On days when I was less busy with my own internal wars, this little conflict played out very clearly on a person's face every time they responded with hostility. OK, long silly ramble about canvassing aside, my reflection is that it would work to have players have the choice of what to feed--either a personality structure that needs defending, though it could be tactically useful in some way, or a more general benefit of some kind (though perhaps not as immediately effective).

    To return to Buffy, it's interesting that of course the title character nearly always chose to defend some pretty freaking neurotic patterns when presented with information, but when the chips were down she usually did choose to trust her friends, which was humbling, but usually less fatal than how Faith turned out. I'd say it was her strength of community that usually allowed her to do that (as someone else pointed out earlier). That's been mechanically represented for a while now; so someone with more relationship aspects, qualities, skills, ratings, or whatever, seems likelier to be able to chance pumping up neurosis for a while until the climactic scene, while someone without those useful attachments is more likely to get burned.

    Does all that make a lick of sense? I'm just riffing here and it's 1:00 AM. So I might be nuts. But I do always enjoy this kind of thing, so thanks Tony, interesting topic.
