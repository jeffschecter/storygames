    \n Tabletop gaming becomes increasingly uncommon, as the majority prefer instead to manage resources and kill monsters online. The few games that survive as actual marketable, money-making products are those that are tied directly to MMORPGS, typically with special items, spells, or classes being made available for the tabletop version that must be unlocked by certain quests or achievements on the online game. The other tabletop games with RPG connotations will all have some sort of collectible, randomized aspect, requiring avid collection and expenditure of money in order to achieve anything.

    ...oh, wait, maybe that's happening already.

    Still, I can see tabletop gaming becoming severely endangered. I'm betting the first smartphone-only RPG will come out in two or three years (unless it has already, in which case I've been trumped again).

    \n\nMay 2011 \n

    \n
    \n
    \n In 50 years we'll have a basically illiterate working class society as video replaces writing in communication. This will do much to detach people from cultural perspective and the traditional respect for the symbols and signs of civilization. Gaming, meanwhile, will be adopted as a medium of social control, a new opium for the masses: inane reward cycles will be perfected; games will depend foremost on pathological compulsiveness, tracking time spent as the main measure of success; social status among the hoi polloi will be measured by in-game accomplishment; subcultural self-identities will be built upon which games you play. Leadership of this brave new world will be in the hands of a materially wealthy but equally inane overclass; class movements are very much possible, but basically random, as people look for success via essentially empty popular endeavour in games and entertainment; a slim, educated magisterial class will be bitter, powerless and despised by those who buy into the new virtual world and feel threatened by the virtues of old.

    In this environment what we recognize as gaming culture will form the extreme tail-end of the culture machine. There will be a seamless continuity of more and less degenerate forms of play ranging from full-blown prepackaged virtual world experiences to cottage industry do-it-yourself games of the sort we know. Even many of those who peddle traditional roleplaying will do their best to commoditize it along the lines of consumption culture models, and the search for the Eldorado of mainstream-palatable roleplaying will never cease, futile as it is when the mainstream specifically desires passive and safe forms of entertainment. Face-to-face gaming will become a rare treat, perhaps engaged in once a year with likeminded people who live scattered around the world. The amount of new game texts plummets as the number of roleplayers is cut in one tenth of the present, but on the plus side the game texts themselves will crystallize and become known as classics of the form. The hobby will be standardized and mercilessly culled to achieve a manageable in-culture mainstream that everybody participates in, because that mainstream defines the hobby. 50 years later (100 years from now) the roleplaying hobby will be basically a historical curiousity (comparable to other dead and crystallized historical phenomenons such as fencing, say), but for the moment it still exists as a living tradition on the fringes of the diminishing literate society.

    So yeah, I guess nothing changes, basically.

    \n
    \n Pessimistically:

    * Recruitment into the activity will dwindle and stop.

    * The last gamers will play only with fellow retirees. I mean I know kids who are 16ish...so they'd be "retirement age" (whatever the hell that is) in 50 years.

    * There will be no economic incentive to create physical works, electronic form factors will never quite lend themselves to actually learning a game, and the last printed RPGs will be the last RPGs, ever.

    * MMOs will absorb absolutely everyone with an interest in pretending to be someone else. Inevitably, MMOs will be fully immersive experiences. With this advancement, literary gaming will come to an end.

    * All the best creative talent will get pulled off to other, more highly paid entertainment or creative opportunities. Those who are left behind will be bottom-rung creators who will be able to fulfill their instant-gratification jones via some future variant of forum "gaming"

    * D&D will continue, albeit largely as an anachronistic curiosity, like knitting or calligraphy

    \n This hobby has been with us a lot longer than the first hacks of Chainmail. Storytelling will always be one of the central facets of the human experience. The formalizing and interactivity of storytelling that basically defines our hobby isn't going anywhere, I think.

    That doesn't mean it's going to look anything like what we have now, even in any recognizable shape, but we're never going to stop telling stories, and we're never going to stop playing games, and now that we've seen the two things can be One Thing, I don't think we'll stop experiencing them as One Thing.
