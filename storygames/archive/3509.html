    \nNot sure if you're still entertaining input to this idea or not, but I recently had actual play experience with a game in playtest that deal with this issue, sort of. The number of named characters (those who could be involved in a conflict) were limited by a hard number, but exceeded the number of players about 2:1 or 3:1 depending on the complexity of the game. Further, anyone could play a character for "colour" and could invent them on the fly. it was certainly nothing like Ars Magica.
    \n
    \nIt was cool, no question about it, but there were issues.
    \n
    \nLack of player motivation, like noclue said, was one. Without character ownership, there was little need to invest in making that character's motivations come true, and therefore little need to work towards conflict. This was especially true for at least half our group, who felt their fun was severely impacted because of this. I don't think that's necessarily a negative commentary - I think it's just what people are used to. If you invite a Chess player over for a game of "tactical wargaming" and proceed with hauling out your Warhammer 40k army, s/he might have some issues with that too.
    \n
    \nThe issue of player motivation could be helped by making all characters belong to the players who initially made them, and make them all part of a faction. Then make players fight for their side rather than through a character. That's not what the game I played did, but it seems to be what you're going for (competing villages, etc). However, if the player motivation is coming from rewarding the player directly rather than through the character (with faction dominance, "story points", finger waves, nachos, whatever), the game diverts from what many players know as "roleplaying". In that case, you need to clearly define your game on the first page of the rules - otherwise roleplayers will misunderstand it for a roleplaying game and might be disappointed when it doesn't play like one.
    \n
    \nWhat I think might help is include some sort of "prop" to make the game clearly something different than a PnP tabletop roleplaying game. For example, if the game involves conflict over resources, include rules for creating a communal map where the conflict takes place. This will take the game further into boardgame territory, which will separate it some from the RPG assumptions. The game we played had charts and maps that drove the conflict and paced the story. This was cool, and should have been played up more - in retrospect, I think we misunderstood them for "character sheets" and "tables".

    \nI love your idea, but as I mentioned above, I have also had a not-so-great experience with multi-character play. It had nothing to do with "just wanting to show up and be entertained", it had to do with the game writer not clearly hammering into my head what the objective of the game was, how to get to the fun bits, and how to recognize you were having fun when you got there. As noclue states, identification with a fictional character is a big part of traditional roleplaying. When the fictional character makes a great achievement despite external obstacles, we feel some of that excitement. In an appropriate setting and mindset, we will even derive some excitement from the emotional lows our character may face when they suffer a setback. If we do not identify with the character, these highs and lows are much harder to achieve. If your game does not emphasize character identification because it tells the story of a much larger group of people, you need to compensate for this. The players that find your idea worthwhile - and I believe there are lots - will happily compensate for it as well, if you tell them how.
    \n
    \nAs a professional game designer I don't need to tell you this, but writing rules is the easy part. Writing rules that are fun to play with is hard. Writing rules that automatically generate a certain kind of fun, when your players expect a different kind of fun, is impossible.

    \n Some questions:

    Does each player control a single group? Do all players control the same group? Do individuals move in and out of groups or between groups as part of the play? How are the groups defined and distinguished? Are there even signifiers of a group, or are you playing a bunch of individuals who on a case by case basis may be more closely aligned with individuals in another player's bunch?

    Do you even care about individuals, or are you role-playing the group as a group (e.g. an entire town), with maybe certain special individuals as traits or aspects?

    Are you just moving the egocentrism of playing an individual to the egocentrism of playing a group? When I play a boardgame or computer game in which I'm manipulating a team or nation, it's still a very egocentric experience. What's the difference between wanting my paladin to gain levels, or my civ to conquer more territory? Or to use an SG context, for my Dog to have catharsis over a childhood trauma vs. my citystate come to terms with a dark aspect of their history?

    \n I see the heart of this thread as being - how do I have my players play multiple characters at the same time, without taking away from the things that make the game good in the first place.
    \n
    \nMy gaming group has dabbled around with such things before. In some campaigns it's easy to play 2 characters simultaneously, or have a pool of characters to pick from, and depending on what the mission is choose one of them to use, or have split groups of characters taking place in the game on separate fronts at the same time, with the focus going back and forth as the GM or the story permits.
    \n
    \nI think what is being touched upon here though is - how do you play lots of characters at the same time, or let yourself go from one character to the next in a quick fashion, without any real regard for what the next character is. This has been touched upon by us in two different types of campaigns, Cthulhu, and Zombies. In a Zombie campaign, just as with Call of Cthulhu, death comes easily should the campaign be pushed in that direction. Unlike Cthulhu, where it is a slow buildup of mystery and suspense with a gripping climactic conclusion, Zombie oriented campaigns typically involve a lot of death, and a lot of people. What we've done is created a character template that is nameless, and backgroundless, but contains various skill sets and stats dependant on whatever system you're using to play the game. Then, at the beginning, the GM assigns each template a name and history within the town. The PC's play their character until it dies, whereby the template is reset to full health, and a new name / history is given and immediately incorporated into the system. If your cop just got his head torn off by a zombie in a field, your template instantly becomes the horrified neighbor watching the bizarre scene from the window. A campaign set up like this tends to remind players that their characters are mortal, and that they have to take every precaution they can if they want their template to begin to get more powerful and not be reset - but also reminds them that it is not about their own character's journey, but is about the bigger picture and the grander scheme of things. It might take 40 villagers between your party, but dammit that zombie infestation is going to be taken out. The same thing can hold true in a war campaign, such as, say, storming the beach at normandy. When one template quickly and instantly dies trying to climb the hill to the pillbox, the template just switches over to the next closest character. It's interesting, and it certainly works, but for very heavily character driven non combat oriented storylines it's useless.
    \n
    \nStill, if none of you have tried something like this before, it's worth it to give it a whirl.
