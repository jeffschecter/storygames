    \n Now Jens, there is an alternative interpretation: Jack might want to be a game writer for video games - they do exactly this sort of stuff nowadays, due to how much literary content the typical adventure game involves. It's not game design, but it's closely associated. Still not much to do with this site, of course.

    Jack: if you want to write plot and dialogue for video games, not only you need to brush up on your game culture knowledge (this is totally the wrong place to start, for instance), but you also need to accept that you're likely not going to get into the vocation by writing game plots and offering them to game studios - it just doesn't work that way. Rather, what you need to do is to build up a writing portfolio (short stories, whatever - something to demonstrate your technical and artistic skill in writing) and a CV (ideally some publishing credit of any kind), and then start submitting these to likely game studios that routinely work on projects that engage separate plotters and dialogue writers (hint: those would be the largest studios, even a mid-range one will have the design team do most of the writing). Presumably at some point you might get into a new project that is staffed from outside the existing studio staff for whatever reason, but even then you won't be writing your own stuff: creating video games is teamwork, and a writer is nowhere near the top of the creative totem pole in the vast majority of the games. Thus your job likely wouldn't be to plan a game in the abstract, but rather to execute specific types of writing to spec - dialogue writing is a pretty typical example where somebody else has developed characters and plot, and it's your job to write this one scene to express a plot point or whatever. Over time a writer can of course widen his expertise in the industry to become a game designer or even developer, which then allows him actual high-level creative input into what the game will be like.

    The only realistic way for a writer - or anybody, really - to get to call the shots in video game development is to start your own indie studio. That's a quite different proposition, but at least you have plenty of creative freedom. Of course, that sort of thing requires vastly more understanding of game creation processes than even being a professional in an established studio team does. Considering your showing so far, I recommend that you focus on improving your knowledge base on the game industry before worrying about anything else. Much will become clear that way.

    \n If you want to break into video games, the absolute best thing you can do is:

    1) Learn basic programming.
    2) Start making your own short form games. Your programming skills will improve as you write more games.
    3) Keep a blog with your gaming/programming thoughts
    4) Then take your "dream idea" and shop around for a studio to help you produce it.

    It's a long scale, probably several years or so from 1 to 4, but it's almost a surefire way to "make it" in the gaming industry as an actual Game (scenario) Writer.

    Good luck-

    Andy

    \nWell, neither the story nor the art shows any great craftsmanship. Low end amateur stuff insofar as I understand it; you still have a long way to go. This is not to say that you can't start making games - like Andy suggests, it's great practice and constructive to just start making games, programming and all, instead of stalking in the sidelines. As we often say: play games, read about games, think about games, write games, and in time you'll improve as a game maker. Practice, practice, practice.

    In case you want pointers on your writing, I suggest you look for a dedicated writing workshop - such exist in the Internet, too. They can show you how to structure and present your ideas in clear, striking manner appropriate to the work and the medium. The same goes for drawing, of course.

    \n OK, since you asked:
    The story outline shows you can come up with plot ideas, which is good (I tend to have trouble with this part.)
    Your grammar is weak \u2014 sentence fragments and run-ons are the main problem. There's very little variation in sentence structure. Lots of spelling errors that weren't caught by the spell checker ("losses" for "loses", for example.)
    Overall, the prose has no sense of style or rhythm. It's about what I'd expect from a middle-school English class essay. It doesn't flow, it's not fun to read. Granted, this is just an outline and not a full story, but if this is what you're pitching to people, it has to be good reading in its own right or you won't get any further.
    The plot is really clich\xe9d. If I were a big fan of cheap B-grade action movies I might enjoy it anyway, but I'm not.
    You don't seem to have any knowledge of the cultures you're writing about, beyond clich\xe9s picked up from kung fu movies. (You say the setting is in Tokyo, but the overall setting seems Chinese. And the character names don't sound Asian at all.)

    My overall reaction, to be honest, depends on how old you are. If you're, say, 15 or younger, you've definitely got talent and should work at it and seriously study the areas you're weak at. Above that age, I'd say you have some potential, and clearly lots of energy, but you really have to focus on learning the craft or you're not going to produce anything useable.
