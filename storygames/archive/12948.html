\n I'm at an impasse of sorts.

I'm trying to decide if I should get back on the design bandwagon and I'm looking for a bit of perspective. I want to hear your stories (a few paragraphs at most) about what it is that made you think, "Dammit, I'll have to just design this thing myself!" What pushed you over the edge so that you had to devote the time and energy to take on designing a game?

Also, what kept you going and got you to finish the darn thing? Whatever your definition of finished is, is fine.

    \n Game design is what my brain does when I'm not forcing it to think about other things. I don't really have a choice, it seems, about whether to design games.

    Publishing games, a whole other thing, is something I thought sounded fun, or at least it would make people on the internet think I'm cool. Actually it was other people's enthusiasm for a game I wrote that made me start the process of publishing it, and stubborn refusal to give up on time and money invested that's helping me see it through to the end. Also, sometimes not publishing games feels like being left out of the conversation about the thing I'm most passionate about.

    \n I was playing and I didn't have fun. We were playing some pretty rules heavy trad games, and I got fed up with the complex resolution mechanics and book keeping. I thought I'd make my own game to show what I wanted. I got started, and ran a few games at a local minicon. People liked my game so I got more involved with it and continued development and went to bigger cons. One day I realised that the game was finished and just needed polishing and then I could print it.

    That polishing took several years to be honest, but since there were people asking for my game, and I wanted to actually complete a project I kept at it.

    My first game was considered a finished project when I had sold enough copies to get the money back that I had paid the artist. The game is still up on Lulu, but I don't promote it anymore. I have some ideas for an expansion, but I'm only really working on other projects.

    --

    The current project is translating my latest dogma game into English. I will consider it completed when I see a review or AP online.

    \n\nSeptember 2010 \n
    \n
    \n

    \n
    \n Rules bloat.

    Bear with me. I was young and tried to navigate my way through the rules for the nearly complete 2nd edition AD&D. I collected far too much and tried to synthesize all of the rules in all the published material. I realized how much raw text was there, so I wound up building a compilation of all of the 2nd ed material that I would use in my games. All of the kits, all of the optional rules etc, organized in a quasi-logical fashion. As a natural extension of this principle I built a campaign world with a massive overwrought pantheon. Got my basis editing skills and started basic digital art that way.

    It was a natural progression from player, to GM, to game designer in my case. If you can't play in a game that makes you happy, you try to run one. If you can't play with rules which satisfy you, make them too.

    What makes a project finished? A physical product. I do all my design online, so when I print a version that I can feel comfortable binding, I know it is done.

    I designed Under the Bed because I wanted to understand how to publish an RPG. I published it because both Ben Lehman and Vincent Baker told me that I'd enjoy it. I finished it because it was good and I wanted to go to GenCon and sell it at the Forge booth.

    \n

    I designed Shock: because it's the game I'd always wanted instead of Cyberpunk and Transhuman Space, and was practicing for with UtB. I finished it because I'd gotten a lot of downloads of the playtest PDFs and knew I had something that at least some people wanted. I published it because that had been my intent.

    \n

    I started Human Contact because we were playing a game and it's what came out of us. I realized it was something that Shock: would tolerate. It not support, and I wanted it to actively encourage certain types of play rather than merely allow for them. I'm finishing it because it's something I'm proud of. I'm publishing it because it will be attractive to both Shock: enthusiasts and new players who like the fiction that inspired it.

    Posted By: jessecoombsI'm trying to decide if I should get back on the design bandwagon and I'm looking for a bit of perspective
    \nThen how about encouragement in the other direction?

    How about stories of giving up, of not finishing a design? I think those stories are important, too, and sheds some very important light on both the design and play aspects of our hobby.

    For example, in my case:

    Ninja Quest: This is a design I started in 7th grade math class, because I was fucking bored. Loosely based on a mashup of Paranoia and Star Frontiers, as well as Fighting Fantasy books. You are a ninja, and must navigate a castle.

    Reason: Because I was in, like, 7th grade. I got distracted by a bug or a girl or Zelda or something.

    Plastic Storage Containers: Another design built upon the rules of Paranoia, started in math class in 8th grade (seriously, what was up with math class? I fucking hated it, even though I was awesome at it) and pre-emptively finished on a plane trip with my family. It was an exercise in creating a ginormous character sheet with all sorts of weird shit all over it in order to basically entertain my best friend Drew. The setting was a far future grimdark world where the last of humanity ventures out to collect ancient relics then seal them within plastic storage containers. The title and concept was built loosely upon the Monty Python sketch "Storage Jars".

    Reason: I actually finished everything about the game that I wanted to (the sheet, about a page of rules). Never played it. It was my first exercise in designing based on a concept, and not so much designing for myself.

    Kyuseisha: This is a game I was working on for a while, but gave up on. It was going to be a Sorcerer world/supplement/setting. I started it the night I read Sorcerer and Sword, because that book fucking blew my mind. Not only was I fist-pumping while reading it, it got me instantly into becoming a hardcore Forge member, and even though it was not the intention of this supplement to breed design/designers, it did in me.

    Reason: Ultimately, I didn't finish it for the simple reasons that:
    1) I could play Kyuseisha using sorcerer, I didn't HAVE to codify it in its own supplement.
    2) Doing so would have been a lot of work anyway.
    3) Eventually, I moved it to its own system, a mashup of a bunch of systems that I liked.
    4) But the design was fun but inelegant. And I didn't feel like making it elegant. This would have been fine for Design (and I may one day finish it), but not for Publishing, which...
    5) ...are two concepts semi-related that I got very confused: It was later I realized that I wanted to Publish, not really Design; and not publish Kyuseisha. I suggest people keep the two seperate. Conversations with J-Walt helped me solidify this separation completely.

    Hegemony and Jesus Christ: The Roleplaying Game
    http://www.1km1kt.net/rpg/hegemony
    Both of these were more experiments than anything. The Reason is tied up in their creation: I was inspired to develop them, and was equally inspired to stop developing them. Hegemony had more of a fun feel to it, and I'm thinking that I may continue the idea of a Massive Multiplayer Tabletop Game in another form one day, but guess what? I'm not inspired to play such a game now, so I'm definitely not going to finish designing it.

    Take Back of Freedom: FIRE CHRONICLE
    http://www.robo-christ.com/rpg/TBOF.pdf
    I still love the idea of this game, but it is an equal-part board game and RPG mashup.

    Reason: I need to play more board games. I came up with some great ideas, but nothing solid enough to spend the rest of my time developing and finishing it. I have some fun ideas for the game at this point, but nothing clicks quite right yet.

    The Ghost Killers
    ...is a game I've been "working on" (it's like my "Hollywood screenplay" for those LA-types) for years now, I think the first playtest was like back in 2006 or 2007? It was inspired by action movies, horror movies, and the RPG Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium. I started the game when Dread was no longer in print, then came to a grinding halt when Dread came back out again. Unfortunately, Dread is... well, it's in print! But no longer supported by community forums, etc (there's still some groups dedicated to it, Facebook and the like). So I've been thinking about it again. I've also been thinking about using the framework with a different setting (Edo Japan).

    I still love a lot of ideas about this game: A sheet for the group "cabal", where pooled resources exist. Character classes ala FEAR/Japanese-TRPGs, where you choose three levels of various classes. Leadership as a powerful role, which basically gets to allocate team resources for things like individual level-ups, etc. It still exists as a mashup of action RPG and resource-management simulation (very loosely based on my experiences playing the Income/Outcome game long ago by Andromeda Consulting - BTW, if you work in a business, this simulation is seriously the fucking bomb. Get your company to get in on it), where you basically allocate resources within your team kinda like Mortal Coil.

    Reason: Job. Life. Tenra Bansho translation (translating the work of a better designer than myself). Again the feeling that the setting isn't quite there, but not sure how to proceed. And for many of the reasons listed below.

    And the best reasons, ever, for not wanting to finish designing a game:

    * Realizing that it's more fun to play the games I have with others than to design something myself.
    * Not having a shitty job with lots of free time. Unfortunately, my job is very interesting, exciting, but keeps me busy about 9-10 hours a day on the weekday in front of a PC. I did most of my idle design-thinking back when I worked crappier lower-responsibility jobs at Duke U, Time-Warner, EDS, Cisco. You'd be surprised how many big-name indie designers have "my job is boring and unsatisfying, but pays the bills and doesn't tax me a lot" as a major reason to take up design during the day-hours.
    * Finding that there's another game out there that does what I want, but better (Primetime Adventures!), or can be made to do so through like a simple GM/Player rules hack.
    * Having a lot of fun, and taking a lot of pride, in helping develop resources for others' games, without feeling the need to "get my own name out there".
    * Realizing that it's a lot of work that I could better spend exercising, reading, hanging out with wife, exploring the city where we live, going camping, etc.
    * Realizing that there's a difference between designing and publishing. We all want to feel like a rockstar. But if you really want to *design*, then design and post on your website. Understand the difference between design and publishing, and that publishing (for money, etc) is not the Telos to the design process.

    -Andy

    Posted By: jessecoombsWhat pushed you over the edge so that you had to devote the time and energy to take on designing a game?
    \nBurning time and energy on design is easier then exercising. I should exercise more.

    I'm used to thinking by writing. I change opinions on things mid-discussion because I'm discovering what I think while I write what I think. So, I can find out what I want to read by writing it - just as I can find out what I want to play by running it. Combining running and writing means I get games I like; sometimes, other people do too.

    As for finishing... Ever get one of those obsessive interests, lasts you a span of time, and then implodes? I get those. Sometimes they last a long damn time, too. Sometimes not. Sometimes they go away and come back. Sometimes the game gets done, sometimes not.

    Probably not helpful to you, but hey.

    Posted By: AndyYou'd be surprised how many big-name indie designers have "my job is boring and unsatisfying, but pays the bills and doesn't tax me a lot" as a major reason to take up design during the day-hours.
    \nI wish I had this problem... I try to find the time to do an hour or two of game design a week because I find that it helps me be more mentally and emotionally balanced and generally more productive in and out of work.

    I do development on my games because I enjoy it, I've written articles on how to do it better, and frankly not enough people ask me to do development work on their games, so I might as well do it on mine.

    I write-up and playtest some of my games (and other people's games too) because I'm interesting in seeing different kinds of play experiences. That factors into my design too, I'll often try to design something further out there on the frontier than anything I've seen before.

    As far as publishing and marketing is concerned, I think that would be grand, but I barely what little I've attempted here has been excruciatingly slow due to lack of time. And it doesn't matter if I'm trying to publish and market a good looking free product, it would still require a non-trivial investment.

    So that says why I start and that I haven't properly finished, after all, old designs don't die, they just get gutted for parts.

    - Mendel

    \n Games aren't finished; they are abandoned.

    With all my videogames but one we finished because we had a deadline where either we were going to run out of money...or with the Spider-Man games there was this movie coming out we had to ship on the same day as.

    With Schizoid, a game Richard Garfield & I and some other folks did for Xbox Live Arcade, I thought it was done, but as soon as it shipped and the reviews came out I found out the hard way that no, it really wasn't, it was too difficult and needed a better single-player mode. (But if you're hardcore and like co-op games YOU SHOULD PLAY IT, IT'S AWESOME.)

    Usually I just get bored working on paper games and leave them abandoned documents on my hard drive in a pre-alpha state. My Supernatural Romance is an exception there - I don't know when I'm going to abandon it, so I've been editing it directly on the wiki, and the latest version will always be there for people to see. You could think of this as inflicting the game on people before it's done, I suppose. I still need to put in the tweaks from last night's playtest...
