I haven't seen anyone do this one yet (here), and it seems like it might be, um, relevant at the moment, so here goes:

Why do so many games, from DND to Exalted, use defined character classes of one sort or another?

How does the inclusion of these sort of defined classes shape the games that use them?
Does that inclusion make these games, and the play experiences they output, more alike, *fundamentally*?

What do you think classes do well? What do they do poorly?

Do you think we play a class, or something like a class, even in games that make no mention of it? That is to say, are we really playing a class when our game focuses on a specific role, like a Dog in Dogs or a Sorcerer in Sorcerer, or even when we assemble a collection of character traits ourselves?

Even though i started RPGing with AD&D, I've always misliked the idea of classes - but now i'm forced to wonder why that is, exactly.

    One suggestion I have seen advanced is that it provides pre-packaged diversity.

    This is interesting to me because it effectively works in reverse - if you have two level 2 wizards in a D&D group, they are going to be strongly similar; the players choose two different classes instead, to mechanically individuate their characters. The effect fades with the degree of customizeability increasing; at D&D's sweet spot, classes make a transition between a classification of characters and a building-block with which to construct optimised models or gimmick characters. (I've seen a character with 7 classes at 14th level.)

    I see that you're not actually using this definition, but something looser; however, to me a "class" is a package of mechanical and descriptive features that one chooses for his character, chosen from a set of specific options. That's minimal and not fully restrictive, as it permits things that I do not consider classes, such as priority arrangements in Shadowrun.

    Based on this, you can say a few things:

    • Classes constrain the mechanical features of characters
    • As a result, they provide optimisation minigames.
    • It is dangerously tempting to over-proliferate classes, which negates both of the above features.

    I don't think that we implicitly play classes in games that don't actually have them.

    In my mind, there are two things that D&D type classes do really well.

    The first is hit points that grow. If you could buy D&D scaling hit points in GURPS, it just wouldn't work out well I think.

    The second is magic that grows in power. D&D forces you to get good at a whole lot of spells, rather than a GURPS style system where you can choose to be really good in one or two spells.

    And both of those fit a theme. They prevent over specialization of characters. A D&D character can't help but get more hit points, fight better, and save better.

    In 3e, with the building block classes, this part is still true. Which is why I think it actually works, even when people play with a wildly multi-classed character - though the multi-classing does make it easier for someone to make a dud character.

    So pre-3e, they also prevented over broadening (though there were those odd elven tripple classed characters, but I don't think anyone ever seriously played them, and the double classed characters seemed to do ok).

    Hi,

    Curiously enough, I find the greatest effect of classes to be not on the characters but on the groups.

    Once you have classes, you immediately have well-defined group roles, which means there will be a natural tendency for groups to distribute those roles among the players.

    I mean, sure, four-player D&D groups that don't have one fighter, one cleric, one thief and one wizard, they exist. Think back, though. How many of those have you encountered, as opposed to how many groups with that distribution?

    In ShadowRun, for instance, which is a game where that same role distribution is just as useful, it doesn't appear as much, IMHO, precisely because of the lack of well-established classes.

    And Shreyas, yes, over-proliferation of classes negates this feature as well.

    Corollary: if you're gonna have classes, have classes, but keep it down. If you keep having that nagging feeling that you need just one more class, best do away with them entirely.

    John Kim has talked a little about the way that early RPGs with their "your job is what you are" mentality fit a certain cultural niche (Midwestern mid-70s), while the next wave of skill based RPGs make "what you can do what you are" in another cultural niche (early 80's social mobility college students).

    Most of the Japanese RPGs that I love (many of them by the same company) use a variety of Class/Level together ALL THE TIME. However, since these games have as their priority goal "Be able to generate characters and play FAST", it's easy to see why they go that route.

    Star Legend: Space Opera RPG: http://www.fear.co.jp/starlegend/index.htm
    Basically, your character is made up of two out of about a dozen archetypes (you can stack the same one twice), like Space Princess, Lawman, Explorer, Tech Head, etc. Those determine all your "charms" (to borrow from Exalted) and I believe many of your starting attributes as well. You never gain more classes, just levels.

    Beast Bind: New Testament: http://www.fear.co.jp/bbnt/index.htm
    A horror-ish game kinda like Vampire Princess Miyu. You choose, again, two classes/archetypes of character from another list of 12-15 or so, including vampire type, changeling, elemental, robot(ic/ish), etc. Those determine all of your "charms" and starting abilities. You never gain more classes, just levels.

    DoubleCross: A superhero action/suspense game: http://www.fear.co.jp/dbx2nd/index.htm
    (one of my favorites)
    Basically, there are about 10-14 classes that correspond to the superpowers available (fire, electricity, pheremone, gravity, bodymorph, monster-form, light/laser control, summoning, superspeed, etc). You choose two of them, or load the same one twice to make that oen more powerful (but most folks choose two diferent ones). These determine all of your abilities. Your attributes are determined by your powers, plus your "two jobs" (real and cover: Most folks appear to be school students but are actually government agents, etc). You never gain levels or other classes, but you can buy mroe abilities. That is, level and class are set at the beginning and aren't used from that point on.

    Arianhrod: A simple/cute fantasy game: http://www.fear.co.jp/ari/index.htm
    You chooose two different classes, and that pretty much determines everything. You're ready for play. I'm not sure if you gain more classes, but you definitely level.

    Alshard: Japan's answer to Exlated (^.^) http://www.fear.co.jp/alshard/index.htm
    (basically a simple, fun, high-concept game that emulates the feel of console games like Final Fantasy. Lots of well thought out gamist elements)
    You get "3 levels" to start off with, and have some 14-18 classes to sink them into (1-1-1, 1-2, etc). Those determine EVERYTHING: Starting attributes, starting gear, starting "charms", other special abilities, combat and defense, simply EVERYTHING. Literally, all there really is to Alshard chargen is to choose which three (or two, etc) classes you want, then write down all associated abilities. near-instant character generation.

    And since the classes are really varied, you can come up with very groovy concepts just by combining the classes in unique ways. You want a brooding Elric-like character? Sounds like Elementer-Samurai-Black Mage to me. Want to emulate Yuna from Final Fantasy X? Sorcerer-White Mage-White Mage. From FFX-2, it'd be more like White Mage-Vagrant-Agent (tech/gun user).

    All in all, this company primarily relies on classes for quick, easy play, role differentiation, and because they implement several classes plus combinations, you can pretty much make up near-anything that comes to mind.

    Raph Koster posted on his blog some clever things about levels in Do Levels Suck? and Do Levels Suck? Part II. I summarized these in my LiveJournal post, Raph on Levels, back in December.

    One of the points that struck me the hardest was that levels create a tiered reward and rotation structure for content (a "content multiplier," Raph calls it). You fight kobolds and goblins for a while, then they get boring, but right about that time you level up and now you're fighting orcs, who require different tactics than kobolds and goblins. You wouldn't dare set foot in Gold Mountain when you were "merely" 15th level, but at 30th level, it is a challenge within your power. It rotates new content into your reach on a predictable schedule, thus keeping you engaged.

  • jhkim
    March 2006
    Brand's referring to an excerpt from an (yet unpublished) essay that I posted on my blog:

    About D&D

    I've also got a more mechanics-focused essay where I talk about class and template mechanics:

    Class and Template Mechanics

    So my definition is that a "class" is a category for the character which has a continuing effect on how the character grows. A "template" or "package" is a fixed grouping of abilities which doesn't have any effect after it is first used. Templates are good for quickstart, but with experience the character will drift away from the original template.

    Classes are a required mechanic. In order to spend experience points, you need to know not just stats, but also what your character's class is. You never drift away from your class, but instead follow it's path. So Vampire's clans are classes by this definition as well as D&D.

    This makes very different feels. Classes form a continuing source of identity. You can grow and change, but it is within the framework of your class. In contrast, templates are what you start out as, and the more you grow the less you look like the template. I think both of these are meaningful as symbols of identity.
