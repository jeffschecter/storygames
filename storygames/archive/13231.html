    \n Clash and others asked what led me to the inquiry.

    Well, I'm someone who neglects some of the things he's created. And I know I've neglected some things to where their existence hardly accrues at all to my creative identity. So all of the games on the list, including the three by me, are because I'm thinking about the kinds of neglect and disadvantage that contribute most powerfully to a game not being part of the designer's creative identity.

    Some of the games, like Mridangam and Thy Vernal Chieftains, were for sale at one point, but now aren't. Some of them exist only as blog posts, or forum posts, or on Scribd, or on some website unlinked from the designer's website. Some of them are by designers with prickly online personalities. At least one of them is by a designer who's banned from posting at RPG.net. And with a couple of exceptions they're all mostly not accruing much to the creative identities of their designers.

    But they also all have advantages that I think might mitigate against the neglect and disadvantage. Some have been profiled on Play This Thing, or on Rob Lang's Free RPG Blog. Some are by women designers. Some have in-yer-face titles. Some are nicely illustrated or have clever graphic design. One is by an RPG.net user with a +31,000 post count. Some have benefited from actual play threads by influencers like Ron Edwards. Some were released under an open license. Some are by designers with popular blogs, or a webcomic, or who've recently published other RPGs which have captured good enthusiasm. Some are by designers with prickly online personalities.

    So, I could have put fifty games on the list and it still wouldn't have been scientific. I'm just curious to be thinking about how and when games still accrue to the creative identities of their designers despite disadvantage and neglect.

    Some of my thoughts:\n

      If you read novels you pay attention to the names of authors. (Unless, I guess, you only read series romances or media tie-in novels.) But lots of respondents have said they recognize game titles, but can't name the designers. In this, I'm thinking, RPG design is not like being a novelist.

      The way different obscure RPGs were recognized on RPG.net vs. story-games shows me that the two communities are very distinct.

      The games I'm thinking about the most are the ones in the middle of the pack, the ones with recognition beyond the designer's true fans, but which are still quite neglected. There Is No Spoon, and The Farm aren't truly as neglected as Mridangam and Zenobia and Crime and Punishment. What about these middle of the pack games has them still accrue to the creative identities of their designers when similarly neglected games like Among the Beautiful Creatures, Awesome Women Kicking Ass, and The Niche Engine don't?

      Only one respondent, Jamey Crook, could name me as the designer of all three of my obscure games on the list. Seems to be past time for that restraining order. In retrospect I should never have shrugged off the Horsies of Happiland incident.

In contrast, a game designer tends to be much more far-ranging in his designs -- whether it's moving from kill puppies for satan to Mechaton, or someone like the author of Tramp Freighters for the Star Wars d6 RPG apparently going on to write some other non-Star Wars RPG books (Mark Rein(dot)Hagen).

    Well, an objective measure is where and how prominently the designer is credited on the packaging. For most novels, the name of the author is on the front cover and usually on the spine as well. I haven't done an exhaustive study, but I think this was uncommon among RPG books. Often the author's name wasn't on the covers at all, and you had to look inside in order to see the designer's name. As another marker, the Origins Awards and several other industry awards would announce the winning games and their producing companies - but not the designer.

    This is more of an issue with corporate games - like Star Frontiers, which didn't have any designer credit at all, just "TSR Staff." However, it has also true of many small press games.
