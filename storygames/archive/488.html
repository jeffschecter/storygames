JDCorley refered to Spycraft's chasing, investigation, and research "minigames" that tack onto the d20 combat minigame. This sounds really intriuging to me. To some extent Burning Wheel works like this, but I'm curious if you could take it one step further.

In any case, the purpose of this thread is to pick brains for the kinds of minigames that might be designed & included in such a as-yet-genre-inspecific game.

    I've been thinking about this, too, thanks to the "ignoring ttacks of opportunity doesn't break the game and therefore does nothing for it, either" argument earlier.

    I think what you need for modularity is common in/outputs on the modules, like Lego.

    So, for instance, they could all have a way of generating and spending common resources (let's call it R for now).

    Let's say we're playing Ghost in the Shell (cuz I fucking love that comic). The modules we're using are Combat, Chase, Investigation, and Politics. Each one uses XP in an identically effective, but differently implemented, way.

    Let's say we're in a Political arena. The players can spend resources to do one of a set number of things: back room, front room, distract, defend, stonewall, leak. Each one costs xR and stands to gain xR+nR. In the Politics game, everyone has the same set of cards: 1 insult card for each other player, 1 back room card for each other player, 1 front room card for each other player, 2 defend, 2 stonewall, and 3 leak. You place one down, face down, on the table. A timer counts down. When the timer rings, your turn your cards face up. You can say whatever you like before the timer rings, making promises to other players, role-playing everything out. You then reveal cards:

    Back Room costs 1R from the target and prevents the target from Defending.
    Front Room costs 2R from the target for each Insulter over 1.
    Distract cancels out the target's action.
    Defend regains you 1R
    Stonewall cancels on action against you.
    Leak costs everyone at the table who Back Roomed, Front Roomed, or Distracted to lose 1R.

    I think that works.

    Anyway, you come out with a different number of R than you went in with. Then you go into Combat with that number. Combat could be a completely different thing, involving dice and penguins, for all I care; it's modular as long as the inputs and outputs are the same.

    Maybe you can "bank" R toward your goals for your character or the story?

    The pacing thing is an interesting challenge.

    D&D has a terribly ununified set of systems. I have to suddenly roll initiative and it becomes a boardgame as soon as the cards are down. Totally different from any other type of issue.

    The point here is that you use a mechanic that's most appropriate to the situation.

    Unlocking is interesting. I think that's something that would have to be either pre-set (that is, agreed upon in the Social Contract arena) or a subject of conflict: I want to go to war, you want to stay talking at the table.

    Honestly, though? It's kinda what we do.

    I could play a Samurai-based Dogs game, where we go from town to town, fixing stuff, and then move our characters over to using Mountain Witch when things started getting interesting between the characters. Obviously, it's not optimized, but it's a good starting position, I think.

    Each of these modules should fit certain criteria. I do think that there has to be some up-front rules outside of the modules about opposition, situation generation, and other basic stuff. Otherwise, R winds up being of variable value, and that's no good.

    Yeah, one (the?) reason D&D combat is fun is because it's different from the rest of the game.

    You can really get overloaded. Spycraft suffered from this. Eventually, they had one new mini-game presented per book (gambling, initiative, searches, investigations, chases, gathering information from contacts, obtaining assistance from Q branch, etc. etc. ad nauseum) They had a minigame for everything and nobody could figure out what the hell to do to make their characters good at one versus good at another.     Actually, now that I think about it, although GURPS combat has a much more unified mechanic, the typical chances of success are so radically different - very high in normal skill-rolling play, middling to low in combat - that it is still differentiated as a mini-game at least in my mind...

    I'm afraid that won't work. What is a minigame? It's a set of certain rules that differ greatly from other minigames and are used in certain situations. So if you use a minigame you must make sure, that there is a fundamental difference from the rest of the game. This is hard enough, if you know what you will include. Including and excluding minigames at random won't work. That is like the "universal RPG" legend.

    Of course, you could say that the minigames have this lego trait, with identical input and identical output. But that's the problem. The minigames have to be meaningful towards each other. In oder to use a minigame you have to include a situation, where it will be used. You have to explain, how to get from one minigame to the other.

    Apart from that using "identical input, identical output" is the worst error possible in RPG currency. That's the same as spending CP to buy attributes and skills and then adding them together for a roll.

    I'm using minigames extensively in my current Fantasy RPG project. The main problem was not designing the minigames, but their relations. So do not talk about possible minigames, that is like: "Shall I call my fifth attribut Strength, Virility, Athletics? - No, not athletics. That's not an attribute." Let's talk about what minigames do and how they relate.

    Here's a thought...

    You don't need to make the minigames _discrete in play_. Maybe you _shouldn't_.

    Take a look at DRYH, or Pretender -- in DRYH, you have two significant minigames (the Exhaustion economy and the Madness timer) that get invoked every time you touch the resolution system. They're both doing different things, but their interface is very clearly defined without being "identical input, identical output"; in effect, the frame system chooses which subsystem to engage at any given time.

    I think it's pretty neat.
