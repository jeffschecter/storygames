    \n Gosh. Sometimes I feel like maybe half of what I know is connected in some way to gaming, if I look at it broadly.

    I was a voracious reader before learning about D&D, but it still lead me to vocabulary that otherwise would have been a long time coming - words like dais and behemoth (I still remember arguing with my friend Ted about how to pronounce the latter - turned out we were both right).

    After becoming a gamer, I probably read just as much, but certain things stuck more or piqued my interest more because of gaming. History, geography, mythology, fairy tales, religion, demonology, cultural studies and all sorts of things like that made it to my reading pile and stuck in my memory more because it all became potential reference material for gaming. D&D gets primary credit for all that, I suppose.

    From Ninjas and Superspies I learned there were a lot more types of martial arts than I had realized (and that some were much more efficient point buys than others).

    My interest in larp for the last 20 years has lead me to practice much more in the way of sewing, woodworking, electronics, and other forms of crafting than I would otherwise have done. So the IFGS gets credit for that. Especially the electronics. IFGS's simulated lockpicking is awesome.

    Running larps and playing lead characters taught me things about leadership, logistics, project management and other organizational and people skills than I would have learned otherwise. In particular, my recent experience as a ship captain in Starship Valkyrie has helped me learn a lot about group leadership and management.

    Writing larp rules strongly reinforced what I learned about technical writing and gave me a reason to practice those skills outside of the work environment which helped me become proficient far more quickly. I wrote the rules for ApocaLARP at just the right time to help solidify my tech writing skills, and it was the longest single document I had produced at the time.

    For the Airship Nonsuch game I just wrote and ran the weekend before last, I learned about the Siege of Montevideo in Uruguay and about the Mapuche native culture in Chile.

    Hell, I've learned a lot because of this forum. I constantly go out to Wikipedia or the broader internet to learn a little more about some interesting sounding concept one of you brilliant people has mentioned. Specifically, I've learned a lot about cultural tolerance/inclusivity/sensitivity/etc. topics that I was only vaguely aware of before.

    \n I think the single most important thing I learned from RPG's is how contingent information can be; how throw-away remarks get turned into solid assumptions, how a slight shift in emphasis can alter how significant a thing is thought of to be, and how what seems obvious from one side of the fence can be utterly opaque from the other. Watching players process, discuss, debate, and seeing how they came to the conclusions they did, was very enlightening.

    It's a very odd position you have, as GM, sitting there, armed with complete information, watching, but not participating, as people stumble their way to a clear understanding. I can't think of any other situation in which that precise thing occurs, and it certainly altered the way I think about people, about how people think (including myself), how and what they say. Further, it became useful to me professionally, when I had a role that largely involved steering conversations toward productive ends; I could spot the same pattern occurring, by which a speculation became an assumption which became a truism, and I could intervene.
