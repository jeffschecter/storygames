My goals and methods and techniques and shit, compiled as a result of all these failed prior attempts:

  • Crystal clear IIEE and stakes setting. Unlike in Pagoda, which SUCKED ASS in that department.
  • Simple d6 dice pool mechanic.
  • Big Messy Conflict resolution after the fashion of Sorcerer, rather than tSoY or BW or D&D. By which I mean, a general conflict resolution system that's presented as a combat system and really is mostly a combat system, but can be applied to other situations when you don't want a single die roll to decide the conflict and there are lots of people trying to do lots of stuff at once.
  • Injury system where wounds result in dice pool penalties, but characters can ignore a portion of their penalty if they're badass.
  • No fucking skill descriptions to write up. If I want to write up lists, they'd better be lists of stuff that's damn interesting. Not fucking skills.
  • A short list of broadly applicable modifiers. Including the rule of currency. Something like what I talk about in this RPG.Net thread: [http://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?t=240001]
  • D&D style spell lists, because wizard-on-wizard conflict in D&D is my favorite thing to come out of an RPG ever.

What I've Got So Far

Basics

Characters divide 14 dice between Deeds, Words, Lore, and Will, putting 2-5 dice in each. They then pick a Role, which gives them a descriptor for each attribute (or, if they want, two descriptors in one attribute, and one descriptor in two others, and none in the last). Then they pick a Talent, which gives them another descriptor.

So a character's basic stats might look like this:

Jim the Knife
Role: Assassin
Talent: Calligrapher

4d Deeds (stealthy, assassin weapons)
3d Words (deception)
3d Lore (poisons)
4d Will (calligraphy)

Tests are called for when characters are attempting a difficult action with consequences of failure, trying to do something better than another character, doing something being actively resisted by another character, or the player meeds a bunch of dice for something and wants to make a linked test. Roll a pool of d6 equal to the appropriate attribute + modifiers. Always roll at least one die. For each 6 rolled, throw another die. Total 'em up.

Your DOS equals the tens digit of your total. For unopposed tasks, you succeed on a 10 or more. For opposed tasks, you also have to score at least as high as the other guy. If both of you roll less than 10, mutual failure. If you both roll more than 10, but get the exact same total, mutual success. The other guy's roll doesn't affect your DOS: if Alfred gets a 33, and Bartholomew gets a 32, Alfred wins with a DOS of 3.

Modifiers:

  • +1d for having a relevant descriptor.
  • GM assigned penalties for doing stuff you don't have the expertise or training for. Like a peasant using a bow, or a butcher attempting surgery. If the penalty would be more than -3d, the character probably shouldn't even be able to attempt the task.
  • GM assigned penalties for especially difficult unopposed actions.
  • Resources, including time, money, and calling in favors. +2d for using way more (like 10x) than you need to. +1d for using much more (like 2x or 3x) than you need to. -1d for being on a tight budget or having a bit less than you'd like. -2d for having only like half of what you'd normally need. -3d for having the bare minimum to even make an attempt.
  • Tie-in. +1d for describing the action in a cool, clever, tactical, or vivid fashion that makes use of the environment. -1d for describing it just like an earlier action, or in a tactically unsound fashion.
  • Currency. The DOS of any roll can become bonus or penalty dice on a related roll.

If you need to apply bonuses after you've already rolled dice for some reason, just roll and add some more dice. If you need to apply penalties after rolling, take away 4s, then 3s, then 5s, then 2s, then 6s, then 1s.

Combat, But Really It Works For Any Type Of Direct Conflict

Declare actions in the free and clear. If two people are being whiny dorks and keep changing their actions based on the other guy's changes, then the one with the lower Deeds MUST declare first, and then can't change in response to the other guy. Figure dice pools for actions, but not defenses. Roll them. Resolve actions in order of highest total to lowest.

Defenses are rolled as they become relevant. You can only defend if you've already acted, chose to do nothing but defend (in which case you get +1d), or choose to give up your action for the round. This goes for resisting spells as much as evading mundane attacks. In close combat, the DOS of your defense against a character becomes bonus dice for an attack against them next round.

This is, of course, pretty much directly ripped from Sorcerer.

Ouch

Characters have a Toughness equal to Deeds + Will (+1 per relevant descriptor). This is used to determine scratch, flesh wound, severe wound, and mortal wound ranges.

  • Unharmed: 0 or less, no associated penalties
  • Scratch: 1 to (Tgh/2 -1, round up), -1d if you have at least your Deeds in scratches
  • Flesh wound: (Tgh/2, round up) to (Tgh -1), -1d each
  • Severe wound: Tgh to (Tgh x1.5 -1, round down), -2d each
  • Mortal wound: (Tgh x1.5, round down) and up, -3d each, automatically disabled

When a character is injured, roll 1d per point of the attack's DOS, add a bonus for weapon, subtract a number for armor. (Some non-attack stuff, like some spells, instead Nd of harm, -1d per point of DOS on a test to resist.) Unlike in tests, sixes don't explode. Compare the result to the character's wound ranges to find the severity of the injury.

Wound penalties apply to all Deeds rolls. Between the round during which a character's wound penalty went up and the next, a character may test Will. If they fail, they are disabled by the pain. If they succeed, their wound penalty is reduced by their DOS. Multiple Will tests overlap, and you can never reduce your wound penalty to below 0. Getting first aid reduces your wound penalty by 1d until you get yourself injured again.

Scratches heal in hours. Flesh wounds heal in days. Severe wounds heal in weeks. Mortal wound heal in months.

    Part II

    Magic

    Spells have a Burden. When you cast a spell, test your Will. The result of this test is used for three things: first, each point of your DOS reduces the amount of Burden taken by one. Secondly, a higher DOS can make the spell more powerful. Lastly, when using the combat rules, it determines when in the round the spell happens.

    Burden is added to a character's wound penalty. It disappears at a rate of 1/hour of light activity, rest, or sleep. Wound penalty dice from burden can be ignored with Will s easily as those from injuries, but first aid won't help you here.

    When working on a spell that takes multiple rounds to cast, you can't do anything, not even defend. So it's a good idea to just chop down the casting time and soak up the Resources penalty to your Will roll, or to have some defensive spells up and running.

    Characters can prepare lengthy spells for quick release or store spells they don't have memorized in their brain by Binding them into their mind, Jack Vance style. Binding takes a long time, and makes you twitchy, but lets you release the spell as a single action with no penalty to your Will roll, no matter the normal casting time.

    A character can't have more sustained + bound spells at any one time than their Will. Having sustained and bound spells makes getting rid of Burden take a lot longer. Characters that are injured or lose consciousness must test their Will, and, if they have more sustained + bound spells than their DOS, lose all the excess ones.

    January 2006 edited January 2006

    That's the sticking point, ain't it? And I've been thinking a bit on the issue since I last posted. There are three big influences to this game's aesthetic. First, the original Dying Earth short stories. Secondly, the Black Company novels, most especially The Black Company, Shadows Linger, and Water Sleeps. Lastly, a D&D campaign run by someone I don't know personally, recorded over on ENWorld as Sepulchrave II's Story Hour, Tales of Wyre.

    The unifying element there, I think, is a focus on the struggles between driven (maybe even obsessed or mad) characters with big magic at their command. And, occasionally, how clever characters with tons of conviction but no sorcery to speak of are able to match themselves against these behemoths of magicians and give as good as they get.

    So that's the types of characters I want to see and the stories I want to tell, and thus the starting point for a reward system. I need something that produces these characters, and drives their players to use them to tell these stories.

    To encourage the creation of that kind of character, I think I'll use a traits system, kinda-sorta like BW's. I whipped up a list of about 100 traits, and divided them into positive, negative and neutral categories. Characters may start with 3-8 traits, and the number of positive traits cannot be more than 2 greater than the number of negative traits. (This isn't so much to enforce balance as to ensure that the negative traits will actually get picked with some frequency.)

    Each trait is an aspect of personality or appearance, a possession, or an element of the character's social position that could draw them into conflict. By becoming a point of contention with other characters, by introducing complications, and so on.

    Characters get check marks by their traits when use of the trait, or playing against the trait ...

    • ... introduces a complication to an existing conflict.
    • ... initiates a new conflict.
    • ... drives the plot in an unforeseen direction.
    • ... helps them gain the upper hand in a conflict.
    • ... sheds a different light on the character.

    You can get a max of one check per trait per scene. Whenever you wish, you may trigger a trait. This erases all of the checks from the trait, but allows you to do any of the following:

    • Add the trait's checks in dice to a roll. This may be done before or after the dice have hit the table. If you trigger the trait after rolling, you can only add in the bonus dice if you narrate a way in which the trait works to your advantage.
    • Test an attribute for advancement. Roll a dice pool equal to the checks in the triggered trait, at a penalty equal to the current dice in the attribute that you are testing against. If you score a success, the attribute increases by one die.
    • Initiate a flashback scene involving the triggered trait. The player may make a number of statements about the scene's setup equal to the checks in the triggered trait. Nothing that contradicts already established elements of the game may occur in a flashback scene.
    • Play out an 'elsewhere' scene involving a character connected to the triggered trait, but not the character who actually possesses the trait. The player may make a number of statements about the scene's setup equal to the checks in the triggered trait. For the duration of the scene, they take on the role of another character.

    At the end of a session, players may petition to have traits added to or subtracted from their character. If the GM agrees, then everyone at the table who isn't the GM or the player of the character in question votes yea or nay, or holds their peace. If the yeas outnumber the nays, then the trait is added or removed.

    Seem kosher?

    Anyway, I was just struck by this little idea while reading over Shreyas' graphical character sheet thread.

    So: A good third or quarter of everyone's character sheet is taken up by a map. Standard fantasy fare, with forests and rivers and mountains and stuff, but no names or labels or cities or political boundaries or anything.

    Oh! Or, instead of having little maps on the character sheets, you start with a big map for the whole group. As a pre-game thing. Like I said, just geography... before you even get to chargen, you've got this map sitting in the middle of the table. The GM lays out a few basic guidelines ("Let's stay away from the non-humans for now, and any concrete evidence of the existence or non-existence of divine beings"), and then you just go around the table, with everybody adding saying something about the map (or going into further detail about something that someone else said about the map) on their turn. Anyone can call BS at any time, and, if at least half the table doesn't like the whatever, then the whatever ain't true.

    For example:

    Alfred: This region (points to a southern archipelago) is The Aquamarine, a confederation of coastal and island city states.

    Benjamin: At the mouth of this river there's a city (draws a little dot), Arjun, that's the most powerful in the Aquamarine. (writes in the name).

    Calvin: Because it's ruled by the sorcerer-prince Camkamodes III, and is at the mouth of the Arja river. (writes in the name)

    Dan: Over here (gestures to another southern region, an expanse of land devoid of rivers on the western edge of the map) is the oasis city of Futh. (draws a dot and writes a name)

    Alfred: It's a small town in--

    Dan: Bullshit! I imagined it as a big city! Any backup? (Ben and Cal support him)

    Alfred: Okay, it's a major trading center in the desert empire of Eruth.

    ... and so on and so forth. I guess the game would come with a few maps drawn up in Illustrator or scanned in, and a bit of advice about making more.
