\n It is possible to create a credible Story Game with solo play? Would you even want to?

I'm thinking a lot about solo play right now. I just finished How to Host a Dungeon, a solo Dungeon contruction game with no system-mandated choices, player resources, or winning conditions. I've got some ideas I'm tossing around for story-driven solo role-playing game. Am I just on crack?

    \n With a bit more thinking, I've come up with some models of solo play that I think might segue into story gaming play.

    Sandbox Play: What can I make out of this set of elements? Making reign characters using the one-roll system falls into this category. Character generation gives you a bunch of lifepaths your character has lived, but doesn't tell you what order they occurred in. You tell the story out of the pieces provided.

    Puzzle Play: How do I assemble this set of resources in such a way as to achieve a goal? The only example I can think of here is the experience of trying to make a Burning Empires character with as high a propaganda skill as possible while passing though the lifepath "beggar".

    Interpretive Play: How do I interpret this set of elements? The In a Wicked Age oracle is an example of this. I've got a few narrative nuggets and I have to decide what in those nuggets corresponds to plot elements and characters in such a way that I end up a strong set of crossed interests for conflict fodder. Maybe Hikikomori falls into this category too. Something happens mechanically, but it's up to me to decide how that becomes a narrative.

    Most of these are essentially mini-games within Story Gaming. I think this is a starting point for solo play.

    \n Tony, the various concepts you mentioned strike me as highlighting something important:

    Solo-play seems to involve a player-response to a mechanically-arranged situation, and it ends with your own evaluation of your level of satisfaction (either a binary win/lose, or something grayer like "Hmm, maybe I can do something to get that propaganda skill higher, but it's pretty high right now, so I'm satisfied.")

    I'm not sure how engaging those are as "story" games -- it does feel like the story elements don't feed back into the system to provide continued play, and in that sense it seems about as much of a story game as, say, "here's a writing prompt, now go write." (This isn't a negative critique, though without inflection I think it could read that way. Perhaps more of a possible consideration.)

    \n What if the soloist takes the roll of GM rather than character?
    \n
    \nCharacter(s) could be created randomly using a lifepath. The "GM" writes a situation which is limited by a timer or word count (perhaps a paragraph of under 100 words). Then s/he writes a list of possible actions the character might take in response, the number of items determined by the type of dice used. The "GM" then determines the outcome of the action and writes a paragraph...etc.
    \n
    \nThis could be combined with cards, event tables, prompts, or even existing game systems.
    \n
    \nI don't know if anyone has done anything like this before. I certainly haven't researched it.
