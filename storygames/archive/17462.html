    \n Obviously some tastes will vary, but our reasoning behind the HP numbers is that we want things to be dangerous but still give the GM enough room that they don't restrict themselves too much.

    The thing we were seeing with lower HP was GMs killing a PC in one hit and then getting really nervous about the players being mad or whatever. The current HP system is there to allow PCs a couple of mistakes without being dead, but still make death always an option.

    The thing to remember (as someone pointed out) is that damage follows the fiction. If something happens that would hurt a PC tell them what damage they take. That often follows from a soft move that was ignored, something like "the goblin archers that you didn't deal with take their shots as you slay their kin\u2026"

    The first fight for new PCs they likely have an advantage\u2014they're pretty well equipped. But after the thief's leather armor is ripped to shreds, the fighter's shield gets mangled by a trap, and the cleric has Cure Light Wounds revoked, well, trouble happens.

    The chance of player death is also balanced against how long it takes to make a character. It's not quite as easy to make a new DW character as it is to make a new Dungeon Crawl Classics character, for example, so DW characters are a little more survivable. That's not to mean they're invulnerable, just that it takes a little more to kill one. That may not be the most old school approach, but it's an approach we've had a lot of fun with and seen a lot of other people enjoy.

    That said, Adam and I aren't infalible. We have a lot of confidence in the numbers as stated but you may want to adjust them some\u2014that's completely cool! There's certainly some taste involved. If you're a GM who's ready to dish out damage and your players are ready to take it then try one of these:

    Instead of using your class's base HP, during character creation roll your class's damage die and add CON. That's your Maximum HP. If your Constitution improves you can roll again and take the better result.

    Instead of using your class's base HP, during character creation roll your class's damage die. Compare the result to CON. The higher number becomes your Maximum HP. If your Constitution improves you can roll again and take the better result.




    Either one of those will put you into Moldvay-ish levels and make every goblin a threat to the wizard.

    Fights
    Sooner or later blades are drawn and blood is shed. When this happens the players are likely to start hacking and slashing, volleying, and defending. Think about more than just the exchange of damage. Monsters might be trying to capture the characters or protect something from them. Understand what the fight is about; what each side wants and how that might affect the tide of battle.
    No self-respecting monster just stands still for their beating. Combat is a dynamic thing with creatures moving in and out of range, taking cover, and retreating. Sometimes the battlefield itself shifts. Have your monsters take action that the players will react to. Make sure you\u2019re making use of moves beyond deal damage, even in a fight. p.171
    (highlight mine)

    The amount of HP is mechanical, and for some archetypal reason it holds this all pervading sense narrative life or death certainty. I find this oddly jarring in a game full of invokable story cues like conditions, tags and move choices. So I use physical counters to rectify this, the players each have a bunch of heart shaped red glass tokens. I tell them that once their last token is gone, their heart stops and they have to face death's dark gates. I also give out conditions all the time, prescriptively following the narrative description. I mean debilities are the obvious choice here. But I use others as the situation deems suitable. The players write the tag down on a post-it and stick it to the top of their character sheet.

    Cleric fails his prayer roll to turn undead? Well, I could have the zombies gnaw into him or his friend's HP, but I would probably say they are terrified! I will invoke that tag (usually requiring a defy danger move) until they manage to revoke it; as I am a fan, but I also like putting the players in a spot.

    \n Paul, there's a slight difference that has changed over time: at some points HP was X+Con, at some points it was Y+Constitution. Con is the modifier (-1 to +2 for starting characters); constitution is the ability score.

    Over time we've tried several approaches. The ones that we went the furthest with were low base + Con (so something like 4+Con for the wizard) with scaling by level; or the current med base + Constitution without scaling. We found the not-scaling made a better game, for the type of game we were going for.

    I'm actually a little ashamed that, until this thread, I had never thought of using damage dice to set HP. I actually think there's a lot of potential there, but DW is essentially a finished game\u2014we're not going to try that change now.

    Another thing to consider, when comparing DW HP and D&D (any edition) HP is that D&D HP takes into account miss chance. So a d6 damage goblin vs a 4 HP wizard will still only get hit maybe 50% of the time, so roughly two rolls per hit, and two hits to kill, so four goblin attacks to kill a wizard. The DW wizard has 4+Constitution HP, so considerably more\u2014let's say 13 HP, which assumes a wizard with constitution as his second lowest score. He also has 1 armor (most likely) so it takes being dealt goblin damage about 5 times to kill him on average. That's slightly higher, yes, but not hugely different.

    And, as John points out, that damage is following form the fiction, so there's lots of other badness likely to come along with it\u2014broken things, lost weapons, people in spots, poison, debilities, etc.

    This isn't to say we're exactly the same as D&D (which of course has changed over editions), just that we're in the same neighborhood. That was our goal\u2014close enough to D&D to feel familiar and create the same kind of situations.

    We're going to ramp up group damage a bit more.

    Before I get to the two options we're considering, a note about the fiction: at some point a large group of monsters becomes all about fictional positioning, not one-on-one fights. Like the goblin warrens in The Bloodstone idol: the sheer number of goblins makes that more like an environment effect, not a large number to fight.

    Anyway, the current rule is:

    When you take damage from multiple monsters at once, take the highest damage amongst all the monsters, roll it for each monster, and take the highest result.

    The problem there is that it doesn't quite scale the way we want it to. Goblins are a good example: by the time you have more than a few you're just going to get 6 damage each time, which isn't quite what we want.

    So we're adding some amount of additional damage per monster. This makes it so goblins scale up a bit more. Our design goal has been that one goblin is dangerous, but a few at the same time is likely death for an unprepared wizard. This change will bring us in line with that.

    Here's the changes we're considering:

    +1 damage per monster beyond the first.

    For each monster beyond the first, add damage equal to half the size of their damage die: +2 for a d4, +3 for a d6, +4 for a d8, +5 for a d10, etc.

    For each monster beyond the first, add damage based on their organization: +2 for each horde monster and +4 for each group monster. For each solitary monster, roll it's entire damage and add it to the total.

      \n Yeah, we want to provide some mechanical support for "well, yeah, getting hit by more guys hurts more too, as well as being surrounded and mobbed and one of them is climbing up your leg and oh god\u2026"

      After much talking with Adam and, of course, John, I think we're going to go with a flat +1 per additional monster. With +2 as a note somewhere, of course.

      I was leaning towards +2, but the advantage of +1 that Adam pointed out is that it leaves more room for the GM to do that fictional stuff\u2014"8 damage and they drag you down to the floor." At +2 the damage becomes so big that it's easy for a GM to feel like anything on top of it would be overwhelming.

      Our use case here was typical wizard (12-13 HP, 0-1 armor) against 4 or so goblins. With +1 per helper, 4 goblins will do 4-9 damage, or about 1/4-3/4 of the wizard's HP. That's a good range for us. It's unambiguously something the wizard has to respond to, since two of those are likely to be deadly, but it's also survivable (if you're not already too beat up).

      And yes, a wizard could easily end up with a little more HP or some temporary armor, but that's good. That means they're playing the game. We're not trying to dictate how many hits a wizard can take, just looking at the fictional facts we're establishing.

      The fighter (23-26 HP, 1-2 armor) can't ignore four goblins either. They'll do up to a third of the fighter's HP\u2014she can take her chances with a hit or two, but not for long.

      Those numbers feel solid to us.

      It also stays close to our existing math, which is good\u2014it's been well tested, and while we want to adjust it, we don't want to break it.

      There's also a nice symmetry with armor\u2014each attacker cancels out a point of armor, basically.

      So that's what we're thinking. We'll be distributing a PDF with this change (and the remaining edits) tomorrow, so any feedback on it today would be awesome.

      Thanks to everyone here for having a reasoned discussion. You guys rock.

      \n A tangent, relating to damage (and courtesy of John Harper): HP isn't actually directly tied to death.

      HP is a measure of how long you can last in a dangerous situation. Fights, most obviously, but that swinging blade trap will take your HP just as surely.

      What happens when you run out of HP, though, doesn't have to be death. If you want to change the deadliness of the game (as opposed to the pacing) the Last Breath move is the place to do it. Consider:

      A last breath move that tracks a resource, call it Fate. You start with X Fate, when you take your last breath you can spend 1 fate to automatically succeed.

      A last breath move that just says "You die. On a 10+ you die on your terms\u2014you have a few moments, minutes, or even days (depending on what killed you) to take care of business. On a 7-9 you die, but leave some benefit to your allies, name it."

      A complimentary "knock out" move. When you take damage greater than Con, roll. On a 10+ you shrug it off. On a 7-9 you take -1 forward. On a miss, you're out of it for a while.
