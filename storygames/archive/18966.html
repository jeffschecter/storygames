\n Heya,

I have a friend who is a professor at a university in Japan. He sent me the following quote:\n
Basically, I'm planning to assign my students roles in medieval society - most will be peasants, but also a few knights, priests, craftsmen, and a king or so - and I'd like to get them into some situations in which they have to act according to their roles. This might be far too much to ask of them, but I want to give it a try. One of my classes has 30 students, so whatever I do, it needs to be pretty self-directed. I want to focus on them playing their roles, so the mechanics should be minimal, if they exist at all. On the other hand, I think I need to give them something concrete to work with, or they'll just end up messing with their phones the whole time. It should use a fair amount of speaking - like a balloon/lifeboat debate - but I'm not sure how I could get that to work in this situation.
\nSounds like he'd need a LARP type thing maybe, but I have zero experience with games like that. Anyone have any ideas? I'd appreciate it!

Peace,

-Troy

    \n Introducing an antagonistic aspect will greatly help to motivate their actions. As a young teacher also, getting teenage students to actively participate in anything that does not have competition (or the chance to impress the opposite gender) is incredibly difficult.

    I recommend a game like Mafia or Werewolf, but with some of the 'special' roles added, such as Hunter or Priest. Rename the specials to Knight, Priest, Craftsman, etc, and give an appropriate name to the enemy, and it should work out.

    \n First, a question: European medieval society?

    As you say, I'd go with a (chamber) larp-based design, or freeform-like design (either in the British or the Nordic sense) and the basic units of such a design would be:

    \u2014 picking relevant situation(s), that both illustrate medieval society, feel compelling to participants, and are critical enough to the involved characters to not allow a completely passive behavior.

    \u2014 handing out characters/roles consisting each consisting of the shortest possible text, which include all informations a teacher deems necessary to productively play out the role, and which contain ties to other characters or events such that they are inextricable tangled with the present situation.

    I was searching online for The Tribunal by J. Tuomas Harviainen, which looks to me like a golden example of such a design, but the scenario doesn't appear to be freely downloadable\u2026 While searching, though, I found this book, which both includes the scenario and looks very relevant to the topic.
