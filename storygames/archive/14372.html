\n Has anyone played Microscope and not liked it?

I haven't played it yet. I bought it and thought its text was in my top 5 best written game texts I've ever read. No small praise. If I was to write a game, I would reference Microscope. Also the sheer number of playtesting is impressive!

But\u2026 (here's the twist) almost everyone I've spoken to (all very different people) in private didn't like the game. Yet looking at Story Games, there are so many threads and so many posts praising Microscope. It makes me wonder, what's going on?

Maybe it's hype backlash. There is so much Story Games hype that when people played they wondered, "is that it?" And the reaction is so strongly positive on Story Games, people don't want to say anything. Or maybe Microscope is such a different kind of game that you really need to play it more than once with minimal expectations (and the hype is creating expectations) but people who don't like it haven't played it enough. I should note, no one I spoke to hated it. The dislike was mainly a mild sense of disappointment or confusion. Maybe the dislike is so mild that it isn't enough to motivate anyone to say anything.

What I'm reading in public is painting a different picture from what I'm being told in private. And Story Games (which I love, one of my favorite online communities) has a negative reputation with overhyping\u2026 see D&D 4E a few years ago. It was "the awesome" for a few months and then many people quickly turned on it.

I'm personally going to play Microscope no matter what. And I suspect my now lower expectations will actually help me enjoy the game more than some of the people I've spoken to.

Ultimately no game is for everyone. And when I read well reasoned opinions based on actual play from people who both like or dislike something\u2026 it gives me a clear picture of who a game is for. It gives me an idea of if the game is for me.

Negative but fair reviews give me more information that only positive reviews. When I got to Yelp or Amazon, I always read the negative review first. If I read, "this was a good restaurant, the food was tasty if you like spicy food, and the prices reasonable but we had to wait 20 minutes to get in\u2026 1 star", then what this really tells me, if I don't care about waiting 20 minutes, the 1 star aside, this place is probably worth checking out\u2026 especially if the positive reviews are strong and the negative reviews don't really matter to me. Same thing with games, a negative review may say, "the art is terrible but the game play is excellent if you like strategy game with no random elements." Well then if the art isn't that important to me, and I like strategy with no random elements\u2026 sold!

But without any criticism or people disliking something to balance the outpouring of positive reviews\u2026 I really have trouble knowing if something is for me. Since no game is for everyone, only positive reviews doesn't give enough context to help you decide if something is worth paying for. And often, "just play to find out", isn't an option.

    \n I find Zac's comment on mediating cues very apt. With Microscope, like with Universalis, the players need to bring their own content to the table, along with their own pacing and structuring (of the fictional content).

    It's a framework that gives each player space, in turns, to push forward their own vision. So I can see that its success or failure depends a lot on what the players bring to the table, moreso than with, say, Apocalypse World, which has fronts and specific moves to structure the experience.

    That's not a good or bad thing, just a style difference that will work better (or worse) in some circumstances than others.

    This isn't part of the marketing, but is inside the book and does a good job of explaining what to expect. I suspect for some people, "you won't have your own character" would be something they would want to know up front:\n
    Microscope works differently than some other role-playing games you might have played, so let\u2019s abandon some preconceptions:

    You won\u2019t have your own character.

    You won\u2019t play the game in chronological order. You may know all about the future, but be surprised by the past.

    You\u2019ll build the story from the outside in. You\u2019ll decide the big picture, the grand scheme of history, and then burrow down and carve out the details.
    \n
    Humanity spreads to the stars and forges a galactic civilization\u2026
    Fledgling nations arise from the ruins of the empire\u2026
    An ancient line of dragon-kings dies out as magic fades from the realm\u2026

    These are all examples of Microscope games.

    In Microscope, you build an epic history as you play. Want to play a game that spans the entire Dune series, the Silmarillion, or the rise and fall of Rome in an afternoon? That\u2019s Microscope.

    But you don\u2019t play the history from start to finish, marching along in chronological order. Instead, you build your history from the outside in. You start off knowing the big picture, the grand scheme of what happens, then you dive in and explore what happened in between, the how and why that
    shaped events.

    You are free to jump backwards or forwards, zooming in or out to look at whatever you want, defying limits of time and space. Want to leap a thousand years into the future and see how an institution shaped society? Want to jump back to the childhood of the king you just saw assassinated and find out what made him such a hated ruler? That\u2019s normal in Microscope.

    You have vast creative authority. You can make whole empires rise and fall at will. Dream up a utopia or destroy one with nuclear fire. You have that power, but remember you\u2019re not alone: everyone else at the table can do it too.

    You create independently, but not in isolation. Each facet you add to history builds on what other players built before you. You expand on their ideas, and they expand on yours. History might not turn out the way you expected. Be prepared to think on your feet.

    When you zoom all the way in to a particular moment in time, all the players share the stage and role-play together to find out something we want to learn about the history. Did the crew of the Icarus know the aliens were on Titan? Did the rebels really fake the government crackdown? Do the knights remember the original meaning of their ritual vows? We role-play and see. The more you play, the more your once simple summary becomes a detailed tapestry, full of meaning and surprises. History snowballs.

    \n Heya, so I ran the 7-person game at Nerdly. Sorry about the long time to post about it: Been busy at day with work, and at night with... well... Terraria. :-)

    Here's what Mike Miller said of the experience over on his blog:\n
    Instead (of playing Puppetland), I played Microscope. We knew we were breaking the game before we started, as bringing seven players (Andy K., JACN, Rob, Seth, me, Julia, Giulianna, and Will) to a game intended for three or four is not the best choice for awesome play. But as the purpose was demo, more than play, we all accepted that and worked on the story of how the refugees from a religious war eventually rebuilt a high-tech hunter-gatherer society on their own world and reconnected with interstellar society at large. It was an interesting story. Perhaps a bit too cerebral, but we were all thinking hard about learning the rules.

    On the whole, I was a bit underwhelmed by Microscope. Not by the fiction we created, that was cool. I was hoping for something more robust in the rules beyond structured brainstorming. While the strict guidelines on when you can collaborate and when you can\u2019t are interesting, I\u2019m not sure that they really serve to enable the group to explore material they wouldn\u2019t have, simply by sitting down at a table and talking. Still, I might buy the PDF and look it over.
    \nSo I've run kinda crazy "This is the taste of the core of the game" demos before, including Beast Hunters for something like 15 people (!!) at once back at GenCon. This was initially going to be a "four people including me to play a 'quick session'", but due to a LOT of folks interested in it, I turned it into basically a 7-person demo (an 8th watched us play towards the end) of what the critical functions of the game are. We all knew well going in that it was not a "true" play of the game, but basically a controlled demo/experiment of what it does and how it plays. No one came away from that game with a better or lesser opinion on it due to the fact that something was supposed to happen but didn't due to the size/time of the game. It was a taste, pure and simple.

    We also went in knowing:
    * I had about 4 hours or less of sleep a night for the past three nights,
    * I had only read about 70% of the book, knew all the setup procedures well but would have to stop and read through the Scene parts to make sure we go all those (which wasn't a problem, again due to the clear writing),
    * That I had only read a little of the great game advice in the back,
    * We were limited on time, and had too many people to effectively judge the game in any real fashion other than the basics of what it provided.

    It's hard not to compare it to Universalis: Both start with world creation, both can get bogged down if you don't say "When" soon (our first Universalis game has something like 40 "facts"; we only had about 9 in Microscope, which was great, mostly because I brought up that comparison to Universalis and everyone nodded their heads sagely; seemed that they had all seen that effect of Too Much Shit in the Start). Also, the other comparison is that the actual Playing of Roles ("role-playing") is not the Telos of the game, it's just one of the three things that the game consists of, which is world-building and story/element creation. Role-playing isn't the center or core of the game. <--- This is the only thing that I saw the game judged on, re Mike above, which is a judgment that can be fairly made of the game without having played a "true 3-4 person sesison".

    Anyway, as we laid the cards out we came up with a world (not Earth) torn apart by a religious schism and war, a return to stone ages, a feudal period, a post-scarcity society and almost transcendence (technology provides, and people return to hunter/gatherers). Most of the start was "black", while the end was noticeably "white".

    We went through all the mechanics with the sole exception of declarative scenes, which is where one person sets up the scene then does a quick "puppet show" in front of everyone else describing who is in it, what happens, and it ends with no playing of roles. Almost like a cut-scene. We got through one full Lens (Julia, focusing on the hunting/gathering transhumanist society), and in the end even created the first Legacy.

    When we actually had scenes, we knew that 7 people was too much. Heck even four people was too much at times. So I did a spot-hack which was basically, "OK, starting with the next person simply say if you want to be in the scene or not. Once we hit for YESes, we stop. The NOs will be in the next scene we do". So in the end, we only had 3-4 people per scene still, and the scenes were short enough that no one lost attention.

    Anyway, after play the game affirmed to me that I want to look deeper into it, and since I now know how it plays out, to run it for a more intimate group of 3-4 people locally. I really like what it professes to do, and in play I see that it does what it says pretty well, but I think I want 2-3 sessions (or at least one full run around the table with each player as Lens) to form a real conclusion.

    The advice is kind of spotty re ending of scenes: It says END IMMEDIATELY when the question for that scene is answered. However, there were some scenes we did where the answer came out and basically someone said "That's It! DONE!" like mid-sentence. I think there should be more advice or a quick actual play example of how to organically end a scene without cutting mid-sentence and also without resolving all the "problems" that appear in the scene. We ended up finding that sweet spot by the end of our round though.

    I liked the Hotseat: I get where it's coming from, it truly does do what it says for Microscope, which is:
    * To make sure that the very well-meaning but unconsciously socially inclusive person doesn't accidentally direct the game so that the game goes smoothly by pushing down the middle of the road, and
    * To ensure true randomness, true "out of left field" results that will pop up in game that are almost incommensurable with some pieces, but critical to the kind of spontaneous, "I totally didn't expect that" kind of effect that really makes a game of Microscope interesting and unique.

    Even moreso than Universalis, I can see this as "The game I use to create the initial gameworld for which we will then use Game X to play", but with a few more sessions I will be able to really form a better opinion of it as a game in and of itself: I'm still very favorable towards it, but need another session or two to solidify my thoughts.

    In the end, the bits that will likely turn people off the most are:
    * Reduction of the importance of actual role-playing in the game; instead, as mentioned above, it is only one of the three legs of the game, not the center or the "point".
    * The "Hotseat": After initial setting creation, cooperation is cut off, and only the person in the Hotseat (the person whose turn it is) can suggest things or say what happens.
    * The fact that everything is procedural, there are no numbers/dice/cards.

    Any questions, ask away.

    -Andy

      \n I played it, too, and enjoyed it quite a bit.

      It gives a good structure for the creation of a nuanced history of a world or culture, with bits that keep you focused on the important, dramatic events of that history, not tangential details (like background details or specific currencies - ironically, perhaps, people into that kind of world-building might be less satisfied with this game: Microscope seems to be more about the flow of history and its twists and turns than detailed world-building).

      That said, it doesn't *give* you much: you pretty much do it all yourselves as you play, which can be a plus or a minus. I wouldn't expect this game to go over well with a group of people who are not into being creative and sitting in the spotlight while the others sit and listen. The "Hot Seat" effect is very very present in this game, and the text even tells you not to collaborate with the other players but go do it on your own (I agree with Andy's reasons for this, up above). More so than in just about any other game I've played: when your turn comes around, you're hanging there and you'd better come up with something.

      The "roleplaying" tends to be fairly rare, brief and focused, and completely freeform, if we understood the rules correctly. So it'll appeal to you if you like freeform roleplaying, but not the extended, "get into your character's skin" kind of freeform roleplaying: you need to set up punchy scenes and play through them quickly.

      Overall, my experience of the game was one of a structured way to get a group to take turns coming up with cool stuff and telling the others what their latest cool idea is.

      I can certainly see why some would want to call it "a toy" rather than "a game". And I can imagine many saying "it's not a roleplaying game", as the "roleplaying" portion can easily get skipped or cut out altogether in play.

      From my limited experience with the game (one session, and it's possible we got some things wrong), it's a great way to create a historical setting full of drama (rather than world-building specifics), and one that a) surprises everyone at the table with its twists, and b) leaves everyone feeling pretty heavily invested in the world/history you've created. It does that very well, given a group of creative and enthusiastic players who are not afraid of "the spotlight".

      \nYeah, these are the things that I didn't particularly care for.
      \n
      \nWith regards to the first I would have preferred to just have the quasi roleplaying eliminated all together. There isn't really enough meat to any of the characters to really sink your teeth into, and no real way to build towards such. To me I'd rather see either the roleplaying aspect elevated, or gone entirely. The middle ground kinda-sorta roleplaying did not entertain me.
      \n
      \nThe hotseat is also a bother to me. Unless I was really jonesing for a game with a particular group of people and Microscope was all they wanted to play, I don't think I'd play it again with the RAW hotseat rules. I simply don't want any one person to have that kind of control over a game that I'm in. Its like all of the worst aspects of railroading magnified...and without the advantage that a rail road normally has of being carefully and lovingly crafted. Instead its spur of the moment whatever pops in your head improv railroading...and EVERYBODY gets to take turns driving the crazy train.
      \n
      \nFor me, if I'm going to invest time and effort and brain power into a game, I'm going to be pissed if it gets completely derailed into something I'm unable to enjoy with no ability to intervene to keep it from going there. That's why there are Challenge rules in Universalis. Frex, I don't like gonzo wacky surreal mad cap crazy. Both collaboration and veto techniques help me ensure I'm not about to play a game that goes gonzo wacky surreal mad cap crazy. In the absence of those...if the game goes there, there's nothing I can do about it, and I've just wasted however many hours of my life on something I loathe.
      \n
      \nNow most game of Universalis I've ever been in never even used the Challenge rules and when they did it wasn't on "serious" game breaker challenges...because the very existance of the rule keeps people from going somewhere where they know they'll lose the challenge anyway. Veto powers work much like collaboration techniques in that sense. Their effectiveness lies in people self editing based on established precedent so as to avoid being vetoed in the first place.
      \n
      \nFor me winding up in a place where I have absolutely no desire to be is a bigger negative than getting to a place I've never been before is a positive. So I much prefer reducing the potential for the latter in order to greatly reduce the possibility of the former. The Hotseat rule takes the opposite view. I suspect it works extremely well for people with priorities flip flopped from mine in that regard.

      \n I think the railroad / crazy train / derailed extended metaphor is a poor way to describe Microscope, because the game is by nature non-linear. It moves in several directions at once, and contains multitudes.

      I've played in Microscope games where the Hot Seat player created something I completely did. not. get. But that was okay -- I could just build elsewhere in the chronology. And my quiet rejection does not prevent the rest of the table from evolving the weird contribution.

      Also, as someone who has difficulty shutting up, I like how the Hot Seat challenges me to shut up. It also challenges me to relax ownership on my contributions to the game, to not plan anything beyond what is happening just this moment. Every Microscope game I've played has achieved storytelling of a far greater magnitude than I could achieve on my own, and I think that the Hot Seat has been key to that.
