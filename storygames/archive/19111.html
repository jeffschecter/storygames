\n I played this last weekend, finally! It was a great session and really got me thinking about playing more of these world-building games where characters and role-playing is not the focus. I guess Microscope is a given next one up.

Anyway, the Contempt Tokens in the game. I really enjoyed how they expressed tension in the community. Most of all, I liked how they blurred the line between fictional tension between community factions, and real life tension between the players around the table arising out of players' decisions.

We had some issues with how to spend the Contempt Tokens. They can be used either to "act selfishly" or to show that tension has gone down due to an action that another player has just taken (I don't have the rules in front of me so I'm paraphrasing). When you use the tokens to act selfishly, are they used in connection with one of the three normal actions (hold a discussion, discover something new, start a project), or is this a new (fourth) kind of action? Different players used it differently and it gave rise to some real, albeit quite amicable, tension around the table.

Happy to hear your thoughts!

Cheers,
Sven

    \n I just posted an AP about the Quiet Year here: http://higs.motd.org/2014/01/10/the-quiet-year-youll-need-a-busload-of-faith-to-get-by/

    In summary, our resource in abundance was 'faith' which was clearly a reflection of some undefined religion. I found there was quite an ebb and flow in contempt tokens. I built up a large stack early and mid game and gave them all away by the time the Frost Shepherds arrived. In my report I muse on how the contempt tokens have a very real effect on play. To me they feel like karma as the term is used in early Buddhist scriptures - a representation of unfulfilled desire, frustration, and existential pain. In that religion when we strip away the ephemeral aspects of character and personality only the karma remains. This goes a long way in my mind to explaining how contempt tokens support rich and dramatic role-playing in a game without characters.

    \n The key purpose of contempt is a method of communication. I know when we play, we have a hard time keeping the table talk to a minimum during people's turns. But really, you're not supposed to talk unless someone starts a discussion. And then you are still limited to a few sentences, perhaps not in the order you'd prefer. Contempt is supposed to be a tool for signaling displeasure (or approval) during another person's turn. So yes, it's a less useful mechanic if people are already expressing their commentary out of turn.

    \n I think that's actually the unspoken focus of the game already: if you want cooperation out of the players, you need their input. You can only get their input by Holding a Discussion. Most players find that to be the least exciting choice: Discovering and Projects are much more immediately gratifying, and that's on purpose. Because they're one-sided. So if you want to make everyone happy, you're gonna have to either hold a LOT of discussions (which takes up valuable turns) or pay close attention to what others seem to want based on what they're Discovering or what Projects they're starting.

    So everyone has to balance this and it makes cooperation and harmony difficult by default. It's subtle and it's why I like the game so much.

    I think that's actually the unspoken focus of the game already: if you want cooperation out of the players, you need their input. You can only get their input by Holding a Discussion.
    \nI like Scrape's thoughts here, maracanda.

    Part of the economy of the game is that it is easy to build up resentment, but it actually costs resources to develop consensus and move beyond oppressive methodologies for building community. And like... there are a finite number of people (so deaths matter) and a finite amount of weeks (so delays matter), and you have to choose how much of your resources are going to be allocated to practice-building versus... like, meat-smoker-building and water-catchment-building.

    The speed at which trust can be built is slower than the speed at which resentment can be built, in real life, in my opinion. And so that's why the mechanics operate in that fashion.

    But also? Try it out! Trust tokens that mirrored and paralleled Contempt tokens sound cool and interesting. I'd love to hear how it works. I'm just giving you my design notes so that you can tinker in full knowledge.
