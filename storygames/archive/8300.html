\n I am now the proud owner of Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium, and it looks like a blast to run and play. There's just one thing bugging me a bit: the typical story structure seems to rely on the party having a confrontation with a group of armed humans somewhere in act II, both to punctuate the investigation with some stabbing, and to wear down the party's resources a bit for their final showdown with the demon. But this means that in practice most adventures have at least one, if not two, military or paramilitary organizations. Feds, cops, cultists, gangs, neo-nazis, terrorists, and the generic syndicate (I love organized crime in stories when it has character and a sense of place/history, like the Barksdales in the Wire, or the Vor in Eastern Promises, but generic mafia like in A History of Violence tends to irritate me). Disciples will run through these groups pretty quickly, especially those that give them a lot of trouble; most will only last a single session. This will quickly make any setting, even a theoretically nice town or city, feel like a place where the rule of law has completely broken down. I also worry that it will detract from the more personal scale of the tragedies demons create. That said, I think armed human opponents are an important part of Dread, for both narrative and mechanical pacing. It seems like the easiest solution is just to find some settings for Dread where the presence of armed factions in a large number of stories feels completely natural. The whole world is being attacked by demons, after all. So, where would you set a Dread game to normalize the paramilitaries/armed groups, and who would they be? Mexico, where drug gangs dump human heads in nightclubs? Somalia, wracked by civil war? Bonus points if you can link to an article or two that helps give a feel for the setting. I'm curious if there is a good part of America to do this in.
\n
\ne.g.
\n
\nAfganistan
\n
\nAfganistan may be a warzone, but it's full of people just trying to live their lives. An hour's drive can take you from a town wracked by graft, corruption and crime, to one chafing under draconian religious law, to one torn between the Taliban and the West. So many flavors of human misery are like a delicious buffet to the demons. Whether the disciples are western troops mistrusted by the people they're trying to save, or Afganis fighting against the enemies of all mankind, they'll have their work cut out for them...
\n
\nFactions - there are far more than one could possibly use:
\n
\n-The Taliban. They once ruled Afganistan, but their subjects were happy to get rid of them. Now they are being welcomed back in many places simply because they maintain the rule of law, but the puritanical Taliban are finding themselves forced to make uncomfortable compromises for the privilege. And where the Taliban are, their Al-Qaeda allies are free to operate.
\n
\n-Warlords and bandits. They get their money from the poppy crop and their arms from us (or at least they used to); the central government is completely unable to control them - will the Islamists have any better luck? Some Warlords run their own islamist groups, while others are more interested in territory and crime.
\n
\n-Tribal militias. They've lived in the harsh countryside for milennia, and are no stranger to fighting. They have no love for the US, the Taliban, or the Pakistani government.
\n
\n-Coalition troops. Their weapons and armor are advanced, but they stick out like sore thumbs. The air force has made many enemies for the men on the ground through destructive bombing campaigns. Their mission is just but they have their hands full just protecting their Afgan allies from assassinations and terror attacks, and sometimes it's hard even to do that.
\n
\n-Foreign fighters. They've come to fight the west in the name of God. The nationalist factions in Afganistan have little interest in more foreigners, though, so they wage their own battles.
\n
\n-The government. By and large the central government is corrupt, impotent, and has little influence outside the capital, but it still has police and soldiers, for good or for ill.
\n
\n(distilled from http://tomdispatch.com/post/175010/anand_gopal_making_sense_of_the_taliban )

    \n Sadly, I think the majority of the planet could easily handle this.

    For Mexico, Richard Grant's travelogue God's Middle Finger is excellent and terrifying source material.

    For the United States, certainly the Mexican border is experiencing accelerating lawlessness. Insane levels of violence in places like Nuevo Laredo and corruption that spill over.

    Anywhere that marijuana is a major cash crop (National Forest lands from Appalachia to the Pacific northwest up to Alaska's Kenai peninsula and Mat-Su valley) or meth is being cooked (everywhere) there will be paranoid pseudo-militias of tweaked-out lunatics or booby-trapping, shotgun-toting farmers.

    \n Just as a side note my Dread FbP campaign takes place in the Wire's Baltimore. I kept some of the show's characters as NPCs. Violent confrontation in Dread doesn't have to come down to neo-nazis equipped with SAWs and Barret rifles.

    Two gun-totting goons in a crowded mall where Disciples aren't supposed to use their powers can quickly become very stressful. Dread Cabal's are usually restricted to one particular area. You can't blow things up in the neighborhood where you buy your groceries (or you can but be ready to face the consequences).

    This is really a matter of how you see the game and the how the Cabal is made.

    However setting the game in a warzone is a nice twist anyway and as Jason points out, they're a dime a dozen...

    \nThis is actually one of the pulls for me to Dread.

    In other emo-style games, where there is a Demon and a bunch of humans being controlled/influenced by it, the players/PCs usually hem and haw about how to take it out with no casualties. Lots of planning, lots of grief if a plan goes awry, etc.

    In Dread, the people being controlled or influenced are usually already scum of the earth. It's not like all the PCs are going to go all psychopath and blow them all away to get to the baddie as if they were zombies. Yet, it's not like the thought of breaking a few eggs is totally a horrid idea and out of the question.

    So the question of "do we break a few eggs for the greater good" becomes more relevant in Dread. In a normal supers game, it's pretty much entirely out of the question, so it's not even a relevant question. It's like those console RPGs that "give" you "choices" like, "Do you say hello to this Major NPC, or do you set him on fire and eat his children?" It's simply stupid given the game, and out of the question.
    And it's not like the game is made up of a bunch of Chaotic Evil baddasses being as badass as possible, killing dudes in the way simply because they can, where the baddie controlling them is just icing on a murder cake.

    It makes that choice relevant, for each character (and player) to answer in their own way, depending on the groups in question as well.

Let's just say that TMWNMTK don't take kindly to high-profile behavior by their agents....
