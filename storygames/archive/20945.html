\n I'm launching a closed beta playtest for a game, and I'm designing a feedback form that my playtesters can fill out.

Does anyone have any advice from their own experiences when gathering feedback on what types of questions or comments they found worked best for them? I know that often specificity is preferable over generic questions, and it's best to avoid leading playtesters to any advice of the "How would you do X?", but more ideas would be appreciated.

    \n I find one of the best types of questions is, "Can you explain X to me?" Finding out what players are taking away from the game in terms of their understanding of it can be a really eye-opening experience: everyone nods and gets on with it around the table, but then you ask them to explain it back to you and it reveals all sorts of assumptions about the design.

    \n Regrettably there's very little in this field we can test empirically. That said...

    First identify the biases, as a gamer who thinks in D&D will find problems where one who thinks in Apocalypse World will not, and your feedback will be meaningless outside that.

    Then ask questions about how well the system achieved a result rather than how easily understood or liked it was, as the former will provide useful design feedback as well as insight into the latter.

    Ultimately it's best to observe as many playtests as possible if only to avoid that additional level of abstraction feedback forms introduce. Because even providing useful feedback requires some degree of designer mindset.

    \n I prefer to talk to people and ask them for feedback so I can drill down more when someone raises an issue. Sheets with a bunch of questions for people to answer have never really been helpful in my case. Unless you are doing it on a suffifient scale, and systematically enough, that you are getting real data points (and most RPG developers don't have the resources or expertise for that). So I advocate a more conversational approach after and during playtests. For an RPG one of the mosts critical questions I've found worth asking is "Did anything seem inconsistent". This is mainly for scenarios and setting but that can be hugely helpful at finding issues.

    Ultimately, I'd say your best bet is to playtest, playtest, playtest and to run the thing in live ongoing campaigns. Without the latter, you risk creating a game that works great for one-shots but might have issues that only tend to come up over months of table time (and I can still guarantee you will miss things).

    Also, take notes. Always take notes.

    \n As an alternative to lengthy, large-scale playtesting (which, of course, would be ideal, in some senses, but is rarely practical), consider the people you know and what kind of feedback they tend to give.

    Some people are really good at it; some people are not. I think identifying just a handful of friends or colleagues who are great at finding mathematical problems, editing issues, or playtesting and reporting on that playtesting could save you a lot of time and trouble. Perhaps it's worth buying those people dinner or taking them out for a drink - or offering to do the same in return (read over and/or play their games) - in exchange for some small-scale but potentially really useful feedback.

    Locate the people you trust to do a good job, and reach out to them.

    \n After more consideration I think the most important guideline it this: Focus on meeting the needs of a single target audience and then ask questions about how well your system met those.

    For example, if you're targeting competitive players, ask questions like "Did system X give you the tools necessary to accurately assess the difficulty of your actions?". And if you're targeting experiential players, ask questions like "Did system X give you adequate control over the intensity of the situation?".

    All game design questions need to be leading questions, as that's how you'll find out if the players got to where you wanted them to go.

    \n@kyleweems, it really depends on what you hope to learn. Here are some example questions (inspired by Ron Edwards' Doctor Xaos feedback sheet), some of which probably won't apply to your game:

    - Are the play materials, especially character sheets, easy to use?
    - Do both sides have a fighting chance at all times?
    - I haven't tested the rules for large (6+) or small (3-) groups -- how did those work?
    - Did people understand how you progress and level up?
    - Did people get invested in the Nemesis?
    - Did anyone become unsure about or stumble over a major rules or fictional concept?
    - Did you enjoy making up the Nemesis on your own, or would you rather have a list of them to choose from?

    it really depends on what you hope to learn.
    \nBut isn't that whether or not your game achieved what you intended?

    Ron's questions are a great example, as they're all about elements he either wants the game to be about or not. It's obvious he wants things to be about the Nemesis, but it's also obvious he does not want things to be about how complicated the character sheets are. But you have to have some idea of your game's goals in order to ask those questions, and the clearer that idea is, the better the questions you can ask.

    Another helpful technique is too write feedback questions for existing games just to stretch those brain muscles. For example, what kind of questions would you ask about GURPS to determine if it works as intended? After all, many find engaging with the character creation system to be fun in itself, but did the designer ever ask about that, or simply assume it was necessary for a universal system?
