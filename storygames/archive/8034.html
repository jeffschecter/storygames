\n So, I've been following this thread on RPG.net (which I heartily recommend avoiding, since it's over 400 posts and very little of it is anything new), and something occurred to me:

It seems like the indie RPG community (setting aside terminology baggage) has largely stopped doing stuff with theory. There was that whole blog diaspora thing, but since then the pursuit of theory seems to have petered out.

I don't know if this is a good thing or not, or if my perceptions have anything to do with reality. What do you think?

    \n Theory is not dead, it's hiding. And that's only half in jest - for example, I haven't had any major hankering for taking my normative theory (as opposed to the non-systematic stuff we do at the Forge every day) public after the Forge theory forums closed down, simply because the alternative forums are full of attitudes founded on ignorance and identity politics. What would the point of doing theory here, for instance, be, when the only interested voices would be those demanding me to justify my temerity in daring to engage in a practice both harmful and foul, galling in requiring one to research prior discourse to understand the proceedings, and one with no practical value to boot.

    Or that's my impression, anyway. Discourse is a fragile thing, easily discouraged, especially when there are so many other worthwhile things to be done. I have little difficulty with sharing my theory thinking with my peers in private, after all, so there's little reason to do it in public. The public has plenty of theory to chew on as it is.

    \n I think that "theory" meaning GNS is pretty well dead after Brain Damage. That doesn't mean it isn't useful to people or that it still won't feature in a lot of the conversation (amongst people who were active in 2005, at least)--but theory used to be this terribly hard to understand controversial thing that promised deep insights and was (at the time) very vigorously defended on the Internet as the key to understanding gaming.

    It's my observation that after BD it was a lot less mysterious and a lot harder to defend in--well, just about anywhere. I think one of the reasons it was shut down on The Forge is that it was getting in the way of actually making games (this is my perception but look at the 2005 now-forever front page thread about "what is Sim" for a prime example of what I'm talking about).

    So, yes, I think it's "dead" but the work--the real work--goes on more profitably without it.

    (and there are other theories and some valuable concepts that are, of course, still in use--but not the single iconic idea of a grand, unified, GNS).

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonIf you're looking for big-T Theory written out in long articles... honestly, that's hardly ever where the most interesting stuff happens anyway.
    \nYeah, that's kinda what I think too.

    Also, check out Levi's stuff (like The Big Muddle). Its detail is right at the edge of what I find tolerable to read about gaming without actually gaming, and it directly ties everything to play. It's really good stuff, even if at times a lot of it seems like, "Well, duh!"
    The only reason we're not all talking about it all the time is because Levi hasn't acted like a prima donna/shitbag online yet. Once he starts throwing tantrums and spewing ridiculous shit, people will pay more attention to his cool essays, I think.

    Levi's pretty much the central theorist regarding RPGs in the past two years. His shit is sensible, and always can point to behaviors seen at the table without having to determine with the terms he is using mean first before holding a discussion.

    The important elements of theory for me are the pieces that you can take and apply directly to gaming. "If you do X, Y may result". "If you make relationship maps, you may gain these benefits at the table. Also, here's how to make them, implement them, make them fun, etc".
    These pieces are small, and not large. Which is why they lack the "bigness" of a unifying theory, those theories that draw discussion like mass into a black hole, most of it involving understanding what the theory is trying to say (and getting wrong/right, mostly on the accounts of its vagueness). Like all the GNS stuff.

    Reasoned critique of games, rules, gameplay style and the like aren't going anywhere. Just the tools that those few who dig into it use are changing, and so are the means of using those tools (more Amagi Games mini-essays, blogs, and S-G "play advice" posts; less "let's brawl over ideas like back on Usenet").

    Hopefully, the desire to make universal theories will be pushed back in favor of more small theories that look at specific realms of activity, which prove more pragmatic/useful in the end. Also, hopefully I'll get a pegasus for Christmas this year. :-)

    -Andy

      And certainly your argument is a credible one, I just personally think that what happened is that a group of gamers (let's call them narrativists, for want of a better term) were poorly served by existing games. A theory, and there were plenty before the Forge and some as well developed (GDS), helped people identify what they were missing, they went out and made games inspired by that theory and there was an explosion of creativity that is now slowing (and that may be natural as you note).

      That's one view on what happened, others are certainly viable and yours is among those viable views, it's just not mine. I think the theory was wrong, but contained a truth, that there is a group of gamers who want stuff that nar games deliver, and I think previous theories ignored those folk. GNS in my view ignored other folk, but those other folk were already being served, so it didn't matter that much.

      But when I said, in the quoted section, that theory might have served its purpose what I was trying to get at was that the advancement now comes through the games themselves, that games are how advances are now made. I supported the Forge closing its theory forums, I thought then and think now that their useful life had passed, and the time had come to develop theory (if one develops it at all) as a part of game development, not something separate to it. Basically, I was agreeing with your first para.

      \n On the academic side of things: I've written (or co-authored) a total of six formal rpg theory articles this year. Some for academic, some for non-academic publication channels. Several are still in peer review, hopefully appearing next year. Many other people are doing the same. At least half a dozen highly suitable ludological journals exist, plus a whole bunch of others may take such texts. And then there's the Knutepunkt book, etc. Journals do exist for those who go look for them.
      \n
      \nAnd on the empirics side: I ran a project of creating test games for several major larp theories, 2004-2007, and published the results. While design-oriented (and in my view too often still bound in obsolete assumptions - as was much of the Nordic larp theory I tested), a lot of the theory-based work on the Forge is doing likewise: actually testing ideas.
      \n
      \nThe best role-playing game I've seen in years, "Stalker" by Ville Vuorela (not available in English yet, sorry) is a product of _reverse_ role-playing theory: The author seems to hate formal rpg theory material so much that he ended up refining an implicit, unexplicated design theory of its own as a response, and used it to create one hell of a role-playing game. So there's new theory appearing all the time - sometimes in highly formal, academic circles, at other times as hidden parts of successful games. But by no means is it dead.
