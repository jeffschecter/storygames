\n I'm thinking about how to organize and present the book/physical product of my longest-in-development game, Mage Blade. I'm actually jumping the gun by a long mile with this, at least somewhat. I'm still struggling with trying to figure out how the character creation works, and the magic system, two incredibly important parts of the game. But all the same, getting an idea of how to organize the book can't hurt. When it comes time to write it, I don't want to be making this up as I go along.

This is an issue I think maybe I'm insecure about.. I've obsessed about this in both of the other games I've completely written (which isn't to say they're completely done; Both are in dire need of heavy playtesting), but I think it's important. A good layout and organized structure allows for the book to be easily used as a reference during play, as well as making it easier to learn the game by presenting the information in an easily understood fashion.

Now, I sort of consider 4 published game systems to be the peers/siblings/competition for Mage Blade; They are D&D 3.5, Vampire: the Masquerade, Shadowrun, and The Riddle of Steel. I feel this way because I've either been influenced by these games in my gaming career in some significant fashion, or because I feel that Mage Blade has a very similar target audience. -Is it easy to read and reference?
-Does it present the information in an easily grasped way?
-Aside from the organization of the chapters, what do these books do right and wrong (in terms of layout and presentation)?

For those books you may not be familiar with, I'm perfectly fine with impressions based just off the chapter lists here.

-Are things that seem like they should be grouped together separate, or things that should be separate seem lumped together?

Help a guy out!

    \n Okay, first, I suggest you stop saying "layout and structure" and start thinking "structure and layout." Structure comes first, way first, and layout later. You are not, by any means, thinking about structure too soon or jumping the gun. Thinking about layout (fonts, weights of headers, spacing on the page) at this point is... not jumping the gun, but it's... daydreaming, at best.

    Second, take a look at How to Write an RPG, a post on my blog that basically says everything that I would retype here. It's a good idea to start thinking structure at this point -- even if you can't quite outline the whole thing yet.

    I would caution against trying to use other books' tables of contents as a sort of 'checklist' for what you 'need' in your book. What your book needs is determined by what your design needs, nothing else.

    \n Josh,

    Thanks for that post. It's good advice, and I'll probably take it.

    On the other hand, I will take your caution, and ask anyway.. Is anyone willing to reply to the questions asked here? If anything, it'll be an idea of what not to do, when it comes time to organize and layout.

    Also, for the record, when I'm thinking of layout, I'm also thinking about ease of use things, like the little chapter tabs on the edges of the D&D pages, or even better the tabs on the edges of the TRoS book that tell you what game concepts are being discussed on that page. Things like which font at which size to use, spacing, etc. aren't really what I'm worried about. Things like header boxes, placement of page numbers, diagrams, charts and tables, sidebars.. All of that I'm interested in. I enjoy thinking about layout by itself, so when I'm stuck on writing or designing the actual game, I can feel that I'm making some sort of progress by working on logos, diagrams and layout.
