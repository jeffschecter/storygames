\n I just put up a post about mixing Microscope and old-school dungeon crawls:

ars ludi: The Dungeon Ouroboros\n
Dungeons loom large in the mental landscape of role-playing games. They are inescapably linked to its very roots. No matter how much we embrace story and issues and explorations of the human condition, sometimes it seems like marching down hallways and listening at doors is in our blood. Just try shouting \u201cwho wants to play an old-school dungeon crawl?\u201d in a crowded room and see what happens. Stampede.
\u2026
But instead of just using Microscope to create the setting and then switching to a \u201cnormal\u201d game, what if you went back and forth? Play a session of Microscope to explore the history of your dungeon, then play a session adventuring in the dungeon, then play more Microscope to reveal more detail, then adventure more, then history more, ad infinitum.
\nI think it'd be pretty fascinating, because you could scratch the basic "march down hallways, kick in doors" itch but inform it with all sorts of game world meaning and context that players normally wouldn't see.

And yes, my example includes an ancient empire of intelligent oozes, because I so want to play in that game.

    \n Hey Shinobi, great questions!

    You mean like a West Marches dungeon crawl? That would totally work. I think you could have different rosters of players in the dungeon crawl, though generally it's harder to add people part way through Microscope games. You could even have different dungeon crawl parties and then mix them (or just their duly elected representatives) to play Microscope together. Everyone wouldn't have to be in the Microscope sessions, but they would be having a different experience. Players who were in the Microscope sessions would obviously have a much richer sense of the dungeon's background.

    I was envisioning the entire Microscope history ending before the current adventuring era. Keep it all in the past. Plus the scope of the history is the dungeon itself, not the wider world (unless you want to do that). If I'm reading you correctly, 3 would be moot because the Microscope history would all be in the past.

    \n Jackson, listen to me carefully when I say that. would. be. awesome!

    Totally different than what I was thinking, much as Shinobi also came up with a totally different angle than I envisioned (including the dungeon crawling present within the Microscope history).

    I think this shows that there are a whole spectrum of ways you can blend Microscope with "now" games: use Microscope to build setting, use it as overarching and encapsulating structure (e.g. playing a different game in place of scenes), use it to revisit and flesh out background (like the Dungeon Ouroboros idea). If you think of the two games as sitting on the scales, the Dungeon Ouroboros is D&D-heavy, whereas yours is much more Microscope.

    In fact that just made me think of another crazy inversion: playing a normal "in the moment" game about people's lives (say, PTA) then diving in and creating the history of their lives within those scenes. You're talking about that time in college in the "normal" scene, you play the harp music and fade, then go make some Microscope history to find out what college was like\u2026 then fade right back into the scene. Okay, now I might be going too far.
