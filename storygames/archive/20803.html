    \n It's not too hard to add a sixth character to Lady Blackbird - if you look around online, you'll see some other people's attempts to do so.

    However, I think six players is getting to be a LOT. Both story-wise - how many meaningfully separate character roles can we get into a smuggling ship? - and in terms of table dynamics. If the players are OK with hardly getting any screen time, then no problem. But I find that 5 players is more-or-less at the edge of practicality.

    Musette, or its more complex version, Muse, works fine for 7 players. (Again, so long as you accept that you won't get to talk as often, it's no problem; however, since you are not limited to a single character in those games, it's not as tight a constraint as in an RPG like Lady Blackbird.)

    Another option, if using a GM-ed game, is to have 2 "co-GMs". This requires some trust, and, ideally, someone you already know well, but lots of people have had success. For instance, depending on the game, one "GM" can be in charge of framing scenes, narrating stuff, acting out NPC characters, and so on, while the other handles all the rules and rolls dice. In a game which is lighter on the mechanical bits, one GM could be in charge of scene framing and narrating details while the other plays all the NPCs.

    \n If you have two groups, there are two ideas in the Fiasco Companion on p. 42.

    One of them is where you have the two games reference each other. You have a shared playset and then split into groups. As you setup, you use the other groups Elements to inform your character creation. Create connections like family connections between the two groups. Then as you play, keep each other informed so events can cross over.

    Doing something like this can make it feel like one game when it's really two.

    Note I have never done this, only read about it. Maybe someone here has and can talk about how it works.

    \n Some games I think I've played with about 7 people and do work that way are...\n
    • Mist-robed Gate by Shreyas Sampat. In my opinion, a great game. But not necessarily the easiest game to explain, facilitate, or play.
      • \n
    • Dread (the Jenga one from The Impossible Dream). Horror game with a very "traditional" storyteller GM role, can easily take that many people 'cause they're just more prospective casualties. I've never played it in the intended horror setting, though - only with a WWI scenario of my own devising. Requires preparing a scenario (though the book has a couple).
      • \n
    • Some or some other version of Geiger Counter, by Jonathan Walton. Again, horror movie, people are going to die fast, etc., but no GM role here. But it's been a long time since I've last played it, and I'm really hazy on the details.
      • \n
    • Enter the Avenger is also supposed to work for that many people, despite 5 being optimal. 7 players means 2 people are going to sit out a round as pure audience, waiting for their turn. It's slightly trickier than that, though, because some of the roles give a much smaller contribution than others: thus, success depends on how entertaining the Avenger is, everybody taking care that chapters are kept as brief as possible, and everybody actually enjoying some audience time. I would never recommend 8, but 7 is feasible insofar that the people sitting out the first chapter are then getting some spotlight as the Narrator and Suspect.
    \nEdited to add: I'm pretty sure I've successfully run a few other games as a GM with 6 other players, it's not that crazy a number. Poison'd has to be one. And Fables of Camelot, for sure!

    \n I have some design history with story games for big numbers. Zombie Cinema scales better than (the somewhat similar) Fiasco, and of course Fables of Camelot takes up to 8-10 players routinely. In fact, it seems I mostly make games that don't choke much - even Fellows of the Julenius Archive works fine with a big group.

    In general you'll want to look for games that enable hanger-ons (like Fables of Camelot) - odds are that the big group consists of some people who'll be happy to stay in the background anyway, so as long as the game fluidly enables this, the mere fact of having many players doesn't need to mean a huge traffic jam. Another thing that helps is enabling multi-channel play (like Julenius), where players can do multiple things at once, so the group can benefit from having enough people to engage in two or three things at once. A third quality you'll want is when the game is built around group dynamics and cooperative conflicts, so it can easily be 4-vs-4 instead of 1-vs-1 (like Zombie Cinema). The theoretical ideal story game for big groups would have all three of those capabilities, of course

    \n I've had some success running The Shab-al-Hiri Roach with large groups. I had ten players at the table once. It's GMless, IIRC, but I sat out as a player and just facilitated and kept things moving.

    You can generally add a sort of GM role to any GMless game, you know, and that helps when you have more than 5 players. The problem with large groups is spotlight time. You can only slice up your 3-4 hours of play time so many ways before the pie slices look like slivers. As GM, aggressively drag players into scenes that they weren't involved in. Any scene with just 1-2 people in it can probably be expanded to fit another player or two.
