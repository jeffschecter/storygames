    \n I'm building an (over the top) PDF of my hack of Anima Prime to the setting of Exalted for use just within my gaming group. Because of the small audience, I went with "landscape in the ratio of MacBook screens" and use the thing in full screen mode.

    Generally, though, I think two choices are about equivalent:

    Large (e.g. 8.5" x 11") landscape. This looks good on laptop screens, works on tablets (assuming they rotate to landscape, which, if they can't, you wasted your money) and can be printed well (though you'll likely forget the "bind on short edge" setting at least once).

    Smaller (e.g. 5"x7") portrait. This looks really good on tablets, and also works well in "two-page side-by-side" mode on laptops. Not as good for printing, but you can convince Acrobat to print in multiple pages per paper mode, so it usually works OK.

    Avoid: if you have a smaller portrait book layout, do not produce a PDF in landscape with two pages displayed per PDF page. This is really fucking irritating. As a user, I can take a "single page per PDF page" file and make it display to me two book pages at a time. I cannot take a "two pages displayed per PDF page" and make it display to me a single book page at a time, because there is no "display half a PDF page" setting. The "two pages displayed per PDF page" is especially annoying if you want to print isolated pages or page ranges.

    \n There is a lag time between when your eyes get to the end of a line and when they pick up the next line. It's small but it adds up. Minimizing that lag time while keeping the mental load of each line in mind is important. That lag time gives your brain space to process. The study doesn't say why people like shorter lines, it just says that people reported they liked it better. It might be that it affords more time to process the text but that wasn't tested for. I can't off hand remember, but I'm reasonably sure they tested for retention.

    \n Isn't this, like, a solved thing? Journal articles have been distributed as PDFs in the academic world way more extensively than games. And before that, people photocopied books. That's pretty much the standard format for distributing text, yeah? And it nicely aligns with exactly what Joe said, plus it avoids Andy's thing about having to do the entire layout twice.
