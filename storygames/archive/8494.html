\n from the AP, jan 13- One Israeli military officer told The Associated Press that Hamas fighters often operate in small groups of up to four and have largely refrained from confronting Israeli troops at close range.

"Their strategy has mainly been to use lots of booby-traps, shooting guns and missiles from afar," the Israeli officer said on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak to the media.

"Soldiers are taking lots of precautions, they are being more careful than the army has ever been before in any war," he said. "Soldiers shoot at anything suspicious, use lots of firepower, and blast holes through walls to move around."

idf combat tactics in the west bank & lebanon was one of the inspirations for my game walking through walls which is sadly relevant again.

we actually playtested it earlier this year, set in iraq, during a stage when I wasn't posting. Wasn't bad - cutting down the strings to get to trigger points was an ok pacing mechanic. The scenes where the army/ insurgents entered the house were the best becasue they triggered lots of consequences. The next version will have a map of a dwelling on one side of sheet, with a grid of characters on the other, and the string pulled in and out between the two sides will help determine the next scene participants.     \nPoint taken.

    Back to the OP: To my knowledge, D&D 4e has great tactics for maneuvering around obstacles and the like (as my Unaligned Warlord Babe can attest to), but nothing for burrowing/tunneling through them (like a wall: You may be able to blink/move 3 squares, but nothing about blowing a hole in an X sqaure area; unless it's a Wizard ability I haven't seen yet). maybe that's an idea for a new class, a kind of Geomancer like in the Disgaea games (people who are kinda weak in battle, but can totally shift around the battlefield with geo-magic).

    A DM could always set up the potential for that sort of thing, like "There are barrels of oil here; hit them with fire for DC 10 with attack or skill, and they blow up these two wall segments", or a spot-ruling thing like "this here is a breakable wall; AC 15 and 10HP but only from magical attacks". But there's nothing in the rules that really encourage that sort of thing. Might be cool to set up for a single encoutner/scenario tho, like going through a Giant Ant Lair or something.

    IIRC, though, the tactical part of Empire of Dust has a given rule, "All walls can be set on fire/destroyed". So if you drop a wall on the mat, it in fact can be blown through; a valid tactic to use. That's probably the closest game I know of that lets you do that sort of thing, because it's coded in the rules (so it's expected), and it's for a tactical battle portion of the game.

    \n I am not familiar with D&D4;, but if I recall, in 3.x all materials have a hardness rating, which implies that they can be blown through. I think the general assumption is that unless it's a door or some such, you just don't try to blow through walls. Walls are there to guide you to the encounters you're supposed to get to. With a more free-form or sandbox adventure, with this feature emphasized, players will attempt to go through walls more often.

    \nAs a matter of fact, I believe in the Eberron Campaign book, the adventure in the back takes into account the possibility that the players may try to tunnel through a wall, at a particular part.
