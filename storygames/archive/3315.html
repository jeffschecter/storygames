\n My first thought was about how cool this thing is going to be. My second thought was about how screwed I am. Setting type takes a long time.

Here's day one's output. It's not perfectly aligned because the header and the body copy are tied up separately.



This is a rubbing showing basic layout of the type. What's going on here is I'm using a two column layout in a five inch square. This leaves me with columns that are about 2 1/4" wide. The first page will have the title up top spanning both columns. All the text in that column? That's the first paragraph.

Bacchanal is two or three hours of GM-less roleplaying for two to six players. Characters are non-residents of Puteoli fleeing accusations of infractions against the Empire. Gameplay follows the characters through events of wine, debauchery, and the cruel sporting of gods, as they struggle to evade soldiers, and accusers, and reunite with a companion from whom they have been separated.

Holy heck, thought I. This six or eight page thing is going to be a hundred pages long. It's going to make Burning Empires look like one of my daughter's Sesame Street books. Holy heck.

After looking at how much type fits on the page, I'm estimating sixteen pages in this thing. However, that's not just sixteen pages I have to lay out. Because of the small font selection we have here, even with using a font with which we have a lot of, I was running low on certain key letters and spacing material by the end of that first column. I expect the printing process to look like this: lay out the left column, print the left column, clean and break all the type down. lay out the right column, print the right column, clean and break all the type down. So if I start with printing page 1 and there's sixteen pages, that's really 32 different prints. Not only that, but if I want to have 24 copies when I'm done, I'm going to have to start with about 40 copies of page 1, because I know there's going to be mistakes as the process goes on.

So, I see a lot of type and time in my future. This is going to be so cool.

Pictures of the press and the basic layout next time.

    \n Photos from last night. I'm still printing my first "get used to the press" work, so not much more progress has been made on the project except for getting a better idea for how many pages will be in the final work. It looks to come out to around twelve. We also had a short class yesterday, spending time at the Special Collections building where I was able to thumb through a book that was over six centuries old. During the lecture I sat in front of a book worth four thousand dollars.

    Here's the Chandler & Price I'll be working on. Behind it is the Vandercook I'll be using for some blind embossing, but it's not as sexy as the C&P. Note the extra flywheel on the right. Most images of C&P presses have only one flywheel.



    Here's a close up of the type I laid out on Monday, so you can compare to the rubbing above. Those long metal strips are called leading -- that's pronounced like the metal, not like someone that you're following. This is where the term leading comes from when laying out type in graphics programs. Everyone I know who talks about leading always mispronounces it.



    This is the galley cabinet. When used to create books, each page would be laid out in a metal galley tray and placed in cabinets like this for storage. Modern day printers still refer to individual pages as galleys, even if they're using desktop publishing programs. Being a big dork, I'm storing my Bacchanal type in galley 69.



    Other etymology bits of interest I've come across during this course include the history of the words "uppercase" and "lowercase". Uppercase letters aren't called that because they're larger than lowercase letters. When the different cases were stored in actual different case drawers, the drawer with the capital letters would be positioned above the drawer with the lowercase letters. To pick out a capital letter, you'd grab type from the upper case. Non-capital letters came from the lower case.

    We're using California Job Cases, which appear to be the standard American layout. It's the QWERTY of type drawers. The things between the t and a are spacers used for between words. Once you start going with laying out type, the job case is very intuitive.

    \n The flywheel on the right side of the press was used to hold a belt that attached to a mechanical... um... wheel turner thing. It was used to make this type of press into an automatic/motorized press. Now it's just used for extra weight, allowing students to slow the press down easier. The press is started by spinning the wheel on the left towards you and pumping on the foot pedal in the center of the machine's front. (Unfortunately cut out of the above image.) Once the press has been started, the foot pumping keeps the press going.

    An image from last week, with the first half of page one laid out in type, resting in a galley. On the right is my printout of the game along with some test strips from staining paper. The wine-colored one was stained with a Shiraz.




    From tonight, I've printed the first half of page two. Here it is next to the first page. The smudging you see on the page is from the tea stain on the sheets and a quick leveling job in Photoshop. These are proof sheets and contain some typos and upside down type. Things like the upside down lowercase s or o are hard to spot until printed. Also, the u and n sections in the job case are right next to each other, so when type gets put away, you'll find you're spelling "snrrounding" instead of "surrounding", even though you pulled the type from the correct location. The pages are supposed to be distressed as you progress through the rulebook. This page two hasn't been distressed yet.

    I printed this half of page two this evening. I'm getting faster with laying out type -- the second half was laid out and ready to be printed, but I finished up about fifteen minutes too late to get it printed. I'll probably get this printed tomorrow and layout the next page -- I'm still waiting on my shipment of paper to arrive, so I probably won't get a chance to print page three tomorrow.

    \n The house smells of wine as pages are dyed.

    We've come to the end of the class and will have six pages printed, which takes the game up through the first part of Scene Resolution. The end of page 6 will have the description of Pluto as the high die. Venus, Wine, and Satyrs should fit on page 7; Bacchus, Accuser, and Soldier on page 8; the last bit of Soldier, Companion, and Minerva on page 9; the last bit of Minerva, The Cup Runneth Dry, and A Beam in the Eye of God should finish up page 10. A Colophon would follow for page 11. These last five pages may be done during the summer as I may have access to another letterpress. However, I do not know if they have the required typefaces. If there's a delay, I'm retaking the class in the fall, so these should be finished rather quickly once classes resume.

    I'll be posting more photos of the project over the next few days and keep this thread updated with, um, updates.
