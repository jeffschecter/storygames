\n I always thought pistol crossbows were an extraordinarily lame D&D 3.0 affectation until I saw a pair of them, one in the Correr museum and one in the Doge's palace in Venice. Also of note:

An eleven-barrel flintlock rifle! (This looked like it weighed about 100 pounds, but 100 badass pounds)

A suit of armor with a reliquary built into the right knee! (A glass compartment for a saint's knucklebone!)

A big key that shoots a spring-loaded poison dart! (This was just cool)

A little suitcase that houses a pair of matchlock gun barrels facing opposite directions! (So you can murder a guy and kill yourself at the same time I guess. Also, your luggage would be smoking.)

Each of these things sounds like something my friend Joel would make up to equip his character with, just to irritate me. What insane real things have you seen that belong in the unstable fiction of your best gaming moments?

    I hope we don't have to limit ourselves to just equipment items--here are a few of my favorites:

    A quirky inventor suggested that the British might gain the strategic advantage in WWII by building colossal aircraft carriers out of icebergs permeated with frozen sawdust. The resulting substance (Pykrete) creates an incredibly durable, unsinkable floating military base that will stay frozen for surprisingly long even in warm waters.

    There's a strange and exceedingly rare gemstone in Tutankhamen's crown. Archaeologists were mystified as to the origin of these stones, until they were traced to fused desert glass found only at the site of an ancient asteroid impact in the Sahara desert.

    The Antikythera Mechanism, found in a wreck in the Mediterranean, is an ancient Greek device which was essentially a clockwork computer for calculating planetary movements made by a master craftsman in ancient times. According to Cicero, Archimedes had one, and there may have been others in the classical world.

    Pope Stephen VII had his predecessor\u2019s court dug up, dressed in clerical robes, and put on trial for heresy. It was found guilty.

    Slobodan Milosevic's daughter Marija Milosevic hired security guards to protect her father's body from vampire hunters.

    b) not a general purpose computer, it was an automated calculator which produced tables of fixed polynomial functions (typically logarithms)
    c) instances of it were built in Babbage's time.
