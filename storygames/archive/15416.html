\n I'm working on a game that has a pretty good chunk of necessary background. There's enough stuff that I can't really build it into the rules discussion, and it is completely fictional. There needs to be a section that lays out the world, the society, the way things operate - no more than half a dozen pages. This is sort of new to me and I'm debating various approaches to delivering it.

Approach one: Info dump. Just the facts, one after the other.

Approach two: Encyclopedic info dump. Just the facts, but colored like an in-setting information source.

Approach three: Snippets from various documents. A trial transcript, a newspaper clipping, a travel brochure.

Approach four: First person oral history. Some dude guides you through his life and world.

Approach five: Footnotes. As background terms and concepts are raised in the rules, there's explanatory text in a sidebar or at the bottom of the page.

I'm interested on which you prefer and why, which you have seen accomplished successfully (and in which games), and if I am overlooking other options.

    \n For a GMless game, I want:

    "Approach one: Info dump. Just the facts, one after the other."

    In the form of a handout for all the players.

    Why? I find without reference important information is often missed in casual conversation. The person who read the game often has a lot to explain (including the rules) that it becomes easy to forget.

    And:

    Approach three: Snippets from various documents. A trial transcript, a newspaper clipping, a travel brochure.
    OR:
    Approach four: First person oral history. Some dude guides you through his life and world.

    In the book itself for the person reading the game. Which one depends on which better fits the mood / tone / feel of the setting.

    And I would also like:

    Approach five: Footnotes. As background terms and concepts are raised in the rules, there's explanatory text in a sidebar or at the bottom of the page.

    A briefer version of the Footnotes in the rules as a reminder why the specific rules are important to the setting as the rules come up.

    \n Jason, how are the players going to bring this content to the table? You might want to make that explicit in the text.

    -- Are they going to read it aloud to each other before play begins?
    -- Are they supposed to have read it individually before they arrive for the first session? (If so, should be freely available online, right? Since not everyone will have a copy of the game.)
    -- Are they supposed to have read it individually before they decide they want to play the game in the first place?

    I would tailor your color to the means of transmission, to make it as useful as possible.
