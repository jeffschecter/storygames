\n So let's say you're playing in a game where everyone has certain GM-like responsibilities at certain times. Like, when it's my turn, you can take my past Achievements, threaten them, and try to get me to spend my resources on those threats rather than the Goal of my turn. If you succeed in getting me to spend, then you've done well, and you earn a point. But there's no strategy involved; the point is just a reward for narrative performance. You did a good job coming up with a cool threat that my character cares about!

What would you want to spend those points on? What would be worthy? What would feel like satisfying recognition that you are in fact awesome at providing situation-fodder for other people's characters?

Context: multi-protagonist game, protagonists don't significantly interact, game takes turns alternating between protagonists, each player has one protagonist, main thing protagonists do is pursue, achieve, and sweat about their needs and aspirations.

    Give me power to insert interlude scenes between the regular turns.

    \n Two other ideas:

    1. As you know from Land of Nodd, you can easily use a currency like this to create more screen time AND better odds of success for your own character. That seems like a good driver (or I wouldn't have built it in, of course).

    2. Some special "unlockables", some kind of achievements you could not otherwise have access to.

    If it's about your character (as in #1), that could mean giving your character a shot at some future or some achievement that would otherwise be impossible. Maybe your character can only have a chance to drive away the Dragon threatening the kingdom... but if you had 10 GM Points, you could go on a Quest to get the Sword of Dragon-Slaying, and then your character could actually have a chance to kill the Dragon (or become King, or whatever).

    If it's more about GMing, then maybe having a certain number of points gives you access to some special aspect of the fiction. For example, if the game is like Dogs in the Vineyard, maybe we each make Town-like situations for each others' characters. But once I earn 10 GM Points, I am suddenly allowed to bring supernatural elements (sorcery, demons) into play when I'm GMing, making my Towns more intense than everyone else's.

    So, we all play looking forward to the introduction of whatever this thing that can be "unlocked", whether it's the player or the GM who does so.

    (Heck, maybe it's even a competition, where the first GM to get 10 GM Points can now try to kill the other players' characters! So we play, and I hope I can earn enough GM points so I can kill off your character before you can get the same 10 Points and try to kill mine in my story.)

    \n David, I think you're getting some good stuff from a lot of people here, but it's along different channels. Not to over-complicate things, but you may want to provide different trees of options here.

    People are motivated to provide good opposition, or really colorful attention getting descriptors, for different reasons. Categorize those GM personas into trees, and let them gather up points for their own special moves.

    For instance, some people GM because they like stitching other people together and fading in to the background. Allowing them to override someone else's decision might be the best benefit there, because they can "see something others can't" and they want to push things in a fruitful direction.

    Some of them just love grinding other people hard and forcing them into tough situations. Allowing them to rewind a few sentences and re-spin what someone else described, or tack on additional constraints, might be their best benefit.

    Some of them like making things into comptetition, so allowing them to nuke another player's character (or their holdings) seems like a great reward.

    It shouldn't be too specific, and certainly some cross-overs in powers will occur. You could even allow people to buy stuff from someone else's list for an additional cost. But if Jake is over there being dark and brooding and mysterious, and I reward him for doing that, it's probably a good sign that I want him to continue being dark and brooding and mysterious while he spends those points, and not random and violent.

    I may be overthinking this, but thems my two cents.

    My first thought was "closed loop" - I'd personally rather get a reward that makes GMing intrinsically more fun than get a reward that makes "my guy" more fun. Keep the reward cycle tight, and I'd be much more likely to actually get the hint. Also: instant gratification if I can use it right away.

    So: what does a GM really want?

    1) recognition for creative effort.
    2) increased narrative control.
    3) increased setting control.
    4) the opportunity to provide an interesting challenge for the players.

    1 is mostly taken care of.

    What if you could spend GM points to... well... keep GMing? You spend a GM-point to set the next scene. You spend a GM-point to introduce an NPC. This is dangerously Universalis territory...

    Posted By: Zachary DonovanI'd personally rather get a reward that makes GMing intrinsically more fun than get a reward that makes "my guy" more fun.
    That's actually my first choice as well.

    As for increased time as GM, I would like the idea, but I think it often comes at the expense of someone else's GM time, and that makes me worry that now it's a competition where if I get out-GMed I lose my chance to be a cool GM and get rewarded for it. I'm certainly not ruling the idea out! I'm just more excited by other rewards right now.

    I definitely like the ideas about increasing what a GM can do with the time they have. Which I guess relies on a starting point where there are certain things you can't do. As Paul said, "unlockables". I'm seeing two major types thus far:

    1. Unlockable Fictional Content

    Zachary, I like your examples!
    - more power to present challenges
    - more power to define and control setting
    - more power to define and control NPCs
    - more power to frame your scene

    These are things I'm used to GMs controlling from the get-go, but in a multi-GM game, I supposed certain things could start as off-limits. The key would be to define those things in such a way that a GM's creativity isn't hamstrung without them. Maybe they should be very specific, but also very high-impact?\n
    Posted By: Paul T.For example, if the game is like Dogs in the Vineyard, maybe we each make Town-like situations for each others' characters. But once I earn 10 GM Points, I am suddenly allowed to bring supernatural elements (sorcery, demons) into play when I'm GMing, making my Towns more intense than everyone else's.

    Fantastic. Brainstorming some more:
    - killing important NPCs
    - establishing global conditions like national power balance, level of magic, presence of super-science
    - setting a scene on a protagonist's "home turf" where all their valuables and family are at risk

    2. Unlockable Fiction-Establishing Techniques

    Most of Arnold's (Harlequin) ideas fall under this category. These are adjustments to the process of establishing what happens.

    Ideas:
    - insert off-screen content when it's not your turn to GM
    - say no to proposed or narrated occurrences and NPC actions
    - briefly control someone else's protagonist for a specific purpose
    - author other protagonists' dreams and memories
    - delay resolution to add or change fictional stakes

    \n I like Harlequin's ideas, there. They inspire me with the following types of "incentives":

    * You get to change a significant rule of the game (or the fiction). Maybe a new Era is beginning, and the Evil Lord has bred a new species of Orks that aren't afraid of sunlight?
