\n Lately I've been working on a really weird Apocalypse World hack called Dragon World, based on 90s fantasy comedy anime and manga like Dragon Half and Slayers. It's revived my thinking about cartoon type slapstick in RPGs, and I've been wondering how to do it better. Toon was the first RPG I ever owned, and I had a lot of fun with it back in middle school, but when I pulled it out again more recently the whole time I was thinking "Cartoon characters don't have hit points!" That cartoon characters Fall Down instead of dying makes perfect sense, but tracking hit points until it happens is rather... odd. Maid RPG's Stress Explosions are another great take on it, since the "damage" leads to the character's behavior changing instead of them being incapacitated.

Working on a comedy-oriented AW hack has been really interesting too, since I keep ending up writing moves with "...and you look stupid in front of everyone" and such in them. AW moves' penchant for spitting out narrative consequences for everything seems to lend itself to cartoon physics, since they're made of a narrative logic of bright colors, and unusually far-removed from a "physics-based" approach.

    \n My experience is from my own stuff, so I'll talk about that. The Cog Wars typically plays as cartoon, with breaks into some very serious shit.

    From that, I can say that being able to set any stakes on conflict, have anything be the opposition, and throw down any condition as an "injury", means that you can have totally on-spec conflicts about blatantly ridiculous things; you can have a bake-off and it's just as solid as a fight. The first time you have a "conflict" that is the mad scientist fighting their lab, and what the lab wants is to explode, things shift.

    \n I've been reading a lot about cognitive biases lately, and something that stuck in my mind for its relevance to genre-emulating game design is the availability heuristic. Basically, people tend to unconsciously use "how easy is it to remember X?" to answer the question "how common is X?". Since entertainment media are designed to produce vivid and engaging moments (rather than uniform vividness) it might be easy to only use those "high points" as a basis for emulating a genre when the "low points" might be just as important for delivering the overall effect. In humor, for example, the setup does a lot of work to make a joke succeed, but the impact comes at the punchline. That might lead us to overestimate the time spent on punchline-like payoff moments and underestimate how much setup needs to be done. I haven't really tested this theory, though.

    Posted By: Neko EwenI think Toon got the part about embracing failure right, but was weak on making failure interesting.
    \nI actually thought that was one of the things Toon did pretty well. You had so many tables for Boggles and other weird effects that you could introduce when things went wrong. I always had fun of thinking of strange things that could happen when the characters failed. It was essentially "What would happen in a cartoon if they failed? Well, do it!"
    There were characters falling down nigh-endless flights of stairs (taking 1D6+1 Bumpity-bumpity-bumpity-bumpity damage), getting balloons shoved on their heads so their breathing caused it to inflate and float into the stratosphere, being duped by rather talentless con-artists into purchasing that highly overpriced statue that just happens to be the Animator's stolen property...all of this resulting from a single failure and someone thinks it'd be funny.
    If anything, I think Toon's failing came from its over-dependence on points. It was like a simplified GURPS when it came to purchasing abilities, skills, and schticks, that it was kind of un-fun when making a character.

    \n In earlier threads about this, I emphasized that you should really watch/read in an analytical way, classic comedies of the sort you're trying to work with.

    Here were some study questions I offered in that last thread:

    Why is Bud Abbott in Abbott and Costello movies when there's a whole cast of "straight" characters? What does Bud Abbott do in Abbott and Costello movies?

    What role does romance play in romantic comedies?

    Why is Hardy funny and not just a nasty old bully?

    Silent comedies are hilarious - why? What about a Charlie Chaplin movie is funny?

    We are really blessed in this hobby that we had not one, not two, but three fucking hilarious comedy games that came about in the early days of the hobby: Toon (already well discussed), Ghostbusters and Paranoia.

    \n Okay! I'm here.

    Lemme talk about Toon! first. When I got it, I fell in love with it, I laughed my ass off reading it. When I played it, it just... wasn't very funny.

    Here's the thing: the book is funny. The rules are funny (1d6+1 bumpity-bumpity-bump damage? Hi-larious). The random tables are a fuckin' riot. The problem is, none of this actually prompts you and your friends to say funny things.

    This is because the system is basically GURPS. As we all know, GURPS doesn't actually, like, y'know, do anything. It's kind of a little robot sidekick that you turn to while playing and say, "Hey, does this work?" and it goes, "Beep boopboop beep, affirmative," and you continue based on that. The system itself provides no engine of play; that's all up to the group.

    To be fair, Toon! makes a valiant (and, for its time, innovative) attempt with its Plot Points (and contingent bits of system). Unfortunately, the Plot Point system is kinda vague and fluffy, too dependent on GM fiat, and no two groups use it the same way.

    So, anyway. Super Action Now! and Knuckleheads are both my own games, so I'm biased about them, but they are great. They're both based around the idea that a comedy game isn't one that's funny to read (although SAN! is) or one that has rules that are funny, but one that prompts you to say funny things.

    SAN! is a really crazy gonzo absurd game. The stuff that comes out of it is comparable to Naked Gun, Chowder, and some of the weirder episodes of Spongebob Squarepants. What happens is, everyone makes up weird characters (actually, they don't even have to all be weird; it turns out straightmen work just fine), then you cook up a starting situation that puts them at odds somehow, and let them try to resolve it. Unpredictable stuff is injected into the game via players' whims and a from-the-hat mechanic (which I have found superior to Toon!'s tables because, once you've read the tables, they kinda lose their magic for this purpose because you end up pre-imagining what would happen if you ever rolled bla-bla-blah), and things gradually escalate until they resolve (often by the world exploding, but not always). The motor of the game is the characters trying to achieve their agendas in the face of increasingly absurd obstacles, because we all identify with adversity to our endeavors, and blowing it up big, exaggerated, and absurd enables us to laugh at it in order to cope with the horror of our daily lives. The moral is, "Life's a bitch, then you get run over by a flying ice cream truck."

    The system is really really clever but not in the ways it seems to be. I wish that I had an up-to-date draft (read: one that reflects how I actually run the game), but unfortunately all I've got is this one. I'm not sure how to summarize how it works...
    ...Here's some important bits, though. Every character has a "Drive" that they try to fulfill. If you ignore this Drive, the character pretty much powers down and your ability to contribute is severely lessened. It's useful both A.) to remind players that they need to, like, y'know, play their characters as at least two-dimensional, not just as pawns, and B.) to bait another player into doing something stupid but funny.

    There's a player (not character) resource called TILT! that is used to fuck with the fiction in various ways. You gain TILT! when your contributions to the game make people laugh. If you run out, your ability to contribute is seriously hampered. It's a punishment (not reward) mechanic that reinforces everyone being on the same page about what is funny.

    The resolution mechanics seem really stupid, and for a long time I thought they were, just a sort of "resolve it and get out of the way" (aside from the fact that rolling literal handfuls of dice is fun by itself) thing, but I've come to realize that I made them better than I thought. You have Dogs-esque player-written traits, and you can invoke as many of them as you can in order to maximize your chances of success. This leads to people coming up with totally absurd, crazy-ass, and (most importantly) funny plans of action so that they can involve as many traits as possible. The damage mechanic is also great: there is no real "damage," there is only Inconvenience. Anything bad that can happen (including but not limited to being dead) is just Inconvenience, and you have to try to figure out how to get rid of it. That leads to people thinking up some very funny stuff as well.

    Knuckleheads is a game that I wrote in response to our own TomasHVN saying that SAN! was a stupid game. It's basically a stripped-down, highly streamlined version of SAN! that focuses on classic slapstick of the Three Stooges and Laurel & Hardy variety. The core of it is basically the same, just more specific:
    Regular Joe protagonists that the audience can easily identify with, engaging in some manner of objective enterprise, in the face of increasingly absurd obstacles and/or opposition in the form of an antagonist who is usually an authority figure and has a personal grudge against the protagonists. These hassles lead to humorous setbacks and increasingly unlikely attempts to surmount them, along with a healthy dose of mounting frustration for the protagonists\u2014often prompting absurd violence between them.
    \nThe original version appears here. There's a for-sale version, but it's basically the same with just a few little tweaks and better layout.

    So, anyway. What it's about is making sure the system presents situations that prompt responses from the players that are funny. There's different kinds of funny, which call for different kinds of situations. You saw what I think slapstick is about a couple paragraphs up. If that doesn't match up with what you mean by slapstick then of course we need to talk about what slapstick means to you; I can work with other kinds of humor. I mean, I've also got a game based on the movie Coffee & Cigarettes and am working on one that's a sitcom.
