\n An edited volume in the Blackwell Philosophy and Popular Culture called Dungeons & Dragons and Philosophy, to which I contributed a chapter called "Player-Character Is What You Are In the Dark: The Phenomenology of Tabletop Roleplaying Games," is available for pre-order on Amazon.

Here's what the publisher thinks we're up to in the book:\n
Do demons and devils have free will? Does justice exist in Menzoberranzan? What\u2019s the morality involved with player characters casting necromancy and summoning spells?

Dungeons & Dragons and Philosophy probes the rich terrain of philosophically compelling concepts and ideas that underlie Dungeons & Dragons, the legendary fantasy role-playing game that grew into a world-wide cultural phenomenon. A series of accessible essays reveals what the imaginary worlds of D&D can teach us about ethics, morality, metaphysics and more.

Illustrates a wide variety of philosophical concepts and ideas that arise in Dungeons & Dragons gameplay and presents them in an accessible and entertaining manner
Reveals how the strategies, tactics, improvisations, and role-play employed by D&D enthusiasts have startling parallels in the real world of philosophy
Explores a wide range of philosophical topics, including the nature of free will, the metaphysics of personal identity, the morality of crafting fictions, sex and gender issues in tabletop gameplay, and friendship and collaborative storytelling
Provides gamers with deep philosophical insights that can lead to a richer appreciation of D&D and any gaming experience
\nMy chapter is about how we try to describe what happens in play, which turns out to be a philosophical issue since the description of lived experience is actually a challenging thing to do when you start to look at it more closely. Anyway, I wanted to let folks here know about it!

    "Description of lived experience" -- can you describe that a bit, Bill? I have a bunch of guesses as to what that refers to, but none are specific to RPGs so I'm probably wrong.
    \nSure, Dave. The focus on "lived experience" (or "lifeworld") is a feature of those approaches to psychology, sociology, anthropology, and other human sciences that are interested in describing what's going on in people's heads. This is called "phenomenology," where "phenomenon" means "a thing as it is perceived" (as compared to a thing as it is). Phenomenology brackets off the issue of how things really are, because all that's available to us is how we perceive them, and tries to generate accounts of what it's like to experience particular circumstances: What it's like to be an employee in a produce warehouse, or to be a mom who reads romance novels, or to suffer from a particular mental illness.

    Or, what it's like to role-play. My chapter dips lightly into the kinds of descriptions of the experience of role-playing that one can find in accounts of play, suggesting that it's mainly non-visual (i.e., that we only occasionally spend a lot of time visualizing what's going on; more often we are feeling some kind of differentiated connection to our characters).

    I'm sort of stealing a page from the Forge, where the concern for actual play manifested in a lot of attention to what was happening in players' heads as they tried to make things work.
