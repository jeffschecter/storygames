\n I'm embarking on four new campaigns this week, all of them with a colonial/new world theme. I'll post some actual play reports here, for your perusal and discussion. Any questions or comments, or insightful experiences of your own, will be appreciated.

The game; Fabula
I'm using the classical fantasy role-playing game Fabula ("classical" = rpg with GM). It's a game in it's ninth edition, but only one of them has been published (1999). The game-method is agile and robust, focusing as much on techniques as on mechanics. It plays faster than the normal classical game; a normal game-session last for 2-2,5 hours. Within that time it is able to deliver a dramatic arc that is both tight and engaging.

The game use a single die (D20), and a single roll against skill produces both success/failure and quality/quantity. So there is little mechanical down-time in this game.

Both GM and players frame scenes. GM set parameters for the scene-framing before a round; in the village; on the road; social scenes; etc. Each player get to frame one scene during a round. The GM may frame follow-up scenes, or free scenes, as many as she find to be right for the game.

Players use their characters as the main dramatic tool, and mainly participate in interaction through character-action/words. The players are 10-15 years old, most of them experienced with Fabula.

The setting: Orianna
Orianna is THE oldest setting for this game; a small kingdom in the outskirts of the known world. But in planning these colonial campaigns, I have reinvented the setting. That is: I have gone back in time, to the era when Orianna was used as a name for the continent, and about to be settled by humans.

Orianna is not an empty land; it has peaceful inhabitants, the green-skinned orcs. They live in peaceful tribes, in a land filled with natures bounty. Each tribe is led by a ring of matriarchs, keeping up order and tradition. They are migrating tribes, following the colossal herds of aurochs from the western lowlands to the eastern highlands of the central Orianna. They use bolas and stone-knives to hunt the beasts, and do not know of any war-like weapons.

The characters: settlers
The player characters will be resourceful settlers, coming to the shores of Orianna with a carefully instilled prejudice against "the beastly orcs". Their aim is to find new lands with lush soil, and to build themselves a new life there, away from the feudal oppression, religious persecution and outright poverty of the olden world.

Premise
This is the basis for the campaigns. There are more details to it, of course, but this larger picture is what I'm mostly concerned with before the actual game-play commences; it is what I need, to know where to put pressure on the players and make them engage in the theme.

I'm making the orcs into a peaceful people, at the mercy of the player characters if any conflict builds into physical struggle. I'll use the initial stages of the game to paint a disturbing and threatening picture of these "grunting half-beasts", to stage the scene for hard misunderstandings and wanton brutality when the PC's meet the savages.

I'll post actual play reports in this thread, and would be grateful for any discussions or observations springing out of the reports.

    \n Ok! Three campaigns started this week. All is well!

    Building characters
    - Simple characters; they are farmers, and know of little else
    - Their main skill is "Rural custom", which covers all kinds of farming, and traditions amongst farmers
    - They are free to choose equipment, but are advised to focus on farmers tools (which they do)
    - We roll for marriages and children; half of them ends up married, and most of those have children
    - Roughly half of them are grain-farmers, and half are a sheep-farmers, as they prefer

    Intro for all games:
    - You've paid your savings for ship to Sangori, a great island east in the world, with lots of green pastures and no greedy lords.
    - Sailing towards the frontier town of Imdoras, you are beset by storms and loose a great portion of your livestock and seed-grain.
    - In Imdoras you are told that a lord has taken power, and that he demands a golden crown to let you settle on the island.
    - You can not pay such a sum, and he is in no mood to bargain, so you leave the greedy lord with tears in your eyes.
    - Later the same day you overhear some folks talking about the land Orianna; wide and lush, and free from greedy overlords!
    - Now you sit at a shabby inn; The Bucket, gulping some bleak brew, talking about what you will do ...

    - In one group I told the players that Orianna is part of the deal; "You have to go there, and then we see what happens"

    Happenings
    - All of them took the bait; we must sail to Orianna!
    - All of them started gathering information on where and how to get there
    - Two groups managed to get maps, but none of them where very lucky with the quality
    - The third group got no map, and has very little to go on when they set off ...

    - Some people told that Orianna is an unsettled land
    - Some people told them Orianna has orcs; filthy, sneaky bastards, cowards
    - Some told them the orcs are cannibalistic, and steal babies from humans
    - Characters started repeating the rumors about the orcs, to other settlers

    - The town is bustling with life; sailors, settlers, and shifty speculators
    - Fistfights and violent quarrels is a daily occurrence
    - Some characters got into fights, but none of them can fight, so they fared badly

    - One of them was accused of murder; a dead sailor he found in the water
    - He was dragged, by an exited mob, up to the lord, to be "judged"
    - He tried to speak up for himself, but the lord was an arbitrary judge, and was ready to chop his head off
    - One man stood forward and spoke up for him, saying that he had seen the sailor die in an accident
    - The character was released, but seeing the lord sharpening his battle-sword made him eager to leave

    Leaving for Orianna
    - One group started north from the town, to find a forest with good timber for building a boat
    - they plan to build a boat, and sail for the mainland
    - They saw some orcs, but managed to avoid them (a player set that scene)

    - The two other groups are ready to leave Imdoras
    - Both groups have a pretty vague idea about where to go

    How they fare in their search for the fabled land of Orianna will be revealed next week ...

    \n Had a fourth campaign start-up yesterday.

    Building characters
    - Same as above.

    Intro:
    - You've paid your savings for ship to Sangori, a great island east in the world, with lots of green pastures and no greedy lords.
    - Sailing towards the frontier town of Imdoras, you are beset by storms and loose a great portion of your livestock and seed-grain.

    I cut the intro short here, and let the players starting setting scenes at the arrival in Imdoras. So we got to play some nice scenes of them exploring the frontier town (both players and GM frame scenes in Fabula). That exploration ended naturally with the characters in audience with the lord, where they asked about where to settle. But the lord demanded a golden crown of each, to let them settle on the island. They said that was too much for them, so they were dismissed, and given a fortnight to come up with the gold, or to sail away.

    I set a follow-up scene: Now you sit at a shabby inn; The Bucket. You've just ordered some wash-porridge and "slemme" (water-diluted brew). You overhear some folks talking about the land Orianna; wide and lush, and free from greedy overlords ...
    - they proceeded to plan how to get to this new, free land.

    The intro worked better with their audience with the lord as an actual scene. It leaves me more to play on, if I want to use the lord in later stages of the game. NPC's resurfacing in the game-world is always good.

    Happenings
    - They learned the hard way that there is no timber for boats in Imdoras; spending days to search for suitable timber
    - They got a map from a nice captain, but it was of bad quality; no info on the mainland, lacking in detail on the seas
    - One character was told off by her husband (NPC), who grew impatient with their progress (a nice first couples-scene)
    - They learned about "woods in the north" (actually in the southeast) with good timber

    - A handful of scenes makes the town small and shabby, and surrounds it by mostly barren lands

    - They did not get to leave the town.

    \n I should add that my standard game-session length is 2 hours and 15 minutes. With Fabula that means 2-5 times the action as in a typical classical rpg-session. The game is faster than most, due to the effective scene-framing mode, and a very streamlined conflict-mechanic.

    In free games for grown ups my typical session is 3-3,5 hours. Most gamers are hard pressed to keep a high, creative focus longer than that. When planning for longer sessions, I serve dinner in the middle (typically lobscouse; easy to make, tasteful and nutrient). We set the pot to boil, and play for 2 hours, eat and chat, and play on.

    \n First group had it's second session today. Here's a list of the action ...

    Traveling scenes:
    GM framing: we set scenes while hiking through the wilderness
    - deers walking in ring around a pond, characters throwing some rocks, one character going closer, but wife tells him to let the strange phenomenon be
    - deers grazing; characters counting more than 50, deers running away, wife of character says he should learn to hunt
    - raindeers grazing a hillside, character sneaking up, but they notice and run away
    - coming upon a log-cabin in the woods, meets skeptical boy; "Are you orcs?" Father of boy inviting characters to stay, characters agreeing to help out in building a barn
    (GM deciding to cut traveling scenes short, and initiate a round of barn-building scenes)

    Barn-building scenes:
    GM framing: we set work scenes related to the barn-building
    - characters and farmer chopping logs for the barn, character hit his own leg (bad dice)
    - characters having a break, wife of one coming with food, husband talking to single character about finding a girl
    - characters and NPC's in joint effort to raise roof-beam
    - characters gathering straw for the roof, working well, going home, meeting children, one child being "run over" by straw-bale, no hurt
    - laying the roof, working well, good mood on the roof
    - farmer thanking characters, and announcing a dance to celebrate

    Dance-scenes:
    GM framing: what do you do at the dance?
    - char A: dancing with wife (good die), making love in the woods
    - char B: dancing, flirting (bad die), drinking
    - char C: dancing with wife, love in the woods
    - char drinking, drinking, singing and drinking
    - char E: drinking, drinking, singing, drinking, quarreling with wife and falling fast asleep
    - char F: dancing, flirting (bad die), talking with farmer and friends about greedy lord

    GM set a scene where the characters leave the farm. The farmer talks with one of them particularly, and is included in the list of friends of that character. Once more they are thanked for helping out.

    Traveling scenes:
    GM points to three players, who was left in last travel-round, and says they may set travel scenes now.
    GM framing: traveling the wilderness from the farm to the coast. I set the scene where you reach the coast.
    - characters reach a hilltop, the sea and the mountains of Orianna in the far distance, something coming out of the mountains, a dragon, characters hide with families
    - two characters sitting by themselves, talking about Orianna and hope
    - characters meeting hunters in the hills, polite, asks directions
    - characters reaching the village of Kyrill in the evening; young people on the beach, beds down in empty cabin
    - characters talking with villagers, two fishermen offers to sail them to Orianna

    Sailing scenes:
    Here we had a small round of sailing scenes. Do not remember much of them, other than one of the two boats crashing on a reef. All people are saved, but some sheeps and corn is lost.
    - They reach the mainland, steep mountain-coast, so they sail south, finds lower lands, see band of orcs
    - Comes to a river-mouth, as shown on map, and make landfall, nice place
    (GM notes on master map, that they have made landfall close by an orc-settlement, and stores that info for later use)

    Settling scenes:
    GM framing: we set scenes with you exploring and settling the land
    - two characters on guard in the night, not very observant, suddenly attacked by something BIG, black-out
    GM follow up scene: next morning; rest of characters awaken to find the guards hurt, but alive, and a great animal dead in the embers of their fire, child shouts about BIG BEAR, but its a kind of enormous ox, dead of sickness (boils in the face and abdomen), they burn it
    - three characters trekking up the river, finds gigantic boulder, climbs it to see the landscape around river-mouth, great herd in the distance
    (GM draws a sketch of surroundings, with woods and hills, river mouth and sea, and their camp)
    - two characters trekking through woods towards herd, finds cattle-muck, comes upon herd of 300 great cattle, no hunters, so they only watch
    - two characters measuring the foundations for their first longhouse, not builders, making it wrong (both rolled bad)
    GM follow up scene: builder character evaluating their job (not experienced, and rolls critical failure), "Good work!" (players knowing this will be a problem later on, but has to play along)
    - characters see ship, it sails in and drop anchor, talks with characters about land, wish them luck and sails south-east along coast

    Session times out. We do experience points, and give thanks for playing.

    \n As you may notice; the main body of scenes is focused on the simple life of traveling farmers.

    I am not very stressed about using their families; it will come by itself, as they start to use the family themselves. They have ownership over their own family as NPCs (they may introduce them in any scenes, but the GM plays them).

    We are working mostly to establish some relationships now, based on things they experience together. We make our time doing this, as a relationship between two characters will grow from itself as they get more shared memories. Next session these relationships will start to blossom for real, and from there on that part of interaction is great fun.

    The bugged map is working well. The characters/players have followed the map, but really has no clue about where they are. And they know next to nothing about the land.

    The herds are of a grand cattle they have never seen before, and lends itself to the strange nature of Orianna. They have realized the potential of hunting (orcs in these lands are peaceful hunter-gatherers), but as they come from lands where only lords were allowed to hunt, they have no skills. One of them is practicing a lot with his bow, though, so they are probably going to develop some hunting skills.
    ______________________

    In my view this is developing the way I envisioned it. I could have done more to "exotifice" the lands, but that is no stress. Small tidbits of "strange" info will do it. Next time I will serve them up a strange "milk-tree", to make the woods a bit strange too. And maybe a fish with wings in the river, jumping the water-falls with great elegance. And that will be more than enough, I believe.

    As it is, I believe the players are really taking part of the grand adventure their characters are experiencing. The barn-thing was great, communicating a sense of farmer/settler/frontier-solidarity.

    The colonization-theme is a novel path of play for me, and I enjoy it very much.

    This afternoon I continue the campaigns with two other groups. I'll post a report of those sessions too.

    \nThe land of Milk
    - game-session last thursday
    (second session in campaign)

    A large group of characters, with large families, sailing from Imdoras in a boat owned by one of them; a fisherman. The boat so full it would sink in a storm. Luck is with them; sailing to Orianna goes without troubles. They even succeed in settling the most favorable spot on the coast.

    Fishing is good in the mouth of the great river. The river plains are lush, the wild cattle comes in thousands, and the forests have trees with milk in them (a white, sweet sap, in abundance). They call their settlement for Milk, and praise the holy Hand, which in its infinite wisdom has led them to this paradise.

    They are indeed in a favorable spot, having landed far to the south in this lush land, and with no orc dwellings close by. The one thing they need to handle, is the yearly flooding by the great river, on the river plains.

    The land of Moo
    - game-session last thursday
    (second session in campaign)

    A small band of settlers, no children amongst them, built a boat from the remains of an abandoned inn in the town of Imdoras. They set sail for Orianna, guided by a worthless map, and ended up sailing up and down the coast while debating wether this was or was not the fabled land.

    They saw orcs, in boats and on land, and grew increasingly vary of their presence. At last they found the mouth of a small river, by a deep forest, and made their lucky throw there. Building a small cabin to share, they spotted (and heard) lots of wild cattle in the hills, and named their settlement Moo.

    The location is good in resources, and they are very enthusiastic about it, but it's a bit close to an orc dwelling ...

    \n One group (described in this post) pressing on with their campaign today ...

    - They wanted to start raising the first house, so we set a lot of scenes related to the construction-work. One of them had a serious accident (dislocated shoulder) and one had the misfortune of falling off the roof, into the sewer pit. The construction went alright. They have a proper longhouse now, as winter is coming.

    - One scene had them wakening to sounds outside house, hearing grunting sounds, thinking of orcs immediately, and then one of them was felled in the dark. His hypothesis was; "Those thieving orcs did it, with a stone-axe!" They all agreed on this, and began thinking of "the orc threat" (really; it was a wild boar nosing about, and attacking them in the darkness).

    - They wanted to scout the surroundings now, having seen orcs several times during construction and after. "We need to establish where they live, and how many they are". Scouted north, east and south, in several small excursions, and happened upon orcs several times. One small band of orcs were "dressed for war, with face-paintings and feathers" (really; dressed up for flirting in neighboring village). One orc came upon them in the hills, above them, so I stood up on the chair and shouted guttural sounds at them, and behind me, while gesticulating a lot, as the orc. They ran off! All of this made them really nervous, and made them start to think of arming themselves and training for war.

    - I reckon the orc-field is ripe for violence next session ...
