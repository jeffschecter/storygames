\n So over the weekend there was some discussion of John Harper's Ghost/Echo and Lady Blackbird over at One Thousand One. While there's obviously a ton of excitement for these types of, umm... let's call them "abbreviated" designs, there's been some concern over how much these games---in their current form---rely on pre-existing player knowledge. That lead to this exchange:\n
timfireYou know what this makes me think we need for this sort of thing to work?

We need a book that presents one or more simplified "models of play", followed by a whole bunch of techniques/mechanics, followed by some discussion of how the various models and techniques interact.

From there, someone could just throw together a little 5-page document like John did and be ready to play.

I know that\u2019s basically how other "modular" games work, but the distinction here is that the "system"/book would focus on play techniques, rather than on mechanics. And rather than have a "game" that simply gets tweaked, the idea here would be to have a generalized "form" (like, let\u2019s say, "jazz music") that is then processed into specific implementations (like, "a jazz song").
\n
Jonathon WaltonThat sounds like an excellent project for someone to take on, and I'd love to help with it.
\nI don't mind heading up such a project, but I have neither the time nor energy to do it all by myself. So I thought I would open it up to y'all, and we'll see if anything becomes of it. For the moment I'm just thinking that from time to time I'll start a discussion on a given topic, people can leave their comments, and when it's over I'll compile the info.

I'll start here, to gauge if there's any interest.

Vision

So what's the deal here? The idea I had writing that post was that we could create a framework of play. As such, the idea---as I see it---wouldn't be to include "everything under the sun", but rather to point play in a particular direction.

Towards that end, at the moment I'm not terribly interested in trying to create something "new". I would rather create something that's usable by the community of "story-gamers" right now, and just highlight the play culture surrounding indie games. As I see it, that would mostly entail cataloging all the major play techniques and popular mechanics that have arisen over the last few years.

Again, the idea would be to create a sort of "how to play" text, so people can just throw together quickie 5-10 page design and be ready to go.

In the end, I envision the info being available for free in some form, but I'm not committing to anything right now.
__________

What do y'all think? Questions?
If anyone has thoughts on what they would like to see in such a project, feel free to share them!

    \n I was thinking something more in line with what John was describing. I realize my earlier post was not the most coherent, but "what you do at the table" totally describes what I was thinking.

    So while it's still on my brain, let me offer up a slightly updated outline (assuming a book format):\n
      \n
    1. It would probably have to start with some general discussion of basic role-playing tasks, like what's scene framing? Who controls each different characters? etc. [This would generally describe what participants do.]
      2. \n
    2. Following that would be a couple different models of play, like "strong GM" (Sorcerer, Dogs), "players drive the action" (PTA, Mountain Witch), "rotating GM" (Polaris, Shock), "Party play" vs "Individual characters", etc. [This would describe general play strategies, as well as elaborate on who exactly does what.]
      3. \n
    3. From there I would probably go into general play techniques, like bangs, flags/kickers, fishing, conflict vs task resolution, etc. Again, this would focus more on the at-the-table how-to's, with a bit of discussion of how various techniques fit together (like, "write bangs that address a player's flags"). [This would describe specific play tactics, as well as expand how players do different things.]
      4. \n
    4. After that I would probably describe specific (popular) mechanics, stuff like Fan Mail, Keys, Fate/Hero/Destiny Points, etc. Here it wouldn't be so much cataloging everything out there, but rather pointing out mechanics that have a significant effect on play, and how to best utilize them in play.
      5. \n
    5. Finally, and only at the end of all that, I would discuss how different stuff can be added together and what additional info is needed to "make" a game (it would be cool to have some sort of "work sheet" people could fill out)... [Hmm, though now that I think about it, I'm not sure if this section would actually be needed, or if the principles would already be implied by earlier advice.]
      6. \n
    6. Oh, and I guess to end it, it would probably be cool to include a couple example 5-10 page (intro?) games.

    \n Of course this is a one-man project - it's not a random collection of technique snippets you're discussing here, but one or more guides to one or more paradigms of game design/play. It's not a trivial task to postulate and verbalize these; for example, to date there are just about two different models of narrativist play floating about, and only one of them is really well-developed (this'd be Ron's Egri-based model of protagonist-choice). And just look at how the old school movement is struggling to define how and why old school games work, or even resemble each other. Anybody writing out these paradigms is doing major theory work.

    \n I think you could structure such a document a number of different ways, depending on the author's understanding of things and how you want to transmit the big ideas to your audience.

    Me, I would start with big principles and then work down to smaller mechanics. Like, Aspects from SOTC, Keys from TSOY, Beliefs/Goals/Insticts from BW, Spiritual Attributes from TROS, Restrictions from Nobilis, etc. all work on the same basic principle: roleplaying certain character-specific things gives you additional resources (either more dice now or more dice later, usually [XP = more dice later, in most cases]). All traits in Dogs work this way too, basically. Roleplay some thing = more dice.

    [There's also the related principle: do something awesome (not necessarily character-specific) and get additional resources, which covers stunting in Exalted, the basic mechanic of Wushu, Fanmail from Primetime Adventures, and a host of other mechanics.]

    Honestly, I think distilling all the various specific mechanics into a handful of core principles would make this easier to talk about in general terms.
