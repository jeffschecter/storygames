\n I was thinking was the GM/Player turn divide in Mouse Guard and how the primary contribution of the GM comes from prep, while the primary contribution of the players comes in the moment.

It's pretty common for the GM to do work before play and basically present it to the other players, but are there games that turn this in its head, or at least have the players also do the same kind of prep?

    \n There are certainly games that make the GM more "responsive." It is literally impossible for the GM to pre-plan how a game of Lady Blackbird, The Mountain Witch, Misspent Youth, Ganakagok, or many others will work.

    The players may or may not come to the table with a bunch of material prepped for play, but they will be in the driving seat, and the GM will be taking on the "react and reincorporate" role that can traditionally be associated with players in other games.

    What kind of games are you thinking about? What have you enjoyed other than Mouse Guard that makes you think along these lines?

    \n I really want a game where the players write a deck of cards as prep: places, things, people, challenges, surprises, etc. Then they show up with their deck, shuffle them together, and hand them to the GM. The GM uses the cards as an oracle. Each player gets a one card character (maybe the GM makes those?). Maybe resolved cards get handed back as XP and the group decides who gets it. If you use a trait/tag pool for conflict resolution, you can pick a word from that card to be a new trait/tag for your character.

    1,000 Blank White Cards: The RPG.

    This is a good point. To take Itras By as an example, it's kind of prep heavy in this sense, asking the players to come up with plot hooks, secondary characters and motivations/goals for their characters. The GM is encouraged to feed of this pool of inspiration when the game starts for realz. The game asks the players to do "work" before play. Hopefully fun, stimulating and rewarding work, but nevertheless.

    Every game with character creation?
    \nI debated explicitly excluding this in the OP, but didn't in case someone had an interesting perspective on it. It's not every game with character creation, because in a lot of games you do it cooperatively, or at least together. And a lot of times when it really is player prep, there's not as much of an avenue to present it to the players, unless you just go around the circle and present back story.

    But I'm mostly interested in player prep after the first session.\n
    There are certainly games that make the GM more "responsive." It is literally impossible for the GM to pre-plan how a game of Lady Blackbird, The Mountain Witch, Misspent Youth, Ganakagok, or many others will work.

    The players may or may not come to the table with a bunch of material prepped for play, but they will be in the driving seat, and the GM will be taking on the "react and reincorporate" role that can traditionally be associated with players in other games.

    What kind of games are you thinking about? What have you enjoyed other than Mouse Guard that makes you think along these lines?
    \nI'm not talking about the GM not pre-planning, but having the players do so.

    I think most, or maybe a lot, of games, have this kind of "GM preps and players don't" setup: D&D, BW, AW, DitV. Universalis is the only thing I've played that doesn't really that this. It may be a limitation of the role of the GM vs player in such games. Players just don't have the opportunity to contribute a big chunk of stuff they've created by themselves.\n
    I really want a game where the players write a deck of cards as prep: places, things, people, challenges, surprises, etc. Then they show up with their deck, shuffle them together, and hand them to the GM. The GM uses the cards as an oracle. Each player gets one card to write their character on. Maybe resolved cards get handed back as XP and the group decides who gets it. If you use a trait/tag pool for conflict resolution, you can pick a word from that card to be a new trait/tag for your character.

    1,000 Blank White Cards: The RPG.
    \nI did something like this with a Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy inspired game I was working on. Before "play" everyone would do some madlibs, put their answers on cards, which were later used as (suggestion) oracles for round-robin scene framing.

    \n There was a culture of downtime player activities in the 90s. Bluebooking and diary writing and stuff. Much of this came out of the world of larping, where games would reward players with XP for doing stuff out of the game. "Stuff out of the game" would include making costumes or playing NPCs but also developing your character's plotlines and things. So in a Vampire game, you would go and conspire during the actual face-to-face larp and then send a GM a note or actual blue book describing what you do between face to face meetings. The GMs would take those bluebooks and use them as fodder for new plots and things.
