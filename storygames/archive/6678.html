    \n We like Story Games but there is this underlining aesthetic underneath many story games that attempts to systemize procedures in an elegant fashion inspired by many boardgames, especially German boardgames like Settlers of Catan.

    Many boardgames do an amazing job using elegant rules to create a vast array of options with a sharp focus on the game's premise. Plus the play aids are usually top notch and most people can jump into the game with very little gaming experience. All this helps push us towards the goal of developing focused games that are very easy to play.

    We love to play games. The more games we play, the more we learn. And it may seem that people who like collaborative story focused games and people who like competitive games would be separate but I've found this community, including myself, to love both sides of the spectrum. I love Dogs in the Vineyard but I also love BattleLore, Descent, Jungle Speed, Memoir 44, Settlers, San Juan, Citadels, Heroclix, and more. Just like many people who love tabletop RPGs can like MMOs just the same.

    That all being said, let's go back to the idea of German boardgame aesthetic to create awesome story games. Additionally, we love social procedures that help make play fun and problem free. D&D 4E has many of these elements. As a community that loves to play many games and have played RPGs for a long time, it's hard not to get excited about 4E. Throw in the fact that it shares many of this community's aesthetics, and you are doubly sold!

    \n Hey Tomas, I'll try to avoid the dog pile and give you my answer.
    \n
    \nI like games with clear well presented rules that just by using the rules delivers a particular play experience. For this reason Polaris and Grey Ranks are among my favorite games. You totally don't have to try to "get it" in those games, just play by the rules and the game delivers. I like that, alot. Its what I try to achieve in my designs and I want to see more of it. This is also foundational to my disdain for games that don't bother to write effective rules to deliver a particular play experience but just expect the players to figure it out and make it happen. I like that sort of activity too...I just don't need someone elses rules for that, I've been playing "let's pretend" since I was three.
    \n
    \nSo when a new edition of D&D; comes out that completely and totally embraces what I consider to be the single most important thing in game design...clear rules, well presented that delivers a particular play experience just by following them...I get WAY excited about it. Excited for this game, because I know its a game whose design philosophy I can respect...even if after some play I find cracks in it. And even more importantly for me, excited by the trend that a game with a following the size of D&D; is likely to kick off. A trend that I very much support and promote.
    \n
    \nI love RPGs. I've long thought we need to learn from boardgames (which are way more popular than RPGs), and learn from computer games (which are way more popular than boardgames) and incorporate some of the elements that make those games successful into our RPG designs. Things like clear rules, well presented rules, options that allow players to be continually making interesting choices, a focus on the highlights and willingness to abstract, gloss, or even fast forward past the "boring bits". Its what I've wanted to see in game design for years, its what's attracted me to indie games, its what's got me roleplaying again after nearly abandoning the activity in favor of eurogaming...and I'm really glad to see the philosophy adopted by the biggest rpg company on the planet.
    \n
    \nSo for me, 4E is something to celebrate. Yay, the most popular game on the planet has just issued an edition that at the very least is heading towards the design philosophy I like best!

    Don't get me wrong, I'm sure the system has issues, but to me it feels like they put more effort into removing the suck than they did in keeping it "faithful" to old editions. Reading it, I got as much of a "These guys have their design down" impression from reading it as I did from Agon or FATE (two of my faves), which means I'm far, far more impressed with the design than I expected.

    Interestingly enough, it seems that lots of "traditional D&D" fans seem upset that so much incoherence was stripped out of the game design, because it does what it intends to do and no more. For example, there's tons of things that are not explained in "in fiction" terms -- they give you the game mechanics and some evocative flavor text, and what actually happens in the shared imagined space is up to the people at the table. A fair number of people absolute hate that about it, but it's not a flaw in the design, it's intended.
