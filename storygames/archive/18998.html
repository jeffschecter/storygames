\n I read a quote that I have heard a million times before. Something along the lines of "Doing anything in a firefight can get you killed, including doing nothing." or something like that. I laughed to myself I thought "Hey like Dread?" to which I had my epiphany. I had been searching for a rules lite abstract war RPG that used tactics predominately as flavor yet still had backing in the mechanics for forever now. I then decided to try my hand at writing hacks. So Dread everybody is the cadaver I'm dissecting. Has anyone else tried this, if so how did it go? What would you change? What would questioners look like? Is there a better alternative?

I also was thinking about using Carry but it seemed to steeped in what it was originally designed to do. I know I'm being vague but even I am unable to describe quite what I'm interested in. I'd love to have the talent to convey that and make my own game but alas. I would love to hear any comments or advice on anything relating to my topic.

    \nI really hope to post an AP (or multiple ones if it strikes a cord) of my experiences as I plan to keep tweaking Dread until I get a decent creature to use as a spring board for my own game. I have also peaked at Unsung and plan on picking it up after school to see how it handles the material.\n
    When you die, you come back as a green recruit. Play until only one origional player is left.
    \nI actually like that as the way of determining when the game ends. Especially as a bit of a metaphor of a war on terror game inwhich it's a never ending conflict, a cycle that repeats with each batch of troops. I would end on a note of the last survivor of the original unit getting ready to go on another patrol with the new guys. So in a way it becomes a game about the evolution of the hardened vetern archetype in play. I love it.

    \n I like the idea of a group of soldiers, and the game ends when all the original members are dead (or all but one).

    Make them an elite unit, or carriers of some kind of secret, or spies accidentally winding up on the front lines, a small religious sect or the last remaining members of a certain bloodline, or, better yet, all related to each other - a family. A band of brothers?

    That's suitably tragic and weighty, and explains why we can have people playing "green" recruits, but don't care as much about their final fate.
