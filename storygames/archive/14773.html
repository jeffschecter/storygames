    The Technique of introducing events into the game which make a thematically-significant or at least evocative choice necessary for a player. The term is taken from the rules of Sorcerer. See also Kicker.

    \n Not necessarily a quick decision, as long as it's a real, relevant decision and you can't not take it. Also, the choice has to matter and to be open-ended, in the sense that the one introducing the bang (the GM, for most games) hasn't settled for any particular outcome in advance, not even in a corner of his/her mind, and not even a couple three possibilities as opposed to just one. It's gotta be an open question, no loaded questions or yes/no questions or A-B-C.

    \n
    \n To get even more technical, a Bang is not a decision choice or a decision - it is GM-presented content that immediately implies a decision on the part of a player. That is, merely demanding that your player choose between love and duty is not a Bang - you need to figure out how to make this choice intelligible and natural in the context of the scenario you're playing.

    This makes the nature of the term itself more clear: a Bang is called a "Bang" to emphasize the modular, sudden nature of introducing this type of material in this way. A moment before everything was going fine for the player character, but now the situation's changed and he needs to make a choice, or several even.

    Posted By: Heikki HallamaaSo how does one come up with good bangs on a regular basis?
    \nLook at the character sheet and the starting situation. In Sorcerer I take my inspirations for bangs from the back of the character sheet and the kicker. In Burning Wheel I take my inspirations for bangs from the beliefs, instincts, traits, affiliations, reputations and relationships.

    I have a few as the game starts and I use some and jettison others as useless as the game progresses. I'm not shy about crafting bangs out of the things listed above and in the moments of play, inspired by consequences of the players' actions. And foremost, I am not invested in the players taking any one particular action, so I'm not creating bangs to pressure them towards some decision, clue or goal.

    Hope that makes sense.

    \n Different games call for different techniques and different GM procedures.

    In some games, for example Sorcerer and Primetime Adventures, the GM should be "driving with bangs" (the term itself came from the Sorcerer rulebook), but in other games it's not appropriate. In Apocalypse World you create fronts, for example, not bangs.

    The objective is still the same (to create a story built on the character's choices) but the techniques are different.

    \n Fronts in Apocalypse World do not have much to do with Bangs as outlined in this thread.
    \nFronts are a series of related threats,
    \n
    \nEach front is supposed to be based around a scarcity: Hunger, Thirst, Ignorance, Fear, Decay, Disease, Envy, Ambition. How a person or group of people with that threaten the PCs.
    \n
    \nEG, In a game I'm playing (not AW, but i'm using fronts etc as written) I have a Clan moving into a kingdom. They are a front related to Ambition - ie, the ambition of the people there are a threat. Inside that front I have a bunch of specific Threats. A Warlord who wants to be the shogun, a spymaster who wants to control everyone, an army which wants to be top dog - all of them have ambitions that threaten the players.
    \n
    \nAnother front is based on Fear, its the natives whose fear of the Clan (and other threats) drives them to action. That fear makes them do bad things for the PCs and hence are threats.
    \n
    \nThey sound vaguely related, but not really I think.

    \n Fronts and Bangs are related on the level of prep - a Front is a specific tool of mental organization for devising Bangs (and backstory, and some other things). The relationship is the same as between flags and Bangs - the former is a conseptual tool that leads the GM towards the latter. Flags help in creating character-specific Bangs, while Fronts are good for creating setting-related Bangs.

    \n Steve, the way I see it (but keep in mind I have still not a lot of experience with the game), the Front are a way to apply setting-based "natural" pressure to the PCs, to cause changes, to keep the situation dynamic. In this environment, a lot of MC's moves will demand some choice from the PCs.

    The difference between Bangs and Fronts is not visible in the produced fictional material (i.e. "the story") because the difference is in the process of play that bring them into the game, at the table, and in the way they are thought and prepared before being used.

    There are a lot more techniques, of course, it's not a binary choice: other techniques are the "screwdown" of Trollbabe, The Cold spiral in Spione, the Town Creation in Dogs in the Vineyard, etc...
