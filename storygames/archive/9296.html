\n The FAIR/UNFAIR-thread by Graham made me think on what the use of "fair" implies in role-playing games. I believe there is a trap hidden in the use of "fair/unfair" as a principle for rpgs. Problem with "fair" on one level, is that it lets the players be sympathetic with their characters. In my view "sympathy" with your character is contrary to playing it effectively. Let me try to explain:

---> A player filled with sympathy will object to any sad happening his character may experience, and make it an action against himself. Such a player internalize the character, and becomes a moaning player-character mesh. In a game full of such players, the GM is left with too thin a line to balance on. The typical game of this type gives the players full ownership over their characters, with no recompense for the loss of fiction-power. The GM is the arbiter of fiction-power over the character in this kind of games, and is too often left to fly with stunted wings. I do not support that kind of muddled engagement and stunted GM-position.

---> I prefer the kind of player-engagement based on empathy; to see your character as an interwoven part of the fiction, and trying your best to work in his interest under the conditions given, while accepting his destiny. This opens up for a much more challenging player-stance, and gives the fiction full sovereignty over the destiny of the character. It also frees the GM in regards to what she may present as consequences for the characters, and that is quite important in classical role-playing games. Believable and varied consequences carries the day any time, in these kind of games. It makes way for the kind of drama that sweeps the players off their feet and throw them headlong into fictions too complex to be controlled by their characters.

I know this sounds like rules set in stone, but I am really only thinking aloud here. So please step in with your own thoughts on it.

Nota bene:
My thinking rest on the difference between sympathy and empathy:
- Sympathy = to feel what others feel, and help them.
- Empathy = too see what others feel, and help them.

    \n Feeling sympathy for the character detracts from the fun if the character is "hurt", yes. A lot of players, and GMs, refrain from certain kinds of hurt by this reason, believing that this will leave them free to produce great game-play.     My take on this is to communicate what kind of engagement I expect from players in my games, and what it implies. Anyone prone to "full ownership" is kindly guided into the more functional "helper of character"-stance. And then we proceed to create as much fun as any other rpg can hold, and then some. The "reformed" players regularly come out of the game-session feeling elated. Even old-timers tend to be enthusiastic: "Blast! That was great! Haven't had such fun since the early days of rpgs!"

    There are still pit-falls for the GM to blunder into (over-ruling, bad consequences, lack of communication, etc.), but the overall potential for great interaction and fiction is strengthened.

    \nSIDENOTE: Simon; the distinction between these two stances is quite important, and not only in games; in real life too. Try to read them carefully, and take a walk on the empathy-side. It has the power of changing virtually everything in your daily life. Go on, don't drag your legs! ;-)

    In gaming the stance that claims full ownership of the character often ends up feeling very personal for it, and being aggressive about protecting it from any "attack" perceived as "outside the rules" (mental and social attacks usually are). Such attacks are labeled "unfair". Even "fair" attacks my leave such a player hurt on behalf of his character. If the character is hurt, the sympathetic player also feels hurt. This makes for quarrelsome players.

    If the character is hurt, the empathic player is likely to play along, taking the effect of such a hurt into consideration in his future interpretation of the character. It makes for players that are fully focused on playing along and contributing to the fiction, while augmenting their character with the effects of the powers at play. This paves the way for seamless and highly believable fictions of great wonder.

    The characters are the pivotal element of classical rpgs, so how we relate to them is of great importance.

    \n Nice use of those distinct terms in a gaming context, vis a vis the authority granted a GM (or any other player). I can attest to the fact that LARP play lends itself MUCH more to sympathizing players, probably due to the high degree of immersion into their characters and how rarely folks portray "unheroic" or negative attitudes ("I am playing my personal ideal; you insult that ideal, you insult ME!"). Contrast that with, say, a character you make in Universalis--everyone can take control of said character and do all sorts of things, some far worse than hurting them (e.g. making them do vile things for their own benefit: it "helps" the character but puts a very unsympathetic light on it). Even if I instantiated that character, I have no ownership and, by rights, I should not be hurt no matter what happens to it.

    So, hmmm.... I'd say that playing from a sympathizing perspective is badwrongfun, in almost all games. Let's consider it as a continuum:
    * I play MageKnight. My army is one legend and a couple of his cronies. Your army houses mine, because of the inherent benefit of multiple pieces in MK's turn structure. If I get bent out of shape, I've broken the social contract--we agreed to "fight to the death," my guys died... OK, no crying, new game.
    * I play D&D. My guy is a fully formed character with (at the least) a tactical role in a game about killing things and taking their stuff. My guy gets killed by a monster with a level-appropriate CR. OK, solve that complication (resurrect) or make a new guy. Again, no crying--you took your shot, you were found wanting, become better.
    * I play For Mature Audience. My guy is a stylized representation of what I want to be (me, the real person, in real life) but is Scarred with dysfunctions that I want to resolve. The other players push at those dysfunctions, tease them, put me into situations where I must portray extremely unfavorable and hurtful attitudes and actions that come from my dysfunctions. Can't get more sympathizing than that--but still no crying. OK, the tears might flow... maybe I should have been writing "no bitching." The game is made to push you to the edge and a bit beyond, and all that pressure from other players is ANTITHETICAL to being actually hurtful. You get hurt, but everyone stays engaged so that no one is abandoned in their self-reflection and self-loathing. They are trying to hurt you, through your weaknesses, to purge said weaknesses.

    The more I think on this as I'm typing, the more I think that sympathizing with a character is just nuts, pure and simple. Tom Hanks nuts.

    Thomas, I suspect that a lot of the Norwegian LARP scene involves either pre-generated characters or at least strong R-maps and/or character traits, right? In addition, the Scandinavian LARP scene seems to push at more "LARP as art" and, thus, drives towards play which crosses Lines or pushes for a strong theme or tone. Contrast that with most US LARPs (I've seen) which give 90% of the character generation duties to the players, and which have a strong notion of "character monogamy" (to borrow a Morningstarism--I read it as "I am the only one who can fuck my character!").

    And I reckon that's sort of what I was getting at with the Universalis example: there's no protagonist protection of a character inherent in the system (other than the weight of Facts as it relates to removing the character from the story).

    But other systems bend over backwards to give players near-total agency over what their characters experience. I've even go so far as to codify that in GLASS (generic LARP rules) by expressly excluding anything like "mind control" or seduction or the like: there's too much internalization of character, in the types of LARPs it's designed to run, and thus too much risk of unfun experiences (not to mention abuse). Somehow, the abstraction or remove of sitting at a table reduces the unfunness of "character polygamy". IMAGINING another PC seducing your PC is one thing; ACTING through it (and its consequences) in a LARP context is quite another.

    And I take it even a step further, with one of the game Toggles (Player Priority)--you can't even DIE unless you choose to expose yourself to that risk (for an immediate mechanical reward). Even the most "nerfed" boffer LARPs out there don't go so far as that, typically.

    But all that said, and even in conflict with my own previous point... I feel that there must be a "good" way to do sympathizing character embodiment that's not "role playing" like they do in psychiatry/psychotherapy. I think it would take a game, however, in which everyone is equally exposed. No GMs sitting back and watching you squirm as they roll out NPCs with whom they aren't even empathic, let alone sympathetic. No "quiet players" sitting on the side, nor turtling players. If everyone's balls are on the table to be hit with a hammer, you'd get a check and balance sort of thing going on... hmmm... which might actually REDUCE sympathetic play, as folks find a least common denominator of personal risk from sympathetic play. No one would hang it out more than the player who hangs it out least.

    \n IMHO, Sympathy vs. Empathy in RPG's isn't about philosophy, player stance, or abstract theory. The distinction between these two is primarily about mechanics. Specifically, does the game have failure rewards or not?

    If the game lacks failure rewards, then character goals are always in line with player goals. (Sympathy) The player wants to succeed, and to do so the character must succeed.

    If it does have failure rewards, then character goals and player goals are distinct. (Empathy) The player wants to succeed, and to do so sometimes the character must succeed and sometimes it must fail.

    Traditional games generally fall into the former category, story-games generally fall into the later. A given player is likely to behave differently in different games based solely on this simple mechanical distinction.

    Posted By: komradebobIn the more extreme circumstances I've seen, combat is acceptable, but player-character death is not. Nor capture. Nor really anything much more than very temporary set-backs.
    I've seen that one too, and are quite nonplussed; how did this become a mode of play???\n
    Posted By: timeLESSCouldnt you use "sympathetic" player-character relationships to give players a joyful experience just like you could give them a more "hurtful" experience?
    Ja, you can, and some players and play-wrights do. As a general stance, though, it is a high-risk position, fraught with pit-falls that makes playing a lot more difficult and non-rewarding than it have to be.

    It is possible to negate the risks though, and it is indeed used in a lot of classical game-play, while alleviated by "rules" on what you have to accept being done to your character anyway. Used this way the sympathy-drive is an opening towards character-immersion, and let players experience the wonders of "being someone else".

    My point is that this is easily done with an empathic stance too, to the same effect, and with much less risk of ruining the game-play, and a far greater awareness of how to feed your character-immersion into fruitful interaction with the fiction, and the other players. So I aim at finding the keys to unlock this stance in the average gamer. Help is appreciated!

      \n I t may be a sound advice, John, and you put good words to part of it: "the problem of folks identifying so much with their characters that they can't take an author's stance and let them be screwed over".

      But I do like the sympathy-empathy dichotomy in relation to this. I feel it may present some extra insights, perchance something that can be useful in a non-game environment too. And more; I am at a loss how to un-write this thread ... ;-)

      I am "polarizing" the terms in relation to most real game-play, yes, but that is to make a point out of their difference. In any normal game you will find both, but the tendency of the players to choose one stance over the other, and their decision of when to switch between them, have great impact on the game-play. By choosing the sympathetic approach players can march happily into a social catastrophe. They don't have to, but the pit-falls of sympathy is so close at hand and many, so it is very likely that tension get so high over in-game events that the game is ruined.

      We are all human, so we are prone to making "human errors". And in my experience we do make those errors, each and every day. But then we correct them by choosing anew; by saying we are sorry, by acknowledging the divide between my feelings and yours, and acting on it, or by trying another approach to patch it up. The thing is that we don't always get the chance to correct our mistakes, due to other humans being hurt and alienated towards us.

      I believe that role-playing games are serious play on some levels, and as such they should be played with extra care and consideration for fellow players. To investigate emotions and conditions in "realistic" characters is at best a beautiful experience, fraught with insights on how to be another human. At worst it is an experience of engagements gone awry, with real relations suffering as a consequence.

      To avoid the "engagements gone awry", I believe a clear understanding of the difference between sympathy and empathy can help us. By choosing an empathic approach rather than a sympathetic one, we give ourselves a much better chance of making great games and great friends out of it. It is very nice to dive into a character and "walk in his shoes", but we don't have to forget how we interact the best with our fellow players while doing it.

      We may choose wisely in this as players, and I believe game-smiths will be wise taking it into consideration when writing their games.

      Posted By: komradebobI'm not entirely sure I'm familiar with "failure rewards".

      Could anyone give me an example?

      Or, perhaps I'm just more familiar with ad-hoc, socially derived,failure rewards.
      \nA failure reward is really just a reward that you provide to a player when his character fails in a conflict. The idea here is to give the player rational reasons to put his character in situations where he is going to fail, and to follow through with that failure.

      Capes has two important resources: Debt and Story Tokens. Story Tokens are a standard resource in that having more of them is always beneficial. Debt is actually a Conflicted Resource, in that having some Debt is good for a player, but having too much is bad. The conflict resolution mechanic is rather involved, so I won\u2019t go into detail about it here. However, one important aspect of the conflict resolution system is that \u201cDebt\u201d can be staked on a conflict to improve a player\u2019s chances in winning the conflict. If this is done, the winner converts the Debt that he staked into Story Tokens, which are then given to the conflict\u2019s loser. So, character failure is a way in which a player can gain Story Tokens if he finds himself in short supply. Thus, it is a Failure Reward.

      Dog in the Vineyard conflicts impose \u201cFallout\u201d on the loser of the contest. The effects of Fallout include injuries to the character, incidental damage to the character\u2019s equipment, and the like. However, it also sometimes rewards the player as well. These include such things as adding points to an attribute, adding a new trait to the character, or increasing or decreasing the potency of an existing Trait (player option).

      Torg has a "Hero Fails" subplot card which guarantees the hero fails at some critical task in exchange for a Possibility Point reward later in the game. The player determines whether or not to play the subplot card, but when and how it is invoked is up to the Game Master once the card is in play.

      If you're interested, you can read a thorough analysis of Failure Rewards in my RPG Design Patterns book, which you can download from here.
