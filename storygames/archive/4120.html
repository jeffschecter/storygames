\n So, one of the things I like to have in my mental arsenal is the "hollywood pitch" for every small-press game. Here, I mean "hollywood pitch" as something which does the following:

- No longer than 2 sentences.
- Tantalizes, but doesn't explain. (Explanations are what happen after you hook someone with the pitch.)
- Talks about the game in terms of a "mash-up" between familiar elements ("It's like Mad Max on the high seas." or "Dead Inside and Lacuna in a fistfight for supremacy.")

Here's mine, to get things started:

Escape or Die!: "The Poseidon Adventure, The Towering Inferno, and Aliens all walk into a bar."

Schizonauts: "Philip K. Dick's Cops and Robbers: The RPG."

Spirit of the Century: "Adventure! getting punched in the face by an angry gorilla wearing a bandolier made out of fudge dice."

Don't Rest Your Head: Stealing from Andy K here -- "Lacuna and Dead Inside get in a fistfight for supremacy. Don't Rest Your Head emerges!"

So, what's your game's hollywood pitch?

Feel free to "hollywood pitch" other folks' games too! Like so:

Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries: "Agon meets The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen, on the clock!"

Dirty Secrets: "Raymond Chandler's Our Town, set in the modern day, just down the street."

    \n Some of you are doing excellent elevator pitches ("let me sum up the cool of this game in one sentence") but aren't quite giving Hollywood Pitches ("it's X plus Y, but Z!"). Hollywood Pitches are about the alchemy of a mash-up. Taking two or more familiar, established, known things from media (games, movies, books, etc), mashing them up, and saying, "... but on a boat" or "... but with fairies instead of superheroes" or whatever "but" you see happening.

    Those of you who broke the Hollywood formula know who you are. Take another swing!

      Posted By: northerainJoe, what was Cheap? From way back in 2007, I'm intrigued.
      \nCheap was a weird and awesome little project I worked on for about a year. The basis was that you were trapped in someone else's narrative, and you needed to find and kill your narrator in order to escape. I didn't teach you how to play beforehand, and there was no text that you were allowed to consult - I simply started trying to win by making you a pawn in my story, and you had to get out.

      There was a lot of stuff about learning how to manipulate situations for personal benefit, especially learning how to manipulate the other characters in a scene. Which would lead directly into a quagmire of confusing self-reflections about agency and power and dominance, if done correctly.

      I stopped wanting to play it, or believing in its having a useful function, and so now it doesn't exist anymore, really.

      There were some awesome mechanics baked into it that I hope to mine for future projects, though.
