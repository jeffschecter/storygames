Posted By: Thunder_GodTomas, we can say the game should be played in an infinite number of ways, the reason we ignore it is because it doesn't sit well with "System Does Matter".

We don't want to play in way X just because we're told to play in way X. We want Way X to be a natural result of playing according to the mechanics.
At least, I do.
Problem is, Thunder, that your narrow focus on mechanics bereave you of a balanced, methodical approach to roleplaying. You narrow the spectre of tools available and stunt the broad field of possibilities in RPGs, by thinking mechanics is all there is to it. There is nothing "natural" in ignoring how the verbal interaction itself may be used to strengthen the gameplay in a roleplaying game that is verbal interaction. There is nothing "natural" in ignoring tools pertaining to group dynamics in games that play out in groups, and has great ensemble-drama as the most common goal.

The upsurge of innovative design in the relatively small community of Nordic RPG-designers, is due to the fact that method (with mechanics as a part of it) has been made the basis for thinking on how RPGs are created and played.

I am aware that "method" may have other connotations in English than in Norwegian. The way you use "system" indicates that it has taken the place of method in your terminology on RPGs. It may be that this is due to the strong mechanical focus of American thinkers on RPGs. My take is that method matters. If you don't know how to play the game, you will play something else (possibly similar), and most likely make a bungle out of the game you pretend to play.

This part: "We don't want to play in way X just because we're told to play in way X", is in my view a bit to juvenile to be of any worth as a principle. As already stated I do not find it "natural" in any way to ignore advice on how to play a game I've bought. My idea is that by reading the game, and following the instructions, all of them, I will give the game my best shot at being played the way the designer intended. If the game don't work that way ... ok, then the reason is bad design, not me lacking the will to try it out.

Please bear with me; not all of you approach game-design with a narrow mechanical-focus, I know. Thunder seem to believe it so, and I'm generalizing for the sake of argument.

    \n Hi Guy! Thanks for pointing out the name-option. And thanks for answering! I enjoy this kind of discussion. :-)

    You ask: Does the gameplay not rise from the mechanics? No, it don't. In most classical RPGs the mechanics is the most prominent feature. This is however, not true for all modern RPGs. I've played lots of modern games of high quality with little or no mechanics in them. How can any gameplay rise from games of no mechanics if your claim is true? The truth is that method is the full picture, to which mechanics belongs as one of several different areas of tools.

    In a recent writing on a GM-workshop I named six areas the GM of a classical RPG needs to master in a game-session: leadership, practical social-psychology, group dynamics, dialogue, setting and mechanics/resolution-system. This may give you some hint on how broad I believe an effective game-method must be, to be an effective support for the GM.

    The mechanics are prominent in many classical RPGs, but they still need to be emulated by some kind of methodical approach. The way we play the game is decided by more than mechanics alone. As a game-designer my aim is to make my method balanced and effective. I have found it easier to do so if I keep my mind open to the fact that a roleplaying session is as complex an affair as any interaction between a group of people. I must strive to make a good basis for great gameplay, and may not do that if I limit myself to vectoring how the die is used (to put it very bluntly).

    As for horror: the horror arise from players being immersed in the story, and actually tasting some of the fear of their characters. You may happen upon such a state by chance, but it will be achieved a lot more often by the tricks and tools of the trade, when those are carefully picked and modulated for the game by the conscious game.designer. Effective game-design has more to it than creating a functioning task-resolution system.

    \n There's a lot more to mechanics than task-resolution.
    I think (one of) the greatest Social Contract mechanics to come in recent years is the Psychology die in Burning Wheel.

    Sure, there are games where you have mood and it arises from non-mechanical issues. I can't argue that it sometimes works, I argue about using it and propagating it.

    Telling the group what to use, what to do, instead of giving them tools that will let those things arise naturally is a band-aid process, one that is used for pragmatic reasons. Often, because the design isn't good enough to produce the desired feel on its own.
    Let's look at the issue of "Inter-player strife", or "inter party trust". You had it arise naturally, or you had a game tell you that it is an expected thing to occur, and as such, players and game masters would affect things in such a manner that it'd occur, even if the rule-set did not contribute to it at all.
    In recent years, games came out which dealt with the issue mechanically, The Mountain Witch, to name a notable one. I posit that in such games those issues would rise organically, whether or not they were told for it to happen or not, because the tool, the mechanic, gives rise to them. This is what I am arguing in favour of.

    Of course, it is best when the mechanics exist, and you tell the group what it should produce, for a heightened effect, and for less time for them to 'figure' it. I am opposed to the text alone replacing any tools, and being the sole source of such rise.
    We could use D&D and tell the group to strive for inter-party strife, we could use the exact same ruleset and tell the group to strive for chivalrism, and so on and so forth. I see those as much weaker instances, as much poorer "Mood creators".

    I am not opposed to the mood text, I'm opposed to it when it attempts to create something that wasn't there before.

    As you said, a game-session is decided by much more than mechanics, it is decided by random and uncontrollable factors, so let me take care of what is for me to control. If I want a game session to go with a certain mood, I can't trust the group to desire it, but I can give them a system that is built towards producing them, and hoping the group won't play the game unless they are actually interested in those results, and will use method, but to bolster my system, not to replace it.

    Also, sure we can use method, and method alone, but then, why are we playing these games? It's counter to the job of game design; just have a group of people sit around a table, and have them tell stories with a certain mood/theme. And while I know I am exaggerating, mood and theme? They're not part of the design process; designing a system that produces them is, but not merely telling the group what you want them to do.

    \nEach session is a different play. Character "archetypes" are chosen by the GM, but the character's themselves are generated randomly by using a Spansh deck of cards, which is also used to resolve conflicts (which are called Lances).

The Acts cover the play itself, generated by a situation, and using the staples of the Comedias of the time.     The game some other quirks. For instance, to use the game's "fate points" you're required to state your actions in verse.

    The game can be either crazy fun, if you play it with a light heart (and it reminds somehow of InSpectres if you play it that way), or dead serious if you chose some philosophical premise for the play (but the entrem\xe9s is always a light note). As you said, it's a peculiar game.\n

    Posted By: Tomas HVMOne of the most effective and ground-breaking designs of recent years are the game "Until We Sink ..." by Magnus Jakobsson. Try it!
    \nLet's break this example down.

    Step 1
    Choose a character card
    Read it
    Add any information you like to the card
    Choose a gender and a name
    Choose whether your character is a tourist or a native. There must be at least 1 or 2 natives.
    Introduce your character
    After all introductions, figure out what your character's opinion of the other characters is (this may be changed during play)
    Figure out what your character is trying to accomplish (this may be changed during play)

    Step 2
    Remove the starting and ending cards and set them aside for further use
    Remove (randomly) 8 event cards from the deck (these will not be used during the game)
    Shuffle the remaining 5 event cards and place them face down on the table

    Step 3 - Day One
    Read the starting card
    introduce the 3rd topic as common knowledge
    Have a conversation
    - describing actions only when it is inappropriate to act them out
    - all other actions should be acted out
    - players should include facial expression, gestures, etc., when acting out the conversation
    - any topic is allowed
    - you may talk about any topic as long as other players are interested in having a conversation about it
    - if you want to talk about the event on the event card, do so
    - anything anyone says to explain the event card is true
    - no character can lie to submit a false explanation for an event card
    - don't have your character object or accuse others of lying "too much"
    - what you say must not contradict what has already been said
    - You do not play out any event that happens outside of the conversation (discuss those events in conversation)
    - You may make up past history and events during the conversation
    - Cooperate to create an "exciting experience"
    - If the event on an event card is explained, mark it as completed
    - You may expand on explanations even after the corresponding card is marked
    A participant may leave the conversation at any time
    When 2 participants have left the conversation, the day ends.

    Step 4 - Additional days
    Draw and event card and read it
    Carry out a conversation as per day 1 (ending it in the same manner)
    Mark event cards completed as appropriate
    After day 4, add the ending card to the deck and shuffle
    Repeat until ending card is drawn

    Step 5 - Ending Card
    When the ending card is drawn, read it
    The location of the conversation changes from an island to a boat
    Conduct a conversation
    Try to explain as many of the events created during the conversation as possible
    The conversation cannot end until all event cards have been explained and marked
    After all cards are marked and two participants leave the conversation, it ends
    Any player may make a final dramatic monologue

    Play Ends

    I would definitely not call this game groundbreaking in any sense of the word. It's just a rehash of improv theatre exercises from the late 50s, 60s and 70s still used today.       \n The epistemological point is actually important to my argument:
      With advice, each group uses it differently, you don't know what they do, and as such, you really don't know where the fun comes from, and you have more cause to suspect it comes from the group and not the design.
      With mechanics, followed mechanics, if there is fun, it's more likely to have come from the design, since it's effects are more uniform.

      In such a way, it meshes with a previous point: do you play the game, or do you play a game inspired by it?

      My wife's only roleplayed in groups that I've run, except for one game where someone else GMed for our group. She's never read a rulebook, she's bored silly by them. She has no problem with using traditional mechanics, but her sole interest in them is their introduction of the unexpected. Any idea that the shared imagined space must be rigorously simulated is greeted by a laugh from her. "You all are imagining it together, so consensus is all that matters," she'd say.

      She's extremely good with things like intuitively understanding social contexts, figuring out where in a hierarchy she needs to put pressure to get something done, etc. She is a social animal. She's also extremely good with procedures, to the point where she's unhappy at work unless she's got opportunities to refine and optimize them.

      When she talks about rules, her take is "It really doesn't matter what mechanics you use; 90% of the game is determined by who's running it and how they structure play." And while I'm a mechanics-monkey, I was struck that her assessment was totally correct for our group, and for any past group I've been in. So that got me interested in looking at what a game might look like that was written "in her language", so to speak.
