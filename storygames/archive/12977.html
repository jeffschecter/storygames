    \n So, I have a Weekly-ish Group: We use Google Groups, complete with a cute group name ("You Burst Into Flame", inside joke of course), and another weekly group that communicates through an older listserv... LOL I just read Jason's bit, this is in fact four entirely different groups. (the listserv name is "I Kill it with My Axe", which is kinda ironic given the new porn video blog D&D thing is called "I Hit it with My Axe").

    Anyway, those are the "established groups". Sometimes the google group, we're all kinda tech saavy so we sometimes use google calendar and the like to coordinate, but it hasn't done much for us. The google group itself is great though: We can post files, character sheets, house rules etc to it and keep it private.

    As for play-dates with people that aren't part of a regular meetup group, there are two methods:

    1) There's an RTP/RDU Meetup Group for Role-playing. Lots of recruitment, lots of games. Very well organized, lots of enthusiasm. i've seen everything from AD&D1e to Mouse Guard to Fiasco to Rolemaster get posted - and filled - within a day.

    2) Primarily, though, we have a kind of yahoogroup for area hippie/small-press gamers in the RDU area. People post events to it, housecons, etc. Folks show up. We do 1-2 events a month using a local yahoogroup (or in modern era, google group). It's that easy. It's a private list, and when another interesting person in the area pops up on the radar who is a cool, stable person interested in gaming, we invite them to the list.

    If I didn't have a regular game, nor access to the above two resources, I might try to organize through RPGNet or maybe The Forge/Conventions Forum: I've met awesome gamers through both and we continue to play to this day.

    -Andy

    \n We start with a particular day and time every week, which we decided upon either face-to-face or via e-mail. Then at the end of each session, we look ahead to make sure that next week is clear, too; if not, we reschedule it before we break up for the night. A reminder e-mail usually goes out mid-week.

    If something comes up during the week, we reschedule via e-mail, usually days in advance.

    If something comes up on the day of the game and you can't make it, you let the group know via e-mail and also by phone -- usually calling whoever's hosting the game and whoever is GMing the game (if those are different people) so they can decide if they want everyone to get together anyway and just do something else fun.

    \n In all my games, we communicate with Email for any changes or rescheduling.

    In my weekly games, it's Wednesday night for one group, Sunday night for another. In the Wednesday game, we cancel if 2 can't make it. In my Sunday game, we play even if it's just two people. They're different groups, and have different preferences in terms of gaming group sizes.

    I'm in charge of organizing both games, but the hosting goes to other players, and I coordinate with them in terms of who's place we're playing at via email messages. That's usually stabilized to one person's place, but sometimes that changes, and we send a flurry of emails if it does.

    In my monthly games, (with a third group) usually the next six months or so are marked out so that people know when a game is. We're a busy bunch, so the Monthly games tend to get bumped via emails and so forth, and those games tend to be the most fragile, and prone to cancellations.

    I've used forums in the past, and even a Facebook Page, but I find email works the best. I find it easier if the email comes to them, rather then them having to seek out the information at some sort of web location.

    We don't use phones at all, unless someone's smart phone is accessing an email. Many of my players either don't have a cellphone or don't get back to their voice mail that often.

    Posted By: Nathan H.How is a Google Group different then say an email sent to five or six people?
    \nUsing google groups, people join as members and the emails and replies go to all the members. Additionally, there are other nice features like "pages" which are sort of like shared googledocs (good for online character sheets, etc.) and file hosting (you could post pictures or a .pdf of the rules for your players to read). They are also nice because you can google search the whole group and all of its discussions to find information quickly. In short, it allows you to tie your game's information into a nice little package and still have messages land in your email inbox.

    \n Let's see... I've got rather a lot of games going on because I'm an addict...

    Weekly D&D-ish: We play this every Friday. We're usually alternating between D&D 4E and Pathfinder, but sometimes I run Deadlands or other games for them (thus far I've also introduced them to In a Wicked Age, InSpectres and Don't Rest Your Head). One of the members is a web-tech-creature and has established a forum for us on his server. Each month we work out what's going to go on the schedule and make changes or cancellations all on the forums. Very occasionally the night of the game a couple of us text the people who aren't on the forum as often to make certain they're coming. The forum itself works better some months than others - most of the members have spouses and children and whatnot, so holidays and vacations can make things crazy.

    Weekly WoD LARPs: We have rotating games on particular weekends of the month (1st and 3rd, 2nd and 4th, etc), on Saturdays. Usually there are two games held that day: one game in the afternoon and one game in the evening. Not everyone plays every venue (currently we've got Vampire: the Requiem, Vampire: the Masquerade, Mage: the Awakening, Geist: the Sin-Eaters and Werewolf: the Forsaken games going). We play in a set of rooms in a nearby college that we've scheduled to have every Saturday through the college's gaming club, which we're all members of. We're also all part of a global LARPing organization, so we have a ridiculous number of e-mail lists on a national and local level. The local lists sometimes post reminders, but most of the time people find out the gaming schedule by word of mouth. This works because there are something like 30-40 people involved.

    Semi-Weekly D&D 4E: We play every other Wednesday, typically like clockwork. The DM usually e-mails each of us the night before or day of to remind us. Any problems we may have we let him know via e-mail (or sometimes in person if we happen to see him) and he announces any changes via e-mail. He's very good about it.

    Semi-Weekly Savage Worlds (Mythic Rome Setting): This is a game I run every other Sunday. I have a Yahoogroup to which I've added my players and I use it to send out reminders a couple of days beforehand. Then sometimes if there are delays the players usually text me or e-mail me the day of.

    Semi-Weekly Indie-ish Games: I run various different games at my place on alternate Thursday nights. The purpose of the group is to introduce my friends to new and different games - thus far I've run Fiasco, InSpectres and In a Wicked Age, with a long list of other games still to go. I've put together a Facebook group that includes a bunch of the people from the above groups and other friends in my area who I know are also interested in RPGs. For the regular alternate Thursdays I create Facebook Events to which I invite all the group members - I usually do it on the Friday beforehand (any earlier and I'd be worried that people would get confused about which Thursday it's on). Then every now and then if I want to do a random game on a different night in between I send out a message inquiring if there's interest - if I get a response I create a separate Event for that game. The number of people who RSVP usually tells me absolutely nothing about who is and is not coming. Sometimes people will post messages on the Event to give me a better idea, though. Most nights I get 3-5 people over. One night no one turned up. One night I had nine people (it was InSpectres night, so it actually kind of worked). Very chaotic but it seems to be working out all right so far.

    So, er... damn. I play a lot of games.
