Serving Story, Not Character

planetary
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
My wife and I recently watched one of the HBO Wire pre-shows ("The Wire: Connected"), and I believe it was the screenwriter, David Simon, who said it (I paraphrase):
Â  We had to kill off [one of the characters]... To do otherwise would have served character, rather than serving the story.
Wow. Yeah.

It doesn't seem like exaggeration to claim that we've all played in games where a given player narrows their vision and presses for focus on and survival of their character to the (in my opinion) detriment of the whole story. I mean, right? I mean, I'm not sure what happens in any gaming group but but own, but this happens all the fucking time, doesn't it? It's even sometimes considered GOOD, isn't it?

This behavior seems like a natural by-product of telling a player, "look, create a guy, and play him - he's your guy!" Of course they're going to go all My Guy Syndrome on them. Why wouldn't they? 1 player, 1 character. As goes the one, so goes the other. Very, very natural.

Contrast this with the sort of high-skill play that we hear about (and that I yearn to someday experience!) - among players with hardcore skills who naturally (or consciously?) play games in that story-forward way. I mean, I can totally imagine Jason M. or Judd saying, "Hell YES my guy dies|suffers|is set back -- that makes a rocking story!"

But let's assume that we want (some) games to foster such skills, rather than rely on them existing in the first place. If so, I'm very skeptical that it's even possible to create a story-serving + skill-fostering game while preserving 1 player, 1 character. If you keep 1 player, 1 character, it seems like it's always going to be an effort to overcome the powerful urge to serve character at all costs.

Cheers,
-JasonN

    Posted By: planetaryBut let's assume that we want (some) games tofostersuch skills, rather than rely on them existing in the first place. If so, I'm very skeptical that it's evenpossibleto create a story-serving + skill-fostering game while preserving 1 player, 1 character.
    I think it's worth looking at the distinction between serving character and serving player. Killing off a character often does serve them, as it says something about what they're about. But it doesn't serve the player, as they are often kicked out of the fun loop by it (they have to create new character and integrate it with play, often by waiting til and appropriate juncture). So in many games you don't get incentivised to have that happen.

    Now contrast with Mountain Witch, where even if your character dies you still have influence over play through trust. Suddenly death ain't so bad, since you can still do fun stuff and be an active participant. Take that one step further, make the player more powerful when their guy's out of play or not in a scene, and people will be much more likely to accept that situation.

    The trick is rewarding the player when bad stuff happens for the character, and making that reward worth having.

    Also, I'm starting to sniff two interesting ideas bubbling up:

    What happens to the player in a story-focused game?

    What happens to the player in a game where the game calls for more character death?

    Now, seriously? I know a lot of folks are all hardcore out there on all forums, all walks of life from ENworld over the The Pundit on killing off characters in D&D and the like; Follow the rules, death happens, get over it and roll up a new character.

    My personal experience with a LOT of D&D (AD&D 2e, some Palladium, etc) in high school and college is that GMs, players, are for the most part kinda afraid of character death. They will often not go All The Way. A major NPC shows up and heals people or helps them flee; the monsters suddenly "roll bad' (behind the screen), soften up, or retreat. Sure, it could just have been that all the people I played with were playing the game 'wrong', but for the most part I think that everyone sort of had this sort of deep-down belief that killing off characters, even 'fairly', was Bad unless the player clearly wanted to go that route in advance.

    The medicine here would be to make character death less painful. I find the pain points are usually:
    1) Being out of the game as per above: Even if there's another character ready to go, usually you have to wait for a good "entry point'. But usually most GMs will find a way to force one quickly.

    2) Character generation. We played a 5-session CONAN OGL game where the players, all rather tough dudes, ended up pretty much running from everyone. 3 snake men? Shit, they're gone. The reason was because combat was pretty slow and boring to begin with, and character generation took like 2-3 hours per character.

    3) Attachment to character: The two above can be fixed "in the rules" (ie "give the next character an XP bonus or 2 extra ability score bonuses or something"). But Attachment to character can probably only be fixed through demonstration in the game text: Carefully crafting the book to make it assume that character death is natural, no big deal, a great opportunity to play another character, a great opportunity to add drama to the game, etc and provide solid examples to back it up.

    No version of most games I've played really took the time to do this, so for most players and the GM, "Character Death" pretty much meant "GAME OVER" for that player (at least a little). And there was no reward for character death save, "it can be dramatic!", but that's pretty ephemeral in games which pride themselves on solid, tangible rewards for (tactical) player choices.

    Definitely something to think about. What's the medicine for point 3 above? Dunno. Maybe some clunky reward rules. Maybe a complete rethinking and careful crafting of the rules text to get people over it.

    I don't disagree, but I think these are two different kinds of play. (1) I'm playing my character [and have a meaningful influence on play]! (2) I am audience and am empowered to have a meaningful influence on play, too. Players who have both avenues open are likely to be playing a fuller, richer game than those with only one.

    Furthermore, character death and re-creation. It's my experience that long-lived characters are somehow more special than shorter-lived ones. This seems quite natural, right? All things being equal, they tend to have more of a history, probably have more established relationships, and so forth. (To say nothing of games where more experienced = more effective, but I'm not sure how many such games are 'story games'.)

    And to Andy's question:
    What happens to the player in a game where the game calls for more character death?
    ...In 1 player, 1 character designs, character death seems to often mean a bunch of work on the player whose character died. This often isn't a bad thing, but it might be, if the effort of (1) creating the character and then (2) integrating them into an existing group (establishing all the relationships, and getting things humming) is LESS FUN than the play you experience once the character is created and integrated.

    Also, my sense from playing more traditional games is that characters who are created after the initial group very often aren't ever as fun to play as the first characters. At least, not for a long while. Perhaps this is because longer-term games tend to fizzle in general, but I don't think so. I think it has to do with the fact that the other players aren't sharing the same kind of experience at the same time. Part of the people at the table are playing established characters, and so there's less of a sense of wiggle room. My impression is that the burden to fit in is pressed much more fully on the new character's player than it's shared by the group.
