\n Did a scenario today, with a handful of humans doing a journey into unknown lands.

They met with orcs that had a very slight knowledge of "human". That lack of knowledge made for some delicious dialogues, orc NPCs vs human PCs. They had a near catastrophic misunderstanding about some "gifts", giving away much of their equipment, and were hard pressed to correct the misunderstanding with little common language-ground to stand on with the orcs.

And then they met with some dwellers (dwarves), who had crippled servants that spoke "human", but with some twinges that made it difficult. The fact that the dwellers themselves, in all their powerful iron-glory, stood as stoney witnesses to the dialogue, made it even more unnerving and difficult.

Making nonsensical sounds when talking for the orcs and dwellers, as they talked amongst themselves, made it even more fun for me as a GM, and contributed to the character/player feeling of being out on thin ice ...

Fun to do!

Do you have experiences to share with us, on the use of language in roleplaying games, particularly strange languages, limping use of everyday words, and more or less bad translations?

    \n I've experimented with this here and there.

    I do love wrapping players up on the "There's really no word for that on this language, but there's a similar one which also means [male sex organ]." The double entendres are enough to trip players up.

    And I'll throw in a bad "They're killing my men left and correct" type mistranslation when someone clever has given me a good example and I remember to write it down.

    Still, my favorite language stuff is committing the players to some deed or exaggerating some great offense when a roll is failed/botched, an then justifying it linguistically. I'm very punny.

    Your session sounds like lots of fun!

    \n I constructed a really, really simple language and script for Aklo for a linked series of CoC games. The script was highly amenable to being scratched into writing surfaces by any being with four claws, and I arranged for an appropriately Lovecraftian inventory of too many consonants and not enough vowels, plus really promiscuous phonotactic rules. I did it to engage one of my less-enthusiastic players, who was a linguistics student, and she really enjoyed it - and that made everyone else have a better time, and got a few of them interested in how languages are deciphered in real life. So that was a lot of success for a half-hour of prep, and I've kept using it for flavor in games I've run since.

    \n I ran a game some years ago, where I introduced a sociolect called "porcelainsh". It was simple; in high circles you spoke porcelainsh, or you would not be listened to. and porcelainsh was like everyday speak, but with lots of foreign words, that we found in a dictionary, and gave meanings by rolling a die and counting down in the dictionary (adopting the meaning we found there). We built up quite a vocabulary this way, and had great fun using it.

    But of course; expanding the vocabulary made for some sweet misunderstandings (and hilarious situations full of danger ...).
