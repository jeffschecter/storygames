    \n Non-time-traveller, so I got nuthin' for part deux.
    Non-weapons-historian, so I can only offer broad strokes, which the grognards will fill in:

    Principle advantages will be your steel's hardness and lightness, and the subsequent flexibility and mobility they provide.

    Your Bronze Age guy ain't wearing much in the way of armor, so disregard most of the rapier's (and rapier-like arms') benefits (i.e., poking between armored plates and through small opening in joint protection).

    So maybe you favor something a bit heavier, so that you can handle parrying as needed. But you still will want something considerably lighter than the typical hacking/slashing sickles or leaf blades, for general positioning dominance. (I was a fencer--speed kills; especially rapid, "free" attacks to arms and legs.)

    You're probably going have a basket-hilt, cross-guard, quillions, notches, and every other mod that lets you trap those shitty blades and snap them.

    So I'm thinking we get you started with a nice Schiavona, maybe a main gauche for close-up work, though a handy buckler wouldn't go amiss against the ax-like hacking blades of Sumeria.

    \n I haven't gone farther downtime than the late Roman Republic, well outside your Bronze Age parameters, but I'd say David has it right. The advantages of modern steel would come in the form of a lighter, longer, harder blade that can blow through the armor and weapons of the period. A Schiavona or Scottish backsword with a basket hilt would do you well. As a beneficiary of a modern diet and and medical care, you should also have a nontrivial edge in size and strength, which in tandem with your steel could make you a badass in melee or dueling encounters.

    But practically speaking? You're still going to have to worry about slings and arrows, not to mention spearcasts. Don't get cocky. A thrown German war ax at Idistavisus came within a few inches of splitting my skull in 16CE, even though I was wearing a modern helmet, steel breastplate, and camo. Since then I use a helluva lot more remote tech to record historic battles.

    \n Hi,

    I've spent many years reconstructing Historical European Martial Arts from period manuals.

    The answer is...not very much difference really. Spear and shield is a vastly superior weapon system to single sword or sword and buckler. Swords are generally a backup or duelling weapon in most contexts anyway.

    Regardless of how hard you sword is, you aren't cutting through any armour short of the thinnest gambeson and also not through a shield, buckler or even a bpiled leather vambrace.

    You would have an advantage against a bronze sword in a duel.

    If you go shortsword (around 35 inch blade) or even longsword (two handed weapon with a blade a bit longer) against a shield user you are toast unless the other guy really sucks. Even a buckler wielder is going to give you fits because he can cut off your direct line of counterattack as he strikes and bind easily after a parry.

    (this last thing is a pet hate of mine)

    Also, katanas are not all that useful except on the quick draw. They are not long enough to offer a secure defence. Also Japanese steel was really quite bad. Euro weapons and armour were superior due to better raw materials and the clash of vastly different cultures in battle creating an arms race.

    \n@Gwox Longer reach is a big deal. The fact that they're lighter and can be moved far faster than a heavy bronze or iron sword means they can strike very quickly with a lot of power. You're also using different muscle groups and you can put your body weight behind your strikes more easily instead of just relying on the weapon's mass to strike.

    But it's mostly the reach and the speed. Add that to the ability to throw it. . .

    If you want to try it out, I suggest this. Get a length of 1" or 3/4" PVC pipe. Cut it to about your hight and wrap the end with a generous amount of foam. Try it out. It's fun and will only take a few minutes to make. The spearmen will be able to strike long before the sword can come into play and be out of the way before the sword can even connect with it.

    The only thing that really killed the spear is the gun. And then they made the gun into a spear by putting a bayonet on it. I can't argue with the effectiveness of a spear that can shoot a round or two.
