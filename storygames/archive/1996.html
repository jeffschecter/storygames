    This game was eagerly awaited by me and several people in our group, but it turned out not to be the game for us. Our group typically plays a game for five sessions then moves on. BE works best if you're willing to give the game the time it needs. Each campaign has a set length, which is very good, but it's definitely longer than five sessions. You cannot rush this game.

    Some of our group weren't that into a game where they'd need to dig into the rules a bit. That also turned out to be a mistake. Every player has to be willing to learn the rules. The rules are complex, but if you can handle D20, you can handle BE.

    Some very important advice:
    - In any game you can learn the rules as you go, but there are some things in BE you need to pay attention to at the start. For each type of campaign (there are three types), there's a list of important skills. Give the list to players and make sure they have a solid mix of those skills. It will greatly enhance things later. Of the three campaign types, I advise playing them in order (infiltration, usurpation, invasion). The game works best that way. Starting with usurpation is also OK. I don't advise starting with invasion.

    If you want gritty, epic, sci-fi, BE is an excellent choice. Let us know what you decide!

The thing I realize now that I didn't know before is that this is not a "GM reads up and creates a big thing and the players show up to be entertained." The players really do need to read the rules beforehand. The group as a whole should start with a small subset of the rules and then work more things in as the game progresses. In fact, it would be very useful to run a Firefight and maybe a Duel of Wits with sample characters before the actual campaign starts so everyone can get a comfort level with the rules.

    But having said that, I think the system is awesome and look forward to playing in someone's campaign.

    Even though Mikael's question in the OP has been answered to his satisfaction, I'm going to chime in to be the naysayer about Burning Empires. Outside the issue of whether this game is suited to one's playstyle -- and on this point it is perhaps the most challenging RPG I've ever seen -- I'm disappointed with the book as an RPG product. Yes, it is beautiful and lavishly illustrated, and even has an index. However, it is poorly organized and sparse on non-rules content.

    One has to flip, flip, flip from one section of the book to the other, back and forth, all through character creation and through the game. For example, to figure out your character's starting equipment, you may have to plough back and forth through the following sections: Character Creation: 8. Resources (pp. 109-113), Resources (pp. 357-372), Technology Burner (373-398), Weapons (pp. 511-523), Armor and Iron (pp. 540-546), and Vehicles (pp. 547-557). The advancement rules are located before the rules on resolving actions of various types or playing out "maneuvers" (the large building blocks of the story.) Some key entries are missing from the index (i.e., for some topics, all the incidental mentions of the topic are listed in the index but the primary entry is not listed). And so on.

    In addition, the book is chockfull of setting bits that are given no context at all. Sure, some of that might be useful as hooks to jog players' creativity, but it goes way past that; there is so much that is unexplained that it is daunting to new players; you have to make up pretty much everything (what's a coeptir? what exactly do Devotees to Fire do? etc.) This massive book actually has very little information on the setting. Though it is lavish, it is not really usable as a sourcebook for the Iron Empires it is based on. Many's the RPG I've salvaged, even though I didn't like the system, because I could use it as a sourcebook for other games, but this is just not possible with Burning Empires. This book can only be used for one thing, to play BE as published; just like this game can only be played in the way it was intended by the designers. For some people that's a feature, but for me that's a bug.

    While I'm perfectly comfortable with the idea that for other people this is a great book and a great game (we don't all like the same things, and that's good!), I think people who are not already fans and are trying to evaluate whether this is a good book for them should know about these issues. If you and your group have a significant crunch tolerance and are willing to put in the effort, the aspects I described may not matter at all. But if you're on the fence and just hoped to learn more about the Iron Empires, this book will not help you very much.
