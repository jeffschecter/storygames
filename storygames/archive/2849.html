\n So, tell me about the online game (that is to say, an RPG/Story Game played over IRC, MU* or a similar text-chat format you were in or are currently are in which was long-running and resilient (if you have a game that's been running pretty much continuously for over a year, say, or just for, like, more than fifteen discrete sessions, I want to hear from you!). Tell me why it's so awesome.

In particular, I'm interested in how (if at all) you elimentate, manage or compensate for the following:

* Absent players.
* High player turnover.
* Scheduling and getting people to show up at the right time.

* Keeping all the players engaged / The Idling Problem (where peopel are reallly slow to repsond owing to being distracted by something else).
* Slowness for other reasons (needing to type, procedural uncertainties like who should speak next, or whether or not you're speaking over one another.)

    \n Mike's Heroquest IRC game was VERY long-lasting. More than a year, and dozens and dozens of sessions. Is it still going? I dropped out a while back to focus on writing.

    We dealt with absent players by having multiple interweaving plots, and multiple windows open for scening.

    We dealt with high player turnover by turning away noone for new characters... and by insisting that visitors at least start the process of making new characters.

    We dealt with scheduling by, well... showing up on time. Not much else to do.

    Slowness is just a fact of live in IRC. Don't expect things to happen at a tabletop pace.

    \n I ran a science-fiction game in IRC that lasted 50 sessions over 2.5 years--maybe 100 hours or so. The game had difficulties, in part because I was thrashing around trying to go from a "trad GM" to an "indie GM" style without knowing what I was doing. But when it worked, it was a lot of fun. I miss those guys: when the campaign ended, we sort of drifted apart, which sucks. They were great players, I thought we got along pretty well pre- and post-play, and I'm kind of sad they didn't keep in touch. You'd figure three years of creative collaboration ought to count for something.

    The Forge is giving me server errors right now, but I'll splotch up some links when I get a chance, one of these days.

    We handled absent players by generally playing without them, sometimes doing one-shots (The Pool, frex) when we knew in advance someone wouldn't make it.

    We didn't have any player turnover.

    Scheduling--y'know, we were always pretty relaxed about it: but at the same time, after 2 years of play, it became routine. I wouldn't have dreamed of missing a session, at least not without a very good excuse.
