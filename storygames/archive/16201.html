\n Hey, so the Dev 4e wacky wild fun(?) thread got me thinking. A lot of folks have had experiences with 4E that they want to share. There were a wide variety of experiences that emerged from playing 4E (from loving it so much as to play it weekly, to dismissing it with hatred). A lot of threads where people post their thoughts on D&D 4E tend to go loopy pretty quickly, usually due to people with conflicting experiences telling each other what they should feel about the Game They Like/Hate. So I wanted to have a Share Your Thoughts thread, one that is heavily moderated and ruled.

So here's the rules. If you don't read them, your post will be deleted.

RULE ZERO: You MUST have played D&D 4E, preferably more than once. If you are posting here because you read it thoroughly but have not played it--you're /wrong/, and GTFO.

RULE ONE: You only get one post. Just one. Your thoughts. You can go back and revise it, adding more or citing others, but no more than one single post. If you have a "Oh, that post there reminds me..." thought, revise your original post or create a new one.
Whispers don't count, you can whisper others as much as you want.

RULE TWO: Addendum to Rule One, indicating the point of this thread: You're sharing your experiences, experiences which for the purpose of this thread are inviolate: No one can judge you on them in this thread, even if you're wrong (here I'm thinking of folks saying "I don't like 4e because there's no wandering monsters", then JD or someone pointing them to the page which explains wandering monsters). If you want to correct people, to tell them that they weren't doing it right or that Rule X Clearly Exists or whatever, please create another thread and do so, not here.

    \n So here's my experience.

    In the leadup to 4e, I had a semi-regular weekend group that met up for D&D 3.5e on Sundays. When 4E came out, we flipped (both Out in enthusiasm, and Over to that ruleset).

    When I saw the grognardia (and by those I mean the ones who would never even try the game before playing it) denounce the game, I pretty much ignored them. The same shit went down around 3e, when people were equating the malleability of character types to WOTG's Magic the Gathering and the like.
    So for folks who decried it a board game, or a tactical wargame with no role-playing, I listened to those comments but then dismissed them. In most cases it appeared to be clearly towers of logic built upon gut prejudices.
    4e is *clearly* a role-playing game. You play roles, how much more fundamental does it possibly fucking get?

    So I played 4e a lot since it came out. One mini-campaign, two other low-level campaigns. I pretty much stuck to the core books, and never got into the piles and piles and piles of expanded material (PHB 2, 3, all the supplemental stuff).

    To make a long story short, after about two years of play I had these realizations:
    1) D&D 4e combat made me look at RPG combat completely differently, forever. It was exciting, dramatic, very tactical without being too "Avalon Hill Bookcase Game-y" (the map tactics, the bloodied conditions and other game conditions). It made combat in other games as bland and tasteless as ash. We played L5R and 1-2 other games right after a 4e campaign, and in each case it felt so contrived and boring, "Roll some dice, look for high numbers, stand toe to toe with an enemy until one falls over after a few minutes". As creative as gaming got in the mid-90s onward, this above statement pretty much summarizes the combat mechanics of most RPGs out there, from Deadlands to Fading Suns to Earthdawn to L5R to Tribe 8 to World of Darkness to Whatever. Forever more, if a game emphasized combat scenes, it either had to be at least remotely as interesting as D&D4e, or had to be tactically interesting/intriguing in other ways, especially in more ways than "Hit rolls"("Defense Rolls") "Damage Rolls" ("Soak/Armor Rolls").

    2) As a GM, I found it much harder to plan encounters than 3e. The balance required meant that it was much harder to Sandbox a 4e game compared to what i was used to with the limited (and less tactically interesting) stats of 3e: Not only was there the monster, there was the terrain to think about as well. All the other RPG aspects were easy enough, it was just really hard for me to quickly put together a freeform encounter, certainly more time than it took with the comparable 3e.

    3) I thought the expanded books were shit:
    * Using the themes in the PHB of Striker, Controller, Defender, they combined them with the "power sources" into (IMO) ridiculous spinoffs. A million bizarre little Prestiege Classes were replaced by a million bizarre full character classes. And because of the mandate of perfect balance, all of them started to feel similar to the core classes, and certainly similar to other combat roles (in terms of effects, dice used, etc). I was going through one of the expanded books the other day at the game store looking at some character classes, being able to read pretty much every ability and trace it back pretty much as a basic reskin of something that appeared in the core book.
    * They had no heart. The D&D 2e days certainly suffered from having too much fluff and not enough crunch, perhaps. In any case, it's the generation of books I grew up on: Even the Complete Gladiator's Book was a book filled with seting info, cultural (to dark sun) info, weapons, kits (character class variants) that were more description than rulesy-rules, etc. Comparing them to 4e was like night and day: No fluff, everything seems to come down to description text for the core elements of the books: New Races, New Classes, New Weapons/Items, DONE: NEXT. 2e suffered (perhaps) from too many supplements that were wordy, that had too much background fluff, but 4e certainly suffers the opposite.
    -- For example Dark Sun, my favorite campaign world. I was so ready to buy that Day One at GenCon a few years back. Then I flipped through the copy (and this was still when I was even BIG into 4e at the time); saw basically the laundry list of "not a lot of setting, just Races, Classes, Weapons, Items/Magic Items, Monsters, and fluff was only inserted into the abilities and descriptors of those elements"... I put it down and walked away. That was actually probably the dam bursting for me, getting me to rethink 4e.

    4) As a player, ultimately, I lost interest because we weren't role-playing, and that's My Thing.
    - D&D 4e is a Role-Playing Game! I still believe that, and argue with people who say that it is not!
    - And yet, in game after game, that very interesting, tactical combat system just took up too much of the mindspace of the rules and the players. It got to the point where like three gaming sessions in a row would end with "Shit, it's time to end the session, and we /forgot to role-play again/!"
    The combats were exciting and fun as shit! But ultimately we were ALL there to Play Roles, and it wasn't happening because of this giant other awesome thing that was clamoring for our attention.

    It's weird, it really helped me get a lock on what I really like and don't like about the hobby, and more able to carefully choose games that I will like and avoid ones that I would not. It got me really interested in combat in RPGs and carefully evaluating combat systems. It also pushed me more towards games that were at their core more "social" (playing Social characters). It also kept me interested in old school style fantasy gaming while looking for new systems to do it with (Red Box Hack, the 13th Age playtest, etc).

    So that's my story.

    -Andy

    \n I just wrote out a long response that got eaten. Here's the abridged version.

    Played a fun Tiefling Rogue and a cool human Paladin. The rogue's game fell through because of scheduling and the paladin's game fell through because the DM nailed a TPK and got gun-shy about running the game.

    \n I gave D&D 3rd Edition a couple tries when it first game out, but 4E is the only version of D&D that actually worked for me. Some of that was simply the timing, but as someone who likes RPGs but not board games, it was my first exposure to that kind of super-tight game design that you don't usually see in RPGs. It wasn't perfect, especially out of the gate, but it was a lot closer to being properly balanced than most any other RPG ever. Pretty much the only thing keeping it from being perfect for my gaming group is that the DM's time commitment is still pretty high, even if by all accounts it's a major improvement over 3.5. We had a ton of fun and got in a good amount of role-playing, but there was a definite sense that it took a lot of effort to get that RP to continue into a combat, without much reward. Although I'm glad the game doesn't let the magic using character poof problems away (something that for me made several other games less fun in the past), I did and still do feel that skill challenges and rituals are good ideas that never got properly realized.

    Our 4E games have kind of petered out after ~3 years of regular-ish play, and I'm glad that I have a second gaming group that goes in for a greater variety of games. I really enjoyed playing 4E and would happily play again, but I've never been the type to be happy playing just one RPG for a really long time. We've also played 3-4 one-shots of the 4E Gamma World, which is an absolute blast and shows that the basic chassis of 4E is much simpler and more versatile than people realize. When I started playing 4E I looked at my friend's shelf of 3.5 books and knew that they would eventually produce more supplements that I could bring myself to care about, and indeed that happened around when Essentials came out. The only thing that makes me a little sad 4E is going away is that between other offshoots in the manner of GW and refinements to the rough spots in the game, it still has a lot of untapped potential.

    I don't really have time to work on it right now, but I want to put what I learned from 4E, good and bad, into a new game called Slime Quest, which as I'm envisioning it right now will be like a simplified anime-inspired 4E with bits of Meikyuu Kingdom, Arianrhod, Mouse Guard, and FATE bolted on. I want subject mater inspired by anime and Japanese video games instead of D&D that drew from Tolkien and Conan and such and is now eating its own tail. ("How do we make sure you can make Drizzt?") I want combat that's tactical and interesting and well-balanced without taking up quite so much time and that robustly allows for creative stunts and such. I want better mechanical support for non-combat stuff and role-playing related stuff.

    \n I'd followed EN World's previews before the game came out. I had the bootleg PHB and DMG docs pretty early on. I got the H-1 preview module as soon as it came out, and that was just in time for me to run it at a small minicon that I organize every year. I had six players of different experience levels, from rookie to grognard, and they all had a blast killing kobolds. However, that module was organized terribly. There wasn't a lot in there to support anything but combat, too. I winged a bunch of stuff, and the kobold prisoner/sidekick they picked up was the players' favorite part of the game -- which is telling now.

    I pretty much adored this game upon reading it and playing it early on. However, I hated that Wizards upped the gonzo factor so much. More weird races, more weird classes. All the art seemed to be full of spikes and horns. It bugged me.

    Fred pulled me into One Bad Egg, a PDF-only 3rd-party publisher of 4E supplements. I edited a handful of these and contributed creative material to the Gods product (Torchbearer has been in my campaigns for ten years or more). Editing 4E stuff is hard, because there's a lot of technical crunch that needs to be pored over. This gig also raised another issue for me: if you create a custom race or class, your players aren't going to use it because Wizards' online character generator doesn't support it.

    I ran two campaigns of my own. Both were designed to accommodate the incredible gonzo-ness of the races and classes.

    The first campaign was Metrocalypse. Basically Oxford, England, gets sucked into the D&D world in 1605. PCs are normal English folk, who get mutated by the new world's magic, becoming elves and wardens and half-orcs and stuff. We played 6-8 sessions of that before it fell apart. I'm not sure why it died. It was a weeknight game and half my players were driving 60-90 minutes to the game, and it probably just wasn't entertaining enough to be worth the drive. That campaign had a good mix of role-playing and combats and by-the-book (broken, but fun) skill challenges. Somewhere on the forum is my write-up of the Christian conversion of a tribe of polytheistic bullywugs.

    The second campaign was Saberpunk. This was a city of millions of people artificially crammed into a city of stacked layers. Cramming people together created magical energy, which the city tapped and used for all kinds of stuff, akin to magitech-power. The setting was high fantasy with dark cyberpunk elements. We played 15-20 sessions before moving on. I was using Stalker0's modified skill challenges, which were awesome. I leveled players pretty quickly (every two or three 4-hour sessions) and it felt like players were mainly interested in what new combat gadgets they could use (i.e., the game's reward system worked well, but it encouraged people to fight more). As the levels increased to around 5 (I think), it was starting to feel like the game was about combat. There was lots of role-playing but it was starting wane. I'm not entirely sure why we stopped playing, and I have an itch to revisit the themes and setting, but not with 4E. Maybe with 13th Age. =D

    For Saberpunk, I created a new race. You could play a Gargoyle, which was one of the city's statues that had come to life. There was a smaller version with some limited flying powers and a large, stompy version. I had excluded warforged from the campaign for thematic reasons but really, the larger gargoyles were very similar. Players seemed interested in the idea, but didn't make any gargoyle characters because the DDI character generator tool didn't support them.

    I also played in a few games here and there. Our local Maryland Heroes' Guild ran a West Marches-style sandbox for a while and I played in 2-3 of these games. Daniel Levine organized a similar sandbox when Dark Sun came out and I played in 2-3 of those. I had a great time in the Dark Sun games; less fun in the prior generic sandbox.

    I also attended D&D Encounters at local game stores five times. Two were purely one-shots in my mind. The last three were at a different store with a different pair of DMs, and they ran a less by-the-books 4E campaign using the Encounters materials. There was plenty of role-playing, silly voices, and some character development (all to my surprise). The play was fun but lackluster. The other players did not "step on up" to the combat challenges, and I seemed to be the only player who cared about tactics. The DM fudged (I knew he fudged, but I doubt other players did) to save our lives many times. I stopped going when I decided to run Apocalypse World and Labyrinth Lord campaigns every week.

    In short, 4E produced some really fun times but also a lot of frustration. I didn't have the encounter-creation problems that other posters have talked about. I found it a breeze to use the rules to snap together a handful of monsters at appropriate challenge levels, even reskinning them as was useful. I often did this during play in 30 seconds or so. Stalker0's skill challenge rules made that part of the game fun for everyone again. We just found ourselves focusing on all the combat stuff.

    The books themselves were a constant disappointment. They were walls of powers with no soul. I'd flip through a 150-200-page splatbook in fifteen minutes and feel like I'd wasted 30 bucks. The Dragon and Dungeon content on DDI was often similar in nature for me, though I enjoyed the design articles and editorials. I never played any of the published modules (other than H-1).

    I'm curious about D&D Next. I'm totally psyched about 13th Age, which is totally a love letter to D&D (OSR and 4E alike).

    \n I am still running a D&D 4E game. I have several veteran roleplayers and several who are brand new to roleplaying. Everyone loves the game and here are a few reasons why:

    1. Very tactical and tactile play. I use 3d terrain whenever I can, everyone likes having minis to represent their characters and where they stand, they like the maps, they like treating combat partly as a skirmish and partly as problem solving, and it gives everyone the same reference points during play.

    2. Those video game elements everyone bitched about, such as triggering quests and buffing characters, are things we actually enjoy. Everyone gets a kick out of those kinds of elements. Which leads me to my next point ...

    3. Simple stories.     \n I've played about five sessions of 4th edition D&D all told. As I tell people when it comes up, my impression has been that it's a compelling skirmish wargame, but not much of a roleplaying game. I think that the reason for my impression is that the game consists of mostly fights, and play between fights does not impact those fights - can't impact them much, really, as the fighting subgame works best when it's preplanned and balanced in isolation from whatever might be going on in the fiction. I know that others have managed to play otherwise, but the combination of the tools and examples set by the official materials has not left me with significant tools for anything but fights over fights with some essentially meaningless fluff pieces thrown in. Not significant tools in comparison with e.g. Mentzer red box, to pick an edition of D&D that provides a method that's deeper and more interactive than traditional illusionism.

    My experience of the game's setting is also pretty irk-some, frankly. I don't like the feeling of safety and status quo the game excrudes. The mechanical focus on balance makes the player characters seem mundane and boring instead of exceptional, and their inability to act with agency outside of the narrow venue of a battlefield enhances this feel - I feel like nothing has stakes in the setting, because it's all just a comforting carnival ride. In this context the fiction is only too easily read by myself as fluffy platitudes: of course these guys have magical flashlights, it's not like we'd want PCs to actually be threatened by the terrors of the dark. Every convenience the setting piles on to make the movement from one set-piece combat to the next feels more plastic than the last, until I simply can't take the setting seriously anymore as anything but a computer game cutscene. Of course the Against the Giants globe-trotting arc involves convenient teleporters the GMNPC uses to transfer the party from one place to another, it's not like we'd want them to have to deal with time pressure and travel logistics and random unassociated events on the way from point A to point B. Of course all setting problems boil down to the need to fight a half dozen fortuitously balanced bad guys on a clearly lineated battle-field. It's not serious as a setting, the events do not feel true to life.

    My disdain for the game's literary qualities isn't helped by the frankly idiotic "fluff" that the combat-centered class and power descriptions involve. I don't know why nobody seems to ever notice this, but they're all just descriptions of killing people in different ways, so these character definitions end up lacking in any sort of social texture. Obvious enough for powers, I guess, but I think it's pretty sad if your "roleplaying notes" for a "rogue" say that the character is "an agile fighter who darts in and out of melee to execute lethal backstabbing attacks." Is that everything you have to say about rogue characters in the context of your fantasy world, really? The classes and powers almost uniformly lack a solid feel for actually meaning anything in the setting. I make mention of this because I think that this promotion of the skirmish boardgame over the fiction in importance is directly correlated with the way I respond to the game: of course it's more of a wargame than a roleplaying game, the fiction is always ignored in favour of the miniatures scenario. What would be more wargameish? If a game became a roleplaying game by just having named characters, your average boardgame would be one.

    A serious procedural problem I've had with the game is it's integrity failure as regards PC death: the game commits the traditional game's typical sin of sort of promising the players a hard fight, but then winking to the GM, shuffling a lot of numbers and intimating that it's OK to cheat if that's the only way to make sure that the party will ultimately triumph. I've yet to play the game with a group that would have taken this participationist agenda seriously, and this "no cheating" attitude has resulted in a relatively unplayable game. The way it's gone, in both the D&D and Gamma World iterations of the system, is that we play 1-2 fights, until our entire party dies against the opposition, at which point we have no satisfying way of continuing the game. We've tried repeating a failed fight in the spirit of "getting it right", but that's actually pretty dull in practice. We've also tried to play character deaths straight, but it's frankly emotionally devastating in this game to have your carefully crafted character you're happy with torn away and replaced after just a short acquaintance - besides, who has the time to be constantly making new characters?

    My current understanding of what the 4th edition is best suited for in theory terms is participationist simulationism, with the goal of simulating D&D fiction. It's a weirdly self-referential creative agenda, I know, but it seems like the best fit: just like 3rd edition, the game goes to great lengths to provide you with a powerful balancing engine for the sole purpose of enabling a fiction that looks as much as possible like that of older editions of D&D, but without the "rough bits". The players are supposed to enjoy portraying their unique snowflake characters, and the GM is supposed to enjoy driving the plot train for the snowflakes to experience. The combat bits are essentially a massive ritual hearkening back to when D&D had teeth as a challenge-based game; as the books pretty clearly say, the goal is to have some combats that feel like they could go against the characters, but the supposed outcome is that the PCs win, so the real reason for playing through the combats is that we just want to pretend to be challenged. I've been toying with the idea of playing the game by the book one more time one of these days, just to give it a chance with myself in the GM's seat. (I haven't GMed the game yet.) If I do, this participationist simulation angle is going to be where I'll go: instead of ruining the game by being difficult with the players, I'll just roll over on everything pertaining to character portrayal while telling the players to not even bother worrying about the external context of the fights - they're allowed to throw in smart quips and show how their characters react to the events, but I've got the plot roughly planned out, so no point in arguing against the plan. No need to stress out about the fights, either - I'll roll dice and all, but we'll work around any set-backs with last-minute cavalry or other safety rails so that the heroes win the day in the end. I sort of suspect that this'll fall flat if I don't figure out some emotional stakes for the combats, though, so I'll probably make it a two-pronged railroad with a "good" and "bad" ending for any given arc; the player character portrayal won't be endangered, and if they fail it'll be tragic heroic failure on the brink of success, of course.

    To sum up: I'm intrigued by the wargame, but I don't see how it hangs together in a roleplaying context without playing it like it's '92 all over again and TSR just published the anniversary edition of the Dragonlance campaign. As far as I can see, the game's roleplaying aspects amount to no more than what you'd get in Warhammer Fantasy Battle - nobody calls that a roleplaying game, but it has the same bullshitting over miniatures (I say this entirely positively - fluffing up a boardgame or such can be great fun alongside playing the game), and the Warhammer "campaign" as explained in the rulebook is essentially the same as a 4th edition adventure: both consist of set-piece combats that are stringed together by plot exposition. The typical standard format Warhammer campaign is the more functional of the two, though, as it's built to assume that either side playing can win any individual battle, and thus there's a clear procedure to follow whatever way the individual combat resolves.

    My future plans regarding 4th edition involve playing it seriously when it comes out as a computer game. I like this exact sort of tactics games in computer form, and it's a common frustration for me when I crack out 4th edition that it takes so much time to set up and play, all without providing much more than I'd get from a computer game. A squad skirmish game in the tradition of e.g. U.F.O or Jagged Alliance would in fact provide me with more strategic agency than I get in a tabletop 4th edition session. Something with a simple strategy game to provide context for the skirmish fights would be just excellent.

If you ignore the text, and simply imbibe the the layout and art of 4e it is a radically different expression than the typeface and b&w simplicity of Moldvay or AD&D or even a modern mash-up like Dungeon World (which I love love love, and play intermittently with my group of long term roleplayers when I'm back in the Blue Mountains).

    So 4e got requested by my regular friday night soir\xe9e. I sat down and had a talk with folks. Some had looked up D&D online and liked the idea of encounters, but our nearest meet was 3 hours drive away, others just wanted to create a character that was 'pretty', a few had read / watched celebrityAP's (that strangely focused on the story rather than min/maxing the tactical game), some liked the idea of their mini being taken 'out of the dungeon'. I think most importantly, the group wanted a change to boardgames. So I bought essentials, picked up a few battlemaps and recycled minis from other games, read Sly Flourish and Chatty DM's blogs with abandon, spoke to Johnzo with my ideas for story gaming 4e and am using his advice wholeheartedly and finally gave it a whirl last week.

    WE HAVE HAD THE BEST TIME!!!!! We shared the DDI subscription for a month, made characters online (essentials only), printed off the sheets and some players have transferred these to their own sheets. We have used the Nentir vale / points of light setting as a sandbox with my using the principles and agenda from DW to 'steer the ship'. We only bring out the battlemap and minis/counters for 'big boss battles or quest finales' and at that point treat everything as per Rob Donohuge's treatise on 'situational HP', and the players know this. They grok the visual representation of the 4 main defences and the obstacles they represent: Circumvent - Reflex, Manipulate - Fortitude, Understand - Will, Smash - Armour Class. We can see the whole 'situation' in this light if required and make role-play connections as determined by the action on the battlemap. This makes it easy for me to prepare that particular encounter(s) ahead of time, whereas all the other 'rolls that matter' are handled as per Chatty DM's take on Skill Challenges, including inconsequential combats. I use an intent and task paradigm, with consequences of failure being spelled out quite extensively. Players help one another through justified skill use with roleplay. Narrative consequences have as much weight as mechanical ones too.

    Its almost as if the tactical 'module' of 4e has taken a back seat. We use it when we want to have a board-gamey tactical beat-em-up, and use a fairly basic iteration (as in what's presented in the red box) but don't have to use this if we don't want to. The story-game tension comes from a very 'play to see what happens' style of gaming that focuses on player specified quests and the stakes that revolve around their resolution. Rewards are world exploration and co-authored establishment, player defined quests and the chance to pursue them, unique magical items, levelling up and the superheroic status (and the narrative / story responsibility that comes with this power). Powers are justified as unique superheroic abilities for each character, the player can author this however they like, and they become a fairly valuable resource to be used narratively in as creative a way as possible following Quinn's 'off the grid' concepts. This is all wrapped up within the glorious technocolour feast that is 4e production. The 'Deluxe GM Screen', the cool collectible minis (a few of the players have tapped into this side of things, despite not actually using the minis much, they just like having them as 'toys'), tiles and postermaps and rulebooks.

    One thing that has become rather cool is the use of the counters from the Monster Box and the GM box for our relationship map, and location of the characters on the world map. This has seen far more gameplay than actual tactical skirmishry. I really like the wet-erase 'plain' map from pathfinder with all the characters tokens stuck to it with stickytack, relationship lines between other NPCs and villains and their importance noted, quests listed and possible rewards in plain view. We just look at that map at the start of play and ask where do we want to go from here?

    In short, I'm glad I waited. If I had started a 4e game when it first arrived on the scene with my regular Burning Wheel gang we would have HATED it. After seeing how systems matter and identifying what your group wants out of a table-top rpg, I've found 4e to be a rather lovable, quirky, grandiose and flamboyant game. It has its warts, but they are far more exorbitant and self-indulgent than the bumps I was used to from Moldvay 'back in the day'. Its still D&D to me; at its core a roleplaying game encapsulated within the mechanical parameters of a skirmish battle game in a fantasy milieu... but the modern, computer game version thereof. I like it, far more than I would have expected.

    \n I played in a couple of campaigns, one was ~12 sessions, the other was half of that. Plus I did a big pile of playtesting for Dungeon Construction Kit (which may yet be published some day, but D&Dnext has certainly put a crimp in its style -- or may yet be its saviour).

    \n When 4e came out, I was still in a regular group with John Harper, Tony Dowler, Brandon Amancio, and Ryan Dunleavy, and we played it weekly through that summer. Everyone but I took a turn as DM.

    John and Brandon actually ran simultaneous large setpiece battles at GPNW 2008, a week before the game came out, but unfortunately I wasn't able to get in on those games. However, later that week John did run a one-off session for us (plus Doug), and I got to play the eladrin fey-pact warlock, who I believe was a pre-gen from the pre-release kit that they'd used at GPNW. I thought the eladrin warlock was a lot of fun, and it made me very excited to play.

    As John said, when we started our regular game, he'd established several house rules dealing with earning and spending action points and dealing with skill challenges that proved to be key to keeping the game enjoyable. (If you look at his blog archive for June 2008, you can find some of them there.) These changes were key for two reasons: one, they replaced weak or uninteresting rules with strong rules that made the game more interesting (by giving us more options or more ways to engage the system); and two, they actually helped to make the game more challenging rather than less. Adding to the ways that we could spend action points, for example, made us more willing to take on seemingly impossible odds and go for the big win, which was more fun and exciting all around.

    In the regular game series, we were all servants of the Raven Queen, and even though we were low-level characters, John established up front that any higher-level servants had been slain or destroyed by Vecna, the Queen's chief opponent in this setting. That put us at the center of the action, which was a lot of fun, and we collaborated on coming up with cool setting details. On the one hand, like previous editions, 4e had the strength of being the default fantasy setting for fantasy RPGs, making it flexible and easy to adapt to our ideas. On the other hand, as other people in this thread have mentioned, in the books themselves there's practically no color at all to the point of being too bland and uninteresting. I do really like some of the meager setting details of the new edition, such as the change in the setup of the planes that resulted in the combination of the Feywild and the Shadowfell. But overall, the difference between the previous editions and this one as far as character and setting goes is like the (visual) difference between brand-name soup stock (Campbell's, Progresso, and so on) and generic black-lettering-on-white-label SOUP.

    As far as characters go, I started the regular game with a tiefling warlock (also fey-pact, IIRC), but I found I just couldn't get interested in the character and didn't enjoy it as much as the eladrin warlock. So after a session or two, I switched to playing a human cleric, choosing that mainly because we needed that role filled. Fortunately, I found it to be fairly enjoyable, and got to do some cool stuff with Turn Undead in a climactic battle in the Raven Queen's throne room. I still really wanted to play an eladrin warlock again, though, and even had one statted up and ready to play. However, that role was already filled, and when we switched DMs around, John and Ryan convinced me to give a paladin a try as that was now the role we needed in the party. That worked okay for me story-wise as I'd had an idea of playing a warforged paladin, which we decided was the embodied (and imprisoned) shell of a god that the Raven Queen had claimed, and that was cool. However, I found the paladin (defender role) even less enjoyable than I had my tiefling warlock. So on the one hand, I think the defined character roles is a good idea particularly for this tactical-battle version of D&D, and on the other hand I was willing to try out these characters I was less interested in, for the sake of party balance, and found that sure enough I just didn't enjoy them so much.

    I definitely liked some of the design choices such as defining everyone's abilities as powers available in a tiered system (at-will/encounter/daily), and making sure even a first-level wizard always has a good effective combat option instead of trying to devise some new way to hide from combat or otherwise figure out a way not to be completely helpless.

    As I said, I never ended up taking a turn as DM, mainly because I never felt ready and willing to give it a try before we collectively lost interest in the game. Looking back at it now, even though the game supposedly made it easy to create encounters, I think I would've felt hard pressed to come up with anything that I would think measured up to what the others had already done, and I don't believe I would've been happy running the game at all.

    And now that I've spent all this time thinking about the game\u2026 Johnzo's game sounds like a hell of a lot of fun, and I could imagine playing again in that game or others, but I'm also thinking about just giving away my 4e books\u2014maybe keeping the Player's Handbook just in case, but I can't imagine that I'll ever need the DM's Guide or Monster Manual that I have, and the Manual of the Planes was just disappointingly flat and mundane-feeling compared to the earlier editions.

    Finally, as for the board game versions, I've played Wrath of Ashardalon a few times, and I found it quickly bored me. Apparently taking that one extra step toward making 4e an actual board game was just too much for me.

I don't know when I will get back to Dark Sun but if I play it in D&D it would be in my favorite of the systems - 4e.

    \n What is D&D 4e to me? The only edition of D&D I've consistently enjoyed playing or running.

    By way of comparison/context:

    BECMI introduced me to formalized roleplaying, i.e., using dice and character sheets and whatnot instead of ad hoc let's-pretend. Playing it was a period of initial excitement--I really dug the solo adventure intro stuff--followed by a series of disappointments. I was disappointed when rolling up a character gave me something entirely different from what I was looking forward to playing. I was disappointed when all the graph-paper mapping the game told us to do turned out to be no fun at all. I was disappointed when the first game I tried running ended in a TPK with everyone paralyzed and eaten by the first monster to appear in the adventure. Never played again.

    AD&D 2e was unremarkable, unmemorable. The only game of it I really remember was a delve into Castle Ravenloft where one of the players had read about all the hazards in the place and twinked out his character to be ready for all of them. Maybe there was a cool game to be seen, but I never ran into it. I enjoyed the Baldur's Gate computer games based on it, though.

    The next edition I played, I don't remember how it's classified. It had a boxed set with a folder of cards that walked you through learning the game. I enjoyed the boardgame setup, running my friends through the first dungeon, though it lost its luster when we got to the point where you're supposed to start tracking encumbrance for all the gold you pick up. The first scenario ends at a trapdoor, beyond which the DM is supposed to build out a dungeon of his own with a white dragon in it. I started working on that, but grew numb rolling on all the charts, and it got shelved.

    3.0/3.5, like AD&D2, left little impression on me. I remember enjoying the idea of picking out a Prestige Class and building your character toward attaining it, then becoming disillusioned when I realized it'd be years of play before I ever achieved those prereqs, and it was highly unlikely we'd play that long. I remember playing a spellcaster and failing, session after session, to guess what spells would be useful for the day's adventuring. Again, maybe there was a cool game waiting to be found, but I sucked at it so bad that I didn't enjoy it much.

    By the time D&D4 came out, though, I'd had some experience with story games, and brought those techniques and analysis to bear. I recognized that the game was first and foremost built to deliver a fun board-game combat experience--imagine that, a D&D that chooses something to be about, and designs to it!--and told my players up front, "This is going to be like a video game. I'm planning to play that up all the way, with quest journals, loot drops, boss battles, and the like." It worked pretty well! People didn't fret too much about the verisimilitude of "powers" and whatnot, because, well, they're video game tropes, you roll with them.

    I also "storygamed it up." I don't care for the traditional GM-player divide with players having control of nothing outside the reach of their respective characters , so I introduced some new rules to allow PC players to introduce new setting elements, NPCs, plot twists, etc. using a deck of special cards. I expanded on the idea of "personal quests" to get some Flags action in there. And I periodically (some two or three times per tier) ran RP-heavy, situation-based sessions using Conflict Webs.

    As a non-GM player, I enjoyed this edition more than any previous one too. I played an Artful Dodger Rogue from 1st through 20th level, and got a huge kick out of the turn-by-turn maneuvering, developing the character build, and acquiring and mastering more and more tricks and tactics as the character progressed. I had some memorable character development and RP moments too, which dovetailed nicely with the effective use of powers, items, and teamwork to deal with the tactical situation at hand. It had the pleasure of dropping a clever card combo in Magic: The Gathering or Ascension, but with the ongoing story, character banter, and social context I love from RPGs.

    To say I "consistently," "reliably" enjoyed the game is not to say it's been perfect, though. I definitely had some sessions that did not work, from both sides of the screen. The main problem was grind: battles taking way too long due to poor encounter design, a DM choosing to drag out a foregone conclusion all the way to last-man-standing, and/or players who deliberated too long over their moves for the turn (reflective more of a poor match of player preferences to the game being played than any criticism of the players themselves). The bloat in character management due to way too many, mostly crappy feats and magic items is simply inexcusable. And most DMs I know, self included, have implemented some manner of house rules to deal with the facepalm-inducing math errors in the levelup progression.

    To sum up, D&D4 is the edition I'll remember fondly, the only one I'll have nostalgia for. I'm considering the idea of, when Next comes out, giving 4e the OSR treatment and writing a game that codifies and enhances all the tweaks, houserules, and informal play procedures that made it work for me, while fixing the fundamental flaws in the game I can no longer ignore. (4e's weaksauce use of fictional positioning, for instance.) I like to think I'm not a "fanboy", since I do notice and acknowledge those very real failures of the game. But when it comes to edition loyalty, this is the one I stand by, and I'd say I like it, warts and all, more than I do even a number of indie favorites!

    \nLet me start with, I started gaming with a mish-mosh of Basic D&D and AD&D back in the late 70's early 80's and then moved on to other games. Fast Forward. 4e comes out and my playing group and I bash the images that herald its release. Cards? What is this thing a boardgame? Insert various other jokes.
    \n

Hit Point bloat, interrupts, powers that key off the a grid, etc. But I really did come to love the game and I think that was from DMing it A LOT. At the time I had a steady group of players and their experience was mixed. Some never got passed the abstraction block, for some it didn't matter and just liked playing. I don't believe any of them ever liked the game as much as I did/do, but none of them seemed to just HATE it with a passion that some seem to.
    \n

I'm not saying that is all there is to the game, just that it is designed to do combat on a grid well. A new, weak, lazy DM will tend to just go that route and then the game can drag and become boring. In that incarnation, yeah you are better off playing it as a 2 hour miniature wargame.
    \n
    \nNow, I'm running Castles & Crusades (because I wanted to dip into that "old school" goodness pool) and playing some Dragon Age. But I miss 4e and hope that 5e keeps the 4e flame alive while getting rid of its warts.

    \n 4E is the pinnacle of D&D game design. It took D&D into the 21st century, much in the same way that 3E took D&D into the 90's. In that regards, 4E was much more forward than 3E.

    I loved it and couldn't get enough. I was playing in two campaigns and running one, even though they were pretty sporadic (once every month or two) it was the most gaming I'd done in a while, and it's still the most gaming I've done since. It's fantastically flexible, both in terms of how you can set up your own world, and how and which rules you might use (e.g. more skill tests vs combats or whatever). The rules are very distinct, allowing you to build fiction flavor into all kinds of categories. For example, you could decide there are no Paladins, which is nothing new, or you could remove all Divine classes, which is something that would have been difficult and potentially unbalancing in older editions.

    But the downsides started to wear on me. First, a lot of the flexibility that is there outside of combat, such as the variety available with skill tests, is pretty flat and doesn't easily provide the players with interesting choices. There's few levers for players outside of deciding to roll or not. I constantly wanted to hack in some kind of Aspect system, but when I tried it just felt tacked on and didn't really seem to do the trick. There is a ton of narrative power given to the DM, but that power doesn't extend to the players except by the DM granting it, and even then it's lackluster compared to other story focused games.

    The combat is fantastic, and it's what I love most about it. But it got to be a paint to prepare for. Some of this work was my own, making tokens for the maps and all of that. But there was also the work of finding and selecting monsters, and printing out their stat blocks (which despite all of the online materials, this was a pain and inconsistent in quality - no thanks to WotC for not having PDFs available). It would be a few hours of preparation. And combats themselves were a separate game. There was no storytelling happening, except for the specific encounter itself designed by the DM (which is hardly an efficient use of the 45-90 minutes a combat would take). It was like we'd stop playing our roleplaying game to play a board game based on our game for a bit, and then pick up the story after that. Fun, but jarring and a lot of the time it seemed like most players at the table liked one part much more than another, and usually that part was "not combat."

    In the end, I stopped playing in all the games I was in, and even though I wanted to start up new ones, I didn't want to face all those downsides. It was too much effort to make 4E into a roleplaying game so I could enjoy the board game, when I could have just played a roleplaying game. Still, 4E was the most roleplaying game out of any edition of D&D. It was just still too far behind where my interests are in game design.

      \n 4e is in many ways my least favorite edition: there's no reason to do anything but run combat encounters. Exploration wasn't an important thing in the rules, but it turns out it was a big deal to me. It also irked me that I dropped money on the pretty books only to learn 3 weeks later that a new edition was on the way.

      That being said, I'm running a game for some coworkers. We've had some good fun with it, but I will likely abandon 4e after this run. I'm finding that for new players, the amount of information is so large, they can't efficiently calculate moves when it's "their turn." I more often then not feel as if everything else that goes on in 4e outside of "their turn" is exhausting.
