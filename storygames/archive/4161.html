\n So, I was listening to the Grimm Studio Podcast this morning and they started talking about "indie" or "story" games in general and Spirit of the Century in particular. I like the 'cast and they seem nice as well, but I felt their comments were off base and, filled with spit and vinegar, I jumped onto their site and posted a reply. They were very welcoming in their reply and I almost regretted my earlier irrascible tone...almost

With some trepidation, I thought I would cross-post here as well. Actually, I think they bring up interesting points that come up when speaking to 'traditional' gamers on the topic of 'story' games.\n
I am in the middle of listening to your comments on indie games and find myself gnashing my teeth and screaming at my iPod. I don't personally care if you like story games or not, but I do think you should at least know what you're talking about before you speak. A few points:

1. Speaking about all indie games while talking about only one of them (Spirit of the Century) is patently unfair.
2. You obviously have never played Spirit of the Century. Your point of view about the game would be more relevant if you know what you were talking about.

To clarify. Yes Spirit of the Century codifies traits called Aspects. Aspects are how you tell the GM what you want the story to be "about." If you have the "Don't tell me the odds" aspect, you are saying "I want my character to get into risky situations." Its not about GM railroading. You choose it because you want it. Contrast that to D&D where you may roll up a thief because you want to be cool and sneaky but the story may be all about battle fields or gladiator combat.

Second, the players are all involved in creating story in the game. That means the players want conflict and complications for their own characters. You mention the GM can force the character's hand by putting a chip down. That's called a compell in the game. The GM *offers* the character a chip for having his aspect create further complications in the story, because the GM is saying "I think it would be cool story if your character did this...". The player has chips as well and if he does not like the GMs offer he can pay a chip to get out of it, essentially saying "I don't think that sounds like cool story." If the player he accepts, he gets the chip. They're called Fate Points, btw. The player spends Fate Points to invoke his aspect in a conflict to do awesome things, like get a +2 to his roll (which is a huge + in SOTC). So if the player has "I always protect little critters" as an aspect and she's fighting to protect fluffy, the player can spend a fate point and say "but, you know, 'I always protect little critters!" and invoke their aspect to be more awesome.

Lastly, the player can compell his own negative aspects. They may be chasing the bad guy but want to add further complication by seizing narrative control. They compell their aspect "I always protect little critters!" this time getting a Fate Point rather than spending one, and say "I see fluffy caught in a tree and a wrecking ball is swinging towards her, what do I do?" And that's the other cool thing. You, the player, can spend fate points to put aspects on scenes to use in conflict like "Its dark in that corner" or "The ground is uneven." You can also put aspects on your oponent rather than damage, like "I push you and you're off balance" or "there's sand in your eyes." Then other players can compell those aspects to get benefits.

My last point is that you may hate spirit of the century and games like it, which is fine with me. But I think you should play the game first, speak second.

My problem, specifically with SotC, is "he can pay a chip to get out of it," There's mechanics for the GM to force your character in a direction. Eventually the player runs out of chips. Don't take our dislike of part of a system as condemnation of a game. I've got huge issues with large parts of D&D, I still play it. So, take the conversation, using SotC as an example, was more about a style rules, over a condemnation of a specific game.

    Let's talk Star Wars.

    Anakin's running around with an aspect, Temptations from the Dark Side.

    The GM compels it, with a sort of, "Don't you think it would be worth your time to listen to the Senator?"

    Do you think it should cost Anakin nothing to resist the Dark Side? Especially given that he put Temptations from the Dark Side as an aspect on his sheet, already flagging it as a cool thing he wants story to happen around? From where I stand, the answer to that question is "no".

    IME, players accept compels 90% of the time -- the aspects on their sheet are there and compellable because that's where the heart of their character's story lives.

    That remaining 10%, where they want to resist it, gains a feeling of real significance when they have to pay for it. Not having to pay for it can have the effect of cheapening the story value of resisting the temptation.

    \n The hosts' comments about Spirit of the Century were quite ignorant. Interestingly, I think the reason for their scoffing can be traced right back to early forge discussion (and before? historians, help!). It is a trickle-down misconception, which is this idea of GM Railroading as the ultimate sin, rather than just an application of GM force, a valuable tool.

    The forge eventually mellowed on this topic, but to maintain the negative connotations of the word, it now defines railroading as "When GM force breaks the social contract". But in the common vocabulary, Railroading is like a swastika in your game in terms of its ability, as a buzzword, to incite instant condemnation.

    Regarding the original thread topic specifically, I'd like to point out that the rules don't specifically prohibit a player from negotiating the terms of a compel into one that preserves the character's status better, or whatever. This is something I have flagged for more explicit mention in future Fate games.

    Example: SotC has a classic love triangle between the iconic characters - Sally Slick has a "Hidden Crush" on Mack Silver, and Jet Black has "Unspoken Love" for Sally Slick. Mack... well, he has "A Girl in Every Port".

    So when Russian supervillain Rocket Red seduces Mack and tries to steal Mack's hi-tech plane, the Century Clipper, for Mother Russia, leaving Jet and Sally on the tarmac as the plane takes off, there's potential for this love triangle to create some tension. I'm talking specifically about the three-player SotC demo I ran to death at GenCon, from which this scenario comes.

    So, in one of the demos, I compel Jet Black's player, and say, "Mack Silver is always doing this kind of shit, and there's probably nothing suspicious going on. You just know Sally's going to check things out and get jealous and disappointed because he's taking some girl he met last night for a joyride. You totally need to convince her to stay on the ground."

    Instead of buying off the compel, the player looks at me and says, "No... actually, here's what the deal is. I'm tired of him always doing this crap to Sally and hurting her feelings, because I love her, and I'm going to fly up there and beat the living tar out of Mack Silver and give him a piece of my mind, regardless of whether or not he's really in trouble."

    Naturally, I gave him the fate point and looked at Mack's player and said, "Man, you're in deep shit."

    Really, what a compel does is signal that drama should play a greater role in resolving events than dice. If the GM throws down for a compel, it's a signal that he or she wants to see something interesting and dramatic happen, for things to get complicated, and the specific terms are the one idea he or she came up with. That doesn't necessarily mean it's the GMs way or the highway - if your GM uses compels to establish absolute authority over your character, dunk their head in the damn toilet, repeatedly, and then exit to the nearest pub.

    Instead, the player should look for an opportunity to call for their own dramatic complication, one that might fit how they envision their character better.

    So, you have "Greedy as a Greedy Fool", and a big crime boss is offering you money to sell out your friends. The GM holds up the tasty fate point. You can say, "You know... I don't see him really selling out his friends. But I do see him lying to this guy about it to get the money, and this guy finding out about it later." If I were the GM there, I'd go, "Make an enemy of a big crime boss in exchange for automatic success on Deceit? Deal," and hand the point over.

    Just sayin'. Many things can be remedied by the bringing of your own awesome.

      \n Buzz is hinting at it, but I'll come out and say it:

      My preference in gaming is for no single participant to be in a position of ultimate authority over "what happens next."

      Here's what I mean by that:
      Player 1: "I stab the Duke to death."
      Player 2: "Nah... he runs away."

      What happens next? I don't want one player to just decide and then that's that. I want there to be an interaction, a series of proposals, events, reversals, and results (moderated by a non-player-controlled factor, like dice) that lead to an outcome. For most games dealing with the above, this means we engage the combat system, over which no single participant has ultimate authority. It would be weird, I think, in most games, for the GM or a player to just decide that the Duke is stabbed to death because the description of the stabbing was just so good or right or whatever.

      So, then:

      Player 1: "I convince the Duke to let the Prince go."
      Player 2: "Nah... he isn't swayed by your appeal."

      What happens next? Do we roleplay it out, and then one player just decides the outcome? That puts one player in a privileged position over the outcome. In most cases, that is probably the person playing the Duke. The Duke's player is in total control over what convinces him and what doesn't, and she controls the outcome of all attempts to persuade him. A lot of people like this.

      But I don't. I don't ever want one player to just decide the outcome of any situation. That's why I use game mechanics.       \n It seems to me that neither side of this debate likes to admit that there are advantages and drawbacks to both methods.

      Some advantages of player-empowering narrativist systems are: 1) greater player control over the effects their character has on the external world 2) shared culpability if someone is unhappy with the events of a game 3) Less work for the GM

      Some advantages of traditional GM-player systems are: 1) gives PCs the experience of exploring a world rather than the experience of writing one 2) allows for more detailed preparation of adventure content 3) metagame social conflicts (you know, actual fights) are more easily resolved because of an appointed arbiter.

      Since this thread has narrowed to be specifically about rules for social combat, i'll point out that there are advantages to both sides of this argument as well.

      some advantages of social combat systems are 1) they even the playing field for players that are shy, or don't like to roleplay interaction 2) they provide an strategic, gameable system for a different kind of fight 3) they disallow the possibility of GM stonewalling

      some advantages of freeform social resolution are 1) They even the playing field for players who are good at interacting but not good at gaming strategy 2) more flexibility for the PCs in solving social situations 3) If the GM is not stonewalling, characters have a better chance of getting what they want from NPCs

      \n Ok, Buzz first.

      We actually talked a little bit about this in our section on character deaths on our podcast. Is it ok to kill a character due to a random dice roll. I always, as a GM, felt a little guilty when it happened. Then Jim pointed something out to me.

      Rarely, if ever, is the situation calling for the die roll random. Indy isn't going to die in the spikes because Winnie didn't make here will save. He's going to die because he brought a 12 year old into a pit of death and destruction and then had the nerve to act upset when he failed a roll. The risk was calculated before the situation was entered. Now, he got out of that one ok. But he stepped into it knowing the risk.

      If your game is: Ok, I wake up in the morning. Roll will save. Sorry you die. Then, no, that sucks. If it's, I see the pit, it's unlikely I'll make it but I have to try. Then you CHOSE to take the chance. You chose that to be the story. Pass or fail. Live or die. I offset my odds by taking a running start. Modifier up. etc. If you're new to such things, the GM and table should take that in consideration, and maybe offer some suggestions. But not do it for you. Let you tell your story up to the point of chance, then see what chance has in store. The whole table holds their breath.... and then screams with joy, or cries with despair.

      In our example on our podcast, it was a mine shaft where the elevator had been cut. It was the only way down (for various story related reasons), and they had to get to the bottom to rescue a husband of a PC from some slavers. They were fixing rope, considering their odds, deciding who should go first, swearing a blue streak that no one took rope use or climb, but pushing ahead anyway. Of the 7 players, Jim, Katie, Anders died. Amber almost did. And the others had to decide if they would push on, or turn back.

      Everyone made the choice. Jim walked around it, and even said out loud "I bet my death is in that pit". Granted, he survived to the bottom, but found himself alone, fighting bugbears over the body of Katie who fell some 60'. Amber, took some daring jumps to help with the fight. She was too late to save him, but was able to clear the landing for the rest of the team to crawl down slower. Clinging to life, she hid somewhere safe until she could signal the rest to come down.

      Every death, though painful, added to the story. Rescuing Connie's husband (her back story choice, not one pushed on her) became a heroic tale, and not just another crawl. Each death weighed on the people taking the risk to go further. Some were stuck half way down, knowing they now had to take a very real risk to go back or forward. The weight of each player falling pulled at the characters. All continued knowing that their character lived or died on a roll for a skill they didn't have.

      I asked them at the end, if they had fun. It was unanimous. The new players, old players, everyone. Each dice roll was an adrenalin rush. The attempts to catch people as they fell past. The wishing they had thought of different ways to approach the problem. And finally completing the mission and deciding to retire their characters. So much death had broken the character's spirits, but not the players. They already had ideas for their next characters, and couldn't wait.

      I'm going to start blending this into the noclue question as I move on here. I think that aspect of mortality is something that adds to games. Or failure. A game is something with an indeterminate outcome. It may be fun to hear/watch an exciting story, and wonder how the hero overcomes challenges, knowing that they're ok, but wondering how it's all going to play out. But if I know the ending to my story I get this sense of drudgery when I get to an obstacle. I know that if I fail to succeed there will just be another opportunity to make up for it shortly. It may cost me more, but the ultimate 'the end' is already written. We're just filling in precut pieces to determine the route.

      Between the two experiences, I'll take the risk.

      Now, like I was just saying, about Indy. Are his movies boring? No. Tense, not in the least. But as much as I try to take the intellectual route, it's fun to watch things blow up. I like pillaging it for ideas for me to try and use in my own gaming. Situations that might come up. But then, Indy isn't something I keep coming back to. I'll watch it from time to time, to get ideas, hear my favorite lines again, and relive some of the days I used to pretend I was him. The reality is, can you watch Indy every week for years? The answer is no. If this was a serial it would get boring.

      But gaming is something you keep coming back to. Weekly, monthly (some dorms daily). Leaving the possibility of real failure out of the mix is disheartening, and ultimately will become borring. "There is much to be said for failure. It is more interesting than success." - Max Beerbohm, 1946
