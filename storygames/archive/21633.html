\n I want a roll over d20 system, but I want to cap it at 20 (meaning 20 is the highest possible result). But I don\u2019t want descending abilities and so on (3 STR is better than 8).

So perhaps there is something that has your ability/skill/trait score reduce the difficulty. Example: your character has an AGILTY of 6 and the DN is 10. The difference is 4, so roll over 4 to succeed. If your ability is greater than the DN, then you automatically succeed. Any special talents can give you Advantage. If you are hindered then Disadvantage.

Opposed rolls work a bit different. Lets say you have a COMBAT skill of 7 and the Goblin is a 5. The difference is 2, so you roll 3d20 and keep highest, while the Goblin rolls 1d20. Or you are arm-wrestling a friend, you\u2019ve got a STR of 5 and your friend is a 9. You roll 1d20, while your friend rolls 4d20 keeping the highest.

Is there anything out there that does this or is similar?

    \nDead of Night uses something like this: you have a stat score ranging from 1-9, with an average of 5. When you attempt a task, you roll 2d10+Stat vs. a target number: the default target is 15 for unopposed tasks, but when you enter a conflict against another character, the target is 10+their stat.

    For example, my PC has Identify=6, so when they try to decipher an old scroll, I roll 2d10, add those results together and then add 6 for my stat. I have to equal or beat 15, so the law of averages says I ought to succeed about half the time.

    If I try to Identify someone's motives, however, then the TN becomes 10+their Obscure stat, so the better they are at hiding things & lying, the harder this becomes.

    James_Mullen said:

    the law of averages says I ought to succeed about half the time.

    \nI am not familiar with that law. But 2d10+6 has a 72% chance of being 15 or higher.

    So somewhere between 1d20+8 and 1d20+9 when it comes to beating 15. People's intuition of probability becomes wonky when it comes to multiple dice, even just two.

    When coming up with conversion formulas I usually try to find a single point of reference, match it up at that particular spot, and then let the rest of it stray.

    Examples:

    If I wanted 2d10+6 to matchup with 1d20+6 when it comes to facing off against each other, X = 6.5 or somewhere between 6 and 7.

    When it comes to facing off against 15, X is somewhere between 8 and 9.

    I converted AW's (and specifically Seclusium of Ophone) "2d6+X vs 7 and 10" to "1d20+Y vs Z and W". I knew I couldn't find a set of Z and W that matched up for every single X and Y so I chose 1 for X (since that's been a common enough mod in the PbtA games where I've had the most fun) and 3 for Y (since that's been a common enough mod in the D&D games where I've had the most fun) to get the DCs 12 and 20 (which I since use for my "twelve/twenty" whenever I want to use an AW-style move). I liked it because it happened to match up with a slight bit of parallel growth by characters from either system.

    I converted Vein's of the Earth's roll-under climbing tables to 5e's saving throws by comparing the average starting LotFP character with the average starting 5e character discounting their two save proficiencies. I liked the idea that your class' choice of save profs could matter in this circumstance.

    If higher: roll two dice, take highest

    Roll over DC with dice to succeed.

    \n I like Vincent's best, so far.

    However, it's not really "highest is best", is it? It's only *kind of* that. If we're going to let go of "rolling higher numbers is always better", then my favourite (which I use in my House D&D) is the "Blackjack Roll". Basically, you want to roll as high as possible, but not go too high. So high is good, but only to a point.

    Here is how:

    * You have some number (a stat, whatever) which tells us how good you are at your best. Perhaps it's a personal record, for example.

    * Then you roll a d20. If you roll less than or equal to the number, your roll stands. If you roll over the stat, you've pushed too hard and overreached. Throw away the die - your result means nothing, you screwed up or circumstances were against you.

    * On a 1, you always fail. On a 20, you exceed your normal limitations and always succeed, miraculously.

    * The "quality" of your effort/success is the number you've rolled.

    For instance, I roll a 12, so I'm winning the race. You roll an 11, so you're close but not quite. Jimmy, though, rolled a 15 and he's not that good to begin with, so he just failed altogether.

    Like Vincent's method, it means high numbers aren't *always* good but generally the concept is there. A 20 is best, otherwise get the highest result you can.

    The beautiful thing is how it does all the d20 math without anyone ever having to do anything but just calling out a number. ("I rolled a 12!" "I rolled a 14!" "I missed the roll." "OK, Sandra wins this one with a 14!")

    I also like the low numbers. (For example, if it's D&D, enemies' Armor Class might range from 1 to 6.)

    The downside is the cap on the range (you can't have a meaningful stat above 19, unless you want to start doing Vincent's "add 10 when you roll below" beyond that range, which solves that problem), but I can't imagine what game I would need a d20 and more than a 18-point spread of stats for.

    Another fun thing is that you can make the distinction between a "miss" (rolled over your stat) and a "fail" (rolled under the enemy's number/target/Armor Class), if your game would benefit from that. (You can have some serious fun with that one! Like whether a blow hits a shield or hits your armour, whether the opponent gets to do a thing, etc.)

    A final fun thing is the natural way you can create a gamble for "extraordinary results". That works like this:

    * Roll your die. If you didn't fail, what was the number? Leave it on the table.

    * Want to push it? Ok, roll another d20. Now the number showing on the first die is your target number/stat/ability.

    * Repeat if desired.

    For example, my stat is a 15. I roll and I get a 10. Do I want to push it? Ok, I roll again. If I get under 10, it worked... it was a 5. Do I want to push again? Now my target would be a 5 or less.

    Lots of fun stuff you can do with that, as you can imagine.

    \n Okay, I\u2019m sold on the baseline idea. Thanks everyone for all of your feedback and suggestions!!!

    There are some issues that must be addressed, but I think they can be managed.

    To refresh:

    D20 roll-over TN design. 1 and 20 are critical failure or success.

    If you roll under your ability score you get your score anyway (except natural 1).

    Example:

    Batman has a fight of 7, so if he rolls a 1, it's a 1 and a fail; if he rolls 2-7, it counts as 7, and if he rolls 8+, it stands (20 is a critical success).

    Robin has a fight of 5, so if he rolls a 1, it's a 1 and a fail; if he rolls 2-5, it counts as 5, and if he rolls 6+, it stands (20 is a critical success).

    Dis|Advantage for certain situations. Roll 2d20 and keep highest or lowest.

    Benefits: Roll over d20 that is capped at 20. Minimal math and modifiers. You can look at the die and get the result quickly.

    Issues: Using the example, if they are facing an enemy stronger than Batman, both have the same chance of winning against that enemy. But if the enemy is weaker than Batman or weaker than both, then they have different chances.

    Other thoughts:

    Perhaps in cases where you automatically succeed (Stat is higher than TN) you gain Advantage. You roll only because you\u2019re hoping the extra die gives you a shot at a critical. Not sure yet, but I\u2019m thinking there is a lot of room here for some interesting effects.

    Or this, the amount you\u2019re skill is over the TN, you increase the chance of a critical success. Example: Batman has a climb skill of 8 and the TN is a 6. The difference is 2, so chance of critical is 18-20. You could flip that as well. Say the TN is 11. The difference is 3. So chance of critical failure is 1-4. This involves some maths, so might now be as attractive to some.

    Another option is, you could just remove the critical success or failure altogether. Only give the chance of a crit on rolls where you would automatically succeed. Example: Batman has a Sneak of 10, and the TN is 8. He doesn\u2019t need to roll. However he can take the small risk of rolling a 1 (5%) for a chance at success. If he rolls above the TN, he not only succeeds\u2014but gets to keep a d20 to use on a later roll.

    \n That's very interesting, but it has some really bizarre and strange side-effects (I know it's not a surprise to anyone - Sandra's analysis shows these things - but it's something to consider if you're going to use this approach).

    First, rolling becomes almost... pointless... for high-ability characters. We pretty much know what result you will get, every time. We know that, unless you roll a 1, you succeed at anything which is below or equal to your score. If we're using the full range of numbers (1-20), as D&D does, those towards the top aren't served well by rolling. There's little uncertainty and *most* rolls will be throw-aways ("Ah, I guess I got a 17 again...").

    (On the other hand, for low scores, it works quite nicely.)

    Second, as Sandra points out, in contests, it's only the better score which matters. In some ways this is interesting, but in other ways it could feel silly, depending on what kinds of things you do in your game.

    For example, if your score is a 17, and you face a monster with a score of 16, you can defeat it almost all the time. However, a slightly tougher monster (17), and you'll have a tie on almost every roll. And if the monster is just one more point tougher (18), then you have practically no chance at all!

    This sounds to me like it could make it very hard to calibrate the game. For instance, a dungeon full of DC 17 challenges would be insurmountable to my friend with his Stat of 16, but a complete piece of cake to my other friend, who's got an 18. Can they even adventure together? Can I present a variety of challenge levels to them?

    Does this mean that DC 15 challenges are the SAME to all adventurers who don't have a 15 or higher, too?

    Depending on what kind of game you're designing, it could be tricky.

    However, I kind of like the idea that, when two opponents are closely matched, even a slight advantage on one side flips the odds completely; there's some interesting in that!
