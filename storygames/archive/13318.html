What sort of game would be the appetizer?
The dessert?
(Bonus question: The wine?)

    \n Appetizer: tapas-like selection of gaming warm-ups, tooled to the theme of the main course but made so that the players throw away the material generated during them and are emboldened for the main course, and their appetites whetted -

    Dessert: here i would put a gaming poem, one that commented backwards on the main course somehow, perhaps how PowerKill works as a dessert for D&D or Shadowrun; here i'm thinking that a dessert for, say, Love in the Time of Seith could bring an interesting character ten years into the future, and have all the players involve themselves in some event with them... one character, many players... or like a game of inspectres could have most players interview a single inspectre for their weekly talk-show. Or a game about how the events of the main course ripple out into the world.

    ie, we expand on the style of game that the author of PowerKill created.

    and for the Wine, certainly we play a second game at the same time as the main game: one that comments on the main game, but never (NEVER!) steals the show. I would look to Brendan Adkins' con-game Rubble as my inspiration... and i would suggest that the Wine is affected by the main game but does not affect back, and that players may only modify the state of the Wine when they are away from the table that the main course is being played at. I'm thinking of a board, thinking of something that can be understood by looking at it. How rich will it be? i don't know. The Wine mustn't be strategic.

    Those are my thoughts on the Wine.


    Matthijs, i love these ideas.

    \n I think a roleplaying poem could be considered an appetizer, but a good appetizer will also reveal some of the flavours and concepts that you'll be more deeply exploring in the main course. A good appetizer primes you for the rest of the meal.

    And so, character creation, grid creation, pitch sessions... these are appetizers. They give you an appetite for the real meal to follow.

    Some appetizers are bold and playful (Spirit of the Century's character creation), some are simple and rely on a creative pairing of a few choice elements (In A Wicked Age's oracle drawings). Some are a buffet of cheeses and crackers and tidbits that you get to pair to create your own tantalizing creations (GURPS character creation).

    Sometimes, rather than putting out appetizers per se, you have your guests in the kitchen alongside you, mixing and tasting and spicing and cooking together (Shock:'s grid, PTA's pitch session).
