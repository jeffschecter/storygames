\n So I started this seemingly innocent discussion in which I asked for help statting up a new character for the Apocalypse World game in which I'm playing. The concept was borrowed from Dogs in the Vineyard: gun-toting holy man comes to town and stirs things up with his rigid definition of what's right and what's wrong by the standards of his faith. It seemed like a solid enough approach and dovetailed well with the other two characters being put forth.

Then I was told that I didn't understand what Apocalypse World is about. Apparently it's about playing schlubs who never amount to anything and when a player makes a decision for their character, it's about getting screwed over. Which leads me to ask: who the hell would want to play such a game?

I don't play roleplaying games to be a chartered accountant. If I want to go to college, settle down, have kids and cruise into retirement age I can play The Game of LIFE. And if I want to be punished for every decision I make regardless of their merit or lack thereof I can spend an afternoon talking with my dad.

To be fair to Apocalypse World, I don't think that's really what the game's about. There might be challenges that a character faces and they may not all go well. My hardened preacher-type may be forced to deal with the consequences of his own actions or even into facing the rectitude of his path. That's a game worth playing. I don't think anyone in his right mind would design a game that specifically sets out to subvert player expectations and goals.

I've raised some issues over time about playing difficult roles and/or roleplaying challenging situations, but never once have I ever heard it even hinted that the game itself could become the enemy and that the characters portrayed should be as limp and ineffective as possible. I ask again: who would want to play a game like that? And more importantly, why?!?

Certainly there are some who find the roleplaying of damaged or inadequate people in bad situations makes for a good time. I'm going to play a game of Fiasco next week and I'm sure there'll be a lot of that. But in the case of such a game, it's not a question of punishing players but of seeing who can come up with the next colossal goatfuck that will drive the outrageous humor. It's the point of the game and rewards players for screwing themselves over. The way Apocalypse World's being tarred, though, it sounds like an exercise in auto-flagellation.

I guess what I'm asking is: why are you playing your games? Do you derive satisfaction from being dissatisfied, or something else? Does it makes sense to create a game whose only function is to crush the spirits of those who play it? Would you play such a game? And what's all this about playing schlubs? Is that really something you'd want to do?

    \n I can't speak to Apocalypse World too specifically but I think I may be able to better articulate what Ralph was trying to get at.

    There's a difference between playing The Preacher, which is a heroic archetype and playing a self-righteous man willing to back up his beliefs with a gun. Ralph is saying you can do the latter but you can't do the former. This distinction in my mind is very clear but it is very difficult to describe. A heroic archetype commands a certain kind of automatic attention and respect on the part of the authors and audience (not necessarily the fictional people of the world). I think Ralph is saying that Apocalypse World does not give you the tools to deliver that attention and respect. If on the other hand, you want to play a self-righteous man willing to back up his beliefs with a gun, that's just find. We just don't know and can't control how much attention and respect he's going to get.

    What Ralph is saying reminds me a lot of how Spione plays. Spione is Ron Edward's spy fiction game that is about as far away from James Bond as you can imagine. It specifically takes place in cold-war era Berlin and the two (always two) spies involved in the story are just ordinary people on the take from the other side. The game is largely about watching the job of being a spy slowly erode away everything the principles care about.

    Or take a look at Seth-Ben Ezra's jeepform inspired game A Flower For Mara in which the players are simply members of a family dealing with the death of loved one. The game involves revealing and sharing a real world grief that you personally struggle with. I've played it and cried very, very heavily while doing it. And yet, I enjoyed it and I'd play it again in heart beat.

    For me, I like intimate personal stories and games like this deliver that in spades.

    Posted By: Sam HThen I was told that I didn't understand whatApocalypse Worldis about. Apparently it's about playing schlubs who never amount to anything and when a player makes a decision for their character, it's about getting screwed over. Which leads me to ask: who the hell would want to play such a game?
    \nBecause it's probably not about getting screwed over, but getting screwed over is something that happens in game to create an emergent behaviour (a little fruitful void theory there).

    Many RPGs use a heroic archetype that involves people wandering about being badass. But what of these other games? Let me first set up the assumption that many RPGs establish, that the fun in games is derived from achieving success, and power, in whatever form the system lists, is the means to success, and that's the core of that heroic archetype.

    But RPGs let you choose your own goals, so what if you choose goals wherein power and badassery won't help you? I'm talking about goals of understanding and empathy (as those issues came up in another Sam H thread), about exploring and finding answers to complex and subtle moral decisions, about navigating cultural and social interplay, and much more. There are three forces that really determine what sorts of problems you'll grapple with in a game, your character design, the gamemaster (or whoever designs challenges) and the game itself. It's best if all three work together. If Apocalypse World doesn't encourage wandering badassery, it probably explores something else. Figure out what it does do, then decide if there's any part of that you want to play.

    It is because my choice of different goals that characters for D&D and similar games (especially superhero RPGs) look like a bunch of schlubs who are just set up to fail, no matter how many enemies they defeat. To that end, I create and play games about characters designed to face the sorts of problems that interest me in pursuit of the goals that interest me. To you the characters in those games may look like schlubs, but to me they look more heroic, more successful, than any badass gun-toting priest could ever be.

    \n I won't speak for Vincent, but for myself so far, all of his game designs see to me to be about taking rather ordinary (or at least not super-heroic) people, putting them in a difficult situation and cranking up the pressure on them to see whether they break, become monsters, or walk away (or more rarely, find some other solution).
    \n
    \nI don't know if he coined the term but asking "even now?" is pretty much a Baker hallmark.
    \n
    \nIn Dogs you're teenagers recruited into a job that will ultimately destroy you if you don't find a way to walk away from it with your soul intact.
    \n
    \nIn Poison'd you're a run of the mill scurvy dog of a pirate pushed from one impossible situation to another until you hang, make a deal with the devil, or find away to get out of the life...with as much of your soul left as you can (or choose to) salvage.
    \n
    \nStorming the Wizard's tower is a little more gentle on the crucible aspect (at least the first chapter that we've seen) but again while you're people of some recognition in your community...its a small community and you're definitely not that super. You're plunged immediately into a series of problems that threaten to destroy or irrevocably alter that community. I predict that future chapters ramp-up the screw-down aspects much higher...though likely not as high as Dogs.
    \n
    \nIn Apocalypse World you're survivors...which makes you at least a little bit special in that the vast majority of people failed to survive. But beyond that you're pretty typical of your archetype. You might be the leader of a biker gang...but its a pretty small gang and there are likely dozens more even larger out there. You might be a hard holder and responsible for 100s of lives, but you're always right at the brink of having that taken away. In fact, the MC instructions for creating fronts and making moves with them is pretty clear in that regard as I recall. There's alot of good specifics but mostly it boils down to "figure out what's important to the character and then figure out a way to destroy it or take it away from them". Again...its a crucible designed to crush you...to see whether you break, or become a monster, or more rarely find some other solution.
    \n
    \nCharacters in a Baker game become memorable because of what they did during play, not who they were before play. I'd say that's a pretty universal non-controversal Lumpley Games trait. Character generation in all of his games is absolutely ruthless at preventing "play before you play" syndrome.
    \n
    \nThis may be more controversal, but I think if you spend more than 1-3 sentences worth of effort envisioning who your character was before play begins (beyond the skeleton of what the creation rules outlines) you're playing in a non optimal manner for a Lumpley game. Take Dog's creation for example. It will tell you that you have a "complicated upbringing", but I think you're doing a disservice if you come up with more than a couple of sentences thinking about what that entailed before play begins. That couple of sentences is all you need to set up the initial conflict, and that initial conflict is plenty to get you started into your initial town.
