\n My friend Aaron recently ran a fantastic coal mine horror scenario set in Ohio, 1932, using Dread (of course). The entire session was played by lantern light; whenever a pull was required, another player would hold a trembling flashlight to illuminate the tower. Highly effective.

    \n I've played Ravenloft by rechargeable electric candle, which was surprisingly good. The best session randomly used the fact that the damned things hardly could maintain the charge they had. We played this best session as resolving a ghost story. Events took a turn for the worst, and also - one of the candles went out. Then another, and another, until the one last candle was there. Events got worse, but the candle was still lit. It stayed for a bit before dimming, but never went out. The players took that with them as they achieved victory over the haunts, and it made the session that much *more* for them.

    Pretty cool.

    \n I ran a Cthulhu adventure where I used a Flashlight with a crank. They had to rewind it to keep it glowing. It helped getting in character for a scene where they ran through a darkened forest with only that source of light. I as a GM was walking around in the darkened room and sometimes lit my own flashlight. Worked well for a scene, not a whole session.

    \n When playing ViewScream (a sorta-kinda larp designed for Google+ Hangout), I use strobe lights, red lightbulbs, and those little LED lights that you put inside your pumpkin. And next time, I'm going to use some xmas lights that I found in the garage. I'll put them behind me, across a black bedsheet. With the lights out, I'm hoping that to the people I'm playing with, on their monitor screen it'll look like I'm standing on the ship's hull, with stars in the background.
