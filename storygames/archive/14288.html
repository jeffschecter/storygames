    \n There are a bunch of simplified-old-skool games out there that have a D&D-like approach but simplify the bookkeeping and math a lot. Dungeonslayers looks pretty good, and there's also Microlite20, Mini Six, etc. These are still at heart tactical, though, with STR and DEX and armor classes, so they might not be what you're looking for.

    The just-released On Mighty Thews [see other threads about it] is very much non-tactical but reads as though it would emulate the D&D fantasy style of play pretty well. Although Simon might do a facepalm if you ignored all his advocacy for Swords & Sorcery [ie. his freebie Pulpy Primer pamphlet] and used his game for typical elfy-dwarfy stuff ;-)

    Edit: There's also Jason Morningstar's Dungeon Squad, which was one of the inspirations for On Mighty Thews. It's intended for "young players with short attention spans".

    \n I don't think it's possible to like fantasy but hate any edition of D&D. At least not for a real, well-adjusted person. The editions are so different, there has to be something you like enough about a particular edition that you're at least willing to play it if that's what other people are playing. The main issue I'd see with older editions is that survivability is crap with a large party, even worse with just one player, and that's something that retro-clones usually try to preserve. I'm not sure off hand how Dragon Age is as far as survivability goes, but at least XP is based on how difficult the encounter was, rather than how difficult it was supposed to be, so if you're having trouble soloing, as long as you've got enough healing potions to start with, you'll level up quickly and be much better able to handle things.

    Posted By: Bret GillanOkay, you guys have sold me on Tunnels and Trolls too.
    \nHere are some reasons why I think it'll work for you:\n
      \n
    • It's a little kludgey, but low on mechanical frills, so the once you got a handle on the initial kludge, you don't have to worry about some new rule waiting around the corner to bite you on the ass.
      • \n
    • This is probably sacrilege in several parts of the world, but edition doesn't really matter a whole lot. There are definite differences between edition 5.5 and edition 7.5 (which, for odd historical reasons, are the two editions that seem to be in competition with one another), but it's more of a spirit of the rules game than a letter of the rules game.
      • \n
    • Speaking of rules, this should sum it up for you.
      • \n
    • That combat system--have both sides roll a bunch of dice, add them and subtract them from the other side--is so delightfully simple. It'll let Carly play an entire party of adventurers without having to worry about what each individual one is doing at each given moment in the combat. And it encourages tactical thought in the form of fictional positioning rather than positioning minis on a combat mat. Plus, it lets you get through a dungeon crawl in a night, rather than just 2-3 encounters.
    • And it's kitchen-sink-y enough to encompass just about anything you want from a fantasy setting. Want to play a centaur? Just multiply your Strength and Constitution by 3 and your Charisma by 2. You're a centaur.
    \nSome things to watch out for:\n
      \n
    • Saving throws won't mean what you initially think they mean.
      • \n
    • No edition is well edited. If you look hard enough, you'll find contradictions or missing data. Ah, but you're an experienced roleplayer, so you should be able to handle these when they come up.
      • \n
    • It's very big on the whole What You Roll Is What You Get sort of thing. Not a lot of wiggle room to change things after the dice hit the table.
