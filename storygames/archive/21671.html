\n We will be using a friends RPG (Mongrel Lite) for an upcoming campaign. I want to figure out the likelihood of a success on a skill test. In his game both the GM and PCs roll for a skill test so I\u2019m hoping to figure out what the percent of success is, but I\u2019m not familiar with the anydice.com formulas. Here\u2019s how it works:

The PC rolls his dice and the GM rolls his dice. If the PC rolls higher than the GM the PC succeeds. If the PC and the GM tie the PC gets a partial success. And if the PC rolls lower than the GM the PC fails.

So if the PC was rolling a d6 and the GM was rolling the following, what would the PCs chance of success be?

PC rolls d6 vs GM rolls the following:
d3 (easy)
d6 (average)
2d4 (difficult)
2d6 (very difficult)

The last time we played overcoming skill tests was too difficult, but he has adjusted the difficulties since. Do you think his tests are about the same difficulty as in other games or do you still think they are too difficult?

If you could include a link to the anydice.com outcomes that would be ideal. I wouldn\u2019t mind sending them to him.

Any help would be tremendously appreciated! Thanks so much!

    \n I agree with Guy - that could be fine for the right kind of conflict resolution system. Probably too harsh for a task resolution system.

    As a rule of thumb (and please understand, this is a complete generalization), I'd say that conflict resolution for many story game purposes works well with odds close to 50/50 - so much so that you could well build a good game that only uses coin flips. Task resolution, on the other hand, is usually used for the sorts of purposes where you want "easy" to mean something like a 85%+ success rate at least.

    There are also various games that work best when failure is a very rare occurrence. For a master's thesis on the matter, consider Epidiah Ravachol's Swords Without Master: a task resolution system with a flat 15% failure rate. The game just assumes that your character is going to succeed in their derring-do a vast amount of the time, and it's all going to be routine business as usual, punctuated by a few dramatic failures now and then.
