\n While I'm still figuring out how to demo A Flower for Mara, I mustn't forget about preparing to demo Dirty Secrets again. Last year, I set my Dirty Secrets demo at GenCon, using fellow designers as suspects and victims. Last year the victim was Ralph Mazza, bludgeoned to death with a copy of Hero System 5th edition, Revised. Who will it be this year?

I'd like to do that again. But I'd like to open it up a bit this time.

So, here's the deal. If you're going to be at GenCon and you'd like to be part of my Dirty Secrets demo, let me know by whispering me your email address. I'll select my victims and contact you so that you can select your Demographics. In addition, I'll put a little ad on your Character card. Nothing too dramatic; more along the lines of an NPR sponsorship. "This Character designed Such-and-Such a game, which is for sale at Booth Blah" or "This Character is responsible for Such-and-Such podcast at http://websitehere.com" or something like that.

In addition, let me know if you're willing to be the victim of the murder. After all, I do need a murder victim, and I don't want to do that to someone who would rather not.

Seth Ben-Ezra
Great Wolf

    \n Graham,

    Brian has the truth of it. Dirty Secrets is supposed to be set in your home town, last week. So when I play, my games are set in Peoria, because it's where I live. This is an important feature of the game that I want to show off. The shared geography is an important communication tool within the game.

    However, as Brian mentions, at GenCon, everyone is from everywhere. But we're all at GenCon, so we'll set our game here. You don't necessarily know the Denny's on University and I won't know...uh...some place in London. But, once we're all in Indy, we will all know that place a couple blocks away from the convention center where the skywalk connects four buildings and completely covers the intersection and (at least in 2007) the steam from the sewers roiled up from the manhole lids, spreading a particularly potent stench as you stepped around the panhandlers....

    I'll bet that there are a bunch of people, right here on this forum, who are nodding their heads, because they know exactly where I'm talking about.

    Also, in the end, the demo works out to be fun. People get to experience the mechanics a bit, the investigator gets to wear a hat, and the players get to experience the immediacy of the game. The fact that it's a little silly lowers the bar of entry a bit for the demo. People ham it up as the investigator and as the suspects. (I particularly remember Alexis playing Ben. That was funny stuff....) And then, the stories of what (say) Matt Snyder did tend to circulate and stick in the mind. We all get a good laugh and maybe--just maybe--the folks that played it will have enough mental prompts that the game will stick in the mind and they will buy it.

    Now, when I'm running full-blown sessions at GenCon, I'm still planning on suggesting that we play at GenCon, but I'll try to encourage the table to be serious. No murders with gaming books, for instance. :-)

    And this reminds me: I need to get my hands on a hat....

    Seth Ben-Ezra
    Great Wolf
