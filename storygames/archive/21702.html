    \n I'd establish contingencies before hand as much as possible. Figure out which things will be fun to do with which configurations of players, and which configurations are a no-go.

    I expect most of these won't be good options, but in case one does fit, here's a list:
    - Continue the campaign as [party minus missing players' characters]. Search the map/options for something that group can handle.
    - Make new characters who are up to some separate but related thing(s) in the same world and might enter the campaign eventually.
    - Actually play those new characters doing their separate but related thing(s).
    - Play out an individual PC's solo character side interest (pursuing romance or religion or collectibles or retirement plans or whatnot).
    - Alternate between multiple solo side interests.
    - Reminisce about what's happened in the campaign so far and muse on what might happen next.
    - Discuss how to make the game better.
    - Walk-through/playtest any new rules/subsystems you've been considering adding to the game.

    And then of course there's "play something else" or "just hang out".

    \n That's something that I really have difficulty understanding: what exactly makes the gaming hobby such that people feel it doesn't merit the same consideration you would give to almost any other thing? This seems to be global, I hear everybody encountering the same types of attitude.

    Maybe it's something in the laid-back and introverted social culture. Like, people participate in game sessions and meet these intensely focused practitioners, and the table culture is very restrained and, well, quiet. So ultimately there's no reason to rank the game over watching TV if you feel like it tonight: the grey semi-persons in the group don't matter, there's no over-arching project relying on my participating today, and so on.

    What roleplaying clearly needs is a certain minimum quotient of irate grandmothers who care about it like it was the bingo night. You could send one of those to haunt the flakes every time they decide to skip on a session for no good reason.

    \n I'm not even sure it is a matter of where D&D sits in their priority order. Some people are, like, flakes all around, being unreliable for any kind of group social activity, be it a party or dinner or night at the movies or outdoors outing or whatever. Since my gaming circles tend to overlap considerably with my social circles (i.e. for the better part of my life I almost exclusively counted my RPG pals as friends) I've noticed people who tend not to show up at a session after confirming are usually the same people who fail to show up for dinner or any other appointment, with no advance notice. People who "just" hold gaming night lower than other priorities are usually able to reliably tell you they're skipping a session as soon as they know. Similarly, some people are just a pain in the ass to schedule anything with, not just a role-playing session: those people usually have one or more very clear first magnitude priority, such as a demanding job, parenting or a somewhat unpredictable SO they never say no to.

    Oh, my guess is: people who ask for social activities other than game nights can probably be talked into having those social activities in addition to game nights. Also: my experience is that (barring people who work on shifts or similarly unconquerable scheduling issues) having a fixed day of the week for games helps attendance somehow. It also makes for a good excuse for insisting on playing D&D no matter how many show up for the session - just because it's "D&D night".

    Do invest in recruiting more "flake" players: the more there are, the less it matters they're flakes - and who knows, some might turn out to be rocks actually.
