\n When I write post-apocalyptic stuff, my reference points are British authors. Most obviously, John Wyndham, who wrote The Day Of The Triffids: the opening sequence was later stolen (in a good way) for 28 Days Later. The other one is J.G. Ballard, who I'm just getting into now. Perhaps a bit of H. G. Wells' The War Of The Worlds, too, and some of John Christopher's stuff.

British post-apocalyptic fiction has a very particular feel to it. It's far removed from Mad Max. It inevitably features very normal people who wander around and see the apocalypse happening. There's an emphasis on their powerlessness, with, perhaps, a suggestion that their humanity will see them through.

However, American apocalypses seem rather different. The post-apocalyptica in Vincent Baker's Apocalypse World isn't something I recognise. Similarly, the Wasteland in Fallout 3 couldn't have come from British apocalyptic fiction. It's much grittier, more frontier-like. It's more heroic, too, in a weird anti-hero way. It feels more like the Wild West.

What are the reference points for American post-apocalyptic fiction? What do I need to read? Or what movies do I need to see?

And, by the way, don't just give me author names or movie names. I mean, give me those, but tell me about them. Tell me why they're important and what's in them. Tell me what an American apocalypse is like. Tell me what to watch out for.

Graham

    \n I was going to say A Canticle for Lebowitz too. It's about the rediscovery of science by the Church in a bombed back to the Middle Ages America and the cyclical nature of history. It's probably not Lovecraftian enough but the Church v State or even Superstition v Science is very HPL..

    \n Most of the good stuff I know has been mentioned already, but I'd also put in a plug for elements of Mike Mignola's _Hellboy_ comic. There are actually two "apocalypses" involved - a lot is made of the fact that Hellboy himself is a mythological creature but is part of a general genocide of mythological creatures in the name of protecting humanity (see esp. The Troll Witch on this theme). There's also an overarching issue of an impending HP Lovecraft-style apocalypse, initially planned by occultists among the Nazis, but in which Hellboy is fated to play a part.

    The second one I'd argue shows something interesting and perhaps important about American apocalyptic thinking. On the one hand, the destructive impulse may be externalized, and we are the City on the Hill fighting the good fight against impending doom (there are your pulp-y Nazis trying to immanentize the eschaton). On the other hand, we are deeply anxious that we ourselves are the greatest threat to the world as we know it, especially because we insist on holding on to power without responsibly confronting it (or, at least wishy-washy liberals like myself are).

    edit: Oh, and while I'm mentioning American guilt-induced comic book fantasies of apocalypse, definitely check out Brian Wood's DMZ. Not a global apocalypse (a local one, in Manhattan), but hits the tropes of heroism and self-reliance, Wild-West-style self-organization you've mentioned, without the gas guzzlers and with the requisite Lone White Male surrounded by a lot of other non-white co-protagonists.

    \n If folks are looking for the reverse, which is "Post Apoc Fiction from a British/Non-American POV", check out the novel "On The Beach". I read it and was completely baffled by it (it's basically an Australian Post-Apoc novel), only realizing later that it's the ultimate genteel post-apoc story. Polite people die slowly and politely while politely keeping up the facade of their society: Continuing to work at their shitty jobs, tipping their hats to each other, etc until there's no human left alive.

    It's a novel that could never have been written in the US, or perhaps anywhere after the Cold War. It's interesting, but unintentionally hilarious.

    \n Graham,
    \nIt's worth noting that Mad Max is Australian, not American.
    \n
    \nAlso, I've found that postapocalyptic (PA) film is almost a different genre than postapocalyptic prose fiction. For example, in PA fiction, you never have black presidents -- a la 2012. On the contrary in post-apoc fiction, black people are cannibals, primitives, victims or aliens.
    \n
    \nAmerican postapocalyptica seems firmly obsessed with destroying the contemporary, morally bankrupt, corrupt modern world in favor of a world in which there is no plumbing and a man lives by hits wits (meaning violence and rude carpentry) and women are put back in their rightful place. For example, in Lucifer's Hammer, Niven/Pournelle unironically quip, "The first thing to die when the comet hit was Women's Lib."
    \n
    \nThe little British/European postapocalyptica I've read seems to have similar view on race and gender equality -- Long Winter, Day of the Triffids, Malville. In Day of the Triffids, we are told that in order to survive, "Men work. Women make babies." Violence, sexism and racism solve lots of problems. However, instead of rough individualism, there seems to be a greater stress placed on community and continuity. At there is in those three books.
    \n
    \nI've read small bit of PA fiction in the last year or so. I've found it the most ethically bankrupt, morally void genre of fiction I've encountered. To me, it reads like thinly veiled race war/fascist fantasy fiction. As a body of work, it's extremely conservative. There are notable standouts which keep the flame alive, per se.
    \n
    \nThe Handmaid's Tale by Margret Atwood
    \nThe Postman by David Brin (problematic in ways noted above, but ultimately hopeful, positive and progressive)
    \nThe Sheep Look Up by John Brunner
    \n
    \nLucifer's Hammer by Larry Niven and Jerry Pournelle is the Hollywood blockbuster of PA novels. It features naked racism and sexism plus a fervent plea for modern conservatism's guiding light to take us to the future. It's jaw-dropping in its audacity, saved (and nearly redeemed) by the science fiction of the apocalypse itself.

      \n If we're mentioning British PA as well now, for a contrast, consider Will Self's Book of Dave. As a linguist by training, I liked this book quite a lot, because it makes a creditable stab at imagining what Standard Southern British English will sound like in 150 years, but that can make it a bit of hard reading for anyone not familiar with SSBE to begin with. It's two books together; the ranting diary of a mentally unstable London cabbie in the present day, with his proscriptions for How Society Ought To Be, and the story of a tribal society post serious global warming that has found this diary and organized itself accordingly. Some brilliant ideas about how people who don't quite understand this "sacred" text interpret it. For example, priests of Dave never look directly at people while talking to them; they look at them in mirrors that they carry around with them, because Dave always looked at people in (rearview) mirrors.
      \n
      \nDrags a bit in the later third, but it's quite interesting, and definitely has a sedate, descriptive pace contrasting with, say, Canticle for Liebowitz (where decades go by between chapters).

      \n Hey, everyone-

      Very cool thread, and everyone has mentioned just about anything I would consider particularly important.

      I'm a comics guy, so I'll second recommendations for DMZ and Y the Last Man for plausible near-future scenarios, and Kamandi for bug-fuck nuts: talking tigers and spike-wheeled siege engines and apes auditioning to wear Superman's costume.

      A couple somewhat more obscure but still fun recs: 'Killraven', a psychedelic 70s post-alien invasion apocalyptic scenario, now available in its entirety on one of those nice, cheap B&W 'Essential Marvel' editions. A quick scan of the back issue bins at your local shop for 'Elseworlds' and 'What If' back issues will reveal a host of various scenarios where superheroes are, to paraphrase Homer Simpson, "the cause of, and solution TO" various apocalyptic events.

      Marvel's X-Men cover this territory as part of canon: Check out 'Days of Future Past' and the 'Age of Apocalypse' stuff (the bad guy's name sort of gives away the premise...). 'Future Imperfect' is a PA scenario where the Hulk is the sole surviving metahuman, he's killed all the others, and it's strongly implied that the world is in the retched state it is because of horrible weapons unleashed to try and stop him.

      'Walking Dead' is the best zombie apocalypse fiction I've ever read: It's a still-ongoing series that's up to about a dozen books now, one of our best-selling TPB series at the shop. It's the first book in years I've read where I really, truly believe that NONE of the characters, including the protagonist, is ever safe, really.

      'Crossed' is an ongoing series by Garth Ennis about a sexually transmitted disease that basically turns the victims into psychopaths. It's not a zombie thing per se, though it does have that vibe. It's mostly interesting because he goes on these weird little tangents that make for really interesting gaming hooks... one issue is about how the government handles the country's nuclear power plants in that situation, etc.

      The current crop of 'Wildstorm' superhero books, Stormwatch, the Authority, etc, are currently dealing with a planetary-scale catastrophe with superheroes being really the only thing holding everything together. I'm not a particular fan, but if you ever wanted to Wild Talents or whatever in this direction, it's definitely all-in on the premise.

      Finally, the recent Mark Waid book 'Irredeemable' is basically "What would happen if Superman decided he didn't give a shit about human life?" It's great, though it's one of those stories where it's not necessarily PA, but you keep thinking the no-shit full-blown thing is just a page-flip away. It's on Issue 9 right now, with 2 trades of the first 8 issues available.

      That's just the tip of the iceberg. There's a pretty good adaptation of the Stand in unspooling right now in GN format, and King's 'Gunslinger' saga is arguably PA as well (there's 4 boosk of that). The 'Angel' comic series picks up where the TV show left off, in PA Los Angeles. It's also got a humorous high-weirdness vibe that apes its original source material pretty well on the melodrama front. It's tailor-made for a PTA game.

      Finally, a hearty and heartfelt recommendation for Martian Chronicles, the first book to really seriously rewire my brain in that way some people say Tolkien or Heinlein or Dune did for them. Funnily enough, I have always said, and my wife can back this up because of the years I've pestered her to get around to reading it, that it was the first time I really UNDERSTOOD that it was POSSIBLE to destroy the world, that we could just let things slide so badly that they'd slip away completely. It basically introduced the emotional understanding of 'apocalypse' to me. The world ends with stunning quiet and terrible certainty in that book. It's the most sublime Apocalypse I an ever imagine experiencing, made all the more haunting by the fact that it's the SECOND one we witness.

      Yikes, that was a bit more than I expected. Seriously, though, if you have a decent local comic book shop, just go in and ask and they'll be able to steer you to a bunch of great stuff.

      \n British Cozy Catastrophes seem to involve the elimination of the lower classes, and after a bit of bother the middle class survivors get resettled and things get sorted out. I recommend Jo Walton's posts on them, either at tor.com or papersky.livejournal.com.

      Philip Wylie's The End of the Dream had a lot of things going wrong, although I preferred John Wyndham.

      My favorite US post-apocalyptic books are Niven&Pournelle's Lucifer's Hammer, David Palmer's Emergence (sadly out of print although I hear rumors it may be getting reprinted), Andre Norton's Daybreak: 2250 AD, and Sterling Lanier's Hiero's Journey, although it may be childhood nostalgia talking for those last two.

      If magical apocalypses count, then I highly recommend Sean Stewart's Nightwatch (and Resurrection Man and Galveston), and Janni Lee Simner's short but wonderful recent YA novel, Bones of Faerie.

      I suspect a lot of American post-apocalyptic literature calls on the American Monomyth and thus feels very Western. (A community is imperiled but unwilling to save itself, so a stranger comes to town and saves them, but cannot become part of the community by doing so. Something like that.)

      Hero Games' Post-Apocalyptic Hero probably has an exhaustive source list and much discussion of genre tropes.
