    \n I've only played with one non-gamer, and he was BETTER at it then some gamers, because I think the gamers, including myself, carried forward some baggage from traditional games. Specifically:

    1. The Heart MUST drive his character's story forward, ruthlessly if necessary. Don't worry about "breaking" a plot; don't think of the Mistaken as a GM. Push hard against the Mistaken, and she can push hard back against you. Just as the mistaken isn't a GM, neither is the heart a "traditional player" with a traditional "player character." Drive story forward, using any and all means available to you.

    For example, I had a kinda crappy scene with me as the heart. I was a general meeting with a corrupt, demonic senator, who was meeting with me in my office to give me a quest to get a mcguffin from a tomb. We roleplayed in circles around each other for a bit, then ended the scene with me accepting the quest. I had this instinct that I should of had my character pull out a magical video of the Senator having an illicit affair with a minor, and then tell him to take his stupid quest and stuff it - I was going to use my men to fight the demons directly. That would of been an awesome scene I think. Instead I hesistated because I didn't want to "break" the mistaken's "plot."

    2. The mistaken does not control every character in the game. The moons should be portraying neutral and friendly characters. One player had a nasty habit of narrating for moon characters as the mistaken. The mistaken is not a GM.

    \nI'm not so sure about that. The reason is because the non-gamers already view it as something unusual. Not necessarily "cultish" but different than what they've done before. I've never presented it as being "cultish" (I'm not even sure what you mean by that, frankly) but when I mention it to people they generally don't know what I'm talking about and are confused. When I try to give general descriptions about the activity, people often express concern that they "won't be good at it." or that they will look "foolish". In the current situation I'm talking about I did my best to push the fact that it's not a difficult thing to do, and that everyone will be playing this particular game for the first time.

    Anyway... I agree that the distinction between gamers and non-gamers is not always useful. In fact, when we do get together for Polaris I'm going to have to fight the urge to explain the game by comparing it to other games, because that will only confuse these friends of mine more. Still, I think it is a useful distinction in some ways. If I say to a gamer friend, "Want to get together and play this new roleplaying game?" they at least know what I mean by "roleplaying game." Someone who isn't a gamer may very well not even have that level of understanding at first.

    \n somethings i want to make sure i do next time:

    frame scenes as a mistaken more, and tell the other players to try it too - i think most, if not all of our scenes were framed by the heart, and as such they didn't all have any sort of interesting tension right at first. i think it might be more exciting if we framed more scenes as a mistaken.

    make sure that i, as well as the other players, are using the key phrases - sometimes i just forgot to use them. the other two players might not have used them a lot for that reason, but i think they might also not have done so because it might have felt "weird." i don't know that for sure, though.

    try to make sure no one argues about things - at least at one point the other two players were arguing over what should happen and i had to break it up and say "Hey, this shouldn't be necessary. This is what the key phrases and the game rules are for."
