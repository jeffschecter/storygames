    \n I think I had an NPC go visit the Gunlugger in the drainage pipe he called home. He was just stopping by to mention totally non-killing-related stuff going on in the village, like how they'd found this sick baby and had been taking care of it.
    I have noticed that my campaign's Gunlugger does a lot of observing, provides cover fire, and generally keeps in the background. The Chopper, possibly because he has people he can order around, seems to "organically" find himself interacting and engaging with others a lot more often.

    I think the key to drawing out your group's Gunlugger is, if they are played by a shy person, to make sure that they have some important Hx connection to a character whose player is more outgoing, social, dynamic, etc.

    I imagine actually playing to the Gunlugger's strengths (killing things, scaring people) could work also, if pressed

    \n Rustin,
    I guess I need to ask a clarifying question: what mindset are you talking about? One which thinks that killing is usually the best approach?

    Personally, I think that a cold, unpredictable killer would make a very interesting addition to an AW game - - it would also give you an opportunity to ask, "WHY are you killing them?" Not in terms of "tut tut, how could you?" but in terms of "what are you trying to ACCOMPLISH by throwing death around like candy?"

    For my game's Gunlugger, the go-to question was, "OK, so you point your gun at them. Are you actually planning on shooting them?" The answer to that question helps distinguish Going Aggro vs. Seduce or Manipulate, at the very least, but it also helps to get the player thinking, "Shit, what's my next step?" It's important to not stand in the way of the player doing whatever she is interested in, including "solving" problems however she wishes. Remember to look through crosshairs at NPCs, and remember to cackle at the awesome arterial spray once in a while. Carnage can be fun, even if we're also really interested in seeing how this fragile, dysfunctional web of relationships works.
    Even if you end up with a grunting, blood-crazed killer with not a care for anyone in the world, remember this: if you got no friends, you gotta find your own food. You got no friends, you got no one else to watch your back while you sleep. Encourage a trigger-happy player to really explore and confront the consequences of their piratical lifestyle - it could turn out to be very interesting indeed, especially in light of Scarcity as our watchword.

    \n Thanks, Judd. I've seen Apocalypse World occasionally go into too much... well, if I can say this in a non-incendiary fashion, into a too "traditional" style of play. Players and characters angle themselves to gain experience and advance, and to collect weapons and power, like in a D&D game.

    That spoils it sometimes, for me. I think the provocative questions are the "killer app" that's needed to take the game out of that mode sometimes, particularly for a character like the Gunlugger. By default, we have no idea what that character cares about, except for his or her gun collection. So give them a chance to make up something they hope for or care about, something we can then use to build interesting situations around.

    \n In my game, our gunlugger started after everybody else, so his starting scene had a little more to go on, but you could simulate that with moves like the starting moves provided in the MC main book that help flesh out the background. Anyway, our gunlugger was hired to protect a hole in the outerwall of the holding while it was being fixed, so he camped outside it, and I had the neighboring tribe whose god was stolen by the holding (bit of a long story there) escort their head priestess to hold them accountable. Mind you, he wanted to kill her right out, but I made it a bit intimidating by saying that the entire tribe (group large) was there and he would have a heck of a time targeting just her; certainly he could try, he'd just have to face their harm in the seize by force roll, which would have wiped him out first game. So instead,he tried to parley with her, and ended up indebted. I suppose the whole point of this story is that sure he should have carte blanc access to the killings when killing is a good idea, but in a story, even the most wild and crazy killers don't murder all the time, and if this guy made it in this harsh world, it's because discretion is the better part of survival, he just doesn't have to use it unless the stakes are super high!
