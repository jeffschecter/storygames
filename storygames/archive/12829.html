    \n Maybe you could provide some specific examples of where you feel the game demands skin-eating? Or nixes compassion? That would probably make it easier to provide specific advice.

    I'm trying not to be rhetorical, because of course my immediate response really is 'just run the game as-written, and then don't add any skin-eating.' You're the MC, you have near-total control over the colour and tone of the game -- whenever something bad happens to the PCs, you get to decide what that means. When the rules stipulate that somebody's gang is 'savage' and a roll tells you that their savagery has activated -- as MC, you decide what savage looks like. If you never decide that it looks like skin-eating, then you're pretty much golden in terms of avoiding that level of depravity. Maybe it just means they start fist fights, or kill a guy, or kidnap somebody with soft skin.

    I mean, as long as you yourself have a fairly consistent idea of the tone -- or even just a kind of basic, default intuitive level of 'what badness looks like' -- you are unlikely to accidentally end up with a game that differs from that. In fact, when I MC, I usually have to remind myself that this is the fucking Apocalypse, already, because my own impulse is always to underplay everything, to make these little subtle gestures of savagery, instead of just up and up mutilating somebody and sticking their body on a flagpole. Our first campaign (which I did not run) had some serious, life-sucks violence, but almost no gonzo action cannibal mutilation style savagery.

    In terms of specific advice, how you build your Fronts and how you imagine the different types of Threats might be important -- just because things are called 'Grotesques' doesn't mean they have to be shambling mounds of flesh, etc. But I think you probably get that already. But I guess I can only repeat that there's not a lot of skin-eating built into the game, so unless you're worried about putting it there by accident you'll probably be fine.

    As for the players having a positive impact in the world, that's really up to the players. They don't need your help, they just need you to acknowledge that their actions matter. The dice and the complicated situations and the whole Apocalypse thing should make it hard enough to be compassionate -- but again, you will be the one deciding how and when and how hard they get kicked in the sack.

    \n Discuss it up-front with the players. If you play it by the book, a lot of the setting is determined collaboratively: the MC is supposed to ask questions and riff off the answers. Get the tone you want to see on the table up-front, as that's something that's important to you.

    After that, it's a question of what moves you choose and how you interpret them. For instance, when a character gets a soft hit on a "do something under fire" move, the MC gets to offer a worse outcome, a hard bargain, or an ugly choice. What qualifies as worse, hard, or ugly is a function of the tone. Also, "badness" -- very tone-dependent.

    Remember that you get to write custom moves. Write ones that reinforce the behavior you want to see. Also check out the hacks and the custom moves in the Apocalypse World forums -- there are a lot of examples there of how different people have written custom moves, or rewritten character playbooks, to get the tone of the game they want.

    I'll post more advice as I think of it, if I think of it.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI'd love to run a game where characters could have a positive impact on the world, could demonstrate compassion without getting kicked in the sack, etc. Is it even possible?
    \nNo. Sack-kicking is mandatory.

    Really it comes down to what you think the PA genre is. Is it tales of small victories in the face of desperation? Is it tales of discovery and redemption with the possibility of meaningful rebirth?

    I think a lot of that will come through how you build out your Fronts. I'd make good and sure that none of your Fronts' dark agendas are especially dark, for one. Like I've got a Front in which the local warlord rules all of NorCal with an iron fist after crushing her enemies in the surrounding towns. So like the PG-13 version of that might be "the warlord arranges trade relations and mutual protection agreements with the surrounding towns." Not especially dark, and leaning dangerously toward establishing a status quo (of which there are none in AW!), and I'm...not sure where the drama would come from in that case.

    Probably you'd need to dial down just how ugly an ugly choice might be -- so rather than "you can get a tankful of gas but that schoolbus of kids you're driving are gonna get machinegunned", you might go "you can get a tankful of gas but the kids in that school bus are going to get antsy and start running around out of control." And then you'd have to not escalate that too badly or too quickly. Maybe a kid falls down a well, or eats something he shouldn't.

    I'm finding the very premise of de-darkening AW so difficult to address with a straight face that I almost can't come up with examples that aren't comically absurd, or devoid of meaningful conflict. I'm a huge PA genre nut and run a pretty grim game of it here myself. It's hard to envision how best to leverage the tools the game gives you, since most of the "rules" exist to load the GM with all sorts of nasty ammo to which the players must react. It's a reactive game, IMO, reflective of the base desperation of the situation.

    Tough question now that I'm sitting here doodling on it.

    \n From my experience running the game, listening to Vincent and John and reading APs (imho, ymmv):
    The game is geared towards characters having a positive impact on the world, it's right there in the system. It just makes it very difficult for them along the way. The characters are there fighting for a better tomorrow. They're a beacon of light in a dark, dark world. This doesn't mean they can't be violent fuckups themselves, but ultimately it comes down to: realizing "we're the people that can fix this world" and dealing with that. At least that's my impression. As a MC, you need to be aware of two things in this context:
    1. you need to jump forward with human, named NPCs and make PC-NPC-PC triangles
    Meaning: give the PCs opportunities to get attached, have people to care and worry about, not nameless, faceless mooks to graze down (because goddamn can they kill tons of people if they get down to it, our Gunlugger's frag score was in the hundreds in a few sessions), something in the world that's worth saving, and something that threatens it. Relationships.
    2. you're looking at your NPCs through crosshairs
    Meaning: in the end, no matter how much the PCs are going to get kicked in the sack (because fuck, it's a harsh world), that's NOTHING compared to what happens to the NPCs. Every time you have a NPC in the scene you're just contempleting how to bury the fucker in the poisoned ground. You're a fan of the PCs but you're downright cruel to your NPCs. At a certain point I believe the PCs will realize that they're not out there to fight, kill and overthrow the NPCs (because that's actually damn easy), but to save them (which the Ungiven Future is all about). The only thing that can seriously oppose a PC is another PC. So...

    (funny thing, I just told you to run the game as written)

    I think the tone can be shifted quite a lot without impacting the mechanics too much. Kinda like the supernatural dial in Dogs y'know. But do be post-apocalyptic. I mean, I can't really imagine a cuddly post-apocalypse. So the brutality is inherent in the tone. Also remember that the PCs, as awesome as they can be and all the superpowers they get, are supposed to be real people.

    So, that said, how much of the skin-eating would you like to remove exactly? Because, much like McCarthy's The Road, while there are cannibals along the road, and coughing up blood, and falling ash and hopeless darkness, in the end, there are people carrying the fire. At least that's how my experience with it goes.

    Posted By: TeataineYou're a fan of the PCs but you're downright cruel to your NPCs. At a certain point I believe the PCs will realize that they're not out there to fight, kill and overthrow the NPCs (because that's actually damn easy), but to save them
    \nThis seems worth highlighting to me. I think most people tend to understand NPCs as 'GM tools' for accomplishing some kind of 'opposition' to the players, and so the basic idea is that they have to push against the NPCs with their PCs and that will produce conflict/story/the game/whatever. But when the PCs push against the NPCs -- I mean, individual NPCs especially -- the NPCs give way. They die, they change sides, they run away. For most players that's a little freaky -- to the point that it might take awhile to even notice -- and for many MCs/GMs it is very counter-intuitive.

    The thing is, the NPCs give way but the situation doesn't necessarily -- Fronts that are based entirely around the motivations of a single NPC are incredibly fragile, and very easy to solve, which is of course why Fronts aren't supposed to be based around NPCs but instead around 'fundamental scarcity' that is expressed by lots of different things. The 'lots of different things' is important because of the fundamental malleability/fragility of NPCs. Also, to get very specific: you should at least as many Fronts/Threats based around NPC weakness as you have around NPC strength.

    And yes, eventually players will start to realize that there is no challenge inherent in defeating or opposing a specific NPC, except insofar as that NPC is part of some situation or problem they want to resolve. But it's really important that the MC be willing (and eager) to let the PCs roll over their NPCs just as easily as the rules suggest, otherwise you will never see that shift and the MC will have to start arbitrarily making his NPCs 'stronger' and 'scarier' out of some misguided notion of where the conflict is supposed to be coming from.

    In other words, if as MC you find yourself introducing 4-harm 2-armour large gangs because otherwise the game doesn't seem exciting enough... you have gone astray.

    \n I came back from lunch early to post my thoughts and see there's already lots of good advice I might have given.

    The point about non-human outside threats is an interesting one.\xa0 We have "Drinkers" in our game as the only real "savage" threat.\xa0 They are human but driven mad by drinking directly from the waters of The Wake, waters fouled more by the psychic toxins of the maelstrom than by any terrestrial poison.\xa0 It's possible even they might be redeemed once the characters have the possibility to reach through the maelstrom to what's beyond it.

    Speaking of which, look closely at the advancing moves section of the rules (p185), and make sure your players are aware of them.\xa0 However you pace or color your game (and I think that's most of the issue), it's important that you don't quickly give them the things that those moves make possible.\xa0 The PCs will likely start to get there in the 7th session.\xa0 Don't cheapen those victories by giving them earlier via overly generous interpretation of what the basic moves make possible.\xa0 Redeeming those NPCs, changing the world.

    Here's the arc as I see it.\xa0 They start as Big Damn Heroes who nonetheless get kicked in the sack a fair amount.\xa0 Then they get that +3 score in the stat they most often use to influence the fiction (and/or a move that allows them to use that +3 stat a couple different ways).\xa0 At this point the PCs can do pretty much whatever they want to do, especially if they help each other and stick together.\xa0 Then they advance their basic moves and start rolling 12+s.\xa0 Transcendence.\xa0 Enlightenment.\xa0 Redemption.\xa0 Carving out some Sanctuary in a hard, hard world.\xa0 It's possible if that's what they want.

    Our game has had a pretty quiet build and tone.\xa0 Yeah, it's plenty savage outside the hardholds (two joined submerged towers), but inside it's been more like a dysfunctional family drama.\xa0 The Fronts I've focused on have been mostly about Decay, Despair, and Ignorance.\xa0 Maybe there's something to that as well.\xa0 If people don't have food (Hunger) or water (Thirst), things can get savage right quick.\xa0 Maybe Fear, Envy and Ambition are more intractable as well and might lead to more sack kicking in some games.

    I think you can also cue your prefered approaches to compassion in the things NPCs want PCs to promise when they try to manipulate them.

      On the other hand, when I say "we've all" above, I don't really mean 'all', of course, and I think the inclusivity (or exclusivity) of that category has direct bearing on this question. What I mean is that these GM "rules" are clearly less necessary for people who already have it figured out (I wish I did! I had some of this stuff, but certainly not all of it, and I try to design games for pete's sake!). But for people new to the hobby, or even just new to this particular way to running a game, they can be totally invaluable, but perhaps most importantly, by expressing them as rules rather than as 'best practices' or 'guidelines' or 'advice' or 'advanced playcraft' or whatever, these players are getting implicit permission to try this stuff out and/or get their friends to go along with it. When it's presented as "the rules for this new game you're trying", however, it's an easier pill to swallow (or at least, I think it is). I too would snatch up a GM advice book by Vincent in a heartbeat, though I think I've seen him say before "that's what my games are for". I just love generalized, pedantic advice. Such a format, however, would be pretty easy for some folks to say "oh, that doesn't apply to my game" or "that's just advice, I already know how to GM, so I'll ignore it".

      So, um, I guess my response is that this *might* be the best compromise we can hope for, since the "this is the rules" approach is probably most useful for communicating these concepts to people not already on board with them, while those who already get it, or partially get it, will be more comfortable with seeing the 'advice' behind the 'rules'.

      \n In preparation for running AW for the first time tomorrow, I've been (of course) reading through the MC bit. And then again.

      I'm going to again tonight, because I frankly fear this fucking game. It's making a skill I've done elsewhere for years feel new again. I welcome and dislike that simultaneously. That's on me, folks, but I know where that gut reaction is coming from.

      The thing that I feel like I'm really getting out of it, though, is "listen, MC, I'm going to teach you how to react *right*." Much of the GM advice I've read over the years isn't about reaction, not really. Some is, yes, but then not so detailed as what AW's doing. To a large degree it's proceduralizing the GM's job of reacting.

      And the way a GM reacts is what breaths life into a game. Vincent gets this, and what he's designed to deal with it is fucking interesting. I have found a similar thing with Mythender: when I reacted in certain ways as GM, my early playtesters would say "Dude, I don't feel fucking mythic. Fuck you." So I see what AW is doing, and I'm all "oh shit! I get why!" If you deviate from those forms of reaction, you're not selling the truths Vincent states is AW. GM reaction to players is key.

      My main problem with the text is that it's not helping me internalize this thing. And the way it's great for someone who is new to GMing is probably to same reason why it's making me feel stressed out about my game tomorrow. Maybe I'm overthinking it, but that's because the text isn't helping on that front.

      - Ryan

      \nSo Tony ran AW for the first time a couple of weeks ago and he also said that he was terrified of running the game, despite having run his own hack, Apocalypse D&D, a whole bunch. But the thing that he seemed to discover -- and I've watched John do this in play too -- is that, when you don't know what to do, you don't have to fall back onto stuff you've internalized (like you do in most games), you just have to look down at the sheet in front of you that explicitly lists your moves, and pick one. Sure, over time, after running a lot of AW, you'll end up internalizing most of them and go "Oh, yeah, here's where I Threaten Badness." But in the beginning -- or when you have a momentary lapse -- it totally works to look down at your sheet, pick one silently, and then go "Okay, here's what happens..." In our games, we even giggle sometimes when the MC says, "Hmm, you failed 3 times in a row, so I guess I'm filling in these sections of this Threat countdown..." as we wait in anticipation for the fuckery that we know is coming.

      MCing AW doesn't have to take the form of that "magical GM" stuff that Brand used to talk about, where you're juggling all these plates in the air while at the same time making it look effortless. Fuck that. It says "misdirect," not "don't let them see you sweat" or "pretend you're the perfect GM." You can totally say, "I'm going to go walk down the hall to get another beer and think about my next move" or "give me a sec while I look at my list" or whatever. Especially the first time you're running a new game (any game), that kind of thing should be expected, yeah?
