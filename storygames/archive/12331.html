\n So my article on structured freeform made it in to the book accompanying the LARP Summit at WyrdCon, called Journeys to Another World. J. Tuomas Harviainen reviews the whole book here, and says some nice things about my piece. But he also points out the significance of the LARP Summit project in a broader context: it's a move toward creating connections among different communities of play, figuring out how to talk to one another, and navigating differences. Which is pretty neat. In any event, I'm pleased with my essay, which excerpts conversations I had with Emily Care Boss, Jason Morningstar, Ryan Macklin, Chad Underkoffler, Paul Tevis, and Frederik Oestergaard in order to talk about one patterns of influence within our little design community. Check it out and tell me what you think, especially in the context of Vincent Salzillo's proposal to do something similar at a Double Exposure convention.

    \n It has to do with both how larp-focused the editors are (just as does the issue of how academic or layman-friendly the book should be) and simply who sends in what. Occasionally the editors may even solicit a piece on tabletop - for example, Emily's 2008 paper on Forge theory was directly asked for by the editors of Playground Worlds (and not jeepers, as Bill says in his nifty paper). Basically, if someone offers a good piece on tabletop, it will quite certainly make its way into a KP book. But I doubt a paper on just digital role-playing would be accepted, so an invisible boundary of what's "applicable enough for larp" does exist somewhere.
