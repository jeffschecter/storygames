Rant about "indie" as a term

jhkim
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
OK, so this is my little rant over terminology -- brought on by the discussion of Greg Stafford and Robin Laws in the thread on "You Know What They Say: Once you go Indie..."

The Forge was founded in 1999 by Ron Edwards and Ed Healy with a vision: to support independent role-playing games. From the top of the front page since the site was founded, it nicely defined independent role-playing games as having a single primary quality: owned by the author. When the Indie RPG Awards were founded in 2002 by Andy Kitkowski, there was also a clear definition outlined in its FAQ: owned by the author (specified as over 50% of the text written by the person who has control over publishing).

But now in several places, I see people using "indie RPG" to mean an RPG typical of the current Forge output, even though Forge participants have released a variety of games from Sorcerer to JAGS to Polaris to Perfect20 to Mechaton. So there's talk about whether Robin Laws or Greg Stafford's games are "indie" even if they aren't creator-owned.

Can you please use a different term?!? There is many years of clearly defined usage -- including from the foundation of the Forge itself -- on what "indie RPGs" are. There are tons of alternate choices depending on what you're trying to say: "thematic games", "story games", whatever. But it drives me nuts to see people talk about Forge-inspired games while ripping to shreds the Forge's own terminology.

    Kuma, here's my litmus test. It's not 100% but it's damned close:

    Can the author of the game (the "dude in charge" who had a significant hand in writing the game) suddenly decide that he wants to print off 1,000 copies, and do it? Can that same person decide that they want their game to never be sold/produced again, and completely shut down production on it? Or can that person decide 2 months later that they want to revise and sell their game again, and do so? If YES to all the above, that's Indie.

    Guys I feel your pain but let's remember that the term indie has been used in music and film for years before The Forge grabbed it for games, and even there the term is subject to dispute. So it has existing associations and if you did not want to carry those you needed to pick a new term. You can't ride the coat tails of an existting term and escape its existing meanings.

    Elsewhere the term means low-budget and not produced by a major studio/label. But perhaps what is more important is that decisions about the product are driven by the creative agenda not the commercial agenda. In other words you don't compromise vision for market share. To make that sacrifice is to 'sell out'.

    Now while The Forge definition of 'crrator-owned' can be a great yardstick for creative vs commercial priorities it's not a solid test. After all the new Star Wars films are technically indie because they are creator-owned, but while Lucas has tried to claim the indie mantle, I don't think most people consider them to be so. Lucas 'sold out', IMO.

    Your definition could lead you down these paths in gaming too, is a heavily comercial product starts wearing the indie tag. Is Rifts 'creator-owned' by Kevin and thus indie for example? Are you happy for him to flaunt that tag.

    I'd stick with a tag that recognizes a non-commercially driven product, instead focused on a creative endeavour. So HeroQuest is indie, Mongoose RuneQuest isn't.

    Bear in mind that you cannot avoid slippage of meaning in language. Any reader comes to the text with their own web of associations with the words you use, that will colour thier intepretation of what you say. See Locke's Essay on Human Understanding, or Derrida's Structure Sign and Play in the Human sciences. You can't control meaning and it will break your heart to try. You can't enforce this so it will escape you.

It's that long term that interested me the most at the outset of all this effort on my part, and which continues to be my focus of attention.
