    \n I'm not trying to derail you here, but it's worth identifying the thing that makes a particular format or approach valuable.

    TMW does this with Trust - you build it up and gain a monumental bonus when you burn it all in a single act of betrayal. It's just too tasty to ignore, Trust looms over everything and colors the game brilliantly. As I recall it works similarly in Cold City.

    In Grey Ranks you get to destroy other people's attributes for mechanical advantage - attributes like "my love of my family". And the game's adversity scales to make this a very compelling option if you want to avoid a spiral of suicidal depression for your little dude.

    \n Here are some things that board games do well that RPGs could incorporate (I'll stop short of saying "should" even though I REALLY want to).
    \n
    \n1) They make it always clear whose turn it is to do something. In a board game there's never any doubt who "has the ball" so to speak, and whose job it is to make the next "move".
    \n
    \n2) There are clear unambiguous choices confronting players and clear unambiguous means to determine what the results of those choices are. By nature, an RPG will never be as tight on this front as a BG; but that shouldn't be used an excuse to not think clearly about this issue in design.
    \n
    \n3) A well written set of board game rules covers how to interpret fringe cases. If you can say "what happen if..." and then go to the rules and find out, that' s a well designed set of rules. Again, by their open ended nature RPGs will always lag BGs in this regard, but a clearly articulated process for deciding what happens in fringe cases that aren't specifically covered goes a long way.
    \n
    \n4) Minimized set up time and a contained play time. Alot of RPGs are already nailing this one pretty well.

    \n I don't think you need to mechanize lying and betrayal. If you have a design that clearly awards achieving a certain end condition and you make that end condition difficult or impossible for more than one player to achieve, then players will find their own desired level of lying and betrayal.
    \n
    \nThe one rule that most board games are explicitly clear about, however, is whether in game agreements are "binding" and players actually have to follow through on what they agreed to do. It is a general pattern that in Euro-style designs they most commonly due while in Ameri-style designs they most commonly do not.
    \n
    \nIt is also typically made explicit exactly what sort of things you can make deals about and what you cannot. Due to the limited number of options it is typically very clear whether a deal has been kept or not. In an RPG where deals might have a wider range of possible things that can be agreed to, a system for determining whether the terms have been adequately satisfied or not may be necessary.
