    \n "I'm going to be the conflict for you guys" might sap some juice out of Universalis. Mike Holmes can certainly correct me here but IME if you add any of the "I'm in control / it's up to me whether to resist your conflict" then players won't be in the same creative space - they'll be looking for the DM-style player to be providing the conflict (or worse, looking to him and trying to jive with his ideas above others) and that means less of the cool stone soup effect that you see when everybody is responsible for making the game fun in the same way.

    Rob "Valvorik" Harper used Universalis to build his next campaign world with his players, and he did it mostly by just sticking to Universalis as-is. I know a few others have tried this as well but I think the god-game part would give it more personality and breadth.

    \n My new game has a setting development aspect. I have not finished this yet (the first playtest is October 17th) but just for perspective on this post I will elaborate...

    The game is for 3 - 6 players.
    Players decide if they want to be Victims or Cultists (game roles).
    There must be at least one Victim and at least one Cultist.
    The Victim's characters are trying to go mad as late in the game as they can muster.
    The Cultists are trying to drive the Victim's characters mad sooner than later.

    When the game first begins the group collaborativly creates a number of characters as there are Victims.
    hen the group participates in a mechanic that allows them to vie for setting structure.
    Based on the final outcome in the Setting Structure the Cultists start with currency (specifics not nailed down).
    With that currency the Cultists create horrors and also with that currency they infuse the Victim's situations with horrors and other elements that drive the Victim's character mad.

    More as I figure out the details more thoroghly.
    No doubt this is far from generic and I am uncertain how much it would apply to your games.

    Just throwing it out there,

    \n If that's in line with the example text - you're playing gods that are getting drunk in bars and storming into each others' offices, right?

    I think when doing the world-building activity you'll need rules that elevate that kind of creation. Universalis fits the bill I think, but I can think of two other house rules that I'd want:

    At least one of a god's passions should be "worthy of a god" - that is to say, it's important that Zeus isn't just trying to bang princesses - it's important that fearing change is part of it as well (or loving the divine order, or hating the primordial, fearing prophecy, etc.) So I'd think that there would have to be a "philosophical" passion built into the gods.

    The other idea is using Theme from Mortal Coil as well. The gods essentially write the "theme statements" in the world, and these could be brought into the conflicts as well. Such as "Evil eats at itself." "Innocence is blind to evil." and so forth.
