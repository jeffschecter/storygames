\n A recent post talked about the desire for story-games that can be played on a car trip.
I am currently reading "Dread" during my morning bus ride. (As a reminder, "Dread" uses "Jenga" as it's core mechanic.)

This got me to thinking. What 'traditional' games/activities can one do comfortably in a car?
I know that some folks play cards (in fact there is a group that plays Eucre on the afternoon bus).
My wife and I often do crosswords or logic puzzles while the other drives.
A google-search showed a lot of either letter-based (I sell something in my grocery story that starts with an "A"... a "B"...), visually based (find license plates from various states), or both (find an "A" on a license plate/billboard, then a "B", ...).

What do You_ do on car trips to entertain yourself or your fellow carmates?


The idea is to find a mechanic that doesn't involve dice or a lot of supplies....

    \n I don't think I've ever played anything that would count as an RPG in a car (though I have done loose freeform talking in character with people whom I am playing an RPG with).

    I've done lots of I-spy games though, and my wife and I often play a word game where each person says a word in turn that needs to be the opposite of the word before (for some value of opposite ... e.g. right, left, arrived, departed, landed, jumped, fell, climbed, leaped ... etc.). I've also played some CCGs (once or twice) but only in the back seat of the car and never with the driver.

    \n While it's true that physical components are not needed, I find the real commodity in the car is concentration, especially for the one driving! (Also for the passengers, who are often talking partly to help the driver concentrate, even if they are talking to each other). I can roll dice in the car easily enough (on the dash, in the glovebox), but I can't expect the driver to hear every word, or make quick responses. I'd also need mechanics where I could remember what was happening after suddenly fumbling for the map / drink / sandwich / toll gate money, or whatever.

    \n I run an RPG for one of my gaming groups in a car on our way either to or from an out-of-town science fiction convention we attend annually. The drive is about five hours, and everybody takes a shift behind the wheel. I GM the game, so sometimes I wind up skipping my driving shift (or I make it up on the trip in the other direction). The players roll their own dice, except for the driver, who's dice are rolled by the GM (I use a rolling app on my Palm rather than physical dice, when I'm in the car). That campaign has been through three different rules systems over the years; we're currently using a homebrewed PDQ/FATE crossbreed. No minis or battlemat needed, only 2d6 required for dice, so it's just component-light enough to play in a car. In terms of concentration for the driver, the trip is basically one straight run on a highway for five hours, so reacting to traffic, following directions, etc. is not really a problem. It probably helps that the flow of our game is fairly slow-and-talky -- although we have had one car-session that involved a space battle, the defense of an orbital outpost from pirate boarding parties, a Kamikaze attack in a disabled fighter-craft, and a John Woo-esque temple-to-temple blaster standoff between one of the PCs and the pirate captain. High action throughout! For the space battle and the outpost defense I did sketch maps on paper and pass them to the players in the back seat. The driver had to contend with just my descriptions of where the other ships were.

    In retrospect, I maybe should have been alarmed that the driver of the vehicle I was riding in was, in her mind, Kamikazeing the figher-ship she was piloting into a pirate spacecraft, and ejecting just before impact...

    \n So this is interesting timing. For the past couple months, I've been working on a car-portable/road-trip-specific RPG. The VERY rough draft of it goes like this:

    * License plates generate lists of numbers and letters

    * Road sign colors generate specific in-game events -- yellow warning signals are dangers, red signs mean fighting, blue signs mean an NPC wants to talk to you, etc.

    * The players have a single sheet of paper. This paper is folded once from top-to-bottom, and once from side-to-side. It is normally read unfolded. Folding the character sheet heightwise reveals a second column of information on the back that becomes available to you under certain circumstances -- like, to resolve some colors of sign-generated events. Folding the character sheet widthwise reveals half of that other back-side column, except obviously upside-down when folded that way. My thinking was that upside-down information represented adversity.

    * My thinking, for car-RPG purposes, was that you'd have one long column of letters, A-Z, that ran down the far left side of the back of the paper. A-M would be entries for "useful" assets -- allies, equipment, safe locations, etc. N-Z would be liabilities like enemies, monsters, curses, etc. It's the N-Z list that would flip "up" when you folded your sheet widthwise.

    * One train of thought I was pursuing was that any given asset/liability in the game would be "resolveable" via three steps: Reveal, Challenge/Transform, Resolve. Verrrry very simple and rough here. So, like, under "A" you might have "My Best Friend and Protector, Philip." But he's not part of the game fiction 'til you've had your Reveal scene with him. Once revealed, the next scene would be either a challenge or a transform (maybe depending on which color road sign brought the element into play) -- a challege might be to the friend/protector role of that character; a transform might change Philip to an enemy.

    There's more than that, but that's the very rough gist. One two-sided piece of paper, a list of 13@ assets and liabilities, random value and event generation based on what you see on the road.

    If there's any more interest I could build out my notes in more detail.

    \n I've used "guess a number between 1 and 10" pretty effectively for resolution. If it's really easy, you can succeed by guessing one of the two numbers on either side of my picked number. If it's harder, expand the 1-10 to 1-15 or 1-20. It's not the best system, but it works when dice are unavailable/unusable.
