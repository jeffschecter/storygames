\n After watching some of the videos and reading some of the articles at Ron\u2019s site, I feel like what is needed to expand the conversations and bring in new folks, is to make things more easily excessable. I was never part of the Forge and I feel as though it\u2019s history is getting in the way of bringing in fresh perspectives to advance the discussion of the subject.

The figurative speech and slippery jargon seems to lead to miscommunication and leave little room for gamers new to the language and concepts. By talking about RPGs in this way without much context, instead of talking about these ideas in the most accessible and plain way that is possible, even Forgites sometimes talk past one another and misunderstand what others are talking about.

By using such language and jargon without also grounding it in plain speech and specifics, the conversation gets obfuscated and people have a hard time getting on the same page and actually having a real meaningful, discussion and exchange of ideas. All of this terminology is slippery and people bring their own definitions, contextual understanding, and interpretational preferences the paradigms to the table, and often end up talking past one another. Almost all of these concepts can be spoken of in a more accessible and clear way without losing anything and without added difficulties. And the terms themselves, if relevant and needed, don\u2019t need to be lost, but simply contextulized within more accessible language. For example, are you coming at this concept from a Behaviorist interpretation or a psychological perspective?

Anyway, I feel like the site is less likely to get new ideas and impute and people are much more likely to be misunderstood. And from a financial perspective, it is less likely to create a larger audience. And creating a larger audience doesn\u2019t have to mean that the conversation gets watered down and less intelligent; by charging money to take part in the conversation more regularly, Ron weeds out those that are less thoughtful and meaningfully interested in the subject.

I personally I think there are a lot of people like me out there who are trying to get a better grip on the stuff and would appreciate people taking a little extra time so that the conversation and the ideas become a little bit clearer. Thanks.

What are your thoughts?

    \n Jeff,

    In most regards, I agree with you. Ron is particularly prone to using technical language and jargon (he himself invents), which makes his writing very dense and hard to follow. The advantage, though, is that he manages thereby to be very precise.

    I think that a call for more accessibility is a really good one, for all the reasons you mention. However, we can't just throw out the utility of established vocabulary between people, especially when it comes about after years of discussion. Without ever using specialized vocabulary, we become much less efficient at communicating with each other.

    Vincent, historically, has been really good at using "plain English" to explain more high-brow ideas. Ron has not (and intentionally so, I think). I don't expect him to change his ways now; that would be quite contrary to his character. (I think he values the precision of his own vocabulary too much for that, which is, from another angle, both understandable and laudable.)

Those, it seems to me, are constantly a topic of debate among the Forge-descended crowd anyway, as shown by this current discussion between Ron and Vincent, so it's clear people disagree about the specifics all the time.

    Thanks for starting this thread; I think making game theory conversations more approachable, relevant, and easier to understand is a very laudable goal (and particularly important when we see people getting personally offended by those conversations, although why they do so is still a mystery to me).

    \n Jeff,

    Here are some interesting things to consider:

    Although Ron was the progenitor of all kinds of wild theoretical jargon and lengthy essays, he always kept coming back to a couple of things.

    First, the best way to demonstrate theoretical concepts is through design. Read, play, and make different games, in other words - you'll learn the most that way. John Harper's list of "games any designer should play" is a good example of this mindset.

    Second, Ron clearly felt that purely theoretical discussion had all kinds of frustrating limitations. He eventually became more and more focused on grounding all discussions in "Actual Play". He eventually even shut down the "Theory" forums of the Forge altogether, and encouraged people to engage only in discussions of actual, real games being played by real people (as well as design and publishing/marketing, of course).

    ---

    A Sidebar on Jargon

    In a any specialized field, people inevitably start to use shorthand forms to refer to older, longer conversations. This turns into "jargon", which makes communication more efficient, but also makes it hard for others to join in on the conversation.

    So, what can we do? The logical, left-brain solution is to make clear lists of terms and to define them very carefully. (As in the Forge Glossary, which should still exist.)

    However, that's not how humans process language and develop vocabulary. Most native speakers, at least, don't learn words by reading their definitions in the dictionary. Instead, we listen to others use unfamiliar words, and we start to shape a vague sense of what a word is from hearing it in various contexts.

    This means that same word (e.g. Simulationist) can mean one thing in one community (e.g. the Threefold discussion of old) and a different thing in another (when Ron repurposed the word, with a different definition, for the Forge). We can try to avoid this by continuously coining new terms (Ron decided to clarify things by changing the name to "Right to Dream", but it's harder to say/type and unfamiliar, so people kept saying "Sim" instead, which was comfortable), but, ultimately, I think, we just have to get better at communicating with each other.

    I would second Dave's excellent suggestion above to approach Ron as some interested in learning and ask him if he could think about making discussion on his site less opaque, so as to more easily attract new voices and newcomers. I bet he would be quite receptive to that - he generally likes to educate and explain, and is very generous with his time in that respect.

    (However, it's also *possible* that he intentionally wants to set the bar high, just like with the cost aspects of his site, in order to limit conversation to those who are willing to take the time and effort to familiarize themselves with his mode of thinking. I don't *think* that's the case, but it's a possibility. You'll find out either way!)

      My take, briefly:

      Yes, the Forge was hard to breach, because "insider conversations" (like that video where Ron and Vincent talk about Creative Agenda!) used a lot of technical shorthand. (As Dave says, above.)

      However, everyone I can think of (and certainly Ron and Vincent!) were very open to newcomers and willing to speak in language they understood, explain basic concepts, ask questions, and so on.       The one thing that - as Eero says - gets really annoying and really old is people coming in and trying to use unfamiliar terminology in order to "fit in". Most of the time, those people came in with some mangled understanding of a concept they heard third-hand elsewhere on the Web, and, instead of explaining what they actually meant, wanted to wrangle terms.

      Ron complains about this in the video, by the way, describing it as a sort of "fetishism", where someone likes the sound of the word 'Simulationist' (or whatever other word) and therefore starts to attach their personal sense of pride to that term. Then, by extension, everything they like about roleplaying must now fit within that term, and if someone says otherwise, boy, sparks will fly!

      That alone is one good reason not to think of many of these concepts as being central to a player's identity - "I am a ___________, she is a ___________," in other words. Discussing Creative Agenda, for instance, gets a lot easier once we separate it from identifying a particular player's identity.

      That's no fun for anyone. Worse yet, it preempts any actual, useful discussion, because we end up arguing about terminology instead.

      Much better to avoid the jargon in most cases, and go straight to the point in plain English, unless you're rehashing an old conversation with someone you know will understand what you mean. (For instance, the term "story game" is pretty much useless if you're looking for something with a firm, explicit definition, but if I'm talking to you, Jeff, and you say "story game", I know what you mean, because we've gone over it so many times that you can productively use it as an effective shorthand with me.)

      \n Jeff,

      I apologize if I sounded dismissive earlier - when I said it was "quite straightforward", I was referring to a lot of things which you already know and understand, because they've entered the modern consciousness of people serious about thinking about games. For instance, it's not a shock to you, I think, to say that roleplaying is a conversation, or that a GM's tasks can be distributed amongst a variety of players (and therefore GMless design is possible), or that a game can be designed which leads us to construct good stories on the fly, and so forth. It's not a shocker to say that "System Matters", or that games can be designed to support different playstyles, which may, in turn, support different creative interests between people. It's not super crazy to say that roleplaying can be seen as a creative or collaborative idea, either!

      All of these were huge controversies when the Forge started up, at least for a large portion of the RPG population. I got into a long-running argument with a major RPG figure (the publisher of an RPG magazine I used to game with regularly), for instance, back in 2006 or so, and he was adamant that roleplaying, as an activity, could not be done, categorically, without prep. "A prepared adventure and a formal character design carried out before play are cornerstones of roleplaying - without them, it's a parody," or something of the sort, he said to me, and I never managed to convince him otherwise.

      The part that confuses people tends to be about the specifics of Creative Agenda, and that's largely because a) it ruffled the feathers of a million souls around the internet, who started to use the terms in their own, usually entirely different ways, and b) it isn't really (I would argue) all that well understood or agreed-upon by most of the people talking about it (with the exceptions of people like Ron and Vincent and Eero, who hashed it out in great detail over many years).

      That would be like reading a handful of forum posts on quantum physics by random strangers on the internet (half of whom are totally bullshitting) and expecting to have an understand of the nuances of how it all works.

      (I'm not trying to suggest that Creative Agenda theory is in any similar to quantum physics, by the way; just that the language around is somewhat opaque, the concepts sometimes counterintuitive, and that lots of disagreements remain except perhaps between experienced experts in the field who have discussed and studied for years. I followed a lot of stuff on the Forge, for instance, and I still found out in the last year or so that my understanding of Creative Agenda theory was somewhat heretical! Surprise! It's not an uncommon thing. But the specifics of Creative Agenda are a very, very small part of the Big Model.)

      I remember spending months at a time reading articles and discussion on the Forge and constantly *still getting it wrong* - for better or for worse (well, almost certainly for worse), that's how it is.

      I'll repeat my earlier advice, with Eero's excellent admonitions thrown in:

      * Read, play, and design games.

      * Engage people whose thinking on games you value in conversations about it.

      * Don't worry about the specifics of any jargon until and unless it becomes useful to you.

      As a distant fourth point, I've tried to send you some very basic and simple resources on this topic, including various links in this thread. Those are a good starting point for questions! (Finally, let's chat next time we have the opportunity to speak instead of typing; I may have some clarifying points for you!)
