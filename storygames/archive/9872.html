    \n Seriously ?

    RBH is our main game since it came out.
    We tried D&D 4e last summer. It was fun but not as much : the fights are much more intense in RBH, the story goes faster and the system is fun without work. And the prep is ultra easy. For now, the only game that can compete on the fun/easyness ratio is Danger Patrol (with a different play experience).

    We played RBH last monday with J\xe9r\xf4me, Marie and my nephew William (15 years old). The game was realy great as usual. We now have Louise, level 4 mage, and Alexandre, formely level 3 fighter dead and reborn as a mystic, now level 3.

    And I played the game with 9 different people. I can't count the number of games I GMed, which is realy a compliment given how I hate preparing for play usualy.

    I have finished my french translation of the game and I am working on the layout and illustrations. There's probably another round of editing also. I aim to make a free pdf available as soon as I can. The thing that slow me is that I am still searching for a good french title.

    I have also almost finished my 7 new classes with a Moorcock-ian / Howard-esque feel. And I have ideas about prep advice (I am totaly stealing from Apocalypse World on that) and monsters creation that I want to try. I realy want to play that special hack of the hack.

    Then I still have plans for my "Raiders of the Lost Hack"...

    So, seriously, are we still playing ?

    Best,
    Antoine

    \n Just to be a little bit more specific than my last post, here are the changes I use (or I am wondering whether to use or not) :

    - the dangers of action : I had a problem with the looseness of giving 1, 2 or 3 dangers to a player's goal. So, now, I use a system inspired by Burning Empires scenes categories. It's 1 danger for a simple goal, with imediate and limited reward (shoplift food, jump a chasm, etc...). 2 dangers for a more intense and complex course of actions than can involve mutiple different tasks and that can have lasting effects. It's like BE's Construction scenes. I usually allow a +2 bonus to a following roll related to a successfull 2 dangers scene. 3 dangers are for out of combat scenes that have as much intensity as a fight and with a dramatic goal. You can kill a NPC or capture a monster (but not kill it : I keep that for the combat only) with a 3 dangers scene. It's the equivalent of BE's Conflict scenes. I almost never use the danger of failing but I keep it for things like assassinations, etc... Most of the time, I throw complications on failed rolls. Speaking of which :

    - for the dangers themselves, I make a heavy use of a list of outcomes shamelessly stolen from Vincent Baker's Apocalypse World's Moves. It's basicaly a good list of general complications. That's gold for the GM.

    - I don't use Show Off actions for monsters. It's just not fun. Instead, I give them big damage attacks or powers that give them tokens on certain conditions (like when they are wounded or when they hurt a PC...).

    - I made a new NPC category : Rivals. They don't have powers or talents, but they can have good attributes and minions (like the monster power) and they can use the Show off and Assist actions. Basicaly, they are the people who are also after the monster or the treasure and have the capacity to steal them from the PCs. I was tired to have only weak humans or monsters. This change is nice. It has a more Sword & Sorcery vibe to it. We had two in the last game : the captain of a lansquenet-like mercenary company and an aged hidalgo commanding the Church of the Dragon's templars.

    - I use lists of types of treasures, NPC-monster relationship, NPC-treasure relationship,... to help me prepare fast and so that I don't always fall in the same old "statue of a lost god" for the treasure... Here is my treasure list :

    Sacred treasure
    Dangerous treasure
    Lost treasure
    Symbol of power
    Source of knowledge
    Family relic
    War prize
    Tax treasure
    Present or tribut
    Living (rare) creature

    Each treasure type gives me hint about the type of complications, I can throw at the PCs.
    Our last treasure was a living creature, two in fact : the last two dragons of the world (for now just babies). Just plain fun. Needless to say, one of the baby dragons is now the mystic's Animal companion.

    - I am still wondering if I like the RBH death rule or not. We had one dead PC at the table and it was a real shock. It turned the campaign upside down, shocked all players at the table (GM included) and sent us in unexpected direction. Still, at the moment, it almost broke the game. I'd like to have more info about how it went for other players. Something I am pondering is to come back to RBH1 death immunity, with 0HP meaning you are at the GM mercy and the possibility to raise the death flag in a fight, risking death for some benefit (most likely a +3 HP boost). But for now, we are playing by the rules.


    That's all I can think of for now.

    Best,
    Antoine

    \n Just ran a very successful RBH2 game at a friend's gaming weekend this last month. Ended up lasting a good five hours and was a complete game, I had 3 players and they all really responded to the system and the setting (though I've been talking about hacking it in another thread I also really like it as written, including the implied setting).

    My regular gaming group ran a four session mini-campaign with it, and everyone was raving about it as well. It pushed us in a lot of creative new directions, we had fights on top of floating cloud-whale fortresses, dangerous forays into the lair of the mist demons, giant sand dragons, the temple of the laughing monkey God, you name it.

    So yeah, I guess you could call us fanboys.

    \n Actually, I am making a RBH2 sword and sorcery hack for Fastaval. Fastaval is a Danish Easter rpgcon. In addition to fiddle with the system and the mechanics of the game, I am also writing a scenario for my hack.

    It's fun and will probably be a nice hack with lots of love to the real RBH2, but with a little more classic S&S action and more Heavy Metal atmosphere.
    The scenario part of the game is called Slavehandleren fra Ascalon (The Slavetrader from Ascalon). It's Henry D. Thoreau meets Robert E. Howard and Fritz Leiber meets Theodor W. Adorno and perhaps Gilles Deleuze (but Deleuze is probably a darling).

    I write it in Danish, but after Fastaval I'll translate it (back) into English.
