\n Back in this post, I asked if there was an easy entry into story-gaming for uninformed newcomers. The discussion quickly established that, no, there isn't and a lot of folks (myself included) think there should be. Discussion then turned to what form such a thing should take, the implications of such and how it would relate to the broader hobby. Eventually I stated that the discussion had expanded beyond the scope of my original question and begged off some time to organize my response. Well, I finally got around to it, so here it is.

What I am Proposing

I believe that its high time that the field of story-gaming was made accessible, available and appealing to as wide an array of people as possible. I'd personally would love to see it become an endevour engaged in by a spectrum of people as diverse as middle-American housewives, Korean film students and pre-teen summer-campers , a form of artistic expression with, if not mass appeal, at least easy acceptance. In the short term and more practically, I think real effort needs to be put into blowing away the wall of arcane babble which renders it confusing even to many of its current aficionados.

As per the original discussion, I agree that two separate but related efforts should be made. First, a general \xe2\u20ac\u0153Story Game Basic\xe2\u20ac\x9d needs to be written that serves as an able introduction to the hobby, one that presents core usable concepts in non-technical terms which most anyone will be able to read and understand. The goal is not to cover all possible permutations of story-gaming, but just to give a workable foundation. I've started work on something like this myself, but by no means do I claim exclusivity. However, for such a thing to achieve its goal, it'll need promotion...which is an issue that needs to be addressed.

Second, an online resource needs to be created to facilitate further exploration of the subject easily. A one page, friendly \xe2\u20ac\u0153Start Here\xe2\u20ac\x9d FAQ, jargon free and with an easy to reference web address (like \xe2\u20ac\u0153starthere.com\xe2\u20ac\x9d as opposed to \xe2\u20ac\u0153www.story-games.com/index/starthere/faqpage377642\xe2\u20ac\x9d), linking to newbie-friendly discussion site was suggested and seems good to me. Basically, a place were those of dawning interest can go without being overwhelmed by the glowering mass of the Forge. Good work to that end has already been started on this website.

Originally it was also proposed by several folks that a means of making story-games appealing to traditional RPG'ers should also be presented, but for reasons I state below this is not of personal interest to me.

Many further particulars of how all this will all be achieved has yet to be determined.

Why I Think It Should Be Done

Bluntly, I think the current state of this hobby is ridiculous. The still nascent story-game community is, in my opinion, suffering from a strongly insular attitude, a cliquish mindset that, at its worst, disdains people who aren't already in the know. More than one person in the discussion that preceded this one conveyed such an attitude either obliquely or directly.

On mere philosophical grounds alone I find this distasteful enough, but there are practical ramifications as well. The indie game field is, I think, starting to suffer from practical stagnation, over-specialization and creative inversion. As a friend/occasional enemy of mine quipped, \xe2\u20ac\u0153they're all aspiring game-artists, too busy writing blogs and games to play, selling to other aspiring game-artists looking for funky rules to write into their own blogs and games.\xe2\u20ac\x9d Stated more politely, as far as I can tell nearly all of the indie-game movement's efforts are being directed towards theory and design with at best token energy being spent on reaching the existing indie and story game audience, and none on expansion. If that state endures the audience and market will shrink, and nobody will be around to pay you to write game books anymore.

And of course, the more people that have been exposed to the medium, the easier time I'll have getting players for my games.

Why I'm discussing this on the Story-Games fora

Obviously, at least in part because I like the term \xe2\u20ac\u0153Story-Game\xe2\u20ac\x9d. More broadly, because of all the online hobby game communities I think, with its focus on practical utility, it is the community most open to the idea and the one most likely to do constructive things with it.

My Underlying Assumptions

I think the effort is worthwhile for its own sake. I also think it is easily feasible. If I, with no sort of formal technical computer background and largely through access to helpful online communities, was able to get enough of a working knowledge of Linux to comfortably use it as my default operating system, I don't see any reason why this community can't get its act together enough to clearly explain what a \xe2\u20ac\u0153R-map\xe2\u20ac\x9d is when someone asks.

I have one huge prejudice that colors my expectations and approach to this, so I better state it forthrightly: I prefer story-gaming as a hobby of interested amateurs taking a DIY approach to things rather than a field of semi-pro designers building up a consumer market. Open-source, information should be free and all that other hippie-commie blather. So I'm not too big on questions about how to make money off the thing, though I do understand it as a useful motive.

Finally, but significantly, I think it is time to recognize that story-gaming is a related but distinct medium from traditional role-playing games and, as it were, cut the apron strings. For the purpose of bringing in new players, it is no longer desirable to make a point of the line of progression from D&D to Prime Time Adventures, and such may actually be a hindrance. With that in mind, I don't think \xe2\u20ac\u0153converting\xe2\u20ac\x9d existing traditional gamers is a priority. I think any who would be interested have come over by now, and effort would be better spent elsewhere.

Well, there you go. Now you can start tearing it apart.

    Posted By: T-BoyDoes it matter if we focus our attention to completely new users or not? I was initially going to say "No, we shouldn't just focus in attracting complete newbies into the hobby," but I realized something; old school roleplayers are essentially the same as newbies.     \nI partially disagree. I think a 'pitch' for RPG virgins is different than one to "traditional RPG" players. Even though both groups are new to story games, they come from different contexts. In particular, traditional RPG players have been playing RPGs and using dice and jargon. Pitching to them should leverage the language and knowledge of their context, to avoid confusion and being dismissed for weird or "wrong" rules.

    I'm no marketer, but I've been starting to brainstorm how Al Ries and Jack Trout's Positioning and "Marketing Warfare" strategies might relate to pitching and positioning story games. One of my favorite marketing tactics of theirs is to identify your competitor's strength and reposition it as a weakness.

    Who are story games' competitors? These consumers seem like good candidates for potential story game buyers.\n
      \n
    • Traditional role-playing games
    • Games of the non-role-playing variety
    • Non-games, like TV, movies, and books
    • Computer games?
    \nI think the biggest pool of potential buyers are the Buffy/Firefly/Lost/Heroes TV fans. They are inspired by their TV shows' setting, but are passive participants. Position story games as a fun, social way to create their "living fanfic". Considering the sheer numbers of TV viewers and the fanfic many already write (without having been tainted by bad D&D experiences), this is a ripe opportunity. Another Ries and Trout positioning tactic is to sell your product where your potential customers shop, not where your existing competitors sell! Imagine if PTA was sold on bookshelves besides Buffy/Firefly/Lost/Heroes books or DVDs, instead of on the bottom shelf of the bookstore's D&D corner.

    I think the second pool of potential buyers are traditional role-playing gamers who want more story. How do you identify these players? I imagine World of Darkness players would be more open to story games than D&D 3.5E gamers.

    Does one pitch them story game accessories to mod their existing game of choice, allowing them to preserve their time and money investment? Or do you pitch them story game stepping stones to their own games?

You get them (if you get them at all) because some individual fruit-cake calls up a keychain manufacturer and forks over some dough out of pocket, just because it can be done.

    Indie Gaming Explosion, Games-on-Demand, Go-Play, Story-Games ... heck, even the FORGE were created using exactly this model: one guy (or a handful) makes something cool, knowing that it will probably flop. They put their efforts out there to be judged by the world ... no safeguards, no guarantees, no apologies. Anything less is worse than useless. It's a distraction.

    Less talking. More doing. Make a Primer. Either it will revolutionize the hobby, or it won't. Put your money where your mouth is.

    Quick tanget about minis and wargaming:
    There actually is a fair bit of experimentation with miniatures gaming out there, but generally it tends to suffer from some of the same issues that affect rpg design.

    First, there are a lot of use "Legacy" type rules/mechanics, the same tendency that has produced the "Fantasy Heartbreakers". Essentially, minis games tend towrds having a couple of cool new ideas married to a bunch of stuff that is there because "that's the way it has always been done".

    Occasionally though, you'll see designers stepping out and stepping back to look at what is really the essence of the thing and you begin to see some very different sorts of designs, including methods that share some common design philosophy with story games ( but arrived at independantly -Parallel Evolution? Cargo Cult design? Who knows).

    Second, there seems to be the problem of commercial viability. The more experimental minis games seem to suffer even a bit more from "niche interest" problems than story-games/indie rpgs.

    Third, there doesn't seem to be any central design forum for this kind of stuff on par with The Forge. Instead, what you get are widely physically seperated designers occasionally bumping into each other online or possibly at convetions ( something I recently experienced). The rapid, back-and-forth conversations that act as evolutionary agents for design are simply occuring at a much more glacial pace where minis games are concerned.

    Fourth, the long-term after-effects of the rpg'er/wargamer split of the late '70s/ early '80s is still having an ugly impact. Reading both rpg and wargame sites, it strikes me that a lot of hobbyists are unaware of developments and even current design trends in the other camp.
