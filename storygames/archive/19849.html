    \n That's the thing; If they give you a list of 'barebones stuff to mix and match' you end up with very samey items.

    I think magic item design in DW is designed to be done in the style of "Think of a cool effect, then create an item around it." (or, occasionally, "Think of a slightly silly sounding item, name, then create an effect for it.").

    "Too powerful" is rarely a concern in DW, because all effects are filtered through the fiction. Avoiding duplication of moves will require a little bit of knowledge of what the advanced moves for your characters are, but doesn't sound like an insurmountable obstacle. A little bit of googling around seems to generate large numbers of interesting items for fairly little effort, but again, I'd suggest going from Idea to Item, rather than picking out some items/effects and then trying to insert them into your game.

    \n An easy way to make interesting magic items is to write a custom move that the item grants a character.

    For minor magics, these moves may have limited or specific triggers; for major magics, the trigger or effect may be far reaching.

    So: what cool fictional actions do you think it would be fun, interesting, or challenging for the characters to be able to do?

    \n I usually think of an interesting fictional effect, and then add to it a cost, limit, or condition to activate it (no custom move, though). I reckon it's hard to stay away from the D&D tradition (+1 to attacks, fire damage, +1 to CA, etc.); players tend to expect to receive magical objects at a rate similar to D&D, and sometimes are not very interested in the stuff I give them, they want things that make them mechanically better.

    The things that yielded most enthusiasm from players were "overpowered" magic items, because they instantly became a resource to be considered when facing an obstacle: a spell scroll that lets you instantly kill in flames someone anywhere in the world (only once), a ring that lets you slip into the ghost world for free, a set of two rings that can be used to divide damaged as desired between both bearers, a bracelet that, when worn, makes you immune to surprise and treachery, but also uncapable of it. That sort of stuff.

    When in doubt, I make the item consumable.

    \n I think previous posters have covered DW magic item creation pretty well - here are a couple of specific techniques I've used to create "stock" magical items, and/or mirror traditional D&D loot:

    Charges: Wands come with x-charge (x=5, 10, whatever you wish). Each wand comes with a move that starts: "When you say the wand's activation phrase, spend charge and roll +charge spent..."

    (Do they know the activation phrase? No? Time to go on an adventure to find someone who does!)

    Cursed items: may or may not have other bonuses, but generally have a conditional XP-gain mechanic attached. For instance: "Whenever you goad someone into a fight, mark XP"; "Whenever you betray a friend, mark XP"; "Whenever you run or hide rather than fight, mark XP". This lets the player retain agency, but still encourages them to give into the curse.

    Private moves: I haven't done this yet, but I can imagine handing one player an index card with the move of their new magic item written on it. Now they know what it does, but the rest of the party is still amazed when they use it to summon beasts from the nether realms...

    \n When I ran "Keep on the Borderlands" in DW, one of the players rescued a hostage and got a magic dagger. For her, I set it up as: roll +Charisma at the start of the session--7-9 hold 1, 10+ hold 3. Then I let her spend the hold to do "magic weapon stuff": throw the dagger and have it come back, deal damage without any danger to her, cut through chains, that kind of stuff.

    The barbarian got a magic helmet as a reward; I let that allow him to locate any remaining members of his scattered tribe.

    The bard got a harp that at the very least allowed him to understand different languages; I don't think we ever found out what else it did.
