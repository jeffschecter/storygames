    \n I've seen Steampunk in two ways (not that there aren't others):

    1) Science fiction, as viewed from the past (Jules Verne, etc). This is just interesting, historically.

    2) In the same way that magic and the supernatural can sometimes be used to take a theme and punch it up in big letters, exagerrating its impact, steampunk takes the effects of the industrial revolution of the 1800s and exagerrates its effect on people. In this case, steampunk technology is a metaphor for people's fears and hopes.

    \n Steampunk is about the fantastic. It's far enough into the realm of the impossible that it doesn't need to apologize.

    Steampunk is about restrictive social mores. Whether serving as an analog for modern social concerns, or simply acting as a foil for daring radicals to rebel against.

    Steampunk is about artisanship. Hand-crafted technology that feels warmer and more human than microchips and molecules. It's big and clunky and you might be able to fix it by hitting it with a wrench. Steampunk extols the virtues of authorship to a modern world surrounded by mass-production.

    Steampunk is about exploration. Usually colonial exploration, with all the baggage that entails. Given that a lot of modern world conflicts seem to be coming out of post-colonial areas, steampunk finds use as a form for exploring colonial ambitions, seriously or satirically.

    \n Steampunk -- or at least, good steampunk -- is about the present. Steampunk is a sort of odd "genre" in that it has all the elements of modern society, just tweaked slightly. This avoids the problems that you can sometimes find in, say, fantasy social commentary, because the presence of magic and the absence of technology (not to mention the absence of, say, democracy) skews things slightly. Steampunk does not have this problem, but any modern-day theme that you might want to talk about has a suitably distanced foil in steampunk -- familiar technology becomes all whirring gears and steam pipes, culture wars become victorian class struggles, corporations become trade houses, and so on. And while they all have curious idiosyncracies and all, they're never (or at least, rarely) so far off the original mark that they can't stand in quite capably for their modern complements in any thematic statement or consideration.

    \nThere are few social injustices in the western world today that weren't present in the Victorian era. Racisim, Sexism, Classism, repressed sexuality, extreme poverty, diseases and so on. The big thing is that where today we mostly try to avoid those things and work to reduce them, back then for the most part...not only were they accepted they were considered a part of Right Thinking. Of course the poor don't matter, of course women aren't as good as men, and of course non caucasian peoples were inferior and the West was doing them a favor by civilizing them...
    \n
    \nSo to play Steam Punk as something more than just quirky tech fluff, means to enter into an age where the roots of our issues today are on full display and encounter them in that context. Just try to play a right thinking aristrocratic gentlemen without either 1) bringing in anachonistic modern egalitarian sensibilities or 2) turning him into a hateful caricature. Try playing a true honest to god period hero...who just happens to adhere to all of society's mores. Oh he may be willing to accept a particular talented woman on her own merits, but he'll do so in full recognition that she's a rare exception to her gender. He may have a particular man of color that he treats with respect due to some special circumstance in their past, but for the most part such people are invisible except when service is required. He may help the poor but he does so because they can't help what they are...a product of bad breeding. Try to play this person as sympathetically as possible, and see what happens.
    \n
    \nThat's what I think steam punk allows. It allows us to explore just how easy that thinking is to fall into and compare it to how easily it is to fall into it yet today.
    \n
    \nThe Steam Punk is just the catalyst for throwing society into turmoil. For compressing 100 years of technological development into 10. For envisioning computers without the civil rights and feminist movements in between.

    \n@Paul:

    The term "steampunk" came from The Difference Engine by William Gibson and Bruce Sterling. Because Sterling invented the idea of "cyberpunk" and Gibson wrote the first cyberpunk stories and novels, their foray into Victoriana sci-fi got the name "steampunk."
    [edit: or perhaps I should say "the popularity of the term" was a result of Engine...]

    According to Steampunk Magazine the genre actually begins with The Land Leviathan by Michael Moorcock, though he used the term "scientific romance," to put himself in the same genre as Jules Verne, etc. There is an interview with Moorcock in Steampunk issue 1 - a pretty decent magazine available for free in pdf.

    Then again, some people will consider Jules Verne and scientific romances as part of the steampunk genre, because it's Victorian-era sci-fi. But are there steam engines in his stories? If he's in the genre, then The King in Yellow must be, too (written in 1895, set in 1920...). My dad was making post-apocalyptic steampunk comics in the 1970s. Were they Victorian at all? Not really, but they had steam engines in them...

    So, what is "steampunk" really about? You could check the wikipedia entry. A quick summation? It started as early science fiction. When the sci-fi genre advanced with the times, some people looked back at the old sci-fi and used it for influence. Then they started writing alternate history sci-fi where obsolete technology was put to difference uses. Then it turned into an aesthetic or style, a "look." Nobody ever really embodied the genre enough to give it a distinctive stamp, unlike cyberpunk.

    \n To me "steampunk" encompasses two type of games I like to play.

    First: Scientific Romance stories.

    These are adventures filled with wonder and the hopeful possibilities of science. It harkens back to an age when science was a glorious, positive thing. When scientists and explorers faced mystery and danger with a strong mind and a strong heart. Where voyaging to Mars was as much about impressing the Martian Emperor with your genteel manners as it was building your Marvelous Invention. These type of games are about the reinvention/rediscovery of lost futures.

    Second: Steampunk stories.

    These are the stories of the underbelly of the past. By moving a story from the present to a psuedo-history one can give the themes of poverty, struggle, class conflict a new poignancy. It allows the players a mental distance. Cyberpunk does this, as all science fiction should. It allows us to analyze our world by emphasizing certain elements and allowing us space to step away from issues to get a bigger picture.
