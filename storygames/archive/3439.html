\n First, a quick re-introduction of the premise of the game.\n
Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple
A group of young quasi-Buddhist monks are on a pilgrimage, guided by letters written by people in need of their help.
\nI'd previously described the "classes" of Do as being "a set of mechanically encouraged behaviors."

I am considering including "powers" with these classes, but that word doesn't feel right. Neither does "stunt" or "feat." I would like the special abilities granted by the class to apply as much to the player as to the character.

Yeah, the pilgrims can all fly. Yeah, there might be some other wuxia abilities on display. I don't want to just make them straight superheroes, though. And yet, I like the idea of weirdness and unusual events occurring in the pilgrims' presence, sometimes caused by the pilgrims themselves, but not always.

Even if they had special powers, because Do is leaning towards being GM-free, I couldn't rely on a GM to introduce situations where characters could show off those abilities. So, I thought it'd be cool if I gave the players some toys that facilitated affecting the game in a manner similar to "powers," but flavored in a different way. In lieu of powers, I'm mulling over giving each class a short list of "events" that are likely to occur when they're around.

As an example of the difference:\n
If you were to take the classic D&D fighter, his powers would include stuff like doing extra damage, attacking multiple targets, and surviving extensive injuries.

If you were to look at the Fighter's "events," they'd probably include stuff like "Ambushed!" "A New Challenger Arrives," and "All Problems Can Be Solved With Punching."
\nBasically, Events could include the stuff that'd normally be considered powers, but they'd also go beyond the scope of the character's abilities, extending to the territory of general scene-framing. It's an even more explicit way of saying to the group, "In playing this character, I am declaring that I want this sort of stuff to happen in the story."

What I also like about this is that you could take any ol' archetype, from pauper to prince, from Batman to Superman, from cute pet to ancient nature spirit, and they'd be equally "powerful" in terms of how they could affect the flow of the narrative, yet they'd still be unique and flavorful.

I might about go through the list of archetypal characters on tvtropes.org and see if this holds up by attributing behaviors and events to some of the characters listed there.

Again, I get the sneaking suspicion that I've seen this somewhere before. Are there other games that take a similar perspective on "powers"? Also, is "Event" the right word for this?

    \n Well, suppose a fighter is good at taking on mooks. In a GMed game, I'd advise that the GM look at that ability and use that as a cue to insert a couple scenes where the fighter can take on a big mob of ninjas, zombies or nameless henchmen. "Ambushed!" would be a way for the fighter-player to introduce such a scene. At which point he'd... resolve it... hrm...

    Gah... now I'm seeing the trouble. "I'm introducing my own opportunities to be awesome" doesn't sound very fun, does it?

    Hey, what if you worked in a card-drafting mechanic? Say your class has a deck of event cards tailored specifically to that class. You select some events you'd like to see in the upcoming scene, the other players do the same, do a little drafty thing, then everyone's got a hand of cards that other players chose to include in the next scene. You get the narrative authority of introducing the events, but still get the element of surprise because you never know when it's coming.

    Or maybe I should've let this idea gestate a bit before posting it. :P
