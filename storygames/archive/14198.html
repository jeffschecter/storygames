    \n For me, for most of us... it's not a problem. If you post on Story Games, you probably have multiple venues to play these games.

    I like Games on Demand, but you don't know what you are going to get when you go there. And after hour gaming, which I also enjoy, isn't very accessible (which I understand for many is a plus).

    But if you are looking to play a specific game, say Apocalypse World, there isn't an obvious way for outsiders to get involved.

    I've been going to Gen Con longer than I've been involved in indie gaming. Gen Con has always been a great way to get exposure to new games. There was one year at Gen Con where I spent the entire time running Dogs in the Vineyard for Paizo, White Wolf, and Wizards freelancers. Quite a few of them were surprised they had never heard of the game before. I don't know if that has anything to do with scheduled events but it does make me wonder about making game more accessible (assuming that's a desirable goal, the answer will be different for different designers & publishers).

    Posted By: MatrixGamerGetting a game up to the level of awareness of a wide audience is tough.

    Think about it. If Dogs in the Vinyard is not know, then what indie game will be known?
    \nGood point but I think when it comes to Gen Con games, a lot of people choose games not based on System but based on Title and Game Description. And then if they like the game, they may try the system on their own. I know this is how I and many of my friends (usually over 40 of my friends go to Gen Con every year) have been introduced to new games. We get hooked into the color, and then stay for the system.

    I should add though, I don't think Gen Con events are about reaching a wide audience. Events are too small and too long to have such a wide reach. Hosting a seminar at Pax is a much better way to reach 400 people in 1 hour vs a Gen Con event where you reach 4-6 people in 4 hours.

    But I think the value of Gen Con is that it reaches gaming group decision makers. Many of the people going to Gen Con take their gaming experiences with them back home. And an event is a great way to connect with them and leave a lasting impression. Many people will buy games at the Exhibit Hall and then never play them. But I've noticed people who play at events are more likely to actually play back home.

    I think this is one of the reasons why Dread has had a strong appeal among D&D players. They've been running several Dread games at Gen Con every year for as long as Dread has been out. And eventually it impressed several key influential members in the En World community and began to spread.

    But to be fair, Dread is also just a phenomenal game, draws immediate attention to itself via the physical Jenga tower, and has a basic system that is appealing to many D&D players with a more story focus.

    6. Make sure to set your events priority by selecting the Priority Number to the left of each event! Tickets will be processed in that order.

    7. You can also view your events in chronological order (so you can start to plan your schedule and make sure events don't overlap) by clicking "View Wish List by Start Time".

    Then this Sunday before Noon EST:

    1. Sign in.

    2. Go to your Wish List.

    3. When Registration opens, the "Submit Wish List for Processing" button will become enabled. I don't know if this is automatic or if you have to refresh your page for this to happen. But when it is ready, click it to register for your events!

    \n Totally makes sense! Same is true for me. I can learn about games online much easier than at conventions. But I don't personally represent a lot of gamers out there.

    I know a lot of people who go to Gen Con that don't spend a lot or any time reading about games online. Looking at the number of community members on sites like Wizards, En World, RPG.net\u2026 most gamers may not be actively reading about RPGs online at all. I know plenty of people who barely have exposure to games outside of conventions, game stores, and what their friends tell them. That said, it is also true that many gamers don't go to conventions at all. That's another reason why I feel game stores are so important (not for selling games but reaching people that are hard to reach otherwise).

    Plus conventions have the added bonus that they are great places to learn how to play new games. Arming with you the knowledge to then teach others. I've noticed an incredible amount of people learn new games via word of mouth, and less so from game text. As an aside, this is another reason why instructional videos may be of great help in the future. They are already popular among many boardgames.

    \n The first Con i ever went to was GoPlayNW. It was amazing. It was awesome. And i only knew a handful of people there.

    The first GenCon i ever went to was the four worst days in gaming i've ever had. I spent the day playing D20 spinoffs with people that only peppered their gaming with character interaction, story points, or anything related to social interaction that didn't involved rolling dice and grinding numbers. What made it all worse was that i went every night to the Storygamer's lounge for afterhours gaming, where i knew a couple of the people from GPNW that were there, but everyone had already joined games and closed themselves off. I got a couple of friendly waves when i first walked up, and on one of the nights Emily Boss was wonderfully generous and came over to talk with me and a friend as we were playing her game Sign In Stranger, but for the most part i just sat in those big chairs in the lobby and looked at the cool kids from afar.

    The rift between Indie Gamers (who are so outside of the norm they're invincibly cool) and Trad Gamers (who are a growing blend of chic geek and the genuine outcast label) is something that leaves me bummed out. I really wish it was cool to just like games. But (and this is just my impression/feelings) it feels like you kinda have to be into one or the other.

    Every friend i went to GenCon with loved it. They only go there, and they go there every year. I keep going to GPNW. I wish there wasn't a rift. But i just can't spend the money on airfare, room and board, and access to the games for an experience that leaves me feeling like i don't get it, or like i don't belong (or just really bored).

    Posted By: Leonard BalseraPosted By: orklordI don't exactly know why, but there is 0 appeal for the formalized structure applied to indie games for me.
    \nThis.
    I don't know about other folks, but I don't go to Gen Con to play games.

    Typically I end up working a podcast or a booth (or, last year, both), so there's no time during the day to play games. In the evening, I want to relax and drink with friends I haven't seen in ages. That's where my priorities are. That's where a number of other folks' priorities are. And then there are those who have as a priority to spend time with those people. The cascading effect is "indieland games, but off-schedule." [1]

    Means I end up averaging two games a year, between Wednesday night & Monday morning. Not that that means I didn't have a great time.

    This year, I'm free of responsibilities during the day. So I'm planning on using Games on Demand and Twitter (and that last piece is super crucial to this plan) to get my game on. GoD is our cultural safety net, but it only works if it's heavily utilized [2]. Many of us get that, so we're looking at a choice between submitting games or supporting GoD. Many are choosing to support GoD. Combine that with the effect above, and there's an understanding of why we have few games on the schedule.

    - Ryan

    [1] Which is unfortunate, since that means we are less inclusive than many of us claim. But I don't think that's inherently bad -- none of us should feel the responsibility of going the extra mile unless we actually feel like it. I've done that as smaller cons where the atmosphere is different, but it's not my bag for Gen Con.

    [2] I did a couple shifts of GoD in 2007, when it was in a hallway in the Hyatt.
