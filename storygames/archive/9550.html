\n I mean a chorus in the Greek theatrical sense, usually a mob of onlookers who provide a commentary on the action. As a GM it's a technique I've used occasionally, notably in Camberwick Green, a cross between Delta Green and 70s puppet show. I would assume the persona of the narrator and say things like "Look children, Pugh has eaten some space mead, isn't he a silly little puppet."

I imagine this kind of thing has already turned up in Jeepform where players can provide a running commentary on the actions of other players.

So, is it a technique that you've ever used, or considered using, either as a one-off for a scenario or perhaps even in game design?

    \n To be fair, my chorus was a chorus of one, which made up for the lack of scripting. But even without scripting I find that characters or players pick up themes in games. For example, in a long Viking RQ3 campaign, one of the characters earned the nickname Craven for seeming cowardice in his very first fight (he was hit by a spell) and whenever he went into a fight after that there were always comments (OOC) from the other players.

    \n I've often thought this would be a great technique to use in a game. Comments from others in jeepform can feel this way. An improv exercise I did once had the group be a chorus of emotions. One player spoke, while the others spoke out the interior thoughts of the speaking character, going from one emotion to another.

    At Camp Nerdly we played The Butterforger with about a dozen players. The players not in the scene played the audience for the trial in the game world, and they were much like a chorus. Calling for meatballs and death for the defendant at various times (though they declared him innocent at the end, since they functioned as a jury too.)

    \n At DexCon last year, we did a reality TV show version of It's Complicated, and occasionally the scene that someone would choose would be just them, directly talking to the television camera about what was going on in the house, "Confessional" style. It turned out to be surprisingly chorus-like, and worked very well, especially for the messy late-game scenes where you have to reveal how you feel about a ton of people.

    \n This is a topic that has been on my mind lately! In my Swords & Wizardry game that I am running on Twitter, the players hired a local teenager as a torchbearer. I've been using her reactions to highlight the genuine creepiness of wandering around in dank tunnels with things natural and unnatural trying to kill you. After all when a severed head starts talking this is not something most people would be blas\xe9 about.

    This has allowed me to put some emotional resonance into scenes without resorting to the rudeness of putting words or feelings into the mouths or heads of the PCs. It occured to me that Tara the torchmaiden was playing the part of a chorus, and I have started pondering how I might use this technique in other games. So I'm excited to hear about other people's experiences with the idea!

    \n I run a Monday night Necessary Evil game. The theme of the setting is a global uprising, so I ripped a page out of Grey Ranks (thank you JaMo), and produce a short Radio Lightning broadcast and distribute it before each session. It functions like a chorus, in that I mean to use it to hold a mirror up to the campaign and the PC's actions. The players seem to enjoy the hell out of them.

    Also, they are fun and easy to make - I aim for a length of about a minute.

    Here are the first two:

    http://www.box.net/shared/miluuv00my

    http://www.box.net/shared/ditp8nr89e

    I like the idea of using a character who is in the story and has personal contact with the PC's, like the torchbearing teenager. This seems like a purer use of the chorus concept. I take it that the idea is to use the unfortunate lass to reflect how normal men and women would look at the actions of a typical PC. Insert the personal horror you would feel at watching a group of people fight toe-to-toe with clubs and daggers, and then pluck weapons and valuables from the corpses of the dead.

    There was a framing concept game that was published a few years back, with the idea that the real campaign action was the delusions of a mental patient, and sessions of the 'actual' game would be framed with therapy sessions discussing the character's actions (i.e. "I staked my sister when she became a vampire!" "Stay calm, Mr. Tevis. Let's explore this...").

    Does this count as 'chorus'? And does anyone have personal experience using this game?

    \n Wow, Sal, the psych-patient approach sounds like a pretty tight, specific medium, but it could contain some really cool 1:1 player/GM opportunities! I wonder if the GM's input could come in the form of "now, that doesn't make any sense" and other, Drama-based nudges to re-establish credibility. Neat!

    For that matter: it sounds, absolutely, like your use of Radio Lightning is functioning as a Chorus - an entity that provides context for the audience. That's what I would consider the role of a Chorus, at any rate, and if the actors are also the audience, this fits the role nicely!

    On a tangentially-related note, I've decided to call the GM the "Chorus" in a new Story Now game of mine, set in mythical Greece: The Hellenes. I like the implication that the GM's job is to portray a lot of different people, and since the Chorus is a group (I think), this seemed to fit the setting pretty well.

    So far I've been using Tara the torchmaiden not so much to comment on the behavior of the PCs, but to provide a "fresh eyes" reaction to the creepy strangeness of the dungeon itself. A severed head that talks is strange and frighting, a room filled with rotting bones is foul and disgusting, and a pack of goblins are unnerving and they are TRYING TO KILL YOU.
