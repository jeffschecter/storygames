How to make Airships Exciting?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nAndy \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2007 \n edited March 2007 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Yeah, I know, "Airships... NOT Exciting? Check for pulse!"

But the Airships side-item in the "Geeky Things You Like" thread got me thinking.

I LOVE airships. In fact, way back when (8 years ago, almost) Mike Montesa, Cam Eeles and I were planning on putting together a game that was nothing more than "AIRSHIPS! Fight!"

Thing is, in play (as GM) I've found it hard to make them seem as exciting as I think they should be. What are some ways to convey how awesome they are... well, actually USE them in play? How can you use them to make them seem more special than just another boat?

Some stuff that I've used:

Miniatures: Using Icehouse pyramids to show facing, size and the like, I had the PCs show off, visually, the types of manuvers in 3-d they were doing to evade and attack the enemy. That level of hands-on, with the manipulation of the ship in mid-air, I think helps convey some of the excitement.

Be on one when it blows up: The PCs were all on the equivalent of a Destroyer, with a huge topside landing platform. It got blowed up: The deck started splitting apart; the ship, while still afloat, was starting to sink gradually (with that feeling like, "any minute now the engines are going to die and we're going to plummet to our deaths"). The PCs scrambled to save people and be saved, meanwhile dodging firebombs and enemy landing parties. Lifeboats (in the form of skiffs that look like floating jetskis with a raft attached) were in short supply, and the PCs had to make tough choices. I think that, even more than a "sinking ship", the whole, "If this 'sinks', there really is no hope of rescue, just a mile drop to our deaths" was a cool experience for the players.

Any other ideas?

-Andy

    \n Andy,

    So totally.

    If you fall off a normal boat you land in the water. This is bad as there are sharks and shit and you may be too tired to swim.

    If you fall off an airship your ass is grass.

    Seriously though, airships are crazy shit because (mostly) they look like normal ships that fly through the air. Falling from the mast of a normal ship is a bad deal, falling from the mast of an airship is worse. Falling from the rail of an airship will kill you for sure, unless you've got special mojo. Play up the insane gut-busting courage it has to take to work an airship. The winds, the vertigo, the mad, glorious, insane and utterly impractical beauty of it all.

    Also, your airship can go places and do shit. Remember the first plot arc of the Authority where they plowed the dude's building under with their ship? Yea, like that. A normal ship is cool, but you can't use it to lay siege to the castle of the giant king at the top of a mountain. You can't use it to get to Denver. Really, its kinda funny, but I've played in lots of games with air ships (or zepplins or planes or whatever) where the GM kinda blinks when you use your ship to, you know, radically alter how difficult something is. Bullshit upon that. When the players say, "Oh, the messenger has a four day head start on us and fresh horses every 10 miles? Well, we'll leave tomorrow after whoring the night away today because we'll be able to overtake him before he gets to the mountains with OUR AIRSHIP" let it happen. When no one else can get past the blockade on every road into the kingdom, let the airship sail on by laughing and flipping off the king's men. I mean, of course there will eventually be the kings airship come to fight you, but then you get that coolness. For the short run let the cool thing be cool, let it do what no one else can do.

    Then there are lots of things that come to mind for making the ship special, but that aren't so different from what you do with any ship-based coolness. The living crew, the repairs, the knowing the boat like the back of your hand, and all that stuff. But those are the things that don't matter much if you're in the air or not. Also, if you can find them, diagrams are awesome. Modding a detailed drawing of a sailing ship into an air ship to give a sense of the vast complexity and scope of the thing is always a way to get players salivating.
