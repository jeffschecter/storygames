\n I've been strongly considering running a game that borrows concepts from Doctor Who.

Probably my biggest issue is escalation - I'm good at escalating in the direction of violence, but not-as-good as escalating tension while keeping nonviolent options open. I want to make sure that the players are free to choose NOT to fight without choosing to suck.

So I have two questions:

How can I maintain the plausibility of nonviolent options open to the players?

    \n Find a copy of Misspent Youth, even if it's the free "eyebleed" pdf on the site. Step through conflict, but don't use violence in your raises.

    It doesn't change a thing! It's still awesome. We've even used the conflict mechanics there to stretch over three days worth of effort. One of the Youthful Offenders used their smarts to infiltrate the securit detail and get the badges they needed to get past a checkpoint, which took six hours of painfully clever effort.

    The Authority responds by taking that blow, nobody knows how, but the gate's been compromised, and their operation is threatened. So they up the plant's production and move all their hostages/workers closer to the chemicals.

    ... Long story short, the framework and the rythm provided plenty of tension, especially when the players stood up to do something. That entire conflict, and many others, have passed without physical violence.

    It may not be what you're looking for exactly, but it might give you some ideas.

    \n Hope it's not a problem to jump in before question 2 is on the table.

    There's two things that come to mind:

    Something that works in the (old) Doctor universe, is letting the characters have interactions with the npcs prior to the plot kicking in. If you've met the kooky old keeper of the lighthouse, you might be able care more about the light being sabotaged, before you even deal with the crazy monster killing folks.

    When you have issues and problems on the line that can be solved without violence, then you can have tension that is cranked up by the threat of violence.

    Secondly, it makes me think of advice in Dogs in the Vineyard, for the gm to ask for smaller things in raises rather than large. The opening scene with the Nazis in the French farm house in Inglorious Basterds illustrates this. Christopher Waltz asking the farmer for something innocuous like a glass of milk hugely increased the tension, long before any violence broke out.

    \nThe existing/current Dr Who RPG does this in a very simple and awesome way. It seems too simple to work, but in its simplicity is volumes.

    Here's the caveat: Your game is *like* Dr. Who but without being Dr. Who, so I'm not sure if this applies, but: In the RPG, just like the show, Dr. Who is constantly pitted against monstrously powerful (combat-wise) creatures: Daleks, Cybermen, monster of the Week.

    In the RPG, they have an initiative system, and it's not a "number": It is solely based on What You Plan to Do. Here is the simple four-point action list, in order of who goes first vs who goes last:

    Talking
    ???
    Running Away
    Fighting

    (I forget what the ??? was: "Science?" "Sonic Screwdriver"? It was genre-emulating tho)

    Anyway, so what that did was basically pull the teeth out of combat monsters in the world of DRWho, and reinforce the genre without being heavy-handed. So if you want to make talking a huge spotlight, steal that initiative system. Even if the game is all about fighty dudes who fight (and only sometimes talk), it will make sure that when it's time to talk, it's a serious and viable option.

    -Andy
