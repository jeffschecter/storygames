\n Greetings Story-games! I'm new to the world of story games. I've played Fiasco, Polaris, and Microscope, each once. That's pretty much the extent of my experience. I've got a good RPG group, and right now we're playing The One Ring. I'd like to find some story games we can play to give the GM (myself) a break from time to time. Problem is, it seems most of these story games work best with smaller groups. Can you recommend any games that play well with 6 or 7 people?

    \n Hillfolk/Dramasystem works pretty well with group that large, as it gives more PCs to have arguments with. There's a GM role, but it is pretty minimal and no more taxing than any other player role, really.

    You could always do the "Crosstown Rivals" setup for Fiasco, where you run two tables of the game with the same playset and share the setting. Tell the other table when something newsworthy happens, and they can incorporate it into their narrative.

    \n Most so-called "story games" tend to need a lot of individual attention and spotlight time, making a 6-7 person game very difficult. The advantage of action games like early D&D for big groups is that things move VERY fast and individual turns involve short decisions of minimal (but cumulative) impact. (Since I think D&D can be a cool story game, the distinction is not mine.)

    I would start by looking at LARPs, like the ones from Flying Monkey, or Skirmisher's Chthulu Live products. White Wolf has a very cool Vampire pre-made LARP called "City In The Sand" that should have gotten a lot more attention because it pre-packages a LARP experience really effectively. 7 people is pretty cool for a small LARP.

    \nSea Dracula needs at least 5 people to really get going but works best with more. Haven't played Animal Crime yet but it looks like it does best with 5-7 players.

    For the most part you are correct that indie story games work best with less people (actually I think that's true with "normal" RPGs too). How about running two games with 3-4 players each? Like the "Crosstown Rivals" Fiasco setup that @NickWedig mentions. Or maybe run an Apocalypse World where each play group is in distant yet neighboring areas \u2014 close enough where news can travel but it takes serious time/effort to go in person so it would be a rare occasion when players would wander to the other area. Or My Life With Master with two nearby towns with warring Masters.

    I second not Monsterhearts as well. Having MCed a one-shot with 6 I can tell you no no no nono no. Keep MH to 3-5. Plus it's certainly not a 'break' for your GM unless someone else is running it.

    \n Burning Wheel typically does not support that many, but it has a few one-shot scenarios that do.

    - Trouble in Hochen is good fun and comes with 6 pre-gen characters. (Hint: if you run it with that many, focus on teams of players at a time to minimize out-of-spotlight time.) It's also part of a trilogy, so if your group enjoys it you can have a little mini-campaign.
    - The Gift is an excellent scenario and - bonus! - even though you technically have to GM it, the team-on-team nature of the scenario makes it sort of run itself.

    I ran an Apocalypse World con scenario The Bowl that worked on a similar principle as The Gift. It worked pretty well. Basically, if you can set it up so that the players are in teams on (at least somewhat) opposing sides, they basically GM themselves and all you have to do is step in and tell 'em to roll some dice once in a while.

    \n Nope, I've run Monsterhearts with six and it wasn't great. I like @Zelbinian's teams idea though, and I bet Dungeon World would work with that many (you're all on a team, y'see - less wandering off...).

    If I had a big group like that, I'd probably dust off Microlite 20 or Basic D&D and get some old-school on; as previous posters have said, the quick resolution of those games works well (hell, or Runequest - or (better) Openquest).

    For more narrative authority, Empire (Beat to Quarters / Duty & Honour) work well with that number - their skirmish rules allow a little bit of spotlight for everyone leading to one resolution roll. But, hey, Napoleonic historical fantasy may not be your thing.
