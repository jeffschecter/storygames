      \n About celebrating players.

      Ages ago, in my little homeland, in a little town we organized a little con. It was all cute and enthusiastic. We would decorate, we would prepare food, we would write scenarii to be played with the popular games of the time. The reasoning behind the scenarii was that local game stores wanted to sponsor our little event but they mostly wanted to give gaming material rather than money. We sure could have handed out these books and accessories through a lottery but it wouldn't have been very exciting or made anyone feel special. A lottery wouldn't have channeled the enthusiasm of our little community. Instead we made it a competition. That was it! All we needed was some referees to walk around and observe every tables and a spreadsheet to rate players and GMs (for instance how good is a GM at describing, impersonating NPCs, keeping the players excited, using rules without breaking the flow etc..)

      It was an interesting experiment. It was far from perfection, and all of us knew it. There was a good dose of rating people you knew better than others. Or rating tables playing your favorite game better than others. But in the end it felt like we were indeed celebrating gamers. And a few strangers managed to impress everybody with their awesomeness, leading to more contacts and cross group pollination. And it wasn't at odd with celebrating our special guest, game designers or illustrators who accepted an invitation.

      I'm not saying it's the only way to celebrate players, but it's possible to bring the spotlight on them. 15 minutes of nerd fame if you want

      Yet, I totally agree with Judd that it's a bit ridiculous to hold game designers in higher esteem than fans that run kick-ass games. The design is only a small part of the equation for a great game. The organizer's experience, prep, delivery, and management of the session is contributing far more, and some designers are actually not exceptional at this, believe it or not.

      In our defense, when we get a designer to come out and run their own game, we can be assured of a few things:

      * The organizer will know the rules of their game and know how to best teach and adjudicate them
      * The organizer will know the intricacies of their game, where it works best, and where it doesn't work as well, and can shape the session accordingly
      * The organizer won't misrepresent the game due to having experienced it in a different culture of play than the designer
      * Most importantly, the organizer has a tangible reason for running an entertaining session, financial or otherwise

      When we stick this tag on one of our events, we confer this assurance to the players interested in the game. It's less about fostering a cult of personality and more about highlighting events we know are going to kick ass. Now, we have a pretty good-sized squadron of people that we know to be terrific organizers, and who represent the games they run at least as well as the designers would, as well - it's just harder to quantify their credentials in public without implicitly questioning the competency of the rest of the organizers we haven't seen wow us yet.

      I'm in favour of the short pedestal Adam talks about; after all, game designers do have something quantifiable we can look at with respect. That doesn't diminish the glory that ought to be heaped upon people who organize or participate in terrific game sessions, or otherwise make the hobby go 'round.

      \n I think the issue is getting confused here. There are two things going on. One is that a designer has created something that people enjoy and demonstrates his or her skills. It's also a contribution to the community. I don't think anyone thinks there is anything wrong with giving them props for that and having them earn our respect and appreciation.

      The issue that I see in this community is a social one, where there is a hierarchy of relationships and status and being a designer automatically puts you way up on that hierarchy. And then people behave in certain ways either to get up there themselves or to be included in the social group that hangs out up there. This is all pretty natural stuff in any community, but that's not to say it doesn't have harmful effects.

      Some of the harmful effects that I see here are a very strong barrier to entry to newcomers who are not naturally socially aggressive. If you don't already have some kind of social connection or reputation in the community, your posts and ideas will often not receive a sufficient level of attention and response. If you don't adjust your language and content to fit certain values and expectations, likewise your posts and ideas will not be well received. Again, this is not unique to this community, but this kind of exclusivity is quite high relative to the rest of the gaming community. I get around and this board is far and away the most intimidating and discouraging for newbies. I'm a braying loudmouth who sticks his foot in the door of the party and starts yelling until I get in a fight or make friends, so I can get beyond it. But I know at least two other really intelligent gamers who are way into storygames who came here and left very quickly because of feeling basically not welcome and have heard similar comments from many others in passing.

      Threads like the one about which games you have played with the designer reinforce the impression of a clique with a fairly steep social hierarchy, where you need to be travelling in the same circles, on the internet or in meatspace, of certain groups in order for your voice to have value here.

      On the other hand, not to harsh on everybody too much, there is a certain level of discourse that this board has reached over time that is not something one can just pick up over night. So I do think the hierarchy can also be seen as a meritocracy where people who have spent the time learning and gaming will have more relevant and interesting things to say. Obviously, people who have actually designed games are going to be pretty smart and experienced about that stuff.
