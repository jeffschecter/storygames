    \n In games with GMs, I usually found it to mean that the GM doesn't allow much "free chit-chat time."
    Once things in a scene stop moving forward, either a conflict is introduced, or a new scene is set-up.

    Once a scene had served its purpose, and yes, that's subjective to the GM's call, a new scene is set-up.

    \n I ascribe to the Matt Wilson spectrum theory:

    Aggressive Scene Framing <-----------------------------------> Playing out every moment, second by second

    ASF:
    "The grenade explodes, and the last machine gun in the pillbox falls silent."
    "Whew!"
    "Later, Captain Miller calls you over to his desk to go over the after action report. 'You say here that the German position was reinforced by troops from Arnheim. Our intel says there are no Germans in Arnheim, Lieutenant. Are you absolutely sure about that?"

    (edited to make that scene cut actually go to a point of conflict)

    \n Good scene framing gives players the elements they need to get right into conflict. The "aggressive" part comes when the scene framer includes elements that were not previously agreed on through the process of play. So you might announce that Kron is going to confront the Chieftan and the scene framer might say "Ok, you're in the great hall and you've diplomatically put your case, but the Chief is shaking his head. You see your rival behind the throne smirk. What do you do?"

    Generally, the player sets the agenda, but the aggressive scene framer jumps to a point of higher conflict.

    \n Aggressive scene framing cuts right to the conflict or hard personal choice such that there is no wiggle room or time to maneuver away of or out of the conflict. While it is found in GMed games, I've also seen it in GMless games where everybody is playing round-robin "kick each other in the story junk." It is a technique supremely useful in getting conflict-averse players to address the game's theme or to produce intense, fast-paced narrativist play. For folks who aren't conflict shy and aren't after breathless bandolier-o-bangs play (or trying to cram everything into a con game time slot), it's not especially useful.

    \n In a lot of games, role-play just sorta happens. There's situation, and players talk about what they're doing. Occasionally, the GM (let's assume there is one for "a lot of games") might advance a sort of story agenda and frame a scene but there's some pressure to make that scene be about what the players are having their characters do. It's not always good cricket to jump ahead or discount the current character situation.

    When you frame a scene, you have the option to change the situation. As I explained above, in a lot of games, this isn't cool (not that you'll find any mention of its uncoolness in the rules, but gamers seem to know it isn't cool). But realize that a lot of play doesn't even happen in formal scenes, with clear starts and ends like you'd expect from a movie director.

    In games with scene framing, someone in a GM role is framing scenes for play, often setting up a new situation, often skipping around in fictional time and place, and often putting pressure on the protagonists. In games with *aggressive* scene framing, as I understand it, there is little or no play "between scenes." Also, the GM role might be putting more pressure on the protagonists, but I am not sure this has to be part of the definition.

    "roll to... oh, wait, you automatically succeed. Make up whatever damage you want."
    Interesting: Fiasco has a mechanic that makes you trade framing authority for resolution authority. You can either choose to establish the scene, leaving your friends to make the resolution hurt, or you can let your friends frame a painful scene, which you'll resolve. So there's a peacekeeping element of mutually assured destruction, or at least there would be if you wanted your character to succeed. In practice, Fiasco seems to thrive on an exclusive diet of characters with miserable, messed-up lives that just get worse every scene, so meta-attacks of that kind don't really matter, as long as they're cool enough.

    I just played A Penny For My Thoughts last night, which offers another way to confer meta-attack authority on the other players. At each branching point in the story, two players other than the scene's narrator offer alternative outcomes, and the rules reward being the author of the outcome that is chosen. So if Alice asks "What did I say or do then?" and Eve meta-attacks her, Bob might choose to offer a gentler alternative and narratively undercut Eve to get the penny.

    All this is a long-winded way of saying that if a game or a group wants aggressive scene framing, the burden is on it/them to provide mechanical support for keeping it fun, or to settle into a style that does the same thing. A group full of cliff-danglers playing Munchausen won't have fun.
