    December 2007 edited December 2007
    (I'll take a stab at answering seriously.)

    (1) I'm very partial to Truth & Justice because it mimics very well the feel of superhero comics. It's also so non-crunchy that on multiple occasion I've had complete newcomers at my game, gaming virgins who came up with concepts that would have been an effing pain in the ass to do in a crunchy system (e.g., Hero/Champions, M&M, etc.) even for experienced players -- and then had a great afternoon doing exactly what they wanted their character to do. Now, if you're a fan of crunch and very exact, predictable effects that are independent of narration or GM adjudication, then this system will annoy you with its fluidity; but for story gamers, it's wonderful.

    (2) I adore Underground. It's dark, tongue-in-cheek, cynical, well-written and smart as all hell. Character creation is a pain, but after that point the system works very smoothly. Now, I know some people don't like the logarithmic effect/power progression system but it always worked well for me.

    Runners-up: Mutants & Masterminds and Silver Age Sentinels. M&M has better options for mimicking comic book effects through the use of Hero Points, while SAS is simpler and makes character creation somewhat easier. Both have fairly well-defined effect descriptions, but both are much more work for the GM than my beloved T&J.

    • GestaltBennie
      December 2007
      My favorite supers RPG? Topic honey, me fly. Allow me to buzz for a bit.

      I like a lot of them, and I've had a lot of work published for three of them (Champions, Mutants and Masterminds, and the original Marvel Super Heroes game). That said, I'm gonna go with my heart and reaffirm my love for the HERO System and Champions, and not just for professional reasons. My apologia follows:

      I realize people these days mostly see HERO as a wargame. I think it needs to be pointed out that in its original incarnation, Champions had some of the most advanced storytelling and plot hook tools of its day: Champion's system of disadvantages, by defining common character traits and frequent plot events, was revolutionary. There were disadvantage systems before Champions, but nothing remotely on its scale. Yes, their implementation was flawed, and the system encouraged players to take more disadvantages than could reasonably come into play. Finding the right balance between the number of traits and hooks to define a character vs. a GM's ability to use them has been a struggle for every game that's employed such systems. I can forgive Champions for not getting this right.

      I've often seen HERO criticized because its math; however, when people use terms such as "calculus" to describe arithmetic I learned in third grade, it's hard for me not to hold the opinion in contempt. Admittedly there's a lot of it in chargen, and it's tough to run power pools on the fly, There are techniques such as pre-recording packages of powers to make the latter easier, and as for the former, I think you can go for a more robust standard of chargen when the game's designed for long-term campaigns. Champs characters are built to last, and the careful construction needed to build one goes a long way with building identification, at least for me. My character feels like a superhero to me from the first panel because the game has encouraged me to take him seriously. Time equals investment of effort equals importance. A hero you'll be playing for years is not as disposable as a cup of coffee, nor (to me) should he be. Admittedly it's not a lot of fun to finish a character and then have a more anal player wrestle with them for an hour after you're done. But there are always things you can do to occupy your time in down times. I realize that if you’re looking for a quick pick-up game, Champions is far from the ideal (though tolerable with computer chargen and aids like the UNTIL Super Powers Directory).

      Lastly, there's the issue of pace. I like the slow combat of Champions, though within reason. I'll accept the paradigm of "game play must equal the pace of the adapted material" when I see folks attacking a Wheel of Time game for being too fast. Time in media experience is often illusory. We don't hold a stopwatch when we read a comc book, instead we're immersed in our imagination and (at least for me) time is divided into boring and dramatic bits. Combat in Champions takes a long time, but that's a good reflection of the importance of the fight scene in the literature. Neil Gaiman once remarked that a fight scene in a traditional superhero comic is like a sex scene in a porno movie or a musical number in a musical. It's the damn raison d'etre. It needs to make a dramatic impact.

      By its relatively slow pace (and making you roll lots of dice) the best Champions battles build a portentous atmosphere that I have not found in systems in systems that want you to get the combat over with quickly. Champions is the superhero game for those who appreciate the fine art of the slugfest. Where they fail is when, as I've experienced too often during the last 26 years, they're not treated with dramatic granularity, and a battle with third rate flunkies is treated with the same solemnity as a climactic battle with a major villain. That's why I like mook rules. However, if you're playing in a combat-loving genre, and your climactic battle is an anti-climax because the system promotes short combats, it's a failure. Fight scenes matter. They shouldn't blur together into the equivalent of a movie that you forget within thirty minutes of leaving the theater. I think this is part of D&D's success as well. Sure, high level combats are annoyingly long, but you really remember the ones that matter. What are often seen as flaws are virtues for many (and would be virtues for many more if they were executed more effectively.)

      Despite its reputation for sluggish, wargamey play, I've found that some of the best, most dramatic games I've ever played were with Champions. If anyone doubts this, go back to A&E in the mid-80s and look at the records of the Texas Rangers games played at GenCon. Revolutionary stuff, well ahead of its time, and well worth studying by all you new shiny story-game kids out there. Heck, I devoted close to fifteen years of my life writing a campaign book for the damn system. I've played a lot of "HERO-killer" games that have come out since 1981, many of which were pretty good, most of which I'd play again in a heartbeat. I especially love M&M, which does many of the things I love about HERO better. However, I keep coming back to my old flame, even though her breasts are sagging and I can't deny the wrinkles around her eyes anymore.
