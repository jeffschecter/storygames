\n I'd like some insight on how systems handle opposing intents, particularly when the issue of physical violence is brought up.

A bit of clarification on GA/SBF:

In my limited understanding of Apocalypse World, Seize By Force triggers when the initiator of violence wants something and the subject of that violence fights back. (At least that's how I've interpreted the two moves - else why are you getting hurt?) Go Aggro, on the other hand, triggers when the subject of the violence doesn't fight back.

A bit of an aside: Is there a reason that "suck it up and force your hand" is only available on the 10+? Seems like it should be an option on the 7-9 since the 10+ is the better result.

Outside of GA/SBF, I haven't seen a system where the intent of the subject of violence is taken into consideration prior to initiating violence...at least not one that does it very well. Burning Wheel and its descendants do to some extent, but many of those systems feel muddled and unclear. There's not a lot of guidance on how to implement opposing intents (major/minor compromise is a bit insubstantial). The Fate system suffers from similar problems.

Are there other systems that explicitly take into account opposing intents? I'm talking about things like, "You want to beat your opponent into submission, and he wants to knock you down so he can run out the side door," then the mechanics determine who succeeds at his intent (with the possibility of both sides gaining their respective intents). Beyond violence, it could be broadened to include things like, "You want to convince the goblin king to help you, and he wants his court jester to pick your pocket while you're negotiating," or "the sheep wants to roam the countryside and you want to herd it back to the rest of the flock."

Apocalypse World's GA/SBF seem to be the closest.

    \n You might want to read "go aggro" again: on a 7-9, you can choose to cave, suck it up, OR pick an option from the list. One of the factors for how effective the move is supposed to be is "how feasible are those options on the list?" In other words, if you can go aggro on someone who can't plausibly barricade themselves in, they can't choose that option (and therefore may be forced to cave).

    As for your general question, there are many, many games coming out of the "Forge era" which did what you're describing here very explicitly. Sorcerer may have been first (although it might not be exactly what you're looking for), but anything from Shock: to The Shadow of Yesterday, and various explicit stake-setting games do that a lot.

    For an easy, free example, look at the optional conflict rules in my game Thus began the adventures of Eowyn.... I believe they do exactly what you're looking for.

    \n Bumping this thread to avoid creating another because I'm trying to explain the concept of intents. I believe that TSOY uses similar language to what I'm trying here, and I would like critiques.

    (Note to self: need a better description for resolution mechanics involving the two forms of intent. "Parallel intents" and "perpendicular intents" are too wordy. Thinking of "contests" for perpendicular intents, as the resolution is contested by another, but uncertain for parallel intents.)

    Intents come in two varieties. There are parallel intents, which are intents that are not directly opposed to one another. Two trains running in parallel won't ever crash into one another, even if one's headed due east and the other is due west. Parallel intents are the same way. They exist alongside one another, and they do not restrict or directly inhibit the other, allowing both to occur in tandem. An example this phenomenon would be your desire to persuade the goblin king to aide you while the goblin king wishes to distract you long enough for his court jester to snatch away the magic torc hidden on your person. The king humors you with an audience, and you may be able to convince him, and his court jester may be able to filch the torc. You can succeed on your intent and the king can succeed on his.

    The contrast of parallel intents is perpendicular intents. Perpendicular intents restrict or inhibit, and so like a train running north and a train running east, the two will collide, leaving one derailed and the other chugging along. Using a slight modification of the example above, a scenario of perpendicular intents would be your desire to persuade the goblin king to aide you while his vizier counsels him against this. The king humors you with an audience, and you may be able to convince him, or his vizier may be able to convince him. You and the vizier cannot both succeed on your respective intents because they're in direct opposition to one another.

    Another difference between parallel and perpendicular intents is that parallel intents require active participants pursuing their goals. Perpendicular intents do not. In the case of you, the goblin king, and his sneaky court jester, you and the king are the primary participants (the court jester is acting in a lesser capacity because the king is doing the heavy lifting of distracting you). In the case of you, the king, and the vizier, there are again active participants butting heads. However, consider a third scenario.

    Suppose you are a ne'er-do-well and cutpurse who wants to pay the goblin king to aide you. Negotiations are a lot of work, and money speaks louder than words. You notice a noble perusing the marketplace, purse plump with gold and silver. You figure this will cover the goblin king's expenses, so you make your move, seeking to liberate this individual from his earthly goods. While you're doing this, the noble is oblivious. He just wants to buy a fresh apple. However, even though he is unaware of your unscrupulous activities, he still has an intent perpendicular to yours: "I don't want you to steal my money." In this case, the noble is opposing your actions passively. He doesn't need to be actively watching for thieves. To resolve your intent of "steal the purse" with the noble's subconscious intent of "don't let anyone steal my purse" is an example of direct (albeit passive) opposition...and thus it is a case of perpendicular intent.

    \n My advice:

    Don't concern yourself with the language. Instead, design your system so that the ability to block another character's intent is clear.

    For instance, if there's a step in your game where where you must choose whether to set aside a die to oppose another player's goal, then the whole thing is never in question - you don't even need to discuss this issue, because no one will ever get it wrong.

    As for common practice, TSOY started using "parallel" and "perpendicular" while other games and designers have used "opposed" vs. "orthogonal". None of these are terribly clear, in the end, though, I think - you have to learn what they mean and then remember it.

    \n What you're describing there seems pretty simple - here's what I'm seeing in terms of play procedures to replicate what you wrote in your post:

    1. A player states an action and a goal and we agree the mechanics should be brought into play.

    2. The GM states what the opposition's goal is in the situation.

    There may be some negotiation, until both are clear.

    3. The player rolls. If they score one success, they get what they want. If they score two successes, they also get to block their opponent's intented goal.

    Sounds like the "acting under fire" move in Apocalypse World terms.

    \n The Annalise RPG did amazing things with orthogonal stakes.

    1. The player says what they want, writes it as a stake.
    2. The GM adds an orthogonal stake.
    3. The player might add another one.
    4. If so, the GM adds another.
    5. Continue until the player stops.

    Being orthogonal, there should never be a situation where, if stake X comes to be, that invalidates the possibility of stake Z coming to be.

    Then you resolve stakes.

    This involves rolling a pool of d6es for each stake (4 stakes, 4 dice) and assigning the dice to the stakes. Generally, it was something like, the higher the die result, the better off for the player for that stake.

    But that didn't resolve things. That just set up the tension. This is what the player would want, given those dice.

    After that, the player and all the other players at the table spend currency to shift dice up and down, reroll dice, and so on, pushing the stakes they care about different ways (for or against the spotlight player).

    When it circles back to the main player, they can either accept the situation as it stands, or mess with it again. If they mess with it, it opens the door for the entire table to do that again, too. Might make things worse, might make things better...

    It's brilliant, because it forces the spotlight player to really decide what is important to them.

    \n@Paul_T: That is very close to what I'm going for, yes. I understand that Apocalypse World has the stakes implicitly set by hard moves, the fiction, and the move itself, but I (personally) enjoy setting the stakes before each roll because it allows me to telegraph risk, reward, and potential outcomes without relying on anything behind the curtain. For instance, from a D&D game I ran, I said, "So-and-so, you could leap onto the creature's back from your vantage point and spear it, taking advantage on the attack roll and doing +2d6 damage. If you fail, you're going to take 3d6 falling damage."

    He failed and suffered the hit point loss in stride, but suppose I had rolled an exceptionally well on that damage and knocked his character unconscious or slain him outright (unlikely, but possible). I would have perceived my own actions as unfair. By granting players foreknowledge of potential outcomes, I perceive my rulings as less arbitrary and more impartial. In addition to these perceptions (accurate or not though they may be), it promotes "hygienic" gameplay on my behalf and forces me to consider the consequences of a particular roll in advance. This naturally cultivates a gaming environment in which rolls are not thoughtlessly demanded out of force of habit.

    What are you hoping to achieve with greater granularity?
    \nA simplistic resolution system works well for tasks but less so for conflicts (imo), but additional granularity is desirable (to me) when handling conflict resolution, assuming said conflict carries some narrative weight.

    I'm sure no one here is a stranger to games that demand a half-dozen rolls for simple task resolution (roll to-hit, now the defender rolls to block/parry, now roll damage, now the defender rolls to maintain consciousness...), but by that same token, the inversion produces anemic gameplay. Certainly one could represent the plot of A New Hope with a single roll of the dice (the Rebel Alliance wishes to Go Aggro on the Death Star, one might reason), but that would be a bowl of watery soup. Even at the very end of the movie, when Luke is about to destroy the Death Star, do we want that entire scene to play out in a single roll? No, I think the majority of players want to play out shooting down Tie Fighters and dodging laser fire.

    How does one represent these scenes and conflicts in a way that is mechanically satisfying while producing an appropriate pace of resolution? I desire rules that reflect an appropriate narrative lens while at the same time providing sufficient structure that one isn't left rolling dice too often or too seldom. An impossible goal, I suspect, but to return to A New Hope: how many times does Luke have to Act Under Fire when piloting to the Death Star's exhaust port?

    Luke #1: I want to pilot into position to fire the proton torpedo into the exhaust port.

    MC #1: There is laser fire all around you, and two Tie Fighters are closing in on you. It's going to be Acting Under Fire to make it there unscathed.

    Luke #1 rolls a 10+ and gets into position to drop that proton torpedo.

    In another universe, far, far away:

    Luke #2: I want to pilot into position to fire the proton torpedo into the exhaust port.

    MC #2: As you're flying low along the channel, and you see Darth Vader's Tie Fighter fly into position behind you. He opens fire. What do you do?

    Luke #2: I am going to try to shake him off.

    MC #2: Sounds like Acting Under Fire.

    Luke #2 rolls a 10+ and evades the Tie Fighters...and yet he is no closer to his goal of maneuvering into position with the proton torpedo.

    I am rambling a bit, so I'm going to stop there. Am I at all conveying any sort of information here?

    \n Many games very successfully leave the *scope* of resolution open, for the group to fine-tune to their tastes.

    Even Apocalypse World does this, though within certain parameters. (One group might have a character roll to Seize an entrance to the compound, then again to Seize the courtyard, and finally to get the Bomb they were looking for, whereas another might say, "Cool, we know what's going on - roll to Seize the Bomb from the warlord in the compound over there!")

    I'd go so far as to say that this kind of flexibility is a really strong desire feature for a lot of games.

    As Dave says, if you *want* a more gradual, step-by-step resolution of conflicts and actions, you probably shouldn't seek to combine "orthogonal actions" into a single roll at all, since that will speed up the pace of resolution very dramatically.
