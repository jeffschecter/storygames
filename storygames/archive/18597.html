\n Hi all,
I've been playing v&s with my kids on holidays and I've had an issue with the resolution method for focus characters. The rules say they get to choose from 3options generated by the other players (difficult, dangerous or both) but doesn't this involve inventing a lot of content that gets left on the cutting room floor? (as the player will only trigger one of the options)
Am I playing this right or did anyone else find this?

As a fix I've adopted the aw-style 2d6 roll with 6- being the "both" result ie failure, 7-9 being "dangerous" ie sucess with complications and 10+ being "difficult". A modifier of +1 for dangerous and +2 for dificut tasks.
This is somthing like the sundered land but not as sophisticated.
Anyway it provides a little less predictability and no need to generate ideas that don't end up in the fiction.

    \n You've manage to re-reverse engineer it back to Apocalypse World!

    I'd be interested in hearing what your experiences with this method are, but I have couple words of caution here:

    The first is that Difficult is not necessarily the most desired result. Part of the fun of resolving a focused crewmember's scene is finding ways to make the non-obvious choice the better result. If what you need in order to get the ship's hyperdrive working is to feed it the heart of a loved one, you might super excited about taking the dangerous result instead.

    The second is that it's the choice between these options that sets the focused crewmembers apart. A focus crewmember can choose to make a Dangerous task merely a Difficult one, and vice versa as the situation requires. This is a big leg up on the non-focus crewmembers who must immediately suffer the unmitigated consequences of their actions.

    \n It works fine in my experience. The main advantage is that you only have to invent 1 thing to keep the story rolling, just like when a non-focus character acts.
    To be honest we didn't really use dice modifiers most of the time, having a 3-way possible branch in the story was sufficient.

    Kids are loving the game BTW - well done!

    I saw some AP on Judd K's page that suggested playing some flash-back scenes first to pin down issues identified in char gen, sounds good.
