    \n They were probably playing Deathwatch, where all the PCs are Space Marines, and there are 'Horde' rules for dealing with big swarms of opponents.

    I guess I couldnt get too upset that they care enough about the game to want something specific enough to give real feedback. Yeah, it might be a bit ego-deflating at the table, but when you're GM, the goal is to facilitate everyone's fun!

    In my experience, these have been good gateway RPG products for the minis game players as well as fans of the universe via the video games. They're 'trad' as hell, clunky and stat-heavy, but in a way similar to the minis game, but with top-notch production values and LOTS of art, which is a real draw given the property. While some of us would have plenty of fun re-skinning a Story Game for this kind of thing, I'd be really hesitant to try and scratch-build all of that with players who ar new and really, really want immersion in that property.

    \n I think in that specific game, the GM has trained the players to play that way. Players roll, GM describes.
    \n
    \nSince the players aren't allowed (in some, maybe most cases) to describe what happens in the world, they will sometimes request more detail.
    \n
    \nBut this example was out of the norm with the introduction of youtube videos and asking the GM to re-describe rather than simply requesting they give more detail. Although it might not be that weird since they will often watch a movie together before starting a series of adventures to show what the world will look and feel like.
    \n
    \nFrom what I've seen, Player Rolls / GM Describes is a very common play style. Among people I know who mainly play D&D;, that's definitely the case. I've been to a few D&D; 4E Encounter games (the 1 hour games held on Wednesday nights to introduce new players to RPGs) and that was the case there as well. Many of the players seem to enjoy it.
