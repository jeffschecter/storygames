    \n The answers are, for me:

    Does that mean we have to rush through scenes, summarizing everything, churning out big events and decisions, without bothering with detail and immersion and experience?
    No.

    Even if we don't have to, do we do it anyway?
    No. Except once.*

    Why or why not?
    Why not? Because stories are built from all those details. It's called Story Now, as opposed to (specifically) Story After the Fact. I don't rush past details because a story without details is not a story.


    Other than that, I don't know. Do you? If you do, can you tell me why? If you don't, but think it is true of Story Now, could you tell me why you think it?

    Also, is this common belief, this rushing Story Now? I always have seen it as a sad outlier kind of thing -- not anything that had really taken hold in any real way. Did it?

    ****

    * The Time I Rushed Story Now

    Whenever I've seen people talking about rushing, it's always been someone illustrating how horrible Nar is because the GM for a game they played in rushed them from one nearly arbitrary ethical test to another. In other words, rushing is seen as a failure of fun, not an actual, valuable principle of Story Now play.

    I think people, when trying something new, tend to focus on the new thing. And this has meant, on occasion, when people hear about the prioritization of issues that matter to the Players, the game is supposed to focus on that and that everything else is sort of an impediment to that.

    But, for me, I've only seen it once in person: A game of In A Wicked Age at a con. None of us had played before. We were really intrigued with and focused on the conflict resolution system. We just rushed from one conflict to another. It was like story as WWE. But, you know, you're just supposed to play the game with lots of talking and world building and creating color -- and then, if a conflict occurs, you bring in the system.

    \n I've played a wide variety of games intended for Story Now, with a wide variety of players who prefer Story Now. I'd say 90% of those sessions were fun for me. I'd say 60% felt too rushed for my taste. I'd say 20% felt so rushed that it almost made play not fun.

    That last category is almost entirely con games. Some were racing the clock, some felt like they were and finished an hour early.

    Many of the players I met in these games were not interested in listening to me try to make my character's scenes more vivid (asking the GM about the room, delivering dialogue not aimed at instantly getting to a conflict).

    In my 16-session Apocalypse World game, some players were interested in more vivid detail and others were not. I found that people were most interested in my scenes when I made something big happen quickly.

    Caveat: my standards may be atypical from years of creepy dungeon crawling with an emphasis on character POV.

    \n It's funny, now that Dave has moved to North Carolina, I've sort of had to become the enforcer of "hey let's describe that more." I think what this is about is color (in the strict Forge-ian use of the term): too little color makes a game uninteresting / feel rushed or artificial.

    I have also seen the rushing, too little color thing happen in con games, but mostly applying the right amount of color is simply a matter of skillfulness (and also managing group preferences, of course). Because here's the thing: even though it is by definition not a part of the game that interfaces with the mechanics, color contributions are pretty easy to divide\u2014for me at least\u2014between useful and not-useful. What exactly constitutes usefulness of a bit of color varies from game to game of course, but basically the good kind of color is that which helps grow the players' interest in whatever the game's main thing is. I'll be pretty aggressive about browbeating people if there's all sorts of shit going on that simply has nothing to do with the premise of the game or session, but I'm also (I think) pretty good about not simply rushing for rushing's sake (unless it's a con game).

    The reason a) long in-character conversations and b) descriptive details of the gameworld or characters sometimes get short shrift in the S-G family of games is because they so easily can lead to meandering lame boringness.

    I think those two elements of gameplay really are like the proverbial spice in the soup: too little or too much are both bad.

    \n Dave, I don't know if this will be a helpful data point or not, but I generally prefer to keep the majority of the color in my head.

    I'm not a verbal person. I can't listen to podcasts without wanting to speed up the speaker so they sound like Alvin the chipmunk because for me, lots of times, the normal pace of conversation is too damn slow. In college I skipped most of my classes because listening to a professor simply speak the words that were already in the book was too damn slow. When people post news clips on the internet I generally ignore the ones that are video or just read through the transcript because I can read about 3 pages of transcript in the time the speaker is still droning on in the first paragraph.

    So when I role-play there is a definite limit to the amount of verbal description of details that aren't immediately relevant that I can tolerate without getting impatient. My brain has already filled in most of the details about how things look, smell, and sound anyway, so unless the detail you're (generic you) introducing is either way cooler than the movie already running in my head...or is different from what's in my head in some way that's going to be important later...its not (usually) a value add to me. The voice inside my head spends alot of time going "yeah, yeah, I get it, its creepy, move on". Too much time spent describing the creepiness actually makes it feel LESS creepy to me. So I suspect alot of things that might seem rushed to others, might seem comfortably paced to me...simply because 5 minutes of verbally described color takes about 5 seconds to internally imagine.

    But point being...that's not because I don't like or enjoy the color...just that I have low tolerance for a large number of words being spoken at speeds far slower than my own imagination has already provided. Many is the game for me where to an outside observer the game may seem all about attention to system or addressing premise with very little color. But to me...there's TONS of color going on...its just mostly inside my head...and I've always assumed that a similar color rich movie is playing inside of each of the other player's heads too.

    In fact, that's pretty core to my assertion that the size of the non-shared imaginary space dwarfs the size of the shared imaginary space at the table. It also means that I've learned to be more accepting of retroactively editing the color in my head to better fit with details that were in other players heads but not yet spoken...because given the choice between verbally outlining every detail so we're all on the same page vs. letting the majority of the details just happen in my head even if that means they don't exactly match what's in your head...I'd prefer the latter going back periodically to sync up when necessary.

    \n Data point-y thing.

    I may have contributed to this perception in some games I have GM'd. One of the things I have come to enjoy about "new school" game design is that I, as GM, don't have to come to the table with tons and tons of color and setting already pre-planned and set up for the players to discover. I can sit back and ask questions and let it grow up from player input as and when needed.

    I have noticed, with some groups, that this approach leads to hurried play. Players, used to looking to the GM for cues, see a lack of detail as a sign that there's nothing here that matters, and zip on by looking for the next set piece. Eventually, I deliver a set piece, since they seem to be floundering. Repeat.

    I've gotten way better at prompting for input, and my regular players have in turn learned that just because I didn't describe something in detail doesn't mean they can't or shouldn't. But I expect that a game where the only places system engages are the big conflicts can feel empty and pointless if you're used to rolling for little things all the time, and I expect that a game where the GM doesn't have a lot of pre-planned content can look railroad-y if you're not seizing the initiative to fill that space with stuff.

      \n I'd echo Judd and add not only misused or used well, but also different seasonings to different tastes.

      Read a Hemmingway short story. Is it rushed? Read a passage of the fellowship traveling through the landscape of Middle-earth. Does he go on forever?

      Raslph's points about the background elements of a TV or film are, I think, well taken. It's one of the things that gave me faith to built a crazy-ass show like "The Booth at the End" -- to know that if I put the focus on the faces of the actors only, the show would still work as long as the actors themselves were interesting. On the other hand, some people hate (HATE!) the show because "nothing is happening" and "We never leave the booth!"

      Meanwhile, I loved what Terrance Malick does in his work (see: "The Tree of Life") where it is ALL about focusing on the details and reams and reams of visual detail and description, if you will. I love it. Other people hate it.


      So all of this can't come down to how much description is offered. It has to be a matter of how it's done, to what effect it's done, and whether the audience (the players at the table) like it.

      Keeping in mind that, "Nothing ever made was liked by everybody," we know that some people gathered at the table might like or not like what is being "made." And this is a huge subject. At the level being discussed.

      So, here's where I am:

      1) When Dave opens with, "People who play Story Now don't care about description or can't do it" -- well, it's an idiocy. Because all people can do it. They're people. They can do it. It might be that it might not be a priority in their play. It might not be what turns them on, they're turning "the camera" elsewhere or whatever. This is where we have to place, say Hemmingway and Kurt Vonnegut side-by-side and say either "One of these guys is wrong," or "Oh, I guess there's many ways of telling a story."

      2) But... if pepole doing something because they think they're supposed to do it a certain way at the expense of what would bring them pleasure or other people pleasure, then that's sad. They might only be trying it out and experimenting with new techniques or ways of communicating narrative at the table (which is more than understandable). But if they're honestly not getting pleasure and don't stop and change they're ways because they think some book told them to do things in a way that makes them not have a good time... well, I'd call that sad. This doesn't mean throwing out the baby with the bathwater. All of Todd's quotes from books above can be tossed to extreme ends where they make the storytelling so chaotic and sparse you don't even know what's going on. I know this is the Internet and all, but in life we of course don't have to live at either one extreme or another.

      3) Ralph's points about wanting to keep his own movie in his head... This is a third and separate subject (if only because Ralph has tatted it as a social comfort issue). This, too, has to be a made of scale... because clearly we're setting up some descriptions -- or we don't know what the heck we're sharing. My own guess is that I'm a more description guy than Ralph. In my view, we're here to share our imaginations with each other. I don't want to be imagining alone together. I think they're correct in pointing out that the game has the tendency for fast pacing - like Fiasco, say.

      Although not specifically about pacing, I have often seen people complain about not having fun due to the tendencies of D&D or other traditional tabletop RPGs. Despite this, I've seen many people have fun games of very deep, nuanced, character-driven play in all sorts of traditional games - often by modifying the typical pattern of adventures and/or changing the rules. They are responsible for their own play, but that doesn't mean that the characterizations of these games as written aren't correct.

      \n Interesting thread. (How's North Carolina, Dave?) (Also, I played Small Worlds once, and was similarly lost in the game, not sure what to do until the game was over.)

      I think that there are a lot of things coming together here, and I've been on both sides of this issue before.

      In no particular order:

      1. I do NOT think that indie game design mandates or forces fast, jerky play with no descriptive detail. Most of the "darling" games of this community can be played very functionally at a nice and slow pace, without deviating from any of the rules or advice in the book(s). For instance, I do not see any reason why a game of The Shadow of Yesterday would normally run slower than a game of D&D, or vice-versa.*

      [ * A significant exception being the speed of combat: some games will make particular procedures very lengthy and/or involved -- like the more recent editions of D&D, for instance. But I'm speaking more of the speed of the fiction here: if that confuses you, imagine I'm just talking about low-level AD&D 2nd Edition.]

      I've often argued with people about this: "Why are you complaining that your Sorcerer [or whatever] game moves too fast and doesn't have enough detail? If you like detail, put it in; if you like a slow pace, slow down." So, I'm totally with Christopher on this one. Play it as you like, right? It makes no sense to blame the book for telling you to remember to have conflict in the story.

      2. I DO think that game design affects the pace of the story and the game very significantly. Some games allow you to move through chunks of story really, really fast, and some block you every step of the way. For example: in D&D, a monster who has 500 hit points and immunity to instant kill magics, etc, generally cannot simply be killed in a quick 5-minute scene. Likewise, you want to become the Emperor of the Great Lands, well, you're starting at 1st level. It's going to take you at least a little while, right?

      Game design also affects the *optimal* speed of gameplay. Like John Kim points out, if you play Fiasco with long meandering scenes and lots of exposition... you won't get anywhere by the end of the session. On the other hand, if you try to throw a D&D game into a final confrontation to save the universe from the Big Bad within the first five minutes... you'll have difficulties, because the game is designed to expect a certain rate of character development, etc, etc.

      Each game has somewhat of an optimal story pace, or at least an optimal range for the pace of the story. I know that when I play game X, I need to push certain elements forward, or it will be rough going. I also know that when I play game Y, it's not as much fun unless we consciously slow down and get into the details somewhat more. Some games are pretty flexible in this regard; other games really have a "sweet spot" in the pace at which they move.

      3. Story Games/Forge games/indie games, and this whole movement of gaming, has a very particularly place in the history of games, at least for the people who began the movement and their own personal experiences. I think the Forge was dominated early on by a whole group of people who were, among other things:

      * Getting older and developing more mature, defined tastes
      * Getting older and finding that they had less and less free time
      * Getting older and becoming more demanding about the quality of their gaming
      * Growing more and more frustrated when games didn't deliver what they were looking for

      So, many people in this crowd were very consciously pushing for faster, more meaningful play, with more drama, more conflicts, etc. Hence that association with "Story Now!"

      I identify with this: one of the things that attracted me to "indie games" was the promise that, hey, we're not going to waste four hours on insignificant details, because the design of the game will help us get on the same page about what we're into and really focus in on it.

      I remember a lot of talk along these lines, both at the Forge and among people discovering those games, and (in my experience), a LOT of went something like:

      "Dude! I'm sick of how slow and bogged-down our games are. Have you heard about [Game X]? It says to Say Yes or Roll the Dice! Doesn't that sound like it could solve that problem?"

      So, at the very least, some significant minority of the whole group of people involved with these new games was very consciously trying to get their games to move faster, to deliver more drama and more punch in a shorter period of time.

      Myself? I love the idea of a slow-burn, high-detail game. However, I get to play a story game once in a blue moon, and usually only for a few hours. So I gravitate towards games that can provide "the good stuff" in a short period of time, and reliably.

      Lifestyle may be a significant factor here. Dave, from what little I know of our respective lifestyles, you tend to have more opportunities to explore slow-moving, detailed games than I do. That alone might be enough to describe differences in many people's stated preferences.

      4. Everything is best in moderation. Or something like that:

      That whole mindset of "indie games design" drove a lot of people to push games faster. They saw games moving real slow, so they designed games, bought games, or offered advice that helped things move along faster. This was a new thing for a lot of people, and very welcome.

      I'm sure at some level this selected for people who wanted that, who prefer a faster pace.

      If you like fast-paced drama, are you going to buy the latest super-detailed combat sim, or a game that you can sit down and play right away and get to what you want?

      So, I think a lot of story game design was driven by or for people who wanted a faster pace.

      Now, we've had a reaction to that. In the last few years, people have started talking about: "How can we make our games *slow down*?" "I miss the detail..." Etc.

      Vincent has said many times that one of his goals in the design of Apocalypse World was to get people to slow down and focus more on the details of play. That kind of design is a reaction to games moving along too quickly, just like many of the earlier "story games" were designed in reaction to people not getting the drama they wanted fast enough.

      Many people are interested and are (or were) exploring design elements like TSOY's "refreshment scenes", which are also about slowing down the progression of story by creating moments and scenes which are conflict-free.

      As niches get filled, new interests pop up, often in reaction to what needs have not been met or what territory has not been explored. I think that's cool!

      \n To finish my miniature novel:

      * I remember the first few times I played "story games" with "indie gamers". I was totally shocked at the pacing of the story: "Wow, this is fast!" I enjoyed it, but, yes, I've played with people whose preference for moving things along was way too strong for my tastes. I still had a good time, but that kind of pace left me short of breath and hoping to slow down next time and enjoy the scenery.

      It doesn't seem like a terribly controversial hypothesis to me to say that people who DO like a fast pace and DO NOT like detail/color a great deal might be drawn more often to "Story Now" design, as Dave suggests. I don't know if it's true, but if it were, it would not surprise me in the least: it sounds like a pretty natural possibility.


      Also, a sidenote:

      What I find fascinating about all this is that detail and color do not necessarily line up with the pace of the story OR the intensity of the story.

      For instance, consider that you could play out a long long long combat in AD&D 2nd Ed. without really knowing anything about detail or color. It is (at least potentially) just SLOW.

      On the other hand, I find Dogs in the Vineyard to be a very slow-moving game. You can play for several hours and only play through a couple of scenes. However, the design of the game gives a lot of intensity to that gameplay: we have lots of detail about what's happening and what is being said, and it is clearly all pushing things forward, all the time. We might have little color, though, and a very slow pace of story development (a single conflict could take an hour to resolve). Something like old-school D&D might be the opposite: we have tons and tons of detail and color, as people ask "how thick is the rope?" and "does it seem like my gloves would slide over this rope very easily?"... but then the actual conflict might be resolved with a single roll, once all those details are established.

      If I was one of those matrix-making people, I'd probably do a blog post about all the different permutations of "high color", "fast story pace", "slow story pace", "drawn-out conflict resolution", "rapid conflict resolution", "rapid mechanical resolution", "lengthy mechanical resolution", and "detailed fictional accounting"... and then give a cool-sounding name to each space in the matrix, of course, and list all the games I think fall under that category. And then we could all argue about it!
