\n A position to discuss:

The first time you play a role-playing game, you don't know what you want. You want lots of things, and you're wondering what this new thing is. You need something that's broad, that can catch you no matter where you run and give you some hints and pointers towards other experiences.

Later, when you're familiar with the form and start understanding yourself and what you like, that's when you need to pick up the fine-tuned games. The ones that do that thing you like, and do it well, all of the time.

    \n The answer is that a lot of the time a game's design doesn't matter very much except to the degree it can provide elements that a particular player can intuitively attach herself to. Game designers hate hearing that game design might not be the central factor in a game working, but there are specific reasons for this, ranging from the statistical problems of bare game systems (where there are generally too few instances for the total set of instances to cleave to expected values), to the barrier between the design and the social structures of particular groups, to the fact that roleplaying games can in most cases by separated into pure game objects and the associated signs (which allows people to restructure the meaning of game events).

    Though I believe systems do matter, gamers didn't come up with the folk notion of systems not mattering because, as alleged by some, they were lying to themselves and in denial of the ocean of ludic pain that informed their unenlightened lives before someone decided to give words like "Narrative" addled definitions. These ideas came from working techniques that arise intuitively from play experience.

    A game basically can't prevent these insights from arising and this is useful, especially for new gamers, as they can develop personal techniques based on a dialogue with the text. A game can often rail against these things (Early Gygaxian AD&D, Sorcerer) and make it take time, but smart people work their way around that. Basically, it takes a person in the room (or by post/email/whatever) to be a dick about the right way to play the game to ruin the ability of most people to develop fruiftul ways to play RPGs.

    \n I am of the opinion that games for beginners should have the following qualities:
    \n1) Easily accessible setting (tolkenesque fantasy, star trek/wars-like sci fi, modern/dark future, etc.). I think that a bizarre setting can detract from the task at hand (roleplaying), even more so for someone who is learning the ins and outs of it for the first time...
    \n2) Specific rules, arbitrary rules leads to misunderstanding. Ideally, a new player can look in the rulebook, see the rules and know what to expect from the game.
    \n3) Specific roles, It should be clear what the characters are supposed to be doing in the setting.
    \n Now technically, a lot of games meet this criteria (D&D, Exalted, SoY and InSpectres), but I think that other games fall flat for new gamers. GURPS straight out of the box or "The Pool" are a good examples of games I would not subject new players to. Not because they are bad games, but because of the whole "paralysis of choice" phenomenon. I've seen seasoned gamers paralyzed by too much choice, why would I subject someone that I am trying to teach good RP'ing to that?
    \n I guess to me a beginner is a special class of gamer, not because I don't trust their knowledge, experience or abilities, but because, to me, its a chance to teach them how not to make some of the mistakes I made. Of course they will make their own mistakes, and hopefully, they will learn from them. But more importantly, I can say, "don't drive down that alley, its a dead end." And maybe make their gaming experience a little more fun...

    OK, ok, enough metaphor: I am (dimly) championing the notion of Allow Beginners To Struggle. Suppose they gain insight into the setting and system *faster* by wading through complexity or broad choice or an odd setting? Conversely, suppose an experienced, guiding hand ends up putting blinders on the beginner, by firmly directing him or her to The Right Way to play this (or all) game(s)?

    What if a concrete, directed RPG leaves a bad taste and, as such, sullies the name of all RPGs in a way that a more open RPG would not have, because it provides alternatives? Or suppose someone playing in a "commonly known" setting doesn't "get" the game's hooks and finds the RPG experience to be inferior to the passive entertainment experience of just watching the original IP?

    Finally, I sincerely reject the notion that "Play Pretend + Structure" is a "rare combination." Why? Well, looking back on my own past, I can think of scores and scores of instances where the two combine, though not in the "roll a die and check a chart" manner. Simple negotiation of legitimate scope and "proper play," and trading or resource allocation, are HUGE aspects of Let's Pretend... and of many RPGs. "Let's play cops and robbers, but you can't leave these two blocks and the robbers get a count of 100 to find hideouts before the cops can come looking." And, further, don't forget the TONS of Structure that pervades other forms of play, like sports or even going to the playground. You learn to take turns, you learn to "play fair," you learn procedures and special cases and....

    So, OK, *in combination* they don't come together much outside of RPGs or stage acting; but (again) I think it's a bit unfair or even elitist to suggest that such combining somehow will frazzle or short-out the mind of a "beginner" to gaming, or will necessarily drive them away from gaming. I bet I could go up to almost anyone over the age of about 8 years old and describe 90% of RPGs as "well, you know how sometimes when you play pretend you get into arguments about who did what first, or what's 'fair' to do when you're playing? OK, in RPGs, we have rules and some other stuff like dice or cards which help us fairly resolve such arguments before they even start. Wanna play?"

    Again, I entreat this thread's participants to define "beginner" in a manner that provides a clear association between (a) such an individual's shortcomings which require hand-holding to get at GoodRightFun and (b) solid, pedagogical notions of how to deal with each of such an individual's limitations. Such info--far more than anecdotes and opinions about what's The Right Way to design and write for a nebulously defined class of entities--would be a solid springboard for designers and writers trying to target particular "classes" of RPG users.

    \n I see it in the same context that computer games are going through at the moment. 'Player training' is all the rage - it's most evident in the Half-Life series: Present the players with a limited set of tools and options, let them master the basics, then layer on the complexity (and up the challenge) as the game progresses until they have mastered the game as a whole. For example, you're not given the Gravity Gun until about halfway through HL2, because you're mastering things like interacting with the environment and basic combat skills.

    I believe the same thing is valuable with RPGs. Start with just some basic mechanics and procedures, then layer on new ones as the players get comfortable. Even with euro-style boardgames, extra procedures can be daunting to new players. The wife and I have started really exploring them with gusto, and things like hidden information, strategic trading and the like take some getting used to for her.

    One thing that I find interesting is that there's an either/or dynamic in play with RPGs. Either you have a very simple game, mechanically speaking, which shifts the burden of narrative onto the players; or you have a mechanically complex game that may provide more support for new players to make choices (given all of the tools they have arrayed on their character sheet) - but there aren't games that do both. There's no basic/advanced versions of Nine Worlds or PTA.

    One of the things I'm trying to do with DEAD is find a way to scaffold in the roleplaying elements. As is, you can play DEAD solely as a card game, with no roleplay required. But then I have included rules for shifting the play away from the cards, so to speak, and to fall into a much looser, narrative style of play.

    In the case of DEAD, I'm acquainting the players with the mechanical side of the game first, then offering support for a more narrative style of play. A more narrative game might include additional mechanical supports for new players who may not be master storytellers.

    \n "Fine-tuned"? Probably not. I think there's good argument for clear, specific, and consistent, but not "does only one thing, and that only if used exactly as directed." Not that that can't work, but that it's not likely to build portable skills and it's only going to "hook" people who are looking for that specific experience.

    On the other hand, I really do think that the "blob of non-specific stuff, find your game in the blob" approach is pretty tough. It selects for the people who enjoy the process of sifting their thing out of the blob - which gives you a hobby full of (but not solely composed of) somewhat obsessive tinkerers and Serious Fans who will put up with/ignore a lot of cruft to get the thing they want.

    My postulates are pretty much

    1. The text informs play.
    2. Play never perfectly replicates the text.
    3. What people want the game to be about trumps what the text wants it to be about.
    4. But, tension between text and people creates stress and increases the potential for "blah" or unpleasant experiences.

    Given that, it's probably a good idea to minimize that stress. The two most straightforward ways to do that are either

    1. Throw up you hands and try not to be prescriptive at all about the game's content. Give tools that are generally useful and let people build their own game out of them.
    2. Be really specific about what the tools in the text are, and what they work well for. Trust people to pick the game based on the experience it says it delivers, and do your best to deliver it.

    Historically, published games have either done #1 or just CLAIMED to do #1, counting on the hacker culture to cover for a lot. There've also been a fair number of games that tried #2, but in many cases the tools for delivering the promised play experience were based more on an assumed set of play culture and soft skills that weren't actually IN the text.
