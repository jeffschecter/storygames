Let us be frank, CoC is a neat book, a neat collection of stories, but the engine, it doesn't really do it for me.

Which system would you use to power the Beast that is Call of Cthulhu?

Me, I'm considering The Mountain Witch.

Imagine a group where one of the Fates is "You are Nyarlathotep", the players know it's in and that someone got it, do you need more for horror?
Anyway, the game is set for an object-oriented adventure, where much of the focus is what happens along the way, just like detectives looking for the Mythos.
Last, and to me most important, is the fact that horror is an internal thing, something you bring upon yourself, not something others induce in you(that's terror). Nothing like the Trust system to make you worry, worry about your fellow players.

Thoughts?

  • Larry
    February 2007 edited February 2007
    Guy,

    Rather than throw out a list of game systems, I'll throw a question back at you. What does "Call of Cthulhu" mean to you? To me, "CoC" seems a slippery concept, similar to "D&D." Some players think of the published epic, railroad-y adventures. Others use it for tense horror one-shots. Some are "Lovecraft purists," and want to excise the "Cthulhu Mythos" from the game and get closer to the spirit of Lovecraft's fiction. Others confess they think H.P. Lovecraft stinks like diapers and relish the accumulated "monsternomicon" of 20th-century weird horror authors. Some get into the 1920's America period detail, others find it non-essential and use a different setting. Et cetera.

    And far too much of it involves playing geeky recognition games with strangers outside a formal game context (Cthulhu for President! Whoo!), rather than figuring out how to all sit down and play the damn thing in a coherent fashion.

    It is hopefully obvious that all of these things are encompassed by the expression "Call of Cthulhu" -- and also that there are mutually exclusive ideas here as to what a particular game activity will actually entail. If we don't make some effort to establish what we're actually talking about, we're all gonna talk gobbledygook past each other like so many loony cultists.

    So... what is it about "Call of Cthulhu" that gets your creative juices going? What kinds of past experiences have you had with "Call of Cthulhu" that were memorably fun, or regrettably sucky? Is there something about "Call of Cthulhu" that makes it distinct in your thoughts from just plain "horror"?

    If you have InSpectres, Jared's free supplement UnSpeakable seems perfect. You don't know where your actions will take you, because only the initial situation is a given; everything else is improvised. The original system was designed for investigation scenarios. The Pit controls how long it takes before things get deadly. Losing sanity means gaining mythos-based skills, but using mythos-based skills leads to more sanity rolls for everyone involved. And the game can't end until at least one person has gone permanently insane.

    There is controversy over the copyright status of many of Lovecraft's works, especially his later works. Lovecraft had specified that the young Robert Barlow would serve as executor of his literary estate, but these instructions had not been incorporated into his will. Nevertheless his surviving aunt carried out his expressed wishes, and Barlow was given charge of the massive and complex literary estate upon Lovecraft's death.

    Barlow deposited the bulk of the papers, including the voluminous correspondence, with the John Hay Library. However, as a young writer with no legal training, his efforts to organize and maintain Lovecraft's other writing stood little chance of success. August Derleth, an older and more established writer than Barlow, vied for control of the literary estate. One result of these conflicts was the legal confusion over who owned what copyrights.

    All works published before 1923 are public domain in the US. However, there is some disagreement over who exactly owns or owned the copyrights and whether the copyrights for the majority of Lovecraft's works published post-1923 â€” including such prominent pieces as "The Call of Cthulhu" and "At the Mountains of Madness" â€” have now expired.

    Questions center over whether copyrights for Lovecraft's works were ever renewed under the terms of the U.S. Copyright Act of 1976 for works created prior to January 1, 1978. The problem comes from the fact that before the Copyright Act of 1976 the number of years a work was copyrighted in the US was based on publication rather than life of the author plus a certain number of years and that it was only good for 28 years with one renewal for an additional 28 years with the Copyright Act of 1976 retroactively extended all works renewed to a period of 47 years [10] and Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act of 1998 adding another 20 years to that. Similarly, the European Union Directive on harmonising the term of copyright protection of 1993 extended the copyrights to 70 years after the author's death, which would be 2007.

    In those Berne Convention countries who have implemented only the minimum copyright period, copyright expires 50 years after the author's death.

The copyright for Lovecraft's works would have been inherited by the only surviving heir of his 1912 will: Lovecraft's aunt, Annie Gamwell. Gamwell herself perished in 1941 and the copyrights then passed to her remaining descendants, Ethel Phillips Morrish and Edna Lewis. Morrish and Lewis then signed a document, sometimes referred to as the Morrish-Lewis gift, permitting Arkham House to republish Lovecraft's works but retaining the copyrights for themselves. Searches of the Library of Congress have failed to find any evidence that these copyrights were then renewed after the 28-year period and, hence, it is likely that these works are now in the public domain.

  • talysman
    February 2007

    Minimal prep, like InSpectres; the GM comes up with an initial mystery or threat and lets the player decisions and dice results direct the rest of the plot. You have to fudge the names of the different stages, but it's the same play structure as InSpectres: get a "call" from a client, research and investigate, suit up and do fieldwork. Main changes you have to make are eliminating franchise dice and scraping off references to a Ghostbusters-like small business.

    The main thing the GM has to keep in mind is that, when the Pit runs dry, things should become more desperate, with more stress rolls as a result (and possibly even a few deaths.) When lucky stress rolls put some dice back into the Pit, the tension eases up a bit, until the dice run out again. So, let the Pit be your guide to the intensity level of the story.
