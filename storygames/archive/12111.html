    \n I absolutely love the mechanization of "paying the price" here. It looks absolutely brutal, and the rules make it look like you'll be paying a hell of a lot of prices. And it's totally wicked that there's an incentive to cross off "my kids" to save your own ass. I mean that 'wicked' in every sense of the word, there.

I will admit that I got a *lot* more out of it after reading the description and then the example, rather than just the example that you posted earlier.

    Also, I like the Lady Blackbird-esque "ready to go" package. It lets you get down to business, which is nice.

    Finally, I was a tiny bit unclear on the 'goals'. Are all of the goal cards available to pursue if you want to, for everybody? Or does each player say "X and Y are my goals" and go after them? Because if all of them are group oriented, or potentially group oriented, that will probably affect how many check marks they should have. Actually, setting how many checks a goal needs is probably one of the subtler ways to affect how much the PCs band together to achieve their goals.

    \n It seems to me that you could minimize the handling time by having player and GM roll at the same time and using the values on the faces of the caution dice to immediately cancel out GM's dice. This would also have the nice effect of making the decision to, say, use your six to cancel out one of the GM's an interesting tactical choice rather than merely a lucky break on your dice. So if I roll six dice and get 6, 4, 3, 3, 2, 1 and the GM rolls four dice for 6, 4, 2, 2 and I group (6), (4, 2), (3, 3) then I get 3 progress and cancel out the GM's 2 (since he doesn't have a 1), which means that the GM could group (6), (4, 2) and extract two concessions from me. But if I group (4, 3, 3) and use the other dice for caution, I cancel out both of the GM's 2s as well as the 6, leaving only a 4 which means no concessions to the bad guys plus 1 big progress for me. Something like that would make the whole process seem more coherent or organic.

    \n Interesting.

    Some more comments:

    1. Why did you choose not to put in any kind after-the-roll choice for the players? Like, say, being able to give up on a Goal or something like that in exchange for not paying a Price. Because sometimes it seems like you could get totally shafted by a GM's roll, and not have any "way out". Is that part of what you're going for?

    I'm thinking of mechanics like Giving in Dogs in the Vineyard, where you can Give on the conflict in exchange for avoiding Fallout you can't afford to take.

    2. Why do characters who escape the planet not need to pay a Price? I think it would be cool to have someone try to escape the planet... only to die on their trip out of the system.

    3. How often in play does it feel weird to retroactively narrate a Price into the fiction? I'd be worried about this. Because the Prices are not linked to what you're doing or what Goal you're striving for. For instance, say you get into a fight, and choose to check off "my kids", who aren't present... what now? Do we narrate in a whole new set of circumstances to explain that? That seems to be the case in your example of play. I could see this becoming a problem particularly as the choices for Prices begin to get narrowed down, later in the game.

    I do like how all the mechanics reinforce the situation in play, and act to lead to wrapping it up, through escalation and worse.

    \nA historical situation is a bit different - I'm far more open to having my real life historical understanding and emotional investment called into question because there's always more to "discover", whereas an artistic one is something where, when the final "The End" comes up, I then solidify my feelings about it in that moment, when I reflect on the work as a whole. In other words, I wasn't told by a galactic historian that the Death Star destroyed Alderaan for no reason, I saw it happen myself.     \nFirst off, I like the Paul/Bill idea of simultaneous rolling by player and GM. That's good stuff, and is hereby yoinked for the next revision. Thanks!

    #1: Yes, part of what I was going for was the sense that once you've committed yourself to a subversive act, you are going to pay the price for your uppitiness. Shifting some Caution over is a way of protecting yourself some, but there ain't no free lunch.

    #2: Escaping the planet is intended to be a "panic button" move, desperation salvage of things gone terribly wrong. So I didn't want to pile on. Your scenario has got some punch, but I think it would too often be the case. Maybe reduced price....

    #3: Retcon of the scene as described is not intended as part of the Price narration. In your case "my kids" case, a savvy player might have put the kids into the scene before rolling, in order to be able to use them as a shield ("wicked" was the term Jeff used.) OR, you could describe running home from the fight, barely shaking the peacekeepers. And when you arrive home...smoking ruins.
