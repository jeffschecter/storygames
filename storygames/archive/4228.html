\n Some friends of mine have started a Savage Worlds Play by Post. The game was loosely structured, and the first scene was fun (for me at least, I don't know if it was for anyone else). The scene was described by the GM on a web page. We added things that we either said or did. Other folks reacted, and overall, while maybe a little chaotic, it was interactive.

Then one of the folks and the GM talked, and the whole system changed. Now there is no general page that encapsulates what is going on. Instead we have individual pages where the GM describes what we see and hear, and we tell the GM what we do. We have to submit our next moves every Tuesday, and he updates our page on Friday with what happened.

So let's say somehting happens in the fiction on Friday. I want to talk to one of the PC's about it. I post something on Tuesday initiating the conversation. The GM let's the other PC know that a conversation has started on Friday. The PC responds back to GM on Tuesday. I get his reponse on Friday. And so on. This really seems to kill any hope for interaction between PC's.

I think that the group really wants to have fun playing this, and the GM is very accepting of new ideas/ways to make this work. My problem is that this is my first PBP and I don't know how it is suppose to work or what are guidelines for making the game run well.

Can anybody help a fellow out?

Thanks!

    \n Well it sounds like - and I may be way off here - like you have one or two specific issues, likely. I'm basing this observation off having played the way that you've played (using Wikis with no GM, in fact), and having had it work just fine.
    \n
    \nFirst, the GM may feel that the game is out of his control, wanting to have control over the narrative. If this is the case, then it may be that you and your GM just want very different things out of play. The only way to be sure is to ask him why he made the change. If he can articulate it, then maybe you can work towards some sort of compromise solution that works for everyone.
    \n
    \nSecond, that one player talking to the GM (or him and the GM) could be overwhelmed by the pace. A lot of times in PBP games one or more players dominates play, because they simply have more time and/or energy to post. This is just a fact of life with asynch play, however, and slowing everyone down to allow everyone to keep pace is probably not the best idea. The rate of play being as slow as it is anyhow. Like James says above, if I had to play only one post a week, that'd suck. Oh, to be sure there are games that I play at more slowly than that at times. But I'd prefer if I weren't artificially limited when I had time. So I can catch up when I am available, as opposed to times when I have limited time and energy to post.
    \n
    \nNow I may have missed by a mile here... but if I have, telling me where I'm wrong may help us nail down the problem. Which is key to giving you some solutions. I have loads of ideas about how you *could* play. But only one of those will fit your actual needs.

    \n Well in a PBEM I'm in, almost all posts go to an open list and anything that needs to stay secret gets dealt with in private email. On a message board, I'd probably have everyone post to a main thread and any secret stuff goes to PM or whispered posts. Though, something I discovered about this open list format is that the characters still have secrets but now we all get to enjoy them. My own secret became much more fun when I revealed it. If your GM is worried about posts getting away from him, have him stick with his strict posting schedule and limit PC posts in between unless there are only PC's in the thread so you can have PC-PC conversations without bogging down the rest of the game.

    \n I had a good friend back in my Vampire-fanatic days who ran a bunch of PBP games that worked by having different sub-forums for each location, and you'd jump around from forum to forum as your character moved. The games inevitably bogged down and ran out of steam, maybe because it was too difficult to keep track of what your fellow players were doing.

    The structure the original poster mentioned sounds almost like that of an old-school Flying Buffalo play-by-mail game. I think most of those games were turn-based strategy games, with interaction between players only at the borders of territories.

    What I've found to work best as both a player and a GM is to have two threads, IC and OOC. I suppose it wouldn't hurt to have multiple IC threads, but that makes it significantly more difficult to keep on top of the game and you're not as likely to follow what's happening with other players, which really hurts the game for all kinds of reasons.

    I also like to use a looser approach to posting, so that you post a bit of internal dialogue or business along with your "move". That seems to raise the bandwidth greatly. I've also been in games where it was part of accepted practice to post interactions with one's pet NPC's, flashbacks, diary entries, etc,. particularly during lulls between scenes.

    \n Since this thread has lurched alarmingly back to life, I should add that I took the plunge and purchased Quest. It's nice, for what it is, but it's not a PBEM game, really, it's a format for PBEM games. Basically, the idea is that every reply in a given scene should neatly incorporate everything that's gone before, so that the final post of the scene provides a tidy summary of the scene for the archives. It's an interesting concept, but I can't imagine using the full thing in a game.

    I'd love to hear about other people's experiences with it.

    \n Danny, it's funny that you picked up Quest, too. I mentioned it to my wife and, next thing you know, I had a freshly purchased copy in my mailbox.

    For everyone else: I guess the best way to describe Quest is as a meta-system. It presents a fairly rigid format within which the game is moved forward. Sprawling threads, overlapping time-frames and the generally messiness of PBeM are all addressed in the way emails are presented. I think using it would take some acclimation for all involved, but it would be worthwhile in the way it keeps things organized.

    I wish there was a way to explain more explicitly how it all works without simply giving away what Forever People ask $2.50 for.
