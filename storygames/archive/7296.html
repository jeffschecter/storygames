    \n I feel like one of the creators or press guys should let the cat out on the actual name. I'm not going to be that guy.

    That said... the game is about groups of people on a long defunct space station, living happily as all their needs are provided for. The game is all about gaining social status for your clique. It uses a totally unique card system (it will come boxed with special cards, like a board game). It's kind of like "13 year olds on myspace: the game... IN SPACE"

    \n Kevin is so right. It is super fun. Both as a story-making RPG thing and as a game with gamey bits.

    It's an RPG that doesn't require violence as the primary means of problem solving. So there's that. You can play an artist in this game and you actually matter -- the way a Fighter matters in D&D.

    It's less transhuman and more posthuman. The transhumanist issues aren't really issues anymore (it's way way far in the future). That was my take, anyway... I don't know a whole lot about how those terms are used among the diehard.

    There's a fun correspondence between in-ficiton stuff and game-level stuff. Like, you have game stats and resources that your character is aware of and uses in their world. You can do that "immersion" thing that all the kids are talking about, if you want to.

    It has mechanics for changing the world.

    \n\nAugust 2008 \n

    \n
    \n The game is very thematically striking. At least in the version that I played many months back, you have a list of skills that sound relatively banal but all do really badass stuff, like allow you to shape social relationships or do other crazy future science stuff, including things that would be considered social science now. Also, the resolution system, with the custom cards with their own iconic symbols, was really satisfying and evocative.

    Strangely enough, the scope of it felt a bit like Nobilis to me, in that things seem potentially open ended and huge in scope. You can almost do anything you could possibly imagine, so it's up to the players to mark out a section of territory that they're interested in exploring through creating a specific set of characters and plot lines. Like, if you create an artist, you're more likely to involved in certain kids of things, where if you make a meme-banker or something, you're going to be doing something else. But the interconnectedness of everything means that characters from divergent backgrounds have perfectly reasonable reasons to be a community for each other.

    Honestly, you could view it as one huge allegory for the internet, where people form various communities within this open environment where nearly anything is possible. It's social sci-fi wherein the main theme isn't "What can we do?" but "Now that we can do anything, what do we choose to do?"

    \n
    \n\nAugust 2008 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Here's how it works in a nutshell.

    Whenever you want to do anything in the game that engages the system, you have to spend flow. To make stuff, the cost is decided by the final desired product. To challenge others, the target's flow is the max. that it will cost (the min. always being 1 flow). If you're out of flow, you can't initiate a challenge or produce anything of value. You're tapped out. If your flow goes into the negatives, that's bad. That means people want you to go away, for reals and forever (i.e.: perma-banned).
