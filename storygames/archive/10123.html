\n
\n I ran my first game of Cold City last night. I had initially picked up the book based on my excitement about the setting. It was rich with all kinds of creepy monster stuff, occult WWII bits and the whole trust-as-a-mechanic thing really grabbed my attention. I read through the book and wrote up a scenario and set out to play. My group is pretty open-minded. They've happily accepted every "weird" RPG I tend to throw at them with excitement. We've played Dogs and Burning Wheel and Mouse Guard and InSpectres but this was the first time that we've all felt let down by an indie game.
\n
\nMainly, I'm posting this because I need to see if I am just misunderstanding the rules of the game. If I'm "playing it wrong" then I'd love to know why and how. As it stands, the game seems really overly simple and not well designed, unfortunately. I think that I get what the author was aiming for - a simple mechanic that doesn't spend a lot of time with the fiddly bits of conflict resolution. A design concept that tries to get the rolls out of the way and then jump into narrating the results of those rolls. The problem I found, though, was that the resolution mechanic was just too simple. Too black-and-white. The game specifically states that if an obstacle comes up that, if the players couldn't overcome it, would stop the story, just give them what they want. A pretty simple rule of thumb obviously inspired by Dogs in the Vineyard. Great, except that if you do that, conflict becomes useless because you're only in conflict over things that won't interrupt the story.
\n
\nI found that the roll being applied in reverse order (initiate the conflict, roll to determine the results, narrate the conflict while steering it to those results) didn't work. As soon as the dice hit the table and success or failure was determined, people lost interest and wanted to move on. There was no tension. It got to a point where I was dreading conflict because it was so counter-intuitive.
\n
\nIt felt, for me, like Cold City was trying to be a simplified Dogs. To boil all the sees and raises and conflict-as-character-advancement into a single roll. It really didn't work for me. The one thing I really did like was the Trust mechanic and we didn't even get to use that. The Hidden Agendas were neat, but never came into play. I think a solution for this would have been to assign the national agendas myself. Have the GM give them, as orders, to the individual PCs. That way they'd be tied to the game. Maybe I, or my players, weren't pushing hard enough for this but it just didn't come out right.
\n
\nAnyway, the setting is marvelous but the mechanics just don't back it up. Next time I play this I think I'm going to just use the mechanics from Dogs in the Vineyard and find a way to attach the Hidden Agenda and trust mechanics to it.

My sense of Cold City is that the mechanics are very strongly inspired by the Mountain Witch (trust, d6s with degrees of success, hidden agendas, the whole bit) but that, in practice Cold City is significantly harder to run than the Witch because it doesn't have the tight scenario (group of ronin climb mountain to kill the witch) to provide it with structure.

Some of that, in my case, had to do with playing with only a few players (like 3), which doesn't give the trust mechanics room to shine, but there were some other resolution issues too. However, I think it's critical, in games like Cold City and Mouse Guard, to narrate the actual in-game events that the mechanical results describe. Otherwise, the story content of the game has no teeth and you're just moving these numbers around. I know you said your players weren't interested in the fiction after the roll, but heading down that path is guaranteed to suck, I think.

    \n One area where I don't think Cold City gives a lot of guidance (and Don't Rest Your Head is the same) is how to pace conflicts. I think the trick to Cold City conflicts is to establish beforehand how climactic you want the conflict to be and set the stakes accordingly - so in order to convince the scientist, perhaps the conflict needs to be broken down into smaller-stake, mini-conflicts. Perhaps they have to first catch up with him (conflict), then stop him diving off the building (conflict), and finally convince him to trust them enough to spill his guts (conflict).

    Re-reading that, that's not the most elegant of examples I've given (what happens if they fail the first one? Personally, I'd be inclined to have that mean that the Consequences from that conflict make stopping him from jumping harder, representing a big lead he has rather than that he gets clean away. What about the second? Again, the Consequences can influence the outcome, and jumping need not = death: 1 or 2 points and he's not in Crisis, just injured, even 3+ points he might be dying or incapacitated rather than dead), but hopefully you get the idea.

    Trollbabe could suffer from the same issue, but solves it by giving every conflict a Pace - resolving the overall conflict is the result of 1, 3 or 5 rolls, depending on the degree of focus. A finale conflict might require winning 3 out of 5 rolls for instance, while a less significant one only 1 out of 1, or 2 out of 3. I've toyed with porting this over into Cold City and Hot War (and Don't Rest Your Head), so maybe this could help?

    Regarding Hidden Agendas, it's my understanding that the GM generally assigns (perhaps with player negotiation) National agendas and players assign Personal ones - that way the GM can interweave agendas more easily. Agendas should be rearing their heads frequently in the game - they act as pretty strong flags, so the background plot should be riddled with opportunities for these to intersect, otherwise, as it sounds like occurred in your game, they can easily be overlooked.

    Edit: had another thought: I think Cold City conflicts are better without the often binary stake setting you see in some games: "You win, the scientist comes with you and spills his secrets. he wins, he jumps to his death". Rather, these form the intent of both parties ("I try to convince him to come with us"), but what actually happens is informed by the level of success and the Consequences chosen by the winner, so you don't actually know he's dead if he wins, it's still open - if he wins with 1 Consequence, I generally see that as a partial success - jumped, but then had second thoughts and is hanging by one arm on the ledge 6ft below, for example.

    A 3 point 'Attribute reduced to 0' Consequence is usually pretty final in a suicide attempt, however.

    \n Hey Adam, I think you're being a little overly harsh on the mechanics here. Not all conflict resolutions play the same or require the same things of players. Like I said, Cold City's standard conflict resolution isn't a step-by-step mechanical thing. It's one-roll and you're done. If you treat it like a step-by-step thing and go in expecting the mechanics to pace the conflict for you, OF COURSE you're going to come away disappointed, because the mechanics don't pace the conflict much at all. But not all games or players necessarily expect or want the mechanics to pace the conflict. I know I was horribly disappointed with In A Wicked Age, for example, because I came in expecting the mechanics to pace the conflicts like they do in Dogs. But IAWA works way differently, so it failed at the table for me the first time (and second).

    I think if games like that have failings, it's in not making it explicitly clear to the players what is required of them and how to approach resolution. That's a very hard thing to do as a game writer, because everyone comes in with their own expectations, but it's critical to get your game to work for a variety of different audiences. So I wouldn't necessarily say the mechanics failed you or that the game doesn't gel with your playing style. It certainly didn't gel with what you wanted and expected from the game the first time you played it, but it's not like people can only play games one way. It's really up to you to decide whether you're willing to make the effort to figure out how to change your expectations to fit what the mechanics DO do, or, alternately, change the mechanics to better support the kind of play that you are already comfortable with. If you're not willing or find it easier to just abandon the mechanics for something else that you like, that's totally cool too, but it's not the only solution.

    \n Quick Question: Are you familiar with other "one roll" resolution systems, like Primetime Adventures?

    The key to making those kinds of systems work right is that whatever is riding on the one single die roll has to be palpably tense BEFORE you deploy the system. You can't start a conflict thinking, "Man the tension's really going to build over this one!" Because it won't. It has to already be built.

    Part of that involves naming the exact right goal for the conflict. I was playing a Primetime Adventures game. It was the last episode of the season. The show was about a family of 18th Century clockwork engineers and their rivalry with a family who worked with electricity. It was nearing the end of the episode and the two families were meeting to have a technological duel. The clockwork family brought this battlemech like thing. The electricity family just brought this odd black box.

    I turn to one the players and I say, "So this about winning the duel right?" And he says, "No, this about ruining their reputation." That was fucking perfect! It didn't matter who won the duel all that mattered was that the result destroyed one of the families socially. Those were the perfect stakes. The group was clearly already buzzing with excitement. I blew my budget. The player blew his fan mail...

    And...

    And...

    Well, I'm not going to tell you to emphasize my point. The fact that there was no, back and forth over the duel or whatever didn't matter because socially and creatively we were all wired over just how devastatingly important this one draw of the cards was going to be.

    Jesse

    \n Oh, I think Jesse has it nailed for me.

    In Cold City when you have a conflict of interest you build up in free play to a point where you want to see how it falls -- then you go to the roll, building in your traits and assembling your pool. The roll then determines the outcome, the traits used how it is happening, and the level of consequence how well or badly it goes. It looks like your expectation was that the roll would start off a back and forth, and that really rocks for your group.

    Cold City just doesn't do that mechanically. It could give someone consequences that they roll over into another arena or a follow-up roll, and consequential scenes and conflicts might spiral off of the first. But that's not the same thing. And what to get for your 9 successes? A couple of Traits and a bunch of dice for my next Influence roll please.

    Mechanics-wise I have issues with the marginal successes and with the general trait whoring where people say "fuck the fiction" and bring in every trait and agenda that they can, which actually drowns out the effects of trust (and the larger pools create an expectation of more success but only create more marginal successes). And Trust is the hook of the game, so anything that smears its effect out is bad in my book. I also don't like the adding dice for using negative traits, but a whole bunch of folk liked it and it does make people bring them in (I'd get the opponent in a conflict to bring them in). But I'm fine with its one roll to determine an outcome, that's a valid way of playing.

    I would say that you missed a HUGE part of the Cold City feel and experience by the following; "The one thing I really did like was the Trust mechanic and we didn't even get to use that. The Hidden Agendas were neat, but never came into play."

    My advice would be as GM, bring their Hidden Agendas into play. Put stuff in the fiction that goes against one player's Hidden Agenda but would advance another's. National Agendas from the GM should pop the play with things that will put PCs into Conflict, make and break alliances and make the PCs question who they can trust (their National bosses? any of their fellow RPA agents?). Ideally National and Personal Agendas will be at odds too, not necessarily directly opposed but not a clear win-win unified path. No one is a truly "company" man in Cold City for it to have that post-War Third Man feel.

    If you've got a group working seamlessly together wanting a conflict being played out in beats. Then... uh... play Mouse Guard. Seriously. It scratches that itch.
