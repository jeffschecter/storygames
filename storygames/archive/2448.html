    Okay, to answer my own questions in a general way:

    I started playing with my current extended group about 5 years ago, just as I was in the process of moving to Toronto. My context of meeting them was always as "Mo's friends" who gradually became my friends. Gaming started up more or less in tandem with the closeness of the relationships -- as I got closer we'd start to game, and as we gamed we'd get closer. So the groups were always used to me and Mo in the game together.

    Of the rest of the group we always had coupled pairs as a big part of the group. The four other people we play with the most often were dating when I met them, and all are married now. Another player was single, hooked up with a girl, and got married a few years after that. For the folks who were already in relationships nothing much changed, as all members were gamers. The guy that got married, however, started having trouble juggling his gaming and his other personal time, as his wife often objected to the going off for 16 hours a weekend to do something without her just after they were married. Now days we hang out with him and the wife (both close friends) and play boardgames constantly, but we haven't played a game longer than a oneshot in months, and I don't know when we'll do so again. That relationship change certainly killed that part of the previous dynamic.

    Between the couples in our games its a pretty common problem for whoever is GM to be harder on their partner than they are on anyone else. I know I've done this as recently as Bitches in the Vineyard, where I was much harder on Mo than the others (shamefully so, sometimes). Other GMs in the group (mostly the boys, GMing for their wives, who tend to be impassioned and character socket players, as opposed to the boys story and setting sockets) have either admitted to the same problem, or have had it admitted for them by the group because its so blatantly obvious. Since we started doing more formal social contracts we've been able to nip this one a little bit, because there just tends to be less unspoken subtext.

    Mo and I do play one on one games together, pretty regularly. The rest of our group, to my knowledge, doesn't. Several of the other couples have tried, but stopped because it either felt silly or because it didn't meet one of their payouts -- or because they found they just couldn't game together without other people to mitigate them. This is despite the fact that we've occasionally had one-on-one sessions across couples (me GMing for one other player, most of the time) without the oddity. I have to wonder, however, if some of that has to do with my "Most Honored and Magical GM" status. (Silly status.)

    Now, all of that is these days. Before 5 years ago, in University and online gaming, the situation was different. Much different. But I'm going to hold that for a post tomorrow, I think.

Dave now joins in games on a fairly regular basis; he's part of my monthly gaming group, and he also played in my Mountain Witch game that I GMed in December/January.

    I have occasionally had some worries that he was gaming simply to please me, but he very much enjoys himself, for all he professes not to enjoy the mechanics of the games all that much (he's apparently even more of a hippie gamer than I am...). When I GMed The Mountain Witch, I was probably more concerned about whether he was "getting it" and having fun than I was concerned about the other players, because they were all much more seasoned gamers. I didn't worry too much about being perceived as favoring him or being too hard on him, since it's such a player driven game anyway.

    On another note, I have to admit that I've sometimes worried a little bit about being a woman gaming with mostly men. I've always had a lot of guy friends, and thus to me in most respects it's not a big deal, but I'm now at a point in my life when a lot of my guy friends are in long-term relationships or are getting married, and it's sometimes made me scrutinize my interactions with them out of worry that I might inadvertantly provoke jealousy. I tend to pay a bit more attention to this than usual in roleplaying contexts, because of the potential for emotional stuff bleeding over a bit. I don't think it's entirely coincidental that one of my friends and I became much closer when our characters became closer, so it's probably not surprising that when our characters started flirting we both kind of pulled back a bit and made sure that everyone (including our respective SO's) was ok with it. He and I had no intention of becoming romantically involved in real life, but we sort of realized that saying "oh, it's just a game" didn't remove all of the potential complications.

    I married who I did in part because my partner is a brilliant gamer with as much interest in gaming as I have. (The former is the icing, the latter is the essential cake.)     I am tougher GMing my husband because I expect more out of him. I also fret a lot more because I am thinking the whole time, "He's seen through my whole idea! I'm so transparent to him, he'll figure out all the puzzles! He won't appreciate it because it'll be so abecedarian to him." And I can't keep secrets from him because, "He's the only one who will appreciate this little throwaway line I added into this completely optional flavour text, so I might as well tell him now." He says he's the same way about me.

    I am willing to game without my husband, but I think I enjoy the experience more with him. I do one-on-one games with him, and games with my family, and I think there's a lot of "shorthand" which means we have better gaming because we don't have to do so much defining, and beat the learning curve.     I will let him bend rules sometimes, but I like to think I would do the same for anyone who asked and had the same convincing reasoning.     The three different longer-term groups I had in table top gaming were all exclusively male and never contained more than one homosexual or bisexual member, so there were no hookups or breakups inside of the groups. The status of players with people outside the game did influence their behavior, but I guess that's another topic.

    Online, of course, it's a whole different game (hah). People hook up all the time, and break up as well. Characters are often much more closely tied to the players than in table top groups. I know several players who almost exclusively play with other people that they have a mutual attraction with. Games fall apart pretty quickly when the OOC "relationships" break up.

    I do play with my wife, quite frequently. We play over chat because that's what she likes best. Chat play is also closer to collaborative writing than table top play, and since we're both writers, we enjoy that aspect of it. We play one-on-one a lot, but we also play group games with others. The one-on-one games allow us to focus on each other (what with the medium being slow and all), we don't have to hold anything back (we're on the same page with Lines+Veils, and they're pretty far out), and we tend to like the same sort of dramatic setups that thrive on the tension between the characters.

    When we play in a group, the only thing that's a bit "out of balance" is that our characters, unless we make them absolutely incompatible or non-sexual, will invariably be attracted to one another, and if left to their own devices, usually end up with very strong feelings for each other that no other player's character could ever compete with. I've found that it's really fruitless to try and prevent that, and instead we're now using this force of nature as dramatic fuel by making sure we set up our characters in ways that they can't ever happily be together.
