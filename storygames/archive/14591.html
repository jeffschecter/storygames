\n Jason Morningstar dropped this nifty one-liner on FB:

"Ogun's Heroes: Red Ball Express vodou practitioners bide their time in a Nazi POW camp, awaiting the day their orisha of war mounts up."

which then led to this exchange-

Me: I like where this is headed.
JM: I am pretty sure it is headed for you to Braunstein it up.

Well, naturally I couldn't not respond to that sort of challenge, right? So I started poking around for th physical bits I'd use for something like that ( turns out most of it wouldn't be that hard to find. Took me about an hour of posting on a minis board to come up with a link for free downloadable Stalag terrain, for example).

But, when it comes right down to it, I'm not really a game designer so much as a tinkerer, so I wanted to call Jason out over hereabouts and see what he ( and the rest of you nice folks) could come up with for concepts to take this from a nifty throwaway idea into a multiform style game.

Side note: Multiform as a term is used to describe a game that is a kind of hybridized LARP plus minis game, at least as I understand the term from discussions of Australian gaming tradition. The minis here are used as a sort of generalized inspiration and positional marker on a map or board, but the concept is not to turn the entire event into a tactical battle game (presumably some minis battle stuff will occur. it just isn't the main focus).

Thhe idea has already morphed a bit. As it turns out, The Redball Express started in August of '44, which makes it a bit late for trying to make events center aroound Mid Summer of '44. MidSummer is also St. JOhn's Day, which is a voodoo holiday of great importance. That's just too good a date to pass up, so Jason suggested that perhaps our POWs were captured instead in Italy during/after Operation Avalanche.

Now I like that idea because it not only opens up St John's day as a possibility, but it also gives an excuse to get Tuskegee Airmen in there ( I've already found appropriate minis- The Tuskegee Airmen were flying cover in Italy. No, I don't know why they'd be stuck in a POW camp with infantrymen either. Just run with it).

Here's the idea soup I've got so far:

Game stuff
Meant to be played with around 20 players over the course of 4- 41/2 hours, not including set up/breakdown for any props used or game briefing/character hand out.

I'm also assuming that there will be break times mid-game, probably to introduce new elements or to summarize preious phases of play and update the entire play group on developments/events they should know about collectively. I've seen this work very well for a one-shot LARP I played in and very much like the structure.

Fiction Stuff

The core character group should be the vodun practicioners

Other groups: The Germans, Non Vodun POWs, maybe a film crew (propaganda for sure, possibly involving Leni Reifenstahl), possibly Josephine Baker ( hey, if the Americans can trot out Marlene Dietrich for German POWs, why wouldn't the Germans do the same with Josephine Baker? Plus she served as a Resistance courier, so that makes it even more juicy. Besides... Josephine Baker!).

There needs to be a cemetery close by or in the camp. I mean, c'mon, we gotta get Baron Samedi in there somewhere. Plus digging gear.

Parallel festivities, perhaps inolving the closest town? Or maybe just in the camp? I mean, escape during some kind of performance pops up in a couple WW2 escape films.

Jazz, jazz funerals, jazz fans.

Arrival of Les rois /Loas

Well, that's what I've got as a starter. I'll open the floor to discussion now.

    \n Honestly, I think the small player group concept is probably the best place to start this game.

    That's how I'd kick things off.

    Even if I were planning to run a massively multiplayer miniatures extravaganza...in fact it would probably be better to start thing off this way.

    Run two groups as prisoners in opposite sides of the camp, one voodounistas and one regular (give each player specific agendas within their group).

    Run another group as local guards who are trying to deal with issues of their own, perhaps their commander has just been assassinated (again give each player specific agendas within their group).

    Run a fourth group as regional military police who have come in to "keep things under control" (again give each player specific agendas within their group)..

    Each group of players has been given a briefing on the rule mechanisms during their prelude story. Players develop an understanding of how their characters work and how their motivations might play out.

    Then throw all the groups together. They'll all have reasons to dislike one another, but each character might find common objectives among individuals from the other factions. That's where the real roleplaying comes in, when players are forced to maker decisions about where their true alliances and motivations lie.

    You've basically got the right kind of set up with what I'm already reading.

    As for translating the table activities to the live side of things, start with a large room, and partition parts off, with tape on the floor, curtains, rows of chairs...whatever you've got access to. Keep a large area free in the middle of the room for the table where the 3D map is displayed. If you've got four factions of characters, you might want to give four specifically named areas around the room to represent their strongholds (two prisoner barracks, an officer's mess, and the former Kommandant's quarters (where the MPs have set up). Then have four more areas that can be allocated various identities depending on the needs of the story. Perhaps creating signs for "Sports Field", "Main Gate", "Secret Tunnel", "Watchtower 1", "Garage/Workshop", "Armoury", etc. When a bunch of figures congregate on a single area on the map, send the players to one of the ad hoc areas and have them display the appropriate sign. If other players want to get to that area, they have to move their figures across the board before they can enter the ad hoc area and interact with the other characters present.

    It's a simple logical mechanism, and for most of the game there will be clusters of players at various part of the map. Players will often be forced to choose where they need to move their figures to engage in the storylines most pertinent to their characters.

    Need any more ideas?

    Specific questions answered?

    \n What I'm seeing, both from stuff Michael has said in the past and from my own experiences, is that a game with the same sort of general situation and set of toys, runs very differently depending upon numbers of participants.

    The end result of that is that very different approachesto the same general set up matter, and you end up with a very different sort of play experience, even when the characters and set up are the same.

    Vulpinoid, a couple of quick questions about the larger sorts of events with a good number of players ( say8-20):

    What sort of staff team does the presenter/GMtend to have and of what size? What sorts of duties or roles do the staff team members have? Do folks tend to use a confederate or two, basically staff members meant to play either a key NPC or two, or just generally play multiple bit parts as they come up?

    How do the staffers communicate events to one another? Are there scheduled breaks in play, or is everything kind of just a rolling series of events?

    Does anyone ever jump in and isolate an area on the table set up when some sort of action scene breaks out? Does someone stay tableside and monitor general character toy movement when players aren't in some of those designated key locations where more face2face LARPish freeformingcharacter interaction is going on?

    Also, on an odd note, do these sorts of things always end in a big Showdown? I mean, the tendency and ingredients for that result are all there for exactly that, not just in terms of situation, but also in terms of the one-off nature ofthese event games and, frankly, the presence of a whole lotof action-oriented toys. Have you ever seen one of these style of events that didn't necessarily end in an enormous bash 'em up ( maybe just a little one?)

    \n I'll answer a few of the easy questions first.

    A good rule of thumb for the GM Team is a primary GM and an auxiliary GM per five players. In a set up like I described, you might have the main GM and a sub GM for each of the factions. When a group gathers in that factions allocated HQ, the auxiliary GM for that faction would be the one who handles things.

    In the small games I described as a prelude to the main game, you'd have a traditional GM/player divide. The GM playing the roles of the NPCs and other factions, but once the big game starts, there is no real need for NPCs. There are enough players around that everyone you could want to interact with is actually controlled by another player, and each of those players will have their own motivations to assist or hinder you.

    The main GM might stand at the table (or the might delegate a "character movement" auxiliary GM), in most cases the movement across the table would be monitored by other players also moving their figures across the table. All it takes is one player to scream "CHEAT!!" in a situation like this and the other moving players will instantly look into the situation. You could even institute a benefit to players who do pick up on one another's discrepancies in this way. In a war camp situation, this might help to ramp up the tension.

    As for some of the trickier questions...NO BREAKS IN PLAY!!! Run it real time.

    The aim of a game like this is to create a dynamic ecosystem that self-administers. In general, the GM team isn't there to provide story or entertainment, they are present to help facilitate rulings when grey areas arise.

    For communication between GMs, I have a set of six two-way radio headset to communicate messages quickly across the team. But you could just as easily use a whiteboard only accessible to the GMs; this might have a map indicating any hidden traps, buried things or anything else that isn't immediately apparent to the players (if an obviously visible change is made, this should be done to the open map for everyone to see). The whiteboard might also display timeline events defined before the game...eg. At the 2hr mark the allies will drop food at a random part of the camp, between the 1hr and 1hr30 mark, the stars will be right to summon a particular loa spirit....or may define things established by the players during the course of the game...eg. There will be a secret meeting between freemasons among the prisoners and guards at the 2hr45 mark.

    These things don't have to end up as a big showdown, but there is an inherent tendency to move that way for exactly the reasons you've just described (one off event, lots of characters with agendas pointed at one another, action toys, a charged situation, etc.) If you write a few characters with specific motivations to prevent things from building up, or prevent showdowns from occurring (it could be great to have a single character in each faction written with an agenda like this, and make this an agenda for an entire faction such as the guards who have the firepower to enforce their agenda).

    To prevent unwanted characters getting involved in a scene, characters can be assigned as door guards; and the relevant player literally stand as a barricade at the threshold to the partitioned area. This kind of thing should be explained to the players at the start of the game.
