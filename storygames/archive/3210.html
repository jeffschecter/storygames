\n

So, like, I own some games that are written. What I mean by that is that, when reading, I get the impression that the author takes the act of writing as a form of art. Dictionary of Mu is like this. Polaris is too. Nobilis, to move out of the circle of people I call friend. There are others, too; these three are just the most distinctly stylised that I have, so they stick out in my memory. These games make me happy. I enjoy reading them because of the sheer beauty of their language, and the sense that the author has written them with attention and joy.

\n

There are some games that are designed but not written and I enjoy playing these, right, but I'm disinterested in reading them, so I end up not playing them. That makes me sad.

\n

    \n Well, once a game text has delivered the game to me I don't expect to spend a lot of time re-reading it. In fact the most extensive use of the game in a world where we play the games is to reference rules during play. To that end a large number of games actively get in the way of being useful rule-books by being literary successes. Obviously (I think) if the writing is so that the game can't be discerned or if it's just such a burden to try to decode that it's not worth it, then it fails in both categories. But I think that a game that succeeds as a great read and fails as a game aid pretty much fails, period.

    To that end, good organization first. I like some good game-themed fiction but it can't be a hindrance to finding a rule while I'm playing because the one thing I'm not going to be doing is reading the book to the players.

    \n Interestingly, I can't think of a game that is well-written in a technical sense but not in an arty sense. That is, I can't think of a game that functions well as a rules manual that isn't, also, written artistically, in some sense.

    If I think about clearly written books, I think about My Life With Master (which has a gothic, literary voice to the text); the Shab al-Hiri Roach (dry and ironic); Dogs In The Vineyard (conversational and occasionally beautiful). All of those are artistically written, in some sense: the text would lose something if it was reduced to a numbered instruction list.

    I'm probably just echoing Shreyas' original point here.
