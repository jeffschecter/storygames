\n Often we seem to crave the epic. Brave last stands against impossible odds. Love that transcends time and space. Star-destroying space battles with the fate of billions on the line. Prophecies fulfilled and thwarted, showdowns against hated rivals (and fathers), armies clashing like tide against cliff. Sometimes games deliver it and sometimes they don't.

What's the appeal of the epic?

When have you achieved it, in what way was it satisfying, and how did you make it happen? Was it the capstone to a legendary campaign or the product of a memorable one-shot?

    \n I think the epic guarantees big emotional pay-off. "Dude, we got the band back together" is all well and good, but "Dude, our music killed Godzilla" resonates at a whole different level. That said, it's to a certain extent lazy always going for the big pay-off, much in the same way as combat is the lazy kind of conflict. Stories that are more satisfying (than the Hollywood blockbuster such as Independence Day) often look at the tiny victories and defeats, the ones which are more scaled to the person and, for that reason, are more touching.

    \n The problem with the epic resolution is that you get drawn into the Golden of SF trap. So the enemy has a new ray gun which fires mesons, so you start slinging comets at them and before you know it, nothing less than colliding galaxies will do as a weapons system. You have to get bigger each time because epic is always more than you currently have. This is fine for one shots, and is the typical conclusion of a Call of Cthulhu scenario, but for campaign play you need to build more slowly through smaller and more personal victories, having only one or two epic events per arc. Unless you really do like gonzo.

    \n You know what's epic?

    Mist-Robed Gate

    This is the game that encourages you to sacrifice your life just so you can share that tearful goodbye with your Beloved against a mountain backdrop swirling in the eternal winds.

    One great example was from Origins 2008, when I ran the Temple of the Crimson Flower scenario with 7 players. As the fighting tournament on the isle unfolded, families fractured, battles ensued over volcanic fields, and capes fluttered. The most epic moment \u2013 the big emotional payoff \u2013 was actually at the end, when only the hapless Gin Seng Tea remained alive, having otherwise watched literally every death in the game happen before his character's eyes. Emotionally battered after his victory over the easily vanquished tournament master, he then fell to his knees and wept on the iconic temple precipice for his fallen comrades and enemies.

    What's the appeal? A convergence of affective threads that, while overdetermined, still has some unexpected element within it. A seed of something both human and eternal contained.

    How did we make it happen? Having a group of players all on the same page about the melodrama, but also circumstantial choices made (i.e., including Gin Seng Tea in every scene anyone died)

    It was also epic when I rolled two natural 10s in order to jump my nomad's motorbike off a burning hover-tank while I playtested Mike Pondsmith's Cyberpunk 3.0 with Mike Pondsmith, but that was also too circumstantial to be repeatable.

    \n Epic. With an epic, your choices have extraordinary resonance; they can change the nature of the entire setting; they invoke the awful majesty of macroscopic events.

    Last year, in the AD&D game I've been running on and off for more than 30 years, we played out the climax of a four / five year multi-weekend fest. It involved a vast invasion force preparing to invade the homeland of the players (not the first time this has happened) but also a ritual which ended with a vast tsunami heading towards their lands. The players' actions decided the end result, and it went down to a last second decision by a single player. If they had failed (and there was a good chance of it), the entire coast and most of the lands cities, all of which they had played in for many, many years, would have been destroyed. Their choices, their successes had consequences. Sure, in a one-off game, you get a Transformers-style buzz out of massive destructions and huge stakes, but, as with lower level drama, it's better if the players are emotionally attached to the outcome.

    They diverted the waters so they slaughtered the vast army which faced them. This year - aquatic zombie invasion. Or some necromantic energy, at least.

    Heroes need challenges worthy of them. Superheroes need epic challenges.

    \n For one of my gaming groups, the Sentinels of Meregoth, the following is true...

    The appeal of the epic: heightened stakes. Epic stakes are vast, global, galactic, wide-screen. And the higher the tension, the worse the odds, the more threatening the opposition or obstacle, the more epic the resolution becomes.

    Also, sometimes things are epic because of random chance: it just so happened in this particular way, and no one had any control over it, but it turned out just the right way, and it was glorious. Rolling 3 consecutive natural 20s in a game of D&D, for instance.

    Here's another example: in a miniatures-based superhero action game, in an eight-foot-long papercraft city that I'd built, Green Lantern and the Hulk and all those dudes were squaring off against a horrific enemy (Darkseid and Thanos had merged into a single supervillain named OverCross, and he'd deployed all these bad guys to pick off the heroes one by one). After sustaining some casualties, the superheroes were able to defeat the army of evil and smite them an all that. It was close -- the heroes were in a bad way, but they were fighting on against the last couple bad guys.

    The Hulk jumped atop a building and glared down at Solomon Grundy. The player said that Hulk was going to rip off part of the building's roof and hurl it down at his enemy. I told him that the papercraft building had a detachable roof, so for the sake of verisimilitude, he could remove it and place it near the Solomon Grundy miniature. The building looked like this:



    And when the player removed the top of the building, everyone then saw that out of all the buildings on this map, this was the one where I had elected to hide a miniature for an ambush. It was the Red Skull, who was clutching the Cosmic Cube (a very powerful weapon), and he was going to use it to summon a bunch of demons from hell.



    Quoth the Red Skull, who had a perfect line of sight on Captain America: "Und we meet again, Herr Rogers. This time, for the last time."



    And the players all screamed that this was, indeed, epic.

    \n I'd resume it to "GM & players make the unexpected happen". From the players side it starts with an out-of-the-box plan or a desperate move that either by sheer luck or good judgement ends up working against the odds and sends all the plot into a different, unexpected direction. From the GM side it can be an inspired unexpected scene either prepared and masterfully railroaded or improvised from the choices players have taken in the session. Also a particularly inspired character interpretation from a player or the GM or better yet, a dialogue between them can also make for epic moments.

    For example, I still remember that session when our three 1st level characters killed a CR5 troll by tracking it to its lair, sneaking upon him when he was asleep and beheading it. The GM wasn't expecting it at all and we all were amazed that it actually worked, since we made it by sheer luck. The GM consulted his friends and finally gave us the XP at the next session. After that in another session, we disabled an explosive trap in a dungeon (one designed to completely destroy the place) and transported a couple of barrels of alchemist fire to blow a pass in the mountains and block the advance of an orc army. Again, we barely made it and the GM only allowed it to see how far we get, since it wasn't supposed to happen at all.

    \n So we finished a six-month or so campaign last week, with an absolutely epic battel for the survival of the world and the fate of humankind. This campaign had started off as very low-key but got sorta epic pretty quickly in a prophecy kind of way. That didn't really feel very epic. the stakes were high, but we didn't know and feel it during the conflict. In the end, all vampires in the world except for a handful perished, which should have felt epic, but it didn't really. Then, the campaign started to get low-key again and built up to a truly epic finale. This was different. All the players were cooperating to make it happen. I think what really made this epic was the buildup. It was really only a single session of buildup, but we interspersed our regular scenes with Signs of What Was Coming. Little scenes of what the bad guy was doing (unearthing the seal that would unite the five vampiric souls and free the Devil himself). We'd have scenes with characters trying to warn everyone and rally the forces who were in the middle of a bloody struggle for dominance (instigated by the big bad guy). Even the scenes which didn't really have much to do with the big climax, the scenes that were just tying up loose ends from the rest of the campaign, acquired a whole new meaning because we knew what was about to happen. A PC even died in one of those scenes, further securing in the mind of everyone that This Was It. And then we let loose the apocalyptic imagery. Clouds of fire and sulphur. Darkened skies. Armies of hell hounds. Winged building-sized daemon.

    And we all felt this was what we had been building up to. This was what we had seen coming. This was the big finale. This was where it all came to an end, one way or another. And we all cooperated to make that happen. The fight was very epic in many ways. The arcs of the characters came to their points. My character finally stood for free, fighting for nobody but himself. Kim's character had to fight off the evil influence that was taking over his mind. Either he did it now, or he would forever be a slave to it. Daniel's character finally found the greater truth she had been looking for, although it was a much darker truth than she had expected. She turned and united with the Devil. And Erik's character was finally free from the organization that had been controlling him. He was finally the hero that he had dreamt of being when he signed up. And he was facing the biggest villain there was.

    I think this was the combination that made it feel epic to us: the coming together of the characters' story arcs, the knowledge in the play group that this was the end, and the buildup during the session, constantly reminding us that the end was near.

    Epic imagery isn't hard to do. Hell's armies, fate of galaxies, fulfilled prophesies and all that. Easy peasy pizza cheesy. But in order for it to feel epic, you need buildup. Lots of buildup and foreshadowing, and then the knowledge in the group that this is it. This is what it all comes down to. Everything hinges on this one battle. This sort of buildup is probably easier to do in a campaign, but I think you can do it in a one-shot. It's more difficult, but with characters the players care deeply about, issues that develop together (separate issues take more time to develop), aggressive scene framing and clever foreshadowing, you could probably do it. It'll be harder, though.

    \n I really dislike what the word "epic" tends to mean in a gaming context. For a lot of people, it means crazy, over-the-top, riding-laser-sharks-to-storm-the-gates-of-valhalla zaniness. I designed a a game that's supposed to capture the Epic Fantasy literary genre (e.g. The Lord of the Rings), but I think when most gamers hear the words "epic fantasy" they mentally translate it to "D&D at level 20+". For me, the epic in Epic Fantasy is about connecting a story about individual decisions and personal virtue to a worldwide scope as way of underscoring the point that those things matter.

    \n Anyway, I would say the appeal of the epic is similar to the effect of the sublime (as understood by outdated aesthetic theories). The epic is vast in scope, it's not "accessible", it's larger than what we can immediately perceive. It transcends the measurable in some way. The army extends beyond our sight. The story spans beyond our lifetime. The hero's strength or courage is beyond what we could muster.

    This makes it "larger than life", larger than our individual experiences. In this it is awe-inspiring (both awesome and inspiring) and it transports us to some other place, some other time, removes us from the here and now, abducts us from the present and takes us into the fiction.

    \n [In this post epic = over the top kickass awesomesauce]

    In the first version of my sword & sorcery game each session usually started off kind of sensible but almost always things would ramp up to whole different scale. In the second playtest the Nameless woman (whose name had been stolen by the soul eating demon Sitaxis) and Durbak, a warrior shaman from the northern wolftribes, stole back the sun from the gods. WTF?

    Another time a lowly thief namned something like Janne Padda ("Joe Toad" might be decent English equvalent) went on to become an avatar of the Night Goddess in the final act. Of course he unlocked (unleashed?) his true form by stabbing his dagger might of darkness into his forehead. WTF?

    In those day the game had very little boundries as to what a successful roll ment. Your task as player (or GM) was just to discribe how things got better or worse depending on the roll. I had a visible pacing mechanism as well, where I'd put a number of glass beads forward to represent the dangers at hand. A successful roll removed one bead.

    In those days the game was supposed to be played in five chapters. In the first chapter the GM had two beads, and each subsequent chapter another two beads would be added (2,4, 6, 8, 10).

    Things I think contributed to the ramp up in epicness:
    \u2022 Instead of repeating themselves people'd start describing more and more crazy actions, giving magic items new abilites, etc.
    \u2022 The act structure suggested some sort of curve up to a climax
    \u2022 Narrating in third person was encouraged, first person dialogue was kept to a minimum. The game was explicitly about doing cool stuff.
    \u2022 Before you rolled you said what stuff you were using ("I'm using Might, Undefeated champion of the Emerald Queen, my Harp of the Ancients, and my enchanted sword") based on some vague idea of what your character was going to do, and then you'd just have to get all the stuff in your description.
    \u2022 In 2007 few of the people I played with had ever played a game where they not only could but SHOULD narrated how fucking cool they were everytime they did something.

    Sometimes, as in the examples above, it got crazy epic and was cool. Other times everyone'd get creative fatigue around act four or we'd have an early climax and call it quits since we realized we just could top what just happened.

    \n I ran a fantasy RPG campaign for about two years. There were many great battles and momentous events (murders, resurrections, betrayals), but in the final session, the following went down:

    1. The Templar discovered that a masked NPC was actually his long-sought archenemy. When the mask came off, and the identity was revealed, there was much astonishment. Two years of searching, and the villain was under their noses the whole time.

    2. The Sorcerer sided with the Templar's enemy. Their goals were the same, as were their methods. Furious with this betrayal, the Drow Templar swore eternal hatred.

    3. The Warrior decided that, rather than rescue an innocent from demons, he would resolve the problem by letting the demons drag the innocent to Hell, then seal the portal between worlds, preventing any further harm. The innocent was also the son of a powerful ruler -- one who had hired them to protect his Realm (which, they pointed out, they had).

    4. The Necromancer shattered a dangerous artifact, but in doing so, she inadvertently erased the Warrior from reality -- the Warrior didn't just vanish, but all memory and record of his existence was gone as well.

    5. A bunch of other brutal stuff happened.

    The group dubbed this an epic bloodletting, as there was no returning from it. Two of the five heroes hated each other, and another one was gone forever, as though he'd never been born.

    We really couldn't figure out a way to continue playing, in no small part because of the emotional intensity of the events. Sure, we could have come up with a new character, or found a way for the Templar and Sorcerer to reconcile. But topping that frenzy of carnage and double-crosses? No way. It was too... epic. Too emotional, too wide-screen (thousands died, an empire burned, a war was lost), too perfect a resolution to a two-year quest for heroism (which, needless to say, was not achieved).

    \n I had several epic moments in the same session while playing Becoming Heroes and although the GM had the right kind of epic rhetoric I feel that mechanics were very relevant.

    The system to solve conflicts is deceitfully simple: you add in traits, and for every trait you get a die to roll.. except that you don't declare all traits at the same time and this makes a huge difference.

    You will start out with the most obvious traits (I use my 'sword' trait, my 'knight' trait etc..) but if your antagonist has roughly the same number of traits you are soon forced to work out creative uses to justify your skills and you keep swinging between desperation and awesomeness as dice are matched by your antagonist.

    For example, an attempt at infiltrating an enemy starship systems ended up with my character taking combat drugs and going on the outside hull of the ship under battle fire so that he could access the manual override controls.. and that was all to justify playing a "neural boosters" and "war hero" traits. It would have never come up otherwise and it was a truly epic moment!

    \n (I'm specifically talking about GMless games, because creating epic with a GM is "too easy")

    We've played many epic Shock and Polaris one-shots. Not every single game, but a pretty high hit rate. The combination of high world stakes plus high personal stakes (and no escape from either) seems to hit that spot. Also the stories are separated but big world consequences of each domino into the other stories (you overthrew the government in your last scene, so my next scene is in the middle of a riot).

    You can definitely play "detached, 5000 foot view" Microscope games, but for a one-shot I find they're a lot more epic and engaging if you follow this recipe:

    - straight-forward history concept (it's about the characters and what happens, not a bizarre setting)
    - tight Focuses, like a specific person or event
    - play lots of scenes & ask personal incriminating questions about the characters (why did Corporal Barnes abandon his friend in enemy territory?)

    Same as with Polaris & Shock: you've already got the big world drama, now you're seeing how the personal drama meshes into it. That's the epic recipe to me. q.v. Once and Future Zombie and Dinosaur Hruck. The latter was both epic and moving, which is more than I expect from a game about dinosaurs.

    Posted By: Evan TornerYou know what's epic?

    Mist-Robed Gate

    This is the game that encourages you to sacrifice your life just so you can share that tearful goodbye with your Beloved against a mountain backdrop swirling in the eternal winds.

    One great example was from Origins 2008, when I ran theTemple of the Crimson Flowerscenario with 7 players. As the fighting tournament on the isle unfolded, families fractured, battles ensued over volcanic fields, and capes fluttered. The most epic moment \u2013 the big emotional payoff \u2013 was actually at the end, when only the hapless Gin Seng Tea remained alive, having otherwise watched literallyeverydeath in the game happen before his character's eyes. Emotionally battered after his victory over the easily vanquished tournament master, he then fell to his knees and wept on the iconic temple precipice for his fallen comrades and enemies.

    What's the appeal? A convergence of affective threads that, while overdetermined, still has some unexpected element within it. A seed of something both human and eternal contained.

    How did we make it happen? Having a group of players all on the same page about the melodrama, but also circumstantial choices made (i.e., including Gin Seng Tea in every scene anyone died)
    \nNot coincidentally, EPIC is your shattered cyborg solider walking into the heart of a volcano with the body of his slain beloved, recently killed by the would-be heir to her criminal empire, who proved to be their son, whom he subsequently had beaten to death with his broken mechanical arm in grief.

    I have found my most 'epic' play experiences to have come out of those sorts of situations, where you set up something where you're attached to the feel and atmosphere you're aiming for, but not (and I think this is critically important, in my experience) to the PCs themselves. It really helps that games like Mist Robed Gate have mechanical cues to encourage a particular sort of narrative, one that pushes the players towards poetic language and overt expression of their internal narratives.

    Every time I've been in a long-term game that actively tried for an EPIC climax or, like, super-encounter, it's fallen somehow flat, typically because the GM was too intent on managing the outcome, and the players were too invested in the fate of their beloved PCs. We have had some 'EPIC' adventures, but they've generally been just because everything clicked that night, or what I thought was just a clever encounter turned out to be just what everyone was looking for that game. It sure helps to have trust, but not so much that the players lay back and expect the GM to do all the heavy lifting.

    (In addition to Mist-Robed Gate, the couple games of Swords Without Master I have played really push the game this way...)

    \n I think I say, "Dude, that was epic!" in my gaming to celebrate when the game situation makes a big, powerful action* uniquely possible or uniquely effective.

    If you have a "smash planets" power that you can use at will, there's nothing epic about that.

    If you have a "smash things" power, and you luck or win your way into the Citadel of "planets count as things now", and you smash a planet, then that's epic.

    Also, rolling 100 on some percentile table that has mostly moderate results but one crazy one is "epic".

    *I think "big, powerful" is actually more about the color of the action's outcome than its attempt. Trying to smash a planet is not epic. Pressing a button and narrating how the world goes through various dramatic phases of upheaval and destruction is epic.

    So what's the appeal? Synergy, a special moment in play, the happily unexpected, bombastic narration, vivid visualization, power-tripping, often with a side of comedic exaggeration. Feeling awesome for a gameplay choice while your character feels awesome for its execution and you both feel awesome about the fictional outcome. I guess that's kind of a generic answer, but I don't see moments of Epic as being that different from other celebrated moments of play.

    As for one-shots: My experience is that I do have epic moments in one shots, but very rarely. Any game which made the odds of a certain outcome likely in a single session probably wouldn't give me an "epic" feeling when that outcome occurred. A one-shot game that promises "epic" play every time would probably fail for me.

Tonks and Brancino have reset their bond with each other twice, and now sit at -3 apiece as they are at loggerheads over an important story generated goal of a Dragonling clutch and its human 'Mother'.
