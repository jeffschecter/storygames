\n\nDanielSolis \n\n\n

\n I've got this massive word count on a current draft for Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple. I intentionally over-wrote because I knew I'd be cutting out a lot with the help of my editor. I'm just curious, as a straw poll, how long you think is too long for an RPG book, in terms of page length.

If you need to, you can break it down by audience. For example, the page count Happy Birthday, Robot! is modeled on a long-ish children's book like Polar Express because the target age group for the game is 10 and up. I imagine a longer page count would be reasonable for an older audience, but maybe not. What do you think?

    \n It's really hard to talk about this in the abstract. In my mind, the shorter the better... as long as you can cover everything you need to.

    Generally, I think most RPGs don't need to be more than 30 pages, though I make some exceptions for books like Mouse Guard and Apocalypse World which are good all the way through, despite being 100+ pages.

    So... if it's good all the way through, it can be as long as you like. However, if you've got a game that's more than 20 pages -- and really, even if it's shorter than that -- you really need a short summary of procedures somewhere in the book, so when people are trying to play it (and not read it), they can just go down the list and do things.

    \n This is like asking "How long should a book be?" Would The Cat in the Hat be better if it were novel length? Does Moby Dick need trimmed until it is only 25 pages long? There isn't one right answer: Every RPG needs its own answer. Some RPGs wouldn't function without multi-hundred page counts, and others would be unbearably full of padding and unnecessary material at 50 pages.

    Something like Happy Birthday Robot sounds like it should be on the shorter end of the scale, but I can't say for sure just how short that would mean.

    \n As many pages as are required to tell the players how to play the game quickly, concisely, and accurately. This may, or may not, involve providing setting material as well as mechanics.

    To a large degree, I think the decision needs to be motivated by one other question: is this a "one book and out" project? If so, then this may lead you to provide more material in the game book, because your intention is not to produce more material after the fact.

    If your intention is to provide a series, limited or not so limited, then I think your first book needs to hook the players and provide them with what they need to know to play the game for one to four months (wether serially or "at least three or four times" if separately), and as such can afford to provide the players with a solid starting point that's intended, out of the box, to be expanded later on (either mechanically, or thematically).

    I look at the fourth edition Players Handbook and I wonder, "what could I possibly cut out of this book without breaking the group's play experience", and I must come to the conclusion, "hardly anything at all". I look at the fourth edition of the HERO System Rules, and I ask the same question, and, actually, come to pretty much the same answer. But both these games depend heavily upon achieving freshness and variety out of specific, rules-bound, modular, mechanical description and so, inevitably, these games must have a certain degree of 'catalog' to them, and to provide richness, catalogs cannot afford to be too skimpy.

    At the opposite end of the spectrum, the exemplars that immediately rise to mind are written by Vincent Baker: both Dogs In The Vineyard and, even more so, In A Wicked Age, provide all that is required for a careful, thoughtful group of players to get engaged and play happily for a medium-term length of time, and they do so quickly, concisely, and accurately. In both cases, the examples illuminate, not describe. In both cases, the answers are in the text (almost, oddly, in the same way that wargame rules are: the answers are in the text), and where they don't, you know what's expected: it's either not mechanically relevant, or it's not a mechanical question.

    If I had to pick one game out of my entire collection that I thought was concise, focussed, thematically rich, and elegantly self-contained, I think "In A Wicked Age" would probably win, hands down. But, by the same token, I think "In A Wicked Age" demands a careful and experienced play group, or at least a group moderated by a patient, careful, and experienced person who can lead others in the right directions when questions arise.

    I see your game as not necessarily trying to address this precisely identical audience -- as a result, I think your game could definitely benefit from more richness in example and setting detail. But not too much more: the basic premise can, I think, be very elegantly packaged up.

    \n I am more and more convinced that RPGs are too long. As the guy who designed Damnation City for Vampire, I think I'm qualified to say. That book could've been a couple of books, or a few interlocked PDFs, and all would've been well. Ultimately, though, it depends on what you're selling. There's not a whole lot of fat in D&D books these days, for example, yet they continue to be good-sized books.

    A lot of games don't require big books, even though they get them. For every World of Darkness core book, there's a Lady Blackbird RPG to be had, I think. They each have their place.

    For my money, I like shorter, punchier PDFs and quick-reading RPG books like Fiasco and 3:16 at the same time that I like a heftier tome like Trail of Cthulhu. Knowing how long to make your book (and how many books or PDFs it actually is) is part of the creative judgment call\u2014it's part of the art.

    \n Something I did with Fiasco: Atomize the rules and use repetition. So each section starts with a bullet list. Then each bullet point gets an explanatory paragraph or two. Then examples, and those bullet points combined in action. Then advice and a replay, then all the bullet lists combined into a cheat sheet, and an index. Depending on your familiarity, you can dive in or skim as needed.

    This ends up being a little longer than strictly necessary, but not by much, and I believe it really aids usability and comprehension. Ralph Mazza basically told me to do this because he is a wise, ancient Gnome.

    \n You know, I'm starting to think my answer is bordering on the vapid, and misses the point of the question, which isn't How Long Should An RPG Be?, but rather, How Long Is Too Long? For me, an RPG is too long when I start jumping ahead in the text to see how the pieces fit together. It's not necessarily about book length, but about verbosity in the individual sections. Rules should explain themselves and then either make with clarifying examples or get on with the next mechanism.

    More and more, of late, I want to get an RPG absorbed as quick as possible, up front, and then explore the permutations and the depth... in that order. I don't want to explore a languid explanation. I want the gist, somewhere, and then I want material that I can sink into once I have the core architecture of the game in mind\u2014and that includes the thrust of the setting.

    But I still stand by my original statement that finding that length is a creative challenge. Too long has as much to do with the style and importance of information as it does with quantity. One writer's smattering of information on an evocative and provocative game world is another's trudge through rambling details. I'll read a lot more about a game world if it's putting me into the trenches than I will if it's a cold overview, but a cold overview can be vital for conveying the breadth of a setting first.

    So some of it is about structuring the information, rather than outright length. Some books are meant to be dipped into, others pored over. Longer books have their place, but I've had something close to my fill of them, of late.

    Is Do going to be digest-sized? That makes a big difference in the page count I'd recommend for it. As an example, I gobble up smaller games lately, but never got around to 7 Skies because I found its size impressive but intimidating for the material. (Not proud of that, but there it is.)

    That's not a much better answer, I know, but you got me thinking about this most of the day, since I've been trying to estimate the length of an upcoming RPG project of my own, at the same time. I'd like the book to feel meaty, but I'd also like it to be quick-to-grok while leaving the reader hungry for more. So that's my bias, lately.

    \nJD has great comments on the development of a particular GM skill.

    Some GM skills *in relation to the game text* are:
    * Learning all the rules, and how they work together.
    * Memorizing exceptions, perhaps even coming up with house rules to manage exceptions.
    * Coming up with a setting/scenario based on the material in the book.

    Another GM skill, the one that JD comments to re the World of Darkness games, is //being able to take a huge, fat RPG book that couples rules and setting*, and Seeing the stuff that is useful to get a game started, Sort it into what is /useful to get a game off the ground/later in the game/not for use in your particular game/, and Implement it in your own game.

    It's a different skill for a different kind of "RPG book build". I don't know many people who actually play WoD (with limited free time) who have read any WoD core book cover to cover, rather than getting what they need to run the game and working from there.

The rules were silly simple, and there over half of each book was dedicated to some kind of cross between fiction and exposition, and I loved it. Never played it, but I think I've read every book in the line.

    Reading this over, I'm not sure how helpful this all is. It feels very subjective.

    \n The question is subjective.

    The alternative to subjectivity is to say this: 239 8.5x11" pages. That's the upper limit of an RPG book's acceptable length and anything longer than that is in error. Also, an RPG should be no shorter than 42 pages. Lady Blackbird is in violation and John Harper has been fined.

    Which is my assholic way of saying that the right length depends on the material and the creative intentions of the designer. Sometimes you want a text in which your reader can get happily lost, as Stephen King says. But sometimes you want rules that seem to inject themselves straight into the head, as if you'd swallowed a pill. There is no formula.

    So subjectivity is fine. Only by comparing our subjective answers against the needs of a specific project can we begin to answer this question. The question, I think, is whether Daniel wants Do to cut like a knife or billow around the reader like a fog. Does he want the book to invite the reader in and hold him rapt from beginning to end, like a novel, or does he want the book to electrify the reader into putting down the book and making notes for play, before she's even finished reading?

    An RPG book is too long when it becomes a slog, and a bad book can be a slog in one page.

    \n I'm going to differ from the other people here. The maximum page count of your book should be determined by the type and quality of its binding and paper. Game books are reference books, so they need to be opened again and again without falling apart.

    As for amount of material. If you can give me 200+ pages of material I'll use when playing, I'm a happy guy, even if I have to pay more for a bigger book. If you give me 100 pages of material and 100 pages of filler or poor editing, I'm not so happy. If you give me 100 pages of material and I feel I need 200 to really get the game bouncing, I'm downright cantankerous. Give me lots of stuff with which to make the game fun. After all, if I'm buying your game, I want your ideas.

    Looking over my shelf, every rpg I've loved playing again and again over the years has a core rulebook in the 200+ page range. Add in sourcebooks and some of them get into the thousands of pages. Reading all that wasn't a slog, it was a joy that made me want to play more. And I did.

    \n I really want to thank everyone here for their thoughtful replies. Please continue the discussion if you find it fruitful as I have.

    For what it's worth, I think I'm going to shoot for something like 64-80 pages That's about double the length of your average heavy box board game from Fantasy Flight Games, which I think is a reasonable comparison for my sort of story-building games. That's also about the length of a manga light novel.

    Just about 2 pages will be devoted to what you might call "non-actionable setting." In other words, the very bare minimum to introduce the central premise of the setting for the game. You might consider flavor fiction "non-actionable setting."

    About 10-20 will be devoted to "actionable setting," or parts of the background that are directly relevant in play. In Do, these are the letters people have written to the pilgrims asking for help and, in so doing, described a bit of their home world.

    No more than 15-20 pages are the core rules, including character creation, storytelling, troublemaking and examples thereof. All formatted in a step-by-step procedure so as to avoid forward-referencing.

    Another 15-20 pages will have advice. Stuff that comes up in play, but is best discussed after you've read the full procedure of play.

    Any remaining pages will be as full and complete an example of play as can fit into the remaining book. Except for...

    1-2 pages that synopsize the whole flow of play, with page refs for further info.

    Seems like a nice reasonable goal for editing this stuff down, I think.

    \n I think it really depends on the game, and the era. Back when I was a teenager, the longer the page count the better - particularly if the cost per page went down with it. I didn't have much money so the more I could buy for my money the better it was, and I enjoyed reading lots. I also enjoyed long and involved character creation, taking several hours most of the time, and detailed rules for everything.

    Now I like something short and quick that I can read in a single sitting of 90 minutes, character creation that comes in under 15 minutes and very few rules to work with - just enough to provide some sort of framework. I also like the books to be smaller - sized like Dragonlance 5th Age, Zorcerer of Zo, Burning Wheel, BESM 2E.

    I'd say with 6"x9" page size, about 200 pages would be tops for me right now. Money is less of a concern, and time is more of a concern. Also, actually being able to play it with people means it should be playable out of the gate within 15-20 minutes, and the complexity should be such that I only have to read the book through once to get it.

    Looking at the age target, I'd say longer is acceptable. At around 12 300-400 pages would have been fine for me, in fact even something of a matter of pride, and I would have loved to read every page meticulously, and still be wanting more. I think if you want parents to be playing it as well, you'd need it to be a lot shorter.
