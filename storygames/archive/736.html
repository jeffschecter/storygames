    Cripes, I've being playing catch-up with this reading group all month - finishing chapters just as the thread for the next one starts up. But this time I've reached synchronicity! And good thing, as I do have a few thoughts about it.

    First off, I would personally not consider a character sheet a Mask. Yes, it offers cover and safety to the actor to be unlike themselves, from a rational level, just as you do with a part and a script in conventional theatre. But it doesn't get close to the physical-affective-cognitive interaction that the use of Masks is described as having. Obscuring the face is cutting off a basic feedback loop - primitive contagion - that regulates interaction in a social space. This is a big deal. I find it fascinating, but reasonable, that this simple step is such an enabler of transformation to such a subsequent degree - the regression, the loss of language, etc.

    So do people roleplay using actual Masks, or analogues that do mask or interfere with natural physiological processes that ground us in normal sets of behaviour?

    Where's the dichotomy? Character is revealed through dramatic action. Stories are made of dramatic action.     Neel, that's true, but I suspect that the 'stories' you produce through standard Maskplay are primal, blunt and undifferentiated, and its very difficult to produce something of the sophistication we expect from fiction, or even obtain using standard gaming techniques.

    I can certainly see the appeal in "man angry, child scared -> child hides ->child finds item -> child angry, man scared"; as is, it clutches at some very fundamental drives. And elaborated upon, using language and context, it could form the template for a powerful and profound story. But by its nature I don't see how Maskplay could provide this elaboration - without the use of techniques designed to promote this. So this is less me disagreeing and more me saying "yes, there is raw story ore in abundance - can we build systems to refine it?"

    People have mentioned how different this chapter is from the ones that precede it, most striking in KJ's change in tone, but also the content and techniques. I think that trance is probably not as far from many of these other techniques, e.g. status play, as it might appear: trance isn't a 'special state' but the end of a continuum of suggested behaviour. Hence it should be possible to dial-Masks-down - inhabit other entitites that do not represent the break that documented experiences do - the "Mask of you but old and weary", say. This would be a less astounding process, from a subjective POV, but might allow the Maskplayer to bring more resources to bear to affect the game-space in way that are interesting.

    Mmm. I get the feeling that we're seeing masks as rather self-indulgent: a raw force that needs to be channelled or coped with. That's not my experience with them.

    I've done maskwork before: as in, I've worn a half-mask and done scenes with other masks. And I've watched scenes played by other masks.

    Story-wise, those scenes are very satisfying. Masks can fall in love or fight or make and break friendships. Sure, it's primal, but they're good stories, without any refining.

    I'm not quite sure about Emily's points. There's definitely an element to which
    Masks need to be observed by an outsider (to make sure they don't hurt themselves or each other).

    But, beyond that, there's really nothing very un-social about masks. They're very childlike and hence very reactive to people around them. The "my guy" issue doesn't really come up. They're not difficult to play scenes with: they're fantastic, in fact, because they react to everything you do.

    Graham

    Graham - Did you experience the "possesion" of the masks the way Johnstone portrays it, or was it something more manageable?
    A bit of both. You're in an altered state, behaving as the mask rather than yourself, but you're aware of what you're doing.

    To give an example: as the mask, I liked a mirror that the instructor was holding and tried to grab it. Eventually, she gave it to me, but I couldn't hold it, and the mirror fell to the floor and shattered. She told me to take off the mask, but I was aware enough that I would have done that anyway.

    So there's certainly an element of being "possessed" (or call it what you like) but it didn't mean I was unaware.

    Re immersion/channeling/self-hypnosis: I've recently done a session of trance-induced shared dreaming (the second in a row; the first one's here). We do this with eyes closed; so, even if we're not wearing masks, we're cut off from visual communication.

    In this one, the goal was to meet and/or become a mythical creature. I chose the minotaur.

    While in the (light) trance state, I was able to get very into the head of the creature, feeling the dark, mute, confused anger of the beast. However, in order to speak, I had to come out of it a bit. It wasn't possible to be both a social participant in the "game" and be completely immersed - the minotaur couldn't speak, after all. I was aware of all this, and made a rational, social choice to speak rather than to go deeper in.
