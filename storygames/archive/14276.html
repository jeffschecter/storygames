Scott Weidemeier spends his time in exactly three ways: working a menial job at a local donut shop, caring for his abusive grandmother, and running The Greatest Dungeons & Dragons Game of All Time. Though overbearing and short-tempered, Scott is a hero to his fellow players--that is, until neo-nerd hipster Miles Butler joins the game, fueling Scott's rampant insecurity and alienating him from his own players. Can Scott overcome his contempt for the mainstreaming of nerdery, or will this clash of the subcultures come to a head?
\nZero Charisma

I am curious about this movie - it looks cute and fun and awesome and in a weird way that I don't see a lot, it makes me feel for both the protagonist and the antagonist at the same time. I'm definitely old-school about my nerdiness (even though I wasn't born when D&D first came out or anything) but I also definitely feel like the "hipster douchebag neo-nerd" that's the main antagonist of the film.

It brings up something I'm really interested in - internal identification and the divisiveness of "nerd" subculture. Anyone have any thoughts?

    \n I think what it comes down to it interpersonal skills and less subcultural stuff. Subcultures are essentially about style, aesthetics and serious leisure. Those are what we talk about most in the sociological literature.

    One often becomes a member in due some kind of exclusion or trauma in childhood or adolescence (if you can forgive the pop psycho-analysis) because they offer acceptance, community and a communal leisure activity. We usually call this 'serious leisure' to indicate that it's not a causal activity but rather a community-based affinity group. The differences between BDSM kinksters, train-hobbyists and Table-Top RPGers is in content and not structure.

    I think where the tension comes from is the perceived differences in social capital and status, and the contest thereof. Robert hits the nail on the head, it's about tolerating shitty social behaviour rather than any kind of actual threat.

    I've notice that in both goth/punk scenes and the RPG nerd scene there is this tolerance of poor social skill and general assholery. I think this stems from the common case that member in this community were or are excluded and stigmatized and don't want to do that to others. The problem becomes this promotion and tolerance of absolutely shameful and loutish behaviour, which any calling out of is decried as oppressive or exclusionary. That outcry is utter bullshit.

    Here's to being friendly and playing games with your friends, hipster, geek or otherwise.

      \n I've banged my head against this topic for a long time. I've complicated relationships out of insecurity and distrust in the past. I regret it.

      There's some part of me that will always be angry when i see someone beautiful or popular wearing a Thundercats Tshirt. There will always be a part of me that wants to shout "Get the fuck out of my subculture and leave the fake-faded Green Lantern patch when you go." I recognize that a lot of this stems from a life in a smallish midwestern city, where i could literally see the people around me go from being bullies that mocked me for liking Star Wars to the hippy-dressed MTV generation with a 30minute-television-special based knowledge of Lord of the Rings. There's part of me that wants them to have to earn this thing i like the way i had to earn it. I get why people want to exclude girls, hipsters, or anyone who just chose to join the role-playing culture.

      But i don't like that part of me.

      I don't really have an answer, but i want to share what i've been trying to do, to cope with my own crap. I'm trying to deal with the individual. Before i sink in honest self-realization or jealousy, having to admit to myself that i'd love to be skinny or gorgeous or fit in with the crowds at a Wilco concert, and before i harden with bitterness, wanting to replay all the times i watched family or classmates roll their eyes at all the weird crap i was saying about elves and blasters and comic books, i try to focus on who i am and who i'm talking to. Especially if it's a stranger to me or a stranger to my subculture.

      I'm trying to allow for genuine reactions. Because, yes, there is something so beautiful and real about a person who wore a Three Wolf Moon shirt before it was a hit joke. I adore those people. Those blessed with a zero charisma are a bit lucky, because they're often being exactly who they are, even when it gets them ostracized or ridiculed. And i am a bit upset when cool people ask me questions about Tolkien or Lewis as if it were suddenly valid because there's a movie coming out soon.

      But everyone has a genuine reaction. Everyone has a passion. And i don't feel like i can afford to distrust someone just because i'm afraid of their image. I have to compare their history to mine, i have to get to know them as an individual. I have to celebrate when they come to my gaming table through D&D or PBR. I'm doing my best to interact with people based on personal encounter. Yeah, seeing a hipster wear a G.I.Joe shirt ironically makes me wish i could wear an Arcade Fire shirt ironically, but i don't want to be that person. I'd rather not get offended, and wonder instead what that persons actual interests are. Because we all have things that make us excited and make us babble on. Everyone has a genuine reaction, even if that reaction is to learn as much as they can about a stereotype to help them be embraced by that culture. Even if that's out of uncertainty.

      I've met people that are out-right Hipsters, and they've made my life wonderful, even my gaming life. I've met people that are the incarnation of stereotype, and i've found that i don't need to foster their social normalcy or help them grow in any way. People are people, one by one, and that's what i have to keep telling myself. It doesn't matter if the person i'm talking to is wearing skinny jeans or yoga pants or a replica Indiana Jones hat and a fake pewter GL ring. What matters is the passions between us, new or old, strange or familiar, hard-earned or simply interested.
