\n Hello there!

The time has come for me to share my current project with you here at the forum. This is my second game (my first in English) and I am very anxious to hear what you think of it at this early stage.



The pitch
\xbb There is no such thing as demons \u2013 demons don\u2019t exist \xab
This is a GM-less roleplaying game about desperate people who have recently crossed an invisible line into the demonic realm. They only know this from the feeling that they now carry within them: A feeling of demonic presence. It grows in the vicinity of conflict as if the demons thrive there, feeding from the hostile energies at the same time as they fuel those conflicts with violence, sorcery, and corruption.

This game is influenced by Hillfolk (Robin D. Laws), Trollbabe (Ron Edwards), Don\u2019t Rest Your Head (Fred Hicks), Nemesis (Dennis Detwiller & Greg Stolze) and Apocalypse World (D. Vincent Baker).

The play-test documents
The webpage annodaemonum.com is up and running, but doesn't really contain much more than the play-test documents at this point.

The rules text is basically 7 pages, written in a similar way to how you would program computer code (to make it things unmistakably clear and concise). You should be able to start reading the rules from the top, and be sure not to miss anything.

The game is to be played by 2-4 players over several sessions. The alpha version is distributed as a print-and-play game with 120 cards that needs to be printed. You will also need 20 tokens and a bunch of six-sided dice.



These are the downloads:
* Design notes
* Game rules
* Character sheet
* Game sheets
* Cards

What kind of feedback do I want?
At this point I'm mostly interested in hearing how well the rules text is understood, but perhaps the most valuable feedback would be that from gameplay - so if you would like to try it out and tell me about your experiences I would be ever so grateful!

Apart from commenting here you are welcome to join the Facebook group and Facebook page.

Thanks in advance! Really looking forward to reading your comments!

      \n So, I played a session of AD at GothCon in Sweden this easter. We were 4 players who never had played together before, but we leveled pretty quickly and there was a lot of good feedback afterwards. The session took 2,5-3 hours, I think. There were many moments where the cards created unexpected events and inspired the fiction in interesting ways. We didn't really have time to follow up on all the story-threads that we created during the startup, but I guess each player chose to follow up on the threads they found most interesting/urgent.

      The story
      The story was about 4 characters in modern day Paris:
      * Henri who desired friendship but couldn't get it as he was sort of an outcast (with a shallow relationship with his girlfriend Claudette). He first felt the demonic presence as he persuaded Pierre (who was a part of Henri's soccer team) to get together outside of practise.
      * Claudette who desired independence but couldn't get it as she was too dependent on her boyfriend Henri. She first felt the demonic presence when her father Jean got physical with her when she tried to quit her job (at his bookstore).
      * Monique who desired acknowledgement but couldn't get it as she stood in the shadow of her co-worker Jacques. She first felt the demonic presence when Marie (her boss) threatened her, after Monique had tried to convince Marie to let her go to an important conference.
      * Jacques who desired order and justice but couldn't get it as his co-worker Charles was creating chaos with their research material. He first felt the demonic presence as he confronted Charles about the research material - he stared him down and sent a frightening chill down Charles back.

      Scene 1: Claudette comes to Henri's apartment as he is getting ready to go out with Pierre. She is upset and wants him to stay with her and comfort her. She fails that conflict and darkness envelops her as Henri leaves her in the apartment alone.
      Scene 2: Monique searches for Claudette and finds her in Henri's apartment. She tries to get Claudette to leave the apartment (as she can see that Claudette is in a horrible state), but she is scared by the dark mess of a figure lying on the couch, and fails to influence Claudette.
      Scene 3: Henri is at the pub with Pierre. He tries to convince Pierre to go to a horse race track together, but Henri gets rejected.
      Scene 4: Jacques is in Marie's office with her and Charles. They talk about the situation with the research material, but Jacques' petition to have Charles kicked off the team is rejected. After Charles has left the room Jacques tries to influence Marie to give him priority to the research. He fails that conflict and the demons fuel it with blood and disease. Marie gets a sudden nose bleed and falls unconscious to the floor.
      Scene 5: Claudette is at the bookstore the following day. She has been working all night (her father's orders) and is a complete wreck unfit to handle the customers. Jean takes over and tells her to go in the back room. Afterwards he talks to her and she petitions him to have the rest of the day off, which he grants.
      Scene 6: Monique finds out about Marie's "accident" and visits her at the hospital. Jacques is also there as he followed along in the ambulance. Monique petitions Jacques to let her go to the conference instead of him. He grants it in exchange for her help getting him into an even bigger conference at a later time.
      Scene 7: Henri goes to visit his father Frank (who is sick and needs a lot of help). They talk about Claudette. When Henri tells Frank that Claudette's father has recently hurt her, Frank gets angry and tells Henri to be a man and do what he can to help her. Henri promises to do so.
      Scene 8: Henri goes to find Claudette at her father's house where she has spent her day off. They have a brief argument. She is upset because she doesn't feel as if he cares for her. He says that he loves her, but then Jean's car rolls up the driveway. Claudette urges Henri to leave (saying that Jean will be furious if he finds Henri there). Henri rejects and there is a conflict. Claudette wins and kicks him out the back door, and then Jean comes in the front door. There is a second conflict as Claudette wants to leave him forever and start a new life without him. She succeeds by taking her mother with her (who apparently also wants to leave him).

      The feedback
      * Petitioning as a mechanic should probably not be exclusive to the MC. There has been several occasions where NPCs have petitioned the MC (as it makes sense in the fiction).
      * Conditions might need to make a more prominent difference inside the fiction - right now they don't really get infused in the fiction.
      * Rerolling dice should perhaps be accompanied with a new discription (as in for example Dogs in the Vineyard) to get a stronger connection to the fiction.
      * There was a short discussion on how to use cards instead of dice, introducing new events in a similar way as Dogs in the Vineyard. I aim to explore these ideas further.
      * You need to be able to discard the whole hand (in case you only get a lot of high-value cards - perhaps during non-conflict scenes.
      * The players expressed many positive thoughts on the system. They liked the idea that when you initate a conflict you take a card from your hand and put it upside down, so that the others know what the character is trying to accomplish but not how.
      * Perhaps the setting needs to be formulated with a few characteristics in order to make it more central and visual.
      * Using history as a way to increase the dice pool for conflicts is very important as it is very hard to succeed in the beginning.

      \n Alright. So I read this thread and tried to sum up the pitching that needed to be expanded on. The welcome-page of http://www.annodaemonum.com/ has been updated with that pitch, but for ease of use I also post it here in an uglier (but textually identical) version. If you want to share your thoughts on this I would be very grateful! Thanks!\n
      \xbb There are no such things as demons \u2013 demons don\u2019t exist \xab

      Anno Daemonum is a GM-less roleplaying game about desperate people who have recently crossed an invisible line into the demonic realm. They only know this from the feeling that they now carry within them: A feeling of demonic presence. It grows in the vicinity of conflict as if the demons thrive there, feeding from the hostile energies at the same time as they fuel those conflicts with violence, sorcery, and corruption.

      The game is played by 2-4 players over several sessions. Its eight-page rules-structure forces a certain kind of narrative with reincorporation and a tight web of people and places. It creates a close-up, personal narrative which focuses on personality and morals.

      What is it about?

      The goal of the game is to explore the stories of your characters through the scenes that you create for them during play. You will find out what they are prepared to do to others to get what they want, exploring your characters\u2019 moral boundaries. You will also see to what extent your character succumbs to the temptations of power, and how they handle the responsibility that entails.

      The creative agenda

      * Together you and your fellow players create a believable and intriguing story that revolves around your characters. You help each other make their lives interesting, dramatic and full of hard choices.
      * The fictional world is there for you to make a personal impression onto. The existence of the player characters never goes unnoticed in the world you create.
      * When it\u2019s your scene you are the \xbbnarrator\xab and your character is in the spotlight. As the narrator you take on a director stance, controlling the camera angles and homing in on the details of your choosing. You have an overarching authority over the scene as well as your character, but you\u2019re never in complete control. Your fellow players always help you play the other characters in your scenes and they control how those characters act and react within those scenes.

      The premise

      This game takes desperate and unstable characters (the player characters), gives them dangerous and unreliable tools (the demons), and urge them to make spontaneous and emotional choices (to get what they desire). They are real people in an unreal situation. This situation however, is one that they desperately want to change, and their only option is to take matters into their own hands. Because once they crossed that border into the demonic realm they were disconnected from society, and now no one can help them except themselves (or perhaps each other).

      The setting and the demons

      All player characters have a trigger-event that pushed them over the border into the demons\u2019 line of sight. And from that moment on the demons restlessly stalk and watch them from beyond, ready to break through when desperation and conflict blurs the human mind. Keep in mind however, that the demons are not characters \u2013 they are not supernatural henchmen that you can talk or interact with. They are an otherworldy force that you cannot understand, reason with, rely on, or tame.

      It is also important to recognize that sorcery in this game isn\u2019t ritual magic. The demonic presence is a kind of sorcery in itself and it is instantaneous, impulsive, subtle and constantly within reach.

      Anno Daemonum creates stories with a few but precise themes:
      * Delusions and doubt of what\u2019s real and not
      * To loose control and act in desperation
      * Vulnerability and to share one\u2019s inner thoughts
      * Desires that lead to irrational choices
      * The protagonists being the monsters
      * Power balance and relationship focus

      How does it end?

      Each player character starts out with a desire that they can\u2019t get. This is what drives the characters toward conflict initially. But, sooner or later the characters are going to get what they desire \u2013 or it might be clear that they never will. And the whole point is to see who they have become at that point, and what they\u2019ve been through. Making this struggle real for the characters is a collective endeavour which in turn determines the pacing of your game. So, when the desires have been resolved, it\u2019s a good place to end your story.
