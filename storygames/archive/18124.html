\n I was at a convention this past weekend with a couple of games that I wanted to run but I didn't get any sign ups (at all) and I became unable to let go of the issue for the whole period I was feeling rejected.

Looking at it now I think there were four things I was missing that put me behind the others.

Firstly I arrived at the convention five minutes after the doors opened and I didn't realise that I was providing my own sign up sheet. So the early birds got the first pass before my sheet was even up. (I really hate sign up sheets.)

Secondly I filled out my sheet by hand on the day which looked much less professional than the others which had been printed, some in full colour even.

Thirdly I might have chosen systems that don't have a high social currency value. I was going to run Burning Wheel and the relatively new unknown, Project Ninja Panda Taco.

I am also a relative newcomer to the con scene. I don't have that reputation yet. But I was frustrated that I was being denied the opportunity to grow it by not having it in the first place. I've expanded my online presence and joined this forum this year as I realised that the communities overlap quite significantly and all focus on their own specific aspects game that I wasn't getting sensible conversations about elsewhere.

So areas to improve... arrive before the doors open, bring a sign up sheet that looks nice, choose a game with a higher darling rating and market myself with posts on forums and tie the online identity to my real self... I need an avatar... And lastly find some way to let go of the ranting imp that gets me when it doesn't work out.

How do you get your convention games going?

    \n Don't sell your game based on the name of the game. Sell a situation.

    For example, I'm running the new game 13th Age at 1d4con at the end of April. I didn't title my game event "13th Age." Instead, I am calling it "Trouble in Trollkeep" and my brief write-up says something like "A handful of young adventurers must keep the March of Trollkeep running after the loss of their recently-deceased mother, the Marchioness, and in the absence of their older brother, the apparent heir. The trolls have not been honoring their treaty with the people, and rangers report increased enemy troop activity along the north road. 13th Age is a new RPG by the developers of D&D 3E and 4E with rules you'll recognize and quickly understand."

    If you're running PNPT, throw out a very specific scenario and give it a catchy name people can grab onto. Same for Burning Wheel.

    For example, if you're running "The Gift," post something like:


    The Gift: Elves vs. Dwarves

    You play a member of the delegations of one of these competing factions in a tense, player-vs-player negotiation where everyone has a secret agenda. Can the Elves win the esteem and aid of the Dwarven King? Will the talks break down into a flash of steel and stone and magic? The Burning Wheel RPG has engaging rules for both a Duel of Wits and Fighting!

    \n Panels are a lot rarer in the UK Con scene. I think only Dragonmeet and Continuum have them and they are very limited in both.

    Knowing your Con is a big start. Some games will fill at some Cons and not at others.

    I always try and do a pretty sign up sheet to grab attention and because it gives me time to make sure the written 'pitch' is what I want to say. I ramble at the best of times, give me a blank sign up sheet to write my pitch on and who knows what you'll get.

    As I was at that Con I know that the game choice was very strong, incredibly varied and the player base quite small. My morning game was really touch and go for players until the last minute and choosing what I wanted to play in the other two slots saw me with an embarrassment of riches. If anything there were too many 'Indie' games up as I know more 'Trad' players ended up in games that pushed their comfort zone in some slots. It really was bad luck that both of your games failed to attract the players.

    As a 'knowing the Con' thing I would have thought the afternoon slot would definitely have filled, the evening slot tends to be quieter and people are more likely to go for known GM's/Games in it so you're better risking in slot 1 and 2 rather than 2 and 3 there as I think the last slot is where the 'Names' matter. An unknown GM and an unknown game can be an issue, or possibly a game people think they know and don't like. But equally I'd think hard before running something 'because it's popular' rather than 'because you want to run it.'

    At that particular Con I'd also say turning up right at the start with sheets ready is a good move. All the games for the day are put out together and there's not a lot of swapping around between games done so timing does help. The worst times I've had there as a player have been when I've turned up just before slot 1 starts rather than just before doors open and I, at least, think the two things are linked. I'm not sure if they will do filling sheets for you to put out. Some Cons will...

    As well as advertising that your game is going to be there and what it is about (especially for an odd game which is why I pushed Ribbon Drive so hard before the Con) ask around about what it's like to run there. The conversation might not help but can at least prime you for what to expect and best methods for that particular Con.

    When your game can't run, try and get in to something else and have fun at it. But sometimes it's not an option, I had 1 Con last year where my game had 1 sign up and there was only 1 space in the rest of the games running so I let the sign up have that (after all they'd believed in my game and signed for it, if one of us was going to miss out on playing in that slot it wasn't going to be them) and sloped off to my room to play computer games and feel sorry for myself. If that's what you do in that situation, it's hard not to do it, but it's almost definitely not justified.

    \n The UK has a number of smaller Cons that run once or twice a year. They get 50-80 players at them and are often 1 or 2 day events as far as the gaming goes. Most don't do booklets as they are run on a very tight (and low) budget.
    They do often publicise the games that are being run on web sites for the event though.

    It means that games not running is a bit more of an issue because the 'customer base' is smaller and so the number of people who are going to be hooked by something out of the ordinary is fewer. Having said that they also feel like they have a bigger percentage of players willing to try games outside of what might be considered the mainstream. The size does mean that if you get unlucky with what people are running the pool can get emptied fast though, in this instance it's possible that a game of Mouseguard took a big chunk of the Burning Wheel friendly players with some shiny new stuff taking the rest.

    \n A few things:

    1. Knowing the Con well is a big deal. If you haven't been to it before, it's hard to know how things tend to play out. Plus, if you don't come to the Con knowing any folks who are going to be there, it can be hard to plug into what's happening. Some Cons are set up well for newcomers and others aren't. Also, no matter if it encourages newcomers or not, you may be pitching games in a way that doesn't work for the Con.

    2. If you're in a new place, showing up and just playing whatever other people offer is not a bad deal. You get to plug into what's already going on, meet some people, and get a feel for it.

    3. Running indie games in regular Con slots can be a crapshoot. Often, people haven't heard of them or are just there to play Pathfinder or whatever. However, some Cons have a seperate indie games track or a large supportive community of folks who like trying different games. If the Con you attended doesn't have those, then drumming up interest in indie games can be tough. Most Cons I go to now, I play nothing but indie games because the community turns out in force and essentially provides a seperate mini-con for ourselves within the larger Con environment (GameStorm, PAX, etc.).

    4. Sounds like you had a crappy time. That sucks, and I hope it hasn't spoiled your desire to try playing games with other folks. Hopefully talking about it here can make your next experiment better.

    One of the things you'll find as you GM at more and more cons is that folks from one con often go to others, and this will work in your favour because if you run a popular game at one con and the players in that game attend another one your'e also at you've got a head start with those people; hopefully they'll tell their gamer buddies about that fab game you ran at *Con. Judd's right about inviting folks to your game, but you've obviously got to get your approach right: too 'in yer face' and you'll push people away, too reserved and you won't get any response, good or bad. Going back to my previous point, if you're by the sign-up table and someone who played one of your games at another con is also there, they might well be prompted to say to their friends 'Hey, sign up for this game, I played it at *Con, it's great fun'.

    As for what type of game to offer, sure, don't bother offering CoC at Indiemeet and hesitate to offer a game poem at Conception, but other than that the main prolem you have with new and unusal games or systems is the lack of exposure, and there's really no way round this but to keep plugging them. W00hoo's right about viral marketing, gathering players in advance via the forums, but aside from that and the above solutions and observations it's the long slog. But don't be disheartened, it's happened to everyone at some point in their GMing career.     \n If people don't jump to play your games, my suggestions are to:
    * Not take it personally. Game sign-ups are about a whole bunch of factors.
    * Find a fun game to get in on, and meet some new people through play.
    * Keep meeting new people in each game you play, and at each meal break you take.
    * Try running another game once more people know your face and name.
    * If people still don't bite on the games you're running, keep joining in and playing other people's games.
    * Prioritizing having The Fun That Occurs over The Fun You Anticipated.
    * Ask people what games they wish they'd seen offered, and consider bringing those next time.

    \n You have received plenty of good advice above. If I had to repeat what worked for me:
    - Throw together a good hook (situation) and don't mention the game's name unless it's required or important. People are only going to browse through it, so make it very short and as interesting as you can.
    - Always be welcoming and inviting when people ask questions about what you're doing.
    - Get friendly with whoever is running the con, because people will ask the staff what's fun and cool to do, and you want them to have you in mind.

    And lastly, just put in a few conventions. We offered up indie/small-press games at Dragon*Con for years before anyone sought us out. Especially at these once a year events, the pace is slow. If you want PLAYERS NOW you have to offer up the game they are already playing. It's hard, but try not to take it personally.

    Thanks for having the enthusiasm to try. I'm so glad to see a new face willing to take games to people.

    Specifically for 3:16 I'd want to know tone as I signed up for the game. Trying to recreate Aliens is one option, Carry On Stormtrooping is another...

    I guess it's pretty obvious I'm not a fan of bait & switch as a sign up sheet method...

    On the 'what I do' front my created sheets will have the bottom half dedicated to space to sign names, show when and where the game is being run, who by and what system. The top half will have a pretty picture on the left hand side, often the cover shot for the game I'm running. The right hand side will be blurb on the game. If it's a GM led game then it'll be flavour text. More often than not if it's GMLess we'll be doing Prep at table so it'll be much more objective 'this is the stuff we'll do' rather than emotive. I tend to add tags at the bottom of the blurb for the time challenged.

    I like the punters to arrive at the table with an idea of what they are going to get (because I like turning up at the table with an idea what I'm going to get).

    Going further, my Con sign up sheet for Ribbon Drive has the following written on it next to the cover picture from the game. I consider this as wordy as I'd ever like to get, but in this instance I'd prefer to put a player off than have someone looking to turn up to a game where they can roll dice, kick ass and take names.

    Ribbon Drive is a game about telling the story of a group of people on a road trip. It\u2019s about deciding who they are, what they want and then discovering if they get it or not. The game is GMless with everyone taking one of the characters on the road trip. It is focused sharply on telling their stories with each player framing scenes along the way and moving the story along, or bringing obstacles that may detour it off in unforeseen directions.
    The game is based around the mix tapes that get played in the stereo while on the open road. Every player can bring along their own themed mix CD, but I have a bunch with me so it\u2019s fine if you don\u2019t have one. We randomly choose a CD and get the game going. We\u2019ll listen to the first two songs and use them to generate the road trip and the characters that are in it. From there the game plays with CD\u2019s in the background, sometimes this comes to the fore, sometimes you barely realise it\u2019s there, but you\u2019ll be surprised how big a difference it has on the game.
    The game is very mechanics light but also manages to shine the spotlight on the wants and needs of the characters within it. What rules there are are easy to learn and often barely needed.
    [tags - GMless, Story focussed, Music based, Heavy player interaction, Minimal system, Adult Themes]
