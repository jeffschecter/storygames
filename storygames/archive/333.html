    I think we should keep this thread as is -- though perhaps with more discussion of the kickers and bangs provided.

    However, another thread where we do an Art Deco style setup and talk about it and what we're doing step by step might be helpful for people. Seeing bangs after they've been generated can be a good thing, but being on the inside when they're being generated and seeing why they come out as they do might be better.

    For example, the first bang I did for peaseblossom I quite liked because no matter how the character reaponds, there is going to be shit hitting the fan. It's obviously an issue for the character/player -- the whole kicker revolves around it. So, being me, I'd go to shove that shit into overdrive ASAP. Have the emperor come in, get the love out in the open between the two of them. Then play up the complexity of the situation. Is he in love with her too? Is it all politics? Can he be trusted? Even if he is in love will he betray her later? Are there other women in love with him, powerful princesses who may not like the general? Will they betray her? Him? Turn her own men against her? If she says no, what then? Will he ever trust her again? And if she kills him... my oh my....

    So when I sat down and read the words "powerful victorious general, could overthrow the emperor, but secretly in love with him" I knew that I wanted to get her in a room with the emperor as fast as possible. Or at least have a messanger, something that would push that button fast and in a way that would make any choice the character makes shake the whole structure of the game.

    The later bangs are things I usually put into group games where there isn't a central party -- stuff to give the protgaonists reason to cross each other's paths. Not things that mandate they be on the same side, or be opposed, but things that give them reason to get into each others world and see how big and bad the other is.

    The middle bangs were things that could happen with the army, general questions for a general, things that let the player show what kind of leader, what kind of moral force, and what kind of commander the character is. They also have the advantage of working with the bigger bangs pretty freely. No matter what she does with the emperor or the ghosts, for example, one of her captains can always be caught doing something regretable.

    And that's why I came up with the bangs I did.

    Suggestion for all of you: if you've never played a game where the PCs have kids, try it! We've all run a dozen games where people's parents are the source of story ("Luke, I am your father" etc.) - but kids are a whole different source of story meat.

    Rob,

    That's a very solid definition for humanity in the game! It would also most certainly change the way I ran things, and the kinds of bangs I'd generate. For the Emperor confessing his love in that game I probably would not run it like a Dogs NPC, heart on sleeve and everything. I would, however, fill the scenes with tense silence in which I say things like "The way he is looking at you, it is obvious there is so much more he wants to say but cannot..." Make it a constant temptation for her to lose humanity. Not to mention all those schemeing wives just waiting to pounce on weekness, blowing the least inflection of the voice all into proportion.

    I know how you feel with the acting issue, as I'm nowhere near the actor that many of my group are -- especially Mo. (She's an immersionist and has 5 years of stage acting and weekly improv and soap experience, how the hell am I supposed to compete with that?) I normally compensate for it by using my words. I can't act all that well, but I can narrate very well indeed. So when I can't quite make the longing but repressed look come out on my face, I describe it as delicatly and poetically (or as full of blood and thunder, depending) as possible. I have another friend who is an artist, and he often draws out lots of pictures and scenes, and describes things how they'd be framed in a graphic novel. Mostly I think it's a matter of playing to your personal strengths.

    Also, I highly second the suggestion to run games in which the PCs have kids. The Heroquest game I ran with my mother in law ended up having 90% of the action occur because of her adopted child and her burried child. It gives a combination of gut-punch and soft and sweet like little else.

    Of course, if her bravest captain is her son, that just makes me want to say that its her son that is found raping a village girl. How's she gonna deal with that? Especially when her fell weapon starts whispering betrayal from her own womb?

    And again, the need for many bangs. Some just don't take up a lot of time. In the example of the barbarian, all the cleaving can certainly be character revealing, and can lead to more stuff down the line. When Mr. Dead Barbarian's tribe now has a blood feud instead of a peace, it could lead to interesting stuff. However, that also may not happen if the PC then does something to send them howling back to the hills. So having your bandolier fully stocked is neccisary.

    As for Demons in Sorcerer, you have found my biggest weakness as a Sorcerer GM! I often have difficulty with demons at the start of the game. They tend to play a bigger roll later on, once I have a sense of the characters sins for me to exploit. Ron would probably tell me off for that, so more demon bangs early on is probably a good thing.

    Working them in is, I think, a matter of using their need vs. the needs of the PC. Tommy, for example, needs money and fame like a drug. His guitar can give that to him, but only if he gives it human misery. So if in the middle of the battle of the bands down at the Jersey Shore a bandmate starts hitting on Jeter and Tommy tries to mollycoddel the situtation and keep both his friend and his bandmate -- his guitar will make it obvious he's going to be abandoned at a key moment in the performance. No more magic fingers, Mr. Tommy. Similar deal with the fell weapon -- when its starting to be obvious that something is going to go to easily, to sweetly, without filling the weapon's need, then BOOM.

    Hrmm... its occuring to me that I may play my demons as too reactive. Bangs require some active step, usually, and my demons always wait around for the PC to do something. Probably best to shake that up.

    Finally, on the issue of bangs being redunant with Tommy's kicker -- maybe. Thing is you never know. I actually used Tommy because I got to play him in a one shot a few months back (after much whining on my part) -- and the whole main thrust of the kicker got resolved pretty quickly. Luckily the GM had some bangs about neighbors calling the cops, the cops calling the mob, the dad showing up to bail Jeter out and Jeter going all Shawshank, and so on. Unfortunatly we never got around to the band, so I find the band-based bangs fascinating because they open up a whole different way the character, and game, could have gone.
