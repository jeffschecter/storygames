    \n First off, I love what you're proposing. Consider also that they are a mainstream form of LARP, specifically. I've heard about many games where people take it as an opportunity to dress in vintage clothing, people are acting as their characters not just narrating for them. Many also fit into a dinner party format, though tea time or a cocktail party could work just as easily. I think the fact that they piggyback on an acceptable form of social gathering helps their mainstream appeal. The real challenges will be finding other exciting things, besides a murder, that could happen in a fixed setting and/or finding ways to bridge from the LARP format into a story telling format. Perhaps negotiations (I could see something like Burning Wheel's The Sword where players decide what to do with a novel object) or espionage (thinking about the find-your-partner mechanics in the board game Incognito). Kagematsu might make for a compelling tea time scenario (nominally in the village tea house).

    \n An important thing to consider about those types of games. They drive story through structure rather than through characterization.

    In other words, your character is more "a person who has this resource (clue, etc)" rather than "a person who feels this way about these sorts of things".

    In other other words, they are structured more or less as a board game. People have interactions that are driven by the formulaic structures of the set up, rather than by the feelings, emotions, etc. of their character.

    Not that this is a condemnation, by any means. It's just an analysis.

    I've been playing in a larp called Starship Valkyrie that does the same thing. The details of who your character is and their background and how they feel and all that is only as relevant as you want to make it. For the most part, your character is a selection of skills. The situation drives the drama. The ship is being attacked. Someone has been hurt. We need to get the doctor over here to treat them because they're the only one on the ship right now who has the skill to do that. But the doctor is busy with something else right now. And we're still being attacked. 2 consoles just got damaged, so we need to get the Engineers in here to fix them. And we need to launch the fighter ships to go out and help. But there's a few different targets, so we need to decide on a course of action quickly so we can get them launched and out there. And there's something weird happening and we need the Science folks to figure it out. And where's the Doctor, isn't she done yet? We have wounded here!

    It's very situation driven. And my wife, who gets very shy about roleplaying and characterization, has no problem diving in and playing a Doctor. Cause she knows what she's supposed to do - she's supposed to react to the situation and fill her role on the ship. And any characterization stuff she feels like adding on top of that is just flavor - it's not the driving action of the game.

    That sort of model makes things very easy for people who aren't comfortable with roleplaying to get involved, because the characterization, which they feel unsure about, is not the driving force.

    \n Actually How to Host a Murder does NOT contain roleplaying.

    The killer does not know they are the killer. They can't lie or cover up. Each "act", there's certain new information that's revealed to each player which they share with the group in some way.

    However, they are proto-LARPs in that people like to costume and use silly accents and joke around about the scenario and speculate about the outcome and so on, so I can see someone who is interested in one getting interested in a roleplaying LARP too.

    \n I should take a minute to plug my old, non-updated page on Murder Mystery Games and in particular my game The Business of Murder

    I'd agree that How To Host A Murder isn't like traditional RPG in that the killer's player doesn't know they are the killer. However, I don't think that absolutely doesn't make it an RPG. There are plenty of story games where character backgrounds are written or re-written during the course of play. I would note that Graham Walmsley's "A Taste for Murder" has the same feature, for example.

    My game, though, is more of a traditional larp, and the killer's player knows they are guilty and is free to lie.
