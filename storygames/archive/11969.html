\n I just finished the first Town of my first non-one-shot Dogs game, and I wanted to share some troubles with y'all. The game has been going great on a number of levels, and I think we all had a good time, but there were a few things that happened which I'd like to share. (If you participate in the lumpley games forum on the Forge, you'll find some of my specific questions from this game there, too, in two recent "Questions!" threads.)

1. You know how they say the players will blame the Steward? Oh, boy, is it true. I tried really hard to make a Town where the Steward was implicated, yes, and definitely Proud, but competent and at least potentially in the right, working hard to find a solution to the Town's problems. And, sure enough, the Dogs pull him down, first thing! They want to replace him with "the most boring man in town", I think--not an existing NPC mind you, just, "let's find someone pretty bland, he's bound to do better than this guy."

So, whoever said that about Stewards? Amen.

2. The *players* in this game are awesome, but the Dogs are something of a Dogs GM nightmare! They move in coordinated, thorough efficiency through the Town, like a skilled group of dungeon crawlers with a standard operating procedure. (Which we all really enjoyed, I think, but, boy, does it put some pressure on the GM in order to challenge them! I have some ideas for what I'll throw them up against next...) Also, there's four of them. It didn't take them long *at all* to figure out they're waaaay more effective when they work together. Look:

They worked together intelligently and coordinated their actions--I believe they were very inspired by the phrase "Three in Authority". I created a Town full of moral ambiguity in preparation for this outcome, figuring they would surely take different sides on certain issues, but they still tended to side with each other in conflicts or just stay out until it was over, even where they disagreed as Dogs, rather than show disunity. For instance, at one point they gathered together a bunch of NPCs and led them into a house, then had each sit down in a separate room. They left one Dog to stay in the house and make sure no one left, and then took the NPCs out to the river one by one and took them on, three-on-one, until they got what they wanted. And the Dog left to stay behind was always the one who might potentially have disagreed with the other three ("You're not impartial enough to interrogate your own grandma. You stay in the house this time, and we'll take her").

That's pretty intense!

Also, I learned this: Take an older lady out to the river, put a cloth over her head and place the barrel of a gun on her forehead until she confesses (and wets herself). Works every time.

3. I think the "mob" rules make a whole lot more sense if you treat the note about "each additional member is a Trait of the mob" as prescriptive and not descriptive. In other words, no matter how big the mob is in the fiction, use the number of Traits you can give the mob to determine how many effective "members" it has. By "effective" I mean, "impacting the mechanics", and use it as a hard limit for yourself as a GM. Does anyone else do it this way?

4. One thing no one ever mentions about Dogs is that escalation depends on what tools you give yourself as a GM, and I just discovered this the hard way. No way I'm going to escalate to Guns if it doesn't make sense in the fiction! So, sometimes it's important to make sure at least some of your NPCs are not senile, pregnant, sick, or babies. It was so much easier to play the tough immigrant dude in conflicts than the old ladies.

5. I'm finding the "quick NPCs" from Vincent's "Dreamation NPCs" post on his blog so much easier than the standard rules for NPC creation. It just moves the game along quicker, and I don't feel like I'm really losing anything. Some people complain about losing Traits, but I just give myself the liberty to hold back a few of the dice sometimes, and roll them in as Traits later if it makes sense. It's the same total number of dice, so there's no balance issue to worry about. And if you care about continuity, you can write down the Traits as you name them, same as with the proto-NPCs.

Mostly I just wanted to share this stuff because it's been really cool and really fun. (But also a real challenge for me: running Dogs for two player is way, way easier...)

I'm very much looking forward to the next Town.

    \n Encourage them to liberally use the Short Term Fallout option... "Have your character leave the scene and spend some time alone."

    Do they have relationships to the people in the town?

    The best way I've found to split the party is to:

    1. Have them choose conflicting relationships to the same important NPCs.
    2. Have NPCs they care about and trust ask them to talk alone.

    Also I try to set players' expectations as soon as possible. When we play Dogs, I say, "if you act together all the time, you will always win, it will be boring, and you won't get a lot of experience... I definitely encourage you to split up." When we play Mouse Guard, I say, "if you don't act together, you will die, work as a team!"

    \n Glad you took it in an affectionate light - I was obviously second guessing!

    You have an unlimited number of NPCs at your disposal. So the Dogs want to lock up and "guard" a frail old lady? That in itself is a conflict - she's got a strapping son and a pair of strapping-er grandsons who don't appreciate seeing Ida both stressed and disrespected. They say no, it won't happen. Go. The lone Dog is directly confronted with some unspeakable evil. Go.

    They want to viciously torture an old woman "in secret"? Nothing's secret. Somebody watches and reports back. Let them know it's happening. Let them see the Mountain People who observe the whole thing and scamper off. Maybe they want to chase them down? Go. Sickly grandma's actively dying from the strain right now. Go.

    I guess I'm advocating for aggressive scene framing and pushing conflicts at them as you lay out what's going on (actively revealing the town in play) - and if they refuse to bite, giving them exactly what they want and moving on quickly, because your town isn't presenting them with a complex moral dilemma. Also: Demons, hate and murder.

    \n Okay, so using relatives is an easy way to split the party. Exhibit A:

    My husband was running this town where dead people were coming back to life. We'd just determined that this was the work of demons and NOT the King, when we run into another woman who'd been brought back to life. Kit casually mentions at this point "Trav, it's your character's favorite sister. She died ten years ago when you were still a child." Instant party split. The rest of us were like "EXORCISE HER" and Trav was like "HELL NO".

    Also, making things bad... It's super fun to have sorcerors, because they can possess totally innocent people and use them as (literal) meat shields in conflicts. I once ran a town where a sorceror had possessed his two sons. The dogs decided to take out the sorceror, so during the conflict I kept taking the blow with the sons, piling as many low dice as I could on them and narrating how it was obvious that the sorceror was making them fight against their will. It was totally awesome because the players were freaking out about having to kill innocent people.

    Just because the Dogs have mechanical power to win most conflicts if they work together doesn't mean that you have to let them feel good about their victories.

    \n Update for those not aware of what the Forge thread it/was: here are my conclusions from that discussion with Vincent. (And he seemed to agree with them.)


    Dogs in Many-on-One Conflicts Against an NPC

    When an NPC makes a Raise against a group of Dogs, you say who has to answer/See. For instance, an NPC shooting a Dog means only that player answers (unless someone wants to jump in the way, or something similarly unlikely appears to be fictionally plausible).

    This allows an NPC to hold her own in a conflict, particularly if it's just talking: she can use a single Raise against all the Dogs at once. (She'll be at a serious disadvantage in any situation where numbers are a problem, though, like a physical fight.)

    Great. But what about the Dogs' Raises?

    In our game, the Dogs would all take turns Raising against the NPC, and pretty quickly the NPC would be out of dice. Not much of a conflict there! Again, makes sense in a physical fight, but not necessarily in a conversation.

    In Afraid, which is somewhat like DitV 2.0, Vincent said that an NPC can re-use all their dice when Seeing Raises the Dogs make.

    However, this doesn't work: now only the highest Raise made by a player actually needs to be made, and the others are pointless. Why even bother taking your turn? You already know how many dice the NPC has to See with, and they're going to be spent anyway...

    So here's the new rule:

    When an NPC is Seeing Raises from multiple Dogs, the NPC may reuse any dice used to See earlier that round. However, each new See must use at least one new die from the NPC's dice pool.

    This makes additional Raises meaningful. The NPC can simply keep all the dice out in front of them, and use that pile until the end of the round, adding a die each time.

    This also allows the GM some flexibility in their Sees: she might want to Reverse the Blow against one Dog, but Take the Blow from another Dog's Raise in the same round. The game remains dynamic this way.

    (We used this rule in the Dogs game which proceeded after this thread, and it worked wonderfully.)
