\n So I played The Upgrade! At Dreamation with Clinton, Joe, Paul, Terry, Julia, and Tom. Emily and Tobias facilitated and had roles to play. It was a great and inspirational game. I'm so excited about this I'm going to pee myself, so watch out.

It\u2019s jeepform; drawing on techniques from tabletop, long form improvisation, the LARP world, and maybe other stuff. You can go read about it here; The Upgrade is available here.

The entire game is positioned as a television broadcast and takes place, on the surface, in real time. You stay in character. The physical space is divided into four sections \u2013 a set of couches facing the cameras for the live broadcast, and play spaces for scenes acted out in past, present, and (possible) future. Character ownership is fluid, and it\u2019s not uncommon for two players, inspired, to jump up and do a little vignette in the future to add some direction and color to a scene underway in the present. This alone was worth the price of admission, because it isn\u2019t something that is done much, or well, in our little world.

About half-way through there was a \u201ccommercial break\u201d and the action cut to a TV mogul\u2019s board room, where everyone took on the role of a producer and critiqued the show in progress. We talked about each character, and crassly commented on their interest to viewers and what could be done to punch up the ratings. Erik needed a makeover. Mauno was probably gay \u2013 was that good for ratings or bad? Basically, we had a metagame discussion mid-game about the direction things were going, how to make them more fun, interesting, and colorful, and what was going well \u2013 all without breaking away from in-character play. Really brilliant and totally stolen as of now.

The show\u2019s \u201chosts\u201d, GMs in disguise, controlled the flow of action when it flagged by prompting interview questions of the contestants, and rolling tape (tapping players to act out scenes in the past, or the present). It could have been strongly orchestrated but there was no need \u2013 once the players saw how things worked, we ate it up. Interestingly, the techniques we latched onto were quite different from those popular with, say, Italian players. We liked the past and present spaces, Italians like monologues.

Playing the Upgrade (and later at Dreamation, Night of Nights) was huge for me \u2013 I\u2019ve been watching this stuff and supporting what Team Jeep is up to and even using some of their techniques, but I haven\u2019t had a chance to try it out until now. I\u2019m very excited about some of the possibilities it offers, and The Upgrade is really a "training wheels" sort of game - it gets deeper, weirder, and maybe better. There is going to be more jeep-style play around here real soon, and it is going to kick ass.

Here's a little video of us after the game.

    \nI played Doubt at Dreamation, available for download here. It was incredible. Why? It challenged me, made me nervous, forced me to deeply connect with the other players, was a great deal of fun, made for great memories to retell later, and questioned how I view roleplaying games. Probably my favorite Dreamation event.

    Jason and Paul, I'm not sure how similar The Upgrade was to Doubt but playing really helped underline a few differences between many LARPs I've played and what little Improv exercises I've participated in. Doubt was much more structured than many LARPs I've played in terms of plot, characters, and scenes. But at the same time, much less structured in terms of player interactions (mostly talking) than Improv. Improv has many guidelines on how to interact with other participants. Doubt seemed to have very large super structures to really get you to buy into the situation and feel what's going on but doesn't want to constrain your reactions to the super structure. It doesn't want you to be a character divorced from who you are. Or construct dialog in any specific way. It wants you to be you and push your own emotions to the surface. It's feel similar to method acting in that way.

    \n I'm not sure where to begin. We should go get Tobias and Emily here, because they are smart and closer to jeepform, obviously.

    Let's see. I have no practical experience with Johnstone, only book learnin', so keep that in mind. But it struck me that jeepform has a lot in common with that, much more than Chicago style. The notion that you can receive casual feedback mid-scene from a director or another player, for example. The flexibility and malleability of scenes. Embracing plot and story (which is anti-Chicago all the way down).

    As a (rusty) improviser, I recognized the techniques and was completely comfortable with them. Interestingly, it was far more comfortable than long form to me - long form improv is always a little stressful for me, the play is leavened with tension and alertness, and this had none of that. I was totally at ease and in the moment. Why? I think it was plot and structure, also comfortable as an old boot from years of tabletop.

    I also played Night of Nights, and that's much more serious, and about sex and lives lived badly and romance straight up, and there was definitely less comfort there. So The Upgrade's light, sardonic tone also helped make it easy to play.

    Ask me questions; that'll help me think about it more. Also, I'd love to hear from Clinton, Joe, Tom, Terry, Emily, Tobias, Julia, or whoever.

    This is exactly how I feel about it, Jason. I love playing improv, but it seems like doing it the hard, hard way. Working with a tense or explosive situation with structure to help you play it out makes it so much easier and gets you in deeper, it seems to me.

    And I love the structure of jeep form so much--it feels better to me than straight larp since you get to watch what everyone else does. Which is often so funny! Or so tense and touching. Then everyone gets to see it and appreciate it, and also to be able to play off of one another based on it. The reincorporation is strong and funny (Civil war re-enactors!), because we are keyed in to what everyone else is doing.

    It was 110% awesome to share this stuff with you all at Dreamation. I am so grateful to Tobias for coming out. And, my plan is to do more, in the western MA area as well as NYC, and wherever else I may be in the months to come! (Nerdly, GenCon, Mace?, Jiffy etc.)
