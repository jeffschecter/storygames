Pseudo-Play and Story Games

James_Nostack
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
I have the Dungeons & Dragons SRD on my laptop, and when I get bored in lecture I haul the thing out and fiddle around with it. Then I get all crazy, and stop listening to the lecture, and next thing I know I'm lying in a gutter in Atlantic City at 5 in the morning muttering about prestige classes and how best to fight pixies.

D&D is, for lack of a better word, "potentially infinite prep." If you had the three Core Books, and no players, you could spend years building your perfect little setting, and designing little adventures to run in your imagination. From a particular design standpoint, this is a brilliant feature: you can "pseudo-play" the game by yourself, and though it's not entirely satisfying it does fire you up to play the game someday. So the fact that the books aren't useful doesn't mean you should stop buying/reading them. (In another sense, though, this is a bad thing, because non-gamers are like, "You spent all that time . . . for what, exactly?")

As I understand it, a huge part of the larger RPG community does this: buy books, read the books, get excited about someday playing the game, then never play the game, but re-read the books, etc. Whether this is part of the collector mentality, or boredom, or whatever, I have no idea.

I've generally broken this habit, since a very large number of Story Games are "finite prep"--in fact, very low (or even zero) prep is a highly desired feature. Furthermore, most Story Games explicitly require creative collaboration from the other players at an early stage of the process. You cannot "pseudo-play" Capes or Trollbabe. Sorcerer and its supplements explicitly discourage "pseudo-play" in the text.

Now, here's my question:
* Are there Story Games which encourage pseudo-play in the D&D style of building your silly little worlds, and populating them, and creating adventures for nobody to go on? (I suspect BW/BE comes close to this, but I don't really know.)

* What point would a "pseudo-play" feature serve in the context of Story Games?

* (This is the allegedly constructive point of this post) When I recover from my periodic D&D benders, I always feel kind of sick, in part because I don't have anything to show for it that my "sober" mind would appreciate. Still--is there something like, I don't know, "Story Legos" where you can fiddle around with, fitting archetypes together, building situations and resolving them, to your heart's content? Like, solitaire for story-gamers? (I realize the most direct way to do this is to write a short story. But for various reasons, including powerful self-criticism, this isn't the lighthearted, relaxing process that playing a game would be.)

    I'm going to go out on a limb here, but my guess is that the difference between a game like D&D or BW and other games such as Universalis or Shock is that the former have a lot of rules for doing many different things. They are built more as sims and thus you can read the rules and get a picture of how the game will play in your mind. You start to think, "Oh yeah, they could find that key in the caverns and then later on do a Circles test to find the owner..." and next thing you know you are writing a fricken story. And the more invested you get in that story the more you will get frustrated when the players don't follow it. Thus the twin demons of "yeah well I never ran the game" or "all aboard the railroad!" I know I have been guilty of both.

    Whereas when you take some of the thinner volumes that have come out lately; the rules seem like more of a framework and there's much less hand holding involved. When I read The Mountain Witch, I really dug the rules but had a really tough time figuring how what the game was going to be like. "The players can call for NPCs or scenes that relate to their dark fates?" Things like that really reduce the 'Dungeon Master' down to more of a director or facilitator role and give a lot of power and responsibility to the players.

    And some recent games are written in such a way that the rules itself are hard to figure out. They are complex enough that you are just going to need to see them play out at the table. There is no "ooh, let me imagine how a game of Polaris would play out" in my brain. It's written more like poetry and less like a technical manual.

    Personally I like having both kinds around. Some books make me imagine and dream a lot and that doesn't seem bad. And when I'm ready to play, there are other books that I will pull out and bring straight to the gaming table without hours of prep.

    And James, I wouldn't worry about not having anything to "show" for it. I'm not particularly sure how much I have to show after reading a magazine article, playing a computer game or taking a hike. But they all stretch parts of my brain or body and I enjoy doing them.

    I have a couple thoughts here.

    First, I use Burning Wheel the same way Chris does. I've played it twice, bought the books (and the Monster Burner,) but will never run the game myself, or suggest to a group we should run it, mainly because of the lifepaths feature. I mean the lifepaths are the reason I won't be playing it and the reason I bought the books, simultaneously; I found that they spark ideas, but that's not the way I want to play a game.

    Second, I think calling wasted effort, or asking "why not just write a story?" is missing the point. Games like these, even if not played, spark ideas, which you can use in other games, or in a story you write, so that becomes their usefulness. There's something about laying out idea building-blocks and describing how they can fit together that makes creativity easier. Stories and movies can spark creativity, too, but it's tainted with someone else's story; roleplaying game books strip things down to bare components. This is why GURPS worldbooks ppeal to people who hate GURPS.

    What might be interesting is a solitaire "game" written only for this purpose. A manual for brainstorming and castle-building, so to speak, that is never meant to be "played out loud".

    I know we have a sort of unspoken rule about not hyping your own shit when talking about these things, but i'm going to break that rule a little here because i'm trying to specifically address this issue with my upcoming game Sweet Agatha.

    The game is really played in two parts, the first being the readers interaction with the physical game "book." As you are reading a story you are asked to physically cut apart the book (like with scissors), by doing this you are creating materials that will be used in play later. Also, you are slowly making it impossable to complete the reading. (this serves a number of both literal and metaphoric purposes, the game is all about the mysterious dissapearance of a young woman, the cutting precludes you from knowing the ending, the mystery litterally falls apart before you're eyes and becomes a chaotic mess of clues and connections)

    I think that users should engage the material physically, i think they should make worlds and stuff, and i am interesting in wholly embracing this activity as one that is integral to the game's play. I am seeking to create games that do this.

    In the course of working on a game that focuses so heavily on reader interaction and the "pseudo-play" aspect of our hobby I've begun co-authoring an article with Nathan "carry" Paoletta that discusses the disconnect between the very single person act of reading instruction manuals and the very participitory nature of actually playing an RPG. Why do we choose such a medium to describe such an act? This article will be (in all likelyhood) published in this year's Push.

    I hope this post is helpful and not masturbatory. I don't want to sound like an advertisement, only, to present that this kind of thinking can (and perhaps should) come at the design level, not just at the end user level.

    Josh,

    I think some of it has to do with game effect or strategic control, or something like that. I've noticed that even in games with large numbers of discrete, crunchy pieces I don't do this as much if I can find a perfect build. If there is one way that is obviously the best way, I'll quickly lose interest in doing other builds on that model. If there is one way that is the best way for everything, I'll be out of interest in no time flat.

    Which, I think, is also part of the PTA thing -- not only does it take 5 seconds, but all builds are going to hit the table more or less equal. There is no best way, but there also is no better way. (In terms of doing something without a game to contextualize in, anyway -- the "better" part of PTA means "hits the themes better so that you'll get more fan mail from the particular people around the table" in so much as it means anything at all.)

    In TMNT you not only have lots of pieces, you have randomization that keeps you from always getting the killer build (mutant weasel with the assassin/ninja training package, acrobatics, gymnastics, boxing, and ninjitsu), and the oddity of the results and the possibility of the kewl factor of some crazy animal (you want a different build with a sparrow than an elephant) means that there are a lot of possible builds that are all awesome. Plus, the fact that the awesome here doesn't just come from the tactical element, but also from the kewl element. Really, you want to make not just the most ask kicking build, but the kewlest possible built (mutant albino camel that maxes out his desert survival and uses thermographic goggles so he can operate only at night).

    BW has some of this, as you want to balance your lifepaths and make them complete and interesting, fleshing out the culture, giving a good balance of tactical options, and doing nifty things with possible character backgrounds and circles at the same time. Sorcerer can have a little of that with the best demon build, descriptors, or whatever, as there is a lot of room for creativity and numbers-juggling there (though it ends up mattering less in game then you'd think it would, most of the time). Dogs, otoh, really doesn't matter -- because of the way traits work there is no way to best out your Dog's traits, though you may be able to cool them out, but without both elements, it doesn't stay fun for long.

    Posted By: Joshua BishopRobyI am, first of all, interested in John's question, which I will rephrase into the terms I was using as I read the thread: "Is lonely fun only possible in games with large amounts of discrete pieces?"
    I would say so, at least for the style of pseudo-play that I'm most familiar with. At the most basic level, the "lonely fun" of a game is probably going to be gone once you thoroughly understand all possible permutations of the game's discrete elements and how they combine to form tactics, strategies, and so on. For example, tic-tac-toe ceases to be interesting once you fully comprehend the extremely small number of ways you can meaningfully place your mark on your turn - barring special cases like programming an AI, you can't have much lonely fun with tic-tac-toe. In contrast, the lonely fun of go is enjoyed by many people as a lifelong hobby.
    Posted By: Joshua BishopRobyThe other thing I'm interested in is trying to articulate what's fun about this activity. Because it is fun, totally. I still enjoy rolling up mutant animals inTeenage Mutant Ninja Turtles and Other Strangeness. But what is it, specifically, that that process provides that is so entertaining?
    From a hardcore, playing-to-win gamist perspective, lonely fun is an essential part of the game experience. Playing a game with this mindset is as much about developing skill as it is about winning, and playing the game alone is vital to this process - it's training. I can't think of a single professional poker, chess, or Magic: the Gathering player who isn't regularly pseudo-playing their game of choice (actually, that's not true, but those oddball players are notable for this quirk alone!). I have some small experience with this from back in high school, when playing in (and winning) local M:tG tournaments was my part-time job and debate tournaments were my hobby. I had to learn how to pseudo-play effectively, simply to stay competitive; every mistake I didn't learn from could cost me real money, after all. After doing this sort of thing long enough, it became second nature: I was engaging in the lonely fun of those activities pretty much all the time, which meant that I was both having fun all the time and getting better all the time.

    This extends to sports, too. You may have heard of visualization-training or imaging-training, where an athlete pictures some scenario and mentally executes their proper response. Also, isn't solo-training for a sport a form of pseudo-play? Those long hours of hitting tennis balls off of the garage or practicing free-throws aren't actual play, after all, but are satisfying all the same.

    Bringing this back to RPGs, I think the same principles apply. If you're playing DnD with a gamist approach, every theoretical character build you make or mock battle you run through and evaluate translates into greater potential effectiveness at the table - you're learning, which is fun, and are more likely to win, which is also fun. However, even if you never apply the skill, you've still had the fun of learning and the practical experience of training your analytic abilities and memory. Since DnD is an absolutely huge game, you can go through these exercises almost endlessly. Plus, while the massive, self-contradictory complexity of the game is hardly elegant design, it has the advantage of taxing one's memory more - at least in my case, since I lonely play without access to my books, then double-check myself for accuracy.

    Any massive RPG that's even vaguely balanced should have similar potential for lonely fun, at least for the type of player I'm describing. I gather from this thread that there's a directly analogous experience for a simulationist player. I suspect that practice in composing a story might have a similar appeal to a narrativist player, in addition to trying to maximize the cool factor that Brand mentioned.

    Anyway, for me, learning is fun. I learn from both pseudo-play and actual play, and I find that each holds a unique learning experience.

    Edit: I suppose it would be best to say that, to support this sort of lonely fun, you need either a lot of rules or a lot of strategic depth. DnD, Shadowrun, and their ilk have the former, while go, poker, and the like have the latter. Either way, you've got a lot of game to think about.
