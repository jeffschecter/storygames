    \n GM'd some 3:16 and while it was the best session I've played it never really took off and one of the players got insight to what the problem might be. Antagonistic GM'ing make players reactive and that's no fun. I bet it's possible to start out with highly player driven play too but other games do that better.

    Anyway, the good part was the stark contrasts. The time spend on the ship was like perpetual prison riot while planets were quiet, almost meditative exploration (much thanks to the background music) and combat became very personal and fast with everyone nearly dying in the first encounter thanks to some bad rolls and overdosing combat drugs. That stuff must carry on to other games!

    \n Apocalypse World

    Our stoner Savvyhead, Gitch, has a thing for this girl named "H" who works at the hardhold's brothel. Thing is, the hardholder's lead bodyguard, a badass named Doghead, has his eyes on H and won't take no for an answer. It's only a matter of time before he gets what he wants, and it's implied every time Doghead comes up in conversation that Doghead's attentions are...unsavory.

    At a loss for what to do to protect H, Gitch does the most logical thing...he lights up his room-sized hookah made of junkyard parts and gets really *really* stoned.

    One of the Savvyhead powers when you have a workshop lets you do stuff with the Psychic Maelstrom beyond the usual Q&A session. In this case, Gitch decides to "isolate and contain part of the Psychic Maelstrom".

    Which part? Doghead's lust, of course. So now Doghead is a bit less frisky, and Gitch's hookah has this purple bruiselike cloud of lust swirling around inside it.

    What could possibly go wrong!

    \n Played the second half of our first Mouse Guard mission. The pair of mice gained a third member of the patrol and quickly hunted down the treacherous Sparrow, a one-eared redfur who had been seen consorting with weasels near the scent border. The patrol found him in the woods with two other mice in armor, a fight ensued and the Mouse Guard prevailed.

    The highlight was the interrogation of Sparrow, where all three players tried to fulfill their disparate goals ("make an example of the traitor" vs. "find out how deep the treachery goes" vs. "prove I am a worthy Guard Mouse to my new patrol-mates"). This ended up with a bad cop - good cop - neutral cop routine and back-to-back conflicts with Sparrow during the Player's Turn. In the end, Sparrow revealed he was working for none other than the exiled Midnight, who has struck a deal with the weasels to return to the territories en masse!

    \n Our first ever attempt at a new homebrewed steampunk-ish, magic-infused GURPS campaign went better than expected.     \nThe Dreaming Crucible:

    Megan is a girl with an alcoholic pill-popping mother and absent father, who always has to watch her baby brother while her mother lays passed out or stays out all night. Her Gift is an enchanting singing voice and her Flaw is that she follows her mom's example and hits the bottle herself. She has a supportive friend, Casey, who comes over and helps her when she gets blitzed.

    One night Jack O' Draughts appeared and stole her friend away. So she followed them into a strange land, and met a surly dwarf who offered his aid, reluctantly, because she was under his protection and any blood shed was on his head. They pursued the blissfully oblivious Casey, encountered Jack in his many deadly or beguiling shapes, and finally caught up with them both on a hilltop gazebo, where Casey, whose lips had never before touched liquor, was lounging drinking, and Megan and dwarf succumbed and joined in, Jack filling all their cups. And so they danced and sang and drank, swimming in a stupor, as in a land far away a mother returned to a house empty but for a crying baby.

    And so they sit drinking and reveling to this day.

    \n Last week, we started Apocalypse World.
    Much of the world is submerged in water, now. Canada stretches from the Rockies (edge of Alberta) to the edge of Ontario. Some of Quebec is now its own island nation, with the rest submerged.
    The PCs are part of a small community (~60 people) who inhabit a repurposed summer camp in the woods.

    Berg, the operator, goes to meet up with the hermit chemist contact she has in the woods. Finds him dead, a threatening message written in his blood on the wall, and all of the flammable chemicals missing.
    Berg turns to Brace, the Brainer. "Can you still read him? Find out what happened?"

    When You Scan The Dead...
    You first need to breathe life back into them. Roll +weird.
    On a 10+, you breathe life back into them momentarily, and hold 3.
    On a 7-9, choose:\n
    • you breathe life back into them permanently, and hold 3.
    • you breathe some of your life into them temporarily (I get to make a move), and hold 3.
    Hold gets spent just like in Deep Brain Scans.
    On a 6 or less, your scan fails, but whether the resuscitation works is up to me.

    Chombies!

    The way that Brace reads people is awesome, too. s/he (gender unknown) places small mirrors over the target's eyes, and a pair over Brace's own, and then rubs their temples until visions start flooding.

    \n Playing my Southwestern-styled cowboy Hackbird game. The setup is, all the characters are in on a plan to save Billy the Kid from imminent hanging, and to represent this plan without wasting game time discussing it, everyone has the ability to make statements about the world (which they can use to their advantage) to reflect the planning their characters have done.

    So, after Doc Scurlock's (played by Paul) son tries to turn them in to the authorities, Lawrence (John Harper) decides he's had enough of this and rides back to town, into the arms of a lady he wooed with some fancy shooting earlier. We play for a bit, then I say: "So, the next morning when you wake up in her room." John's all "actually, I'm not there. Go back to everyone else."

    So we play for a bit, I keep offering John scenes, he keeps saying he's fine, stick with everyone else. Finally, as the train carrying Billy is headed towards the ambush point, John pushes forward his tokens and says "I'm using my ability to make true statements about guns, the military, and war: the car ahead of Billy's has some cavalry men in it, and I'm there dressed as one of them. I'm bracing myself for when the dynamite blows the train of it's tracks, then I'm going to grab Billy and meet up with everyone else."

    He pulled a complete Han Solo. I loved it.

    \n GPNW: Mist-Robed Gate, set during the last days of Atlantis

    We spent a little too long on the brainstorming, I think, but the characters were amazing. Our basic premise was that every Atlantean was born into slavery and had to work their way out over the course of 100 years to become a freeman. The city itself was an architectural marvel built into an atoll, with its lower levels and bowels dug way down below sea-level, holding back the water with stone and buttresses. We had:

    -- Jackson (a 300+yo judge/astronomer who was our chief supporter of the status quo)
    -- Matt (the recently arrived Greek ambassador being blown off by the High Architect and his staff)
    -- Joe (the apprentice of the High Architect, dressing as him because the Architect is dead and no one in the city can know)
    -- Lesley (a priestess charged with reading the city's future in as annual festival, who has visions of its destruction)
    -- Lukas (a gladiator and leader of a potential slave revolt, killer of hundreds, who just earned his freedom)
    -- Shannon (a Roman immigrant scientist and physician, studying the killer plague and hopful of earning full Atlantean status)
    -- Daniel (the High Architect's estranged wife, a designer of rituals masks and perhaps the one really responsible for his work)
    -- me (a member of the specially-marked elites, born free, but sentenced to slavery for a crime; I killed the High Architect)

    Highlights:
    -- Shannon and Lesley's characters straight-up murdering Lukas' gladiator and my creepy murderer dude.
    -- Joe and Daniel having an architecture fight, where Daniel's character ran up walls to draw diagrams in chalk while Joe's character impersonated the High Architect and flooded the cracks in the walls with flour paste.

    The main regret was that we weren't able to finish on a high note, since it was getting late, my brain was ceasing to work, and we weren't able to play out the big festival where Lesley's priestess would reveal the city's doom. But there was so much great stuff going on that I still found it really exciting. Makes me want to play more Mist-Robed Gate, definitely. I very much liked playing it in a non-Asian setting, since I study China-related stuff all day long for school. And I kinda want to see more of our vision of Atlantis, which was really really fucking cool. Maybe I'll have to write up a playset for MRG Atlantis.

    \n GPNW: Finally got to try out Danger Sxool, my Mouse Guard hack for running New Mutants-style junior X-Men. I wrote it on a whim back in April 2009 before John released the Danger Patrol alpha, kinda as a joke, with Professor Danger as -- I guess -- a former Psychic Professor from the 1920s-1930s running a school for young supers. But I'd never gotten to play it.

    Like the MRG game, it was way more awesome that I could have guessed and, unlike last night's game, we were actually able to play it to a very satisfying conclusion and even figured out a few cool things about the hack as we went. It helped I think that 2 players had played Mouse Guard and one had read it, but we threw down (with four folks I had never played with or really even talked to extensively) and it was really great.

    The characters were:
    - (the) Armadillo, a kid from Texas with bony plates for skin
    - Hexen, a girl with shadow control who turned out to be a shadow demon princess pretending to be a normal girl
    - Red Shift, the team leader, with force field control, and
    - Hostile Takeover, a mind control specialist bound for business school

    We started out in media res with the kids fighting the Dark Pharaoh in the destroyed remnants of the school. They destroyed the ancient magically mummy king and it was revealed to be simply a simulation in the Danger Gym. But the Danger Gym quickly malfuctioned, locking them inside to the dismay of the adult members of the Danger Patrol who were struggling to get the students out. They fought their way through a jungle training program (set at a ludicrous XX difficult level meant for training the Patrol, not students) and discovered that an alien virus had corrupted the Gym and was hiding in the simulation inside a hologram of the Celestial Sphere of Water, a super-powered cosmic artifact. Eventually, the students triumphed over the virus, thanks to Hexen pushing her powers negative, which forced her to take the condition "Revealed," showing her true form as a shadow demon princess.

    The players were both thoughtful and hilarious and it makes me really excited to work on this hack again.

    \n I got hold of a nifty old Spycraft module called Gentleman's Agreement a while back, and was faced with a dilemma - did I really want to learn the insanely complex, d20-based Spycraft rules back to front, just so that I can run a three to five session espionage campaign for a bunch of people who don't like rules-heavy systems anyway?

    Solution: Agent S.E.V.E.N. These so-called "1PG" rule sets from Deep7 (which are actually about eight pages long apiece) are terrific devices for doing quick hacks of adventures that are written for more complex systems. The suggested rolls for various skill and combat checks were easy as pie to convert, and play was refreshingly seamless and story-driven.

    In our first sitting, four of the agents managed to sneak into the secret GeoWave research complex disguised as garbage men, after hijacking a waste disposal truck. Their hijacking scene was so utterly lame (how did they ever get the idea to try garotting an utterly blameless sanitary engineer in the middle of a mountain road in broad daylight?) that I forced them to hide out in a dumpster as payback. Two other agents were more successful posing as EPA agents, and managed to sneak out of the fortified compound in Colorado with an armload of stolen files without any violence to speak of.

    A few minutes later, just before we called it a night, the squad's computer hacker had been shot off an ATV and was bleeding out slowly in an isolated clearing in the forests just north of Chichibu, Japan. If we'd had to go through all the dire die-rolling and table-consulting mandated by the d20 system, we never would have been able to end on such a cool cliffhanger.
