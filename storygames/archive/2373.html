When "GM as impartial referree" just doesn't hold up. . .

Joli
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
So I'm GMing Over the Edge the other night and we had all the PCs out individually hunting down their various agendas (which includes, to some degree, hunting each other), and I decided to have them all meet up at the Winds of Change. The Winds of Change is a roving casino run by an otherdimensional hostess, at which people gamble for the ultimate stakes--life&death, age, beauty, happiness, heart's desires.

One PC, Frank, is looking for another PC, Gaelinar, because the latter killed a Satanic Cultist while a ritual linked to the cultist's soul was taking place, at the same time as Frank interrupted the ritual. The upshot, the vile satanist's soul is now inside Frank's body, fighting for control. Frank turned to Sir Arthur Compton, a powerful, crafty and decadent sorceror (who in my head is played by Christopher Lee, ), and Compton told Frank to bring Gaelinar to him and he could remove the possessing soul. Compton hasn't said, but I think everyone's pretty well guessed (correctly) that he means no good to Gaelinar in this endeavor.

So Frank and Gael meet up at the Winds of Change and Frank just asks him to come with him to Compton, which Gaelinar declines. He will, however, agree to a wager. He'll go with Frank if Frank wins, but if he wins, Frank has to do a job for him, to be named later (Gaelinar is a nasty criminal type and Frank is a photojournalist jailed for Seeing Too Much, recently released and keeping his head down). So far so good; this sounds interesting and it's getting late, so I end the session there, the actual game to be determined and played out next week. Yay, dramatic tension.

Then Gaelinar's player takes me aside after the sesion, and gleefully relates how if he does lose the game, he's going to follow Frank to the gates of Compton's estate, then make a run for it. I'm like, "You DO realize that the stakes at the WoC are supernaturally enforced?" He's like, "yeah, that's why I chose my wording very carefully." He's thinking the deal requires him to go to the edge of the grounds and no further. I'm like, "I dunno. . ." and he says we can talk more about it later.

So now I'm left with this bad taste in my mouth, and wondering what to do about it. this would irritate me enough if the deal was with an NPC, that is, just between him and me, but I'm twice as miffed that he's doing this to another player. I don't think there was any sense around the room that we were engaging in a "word-the-wish-spell-just-right" style battle of wits; I was just veiwing the exchange, and I'd bet Frank's player was too, as a straightforward, "what's at stake in this conflict" bit of haggling. One player setting it up so even if he loses, he wins, seems pretty dickish.

I'm also dubious about wthe merit of his claim. His exact wording was, "I'll go with you on your little walk to Compton's." I can see where he's deriving the interpretation--he said "Compton's, not "Compton, so he doesn't actually have to meet with the guy. But it just seems so. . .flimsy.

But fortunately, I found out about this before the crucial moment, so I can consider what to do about it. Except it's kind of unfortunate, because I feel my hands are somewhat tied. How do I intervene without being quite obviously biased? Or outmaneuver him in-game without being fundamentally unfair? The concept of "GM as referree" breaks down here, because if I rule in favor of Frank's side, that the deal does bind him to honor the spirit of the request, even if my ruling is the objectively "right" one, I'm also obviously ruling in favor of the side I prefer. And if I deal with it "in-character," for instance by having Compton cast sorcerous wards that ensnare him when he's 30 feet away, I'm acting on my desire to not let him, the player, get away with this (in my opinion) asshatted stunt.

As far as the wording of the agreement goes, I think I've got an out. "I'll go with you on your little walk to Compton's" implies that he has to accompany Frank on his entire journey, which includes going in the gate and up to Compton's study. But again, I'm only calling that into play because I know the player's trying to pull a fast one, and I want to block it.

So what do y'all think? Is there a way to deal with this issue without being a punishing GM slapping hands and saying "you're playing wrong, do it right!" Or should I deal with it at all? I'd still be scratching my head on this if it were just a player-GM issue, but as it's a player-player issue, I really, really don't want to gyp the other guy out of his reward, should he win.

(Which is another frustrating aspect of this problem: Frank may not even win the conflict, in which case I'm expending brainpower and angst over an issue that'll never come to bear.)

Little help?

Peace,
-Joel

    First of all, your situation does not really implicate the 'GM as impartial referee' idea. A GM playing that role would simply decide whether and how the rules of the Winds of Change apply to the wager and enforce it impartially. If either player was disappointed or upset, that is not the GM's business. Much as a pickup game basketball player might be upset at a call that they stepped out of bounds when they feel they did not, a player in these games is expected to "get over it" and keep playing. After all, perhaps next time the call will go against the other guy, or perhaps they were out of bounds after all. The implication is more along the lines of the "casual player vs. hardcore player" divide - are players meant to be held to every word that comes out of their mouths, or not? There are good things and bad things about each side, much like the "touch-move" rule in chess. Plenty of people let people touch whatever piece they want in casual games of chess, but touch-move is rigorously enforced at tournaments.

    That said, the social-level solutions people have suggested are good, but I wouldn't say anything to one that I wouldn't want them saying to the other.

    Joel,

    Everybody is trying to be nice by sympathizing with your belief that this is an immensely complex situation.

    But, really, it's not.

    G's player made a bone-headed move. He's gleefully happy about his bone-headed move. This is where the role of GM as referee is both liberating and painful. Your judgment doesn't depend on how gleeful G's player is about his supposed cleverness. You need to make your judgment based on what you think should happen.

    You clearly think that G should be held to the spirit of the deal, and so do I. It makes for a much better story. It also happens to be a pretty sensible "What would happen" sort of judgment. And it's fair to F, not holding him to an "examine every syllable for loopholes" level of totally unfun gameplay. It's the right decision.

    So you make it happen. G tries to escape at the front gate and he can't. That's your judgment, you deliver it, you continue the game.

    G's going to be upset, and he's likely to rules-lawyer (as, indeed, he's already doing in preparation) and that's when you tell him "Man, you took a big gamble with powerful magic you do not understand. That's gutsy. You got bit in the ass this time. That's the risk you take when you make a gutsy move. Suck it up and move on."

    By revealing his little plan to you in advance, G is trying to buy himself a little insurance. He's making sure that you consider his side of the argument all week long, and agonize over it, and (he hopes) come around to his side. I'm a person who generally has a very optimistic view about human behavior, but his attempt to get you on his side is so utterly transparent that even I can't see any sensible reason for it that doesn't boil down to social bullying. Your job is to hang tough, to make a judgment based on the relevant factors, and to stick with it.

    I'd just like to add my voice to those already raised in this manner:

    Players tricking players is cool if we're in Gamist play, where showing each other up and getting props for it is the reason we do it. It sounds like exactly one of your players is playing that way, though.

    Consider: I'm playing a game that takes place in the court of Queen Elizabeth I. In the fiction, the Spanish ambassador fools Her Majesty's top advisor into talking her into marrying King Phillip of Spain.

    If we're playing in Gamist fashion, I'm showing everyone that I can trick someone; maybe I've lied, or maybe I've used my resources in a clever way so that the advisor thinks that he'll get something that he won't. If the advisor is a protagonist, this likely means that the ambassador's player has some hidden information that the advisor can't see.

    If we're playing in Narrativist fashion, though, it's more profitable to tell the players what's going on all the time. That way they can fuck their characters over real good. Making a deal that turns south is great story fodder. Make sure everyone can support that story as you're building it.

    What you have here is a clash of Creative Agendas. It might be a short-term problem. But I suggest that you talk with the player who confided in you and say, "Listen, you can either take the deal in the spirit of the agreement, or we can discuss it in the open; we're not tricking each other here."
