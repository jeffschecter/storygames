\n We've talked about the games that influenced us in the past and have directed our course to the present, but let's talk about what game or designer today you see as leading the way into the next phase of story gaming. There's a pretty good argument to be made that gaming is just a continuum, meaning these games are like a conversation that build off what has come before. I'd argue that there are some games I've seen that don't have any sort of concrete antecedents. Microscope for example. I know there are similar concepts, but the execution of Microscope is one of the smoothest set of rules and play I've encountered and I can't really say I've ever played anything else like it. And the game even seems to be Internet ready in the ease with which it can be played online with little adjustment.

So who or what do you see as a signpost of the future of gaming and where we are going next?

    \n I think I need to clarify what I mean by adaptability. I'm not nearly as well-versed in gaming or story-gaming as most of you, but I've yet to play a game that was so readily adaptable with it's rules as Apocalypse World. Is your world a special little snowflake? Do the current moves not support some things that happen? Make 'em up. On the fly, even. Plenty of other games let you do stuff like this, but usually it takes a fair amount of work. So I guess what I'm saying is, I think the utility of on-the-fly adaptability is pretty well demonstrated by *World games, but they way in which they do it is only one possibility. There's a lot of room to explore there. So I think it's a decent candidate for a place where game designers decide to spend their time in the near future as they try to push the boundaries of what we think of as RPGs. Of course, that's probably wishful thinking.

    \n Structures on how to play, and more importantly, and on how to encourage a creative environment is probably something that will occur more in games (or so I hope). Techniques put in structures like "The first act is always about ... " or in game rules like "If your character isn't in the scene during a conflict, you may cheer on another person by sending a die that way" (to support a technique to create "active listening").

    The thing is why Apocalypse World is so easy to use in other games is that the rules are more techniques than actual game mechanics. Face it, it's just traditional play described in a pedagogic way. It's weird to say that this is the melody of the future, because Robin D Laws has done this for twenty years, but I think people (game designers) are more aware about this and have now different sources to draw structures from.

    Play Unsafe: the RPG isn't something that's totally impossible. You just have to design roleplaying games from a different point of view.

    \n I'm going to come at this from a different angle. I don't know what's going to happen with story games, particularly, but I predict that RPGs in general will continue down the path that Euro board games have already blazed: games that deliver a single evening's worth of entertainment that keeps a group of 3-5 adults strongly engaged, largely episodic. There may be some light continuity elements, but they will be optional. RPGs will become more specialized in their topic, setting and presentation, approaching the Pelgrane Press mission statement of games that can be taught to and understood by a group of adults in fifteen minutes or less, and played in under three hours.

    In short, game design will be informed by the logistics of getting people together to play as much as (if not more than) anything else.

    \n Well, radical innovation isn't always the same thing as a signpost for the future. Usually, a good indication of the immediate future is the thing that people are gathered all around. Pick your favorite social science, they'll all predict more-or-less the same thing. *W games are the new hotness right now (btw, I hate lumping them together like that because, besides the move mechanics and the MC advice, they're not very much alike; I love AW, am meh about DW, and would likely never buy Monsterhearts). This makes it rather likely that, in the near future, the engine's influence will be felt in a lot of games. In the not-so-immediate future? The safe bet is the pendulum swinging AWAY from the current hotness. What this breeds is hard to say. It could be the embracing of the past (hey, it happens), or it could be pushing new boundaries, like The Quiet Year is doing right now.

    That said, probably none of that will happen. I don't actually have a way to read the preferences of indie/storygamers as a whole so... a lot of that analysis likely rests on flawed assumptions.

    I am definitely looking forward to more awesome indie games as the years go by!

    \n The hackability/adaptablity of Apocalypse World isn't the innovative aspect of it \u2014 GURPS got the "Adaptability Award" a long time ago \u2014 it's the playbooks, moves, and the role of the GM and how all these pieces fit together to support the fiction and enjoyment of playing. Lots of *World hacks have been made using these components with some more successful than others. I love AW and it's hackability and there is clearly more mileage to get out of *World. However, I think the more interesting future explorations are in finding where AW (and other systems) break down \u2014 what kinds of stories is the system bad at telling? Where does the game break down? How about we force it to break? Jam some round pegs in those square holes to see what works and what falls apart? What happens if we combine two very different games like The Quiet Year with AW? \u2026It's the cracks in the gaming landscape which are ripe for discovery for new ideas.

    \n I have to say the GM philosophy of the AW games is what sticks out to me most. After DM-ing in 4e I felt like the players just reacted to my story. I haven't gotten a chance to play AW yet but I get the feeling the GM becomes the facilitator, not the director. It's a strong parallel to the new philosophy of education I see in my workplace, the teacher doesn't stand in front and put knowledge into the brains of the students. Instead, they propose ideas, ask questions, make critiques, etc. The goal is to get the student thinking, keep them on their toes and engaged. It's the whole "Yes and..." idea from improv.

    What I love about the future of these games is the anticipation of the variety that is to come. Already there are so many games that challenge notions of storytelling, character, mechanics. I get a total nerd rush when I encounter a game system that makes me think in a different way. I also think the Internet really is the future of these games moreso than any game system or mechanic. This board alone has allowed me to discover hundreds of new games (and spend way too much money).

    Thirding the notion of synchronous distributed play as being a big thing waiting to happen. I recently talked to some Portuguese and Brazilian designers who are also exploring designing for play-by-hangout as a default mode.
    \nI was sort of thinking about the influence of non-American designers when I thought up this thread. For anyone that's a movie geek, we know that the tropes of film have been interpreted in a very different way outside of the U.S. Think about French New Wave and Japanese auteurs (Ozu, Kurosawa). They are part of the larger conversation of film, but direct the conversation onto different aspects, while feeding back into the larger one. I'm excited to see how cultural experiences outside my own interpret the tropes of gaming. I love to read over what places like Misery Tourism are doing, just because it's so unlike what "roleplaying games" are defined as and it seems to challenge the idea that game are something that you should derive escapist enjoyment from.

    Its the argument I have with my friends who only want to see Michael Bay-esque blow 'em ups or twee cutesy Wes Anderson fare. I get why people enjoy those repetitious film concepts, but some times I want to watch a documentary that gets me angry and doesn't leave me feeling good. Some times I want to be made to think. And my hope is to see a little niche for games that do the same thing, challenge my precepts about politics, religion, etc. and make me think for a long time. A game like Durance makes me seriously think about hierarchical structures in societies and the ability or inability of the citizens to move on these ladders. But then some times I want to just dungeon crawl and loot. I love that there's that amazing variety for me to pick from.

    \n RPGs have a lot of unnecessary assumptions baked in, stuff like long-term play, a limited palette of components, being purely analog, etc. I'm hoping we'll keep doing more and more to challenge those things. I want to see RPGs that are partly electronic, that go hog wild using board game components, that deal with totally different subject matter, and so on. How much those can come to fruition I think is going to depend a lot on costs, but stuff like POD has kept getting better and cheaper. The Game Crafter (which figures heavily into my current project) is still a bit pricey to use, but the quality and variety of things they can make has just kept getting better with time, and OBS is starting up a DriveThruCards.com site next month as well.
