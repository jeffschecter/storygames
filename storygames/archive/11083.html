    \n When I was traveling abroad I listened to episodes of Sons of Kryos and Have Games, Will Travel. They were my chance to listen to discussions on gaming on long flights across the ocean. They inspired me to want to start a podcast of my own, but it was really intimidating. What would I have to talk about? Who would want to listen to me?

    I started really rough, recording actual play of our Burning Empires game, Prometheus Chained (iTunes link). I learned how to sound edit, and the sound quality moved from awful to bearable. The microphone was always out of the way, and mostly ignored, so it made recording easier. I published it, linked it in the Burning Wheel Forums, and I was amazed to see a few people actually listen to it! My proudest moment with that project was when Christopher Moeller, the creator of the Iron Empires Comics, made us an Itunes cover for our podcast.

    So I now knew that I could do this. And that if I put it out there, someone would listen to it.

    I talked to my good friend Kyle about my dream of producing another podcast project. We had conversations about the topics we'd examine. And then one night, we decided we'd throw caution to the wind and record. Earlier this year, we published the first episode of You Can Hack It. We just finished Episode 8 (Which will be out on Monday), and though it's a hell of a lot of work, every time I see one of our episodes go live it's a proud, proud moment. I really love the sense of creating something and sending it out into the world.

    I think that the first couple of Gaming Podcasts showed us this neat new way of communicating ideas. And if you look at the proliferation of gaming podcasts coming out, I think Judd is absolutely right.

    \n Heya,

    IMHO, it would be hard to call this era of Podcasting a Golden Age. It's more of an Innovation Age. All the podcasts had to invent their techniques and the norms for podcasting on the fly. This made for some awesome new stuff and whole new methods for communicating about games. However, the initial age of something new (like podcasting) gets to a point where further innovation by the founders get more and more difficult or requires them to revamp everything they're doing. I think Podcasting is at that point. It's going to shift from the Innovation Age to the Growth Age where there are a wide selection of podcasts on a myriad of topics. At some point that Growth Age will reach an equilibrium and things will go along like that for many years. Then, at some point, people new to podcasting will look back at what the original guys did, think it's amazing, and re-revolutionize it. That cycle will repeat several times.

    Peace,

    -Troy

    I'm not super happy with the gaming podcasts right now. 3d6 feet still rocks (awesome talk about the industry), but I miss the glory days of early Paul Tevis when he was dropping the knowledge and less confident, Sons of Kryos of course. What I really miss are the nice podcasts that are open-minded and go in some depth about games and gameplay. When I say open-minded, I mean not adhering to any one agenda or group but open to gaming in general. I loved Midnight's Lair and especially the Godzilla gaming podcast for this.

    I've recently added Pulp Gamer: Out of Character which can get quite interesting, but is more of an enjoyable general listen and The RPG Circus, which has good moments, but needs to pick up its energy a bit.

    Currently, I feel like The RPG Haven Podcast (of which I am producer and co-host) is the only podcast out there that is addressing the independent game producers outside of the Forge/Storygame clique. I have heard dozens of interviews with Vincent Baker and Luke Crane, but I'm both proud and slightly saddened to be the first person to interview Simon Washbourne, Cynthia Celeste Miller and Chris Rutkowsky, designers who are all doing very interesting things design-wise and market-wise.

    What's getting really interesting is our Friendly Neighbourhood Gamer project, where my co-host MountZionRyan is interviewing gamers who aren't connected into the internet scene, finding out the social patterns and codes they follow in their groups. It is much more fascinating than I had thought to hear how other people play.

    \n For my own show, Master Plan, I have to confess that working in the hobby + full time job has meant that I've only put out one episode some GenCon. But I suspect that my own media production will pick back up in early 2010. I shamefully haven't put together the interview I did with John Harper back in fucking June, because of stuff like The Dresden Files RPG, conventions, Mythender, The Voice of the Revolution, etc. (Or the other four interviews I have backlogged.)

    For the podcaster culture in general, I'm honestly excited about fewer podcasts these days, but that's because of me & my interests and not because of podcasting. I could wax a bit about independent creator spheres and what happens as they grow large, shit like that, but I won't. I will say that tastes shift, people get tired of doing the same thing, and (speaking as someone who just realized today that it's his third anniversary as a podcaster) our excitement wains as time goes on. So, the "old guard" (of which I doubt I'm a part of) is looking to do different stuff, even if it's podcasting in a new way (look at Judd on CP, for example).

    All that is to say: sure, the Golden Age of Podcasting is over, but that's also making room for a new Golden Age to begin, one that doesn't feel weirdly pressured to compete with ongoing efforts of folks like Tevis or the SoK crew or shit like that. One that'll come up with cool stuff. One that's coming up with cool stuff right now, but many of us aren't sure where to look to find that.

    Unfortunately, you cannot hear my inflection (which is why I prefer podcasting over blogging, incidentally), but I'm intending to sound wistfully hopeful. Like, "sure, that thing you liked has come and gone. wasn't it cool? and isn't it awesome that other people are trying to find new ways to fill that void? yay!"

    Also, this is just my damned opinion, nothing I offer as fact or truth.

    - Ryan
