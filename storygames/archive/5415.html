\n First go at Kiran Calligraphy for a fan who wants to use alien characters as handouts to his players. I usually have all this type of stuff floating around in my head so mostly a case of taking the abstract pieces and creating concrete implementation. For me, it is easiest to handle such things with the language in one hand while working on the calligraphy alongside.
\n
\nMain Questions:
\n1. General impressions? Too oriental?
\n2. Any thoughts on the structure? Should characters be tonal instead?
\n3. Suggestions on words that should be the top of the list to be defined?
\n
\nInput welcome.
\n

\nDefinitions
\nKrios-Kjet
\nDictionary
\nKiro = 1. Home; 2. Homeworld
\nWassat = 1. War;2. Conflict
\nObiri = 1. n. Sister race to the Kiran. 2. Adj. Inferior, lacking merit. 3. Adv. Cowardly
\nOsee = 1. The number 1. 2. A single strong warrior
\nOnee = 1. The number 2. 2. A loyal companion
\nObea = 1. The number 3. 2. A trusted third
\nOree = 1. The number 4. 2. The last in a line
\nOkee = 1. The number 5. 2. A person left behind during war. 3. An unlucky number

    \n A few things to consider with calligraphy or any other kind of writing include:

    What is the instrument that was originally used to write these characters? Was it a brush covered in ink, as in Asia? Was is a shaved feather dipped in ink, as in the West? How was the instrument held? Like a pencil, as in the West? Like you're giving the "thumbs-up" sign, as in Asian calligraphy? How does the original instrument and writing position affect the way the characters are formed? For example, how is it used to make both the thick and thin lines in "Obiri"? What order are the strokes in "Wassat" generally written in? For example, Chinese is generally written top-to-bottom, right-to-left, horizontal-before-vertical, and inside-before-outside. Probably, over hundreds of years of writing characters in a specific method, the characters will change to make them easier and faster to write. For example, in Chinese, squares are written with three strokes instead of four, which is a lot faster. If you have a clear sense of how the writing works, then your characters will look more consistent and, ideally, reflect something about the culture they come from.

    \n Here's a couple of thoughts: Not a direct response.
    1) It's definitely brush-stroky (rather than calligraphy-pennish, which would give a western feeling) but it doesn't look at all like Chinese.
    2) The characters are too wild: in wasat and kiro there's tiny little line variations everywhere. Either these aliens have extraordinary pattern recognition and muscle control, or (more likely), these are written very sloppily.
    2a) The variance in line-width is particularly hard to pull of when writing. You'd either need multiple brushes for one character or you'd need extraordinary muscle control, in which case I'd expect the characters to be more detailed and less chaotic.

    \n Pointing to Johnathan's post above: Think about the tools that the ancients in this culture had access to.

Cuneiform, basically.

    If you're creating an alphabet not originally English, make sure to invent characters for new or unexpressable sounds. The Russian zh, or sc'ch are cool examples of that.
    Or make a character for a full or glottal stop. Or again, pulling on Russian, the malkiz'nak/tvorgi'znak, the "hard sound/soft sound" letters (really they don't do much, but they could add some creative alien-ness to the language).

    BTW, are Kiran humans? Or are they an alien race with a different physiology (claws, etc). Cause that might show up in the thinking/lettering as well.

    \n Sweet!

    Here's the basics: please ask for clarifications, etc.
    No script (on Earth) has variable line width. There are reasons for this: it's not just dumb luck. The primary reason is that we much more easily recognize shapes than patterns. The secondary reason is that variable line width is am absolute son of a bitch for writing. Therefore, if an alien script has variable line width, we have to assume that, first, they're super good at pattern recognition and, second, that they have such astonishing fine muscle control that it's not only easy to do variable line width with a single implement, but that it is not detectably easier for them to write with consistent line width instead. So that says something really interesting about the species. If this species doesn't have those capacities, I would imagine that their writing would look different.

    Secondarily, the top three drawings (the non-number characters) seem very "scribbly." Look, for instance at "Kiro." There's a tiny white dot in the middle of the character. So, seeing this as a primal character form, I have to assume that that tiny white dot is integral to the meaning of the character: if it wasn't there, the character would either mean something else or perhaps mean nothing at all. Why do I make this assumption? Because, over time, scripts tend to evolve to the simplest possible forms, leaving off anything un-needed for the meaning. There's a lot of things in those characters which make me go "Is that angle/dot/swirl meaning-bearing? Really?"

    Most of my problems are with the top three. The number characters are really nice. I get the sense that these are scratched into clay or dirt, using a number of different fingers at the same time. The fifth one is a little bit awkward-looking, though, but that's because my assumption is that it would be written with five fingers at once. Is that true? How many fingers do these people have?

    \n Some thoughts:

    Left handedness: As a left handed I have to say... specularly reverse all your characters

    RTL (right-to-left) languages will naturally tend to be slanted towards the left: I learned stenography with the italian system "Cima", and it's a graphic form of writing that both abbreviates AND is designed for speed of writing. Being developed for right handed people it is full of lines that go from bottom-left to top-right (like the forward slash character "/"). It was a hell for me as a lefthanded to learn and write it.

    --EDIT--
    Here is a (horrid) webpage with some samples of a textbook for that writing system
    Stenography with the Cima System
    --EDIT--

    Looking at your "number" characters: I like the looks, but they are awkward for me to write.

    If I think at a natural way to draw them, I imagine mirroring them either horizontally (the main stroke becomes top-right to bottom-left) or vertically (the main stroke becomes bottom-right to top-left). Either way I'm pulling the writing point toward my palm, and I am not covering the already written part of the character with my hand or the writing point.


    As for the word you are looking for, it's not "tonal", but "phonetic". Phonetic languages and writing systems represent the sounds of the syllables with one or more graphic signs, and those are VERY consistent. For example, Italian (my native language) is written phonetically. We have less phonetic sounds, true, but those we have are represented the same way every time. If an italian reads a word he has never seen or heard he knows how to pronounce it. The only point of confusion can be the tonal accent, which has a default, but it's not always that consistent. In those cases the tonal accent is often explicitly written.

    E. g.:

    "Anc\xf3ra" means "another time", or "again"

    while

    "\xc1ncora" means "anchor"

    I think that referring to different alphabets/writing systems you are thinking about the different japanese systems: Kanji is derived from chinese writing and is the "main true language/writing", while Hiragana and Katakana are phonetic, and in particular Katakana has a pretty simple transliteration method to reproduce (more or less) the perceived pronuntiation of the foreign word that the japanese need to represent.

    \n Hi Bill,

    A couple comments on "claw-writing": if they have different-sized claws/fingers that they use to differentiate lines, then line weight is subjective, based on the individual. This could introduce ambiguity in single characters. If the character is relatively simple, it will only have a few lines in it, and a viewer may not be able to tell how heavy those lines are, without reference. Are those thick lines drawn by a small Kiran, or thin lines drawn by a large Kiran?

    This would be more of a problem with an alphabet (one sound = one letter, or thereabouts), where symbols need to be simple, since you write a lot of them.
    If symbols represent syllables, they can get more complicated, and if they are like Chinese, each symbol will probably be very different and have multiple line weights.
    It looks like you are going for complicated ideographs, so this may not be a problem you want to introduce into your calligraphy.

    (To be clear - all scripts have problems, and building disadvantages into your fake scripts gives them character. I'm not suggesting this as a problem that needs to be solved, just something that might give it some color.)

    Also, I think you should take another look at cuneiform. Even though it's mostly pressing marks, not lines, you can see a lot of parallel, or stacked marks. A being with multiple claws doesn't need to write with just one at a time. They could drag two or three claws across the clay (or paper), making multiple, parallel lines. You don't have anything like that in your calligraphy so far. If you want to get really into this, try fingerpainting with ink, and see what you come up with.

    The other thing, is that you can poke holes in the clay with your claws, creating dots. Multiple dots, in certain configurations, if you use multiple claws/fingers. This doesn't translate over into ink and paper writing, though. Instead, the Kiran could make dots with their knuckles and finger-joints. This will end up changing the configuration and whatnot, but all scripts evolve over time. Even the touch-pad is going to change the ink and paper style.
