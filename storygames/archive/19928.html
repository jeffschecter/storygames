\n Ok, we\u2019re off the ground with this one. I had an idea for a system a few days ago and wrote it up last night. Resolution is mostly through player voting based on the roleplaying. My original concept was for the characters to have a bunch of skills, which they would roll against at various points, but I now think that would be rather clunky, and would be likely to impede the flow of what is essentially a knockabout \u2018nutty professor\u2019 type game. Anyway, let me know what y\u2019all think\u2026

    \n Example scene:

    Tesla and Fermi are in the grocery store. Fermi is engaging the hapless store clerk in a conversation about trans-uranic elements, when Tesla, who\u2019s been examining some dodgy wiring at the back of the shop, comes over, sees Fermi talking and gesticulating and says flatly, So, did you get the eggs? Fermi rounds on him: Eggs? Eggs? What is it with you and eggs? You\u2019re obsessed with eggs. He looks over at the GM, and asks if the shop has eggs. The GM replies Yes, there are several boxes on the counter behind you, at which Fermi whirls round, grabs the boxes, opens them, and starts randomly throwing eggs at Tesla, who after a few seconds pushes Fermi back against some shelving, which collapses onto the floor with Fermi joining it soon after. Tesla starts vainly trying to rub the egg mess from his suit and cursing Fermi, saying Damn you Enrico, I just bought this suit last week, and now you\u2019ve ruined it.

    At which point Marie Curie enters, looks at Tesla wiping his suit, and says to him I know what you need for that. Tesla looks at her, sighs heavily, holds up his hands and says No, don\u2019t tell me\u2026 and they both say at once, Radium!

    \n So I get the joke here, of using terribly interesting people to tell the most banal stories imaginable, but I'm struggling to understand what keeps players engaged.

    From first reading, the voting mechanic looks like a prompt to try and make each other laugh as much as possible. Problem is, the normal social incentives for this behavior are already so high that the voting structure is, I think, somewhere between superfluous and obstructive to the goal.

    What problem does the GM solve in this game? Given that you're also assuming there will be some inactive players in each scene, the GM's role here feels pretty week. Being subject to another player's authority on the imaginary space will also limit improvisational freedom.

    Basically, the most compelling version of this that I can imagine looks something like a Fiasco playset, and I'm wondering what you want this game to do beyond that.

    I also don't hate like the original idea of using some defined set of traits. Like, if each character has three skills, but they're all things like "planar algebra," and "string theory" there's comedy in hearing players justify of the use of these skills in everyday situations.

    \n Thanks for your comments dude. All feedback is welcome grist to a game designer\u2019s mill, helping them as it does to crystallise their ideas about their game. I\u2019ll speak to your points directly, but before doing so I\u2019ll make a couple of observations. First, I think we\u2019re coming from quite different directions here, and that fundamentally Great Minds isn\u2019t your cup of tea (nothing wrong with that, most games are not for everyone), so I\u2019m not sure as to what extent I'll be able to overcome your objections to it. Secondly, as I say above, what you\u2019re seeing is only a first draft, and is subject to potentially radical change during playtesting. \n
    So I get the joke here, of using terribly interesting people to tell the most banal stories imaginable, but I'm struggling to understand what keeps players engaged.
    \nIf it was a case of terribly interesting people telling the most banal stories imaginable it would indeed be unspeakably dull. But it\u2019s not that. It\u2019s more a case of fish out of water, i.e. people who have been to a large extent cosseted in their ivory towers previously, but who are now having to do things that are way outside their frame of reference. As to what keeps the players engaged, since players\u2019 motivations, likes and interests are extremely varied, and unpredictable session by session, I\u2019d struggle to explain how most of the games I know keep them engaged, so I won\u2019t attempt to answer that here. \n
    From first reading, the voting mechanic looks like a prompt to try and make each other laugh as much as possible. Problem is, the normal social incentives for this behavior are already so high that the voting structure is, I think, somewhere between superfluous and obstructive to the goal.
    \nThis kind of voting mechanic is becoming increasingly common nowadays, appearing as it does in games as diverse as Forsooth and Tenra Bansho Zero. It\u2019s not really about making each other laugh (although that may be a side-effect), it\u2019s more a way of giving players an incentive to actively roleplay their character. \n
    What problem does the GM solve in this game?
    \nDoes there have to be a \u2018problem\u2019 that having a GM solves? Can\u2019t you just \u2018have a GM\u2019 who has role x, y or z? Assuming the latter, the GM\u2019s role is to set tasks for the players and assess the degree to which they have solved them, and to act as a provocateur, introducing NPCs that have the effect of wandering monsters, in other words to be a sort of puppet-master. He or she also is in charge of dishing out (or withholding) the d4s. However, someone on another forum suggested having a rotating GM, which I could definitely accept, and which is something that would help in games where there are only three players (including the facilitator). Part of my reply to that comment was that I\u2019d never been of the school of thought that says that all storygames must be GMless, but I'm fine with having GMlessness as an option for those who prefer that mode of play. \n
    Basically, the most compelling version of this that I can imagine looks something like a Fiasco playset, and I'm wondering what you want this game to do beyond that.
    \nI don\u2019t want the game to do anything as such, I just want a game where players do stuff during it, which the players may or may not find enjoyable. Secondly, I don\u2019t think it\u2019s a very helpful starting point in terms of game design to have a particular game (in this case Fiasco), which you map your own game onto to varying degrees, or shrink back from, or do slightly differently from, or go beyond or whatever. I think it\u2019s much better to just come up with an idea for a game, decide what you want the players to do, and come up with some mechanics to allow them to do that. \n
    I also don't hate like the original idea of using some defined set of traits. Like, if each character has three skills, but they're all things like "planar algebra," and "string theory" there's comedy in hearing players justify of the use of these skills in everyday situations.
    \nNo, they\u2019ll still be there, in fact they\u2019ll be the bedrock of the char sheet, but my idea now is that, rather than have the players rolling against them during each scene, they\u2019ll use them as a basis for both the roleplaying and the solving of the tasks.

    As I say, I think we\u2019re coming from different directions about both this game and game design in general, but thanks again for your comments, and I hope I\u2019ve gone some way towards answering the points you raised.

    \nI may in the end opt to publish the game as a generative system, in which Great Minds will be but one playset, as with Fiasco, but my personal preference is for the way I currently have it, i.e. a default game, along with suggested alternatives, as with Hillfolk and DramaSystem. \n

    \nOk, so I\u2019m definitely going to include in the book instructions for generating characters and playsets. Try this for size:

    Premise
    The characters should have been forced to leave their previous environment for one reason or another. They could have been expelled by the local Lord, be fleeing a revolution, been sacked from their jobs, or have moved due to various unforeseen circumstances. Whatever the reason, they are now well out of their \u2018comfort zone\u2019, and having to try to get by in surroundings- and among people- that they don\u2019t understand, lack empathy for, or are in some other sense way beyond their previous frame of reference.

    Character generation
    Character generation should be fairly pared down, with some characteristics relevant to the scenario type, for example the Great Minds of the title have education, career trajectory and notable successes (Nobel Prize etc.), whereas the French aristos of the Sacre Bleu playset have their previous position at court (such as Chancellor, Master of The Bedchamber etc.), jealousies and rivalries, what kind of estate and family they had in France and so on.

    Scene Zero
    Scene Zero places them in their new abode (whether poky flat, farmhouse, tenement building etc.), and again they will be the sort of people who would chafe at these new surroundings, with the claustrophobia perhaps engendering internal antipathies and flashpoints (over who should take out the rubbish or clean the oven, who\u2019s always leaving their socks drying on the hearth, and Albert, must, you smoke that filthy pipe in here?, and so on).

    Tasks
    The tasks should ideally challenge the characters by forcing them to do things that stretch their patience with their new surroundings (and each other) to breaking point, or simply- as in the case of the scientists- be so mundane as to open up a yawning chasm between their previous experience (for example working out how gravity operates) and their current exigencies (getting a cat out of a tree, or a gravy stain out of a pair of trousers). Also, there should be plenty of scope for encountering NPCs, who could comprise anybody from passers-by, neighbours and local shopkeepers (or inn-keepers in the case of the Elizabethan playwrights) to Police officers, street sweepers or officials of one kind of another.

    However, right from the beginning the party should have explained to them the final goal, whether a party, or official engagement, or some other event, at which their performance will decide their ultimate fate, whether it be a high profile job in their new environment, or being able to return to their previous one, and for which their successes along the way will improve their chances (in the form of a gradually increasing dice pool).

    Final scene
    This is the event at which the characters\u2019 fates are sealed, whether for good or ill. After rolling the dice pool they have accumulated by that point, the resulting score will determine who will succeed, and by how much. The players then roleplay their success or failure.

    \n In response to the following post on another forum, I came up with another hack, this time involving Graeco-Roman gods!

    Are there currently any mechanics systems for player owned stores? Thor decided enjoys fighting frost giants but has decided he wants to open up a bakery. He is going to hire staff, pay for the construction of the building and buy supplies for his bakers. This is all easy the next stage is profit & customers

    Ye gods!

    Premise:
    You're a bunch of gods that have been chucked out of Olympus for various misdemeanours and exiled to Earth, where you still have your powers but have to use them for the good of humanity. Trouble is, it's very tempting to just fry someone with a lightning bolt rather than say I'm sorry, I think you'll find that's my parking space. In order to try to fit in, and to make some money, you have all decided to open a bar. After all, what could possibly go wrong?

    Scene Zero:
    Each of you in turn relates what you did that annoyed Zeus so much that he sent you to live on this miserable, backward world, populated by mind-numbingly primitive lifeforms.

    Tasks:
    In each scene, the GM creates an event - running out of beer, having a spot Health & Safety inspection, reporting a burglary to the Police etc. - which you have to try to deal with without reducing anybody to a cinder, turning them into a pig or causing an earthquake etc.

    Final scene:
    You're summoned back to Olympus after your period of exile, and called upon by Zeus to give an account of yourselves. According to the way the dice fall, you could then be either completely re-instated to the council, sent back to Earth to make up for all the mayhem you caused while you were there, or exiled to Earth permanently.

    Note that leaving aside Zeus/Hera, the G-R Pantheon is equal parts male and female.

    Character generation should be fairly pared down, with some characteristics relevant to the scenario type, for example the Great Minds of the title have education, career trajectory and notable successes (Nobel Prize etc.), whereas the French aristos of the Sacre Bleu playset have their previous position at court (such as Chancellor, Master of The Bedchamber etc.), jealousies and rivalries, what kind of estate and family they had in France and so on.

    Scene Zero
    Scene Zero places them in their new abode (whether poky flat, farmhouse, tenement building etc.), and again they will be the sort of people who would chafe at these new surroundings, with the claustrophobia perhaps engendering internal antipathies and flashpoints (over who should take out the rubbish or clean the oven, who\u2019s always leaving their socks drying on the hearth, and Albert, must you smoke that filthy pipe in here?, and so on).

    Tasks
    The tasks should ideally challenge the characters by forcing them to do things that stretch their patience with their new surroundings (and each other) to breaking point, or simply- as in the case of the scientists- be so mundane as to open up a yawning chasm between their previous experience (for example working out how gravity operates) and their current exigencies (getting a cat out of a tree, or a gravy stain out of a pair of trousers). Also, there should be plenty of scope for encountering NPCs, who could comprise anybody from passers-by, neighbours and local shopkeepers (or inn-keepers in the case of the Elizabethan playwrights) to Police officers, street sweepers or officials of one kind of another.

    However, right from the beginning the party should have explained to them the final goal, whether a party, or official engagement, or some other event, at which their performance will decide their ultimate fate, whether it be a high profile job in their new environment, or being able to return to their previous one, and for which their successes along the way will improve their chances (in the form of a gradually increasing dice pool).

    Final scene
    This the event at which the characters\u2019 fates are sealed, whether for good or ill. After rolling the dice pool they have accumulated by that point, the resulting score will determine who will succeed, and by how much. The players then roleplay their success or failure.
