Tables to Dice to ... Tables?

Blue
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
I put this in little ideas as I'm not sure where this would go from here, but the concept is intriguing.

On my bookshelves (my gaming bookshelves) I have a number of games that use lookup tables to determine results. You know the ones, I roll dice and look up what happens or you and I roll dice and we cross-reference to see what happens.

I remember a distinct rebellion against tables in games that followed some time after. "Looking things up on tables slows things down." And being a math-oriented guy, I remember working out some dice-only results that translated some of those tables to table-less play.

This morning, I was listening to the Vintage Gamer podcast episode on "The Fury of Dracula". In it, tables are used to determine the results when two combat strategies (one for Dracula and one for the hunters) are choosen. Specifically, the table referenced is that of the winner which is determined by a die roll. Now this made a certain amount of sense to me - as what happens to Draacula when Van Helsing successfully punches Dracula should be radically differnet from what happens to Van Helsing when Dracula successfully bites the hunter.

Which got me to wondering - is there a place for reference tables in modern games, particularly if we can find a more clever way to use or include them?

I will admit part of this is inspired by other discussions here, including the character sheet design competion (which should be moving to game design phase any time now). Could a character "sheet" be a hand of cards each with a different table on them - representing a different range of results when different strategies are applied against one another?

Another idea, could a different results table be used depending where you are in the story (fixed or random)? For instance, modeling along the lines of "With Great Power" - early in the game, results tend to favor the bad guys; but as the story arc progresses, the heroes gain the advantage. Applied randomly, this concept seems like an extension of the old TSR SAGA rules where the GM played one card that applied for the round.

The designer in me actually fears this idea a bit, as designing multiple/many different tables sounds like a lot of work (and a lot more playtesting), but finds it conceptually appealing.

Any thoughts?

    Tables in session prep is good.

    Tables organize large amounts of information such that you can winnow out the 99% of the information you don't need quickly.

    I'll make up a new term here, highly irreverent and probably soon to be forgotten: the PIS. Possible Imagined Space. The PIS is the multiverse of possible imagined spaces, as infinite as the game will allow (some more than others; Waiting/Tea has a very small one). You need powerful tools to navigate it in session prep.

    Tables in play is bad. If you're discarding 99% of the information you're encountering in play, then you're encountering too much information.

    In one sense, I'm very against tables as part of resolution. On the other hand, the way Cold Iron uses a table in resolution to make use of the normal distribution for open ended resolution is just hard to pass up. Sure, it's possible to do have open ended resolution without a table lookup, but such methods always have ugly mathematics. One thing that makes the Cold Iron table not so bad though is the fact that it's easy to memorize the most useful points on the table. Usually you have a 50% or less chance of success, so you only have to memorize the top half of the table, combined with the fact that 70% of the top half of the table falls into 8 slots. What it tends to mean is that if you have to look up the result, something exciting is about to happen (you either rolled high, or you have much more than a 50% chance of success, so your low roll is still going to be a success).

    Tables used to delay prep into play are also ok. Random encounter tables, when random encounters make sense, are good. Random tables to generate some bit of color when a player asks you for a detail you hadn't prepared are good.

    Having to determine the right column to look up in a table, then generate a result, modify it, then look up on a sub-table, etc. ad nauseum is bad. Especially when halfway through the process you are reminded of a niggling factor which changes the column...

    Tables that can trivially be removed are not good (for example, the original D&D table, that was replaceable with a THAC0).

    Fudge amuses me here. In the attempt to de-math the system, a table is used to convert numeric results to words, except you have to convert back to numbers in your head so you can add them (quick, what is superb+fair?).

    Frank

    So you've got a table of six by six fields, based on the roll of a six-sided die along each axis. When it's your turn you roll the dice and pick whichever ordering you like better (so if I roll a 3 and a 1, I could pick (1, 3) or (3, 1)). If that space is blank, say what you're doing and write a vague description of it ("kick some ass" or "sleep with him"). If it's not, you do what it says in the slot, preferably by finding a new and interesting interpretation of the phrase. Then pass the dice left. Game ends when the last space is filled in.

    So you've got a universe of possibilities that becomes increasingly constrained as time goes on. Does anybody remember the Castle Terminus at the end of the universe in Neil Gaiman's The Books of Magic? Would this be a good way to play a collection of archetypes slowly winking out of existence?     I think that one shouldn't consider any method bad a priori. The question with any mechanic is whether or not the search and handle time involved justifies the quality of the feedback. That's going to depend entirely on the specifics of the design. Tables have high search and handle times. So the results had better be damn good to justify them. But I'd say that's far from impossible.

    What other people have said can be crunched down into the elegance principle, which applied here says that if there's a way to produce the same quality of feedback using less search and handle time, use that instead. Pretty obvious.

    So I wouldn't discard tables out of hand. Just ensure that it's worth it, and that there isn't an easier way.

    Mike
