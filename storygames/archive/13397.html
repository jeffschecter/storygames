    \n I took Kagematsu and Maid to MegaCon last year (which is primarily anime, gaming is kind of an afterthought) and both worked out well.

    I heartily second The Mountain Witch, but hey, don't be afraid to hack some systems too.

    Misspent Youth could easily hack into Akira. Two quick ideas for that one: Have the group of kids before Tetsuo's powers awaken, with The Authority being represented by the Army and the Japanese Government. Or crank it up to eleven, and have Kaneda's group of Youthful Offenders fight against Tetsuo and Akira as The Authority. Heck, do both, one right after another.

    Remember Tomorrow is cyberpunk, and could be thrown into any anime setting, really. Wide open playpen for conflict and fun.

    Yes to Panty Explosion. Double yes. It has a depth of play that isn't indicated by the title. Or, at least, it can with the right group.

    Yes to that Anime Convention Fiasco playset.

    \n
    \nWell since Kaidan is listed above and I am the concept creator for that setting, I thought I would respond. I am half Japanese and have made a study of Japanese history, culture, religion and folklore all my life - as well as a gamer. I've been 'meh' about much of D&D's venture into the east with Oriental Adventures, even with L5R's world (which seems more like China to me). While Kaidan is not Japan, so I didn't need to match history, it is more authentically Japanese than any game setting previously design, at least IMO. There are lots and lots of details many of them little known to the west included in the setting.
    \n
    \nKaidan is the Japanese word for 'ghost or horror story' which is the concept it is built around. Kaidan is a Japan-inspired horror setting, with elements of Oriental Adventures and Ravenloft built in. The social caste system is paramount to the system. PCs are tied by birth to their relevant social castes and forced into a cycle of reincarnations (a twisted version of it) linked with their karma score, and cannot only move on the social ladder through death and soul transference.
    \n
    \nTo automatically assume, that this must not be an authentic Japanese setting without even looking at it, seems a bit off putting. Give it a chance. So far reviews for all three adventures combined (about a dozen in all) have all been 5 star ratings.
    \n
    \nCheck it out!
    \n
    \nMichael
    \n
    \nPS: and why not Indian/Mongolian/Chinese/SE-Asian? I am Japanese and I wanted to capture Japanese culture as it has not been effectively done in any previous treatment. Perhaps I can look at doing something for other Asian cultures down the road, but Kaidan is of my heritage so it was important for me to get it right.

    \n Sengoku is good, but it's truly Japan, I needed an analog that I could work into using for Pathfinder rules. Sengoku wouldn't really adapt well to a D&D trope game - it too realistic in its approach with magical things that are more subtle.

    Plus everybody else publishes settings that are Sengoku or during the Tokugawa Era, I wanted to build a setting of the rough times before Sengoku just after the fall of the classical Heian period - as a build up towards a Sengoku political atmosphere. Also being an older time, it has more of a ancient quality that I sought in my setting design.

    But Sengoku is good is you want to more closely emulate an historical Japan.

    \n But the other thing, while my setting focuses on the technology and tropes of pre-Sengoku Japan, it is not Japan rather just a fictional analog. I don't want real Japan, that comes with too much baggage and expectations. Being a complete fiction allows me to bring in as much concept for the real Japan that I want, while not having to comply with realworld history. While elements of Kaidan represent an answer to the 'what if' situation where the Taira actually win the Genpei War against the Minamoto, thus the Kamakura bakufu never forms, and the true post-Heian Period is never realized in the historic sense. My setting establishes a shogunate headed by the Taira clan, with an undying child emperor, Antoku (in the mind of a five year old) remaining on the throne so that his relatives at court remain the rulers for eternity - or at least until Kaidan is somehow negated or destroyed. My real goal is bringing the many ghost and horror stories of ancient Japan to the forefront as the kind of encounters that adventurers in Kaidan can face, and touch upon tropes hardly known in RPGs or other western media, today.
