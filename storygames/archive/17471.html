\n An interesting thing emerged in the recent resurrection of the "Brain Damage" discussion, which was a critique of the whole idea of Story Now based on a critique of Lajos Egri's ideas on drama. I found that interesting because it implies that Egri's ideas are some how definitive to the idea of Story Now. I really don't find them to be. Egri's ideas were certainly important to Ron when he wrote those old GNS essays, but since then things have moved on, I don't think Egri has been part of the discussion since, uh... I'd say 5-6 years.

I still think the idea of Premise--a moral/dramatic question that is answered by the fiction--is a real thing, but at this point I recognize Premise as an emergent property of Story Now, not something that is necessarily planned for. In other words, Premise is something that largely becomes apparent upon reflection of the fiction, going into play (and even sometimes during play) you don't really know what the true Premise will be.

At this point I would define Story Now something like this:\n
Story Now is play in which the purpose and focus--consciously or subconsciously--is the expression of some sort of emotion, idea, and/or theme through the medium of improvisational storytelling.
\nThere are three important bits here:

1) It's improvisational
This is the "now" part of Story Now. There's some leeway in how you define improvisation, but the idea is that you create the fiction as you go along.

2) It's about "expressing" or otherwise communicating some sort of emotion, idea, or theme
Story Now is inherently artistic. This is why the idea of Premise or theme comes up, because the participant--either consciously or subconsciously--is expressing and communicating something inside themselves (like all artists do) through the decisions they make in play.

3) This "expression" is the purpose and focus of play
In my mind this is the most difficult part to discuss, because theme can emerge as a byproduct of play those priorities lie elsewhere. But Story Now requires that all of participants--again, consciously or subconsciously--are actively involved in the creation of this "expression".

Please note: While Story Now is inherently about storytelling, I make no attempt to define "story". I don't believe that's actually necessary, I'm perfectly fine with people using their own definitions and structures of "story".

    \n Egri's ideas aren't specific to Egri. I perpetually recommend David Mamet's 'On Directing Film' which offers the same theory of theme/premise as Egri. And Mamet's book explicitly traces its ideas back to Aristotle's 'Poetics'. There's a vast body of scholarship based on Aristotle.
    So... to divorce Story Now from Egri is to divorce rpg theory from kindred areas of culture, such as literature and theatre.
    I think there's a case to be made For such a divorce, and a case to be made Against it.

    Pro-Divorce: If you want to emphasize the ways that rpgs are unique from similar forms, and develop that uniqueness, rather than copycat literature and theatre.

    Anti-Divorce: if you want to benefit from the centuries of wisdom developed in other forms of storytelling, keep rpg discussion intelligible to people schooled in those other areas, and make games which emphasize the best aspects of those other areas.

    I am in the anti-divorce camp. For the last handful of years, I see Vincent Baker as tending toward the pro-divorce camp... to name just one example. The pro-divorce camp seems predominant to me, but maybe that's just because I'm so firmly on the other side.

    Story Now was formerly in the Egri camp, and I don't think it's possible to divorce it from that, without Story Now evolving into a different thing... which would (confusingly) maintain the same name.

    \n So here's a cheesy and simplistic example:

    A player is feeling really happy, but goes to play that woe-is-me vampire romance Jason mentioned earlier. If the player acts out an angsty vampire because that's what expected for the genre, then they aren't expressing the happiness they feel inside. Thus it's not Story Now. A Story Now player would want to somehow express that feeling of happiness, maybe by generating a theme of hope or redemption or something.

    A place where it can get complicated is when a depressed or frustrated person goes to play that same woe-is-me vampire romance. In that scenario, the angsty vampire provides a platform for the player to vent their frustration/anger/sadness/whatever. Under those types of conditions, the genre tropes play into the thematic/artistic statement that the player wants to make, and thus enables Story Now, rather than hinder it. I think this is something that happens a lot--players choose genres (either consciously or subconsciously) that contain themes that they want explore (ie,"express").

    \n This is what I have to say about keeping or abandoning Egri:

    Once we accept that there's this thing we're trying to accomplish--namely, express some sort of artistic impulse through improvisational storytelling--certain questions naturally arise: How do we accomplish that goal in a meaningful and satisfying way?

    \n Jason makes good points, certainly. That's narrativist play he's describing, it seems to me; he's taking artistic responsibility, he's making brother Brown act in the way that seems true to life to him and to hell with genre expectations. I also don't see much credence in the idea that the player's personal emotional life would necessarily be reflected in narrativist play; I've had that sort of play, but most of the times I do solid, basic narrativism it is no more personal than any other creative agenda.

    Perhaps if I had to explain narrativism without Egri I would focus on how the players view artistic responsibility at the table. If your play is performance that you need to get right, then that seems like simulationism, while if your play is a statement you have a right to as an empowered participant, then it's narrativism. Note that I don't care whether your performance or statement has a solid basis on e.g. genre here - you can do very genre-faithful play that is still narrativist as long as your relationship to this background expectation is narrativist in nature. Thus e.g. Dust Devils remains narrativist even as players will usually reference genre all the time; the taste of their play is that they discuss genre and relate to it, not that they instantiate it faithfully.

    Fortunately I personally have no need to get rid of the Premise and other internal constructions of the standard theory of how narrativism works, as it seems to be a bit tricky to come up with useful and true alternative formulations. Still, it's useful to work on this sort of thing, as it might produce an alternative internal model; if it did, that in turn would enable new ideas for game design. Interesting topic in that regard.

    \n Whose idea was it to start unearthing old discussion topics, anyway? The only solace I can offer to the easily annoyed is that you don't have to participate in the pointless discussions. That's what I do.

    About moral vs. artistic, Jason: you've expressed your dislike of the "moral" thing in the past, and it seems to me that this might just derive from a somewhat narrow reading of what is intended with the word. When I myself use narrativist theory, that "moral premise" thing gets to stand in for quite a wide range of thematic potentials. I don't feel the term entirely far-fetched myself because it does draw attention to how socially pertinent the premise and theme necessarily are, even when they go past ethics into other matters of life. Ultimately theme is about philosophical communication and characterizing it as "morally based" is just a particularly tight-assed way of describing this. "Morality" is often used in ethics to distinguish discussion of human behavior from discussion of goodness proper (ethical good vs. moral good), which I understand is the sense when Egri claims that literary theme arises from a moral premise.

    This is of course not to say that literary theme is the entirety of artistic expression. There are artistic (and aesthetic) qualities that fall beyond the scope of narrativism; Ron seems to think that these are non-dramatic artistic expressions, and I agree with the idea to a large degree. For example, poetry does not necessarily get structured out of a moral premise because poetry does not have to involve storytelling or drama in the Egrian sense; it's literature and art without being story.

    This comes back to the idea that you can have artistic impulses without them being particularly moral in nature. I agree insofar as there are arts that are not about constructing compelling stories, but I also think that these types of artistic impulses simply don't pertain to narrativism directly. You can express an artistic impulse for beautiful expression alongside any creative agenda, for example, and we regularly do. I've had some moments of rather beautiful narration in our fully gamist D&D campaign, for example.

    Perhaps we could attempt to develop some example of story creation as a creative act that didn't have a moral core (in the wide sense), but all the examples of such that I can think of are of a nature where I'm quite comfortable dismissing it as a non-dramatic story or a creative act that has other virtues aside from plot (which is basically the thing narrativism is concerned with - how to have an emotionally compelling plot).

    \nI'm fine calling a GM's campaign material an authentic artistic expression (given that it meets the prerequiste definition of "artistic", yadda yadda), but that doesn't necessarily make it Story Now.

    - If the campaign plot is all prepped ahead of time, and the GM is essentially just running through their script, then that play fails on the improvisational front.

    - If the GM is artistically engaged in improvisational storytelling, but the rest of group is only engaged in a tactical or simulationist sense, then that play would be classified as incoherent, since the group is divided on their purpose and focus.

    Nowadays I don't really adhere to the Big Model in my own thinking (and some of that might be showing through in this discussion), but I'm not trying to push that here. I really believe that what I'm saying here is in line with the original essays... Or rather, I think I'm peering through the original Narrativism essay and uncovering what the essay was pointing towards.

    Here's the section of the essay where Ron defines Story Now:
    Story Now requires that at least one engaging issue or problematic feature of human existence be addressed in the process of role-playing. "Address" means:
    - Establishing the issue's Explorative expressions in the game-world, "fixing" them into imaginary place.

    - Developing the issue as a source of continued conflict, perhaps changing any number of things about it, such as which side is being taken by a given character, or providing more depth to why the antagonistic side of the issue exists at all.

    - Resolving the issue through the decisions of the players of the protagonists, as well as various features and constraints of the circumstances.
    \nCan it really be that easy? Yes, Narrativism is that easy. The Now refers to the people, during actual play, focusing their imagination to create those emotional moments of decision-making and action, and paying attention to one another as they do it. To do that, they relate to "the story" very much as authors do for novels, as playwrights do for plays, and screenwriters do for film at the creative moment or moments. Think of the Now as meaning, "in the moment," or "engaged in doing it," in terms of input and emotional feedback among one another. The Now also means "get to it," in which "it" refers to any Explorative element or combination of elements that increases the enjoyment of that issue I'm talking about.

    There cannot be any "the story" during Narrativist play, because to have such a thing (fixed plot or pre-agreed theme) is to remove the whole point: the creative moments of addressing the issue(s). Story Now has a great deal in common with Step On Up, particularly in the social expectation to contribute, but in this case the real people's attention is directed toward one another's insights toward the issue, rather than toward strategy and guts.
    Now, Ron does specifically discuss expressing one's artistic impulse (ie, "engaging issue") through conflict. I do think that confuses "what" we do with "how" we accomplish it (as I implied earlier in the discussion). So in that regard I might be broadening the definition a little, but I believe people's understanding of Story Now has already been broadened over the intervening years, so I don't believe I'm rocking the boat right now.
