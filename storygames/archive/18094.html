    \n The epitome of the kitshoutenketsu style is slice of life 4-panel manga like Azumanga Daioh and Hidamari Sketch, and for a while I've been trying to make a game based on that genre called Raspberry Heaven. I ended up having to drop my attempt at closely cleaving to the 4-part structure, and in its currently incarnation the game is, well, a positive Fiasco.

    Ben Lehman also did a game called Clover, which is a slice of life thing heavily inspired by Yotsuba&!, a newer manga by the creator of Azumanga Daioh.

    I've found that a lot of the "how" is a mixture of the setup (being willing to say that in this game you're not a wizard or whatever, you've little Clover and her daddy) and the things you take away from a typical RPG experience (at least in the slice of life manga take on kishoutenketsu there's basically no need for any kind of conflict rules per se).

    \n So, in 2009 I released a game called Ribbon Drive. It's about going on a roadtrip, and being open to the possibility of self-discovery. Everyone has ideas about the future, and at least one person abandons those ideas and learns to live in the present moment. The game isn't about conflict, but about reflection and the possibility of a twist in perception.

    I'd not call it a kitshoutenketsu game, though, both because that's a new term for me (and not really mine to adopt), and because Ribbon Drive was intentionally designed as a response to conflict-heavy games. I think the article does a good job of pointing out that it'd be difficult to design a Western-influenced* game that didn't engage the conflict-centric 3-act structure - either by emulating it or by explicitly rejecting it.

    I feel like this structure is very different in RPGs than it is in fiction. Like, in fiction it creates this tension between our expectations and the contents of the story. RPGs have a harder time doing that. Instead, I think such play either falls flat or becomes about our struggle to present characters who are ok, and do ok things.
    \nI think this is more influenced by traditional thinking in how to build a story rather than the truth. And it doesn't have to start with an OK statement. You can start as a drug addict, a homeless, newly divorced, but in the end; everything comes together instead of one dominating another. You can get insight about your life, fall in love or forgive another person. The story ends with a change, but it doesn't have to be a change on someone's behalf.

    I played a rather railroady scenario once where a family was having dinner with a man. They talked about the week that passed, but instead of talking about it, I framed it as scenes. When the scene was over, I jumped back to the dinner talk. As the session went on, information came up about the man that made the players wonder why they were having dinner with him. In the final scene, they realized that they was there to tell him that he was going to die.

    An insight.

    \n My apologies in advance for everybody who may feel this is just an off-topic interruption. If in my poor experience in trying to explain myself in english, I manage to pull this off, then it may become useful to the main topic later. If not, here goes nothing.

    I'll probably never know if it's a translation problem or something else, but in spanish and specifically in terms of literary theory, the word conflicto always translated as conflict means much more than just violence or competition. It's universally applied also to situations where there's a struggle and/or a change in the status quo.

    Now, though it's always true that there's two opposing forces in a conflict, these aren't always human per se. There's the conflict between man vs. man, but also there's the conflict of man vs. nature, man vs society, the man vs himself and nowadays, the conflict of man vs. technology.

    On top of that, the word "man" in all those kinds of conflict is actually used to define "humankind's usual viewpoint about the world" expressed in the form of a character (or group of characters) that the readers identify with. It can even be an alien squirrel which can think two things at once, but nevertheless the readers will identify with it if it's got some trace of emotions, principles and plausible reactions (at lest for us) to the world he/she/it/they live in.

    Now, this is the base, but again it isn't as rigid as it seems. And that's the beauty of this theory: you can stretch it for example to the idea that there can be conflict between the way the reader thinks the fictional presented world is/must be and the way the writer's final reveal presents it in the end of the story. And that conflict won't necessarily generate any violence from any side, but is actually engineered by the author to pick up the reader's curiosity and then subtly slapping him in the face with sudden realization, all made to enhance the emotional response of the reader.

    So by all means, satanize Combat, Competition and Violence if you want, take them away from your lives and/or fictions, but please, don't do that to old poor Conflict. He's just being totally misunderstood.

    \n WarriorMonk,
    In American English, this is the definition of Conflict as well. I think the contrast that is trying to be made is that in a Conflict (even a Man vs Nature Conflict) it is expected that one side (Man or Nature) will win.
    If I understand this correctly, the idea is that there is one aspect (say Man) and that an expectation is setup, but is confounded in the end.
    So, in a man vs nature conflict, it might be that a character is struggling between Man's laws to not steal, and Nature's requirement to eat to survive. Part of good story telling in this paradigm is that this conflict is highlighted and in the end, one of these ultimately wins out (the man steals to get food or dies of hunger), In this example, there is no one fighting against the Man, but there is a conflict.
    While in this other story telling paradigm, the idea is that there is only one viewpoint/side and it is going to proceed in an unpredictable way.
    A man is going home with bread he has stolen. And right before he gets home, he gives it to a homeless family. Here there is no conflict highlighted. It is setting up an expectation, he is going home, will he feed his ill-gotten gains to his family, will the police be waiting for him, etc. The twist being that he gives it away. Nobody won, no one had to subsume their stake in a conflict in order for the other side to win. No viewpoint was proven to be flawed or incorrect except the reader's expectation.
    Clearly in these examples, it is almost the same story, but it is told from a different perspective and a different technique.
    I hope that helps.

    \n Rickard, thanks for the cartoon, that's great!

    I think creating such a story as a collaborative RPG faces the same challenge that murder mystery games have traditionally faced. Since not just any ad-libbed resolution will suffice, how is the resolution decided? Does someone plan it before hand? Does someone have to/get to ad-lib it when the time comes? Does everyone help decide it when the time comes? Do we employ mechanics or points or a competitive race to produce a rules-based resolution? It's tough, because we don't just want to identify a killer, we want to see how the killer's identity connects back to what's already been played.

    Honestly, I'd be inclined to GM it old-school. Come up with a cool secret truth and then pick the right moment to reveal it to the players, foreshadowing all the while. "Meanwhile, elsewhere, a boy sits on a corner and waits..."

    \n It strikes me that the overarching structure of a three-act improv Harold might follow this structure.

    A1 A2 A3 are three separate scenes.
    B1 B2 B3 take the first three scenes and carry them further.
    C1 C2 C3 C4, etc take characters, situations and other elements from the first 2 acts and mix them together.

    While any given scene probably consists of actors pushing toward conflict, from a 10,000 foot view, you might see the narrative reveal of a given Harold as "Oh, these two people are married," which really has no conflict to it at all. This would seem to fit the earlier idea that kishoutenketsu narratives feel like jokes.

    This would mean that there are workable systems that let people create these sorts of narratives in groups, on the fly. Although I think the murder mystery caveat, that not just any resolution will do, certainly applies.

    \n Ok, I see the use of kishotenketsu in a game in this fashion: First act would present PCs in their own daily routine. Second act would let each player take a part of the world of the other players (a place, an npc or group of them, etc) and give it a twist. Third act would have players trying to meet ends and connect things, sorta Fiasco way. You can built a lot of mystery or a threat in act 2, but act 3 won't necesarily mean a combat or the world destruction. It could mean the player adapts to the new situation and roleplays it as if she knew it all along or whatever she wishes and finds more entertaining.

    Wow, that was the shortest game I've ever written. Let me know if you ever get to playtest it.
