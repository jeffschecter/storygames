    \n I like you and I like cyborgs so those are two positives.

    Game wise, it depends but in general the following will greatly increase the chances I will play:

    - Rules are written so no one has to read them before we start playing. Instead, we can read the rules as a group and start playing almost immediately (see the D&D 4E Redbox).

    - Prep is minimal, done as a group, or example prep materials are provided.

    - Pregens are provided or character creation (if the game has that) can be done as a group in less than 1 hour.

    - Reference material is provided that summarizes the rules and includes any lists, charts, or detailed mechanics we may need to reference while playing without having to open the original rules text.

    - Can be played as a 3 hour 1 shot with the option to play more.

    - If it's an RPG, what we describe in the fiction matters mechanically somehow.

    - People I know also want to play.

    \n Simon's heading the way I think helps best: general ideas to explore.

    I'm interested in transhuman concerns, in general:
    * Who am I, if I can shard my personality into cyberspace then re-integrate the copies later?
    * How much of my mind can I augment before I am not longer "me" in any recognizable (i.e. to others... to self?) sense?
    * What will it mean to be "skilled" if I can hardwire my reflexes and nervous system to automatically and accurately perform extremely complex tasks?
    * What will "art" become, when people can 'see' any EM spectrum, 'hear' any pressure waves, 'smell/taste' even trace chemicals?
    * How much of my physical form can I change or augment before becoming "inhuman"? How much more can I change before I no longer CARE about that distinction?
    * What do the 'gods' do on a slow Saturday night?

    \n The forum ate my post, so here's the highlights.

    1) The emotional elements of the rebirth of the individual and the separation from the past life resonates particularly well with me in the angsty cyborg stories.

    3) I heartily recommend including a point of tension in your mechanics, as explained by This Episode of Master Plan.

    \n I definitely dig the idea of putting metamorphosis at the core of the game. Personally, I've always dug the internal conflicts that cybernetic stories deal with. What does it mean to be human and what does it mean to be a machine? I love watching a character struggle with those ideas, and act on what they think is the best.

    I envision some kind of sliding scale, and the actions you take move you closer to either humanity or machine.

    Rather than full-on cyborgs, I think taking the cybernetic augmentation approach would make for more interesting conflicts. You could play up the decision to stay as "human" as possible, and then play up even more the decision to finally get a modification. I imagine a dramatic scene of a defiant character tearing out a cyber-eye or some such thing, and another scene where a character full of grim determination gets the last augment he needs to finally achieve some goal. Like the boxer who finally breaks down and gets cyber-arms like everyone else. I'm reminded of R.A.X. Coswell from the game Eternal Champions.

    So, in short, I'd play it.
