\n Many games have rules where every player basically has a turn for their character to have the spotlight, move forward in their story for one scene, and then pass the turn on to the next player. (I'm reading Contenders now, for example.) Is this always the best way to do it?

In particular, I'll be running the game in a one-shot environment where people are learning the rules. Given the slight time pressure, I wonder if it might be better to tell one character's story at a time, or at least go far (like up to half-way through one character's story) before cutting away to give that character a break.

Of course, this makes it harder to fit in the multiple threaded intertwining stories, but it allows more focus on single threads and a greater chance of seeing one complete if you don't have an unbounded evening's worth of time. Thoughts?

    \n There's a social dynamic at play - you're dealing with player-level spotlight time. So if you want to tell a longer story focusing on one character without cutting away, it really needs mechanisms to engage everyone at the table to sustain the fun. I think so, at least. This isn't a hard-and-fast thing (screen presence in PTA requires patience and delayed gratification and supporting your fellow players, for example), but I think it's worth considering.

    One intermediary step is to encourage the interruption of scenes with flashbacks, bobs, weaves, etc.

    \n I'll second Jason's comment. Ganakagok passes the spotlight around the table in the way that you're talking about, but people tell me that they stay engaged as players even when the spotlight isn't on them. One reason for this is that they have in-game resources that they can invoke to affect the outcome of the other player's scene (perhaps via a rationalizing flashback, as Jason suggests). Another, more subtle reason is the extent to which players' individual scenes contribute to an overarching narrative, so that earlier events are worth paying attention to as grist for narration when your turn comes around. In other words, to the extent that each character's individual story contributes to a larger narrative (even if that larger narrative is disjointed and weirdly articulated, like Pulp Fiction), player interest in each other's stories is heightened.
