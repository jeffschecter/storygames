\n I didn't enjoy many boardgames till I played Settlers of Catan.
\n
\nMany boardgames before (to me) were long, slow, convoluted, took long to set up, mid-way through the winner was often a forgone conclusion, were clones of other popular games, and were loaded with baggage of "this is what we do because this is how we always did it."
\n
\nNot all boargames before were like this. Just the popular ones I played. Plenty lesser known games were like Settlers before Settlers.
\n
\nBut when I finally played Settlers I was immediately hooked. It's fast, easy to learn, finishes in 90 minutes, streamlined, relatively quick to set up, it's unclear who will win till the very end, and no matter whose turn it is, you have a compelling reason to pay attention.
\n
\nAnd it didn't have that rule I dislike in boardgames where if X happens, you lose your next turn. Which is effectively losing 2 turns. Add this to an already slow game where when it isn't your turn you don't do anything and I was instantly turned off.
\n
\nSettlers was not what I was used to. And it got me back into boardgames.

    \n Not many boardgames in the U.S. were like Settlers before Settlers. I would point to Acquire as an exception to the rule.

    Germany, in particular, has a strong tradition of family board game playing, and so acted as a nice incubator for streamlined, approachable, games that avoided big downtimes and player elimination. I imported Settlers in 1995, and was hooked on the style. Now, I only infrequently play "American-style" games, and tend to stick to the "Eurogames" as delivering better bang-for-the-buck. Time is so precious, and it's cool to play with the Daughters.

    \n I thought of this thread tonight as I was playing Citadels.
    \n
    \nFor anyone unfamiliar, it's a Euro game with rotating roles that are picked in secret. One of these roles is the assassin. The assassin's turn always comes early in the round, and the assassin is entitled to "shoot" any one of the later roles. The player with that role then loses their turn.
    \n
    \nIt's only one turn lost, not two, since the other player doesn't have to roll badly or land on a "lose a turn" space before the lost turn takes effect. But it's still a significant handicap. Citadels is the only Euro game I've played so far that has a mechanism like this, and I found it really jarring.

    Posted By: deadlytoqueI wonder if that's because it's an "American-Euro" in that FFG generally published much more "Ameritrash"-style games (to borrow a term I learned on boardgamegeek).
    \nMy daughter bought me Citadels for my birthday a couple of years back, and I found it a fairly brutal game compared to other Euro-style games I've played, which seem much more forgiving to beginners ( I've generally only played a couple, and then only one or two games of each). One thing I have noticed about Euro-games in that limited experience, is that they seem to have multiple paths to winning in a way that Ameritrash games don't. I've also noticed a tendency to have different strategies depending upon what stage of the game players are in.

    Strangely, also, I've usually also been right in there and competing until the last with Eurogames, and am left with a strong urge to shake my opponents hand and say something like "Damn-Good Game Old Bean!!!" and mean it at the end of Euro-games in a way I don't feel in Ameritrash games.

    I suspect that has to do with some mechanical effect where competing until the last in Eurogames actually has some change of pulling off at least a close loss, where Ameritrash games tend to have a long slow death for the loser(s) that's inevitable and foreseeable, but they don't have a point where you can honorably admit to the loss without seeming a whiner and undercutting the winner's win.
