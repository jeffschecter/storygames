\n The PCs were holed up in a ratkin village after the previous session. They had just spent some time learning magic from Francis, the wizard who leads the village, and his fellow ratkin James. That's when the Maldorean army showed up.

Here are the main PCs:
Squig, a goblin addicted to destroying things with Destruction magic.
Armitage, an elf Zaru who seeks out Zu words even at the cost of others around him.
Rose, an Ammeni thief who wants to help people and make a buck in the process.

The NPCs are:
Francis, an albino ratkin wizard who uses telekinetic Three-Corner magic. He's the de facto leader of the village, with his litter mate James.
James, a ratkin who is very protective of his village and who's learned a bit of magic from Francis.
Alessis, the experienced commander of the Maldorean army.
Vincento, Alessis' sergeant and a demolitions expert.

Here's the quick recap:
The PCs were holed up in the ratkin village when the army appeared. The ratkin fled east over a bridge, until Vincento blew up the bridge. The PCs and most of the ratkin escaped, but Francis was captured. We ended the session with the ratkin and the PCs running deeper into the canyon, while the army camped in the village with their new prisoner.

The highlights:
Armitage wanted to create a bridge with the word "wall/stop/stopping". I pointed out it was supposed to *stop* something, so he would only be able to do that if he jumped into the canyon and then said "wall" in mid-air to stop him from falling - and if he failed his Zu check he would fall into the canyon and take Harm. That was hilarious. :-) He agreed and was ready to do that, but then he added his other Zu word "runner/run/running" to create a wall for runners. We agreed that would qualify as a bridge and he wouldn't need to jump first.

My Key Scenes sang. They all got triggered, including "meeting the army leaders", "explosives blow someone up" and "Francis throws someone telekinetically". I loved those moments, and so did everyone else.

For the future:
I'm not quite used to buying Keys on the fly. Looking back on it, there were a couple of moments where the PCs or NPCs motivations clearly changed, and I didn't buy (or encourage a player to buy) Keys to farm XP off those motivations. That's just a question of my own experience, I think.

I have a suggestion for the elf. Armitage the elf's Eternal Question is about collecting magic knowledge, and he has Key of Self to ignore requests for help. But he was helping the ratkin, even at risk to himself. I'm going to suggest to Geoff (Armitage's player) to buy off his Eternal Question, and use the advances to buy Key of Conscience and another Eternal Question, namely "Is it better to help yourself or help others?" Then he puts himself in situations where he has to choose between the two, and regardless of his choice two Keys will ping.

I'm a bit confused by the noun form of Zu. The rules say that anyone else in the scene can make a Resist check against Zu to prevent the creation of the object. But how does that play in the fiction? Is the resister staring at the Zu-speaker, concentrating? I'm unsure what that would look like.

Great times! All comments are welcome.

    \nI should really know the answer to this question, shouldn't I? Ok, looked it up and read it and have an answer now:

    Bringing something into being via zu is rewriting reality. It's not even that something comes into being: you've re-written reality so that it was already there. Anyone present who is observing what is happening can be a passive resister to this re-writing. They don't have to do anything fiction-wise except be, thereby making it harder to change reality.

    In your game, feel free to make that more active: those in the know understand the nature of reality and can actively fight to keep it from changing around them.

    Re-reading the text, it almost makes me wish it was always resisted, but I think that would have some unpleasant in-game effects.

    \n About buying Keys: if tracking Keys for NPCs seems too much bother, don't do it. I myself don't track XP for NPCs, for instance; if a NPC returns later I'll just give them new crunch (one Advance per session passed) if it seems appropriate. Most of the time NPCs don't need to progress at all, though, as most of them are static by nature.

    As for PCs, it seems to me that you don't want to get Keys for incidental one-off occasions. Better to hold Keys that concern the character's long-term nature and inclinations. (Yes, this is difficult for mercurial players who abhor having their character actually defined as a personality. I recommend a Key of Mercurial Zanity for those.)

    Regarding Key Buyoffs, remember that a player doesn't have to do it just because they fulfill the condition. It's not a bad idea, but it needs to happen because the player himself recognizes that their character has changed. It's not a game of gotcha like D&D alignment violations.

    Good use of Zu there, I like the "running wall" = "bridge" etymology. Exactly the sort of linguistic fluff that I want to see from Zu users.

    If you need some specific crunch for the Maldorean army, The World of Near has some ideas.

    \n The transcendence issue is interesting because of how it exposes assumptions people have about roleplaying games. TSoY tells us that you're playing to find out your character's story - but many a player will respond by vehemently claiming that they do not want to find out this story, they want to experience the immanent existence of the character stuck in time. Understandably Transcendence is just a big pain in the ass if you want to both see numbers getting bigger (character "advancement"), but don't want the story to end.

    (If a player can accept immanence without numbers getting bigger, then the solution is simple: accept that being a Master is what the dream of "endless adventure" is all about, and you're simply not getting any better in the context of this game. Stop spending Advances on that Ability, and ideally sell off all of your Keys as well; gain xp slowly from Key scenes, tell many stories of this character's inherently aimless adventures around the world, as a drifting presence and witness to other people's stories. It's nobody's fault but your own if you hasten towards the end of the story.)

    I provide a few techniques that might or might not be helpful in the Solar System rules text; I recommend reading the section on Transcendence in case there's something there you can use. For example, the concept of character reincarnation/reincorporation could be useful in reinventing a character as something different.

    When I am the Story Guide, and I recognize a player who desires immanence instead of achievement (that is, one who isn't interested in seeing how his character's story ends, but rather wants to detail their lifestyle and experience of being them), I react to this by providing non-mechanical downtime opportunities in the game. My theory on this matter is that a player with these sorts of desires may best be satisfied if we can manage even once during a campaign to talk about their character in an immanent, completed manner: not merely "what are you doing right now", but "what you are doing in general". For example, a time-skip of five years, during which time the character solidifies a social position and becomes a well-known legend in local parts and abroad as the thing that they are. We could just talk about the events of this down-time in general terms for a while, with no dice or anything. This probably isn't everybody, but at least some players seem to crave for this sort of identity accomplishment more than accomplishment of dramatic goals; once they have it, and have solidified their own character in their own mind, they might be much more excited about the opportunity of finding out how their character's story ends.

    Remember: when the mechanical engine is running, all things are constantly pointing towards sharp resolution of dramatic events, akin to how a movie won't waste time on empty exposition. Nothing, however, says that you can't have less busy moments in your campaign.

    \n I don't see him getting less bored with Destruction 4 if transcendence didn't exist than with Destruction 3 -- they are both just numbers, arbitrary limits. The ideas Eero mentions in the text are great.

    Since TSOY came out, I've played quite a bit of Apocalypse World, and really like their high-level character ideas. If I were redesigning transcendence today, I think I'd allow for the same situation -- resolve a big issue, leave a mark on the world -- and require the character to step out for a session. Then you could introduce a new character. If you want your old character to come back, sure thing; but they're changed, too. Maybe halve their scores, or maybe just allow them to be re-arranged so you're great at something else, Gandalf the Grey->White style. I wouldn't allow a character who'd transcended to do so a second time, however.
