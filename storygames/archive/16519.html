    \n This is probably obvious but there is also the issue that with an RPG, a large portion of the experience is out of the creator's control.

    You are provide a system that allows for unpredictable emergent play.

    You may show your game to people who you don't want to offend, and they may give you a thumbs up. But they may then be offended by what people actually do with your game, which is out of your control.

    I have friends who refuse to play the Skyrim video game. Why? Because Skyrim players made youtube videos of emergent game play that my friends found offensive. I don't believe this gameplay was intended by the creators of Skyrim. It's emergent play.

    I love Dogs in the Vineyard but people have done things I personally wouldn't feel comfortable with it. But that's part of the point of an RPG. Players have a lot of freedom to explore, make choices, and be creative in their own safe spaces. If they then publish their gameplay and I find it offensive, I'm generally going to try not to blame the game creator.

    But some people will blame the creator.

    \n I'm not sure what you get out of setting it in a theocracy that you don't get even more out of setting it in a straight up "this is the only thing that's different about the world" setting. I assume that the theocracy idea is intended to bring the anti-gay possibilities into stark relief and higher contrast. But I think that by making that part of the setting less normal, you lose some of the subtlety and impact that could be present if the game was just set "right here, right now, plus supers (who are gay)".

    I think having a more "here and now" setting gives you more interesting and subtle possiblities.

    You have the possibility of people who are jealous that some of the population has superpowers and they don't. And they used to be gay friendly. But now all these people have these awesome advantages that they're jealous of. Presumably some of those people would start associating all gays with the ones they're jealous of. There's some extremely fruitful ground, there. And I think that type of character would be de-emphasized by the theocracy idea.

    Also, I really love how this concept ties the ideas of a superhero secret identity and the gay closet together in really interesting ways.

    Presumably you'd have some of the gay superheroes who would be in, for example, marriages of convenience as part of how they protect their secret identity. Which leads to some interesting potential for thinking about how that sort of thing is an everyday reality for some members of real society. There's some good stuff there.

    And there could be variants. Like the Will & Grace setup - the straight girl who likes her gay friend as more than a friend, etc, etc. Not that you'd want to emphasize sitcom hijinks. But the premise of the game invites some interesting character thoughts that otherwise would be less likely to be thought about by some players.

    Ooooohh!! You should strongly consider taking a Smallville type approach to making the non-supers characters just as interesting and important to the story as the supers. This would be the kind of setting where having the players be less than half supers characters would potentially be tons better than playing a supers team. Maybe mandate that there be specifically exactly one supers character per group. There might be a lot of fruitful (Just realized I'm using that word a lot in a post related to gay interests. I'm 95% sure that's just a weird coincidence.) ground in that limitation.

    Overall, I'd say the key would be to approach it similarly to how you'd approach issues of potential cultural appropriation. Do your research. A lot of it. Make it super apparent in your writing that you've put your time in to approach the subject with respect.

    After that, some of the people who play it will be assholes. You can't fix that. But you can do your part right.

This can get out of hand very fast.

    I can think of two ways to "patch" the concept so that it would work for me in play:\n
    • Superpowers as magic, rather than a mere "physical" fact. This means intentionality is important, as well as ritualistic behavior and meaning \u2014 making it easier to correlate such powers with real-world homosexuality than it would be for Superman-like powers. Maybe feeling same-gender attraction is a prerequisite for developing powers in the first place, or maybe involving oneself in same-gender relationships is part of the necessary ritualistic regime for maintaining powers. This makes the metaphor a better fit, in that it can more comfortably be stretched to accommodate various real-life cases.
      • \n
    • Instead of "being homosexual", anybody with superpowers is somehow genderqueer \u2014 including any deviation from the mainstream cisgenderheterosexual paradigm: people who identify as homosexual, people who don't fit in their assigned gender, people who see themselves as asexual, etc. Maybe what superpowers come bundled with is, actually, just a heightened perception of how artificial and fucked-up the assumed social norm is, which makes them more conscious of/responsive to their own individual way(s) of defying it. This actually changes the metaphor to a broader one which I think is less at risk of breaking under rough handling.
