\n Hey everyone, just wanted to announce that my game, Mecha, has finally been released just in time for Gen Con. Some of you may have heard me talking about it, some of you not so much, so let me give you the overview.

Mecha is my rules light (maybe rules medium) game for taking the cool parts of mecha anime (pilot badassery, crazy combats, cool visuals) and blending them with lots of ways to role playing. The core book has all the rules, plus three settings for causing all sorts of mayham: Godblind (Big O meets Dark City), Steel Gunner: Destiny Tomorrow (Gundam, written with lots of help from Clint Krause), and Special Research School (all the fun of high school with 100% more big robots.)

I know Mecha is story gamish than a lot of games on here, but really the non-combat mechanics of the game are heavily inspired by a lot of the games I found out about here, especially the set scene structure and character advancement.

Anyway, I'm pretty excited. You can read more about it here: http://www.myheroicjourney.com/mecha

You can also preorder the game or buy it on DriveThruRPG.com (right now, it's number #4 overall on the site. Hopefully it will stay there for a bit!)

Thanks and happy fighting.

    \n Chris can obviously answer best how Big O inspired him, but as for a general outline of what Big O is, I've always thought of it as "Batman with Giant Robots" (which I've always thought of as flippin sweet!).

    You have this city full of people who all woke up no memory of the past a few years back. The main character, Roger, is a professional freelance negotiator
    (and millionaire playboy). In his spare time he fights giant monsters/robots/whatever with his own giant robot, the Big O (nobody knows who pilots it, though) The series has a very 1930s kind of look to it that I thoroughly enjoy. I highly recommend it if you like giant robots!

    \n Darcy/Jeff,

    Thanks for the questions. What really attracted me to Big O was the mystery aspect. Not so much the mysteries that Roger Smith has to go investigate, but the backstory behind what happened to Paradigm City, why no one can remember what's going, and the idea that there seems to be sinister forces at work in the world. This very much plays into the Rufus Sewell movie where a man who finds he has special powers goes out to free this strange city in the middle of nowhere.

    That's what I had in mind with Godblind. In Godblind, everyone exists in a Living City, which is ruled by seven Gods. If you think a seditious thought, the Fires from Heaven zap you. Dead. But if you are a good citizen, you go to work, the gods take care of you. There is no disease, no poverty, everyone has a job which suits their talents (and tends to be things like weapons research, biochemcial research as well as art, actor, football star, etc.) Every year, the gods hold poetry, acting, and sports contests as well as beauty pagents and take the winners to Heaven, which is literally above the clouds, where they live "in glory forever."

    So the players can play Godblind, which the Free City (a sort of cancerous growth inside the Living City) has recognized have the genetic ability to be completely invisible to the gods' equipment. To these brave few, the Living City gifts mecha! (Because what else do you arm teenaged freedom fighters with???) Characters can also take on the role of Godseed, who are the offspring of the gods and humans who won the pageants. They know a little more of what's going up in Heaven (the hedonistic and cruel lifestyles of the gods, for instance.) Godseed are also given mecha by the Free CIty and effectively remain invisible to the gods.

    After that, battle ensues as the Godblind try to topple the gods and free the Living City.

    So the game stresses slightly different aspects of Big O than Jeff mentioned, though the setting is certainly up to something like that. The Godblind might be a little bit older and more ingrained in the infrastructure of the Living City before they strike out.
