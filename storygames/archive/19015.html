\n I've been reading the OSR threads with interest, and they've seemed (to me) to describe a very particular style of play:\n
    \n
  • Dungeon are lethal - player skill is vital.
    • \n
  • Characters die frequently, particularly at early levels.
    • \n
  • Characters gain their character-ness through play.
    • \n
  • The DM is expected to keep things secret from the players
    • \n
  • Rolls are for what's plausible (rather than what's cool).
    • \n
  • Experience points = treasure, however you get it.
    • \n
  • Story isn't the point. If you die by falling into a pit, that's just how it goes. The whole is the working out of DM-mediated physics and player-skill.
    • \n
\nIn all of this, there seems a fair bit of nostalgia, but I used to play D&D from the late 70s onwards, and for me it was a bit different:

1) When my Dad first bought the game and introduced it to myself and my brother, none of our characters ever died. The emphasis was on narration and atmosphere and fantasy as a source of wonder.

2) At school, we had a lunchtime club, and our dungeons were rarely lethal. At university I played in a campaign that lasted years, helping people in a village who were dying due to an ancient god rising. My favourite books were Legends and Lore (particularly the Melnibonean section) - and I tended to steer clear of DMs who regularly killed characters. For me, the attraction was the sense of wonder and dramatic sweep of it all.

Now I'm not saying this isn't compatible with the OSR style (I was amazed by the thread about the guy running around with a map, for example). But I don't remember playing that way very much myself.

I wonder what people's thoughts are around:

a) How much the style discussed in these OSR threads is new, as opposed to the dominant game type of the time?

b) How much it naturally emerges from the ruleset (rather than, say, the culture of the people round the table)?

The reason I'm asking is that I'm actually really nostalgic for my old way of playing, but I can so clearly see the OSR gametype is coherent that I'm beginning to wonder if I was trying to bang a square peg into a round hole for all these years. Do you think it's possible to take, say, the 74 D&D ruleset and play other ways coherently, or will the rules always fight you unless you drift them, and you're better off picking up a more modern game?

    a) How much the style discussed in these OSR threads is new, as opposed to the dominant game type of the time?
    \nA pretty important thread in the OSR is people who want to know how Gygax and Arneson played the game, both back then and later on, and then play that way and also build on top of that style.     \nPlay the rules exactly as written and you'll get what you put in your bullet points. Especially with Basic D&D, as it's the clearest statement of rules. The culture of the people around the table always plays a part, of course, but the OSR is generally using these rules for what they're good at.\n

    \nNot really, "fight you" is putting it mildly, and you're better off playing a game that supports the way you want to play, modern or not. You, for example, should probably play Dungeon World.

    a) How much the style discussed in these OSR threads is new, as opposed to the dominant game type of the time?
    \nFew people tell me that they they'd have played like this in the '70s; many have told me that their play was more varied, experimental, meandering or outright contrary to the approaches that are dominant in the OSR. On the other hand, the textual sources are much more supportive, the time period saw the publication of many, many rpg texts that don't make even a little sense unless you assume a clear challenge-orientation to them. This makes me conclude that while D&D was used for all sorts of creative agendas early on (makes perfect sense - there were few alternative games to go to), the core culture of developers, branching out from Gygax's social and geographical proximity, played in a more OSR-like style than the hobby on average.

    It should be remembered that the OSR is technically not defined by a particular creative agenda or technical solution, though; as I see it, the OSR guys have historically been just messing about with their favourite game, and they've found each other by the virtue of similar surface color - they all dig "old D&D". I expect that their thing gains in definition over time, of course, and already one can say with justification that the OSR is starting to crystallize some dogma about what is old school and what isn't. They are in a fundamentally different situation form the '70s roleplayers due to the opportunities they have for comparing games and creative purposes against each other, understanding the full scope of possibilities roleplaying has to offer; D&D has never been as sharply defined as it is when it can be laid down against all these other games.

    All in all, I'd say that the challenge-based playstyle that is so prominent in the OSR was without question present in the '70s, but it was also without question not the only way the game was played. Whether it was "dominant" would depend entirely on with whom you played. For me it is sufficient to say that it's the playstyle that is the most interesting of those practiced at the time, simply because it's the only one that went effectively extinct in the rpg publishing culture later on; I can get better story-telling play out of any number of games today, while old D&D still stands as a competitive classic of challenge-based roleplaying. (Of course, if I'm wrong about this and there is some amazing other way to play D&D where it also rules the roost as the best game ever, I'm all ears.)\n
    b) How much it naturally emerges from the ruleset (rather than, say, the culture of the people round the table)?
    \nI'd say it's pretty evenly balanced between these; I would not expect the D&D rules, skimpy and prone to irrelevant mechanizations as they are, to lead anybody into applying them in this manner without other support. On the other hand, I do think that this is easily the most natural way to use those rules. So I'd say D&D is like a poncho: if you don't know what that is, you well might end up using it as a wrap-on skirt, but once you encounter a real wrap skirt you'll realize how clumsy, over-sized and heavy your poncho is, and once you see some people wearing ponchos you'll realize that this was actually the way this thing was supposed to be used in the first place.

    The way I look at this as a game designer is that D&D is very strongly supportive of its mode of play, but the game just has not been written down in a satisfactory manner yet. When I look at D&D as an abstract entity devoid of the specific foibles of individual game texts, I see a game that is very coherent, and one that should provide its experience reliably. The reason for why it doesn't historically is that it's been written in muddled ways (or perhaps in out-dated ways, in case of the best versions - they're perfectly playable, but you still can't understand them unless you're lucky or you've seen somebody wear the poncho correctly), even if play has been clear.

    \n I don't know that this phenomenon is under-represented in discussion. Taken for granted, perhaps? I mean, some people do play in isolated cliques, but others play in circles, and I myself play in circles, and I don't recognize that I would be somehow ignoring the social dynamics of this.

    Setting that up, though, it's basically exactly the same as if you were just setting up an isolated clique, at least in my experience. The phenomenon of growing circles of associated GMs, several concurrently running campaigns, complex character stables and so on, all associated with a healthy local D&D ecosystem, seems to me like an emergent outcome of success. It takes little except that your game is energetic enough to attract new blood regularly, and your players are good enough to decide to turn to GMing for a change of pace. Do that for a year or two, and you have that big pool of players with all the associated phenomena.

    Why it doesn't work this way with other roleplaying games aside from D&D, that's an interesting question. I would say that it's because D&D is so massively campaign-focused, which makes it more rewarding when you put more work into it.

    \n I'm loving these OSR discussions as an invitation to reflect on and better understand my experiences (started with the Holmes Blue Book) and also to consider what the OSR style might offer me now in this stage of my gaming life.

    What fascinates me currently is how D&D evolved, gave rise to new rulesets, and brought us to this point of being able to turn around and look back. Advanced Dungeons & Dragons was a response to Dungeons & Dragons and was wildly popular with the existing audience of roleplayers at the time because it gave them something more than basic D&D. Many players at the time wanted more, or suspected more was possible, is what I'm saying. Now those of us looking back decades later - and having played many games and systems that evolved from the ancestral beginnings of RPGs - are looking back with an enormous amount of context that didn't exist back then. I became frustrated by basic D&D and even AD&D because I sensed and wanted more from my gaming. I got all that eventually. But now with the benefit of all this surplus of RP goodness, I can look back on those simpler times and appreciate the elegance of the system and enjoy them in a way I didn't in the day. I guess what I'm trying to say is I'm fascinated by how I have come around to enjoying something now when I found it limiting and frustrating when the only games available were much simpler.

    (Topic for another thread might be how certain rulesets and styles suit the psychological needs of the gamers and how those needs change as we get older. I wanted (needed?) something very different from my RPs when I was in my teens and early twenties than I want or need now.)

    Honoring the OP:

    a) How much the style discussed in these OSR threads is new, as opposed to the dominant game type of the time? - I'd maybe add my POV is the appreciation for the style is mostly new and that the dominant game type of the time varied widely as we were stumbling about exploring this RP landscape. Some folks ran the games as intended but most the folks I knew were busy experimenting with house rules as we were exploring and probably some of those experiments resulted in new game systems that continued the evolution of RPGs.

    b) How much it naturally emerges from the ruleset (rather than, say, the culture of the people round the table)? - I now realize that if you do stick to the ruleset as given, and let it do its thing, it can provide a rewarding experience. Again, expectations play a huge role here... if what you yearn for is something more/else/different, you'd do well to try a different rules set. Which again was the impetus to house-rule the sh!t out of the games were were playing back in the '70s and '80s. Remember the ruleset is only a bunch of inert tools - the style of play might be directionally informed by the rules but only springs into existence when the people round the table breathe life into it. The play between people IS the style.

    \nA solid point I hadn't considered, and important to context of these things. A lot of the OSR stuff people are talking about in these threads is one part of old school gaming, expanded upon, and in contrast to stuff that's more current design/playstyle-wise.

    Looking backwards a bit:

    So I'm pretty fascinated talked about in Playing at the World, how approaches and mechanics in games develop and evolve through accidents, quirks of fate, and a changing play context. And even how this stuff changes depending upon a player's age at any moment, life circumstances as they age, and contact with other gamers ( and what those gamers like and have experienced).

    Of course, backtracking the changes is tricky with a bunch of different people doing stuff simultaneously and varied levels of contact between them.

    For example, it took me a long time and a lot of consideration and pondering to figure out why D&D took off with a segment of wargamers, how it tied into a bunch of different experimental approaches to minis wargaming, and what that means to how those early rules mechanics and approaches reflect that.

    A lot of it is really non-obvious without the context of the social groups, the organization of play, and the environment.

    Peculiar stuff like alignment, fast, random chargen, XPs for gold, differing advancement rates/level caps, sheer deadliness of combat, the end game warlordism, and neutral refereeing ( and what that even means exactly) make more sense once you know about that stuff. It also starts to make more sense about why people started moving away from those things(and related mechanics) as other factors in the environment changed.

    D&D was definitely a moving target. I started playing in the late 70's / early 80's and the game was going through changes the whole time.
