\n Is it OK to design a game where the amount of opposition that can be used against characters is limitless, or is it better to design a game where the opposition is limited mechanically by some kind of economy or other rule?

If your take on it is that neither is better, that they are merely different, how are they different? What kinds of play experiences will these two separate design styles generate?

    \n I think the opposition should be based on the internal logic of the game world and the situation at hand.

    Take, for instance, a Shadowrun game.

    The team of runners heads into the complex and the hacker dives into the matrix to slice his way into the security system. He sets off an alarm, and the IC (intrusion countermeasures) come his way. The amount of IC is both limitless and limited. It's limited in that the system can only run so much at a time, but limitless because whenever resources are freed up by destroyed IC, more can be spawned. He has to do what he can and get out, but won't be totally hosed by being surrounded by infinite IC at once.

    In the meat world, the IC has scrambled a security team to the hacker's location. Four guys, armed and cautious, arrive. Overall they're a small part of a larger security force, but that force is most definitely limited: there aren't infinite Joes on the payroll. However, you also get to tack on Lone Star's private police forces, and might even warrant a military response if the runners break out their Panther Assault Cannons. While finite, the resources are effectively limited, since escalation can occur until the runners escape or die.

    Or there's a Rolemaster campaign at the next tabnle over. The low level PCs have heard of a bunch of orcs trying to make incursions into the Shire for unknown reasons, so they head out from the Prancing Pony to find out what's going on. The band of orcs numbers 23, which is a lot for a low level party to handle unless they're smart, but it's most definitely finite, and there's no help in sight.

    In either case, if you try to set some sort of boundaries in the system, you limit the number of situations your game can portray.

    \n It depends very much on what kind of game you want to design. Rune is designed for hyper-gamist GM-vs-Player dungeon-crawling where there's actually a winner at the end of the adventure, so having opposition be limited not only makes sense but is kinda necessary for the game to work, IMO.

    On the other hand, Wushu has no limit on opposition whatsoever, besides the GM not being a dick and, arguably, player Veto. But it is designed for freewheeling action with lot sof player narrations, and so a balanced opposition just isn't important.

    Both are cool games, but they do very, very different things.

    \n Also limited: Agon, Beast Hunters.

    I think there's a place for each, it really depends on your design goals. In Agon, there's only so much Strife because it sets a limit to the amount of Glory to be won. It allows for winners and losers. In Beast Hunters, the player sets the limit (both pool and maximum expenditure). It's a self-imposed difficulty level, which allows the player to step up by choosing higher limits and facing bigger odds. Both of these are clearly Gamist designs, and the limits work well for that.

    In Shock:, when we played it, the limit was primarily more of a story pacing mechanism. And it worked very well as that.

    But I can clearly see that many games work better when there is no limit, because each situation can be judged on its own merits, and maybe you don't want to have a pacing built into the mechanics but instead want it to be flowing from the fiction only.

    I want to say that neither is better than the other, but I'm not sure that's true. I think having a mechanical limit, or a finite resource, potentially evens the field between player and GM, which might make things fairer but could also make things competitive between them. Alternatively, having no limits potentially reinforces the GM/player divide, as the GM gets to play with a different set of rules. But equally, he is more able to facilitate the story by reacting to players and challenging characters. Maybe ;-)

    Posted By: James McMurrayIn either case, if you try to set some sort of boundaries in the system, you limit the number of situations your game can portray.
    \nIf you design your game well, you mostly limit the bad situations.

    My biggest problem with almost all Unlimited GM resources games (especially games with lots of fiddly bits like, say, Exalted or Weapons of the Gods), is that they only rarely provide useful guidance on how to provide mechanically fit opposition. The GM spends a bunch of time writing up the antagonists and throws them at the PCs, but then usually has to fudge the battle because they ended up being weaker or more powerful than he expected, relative to the players. I'd rather the game designer take responsibility for that.

    In more gamist contexts, it is very difficult for the GM to Play Hard when he has unlimited mechanical resources. You inherently have to pull your punches, because you are mechanically vested with the power to instantly quash the players flat (via the Gods, or orbital laser cannons, or whatever Ultimate Power the setting has). I would rather have my resources laid out.

    An interesting middle-ground is The Shadow of Yesterday. Theoretically, the Story Guide's mechanical power is infinite, but there are limits to how much he can apply Right Here Right Now. He can throw a bazillion antagonists who are all Grand Masters in the relevant skill at the players... but they can still never roll a result higher than 7, a value that is [theoretically, at least] in reach of the players, at least if they've had time to advance.

    \n Limits for the GM to provide opposition do the same things that limits on the players do: First, as people have pointed out, they help the GM know that they aren't going overboard.
    \n
    \nSecond, they are constraints, and constraints spur creativity. Give a GM a dungeon and tell them that they can only have one goblin to populate it, and that's gonna be one fuckin' clever goblin, I'll tell you what.
    \n
    \nPersonally, I like most constraints, because I'm a pretty mellow GM: Whatever the game system pushes me to do? I'll do that, and I'll make it work. I build my GMing from the bottom up ... deriving my overall direction from the summation of lots of small decisions.
    \n
    \nI think that for people who build their GMing from the top down, creating an overall direction and then deriving all their small decisions from it, limited resources can often pose more of a problem.

    \n Robert,

    Obviously, without knowing more about the game, the answer is simply that they're better for different things. Having finite resources for the GM (or other antagonist player) certainly lends itself to gamist design. It's just easier to compete when the playing field is even. On the other hand, I don't really believe the reverse is true; that unlimited resources lend themselves to non-gamist facilitating designs, or that finite resources can't work outside a gamist context.

    Personally, I just recently realized that gone in entirely the wrong direction on this very issue in my own design. One day i just looked at the thing and saw that it was encouraging behavior that I didn't want the game to have. So, back to the drawing board. So ask yourself, what benefit do you think your game can gain from these two options? What sort of things do you want to encourage/discourage?

    Oh, and lest I forget: Every bit as important is the question of whether the players will have limited or unlimited resources, for exactly the same reasons.

    \n Another way to look at the ability to produce infinite opposition is as the ability to veto. E.g. players try X, the GM assigns infinite opposition, implying automatic failure.
    \n
    \nSo infinite opposition is good whenever you think someone having veto power is good. We've identified one such case - when you want someone around to defend genre conventions or internal story logic. (To use the rob the donut shop across the way from the police station example, the players suggest "We rob the coffee shop and succeed" A GM who puts in an insurmountable number of mechanically high-powered entities in the shop is implicitly responding "Veto - out of genre. We are trying to emulate a realistic setting and we have established that your characters do not have the capability of doing that") Another case is when one player was expected to do alot of pre-game work, and needs a tool to defend his prep-work from being made irrelevant (without such a tool, that person won't have the incentive to do the prep-work). So if you expect classical GMs to have highly preped settings and stories, it helps to give them veto powers related to those. If you expect players to develop highly complex PCs, it helps to give players veto powers related to permanent changes in those PCs. (Conversely, if you WANT to DISCOURAGE prep-work, because it may interfere with free-wheeling creation at the table, then you might not want players with veto powers during play) Another possibility is when there is massive difference in player skill or motivation. Basically, if you WANT one player to be dominant, give them the tools to be dominant.
    \n
    \nAs has been discussed, you can also temper the power-balancing impact of veto's by giving everyone the authority to veto. So if you want, say, the genre-enforcing effects of veto power, but not the power-balance ones, give veto authority to everyone (like Wushu, or arguably Polaris)
    \n
    \nHowever, on a specific point of finite opposition leading to metagame realizations (which CAN be a bad thing - I think the point of this discussion is to identify when things CAN be bad, not when they need be always bad), this can be tempered by systems that have a reset-clause. For example, in PTA budget refers to an episode - not a session. You can have one episode last two-sessions, or two-episodes in a session. So if the producer thinks he's running low on budget, he can end the episode, refresh the budget AND refresh the player's resources. What finite budget does is prevent the GM from forcing the players from spending all their resources, and then the GM gaining eventual de facto control of the game.
    \n
    \nI'd also point out that even if you are kinda ambivalent about shared authority and the like as I am, I have found that limited GM resource pools really do help with general game logistics. It just makes life easier if you don't have to worry about creating balanced encounters. I'm quite happy to let the system take care of that, so I can focus my energy elsewhere as a GM, even in a mostly "traditional" framework.
