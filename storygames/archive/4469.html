\n Fate was an intentional effort to take Fudge out back, kickin' and hollerin', and give it a shot in the arm -- to show that it could be extended to be something exciting again.

Sometimes, it feels like we instead gave it a shot in the head.

Fate's not always the right fit for Fudge -- particularly with some of its "hippie" or story-game tendencies. Nor should it be -- in sticking to the Fudge ethos, Fate's really only ever meant to be one particular kind of "build".

Keeping Fudge a little closer to its traditionalist roots, but retaining its build-anything, DIY ethos -- how would you awesome-o-fy Fudge?

    \n Yeah, I'd want more fiddling. FUDGE is too clean.

    Let's think of some things.

    There's no reason to always roll 4 dice. Ideally, you want skilled characters to be able to roll less dice, so that they can generate more predictable results. Maybe you could toy with that?

    Get rid of the stupid adjectives. Unless you're using them in a structured freeform sense, rather than as difficulty numbers, they're counter-intuitive in the extreme.

    Eliminate opposed versus unopposed rolls. My thought on this is to always have two sides roll. If you can't find someone to roll against, you can't do it.

    Oh, hey, you could do something where characters had "potentials" and "masteries" for various things. So, uh, huh. Let's say that you have a potential of 9 in sword fighting, but no mastery. I think that that means you roll 9 dice to your base of 9, giving a 0-18 range. Mastery allows you to reduce the number of dice that you roll. Additionally, all rolled +s give you experience points, which boost your mastery, so the best possibile result is an untrained person who's very lucky.

    I might want to work out a fiddly combat system around this, and use it for martial artists.

    \n I tend to agree about the adjectives- I've never found them especially useful, in terms of classifying my abilities (or success results). In my head, I'm always doing the math and then trying to remember the right terminology. "Lessee, I'm... Fair at Juggling, so I got an... *rolls, adds* uh, Awesome success?"

    I think the character development system in FUDGE is a real weak point- it's always seemed like an afterthought to me. If I were doing a Fudge-mod, I'd try to address character advancement, dealing explicitly with issues of pacing- how much advancement is appropriate for the length of the game run (i.e. a single pick-up game, a short series, a long-running campaign, etc)? How do characters develop- can they lose traits as well as gain them? How do you make those achievements and changes meaningful to the players?

    I'm also interested in seeing more systems deal with character resources- money, goods, connections, favors, etc. I'd like to see a resource mechanic that can help drive the story, creating a real sense of desperation and excitement as the characters struggle for material necessities. Fudge doesn't really address this, as far as I can remember. The question is how do you make resource-management mechanically clear and balanced while at the same time making it interesting and fun?

    \n(E) IMPRESSIVE
    \n(F) BRUTAL
    \n(G) LEGENDARY
    \n(H) OVERPOWERING

    \n In reading this, I pondered a different idea, summed up with one of the mottos I try to live my: "Who dares, wins." It was to simply roll any number of dice you wanted, knowing the more you rolled, the more zero-focused the roll is and the greater the potential is in both directions.

    That lead to another idea. So, taking the ladder (and some numbers to go with it), here's the change of my idea:

    Legendary [+5]
    Superb [+4]
    Great [+3]
    Good [+2]
    Fair [+1]
    Mediocre [0]
    Fair [-1]
    Good [-2]
    Great [-3]
    Superb [-4]
    Legendary [-5]

    Then, in your stats for whatever you're rolling, you have a number of dice you can roll for that, like 4 Fudge Dice in "Killing the Vampire King." When that comes into play (however the rules dictate "coming into play"), you roll those dice and hope that you get a grand result on one side or the other -- it's the absolute value that is desired.

    The fiddly bit would come in where you burn yourself out to push yourself farther. If you need to roll more dice after getting a low result, you could "burn out" by reducing the dice in that trait by one to add two dice to this roll. I'm thinking this is a permanent, "you need character points or need to do something special to un-burnout" effect, rather than a refresh like FATE's Aspects.

    This idea could be crap, as it just hit 10 minutes ago and I'm firing it off before I forget.

    Posted By: Ryan MacklinHere's what I visualize. Leave the ladder as is, you still have skills (or whatever) with a rank on that ladder. (Snip)
    \nI was thinking about this last night a bit. An ORE-hack that I've always been fond of: Save the waste dice (the unmatched results of a roll) for later use, but in doing so you have that many fewer dice to use in future rolls until you spend the saved dice.

    For example, if you've just tried to stab an orc using Dex+Stabbing, giving you a dice pool of 6, your results might be - - 0 0 0 +. As per your example, you could use that single + right now for a plus 1 shift on the ladder, but you might also save it for later.

    Let's say you do decide to keep it and you're trying to stab the orc again. Because you're saving a die, your Dex+Stabbing die pool is now 5 instead of 6, but at least you know you're guaranteed one + result. This roll will just determine if you get any other +'s. You roll, and the results are... + + 0 0 -. Now you can spend the one die you saved from the previous roll and get a set of three +'s instead of just two. After spending the die, your dice pool would return to its normal size.

    If that roll had gone another way, resulting in + 0 0 - -, you could save that lone + again, lowering the dice pool for your subsequent rolls by two until you spend those two +'s.

    Fudge ORE doesn't quite feel awesome enough yet though. The humble 0 is getting the shaft.

    The other idea I had was to flip things around ORX-style, where the dice roll doesn't determine success, it determines what you did to succeed. If we go with the old Tri-Stat standby of Physical (+), Mental (-) and Social (0), the symbols have no positive or negative connotations, they just represent the three different ways you could've succeeded. The size of the matched sets determines how high up the ladder the mental, social or physical success goes.

    For each conflict, you roll your dice pool (as determined by some fourth stat or confluence of the tri-stats), and read the resulting sets. If you rolled six dice, resulting in + - - 0 0 0, that represents social success with a +3 shift on the ladder, a mental success with a +2 shift, or a physical success with a +1 shift, You can only choose one of these ways of succeeding, leaving some other dice unused...

    Combine this with the first idea about saving the waste dice and you have some interesting mechanical fiddliness. Using the roll in the previous paragraph, you've accepted the social success, but you may also save some of the unused dice for a future roll. If you saved the two -'s, it means you're that much more likely to succeed mentally. It's a very direct way players to control how their characters behave, without getting into the fluffier narrative territory that may be off-putting to traditional-game players.

    Not to extend this post even further, but I'm now seeing an implied fourth and fifth stat in addition to Mental/Social/Physical.

    The fourth stat would determine how many dice you're allowed to keep. Let's call it Yin, because it's focused on the internal, like inhaling. Yin the size of your metaphorical backpack. If you rolled + - - 0 0 0, and your Yin is 2, you may keep up to two of the unused results from that roll. You could keep one now, saving an open spot for another result later; or you could keep two now and fill up your pack immediately.

    The fifth stat would determine how many dice you're allowed to spend from your reserve. Let's call it Yang, because it's focused on the external, like exhaling. Say you've got a + + stored in your reserve, but your Yang is only 1, you could only spend one die at a time for a roll.

    This presents a tricky issue about whether there's any purpose in Yang ever being higher than Yin, but that's beyond the scope of this already lengthy post. :P

    \n Ryan, one small correction, rolling more dice does make the curve steeper, yes, but that's because there is a wider possible range with less likely extremes. In point of fact, the more dice you roll, the higher the actual standard deviation becomes. I made the same mistake about a similar system, and was corrected on this (by Walt Frietag, I think). People assume that more dice means that there standard deviation goes down, but this is not true. What goes down is the ratio between the standard deviation and the number of possibilities in the range.
    \n
    \nFor instance, the standard deviation for one die is 1, which over three potential results is quite broad, or a ratio of .333. For 2dF, the standard deviation is 1.22, over a range of 5 possible results, or a ratio of .245. So the bell gets narrower looking, but the actual breadth to expect from the results gets wider.
    \n
    \nBoth effects are important to consider.

    \n Mike,

    If you want to get really pedantic, there is no curve at all because this is a discrete distribution, not a continuous one...and a finite one at that. But this distinction serves mathematical rigor far more than it does the discussion.

    The ratio between the standard deviation and the range of possibilities is probably actually the more useful statistic when evaluating a dice mechanic since the result range itself is purely symbolic in nature and can be rescaled at will.


    On another note, the bonus die mechanic from TSoY is very interesting. As has been pointed out, it leaves the minimum and maximum results untouched, and it increases the mean result while decreasing the standard deviation (now a more useful number since the overall range is unchanged). You have both a higher average result and a more predictable result.

    So, in a TSoY-inspired system, an additional skill level increases your result by one across the board, but a "luck point" merely give you a more stable positive dice result. It is interesting to picture what might happen in a system where these roles were reversed: a higher skill level gives a bonus die and a luck point gives you the straight up +1. In the original TSoY-inspired system, spending luck points is good for a guard against bad die rolls, but it is a tactic that won't help you win out against a higher skill level in the long run.

    In the reversed system, I'd truly rather be lucky than good. A higher skill level is nice, but your supply of luck points will be the major determining factor in whether or not you win conflicts unless the difference in skill levels is significant and/or the supply of luck points is low overall. That automatically feels like a less gritty and more epic system to me. Even if I am the only one, I find that interesting.

    \n Yeah, I think Elliot and Scott have the implementation that I like best (I've seen this elsewhere, but can't recall where... Fred or Rob?)
    \n
    \nThere are math terms being tossed about here without, I think, an understanding of what they mean precisely. So let's skip the technical terms for now. To be clear, more dice have the following effects:
    \n
    \n1) the average roll will tend to be farther away from zero than with fewer dice. People who understand the probabilities often make the mistake of assuming that the reverse is true... I did.
    \n2) the chances of rolling a zero go down. In fact the chances of rolling any particular result go down the more dice you roll.
    \n3) the chances of rolling a result at the end of the range of possible results goes down even faster than those in the middle.
    \n
    \nPlotted, these altogether are what produce the "bell" shape in the curve. The more dice, the more "bellish" the shape. This is more centered on zero than a "flat curve," which is the result of rolling a single die. But the more dice you roll, the further out it spreads the average distribution. The average just spreads out more slowly than the range of the curve does. So as a proportion of possible results, more dice means more "zero based." But in terms of actual figures compared from one curve to the next, it's less "zero based."
    \n
    \nSo for what we're talking about here, more dice means "wilder" results, since it's all on the same scale. Not so much in that we'll tend to get very high or low results, but we will tend to get higher or lower results on average than rolling less dice.
    \n
    \nClear as mud?

    \n The graph's a little wonky but it gets the point across. So now we can see what Mike's talking about. As you roll more dice, the chance of hitting a given absolute range centered on zero decreases. E.g. with 1dF, you're guaranteed a result between -1 and +1. With 4dF the chance of getting a result in that range decreases to 51/81 or about 63%.

    The seeming paradox is that if you look at, say, the middle 33% of each range, the probability of hitting it goes up. With 1dF, the middle 33% is exactly zero and the chance of hitting it is 33%. With 4dF the middle 33% of the range -4 to +4 is -1 to +1 (the middle three results out of the nine possible) and as I just mentioned, the chance of hitting that range is close to 63%.

    The solution to the paradox is to note that we're comparing apples and oranges: absolute ranges vs. relative ranges. People always make this mistake about the Central Limit Theorem (that's what we're dealing with here in Probability Theory): the theorem says the more you flip a fair coin, the closer the ratio of heads/tails gets to 1:1; people think this means the more you flip the coin, the smaller the absolute difference between heads & tails. 1028:1000 is closer to 1:1 than 5:1 is, but the former is an unremarkable result for a fair coin even though it has an absolute difference of 28.

    With Fudge dice, we're only interested in the absolute ranges. So, more dice => more likelihood you'll get a result that's far from zero.

Even if you got rid of the "bonus cannot exceed the penalty" (in which case you could give yourself a 10% chance of getting a +4 vs. a 90% chance of getting a +0) it just ends up pushing the math to places where I get tempted to say "why not set aside the dice and just Take Zero on everything".

      \n How to Combine Fudge Traits with the Pool, While Using Fudge Dice (works with regular d6s too, though)
      ---

      You always roll one die. If you want, you can gamble more from your Pool.

      Roll them dice!

      1. Take all your (-) results and put them aside.
      2. Do you have at least one (+) result? If so, count your remaining dice. Increase your effective trait level by one for each die remaining.
      3. If you did not have any (+) results, lower your effective trait level by one for each die in the (-) pool you just set aside.

      I recommend you use Anti-Pool with this, or stick purely to contested rolls (i.e. against someone).

      Under this system, one die roughly equals a Trait level (Trait levels are a little more powerful, though).

      Therefore, unless you're using huge Pools, I'd recommend the following reduced Fudge ladder:

      (Incredible)
      Superb
      Great
      Good
      Mediocre
      Lousy

      (These rules may seem a little weird. But, trust me, this is the only way the math works out. This took me a long time to figure out.)

      \n When I first read Paul's Fudge 1d6 mechanic, I thought "exactly -- good and bad results needn't have equal probabilities." I was also pondering the Snowball mechanic, itself derived from The Pool. OK, so what if we invert this, and make failure more likely with increased numbers of dice?

      The deal: roll some number of d6, at least as many specified by the GM, more if you like. Rolled a 1? That's bad: count the 1's for your down-the-ladder result. No 1, but rolled a 6? That's good: count the 6's for your up-the-ladder result. No 1 or 6? You've "rolled a zero."

      The more dice you roll, the lower your chance to perform at or above trait level: more dice effectively represent greater stress, chaos, or potential for bad things. The odds for getting +1 or higher are less straightforward: they peak at 4 dice (28.5%), then slowly decline, so there is incentive for taking moderate risk. This also means that the probability of a zero result (no 1's or 6's) shrinks rather rapidly: two-thirds to the Nth power for N dice. (This is one reason why I'd use plain old d6 rather than Fudge dice: the extremes are too heavily represented, and the probability of a zero result is less than 4% at only three dice.)

      This provides another way of offering bonuses or penalties. Cool under fire? Great -- you don't have to roll as many dice as the poor fellow next to you. Extra dice also don't lower the mean result too greatly: rolling six dice gives an average result of -0.6, and extremes (+/- 4 or greater) are still very rare (less than 2%).

      Is it still Fudge? I think so. The ladder is still there. There are tons of Fudge dice techniques, not all involving Fudge dice. This one is just more failure-ish. More importantly, would this be fun? Are there any well-known games in which a player's rolling more dice is a bad thing?
