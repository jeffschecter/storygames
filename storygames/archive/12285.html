    \n A friendly game is probably more suited for this style of RPG than a competitive in my humble experience.

    If it's competitive, like you said, people become wankers and just try to one-up each other with truths (facts in HotB) that make their character the King of the World, Inheritor of All, or Everyone Owes Me A Favor or whatever. This quite literally happened in the Houses game I played in.

    However, if it's a friendly game, you're more interested in creating a compelling story than one-upping each other. And, suddenly, you're riffing off each other's facts and building a narrative.

    I prefer to go with the inconsequential facts that might spur something more interesting down the line. Is her hair brown? I don't know... But, it turns out she WAS pregnant though. Pregnant aye? Well, my wager is that she also has a locket with the initials M.J. on them. Hmmm... Those are the initials of our Daimyo. Or, whatever. Now all of a sudden, there's suspicion our Daimyo might have been visiting this concubine and possibly even impregnated her. Did he? I don't know. What does it mean if he did?

    Sometimes it's good to have an idea of what direction you want to take the story in, but sometimes you just gotta wing it. The key is to make those truths able to be riffed off of, or be a seed for a future use.

    \n Fact economies suck as tools of creative agency in roleplaying games. The successful implementations do not make you answer the question of "how do I win this situation using three facts" or the question of "how do I protagonize my character using three facts", simply because facts by themselves are meaningless: as has already been mentioned, the mere facts can always be undermined by further facts, and nothing needs to actually mean anything for the game unless the players give it credence and respect the facts as dramatically significant. This will not happen when the facts you introduce into the game are perceived not as interesting or credible, but rather merely opportunistic. The very best outcome you can hope for superficial and fictionally unexciting facts is that the other players ignore them; the worse outcome is that they'll actively attack your contribution with the fact economy. How do you force the other players to care that it was Bob's dagger and reincorporate the fact into their play? The answer is that you don't, and thus the fact economy by itself has done nothing for your creative participation in the game.

    Donjon is a good example of explicitly adversial fact economy. I think that I'm not stating anything controversial if I say that the game tends towards comedic, chaotic, incoherent fiction that mostly gains virtue from a shared creative understanding between the players; the fact economy of the game works fine as long as everybody follows the unstated rules of good behaviour and do not overreach, do not undermine the contributions of the others. In this regard the game resembles Once Upon a Time a lot.

    What, then, is the effective way of using fact economies? My experience is that the fact economy in Universalis sucks if you try to fight other players via it: "No, actually an even larger elephant suddenly appears." or "What seemed like character A was actually character B in disguise!" are pure undermining of whatever situation might have been developing; continuous undermining of any constructive efforts and refusal to commit to the fictional situation as it is leads to nothing more than a shallow form of parlor narration. Universalis, for it to work correctly, needs you to step back, accept the facts on the ground when they've been sufficiently established, and play some conflicts now and then instead of trying to force your own agenda on the game by introducing more and more facts.

    The more functional approach is explicitly coded into the rules of Spione: in that game the fact economy is used as a carefully metered reward and dramatic pacing devise. The rules explicitly separate facts that are allowed to be undermined from finalized facts, for instance, and fact-setting is so valuable and done so irregularly that the players are naturally driven to resolve dramatic tension by the use of those facts. Thus no fact wars are waged, as the players lack ammunition and important facts get cemented out of reach as certainties as play progresses. The fact that this resembles the way narration-sharing conflict resolution games handle the narration sharing is not a coincidence. Most importantly, the players are not in symmetric character-playing relationships with each other, and therefore do not have pure oneupmanship motivations driving their play; rather, a cooperative spirit permeates the whole thing due to the specific roles the different players take towards the central characters of the game. The end result of all this is that the facts in Spione are not actually "facts" in the all-inclusive way normally used in fact-setting games; rather, they're just a right to resolve dramatic issues interpreted through the lens of certain and uncertain information, as befits a spy game. The only kind of "fact" that game includes is the kind that resolves drama.

    How to implement these principles in HoB and such games? I'd say that a firm GM adjudication of the use of the fact economy would be a good thing: have the GM make clear that frivolousness will be ostracized and undermining of facts with other facts is not allowed. Winnow the constructive uses of facts from the destructive mercilessly. It's a crutch, I admit, but then I don't really get how else you're going to use an ultra-generic narration sharing mechanism like that for satisfactory dramatic play; just like Donjon, it's going to fall into chaotic comedy (or mere frivolousness) unless the group establishes a shared creative accord to regulate the mechanical principle. The difficulty is that without tools such an accord will by necessity be fiat-driven and/or implicit; you just have to feel what types of narration the other players will expect and like. I'm sure that it gets easier with an extended campaign as the players learn to know each other's preferences.

    (Those who've had successful play with HoB shouldn't feel that I'm pissing in your morning cereal here. I completely believe that the shared creative accord mentioned above is not impossible to establish. It's just an illusion to think that the wager mechanics of the game will help with this task. Either you get there and are thus able to use these mechanics without constant fighting, or you don't, in which case those mechanics are a great way for the players to disrupt the game.)

    \n Michael: I'd like to present the idea that the distinction you pose is merely semantic. I say "merely" because many people playing a game like HoB might not be interested in engaging in linguistic sophistry organically intermingled with the texture of the fiction. (Some might, I recognize.) Just consider: if a group really thinks that the limits of fair play reside in the exact semantics of the statement "It's Bob's dagger that killed the guy!", then it stands to reason that the way to defend the meaningfulness of your statements lies in careful phrasing of the same: you can't in fact just say what the characters see or what the universal audience sees or anything like that as commonly seen in rpg narration; you'll have to use your facts efficiently to fight the semantic challenges posed to them. "It's Bob's dagger with Bob's fingerprints on it." is a stronger statement that counteracts the possible counter-argument you presented, for example. If we allow the play to devolve into this level, we're engaging in sophistry that many won't find at all pleasing as a play experience - they're there to play an imaginary game and not engage in outwitting the opposition, after all.

    Furthermore, that same legalistic rigidity acts in the opposite direction as well: if we deem that "It's Bob's dagger." is to be interpreted strictly as a factual statement within its own semantics, we have to conclude that the only way to introduce the "Ah hah, the dagger was a counterfeit after all!" twist is to state thusly from the off-set: "It's a counterfeit of Bob's dagger." would set up this particular plotline quite nicely, but then it's all preloaded into the statement - isn't that just a little bit dull? Wouldn't it be more exciting if you didn't try to preload the plot twist (revealing it to everybody at the same time, note), but rather could just say what the things seem like at the moment? Should you say that "It seems like Bob's dagger, but maybe it's not, after all?" Talk about foreshadowing...

    To me it seems obvious that the narrative tool people are looking for in fact currency mechanics usually is the ability to input dramatic content and have it acknowledged, respected and incorporated by the other players. There is no shortcut into this, and carefully phrasing your one fact to make it inescapably meaningful will inevitably fail if the willingness to acknowledge it is not present in the rest of the group. For this reason the real meaning in "It's Bob's dagger!" is probably not going to be merely literal: what the player wants to achieve by introducing this fact is to cast doubt on Bob's allegiance, motivate Bob to action by proving his innocense, introduce the idea that Bob really might be the murderer... all fine goals, but the important thing to realize is that those are all going to get undermined if the other players aren't reading your contribution and appreciating it for these possibilities it presents. Merely being very literal in reading the fact for meaning won't do anything to ensure that your fact will matter.

    Or, to put the same in a nutshell: does it bother you if the facts you introduce get ignored and everybody instead hares off in random directions as per the supposition in the OP? The reason for that has nothing to do with the fact mechanics and their application, but rather with a lack of creative consideration for the facts that the players introduce. If a game doesn't have any tools that enforce or encourage such consideration, then it's up to the group to get to that level of cooperation on their own. I'm reminded of Zombie Cinema, which actually has its own form of fact currency in the shifting scene framing turns the game features: the only thing preventing these scene frames the players provide from haring off into directions unknown is ultimately the internalized cinematic experience of the players combined with the purpose the players have in framing the scenes. If we remove the coordination provided by the shared genre understanding, we get a very similar system to the one HoB has in that there is nothing in particular ensuring that the players frame scenes that actually build upon what came before in a meaningful manner.

    \n I've been thinking about this. Let me see if I can ofter some more practical advice. This is going to get kind of academic. I suspect John will want to strangle me.

    Okay for purposes of this discussion I'm going to offer a definition of "Story." PLEASE don't get caught up in arguing about this definition. I'm just offering it here to contextualize what I'm about to say. Story is the *development* and *resolution* of a situation that engages an external audience.

    So you'll notice there's kind of two phases there. Subtle point 1) there's a lot of back and forth, overlap and grey areas between the two. Subtle point 2) some story formats have LOTS of overlap because they actually consist of multiple stories. Soap Operas work this way. While one story is resolving another is beginning to develop. My point is that there's not necessarily a hard line down the middle through the two phases.

    I would suggest that in a functional, creatively attentive group Wagers are used two different ways across the two phases. During the development phase Wagers are used primarily to create situation elements and to tie situation elements together. I would suggest that Cunning and Wisdom are used a lot fairly early in Houses game.

    At some point that situation hits creative critical mass and play switches over to mostly being about resolving the situation. I would suggest that Cunning and Wisdom begin to see decreased use (at least for purposes of raw external fact creation) and the other Virtues become more prominent with Cunning taking a dramatic shift from raw observation to calculated action.

    When that happens the game works relatively well. My personal complaint is that there aren't a lot of tools in the game to keep that process on track and so you have to more or less "manually" (for lack of a better term) avoid some pitfalls.

    Pitfall 1: Under development in the development phase. If the GM comes with TOO much pre-developed, aggressively wagering PCs will walk all over his hard work. If the GM under-estimates the degree to which the players are willing to participate in situation development via the wager system then he has no ground to walk on. The game will stall under these conditions.

    Pitfall 2: Never switching phases. The game basically stays in development mode and the players get caught up in adding more and more detail without ever actually doing anything about it. Basically creating an elaborate, maybe even interesting portrait that never actually moves. A variant of this is when some players switch phases and some do not. Such that the the players who are still creatively in the development phase being introducing things that feel too complex or incongruent for the players who have creatively entered the resolution phase.

    Pitfall 3: NPC Theater. Some players will get caught up in orchestrating the lives of the NPCs to the point that they never have their own character do anything. They will basically jigger things so that the GM (or the rest of the players) are forced into telling a story for their amusement. In an extreme case the player figures out how to jigger NPC rivalries and relationships such that his own character can get what he wants without actually ever having to raise a finger.

    On top of that the game is long-form. That actually makes identifying the end of the "resolution" phase kind of difficult. WHEN is a given story over? My general creative guidelines is that all PCs have resolved personal crises such that they would have to re-write significant portions of their character concept to fully participate in another story. There is WAY too much going on in a Houses game to use that bench mark. The Romance system alone lasts whole SEASONS there is NO WAY you will resolve all the issues tied up in a Romance in a single story. So identifying and picking a marker for "this story is over" is troublesome as their will likely be A LOT of dangling personal issues for the PCs unresolved.

    But the general principle is relatively simple.

    Early wagers are used to introduce situational elements and create ties to them. Later wagers are used to resolve the conflicts inherent in that situation.

    But that basic through-line is kind of difficult to manage within the scope and breadth of Houses.

      \n For what it's worth, I don't really read these HoB discussions as saying that the game doesn't work. To me it seems that it works just fine for what it is, within the cultural context it's presented in. The Sorcerer fan (myself, say) just has to get over the fact that not all games are written for him. This shouldn't be difficult at this point of time, different strokes for different folks is pretty well-trod wisdom by now. Gamers need to accept that not all games are for them, designers need to accept that not all dislike is an attack on their game.

      The reason for why HoB doesn't quite work is an interesting topic because I think that the game is a very clear, modernized and stream-lined representation of the goals and techniques of typical '90s mainstream game design (the sort John in part defined at the time). Thus many people around these parts face in it a style of gaming that they've left behind, but riven of many of its external signs. This is sort of similar to how people set up Spirit of the Century and Shadow of Yesterday side by side due to superficial mechanical similarity, never grogging the fundamental differences in underlying creative drives of the games. In this regard HoB is clearly of similar ilk to SotC and not at all like Sorcerer or TSoY; gamers coming from the latter will look at the game in just the sort of confusion we've been discussing in this thread, as the game doesn't serve their interests too well.

      A few words more, simply because it seems to me pretty unfair to characterize your average "indie" game fan as a passive consumer without initiative of his own. The following uses GNS terminology because it's in those terms that I understand HoB and how it's received by the public. Obviously there are several people participating in this thread who think that GNS is so much balderdash, but I beg your indulgence - this is just how my brain works at the moment, no need for you to climb onto the same ride if you don't want to.

      Putting aside the considerable literary virtues of Houses of the Blooded, the game itself does not orient the group for narrativistic play, and it does not provide for clear instructions that minimize "murk", the procedural uncertainty typical of traditional roleplaying. These two are separate issues, but we see both of them surfacing and entangling in these HoB discussions. For instance, I view Hans's writing about how the game doesn't instruct you sufficiently in this light: in truth the text of HoB is, it seems to me, perfectly careful to outline and describe the techniques and attitudes necessary for approaching the game successfully, although in a pretty conversational and eagle eye perspective instead of procedural terms. The part that's missing, and the reason for why Hans and myself among others feel disoriented by the game, is the narrativistic premise, a challenge to the opinions and values of the reader. When I wrote upthread that the game does not explain to me why I should care and towards what direction I should focus my attention, this was the same thing in different words: aside from any lack of sufficient examples or whatnot (which I don't think is true), the game lacks the drive and challenge that orients me so easily with a game like Sorcerer, to pick the example trotted out already. When you approach HoB from this narrativist viewpoint, without realizing the game's nature, it does really seem like you don't quite know what to do with the game, as it does seem like it lacks a point in this regard.

      I think that it might be easier to understand this incongruence in how people approach the game by looking at the "murk" and the creative agenda issue separately. First of all, I should say that I recognize that "murk" is a very biased term for what is essentially celebrated as interpretative freedom and primacy of the play-group in some circles; still, that's the word I'm stuck with for now. My actual point is that I'm not very inclined to get onto a high horse about it if others want to write games that intentionally leave a lot of freedom for the play group in terms of who does what and when. Houses of the Blooded provides plenty of material for understanding the game, but it does not lay out clear procedural instructions for what to do when and how; it does not give bulleted pre-flight checklists, no numbered steps, no explicit instructions for what things a player should concern himself with when playing, no grand theory of any of it. Rather, it's written into an older mold: the assumption is that the players can cooperate with each other, the hope is that they will find the fictional color and issues of the Ven interesting, the expectation is that they will use the game akin to a toolbox in building up a campaign of their own, utilizing the provided tools at their own consideration instead of expecting the design to carry the responsibility. This is a different conception of how to write a roleplaying game, and perhaps a different idea of what the game fundamentally is, but I can't really declaim it as inferior; merely different.

      The creative agenda issue is the one that is the killer for me personally, though: insofar as I can see, the simple reason for why some people don't get HoB despite being fans of all sorts of dramatic "narrative games" is that the game forefronts experience at the expense of choice. I might be wrong about the intention, but the message I got from the game was that my task as the reader and the player is not particularly to have any opinions about the Ven and their lifestyle; rather, my task is to learn of the Ven, imagine them and breathe life into them by the art of story play with my friends, appreciating the vision of their cruelty and passion as lyrical art. In other words, I should approach the game as simulationism instead of narrativism. All well and good, I think, as the very decision to interpret the game as a sim game text surely makes all the various rules click in ways that just don't happen if you try to approach the game like Sorcerer or some such. This whole wager thing, for example: I can totally see that if a player actually has a poetic vision of who his character is, and he wants to express that static image, then he's going to find utility in wagering and manipulating the situation in ways that spotlight this great character he has in this great setting. What the player wants is to exercise his creativity by participating in the storytelling and by showing off his creations, such as this character, which is his special personal project in the game. He wants to run the character through his paces, show him doing all the things that he's supposed to do. The brilliance of HoB as a simulationistic game comes in here in the way the Ven practically have a checklist of the "paces", what you should expect to be able to integrate into your character concept: play HoB and you can expect to get to describe/play witty banter, violent duels, steamy romances, bitter betrayals and so on and so forth. This is totally the list that most Vampire players (and many larpers) I've met yearn to concretize in their play, no matter the game. HoB is the right game for them because it totally allows them to just dive right in instead of having to mess about with the externalities the rest of us are interested in.

      All of the above is pretty lackluster from a narrativist viewpoint because no value-based tensions are described or invited by the setting description. You can't play the human barbarians trot upon by the Ven, and neither are they humanized in a way that would invite sympathy from the Ven PCs, to pick an arbitrary example. Likewise the nature of the Ven themselves is static, there is never a challenge for the player characters to rise above the limitations of what they are. Similarly the Ven society itself is both intensely political and devoid of any real issues to be political about; the very idea of positing a "political game" that will have plots and counter-plots and cunning ploys a priori, with content added as an afterthought, is the very acme of dramatic simulationism - no wonder players expecting premiseful play feel confused. There isn't really any compelling reason for your character to want or do anything particularly out of the ordinary, as the game is written: your character starts within a basically static envelope of expected activities (dueling, romancing, etc.), and he never leaves that envelope to make a statement about anything. Thus, even as the game basically has all the fictional elements you'd expect of a highly dramatic narrativistic game, it's nothing like that - it's dramatic, but in a different, experiental and poetic way. The creative drive is not towards establishing meaning, but towards celebrating it. A schoolbook case of simulationism.

      Now, simulationism is fun, but for me it's not quite as fun as some other things. To get back into the murk thing, a difficulty of HoB from my viewpoint is that I don't care quite enough about celebrating blood opera to do the work of getting the game to run. As points of comparison, I totally can get over the same hump in Dead of Night because it's more gamey and procedural - the creative agenda is the same and I'm about as interested in horror movies as I am in Ven fiction, but it's simply miles and miles easier and more fun to set up DoN (creating monsters in the game is net fun alone, for instance), so that's why it gets over my play threshold. The opposite example is any game they've written about Glorantha: they're mostly just as murky and old-fashioned procedurally as HoB, but because the setting just oozes meaningful tensions and expects me to take a stance, I'm highly motivated to get over the hump, craft a campaign framework by my own hands out of mere setting fluff and go play, throwing out judgments about Lunar philosophy all the way.

      Still, this is not a claim to any absolute and objective brokenness of HoB, just an explanation of why the game's not doing it for me. I've given it many positive soundbites here in Finland as it comes up now and then, and I routinely recommend it to people who like Whitewolf games or AEG stuff - as I usually say, the game is a honest expression of games of its nature and even if that style's really not my thing, I recognize quality when I see it - the system is much more streamlined and to the point of its creative goals than anything they did for this type of dramatic simulationism through the '90s, for instance, and the instructions are more functional as regards the responsibilities of the players vs. the GM.

      \n I'll be happy to tell about my impressions, John, although it's been a while since I read the game text; I'm not able to quote line and verse, for instance. With that in mind:

      The main reason for why I didn't find HoB inspiring as a tool of narrativism is that its focus as a text is squarely in the form of passion instead of its content. The game describes carefully how romance works, for instance, but it doesn't really have much of human context for why I should have my character fall in love aside from the fact that this is expected and it's fun to play romance scenes. To the contrary, the book seems to revel in the idea that the Ven do not think like humans do, and their love is more like a sport than a passion. I'm not really feeling the Ven as human beings with human concerns in the text in general: everything they do is couched as a convention and part of their Ven-nature, even such sublime mystery as love is regulated and thoroughly explained for both its function and its proper place in society. I'm sure that you can reach beyond this in play if you feel like it, but purely as a text HoB never makes me stand up and pay attention to any human issue: the Ven have no external enemies, they begin families as a matter of convenience, their politics are practical powers truggles devoid of philosophy and so on. I'm not getting the sympathetic relationship to the people of the setting that seems like a necessity for narrativism to me; if I don't care for or appreciate the people our stories concern, then those stories carry power only of poetic aesthetic, not the sort of thematic punch narrativism expects.

      Perhaps this could be put succintly: it seems to me that we care of what a HoB character does because it's cool, not because we care about the act itself and the person doing it.

      This same philosophy of aesthetic celebration seems to pervade how a player character is and what it is for in the game. I often speak of "princess play", meaning the sort of roleplaying where you're not playing your character so much to find out who he is and what he does, but rather to display your predeveloped piece of character creation art. This is what the game's rules and procedures seem to encourage with the elaborate premade family backgrounds and other details: you're creating a lot of poetic detail that will get a chance to be expressed in play. What you're specifically not doing in the game is to build up a sympathetic protagonist - your character is not meant to be sympathetic, but rather impressive, the sort of personage we would use as a NPC in The Shadow of Yesterday. Impressive and stylish to look at, to be sure, but perhaps not a dynamic actor of great human passion. The wager system especially seems like it underscores this point, as it does not tell a story of how your character wins or loses: the outcome and the consequences are really not what we're interested in, but rather the process of resolution, the beautiful images of what happens. Choreography your character engages in for the sake of aesthetics, as a doll. Satisfaction for you as a player is found in putting your character in the middle of the events and expressing his nature as a magnificent bastard (or whatever that character's nature is, within the constraints of the setting).

      In the interest of demonstrating differences like you asked, and without wanting to make any claims about relative quality, I'll reflect the way I understand HoB against a game I know well, The Shadow of Yesterday. TSoY is a very similar game to HoB in that they're both fantasy games in original settings, and both have a fair share of pulp fantasy in their ancestry. They are also similar in amount of crunch, social footprint of the campaign and so on. I should make it clear that the following is very likely exaggerated for effect when it comes to actual play: I'm emphasizing the differences in attitude, so this is natural. Besides, what do I really know about how the HoB text should be really interpreted?\n
      • Player characters in TSoY are different from each other in culture, social position and beliefs, and encouraged to be so. They are likely going to live different lives and struggle over different issues, each according to their nature. They are expected to change their world, which is in a fundamentally unstable state. PCs in HoB are uniform insofar as their larger setting context is concerned, sharing a base of values and expectations. The general sorts of content their lives have are largely defined by the Ven lifestyle. Their struggles and passion are expected to be played out in the ways planned for in their lifestyle, and no fundamental change to their lifestyle is to be expected out of the game - the setting will be the same after the campaign as it was before, the point of play was strictly in the journey and not the goal.
      • The concerns of PCs in HoB are primarily characterized by external requirements of form: a Ven needs to have romance in his life, therefore I will now introduce a romantic partner for this character. The concerns of PCs in TSoY are primarily derived from their conditions: this character encounters a fetching person of the opposite sex, will he fall in love? The players of HoB act to fill the scenario of play with content because their characters need content to exist with; the players of TSoY experience content to form stances towards it, have their characters make choices and experience the consequences.
      • The setting of HoB is concerned with documenting the everyday reality the campaign exists in. It is static and concerned with how things usually are. The setting is exotic and many things that are ordinary for the characters in it are strange and luxuriously intriguing for the players. The setting of TSoY focuses on the fault-lines - the ordinary things in the setting are also ordinary for the players (genre-ordinary, at least) and passed on quickly, while the extraordinary and strange things are also points of tension and instability and human suffering, problems that will blow up on the heroes of any story.
      • The HoB wager rules and GMing advice focus on distributing narrative authority to enable the players to express their characters and create a complicated plot. The TSoY Key rules and GMing advice focus on providing the characters with dramatic choices that cast light on their fundamental nature and challenge them to change. The goal of play in HoB is to revel in the Ven aesthetic, while the goal of play in TSoY is to figure out what the past means for the future, if anything.
      \nI'm not particularly offended by this idea that a story fetishist wants the game designer to hold his hand, by the way. I think that there's nothing wrong with solid game design that makes the game, whatever it is, easier to engage with, but I certainly know a lot of people who disagree on this as well. Might be that this is because I'm a game designer of sorts myself, so characterizing my play as suckling at the designer's teat is just putting my creative contribution from one box into another ;D In any case this idea of story vs. combat fetishism is interesting in that it sort of implies a different observation: is it that "flexible" gamers who do not focus on (fetishize) particular facets of play are happier with a more vague and organic game text? Perhaps, at least that seems to be suggested by John's observations. Should we all ask less of the designer? As I suggested earlier, from my own viewpoint this matter seems more like a consumer motivation issue - with the scene living an unprecedented culturally rich time, I have plenty of luxury to pass on games that present me with too much work for too small a payoff; the games that get play from me thus end up being ones where I really like the fluff and am willing to put up with a less than perfect game text, or ones that make play so easy that I'll do it even if I'm not extremely passionate about the fluff. Interestingly my own game Zombie Cinema fits in the second box for me: I can't imagine starting a game of All Flesh Must be Eaten or anything like that, zombies just aren't at that level of obsession for me.

      In other words, yes, I do usually like succint and conscise game texts over a hundred pages of exposition and advice. That seems awfully unfair to something like HoB (which has a very rich and honest game text, as I've mentioned), but the fact remains that pedagogically speaking the short text that turns difficult concepts into clear procedures is often the more valuable to me. This is what I tried to express when mentioning Hans's critique of the game text earlier: I don't really think that HoB is badly written, I just think that the things Hans is looking for aren't there, and therefore it seems to him like something was left unexplained. This is, of course, a Forge theory attitude I'm displaying: a belief that some creative concerns are fundamentally incompatible. A more traditional take would be to believe that a game can be right for all people, I guess.

      \nHans,

      Along these lines my problem is that the rules mix and match two different attitudes toward the culture. The ven have all these crazy laws right? And the text challenges you break them, right? My Story Now brain immediately latches onto those sections of the text raising this question: "When is cultural/social transgression worth personal gain?" When do you go fight a duel WITHOUT assembling a jury? When do you murder someone in someone else's home WITHOUT getting the host's permission? When do you play by the societal rules and when you do break them?

      So SOME culture rules in the text clearly fall into that category. But OTHER culture rules are tied directly to the mechanics. Consider how the Romance rules work. The Romance rules conform directly to the predator/prey ritual valued in the culture. There is no way to challenge the ven's culture idea of Romance *and still engage the mechanics of the game meaningfully*. The sub-system becomes irrelevant if you want to play a character who challenges the cultural definition of Romance.

      You see this in the LARP rules for dueling. It talks about how in Ven culture it's extremely rude and and a grand breach of etiquette to interrupt a duel. Which of course gets MY mind thinking about what might be important enough to my character that I would be willing to breach that etiquette. At least in THAT example it's pretty clear that the REAL rule is simply the social conceit that if you want in on a duel you have to be in on it with the other players before it begins. It's a social rule of the game *masquerading* as a cultural rule and it's pretty easy to get the dramatic effect you want if you're attentive to it: Go buy into the duel at the beginning with Style then *pretend* your character is jumping in the middle to interrupt.

      Less so the murder rules. There's no way to murder someone as a deliberate character statement that you're willing to defy the host's hospitality.

      It's really Houses dual nature where cultural rules that demand breaking and questioning lie side-by-side with cultural rules that are tied so deeply with the mechanics that to question/break them is to not mechanically engage the game. And in some cases it's difficult to distinguish the two such as assembling a jury for a duel. Is that a cultural ritual to be flaunted if my character's passions demand it? Or is it a social game rule justified via culture designed to protect other players from being drawn into duels "unjustly"?
