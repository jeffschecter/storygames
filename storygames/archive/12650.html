\nThis thread reminded me to post something that's been on my mind lately.

When we talk about growing the hobby, occasionally the topic comes up of "what if roleplaying became trendy and suddenly asshole jocks were playing it and then it would suck." Everyone generally agrees that's unlikely, but I think I have a handle on why that is. It has nothing to do with technological options like video games or TV being easier / more appealing. Or rather, they are easier, but not because they're technological.

Rather, it is that anything that can be considered real roleplaying* requires a willingness to be vulnerable in a social situation. This isn't about Creative Agenda, though I might argue that the (need for) vulnerability is most profound in Narr games. It's simply the nature of the activity that when participating in it we open ourselves up to have our very deeply realized personal visions fail, be criticized, or even be subject to ridicule.

Think about the stereotypical group of beginning gamers: awkward teenage boys. They're looking for closeness and connection with other people, as well as the sort of "awesomeness" that is packaged into the idea of being a kickass wizard or whatever. But sometimes they don't like what they find so much. The kinds of recrimination and arguments that such groups often experience don't just have to do with bad rules design, but more to do with people who are at an extremely emotionally vulnerable stage discovering that, hey, this roleplaying thing can really hurt when it goes badly. Conversely, people who were awkward teens or tweens when they started gaming but who managed to have functional experiences with it will often really grow and change as a result.

Nota bene: GMing also requires this willingness to be open and vulnerable.

Matt

*This isn't a thread about 4E, but maybe part of its appeal, but also part of the reason so many people pan it as "not a real RPG" can be explained not only by how close it is to a boardgame but that *along with* the fact that it's quite forgiving.

    \n I would take it one step further, role playing requires a willingness to be vulnerable in social situation, which requires trust. Role playing games are an expression of how the world works and how people work. Gaming shows people these truths by explicitly modeling human psychology, sociology, and physiology and by explaining a system by which these factors interact with their [un-] natural [perhaps quasi- or sur-] reality. Vulnerability and weakness is an inherent part of this inner and outer exploration, however, I think more importantly it requires trust. One big difference that I see among gamers is that, I believe, they are more accepting and trusting crowd. In many ways, good RP is the social equivalent of a "trust fall".

    \n It doesn't work as a general principle for me, unless you define "real roleplaying" so that it's a tautology or your standard for "vulnerable" is exceedingly low.

    I mean, every one of those asshole jocks that are so much fun to demonize has had a coach criticize his play style, his attitude, his aggressiveness level, his physical technique for playing the game. They (I might say "we" here, but it's been a long time) are *accustomed* to having their best physical efforts fail, be criticized, and be subjected to ridicule. An unwillingness to risk that level of vulnerability is not why asshole jocks are not playing roleplaying games, because if it were, well, they wouldn't be playing sports, either.

    And most of the teenaged boys I've seen arguing over roleplaying games -- it's not that roleplaying hurts when it goes badly, but that when you equate you winning with your character winning, and your character loses, you feel like you have lost, and losing hurts; but because it's a shared fantasy and there are a couple hundred pages of rules, you might be able to argue your way to winning. The asshole jocks are doing the same thing -- but instead of arguing over whether it was right that Thog the Mighty-Thewed Warrior died when he took on the red dragon and rolled two 1s in a row, they're arguing over whether the Red Sox would be having a better season if Josh Beckett had not wound up on the 60-day disabled list. And they're not identifying so strongly with Josh Beckett as at least one gamer is identifying with Thog, so it's far less personal.

    I would buy a claim that many roleplaying games are more satisfying if you allow yourself to be vulnerable, but I don't think it's anywhere near a requirement for everything I'd call real roleplaying.

    I find it interesting to think about how that requirement for vulnerability shapes the kinds of games that people play, and the kinds of people who play games.     \n There is one extremely salient difference between the performance anxiety of RPGs (and other sorts of social/performative activities) and the sort that athletes (for example) deal with. In sports, we keep score. You have a (theoretically) objective measure of whether you did a good job. Even in the performing arts, the audience is a "thing" out there, not part of your social environment in the same way as the members of your RP group. I don't know about "vulnerability" as such, but (at least the creative dimension of) RPGs definitely require putting yourself up for judgment by your friends in a way many other things don't.

    Which may say some things about the enduring popularity of rules systems that try to remove judgment from the activity - whether "physics-model" crunchfest or "procedurally complete" story game.

    \n Hey Charlton,

    Those things are definitely not what I am saying.

    I think, in your regular Tuesday night game, you can proceed with no social fear because what you've all sat down to do is a known quantity, right? Everyone at your table understands how role-playing games work, and you understand the "rules" - not the game rules, like a shovel does 2d4 damage, but the social context in which a role-playing game happens, the social context in which one person says "The vampire penguin leaps at you!" and another person responds "Oh yeah! I wham it with my shovel. 2d4 damage!".

    Someone new to all this who sits down will likely experience some confusion or anxiety or fear or what-have-you because they don't know that stuff (yet). How do you talk? How do you know what to do? Do you have to speak in dialogue? How do you win? What are the weird dice for? What if I'm not clever, or entertaining, or funny? Etc.

    So that new person can either take refuge in jokes or mockery or hyper-violence - any of which will protect them socially (and emotionally), or they can engage with the game in a more serious way - playing their character and contributing to the game fiction. Which requires them to be vulnerable. Which is bold! And leads to a more fun game for everyone, I think.

    Obviously this is different for experienced gamers, but I'm talking more about new players, which I think is the context suggested by the OP.

    \n The vulnerability isn't about not knowing the rules or fear of not performing well (although those things also happen) the vulnerability is saying "I care about this" or "this makes me excited" or "I don't know how I feel about this". The vulnerability is putting a small piece of yourself, something you care about, something you're invested in, naked and weak, entirely into the hands of your friends and their imaginations.

    That's such a scary thing that many people respond by armouring their little token of themselves as much as possible - making it the toughest, hardest, most impenetrable thing in the imaginary world. Others respond by not caring about their little token much at all, or pulling back from caring about it as soon as possible.

    I, personally, am very afraid of being judged. A good, healthy kind of fear. The kind that keeps me invested in the game. The kind that makes me want to forgive any lackluster contributions of others, so that in turn we'll have more trust between us. The kind that makes me want the whole experience to work.

    When we are in the spotlight, it can put us on edge. But if we can manage to handle that edge, that anxiety, we emerge more attentive, taking greater stewardship over the results. Feeling more accomplished when it goes well.     I don't think that "creative people" face unprecedented vulnerability. But it's a different vulnerability than you face when you're on a basketball team. It's "I made this, and think this is the direction we should go," versus "this is the limit of what I am capable of." Different kind of spotlight, different kind of vulnerability.

    \n I don't know if this is quite on topic; a lot of the focus of this thread seems to be on performance anxiety and the thesis that even the most basic forms of role-playing involve some measure of vulnerability.

    But reading the thread invokes these possibly tangential observations. I've had a lot of pretty good role-playing recently and the two games I'm playing a bunch of are Apocalypse World (one-shots and two campaigns) and Love in the Time of Sei\xf0. I don't think of myself as "emo porn catharsis guy" (maybe I'm just in denial - in my defense one of my favorite games is Danger Patrol!), but it occurs to me that player willingness to be vulnerable is one of the key components in what's made some of my recent gaming so excellent.

    You take a game like Apocalypse World and you pitch it to an array of gamers, people already comfortable with pretending to be other people, maybe talking in funny voices and rolling oddly-shaped dice, etc. They look though the rules and read a couple APs. Holy ****! The PCs might get in each others faces and fight with each other! They might even... have sex with each another. I might have to pretend that my character is having sex with someone else's character. Probably even someone who's gender is not in alignment with my sexual orientation!

    Love in the Time of Sei\xf0 - Same thing! And my character will probably die or lose in some other manner! And it's GM-less; I'll have to improvise a bunch of stuff. I'll totally be in the hotseat!

    My point is that both of these games have a natural filtering dynamic for their audiences. Unless the participants really drift the game, it's going to involve some significant vulnerability. For me personally, that's where a lot of the value and fun is.

    Inside of both of these games I'm now recalling that there's an additional level of vulnerability that can make some really golden scenes. Aping their real world counterparts in emotional nakedness, it's the post-coital scenes. Talk about vulnerability! Even if you totally fade to black on the act itself (my common preference), you've got two fictional characters being played by two real people and there was likely some heavy emotional grist that led to that act. Now comes the even harder part. What do you say to each other afterwords? It's different based on context, but it's almost always really good. Like the fictional conceit has prepped both of its actors to express something transcendent or, at a minimum, real.

    Sorry if this was digressive, Matt. Maybe it's the case that at least part of what prevents role-playing games up to now from becoming the next soccer, cross-stitching, or even World of Warcraft, is the willingness to be vulnerable. But it also happens that it's a significant part of why I love this hobby, at least as expressed in singular moments of the kinds of role-playing games I enjoy most.

    Take even bunch of couples and singles and put them in a room together with the express social agenda of cutting loose, hanging out, and getting to know each other. Maybe a bunch of them have known each other for years even. Add alcohol, its suppression of inhibitions and the accompanying understood permissiveness allowed by same to "get real" and maybe be a little vulnerable. And you STILL mostly get status games and a bunch of tedious small talk about inconsequential bullshit, stuff I can scarcely tolerate in my less generous moods. Being vulnerable is difficult and rare. I'm pretty sure that's built into the definition of the word.

    Maybe this hobby will always be small but I'm thankful I've found people within it that are willing to express themselves creatively in ways that are especially vulnerable (being creative and sharing your creations with others is inherently vulnerable). It's interesting (basic?) that it's largely done via avatars.
