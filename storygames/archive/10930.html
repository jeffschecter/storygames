Today I got a package from the UK in the mail \u2013 the test print of my new game "While the World Ends \u2013 A story game about a future on the brink of change". Since the test print came out OK I have opened the Lulu shop to the public.

The game was written as my entry in the "Write an RPG in 2009" challenge on the WRNU boards. It was first released in Swedish, but after some discussions here I decided to translate and release the game in English too. Thanks again to Jens, John and Micah who proofread the game and gave it a lot better language than it would otherwise have had. And thanks to Seth that got me started.

It is a game for three to four players that can be played in an evening. It is GM-less and no-prep, or rather everyone does the prep together at the beginning of each game.

Before the game starts the players generate a futuristic world together, decide upon an important change that it is about to go through and two possible outcomes of that change. When the game starts the players follow the adventures of the main characters as they go through the story pursuing their goals and avoiding their fears. At the same time the players try to influence the story to lead they at the same time compete to drive the change into causing the outcome that they strive for.

That is, the outcome that the players strive for. The players are divided into two teams that compete to decide the outcome of the change, this is an RPG where you can actually WIN the game. Also the game is good fun... and Awesome!

While the World Ends

--

Edit: Two years later I have an actual webpage for the game. wilper.wordpress.com/while-the-world-ends/.

    \n Hi again,

    My game While the World Ends has been around for a year and a week now, and it is slowly working it's way into distribution other than Lulu. However, for stores to carry it they need margins, so I will have to RAISE THE PRICE in order to sell the game. Isn't it ironic?

    If you already spent all your money on Thanksgiving / X-mas stuff but still want to play an AWESOME SWEDISH INDIE RPG, send me a whisper. For half a promise of a play report or review posted somewhere online I'll send you the PDF for free.

    \n ivan: I will not speak for Sam here but...

    There are two ways to play WtWE, they are not explicitly explained in the book, but emerge out of actual play.

    The first is "active", where you decide what you want to do or want to happen, play according to that and then update the mindmap accordingly.

    The second is "reactive", (and I think this is what Sam is refering to) where you look at the mindmap, and it will tell you where your story will proceed and how to follow that route, and you play by that.

    These two ways, or perspectives of play if you will, are totally interchangeable in play. You can freely swap back and forth during play, and one team can play one way, while the other plays the other, without any adverse effects on game enjoyment.

    --

    Also, for the prep phase before the story starts there are clear instructions for what to do when and in what order to get everything right and create an evocative setting. You can pick up the game, sit down with three friends and start from a blank slate and the game will entertain you by guiding you through an interesting story that was inside you all even if you didn't know it. (I'm not saying that this is a unique feature of WtWE that can't be seen in any other games, just that I find that WtWE is very good at doing it.)

    \n Last night I met with some players and we played the A Thousand and One Tales from the Palace of the Mad Sultan hack, and at the same time play tested a variant set of rules for three player games.

    The hack was inspired by Meguey Baker's 1001 Nights game, and shares the general theme of courtiers in a sultan's palace. But it played very differently. It gave a similar experience, but on a smaller scale. We were three players and the game took two and a half hours.

    The sultan's favorite concubine was carried away by a giant eagle after having released all the songbirds from their cages. The concubine's cousin, a gardener girl dressed as a boy, scaled the walls and set off to cross the western desert after seeing the night roses bloom one last time.

    And the poor guard ended his days on the bottom of a well, cursing the sultans plot to make him go down there in search of an ancient secret passage leading into the harem.
