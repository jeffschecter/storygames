Playtesting ... Better

Andrew Kenrick
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Play Advice
I'm running a little experiment at Conception at the end of the month that I've dubbed "Playtest Camp." It's arisen from my plan, as in many other years, to take some of my in-progress designs along and subject them to friends. Except this year, I've opened this up, and hope to take a small number of other games along to playtest. But more than that, I'm planning on printing a bunch of these docs out and getting other people to playtest them at the con, not just me.

As I've been putting together playtest docs of my own to take with me, I've been doing so with an eye to the possibility that someone else might pick them up. So I've been asking myself some questions, and I'd like to throw them open to you guys too. I'm not just talking for the purposes of Playtest Camp - I'm talking for the purposes of playtesting, period. I'd like to talk about how we playtest our games, or rather, how we can facilitate other people playtesting our games.

How can we make our playtest documents as user-friendly as possible? How can we maximise the chances that someone will not only pick the document up but will read and play it? How can we avoid overwhelming/befuddling the user so they don't play? And how can we make it as easy as possible for the user to provide the designer with the most useful possible feedback?

My ideas at the moment are revolving around reducing the amount of time the user has to spend learning the game rules to start playing. I'm thinking some up front briefing sheets to get the players as comfortable and onboard as possible, and on with playing the game and seeing what works.

Also, I think actual, factual directed questions and design outcomes wouldn't go amiss at all. If you can point the user in the right direction, even if it's something as simple as "we've tried this, we think it works - does it work for you?" I think it can only help. And, as with usual design threads, these questions have to be more specific than "whaddaya think."

So what else? Feedback sheets? Post-game debriefings? Design commentary? Examples a-go-go? What?

It also occurs to me that this might go hand in hand with Paul talking about ashcans, but I'm not sure yet.

Talk to me!

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarA playtest is, after a fashion, ausability test.
    It is, to an extent, but a playtest will always run into useability problems that a finished game won't ... or rather, it will run into different problems. People pay money for a finished game and therefore feel more obliged (guilty? likely?) to play it, to work it into their regular schedule. The game's useability comes into effect at the play stage, but not necessarily the purchasing stage.

    A playtester has no such obligation, and may have more trouble fitting it into their regular schedule as a result, unless they put aside time for such things. The game's useability comes into effect here, making the difference between successful play and unsuccessful play, but there's also another useability issue - that of the useability of the playtest document itself, which can mean the difference between the playtester not even wanting/trying to play it.

    Hey Andrew,

    People pay money for a finished game and therefore feel more obliged (guilty? likely?) to play it, to work it into their regular schedule.

    I don't think this is true. Gamer culture attaches no responsibility to the customer who purchases a game. The transaction is simply considered to give the purchaser ownership of the game object, to read it or not, use it or not, however as he will. Producer and Consumer are roles within the context of commerce. Not being further obliged to a Producer after a purchase transaction is a social protection we afford to a Consumer within this context of commerce.

    So, with the goal of actually provoking playtesting, some folks try incentives, esteem rewards, non-monetary transactions, or social mojo. These can work, but they can also be quite uncomfortable. Because the relationship still has an Authority/Resource context analogous to the Producer/Consumer context of commercial transactions. And so it becomes an uncomfortable social pressure.

    I think if your goal is to provoke playtesting what you need is a alternative kind of transaction that engages the purchaser differently than the traditional Authority/Resource or Producer/Consumer purchasing transaction contexts.

    Paul

    The very best playtests are also an advertisement for your game.

    "Hey guys, I playtested this fucking cool thing from the Internet."

    I strongly think that playtests should be conducted by strangers to you and you should not be involved in the game.

    If they fumble and misstep and are miserable and they all hate your game, that is important information. If they don't understand something, that is important information - more important than their understanding of the thing they don't understand.

    As to how to be a good playtester, here are my rules of thumb (not always adhered to completely):

    Use every piece of the game more than once. If there's a character creation section, make 20 characters. If there's a situation creation section, make 20 situations. If there's a setting section, design 20 campaigns and/or adventures. If there's a combat section, run 20 combats.

    Go from setting to system and back. If the setting makes you think "hey, I bet my guy would be climbing this mountain", go to the system and see if you can have fun mountain climbing. If the system makes you think "I bet everyone here carries swords, they rock!" flip to the setting and see if everyone indeed carries swords.

    Take notes on everything.

    This is hard work and usually necessitates compensation, in the form of money, food, or credit.

    The mission I have in mind for ashcans is for them to be a design step for games that aren't delivering on the designer's goals. You don't do an ashcan because you can't organize your own local playtesting, or because you can't get a game complete by GenCon. You do it because you've gotten to the point that you need to gather some of your eventual target audience into an engaged feedback community if you're ever going to solve the design hurdles that are holding the game back.

    And if this is where you're at, then yes, I'd recommend you write a game text that speaks directly to your design goals, and directs attention to the design problems that you know exist.

    Setting playtests are absolutely vital because settings are of paramount concern to many gaming groups. Remember that not every group buys "system does matter" to the same degree and nobody is going to be convniced about it by reading your game text because their group's social structure will shit on your game text every time. Plenty of people cheerfully play GURPS Forgotten Realms, Storyteller swords and sorcery, and I myself scoff at people who whine about the "feel" of a game. (God dammit, I have no feelings, I am an unfeeling monster, what I want is meat, not suet!)

    The questions to be answered in setting playtests are crucial:

    Does the setting I've created both permit and encourage the kind of play I want to see?

    Is the setting easily customizable to individual groups' tastes? (And have I given decent advice on how to accomplish this customization?)

    Are players moved and excited by the setting material to want to play in this game? To want to make a character? What sort of characters do they make and how do those characters fit into the setting?

    This is another area where it's important to do hands-off playtesting. If you wanted a political, Machiavellian setting and you got back a bunch of crime-and-punishment action-and-melodrama play reports in your setting, you know your setting materials have failed to produce what you wanted them to produce. You might look at your espionage setting and see "Smiley's People" but if a big chunk of playtest GMs look at it and say "Hm, looks like Spies Like Us", you have an issue.

    Note that not getting to where you want with a playtest - whether it be system or setting - is not necessarily a bad thing. If you're getting a wide variety of reports but everyone's having fun, you're on the right trail, even if about eighty percent of the reports have nothing whatsoever to do with your "vision" for ideal play.

    Setting playtesting is of vast, VAST importance. For a lot of groups, it's more important than your system because they don't care about your system. I know, I know, everyone who is virtuous and good and right-thinking does, but as Lyle Lovett says, "down here among the unclean, all your good just comes undone."
