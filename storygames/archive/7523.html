    \n I think both the "magic" and "realism" in "magical realism" are traditionally things that RPGs have been not so great at.

    Magical realism style "magic" isn't like the magic in fantasy fiction. It isn't a concrete thing or a force. It's more like the magic in myth, where impossible things happen in a pseudo-storybook way that supports the themes of the fiction. It's an over-romanticization of reality, basically. You know how in gothic novels, everything is impossibly overwrought and dripping with angst? It's like that except the magic in magical realism causes things to drip with... er... uncanny coincidences and powerful imagery. Sometimes the premise itself of a novel is somewhat fantastical, as in Midnight's Children, where it's highly allegorical. But this isn't magic you can turn up to 11. The magic isn't the dominating force in the fiction, but a kind of color or embellishment. Nobody focuses on the magic in magical realism, they take it for granted. That makes it hard to turn it into a traditional RPG mechanic.

    The "realism" is also tricky because when you "mechanicize" behaviors in an RPG, you structure them in such a way as to make them unnatural. You provide incentives for doing certain types of things and disincentives for doing others, which makes the task of "acting naturally" more difficult. Honestly, I think a more Jeep-like or freeform approach is much, much better for getting at realism in roleplaying, for that reason.

    I definitely think magical realism can work in roleplaying, I just think it would need to build on a lot of the more loose and freeformy stuff that people have been working on recently, rather than a game that strictly mechanically structured.

    \n My personal take is that to establish realism you need a system that is internally consistent. If a fireball burns through wooden doors one day, it needs to burn through them the next. Although I haven't played it, I'm very interested in Mutant City Blues because it takes super powers (which a crafty GM could easily replace with magic) and codifies them to a forensic level, allowing the characters in the game to understand supernatural powers in much the same way that a crime scene investigator understands ballistics, DNA testing, or criminal profiling. Nothing turns back the clock to see exactly what happened, but MCB cops are able to make some assertions about what happened, based on an understood structure. This idea however requires a lot of prep in advance.

    The alternate, which I think GM Steve was getting at is to use Mortal Coil (or a similar system) where realism is established by creating the rules of magic as you go along. This allows players to make assertions like "We all know magical fire leaves behind a residue of the caster's emotional state", throw some tokens forward and that becomes law. The more this is done the more defined magic comes and the more the players feel they have a grasp of its rules (of magic). Personally I prefer this method because it takes the work off the GM and give the players a greater sense of ownership and responsibility (if they create wildly contradictory rules of magic, it's their own damn fault).\n
    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonThe "realism" is also tricky because when you "mechanicize" behaviors in an RPG, you structure them in such a way as to make them unnatural. You provide incentives for doing certain types of things and disincentives for doing others, which makes the task of "acting naturally" more difficult.
    \nThis is always a problem with granular mechanics. It doesn't just affect magic though. If a fighter in D&D has a +5 Holy Avenger of ass kicking, there is NEVER a reason for him to punch an opponent (at least one he wants to kill) even though cinematically, it would be really cool. The solution (in my mind) is to remove the granularity. Take a system like Wushu or PTA and anything you describe is exactly as awesome as you make it.

    \n Magical realism seems to demand a couple of things that might be tricky to pull off at the gaming table\n
      \n
    • A blending of subjective and objective views of reality where the subjective view of significant events occasionally overwhelms the objective.
        \n
      • The trick here is identifying which moments are "significant," and which character's subjective viewpoint becomes the objective viewpoint.
      \n
      • \n
    • Characters within the story fail to properly appreciate the impossible/wonderous/miraculous events around them
        \n
      • When a player does create an awesome blending of the real and the fantastic, the characters in the world are forced to downplay its wonder, even if the players are excitedly high fiving around the table. This disparity might be a potential mood killer.
        • \n
      • \n
    • The reader's role in Magical Realism is that of the outsider, who appreciates the strangeness and irony inherent in the genre.
        \n
      • Players who take control of characters are switching viewpoints between the outsider and the insider. Trying to roleplay a character such that their motivations and actions result in maximum irony without falling into a surrealist mess might be difficult.
      • \n
    \nPerhaps the solution is to start play with the brainstorming/roleplaying of a non-magical story skeleton. The players have a beginning, middle and end to set magical scenes in, and guide the behaviors of the characters. Scenes may or may not be played linearly through time.

    For example, a realist story might be: young man received a windfall of $1,000,000 and decides to take a round-the-world trip looking for good causes to donate the money to. However, without a set of criteria for "good cause," compounded with the fear that he might be robbed/killed, this proves extremely difficult. Eventually, he resorts to leaving bundles of money in places where he thinks deserving people will find it. (This is more or less the plot of Dave Eggers' "You Shall Know Our Velocity!")

    From that skeleton, scenes are formed. It might also benefit from formally identifying the humanistic themes to be explored (in the above example, the novel dives into themes of loneliness and isolation, guilt, absolution, and grief).

    I think that magical realism might benefit from some of the common restrictions in jeepform: namely, fixed characters and plot. Not to say that magical realism needs to be jeep, but that the signature parts of magical realism are a bit like a jazz riff improvised around the set of chords given in the theme. I feel like the crux of the issue here is figuring out how to communicate themes to facilitate that improvisation.

    \n Here's a hopefully illuminating example:

    The plot of Midnight's Children, according to Amazon, is: "Within one hour of midnight on India's independence day, 1,001 children were born.     \n This is a complicated topic, obviously, since there won't be clear boundaries on this stuff. (The wiki article quotes a Gene Wolfe quip: magic realism as "fantasy written in Spanish.")

    Here are some bullet points from the article I think are of interest:\n
      \n
    • The fantastic elements may be intuitively "logical" but are rarely explained
    • Characters accept rather than question the logic of the magical element
    • Exhibits a richness of sensory details
    • Uses symbolism and imagery extensively.
    • Presents events from multiple perspectives, such as those of belief and disbelief or the colonizers and the colonized
    • Creates uncertainty in the reader, who has to discern whether to believe in the magical or the realist interpretation of the events in the story, and to what extent.
      • \n
    \nSo, from these bullet points (which are at least a kind of guide), I think there are a lot of things we do in RPGs that would have be re-tooled for Magic Realism.

    We're used to the "magic" being manipulated by the protagonists in RPGs. But not so much in Magical Realism. The Magic is "out there" -- sometimes seen by a protagonist, sometimes not, but always seen by the READER.

    The uses of imagery and sensory details depends not on immersion or seeing things from a PC's point of view, but an author's or narrator's point of view. Players must step outside the description or details for the enjoyment of the rest of the group -- describing things that might not even be from the PC's point of view (though the PC might be seeing it) but as a condition of the world. (The woman who simply ascends because she is too beautiful, from One Hundred Years of Solitude... We don't get this from her point of view. We don't get it from anyone's point of view except the author -- who is telling this fact to the reader.

    Expanding on that point, there is a "reality" beyond the reality of the characters -- the reality of fiction and details that normally are the province of a GM in a game, but I think would be better served by everyone having a crack at bat.

    Most RPGs are built more along the lines of Dramatic Narrative (that is, conflict between characters, like in plays or movies). Magic Realism, I think, is more the province of Narrative, which is work of novels and prose, where the accumulation of details, the choice of words and descriptions is part of the joy of the experience moreso than the plot or conflict. (Here I'm stepping outside the works of say, Crichton or Grisham, which are kind screenplays waiting to be written, and sticking with work that really only works effectively in its native form of novel.) So such a game wouldn't be about "What does the PC do?" but "What does the Player say?"

    Shifting POVs would seem to make sense. Either sharing characters, or switching characters, or shifting in and out of Player/GM role, or something.... I don't know. But something where the Players are attached to seeing the world from one POV but seeing the world as BIGGER than one PC can imagine -- on a regular, poetic basis would be key. (If such a thing is possible. I'm just talking here!)

    Mundane activities -- and I mean that literally, so that "war" is mundane -- is the "focus" of activity for the PCs... so that the Magic Realism gets to pop out in contrast.

    Genuinely giving up on building Plot (again, a needed focus for Dramatic Narrative, but not so much for sheer Narrative), so that the moments of realization, emotion, sensory detail, realizations about life and so on are the focus.

    Anyway, those are some thoughts about the RPG version off the top of my head.

    I think it'd be a radically different game than a lot of other games -- but my guess is a game or games like this are already out there and I haven't played them yet.

    Me too, Jesse. I think there's a literary fiction/genre fiction distinction here too. In literary fiction, often, very little actually happens as a result of the characters' actions, or the central characters' actions anyway. The story isn't about what protagonists do, as in genre fiction, but about what characters experience.

    Personally I usually find that unsatisfying, and I don't think it's essential to either magic realism or literary fiction - certainly in pre-20th-century literary fiction, protagonists were frequently doing things. I tend to reject that as a necessary distinction between genre fiction and literary fiction and look for well-written, evocative, non-cliched fiction that also involves characters who are protagonists, who are having an impact on the world.

    So there are a few ways we could approach a magical realist RPG, and some of the design decisions we made would depend on our stance on the issues above. If I were writing one for my tastes, I might stipulate that the magic, simply as such, is not a tool which people can pick up and use to achieve things. It's an element of the setting which is largely colour, without direct mechanical effect on events. However, if you give it meaning - if you link it in to what characters are like and what they think and feel and what their issues are - it can become an element of changes that go on internally for the characters, changes of perspective, feeling, priority - which is the literary side - and these in turn can be the basis for taking effective action in the world, for bringing things about in the fiction, which is the other side.

    If your tastes run more to the kind of fiction which is usually put in the "magical realism" category, where characters don't have much agency, you could drop that second part.

    Layering of detail would have to be an important part of emulating this particular type of writing, too, so again, if I was designing this, it would probably require a certain amount of description before an element of the world would be powerful enough to have an effect.

    \nHuh. Neat. In reading the first thread (on story and scene framing) I was reminded of the "Burkean pentad" of act, scene, agent, agency, and purpose (with which I must confess only a passing familiarity, but it's on my list of stuff to learn). Kenneth Burke argued that in any given instance drama emerges from the "friction" between some pair of those elements. For example, a little girl (agent) may curl up and shiver (act) beneath her blankets (agency) because she is afraid of monsters (purpose) in her bedroom (scene). Burke talks about the relationship between any given pair of elements as a "ratio," so the "scene-act ratio" is the tension or friction between where the action takes place and what takes place there. So if the bedroom were warm and cozy and decorated with smiling princesses but the little girl nonetheless remained huddled in a ball, you'd say the scene-act ratio was a source of friction, contributing at least impressionistically to the drama. Right? Because it says something that the little girl's room offers no comfort.

    In any event, the value of this to Humble Mythologies (or any other magic-realist game relying on explicit framing rules) might be as an "inventional tool" -- a way for figuring out where the magic breaks through in any given scene, if it does. So you frame your scene by relying on the Burkean pentad (which is basically just who, what, where & when, how, and why) and then let your game mechanics tell you between which pair to introduce the friction.

      \n The key thing to me, which may be only me, about Magical Realism and why its often difficult to do as an RPG is that in magical realism you may violate the laws of logic but may never, never, never so much as push towards even the slightest violation of the laws of emotion and humanism.

      Be so pretty you fly off into the sky and are never seen again? Sure, no problem. However, see below.

      Do anything, anything at all, that does not feel emotionally and personally real and blood honest? You've fucked it.

      Of course, the crux of that is that to really hit magical realism you have to believe, on some level at least, that the suspension of the laws of logic is real.       \n For my game, I've thought a lot about magical realism and how it can apply to games. With The Fifth World, I want to to give domesticated people like most of us a glimpse of traditional ontology and epistemology. Part of that means modeling the game on oral tradition, but it also requires examining bridges already built\u2014like magical realism.

      I don't think magical realism could have emerged anywhere but in Latin America. There, traditional people have had a long time to live alongside and with domesticated people. I think really getting to the heart of magical realism stems from that. From domesticated epistemology, magical realism involves this surreal suspension of logic, where the interior state finds expression in the exterior. But in the traditional epistemologies that inform so much of Latin America, this does not conflict with realism whatsoever. Interior does not differ from exterior; imagination and intelligence do not originate in the human skull, but form an ecological relationship that humans partake in, rather than create ourselves. I think the intense creativity of magical realism and its vibrancy in modern literature really illustrates how much the ecological concept of "edge," so critical to permaculture design, extends into our traditions. I see magical realism as an obvious edge between the "world-as-machine" epistemology of the post-Cartesian West and the animist epistemology of traditional people.

      The implication there, I think, illustrates why we have such a hard time coming up with a mechanic to drive a game in a magical realist direction. You can't tack on magical realism as an afterthought; you can't put it on a bullet list of criteria to meet. It has to emerge from the most basic, underlying logic of your project. I have grave doubts that you can really do magical realism justice unless, to one degree or another, you can take magic seriously as a real thing. I've just finished a playable, playtest draft of my game, so we'll see if it works out on the table as well as it does in my imagination, but I abandoned the hope of any kind of magical realist mechanic some time ago. Instead, I based the most basic, underlying assumptions of the game in a traditional epistemology. For instance, that people don't have attributes, traits or characteristics; that people exist primarily as nodes of relationships. I think the magical realism will follow from those premises; after all, the vibrancy of magical realism in literature really comes from nothing more than the injection of traditional epistemology into the domesticated imagination.

      I have seen a trend among those books that have helped me most; they all seem to start with a proposition of, "What if we try to understand traditional cultures in their own terms, and dare to ask, if only for a moment, how their outlook could make sense, without dismissing them as either liars, fools or madmen?" A tall order, as Tim Ingold points out, when confronted with stories of how people changed shape and flew through the sky, admittedly. In addition to the corpus of magical realist literature, I would recommend The Spell of the Sensuous by David Abram, Animism by Graham Harvey, The Perception of the Environment by Tim Ingold, No Word For Time by Evan Pritchard and The Lost Language of Plants by Stephen Harrod Buhner (reading this one now, and it's not nearly as "hippy-dippy" as the title might lead you to expect). The easiest to get a hold of and read I linked above, an article by Joe Sheridan & Roronhiakewen "He Clears the Sky" Dan Longboat, "The Haudenosaunee Imagination and the Ecology of the Sacred."

      \n Well, maybe someone like Thorne Smith might be a bit avant la lettre to the movement and give you a perspective?
      In a Thorne Smith novel, the protagonist usually pusues a disatisfying modern life while a supernaturally-endowed hedonist tries to shake him or her out of it.
      That role is taken away from an active antagonist in the magical realism novel and transferred to the setting itself.

      So, you have a group of characters in a community, any community, and all of them are dissatisfied somehow.
      Each player develops a social life and concerns for a character as well as a central disatisfaction - something the character can't breach by normal means.

      Then the players all brainstorm one impossible thing that all of them have experienced. They have all experienced this impossible thing, or, at least, an aspect of it, but they might not have all experienced it at the same time... or in the same place... or in the same way.

      During the game, the players pursue their characters' lives.
      It's mechanically easy to do something mundane and emotionally unrewarding, something you are expected to do. Something your role in society demands.
      You should probably make a list of these demands at the start of the game.
      For each one, list something nice and mundane that your character should want, but doesn't.
      (A gold watch. A pretty painting. A rich husband. A political position. Tenure. It really depends on the individual players' tastes.)

      Whenever you try to do something more emotionally rewarding, you have to add weird shit to the one impossible thing at the center of all this.
      It calls to you. You hear the story of that woman who was so beautiful she flew. The elevator exists again, this morning.
      White Cordelia is being served at the bistro. The weather argues with you. The children explain how to find the secret color in the crayon box.
      Each level, layer, die, card or point of weird shit you add disrupts your life more - ripping away another... uh... social hit point.
      Add a stigma or a problem or a new obligation your pursuit of this weird shit has imposed upon your character.

      If your physical rewards outstrip your emotional rewards, you hit a breaking point.
      The other players describe how that one impossible thing tries to consume you. As if you don't belong in the world and it does.
      You can try to fight it, but at a dear cost.
      The alternative is losing everything... or your stakes or however you wanted to set that up.

      If your emotional rewards outstrip your material/physical rewards, you transform.
      The material world accepts you as a new kind of impossible thing. Or a new part of the old impossible thing.
      The laws, duties and rules that you live by change.
      But does that really bring you satisfaction?

      Please don't ask me for research to back it up; it's assembled from so many sources that I have no way of doing that. That, and I'll second William S. Burroughs when he says that it's not necessary to prove, only to state.

      Here's hoping this ain't a can-opener to a can of worms.


      THING #1: Rational, Magical, Communal
      There are, that I know of, three general worldviews. By "worldview," I mean a model for the nature of reality and causality.

      The rational worldview says that the world we experience is an objective reality that can be measured, perceived, and comprehended. It says that causality is linear: a cause produces an effect at some point later in time. This should all sound familiar, because this is the worldview used by nearly all Westerners, as well as all the "modern" cultures. This is also the worldview used by early cultures in their so-called sympathetic magic (not actually magic at all).

      The magical worldview says that reality is abstract and incomprehensible, and that the world we experience is a metaphor for that reality. It says that causality is synchronous: one abstract force overcomes a conflicting abstract focre, and as a result we experience both the cause and the effect. This is a simple concept but hard to explain. For an example, let's say you shot me to death. By a magical worldview, I didn't die because the bullet damaged vital organs; what really happened is that, in some abstract arena, your power overcame mine and as a result of that, you had an opportunity to shoot me with a gun, you pulled the trigger, the powder detonated and propelled the bullet out of the gun, the bullet hit me and damaged vital organs, AND my body shut down from the trauma. The role of time in all this is irrelevant, because time too is just a metaphor. I believe that this worldview was common in ancient cultures, and I also believe that it is common in young children even today. It's also the approach necessary to make sense of most religions, especially Eastern ones.

      The communal worldview says that there are many different worlds (dreams are a world, memories are a world, thoughts are a world, the Land of the Dead is a world, specific times of day are worlds), and that they are all real, and that they are all subjective. It says that causality is relative: the functioning factor is your relationship with things. That is, if you stub your toe on a chair, it is not because you moved your foot where that chair happened to be; it is because your relationship with that chair is strained in some way--the chair was angry at you, so it hurt you. If you caught a deer, it was not because your tracking skill enabled you to; it is because, through tracking, you contacted the deer, created a relationship with it, and it offered itself to you. This worldview is common in aboriginal cultures, and it was probably also used by early Britons and Picts. It's also sometimes known as animism.

      (Note also that most people who hold a magical or communal worldview would not describe it in the logical terms that I used. They instead would use experiential, anecdotal terms that sound like symbol, delusion, and/or falsehoods to Western ears.)

      These worldviews, while seemingly contradictory, are not mutually exclusive. It is not uncommon for one person to use more than one. In Europe in the Middle Ages, the dominant worldview was a combination of the rational and magical. This sort of cognitive dissonance sounds very strange and impossible at first, so I'll give you an example of how it works. Let's take the oil situation these days. According to a rational worldview, there is a limited amount of oil in the world, and as it is used, the amount decreases; therefore, we are running out of it. According to a magical worldview, OIL (as a concept) is losing its power. According to a communal worldview, our relationship with oil has become aberrant and dysfunctional.

      The thing is, if you look at these explanations with an open mind, they aren't contradictory. I'd go so far as to say they are all true. That is how a culture can use more than one. Interestingly enough, no matter which one or combination thereof is dominant in a given culture, that culture's sorcerors and wizards (but not necessarily their shamans) will almost always use all three, picking between them for whichever is best for the situation.
