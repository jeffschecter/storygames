\n I've been working on some maps that I put together to use for games like In a Wicked Age, Red Box Hack, Trollbabe, and so on. I thought I'd make a little downloadable map book with ten or so maps. But I keep waffling on whether it's useful, or if there's a way I can make it more useful. I'd like some feedback.

The maps look like this. They are small continents or island groups with evocative features:



Gamers
What would add value or not add value to a book like this:
- Situation seeds, a la Wicked?
- Name lists, perhaps with pirate, seafarer, or explorer themes?
- Location features, such as "buildings on a port town" or "landmarks on an island"?
- Hints on using these maps in particular games?

Game Designers
Let's say I was to include a paragraph of text (with your permission) about a way to use these maps in your game.
- Is that even relevant?
- Would that make you happy?
- Would that NOT be cool with you?
- Got any ideas what that text might look like?

    \n I love this idea. Lord Minx is right, though, don't add too much information. The names on the example map strike the right mix for me, though, rather than his suggestion of pure maps w/o text. Names are important. They're evocative:

    If I were playing IaWA with my pirate oracles, and laid down the example map along with the initial oracle pulls, the players would all be able to grab onto the labels on the map as imagination-fodder. If there's buried treasure, is it at the beach of Glittering Sands, or is it hidden on one of the (far more dangerous sounding) Eleven Wrecks islets? Do the Fire Pirates all braid matchcord into their beards ala Blackbeard? Or are they called that for some other reason? 'Gracemoor' sounds like a civilized sort of town, but the architecture of the icon on the map looks... different. More imagination-fodder there.

    Lists of names, maybe as little sidebars to the map, would be cool. Names, in my opinion, are sometimes very hard for players to come up with on the fly. Especially evocative or thematic ones. Most times, I don't have this problem myself, years of GMing have made me practice until I'm fairly good at riffing off a dozen non-jarring NPC names when I need them -- but I've seen players absolutely founder trying to do it.

    Other sidebars with lists of name-seeds for landmarks or buildings in a town would be useful for the same reason.

    Speaking of towns; including at least one or two maps of towns with buildings labeled (like the map-tiles of yours I've seen for Stranger Things) would be extra-cool. I love the look of your town maps, and again, named locations are sparks for the imagination. A wild-and-wooly pirate port-town would be lovely, (now that I'm thinking pirates), with locations like 'Plunder Bay' and 'Hangman's Square' and 'Governor Lynch's mansion', and so on.

    One thing to think about; the ability of users to re-purpose the maps. In my example, I took what seems to be intended as a general-purpose 'fantasy' map and ran in a different direction with it as an island in a mostly-historical Caribbean pirates game. That wouldn't have been as possible if the map included more explicit fantasy elements. Example: I initially mis-read 'Eleven Wrecks' as 'Elven Wrecks' and started thinking about where the Elven Invasion Armada went down, and why. Different game-seed, there... Re-purposing is a two-edged sword, of course. Explicitly-themed maps, like, say, a map of an imaginary Old West territory, or the Clanlands of the Orks, or of a Superhero Metropolis allow you to be very evocative of that theme, but then the map is really only useful for that theme... Both sides of the idea are good, I don't know which I personally would like better.

    EDIT: What Jason said! Doing labels as overlays allows you to do themed label sets...

    One way or another; if you produce this book, or a series of books like it, I will buy them. I love maps!

    --
    DainXB
