    \n I'd pick three games you really love and let them decide between them. Don't edit your choices based on complexity or theme; they are adults who can handle it. Part of this exercise is demonstrating the rich ecosystem and ease of prep and play that is part of the hobby. If you pick stuff that can't be resolved in three hours, wrap it up and see if they want to keep going another time, or try a different game.

    Three players, or you plus two players, is a wonderfully luxurious set-up for any game.

    Fiasco for three goes really fast - just 12 scenes, so it can get a little incoherent. It is not ideal but it would work if everyone was excited about it.

    \n Dave,

    By the book, you're supposed to call it out formally, yes. In practice, it's often so obvious that no one needs to say anything. If it's not the latter, though, you as the GM can call for conflicts and name stakes - a good way to push things along.

    Sister Petulance: "You aren't needed in Sleepy Brick Town! We have our own Watchdogs."
    GM: "And that's a conflict. What's at stake is your right to be in Sleepy Brick Town."

    That's too heavy-handed for most games, but gives you an idea of the possible range. If you're at a con and you have 20 minutes left, this could be a thing you could try.

    Thanks! Great answers.

    Are you definitely not endorsing playing with three players and extra dice, then? I mean, that option is explicitly called out in the book, but I could see it being one of those things which sounded good at the time but never worked out right.

    \nSure. I've played it a lot. In fact, according to Ben, I've probably played it more times than he has.

    Skew is very good to introduce people to role-playing games, because:\n
    • it's very easy to explain, in general, broad terms. That's because the core mechanic is "we're taking turns adding to a story".
      • \n
    • it's very easy to explain, in a stricter sense, and to facilitate. That's because the instructions are written in a step-by-step fashion, by dividing the game up into "phases", and each rule change or new rule introduced means entering a new "phase". Plus, the instructions for each phase are concise enough that you can literally read aloud when the time comes, and it doesn't detract from the game at all.
      • \n
    • it's a SF game - the kind of SF which is based on regular everyday life in the real world, with a big twist. Thus, everybody has references for it, which mitigates blank-page syndrome. And a lot of world-building happens through play, organically and quite effortlessly.
      • \n
    • it's a good, complex (but uncomplicated) game which provides both a venue for self-expression and a reasonable intellectual challenge. It involves both creating a mystery and solving it. And the design opportunity afforded by "phases", to introduce intriguing changes of pace into play, is used to good effect.
      • \n
    • it does school players into various common aspects of role-playing games, from the very basics such as cooperation and a tension between cooperating with and surprising each other, up to interesting technicalities, such as that there's a "master of ceremonies" who facilitates play and there's also a (separate but possibly overlapping) GM role, with specific creative authorities - but the latter gets introduced late into the game, after starting from a position of equality with all players in identical roles.
      • \n
    • it's a short game, a one-shot running from about 30 to 90 minutes or so, which is much shorter than most other RPGs, and a reasonable enough time even for people who aren't into storygaming already.

    Thanks, good stuff! I have the same question(s) about Downfall.
    \nDownfall isn't like Microscope at all. The only similarity is that you do build a world, but the process is entirely different. You pick the flaw at the root of your society's collapse, then you follow steps to build the nature and traditions of that society. Then you make three characters that are at the heart of that collapse (the hero, the pillar and the fallen) and role-play those characters to see how it all falls apart.

    So lots of neat world-building that leads to lots of dramatic role-play.

    The world-building steps very cleverly to make it easy to create an intimate world without any big jumps.
