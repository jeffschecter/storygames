I got permission from two groups at Gen Con this year to record our game sessions at the after-hours game. Unfortunately both of these games took place in the Embassy Suites lobby where the background noise is non-trivial. You can still hear most of what's going on in the games, but it's not exactly easy. I'm linking to a pair of 32kbps MP3 files (both between 30 and 40 MB), but I've got lossless versions if someone wants them. Especially if they've got crazy-magic software that can remove background noise. So, without further ado...

Drifter's Escape (2 hours). This game took place on Friday night. Ben Lehman rounded up a group of four to playtest his game-in-progress, it went pretty well. The players were: Ben as the Devil, Brennan Taylor as the Drifter, Carrie Benstein (I know that I mangled that last name, I assume Joshua Newman will correct me) as the Man, and myself as the Bystanders. It was a ton of fun. Unfortunately my battery died right after we finished the game, so I don't have a recording of the after-game discussion, which was good stuff.

Agon (2.5 hours). This one happened on Saturday night. Jonathan Walton ran a game for Shreyas Sampat, Selene Tan, myself, and Ian Burton-Oakes. We took a break in the middle and I paused the recording, and then forgot to turn it back on for about fifteen or twenty minutes, so it's somewhat abrupt. Again, this was pretty fun, but we ran into a sort of unpolished point in the game. Jonathan talks a bit about it in his post-con review of the game. My thoughts from the game are that Agon rocks, but needs some more polishing before it's really going to live up to its potential.

So, I'm hoping that some of the players from these games will chime in with their thoughts. I haven't listened to the recordings yet, so I haven't had a chance to go back and see just how much I forgot and how different the game was from the way I remember it, but... Well, I figured I'd share now anyway.

Thomas

  • Thomas Robertson
    August 2006
    Ben,

    Sure! I'll go ahead and provide my current thoughts, and then I'll provide them again after I listen to the game recording.

    You asked for a specifically moral bent to the evaluation, and it turns out that I'm having some trouble forming one. The game was, from where I was sitting, pretty much devoid of moral value. I don't think the game (as a design) is, but I do think our game (the actual play) was.

    I feel like there's a tremendous amount of potential within the mechanics for compelling 'the lesser of two evils' type choices, but I feel like we weren't making those sorts of choices in play. Which is okay, not all stories must have morality as a primary focus.

    I also think that any sort of moral examination was hampered by the shortness of our game. The Man and the Devil, quite explicitly, are evil. You pointed out at least once that you didn't like our stakes because what the Man demanded was not morally questionable really. We only had one 'good' person, and after he was bought to that status he didn't have much impact on play before his death. I suspect that given more time to play (maybe another four hours or so), that we would have had a better chance to see what our world was like with actual moral people in it.

    So, I don't think that the game was morally powerful for me. But I do think that it was great as a story-generation engine. I really love the interplay between the Man and the Devil.

    I do have some concerns regarding end-game conditions, but I need to think on them a bit more before I'd be willing to put them forward. I'm not sure that they're actually problems.

    So, thoughts?

    Thomas
