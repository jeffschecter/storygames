\n So, I just bought a copy of Burning Wheel Gold. One of the other guys in my gaming group also bought a copy. We've talked our group into playing. Based on what I've heard for several years, I'm excited.

I've read reviews and actual plays that have raised questions in my mind. I suspect the answer to my questions are probably, you know, in the book. So, specifically, it is my understanding that the hub and spokes (which I already have) is, itself, a playable game -- which makes it sound like the other subsystems: duel of wits, fight, &c. are somewhat optional. Do any experienced players have recommendations for different configurations. By that I mean, are there subsystems you keep, some you replace, and some you ignore? Is the game best for you if you keep every speck of it?

I can imagine people saying things like, "wait until it arrives and play it as written; then you can start fiddling with it once you have the complete experience." And, right right, I'm sure that's what we'll do. But, forget about that advice for a second. I'm still curious about what different groups do. It's methadone time. Thanks.

    \n I like to add all the subsystems, but only as they're important.

    Basically, they're all implicitly available, but are only used when it's interesting to add some depth to a specific scene.

    So, I'll never say "This is a game about politics and not about fighting, so we're not going to use Fight! at all," but I will say "This duel between you and the Evil Chancellor seems really important and dramatic. Maybe we should break out the full Fight! rules for this?"

    \n I agree with FigureFour AND John. I like to think of the subsystems as zooming in and going slow-mo. Don't do it for goblins or buying shoes. It takes a long time and not every interaction matters as much as every other one. This is the director John Woo's problem. Everything is in slow-mo. You want to do it for stuff that you care about.

    Also, there is a rule where the GM picks how zoomed in (which subsystems) to use, but then the players can zoom it in or out one level depending on how much they care. The GM might pick Fight! but if no one cares, do Bloody Versus and then get to the good stuff.

    And if you want to zoom way out and speed it along, don't forget Say Yes.

    (This interfaces with bangs; the GM can see what stuff seems challenging and important and keep banging the beliefs that get "zoomed in on". Make the movie full of good parts and just imply the other stuff to keep the fiction justified feeling).

    Note: I think you might be asking how much effort you need to put into getting everyone on board with all the rules. I'm not going to sugar coat this for you; BW rewards rules mastery. Not just in a min-maxy way, but it is simply more fun if you start to grasp the way the systems interlock, and everyone at the table should get at least somewhat on that level.

    Play The Sword. It is well-crafted, easy to run, and perfect for showing off a few of the sub-systems.

    \n Listen to everything so far in this thread. (Although I didn't know about the zoom-in zoom-out rule, is this new in Gold?)

    Also, general recommendations for new players/groups to BW:
    1) Stick with the Hub and Spokes as you learn the game (as it tells you to).
    2) Duel of Wits is the first main sub-system you want to use because it is awesome! (and the easiest)
    3) Avoid using Fight for your first time during a long campaign. Practice with some pre-gens and a one-shot or something. Gold may have made it easier to learn and less dangerous, I don't know.

    \n I think he meant what he said. Rules *mastery*, not a read-through or a tutorial. BW is simple enough to start with, but you'll find that the more fluent you are, the more fun it is. Additionally, it's geared towards long campaings - some system rewards only show up after several sessions.

    (I only ever used Hub & Spokes, with a single instance of DoW, and I'm sure there's plenty for me to discover yet).

    (Also I'm surprised noone mentioned this yet: get the Adventure Burner book if you can; it was written for BWR so sample character stats may be slightly off, but still makes essential reading because BW core book tells you what and how, but not always why - whereas Adventure Burner points out the reasons for mechanics and helps you use them better or strategise).
