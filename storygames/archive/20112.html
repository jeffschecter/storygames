\n Thought I'd throw this question out to the community. Anyone have any good examples of game mechanics that deal with humanity lost and then later regained?

I'm looking for something along the indie story game variety. Odd is fine.

I'm working on my game Unstoppable Power which involves people who get super powers and how it warps them away from what it means to be human. I've got the mechanic down with how thew loose humanity. I also want a redemption mechanic which allows them to regain their humanity by doing actions like reconnecting with people they left behind and paying respects to people that died either by their hands or through collateral damage due to their their negligence.

    \n There is Sorcerer, of course.

    Humanity in Sorcerer isn't a measure of degeneration like in WoD games. It's how close you are to the cliff edge.

    Humanity can be formally defined, but doesn't have to be. Ultimately it's the group's sense of right and wrong. When your character does something that is a potential humanity loss you roll Humanity vs itself (it's a 50-50 shot whether you lose a point)

    When you do something that demonstrates humanity you roll to gain humanity using exactly the same roll.

    \n So here's what I've learned from publishing two games on this theme ("Det sj\xe4tte inseglet" and "Svart av kval, vit av lust"): You don't need a meter telling you how your humanity looks at any given moment*. It's enough to tempt the player to make the character commit inhumane acts. In these games, one of which is about tragic swashbuckling heroes and the other about vampires, players tempt each other with mechanical advantages for doing immoral things. If you refuse, your effectiveness is eroded and you're not likely to be able to achieve your goals. If you give in, you are powerful enough to achieve those goals, but in the process you're becoming someone you despise.

    The beauty of this system is that the humanity is all in the players' perceptions, and it's tested time and again, probing to see where you draw the line. You don't want to imprison your friend? Ok, next challenge will be stealing from your father. Oh, you're prepared to do that? Ok, next time we ramp it up a bit. After a while, maybe the imprisonment of your friend is a pretty small step to take. By testing the characters, their humanity is evaluated, and as they commit various heinous acts, it deteriorates. But there's always the possibility to redeem yourself and do the right thing. The game never forces you to do these things. But if you have a last-minute conversion and decide to be good, chances are you'll end up paying with your life.

    * This doesn't mean you SHOULDN'T, just that you don't have to.
