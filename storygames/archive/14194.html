    ABSTRACT

    There is a need to integrate a range of stakeholders, both military and non-military, into the planning and conduct of operations, both civil and military. These endeavors necessitate the inclusion of many stakeholders, each of which bring diverse agendas, values, and differing degrees of experience and training. Anecdotes suggest that techniques used to inform planning and subsequent operations often fail to reflect the complexity of such multi-agency environments. We report on a technique that has been used successfully to explore the issues evident in multi-agency planning and operations. Specifically, we report on the use and suitability of Engle
    matrix gaming methods to simulate the complex nature of the problem space. The key to success of these activities is related to the level of preparation and we discuss what issues should be considered with regard to the scenario, personnel and overall planning.

    \nComposing Effective Environments for Concept Exploration in a Multi-Agency Context. Helen Mitchard and Simon Ng (Defence Science and Technology Organisation, AUS)

    From the conclusion: "Although such games prove little in a narrow scientific sense, the motivation for gaming is to encourage creative, innovative thinking about problems that resist analysis with more conventional approaches and methods. Given our tendency to ascribe similar thought processes to others whether probable or not (Witlin 2008), the use of Engle matrix games to investigate possible reactions is a valuable and often neglected technique. The role-playing nature of Engle matrix games provides insights into the special problems of command and control, which despite its origins is not only a military term. Engle matrix games are important educational experiences, providing participants an opportunity to become aware of facts associated with possible conflicts. Positions, expectations, perceptions, facts, and procedures typically are challenged and improved as the game proceeds. For these reasons it is suggested that the Engle matrix game technique may be usefully applied to interagency coordination issues."

    Well done, Chris.

    \n Well, the contacts that lead to this started over twenty years ago. I first wrote about Matrix Games in Wargame Development's newsletter NUGGET in 1988. WD included a lot of gamers from Sandhurst in England. That lead me to meet people in the British Army who then used the games as early and 1995. They met Australian military people at conferences - I'm totally out of the loop on this - who contacted me maybe ten years ago. We spoke on the phone but mainly emailed. I wrote up a paper for them on the game and how I thought it could be run on computers. I've been out of the loop since then.

    Now the links to the French Army I got through the Forge.

    So I guess it is all about meeting people and seeing what happens. I've not asked for money for anything to do with these games so that wasn't a barrier. I just hope that they can help improve planning and save lives. I'm alway open with all these various people about being Muslim so that that never becomes an issue. I think better planning will save Muslim lives as well as soldier's lives.

    I think it is cool but I'm really not part of that world. I write hobby games and use Matrix games in psychotherapy. The closest I've came to the military was taking for ROTC courses in college (free credit!)

    Chris
