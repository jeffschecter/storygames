I just downloaded my pdf. I've skimmed it, but haven't read it in depth yet. These are first impressions.

The good:

Robin Laws seems to have worked in some of his ideas from Gumshoe.

The illustrations are pretty good, even if the layout is uninspired.

The bad:

I never played HQ1, so it's still a bit alien to me. The dice mechanic seems a bit unnecessarily complicated, especially when it comes to contests. I thought we'd gone beyond having to compare results on charts.

The pdf is $20. They don't have a bundle. So, if you want the physical book as well as the pdf, you're out $50 plus shipping. I think the Evil Hat guys (free pdf with print purchase) and John Wick (Houses of the Blooded is a mere $5) need to school them on this one.

The company they have doing their electronic billing/distribution hasn't exactly worked out the distribution thing. I had to go to their support site and mess around for a few minutes until I stumbled on a download link. They haven't emailed it to me yet.

    \nThis edition is in the form of generic RPG rules, rather than being specific to the Glorantha setting.
    \n
    \nI won't attempt a review, but this edition contains story-games goodness such as the following.
    \n[ulist]
    \nOn the first page of text, there is as clear expression of creative agenda as you could have: "Where some games reward memorization, an instinct for math, and the willingness to comb through multiple rulebooks... HeroQuest tips the scales for creative improvisation, verbal acuity, and a familiarity with the techniques and stereotypes of popular fiction".
    \n
    \nShortly after there is a sub section titled "Thinking in Story Terms", which contrasts simulative and narrative approaches to a chase scene, and declares its allegiance to the latter.
    \n
    \nAn entire chapter titled "Playing Stories" provides rules for choosing target numbers to create story rhythm, and advice on choosing between the contest types to provide pacing.
    \n[/ulist]

      \n Mikael,

      Your examples of Don't Rest Your Head, Spirit of the Century, and Sorcerer don't seem to make any sense in the context of what some have written about HeroQuest 2.

      In none of those cases is the GM pre-determining whether the Player will succeed or fail at the role.

      In Don't Rest Your Head the GM is assigning a greater cost (in terms of fictional result) to get better odds to beat the GM's dice. The Player can either increase the odds or not, according to his own desires. It's a gambling decision. The Player gets to decide when he's willing to risk greater consequences or not. And even then the outcome is not pre-determined.

      In SotC the GM isn't saying the Player's Character will succeed or fail, but only to reduce odds for appropriately interesting moments based on the Player's choice of behaviors for the Character (and certainly it has nothing to do with the rhythm of the story the GM has mapped out in his head.)

      In Sorcerer the GM's assigning of Bonus Dice or Penalty Dice have nothing to do controlling the pacing of the narrative, nor determining success or failure. They are given for the same reasons that Fan Mail is given in Primetime Adventures -- the narration is fun and pleasing. It's the GM's way of saying, "Thanks."       \n I think Heroquest 2 is a great and coherent design, furthers the state of the art in a particular field and also differs from Heroquest 1 quite a bit. Here's why:
      \n
      \nHeroquest 1 presented a well-liked, quite coherent core mechanic for solving any conflicts with any abilities and without scale boundaries. However, it didn't define nearly as coherently how this system should be used: what should be modeled, where the focus should be, how resistances should be set etc. This allowed the ruleset to be drifted to both Story Now and The Right to Dream play but also resulted in a lot of confusion and incoherence. Mostly, the ruleset itself actually proposed a world(Glorantha)-simulating approach with mastery scales set for various Gloranthan entity types, complex rules for different kinds of magic and heroquesting, and so on. Yet mostly these weren't bad for Story Now play either, because Gloranthan reality itself focuses much on communities, passions, magical imperatives and their conflicts - prime material for premise-building.
      \n
      \nNow finally on to Heroquest 2. The core mechanic is completely the same, although it too has been honed to perfection (augmentation is better, extended contests are better). But what is more important is that now the text is actually a very coherent guidebook on how to apply that core mechanic from a particular perspective. And that perspective is now the simulation of dramatic story.
      \n
      \nThe pass/fail cycle is at its center, and it's a great design. It's really simple: for each success it ramps up the resistance of the next conflict and vice versa, leaving the GM to explain this by introducing enough circumstances in scene framing to make it believable.
      \n
      \nBut what it actually on a deeper level is is a feedback system operating within the game, dynamically guiding (but not forcing, thus maintaining suspense and the feeling of surprise) the game to follow the dramatic structure of good stories. It doesn't stop there. There are now possibilities to tweak the resolution system to suit particular dramatic situations (pyrrhic victories ala 300, different consequence tables for rising action/climactic scenes, ...) as well as optional actions in contests that allow particular story-relevant actions like risky gambits in desperate situations.
      \n
      \nIt's not illusionist either. Nowhere in the text does it say that the GM can fudge results any which way. All resistances are decided before the roll of the die and even the quoted and much maligned mock contest rule only goes one way, being actually a refinement of "Say yes or roll the dice" into "Say yes or roll the dice or secretly say yes and roll the dice for suspense".
      \n
      \nSo HQ2 is to my mind the first really coherent story simulation game. Now, what this means to creative agendas is actually not so simple and quite interesting. Certainly it means that this is very much your game if your Right to Dream is about celebrating dramatic stories. It also means that HQ1 was probably a better choice if your Right to Dream was about world simulation.
      \n
      \nFor Story Now, the situation is much more complex. Being generic, there is no longer by default the rich foundation of Glorantha to create premise, yet there are still rules for communities (and better ones than HQ1 at that), and now all your abilities are those that make you a unique and interesting protagonist, as it is stated that all those boring common abilities are subsumed by your occupation keywords and such.
      \n
      \nFrom a rules perspective, the text itself doesn't really give you direct guidance on setting up or resolving situations interesting in the Story Now sense. But if you already know how to do so, the rest of guidance on applying the system is so congruent with how Story Now stories usually play out that it'll probably support it very well (but maybe not as good as HQ1 did when you created your guidance if you have acute Story Now instincts).

      \n Here is my summary for HQ 1 owners.\n
        \n
      • It's generic. Glorantha is confined to an appendix as a sample setting. You don't have to extract the rules for other settings
        • \n
      • There is advice on creating other settings for HeroQuest, particularly on how to model magic, technology, and superpowers
        • \n
      • Extended contests have been simplified. They are faster in play and map better to simple contests. In effect each round is a simple contest that allows you to score resolution points toward victory instead of deciding the contest. First to five wins. In fact the other night we appreciated that a simple contest can become extended by treating the simple contest as the first round of an extended contest when you make the wrong call
        • \n
      • Group simple contests make a mass effort by the players much easier. Previously we modeled this as one 'lead' who everyone augmented. Now everyone rolls for their side, totals their resolution points and the side with the highest total wins
        • \n
      • Consequences are different for rising and climatic action allowing winners to be suffer adverse consequences at the moment of victory in the climax. A big complaint on HQ1 was that it was binary win or lose
        • \n
      • One augment only. Under HQ1 augment hunting had become the 'game within a game' as folks scoured their sheet looking for bonuses. From something that was supposed to reflect 'he killed my brother, now I strike him down in revenge' for a lot of groups augments became 'I'll get back to you when you finish totaling the numbers on the sheet'. So the change here is explicitly to require augments to enhance the game for everyone around the table not just be invisio-advantages for munchkins. To compensate the numbers are much bigger. +6 or +9 is common for an augment now.
        • \n
      • No special rules. Remember how in HQ1 you had bonuses for equipment, positioning etc. No more. It's either an ability like Knightly Armor 10W or it has no mechanical effect. The only real modifier now is a Stretch, when you really bend what an ability could before and a specific ability bonus to script protect narrower abilities in comparison to broader ones.
        • \n
      • Clearer explanations. Remember that because of Glorantha, HQ2 will sell to people who have no other exposure to story games.
        • \n
      • Better explanation of keywords and variations like umbrella and package. For example, you can use a keyword like Thief directly.This reduces the need for so many abilities on the sheet. We just assume you can do anything a thief would be able to do. Genre packs are the key to understanding a keyword in a setting.
        • \n
      \nFrom an actual play perspective we have been using this version for over a year now as play-testers and it is far more streamlined and easy to use than HQ1 in play. Better simple contests, better group contests, and changes to augments were particular highlights for us.

      And yes I use the pass/fail cycle as a tool. It's just noting down prior contest outcomes and using it as advice for forthcoming resistance. And yes sometimes it helps me figure out what resistance to call (and to think about what kind of obstacle should be next to justify that resistance).

      \n Let's talk about how the pass/fail cycle works in actual play (I can't talk about mock contests, we have never needed them as a tool).

      Last week's HQ2 game is typical for us.

      We were playing in Dragon Pass in Glorantha. I have a lot of setting notes based around an Orlanthi clan in Sartar (this setting 20 years later). The Lunar Empire has recently been pushed out of the clan's land and they now lie on the border with those clans still under the Empire. Many of the clan converted to the worship of the Lunar gods during the Occupation, and the clan is uncertain of its direction, support the rebellion against the Empire or not.

      I have a lot of setting notes but unlike the notes above I don't have fixed numbers for all the narrator characters instead I have notes on significant abilities or extraordinary abilities which equate to Hard or Very Hard resistances, the things these guys are known for.

      Preparation for a typical session for me is to put together some bangs based off what happened last week and the 'flags' on the players sheets. As an example, two of the players are brother and sister-in-law. The brother is an Orlanthi traditionalist and the sister-in-law a Lunar married to his Lunar convert brother (a narrator character). So they are on opposed sides of the conflict. In a previous week the sister-in-law had killed a young man on a cattle raid.

      Much of the play was taken from one bang that the chieftain of the clan wanted the brother to reach a settlement with the neighboring clan over the killing. My notes were little more than that.

      From that point on the players explored the outcomes of that request.

      As contests occurred I made a note of the contest outcome (marginal victory, minor defeat). When the player tried to take an action that I did not believe should automatically succeed (just say yes or roll the dice in action here) I used 3 things to figure the resistance: notes about the narrator character if any as to whether they had significant abilities to bring to play, then my instinct for how hard this should be, finally the suggested next resistance from the pass/fail cycle. (This is explicitly how HQ2 suggests you handle it btw). A lot of the time pass/fail does not come up, but sometimes it does.

      Putting numbers against anything other than setting elements would tend to mean that I had a pre-prepared plan for what should happen, which would encourage railroading or illusionism. So I always need to create some numbers on the fly.

      So the above situation panned out to the two brothers and their sister heading north to try and reach a settlement. The sister-in-law wanted to arrive during the funeral, and then narrated walking down among the grieving relatives, smearing herself in mud and ochre and joining the grieving mass at the funeral pyre. When I needed a roll to decide how the mob would react I had to come up with a number. There were no named narrator characters to draw from, my gut told me that this was pretty tough Hard or Very Hard, in all likelihood despite cultural taboos around hospitality she would be lynched and the pass/fail cycle suggested that this was a Moderate resistance. In the end I opted for Hard. The player scored a Major Victory and ended up in a weeping embrace with her victim's mother.

      So there is nothing more here than a way to set a difficulty rating on the fly as you do in say Burning Wheel. Robin is just suggesting you take into account another factor, the dramatic logic of the story. There is nothing less arbitrary around that as a factor over my perception of how hard something should be in the real world.

      And of course you never need to go to that third step if you don't want to, just stop at the first one, or two. It's explicitly advice.

      We're the Heroquest fanbase and we're trying to discern if there is value for us in a new product.
      \nHQ2 isn't going to cover any new ground for anyone who's been playing Forge diaspora games. It doesn't cover scene framing, the mechanical reward system isn't aimed at anything beyond "Hero Points for showing up", and it hits "Say Yes", but in a somewhat clumsy fashion. As I noted above, the GM's advice sits firmly in the 90's, extolling the value of creative collaboration with just a little bit on how to structure that, and some contradictions along the way. It's definitely not a teaching game as a text.

      What HQ2 does do, is provide clear and less kludgy rules from Hero Wars or HQ1, strip away Glorantha as a setting, provide a meaningful set of difficulty scales you can use. The community resource rules work as a form of group flags, and set up a non-crunchy setting resource tool, which might be interesting to anyone who found Reign, Burning Empires, or Houses of the Blooded too crunchy in that regard.

      It would be a good game to go to, provided you're willing to accept some incoherence and drift it, if:

      - You haven't checked out the Hero Wars/Hero Quest rules and have been meaning to
      - You really like the HW/HQ rules but wanted some streamlining
      - You want to use a "mainstream-ish" game as an entry point to introduce dirty hippy gaming to a mainstream group scared off by stuff like Grey Ranks and Bacchanal and are willing to give some drift.
      - You want a game that supports improvised Sim play w/o requiring immense prep
      - You want a game that gives good tools for pacing Illusionist play and providing the "rollercoaster experience" to a group.

      With that in mind, let's look at what I wrote:

      "The business about the GM cheating and adjusting difficulties to pre-set success or failure matches material I've read on Laws' blog: He's into this whole thing about "rising" and "falling" rhythms of a story based on whether the hero is succeeding or failing (ike a good James Bond movie or whatever).       I also wrote:

      Laws' is still trying to make sure a GM is the "writer" of a story -- which I'm not interested in. I get the feeling you're not either. But he seems pretty attached to it right now.

      So, this comment sprang from Robin's blog. He's been writing about this pacing issue for more than a year, and it was clear he was sewing these ideas into HQ2. He drew his charts of stories moving up and down, diagraming how a story works as a writer maps these beats up and down.

      Now, this makes little sense to me in an RPG. And full disclosure: any game that builds any kind of "story structure" into it turns me off right away. In my view structure is built on the fly in an RPG. The ups and downs will sort themselves out just fine. The goal is create a game that produces unexpected results with ups and down, with interesting narrative results no matter what the mechanical result. (Please remember that word "unexpected." This is a structural decision; it determines if the scene continues with a thrilling shipboard combat, or concludes with a frustrated hero sputtering in the polluted waters of the Hudson River. After making this choice, they then construct the sequence to be suitably sensational, however it comes out."

      "Just as fundamental differences separate literature and film, roleplaying is its own narrative form with its own distinct dynamics. HeroQuest emulates the decision-making of older narrative forms but adapts it to the requirements of roleplaying. Chief among these is the need for uncertainty to surround all noteworthy conflicts."

      And then, below these paragraphs there is a chart: THE LOGISTICS OF STORY OBSTACLES. In it, the GM decides the difficulty based on story needs. (I'm presuming the GM decides this, but I've read on a couple of threads that the players might determine the resistance.)

      I think it's significant that the issue isn't "difficulty" but PASS or FAIL. The number is set to weight toward and expected outcome.

      Now, I may be unique in this, but I have never set values with an anticipation of outcome in any game. Sometimes the challenges will be tougher or easier. But this focus on outcome seems peculiar and strange to me.

      When I (and others) suggest that the game is written to have the GM push the story one way or another, this is what we're referring to.

      Basically, I'm looking at a chart that says a) I should know if I want a character to Pass or Fail a conflict; and that b) I should assign a low or high resistant accordingly.

      Can you explain how this isn't the rules telling the GM to push the outcome on the characters? At the extreme ends the GM could simply set resistances that are so high or low as to make rolling useless.

      At the less extreme ends, since we're looking for the outcomes first, the question becomes, "What does this mean?" Why is the GM focusing on a desired outcome.

      Now, let me be clear about my own preferences. One of the reasons I'm in love with the Sorcerer resolution mechanics is that the results of conflicts are wildly uncertain. There is simply no way to jimmy the conflict mechanic to pass or fail in Sorcerer. The odds are higher or lower, but never certain. And because of the way the mechanics are set up, what a conflict is about at the start of the rolling of dice is often not what the conflict is about by the time the dice stop rolling: It is possible that enemies will end up becoming allies; that to save a friend a PC will take an action that puts his soul at risk, an action he would never have anticipated before the conflict started; that a PC who starts bargaining with a powerful NPC will end up desperately trying to murder his would be business partner as his own Will is beaten down. All this happens because of how the dice fall in a series of "concentric" rolls of ever expanding effect and the Players make decisions based on the shifting balance of dice.

      When I wrote "unexpected" this is the kind of unexpected I enjoy. Now, that's me. But I want to provide the baseline of what I see as really, really fun. I simply might be blind to something that is right in front of me.

      Let's leave aside, for the moment, that the LOGICS OF STORY OBSTACLES is optional and one doesn't have to use it... can you explain to me how one does use it?       \n Hi Chris,

      Sorry for the slow reply, got hit by a virus.

      I found your post interesting because it made it much easier to understand where your reading was coming from. So I can see your reading now. It\u2019s not the same as my reading, but that\u2019s not trying to imply that one is more true. Every reader interprets a text according to what they bring with them.

      However, I thought I might share my reading of the pass/fail cycle with you, to see if you can see my reading as well.

      I think that the main chapter we are concerned with is Playing Stories. My reading of what that chapter covers is as follows:

      A pass/fail poetics
      Choosing type of contest
      Pass/Fail cycle

      Let\u2019s exclude the second section because I hope that it is less controversial

      Now, in my reading, the first section is Robin\u2019s poetics of the kind of literature HQ2 seeks to emulate. He suggests that you can see it as a cycle of successes and failures used to create tension

      The pass/fail cycle serves its purpose by allowing the author to orchestrate a pattern of tension and release. p.68

      But its pretty clear Robin is talking about fiction here not a game.

      In fiction, the author creates excitement by manipulating the rhythm of successes and failures. p.68

      He is using this as a justification for picking numbers based on the previous successes or failures of the group. So the point here is to pick up on the idea that in fiction, the difficulty of the obstacle reflects the point in the dramatic cycle.

      But Robin is explicit. While the author has control in fiction, the narrator in HQ2 has no such control. Outcomes derive from player decisions and the outcomes of rolls. The narrator reacts to this and does not control it.

      Narrators, rather than creating a single plot line for the hero to move through, must anticipate that the heroes will either succeed or fail when confronted by any noteworthy obstacle. Either by preparing in advance or through improvisation,they must be ready for the story to branch into many possible stories. The decisions of the players, in tandem with the game\u2019s resolution system, determine which story actually takes place. The result is also a linear story, but the process must be at all times open-ended, so that the players have determining influence over the outcome of the story. p.68

      Note that pass/fail is used to help set resistances based off previous events. It is an alternative to \u2018narrator determines resistance\u2019 and as such (and Alex pointed out) in many ways less illusionist, because it takes the decision away from the narrator who no longer \u2018authors\u2019 the difficulty

      To use your pass/fail tracking chart in assigning resistances, check the most recent pair of arrows against the Pass/Fail Resistance Assignment Table, below. Treat each major defeat as two defeats, and each major victory as two victories. p.72

      So I think that HQ2 is not suggesting that the narrator decides outcome, but allows a mechanic to determine resistance. The pass/fail cycle is a justification for why \u2018the last outcomes\u2019 are a valid way to determine the difficulty of the next outcomes. Pass/Fail is trying to avoid narrator chooses resistance to favor an outcome not support it.
