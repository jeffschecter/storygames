\n Matt Snyder wants to "un-geek" role-playing games.
\n
\nHis point is that being a "gamer" requires buy-in to a geek subculture of inside jokes, knowledge of complicated and largely insignificant history, respecting certain people (aka Gary Gygax), being a fan about different media and games, and other details that are unrelated to actually playing the games themselves.
\n
\nHe sees the challenges to getting "non-gamers" to try an RPG to be:
\n* Stigma with the activity
\n* Learning curve
\n* Nobody outside of "gamer" subculture even knows about them
\n
\nI used to somewhat agree with these 3 points, but now I don't. I think they're completely wrong.
\n
\nIf someone wouldn\u2019t be interested in playing any kind of more involved tabletop game or a parlour game that involves more active participation \u2014 they won\u2019t be interested in playing an RPG either. It's not an RPG specific thing.
\n
\nThere might be social stigma attached to raising your hand in a staff meeting at work and suggesting an office D&D; party\u2026 but there\u2019d also be stigma to suggesting an X-Box party, or a NASCAR party, or working on model railroads as an office.
\n
\nIf someone mentions that they like videogames, there\u2019s no problem talking about World of Warcraft, and unless they express a lot of scorn for MMORPGs, there\u2019s no reason not to bring up RPGs with them.
\n
\nIf you\u2019re having a conversation with someone and they tell you they enjoy more involved boardgames or parlour games of any kind there\u2019s no issue with bringing up RPGs.
\n
\nIf you\u2019re talking with someone about model railways, there\u2019s no issue in talking about tabletop wargames like Warhammer 40K \u2014 and from there, there\u2019s no issue to talking about RPGs.
\n
\nIt's all about knowing the interests of the person you're talking to.
\n
\nAnd Facebook makes this incredibly easy. You don\u2019t have to guess what people\u2019s hobbies are - - they list them on their profiles. Not only that, they add all these RPG type games and you get updates about which games your friends are playing.
\n
\nThere are 777, 000 users of the \u201cHarry Potter Magic Spells\u201d application (hey there\u2019s ANOTHER one of my friends who I could talk to about RPGs).
\n
\nThere are over 8 *MILLION* people who\u2019ve installed the \u201cVampires\u201d application on Facebook. You have a vampire character who grows in power as you fight the other vampires of your friends. It\u2019s totally RPG inspired. Not to mention all the other applications with 100s of thousands to millions of players: Knights, Werewolves, Wizards, Armies, Zombies.
\n
\nAny of your friends have that installed? Yup. Feel free to talk to them about RPGs.
\n
\nIt's true that some RPGs have a steep learning curve (D&D; 3.x I'm looking at you) but others don't at all. Older versions of D&D;, Risus, and any sort of rules-light, you say what you want to do and the DM interprets systems are easy for beginners to learn. There are plenty of board games and even videogames that have comparable learning curves.
\n
\nAs for the claim that people outside of the "gamer subculture" don't know about RPGs... the recent press coverage of Gary Gygax's passing away should dismiss those notions entirely.
\n

    \n The problem is not how internal members of this subculture regard each other, it's how external society regards the subculture. It's easy to say, 'who cares what they think!' but societal pressures are real. For example, I'm a gamer, but I don't admit that to non-gamers unless I know them well. In some respects, that's my own issue, but I ain't hardly alone in feeling this way. Gaming isn't listed as a hobby on my Facebook profile.

    Every group has its zealots, but not every group is judged by its zealots like this one is. Look at sports: if you meet someone who watches football, you might formulate some opinions, but I'd say for the most part, you don't immediately picture them in full body paint, screaming at the top of their lungs from the bleachers in the snow. When you tell a person that you're a gamer, I think that the image that your average person is going to hit on is something akin to the Comic Book Guy from The Simpsons. Basically, despite being a somewhat normal, well-rounded fellow, if I told average joe that I was a gamer, I'd be judged according the worst geek in the world. If my Dad told the same average joe that he enjoyed watching hockey and baseball, it's unlikely that he'd be seen as remotely abnormal or judged poorly.

    That's what we have to deal with. And for the record, Vampire applications on Facebook and WoW in no way are equated with gaming in most peoples' minds. They might be slightly dorky, but they don't actually involve role-playing, which is nebulous, weird and embarassingly immersive.

    The worst of us are our representatives to the outside world, and I don't like them any more than those outside the hobby do. I don't know what to do about it. Things like comics and video games have shouldered past rpgs and acquired some legitimacy, but I don't know how this hobby can wash the perceived slime off, because it doesn't seem to have the power to grow in popularity outside some unforeseen cultural shift.

    \n I'll share a piece of something I've experienced about all of this.

    Most of my gaming happens with non-RPG geeks. Plenty of them are anime, comics, videogame, theatre, computer and fandom geeks. But I can say this with almost certain confidence -- I seem to be the only one of them who is more than passingly interested in role-playing games of the non-electronic, offline kind.

    Part of this is because, frankly, of cost issues. RPGs used to be freaking expensive where I am (Malaysia). They still are, but at least, now you can rely on PDF downloads, games that rely on cheaper d6es, that sort of thing. That's no longer an issue.

    What's happening instead is that a great number of people don't know what RPGs are; or, you know, associate it with electronic gaming. Seriously, people don't know here, that you can play RPGs without having a computing device anywhere near. Some of them remember playing old D&D games, or perhaps Robotech, or GURPS or whatever... but unless you had the money or the wherewithal, you wouldn't know that people still play these games.

    And there's the stigma. Up until a few years ago, my father associated D&D with BADD (yes, Pat Pulling). My brother actually actually said, "What, got no life, is it?" when I let slip that I had gotten a bunch of friends over for a game of T&J. I'd like to say I didn't care, but let's face it, I've never actually experienced the subculture enough to feel like the hobby was "okay" or "normal". Talking to people about it tends to be uncomfortable, even to this day. There is a lot of baggage to push through when you try to sell the idea of gaming to people.

    Maybe all I need to do is gain a positive attitude. Maybe; heaven knows I try, and I'm very grateful that I have a wife who is supportive of what I do and is able to lend her enthusiasm and skills to help me get my game on. I appreciate the people who made it easy, cheap and non-time-consuming for me to actually play (I have to thank Chad for that, with his PDQ system). I'm not exactly representative; I get that.

    But there are days that I wish trying to get some game wouldn't be so goddamn fucking hard. Which is why I appreciate what people like Matt are trying to do; if in the end what happens is that there's one less barrier of entry for non-gamers and people who play with non-gamers like me, I'm all for it.

      \n When I was younger I was a lot more shy and had trouble speaking in front of a room of people. I might still be introverted, but I have no trouble walking into a room full of strangers and talking with people, or having to suddenly address a room full of people.       \n
      \nHere are some tips on public speaking. I think they apply to this topic as well, and I'm very confident those who have had success with talking about their hobby (myself, Henrique) are doing these things, while many that have had trouble are not.
      \n
      \nKnow the needs of your audience and match your contents to their needs.
      \n
      \nYou need to be able to read people and know if they're a good person to talk to about your hobby and any particular game. If you're picking the wrong people to talk to, or bringing up games that YOU like but they probably won't... you've failed before you even begin.
      \n
      \nKnow your material thoroughly. Put what you have to say in a logical sequence. Ensure your speech will be captivating to your audience as well as worth their time and attention.
      \n
      \nDon't ramble or blurt out something they'll find strange and hard to relate to. Immediately jumping into details of your gaming session is probably a bad idea.
      \n
      \nKnow what your strong and weak points are. Emphasize your strong points.
      \n
      \nFun. Social. Active Participation. Time Consuming.
      \n
      \nBody language is important.
      \n
      \nThis can be hard without confidence and experience.
      \n
      \nSpeak with conviction as if you really believe in what you are saying.
      \n
      \nThis is the point Henrique and I have been making about confidence. If *you* think it's a hobby you should be embarrassed about talking about, and that there will be social fallout for doing so... you're going to fail.
      \n
      \nMaintain sincere eye contact with your audience.
      \n
      \nThis can again be difficult for a lot of people without confidence and experience.
      \n
      \nSpeak to your audience, listen to their questions, respond to their reactions, adjust and adapt.
      \n
      \nVery important.
      \n
      \nPause. Allow yourself and your audience a little time to reflect and think. Don't race through what you have to say and leave your audience, as well as yourself, feeling out of breath.
      \n
      \nDon't ramble.
      \n
      \nAdd humor whenever appropriate and possible.
      \n
      \nThis is my point about choosing a "fun" game instead of a serious one, and embracing all the fun pop-culture references. Don't be offended by videos like Dungeon Majesty, Tom et ses Chums, or Fear of Girls -- humour is a good thing.
      \n
      \nKnow when to STOP talking.
      \n
      \nYou need to be able to read the reaction of your audience, know when it's going well, when to take it further, and when to make the conversation a bit lighter... and when to change the subject.
      \n
