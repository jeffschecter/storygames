    \nJason: Nobilis is game about gnosticism, Platonic ideal concepts embodied in people, and an interesting combination of "mythic fantasy" (with its melting pot of global cultures) and urban fantasy. It plays around with a lot of the same ideas as Gaiman's Sandman comics, but also rewards players for clever thinking and wordplay. It's hard to compare the setting or premise of the game to anything, though it shares some conceptual space with Kult, Unknown Armies, and maybe Mage: The Ascension. You play lesser gods in the contemporary world, with a fair bit of White Wolf-y social angst and emo descriptions. It's beautifully written and mechanically progressive but -- at least in my opinion -- unclear about larger structures of play, especially on the GM side of things, in the same manner that a lot of White Wolf games are. My biggest issue with Nobilis is figuring out how to structure a session of play. I want Fronts or a GM Turn/Player Turn split or some other structural guidelines. But the mechanics themselves are pretty awesome. It's not a total success, through and through, but it's a solid and inspiration game that made a number of important mechanical and thematic developments.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarThanks guys. I have seen the coffee table book and could not make head nor tails of it. For a game that no one plays or understands it gets a ton of love.
    \nI think that's an over-generalization. I've run three long running games, and a pick-up one shot game of Nobilis (while taking a 2 hour walk with some friends from out of town, where the Nobles where Bush cabinet members fighting a Nettle rite on behalf of the Founding Fathers).

    In many ways Nobilis, at least how I run and experience Nobilis, is a game based around Never Saying No (the Monarda Law, which is indeed a critical part of the game). But as the Hollyhock God you need to be as clear and direct about the consequences of the yes as you can. The rest of the rules of the game, the setting, and the myriad creations of play (whether establishing what falls under your domain of boundaries or what the Ayn Rand Excrucian Clone might be willing to do to you) help to shape and guide those consequences. The question the game asks is never can I do this, but rather should I do this, or perhaps should I do this in this way.

    As for GM tools, personally, opposition structure wasn't the bar that was hard to reach. It was never hard to stat out opposing Imperators, Nobles, and Excrucian shards.

    Lastly, I also never found recruiting for Nobilis difficult, perhaps it was the concept of a Sandman RPG or what not, but as a concept I found it captured player's attention, and I rarely found the process of creating Nobles to undermine their interest. (I admit the So You've Become Enobled Pamphlet was a huge help in one of my later games.)

    - Mendel

    \n I've been playing in a Nobilis campaign for a few months. I'm enjoying it greatly, though I feel like I still have no idea how to run it. The core of Nobilis is presenting the characters with mysterious circumstances and insoluble problems which they then proceed to tackle using their extremely-powerful-yet-bizarre-and-specific tools. This typically creates social fallout for the characters, either within the party (due to differing creeds), or in larger Noble society. Plots operate at three levels: personal, ontological (affecting the noble's estate), and official (the noble's role as soldiers in the war against the excrucians), and these things play off against each other. The mechanics are shockingly effective at adjudication for a system where you can do virtually anything.

    Disclaimer: I have not actually read the text.

    *blink* Seriously? Nobilis is about kicking ass in a superhero kind of way?

    I thought the rules-as-written (and certainly the way we played it) made it clear that once you're involved in physical combat, you've kind of already lost. Violence being the last refuge of the incompetent, etc, etc.

    In the Nobilis campaign I played in, all conflict was fundamentally social conflict. It kind of has to be. EVERYTHING has some sort of person/personality behind it. Whether its the spirits of clouds and trees and cellphones or the higher elements of Nobilis and their Imperators. Any theme or idea that comes out in the story will probably have a person/personality behind it. Sure those spirits/Nobilis/Excrucians/whatever might have contrary views and sure there are attacks against Creation via such things as flower rites, but it seems to me the solution never involves dropping a building on someone. In my opinion, that's what made flower rites so cool -- they were these small stories about specific people and events whose results could echo across the world tree.

    Our games wandered all over the place with regards to how deep the dreamlike/metaphorical space got -- we had very personal scenes where the Power of Revenge had to deal with the fall out of his old mortal life (and he didn't make things easier by making the man he hated and loved most in the world his first Anchor) all the way up to the campaign's climax which involved weaving an Excrucian INTO reality to become The Apple that Eve Ate via the Power of Seeds. And in my experience was never, ever about kicking ass as some sort of superhero.

    Posted By: Potemkin
    In my experience, under the veneer of exceptional writing and conceptual design, Nobilis isn't any different from your standard White Wolf title ("You're a special super-human who fights in secret war against the forces of darkness" - sound familiar?) - and should be played exactly the same. HG sets up enemies and NPCS, players knock 'em down. Oldschool at its best.
    \nThis doesn't seem to match my reading of the text or my experiences in play at all.

    The successful games of Nobilis that I've seen worked much more like Dogs in the Vineyard: the GM sets up some tangled mess of an insoluble problem that pulls the PCs in multiple different directions at the same time. Like in Dogs, the insoluble problem forces the players and characters to make difficult moral choices. Half the time, the PCs solve the insoluble problem through creativity, and do their best to solve the problem while making it worse. After the PCs deal with the situation, it produces a variety of consequences and has established a moral standing for your character. So then the GM pushes the morality a little further and/or brings in the consequences of your last action in the next Bang that they introduce.

    \n Mr Teapot: the successful and satisfying games of Vampire that I've played worked much like the game you describe, actually.

    It seems to me that you can play either Vampire or Nobilis with a very bright-line sort of philosophy, where the characters are the unambiguous good guys. Or it seems to me that you can play them with a lot of conflicting forces and divided loyalties, where the characters have to make tough choices and often wrestle with conflicts with themselves and with each other. The mechanics in Nobilis seem to me to support either play style solidly, depending on what you choose to emphasize; the examples of play seem to suggest gnostic superheroes, while the game fiction seems to support a more complex game.

    The way I managed that level of complexity in Vampire was by thinking not of the PCs versus everyone else, but by having something like a dozen different important NPCs and factions at a time, each with its own goals. The PCs were known to be friends, but several of the important NPCs were not above trying to drive wedges in between them, and each of the PCs had goals as well. The end result was a complicated matrix of relationships and loyalties: the situation in the city was a tangled mess of allegiances, rivalries, and favors, and anything the characters did to resolve their problems was often the least bad option in a set of unattractive options, and often had unintended consequences. It was beautiful, and gritty, and complicated, and messy, and probably the best long-form game I ever ran.

    Posted By: Mr. TeapotThe successful games of Nobilis that I've seen worked much more like Dogs in the Vineyard: the GM sets up some tangled mess of an insoluble problem that pulls the PCs in multiple different directions at the same time. Like in Dogs, the insoluble problem forces the players and characters to make difficult moral choices. After the PCs deal with the situation, it produces a variety of consequences and has established a moral standing for your character. So then the GM pushes the morality a little further and/or brings in the consequences of your last action in the next Bang that they introduce.
    \nThat's not to say, Mr. Teapot, that my game didn't contain these things. It had complex relationships, philosophy (the Locus was Plato's Cave, trying into a general theme of illusion and perception - helped greatly by the Power of Mirrors) and consequence. It's just that, after a long evening's gadabout, the players want to throw down and let their creative juices fly in adrenaline-pumping battle. I don't think the label of 'gnostic superheroes' is pejorative, nor should it be avoided in play; it's one of the better halves of the game. It is testament to the quality of Nobilis that it's combat requires players to be twice as creative in half the time - in terms of the imagination, men are forged from boys in the heat of a full-scale Excrucian incursion. Kicking ass lets players be just has inventive as dealing with complex r-maps or moral labyrinths, perhaps even moreso.

    Besides, all this talk of 'morals' is making me uneasy. Especially when you consider how Nobilis presents Heaven and Hell.
