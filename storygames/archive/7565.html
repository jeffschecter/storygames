    \n There are many ways to use dice. These are some of my favorite.
    \n
    \nCenter System
    \nThe defender or task has a number ranging from 7-12 with lower being better.
    \nThe attacker or task attempter has a level of 1-6 with the lower the better. (This can be set by a single die roll each battle or at the beginning of the game)
    \nThen to be successful the character rolls between the two numbers. This works for all sorts of dice.
    \n
    \nTask System
    \nThere is also the classic task system where the task is rolled to give it a value and the character must roll over or under that task value.
    \n
    \nDoubles
    \nWhen using 2D6 there are good odds of getting doubles. You can roll a single die before the task. When the task itself is rolled using both dice, doubles of whatever that single rolled die could be automatic success, failure, bonus etc.
    \n
    \nFate
    \nIf you have a good tray you can also toss one die into a tray. Then toss the other die to try and change the one still sitting in the tray (silly I know). How much it changes from the original number is what you are looking for since it will be a 1-5 number. If the sitting die is not change then the value is zero. You can then use this value to influence future rolls. I have used this with my players as a sort of fate point that you roll at the beginning of the game and then you have that many points to use throughout the rest of the game. My players have gotten pretty good at hitting the single die just right.
    \n
    \nHave you rolled dice today?

    \n You should read PDQ, whose core rules are free. It's been licensed a few times, and has been around a while, so it's pretty solid for 2d6+something.

    http://atomicsockmonkey.com/freebies/di/pdq-core.pdf

    It makes use of the trend toward 7 for 2d6. A character has player-made traits (Qualities) that are +2, +4, or +6. To beat a task, you roll 2d6, add your trait, and see if you beat a pre-determined difficulty number. It sounds arbitrary, but it's a good meter... something of good difficulty might be 7 (the average roll) plus 2, or 9. Someone without relevant bonuses will probably lose out, but someone good at it has an average shot at it.

    Complications are added in Truth & Justice, the superhero RPG, using super-scale damage and ability. Someone with a super-power at something adds 7, or 9, or 11, to their roll versus someone with mundane ability. There's other considerations as well. (T&J also has 2d6 tables for super-power origins in case you're stumped.)

    All the games I've run are really supported by the system. Not too much flubs, not too much critical success.

    \n I came up with a craps-based system I was going to use for a game about hoboes or something, but I never actually tried it. Here's a paste from the post I made on the Forge about it:

    Here's the way that craps normally works:
    One player ("the shooter") has the dice. He makes a bet ("shoots for" an amount). The other players then match ("fade") his bet. They don't have to contribute equal amounts; they can divide it up however they want. The House can also fade. If there's a portion of the original bet that is not faded, that portion must be retracted by the shooter; however, the faders cannot exceed the shooter's bet (so the shooter sets the upper limit of the risk, but the faders set the lower limit).

    Next, the shooter rolls the dice (2d6). If their total is 7 or 11 (a "natural"), he wins the pot. If their total is 2 ("snake eyes"), 3 ("tres"), or 12 ("boxcars") (collectively, these are "craps"), he loses the pot, and the players who faded win double their own bets (that is, if I faded $3, and the shooter lost, I win $6). If he rolls anything else, he has to "make a point." This means that he re-rolls the dice as many times as necessary until he rolls the same total as initially (win), or he rolls a 7 (lose).

    If the shooter wins, he retains the dice and may make another bet or pass them on to the next player. If he loses, they pass to the next player by default.

    There are usually also several side bets among the other players, as to whether the shooter will make his point, or will roll a natural or craps on his next throw (termed "right, coming out" or "wrong, coming out," respectively). Note that while the shooter is attempting to make a point that craps and 11 mean nothing; only 7 and the point he is trying to make mean anything.

    Now, to transform this into conflict resolution:
    It's pretty clear that the shooter is the guy with the initiative, in whatever sense (which implies that this system uses initiative, unlike some others). Clearly, the bets are over "what's at stake." A simple way to do this is to have the shooter say, "I'm shooting to sneak up on this guy and knock him out with my blackjack," and then the house would fade with something like "He might hear you coming and punch your lights out," and then the win/loss of the dice determines which happens.

    But here's a better idea: what if everyone's got a Resource, indicated by real-world chips, and one chip equals one effect? So, "sneak up on him" is one chip, and "knock him out" is another, making it a two-chip bet. This could be faded by two chips, meaning that there's two effects (probably of a inconvenient variety) that he might suffer if he loses. Or it might be faded by only 1 chip, and the shooter would have to decide which effect he's going to sacrifice at the moment. I mean, he could still shoot to sneak up on the guy, and if he wins he retains the dice, so then he can shoot to hit the guy in the head.

    Now, when it comes to making the point, sometimes those re-rolls can go on for a while. It occurs to me to either:

    1. Limit the re-rolls, based on Effectiveness stats of some sort.
    2. Make the re-rolls mean something. I'm not sure how to implement that, though, other than provide more opportunity for the use of side-bets below.

    Concurrent actions during the same round of resolution could be handled by side-bets, using the same effect currency as normal.

    Note that when the shooter is making an action unopposed by a character, or opposed by an NPC, the house (GM) would probably take care of all the fading, but if the action was against a PC, then that player would probably have to do the fading. But someone else could always fade on your behalf, if they wanted to impose negative effects on the shooter--or if they wanted to make sure that the shooter got the full stakes he wanted.

    \n You can have different coloured dice and have a table with 36 different result (1-1, 1-2, 1-3 ... 6-4, 6-5, 6-6) where one die is added to the skill value and becomes the singular and the second die becomes the ten on the table. Certain (mechanical) situations can allow you to switch the two dice. Got this idea from Warhammer 2. Or you can use the dice as a D66 with the same switching mechanics. Unknown Armies uses this.

    I think it's in the Babylon 5 RPG that they have a negative and a positive d6. The player roll both at the same time and the lowest one is used. If the negative one is lowest, it subtracts from the skill value, and the positive one adds to the skill value. So if you roll 2 (negative), 5 (positive) the final score is [skill value]-2 and if you roll 4 (negative), 3 (positive) the final score is gets [skill value]+2. This means that the average roll is your skill value, which makes skills and target numbers using the same values. Btw, if you roll a double, it's counted as \xb10. Because this is the same thing as [skill value] - 1d6 + 1d6, but without the calculation, it's something you can use for Feng Shui as well. (The exploding die is re-rolled without the opposite die.)

    If one of the dice shows a six, add and re-roll. Keep doing this as long as sixes are rolled. If both of the dice show sixes, then something special happens.
    \nSo far it's proved to be pretty simple yet varied having an open ended option and a "cool shit happens" on doubles option.

    \n I have a couple of d6 resolutions I am working on, this is for a narrative descriptor, the target number is between 3-11 usually between 6-8 though.

    TASK RESOLUTION

    Roll a pair of conventional six-sided gaming dice, add the two dice together to generate a random score.

    12, Box Cars= PERFECT
    *Double Higher= DISTINCTION
    Higher= SUCCESS
    **Equal= MIXED SUCCESS
    Lower= PARTIAL SUCCESS
    *Double Lower= FAILURE
    2, Snake Eyes= ERROR

    * Indicates a dice roll with both dice being the same number, or Doubles.
    ** Equal Doubles is both ERROR and SUCCESS results, otherwise Equal is both FAILURE and PARTIAL SUCCESS results.
    12 Perfect Success.
    2 Failure that results in a detrimental outcome.

    Roll again to determine the effect modifiers (more charts).

    \n I'll mention d66 again. It has the advantage of being linear rather than a bell curve. It's also easier to print and fill a 36 position table than say a d100. With roughly 3% steps in values, if feels like a wide range without being too watered down. A +1 or +2 is still a nice nudge without going way over the hump on the bell curve. While this applies to all all 2d6 rolls, I like the clatter of a pair of dice rolling rather than a single die.

    There's also the nostalgia factor for me since the first RPG that my brother and I wrote was d66 based (there was no game store for that back in the day.)

    \n I am using a mechanism that uses three dice, but if you want to use two skip to #2A.

    #1 Roll three ordinary game dice (3d6)
    #1A If Triples it is a Critical Success.

    #2 Remove the highest dice.
    #2A If doubles remain it is a Critical Success or Failure.

    #3 If is equal or lower than your target it is a Success, higher a Failure.

    Target number is between 3-11 and usually between 6-8.

    By the way, 2d6 odds are always easy to figure out "by hand", even without math: you can just write out the 36 possibilities and see how they pan out.
    \n2DN is always easy to calculate. N is the number of sides on the die.

    1) Average (any dice combination): ([highest outcome] + [lowest outcome]) / 2
    2) Chance to roll minimum/maximum (two dice): 1/N\xb2
    3) Chance to roll average (two dice): N/N\xb2 (=1/N)

    The chances then decrease with 1 for every number from the average or increase with 1 from the minimum/maximum.

    Examples
    1) 1d8+3
    Average: (4+11)/2 = 7.5

    1) 2d10
    Average: (2+20)/2 = 11

    2) 2d10
    Chance to roll minimum/maximum (2 or 20): 1/(10*10) = 1/100
    Chance to roll 3 or 19: 2/100
    Chance to roll 4 or 18: 3/100
    et c.

    Chance to roll 2-4 is (1+2+3=) 6/100

    3) 2d10
    Chance to roll average (11): 10/(10*10) = 10/100
    Chance to roll 10 or 12: 9/100
    Chance to roll 9 or 13: 8/100
    et c.

    Chance to roll 9-10 is (8+9=) 17/100

    If you use two dice of different colors, each could mean something different. From that, you can get a lot of information: something like Godlike's speed and effectiveness, or natural talent vs. trained skill. Or even better: the white die is the good in you, and the black die is the evil in you. You don't have to accept the entire result of the black die if you don't want, but sometimes it rolls a 6 while the white die comes up 1 and you need to do something immoral for victory.
    \nThis is how I structured die rolls in my light dungeon-crawler Delve, but with the two different colors determining which direction narrative control goes. You roll a white and a black die, and pick the one you want to add to your trait. If you picked white, you (the player) get to narrate the specifics, while if you picked black, the gamemaster gets to narrate (NB: the gamemaster is explicitly set up as a semi-antagonistic figure). The interesting choice comes thus out of whether you might want to pick a black die that would succeed over a white die that would fail. There's a plot point system that modifies this decision somewhat in that you can make a die choice and then buy/sell the narration, but that's the only other twist.

    \n " I also was looking for odd and interesting ways to use 2d6."

    I've been playing around with the idea of pattern recognition of standard D6 pips. I think of it as "Casting Runes". I actually use three dice but the basic idea remains.

    I use three patterns: a center pip, a line of three, and a four corners. It breaks down to this:

    #.....Center....Line ....Box
    1..........1.........- .......-
    2..........- ........- .......-
    3..........1........1 ........-
    4.......... - .......- .......1
    5..........1........1.........1
    6..........-........1.........1

    In other words, 2 meets none of the criteria, 1 matches a single pattern, 3 matches two patterns, etc. 5 meets all three. So rather than achieving a "target number", you are choosing the patterns that best suit your needs at the moment (depending on what significance you attribute to the pattern - ie. attack/defense/maneuver or physical/mental/social. This can give the player a choice of actions rather than a static result ( two Centers may be preferable to three Lines in a given situation).

    A variation would see 5 and 6 as two Lines, making Lines the most common result. 2's are always null. Sounds clunky on paper but if you have some dice handy, give it a try.

    Just a little food for thought: Imagine patterns as a way of approaching a problem/ task/ conflict/ obstacle.

    Lines represent linear thought - straight forward solutions to a task "I hit it with my axe".

    Centers represent getting to the heart of the matter - what is my actual goal; to kill the orc or just to get past it somehow. Conflict resolution.

    Corners represent "thinking outside the box" - dramatic, cinematic solutions ( befitting the tone and setting). Doing awesome stuff.

    Instead of applying a preset list of modifiers, the dice represent the conditions/possibilities inherent in a given situation. You don't do something awesome (for example) just because you rolled high with a bunch of modifiers - instead, you HAVE to do something awesome because you rolled a bunch of Corners and that's the only thing that will work in this particular situation. Not "it's so crazy it just might work", but "it WILL work ('cuz the dice say so) now I have to come up with something crazy!" I'm trying to marry mechanics and colour. Really gotta go now.
