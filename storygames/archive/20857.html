\n My gaming club has been getting more members and we are having to divide into groups to game. I can see an issue arising, that as we have to continue to divide into more groups that I will likely have problems getting enough facilitators/GMs to cover all the groups for every session. I'm trying to encourage people to take the plung and try out the role, but at the same time I want to be sensitive to people who are uncomfortable doing so (one never knows if someone suffers from social anxiety for example). Has anyone dealt with this issue? And if so did you find a way to recruit more people willing to facilitate/GM? I know that this is a common problem and it may be a useful discussion for those facing a similar situation (even for singular groups). Any feedback would be greatly appreciated As always, thank you for your time and efforts

    \n If the purpose of your club is social, then don't; if people don't feel like it, those that can't get matched into a game can just hang out and socialize. Have some board or card games around, or go out for coffee or whatever. If the purpose of your club is to improve people's gaming skills, make it mandatory and lower the expectations of those attending; some people are going to need lots of practice to get comfortable, let alone good.

    \n What I've done in similar circumstances is that I plan ahead and indoctrinate old players into new GMs over time. For example, I might select a game specifically with an eye towards it being something a given player might like to run as a GM, then we play it for a month or whatever, and, should the player be interested, they will have a good starting point for running the game independently later. I might even participate for a bit as a player if the game is complex and the GM needs encouragement, but sooner or later they'll be able to operate independently, freeing me up to train more GMs (or just play, or run games, or whatnot).

    For explosive growth it's possible to intentionally start your first group with GM-grade players, train them all at once, and thus get multiple tables worth of GMs out of the deal. You could theoretically put these new GMs into training duty, too, if you needed a very large amount of GMs for a single game. Difficult to think up a scenario where you'd need that many GMs that quickly, though.

    Of course this relies on you recognizing the potential GMs out of the player pool, but I don't think that's too difficult: you just observe your co-players, pick out the ones who seem motivated about the art form, and get to know them a bit. Ask them about it, and let the idea mature over time. If they're interested in GMing, they'll pay more attention to the game as it is played, simply because they're reflecting on the process, pondering upon whether they could run the show themself. Give them a game text to read, too, or whatever - the particulars depend on the game.

    My experience with random player sampling (a new gaming club, for example, built entirely out of newbies) is that about 7/10 players are casual, 2/10 are fair-weather GMs, and 1/10 has the natural inclination for the task (propensity for leadership, artistic vision and project responsibility, mainly). Because a certain fraction of the casual player base drops out over time, my experience is that older groups approach a situation where about half of the players are casual while the rest are potential (or actual) GMs; this means that a given circle of gamers should not have a dearth of GMs over time, assuming that they foster each other's growth and encourage trading off responsibilities.

    One more observation: you will probably find it easier to get over natural hesitation if you focus on non-GMed games when first introducing people to running games. The reason is that even a person who suits the GMing task will be naturally hesitant about taking on all the involved responsibilities at once. That same person will probably be much more comfortable running a non-GMed game that resembles a boardgame in terms of operator tasks: they can learn the rules, explain the rules and call the rules during the game, without having to put themselves on the spot as the designer of an adventure or whatever. I'm thinking of games like Fiasco or Zombie Cinema - I've seen several people get their first taste of game facilitation with the latter, and it's been a functional stepping stone into becoming a GM. Once you're successful in facilitating a low-pressure game like that, it's easy to start thinking more ambitiously.

    \n I concur with Jason about D&D dungeoneering - it's definitely one of the easier types of games to start GMing with. On the other hand, many strains of story games both trad and new-style are actually really difficult to GM, simply because they wade so deep into independent artistry. Like, if the game requires you to outright impose an artistic vision and then deliver that for the other players, that's quite a threshold to cross. The right sort of person will do it, even without anybody encouraging them into it, but it's not going to happen in a social setting where somebody else is already GMing - much easier to be a player in the group than take on that challenge.

    Regarding learning games, my instincts say that it's not the easiest and most encouraging way to become a GM by picking up a game like homework, study it and then run it. This is, in fact, almost like the "cargo cult" situation so familiar to some of us. I, for example, learned roleplaying originally from scratch, with no experienced gamers in town; we only had books to learn from. It's not nearly as easy as getting to play with others and learning the best practices from experienced players. I feel that asking a new GM to run a game that they've never played before is sort of half-way into that already, even if they've had the opportunity to play other games before. (I suppose I particularly think this way because of how much I appreciate game-specific play technique - I think it often results in inferior play when you try to forcibly apply experience with one game to some entirely different game.)

    Considering your needs as a club, couldn't you have players facilitate/GM games that have been played before, but for new people who missed them on the first pass? This arrangement would have certain natural advantages to it:
    * The "debut date" for the new GM would be set in a sort of organic manner by the entrance of new club members; you could just ask the soon-to-be-GM to run a game once there's a group's worth of folks who weren't there when that game was last featured. Might be next week, might be next month. If some old-timer wants to join in for a second run of the game, well why not.
    * The new GM is in a natural leadership position compared to club newcomers simply because they've been there before and know the people and the way things are done. This situates them naturally to command attention and teach the game to people who joined the club after them - they're a "sempai", as the Japanese would have it
    * Games that people liked before would get a natural rerun. I've myself conducted similar short-game clubs, and I've found that they benefit from a certain amount of re-exploration of old ground: many games have depth to be appreciated on a second run, and the occasionally returning to an old favourite helps newcomers get up to speed on shared experiences that the old-timers have together.

    Also, as you say, you probably should have different games at different tables once you go to multiple GMs. Trying to synchronize and train and prep several GMs to run the same game during the same night is likely not worth the trouble when you could instead have GMs each facilitate the games that they are particularly interested themselves. This way the players in turn get a range of choice, too; I remember a certain game club in Jyv\xe4skyl\xe4 that used to make this exact thing quite a feature, as each weekly session of the club would begin with interested GMs pitching their games to the crew, after which the players would separate into tables on the basis of interest. It can be a bit cruel and exciting as a creative process, as it's possible that nobody wants to play your game, but that's the creative life for you.

    If you want to go with the idea of newbie GMs running new games from scratch, I would suggest working closely with them on it: tell them how to prep, review the prep with them (if they want to - I'm not suggesting a pushy attitude), set clear deadlines for when play will happen, and listen to their worries. Run a practice scene or two if they need it. You should take on some responsibility as a mentor, and it would be best if they could confide in you in terms of artistic doubts and technical uncertainties in advance. They may or may not want you to observe their play, too - I've seen new GMs appreciate an observer/prompter, but also those who don't want anybody second-guessing them, so it's best to ask them about it.

    Part of the reason for the micromanagement style I suggest is obviously about a certain sort of psychological manipulation: by taking the GMing debut seriously you encourage the future GM to commit psychologically to the idea that they'll be doing it; by showing them that you care by mentoring them you motivate them to do it, so it doesn't just remain idle talk. I think that being a slight bit intense is better than being thoroughly laid-back about this sort of thing, if you want to encourage the other person to actually go through with it.

    (Obviously you can also be too intense - it's in fact pretty easy for an experienced roleplayer to be too intense, just like any practitioner of an art. Do keep in mind that it's all in good fun, and GMing isn't a performance sport - at least, it shouldn't be according to my Very Serious Roleplaying Philosophy.)

    \n Are parlor games within your 'mission'? If not, you might consider them -- stuff like charades or pictionary or exquisite corpse or whatever, games that are all about having fun with social interaction and creativity but nowhere near as intimidating to newbies as the prospect of running a full-on RPG. (Mostly focusing on games where storytelling or improv skills or collaboration are highlighted, of course, so that it's building the kinds of fun experiences that translate well to RPGs.)

    You might also consider offering 'workshop' sessions where people who are interested in learning how to run a game (or better understanding how other people will run the games they play in) can get together and experiment/test/share best practices in a low-expectation environment (i.e. "we're going to play a couple of short scenes and see how this works" vs "we're going to have a lot of fun playing a full-on session").

    edit: a session that's just a round-robin kind of thing where an experienced GM runs a standalone scene and discusses it afterwards, followed by giving potential GMs a chance to run standalone scenes until everyone's had a shot at it seems like it might be particularly useful for getting peoples' feet wet.

    \n Lots of great advice here. I'd also recommend picking games which have relatively simple rules and allow input from many participants (instead of relying on one authority) when it comes to resolution.

    Rules which are "publicly visible" (open to everyone) and don't require one person to make all the judgement calls are great. Apocalypse World moves can be good, if they are printed out and placed in the middle of the table for everyone. Any boardgame-like rules (as in Eero's Zombie Cinema) are great. GMless structures and round-robin games are great. Parlor LARP-type games, where the rules are read by everyone together...

    There are lots of RPGs out there where scene framing duties rotate from scene to scene; these seem like a natural go-to for "GM training" - everyone is doing it together, and can learn from each other. If your scene seems to fall flat, you know that you can just move on to the next scene and let someone else run it - the game isn't going to stall just because you're having a hard time. In a round-robin scene framing "GMful" style, it's also very natural to ask for advice in that kind of situation, whereas being "the GM" often leads people away from doing that naturally - the players are all looking at you; it's not an obvious to "turn it on them" and ask for their help.

    The most helpful thing I've found for learning to GM is playing in a game run by a GM who is really good at it, and then having that person explain what they did, why, and how.

    You get a sense of what a good session of that game is like, and then the GM can show you how they made it work. That gives you the experience, the tools, and a model for doing so yourself. Many of my best roleplaying experiences have consisted of playing in someone else's game, enjoying it, asking them some questions on how they did what they did, and then going to repeat that in my own way or style. I think that *most* games can't simply be grasped "out of the book". We're getting better at writing good guides/books, but it's still nothing compared to actually playing the thing with people who know how to make it work (no level of detailed explanation is going to get you there).

    \n I agree about GMless games. However, a lot of them require particular skills or particular approaches to "work"; so it's good to have someone on hand who's played before, or a good guide.

    Another thing I've seen work sometimes - particularly if there is in interest in learning, and people who want to get their feet wet - is to have some "gaming experiments". These might be interesting for novelty's sake, and attract people.

    For instance, a gaming club I was in once had a one-shot adventure with a single PC and four GMs. The first GM would "start off" the adventure (while the other three frantically took notes), and at each hour, on the hour, the GM seat would switch to the next player. I wasn't part of the game, but everyone involved had a blast.

    I like the idea of forming a "team" to GM games. Takes some of the pressure off any one individual.

    One way I've seen this work is a dual-GM game, where one GM is in charge of framing scenes, narrating events, and so forth, while the second GM is in charge of keeping track of the rules, rolling dice, calling out modifiers, and so forth. You could even add a third GM who plays all the NPCs. It's worth experimenting with!

    "Murderous Ghosts" is a great game to take a look at, as well, because it effectively teaches one player to GM, without requiring any prior experience or knowledge. It's for 2 players, but you could have a "team" on either side, taking turns making decisions.

    Finally, sometimes it could work to play into people's competitive instincts:

    At that same gaming club, we had something called "The Gaming Circle". Everyone who signed up took on a certain date (games were on Saturdays), on which they would run a one-shot. During the other Saturdays, the current GM would grab players from the Gaming Circle pool for their own one-shot. (There were about 10 people in the club, and only about half available at any given time, so it worked great.)

    Other than regular gaming for everyone, the reasons it worked really well:

    * The general level and expectations kept rising. A few people ran really fun games, and everyone else wanted to step out to match that level. The games kept getting better and better! Everyone wanted to match or outdo the previous game, and some of the best gaming I've ever seen came out of this "Gaming Circle". Each person could plan months ahead and really put some thought into running a game they were excited about.

    Some games would spin off into campaigns, and develop their own game groups, if after a one-shot enough people clamoured for an "encore".

    * The "one-shot" environment encourages forethought and care, but also allows for a variety of experimentation. People wanted to try fun things, new things, and there were many neat ideas (like the above 4 GMs/1 player construction).

    * People who hear about great games and want to be part of it would know that they need to step up and organize a game themselves - everyone was responsible for one Saturday when they signed up. This could make it a desirable group to be part of, and make people think about running games, learning systems, and what makes a fun session. "What would I need to do in order to be able to run a game as part of this?"

    I could see all of these being a downside, as well, but I think that, so long as you provide alternatives for other players, it shoudn't be a problem.

    \nJeff, I think you understood what I meant, but I should clarify:

    I don't believe in a "Best Practices" list that's applicable to "being a GM". Instead, I'm suggesting that - particularly for a game club situation like you're envisioning, where lots of different games will be played - a good GM is (among other things) someone who knows to approach each game differently, figure out what it demands, and then be able to both communicate that simply and deliver on those fronts.

    As a simple example, in some games it's your job to "say yes" and try as hard as you can to bring everything the players are interested in into the game; that's what makes that game work. There are other games, though, where the very opposite is required: your job is tread hard on players' toes and make them suffer, never pulling your punches. A great GM knows which is which, can tell the players how and why it's going to happen, and then deliver on that promise.

    The AW model of presenting that - Agenda, Principles, and Moves - could be a good form of presentation, of course. The ideal form would be a short "summary sheet" of some kind, combined with examples of play illustrating points (videos, comics, text) - although a live play-through beats all of that.

    When I think of games I feel really confident running, it's usually because either:

    a) I've run it a million times, and figured out what makes it sing over time (by trail and error),

    or

    b) I played the game with an awesome GM, and then got a chance to chat with them about it. I came away with a clear understanding of how to make that game rock (and this is what I would qualify as "best practices").

    For me, at least, b) is much, much faster than a).

    (You might consider seeing if there are any cool game people in your geographical area who would be up to doing a "demo" of sorts, as a guest at your meetup/club. If I was nearby, I'd happily do so, for instance: run a game, while talking through the decisions I'm making, and sharing my prep before or after - whichever makes more sense for that particular game. Experienced GMs often have a "bag of tricks" which helps to get things going quickly and easily, and those are often really fun tools which don't appear in any of the books themselves. The same may go for game designers, who would get an opportunity to play, meet people, and potentially create a whole new audience for their games. "Come to my club, and I'll buy X copies of your game if you run it and then share tips on how to do so with the participants!")
