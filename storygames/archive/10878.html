\n This is what I thought the thread of successful gaming was going to be about. http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=10873&page=1#Item_8

But it wasn't.

And obviously, the first answer to my thread topic is "I had fun."

But I think folks have different criteria for success at the table once we move past that initial point.

Did the story mover fast and far enough?

Was there enough spotlight time for YOUR character?

Was there enough problem solving? (puzzle, tactical challenges, alliances with NPCs?)

Was there consensus among the players in the direction the game was going? Or was it always designed to be a somewhat adversarial campaign?


These are some of the possible leading questions I came up with from the top of my head. I'm sure there are more questions and I'm sure there are some cool answers out there in Internet Gamer Land.

    \n As a GM, I define a successful day (or evening) of GMing as one where the game won't stop.

    My last session, the story reached a good point to end for the night (it was D&D and they all leveled), so I finished the roleplaying and let them crack open the books. (They had just reached level 17 -- 9th level spells! And this campaign had started at level 1 back in 2005!)

    As we sat there, however, they kept talking about what had happened in the game, and what they were going to do next. Then they turned to me and said, "Hey, can we use that artifact we found to ask a question about the future?"

    I blinked and said, "Sure." So we got the dice back out and roleplayed through it, with one player risking his sanity in order to get a glimpse (however vague) of what the future holds.

    And then they had to discuss, in character, what it meant and how it affected their plans. It took us a while before we got back to leveling up the characters.

    \n It depends on the game, and whether I'm GMing or playing. I can make at least one generalization, though.

    Sometimes after seeing a really good movie or watching a good TV show, I find myself replaying scenes in my head after the fact, thinking about how awesome this part was, or what the implications of that part was, or how sad that scene was. And it's not always about the 'splosions and plot twists, sometimes it's more meditative. Solaris (the Soderbergh version) and Gattaca are good examples of the latter. Neither movie is "teh awesome", but I found myself turning both of them over in my head for a long while after I saw them.

    So, it's not an absolute requirement, but pretty much any game that gives me (and hopefully everyone else!) that same feeling is probably a winner. My indie group tends to e-mail each other a lot after we get home from the game, which is a good gauge on how mutual the "good movie" feeling was.

    \n A successful game is one in which during the game I feel engaged, and after the game I feel excited. During the game, I think engagement comes from good pacing, in the sense that the game is constantly moving; i.e., I feel like we were getting somewhere\u2014accomplishing things\u2014the entire time. (And those accomplishments can be addressing premise, basking in the glow of genre, kicking ass, whatever.) After the game, the excitement comes from looking back at how many amazing things happened, and how every moment was well spent. I think, yes, I really want to do that again!

    As you can guess, an unsuccessful night of gaming is the converse of all the above, and I think we all know what that feels like well enough that I don't need to explain further.

    \n I think the indicator for me of an excellent gaming night is that feeling of "Ooh, I want to run a game!" after it.

    If the game has been great, I feel like doing more gaming and my brain is wired into that "flow" of RPGing. If I've been GM:ing, I can't wait for the next session and start planning for the it right away. If I've been a player, I get really inspired, and start thinking of a new game idea I want to run to my friends.
