\n So, I don't know of any RPGs that are designed around the PCs being evil, but I sure would like to. Can anyone recommend some good ones to me, or tell me about their experiences playing any, good or bad?
\n
\nI was just talking with someone about why playing villains is no fun, and I haven't made up my mind yet but his reasoning was villains are proactive and heroes and even ordinary people are reactive, and it's much easier to play a character who is reacting to other things. While that sentiment sort of makes sense, I'm not really sure if I agree that games starring villains and evil people are inherently less fun.

    \n First, I disagree with the notion that heroes only take reactive action in the face of pro-active villains but I'm not quiet sure that's the topic here so I present:

    Acts of Evil is in development by Paul Czege in which you play straight up evil cult leaders.
    My Life with Master also by Paul Czege is about minions working in the service of an evil master.
    Sorcerer, while not about villains explicitly (in fact just as much room for heroism in this game) is about people who break cosmic law to get stuff done.

    \n Back when I was a huge World o' Darkness fan, I preferred playing the antagonistic factions, like the Sabbat and the Technocracy. But neither of those was Capital-E evil, at least the way I played them -- more like paranoid posthuman fanatics and paranoid technofascists, respectively. This was enough to get me personally labeled Evil by fellow gamers, but that's the Internet for you.

    This continues to be of interest to me. For what it's worth, Dogs in the Vineyard offers me much of the "playing the bad guy" enjoyment that the aforementioned games do; my Dogs characters are generally dubious guys who kill and meddle in the service of a greater good that they try hard to believe in.

    Lacuna gives me a bit of this feeling, too, but I don't have nearly enough play experience to say for sure.

    \n Necessary Evil is a fun supplement, but it isn't really about being evil. The storyline makes the tacit assumption that the characters will eventually repent -- or at least be goofy Silver Age villains instead of truly wicked individuals. Then again, my players were chomping at the bit to pull off complex schemes and completely ignore the published setting info.

    Truth told, I was worried about running it because of the horror stories I've heard about "evil D&D" campaigns where players took evil to mean two-dimensional. The villainous aspect did work though.
