\n For those of you who love Sword and Sorcery stuff (especially the classic imagery of Conan), you'll probably be familiar with the works of Frank Frazetta.



His work graced the covers of dozens of books (if not hundreds), gave us much of the quintessential imagery of the fantasy genre, and inspired a generation of gamers, game designers, comic artists and other creative visionaries.

Like the passing of Gygax and others, he will be missed by many.

Feel free to add your own favourite Frazetta images to this thread, or thoughts about specific Frazetta images that may have inspired characters or games over the years.

    \n One of the best if not the best in his chosen oeuvre.

    All of his John Carter stuff launched a thousand ships in my imagination.

    But the one that first arrested my attention around the age of 10 was the cover of the first Conan paperback in the Lancer/Ace/Prestige lines:



    I was like, "What the hell is going on here? That dude is almost as scary as that... that... what the hell is that thing? A man-ape?"

    The interior was as good as the cover's promise: "The Hyborian Age" (Part 1), "The Tower of the Elephant", "The Hall of the Dead", "The God in the Bowl" "Rogues in the House," and a couple other stories.

    Forever hooked.

    I met a lot of your talented imitators; I regret that I never actually met you Mr. Frazetta. R.I.P.

    \n Frazetta came into my life as a teenager through Edgar Rice Burroughs' John Carter books. This is the image that first held me captivated, as the gorgeous wrap-around dustjacket on a tome from the local library:



    The taut energy of bodies in motion, the pulsing masculinity, the strange otherworldliness, the sheer painterly quality of it--I can't say enough about it. I used to stare and stare at it.

    I was also enthralled by the pen and ink illustrations on the interior. They were lush and beautiful companions to the story, and pulsed with the same energy and motion from the covers. And in addition to that masculine force, they really informed my visual concept of femininity, with pictures like this one:



    The grace, the poise, the wide, non-barbie-doll curves, I soaked them in. It was female power as well as female beauty. It helped me, gradually, to reject the more debased imagery of the female that I was surrounded with at the time--the founding work of Image Comics, for example.

    I blogged about this in more poetic detail.

    Thanks, Frank.

    Peace,
    -Joel
