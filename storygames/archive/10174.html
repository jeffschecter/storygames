\n My gaming group recently acquired two new players who haven't really gamed before. My friend James and I decided to just ask the new players what sort of media/story they were interested in. After some discussion it was decided they'd like to play something set in the dark ages with vampires, werewolves, and zombies with some sort of vague apocalypse theme. I suggested that we try the Pool even though none of us have tried it before. I thought a simple system that supports a story oriented approach would be ideal, and everybody seemed excited about trying it.

James is running the game, and he proposed one change to the Pool's rules--he wasn't so excited about people losing all the dice they risked if they failed to roll a one. He thought that ones would remain successes, but you would only lose the pool dice that you rolled sixes on. I'm not sure how that might effect play so does anybody who's a Pool veteran have any ideas?

We generated out characters the other night and chatted about the setting a bit more. We're going to play next week.

Also any other advice about the pool as a system for new player might be useful.

Thanks!

    \n If you only lose dice on which you have sixes, you will likely have a shit-ton of successes most of the time because your die pool economy tilts toward keeping pools high and the sense of risk low. IMO that will make conflicts feel much less significant and suspenseful. This may be a personal preference thing, but all the variants of the Pool I've read keep some form of volatile dice economy to drive the game. I think it's a critical piece of the game, and recommend you play it straight first to benchmark it before tweaking the rules.

    \n My experience with The Pool is that it reads as simple but offers interesting challenges in play. Hence the utility of playing it straight first to get a bead on how it operates on the table. That said, it's very tinkerable. I've just played a few games--Wushu, for instance--where I tinkered before actually playing the default rules, and by doing so unearthed a ton of odd undesired results. You could just as easily start with a tinkered version and use that as your benchmark.

    Posted By: Kropotkin
    I can see that as a possibility, but I think what James is looking for is to avoid the frustration a player might feel at getting hosed in one roll and then trying to build things back up. Or is that part of the fun?
    Anybody else have a boring experience with Puddle-styled play?
    \nThat is indeed part of the fun, it also dramatically changes the way people approach making dice rolls to the extent you aren't playing the same thing anymore.
    I've played the Puddle, and I agree, it felt bland to me too.

    The anti-pool method works because you still have the same decision before rolling the dice: "how much do want to risk to succeed?" but avoids a natural temptation players have under the standard rules: "I'll put most of my pool on every roll, because what's the chance I'l fail," followed by, "oh no! now I'm really frustrated because I didn't really think that would happen."

    A very important component of the Pool is this:
    when you make a roll, you are showing the other players how much this roll matters to you, by how many dice you choose to risk. You can happily run through a session of the Pool gambling zero dice one every single roll, then suddenly you hit something you want to win, and the tension is palpable as you agonise over how many dice to risk. For another player, every roll might be important, and they'll face that delicious agony every single roll.
    Your variant, and the Puddle, don't have that, and it's very important to the feel of the pool, in my experience.

    The session went pretty well with lots of interesting conflict and nobody felt like they'd gotten screwed by the dice. Everyone made characters that are interesting and while there's real tension among the PCs we were all having a great time at the table.

    Our caravan was attacked by werewolves (who killed almost everyone except the PCs) and just when we thought we'd been rescued by a group of knights--they turned out to be vampires! (Or at least that's my theory).

    We'll see what happens as we're playing again on Thursday.
