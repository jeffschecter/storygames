\nNOSTALGIA

Start by reading this aloud in a group of friends. No other preparation is necessary.

We are ...
We are old. Our lives have been long. Our loved ones have died. We are gathered here and now, to walk down memory lane, toward those golden times around the year 2008. Here is exactly where we are. Now is exactly 30 years into the future.

We are ourselves, thirty years older, and with the loss of all our loved ones in the baggage; wifes, sweethearts, brothers, sisters, children. The only ones left to us in the now of this game, is ourselves, us; a group of old friends gathered here today.

Our friendships goes back at least thirty years. We have been through a lot together.

The Changes
The changes has been frightening, and in some cases: violent. The world is a harsher place now, dominated by a new superpower: China, and it's totalitarian influence in both commercial and political spheres.

USA has broken up into thirty single states and small confederations, each of them struggling with the aftereffects of the economic breakdown around 2013, and none of them getting any help from a world-community with no interest in "weak" nations. the whole twin continents of The Americas has gone down with USA, in a chaotic disintegration of all that held these continents a place for happy people.

Europe went down with the economic crisis too, triggering a wave of unrest and riots around the continent, ending some years later with the resurgence of fascism. Some pockets of democracy still persist, but lets face it: democracy has been out of fashion the last twentyfive years. The fascist states are little more than robber-baronies, squabbling over the remains of a dead civilization.

Africa is a total human catastrophe, going through what can be described as a second colonial era, with cynical Chinese entrepreneurs plundering the continent for resources. The continent is a quagmire of sickness, hunger and social disintergration, with no real states to talk about, a tragedy continued from it's unbelievable harsh history. A history devoid of hope.

The Middle-East has seen no changes, and that in itself is a tragedy. From Pakistan to Morocco the Persian/Arabic/Islamic-peoples suffers like ever before, struggling with fanaticism, tribe-wars and an ongoing conflict with the religious theocracy of the highly militaristic Israel. The political paranoia and social psychopathy of the Islamic peoples continues to grow.

China and it's neighbors; Korea, Japan, Vietnam, Indonesia and India; are the new developed countries, experiencing wealth and happiness like no other place on earth ever. The new technologies developed in these countries, and their military might, has ensured an unpolluted world where no powers threatens their hegemony. No polluting industry is permitted, neither in Asia, nor on other continents. The Asian economies are growing towards heaven under the new production-paradigm, powered by wind and wave and solar-energy. The Asian peoples dominate the world at large, as far as they care to involve themselves in the chaotic Rest-World. Their compassion is not high, with old super- and colonial-powers.

The Club
We, the friends, sit here now, in our annual gathering, our "club" named Nostalgia. We have gathered here to remind ourselves of better times, when life was normal and times where happy.

The club has had more members, but many has succumbed to evil times; hunger, plague, war and violence. We are the ones left. This club is our last sanctuary, a way for us to escape the harsh reality, by talking about the happy days back then, in the beginning of the millenium, around 2008.

We will talk about all good thing of the past, and try to avoid talking about the changes and the present. All we say about the changes and the present are unwanted, and will be met with sharp glares and short reminders on what Nostalgia is all about; this is a club for happy memories! All we say about the past, the happy days, are true and will be met with nodding, supportive sounds, or positive comments. The sorrows we express, over lost ones, will be met with understanding. We all have them. But we generally refrain from delving on the sorrows. They are too many.

We will talk until, one by one, we grow silent. One by one we will turn our attention inwards, on loss and sorrow and anxiety. And we will sit silent until all have quieted. Then we will, one by one, let out a heavy sigh.

When all have filled the silence with a sigh, the game is over.

    My questions:
    - Can games be used for social and/or political purposes?
    - Is it possible to enact changes in the real world through the use of great game-design?
    - May games be used to change our lives?

    I do not intend to discuss our lust for or will to change the world through games. This discussion is limited to the actual possibility of such life/world-changing games.

    Are such games possible, and if they are: how?

    How would you make such a game?
    - without preaching
    - with engaging gameplay
    - on a theme that lends itself to interaction
    - in a way that makes people play it

    Posted By: Tomas HVMNone of the speculations given on Asia is meant as more than quite improbable ideas.
    Hugely, wildly, utterly improbable, even. China is so messed up these days, it's not even funny. Thirty years away from utopia and peaceful coexistence with its neighbors? It would take them thirty years just to start dealing with the problems they're having now, they certainly wouldn't have solved any of them.

    But that's beside the point. You could just as easily search-and-replace "China" with an entirely fictional nation, even one that's basically China in a cheap costume, and by doing so you'd neatly sidestep the whole issue without (I think) noticeably blunting whatever effects you're trying to achieve. After all, your goal isn't to say "rah rah China's gonna get its shit together and be awesome someday," it's to...er...well, get whatever it is you're going for. I'm afraid I'm not entirely clear on what exactly that might be, sorry.


    Anyway, the sticking point as I see it for the various goals you've suggested (the serious intent, the potential to change lives/society, etc.) is that the game has to be played, and it will have to be played by people who want those things, too. You as a developer sitting at your keyboard can want it as hard as you like. You can cross your fingers, whisper prayers, stamp your feet, yell, plead, and whatever else you think might help...but what people take away from the game you wrote is always going to depend on what they wanted to take away from it. You're not going to trick them into it; no matter how crystal-clear you make your intentions in the text you wrote, you don't get any say in what happens in their game or what (if anything) they'll get out of it.

    (But the flip side is also true: if they have the serious intent and the desire to be changed and/or promote change, they'll do it, and you could maybe write the game so that those people have a nice toolkit of things to use towards those goals which they might not have thought of on their own, and that would be pretty good game design. So it's not necessarily a waste of effort to design a game towards those goals.)

    \n It's interesting that you mention Shakespeare, because the corollorary of the question "can games change the world" is surely "can art change the world" or "can fiction change the world", to which the answer is most certainly yes, it can, and has done many times.

    Perhaps not. What a great novel offers to its readers is a participation in a particular fiction, which is disseminated into the minds of many people, and changes their perception of the world. Although one individual reader may take slightly different things away from the fiction than another, because their own experiences of the world differ, en mass all readers read much the same thing, and with sufficient meaning, and sufficient numbers, that can change cultures, as Shakespeare has.

    The roleplaying game is acting on a more basic level than a novel or movie. I think there is some merit to Hituros description of it as "more like a writing technique than a book".

The single most important skill for a gamesmith to have, is the ability to create an effective and purposeful method for his roleplaying game.

    \n How to design such a game, by which you mean a game you take seriously? I think that there are three approaches, all of them relatively simple.

    The first is to pick a topic that concerns you, then design a game around the issues of that topic, taking the whole thing seriously and researching the issues involved. This is like what you did at the start of the thread, but you would have to carry through and do the necessary setting research and take your material, even the smallest parts of it, seriously and be sure to get it all right, rather than tossing it off and going "well, this part is okay to be wrong, because it's not important to the main thrust of the game." Details are everything!

    The second is to pick some material which is of deep interest to you, whether or not that material is "serious" in any way, and then, while designing it, take it as seriously as you can and do not shy away from serious human issues that crop up around it. You cannot know when you start what issues the game contains, but if the fiction around it is compelling, strong human issues will naturally emerge if you do not try to dismiss them, cover them up, or keep yourself in willful ignorance. Polaris is, to some degree, an example of this: to start I was simply inspired by several accomplished fantasists, and in the process of writing discovered issues of duty, loss, activism, and burn-out.

    The last is to write a game in a cathartic mode: to fix problems in your own heart or at least examine or address them. In this case, taking your own work seriously is not an issue, because you will naturally do so: a mind will brook no attempts, even its own, at trivializing its own demons and concerns. Bliss Stage is primarily an example of this, despite its anime trappings, where I am seriously looking at my own problems about sexuality, memory, trauma, and social relationships stemming from being an abuse survivor and PTSD sufferer. The game needed to be written simply for my own survival. In my defense as a genre writer, many other entries into this genre come from the same source, including Evangelion, the most famous internationally.
