    EDIT: Now I want to find my old Crim books, and see how this is going to play out. (We didn't cover insanity last year, so I don't remember off hand.) It's actually a fascinating question from the standpoint of intentions. Most states break their murder statutes down into greater or lesser categories, depending on the frame of mind of the murderer (i.e., Murder One, manslaughter, etc.). Typically the defense on this is some variation on, "I didn't know that would happen/didn't want it to happen." Here, you can't say that, because in a fantasy role-playing game, you draw swords to kill d00dz and take their stuff. But what happens when you're authorizing an imaginary sub-section of your personality to do this?

    This is going to be a great opinion to read. Let's hope the judge settles the "I Was Just Playing My Character" issue once and for all, so we can all cite to it on RPGNet.

    These cases are always interesting in terms of how they assign cause-and-effect relationships. Sensible judges generally seem to conclude that "after that, therefore because of that" is the fallacy it is, and to attribute whatever other oddness is in the person's life to their mental illness rather than the reverse.

    By coincidence I've just been reading this report of a judgement on a case where the hypnotist Paul McKenna's show was claimed to have triggered off a man's schizophrenia. The judge found that this was not probable, that if there was any connection at all it had been simply that the schizophrenia was about to emerge and any unusual emotional experience would have triggered it sooner or later, and that in any case McKenna had not been negligent.

Yes, in no way can you attribute any fault on D&D or RPGs for the guy's state.
