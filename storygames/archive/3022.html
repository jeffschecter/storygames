\n Examine this sentence for jargon. How hard is it for an outsider to get the jist of what this person is talking about?

"I hate it when I GM a game because I have to play all the NPCs. So now I just roll a d20 and look at the reaction chart whenever my PCs want something from an NPC."

Now, examine this sentence.

"Man, our session last night was really cool. I felt like my character was really a protagonist, because we had some really good bangs and we finally got our teeth on Narrativism this session."

Which is more inaccessible to an outsider? Why?

ADDENDUM (edited): At your option, re-write both sentences using as few jargon terms as possible.

yrs--
--Ben

P.S. Both of these sentences are monumentally stupid. They're written to showcase the vocabulary, not for content. Please don't critique these as gaming style things. Just put yourself in a position when you don't get the context, and say which one makes more sense and why.

    Anyone who does understand roleplaying games will understand the first.

    Anyone who doesn't understand Forge RPG theory will be confused by the second.

    The first example has the advantage of being almost self-consciously gamer-speak, or at least, uses a lot of abbreviations, which tip off readers to the fact that they're seeing something which isn't plain English.

    The second, on the other hand, has almost no clues of that sort, and thus would lead someone to assume that they're not actually seeing unfamiliar words, but they're seeing familiar words (protagonist, bangs, etc.). So they would assume that they're reading something they could understand using normal English.

    There's one clue, the capital letter on Narrativism (why?), but it doesn't speak to the notion that there's something called a creative agenda out there. Instead, it sounds like some sort of in-game thing. Maybe there's an evil cult or supervillain called Narrativism and you finally caught him.

    These are my thoughts.

    The latter one isn't subject to acronym expansion, nor any sort of direct replacement; you have to understand the concepts and how they fit into a specific theoretical model to fully grasp what's being said.

    Let's try rewriting, though.

    "I hate it when I gamemaster a game because I have to play all the non-player characters. So now I just roll a twenty-sided die and look at the reaction chart whenever my player characters want something from a non-player character."

    That's the acronym expansion version. And it instantly becomes a lot more understandable, or apparently so to the casual reader, because the main source of confusion (what do all these abbreviations mean?) is eliminated.

    Now I'm looking at:

    "Man, our session last night was really cool. I felt like my character was really a protagonist, because we had some really good bangs and we finally got our teeth on Narrativism this session."

    ... I don't know if I can actually rewrite this, because my grasp of Forge RPG Theory is weak.

    I don't even think that's a correct "translation" by me.

    Of course, the thing is that none of us are really writing jargon for "outsiders," so the question itself seems a little ambiguous. The real question is who your audience might be, and what you can assume they'll know.

    Both of these sentences are taken out of context, or rather, without any context at all, and context matters for a lot. If inserted in the middle of an explanation of Narrativism, there's nothing wrong with the latter. If posted on someone's roleplaying journal, there's nothing wrong with the former (even with unexpanded acronyms).

    It's all about good writing, ultimately, and most gamers don't know how to write well, including a lot of game designers. Most game designers only know how to use jargon.

    By "more accessible," do you mean:

    (1) easier for the hearer to do the work of interpreting or unpacking, or

    (2) on its face more intelligible?

    Because arguably the acronyms are doing something funky there: "GM" means "game master"; the latter is more intelligible than the former, so in a sense you're handicapping the "traditional lingo." Regardless of whether or not you use acronyms, however, the word signals that it belongs to the "code" of role-playing language. But a word like "protagonist," which is on its face more intelligible than "GM," is not obviously within what linguists would call the "register" (i.e., bank of words) of roleplaying: it could belong to literary criticism, for example. The slightly greater ambiguity may make for lesser "intelligibility."

    But it's a blind alley to talk about whether or not a sentence is relatively accessible or inaccessible to "an outsider," so let's not do that. I think it's better to consider what a hearer needs to know in order to make sense of the sentence; or, better, what sense of the sentence a particular interpreter can make based on what they already know. What do you think of doing that, instead?

    2) Have everyone, since we all use technical jargon, think critically about how outsiders are going to perceive technical jargon when it's used in front of them, and what sort of reactions that they might have.

    My thoughts:

    When approaching the first sentence, my instinct as an outsider is just to give up and go away, unless there's some pressing reason why I should want to understand it at all. It's totally unapproachable. I don't have a clue what the guy is talking about, and my reaction is going to be just to move along.

    With the other one, it's very different. Because the roots of the words are more obvious to me, it's possible for me to construct an idea of what he's saying in my head, even if that idea is totally wrong. So, it's not just "blahblahblah" it's actually words, kinda.

    Now, here's the place where things break down. I, Ben, in this situation (and provided that there's any interest) will start asking about what things mean. "So, by, wifflfish, you mean ..?" But, this behavior has totally been trained in me by talking on the Forge. Pre-Forge Ben would totally just assume that he'd gotten the words from context, jump into the conversation, and use them to mean what he thought they should mean.

    Thoughts on responses: Ironically, not a single person imagined that an uninitiated person would lose their shit and demand that everyone explain what the hell they were talking about or they must be elitist assholes who can't speak or write. I guess we're all picturing a very low-key listener.

    3) Rewriting. This was Fred's idea, I'll let him speak to the purposes.

    "I hate it when all of my friends have gathered together and we're playing a game which is also sort of an improvisational acting exercise and sort of like Dungeons and Dragons but not, and I'm the one who is appointed to hold all of the mechanical and fictional authority, except when other people get really angry at me and threaten to break down the game, because in those circumstances it's necessary for me to portray and make decisions for all the characters that occupy the fictional world that we're creating / imagining via our play of this game, excepting those few characters that the other players have been given authority for, most of the time. So now whenever those characters who the other players have been given authority for, most of the time wants to interact with another character that I have authority for, most of the time, I just roll a twenty-sided die, which is an odd shaped sort of die that you use to play these sorts of games, or at least some of these sorts of games, and using the result of that die roll I consult a table which lists different possible attitudes emotional reactions, and just use that random result to inform that character's reaction to the other character's action."

    "Man, last night when we got together to mutually contribute to a story mediated a game-like mechanical structure, I had a really good time. I felt like the character in our shared story that I was given authority to make decisions for really came into his own as a protagonist, and that I was able to make, via that character, some really key decisions about the course of our story and the over-all fiction of the game, and my input was really recognized. The reason that I got to do this was because one or more of the other players had thought of fictional situations in which my character had to make immediate and important decisions, and those decisions definitely had an impact over the rest of play. We finally got our teeth into the core purpose of what we're playing, which is to actively engage with the fiction as co-creators responsible for the outcome of the fictional events, rather than as passive audience to a pre-planned structure."

    You may disagree with some of my use of the terms in the first sentence. This is because these terms mean different things to different people (and in different games) and I had to make a decision about which explanation to use.

    More analysis in a bit.

    \n So, given that jargon isn't going to be intuitively comprehensible (which, frankly, it will never be) is it better to have and use jargon which is unaccessible (like in sentence #1) or psuedo-accessible (like in sentence #2)?

    I think each has it's weaknesses and strengths. Here's my take on those -- others feel free to disagree, as this is not well thought out and written off the cuff.

    The weaknesses of unaccessible jargon are that it is forbidding to most people and it creates a very steep learning curve to be able to participate in discussion at all. I think that this leads to a culture of cliquishness and insularity (I see this in science fields, also) and a general mistrust of outsiders. Eventually, this becomes totally un-examined use of jargon all the time, which is a problem especially when the mean of the jargon words varies locally.

    I hadn't thought about this before now, but: When people talk about making role-playing more accessible, they never talk about language. Why not? It seems like a very obvious place to start with the accessibility.

    The strength of inaccessible jargon is that it is considerably less prone to being misunderstood and also somewhat less frustrating, because you either learn it or you don't: It's very hard to get it wrong.

    The biggest weakness of pseudo-accessible jargon is that it is prone to being misunderstood and, furthermore, these misunderstandings are prone to spreading, because someone who misunderstands the term will think that they understand it, and will then go on to "teach" this misunderstanding to others. Other weaknesses are that people will get more easily confused by the terms (because they aren't as clearly identifiable as specialized jargon terms) and that trying to learn the intended meanings of them can be quite difficult, because there are other associations (accurate or not as they are) with the words and those need to be unlearned, which is frustrating as all get out.

    The advantage of pseudo-accessible terms is that, often, someone without a lot of context can get a pretty good idea about what's being talked about, even if they don't pick up all the details. Also, they're much easier to learn (because the more ordinaryish words serve a mnemonics) and integrate much more naturally into the flow of language.

    Other thoughts on this?

      "Accounting for Taste" has a good point. There's not much of a problem with jargon in general. Human beings a animals that speak, and they will make up new words and phrases to use when speaking. And, when confronted with a new word they haven't heard before, they will try to figure it out, or ask. They only get frustrated when there doesn't seem to be a way to figure a word out, or a way to get a correct definition, or when the people using the new word throw up artificial barriers and act like "gatekeepers" to some kind of mystery.

      \n

      Much of the jargon and slang used around here isn't that bad, because it's based on firm visual metaphors or analogies to something most people are familiar with. The problems, I would argue, come from jargon that isn't firmly grounded in a visual metaphor or an analogy. This mainly seems to happen when the jargon refers to people's intentions or goals -- in other words, stuff you can never really experience. That's why GNS is so problematic: ultimately, they are terms for why people do what they do, and since you can never really know that, people get into arguments about why some people do X, or which intentions or desires are the fundamental ones that all others are mere shadows of. That's also why Push and Pull have become problematic: it started as a simple analogy to indian wrestling, a way of creating changes in fiction by applying force or creating a spot for force to move into, but it later picked up a lot of baggage about what people's intentions are, and things became murkier.

      \n

      In the examples Ben gave at the top of the thread, the first example is pretty impenetrable to an outsider, even if the abbreviations are expanded, but all the jargon words ultimately refer to observable procedures or objects, so the jargon is relatively easy to learn.

      \n

      In the second example, "session" is technically jargon, but it is used almost identical to the way it's used in (a) music recording, (b) therapy, (c) education, and (d) a court of law, so an outsider overhearing someone talking about their "game session" won't know exactly what that entails, but will get the basic concept immediately. The word "protagonist" is similarly coined by analogy to a literary protagonist, but that's not as well-known a word; anyone who has taken college-prep English in high school or read actual film criticism will know it, but there are plenty of outsiders who won't know the term. Furthermore, some gamers use the term "protagonist" with a little extra baggage: it's not just the hero of the story or the person who makes the plot happen, but it also involves whatever the player of that character intended to happen to that character -- and the murk creeps in, slightly. "Bangs" is opaque in the same way that "NPC" is opaque and certainly has a smaller community of usage, but it's just an onomatopoetic name for something grounded in observable behavior, so it's not murky, like the deceptive "protagonist" or the last jargon word "Narrativism", which is definitely tainted with concerns about intention.

      \n

      So, not only is the second example easier to misunderstand by an outsider, it's 50% easier for more experienced members of the community to disagree over.
