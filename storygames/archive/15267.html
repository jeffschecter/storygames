    \n I'm not using it as a weapon. I'm not even trying to attack the fellow that enjoys Perfected. I'm just saying that Perfected doesn't have enough structure or answer enough questions for it to really be considered a game. A toy is a thing that you can do fun stuff with, but isn't a game. Take Daniel Solis's Writer's Dice for example. That's a toy. Can you play a game with it? Yup, and I even purchased a pair. But the fact remains that Writer's Dice, a physical toy (or tool, if you like) not too far removed from the metaphorical toy (or tool) that is Perfected, are not in and of themselves a game. Can you play with it? Yes. Does that make it a game? No. A game is something you play, whereas a toy is something you play with. Does that mean I dislike it? No, I'd play with that in the backseat of the car on a road trip with friends, but while you can make a game out of it, that page is not a game in and of itself in the same way that, say, a roleplaying poem is.
    \n
    \nThink of that image like it was a tower of Jenga blocks. The tower of blocks is a toy that can be fun to play with. You can build things with the blocks and knock them down, you can play pretend with them, you can play "2001: A Space Oddysey/2010: Oddysey Two" with them, pretending they are monoliths, heck, you can pretend they are carrot sticks if you like, I don't mind. But Jenga blocks are not a game unless you know the rules. Can it be hacked? Absolutely! Hacking is awesome, just look at the Donkey Kong version! but in the end, it isn't the blocks, but a distinct and complete set of rules for what to do with the blocks that makes it a game.
    \n
    \nIn my opinion, therefore, Perfected is a pile of wooden blocks that you can do with whatever you like, which to me smacks more of a toy than a game.
    \n
    \nI hope this doesn't hurt anyone's feelings, but if it does, I'd love to know why. Why would it in the slightest invalidate your fun if you were playing a game you made up which utilizes a certain toy vs. playing a game that already is a game?

    \n Playing catch is a game, though a simple one. "Catch" is in itself a goal. The football is a toy.

    I didn't use "playing" here because it's cheating to call "playing" a goal and use that to prove something is a game. You can play with toys, and you can play games.

    Stacking blocks is a game, though a simple one. "Stacking" is a goal. The blocks are toys.

    "Monopoly" has goals built into the rules. If you "play Monopoly," you more or less abide by some set of rules, which include or imply goals. In the case of Monopoly, there's a victory condition everyone should strive for. The rules are more important than the game materials for defining what Monopoly is. In fact, people clone the game materials and change them around all the time (e.g., My Monopoly). If you played with the board, houses, tokens, and money from a Monopoly game but didn't know the rules, I think most people would agree you were not "playing Monopoly." At worst, you are using the pieces as a toy. At best, you've created a new game by introducing your own rules, structure, and goals.

    Role-playing games are more complicated. A lot of them don't really explain what a player's goals are, but players seem to know what their goals are anyway. The RPG itself might not be a game without that creative input, but most people know how to use it correctly. It's as if the actual victory conditions were absent from Monopoly, but everyone at the table knew that you were supposed to "win" by accumulating more money than everyone else.

    Imagine an RPG that has just a character generator, and nothing else. Is that a game yet?

    Imagine an RPG with a character generator, a rule that says "roll d20 to figure out if you succeed," and no setting, implied or otherwise. Is that a game yet?

    Imagine the same game, but with an explicit rule for accomplishing tasks, but nothing implying what kind of tasks a character can do. This is very difficult, because skills and attributes suggest goals, right? But imagine that the character's only stat is "EFFECTIVENESS" and that you roll 2d6 + EFFECTIVENESS and if it's 8 or higher, you succeed. Is that a game yet?

    Now, I haven't read Jocelyn's thing yet, so I don't have any opinion about it. But I have opinions about where toy stops and game starts and how to apply those ideas to role-playing games. These are those opinions.

    \n Thoughts from one designer perspective:
    A game is something to be played as I intended. A toy is something to be played with however you want (though I might guess what that'll be).

    I will put work into making sure you know how to play my game as I intended. I may put work into guiding your play with my toy.

    Games can reside within toys. A book full of games is a toy. A fantasy world full of quests might be a toy, and picking a quest might mean you're now playing a game.

    A toy that's only good for one thing probably ought to be a game (i.e., players should definitely know how to use it for that one thing).
    A game that's good for many things besides the designer's intent will likely be used as a toy.

    \n I've personally always hated the "that's not a game, that's a toy" argument, as it always seem to imply that "yeah, that wacky thing that YOU DO, that I DON'T do is stupid, so therefore it's can't be a game" kind of mentality.It always feels like the person making the assertions needs to have his little hobby be more important or deep than someone else's hobby that is "simpler" than his. Along the same extensions extensions of "hard-core" and "casual" gaming, and recently as a way for traditional video game guys to debunk social games.

    My personal definition of a game has always been pretty open: "Any activity done for personal enjoyment with an agreed upon set of rules, whether explicit or implied, written or unstated, is a game."

    Of course, this opens up the world to all sorts of wacky conditions. I'd rather focus on the enjoyment of the "players" and not worry about if the goal is tough or competitive enough.

    Granted, this opens up the can of worms that basically something like knitting is a game, in the sense that you are doing for personal enjoyment; but ultimately, there will be some set of goals and rules within a game of knitting that might not be apparent...things like "I want to get the sleeve done by tonight," and "best looking sweater ever," which align pretty nicely to the silly achievement lists that modern videogames have.

    Now, I guess there's two questions that come to my mind: (1) Are there any practical uses to the distinction? (2) What's the minimum amount of structure a game requires to actually be a game? What's the tipping point between toy and game, so to speak?
    \nTo me the tipping point is when method overtakes function in importance. A d6 is a random number generator, I can do lots of things with it (some that don't even include number generation). It becomes part of a game when the method of its use becomes of primary import.

    Words on a piece of paper can be used for a lot of purposes. The game emerges when we have a method of determining those words and how they work together.\n

It's like saying that a professional NBA game and a game of H-O-R-S-E are the same thing, they're not even close.     \n Good points. I think part of the problem is the use of the term "toy" specifically and what that term means to different people. To some people, it's just a term used to describe an implement for play. But for some people, it can have the (sometimes negative) connotation of something that isn't serious or belongs in the hands of a child. I know that not everyone who label's a self-proclaimed-rpg a toy does so with negative intentions, but it should be somewhat understood that the designer of said rpg could take offense. Consider these parallels...

    "It's not so much a song as it is a jingle..."

    "It's not so much a painting as it as a doodle..."

    "It's not so much a sculpture as it is a wad of clay..."

    My point is, people are obviously on two sides of the fence on this topic, so it may be an issue of political correctness, but out of respect for your fellow rpg-enthusiasts and designers, it's probably not the best approach. You're throwing at term at someone that can possibly be misconstrued as a bad thing, even if that's not how you intended it. The way they see it, you're basically saying that what they intended to be one thing is actually something less. Instead of calling it a "toy", why not call it a "tool"? Does it not accomplish the same thing without the potential negative connotations? If you don't consider it a game in and of itself, then would "tool" not be another way to say that without attaching what a lot of people would consider an insult?

Do you start with a new plot of land and not build anything? No, you have an inherent goal of building a city. Do you leave your buildings unconnected to the power grid? No, there is the inherent goal of connecting your buildings to the power plants. While those goals might not count as a game to you, they do to me.

    Posted By: masqueradeballCandy Land is a game to most people, and its useful in some context to understand it as such.
    \nCandy Land is a particularly interesting example.

    Many folks, myself included, would argue that, in addition to goals of some sort, an activity needs to have some sort of influence of skill or choice in order for it to be a game.

    Candy Land has absolutely zero influence from skill or choice. There are no choices. There is absolutely no way to do better or worse at a game of Candy Land. Once the deck has been shuffled, the activity is 100% scripted from that point on. The absolute only way to influence the outcome of the game is to somehow change the order in which the cards are drawn - for example, to go to the bathroom and take so long that the other players decide to skip your turn.

    Because of that, I would argue that Candy Land is not a game. It is an activity. Even though it has goals. Even though you can win.

    For an example of a similar activity, take out a coin. Call heads or tails. Flip the coin. If you called correctly you win. However, your choice was not meaningful. You weren't playing a game.

    The inclusion of meaningful choice or skill, however, is a bit more controversial when it comes to defining a game.

    \n As I understand it (and as has been mentioned), the distinction originally came from Will Wright. He meant "toy" positively. Perhaps, he suggested, SimCity wasn't really a game at all. It was just a thing that was fun to play with.

    Greg Costikyan then extended the distinction, by adding a definition to it. I think the definition is interesting for discussion, but not to be taken as truth.

    Since then, the phrase "This isn't a game, it's a toy" has been adopted as a perjorative way of dissing games that don't look like the games you're used to. Used that way, I think it's worthless.

    Remember that, in the original conception, "Toy" was a positive thing. In a recent talk, Tom Chatfield suggested we all design better toys. I agree with him.
