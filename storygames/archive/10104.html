\nThis article posits a (largely anecdotal) correlation between interest in complex games and a lack of challenge in daily activities (and vice versa). For all its lack of any real scientific rigor, I think it makes a sort of intuitive sense--I can see the same correlation for me (though I also add "social engagements" to the "challenges" list).

So, as a combination of poll and sound board:
* Do you find that you gravitate towards simpler games when busy at other activities, and more challenging or complex games when generally bored with the day-to-day?
* Presuming we find a strong correlation, what can we do to introduce, market, and promote games to take advantage of this tendency?

Could THIS be the crux of the whole "mundanes don't like crunchy games" notion: they are too challenged by daily life (work, kids, social) to have the patience and "head space" to learn a high-crunch game? Is this why "the new wave" of games (both RPG and PC) seem to do MUCH better in the market when "casual" or "rules light" or "tightly focused"?

    \n Frankly, I think the relationship is backwards: people who are used to complexity take it in stride, while people who are not do not. It is like scheduling\u2014your friends who work 50 hours a week always manage to have time, while your couch-surfing friends can't ever seem to make it. There is a general trend towards simpler activities across the board. A hundred years ago, people would read long novels just to keep busy. When you have work, kids, the internet, tv, movies, baseball, and a zillion other things to distract you, taking time to master something is less appealing. This is partly related to the fact that rural people are used to being jacks-of-all-trades, while city folk are used to being hyper-specialized.

    Then, there is the (probably exaggerated) notion that Americans are more extroverted and stupid, while other cultures are more introverted and intelligent.

    But what do I know? I've lived my whole life at 11, and I have studied games since I was old enough to hold a crayon in hand. Work is irrelevant to my engagement with games.

    \n I would guess that stressful lifestyle = simple preference but that complex challenges = complex preference. Generally, folks who choose to engage in complex challenges do so because they enjoy mastering complex systems, and this would carry over to entertainment.

    But thinking about it, this is nuanced because many of the most system-centric people enjoy elegant systems but don't enjoy unnecessary complexity for its own sake. Usually, the simple, elegant solution requires a much more complex understanding than the complicated solution.

    \n I'm going to distinguish between crunchy and deep games. Go is an obvious example; not many rules (ergo, not crunchy), but still deep.

    My tolerance for "crunchy" games is tied directly to the amount of time I have to establish rules mastery over the game. This has been the biggest barrier for me to get Mouse Guard or Burning Empires to the table. I need to learn the game before I can teach the game, and both of those games have a high crunch factor. So, yeah, life and kids and such interfere with my ability to engage with a crunchy game.

    However, for my analog gaming, I will invariably prefer a deep game to a shallow game. In fact, the more stressed I am, the more I want to play a deep game. I think that this is because a deep game is generally rational. Stress often comes from facing a lack of control in one's life, and a deep game (especially a deep board game) can be a ritual of order, a haven from an insane world, a place where the rules are knowable and controllable.

    So, that's my nuanced answer.

    \n Crunch vs. Deep is a good distinction.

    The rules of Go:
    1. The board is empty at the outset of the game.
    2. Black makes the first move, after which he and White alternate.
    3. A move consists of placing one stone of one's own color on an empty intersection on the board.
    4. A player may pass his turn at any time.
    5. A stone or solidly connected group of stones of one color is captured and removed from the board when all the intersections directly adjacent to it are occupied by the enemy. (Capture of the enemy takes precedence over self-capture.)
    6. No stone may be played so as to recreate a former board position.
    7. Two consecutive passes end the game.
    8. A player's territory consists of all the points he has either occupied or surrounded.
    9. The player with more territory wins.

    \n Hmm. Yes, there seems to me to be a good bit of nuance to this question. In general, I tend to agree with Julian in this, and also this the crunch vs. depth thing folks are mentioning fits here as well ( especially after Caesar_X introduced me to several German-style board and card games).

    I think there may be a couple of other things to consider, (especially when this concept is applied to rpgs/sgs).

    How often do people play a given game?

    My guess, based entirely on personal experience, is that people who are engaging with a game regularly, are going to add depth and/or crunch, no matter how the game as written starts out. However, the style of crunch/depth they add in on their own is going to in some way reflect what they see in the original game mechanics or what they ae utilizing of those mechanics.

    For example, in a relatively lite'n'tight rule-set that is engaged with regularly, I would expect players to add depth in terms of characterization and world-builing and developing plot/storyline. I would expect a similar trend from players who were technically using a crunchier system, but one they themselves weren't engaging fully ( the classic mainstream sort of RPG, for example, on the character players' end of things).
