I know a lot of people have plenty of gaming options close at hand, and don't see the need to listen to actual-play podcasts, but this one is GM'd by Mark Reed, a friend of a friend who wrote a True20 game I liked (Bounty Head Bebop).

What grabs me is the "most popular RPG gameplay podcast in the world!" claim. This is a vanilla D&D campaign - granted that it is run by someone I think is a damn good GM, but MOST popular? More popular than RPGMP3? Than Yog-Sothoth?

The statistician in me raises an eyebrow. There's no data to support or refute this claim, so the social scientist me has to go away unsatisfied.

Am I the only one who's curious about comparative podcast audiences? A lot of the netspace of the RPG hobby (as I experience it) is in podcasts. Unfortunately, there is no Louis Harris or Nielsen, so good information is hard to come by. I would be really surprised (and smugly satisfied) if Master Plan or The Podge Cast had more subscribers than the official WOTC D&D podcast.

But there's no way to tell one way or the other.

    \n You're right. Download numbers are only part of the picture. I know that RPGMP3.com puts out about 1.5 to 2 TB of audio every month through its two feeds. There are drops in downloads during the end of each semester; finals week, I imagine - which is interesting information all by itself. The only reason I know this is because the site admin was nice enough to chat with me and let me know.

    I should just accept this as the landscape of the field I'm going into. It is hard to get useful data. Almost everything that is available is either published by parties with a vested interest, or the result of a poll of a self-selecting population. RPGs aren't the only example of this. I live in NYC, and I would love to research a paper about the economic impact of Off-Broadway theater. There are a lot of interesting cultural institutions I would like to look at more rigorously, but unless I come up with some clever way to get to the raw numbers, it isn't going to happen.
