I've seen some impressive character sheets and other diagrams produced by some folks here, and thought I'd start a thread to ask about what tools people are using. I'll kick off:

PDFs: PDFCreator (http://sourceforge.net/projects/pdfcreator/) - free software, lets you save from any Windows program to a PDF by treating it as a printer.

Vector graphics: Inkscape (http://www.inkscape.org/). Free software, and easily as good as my old copy of CorelDraw, so I've switched over.

Maps: AutoRealm (http://autorealm.sourceforge.net/), which despite describing itself as "the free GNU mapper" is actually for Windows. It's a little clunky sometimes but it does the job. Fairly easy to use, it just has a few quirks which you need to get used to, and the included icons are not always very slick-looking.

Any alternatives/additionals?

    Adobe: A sane person (at least in this industry) does not buy Adobe products as the cheapest way to get any job done. No, no, no. That way lies madness. It's always cheaper to do something with a different piece of software. Always.

    A sane person, strangely enough, buys Adobe products for no specific reason. I bought myself a partial set, piece by laboriously saved-for, retail-priced piece, because I wanted to have the ability to conceive of a project and make it a reality without worrying about my tools, at all. It's an investment in empowerment.
    There's a wonderful story with a similar moral: shortly after the Xerox research people figured out the photocopying process, they were running very low on funds, and wanted to sell the patents, the prototypes, all that jazz. They wanted someone to basically cash out their investment in the technology in exchange for all future rights and profits. AT&T commissioned a consultant to give them a cost/benefit analysis. The consultant came back and said "Even if the photocopying process takes over the entire market for carbon paper and mimeographs, it will not reasonably repay the investment being asked of you at this date." So AT&T didn't buy the technology, Xerox muddled through, photocopying created a market vastly larger than carbon paper and mimeographs ever dreamed of, and everyone learned an important lesson in economics.
    Adobe products do the same thing, on microscale, for me. Before I bought them, if I had compared their price to their benefits on every project I had attempted before that, it would have looked like a crummy deal. But once I bought the software, it was suddenly much easier for me to undertake such projects.

    I need a nicely printed book? Illustrator. Screw resolution, go all vector. And as an outgrowth of that, at some later date I need a big banner, with graphics thousands upon thousands of pixels across? No sweat. Take my vector artwork, scale it up painlessly, composite it with some other stuff and export a billion pixels worth of banner, all in about fifteen minutes.

    If you want to produce a product stay the heck away from Adobe. Buy some of the much more reasonably priced products that people have mentioned, and make them work for your purpose. It gives a sense of satisfaction.

    But if you want to become the type of person who looks for opportunities to shove graphics around, who in fact invents reasons to undertake complicated design tasks just for the hell of it, then you may want to start saving up your money, because Adobe has products you should look at.

    Oliof: I'll call you a freak. ;-) I got my Master's in CS, did my thesis in LaTeX... and even then couldn't do half of what I wanted without referring to a manual.

    IMHO, markup languages run into two big problems for doing games:

    #1 - Page layout is a very visual thing. A game, like a magazine, isn't just about presenting information -- it's also about style and looks. Diagrams in a thesis or article are in support of some point in the text, and thus naturally associated with parts of the text. Artwork in a magazine or game, though, is not necessarily related to any part of the text, so it feels more natural to simply place it rather than imbed it as codes in the text.

    #2 - Markup languages, like all command languages, have to be learned. For a casual user, an interface with menus, dialogs, inspectors, etc. is good, because it affords the ability to "look around" for how to do something. In contrast, with LaTeX, if I can't remember how to do something, the only thing I can do is dig out the manual. There's no "I think that was on the XYZ menu..." possible.

    #2 can be fixed by using LyX or some other application that can create LaTeX for you... but at that point, LaTeX just becomes another file format -- one with good style support.

    As mentioned above, I'm currently using Apple's Pages 2.0. For me, it's much easier to use than LaTeX, while maintaining a style-centric way of doing things. It also provides wonderful ease of use -- I can literally drag-and-drop a picture out of pretty much any of Mac app (including the web browser) and have Pages take it. Same thing with much text.

    On the file format side of things, a .pages "file" is actually a directory, with an XML file that has the text and formatting information, the graphics files you've dropped in stored as files, and a couple of subdirectories for the program to store pre-generated page thumbnails and such. Thus, while there aren't any other applications that can directly use it right now, everything is recoverable if something were to happen.

    I've seen people use structured text for things like monster books and spell books, and that seems to work well, since you've got a lot of things where there are specific "types" of information, and you want to just pour them all into a standard format, then set them into a document... but it seems to me that there, an even better thing to do would be to make a database, then a way to generate the structured text from it.

    --Travis

I don't think it's apropos to discuss the nature of piracy in an information economy here (we'll take that to a game of Shock: sometime); suffice to say that you don't court trouble by using Free or cheap software that you've paid for.

      Here's my thing about Scribus (and other OSS design apps): they're designed for and by programmers. Since designers like me are usually technically knowledgeable but not skilled, we can complain about something, but not fix it ourselves. And then the culture clash between designers and programmers begins. Occasionally, a programmer will be able to design and will generate something like Camino. I'm using it right now because of its shiny excellence. But it's a rare thing. So when I go to the Scribus site and say "These things break the MacOS interface guidelines" and the answer is "That's MacOS's fault", then all mystery as to their nonpopularity evaporates.
