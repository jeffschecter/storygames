\n I couldn't sleep last night without tinkering with Jason's index card scene-framing idea some more. (Jason, you need to come up with a succinct term for this method quick!)

Many apologies to Mr. Morningstar for my shameless ripoffery, but I tried adapting the idea for Do with the following results.

I discovered that scenes in Do are essentially the same as in Jason's original format, but with the addition of a Teacher and Student who intervene in an attempt to resolve a conflict between primary and secondary characters. (Other differences and notes are in this LJ-post.)



The teacher is the pilgrim who would teach for that scene. The student is the pilgrim who would learn something for that scene. The same name is put on the index card to avoid situations where a pilgrim would teach themselves. In the teacher corner, the name has the title of "Sifu," or "Teacher." In the student corner, the name has the title of "Pilgrim."

The two residents are NPCs inspired by the letter. Residents may appear more than once on different cards if players find that character particularly interesting. This leaves open the possibility that the resident would be in conflict with themselves, which might be juicy.

And finally, the conflict is the type of argument the residents have with one another. The conflict-symbols stamped on the letter are suggestions, but you can write down whichever of the seven symbols you wish.

This is an example of three cards already filled out:







And this is how the cards are put together to frame a scene:



The relevant elements are in black. This scene is framed by Sifu Ready Glass teaching a lesson to Pilgrim Poetic Bell. The central conflict of the scene is between the Queen of the Land and The Ravenous Beast! The conflict is one of Sword, meaning that it involves affairs of violence, war, and weaponry. The location of the scene is in The Poor Side of Town.

Rotating the cards produces this:



In this scene, Sifu Poetic Bell teaches a lesson to Pilgrim Burning Fresco by resolving a Conflict of Heart between A Talking Frog and The Ravenous Beast in The Castle.

Anyway, I'm amazed at how naturally this technique produces an emergent, Clue-like syntax for scene-framing.

I'm interested to see how other designers might adapt the concept to suit their projects, or how gamers would use it to suit their particular playstyles. How would you tweak it for your game?

    \n Joshua's blogpost here has some interesting implications for using index cards to frame scenes in Full Light, Full Steam.\n
    Back in the day, dramatists used to write plays that had three \xe2\u20ac\u0153unities:\xe2\u20ac\x9d unity of time, unity of place, and unity of action. That is, the entire play took place within a single day, the stage represented one place throughout the play, and there was only one plotline with no subplots.

    ...

    Much as the classical unities were a reductive idea overzealously imitated, they can be useful tools in fixing this problem. To put it simply, a \xe2\u20ac\u2122scene\xe2\u20ac\u2122 in Full Light, Full Steam should possess at least two of the three unities, and should probably start off with all three.
    \nHe goes into more detail, but in general it seems like a good fit for this method. Have everyone write down a place, a time and an action on the cards.



    Then you can arrange them frame FLFS scenes according to the three unities that Joshua described.



    From this, I'd be tempted to introduce a house rule that I think would make scenes quite epic.

    Act Framing
    Each combination of time+place+action is a distinct Scene. A collection of Scenes is called an Act. In Full Light, Full Steam, the Scenes within an Act all either share the same time and place, time and action, or place and action, but never all three.

    To frame a Scene, place three cards together [as shown in the previous image]. The Scene now has a time, place and action. This is the status quo from which further Scenes in this Act will deviate. If everyone who wants to resolve the Scene has had a chance to do so [by whatever means offered by the system], but have not yet succeeded in doing so, one of these unities must change. Only this unity may change until the Act is complete.

    For example, the Act begins with a Scene featuring Diplomatic Negotiations at Her Majesty's Lunar Base in Dead of Night. The Scene goes unresolved, for whatever reason, and a player decides that Time will be the unity that changes throughout this Act. So, after every unresolved Scene, the location and action remain the same, but the action continues over the course of many time periods. The negotiations have lasted until Lunchtime. Swap the card again, and the negotiations continue until Dawn. A montage, basically.

    Where it gets epic is when Acts have same action and time, but swap out location. You have an opening Scene of Gunplay in the Cockpit of a Zepellin at Dawn. If it goes unresolved, the gunplay continues into Her Majesty's Lunar Base. If still unresolved, the gun battle proceeds into an Opera House. Pretty cool, I think.
