\n Howdy all.
Had an interesting discussion over the last few days while trying to collaborate in making a game.

Now, I'm trying to get a feel for how common these different styles of game are...

The advancement game - The game is about, essentially, getting more points on your sheet while having interesting challenges. I am the farmboy who picks up a sword, fights things to get better at being a swordsman until I kill the man who killed my father - then I go to take on the kingdom's champion - then I take on the uber monster - then I take on the gods - then I take on.. etc etc. Each time I want to find the net bigger challenge, have adventures that make be good enough to beat it, then find the next challenge.

The Iconic Game - This game is essentially about I am a hammer, how do I make the problem into a nail. I am superman I win any problem that is just a physical challenge. There is a problem presented where I cant (for now) make it a purely physical problem (he has kryptonite, there are two issues happening at the same time and I have to choose who dies, etc) I have to make choices that will change my character - (I become heartless so I no longer care that I'm killing innocent bystanders so I can eliminate the person using human shields, or I become cunning enough to get the BBG away from the human shields) but those changes make a new problems for me (as a heartless bastard I am no longer beloved by the people, how to get their love back? or, because I wasn't heartless I let important people die so we need to replace those people, etc).

In Advancement, you want to have ever increasing points/dots on your sheet, you want ever escalating challenges - you want to improve your best skills and always use them. In Iconic you always have around the same points on your sheet, but you move them around, you want to be challenged in where you are weak.

Which type of game do you like? Is there another kind of game as well? What type of stories are these games good for?

    \n In Iconic play games. you are playing something like "House MD" or "CSI", or "Sherlock Holmes" etc. The show is apparently about a doctor or police or investigation, but actually, the show is about their other issues which always change.

    Posted By: DionysusWhich type of game do you like?
    \nAll together, now: "IT DEPENDS."

    Tell me what the premise of the game is, how long we'll be playing it, and what the characters are capable of accomplishing right off the bat, and I can come up with a halfway reasonable notion of whether I'll be happier with or without character advancement.

    Generally, though, my gut reaction is this: if we're going to be playing the game for a long time, and if I am not able to make the character I want to play at the general level of ability that I want to play at right out of the box, then I want Advancement: and fast Advancement at that, not an achingly slow progression that will only get me to where I want to be after a hundred sessions of play. Ideally, I want to play that game for 16-20 sessions and have the character I want by at least the 10th session, if not sooner. If the system offers something cool and interesting at a higher power level (new powers or expanded tactical options or whatever), I think it's good to be able to take advantage of it, and I get annoyed if we never do. If I'm learning the system for the first time, it's nice to start small and work my way up, acquiring new abilities, greater power, more options, etc., as we go. I like having something new to look forward to, and knowing that the way I'm playing this week is not necessarily going to be the way I'm playing four weeks from now.

    If, on the other hand, I can get the character I want to play right away or if the premise of the game is such that there's no such thing as a higher power level, or if the system has no real learning curve, or if we're not going to be playing the game for very long, then Advancement is pretty irrelevant. I won't really miss it if it's not there. \n
    Posted By: DionysusIs there another kind of game as well?
    \nAre there other types of game? Sure, at least conceptually: there's "Degeneration," for one, where the character gets worse at things as the story progresses. In that type of game, resources get used up and never replaced, whether they're some kind of game currency or dice pools or whatever. Victory and defeat both come at a price, and resource management skills are what separates the losers from the winners; the winners know how to ration out their efforts only where they will do the most good, so as not to fritter them away pointlessly. (I'm pretty sure there are games of this type out there, but I can't name any of them. Probably someone else here can, though.)

    I can also imagine a "Transcendent" sort of approach, where the game itself changes as it goes along, making the old character sheet irrelevant: your character no longer interacts with anything he used to, and instead switches over to a different playing field with different rules and different kinds of interaction. Sort of a metagame-y thing, where the player is driving some concept larger than just a character. Maybe the whole set of game mechanics is different at each stage of that kind of game?\n
    Posted By: DionysusWhat type of stories are these games good for?
    \nThe obvious answer would be that your "Iconic" is particularly well-suited to one-shot games or playing very focused, short-term scenarios, because on that brief a timeline "Advancement" simply doesn't make much sense. It might also work pretty well for emulating particular types of media (comic books, TV shows).

    Advancement's great for games where you need to work your way up to doing grander things for whatever reason (you're learning the game, you're going to be playing for a while and want to be able to shake things up, your desired concept demands more capabilities than a starting character can possess, etc.).

    What I don't think is good is when a game promises one thing but only delivers the other. I remember a lot of games whiffing on this level (7th Sea and WoD games in particular), where they'd provide all these awesome high-powered abilities and great high-powered concepts (running a city, mastering a type of magic, being Zorro, etc.) and made it clear that all of these things were attainable by player characters...but then had an XP cost chart for new powers that was ridiculously expensive, and paired that with recommended XP awards that only dreamed of being described as "miserly," thus making it impossible to ever earn those abilities in the course of a typical game. Grrr. So infuriating.

    \n How about Episodic as another category. The object of the game, more than anything, is to explore the next small challenge - to accomplish the next small goal - to resolve the next small situation.

    I think if you take your average D&Dish advancement game and strip away the mechanical treadmill (which is all too often countered by consistent rubberbanding that renders it nearly meaningless anyway) and this is what you have left. Every 1-3 sessions is a new episode. The overarching story is rarely as coherent as the episodic resolutions.

    Frankly, episodic games with an excessive advancement focus generally peter out for me. Because the mechanical growth is only exciting for a little while, and the rubberbanding galls.

    If I were going to play in an advancement focused game, I think I'd want it to be done West Marches style. That sounds like the only way to do it really well.

    Posted By: RobMcDiarmidI think if you take your average D&Dish advancement game and strip away the mechanical treadmill (which is all too often countered by consistent rubberbanding that renders it nearly meaningless anyway)
    \nThis is an interesting side point that's perplexed me for a while: why systems with character advancement rarely give explicit, practical advice about how to make that advancement feel meaningful beyond the usual "now you can face greater challenges" thing. I mean, ten kobolds in a cave are a real problem for a party of first-level characters, and not a problem at all for a party of fifth-level characters...but if you follow the usual advice about presenting characters with challenges appropriate to their power level, a party of fifth-level characters will never find ten kobolds in a cave. That denies everyone at the table the opportunity to see just how far they've come since the start of the game, and kicks the idea that advancement is a kind of reward squarely in the crotch.

    So the last time I ran a D&D game, I made a note to myself when the party got mauled badly and barely survived an encounter, and later pulled that same basic encounter out again when they had some more levels under their belts so they could stomp all over it. Despite the fact that there was no mechanical reward for doing it (the treasure and XP gained were so small as to be entirely irrelevant), everyone at the table spent the next four sessions basking in that moment, bringing it up in-character and out-of-character. It was kind of a shock to me how well it went over, to be honest, and left me thinking that there's some kind of fundamental principle that needs to be re-examined about the nature of advancement and challenges in general, that the fun of barely overcoming a great challenge is actually magnified by being able to return to it later on and finding absolutely no challenge in it whatsoever.
