\n I'm trying to stop using the term actor stance because I've been told by actors it's a bit of a misnomer. Be that as it may, here's my current usage (will update this post later).\n
Identifying stance
When you act as your character [whether you're speaking in first, second and third person, "Helen steps carefully to avoid the trap!"] because you identify with them.
\n
Detatched stance
When you act as if your character is separate from you. [whether you're speaking in first, second and third person, "I step right into the trap!"]
\n
Dissociative stance
100% synonym with "Detatched stance", I had two terms for the same thing for a while before I settled on "detached".
\n
Author stance
Subset of detached stance. When you make decision for your character in order to have interesting consequences.
\n
Pawn stance
Another subset of detached stance, separate from author stance. Your character is your playing piece, you can be rough to it as long as it furthers your goals. One of many examples is having your characer taking short term damage to get a long term benefit.
\nI'll update this post as I learn more. I love the endless-updating here on S-G. Gives this forum a little bit of best-of-both-worlds of forum and wiki.

    \n I think she's just assuming that nobody would declare their character stepping into a trap if they were doing decision-making strictly from an in-character viewpoint, and therefore the second stance has to have some sort of external motivation rather than the character wanting to step into a trap.

    For example, the given game might work in a way where the player actually knows in advance that there is a trap, while the character does not. Therefore the player can declare "my character walks into the trap" as a way of saying that "no, I think it's realistic that my character would fall into this trap here, there's no reason to think that they wouldn't". This is "disassociative thinking" because the player is thinking about an external issue such as realism or dramatic pacing instead of what their character would want.

    In the first example the character has evidently noticed that there is a trap because they're carefully walking around it.

    I don't expect that Sandra meant in either example that the character knows about the trap but decides to step into it anyway - that would be pretty weird, unless the character has mental problems, but if that were the case then obviously they would also do that in actor stance.

    \n My own stance-work in recent years has been more about specific heuristics that should be used with specific types of games, rather than about top-down categorization of stances. There's more to be said about a stance than whether it is an actor or director stance.

    As a bit example of what I mean, I'm pretty happy with my stuff on "advocation play", which is a term I like to use for a specific stance that many forgite narrativistic drama games expect of a character player. I don't even know - or care - which stance category it would be placed in in the traditional stance theory; it's much more important to me that I describe the specific psychology, sociology and play technique of advocation play precisely, so as to be able to instruct players and write game texts appropriately. I find the idea of "flashlight dropping" similarly useful (I have this memory that I picked that one up from some post of yours), and have been using it regularly recently. A third example is "princess play".

    All those examples are deeply stance-related, and it would not be wrong to characterize them as clustered constellations of stances+techniques+agenda. The issue of what a player is thinking while doing decision-making is absolutely central to understanding and using this sort of stuff in game design work, while the classification of these stances in stance theory terms is much less so, it seems to me.

    Perhaps what I'm saying is that I don't see the immediate utility in the Linnean classification of stances. This is somewhat ironic considering how big I am on GNS theory, but that's how it goes - it's apparently not classification for the sake of classification that captures my interest.
