Do you like stories about happy, functional, well-adjusted people who fall in love with the right people, say the right things, and live happily ever after?

If so, this is definitely not the game for you.

It's Complicated is a about personality quirks, secrets both dark and embarrassing-- but most of all, it's about convoluted, messy, compelling, and occasionally one-sided relationships. This game is designed to facilitate play in the style of movies like The Royal Tenenbaums, and television shows like Ugly Betty and Pushing Daisies.

And you can own it for $13.00! (With $5 shipping.) I'll have them a week or two before GenCon. You can preorder here!

It's probably going to be around 50-60 pages, 4.25" x 7", with color interiors. Art by Shreyas Sampat, layout by Nathan Paoletta, and game by me! It's a rules-light romp of relationship-map twister-- there are a couple threads on The Forge from various stages of development, and I'll be happy to answer any questions about the game.

I'm really excited. I hope you are too!

    \n It hasn't changed too much, but much has been added to the text, and little things that smooth out the play experience have been added.

    You still only have two traits-- Oddities and Dysfunctions. Oddities are the deep-seated secrets, skeletons in the closet, and hidden abilities or traumas that the characters keep from the world. (Like in Pushing Daisies, for example, the Piemaker's ability to resurrect people-- that's an Oddity.) These are the kinds of things someone would usually keep to themselves, unless they feel particularly close to or trusting of someone else; or else, the sort of thing that accidentally slips out at inopportune moments. Dysfunctions are social issues-- things which create problems or friction within interpersonal interactions-- which spring out of your Oddities. As a result, Dysfunctions are personality quirks which act as an outward expression of an inward issue. (So for the Piemaker, his Dysfunction would be weird intimacy issues.) It's best expressed as "Due to [Oddity], I [Dysfunction]."

    There are spaces for each of these traits on a gameboard, and you draw lines between your Oddities and Dysfunctions to show the relationship between them. When your line crosses someone else's line, you demonstrate how your character feels about the other one-- but that person can't reciprocate until they draw their next line. It leads to a lot of pent-up tension, which is a lot of fun, and a lot of rapidly changing relationships in endgame.

    Changes from the original text include: everyone now lays their first Dysfunction before the first scene begins, in order to give everyone a base to work from; play does not begin until there is a Crossing or Touching relationship; that kind of thing. The game text itself has a lot more examples of how Oddities and Dysfunctions interact, tips on what to do if it's your scene (and what to do if it's not your scene), advice for framing scenes with multiple relationships, and that kind of thing. In my experience, veteran Story Gamers never seemed to have that much of a problem with those sorts of things, but from what I heard in outside playtests, advice like that was necessary for a lot of players not used to something this loosely structured.

    If anyone wants to try their hand at it, I'll be running a couple sessions of this game at DexCon! Or I can try to get some time to get an online one-shot going on IRC.

    \n Good questions, Brian.

    IC is GMless, and plays best with 3-6 players, although 2-player playtests have gone well; the participants just played two characters each. The text also includes ideas for how to pace a smaller playgroup more tightly; I'm sure those lessons could be applied in a two-player situation to make the game go more smoothly without the use of multiple characters. Over six, things get pretty convoluted pretty quickly; the narrative arc is much shorter. But that can be good for one-shots! I've played it with eight people, but personally, I like it best with 4-5 people.

    The game is turn-based for spotlight, and the scenes are pretty front-loaded mechanically. There are at least two things the spotlight character is trying to do in each scene: reveal (to the audience or the other characters) an Oddity and/or Dysfunction, and reveal how they feel about another character. The other characters in a scene push to set up those revelations. Scenes tend to be short and punchy, and no one's out of the action for very long. Plus, since there's a twist in every scene, they're pretty entertaining to watch.

    Things start out pretty broad, but by the time you get to endgame, it's pretty thick with intrigue and dramatic twists. If you want more information, you can look at previous It's Complicated posts in my blog. There are some AP reports, as well as transcripts of online playtests. ("The Mushroom Kingdom is a Constitutional Monarchy" is my favorite.)

    Paul-- that's right. When it's your turn, we use Paul-based resolution mechanics, ie, what you say goes.     \n Feedback of visual design? My favorite threads!

    * I'm not the biggest fan of the font with that kind of artwork. The font feels "techie" to me, while the art is more whimsical than anything else. As I am sure you've done this in a layered tool, just try scrolling through other fonts for those layers until something "clicks".

    * That shade of green lettering on white (title) isn't popping very well. Maybe consider a black outline... or a totally different highlight color--maybe the dark green of the brunette's shirt and guy's pants? Obviously, the background of your subtitle band should match whatever color you pull from the art for the title text.

    * I'd narrow the subtitle band a bit, no matter what color it ends up being. It dominates the composition, drawing the eye more than a subtitle should; almost dragging the eye past the art with its neon-ness (exacerbated by the low contrast of the title, with that color, which makes the title almost disappear at a glance).

    Otherwise a pretty solid composition. I am surprised at the artwork--Oddity and Dysfunction struck me more as something wanting an artsy, post-modern look; maybe a pastiche of suggestive torn photos, portions of notes and personal ad clippings, a coffee stain and some spilled wine (or is it BLOOD?!?). But I suppose if your play examples keep the somewhat light tone, it will all be of a piece. If you're examples of play are dark and fraught with emotion and pain, the cover is going to jar with the average reader--it could even, theoretically, qualify as misrepresentation of the purpose of the game and "standard" play. This cover says "Friends" or "Football Wives," not "The Big Chill" or "Ordinary People" (sorry for the old-ass examples--I don't watch much "drama" these days).

    Hope this helps;
    David
