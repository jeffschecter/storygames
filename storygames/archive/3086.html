\n Got a crazy idea for combining gaming and non-profit activities?

During the planning of Go Play Northwest, we discovered we'd need to create a non-profit organization for the purposes of the convention (this was because our venue caters to non-profits). During the discussion someone (I think it was Mike Sugarbaker) suggested that non-profit status might have some additional possibilities beyond hosint a convention.

So let's say you had a non-profit charter and your mandate was to support, educate, and share knowledge around gaming. What activities might you do? How might you go about raising funds? If you had funds, where might you disseminate them?

And on a related topic, has anyone considered setting up a game company as a non-proft? Can you think of any reason you'd do this?

    \n The Live Role Playing Society of Edmonton (LRPS) has been a functioning, registered, non profit society for...20 years? The exact number escapes me, but it's something like that, anyway.

    NPS status gets you into locations you might not otherwise be able to get (we've LARPed in empty Red Cross buildings, City Hall, various gov't locations, and so forth). Sometimes a reduced rate, sometimes being an NPS is the only thing that gets you in the door.

    Depending on the nature of what you're doing, and your local laws, it can help you get a cut rate on liability insurance.

    I'm not sure that it gets a game company much of prime use, but it can be very helpful for a gaming organization.

    The trade-off is formalization, and a certain degree of small-group politics.

    James

    Posted By: tony dowlerAnd on a related topic, has anyone considered setting up a game company as a non-proft? Can you think of any reason you'd do this?
    \nJohn Tynes has said that Pagan Publishing should have been a non-profit:\n
    An RPG.net history of Pagan PublishingThird, and perhaps most importantly, Pagan Publishing had the huge advantage of never actually being a professional publishing house, despite their attempt in 1994. This isn't reflective of the quality of their publications, which were generally top-notch. Instead it reflects the fact that Pagan Publishing didn't have to worry about paying the bills. They didn't have staff, nor warehouses, nor offices. If they wanted they could take a whole year working on a single book, and they often did; if a full-time publishing house had tried the same, they'd quickly be out of business. Years later Tynes would say that he wished he'd set Pagan Publishing up as a non-profit, and that really encapsulates the feelings that the Pagan authors had about making top-quality books--not a living. Any largely volunteer organization, given the appropriate skills and talents (which Pagan had in spades), can easily produce better products than any second- or third-tier RPG company, as a simple matter of economics, and that's the superior position that Pagan Publishing was working from.
    \n
    And I think that gives some good ideas why. It's definitely worth considering doing, if you are making games for the love of it and not the profit potential, which is pretty small.

    \n I'm working on setting up a non-profit educational foundation. It's not a horrifically bad process, but it does take a little while, and a good chunk of money.

    (Side note: We REALLY need an accountant who can help us set this up, if anyone knows someone willing. The "project your income for the next several years" part... hoo boy.)

    As for activities I might do if I had a gaming-promotion non-profit group...
    * Pay people to write games for inner-city kids, or have a "game drive" for said purpose.
    * Create a set of Anti-Chick-Tracts. Not as much against Chick, which I admit is a worthy purpose, but explaining that certain things aren't evil, which is perhaps a loftier goal.
    * Many gamers are college kids. Hold a "used book drive" where they donate their old textbooks. You then either sell them to schools to get money for your group, or give them to worthy causes. The hook: hold the drive at gaming stores near colleges, and get them to give discounts based on number of books provided.
    * Create a series of "games as job manuals" that companies (esp. other non-profits) might be interested in buying for new employees. This both promotes gaming and gets you money.
    * Print various publications related to gaming, such as the "principles of successful RPG design," or collections of essays. Make them available through Lulu and Amazon, and offer them cheaper to upcoming game designers or LARP coordinators.
    * Create a "gamer networking" site similar to Facebook or LinkedIn, but with specific tools that gamers might want. Emphasis on either making contacts who will help you create your games, or finding people to game with.
    * Contact gaming companies. Ask them to give money for a charity (not you). Publicize this intensely (using volunteer effort) in non-gaming circles, to get gaming into the public eye in a positive way.
