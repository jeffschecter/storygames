    Re: MMOs that offer collaborative story development - Unfortunately no, not at this time. You can do this yourself within the context of the game, but it's essentially a form of LARP.

    \n

    I have ideas that move in that direction that are far, far more ephemeral than my hopes of publishing one of my tabletop RPGs some day soon.

    \n

    Re: Text-based interaction - Take your pick. AOL Instant Messenger, Yahoo Instant Messenger, IRC (in its sundry forms) ICQ, even Google Talk are all good for this. I've found AIM and IRC to be the best for chatroom roleplay. There are also virtual tabletops that allow some sort of graphical interaction alongside the text-based chat. There are also a few decent voice-chat programs that are good for RP as well. E-mail and message boards are used frequently for "play-by-post" games where trying to schedule a time to get everyone together is more hassle than you have time for.

    \n I roleplay quite a lot on City of Heroes on the Union server, which has a fairly large number of roleplayers on it. (In the US, the RP server is Virtue, I think)
    \n
    \nReasons being:
    \n
    \nCharacter creation is extremely flexible, meaning that most heroes (and villains) look distinct from each other and you can represent a broad range of in-fiction backgrounds (mystic wolfman, time-travelling cyborg, guy who can shoot good, time-traveling cybernetic wolfman who can shoot good, etc).
    \n
    \nEach Supergroup (a player created guild) can have a base, and it is also extremely flexible. In a large Roleplaying Supergroup you can expect to find a massive complex with a bar and other recreational areas, perhaps personal living space, a tech lab, an arcane library, etc, etc. It takes some expertise and trickery, but you can even make things like decent looking high-rise flats, swimming pools, hanger bays, gardens, and so on.
    \n
    \nLastly, players can now create their own missions and story arcs, using the Mission Architect. Everything from the dialogue, what the baddies look like, where the mission takes place, what the mission objectives are, etc is now in player's hands. This is fantastic.
    \n
    \n
    \nOf course, there are still limitations, but we find using a forum is good to arrange these in-game events, and we have an out-of-character chat channel, etc.
    \n
    \nFor example (excuse my gushing):
    \nIn an ongoing arc with my supergroup, the demonic power that fuels our leader's abilities became dominant (for an in-fiction reason), so after trashing the base (editing it slightly) and some roleplay, he went AWOL and someone else assumed leadership. All in roleplay: The new leader organised a meeting, and we decided our plan of action to track him down before he A) does any harm to himself harms our supergroups reputation. On the forums, we narrated what our characters were doing to track him down. Now, the ex-leader's player is using the Mission Architect to create a suitably dramatic story arc where we will bust in, disrupt whatever evil schemes he's been up to (which have been hinted at from interaction on the forums), and then fight an NPC copy of him (with identical appearance and powers, custom dialogue, etc). While his main character is unavailable for casual roleplay, the ex-leader's player is simply playing as another character.
    \n
    \nOut-of-character-ly, I have arranged to introduce a new character I want to play by also putting him in that story arc as a boss type character. He's an indignant demon that has been bound in a magical cloak. I don't know anything else about the mission yet, but I've supplied the dialogue and appearance too. Excellent stuff for me: I get a nice dramatic intro for my character.
    \n
    \nWhile all this is going on, there's lots of individual smaller character-developing sub-plots going on (not usually planned, just naturally developing from the free roleplay).
    \n
    \nIt's different from tabletop, and you have to hunt it down, but you can definitely roleplay in a satisfying way in an MMO, or at least, this one.

    Richard (Lacy): That's kinda what I was talking about when I said LARP. You can manipulate the environment in a way that is conducive to RP, but it isn't the same as the game offering collaborative story. You can do it yourself, and some games give you some sort of tools to do it (UO was one of the better ones early on, and SWG was doing a decent job of it before the NGE) but the games themselves don't really encourage it.

    \n

    What is needed, I think, for an MMO to really encourage collaborative story in the TTRPG sense is to allow the players to affect the actual world. I don't mean creating missions/quests* and building houses, but to take the setting properties down off the pedestal, and put them within the players' reach. Let a villain plot to blow up a building actually demolish that building if it succeeds. If the heroes save someone, let the NPCs talk about it... even when that hero ISN'T passing by. I want to be able to keep hearing about some hero that's been doing good work in an area, then when I meet them, it's like "Dude, I know you! All the people on Talos Island are talking about how you took down the Tsoo!"

    \n

    But that's difficult to do with thousands of players on a server. The mission building in CoX is a step in the right direction, for certain. But it's a small step. EVE is doing other things right, like the detailed "corporation" system, and the incredibly strong player-based economy. Baby steps. The main problem is that majority of people don't want to roleplay in their MMOs, even tabletop roleplayers. They just want to play a video game in a massively multiplayer environment. While that's true, there won't be a big push toward expanding features that facilitate RP.

    \n

    *For the record, Saga of Ryzom, a semi-obscure MMO of French origins, did it years before CoX. I can't say anything about how well they did it, as I barely poked around with it, but they did it first.

    \n Often, when people ask for "real roleplaying", what they want is to have higher stakes of ownership and responsibility in the game. But this is difficult for MMO games, as they have to cater to a wide base of paying customers. Allowing "real" consequences to actions (in the sense that they'd impact anything but your own play) means that other players could influence your play. In my experience most online gamers are pretty wussy about this sort of thing, they don't like it when somebody else comes along and "ruins their game". It's pretty impossible to allow real interaction and real consequences while simultaneously protecting everybody's investment into the game - it'd require a player population and a gaming ethos that is not present in current MMO environment. As it is, almost anything persistent you'd allow a player to do in the game could potentially trample on what somebody else perceives as their fun. Burn a building, and there's somebody else out there who is now lacking a building. In a game that has no point aside from character development these set-backs are not "play" - they're setbacks, just like if your computer crashed. I'm not surprised that MMO designers explicitly limit player interaction with setting and each other to mutually agreed-upon forms.

    Tabletop gamers rarely think about it, but they're actually pretty well off - there's no problem at all with having your character assassinate the king and instigate a civil war on tabletop; your freedom is absolute, the spotlight is always on you, nobody is going to complain that you ruined the game for the thousands of NPCs affected by your choices. MMO gamers are the tenant farmers of an imaginary world in comparison, with limited and carefully enumerated privileges. It's no wonder if that leaves something to be desired for tabletop adventurers.

    I've played in some AWESOME games using virtual tabletops: My Life with Master, Zorcerer of Zo and Risus in Fantasy Grounds, Primetime Adventures and Grey Ranks in Vassal, In a Wicked Age using MapTool.

    EpicTable

    Just over a year ago, I noticed a post over on Four Ugly Monsters by a guy (John Lammers) who was producing a VT. He asked what people wanted out of a VT, and most answered along the lines of "we don't need another VT, so don't bother."

There is a running log of chat and actions for actual play posts.

    John is working with several designers to build support for many well loved indie/story/narrative/fiction games into the initial release. I suggest checking out his websitefor updates.

    Basically you have the wrong impression here.

    \n

    The 'get to higher levels' thing is actually just a barrier-to-entry for the endgame, which is a series of instanced dungeons calling for groups of varying sizes and including challenging battles with various levels of complexity. The emphasis here is on interface mastery, system mastery, and successful teamwork. To succeed in advanced instances and raids (AFAICT a 'raid' is just an instance calling for an extra-large group and a larger block of time), you need a group that's carefully system-optimized and knows how to work well together.

    \n

    You're right, roleplaying isn't really a big deal if you're on the instance track. That part of the game really emphasizes the 'character as pawn' attitude.

    \n

    There're some sandbox elements like crafting, the auction house economy, and the occasional opportunities to get on on side or another of a running faction war, but it's not really systemically possible for software-supported roleplay that incorporates the NPCs to exist. I don't even know how you'd begin to handle the storage burden of that, let alone the problem of designing those interactions.

    \n

    Have you looked at the RP servers, though? I understand that the play culture differs there.

    \n I looked for something like this in Second Life on a drunk weekend.

    I'd say LARP would be a better comparison than tabletop - There's this definite shift in social/game dynamics when you're forced to make this conscious decision of who to interact with at any given time. Which, combined with the fact that admins are in the position to desire quantity of players as opposed to players they know well typically leads to cliquey play.*

    But there's definitely games with collaborative storytelling - Its just not particularly well interfaced, and can be drowned out in a sea of people who want to run around with capes and fangs.

    Also, there seemed to be this odd divide between 'mature' games and 'kiddie' games, with the 'kiddie' games defined by much more involved combat minigames that drowned out most of the social interaction, and the 'mature' games defined by anime maids with huge male apendages.

    * (I think a cliquey atmosphere can work for some games particularly well, especially "there's a bunch of factions and they fight and they're kind of petty and highschool".)
