\n So there was this thread, but it doesn't seem to apply as Autism isn't a mental illness (right? the DSMIV-savvy can correct me).

I work for an agency that, broadly, supports adults with disabilities. I've started working with this 23 year-old woman who has Autism. She's typical-functioning mentally, and lives on her own. My job is basically to help her get out in the community and do what she wants and also to assist her in cleaning her apartment so she can keep living independently.

She's super geeky, walls of manga & anime & gaming books (in fact she was at Kumoricon this weekend and modeled her Edward Elric "crossplay" costume for me--pretty cool). We started talking about D&D today, and she pulled out her 3.5 books and began describing her campaign world at length, and bemoaning her lack of a group. I pointed her to some websites and D&D gaming days, but she would have none of it because "at those gamedays all they want to play is 4th Edition." I asked her what she didn't like about 4E, and she deadpanned, "I heard something horrible. If you hit a skeleton in 4th edition you can get a critical hit." I'm pretty sure she hasn't played any version of D&D, but to her the idea of this was anathema (she also said she doesn't want to have to buy a bunch more books, which is a good reason).

Interacting with her has been fascinating. After the first day I worked with her I left thinking, "she's so much like the worst of the gamer stereotypes--not socially adept at all, obsessive to the point of not being able to have a normal conversation, kinda smelly--damn, maybe all those terrible gamer stereotypes I've met are just autistic!"

I feel for her, because it's not her fault she's autistic, but her autism pretty much means it'll be incredibly difficult to find a gaming group, and I see her pining away over those books and creating things in her mind that she'll likely never get to play out with other people. It's like when you're not gaming regularly and just noodle over your books, but turned up to the max.

TL;DR--I work with an autistic woman who wants to get her D&D on but is finding it difficult to find a group because of her disability. So: Is anyone here on the autistic spectrum? Are you gaming? How is it going? Is anyone gaming with someone who has autism? What can I do to help her out? I've thought of suggesting we game during my shifts with her*, but I think it'll take some more time before I win her trust, plus she's really locked into the idea that gaming is a DM + 4 or more people.


*and, well, I don't really want to game with her. It might be fun for an hour or so at work, but really? I'd never invite her over to my house to game, simply because hanging out with her isn't fun.

    \n There are lots of threads about this on RPG.Net. Common theme seemed to be that while the obsessive focus and social awkwardness associated with autism don't interfere with gaming much, but the "mindblindness" about social cues and spotlight time are really problematic. It's kind of a commonplace in the neurodiversity community that some people do a lot better with online communications than FTF, anyway, so getting into some discussion forums is a great idea.

    Besides, as Penny Arcade pointed out, you're not really a D&D fan until you've got an edition to cling to and a new edition to hate on, so that's completely normal.

    \n I've played with a few people with Asperger's of varying severity, mostly in con games. The first time was really confusing, because no one knew why things got so awkward. The second time the same player gave us a heads up -- "Hey, I've got Asperger's, which means..." -- and that made a huge difference.

    Most of the issues were pretty easily overcome. The one that remains daunting to me, personally, is actually telling a person with Asperger's flat out that they're dwelling on something or missing some social cue, simply because I feel rude when I do it, even if they don't experience it that way. That was part off that first player's heads up: "If I'm being annoying, tell me 'cause otherwise I won't know".

    \n Just a note from somebody who works in mental health: autism, aspberger's, and autism spectrum cover a heckuva lot of territory, from people who can't speak or dress themselves to the person mentioned to people like Temple Grandin who are internationally acclaimed authors. So be cautious with generalizations! Also, I wonder if she'd be interested in anything along the lines of figurines-hexmaps-boardgamish RPG's-Warhammer-etc... Some concrete markers might be helpful, and the really structured interactions in a wargame might be easier for her to handle initially than a free-flowing RPG.

    Also, I suspect that "getting out and acquiring skills while playing a game you like" is probably more workable than "learning skills from reading or playing a game."

    \nI'd say no.
    Some gamers are somewhere on the autism spectrum, and may present elements of those stereotypes.
    But other gamers may have unrelated personality traits, and present the same elements.

    I noticed when my friend was diagnosed with depression, and started seeing the traits of other people as signs they had depression.
    Another friend had a sleep disorder (the same one as me), and as she learned more about it, started diagnosing people she knew as having the same sleep disorder.

    People's personalities are very complex, and when you examine people through a particular filter, you can see lots of things that suggest a particular psychological or personality disorder, but you're probably being misled by your own expertise and narrow focus. I remember a science documentary where a group of psychologists and therapists were given the opportunity to watch a group of people, each of which had one of 6 psychological disorders, and were asked to identify which was which over the course of a week. Their success rate was not high - and that was no surprise to them.

    \nI agree with this assessment.

    I'm pretty confident that I'm not anywhere on the autism spectrum. I'm reasonably alert to social cues, have no trouble with conversational back-and-forth, and am generally quite comfortable meeting new people and coming off as socially normal, not "slightly off" in that way even high-end-of-the-spectrum autistics generally seem.

    Here's the "but"\u2014and I had an experience today, spending time with non-geek distant relatives, that reinforced this view\u2014I am never going to be able to function at peak social capacity around non-geeks. There's simply too much of my personality and my identity wrapped up in geekdom of various sorts\u2014and not just gaming\u2014for me to ever be able to completely be myself.

    Being a geek of some stripe is its own thing. It's almost more like an ethnic group or even a religion or maybe even a sexual orientation or gender identity, than it is like a disorder. There may be nothing "wrong" with a person psychologically, and they still have a certain level of need to be around fellow geeks. I know I'm not alone in this.
