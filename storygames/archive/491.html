So, over in Making the first session rock hard I wrote up a series of questions for making a rockin' bang:

  • What was your character trying to achieve before the story started?
  • Why do they, as a person, uniquely need to achieve this?
  • How did it go wrong?

I am planning to have a more specific version of these questions in Misery Bubblegum, and I want peoples opinions. The questions go like this:

  • (1) What was your character trying to achieve before the story started?
  • (2) Why do they, as a person, uniquely need to achieve this?
  • (3) Who prevents you from achieving this?
  • (4) What trait(s) of that person act against your goal every time they appear?

So, the ridiculous version:

  • (1) Eat the Road Runner
  • (2) I'm a very hungry coyote
  • (3) The Road Runner
  • (4) Very fast, incredibly lucky

For the less ridiculous version:

  • (1) Be an uncaring nihilist
  • (2) I've been hurt, and I refuse to be hurt again
  • (3) Veronica
  • (4) Sweet, sincere, cares about me

Then you get into the question of "Who is really stopping you?" ... do you give the "truth" or do you say what the character believes? I'm inclined toward the latter. This sequence ...

  • (1) Have Gerald fall madly in love with me
  • (2) He's the social king of the school, I'm the social queen, I need this for my status
  • (3) That mousy little girl, Jessica who keeps getting Gerald's attention
  • (4) Sincere, friendly, innocent

... Seems much better to me than this one ...

  • (1) Have Gerald fall madly in love with me
  • (2) He's the social king of the school, I'm the social queen, I need this for my status
  • (3) Gerald
  • (4) Cold, distant, views me like a little sister

... though both work. It's just that the second one is much closer to engaging with the real issues, rather than frantically trying to shame and attack Jessica as a sort of proxy-war for Gerald's attentions.

Right. Now I'm rambling. I could use some thoughts. Thanks in advance!

    See, I'm not convinced that I want players thinking about "the truth."

    Like, if JD wants to be a nihilist, and Veronica somehow moves him to emotion, and that's a big problem for him ... I'm pretty sure I don't want the players thinking too far beyond that. Because, really, the story of "I'm going to use Veronica as a prop to explore my own purely internal issues" is ... it's not as good a story as "Veronica makes me feel these bad things, so I'm going to do my level best to corrupt her into my own death-embracing life-path, because ... because ... I have to!"

    Also, as with other Misery Bubblegum setup issues, this character creation step is not a once-and-forever thing. You redo this at the beginning of each session ... maybe the answer this week is the same as last week (Buffy is still the reason why Faith can't just be an irresponsible punk slayerette) or maybe the answer this week is different from the answer last week.

    So, yeah, it's sort of flags about "What underlying issue do we want to explore?" but it's also flags about "What particular context will we use to explore it this session?"

    But people have a solid point about how multiple people are going to stand in your way in different ways. Is this something to explore one session at a time (this week the enemy is Jessica, next week the enemy is Gerald) or should there be multiple answers active in the same moment?
