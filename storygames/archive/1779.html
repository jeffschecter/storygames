This past weekend, I ran four games of Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium (Unrated Edition) at MACE. Three on Saturday, and one on Sunday morning (after which, everyone packed up and bolted, so I decided not to stick around for my 2pm slot, seeing as how no one had signed up for it).

The games went well, and the players were mostly of strangers who had heard about the game, but didn't know much except that it was modern horror.

I first explained the nature of the game setting and mechanics, and then walked them through character creation. The process took about thirty to forty-five minutes. We then jumped right into gameplay.

The game played well, which was fun, because I hadn't run Dread with anyone outside my gaming group since it the first edition debuted at Trinoc*coN 2003. Playing with people you don't know is always kind of a gamble, but I definitely enjoyed the sessions, and everyone else seemed to get a kick out of it as well.

However, there were some issues.

1. Breaking The Law

I found it interesting that despite the core concept of the game (the characters are people with nothing left to lose, essentially suicide agents on a one-way mission to seek and destroy as many demons as possible before dying a horrible death), the players were often reluctant to, say, break down doors or punch strangers. It's interesting to me, and I think that it might be setting-related. People who wouldn't hestitate to burn down and orc fortress or take a swing at an ogre may still hesitate to do something "illegal" in a game set in the "real world". I guess. I don't know.

It really took some coaxing to get people to just cut loose and flip out. During a car chase, when Andy K's character jumped out of his car and landed on the hood of the mafia dude's SUV and opened fire, shooting the guy through his windshield and killing him, the other players were startled. It was like someone had flipped a switch. Up until then, I'd been saying, don't hesitate, do what you must, you're badasses, go for it, you're not going to get arrested, and if you do, you can disappear or hypnotize the cop or whatever. But it wasn't until someone in the group got loco that everyone else realized that it was "safe" or whatever. I'm thinking that I might need to find a way to explain this more clearly in the rules somehow.

2. Fly Into The Danger Zone

The players had access to Fury, which is a dice pool that the player can call on to perform crazy stunts like the Cock Punch or the Suicide Run. However, even though these stunts were listed and described on the character sheet, the players appeared wary (for lack of a better word) of using these stunts. Again, it took some coaxing to get them to kill points of Fury and do unreasonably dangerous things.

Of course, I know that the pressure of playing with strangers at a con means that people are just a little less likely to cut loose the way that they would in front of their friends, so I'm factoring that into the equation as well. And don't get me wrong, I'm not trying to say that the players were a disappointment to me because they didn't jump through my hoops and dance for me like well-trained monkeys. I just wanted to convey to them that they were free to rampage through the city like monsters themselves, but I was having a hard time finding ways to say this without just giving orders ("Hey, why not just break that door down with your shoulder?")

I do feel like I should point out that I liked the groups, and everyone was really into the role-playing, and the games went well. So I guess I'm saying that I feel like I either didn't explain something properly, or maybe it's just not conveyed sufficiently in the rules and whatnot.

Given that my system is more or less final, having been playtested during the four years since the first edition's release, I'm more concerned about the game's text. As a user manual, it's got to convey everything that the player needs to know about the game experience. Then again, are directives like "don't forget to blow shit up and do stupid crazy neckbreaker stunts" really appropriate? At that point, it's almost like I'm saying, "If you don't play the way my gaming group plays, you're not having the right kind of fun."

Then again, given the way that many horror RPGs involve the use of characters who are considerably more fragile than the opposition, it may be that the players have been conditioned into a kind of timidity. Perhaps the answer lies in the way that I describe the game. Calling Dread a horror-action game may lead people to envision games like Cthulhu, in which the players are generally outgunned by the monsters and demons. Maybe I should call Dread a supers game?

Hell, I don't know. I just want to make sure that the players don't spend all their time stalking about with flashlights, peering nervously into shadows.

Overall, I'd say that this didn't happen too much in my con games this weekend, but there was enough trepidation that I wondered if maybe there was something amiss in my approach to the game experience.

Of course, any feedback from attendees would be greatly appreciated.

-- Rafael

    Hi, Mark,

    Thanks for the feedback!

    I guess I should present some context for those not familiar with the game. Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium is a horror-action game in which players are Disciples, people with nothing left to lose. Heroin addicts, ex-convicts, alcoholics, the falsely accused, the despondent, shell-shocked soldiers... basically, people who had hit rock bottom before getting recruited into the war between heaven and hell. The Disciples hunt demons, which amounts to a suicide mission (which is fine, because they're obviously expendable -- at least, according to those who have recruited and trained them).

    Each character has a one-word Drive which summarizes his or her primary motivation for going on in this new life. For example, a drive of Vengeance might mean that the character seeks revenge against the demons that slaughtered his family, or a drive of Protection might point to the character's drive to shelter innocent people from the horrors in the darkness, etc. During gameplay, a player can invoke Drive to gain a bonus to any actions (by describing the way that the player feels about the situation).

    The Death Spiral is my name for a diagram that I use to plot out the events in a given scenario. It consists of several locations, grouped in a circular grid. Each location is accompanied by a description of any evidence, clues, or other information at that place, along with notes regarding any adversaries that the group might encounter along the way. Since each game of Dread begins with an adventure hook that can lead to several different locations, it's possible for two groups to have wildly diverging experiences while investigating the same case. I call it the Death Spiral because the adventure hooks are located at the edge of the circle, but the demon's in the middle, and if you trace the players' route from location to location, you often get this wacky spiral that leads to the center.

    So --
    Posted By: Mark Causey If you tell us that we're killing demons because that's all we have left to do in life and that we're not doing it for some warped sense of responsibility (which maybe I inferred instead of understood), maybe the idea will come across to raise Hell when fighting Hell.
    I really like "raise Hell when fighting Hell". That's a great line, and I might just swipe it. But yeah, the notion of responsibility is an interesting one. It's definitely implied in the rules (directly communicated, in fact), in the sense that the players can't just murder indiscriminately. But I guess I'm thinking more about the reluctance to just break into a dead guy's apartment to ransack the place for clues. I am accustomed to a gaming group that regards the law as a minor inconvenience at worst, so perhaps I need to just include a section on acceptable behavior for the group.
    Posted By: Mark CauseyWill non-con play involve your Death Spiral hitting on the Drives of the characters?
    It's not covered explicitly in the rules. Drive is only one word, because a concept like Justice is easier to work into gameplay than "I want justice for the downtrodden homeless that sleep on the sewer grates in West Baltimore". And so, the way it's worked in the past, it's just up to the player to find a reason to invoke it. Since it's such a broad concept, it shouldn't be too hard to come up with a way to justify its invocation. Of course, in a one-shot con game, with so much going on, I wasn't surprised to see that it wasn't used very much, but in my experience, players find ways to work it into high-pressure situations towards the end of a session (as the stakes increase). So no, I hadn't really planned on making Drive a part of the Death Spiral.

    For example, with a Drive of Atonement (let's say the player was an alcoholic police detective who botched a case and let a serial killer go free because he screwed up the evidence somehow, and the killer promptly went out and murdered someone else, and the ex-detective feels that he must atone for his failure because it's his fault), the player alone should really be able to decide what particular action would help him atone for his past misdeeds. If I were to include a note saying, player should feel atonement when this or that happens, it might not work, because often, large portions of the Death Spiral remain unexplored. It's not necessary for the group to visit every location, or get into every potential brawl with bad guys prior to taking down the demon. So my planned Drive invocation might never take place, because the team never did what I expected them to, or the player didn't think to invoke Drive when I hoped he would. Instead, I let the player decide what works, and just roll with it.
    Posted By: Mark CauseyAs for the Fury refreshes, I can see why you don't want to announce them early on as they might be circumvented later and all that, but DO ANNOUNCE when we've reached a situation with one of those as the possible conclusion.
    Now, that I kind of like.

    Ah -- Fury. So, along the way, this dice pool, Fury, can be refreshed by completing certain secondary objectives. The primary objective would be to kill the demon. Every game of Dread ends with a demon blown to smithereens. But, along the way, the characters get mixed up in the lives of the people affected by the demon. They might have an opportunity to help those people. For example, if the demon is a defiler, then it's a possessing demon that's taken over a victim's body. A secondary objective in this case might be to save the life of the possessed person. If the players manage to exorcise the demon prior to killing it, then everyone would get back a little bit of Fury. If not, and if the group has to terminate the demon by killing the victim, then there's no Fury bonus.

    I hadn't thought to tell the players about such moral choices, but perhaps it would help to make that an option. Some groups might prefer not to be told, as it robs a scenario of moral gravity somewhat (essentially, reducing a decision to maximizing a gameplay mechanic, as opposed to a gameplay mechanic that rewards certain kinds of decisions). But I definitely need to think about that some more.

    Wow, thanks again for the great feedback!

    Also, when I have more time I too will chime in with stuff.

    But the quick:

    1) Yeah, I think you need to spell it out "Raise Hell to Fight Hell"

    2) I think emphasizing that the characters often work for the greater good, and start to develop their own sense of law, which usually results in ignoring law-laws. This is actually probably my favorite part of the Dread experience. Breaking into houses, shooting mafia dudes or skinheads without fear of repurcussions (well, some fear, but also the badassitude knowing that I could literally get away with murder through my own spell or strength resources), and yet coming back to knowing that it's all for the greater good, and likewise avoid doing evil things, driving a car through a crowd of kids, etc. Every PC in Dread would be "Chaotic Good" if it was D&D.
    There's the badassitude, but it also eventually leads to a sort of Thoreauian questioning of societal laws in play. It's awesome, and it's hard to get there if you come from a background of CoC or other horror games like, "If I break into the clinic the GM will have me running from cops for the rest of the session".

    3) Maybe a word about how plans are cool and all, but to not worry too much about repurcussions until they happen (or if it's something BIG). And as the GM, to not force the PC to be held accountable for every B&E. Ex: If a PC gets caught raiding the files of a police station after dark, and gets chased by the cops... let them get away (eventually?), and simply "close down" the police station as a place to get more info for that adventure (guards on alert, etc) so that they have to find another way. Don't have the police chase them all over town, put up wanted posters, actively hunt them down, etc. This may sound natural, but in most "break the laaaaaaws!" games the GM is encouraged to take the players to task every time they step outside of the law.

    4) Same as above, but with "making plans" instead of "laws". Laying plans for an op is cool and cathartic, as well as good exercise for the brain ("Oh, but what if there are guards there? We better take the sewers"). But maybe it needs some advice for groups so that they don't hit a Loop of Fear, afraid to act rashly in case they are caught off-guard. "This ain't your motherfucking Twilight 2000, bitch. Grab that shotgun and charge the gates if you want to!"

    5) I love the fury refresh incidents as-is. They are creative landmarks in the adventure, and by keeping them hidden (that is, not saying "Hey, if you rescue that guy you all get Fury back") keeps the players more alert and aware of the environment and scenery. I can't think of a reason why they need to be revealed as they appear. I think it would be cooler at the end of the session, after the epilogue, to almost imagine the "LEVEL CLEARED" screen, where we see how the players got their scores. Then the GM comes out and reveals the 1-2 Fury Marks that they missed. That way they find out anyway, and yet you're not slapping their asses in the adventure, riding them to go out of their way to grab at your +2 Fury Donuts as they appear. If anything, make "revealing the Fury Marks as they appear" some kind of optional rule.

    Posted By: Mark CauseyAlso, what was that last minute thing about blowing Fury at the beginning for the stuff down at the bottom of the sheet?
    Yeah, the Longshot is something that's done at the beginning of every session during campaign play. For a one-shot, like our game on Saturday, it's not really applicable (which is why I only mentioned it in passing).

    At the beginning of each session of Dread, each character has 12 points of Fury. These can be spent in-game to perform stunts, or they can be used at the very beginning of the game for a Longshot. The Longshot is Dread's advancement mechanic. You gamble all 12 of your points of Fury by rolling 12 dice. Any 1s or 7s are tallied, and if you get three of either, you get a new background or a new spell. It usually takes a while to accumulate enough 1s or 7s. The upside is, if you've only got two spells, this can help. The downside is, you don't have access to the Fury (and therefore the stunts) that your teammates have. But it might be worth it. It's a gamble.
