    \n To me it matters only in so far as it raises awareness that a game is made up the entire social dynamic at the table. Many gamers who still believe that "system doesn't matter" do so because basically they've settled on a "game" of their own making without really be aware of that. All changing the game does for this person is change the color but the actual *game* (i.e. rewarded social dynamic) remains constant from campaign to campaign.

    \n What Jesse said.

    I'd only add that for me I use the words this way (and this is only for my own thinking, not some from-on-high declaration)...

    * Mechanics are where uncertainty and the fiction meet. ("I'm swinging my axe and I've got a Strength of 3, so I'm using 3 dice.")

    * Procedures are all the things the game says to do with moving forward with the game to play it properly (ie: as written) (In Sorcerer, all Players author a Kicker for their Characters; if you don't do this, you're not really playing Sorcerer; "If you want to do something, check your Character's Score in the relevant attribute and pick up that many dice")

    * Techniques are the things I do as a player (of any flavor) to help along the Mechanics and Procedures. (If a Player gets stuck on creating a Kicker, as Game Master I'll ask questions, tapping all the other information the Player has already written down on the Character sheet. Asking questions of the Players in Sorcerer is a Technique for me, because it really isn't in the Sorcerer rules anywhere. In Apocalypse World, asking questions would be a Procedure.)


    You can find a much more sophisticated break down of these kinds of things at this amazing post by Vincent Baker over at Lumpley.com. But he thinks much, much harder about this stuff than I do.

    \n And, because I'm in the middle of a fat build at work, I'm going to answer my own question before giving many other people a chance.

    Correct my thinking.

    "Say 'try a different way'" "Write down an issue." "Everyone gets four scenes." "Move the characters tokens to the stage they're on for the scene" "Decide what hollywood actor would play your character" "Choose whether you escape into the night, recant, or burn at the stake" - procedures

    "Don't narrate another player's character's thoughts." "Make Apocalypse World seem real" "Try to make good things happen for your character." "draw a veil if the subject matter is uncomfortable for you" - techniques

    "2d10 + Skill vs. TN" "After your fifth experience point, mark improvement." "When you fall below 0 hit points, you're unconscious." "If you lose the roll, get back your pool dice +1" "When you act on your key, gain xp." - mechanics

    But then there's gray areas. What's Archipelago's "Draw a Resolution card and interpret the results" - is that a procedure, mechanic, technique - or a mix? Polaris's flowchart of key phrases - procedure or mechanic? "Roll 3d6 to determine each stat" - procedure or mechanic?

    One difference is purpose: a mechanic is usually there to softly incent player behavior. ("I better not roll, because I'll probably lose," or "I better roll, because I'll get xp" or "I better act roguish, so I can draw a card." or "I better play to my weaknesses so I can go on the owe list.") Mechanics might take a while to kick in, because the players won't realize until after multiple playing the value of the incentives. And a player might always choose to ignore the mechanic.

    A technique or procedure, otoh, usually demands a certain player behavior. It's the rule. You have to do this. If you don't "Make Apocalypse World seem real", then you're not playing Apocalypse World.

    Procedures are directions. Recipes. The way indie games have explored these over the past several years have really blown the lid off, IMO. The setup phases for In a Wicked Age or Fiasco - Apocalypse World GMing - they're more likely to deliver consistent experiences to different groups than mechanics, like Jesse said.

    Techniques, too, but they are more vague. Like, okay, *how* do we make Apocalypse World real? *How* do we make good things happen, and what's a good thing?
    The ultimate vague technique would be "Tell a good story and have fun." (Yay, I've just created the best story game ever.)
    Still, I think they're probably more effective at delivering consistency than pure mechanics. Simply telling a player to "Be a hero" might be more likely to produce heroism than crafting a set of mechanics that rewards heroism.

    \n@Jamie: Well said! It's important to act like procedures are just as binding as mechanics, too. Once, when I was playing Storming the Wizard's Tower, one of the players elected to make his character a drunk that lived down by the harbor.
    The GM very politely reminded him that the PCs need to be respected, or at least important, members of the community; they need to be people who will be listened to and who can reasonably expect the help of the community. If this procedure is not observed, it's possible that play can end up focusing a lot of time on convincing the community to hand over needed resources and, generally, for the community to accept the PCs as authoritative. Both those things go beyond what Storming is about. Procedures are play advice - they give context and advice on how to effectively use the mechanics as intended.

    At the far end of the "procedures" spectrum is a game like Vampire: the Masquerade, Revised Edition. You sit down, make some characters, and then.. .what? No specific hatred towards White Wolf is intended, here - they were part of a more or less hobby-wide tendency to use dharma transmission (a little Zen humor there) to communicate how one should play games, run games, etc. It's fascinating to look back on our history and see how relatively recently some designer suggested, "Hey, maybe we should have play advice for our game, as if our game were distinct enough that it merits being played in a certain way."
    I know that this mindset, that all games only differ mechanically, not procedurally, was how I used to think, back when - there was this sense, not just felt by me, that one could someday find THE game, the game with the best mechanics, that could handle all our favorite genres correctly, and then we would not need to look for games anymore.
    It was this notion that games weren't meant to do different things from one another, but, inversely, they were all attempts to do one unclear thing correctly: make the "best" possible RP experience. Yes, White Wolf put out articles on tone and such, and D&D3 had an essay near the front of the DMG about play styles, but that was about it.

    \n Jonathan, you're probably right about that. And I probably will read Apocalypse World during the lull between finishing the first, whole reader-ready draft and waiting for my readers to send back notes.

    As for another renaissance in design and play, I agree. In fact, a few years ago, on the Forge, somewhere (I can't find a damned thing anymore) I said that this issue -- making clear how we play -- is the next issue that has to be addressed.

    I want to add, I don't think this is just a matter of GM style. It's players-at-the-table-style. Everyone at the table is interacting with each other with ideas, and different ways of interacting are... well, different. With different effects.

    The importance of so much of Vincent's game design work, in my view, is that he keeps some of the traditional properties of the Game Master (most importantly, for me is the Game Master being able to apply pressure to the Player Characters) but codifies and sets up the expectations of the pressure so things so don't go all crazy with the Players trying to guess which way they are supposed to jump, try to mind-read their way through the session, tip-toe around the Game Master's expectations, or fear breaking the fun for other people.

    His design does a bunch of other stuff, of course. But this aspect, the Game Master applying pressure on the Characters in a codified manner without it becoming bullying against the Players, is really, really vital. There's so much of value in having a Game Master around to do that -- but now its really of value because the procedures of how to do that effectively are becoming more and more explicit.

    As for the Players, when the Game Master's tools or whateverthefuck we're calling them are explicit and clear, they have a better idea how hard they can push with their characters without breaking the play experience. (And in these games like Sorcerer, In A Wicked Age..., Dogs in the Vineyard and so on, they can push pretty fucking hard!) This, too, is really important for me. Being able to say to Players, "Go! Go! Go!" without people worrying they are going to break someone's fun or the group's play is crucial for me -- whether I'm a Game Master or Player.

      \nJamie: I don't know about the adventure writing for Shadowrun, but the adventure writing for In Nomine (which I think was derived from White Wolf stuff) came in basically 2 different varieties:

      1) "Seeds": These were basically the premise of an arc worth of play, 2-5 sessions worth, written in a few paragraphs. Typically some dude was trying to do some thing and the players were called in to do something about it or had some vested interest in making sure things worked out a certain way. They were the equivalent of "bangs," if you know that bit of Forge jargon. Sometimes they included a statted up NPC or two or an artifact or something, but often they were purely descriptive, with the rest left for the GM to implement.

      2) "Scenarios": These typically started with a "seed" or sometimes several seeds that would bring the characters together in different ways. Then there would be a section describing the various factional interests that were in play, with stats for the important characters in each group. Then there would be a list of locations or pre-set scenes that you would do stuff with when the characters decided to check out specific places or track down specific individuals. More NPC stats, and often short descriptions of how the NPCs would react if the characters approached them in certain ways. Inevitably, some of these interactions were assumed to lead into fights, and there was often a brief description on how the fight might go. But, ideally, the scenario was set up to be relative open-ended, in a "play to find out what happens" way. The characters could do whatever they wanted, seek out whoever they liked, approach things in whatever order, etc. The poorly written scenarios tended to have obvious linearity, where characters would probably go A -> B -> C, but the better ones were basically just like Fronts and Threats.

      In both of these cases, the fact that there was written adventure material didn't really change the fact that you were basically supposed to run it like you run AW, more or less. The GM was ideally supposed to play all these interests but not have a story in mind so much, though there was definitely a tendency to railroad as well, in practice.

Procedures are any processes to follow in play, and mechanical procedures have particular, usually quantified aspects that are reproducible. For example, evertime I make a saving throw in D&D 4e it will be done the same way for my character, with different input given based on what power was used for the attack on my character. In Primetime Adventures, the process of resolving a conflict is mechanical: number of cards based on screen presence, additional cards based thrown in based on who wants to use their fan mail points to do so, etc.

      Other kinds of procedures that might be useful to distinguish would be guidelines (eg the GM has the power to introduce threats), principles (eg start each session with a stranger coming into town [see Vincent's discussion of this over at anyway]), narrative structures (eg the play within a play in Doubt that gets played out in parallel to the character's real lives), and probably tons of other things.

      \n For me:

      A mechanic is a procedure I feel compelled to pass through a validation test. What's compelling me? Who knows. Social contract mostly -- we all agreed to play by X rules. This is definitely the head space I'm in when I'm running AW: when a particular procedure is granted the imprimatur of "mechanic," there is, for me, one more step it has to pass through before it enters the fiction: Is this statement a "move?" Can I rationalize it into a "move?" Is some other "move" more-correct or, I don't know, less of a stretch to pack into an existing "move" (even under the cover of not naming the move)? Sometimes that validation step is no biggie, especially when I've really internalized any given game and its philosophical assumptions. Sometimes it's just a lot more work.

      BW is packed with mechanized procedures as well (SYORTD, Let it Ride, Intent + Task), and there's the additional layer of democratized procedures as well (trait votes, setting creation, collaborative character building and Belief workshopping), which I think complicates rigorous analysis.

      Lots and lots of mainstream RPGs have toyed with mechanizing stuff that was previously left to the discretion of a single player. In every case, and I think in the case of indie hippie games as well, it's really just a matter of shifting around authority: This thing that used to be in the purview of the GM's discretion (advancement awards, what to say next, when or how to grant a player speaking time) is now in the purview of The Rules, which we all agree to grant higher authority than any given player.
