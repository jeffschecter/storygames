    \n@Mark_Causey's one-shot was great fun. It came with pre-built PC's though. That's one way to do it, although I let me players make their characters because I think that's one of the most fun parts of this game.

    I think your plan is pretty solid. Have an idea of a plot that's basically just there for the PC's to fumble around with. The plot is not really where the fun of a Monsterhearts game is, it's just the red herring for the players to get into messy situations and the drama basically plays itself out from there. The most fun for players in a MOnsterhearts game is playing their characters and gaining XP by making messy situations for themselves, so basically just give them the tools to do that by creating easily overcome opposition, and NPCs and situations that poke at the characters' fundamental issues.

    So, for example, if you have a Serpentine in play, make sure one of the NPCs is messing with his family. If you have a Ghost, make sure you have someone who really makes her feel invisible. If you have a Werewolf, make sure you can trigger their rage. Then point it at a PC.

    Always frame a scene with at least 3PC's in it, if not all of them at once.

    Go with the flow of what the players want, and less what your plot demands. Change plot as necessary.

    Set up difficult and painful choices. Players love that emo shit.

    Don't forget the horror! Take some of your favorite TV horror tropes and throw them in there. Make it bloody and gory, and do it suddenly in between the romance and high school drama. Or do something really creepy. Some of the best ways to do horror is subtly, the scariest things are the things not fully on screen.

    Let the players make their characters, and adjust you pre-written ideas a little as needed.

    After the characters are made and you go around the table describing each one, ask them provocative questions based on their description, background, and skin.
    "So you live in a trailer park. How did you feel when you brought your first boyfriend home to meet your parents?" If you can, ask leading questions involving some kind of past romantic or twisted relationship. I've found this really grounds players in their characters and in the themes of the game right away.

    Probably overkill on my advice here, but I hope this is helpful to you!
