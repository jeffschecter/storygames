\n So, first off, the only GMless games I'm familiar with are Kingdom and Microscope. I like both of those games. What they do, though, is very different from the "adventure fiction" style of some traditional RPGs. I'm curious about the possibility of Myth Arcs in GMless games. Myth Arcs, for those who haven't heard the phrase, are long-term stories with secrets and questions that are paid off at the end of a particular section of a story's plot. They're often my favorite part of long-form storytelling.

Those of you with more GMless experience than I have (read: all of you), have you found a way to incorporate these kinds of grand mysteries into GMless games? Or is this type of storytelling truly reliant on the separation of storyteller (GM) and audience (players)?

    \n It\u2019s possible to do a Myth Arc with GMless games, but how you do so is going to be contingent upon what your design goals are for the game at large. How easy it will be to design will also depend on your other game design goals and exactly what your trying to do.

    I\u2019m not aware of a game that does an awesome job at this\u2014probably, because it\u2019s kind of like swimming up stream and designers with this goal just choose to use a GMed form.

    Anyway, if you want to add a mechanic that will help to give this affect, with an already existing game, I can likely come up with a solution for you if you give me more detail about specifically what your after and let me know what game you\u2019d like to use for the foundation. It would obviously be a less then ideal solution, but you could refine it over time... it\u2019s a hard design to do well, so you might have to settle for 80% of what your after\u2014unless you convinced a good designer to put a lot of time and energy into it\u2014depending on exactly the gameplay effects your after.

    P.S. I would probably us a PbtA style game and adapt it to a rotating GMed game, and then use a myth arc scaffolding-design tech in which one would plug in big questions (based on things players wanted answers to as the plot emerged). Big questions would be continually added based on how the narrative emerged. Nothing would be framed narratively or plotted out before hand, the scaffolding would simply be a tech tool for these things to emerge. The plot would be driven by the answers to these big questions, and would have secret information emerging in play rather than being prepared beforehand, of course building to the biggest question being answered and the mystery being revealed, once it built to the final mystery (ie question and answer). None of these questions would be formulated before play, they would come out of play and be formulated as a result of the narrative and players wanting to answer narrative questions. Anyway, this would be the main mechanic and you could do a lot of other stuff depending on what you wanted to do.

    \n I'm only aware of a few GM-less rpgs that do long-term play, period. So that is its own design challenge, I guess (how long-term are we talking anyways?).

    As far as mysteries go I like the approach Lovecraftesque takes: Every scene has someone describing a clue, just something that is off about the scene. Then after the scene is played, everyone "leaps to conclusions" secretly writing down what they believe is behind the clue. This continues throughout play with the players revising their individual conclusions based upon the clues that come into play. The game ends with someone just deciding to present their conclusion as the truth, the payoff, if you will. That way no one knows whats really going on right until the end.

    Another technique (from Directions Storyplaying System) is just assigning the mystery to a single player Archipelago-style, who is then responsible for revealing it over the course of play.

    Swords without Master (very weak GM-role) has Mysteries that are written down on a card during play and put into the middle of the table. In the endgame players may "reincorporate" those into the story by answering the mystery in sort of an epilogue-fashion.

    I've recently tried to implement a mystery in my character's goal in Remember Tomorrow (GM-less Cyberpunk game built for campaign play) and it just paralysed the other players. The lesson is probably to state as clearly as possible who is allowed to resolve that and over what timeframe they're supposed to do it.

    \n My 2cts.
    Every dramatic conflict is a promise that the plot moves forward. In a mystery these conflicts are just questions. The fuel is curiosity (usually with a twist of fear or something, but not necessarily).

    I like a dynamic narrative structure, with short term along with long term conflicts. Empowering players to adjust the tempo of the questions and answers to their fiction is a way of ensuring the reveals will come at the right time for them.

    Most games severely frame dramatic conflicts, in 2 ways : 1\xb0 players can create only short term conflicts, and 2\xb0 only the rules or GM can fix long term conflicts (with the excuse of enforcing a genre, warranting a neat story, or any other pretext really).

    I know of 3 games that have dramatic conflict creation and resolution at their core, and all 3 are GMful (not GMled) : Annalise (claims), Capes (goals), and my own Utopian Chronicles (objectives).

    I am not saying your game should use such a mechanic. At all. You can certainly look at these games. They do mystery and surprise very well in my opinion. It's like : some things that seem complicated within other frameworks are very easy with this dramatic mechanic. But there is a "and vice versa"...

    TLDR : You can totally do mysteries and reveals without a GM or authoritarian rules. Your game can include the tools for the players to navigate through their own homemade mystery.

    \n As @hyades mentioned, in Directions Storyplaying I used to have a "Secret Keeper" - one player as the final guardian of a mystery.

    With the consulting help by Ron Edwards, I've recently redesigned and streamlined Directions. I've moved out the "Secret Keeper" concept and replaced it with a "Mystery Plot Sheet":


    This approach has been more satisfactory because it enhances the GMless collaborative approach in Directions Storyplaying.
    How it works:
    - With rotating hosts, players roleplay different scenes. In these scenes, various questions and clues related to the mystery pop up.
    - Both during in-character roleplaying as well as OOC brainstorming, there is speculation (guesswork) about plausible resolution for the mystery.
    - Ultimately for the final resolution of the mystery, I usually use a "Random Table on the Fly" (core resolve mechanism for Directions). So, players brainstorm up to 6 different possible outcomes (i.e. Who is the murderer? - 1/2:John 3/4:Butler 5/6:Widow). Then, during roleplaying at a suitable moment, a die is rolled and the mystery is resolved (roll a 4: it was the butler!).

    This is not a fool-proof method and to me mysteries still remain the holy grail of GMless play. Nevertheless, this allows collaborative GMless play that gradually navigates towards resolving a mystery together.

    Another approach (sorry, don't remember the source anymore) I liked:
    Players write index cards with potential clues for the mystery. They shuffle the index cards and build a pile face down . Whenever they research, they draw a card and find a clue.
