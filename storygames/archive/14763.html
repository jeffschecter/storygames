    \n The "D&D Edition Wars" panel I was on had a standing-room-only, turn-people-away audience that got obscure jokes about every era of the game's history. Unless they do a lot to make more workshops happen (I can't figure out why this doesn't happen, it didn't seem hard for the Escapist to put this one together) there will be enough gamers who recognize your names and flock to check out what you have to say that you'll never have an audience who doesn't know how to play RPGs.

I'd be glad to talk about running games for kids, as party entertainment, etc.

    Tyler, having an audience member as facilitator can be helpful - at least I like to think so because I don't know anything about art except for enthusiasm when I moderated the "D&D in Contemporary Art" panel.

    \n There is a large table top contingent at pax but I think there is also a fairly large group of people who are interested in playing rpgs but simply don't know how. So long as they knew a workshop was happening i think they would attend.

    The effect of the penny arcade DnD podcast and gabe posting about his 4th ed game have really made that audience want to play.

    I would be cool with moderating a panel style workshop, if other people are interested in having me do that and would want to be on such a panel.

    here is a blurb

    How to play table top RPGs!

    Interested in playing a table top RPGs but not sure how? let these experienced players and designers share their basic strategies for creating a successful game night. Learn to pick the right RPG for your group, how they find player's and how to get the most out of a game. Table top RPGs are one of the most rewarding experiences you can have at the game table and we want to share them with you.

    basic structure of the event would be such

    Each panelist would bring one game book that they have successfully read and then played on their own. We would cover the process they used to play that game.

    1 Why we chose this game? This would cover what makes rpgs different then one another and what sort of games work best for diffident groups

    2 How we got other people interested in playing with us. This would cover how we navigate the larger social world of gaming, some of us play with natural friends, some meet in stores or at events.

    3 how we prepared for our first game.

    4 how we kept something going.

    Q&A

    Posted By: Ben LehmanI feel like those are probably both not great ideas. So "GMless games, what's up with them?" or "how to hack a game" with examples from Apocalypse World seem like better bets.

    \nBen, I might agree with you on the Apocalypse World issue, but as a guileless newb, I couldn't disagree with you more on the "GMless games, what's up with them". I'm brand new to Story Games (first exposed in June at FabReal) and I would never have sat through a too basic "What's a GMless game" workshop or panel. Don't underestimate both the popularity of Story Games in the PNW and the sophistication of tenderfeet like me. We should run what would be interesting to us, and it will likely be interesting to many new kids. As long as your workshop or presentation has a suitable introduction, I think you'd be surprised at how accessible it will be. Many participants won't be advanced enough to internalize everything you say, but any exposure to the higher level stuff will trigger saliva in the right person.

    Anyway, that's my two bits.

    I'd attend a workshop or panel on:

    -Kickers and Bangs
    -Story Now
    -Lines, Veils, Nobody Gets Hurt, I Will Not Abandon You (panel of folks?)
    -How to get effective critiques on playstyle from gamer strangers

    I feel like many of our ideas could be combined into rad monster workshops.


    Whatever you do, make sure to drop lots of links to places where you can find local gaming groups.

    \nThere is a scene in Blues Brothers where a bar owner is asked what kind of music they usually have at their bar. She answers "we have both kinds: country and western".

    The PAX audience is much like this: "we have both kinds: D&D and Storyteller". Some portion of the audience secretly feels that the games they run/play in don't give them what they want, but have no idea what that is, or that there are other ways to play out there. The type of person who would come to something called "What to do when D&D no longer works for you" is exactly the type of person who will "get" a "workshop about those things".
