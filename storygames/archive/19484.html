    \n Some more thoughts and suggestions:

    * I find "Everyone plays the Detective" confusing on a first read. How about, "Everyone takes turns playing the Detective"?

    * Why is it important to roll the die in the open? Just curious.

    Would the game work if the die was rolled after each question was asked, for example? (That's effectively a "hidden" roll.)

    * When you play, do you tend to assign suspects to certain players, or not?

    * "Now the questioning begins. The Detective ask up to three questions,"

    This is an error. How about, "The Detective will ask three questions"?

    * "The game is over. Discuss what you have accomplished, and what to be changed upon next time."

    Grammar trouble. How about: "What MIGHT/SHOULD be changed next time."

    * "If the same Suspect appears more than once during the draw, ignore it"

    Why is this? Wouldn't this be an interesting opportunity for drama? Add more details to make them seem more guilty, or maybe make an argument for why they're innocent, or have them reveal some kind of twist. What's the logic here?

    * "Tie the suspect together to something on the paper"

    This is just awkward grammar. How about: "Tie/connect the suspect to something on the paper."

    \n I see what you mean, but I think it's hard to create suspects, or understanding them, without knowing the place. It's like when you frame a scene. The players can't move in the scene unless you say the place. If you want to do a large narration, leave the place to the end. I wonder if not mentioning the place until after the suspects have been created may create a difficulty to understand the game. That's why I placed the apartment at the beginning and then repeated it before the actual play will begun.

    If you will play the game, try what I wrote by leaving out the place until later. And try to say something like "We're on a space ship" instead of an apartment and see how the rest of the group will react. They will probably have an option about that, don't you think?

    I think, if I get a chance to play the game again, I will try this myself. I also believe that changing the setting will change the motives, so it's important to even mention it. One motive could be about an alien life form, if you're on a space ship, and what kind of motives would be created if the game would take place in a mansion during the 1890s? I toyed around with expectations from a setting with This Is Pulp, where you start off by choosing between, and later creating tropes in, different land areas. South America will give inspiration to a lot of different tropes than China.

    \n Haha, yeah but ...

    ... I thought that reading that incredibly inspirational text could be a kick-off to actually start playing. To break off from, or create a pause between, spawning suspects to actually talk to them.

    I could probably restructure the whole thing but then I also need to rewrite the text. If I had When & Where before The Suspects, I think that would lead the group to believe that they are about to play the detective right away. At least as how it's written now.

    If I take a step out of me as the game's creator: it may seem like I'm shooting down all of your ideas but I realized that this game is rather complex. A lot of things depend on each other, not in a mechanical way, but in a social and "learning" way. Like why the die should be rolled in the open. I think the ideas are sound in how the game is built and explained, but the ideas can be executed in a lot better ways.

    I agree that the whole setting thing in You sounds off, especially when it should be about the detective. But I don't really want to bunch together the same information on one place, because repeating is a good thing. That's why I, for example, mention the assistant twice, after the actual headline. That's why I got the different results of the die roll to start off with the same sentence. This is something I learned from feedback from previous games, and now experimenting with.

    So see this game as a learning curve for me, and I really appreciate any kind of feedback in how you interpret my text, and where your thoughts went - like why anyone should roll the the die in the open. Feedback like that gives me a lot.
