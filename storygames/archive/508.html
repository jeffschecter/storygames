Initial Conditions and Emergent Patterns

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

Over in Ready Made Out of the Box JDCorley is making an interesting point ... that (given the Lumpley Principle) it's not so easy as you might think to separate questions like "Are you changing the actual written rules of the game?" from questions like "Are you defining the situation?" They are, after all, all part and parcel of the overall Lumpley System.

Two examples that spoke to genuinely got me thinking were this: Town Creation in DitV is a definition of the specific scope of things that will be examined in an episode. How do you differentiate the way that impacts what moral issues you can engage with from (for instance) changing the Escalation sequence, which also impacts the moral issues you can engage with? Second, if you change the rules of D&D so that certain attacks aren't as prominent, is that as big a change as setting the game in a court intrigue setting where combat as a whole will be less prominent?

Cool stuff. I don't think the classic "But it's written down in the rule-book" answer is a clear-cut difference, precisely because of the aforementioned Lumpley Principle. In other words, what I thought I knew turns out to be just me not having thought things through. Awkward!

But here's the thing: I think that it's possible that these things are different ... but not on the question of whether they're written in the rule-book. I'm going to tackle the Dogs example and then see how the D&D one looks after I've rambled a bit.

Town Creation in Dogs sets initial conditions for the game. It sets up the people who are present, the questions that are going to be asked. It doesn't establish the answers the players are going to give, and more to the current point it doesn't establish the procedures that those players are going to follow in creating those answers.

Now the rules of Dogs are going to create some emergent patterns no matter what initial conditions are first fed into them. For instance, my experience is that there will be escalation to physical violence between Dogs ... that one side of that will escalate to gunplay, and the opposing side will ratchet back down to Just Talking, and that the fallout from that conflict will go into d4 relationships between the two principles. Now that's pretty ludicrously specific (though if it sounds familiar to you then I want extra points!) but I do feel that if we abstracted everyone's experience we could see the more general emergent patterns that DitV rules create, irrespective of the starting conditions.

I mentioned that I was going to ramble, right?

So I think I've led up to the distinction I see, yes? There is a distinction between running through Town Creation and changing the mechanics of Dogs. In the one case you're changing the initial conditions, in the other case you're changing the rules that create the emergent patterns.

Now ... is this the same thing in D&D? I actually think no, it's not. Again, this is just off-the-cuff talk from my personal experience, but I don't think that the parts of the system that create emergent patterns are housed in the game mechanics for D&D. A great deal of what carries forward from one scene to the next is created entirely by group fiat, and so anything that directly influences that group fiat is going to have a powerful effect on emergent properties of the game.

In Dogs, for instance, it doesn't matter whether the group all thinks the Steward is well-respected by the townsfolk. If a Dog wins stakes of "Humiliate the Steward" then that Steward is humiliated, and next scene he's still humiliated, and frankly he's gonna be pretty well humiliated until somebody goes in by the rules and gives him his dignity back.

But if you're playing a Court Intrigue game in D&D, and somebody somehow humiliates the king (not even sure what you'd roll for that, but anyway ...) there's absolutely nothing that even implies that the king should stay humiliated, or that there should be any great consequences from that action. Indeed, the group fiat might say "Hey, if this were a dungeon then yeah, we'd have killed him and that would stick ... but the situation is court intrigue, so no matter what happens the king is going to retain his dignity."

See right there? That clever bit where I "proved" that the situation of the Court Intrigue would change the emergent properties, just by writing an example where it does? Sounds plausible, doesn't it? Shame it isn't actually an argument. It's just a plausible example.

The argument would be something like this:

In a game where emergent patterns are created by explicit rules (e.g. DitV) the situation is just an initial condition, because it's impact into the explicit rules happens once ... after that the new conditions created by the explicit rules override the original conditions.

>

By contrast, in a game where emergent patterns are created by implicit rules and social feedback (e.g. D&D) the situation has a powerful impact upon emergent properties, because it impacts the implicit rules over and over ... as people decide in their own heads what the outcome should be they refer back to the initial situation as a road-map to understanding the goals of the group as a whole.

Wow. I wonder if this is my longest post ever on this board. I guess JDCorley really did inspire me to some thinking. My brain hurts a little. Also, it's late. So maybe I'm just babbling.

What do you think?

    Hmmm. Tony, I mostly strongly agree with what you're saying - except I think that if you dig into it further, there's a distinction between emergent system (for example, escalation and de-escalation in a Dogs conflict) and emergent story. There's emergent system in most games that I've played, and following the rules is gonna get you there, no argument. Emergent story is more complicated, because it sounds like you're talking about how players and GMs reason about the fictional world of the game, and how closely they're bound by the mechanics which constrain the story.

    I'm not sure your examples of the humiliation of the Steward and, to take it away from D&D, the humiliation of the local Prince in Vampire are actually all that different, except for two things. First, Dogs explicitly enforces narrative continuity between scenes, as once stakes have been won, they can't be un-staked without explicitly undermining them. (Though I'm not even sure that's entirely accurate for the way all groups play; I'll have to check to see whether that's actually stated in the rules!) Second, the point at which the group decides whether the Steward is humiliate-able is when they agree that those are acceptable stakes for the conflict. You're sort of comparing apples to oranges, because there's no such explicit group agreement for humiliating a Prince.

    (If Vampire is a 500 pound gorilla to D&D's 800 pounds, I can find another example, by the way - but that's what I played in college, so I know it pretty well.)

    In any case, Tony, I actually think that I agree with you despite disagreeing with your example of narrative emergence. Games do encourage particular kinds of stories and of narrative reasoning. Every game has an unstated notion of what is plausible, possible and reasonable, which is encoded both in rules and setting. Vampire and Dogs are both games about morality, but they certainly encourage very different scopes of conflict, moral questions, and styles of play. I'm just not sure that Dogs enforces Steward-humiliation without group consensus any more than Vampire does.

    JDCorley:

    We all know there is a difference. But over time I've come to believe that the difference is not very important - if it is important at all.

    The division between initial conditions and emergent patterns is a division that sheds light on some questions, and is meaningless in terms of others. If we were talking about (say) "Who gets to narrate what? What sources of authority are relevant?" then I would say that initial vs. emergent sheds no light on that question. I might even say it's "unimportant", but that's short-hand.

    On the other hand, if we were discussing that question then I would say the difference between situation and rules sheds a lot of light on how people think about the question.

    The question (to my mind) that we were peering at is something like "When you go from the platonic notion of a game to the practical instance of actually sitting down and playing with your friends, what do you create? When do you choose to create exactly in accord with the theoretical construct of the game, and when do you create something the game doesn't direct you to?"

    I don't think the dividing the things you create into rules vs. situation sheds a whole lot of light on that question. I do think that dividing it into starting position vs. emergent patterns sheds a fair amount of light. Again, I would say in this context that the division is "important", as a shorthand.

    I suspect we're on different pages, or perhaps that you haven't yet considered that this question could have been unimportant the past 99 times, but be important now. Probably just the different pages thing, though.

    <code>Every game has an unstated notion of what is plausible, possible and reasonable, which is encoded both in rules and setting.

    Uh ... no. Some games have stated notions of what is plausible, possible and reasonable. Capes, for instance. If I win "Goal: Humiliate Major Victory" then he is humiliated. Period.

    True, but this isn't quite what I meant. Some games do state these notions, but not all of them do, and even the ones that do also have unstated notions - even if that notion is "cause and effect" or something that seems equally obvious to us.

    What I'm trying to say is that we, as human beings, have to have ways to reason about what might make sense in a story. Sure, in Capes you can make the Stakes whatever you want - but that doesn't mean that it will actually make sense to the players. The game (as I've read it - admittedly I've only played once) seems to draw pretty heavily on notions of what a superhero story is about. This is going to influence the ways that players make sense of what goes on in the story, the kinds of stakes they are willing to set and accept, what actions seem plausible, what will make the group go "CHEESE!" and the like. Very little of this is encoded directly in the rules, because all that work of narrative reasoning goes on in players' heads. I'm not disagreeing with you about how games can more and less actively support the Stakes that players introduce, but I do think we're talking about slightly different things.

    So, for example, in Dogs it's hard to imagine a group that wouldn't accept Stakes of "Humiliate the Steward" - not just for you, but for me, too. To me, that's because the Dogs book itself says that the Dogs are the authority, so it's totally reasonable that they could humiliate a Steward. I would have no problem accepting those Stakes, and I find it hard to imagine a group that would reject them. In Vampire, though, humiliating the Prince is not an obvious and logical extension of the setting; it may be something the players would like to do, but it's a lot less plausible than humiliating the Steward. Combined with the lack of clarity in articulating stakes and goals, it would be easy for the GM to overturn or ignore the Prince's humiliation, while doing it for the Steward would be much harder to justify under the particular narrative contract that each game implies.

    In other words, while "situation" may or may not have anything to do with the rules of the game, depending on what game it is, it helps us build mental models of what is reasonable in actual play. These mental models help us make moment-to-moment narrative decisions and judge the narrative decisions of others in ways that have little or nothing to do with the rules, but fall much more under the 'social contract' aspects of play.

    Does that make more sense?

    --Jess

    PS: Going back to the original post, I think that this ties back into the distinction that Tony was originally making about initial conditions vs. emergent behavior. Some games are better than others at making people explicitly create this kind of narrative contract, and town creation in Dogs is a part of it. When you create a town, you're making a concrete version of your mental model - setting up, in particular characters and situations which are both emergent the structure that the game provides (both rules and situation) and are also initial conditions for actual play. So I think that whether you see something as an initial condition or an emergent pattern depends on where you draw the line, and whose perspective you're looking from: GM, designer, or player.

    Tony,

    To me it just sounds like you're highlighting the difference between situation-addressing conflict resolution and situation-ambivalent task resolution.

    In Dogs, the rules impact the situation because that's what they do.

    In GURPS (or whatever), the rules impact where you're standing and how much damage you do, because that's what they do.
