\n I'm starting to become aware of what I'll call counterbalancing mechanics in card and board games. These mechanics allow players to include or prevent the inclusion of certain other mechanics in the game they may or may not enjoy. I'll give two examples:

1. In a card game called Battleline, there are special cards that can be played to either screw with your opponent or basically serve as wild cards to make the game feel less deterministic, more random, and to otherwise throw a wrench into your opponent's well-laid plans to count cards and win the game. The counterbalancing mechanic for these cards is that any player may only play one before giving his opponent the opportunity to play one. Thus, if I don't like these cards, I'll simply never play one and my opponent will only ever get to play 1 the entire game. He has the consolation that at least he got to play one to help him out and I never got to play any. I get the consolation that our game wasn't full of these cards being played.

2. In another card game called Asante, there are animal cards which (I recall) are the only cards that directly impact the other player in a negative way. The counterbalancing mechanic of them is that, instead of playing animal cards, you can retain them in your hand to block another player's animal from being played against you.

It struck me how both of these examples, while they don't radically change how the game is played, allow players to essentially vote during play on whether a mechanic with a particular style that they may or may not like will make a presence in the game, and even how much. In these examples the styles or play could be described to be "increased chaos" and "screwing over your opponent" or whatever. I think the concept could be applied to much more, however.

So I'm wondering about some of the more traditional or classic designs for roleplaying games that some folks tend to call "toolboxes". The kinds of games where different gaming groups came up with their own ways to apply certain rules to meet their own agendas (knowingly or unwittingly). What if there had been built-in counterbalancing mechanics for some of these various ways to approach the rules? This is a hard one for me to come up with an example now because it would require me to have played enough of a particular game with enough groups to discover very specific and consistent patterns in such kinds of rules applications. But I have noticed these trends over the past ~15 years of communicating online with table top role-players, even if I can't recall an example right now.

But now this is in my head and I'm going to be on the lookout for it. It might make for some fun design.

    \n Ok, here's my first idea after thinking a while. Probably not the best thought, but I'll get it down.

    I'm the GM and I decide to write a little plot in my usually very sandbox game. I would like for the player characters to have an encounter with a gang on their turf right at the moment they're running away from a heist they pulled at the bank.

    But one of the PCs decides to open a manhole and drop down into the sewers. In true sandbox form, I would let them handle the situation however they want. In true railroad form, I might tell them all the manholes on this street happen to be cemented shut. Some players like sandbox, some like to be railroaded (to some extent, although I think this is a minority).

    So a counterbalancing mechanic might be to say to the player (or players) "Your destiny isn't to go down into the sewers today" and offer up a destiny token. If all players take the token, one of them (or myself) can narrate a reason why they can't take the sewers, like the manhole being too heavy, etc.

    Otherwise, they can say "screw destiny" and not be railroaded, if they're not okay with that right now. This is a kind of meta-gamey mechanic, so some might not be okay with that.

    Hm, maybe "meta-gamey" rules or player-controlled story narration is another mechanic that could utilize counterbalancing though....

    \n It seems natural to me that card and board games get more mileage out of mechanics-counterbalancing. A lot of the stuff a given roleplayer might wish to include or exclude from play isn't covered by mechanics. "Try another way" from Archipelago (and others) is a nice example of a counterbalance to players' narrative freedom. Pre-game "is there anything we really do/don't want to see in play?" rituals have some degree of popularity as well. I think your railroad/not-railroad example falls into this category -- the player isn't really turning off a mechanic, they're turning off a GMing approach.

    That's not to say that toolbox RPGs can't have subsystems that are subject to limits or costs. But I suspect that'll only work either (a) up-front, before anyone's invested in avenues of effectiveness that a rule change would shut down, or (b) on really marginal stuff that no character concept or GM plot relies on. I think it's harder to change a play strategy when you're using a single character than when you're using (e.g.) a fluid deck.

    \n Oh! Swords Without Master has this in the form of All That Deserves a Name. By Naming something, you make it subject to being endangered just as you can be, but you also protect it unless you decide to let it perish. Naming something lets you bring it into the story at the cost of subjecting it to the Overplayer's battering-about. You can bring an army into the story by sheltering it on your sheet, at the cost of watching the Overplayer threaten it, beat it back, even inflict casualties.

    It's very narrative, not at all mechanical, but I think that counts.

    I keep thinking of that situation in a game where a player A really doesn't like fights, so s/he buys really high combat abilities and invulnerability to physical damage for their main character.
    \nIn my experience things that causes this problem are the referee railroading his players into situations they don't to deal with. Or the players don't want to deal with the consequences of their decisions. Both cases mechanics won't fix the issue. If mechanics are altered in an attempt to resolve the issue then they rapidly wind up either not reflecting the setting or the setting alters to conforms to the new mechanics. In either case the campaigns suffers.

    In the first case the referee need to learn that the points of the whole experiences is for the players to experience an alternative reality as their characters which includes the freedom to choice how they make their way.

    In the second case the player need to realize that their choices have consequences, if they screw up their choice then it will cause unpleasant consequences which could mean total failure. WIthout the possibility of screwing up then the feeling one gets from achieving a desired goal is lessened. And players need also to realize that a total screwup and subsequent events could make for a far more interesting experience than what was originally planned.

    When the two problems show up at once well fireworks ensue.

    Also realize the above two are not absolute, they exist on a spectrum and for most players with this problem is only for specific instances and specific aspects of the game.

    Specifically for what I quoted, if a players doesn't like fighting he shouldn't pick fights. And if he can't avoid not getting into fights then referee needs to told firmly out of game that he not focusing enough on the non-combat aspects of his campaign. And if the referee is not receptive to this then it is time for another campaign run by somebody else who is more in tune with what the group wants to play at that time. Not amount of new or additional rules mechanic is going to alter this.

    \n Here's an interesting one I encountered in SW d6 the other day. Doubt it's official.

    The higher the difficulty is, the less severe minor failures are.

    So for example, if you're doing an easy task, say DC 6, then if you roll that 1-5 you're really screwed. OTOH, if you're doing something incredibly hard, with a DC of say 21, and roll a 15 or so, your failure won't be as bad.

    This rule is interesting on a number of levels. For one thing, it really forces the GM to think about if they even want you to roll at all for easy stuff!
