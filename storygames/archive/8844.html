    \n The game itself is kind of irrelevant, I think: it's the kind of prep that matters more to me. The prep I like most:

    * I like thinking about the PCs. I think about what the players said they were going to try next time, I think about what sorts of things they like doing with their PCs, and I like to try and come up with situations and/or set pieces that will click with them. This is also the most productive prep I ever do: it almost always pays off somewhere down the line.

    * I also like thinking about the NPCs, figuring out what they can do next and little personality quirks or twitches I can give them so that they'll be fun for me to play. I'll often rehearse little bits of dialogue for them and daydream a bit about scenes they'd be in. This is also kind of a way for me to get to play the game when we're not playing, or even when I'm not running a game at all.

    * If I have a great idea for some kind of "feelie" I can do (an in-character document, a photoshopped image, something like that), I'll happily waste hours doing that, even if it's only going to be looked at for a minute or two. That's basically a chance for me to get all artsy-craftsy and pretend like I'm prepping! (Pretty useless as far as the actual game goes, though.)

    ...the first two parts are actually why I don't like games where I can't do any prep before the session. Those games are stealing the fun parts from me! Worse yet, they're making me use the shitty raw ideas I come up with first, rather than the awesome refined ideas I would finally arrive at after two days of idle thinking about them! Why in god's name would I want to have less fun GMing, and have the game session be worse on top of that? That's crazy talk, is what that is. That's game design I can live without.


    Incidentally, the kind of prep I hate doing the most is making maps. Boring, boring, boooooooooring. So. Fucking. Boring. I don't care about the map. I don't want to draw it. I don't want to think about whether it makes sense, or have to refer to it later, or push it at the players and tell them to look at it. I'll use a map if one is available, but if I have to prep a map on my own? Yeah, that ain't gonna happen. I'm going to give a brief and abstract text description of the layout ("This country is to the north. This neighborhood is across the river. This store is a few blocks away.") and if pressed to draw it out, I'm going to do the worst and most half-assed sketch imaginable and say "this is not to scale."

    I'm not a huge fan of preparing stats or complicated spell lists, either, although I'll do it if I know it will speed up the actual game. D&D3e was so much smoother if I prepped by putting all the relevant information about the monsters and spells I'd need to use in one place, and so I didn't mind taking the time to do it. Fifteen minutes on a weeknight writing up a couple of combat encounters versus god only knows how minutes of frantic flipping through sourcebooks during an actual game on Saturday is not a tough choice, believe me.

    \n Everway! I have a blast coming up with sphere ideas, indulging my worst "world of hats" tendencies ("A sphere ruled by illusionists! Matriarchal illusionists! And then a sphere that's a giant maze of twisty little passages, all alike, wherein it is very dark and the PCs are likely to be eaten by a grue.") Making little sphere networks filled with weirdly named places ("Hmmm, should I put Bitter Diamond next to Blue Lotus, or Rule of None?") and then figuring out what those places actually are is just the right sweet spot in amount of prep work needed.

    Plus, NPCs are hella easy to stat out, the power system is fun to play with, and I like to randomly draw cards from the Fortune Deck for Virtue, Vice, and Fate, and try to figure out what they all mean together. (I like to do that for spheres, too.)

    So, yeah, Everway. Still awesome after all these years.

    \n I enjoy D&D4 prep, especially making cool battle maps in photoshop. Also, re-purposing monster stats to fit some thing I invented.

    I like making a custom character sheet for a game, to provide some color. The sci-fi game sheet looks all techy, the western game sheet is dusty and woodcut, etc.

    I really enjoy running a few sessions of something with no prep -- just riffing, escalating, and playing the NPCs -- then creating a backstory and NPC motivations between sessions that fit the facts we've established so far. It's kind of like a weird writing exercise, working within the constraints.

    I enjoy making Dogs towns, but only because doing so makes me desperate to play. If I don't have a Dogs game on the horizon, I don't make towns for their own sake.

    I enjoy prepping Agon islands and quests. I big reason for writing the game was an excuse to do more of that kind of prep. I like having a bunch of color and some potential obstacles at hand, with plenty of questions waiting to be answered by the players. Prepping for Mouse Guard has a similar feel, to me.

    Lately I've been prepping Poison'd a bit, which is weird, I know. I've played the default starting situation enough times that I now want to have a different opening scene. I'm coming up with a handful of variations on the "oh shit" moment to try out in future games.
