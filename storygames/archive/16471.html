    \n We did an episode of the Yellow-Menace podcast that discussed LB at pretty considerable length. You might find it helpful. In general I wouldn't stress too much about screwing up the system's economy - the consequences of failed rolls and conditions are pretty fuzzy, so they're unlikely to produce any sort of vicious circle - failure often leads to interesting consequences. I generally find that the game works best if you push the players pretty hard from a difficulty standpoint, so they use pool pretty heavily. Refresh scenes are one of the absolute best things about Lady Blackbird, so it's good to make the players want them. As the game is running, I take note of events and dialogue that could fuel fruitful exploration during refresh scenes, and I'll suggest them when the players are starting to get hard up for dice, or burdened with conditions.

    \n Push on the characters basically as hard as you want.

    Encourage refresh scenes.

    Try push towards a conclusion hard as it's a con.

    Given the genre, just make sure the action only lulls for refresh scenes or in-character dialog.

    So, run it like AW but without the mean teeth and scarcity; your front and etc all right there. 'Rift' of player action and suggestion. "Yes and.." and "Yes but..." a lot. So, only veto stuff that is actively disruptive to someone's fun. I find that if you lean on the characters, the players will come up with cool stuff on the spot.

    \n Just some advice from my last year in Skype gaming, your best investment is a comfy headset with microphone that you can wear for hours. This gives you some freedom of movement and general boost in sound quality. Options such as laptop mics or desktop mic tend to be sub-par and fixed (so your volume drops when you move about.) The latter two notoriously pick up fan noise and every little movement on the desk or keyboard (playing with dice and poker chips are the worst.)
