Banking on a game's future

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2008 \n edited October 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In some of the intellectual property threads, people have been saying "Hey, if a game suddenly goes out of print then any stuff you've created for the game loses value."
\n
\nI think this is based on a very intuitive assumption, but I'd just like to drag it out into the open so people can take a look at it: The assumption is that the value of something is not merely its value today, but also includes a best guess about its value in the future.
\n
\nSo, suppose I hear that Clinton Nixon's making a new game, and I get in on the ground floor of playtesting, and I make a whole lot of material to support that game. There's it's value today, which is the amount of fun I can have with it in concert with the couple dozen other people who also have access to the product. Then there's what I guess about its value tomorrow, which I probably hope will be very high, when Clinton's game revolutionizes the industry, and I get fame and wealth from having been associated with his genius.
\n
\nIf he then decides not to publish the game, Clinton has in no way reduce the today-value of my work. He has, however, radically changed the perceived value of my bet on the game's future.
\n
\nSimilarly, if someone takes their game off the market after a good, solid run they have impacted nobody's today-value. They have somewhat changed the perceived value of any bets people placed on the game's future.
\n
\nIf I might be so bold as to offer: Fans of a game are often wildly optimistic about the future of a game they like. That means that they routinely overvalue these futures, and then when things (often quite predictably) don't work out that way, they feel that they have been deprived of some real value, when in fact they are merely finding that their guess at a future value was incorrect.

    \n Yeah, sure, I'm in on this. But rather than dickering the points raised (which I've been doing in other threads), I'll drop a more cogent whole. Maybe it'll be useful, maybe not.

    More and more, to me, a game has value because of the creative community that interacts with it and with one another, by the advice, the culture of play, and other such effects that apply. A vast amount of value attaches to the d20 system for me in this way.

    A single book or game can bring with it dozens, even hundreds, of interesting views on applying and modifying it, whole supplements worth of ideas. When a game goes "dead", that doesn't just devalue the potential future; it chills the network of today.

    A game is also a community.

    We don't make games purely for the money. In large part, we make these games because we want to produce entertainment, provoke thought, share ideas, with one another. In short, we do so because, in the context of gaming, we love each other. And we live in a time, and interact in a medium, where the reach of that love is multiplied, where it's lifespan can be "As long as we like".

    I often think it's a damn shame when some aspect of that goes wrong, and someone feels compelled to cut short that reach and that lifespan.

    I think that value is lost when that occurs.

    Call it entitlement, if you like.

    Posted By: Levi KornelsenNot as a sin. An end to generosity is not, by definition, miserliness or stinginess.
    Fair enough. Not a sin. The reason I used the phrase is that there is (in what exact avenue of religious history I forget, but I hope I can be forgiven) a distinction between sins of commission, where you do something and sins of omission which are by their definition the results of inaction.

    Setting aside the unfortunate phrasing of "sin", I think the distinction between commission and omission is an interesting one here. For my money, every single day that a game remains available to the public is an act of commission by its author. There's work involved there, rest assured. The day that it ceases to be available to the public? That's omission. The author decided not to keep on working.

    Reversing those in your mind, so that you think that keeping the game available is a non-action, while letting it lapse from availability is an action ... that's really strange to me. I am hard-pressed to get my head around that thinking, except inasmuch as (like I said above) it is because people think that anything that varies from what they expected must have been pushed off of that (to their mind) natural and inevitable course by some external influence.

    Does that make sense?

    Posted By: TonyLBPosted By: JesseSo when you take the work away you just altered the lives of the sum of all those people out to the extinction of mankind.
    No. "Altered" implies that itwasone way, and now it's another. The future doesn't have some present existence that you can reach out and change. There is not astatus quoof the future, which people change when their actions don't measure up to what you were expecting.

    It's that very future-status-quo argument that's getting under my skin. The whole notion is both (a) intensely illogical and (b) emotionally hinky in some way I have yet to put my finger on.

    Excuse me, but I have to interject. The expectation of the future does exist in the present, and it does have value in the present (discounted by the rate of interest). When unexpected changes happen, the present value changes.

    Right now, everyone has to form expectations of the future, usually with probabilities of one future or another, in order to make decisions in the present. Should I adopt the Game System License or the Open Gaming License? Should I start a game of Burning Empires or wait until I get my hands on Mutant City Blues?

    Further back:\n
    You lost the value the instant you bought it. The only thing that happened later is that you learned that the value had been lost. Right?
    \nNot right. The value should have remained the same, or increased, the instant you bought it, because you realized its present value (by reading it, or shelving it to read later) and had expectations of its future value (by running it or buying supplements). If those expectations aren't realized, then the value only drops when you find out that something has changed. EDIT: It could also drop in the present if you read it and don't like it.

    Note that "running it" can't be taken away from you, but if the game doesn't become popular, then fewer people will be interested in playing it. You're betting on the future existence of a network externality, a large pool of excited players, which has value until it is realized and loses value when it becomes evident that won't happen.

    -Grant
