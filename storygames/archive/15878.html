\n My son is a big fan of Doctor Who and likes to dress up and pretend to be "the Twelfth Doctor" with his sonic screwdriver and his pad of slightly psychic paper. He's also done a tiny bit of roleplaying when we played Wushu a couple of years ago. But even when we did the Wushu thing, I ended up having to simplify the rules even more than they already were just so he could grasp the mechanics. He's autistic, and even though he's older now than he was the last time we played, he still has issues with complexity.

I'd love to do some Doctor Who roleplay with him \u2014 suggestions for straightforward scenarios he could work through are appreciated, so drop them here if you have any, because I'm not as well-versed in Doctor Who lore as he \u2014 but something like the Cubicle 7 Doctor Who game is way, way too much for him to handle. Does anyone have an idea about an uncomplicated resolution system that doesn't just boil down to "let him do whatever he wants"? I was thinking some variation on Apocalypse World's "roll two dice, 10+ is a flat-out success, slightly lower has complications, etc." might work, but I'm not a game designer by any stretch of the imagination and I don't know if that mechanic can be bettered.

Thoughts? Suggestions? Random commentary?

    \n Would this be too simple?

    The game begins with ten red stones on the table. This represents the problems on the new world you're visiting. When you land on the world, you don't know what the problems are.

    Each turn, you can do one of three things to help: Talk, Search or Make. When you Talk, this means you're making friends with someone or trying to scare someone. When you Search, this means you're finding clues about the problem. When you Make, you're creating a new gadget or helping to repair an existing one.

    After describing what you do to help, roll d6.

    If the result is 1-2, add that many red stones to the table. This means something you did made the situation worse or simply that you're running out of time.

    If the result is 3-6, remove that many red stones. This means that whatever you did is starting to help. That's no guarantee it'll work a second time, though!

    When there are no more red stones on the table, you saved the world!

    \n Daniel, I don't think anything is too simple when it comes to playing with my son. I'm sure he'd be happy with just freeform back-and-forth, but I want to impose some mechanical limits on that so it's more like we're playing a game and not just telling a story together. If that makes sense.

    Anyway, on to the stones: is there a point where there are a maximum number of stones on the table, where the Doctor fails to resolve the situation, or should we remove a failure option entirely from play to discourage a negative experience? When you say "you're running out of time," that makes me think there's an endgame in mind that isn't necessarily, "And then the Doctor saved the day." I'm not sure how my son would react to that, though.

    Some folks might think it's pointless to have mechanics at all if there's no way to "lose," but I'm trying to (as I say above) insert a game element into something that would otherwise just be talk. That way, should we decide to play other roleplaying games in the future, it won't be as much of a culture shock to suddenly be playing with rules.

    \n RichD, my son mostly likes the exploration/problem solving elements because they are very concrete things he can understand. For example, there's an episode \u2014 I can't remember which one, because I don't really watch the show that much \u2014 with really involved elevators that decontaminate the riders and he was completely fascinated by that. Weird, world-building things attract his attention, like the watchful heads in "The Beast Below" that change expression based on a person's behavior. So even more than "fight the aliens," he likes the Doctor's discovery of new and interesting setting elements which, while relatively simple in concept, works out to be fairly difficult in practice, especially if you're not the sort who comes up with stuff like this regularly. Which I am not. I write crime novels, not semi-fanciful science-fiction.

    Using the session of Wushu that we played as a model, I figure a successful Doctor Who game will involve the introduction of two to three novel setting elements that trip his particular triggers (elevators/mechanical stuff, computers, funny aliens) and then maybe a couple of problems to solve. He's not really ready for something that'll take hours and hours to complete, so figure 90 minutes on the outside?

    I know this is a tall order, which is why I'm crowdsourcing the design process. Making up games for kids is hard. Making up a game for a kid who doesn't think like other kids is an order of magnitude more difficult.

    \n Ah - the perfect time for you to learn about Matrix Games (I knew I'd get you to look at it somehow!)

    First, you son plays the Doctor. He makes things happen the way the Doctor would.

    You play everyone else and your job is to provide a steady stream of challenges to him.


    You start the game describing a scenario. You could even use the intro to a real Doctor Who episode. That is where the game starts. You say "And now the story goes off in a different direction..."

    You make up an event that happens next. Something interesting but not too strong a move. Think about what you could say happened in a paragraph.

    Then say "What happens next Doctor?'

    He makes up something, then you make up something, etc.

    Pretty early in the game jump in and challenge something he says. You say what happens instead. Both of you roll 2d6, the high roller wins, re-roll ties.

    Walk your way through the story and of course make certain that the Doctor wins. Your son will be practicing a lot of creativity and quick thinking with rules that almost don't exist.

    The written rules look something like this.

    1. Every player decides up front whither they want things to work or fall apart.
    2. Play goes around the table. Each player in turn says what happens next.
    3. Players may jump in and challenge any action if they say what happens instead. The high roller wins.
    4. The game ends when the players have resolved the action of the story.

    I find that games seldom last more than two hours (maybe only a half hour).

    You can't get simpler - and I have played such games with autistic children in the past (nearly twenty years ago now).

    Chis Engle

    Plot idea #2: He likes elevators, huh? Well check this one out: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_elevator.

    The Doctor arrives on Earth in the year 2250. The TARDIS has parked itself in a warehouse in the middle of the military base that houses the Space Elevator Control Center or as it is nicknamed, The Ground Floor. The Doctor is swiftly detained by authorities but some fast talk or psychic paper should get him on the side of the military. The elevator is not yet complete but is built enough that supplies and personnel can ascend via the elevator instead of by rocket. The base has been experiencing some troubling power fluctuations over the last few days. What they don't know is that energy based aliens trapped within the Earth's Van Allen Belt have tapped into the elevator's systems and are turning it into a massive aerial so that an invasion force can descend via the elevator cable and bypass the Van Allen radiation belt that has stopped all previous attempts at invasion. For added tension, the TARDIS was loaded onto the last shipment sent up to the satellite tethering the space elevator so the Doctor will either have to risk a trip up the cable or cobble together a rocket from the abandoned shuttles disused since the elevator could carry up its own cargo.
