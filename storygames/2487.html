The power of central authority

TonyLB
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
Over on another board, folks were discussing the benefit of God-GM play, and we got a little side-tracked (my fault!) on the question of whether central authority was a more workable social contract than others with more distributed authority. Max, quite reasonably, pointed out that he'd been in a lot of GM-less games that didn't work out too well. I have too! Is that just an accident? Nah. But, by that point, it was totally clear that I was off on a tanget, so I took it to PM with him, and he liked the PM so much that he recommended I repost it here, and people could discuss it more broadly. So, here it is! My fault, all mine, all the time, fault. Don't blame Max. He'd be sad.
Balbinus wrote:That said, my actual play experience (admittedly in con games which may be very relevant) is that it tends to still veer over time into gonzo, now that may just be poor luck on my part but it makes me less keen on shared authorial powers.
No, it probably isn't bad luck. Central authority is by far the most robust system when (as a system) it's built slightly wrong. It's like the Kalishnikov of the social contract world ... abuse it all you want, it's still probably going to work just fine. So if you're building house rules, and you use a central-authority system, you can probably screw up ten or fifteen other things, and never notice that they're screwed up.

All of the systems that I've seen that make shared authority work are trickier than that. They can still work, and they can take a pounding, but only if they're built right.

By contrast (and I may be reading too much into your experience, in which case please do rein me back in) I have seen a fair number of shared authority systems that people built wrong and never tested ... particularly at convention games.

A budding designer says to himself "Everyone should play the way my happy group at home plays, with shared authority and all that jazz, so I'll run a convention session that plays that way!" and so they make a system which is in all ways like a GM-central system, only they rip out the GM.

It's like saying "Freon is bad for the environment, so let's make a refrigerator with no cooling system of any kind! It'll rock!" Predictably enough, if you pull out the freon without figuring out what's going to take its place you end up with a refrigerator that performs very poorly.

So I'm totally on board with your experience. The fact that I know that GM-less systems can be built to work does not even begin to imply that any random GM-less system will work.

    This also seems to dovetail nicely with the partake vs. create gamer distinction being discussed in another thread - do you think that a distributed authority game can work with a group of players who prefer to partake?

    I have two thoughts on this.

    First, people who partake don't do so in exclusion to creating. They just don't care about creating and will be turned off by products that emphasize creating or enforce creating. So, if you distribute authority without requiring requiring creativity, you should be OK, with the proviso that partakers are mainly afraid of anything that inhibits the partaking experience. In other words, they don't want someone in their group to go out of control and start creating stuff that isn't part of the experience they bought into in the first place. "No Buffy in my Star Trek, dammit!"

    Second, a game without central authority is not necessarily a game with distributed authority, if you're limiting "central authority" to mean central human authority over what gets created. You can assign "creativity" to a process, like rolling on a result table or letting a book fall open at random, and let the players elaborate the resuts as they see fit.

    I think you can implement this with a combination of Donjon-style skill use (searching for a secret door creates one) and a rule similar to "say yes or roll the dice": create, or roll the dice. Most of the details deal with how to protect a group of protectors from a "rogue creator".

    I'm predicting that some groups organized on these principles will fall into a pattern resembling central authority, but it's more of a first-among-equals situation, where the additions of one person become respected, so that person creates more and more of the fictional material and the other players fall back to merely elaborating... but because the authority is "officially" distributed, the balance can shift at any time.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarTalk more about building right, Tony.
    Uh ... 'kay.

    I think of a lot of things (particularly in this type of game-design) as being cycles of feedback. Some cycles are positive: the more something happens, the more it will happen in future. Like sticking an electric guitar in front of the speaker it's plugged into, this will generate the most extreme behavior that the instrument is capable of. Sometimes it will do so quickly. Sometimes it will do so slowly. But it will do it, given time.

    Some cycles are negative: the more something happens, the less it will happen in future. Like pushing a rusty pendulum out of position, this type of system will allow variations from the mean, for a time, but will always wind its way back down toward that middle position, no matter how hard it gets pushed.

    I hope nobody is surprised to hear that I think the gonzo-over-the-top-explosion common to poorly designed decentralized games is a positive feedback cycle. At its most basic, I've seen this type of thing happen when nobody knows (starting out) what the limits are on their narration. Each narration emboldens people to step a little bit further. Each gonzo notion makes it more likely that gonzo notions will occur in future. Feedback, in its most classic "unbearable fingers-on-chalkboard noise emitting from a poorly adjusted instrument" sense.

    The first thing ... the very first thing ... that I think you need when designing a decentralized system is to know what negative feedback cycle is going to rein that effect in. Often (but not always) this will be the judgment of the players. Systems like Capes, PTA, Polaris and Universalis all have a step (often well hidden, but it's there!) where in order to take the next step into the gonzo, you need resources that only the other players can give you. Those resources are choked off when you go gonzo in a way that not everybody is on-board with. That's the negative feedback cycle: Go further gonzo then the other players enjoy, and you'll find yourself starving for resources, unable to take any further steps other than back toward the consensus center. The pendulum swings back, all without anyone having to stand up and be "the one who shuts you down."

    Everyone with me so far?

    Everyone get that having one guy (a.k.a. "the GM") apply that centering force directly is also a perfectly legitimate and reliable way to get that negative feedback?

    Also, I don't think it's enough to just hand people creative control, I think you need to provide a degree of support as well which people can use or ignore as needed.

    Take Wushu, a common actual play criticism I see of Wushu is description fatigue, people have moments in which they are inspired and moments in which they are getting a bit tired, but the rules provide no support when tired. As a result, the game can bog down at those times, as people struggle to come up with interesting material to add to the game.

    Ironically, the result can be that although the participants chose the game because they wanted direct creative input, not merely to participate, they end up feeling less creative as there is no support structure to assist them at moments when inspiration doesn't strike.

    There is also an issue of what you are seeking to create, a committee creation will often lack any real power as a story or indeed even as an experience. On rpg.net every now and again someone starts a create a setting thread. The rules for these threads are simple, each poster adds a setting detail but may not contradict any previously contributed setting detail, though they can qualify it. Essentially, it's a near systemless world creation game.

    In practice, the settings created are often not that great. Given enough contributants, someone always adds in magic, zombies, mecha or all three. The settings end up surprisingly uncreative, often very recognisable gamer type settings, even though the only participants were people wishing to actively create and even though many participants come up with highly original contributions. It's a kind of tragedy of the commons. Again though, the interesting bit (for me anyway) is that nobody is there simply to participate, everyone is there to be a creator, but the process is such that the creation is frequently not very interesting.

    Of course, it could just be that most people ain't that creative, but I think that's too simplistic an answer. I think the real answer is that the minimal rules of the setting creation game provide no real basis for supporting creativity.

    February 2007

    "Agree on creative input or roll the dice" might be a bad choice, because of the inspiration fatigue Max mentions. That's why my suggestion wasn't this, but "create or roll the dice": when you are handed creative control, you either add whatever you are inspired to, or roll dice to determine what is added and elaborate what the dice add. That's part your safety net. The other part is that there's no restriction on suggestions, so anyone can offer something to add at any time; it's just up to the one with current creative control to decide which suggestion to use.

    This doesn't address "partake protection", but I think that's better handled with a combination of creative guidelines you must use to fit the genre plus allowing suggestions, so that when something is added that not everyone's on board with, people can voice their objections. If the current creator doesn't back down, that's a broken social contract, anyways. "Agree on creative input or roll the dice" won't work here because the odds are someone's going to suggest a dealbreaker, dice for it, and win, which is still a broken social contract.

Also, in Sex and Sorcery you start off with characters and kickers and such -- and so before you hit the dice rolling AP part of the game you have some additional background and idea of what the players are focused on. Folks also have little problem starting out in Crime and Punishment, both due to the genre and the fact that you write your shit down first, following a formula that's pretty easy to get into.

    So genre, formula, and at least a few points of context (pictures, characters, pregenerated ideas) are always most helpful. Capes' click n lock are awesome here, because they let you physically tinker and from there get ideas as you move bits about.

  • talysman
    February 2007

    Protecting partaking I think is secondary, I'm not sure to be honest that shared narration games are best suited to those looking simply to partake. A more traditional GM led game would probably deliver that with less effort.

    Right, but as Tony mentioned at the start of the thread, we already know that central authority works for protecting partaking. The question here is whether it's necessarily the only way to protect partaking. Is central authority the only thing powerful enough to protect that kind of play? Is there necessarily a divide between high-creative-demand games (which work well with shared authority) and low-creative-demand participatory games (which work well with authority invested in a single creator?) Can you make shared authority work for the latter?

    And it's true, relying on objections is weak game design, which is why I didn't suggest relying on them, but rather pairing it with the creative guidelines. A very rudimentary example: in a move to do away with fixed skill lists, I've been using the idea of skills built on a template of adjective + concrete noun, like "elven sword" or "American automobile", which would grant bonuses when using/fixing/recognizing/remembering things about the noun in question. The idea here being that a setting can define historical limits on which kinds of adjectives and nouns are available and list exceptions to the adjective or concrete noun restriction that make sense for that setting (like allowing cultures as nouns in a modern day globetrotting setting, or adding adjectives like "elven" to a fantasy setting.) That's not a full-fledged set of guidelines, yet, but it suggests a way to handle them: make them production rules and restrictions, so that anyone following the rules can create something that fits within the setting.

    For "create or roll the dice", players choosing the first option would add something using production rules. Players who choose not to create out of whole cloth (because of a lack of inspiration or stage fright) would roll dice (or use some other randomizer) to select generic descriptors that become seed ideas that can be elaborated. We've seen things like this before in the form of GM aids: D&D/Toon random tables, or Rolemaster's rune cards (described in the Companion series,) or Whimsy Cards. It's something I used in the second draft of Co9C (motifs) and I'm using something similar in the Kanthe dungeoncrawl game I'm working on. The hard part of designing Kanthe is picking a good starting set of generic seed ideas that players can use. Once I have a good set, it's mostly gruntwork, writing out the production rules for each building block.

    I'm about to playtest the solo mode of the most recent version of Kanthe, but once I've finished, I'll post some details on this as an example of "create or roll the dice" in action.

    Fascinating link, thanks Andy.

    What I find interesting is that most story games have systems that demand shared responsibility for the "rules" (fan mail, gift dice) and the story (shared character creation, player approval moderation of input). I see the ritual negotiation in Polaris as integrating both of these factors very tightly.

    If the rules of the game already expect this level of input from the table, the main issues then become:

    - how to generate creative input
    - how to control creative input

    Having a set of creative "rules" (Comics code, Tenets, strong setting material) helps to do both, as they act as a creative constraint (look at GameChef for another example of this). Less obvious creative constraints include "shared character creation, all characters must be linked to each other in some way". Kickers and Bangs are the same, but directed (usually) at a single player. The rest is "just" social contract.
