\n This isn't so much of a big deal, and I certainly don't have any sort of authority around here, but I'm not a fan of user names. I prefer real names.

I feel like I can interact with you on a more human level if I know your human name. I use my real name because I want to be held accountable for what I do and say online, for better or for worse. It's easier for Wombat666 to call L33T801 a moron than it is for Brennen to say that to Alex.

There are a few exceptions, usually folks I've interacted with for a long time or have met in person, but I usually have a hard time giving the same weight to a statement made by as user name as I would to the same statement made by a human name.

I'm not trying to start a major flamewar, or make anyone change anything (unless they want to). Honest, thoughtful discussion is encouraged.

    \n Metadata, such as usernames, is how I've made my living for the past five years. My overall opinion is that these forums find a good balance by providing people the freedom to reveal or conceal their identity. Here's a few issues that don't seem to have come up yet:

    Be careful of believing the "real names" you see, because they might not be, even on the rollover. We're gamers. We know how to open up a phonebook to find a decent false name, and there's no identity verification process used by this site. You can claim your real name is Brennen Reece, but I have no way to verify that short of spending some good money or effort, enough that a username wouldn't hide your identity anyways.

    User names can be useful for differentiating people with the same name. For example, there are at least two Grahams that use this forum. If we're both talking in a thread, you'd have to use our full names, but a username differentiates us in fewer keystrokes.

    Real names are better for connecting people with their work, unless, of course, people also write under an assumed name or business name. Just ask any guy who writes pulp romance novels.

    There can be privacy issues, both legally (e.g. under Canada's laws, organizations are liable for any unauthorized use or release of any private information collected) and personally (e.g. worries about prejudice for reasons of gender).

    \n Anonymity (no known identity) is different from alternative identity (having separate identities for separate purposes), which is again different from obfuscated identity (having only one identity, but revealing only parts of it). Any and all of those are appropriate in different situations and for different reasons. I wouldn't trust an obviously invented person, for example, but do we need trust on a web forum?

    I myself practice complete transparency due to ideological reasons, believing that truth is better than lies. I do recognize that this is a sort of luxury that I couldn't afford if I opted to work for the Man, say - Epediah lists pretty good scenarios for what might happen as a consequence of transparent web activity nowadays. I have to admit that I admire people who share my habit of transparent Internet activity, simply because their choice affirms and strengthens the social trend of making Internet part of the world, and allowing all people the luxury of having opinions and being media activists therein without fear of reprisal. The opposite choice of hiding your identity in fear of consequences affirms the idea that public speaking is only suitable for professionals who control and profit from their public presence in a careful manner, and the average Joe shouldn't dare to do it, or he might lose his job or go to jail; should the majority adopt this received wisdom, that'll make the road towards strong digital democracy that much longer. There is strength in numbers, so I can't help but think that everybody who chooses to conduct their Internet business with a strong, real identity is doing their part to make Internet be a bit more about people and communication instead of faceless masses and premeditated corporate media.

    Aside from the slight ideological admiration, the real name mostly doesn't matter to me in a forum context. I don't much care if a person presents himself with an obviously fantastical name, for example; a non-arbitrary alternative identity is good enough for most purposes. Complete anonymity starts to matter due to how people react to the power provided by anonymity - that power is rarely used responsibly, which makes me inclined to discount anonymous contributions.

      \n Thinking about this more, there seem to be two schools of thought on this.

      The first goes like this: the internet is a place that can lack the civility of the real world because people are not as accountable for their actions as they would be face to face. So, if we run our community by ignoring the fact that we are faceless strangers thousands of miles apart and, instead, conduct ourselves as if we were a small face-to-face community where everyone knows each other on a first name basis (sort of like Cheers), then people will automatically conduct themselves closer to how they would face to face, and better communication will result.

      The other school of thought has three objections concerns with this:

      1) This approach ignores the reality that we actually are faceless strangers thousands of miles apart and that this fact really does matter. In some ways (some of which are mentioned in previous posts), pretending that this is not true actually makes communication worse (particularly if some don't buy into the delusion).

      2) The "hey, we all know each other by our first names, we're like family" approach from strangers is also used by another group of people on the internet: spammers, scammers and other criminals. Years of conditioning against such cretins builds a strong reaction against the "Hi faceless internet stranger! I'm Bob! What's your name? This isn't really the faceless internet around here. Come be my friend!" approach.

      3) On a subliminal level there is an underlying accusation of "we're certain that if you don't give your real name that you will not be able to conduct yourself civilly here." No one actually says (or even means) this, of course, and this is not a huge deal, but just as aliases give some a reduced first impression, a policy like this can hit the wrong chord in someone as well. Mixed with this is also a feeling of "we think it is perfectly appropriate to demand that you sacrifice your privacy to play with us" that just rubs some people (like me) the wrong way.

      I suspect that one reason real names seem to be a bit more favored at Story Games is that, when this forum started, many of the "alpha posters" (or whatever the term is) had already met face-to-face at least once. (I agree with wundergeek Anna Kreider that once you meet someone, you are more likely to "hear" their posts in their own voice and inflection, and that this matters a lot more than you might think.)
