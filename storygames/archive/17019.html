    \n If it were me, the game would start in media res with the PCs having just discovered/captured the ...thing. Who are the PCs? Normal folk. It'd be as much about the characters and their everyday lives as it is about discovering the details of the cryptid (could turn out to be an alien or robot too). I'd want it to be able to go multiple ways - maybe it's a fiasco where you take it home and it wrecks your house and then eats you before you can line up a buyer, maybe the government wants its experiment back but it's giving you those puppy eyes, maybe it's a chupacabra really ugly dog but your kid will be happy with it and it's free, or maybe it's not the last of its kind but the first to arrive. I think I'd want players to have a say in the cryptid to start off with (world- cryptid-building) but the GM to be able to reveal new abilities/others who know about it/more like it/etc as complications during play (hard moves?).

    \n I am all about asking my players provoking questions to get things started so here goes:\xa0

    "So this weird thing showed up recently, what are you most worried about with it around? Why?"

    Are they worried about the danger that the thing presents or that the thing is in? Are they worried about discovery? Are they worried about someone being a bad influence on it?

    \xa0What is going on in your life that this thing might be able to fix? How?

    \xa0That could be any thing from proving to your wife that you are ready for kids by taking care of the cute alien to needing money to keep your house and a T-rex pelt will surely make you the money you need.\xa0The job of the GM is to make sure that whatever the player wishes that the thing could fix can't be fixed by the creature.\xa0

    Marshall's idea about letting the players define the creature is brilliant. I would play that bit, as much as possible, like the blind men describing the elephant with the GM, or the best artist, drawing the image of the thing they are describing.\xa0

    \n Well, I have notes on this.

    For me the interest in such a game lies with the fact that cryptozoology is about hunting down mystery. In a world where the deep edges of the map are increasingly colored in, what else is out there? Is there anything out there? I personally have this tension with Cryptozoology: I want them to fucking find the Sasquatch & the Loch Ness monster, y'know? Because that would be awesome. But I also kind of love that maybe there are things out there that we haven't found and will never find, right under our noses.

    Cryptozoology also lives in tension with science. It's not a science, but if it actually finds something, "Science" takes it away and brings it into its domain.

    So, what would the game look like? I dunno, but those are the themes I'd want to play with.

    \n The thing that intrigues me is the notion that you can be obsessive about it, and devote your life to it, and never know. I'd make a game where the truth was always ambiguous and beyond your grasp, and the longer you pursued it the crazier it made you. I'd make it abusively cruel, like Fat Man Down, where the players don't even know it isn't possible to find a cryptid, and make things like family and savings and reputation all burnable resources to "get closer to the truth". As you destroy your character's life you are rewarded with blurry photos and anomalous hairs. The only way to win is to stop playing.

    \n 4. Having just found a cryptid, what now?

    Jason - It'd be neat to have a procedure that uses a bunch of tags to generate the cryptid but then you deconstruct the cryptid you've made to generate the 'convincing' evidence that you find along the way. Like, you'd use the detail 'mangy fur' to help you design your chupacabra but then when you go looking, you find bits of mangy fur, thus confirming your belief in its existance. The circular logic of such a mechanism strikes me as particularly appropriate. That could also create a game timer - the game ends when you've found all this evidence which 'proves' the cryptid's existance (though you still haven't found it).

    Introducing extra tags later could spark debate among the PCs experts - a feather, chupacabra don't have feathers - what if they do - but they don't! - maybe they eat something with feathers? - I'm writing a paper about how they have feathers - etc..

    The way information about cryptids is transmitted is also interesting - personal journals, documentaries, conventions, books, "papers" in "scientific journals", transcripts of calls to 911, etc. It'd be cool if the game generated some sort of faux cryptozoological document.

    \nIn the real world, when people find these blurry photos and anomalous hairs, they have a peculiar type of self-delusion that convinces them that such evidence is crucial. Importantly (and this is the part that interests me), they are usually wired into a subculture that exists almost entirely to reinforce this self-delusion. So, you get a weird echo chamber filled with investigators in total denial with a collective persecution complex.

    \n The nice thing about Cryptozoology and roleplaying is that they have a lot in common:

    -The vast majority of Cryptozoologists do not make their living doing this and squeeze it in as a side-job or hobby
    -It is a dubious activity looked down upon at worst and looked weird upon at average by the mainstream
    -It's fucking strange, and people tend to be obsessive about it
    -It's fucking cool, and it can draw interesting and intelligent folks in

    I always love it when games not about games can be a commentary about game culture and the thing they're about simultaneously.

    \n All the players are fledgling cryptozoologists. Have everyone write yes or no on a 3x5 card and place it face down. This is their vote as to if the cryptid exists. The goal of the players that vote no is to get the other players to spend more resources than they do. They have to be convincing or they'll be ferreted out. No one player can find the cryptid by themselves but working together they will all attempt to spend as little as possible but still do what they can to find the creature.

    Once some threshold is reached, the cards are flipped. If the no votes win, the search is a bust and all resources spent are lost. If the yeses spent more than the no votes, the no votes win having not ruined their lives. If the yes votes win the creature is found and they are showered with praise and fame.

    Edit: the no votes have to have a compelling advantage to say no and be party poopers. Something like they get praise and fame by exposing the other players as charlatans and frauds. I guess it really depends on if this is a one shot or a continuing game. The next game, maybe they start off with more resources and better reputation?
