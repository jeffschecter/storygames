\n Hi guys,

I have been working on an action hero game called 'Reel Adventures' for a little while now. The basic idea is that it is a very low setup time game, where everything is defined during play by a resource called presence, which everyone starts with. When you run out of your starting presence the only way to get more is to overcome pitfalls set in your way, so basically you are defining yourself through action.

The other facet of the game is that you create a nemesis to defeat. The game is split into 3 acts and a finale. In each act each player takes it in turns to frame a scene, maybe gaining a resource, history or trait that adds to their depth. Each scene they frame they gain momentum as well, which is essentially the force of the plot against their character. If, at the start of a scene, a player has more momentum then depth then they must frame a nemesis scene, and flesh him out a bit more.

Simple, I think.

The problem is that I have been inside the game so long that I am getting stuck as I approach the end. Their are some small things that need sorted and I keep getting myself in a muddle as to how to fix them.

1) At the moment there are a limited number of things a player can do to their character in a scene, and that is currently making it very hard to define yourself through action. If you create a pitfall, which you do as an action, and then overcome it that takes up your two slots for a scene and doesn't allow you to increase your depth which would be ideal. I could just increase it to 3 slots but that has proved too powerful in the past and negates the delicate momentum/depth balance.

2) I was explaining the game to a friend the other night and she said that she couldn't really identify with the characters and that it was more storytelling than roleplaying. This is a fair point but it got me wondering if the game should involve the players more.

3) At the moment you basically set pitfalls for yourself which is a bit dull. One idea is to have scene framing such that the narrator says what they want to do and the other players round the table narrate enemy, allies and further complications into the scene.

4) We were discussing over on the CE forums whether the game is Sim or not. Now I don't really care too much for those definitions, I just design a game until it works and if people want to call it that then fine. I was wondering what opinion here would be though.

Well that is all for now, I can email anyone a copy of the game if they want to get a more indepth look at it.

Cheers

Iain

    1) At the moment there are a limited number of things a player can do to their character in a scene, and that is currently making it very hard to define yourself through action.     \nIt's difficult. OK.

    What would you want a player to do during a scene? It sounds as though you want something like:

    1. Tackle a difficulty (whether that's already created or not)
    2. Add something to your character based on the result

    so that, as you say, you're defining your character through action. If the difficulty is, say, a guard; and you pull out a gun and shoot him; then you add the trait "Big gun".

    Taking that in conjunction with your third point:\n
    3) At the moment you basically set pitfalls for yourself which is a bit dull.
    \nit'd go something like:

    1. Overcome a difficulty that's created by someone else (either on the spot or previously)
    2. Add something to your character based on the result

    If you want that, you could change the central mechanics to do that.

    I am getting the feeling that the central mechanics need a big tweak of some sort: at the moment, it looks as though you've got the classic problem that the most effective way to play isn't the most fun.

    By the way, the problem about setting pitfalls for yourself is a classic Forgey thing. It's variously called the Lesson From Chalk Outlines or the Czege Principle. Might be worth a search.\n
    4) We were discussing over on the CE forums whether the game is Sim or not. Now I don't really care too much for those definitions, I just design a game until it works and if people want to call it that then fine. I was wondering what opinion here would be though.
    \nI do understand the suspicion of those definitions.

    I guess I think it might help to clarify whether Reel Adventures is:

    1. A celebration of action movies, where we all revel in taking part in our own action movie (Simulationism)
    2. A character-based game about heroism or (Narrativism)
    3. A game where the fun is in the narrator throwing down challenges to the players (Gamism)

    (I don't really think it's 3, just including it for completeness).

    I'm not an expert on these terms, by the way, so I may be using them wrong. But I do know that, when designing A Reason To Murder, I wanted the game to be:

    1. A celebration of English murder mysteries
    2. An exploration of family relationships and
    3. A game where you try to pin the murder on someone else

    And, once I realised I couldn't do all of those in one game, it got easier.

    If you decide that (whether it's a celebration of action movies or a character-driven game), I think that'll solve your second problem:\n
    2) I was explaining the game to a friend the other night and she said that she couldn't really identify with the characters and that it was more storytelling than roleplaying. This is a fair point but it got me wondering if the game should involve the players more.
    \nIf the fun lies in celebrating action movies, identifying with characters doesn't matter: they'll probably be generic as all hell. If it's a game about characters, the characters are all that matter.

    How does that sound? Does any of it make sense?

    \n Sounds like you're making some big changes since I went round that night. As always, it doesn't matter what I want from your game, it matters what you want it to be, so I'm glad you're only taking some of my advice. I know I look for games where I identify strongly with my character, so if something is billed as an RPG, that's what I expect to see; this isn't always true though, but I would recommend that you take into account people's expectations when pitching your game to the crowds and masses. I've been involved in several games which people didn't really expect to be high in the character interaction but which I thought would be, so I was quite unsatisfied because I didn't realise why no-one was playing the game 'right'.

    Regarding your post and ideas: I'd worry about allowing the Threat pool to reduce too much; you have to build to a climax in an action movie. It is true that the threat has to be completely nullified at the end though, so perhaps you could work that it somehow...?

    I like the idea of removing the totally arbitrary stats and replacing them with backgrounds; I think you might have noticed the stress I was putting on motivation for action heroes, and I would say that background and motivation are the key reasons why Action Heroes do anything.

    We should definitely get in some hardcore action movie viewing time at some point. Perhaps a day of Die Hard/Lethal Weapon/Dirty Harry-ing in the not too distant future?
