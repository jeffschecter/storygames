Where is the money?

TonyLB
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games

This thread arose out of Fred/iago's reiteration of a common concern: namely, that the process of producing, marketing and selling your game is, for many, far harder and less intuitive than the act of creating the game itself.

So, I have ... knowledge. Wisdom? Maybe. Knowledge? Definitely. And I'm totally keen to help people not be mystified by this process.

Producing

You print books. All other things being equal, you want to print them as cheaply as possible. The basic factors that will conspire against that: Quality printing (quality costs) and small print runs.

Personal Experience: Capes is 160 pages, 6x9, perfect bound. It is mid-upper range in terms of book quality, and prints in batches of 100. It costs $5 per book.

If you want a better book (hardcover color holographic, for instance) you pay more than that. But, mainly, if you want to print smaller print runs (particularly if you want to print on demand, one at a time) you pay more for that privilege. If you're willing to tie up some of your capital in inventory you can save money by taking advantage of economies of scale.

Marketing

People are not going to spontaneously decide one day "I wonder if there's a game that does the following exact things," and search and search until they find your web-site. They should. It would be the responsible thing to do. But they won't.

They will look into your game because they are infected with enthusiasm, which almost always happens in one of a few ways:

  • They hear it talked about or referenced, not once but many times.
  • They read actual play
  • They get a chance to play it before they buy
  • They know you, either personally or through reputation, and transfer their personal feelings about you onto your products.

The act of marketing is, quite simply, making as many of those things happen as possible. There are (to my way of thinking) two venues that provide obvious benefit for very low cost: Internet (where you broadcast your enthusiasm and coolness very ineffectually, but to a vast number of people) and conventions (where you broadcast your enthusiasm very effectively, but to a much smaller number of people).

Whatever your individual tactics turn out to be, I recommend that you keep working at them incessantly. People who keep hammering away at the very basics sell more games. People who make an occasional effort, then lapse into passivity waiting for customers to come to them sell less games.

Marketing is where many of your expenses above-and-beyond the books will come in. It costs money to travel to conventions, it costs money to buy in on joint print advertisements, it costs money to maintain a web-site, to print flyers, to create a poster, to laminate demo-cards, etc., etc,. etc. These costs are (if things go well) a way of plowing your profits back into growing your business, so it's not wasted money, but you should figure for it in your costs.

Selling

The physical act of getting your book or PDF into somebody's hands, and accepting their money in return, can be a bit daunting too. It's really easy at (say) Double Exposure conventions: they let us set up a table, charging us nothing, we hand books to people and put their money in our pocket.

But most of the time it's more complicated: If a convention doesn't give you free space then you have to pay for it (and think real hard about whether you want to spend, say, two hundred dollars to sell books at a convention that may only sell ten books).

And then, most sales happen online these days. You're not physically handing your book to somebody, or accepting their cash and stuffing it in your pocket. Someone is accepting their money online (usually by way of PayPal) and that same someone is shipping them the book (or setting them up to download the PDF).

If that someone is you then you have some costs: storing your inventory (often a cost only of closet space), maintaining a web-site, and like that. But, mainly, the cost is going to be of your time. You'll be going to the post office a fair amount, shipping books all over the place.

If you partner with someone else (IPR, Key20, etc.) then they will handle the fulfillment of those orders for you. And, of course, they'll charge you for it.

The ultimate extent of this is traditional distribution. Distributors advance retail stores credit, and push product onto bookshelves (with various levels of aggressiveness) and all that stuff. And, of course, they charge an arm and a leg. I don't think mainstream distribution has much to offer a starting indie publisher, although I can talk about my distaste for the model if you haven't already soaked it up from the atmosphere.

Pricing

Say you've got a complete game that costs you $5 to produce. You are prepared to market it, and sell it. What do you sell it for?

Tell me you didn't just say $15. I said $15, way back when. I mean ... the point was to get the game into people's hands, right?

Y'know what? NO. The point is to increase your own ability to maintain and support the product in a variety of ways all of which cost money. Marketing it costs money. Selling it costs money. That money, plus the price of your book, is your overhead. Your price must reflect your overhead.

Personal Experience: I expect, within the next year, to release two products, each for $7. But, they'll each cost less than $2 per book to produce, and I don't expect to market them worth a damn. They'll get marketing from the coat-tails of Capes. So I don't have as much overhead. If the books don't take off, of course, I know exactly where to lay the blame ... my own stupidity.

So what happens if your price doesn't reflect your overhead? Well, no matter what your sales are like, you will discover a few months into the process that you simply do not have enough money to do all the things for your book that you'd like to do. You don't have the cash in the business account to buy a banner stand, or to print a banner to put on it. You don't have the cash to go to GenCon this year. You don't have this money because past marketing has not paid off in cash ... it's paid off in sales, but because each of those sales is not producing enough profit, it hasn't paid off in absolute dollar terms.

When you do not have enough money to continue to grow your book, you will either (a) spend more money than you've earned, which is a slippery slope into really really bad business practice or (b) scale back your marketing efforts, which is a vicious cycle of ever-dwindling sales and profits. Those are not good options. They are not good for you, they are not good for your game, and they are not good for the many, many people who will never get to hear about your game because you don't have the wherewithal to get the message to them.

'kay. I've rambled in a long-winded and wandering fashion. If anyone has questions or comments, I rather expect that's where the useful material of this thread will emerge.

    Okay, first the low-hanging fruit: Now ... I'm not an enemy of Lulu. Clinton works at Lulu and they seem to be really good folks doing really good work, with some price-points in certain combinations that are very compelling. But $6 for an 84 page softcover? That doesn't strike me as a good price-point for your purposes. It might well be for somebody who is selling less units, but you should be taking advantage of your high volume to reduce your prices.

    You've sold 47 copies at $3 gross money for you on each. You currently have no inventory, and have made $141. Which is good, yes!

    But suppose you'd done a run with a short-run print company, for 100 copies at (say) $3 each? You'd spend $300 up front, then have sold 47 copies at $10 gross money for you on each. You would have made $470 gross, minus your cost of $300, means $170 net profit to date. And you'd have 53 books of inventory, which would be another $530 of money with no further costs, if you ended up selling them all (which you probably would).

    Now 84 pages is a tricky page count (a little bit too big for 16-folio saddle-stitch, a little too small for really effective perfect binding) so you might not get as good a deal as $3. You'd have to shop around. But even at $4 (which I imagine you'd be able to find) you'd already be within about ten sales of catching up with the $6 POD, and from there on it's still all pure extra profit.

    Like I said, that's low-hanging fruit. If you're pretty sure you're going to sell 100 copies within a few months, it simply makes more sense to save money on a 100-copy run than to pay more for 100 1-copy runs.

    Now, your price point. Not the low-hanging fruit. :-)

    Your argument ("How many less would I have sold at $13?") is, in economics terms, an argument about elasticity. How much will people shift their buying to other products (or away from RPGs entirely) as your price rises, and conversely how much will they buy more of your product as your price falls?

    You seem to be making the argument that demand for RPGs is extremely elastic, with respect to price. The number of people who would buy at $13 is less than half the number who would buy at $10.

    All my experience is that demand for RPGs is in the long term extremely inelastic. Note the "in the long term." RPGs generally, and indie RPGs particularly, tend to be evergreen products. They continue to generate sales month after month, year after year, in a way that other products don't, and people can keep something "on their list" for years, but they do eventually buy.

    What I am arguing here is that if you priced your product at $13 you would have had far fewer sales in your first four days. Granted, without contest. But I suspect that within a month or two, if you continued marketing (so that people don't forget about you) probably 90% of the customers who bought at $10 would have eventually chosen to buy at $13. In fact, I suspect that (if DRYH is a complete system) they'd buy at $15 ... possibly even at $20, though there is some elasticity as price rises.

    So ... y'know ... not going with the distribution-retail chain? I'm right with you there. But pricing yourself deliberately low because you think your product isn't good enough to attract interest in any way other than a ridiculously low sticker price? I've done the same thing, and concluded that it was a serious mistake.

    That is the statement of a person who is operating a business by spending money they have already earned rather than spending the money they hope to earn. That's how you make a business where money trickles out rather than one where money pours in. (EDIT: Out, from the business, into your hands ... rather than out of your hands and into the business ... sorry, that was unclear)

    Now that is where I see the huge strength of Lulu in this market. They let you bootstrap off of a pure and undiluted strategy of "no external investment." I could totally recommend the following strategy:

    • Find out precisely how much a print run of 100 copies is going to cost you.
    • Sell through Lulu until your pure-profit accumulates that much money.
    • Plow that profit into a short print run.
    • Ship that short print run directly to a pre-arranged fulfillment house and seamlessly switch your sales over to them.
    • Use the profit from the first 100-copy batch to pay for the next one plus pay for you to attend cons, advertise your book, etc.
    • When you have enough left-over money for a larger print run, do a larger print run instead of a smaller. Profits increase, and you have to do print runs less often.
    • Eventually get bored of marketing and expanding, and just let things run in the same profitable rut, spending your excess profits on posh lifestyle (get the fancy ramen, and maybe some imported beers!)

    That bootstrapping from nothing has a great deal of appeal to me, particularly because at any point in the process (including before your first sale) the whole thing can collapse and you can walk away with a fun experience that paid for itself.

    Sure. It took a day to gel in my head what exactly I was talking about.

    The content of a game helps determine its pricing. Burning Wheel is a Fantasy game in a sea of Fantasy games. You have to play it to find out that it's actually one of the best ones.     Fortunately. the big publishers have done a lot of our work for us: D&D and GURPS cost like $70 for the basic books and WoD games are $40 for the worldbook and your monster of choice.     For the record, I don't think your success is because of your low price. I think your success is because of your excellent product and fanatical devotion to its promotion. But the pricing almost certainly gave you a leg up. I'm sure glad you raised it, though.
