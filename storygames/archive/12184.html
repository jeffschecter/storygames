\nLike a rat leaving a sinking ship, I am reposting a comment I made in the "[GNS] Please Stop" thread. For it to make sense, you have to know that a French sociologist named Roger Caillois produced a typology of play in his book Man, Play, and Games in which he described play as comprising four different elements, including agon (competitive games and sports), alea (games of chance or fortune), mimesis (games of make-believe), and ilinx (playful vertigo). I wondered if the Big Model's notion of Creative Agenda could be usefully reconstructed with Caillois's types, and it turned out that some other folks had similar thoughts. So I posted this right before Andy pulled the plug on the thread, but he said it'd be cool if I reposted it in a new thread.

Part One: Why Bother With Alternatives to GNS?\n
Posted By: Eero Tuovinen
I especially agree that while challenging the existence of CA seems ludicrous to me, I could well imagine alternative or even more useful descriptions of of the entire field than GNS. . . . because Creative Agenda has many parts that can be tickled better or worse by different approaches -- different techniques. I'd like to say that the best thing I get out of GNS is that once I'm able to clearly verbalize that I want to do premise-ful play or whatever, that allows me to set aside all the other vistas and focus better on the smaller nuances that were never available to me when I spent all my time trying to negotiate the big basics.
\nYes, exactly. The notion that descriptions of CA like GNS should be at root pragmatic game design tools strikes me as dead on. The further implication is that, because of the multiplicity of possible agendas for play, any particular description of "why people play" or "what people get out of playing" serves a normative function as well, helping to create the culture of play it purports to describe--at least insofar as people subscribe to it as a guide to design and play. This is as clear a case of the reflexivity of knowledge as one could ever hope to encounter.

In the case of GNS, one thing that it does very well--as Eero rather astutely points out--is call attention to a mode of play that it calls "narrativism." When you think about it, you realize that this mode is actually rather obscure, a kind of play that works by having one stand in a kind of alienation or distance from the character one plays in order to more strongly appreciate the moral weight of the decisions the character makes--this in contrast to the strong identification with character that is the desideratum of immersive play and the elision of character that is the hallmark of competitive play.

One side effect of this way of slicing up the phenomenon, as people use it to explore the design space that results, is that purely gamist designs become interesting as a way of examining the negative space of narrativism--in other words, of challenging the basic assumption of narrativist play and saying, hey, what if there's no character, not really, it's all just a contest between us here at the table, via the medium of a set of rather elaborately detailed pawns. It recapitulates the hoary old narratology versus ludology debate in a new form.

But another side-effect is to make "simulationism" inexplicable, or even pathological. I remember seeing a post on Vincent Baker's blog to the effect that while he understood gamism and he understood narrativism, simulationism struck him as puerile wish fulfillment power fantasy at best. I seem to recall the subordination of simulationism in some other context having driven David Berg to distraction, given his efforts to make sense of and reclaim the term. And I think the reason for that is that once you make yourself aware of the distance between player and character, and use that as a tool in your designs, then you have a tough time with a mode of play in which minimizing or hiding that distance is a sine qua non.

So in devising an alternate set of CAs, the answer to the "why bother" question (i.e., "why bother coming up with an alternative to GNS?") has to be in order to reveal a different space for play, or to open up new possible avenues for design, or minimally to render us more sensitive to the alternatives that may exist.

Part Two: Reconstructing Creative Agendas

What follows should be read with an eye on Eero's remark that the absence of narrativism from Callois's scheme has to be attributed to the paucity of "premise-ful" modes of play in the instances of play he examined, and that were he around today he'd surely incorporate that into his scheme. In other words, our theories should be constructed in light of the empirical evidence available. On the other hand, I always assumed that the reason that Caillois identified four categories--rather than three or five, say--had something to do with the linguistic structuralism associated with the Paris School of Semiotics, in which a huge semantic space is produced by the conceptual entanglement of opposites. So a "positive" term implies (the existence of) a corresponding negative, like yes implies no. But those terms can be "entangled" as both yes and no (i.e., "maybe") and as neither yes nor no (i.e., "I don't know"). Similarly, agon (skill) implies its opposite alea (not-skill: luck or chance). And those opposites can be entangled such that "both agon and alea" can be understood to mean mimicry, as successful mimicry is at once a skillful (albeit not wholly competitive) performance as well as a willingness to subject oneself to the dictates of fortune. By the same token, ilinx is clearly "neither agon nor alea" in that the dislocations or dissociations one experiences in ilingic play obviate both skill and luck.

Now, in this post-structuralist era, we don't believe that our categories are in any way essential or fundamental; they are analytic conveniences only.

So what happens if we imagine agon, alea, mimicry, and ilinx as the corners of a semiotic square? First of all, we notice that the underlying distinction in both the agon-alea and mimicry-ilinx pairs has something to do with the activity of the player; in the former, the player is active while in the latter the player is passive, in the sense that he or she is the object of the forces acting in the game, whether that\u2019s the turn of the wheel of fortune or the elasticity of the bungee rope.



Secondly, we notice that the underlying distinction in both the agon-mimicry and the alea-ilinx pairs seems to be that the former in both instances presume the unalloyed agency of the player whereas the latter see the player as part of the field of play. In other words, whereas in both agon and alea you play the game, in both mimicry and ilinx the game plays you. You adopt a new persona, take on a new role, feel scared or excited, or otherwise lose control of yourself.

But these dualities are capable of being entangled, and we can use those entanglements to rebuild the CAs. We\u2019ll follow Eero in suggesting that the combination of agon and alea is the Contest, in which players are interested in victory as the outcome of play. Conversely, the combination of mimicry and ilinx is Drama; players are interested in experiencing thematically resonant moments as the outcome of play. So far we\u2019ve reconstructed the Gamism/Narrativism dichotomy in slightly different terms.

Looking at the orthogonal dimension, starting with the combination of agon and mimicry, I\u2019d suggest that the appropriate metaphor or model is the Pageant, where players are interested in the skillful performance or faithful recreation of alternate roles. Its converse, the combination of alea and ilinx, is the Thrill Ride, in which players are willing to subject themselves to external control in return for the promise of a powerful or pleasant emotional experience. The effect of this, I\u2019d say, is to reconstruct Simulationism as a kind of \u201cagonistic mimicry\u201d that stands in the same relationship to \u201cImmersionism\u201d as Narrativism does to Gamism.

    \nOkay.

    So, I don't know if you've ever seen my blog Play Passionately but it's pretty much dedicated to tearing down this notion that you, somehow have to "distance" yourself from the character to achieve effective Story Now play. My whole argument is that in doing so you actually end up making "The Story" this artifact that sits on the table that everyone kind of wrestles over. Play becomes a lifeless intellectual exercise.

    The heart of my ideas is that for stronger more powerful Story Now play one should construct a character that is burning with issues of deep personal importance to the player himself. As example here is a character I would never build:

    Lieutenant Smith knows that his best friend since high school and fellow officer is dealing drugs at the local boot camp. Smith's commanding officer has just put him in charge of investigating the drug supply problem.

    Pretty weighty character right? He's rife will all kinds issues about friendship and hierarchical authority and the like. So he must be perfect for Story Now play, right? Not for me. I have absolutely no personal investment in these things. I would probably watch a movie about this guy. It might even emotionally move me. But there's a difference between CONSUMPTION and CREATION. I have no problem consuming a story about this guy because I trust the creators will fill the emotional gap I have through effective communication via the medium. But if you put me in charge of actually creating this guy's story, I'd do a pretty poor job beyond my intellectual understanding of his issues.

    On the other hand here IS a character I've created.

    The character was a young prince who had an older sister. She was about to become queen and had royal suitors from foreign lands. Also the young prince's best friend was love with the young prince's sister but he was of too low a station to be worthy of marrying the would-be queen. My character pretty much could not handle this. He wanted his sister to abdicate and marry his friend. He wanted the three of them to run away from the kingdom so they could be friends forever like they were as children. The result was that he grew more and more, obsessive and more and more over-protective of his sister. In the end he tried to kidnap her but he failed and was forced to go into exile.

    THIS character I have intense emotional identification with. I have a deep personal connection with The White Knight Complex (i.e. a man's desire to protect and rescue the women in his life). I do not deal well with changes beyond my control. I am an only child and have always felt the lack of siblings, in particular I have always wanted a sister.

    If you want to know just how close to home this character is I will say this: This situation partially mirrors a rather difficult real-life situation I was dealing with at the time. Even CLOSER to home, the other party involved in the real-life situation was an active player in this game. She knew full-well what this situation was a metaphor for and she had enough trust and faith in my creative honesty to actually HELP me develop this situation for my character rather than perceive it as an attack. Quite frankly she was the perfect player to have at the table because she could provide exactly the right kind of adversity I needed to fully tackle the issues at stake for my character.

    I can't really imagine any stronger character identification than that. When people tell me that they find Story Now, "distancing" the only conclusion I can draw is that it is because, to them, high identification Story Now must seem frightening. And, quite frankly, it is. In Story Now play the issues are "live." There's no guarantees or safety nets which means that if you are personally deeply involved in those issues the opportunities to get hurt are quite numerous.

    But it's in that pit of living writhing personal issues that I like to play.

    \nThe Author and Hero in Role-Playing Games

    The idea that there's a variable distance between the player and character in an RPG (broadly construed) that depends on how you approach the game is more controversial than it might appear at first, since you'd expect the assertion that "you are not your character" would be accepted without demur almost everywhere. Not so fast, though! It's possible to argue--and it has in fact been argued here at Story-Games that only gamers worry too much about the distinction. The position that you both are and are not your character in a way that's mediated by the game in question is taken by Luke Crane at the end of that discussion.

    So when I talk about "distancing" I'm talking about the position the game takes with respect to the question, "Are you your character?" The answer is usually going to be "Yes and no," and the specific configuration of when yes and when no is an important element of game-play. Even if you've never heard of the literary critic Mikhail Bakhtin, who talks about the way the author can manipulate the authorial distance between the character and himself or herself to create a meaningful narrative effect, the idea that the author isn't the character but is in some fashion responsible for the character (in all the different ways we can mean "responsible") won't be alien to you.

    There's an old discussion on the Forge (503 Service Temporarily Unavailable at the moment) where Ron Edwards talks about playing kpfs and being struck by the experience of play. You create this unattractive pimply loser character who is essentially an ineffectual jerk at the lowest rungs of the hierarchy of the damned, not trusted enough to commit anything other than the most banal of evil acts. And you play that character, who both is you and isn't you at the same time, and in the end if you're lucky you feel some semblance of sympathy despite your disgust for this character because, well, you made him.

    Ultimately, I think this is point of what I'm going to call Drama-oriented games: to in one form or another appreciate the character not as an alter ego but as a fictional one. In a Contest-oriented game, there may in fact be no character as such; I have a "character," but it's primarily a tool for playing the game: think the sort of D&D dungeoncrawl where you say "we don't need no steenking role-playing here." Call it stance if you like, but it implies a fundamental orientation to the game rather than mere ornamental technique.

    Another way of dealing with the player/character gap, though, is to minimize it. At Knutepunkt in 2009, I listened to a talk by Johanna Koljonen on the tools larpers use to deal with the "hole" produced by the fact that player has far fewer in-character cognitive resources than the fictional character would were the character real. One I remember I particularly liked, she called "filling the hole with stupid." She talked about these Nordic larpers playing German guards in WWII. They didn't speak German for the most part, but they could be taught a few words and phrases: "Waffen haben," "Cigaretten haben," and so forth, using these to inhabit the characters as their fairly limited alter egos.

    \nThis is a real methodological concern. I will cop to playing fast and loose with Greimas's method, but only on the authority of Fredric Jameson, who recommends stealing the bits and pieces you like, although he lets us call it bricolage. The approach that I usually take involves reconstructing a system of distinctions created by someone else. In other words, once you've established the essential opposition between your simple positive and simple negative terms (e.g., "yes" and "no"), identifying the complex terms ("maybe" and "I don't know," e.g.) is usually a matter of figuring out the underlying shifts of meaning used by those who employ the terminology. And usually that shift involves an underlying dimensionality, like the shift between certainty and uncertainty in the example I've been using.

    I think I'm doing something similar here, in reconstructing Caillois's typology as a semiotic square. The exact sticking point as you recognize is in identifying mimesis and ilinx as the complex positive and complex negative terms respectively. In this case I have to admit that I'm relying on linguistic introspection rather than anything more rigorous, but bear with me.

    When we start with agon as the simple positive term, we're implying the existence of not-agon; agon's opposite. I take that to be equivalent to alea because of the way that I recall Caillois talking about them, where agon involves an active striving and alea a passive reception of fortune. That's the essential duality. Now we have to explode it; we have to imagine the thing that is both agon and not-agon, both active striving and passive reception, and we have to find the term in the universe of discourse that we're reconstructing that most closely matches that concept. Find that, and you've found the complex positive term.

    I want to assign that position to mimesis and the complex negative position to ilinx because of the parallel shift from activity to passivity that I see in each pair. Agon : alea :: mimesis : ilinx. The player of make-believe exerts his or her imagination in the same way as the chess-player exerts his or her rational faculty; the lottery ticket buyer subjects himself or herself to the operation of fortune in the same way as the sky-diver subjects himself or herself to the operation of gravity.

Now, Caillois doesn't talk about role-playing, but I recognize in agon/alea the idea of RPG as game (i.e., a contest) and in mimesis/ilinx the idea of RPG as non-game.     \n And now, my long-promised description of "Sim as Discovery" - - courtesy of Valamir at the Forge.\n
    Simulationists view conflict as an opportunity for Discovery and to answer the question \u201cwhat if\u201d. Discovery touches on the very nature of simulation, which is at its core a grand experiment. The experiment is to set a course of events in motion and observe what happens. This requires strong commitment to the in-game causes and established thematic elements within the SiS because in order to be a valid simulation, each element of Exploration must be true to its nature or else the answer to the \u201cwhat if\u201d question will be a false one.


    Expanding on Simulationism
    Simulationism has long been a problematic agenda to understand. In my effort to more clearly define it I have elected to replace the Right to Dream with Discovery, not as an additional term, but because I think Discovery more accurately relates to what Simulationism is about. In my opinion, the Right to Dream is what all of role playing is about and is thus more accurately applied to Exploration than to Simulationism. Dreaming is a rather passive endeavor. But Discovery requires decisive action and focus and is more appropriately on par with the other two agendas.

    I have also tried to return Simulationism to its roots which have been lost in the mists of ancient Forge discussion. The goal of Simulationism must be, can only be it would seem to me, to simulate something. Not to explore, not to just \u201csee what\u2019s out there\u201d. Those are passive acts of observation. Simulation is like an experiment which seeks to answer the question \u201cwhat if\u201d. Before one can observe the results and get that answer, one has to set up the experiment and start it in motion. That is the active piece of Simulation in my mind. The conscious commitment to not just observe events but, through ones character, to be the catalyst for those events.

    Consistency, verisimilitude, in-game causality, these are all aspects of Exploration that are held in common in all three Agendas. What constitutes an acceptable level of consistency, verisimilitude, or in-game causality for any given set of Setting, Character, and Situation is determined by Color (as agreed to by System). These elements are given special emphasis in the course of a Simulationist Agenda only to the extent that they are necessary and for the purpose of providing an accurate context for simulation. As an experiment designed to answer the question \u201cwhat if\u201d, the parameters of the experiment must be carefully controlled. In a chemistry experiment these parameters include temperature, pressure, precisely measured quantities of ingredients, etc. If any of these are off, then the experiment is off and the results are false. In a role playing simulation these parameters can include tight restrictions on player knowledge (to prevent biasing the experiment), and a rigorous approach to ensuring that each element of Setting, Character, Situation, and Color remains strictly true to its nature. Unlike Narrativist play where addressing Premise may require the intentional challenging of this nature, such a challenge is inappropriate to Simulationist play unless that is the variable being tested in the \u201cwhat if\u201d experiment. If it is not, then those elements must be held as constants for the experiment to be valid.

      \n It may be a little too early to make any judgments about the utility of this model. After all--not to make any invidious comparisons--the Copernican model was actually worse at predicting the position of the planets in the night sky when it was first developed than was the Ptolemaic system it eventually superseded.

      Of course, that brings up the point that these aren't scientific theories about a natural phenomenon; they're humanistic accounts of a social one. And that means that they are reflexive, in that to the extent that they are accepted by participants in the phenomenon, they shape those participants' experiences with and understanding of that phenomenon. For example, if we play together and have a bad time, if I accept the Big Model I am more likely to say to myself, "Huh, that guy has different aesthetic preferences than I do," than "Huh, that guy is an idiot," whereas if I don't then the reverse is true.

      But it is not true that the only way the Big Model locates problems in play is at the level of incompatibilities between players in terms of their aesthetic preferences. So even though the first place people tend to go when a game session doesn't work is CA, maybe the problem lies elsewhere. Maybe it's a social contract-level problem (i.e., people not getting along). Maybe it's an exploration-level problem (i.e., the diegesis isn't compelling). Or maybe it's a technical problem (i.e., the game is broken). It takes a fairly thorough description of the game session and then a sensitive and sophisticated reading of that description to really make sense of what's going on when a game session doesn't work.

      Anyway, it's not entirely clear to me that I want to be in the business of diagnosing dysfunctions in play. You see how much joy that has brought to Ron Edwards' life. The task at hand is to see what happens if, unsatisfied with GNS within the broader Big Model of Forge theory, we adopt a Caillois-inspired typology of Creative Agenda.

      Caillois's Modes of Play In More Depth

      If the agon-ilinx and alea-mimesis pairings make sense to a lot of people, then I have either (1) misused them by placing them in a semiotic square (because terms across the diagonal are supposed to be contradictory, in contrast to terms on the same horizontal, which are contrasting or opposing, and terms on the same vertical, which are actually complementary) or (2) explained them poorly. I'm going to go with #2 for now, especially since in looking back I see that the definitions are sort of buried in the earlier thread.

      I don't have Caillois in front of me, but it's not the case that he intended to mean "alea" and "agon" as merely resolution mechanisms; rather, he defined them as modes of engaging in play. To play agonistically is to compete, to strive to win, to place one's will in contention with another's. In contrast, to play aleatically is to surrender oneself to the vicissitudes of fortune, to accept the judgment of chance as right and true beyond the reach of skill, intellect, or prowess. I said the underlying dimension seemed to be active vs. passive; it's more appropriate to say active vs. receptive.

      Similarly, mimesis is imitative, make-believe, a mirroring of someone or something else; to play mimetically is to actively pursue some imaginative end. In contrast, to play ilingically is surrender to some consciousness-altering force or circumstance: jump out of an airplane to feel the rush, or spin around so that you feel dizzy. Thus, while both involve a consciousness-shift of sorts, mimesis is active and ilinx receptive in the same way that agon and alea are active and receptive.

      In the diagram above, I played a little fast and loose with the underlying dimensionality of the shift between the agon/alea and mimicry/ilinx pairs, calling it the difference between roll-playing and roll-playing. It's more precise to say that agon-alea is egocentric, being focused on the self or the particular identity of the player (so you could say it's ludiocentric or player-oriented if you wanted to avoid the negative connotations of egocentrism). In contrast, mimesis-ilinx are fingocentric, which is a word I made up to mean "contrivance-centered"; they don't regard the player as this monadic entity that wins or loses but rather as something mutable, pliable, or changeable via play.

      So, in this view, agon and ilinx are incompatible because one is active and ludiocentric and the other receptive and fingocentric. You can't both actively strive for the supremacy of one's self and at the same time be receptive to its alteration; nor can you actively alter your self while being receptive to fate's judgment on your monadic identity. At least, I seem to recall Caillois talking about play in those terms.

      But my attempt to cram Caillois's modes of play into a Greimassian semiotic square is reflexive in the same way as GNS is. Other views on how to use Caillois to make sense of RPGs are certainly not pre-empted by this one; our friend Jiituomas has proposed an alternative that sees role-playing as fundamentally ilingic: we seek consciousness alteration, and use the other modes of play as needed to get us there. This is a compelling view, and one that's more interesting than regarding RPGs as pure mimesis, which some scholars are wont to do.

      Expanding the Semantic Rectangle

      But there are two semiotic squares in the diagram above; the outer square entangles the inner terms not as contingencies (e.g., the intersection of agon and mimesis is the Pageant) but as a whole new field of meanings that draw upon but are not wholly defined by the interior square. So, whereas the inner square is a typology of modes of play, which I take to mean the basic orientation of players to the act of play, the outer square turns out to be frames of play. In other words, the terms in the outer square imply some of the social role and behavioral expectations that exist when the game is framed in that way. For example, a Contest implies the existence of competitors who are expected to oppose each other directly and adversarially, It also potentially suggests the presence of a judge, referee, or umpire. Some contests have spectators, who may or may not be partisan.

      Similarly, a Pageant may have competitors, but their engagement with each other is not via direct antagonism; instead, they simply enact their stage personae for the edification and delight of onlookers, winning via the formal and informal plaudits those who judge.

      By the same token, a Drama will have actors who enact personae within the play (of the game), but that enactment is intended to reveal something about the character being played rather than to demonstrate the skill of the actor ("Genius!"). Why bother revealing things about a fictional character? Why, indeed? It has something to do with producing meaning, I guess.

      Thrill Ride play is less interested in creating meaning (i.e., in "saying something") than it is in crafting an experience. The best example I can come up with of a Thrill Ride game is the jeepform GR. One player is the victim of an assault; the others are its perpetrators. The perpetrators must describe what happens; the victim describes what people feel. The result is a visceral lesson in power and its abuse. In that game, feeling comes first and meaning comes later, if at all.

      Moving Forward

      It's my hope that if nothing else this model (or model section, if you like) can be used as a device to imagine new ways of playing, to spark a new idea about how to handle a particular game, campaign, or scenario. I think it also points to a broader critique of the Big Model, but that's too much to get into. To the extent that one needs a tool for diagnosing problems in play, this model could help by pointing to the tensions within a particular frame. For example, the Contest is characterized by the tension between skill and chance, between the necessary agency of agon and the required receptivity of alea. Too-demanding skill requirements or the subordination of skill to luck against expectations can frustrate players. In contrast, the tension in Pageant play is between ludiocentric agon and fingocentric mimesis; problems can arise if player performance is over-rewarded or under-acknowledged, or if characters are inadequately developed and portrayed.
