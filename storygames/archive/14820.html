\n So, the thread about the death of GM craft got me thinking about ways to hack the brains of trad GMs to improve their craft without sounding like hippie advice. For the purposes of this thread, the trick is to disguise the techniques into a tool that trad GMs already know and love: the random table.

The best part of random tables is that you can use probability to make them not-really-all-that-random.

Here is a (bad) example:

When a player in your group comes up with something you did not anticipate, roll 2d4 and consult this table:

2 - Provide another player with information their character might use to suggest the first player's idea isn't in the group's best interest.
3 - Suggest an alternative that incorporates some element of the player's idea.
4 - Allow the idea, and add something more to it that helps you incorporate it better.
5 - Roll with the idea. Figure out how to incorporate it as you go.
6 - Allow the idea, on the condition the character is willing to pay some small price for it.
7 - Create some kinds of surprise event, during which the players idea becomes moot.
8 - Create some obstacle that makes the player's idea very costly.

OK, so that table sucks. Make some better ones.

    \n When you feel like bringing real-life problems into the game, roll 1d20...

    On a 1, go ahead and stick in a really thinly-veiled grudge monster and trash someone's character over something they did to you in real life instead of working it out like an adult.
    On a 2-19, get over yourself
    On a 20, consider it, but then work it out like a well-adjusted person and be more generous on your next round of handing out treasure.

    When you want to do something but forget the rule for it, roll 1d30:

    On a 1, spend forever flipping through the book, grinding the game to a halt over something like the modifier for targeting a grappled in darkness during a hurricane
    On a 2-7, flip through the book briefly and then make your best guess
    On a 8-26, ask around the table for what people think it should be then make a call based on experience
    On a 27-30, make something up that sounds interesting with only brief consideration for what the actual rules would be.

    \n I have to say there is a certain brilliance to this idea. Randomness is the god that we all bow before (or at least have no power over), so it might be a wonderful tool to handle those problems that are socially-based. Clearly, we all want to "talk to your players", but it is much easier on paper than in practice. A random table takes the power of decision out of everybody's hands. Also a way to handle situations that might delay the playing of the game. I don't have it in me right now to come up with another one, but I can imagine that there are several other situations where a table like this could come in handy.

    \nElement Generation Table

    You need to come up with an NPC / location / item on the fly. Roll 1d6.

    1- Create the element in a way that ties into the scenario's plot.
    2- Create the element in a way that ties into the setting's current location.
    3- Ask the player with the most obviously relevant skill relating to the current scene to come up with it.
    4- Ask the player with the least relevant skill relating to the current scene to come up with it.
    5- Ask the player with the most relevant background information (excluding skills) to the current scene to come up with it.
    6- Ask the player with the least relevant background information (excluding skills) to the current scene to come up with it.

    \nPersonal Connection Table

    You need to have a reason why the seemingly two-dimensional villain is utterly unlikeable.
    Roll D4
    Ask a player to do one of these things.

    1 - Imagine someone from your childhood that frightened you.
    2 - Imagine someone from your work that disrespects you.
    3 - Imagine someone from your social circles that ignores you.
    4 - Imagine someone who broke your heart.

    Player decides if these connections are identical for their character and the villain, or if the villain just exudes all of these elements as a remixing of the person imagined. Player then describes said dbag to the GM, who tries to play up to that archetype.

    \n This is for skill-driven games like GURPS or Hero. I've actually used it. It answers the question, "what skills will I, the GM, try to highlight in the next session?" It turns skills into flags.

    Make a table like this:\n\n\n\n\n\n
    RollPC1's skillsPC2's SkillsPC3's Skills
    1Animal HusbandryAcrobaticsAnimism
    2BoxingBoatingBaseball
    3CannonadeCarousingChess
    ..and so on
    \nBefore planning a session, roll once or twice on each column. Ruminate on the results, let them suggest NPCs, challenges to overcome, backgrounds, whatever.

    \nIndex cards are the new d100 table It is official, beacause I just said so.

    I love the "trad-game" tables, because they ooze with clever ideas. Sadly they can get tired way before all of the best ideas have been mined out. It is too easy for the a particularly colourful entry to come up again too soon. Critical hit tables were particularly bad for this. If you manage to place an arrow directly through both earholes the first time, that's pretty neat, but it loses credibility pretty quickly.

    Index cards can be seeded and weeded easily.

    They have all the specifics you thought up there on the card.

    You don't have to fiddle with probabilities in the same way as you have to with lookup tables

    If a particular result looks like it is getting tired you can just retire it for a while.

    On the minus side
    It can be awkward to manage more than a handful of stacks.

    Mixing rare and common entries can be a bind.

    \n This is something I use with my group of players after character creation, in our generic homebrewed mediaval fantasy game. It's a quick background generator:

    Roll 1d6 for family:
    1) You're an orphan, only son, raised by a distant relative
    2) Raised by your Father alone
    3) Raised by your mother alone
    4) Mother and Father still alive
    5) You've got your mother and father plus 1d6 brothers/sisters alive, one of which is -roll 1d4:
    1) well known adventurer
    2) a genius, younger than you
    3) always getting in trouble anywhere
    4) also an adventurer, younger than you and following your steps
    6) Big family Clan: Parents, uncles and aunts, grandparents, 2d6 brothers and sisters, roll twice in the previous table.

    economic situation: roll 1d6
    1-2 Poor, assign 5 copper coins to inventory. You've lead a life of (roll 1d6)
    1) slave, beggar,
    2) performing some act at the market
    3) surviving on your own in the wilderness
    4) being a servant
    5) working your @$$ off in a farm or other place
    6) exploring and hunting monsters
    3-4 Doing ok, assign 1 coin of gold to inventory. You've lead a life of (roll 1d6)
    1) exploring and hunting monsters
    2) entertaining people, specify how
    3) surviving on your own in the wilderness
    4) jack of all trades
    5) working in a farm or other place
    6) service for a noble
    5 Rich, assign 10 gold coins to inventory. You've lead a life of blah blah...add as many other economic situations you can come up with

    Because of the life you've lead your family... (roll 1d6)
    1) Has expelled you and denied your inheritance for dishonoring them.
    2) doesn't want to know anything about you
    3) Don't care about what you do
    4) understands, though they don't like it very much
    5) respects you more
    6) Completely supports you.

    there's also a roll for the history of your family and ancestors reputation, however the fun begins here. Roll 3d6 for the next 3 tables and made up the story of why your character started travelling with the things that come up on the table, other PCs backgrounds and the setting in general :

    Breakpoint (roll 1d6)
    1) Debt (of blood or monetary)
    2) challenge
    3) robbery / kidnapping
    4) sickness
    5) Accidental Death
    6) Assasination or massacre

    of/to/from
    1) nemesis
    2) close friend
    3) local authority
    4) relatives
    5) romantic interest
    6) whole tribe/village/town/city

    that sends character in a travel for
    1) search of a person, item, information or other solution
    2) revenge
    3) escaping
    4) training
    5) redemption
    6) search for money or influences

    Add a table for other equipment according to economic situation and you're done.

    However tables shouldn't be used all the time on every single session, they are tools best left for those times when the GM or the players are short of ideas and need something quick and shallow. They can be used to generate anything from monsters to dungeons, but it's true that they become old soon if you keep using them without refreshing. A cool option would be to have index cards for every item on a table, roll dice on those as if they were a table and change/rearrange the order of the index cards to keep the trick fresh.

    I don't reccomend rolling for tables in the middle of a session (and even less during a combat) since the operation of rolling dice, checking the table and coming up with an idea to make it all plausible can take valuable seconds away when you actually need a quick reaction either as a GM or from the players. I'd say this is part of the prep, not something to be used in-game. That's why I've got my doubts about the Mythic GM emulator quickness. I'm specifying the wasted time is just SECONDS, if you're ok with it fine, but I don't like at all the effect that small pause has in the game flow.
