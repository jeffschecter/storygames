For some time now I've come across a notion that there would be some issue with striving for success (in character that is) while playing a drama oriented game. I don\u2019t think that\u2019s the case.

Drama isn\u2019t about winning or losing, that\u2019s apparent, but it\u2019s definitely about people trying to get the upper hand, or at least cope with some crisis. Sometimes against overwhelming odds. And while trying they need to deal with their issues, their conflicted lives, their relationships and so on. But they\u2019re really trying. They want to succeed. They want to beat the devil. But it\u2019s uncertain if they\u2019ll succeed.
Play to fail is also a way to achieve drama, but using a different stance.

However, maybe no one is actually saying this at all. Maybe I misunderstood the intentions with those comments and threads, but in the case I didn\u2019t, would you agree that there are such notions out there? And if there is, do you agree with them?

    \n I doubt that anyone here thinks that striving for in-character success and creating drama in the story are incompatible. Many systems are built around the premise that the player will strive for in-character success, and then build a system around this that turns this striving into the source of drama. Would Sorcerer work if the characters didn't try to be successful? No, because if they just accept failure, they won't be tempted to summon demons, and there is no game. Similarly, the key to running My Life with Master is to make sure that not just the characters, but even the players themselves, want the master defeated and will do everything they can to make this happen. Dogs in the Vineyard constantly asks you what risks you are willing to take as you try to succeed; if your answer is "none, I don't need to succeed," it falls flat. If you play 3:16 and nobody is interested in getting more kills than the other characters, the system isn't going to work. And so on.

    \n It is the most basic way of building a drama-game, especially a Forgite narrativist one: select your subject matter, recognize the natural protagonist in the situation, give the protagonist to one player to play, and direct him to determine and express his character's desires, and to make choices for that character's best interests. This is fun not because you get to write and stage a play, but rather because you get to experience the struggle (agonism) of the sympathetic main character (protagonist) from an internal viewpoint. It is also fun for the other players because they get to see how you, the specific person, deal with this combination of protagonist identity and conflict-rich situation.

    It is notable that the above type of game still does not prefer for the players to adopt a na\xefvely triumphalist attitude towards their characters; the player still needs to enjoy it when their character suffers legitimate defeat, otherwise they cannot be convincing about depicting the struggle. There cannot be struggle without real respect for tragedy. So the player's task is to play-to-win, if you want to put it like that, but his enjoyment ultimately derives from credible and powerful portrayal of the protagonist in his struggle, not from merely winning.

    As for whether somebody somewhere thinks that this kind of game does not exist, I'm sure that there is somebody like that somewhere. I also hear that there are people who think that the Earth is flat. It's such a dumb way to frame a question, to ask whether there might be somebody somewhere who thinks something stupid that you disagree with.

    \n I believe I see what you're saying. And it's a tricky subject. I've seen people fall in to this, citing it as a reason they "won't play those story games" but that's another story...

    It's highly situational. In Fiasco, you play towards failure or success, but you play it fully. You are freed from the burden of choosing S/F when you pass it to someone else. Just accept the decision and go for it.

    In Polaris, you are burdened with always clawing for your character's success. There it would be very difficult to pull off drama if not for the role of The Mistaken, who will bend and twist all your hopes and aspirations until a really tough decision point is reached.

    A lot of games aren't built assuming you will play one way or the other, or (more frequently and insidiously) they assume you will behave in a certain way but make no mention of that assumption in the game text.

    It's easy to see why you might get this dichotomy in your mind, and there are plenty of games out there that would let you get frustrated, unable to address it (within the game mechanics.)

    Are you thinking about a specific game? Are you remembering experiences you've had in the past?

    Ok, so lets say that you have a Weakness-trait (i.e drug addict, corrupted cop, self-loathing and such) that gives you a bonus die or two. When you are out of dice you need to succumb to your weakness in some way.
    \nThat kind of game mechanical bonus has IMO nothing to do with drama.

    1. Either you do it [activate a negative trait] for the bonus, and then you're missing the point of WHY you're doing it.
    2. Or you do it because you think it's fun, but then you don't need the bonus, because you will do it anyway.

    I would instead form the session around playing out the weaknesses. To create a situation that includes the weakness, play out scenes that leads up to that situation, and then finally play out the consequences of the situation where the weakness is involved.

    It doesn't matter to me if the player's and the character's goals differ.

    a) If they are the same, then the game should try to create bleed, so the player feels for the character. Despair would probably be the key feeling to strive for.
    b) If they differ, then the player can take a step back and treat the character like a puppet. It's possible that schadenfreude is the key feeling here, no matter if the character win or loose. If it wins, it's about the joy of other's misfortune and if the character looses, it's still about the joy of other's misfortune because the player and the character are separated.

    It's two different ways of playing, and also two different ways of designing a game. The common element is however tension.

    Ok, so lets say that you have a Weakness-trait (i.e drug addict, corrupted cop, self-loathing and such) that gives you a bonus die or two. When you are out of dice you need to succumb to your weakness in some way.
    \nBreaking the Ice uses this mechanic all the time. First, you need to make your character look good. But usually, that doesn't give you enough successes; and you then have to earn re-rolls and special bonus dice by making your character look bad and incorporating their problems and weaknesses into the fiction.

    In that game, it works very well because the game is about how love requires vulnerability. Real success is only possible when you are not successful at projecting a "perfect" image of yourself; people can only come to love you if they can see you for who you really are, rather than for who you think you must be.

    In other games, it might work well or badly depending on how it is used and what those games are about.

    What do you mean mean by "bleed"? How do you achieve it?
    \nBleed is a LARP term meaning that the player's emotions spills over to the character's and also vice versa. I would say it's a part of immersion (in character).

    Most immersive (in character) games I played are kind of lazy when it comes to create bleed. They assume that the player itself will bring it to the table. There are multiple theories in to achieve bleed, but I'm not the right man to talk about it because it was years ago that I actually had that play style, and I did it mostly the lazy way (where players bring it to the table).
    \xd7 In Beyond Role and Play (free pdf), there's talk about a constant loop between character, player and setting, where one part has to adopt to a previous incitement in the loop.
    \xd7 Others around you can help out by throwing themselves into an emotional state. The closest example I can think of is Nicole Kidman's performance in The Others, where her played-out fear affected me as an audience and made me feel it too. This is caused by mirror neuron.
    \xd7 You can possible achieve bleed through flow. You have probably read Arvid's Flow-er Model, although he probably means all kinds of immersion (in setting, in fiction, and so on).
    \xd7 You can probably do theater exercises to leave your mind open and closely connected to your emotions. I've been spending my summer reading books for theater teachers, and they are filled with this kind of stuff.
    \xd7 There is also the theory of spatial presence, but that one is as broad as the Flow-er Model, even if it uses somewhat the same strategy as the first point in this list.

    Read the first link in the first link above. That will give you some suggestions of Jeepform scenarios that creates bleed. At least according to the authors.

    However, theres a third option:

    3. You succumb to your weakness, not because you want to (cause you don't) but because you need to.
    \nYeah, I like that too, but if the game system wants the player to do something, it shouldn't bribe the player with dice, but instead make the player do it. I'm talking about it in my thread Bribes and judgement, which is basically about feedback loops. Having a system that eats dice and then a reward that gives more dice is a kind of zero sum feedback loop (which I have nothing against). A positive feedback loop is seen in D&D, where you get XP for hacking monsters so you can increase your values and hack even more monsters.

    However, getting XP for roleplay - which isn't something connected to game mechanics at all - isn't a feedback loop. It's just disconnected. It's like if I sneeze, and you slap me. There's no connection at all. If you play out weaknesses, more situations should instead occur so you can play out even more weaknesses, which means that the game builds up more and more (positive feedback loop). But to think like this demands that you go beyond game mechanics, and instead think of how to build a story. In how the system (which the game master is part of) should react in the fiction.

    This is what I like in games that builds agendas (for PCs and NPCs), because that's a way of formalizing reaction within the fiction. When one agenda is disturbed, a repercussion will occur. And each participant reacting to another within the fiction is nothing more than communicate in creating a story. I wonder what more ways there are to formalize communication like this.
