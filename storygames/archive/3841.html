\n So, I will be "running" The Shab-al-Hiri Roach at the upcoming Gateway Strategicon here in LA. I've been thinking about this game and I've identified some common problems I've observed with it. I thought I'd throw up these observations for comments and analysis.

Pacing. There is a problem that is most prevalent among people who have played The Roach before although it can happen among overly enthusiastic first time players who are familiar with the game through reading others accounts. What happens is everyone remembers the awesome of how a game of The Roach *ends*; usually with lots of whacked out creepy ritualistic murder and mayhem. Then when they go to play a second game, eager to get back to that mayhem, they *start* the game at that high level of wacky mayhem and then wonder why the game peters out by the second event.

Solution: Start small. Frame scenes and conflicts around relatively ordinary things such as that dispute over departmental budgeting or that pesky student who thinks they deserve a higher grade. Even if you are roached in the beginning go for creepy rather than violent; make inappropriate comments and invade personal space rather than sacrificing the head master on the convocation dining table.

Character. I occasionally see people make characters with absolutely no redeeming value what so ever. I think this stems from the notion that the point of the game is to demonstrate how the unroached character is almost the same as a roached character.

Solution: While the suggested notion above is partially true I think it ultimately makes demonstrating how an unroached character is NOT like a roached character all that more important. By the end of character creation players should have some sympathy for their character. It helps if a character cares about obtaining something understandable (if petty) for understandable reasons (if misguided).

Unfortunately the game itself doesn't really provide much structure or guidance with these two problems. I was wondering if a) anyone else had encountered these or similar problems, b) my proposed solutions sound reasonable and c) anyone had any suggestions on how to facilitate these solutions while playing the game?

Thanks.

Jesse

    \n Those are great observations, Jesse. The game can suffer (heh) from the "wide-open gonzo narration" problem you sometimes see in GMless games for sure. I've definitely seen both problems you identify, and I support your solutions 100%. I think communication will go a long way - just pointing out these common difficulties before play will be helpful. Challenge people to keep their Roached-ness as subtle as possible for as long as they can. The cards will do the work of making it pretty public soon enough.

    I have not seen the character problem quite as often, and as a player I really try to give this to other players - it's absolutely acceptable to provide another player's character with an errant step-child, or a keynote speech to write, or a grant to receive if only the committee from Washington misses certain irregularities. If you tie these into a character's enthusiasms, the player is usually thrilled to play along. I gave a really satisfying "gift" in a recent game, where an un-Roached character was given the choice of losing her University tenure offer or committing murder. The player squirmed and found a really awful, creative solution that re-affirmed her character's shaky humanity.

    \n Ask away Christopher. Here's what I see as the central points of the endgame.

    1) You can voluntarily become roached.
    2) You become roached if you draw a roach card 1 in 10 chance.
    3) You can not become unroached unless you draw another roach card and cross off an enthusiasm.

    Remember you only draw cards at the top of an event. So that's 6 cards total over the course of the whole game.

    So you see, the win condition is mostly random. If you do not draw another roach card before the top of the last event or if you are unroached and DO draw a roach card at the top of the last event there is NOTHING you can do to "win." The game is about what you will do in the HOPES that you will win, not so much about winning itself.

    \n I think I just had a light-bulb go on. Consider life without the Roach. The win condition would simply be the player with the most Reputation. But that's just everyday life at Pemberton. That's the "game" of academics.

    Now consider the fact that this game has an optimal strategy. Full Professors should get into conflicts about Status with Assistant Professor PCs. Assistant Professors should get into conflicts about Everything Else with Full Professor PCs. This is optimal because it will both gain you points and cost them points. A slightly less optimal strategy is for Full Professors to get into Status conflicts with NPC students and for Assistant Professors to get into Everything Else conflicts with Luminary NPCs. This maximizes your gain potential but costs your opponents nothing.

    Enter The Roach. The Roach doesn't care about your petty game of tenure and reputation it has larger incomprehensibly Lovecratian designs. The Roach literally removes you from the game. And that's the irony of The Roach: it makes you the best at a game you're no longer playing. The comedy is that the characters still think a roached character is playing their power struggle game and thus must step up to meet this "new strategy." It's like a bunch of people suddenly trying to house rule Clue to beat their friend who's playing Monopoly at another table.

    Here's the real punchline though: This puts players with Roached Characters in a position of judgment. Because they are no longer playing the game they can step back and decide just whose day they're going to fuck up and why. That's where the "moral" choice in The Roach lies. It's not in the decision to be roached or not but WHEN you're roached who bears the brunt of your torment? And when you're unroached how far are you willing to go to compete in a game whose rules you suddenly don't understand anymore?

    Makes sense?

    Jesse

    \n Hi Guys,

    Thanks for all the info.

    After mulling it all (and reviewing the game from a year ago in my head), here's my "ah ha" moment. Jesse, I'm looking at your notes in your first post, thinking about the game last year, and this is what I came up with:

    I don't want to be told that it's an academic satire. Or, rather, that's not going to help me one lick. There are several reasons for this:

    1) I need clear "entry" points to things like RPGs. (or movies, or whatever). When we say, "This is what the game is about..." or "This is the focus..." or "Here's how you start..." I"m looking for that seed crystal that's going to let me grow out the game from that point. By focus on genre ("satire") and results ("wacky mayhem") and environment ("academia") as that seed crystal, I get confused. Because now we're heading toward Sim-land -- not Nar.

    2) It turns the game into an abstraction, and not about people. If Ron is right about it being a game about judging the characters, and Jason is right about it being about how the characters behave, then I (simple-minded man that I am), what the first and primary focus to be the characters. I don't want to hear about satire and wacky mayhem, because, as Jesse points out, everyone's going to rush for that. Why? Because if the game is described as being focused on "academic satire which often devolves into extreme versions of collegiate eccentricity and pranks resulting in wacky mayhem" then why wouldn't I go for that stuff right away? After all, that's the focus of the game!

    3) But if, instead, the person leading the game (not the GM, but the "leader" of the session), were to day, "This is a game about a group of ambitious academics," I would be plugged into the game right away. Because that I get. That's human. because I don't want to sit back and judge the characters. I want to be engaged with my character and judge.

    4) I believe the emphasis on the setting ("It's academia! It's crazy!") is a red-herring -- both as an into to the game, and in discussions. You could easily and happily run games of: Roach in the Vatican, Roach in The West Wing, Roach at Warner Brothers.... and the game would play great. You'd switch out the stock characters from Roach with foreign dignitaries, personal assistants and young theologians and so on, and the game works just fine.

    5) Because the game isn't about the setting. It just isn't. It's about the petty games people play for their sense of -- entitlement, self-worth, status, what have you -- which for right now I'll sum up under "ambition."

    6) The setting, the wackiness, and so forth will all come about, of course. Jason does a great job in the rules of plugging the character into the academic life. And the craziness will simply come about because of the rules -- the cards, the commands... I mean, it's just there! The game will force that. The players don't have to force that. If they create an academic with ambitions -- and actual person who they could actually believe in -- once you get that going, and have that person and the rules meet, comedy and strangeness will ensue.

    7) For this reason, I think giving instructions on holding back on pacing, starting small and so forth put the focus in the wrong place. At least for me, anyway. Because it's still all about "where we are going" and it's still about that abstract thing we're making, but it's not about the characters.

    I was just watching the second season of Twin Peaks a few weeks ago. I hadn't seen it for years. And what struck me was that while I remembered everyone talking about the wackiness of the show, the truth is that while it is very funny and wacky, it's really quite grounded in the experience of human beings. There were scenes that brought tears to my eyes -- just a father talking to his son about his dreams of happiness for him, for example.

    Without that you get -- well, you get the second half of the second season, after Laura's killer is revealed and David Lynch had moved on, and you really got the feeling that writing staff said, "Well, the audience keeps talking about that bad hair morning Cooper had -- so we need more small town satire with wacky mayhem." And so we got all the crazy, but it was ungrounded and the show fell apart.

    8) I think The Roach is the same way. If there are not real, solid, empathetic characters I can get behind, then the wackiness doesn't matter. But instructions for how to pace and play later won't help me (me at least) if I don't start off on the right foot. The right foot for me is, "The focus of this game is on a group of ambitious academics." Issues of humanity and situation are introduced right away, and it gives me a hook as a human being into the game.


    Jesse, I hope you understand I'm not parsing your language to say "Gottcha!" -- but because I'll be running a few games in a few weeks and these things matter to the games I'm going to be playing and running. And phrasing those few key words at a Con where those few words set tone and style are really crucial.

    At the game we played last year (and I know there are other sessions and reports, I'm just talking about the one I know) I didn't get any sense of investment in the characters. When I spoke once to Ron about the events in the game he said, "It sound like everyone forgot about humanity."

    I'd agree with that. And I"d say focusing on characters everyone can actually invest in first is the way to kick off the session. Once you add the rules, my guess is a lot of these other matters will fall by the wayside or take care of themselves.
