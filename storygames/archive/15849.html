    \n Do you want a real answer Tomas?

    The real answer is that "role-playing game" was originally a good-enough-for-now, placeholder term. One of the people associated with that early TSR inner circle ( I forget exactly which) had a parent working in some kind of therapy field where "roleplaying" excercises or games were already being used as a technique.

    The term got borrowed and stuck.

    The reason a term was needed was because the old grogs from the minis wargaming hobby that D&D had sprung from were kinda dicks about this whole new style of play and its swords'n'sorcery themes ( rather than merely historical what-ifs?) and wanted to essentially eject this sort of game and this sort of play from the proper hobby of wargaming.

    I'm sure you can draw your own parallels with the current roleplaying hobby and various sorts of not-always-just-one-character-and-divided-traditional-GM-duties-games.

    \n Just to throw another angle at this:

    Have you guys noticed that D&D4e goes out of it's way to specify a definition of "Role" to mean "your area of tactical expertise within the group", specifically, whether you are a Striker, Controller, Leader, or Defender. They use the term with that specific meaning extensively and, it seems, exclusively.

    By extension, role-playing in D&D4e would mean: successfully filling your assigned niche during combat.

    I find this attempt to subtly redefine the words role and, by extension, roleplaying, to be very very interesting. If I were a conspiracy theorist, I'd be seeing Orwellian newspeak overtones in this action.

    I mean, as far as I'm concerned, it fits extremely well with what, in my opinion, D&D has always been - a set of tactical combat rules that, if you really want to, you could use as a framework around which you do some group-based story creation. I think it's kind of neat that, in 4e, the game finally fully embraces what it is actually good at. I see this attempt at redefining the core terms to be part of that effort, though perhaps it is further marginalizing to other games that come under the same blanket terminology.

    All the more reason to continue to use the term story games. Even though that's not perfectly defined, it's closer to a meaning that fits activity, especially if the meaning of role and roleplay is being redifined in a different direction.

    Posted By: TomasHVMWhy "role-playing games"?

    You all playcharactersin these games, so why do you call it rpg?
    \nBecause that's not all you do.

    Your role might be playing monsters and characters and set up and determine obstacles. Your role might be primarily playing one character, but along with that you (probably) make die rolls and interpret the results, or at the very least communicate the results, you make decisions based on external information, like in another person's character in grave peril, like which action isn't just an optimal character decision but will also create fun and interesting changes to the story, or like you have to leave in 20 minutes to have dinner with the kids and so just wrap up whatever you're doing and find a stage exit.

    I've played with people who act as if they are only character players. All their decisions would be based on what benefits their character or what matches their character's personality. They're generally assholes.

    Posted By: Simon PetterssonHeck, what's with the "playing" part, by the way? In Swedish, French, Spanish and (I think) German, it's just "role games".
    Some of those languages have other connotations on the word "play" (leik/lek/spiel/jeu). In the Nordic countries it is commonly used as "childrens play". It is not directly translatable, as many other words and phrases. Languages differ in how they organize the words. Some languages even have words for things that don't exist in other languages. Sometimes those words are loaned. Like our fjords, and trolls ... ;-)

    "Table talking games" is a sweet one!

    As a derivate of the interactivity of soccer, we could call it verbal tableball

    Alternate history-lesson;
    - Verbal tableball games once grew out of soccer, you know, as soccer players got increasingly fed up with there being no socio-phsychological side to their simulation of conflict. It all started in the wardrobe of the English soccer-team Liverpool, as they realized that the song "You'll never walk alone" indicated some kind of interactive adventure where possible; them walking like bold warriors in a landscape more varied than a soccer-pitch ...

    They started playing by themselves, talking themselves into an exciting adventure, setting up different goalposts, and fighting as a team to reach them. Alas; the game soon ended in a big quarrel ...

    That was when the referee came in, got involved, and became the first GM!
