    \n Saving a soul is easy, saving the world a pipe dream. But doing the former can help strive for the latter, so I'll just ignore save the world stuff as it only leads down the road of idealistic platitudes.

    But how?

    By incorporating techniques of drama therapy. You're already half-way there by roleplaying. The tough part would be bridging the divide of something that we (here) use as entertainment to something that's real work. There's certainly a middle ground where you can do a little of both, but to do more than cathartic emo-porn you're going to be dredging up real emotions and I don't know anyone who finds that to be a game that you do in your spare time. There are plenty of tools in use that does just this. Looking into drama therapy programs is the place I suggest to start.

    Indeed! I believe role-playing games can be invested with themes and conflicts that makes sense, and makes fun of the sense, in other ways that we see in the classical game (and in most modern games too).

    Games of serious intent, and still interesting as games, are beginning to surface now, looking into themes of slavery, relationships, death and so forth.

    \n I really dislike the language used in the original post, "save a soul" sounds very preachy, patronizing and holier-than-thou.
    I don't like the idea that "souls" need to be "saved", and that it is the healthy outsider that knows what's best for the sick insider.

    I just needed to get that out of the way, because I had a pretty harsh reaction to the language you were using.
    Hopefully we can move on past that, though, and talk about some of the content of your post...

    Roleplaying can help people learn more about themselves.
    Roleplaying can support the safe and unhindered exploration of serious issues.
    Roleplaying can help us understand others. Even when we roleplay about elves and wizards, we improve our ability to see and empathize with the generalized other.
    Roleplaying can teach us.
    Roleplaying can empower us.
    Roleplaying can strengthen our imagination, teaching us to think in new ways and invent with new vigor.
    Roleplaying can help us learn to express ourselves in different ways.
    Roleplaying is thinking in a different way (just like music is, or math is, or sudoku is), and thinking in different ways helps us use more of our brain.
    Roleplaying produces, which helps us step outside of the consumer-focus of our society (or, at least, my society).
    Roleplaying is an intermediary between consuming (source material, others contributions) and producing (co-creating fiction), allowing us to and training us to interact with the culture we are immersed in.
    Roleplaying increases creative problem-solving skills.
    Roleplaying allows us to test out other personalities and conflict-resolution patterns, which is a way of testing out a "new you" that you might not be ready to test out in the real world.

    YMMV, and some of these things are game/group dependent, but by and large I think this holds for both D&D and My Life With Master, say.
