    Here's my take on this, as a librarian:

    Brand's right in that depending on the library's collection development policy, donated game books might just be sold of by the friend's of the library group. A lot of libraries have a policy of not putting donations on the shelves, mainly because the stuff people donate tends not to be stuff that actually circulates, and circulation statistics (that is, check-outs) are what tend to determine funding.

    The other problem is, even if the library accepts donations, there's a chance that the book will sit on the shelf and eventually be discarded, unless the librarians know to direct people to it, or unless there's already a community need.

    So what's the best way to get games in libraries? First thing, make friends with a librarian. Extra bonus points if you can find a librarian who's already into gaming. Teen/young adult services librarians would probably be a good place to start. Tell them that you think story games are great, and have a place in the library. And then, here's the biggie: tell them you'll help them get teens interested in the things. Because even a pro-gaming librarian is going to be skeptical if they don't already have a population using their library who's into gaming. Offer to run an afterschool game. If you have a lot of energy, offer to do it on a regular basis. This'll get kids interested, and in the process, it'll make it evident to the library that having storygames on the shelves is a good thing.

    Some years ago I operated a game store, and local libraries were some of our most loyal repeat customers. Mostly they would buy the latest board games, the ones on the Spiel des Jahres shortlist, and classics like chess sets and Trivial Pursuit. Into some of them we managed to sneak the occasional D&D Starter Set or Das Schwarze Auge Basic Set.

    In the wake of the fourth book of Harry Potter being published we offered to two or three libraries the organisation of a Harry Potter Game Day at their venue, at a date to be set by them. They would have to do a little marketing (a poster in their rooms) while we would bring the games and people to demonstrate them. Among the games we had selected was a Clue/Cluedo-like boardgame named Elixir (close enough to Harry Potter) - and a role playing game set in Hogwarts. One of our employees had created a minimalistic rule set, just a character sheet that was in fact the report card of a pupil, and a very simple die roll mechanism in which the player had to roll against his character's grades - very intuitive.

    Two libraries took the offer, and the game days were a big success - for the libraries. Not that much for our store, because there wasn't a commercial Harry Potter RPG that we could sell to all those enthused proto customers!
    But by the time the Lord of the Rings movies came around we totally had forgotten about that kind of marketing.

    From my experience libraries love that kind of event. They also have to compete against modern, electronic media. They need events that help them drawing attention to them, their services, their books, and reading in general.
    (They actually booked us during christmas season to give talks about board games and gaming trends.)

    And...

    IF I ever finish the RPG design I am working on (and off and on and off) I absolutely want to do playtest sessions at a local library. (It is a beginner level game aimed at children.) You will get AP reports, either here or over at the Forge, if it happens.

    Dirk
