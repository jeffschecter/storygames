    January 2007
    Andy,

    I wouldn't consider the GM's guide optional. I mean, if you have first edition you could maybe play with only the player's guide... but even then I don't know that it would be easy. There are too many things (such as full info on setting difficulty numbers) that aren't in the PG.

    As for the character creation chapter, it seems more in order to me. I don't have my 1st ed to compare to, but there is a wording convention used that once twigged to should keep everything in order. Attributes are referred to in terms of "value" (the number of the attribute) and "step" (the number you actually use to determine you dice size) consistently. So if something says, "Willpower value" it means the number you generated. If it says "Willpower Step" then it means the other.

    There are a few sections that still read a bit awkwardly, like "you add your talent rank to your attribute step in order to get your talent step" -- but the terms "value" "rank" and "step" all seem to be used consistently through the book, and with examples frequent.

      Equal Opportunity game Lovin'?

      My choices are based on games written for intent. I choose games where the players are more involved integrally and where the game helps build character driven plot.

      Posted By: PaulJust (professionally) curious: did you (or your GM, whichever) usethe Trait Sorterfirst, or did you just dive into the books?
      Hey Paul: We just went through the books. The parts we were hung up on was:

      * The amount of raw data to digest at first: Skills are easy enough to work through, but Advantages/Drawbacks took a majority of time.
      * Sorting out the supers/paranormal stuff from the normal stuff. It took a while to navigate with them all put together.
      * Trying to decide if some personality stuff should be "coded" in the rules (as advantages and disadvantages) or just played out in character.

      I can't find where I posted it, but our experience was pretty consistent across the three folks who made characters: First time took a while (40-60+ mins) and was a while to get through, but after that it took us each about 20 mints total to make second, third etc characters.

      What we ended up doing was:
      1) Coming up with a character concept.
      2) Digging into Disadvantages first. The ones applicable to your character type stand out more, and not only helps you choose natural ones first, but also acts as a buffer so that you don't get to them, buying tons of disads just to balance out the good stuff you already took.
      3) Then run through advantages. Only jot down the point costs for the ones that you think might be critical.
      4) Then go through the skill section, but rely on the Skill List in the appendix: Just jot down every skill, skill rank (Easy/Hard/etc), and base attribute first. Make a laundry list, and go nuts.
      5) Only then allocate points to the Advantages, then to Skills. That way, there's much less balancing and rebalancing and further rebalancing of all the elements to make that starting character.
      6) An interesting side-effect of doing the above is that you don't have to erase the skills and advantages that you didn't have points to put into: You leave them on your sheet, and they become a pretty good guide for areas that you'll probably be wanting to spend future XP adds on.

      One interesting note: We played a campaign of Conan OGL for about two months. That was actually much harder to go through, and when we made characters it took about 3 hours each (we had 4 players and 2 books between us then). Reason for the difference: In GURPS, at a glance you can 'get' the idea behind a particular advantage or skill from the name alone. However, with Conan OGL there was a laundry list of feats that provided very specific effects that sometimes throws you off (how one feat might have an effect that gives you a massive boost to Parry or whatever, but it takes about two reads to realize this, plus looking at ALL the other feats to make sure there's not one that's 'better'). So in that regard, we found GURPS much, MUCH cleaner in making characters. It was a lot of number crunching, to be sure (more than we're used to), but there didn't seem to be as many hidden benefits or drawbacks to taking certain skills or advantages that couldn't be gleaned from reading the Name of the skill/adv, or just a cursory glance at the description.

      Annnnyway, that was our experience. The first adventure of our year-long GURPS campaign gets thrown down this week.

That was totally different to how some other groups thought was the "proper" way to run Vampire (by creating a machiavellian mystery which the players breadcrumb their way to the end of) which in turn was different to how a third group I knew thought was the "proper" way to run the game (by creating totally BAD ASS wish fulfilment characters and wailing on drug dealers) and also different from group 4 (who all played evil fucker vampires who tortured and murdered their way across the US). The thing is, according to the book as written, we were all correct. There was no real coherent single way to play that game. You could sit down with a group and they'd be saying "we're really into character development and the personal horror thing" and then their characters are all fucking death machines who kill stuff all the time and the GM really just racks up the 9 pins for them to knock down. So it was all very hit and miss finding a group who wanted the same kind of game you did, even though you were all using the same rulebook and setting.

      That's much easier with hippy games. They tend to wear their intent on their sleeve in terms of mechanics which are actually for whatever the game is about. I mean, can you imagine a game of The Shab Al-Hiri Roach where the roach was de-emphasised? It's most of the rules and one half of every card, you can't miss it and think the game is about something else.
