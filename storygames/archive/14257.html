\n What it says on the tin. Sometime soon, it is my task to organize a game of Faery's Tale (Deluxe, if it matters) for my three nieces, aged 11, 9, and 7, and nephew aged...um, either 4 or 5. I know he's on the cusp, anyway.
\n
\nI don't think I'll have any problems with imagination, as they are chock full. After a brief explanation of the four types, the boy for example has exclaimed that he wants to be a Sprite named Jake, who has a blue mohawk and rides a squirrel. The eldest girl has decided to be a Pixie with leaves for wings named Isabelle. Obviously, these kids are awesome.
\n
\nSo what I'm looking for here is anyone with experience running the game, either with adults or kids, and any tips you might have for me that I may be overlooking. Thanks!

    \n I will have to think about it, but one suggestion I would make is to give them a villain they can't just fight... Like a giant who is too big to notice them, even if they take a swing at him. The reason I suggest this is, if the five year old is like my guy of the same age, his solution to everything is to bash it. Give them a reason to think of something more clever, a reason to negotiate or to do something prank-like, and then let the idea work, but maybe not in the way they expected. Once they get into it, the narration will kind of feed itself.

    \n I've only played it with my daughter when she was 4 and 5 - which means she's a completely different sort of person from a 6-9 who again is completely different from 11. Might get crazy having all those age ranges at once!

    My daughter absolutely needed it to be diceless when the stakes mattered. She wouldn't even roll the dice if there was a possibility of failure. But if her attribute was simply lower than her opponent and I said, "You won't be able to outrace him, he's too fast" or whatever, that was fine. And if it was just a tournament she was playing in, rolling dice to see who won the game of hide-and-seek or whatever was fine. You might have the same issues with the nephew - you might ask the other kids if they're okay with the nephew not rolling dice. Or maybe he has thicker skin than my daughter.

    My daughter also went off the rails. "Okay, so the frog prince wants your help. Will you help him?" "No." My solution was to let her - "Okay, he goes away, sad" - and then pull another scenario from the booklet and run with that. It made things sort of sandboxy - she could pursue a mission or not. And, maybe this is me being a little too harsh, but when she didn't pursue a mission there would be consequences. The princess married an evil prince instead of the frog prince and the evil prince had a vendetta against the fairies (some of my favorite sessions came out of that) ... or the fairy queen is upset with her for disobeying. Also, did a lot of "Yes, and" - she wanted a cat friend and the cat friend would get new powers every session. Awesome creativity and I let her go with it.

    Also, we drew the map of the fairyland and neighboring kingdoms together. Automatic fun.

    \n My guess is that the 9 and 11 year olds will be OK with a reasonable amount of non-focus time, but the younger ones may want to be in focus a lot.

    One possible solution - pair up an older one with a younger one, putting the younger one in the more "in charge" role. Like have the 11 year old play a character who is assigned to guard and protect the 5 year old. And have the 9 year old paired up with the 7 year old.

    You'll have to feel it out and see if that seems to deprotagonizing for the older girls. But it might help.

    My 10 year old son is playing in a game with 6 of my adult friends. There's too many players, but most of the time he's ok with having a fairly big chunk of time where he's not in focus. So 4 kids may work out OK. But I think pairing them up might be helpful.

    \n I am one of official demo-monkeys for Firefly Games, especially for FT. If you take a look at the credits, you'll see my name in there.

    One of the important things I learned is to keep the adventures fairly short for the young ones, somewhere about half an hour. The seems to be the squirm point for most 4-5year olds. Also, do not play in an environment with a lot of potential distractions. If you are in a room with toys in it, they will want to play with them when they are not in the spotlight.

    Unless their parents are also playing, playing with children is a lot like herding cats. They haven't learned much in the way of social skills, and they may not see the GM as an authority figure. Keep the group small and/or get the parents to play.
