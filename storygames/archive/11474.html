\n I've written a Trail of Cthulhu scenario for Pelgrane Press called "Castle Bravo"; the PCs are sailors and scientists aboard a U.S. aircraft carrier in the Marshall Islands during secret atomic bomb testing there in the 1950s. At the beginning of the adventure, an atomic bomb goes off, and then things get worse.

I've gotten feedback from playtesters that I've incorporated into what is pretty close to the final version, but I want to run the adventure again myself to make sure the changes work, at least for me. So it's on the playtest track at Dreamation; I'm running it Friday and Saturday nights at 8 pm. Vinnie says that he'll try to populate the playtest games with at least four players each, but that it's perfectly fine to invite folks to play in those games as well.

So I wanted to check and see if anyone wanted to play "Castle Bravo" at Dreamation, so I could invite you.

    Posted By: JuddHow'd it go, Bill?
    \nGood question, Judd. Ultimately, I was disappointed in myself, because I thought it was further along. On the plus side, I got a ton of insightful, incisive, and actionable feedback from some really good and engaged players who had a good time with each other if not with the adventure per se. I hope some of them will comment with specifics about their experiences.

    I had four hours to reflect on the adventure--which I ran twice at Dreamation--while I drove home Sunday afternoon. Ultimately, the players who were most satisfied with it were I think the Lovecraft aficionados, who appreciated the adventure's homage to specific Mythos elements. And so while it was a fine evocation of Lovecraftiana, it just fundamentally wasn't creepy enough.

    So I intend to incorporate suggestions from Frank Hablawi, Joanna Charambura, and Sam Zeitlin in particular about fixing it to make it a stronger experience. I was struck by something that Kevin Fitzpatrick said, to the effect that Trail of Cthulhu plays like it is trying to recreate the experience of reading Lovecraft's prose, and thus feels very passive (Remi Treuer used the phrase "dungeon of words" about his own encounter with the game, which I think has a similar import). I was made aware of this issue when I ran a game at GenCon, but I thought I'd learned enough to craft an adventure in such a way that the process of data gathering feels more active. Clearly I have more to learn.

    But I'm actually very confident in my ability to take what I learned this weekend and use it to strip down the now-bloated playtest document into a lean, mean ToC adventure.
