\n I don't get it.

I honestly think that Donjon is Clinton's best game (although I've never played Princes').

Everybody in gaming over the age of, say, 25 has an incredible fondness for old school fantasy, the Red Box, and the dungeon crawl. Donjon recaptures all that, while still being 100% story game with some really clever and fun rules.

Yet, none of the forums ever talk much about it, and I have yet to be able to sell anyone on playing it.

Maybe it's my pitch, which has been along the lines of "it's main rule is that if you're successful at something, it makes it happen... you search for a secret door; and if it's successful, it's there."

I think I'm changing my pitch to: "It's a story game for dungeon crawls."

At any rate...

While I have you all here, I just wanted to ask, "Why don't you love Donjon as much as I do?"

Is it the huge d20 dice pools? Is it the game art? Is it the approach? Is it that you just have no taste in good gaming?

What is it? Why aren't you playing this awesome game???

    I was afraid someone might say that! The thing is, I've tried since to recapture it in words, but it never works. Suffice to say it involved a fighter in full plate armor diving into a black pit after another player had just narrated that the sound of running water could be heard down there. It also involved numerous attempts by said fighter to not drown, all of which were disastrous failures thanks to terrible, terrible rolls that were against all odds.     Also, Donjon is cool because it's the only game I've ever played where I had an elf with a goddamned cloak of elvenkind and goddamned boots of elvenkind, goddammit, because I used the treasure seeking rules to find them.

    They were... underwhelming in terms of power level, sure.

    But they were mine.

    \n I *literally* just heard through the grapevine (aka Chris at IPR and EndGame) that Clinton is "shopping around" for a new printer for Donjon and TSoY.

    Nice print copies may, yet, be on their way! (Unfortunately, this news came as a response as to why I can't run it at the series).

    I have the PDFs of both Donjon and the B-1 Supplement, but printed out the CC web version mainly because it took up a bit less ink and was easier to format for me.

    I can understand the d20 thing. It really is a *lot* of dice. But, to me, that's part of the appeal of the game. It's actually a bit absurd rolling that many dice. I think that adds a bit of humor to the whole thing. Not that I think Donjon should be relegated to the status of "humor game." I think even with the obscene amount of dice, it's suitable for more "serious" games (but definitely works best for it's intended purpose of "recapturing the feeling of old school gaming.")

    I'm actually a little (but pleasantly) surprised by all the favorable responses in this thread.

    I once had a guy look at me like I had lost my mind when I was asking if anybody would be interested in playing it. "I dunno... that sounds really weird..." was his response when I explained how the players get to partly narrate their successes (1 fact or 1 die). Although, truth be told, he didn't really seem to be a story gamer-type at all... and we were playing Rifts (god help me, I hate that game but was desperate for some gaming) at the time.

    \n I'd like to put in a dissenting opinion.

    a) I adore Clinton's designs and hope that he counts me as a friend (if a distant one).
    b) I think Donjon is important design work and important for me to have played.
    c) I won't play it again (in the way that I'd play ANY game if someone says "hey we're playing a game wanna join us?" but I won't seek it out to play)

    Why? Although I kinda dig the d20s I kinda liked the d10s better (although I have really been enjoying the few Other World games I played with Mike). I like pairing off the 10s then 9s, etc. I find the d20s generate higher scores but also tended to be a little more "gonzo" in the results (if that makes any sense). Also I didn't like how often you rolled, especially in combat. I know what it's emulating but I did't like the roll to hit/roll for defense/roll for damage/roll for toughness, etc.

    I definately think that a good bit of Donjon goes into my RBH games which I had a better experience with. Now that's a pretty subjective thing, no doubt. But I certainly think that Clinton's produced more satisfying designs for my playstyle.

    Just my opinion,

      \n I dunno, Andy. Maybe.

      I love the hell out of Donjon, as I think my posts above show. But things already tend to slow down during combat. My overall experience matches what some folks have been saying above (and I think this issue came up more than once on the Forge back in the day, as well): basically, too many dice need to be rolled too many times in succession to get things done. And this can slow down play at the wrong times. We played by the book for a while, and eventually we decided to cut the number of rolls nearly in half by folding attack and damage into a single roll (and doing the same with defenses). This helped considerably. And it's worth noting that we also never bothered to use d20s to play, actually -- we always just used d10s because we already had dozens of them, and the core die system is the same as Sorcerer, so we knew we could get functional results with d10s.

      Anyway, when it comes to practical measures to improve game play at the table, I have to admit that I'm skeptical of any suggestions that would slow down play even more than the already sometimes slow pace of the core system. And this seems to be one of the things that a fair number of players of the game keep bringing up, so it's a good candidate for being part of the system that could use some revision. It isn't a total deal-breaker, but it isn't a non-issue. I think it's a real phenomenon.

      Posted By: vertigo25Tell me more... this intrigues me
      \nIf I remember correctly, by the book each attack in combat requires an attack roll versus the target's parry or dodge roll, and then if this roll is successful, you follow it with a damage roll against the target's defenses. Since all rolls are opposed (no static target numbers), this means that to resolve every single attack in combat four rolls must be made (two by the attacker, two by the defender). We reduced it to one by the attacker, one by the defender by collapsing Attack/Damage into one and Defend/Soak into another. Now, when you do this, you do lose some of the character distinctiveness because you don't have (for example) a character who is inaccurate (low attack roll) but strong (high damage roll), or hard to hit (good defense rolls) but easily injured (no armor, low Wherewithal). We were willing to make that sacrifice. I do remember it took a bit of juggling to decide what to count for each roll. I think we just used Virility + one relevant ability + weapon damage for attacks and damage, and we used Wherewithal + one relevant ability + armor for the defense roll. This speeds things up, and we preferred the faster paced combats that it provided.
