    \n I run some good action scenes, and I think of everything like a movie. I describe it cinematically, like "now we're zooming in, her hand gripping the sword..." or "this is totally when we focus on the two of you and the rest of the battle gets blurry and silent in the background..." or "yeah, glass is shattering in slow motion and your arms are flailing like you're swimming through the smoke..."

    So yeah, think like a movie and describe it just like that. Cinematic logic takes precedence over everything: bad guys catch up when it makes sense, our attention is drawn strictly to the action in every situation. Skip over boring stuff: cut to the dramatic moments of exchanges, like when there's a chase we see them on the roof, then in the stairwell, then crossing the street, but we don't follow them through each and every hallway. Just the moments with interesting obstacles. It's like you're doing the filming and editing at the same time. That's how I think of it, at least.

    I run some good action scenes, and I think of everything like a movie.
    \nThis, but I would like to broaden it. What you need to do is to think swashbuckling, and things will sort itself out. I've been playing descriptive-heavy combats for six years now and I got two mantras that I use all the time during battles.

    If they can do it in an easy way, then they can do it in a hard way
    If they can throw a grenade to take out some mooks, then they can leap on the wall and fight them for a minute, or tear the roof down over their heads. Don't give modification based on what they do, but by what they wish to accomplish. Personally, I think the difficulties in Lady Blackbird is set to high.

    They are action heroes!
    This follows the previous mantra. Would an action hero on the movie screen get away with this? Then the characters will get away with it too. Namedrop a few movies before you start to play - even before they get their characters - so they can use them as inspiration. Zorro, Pirates of the Caribbean, Star Wars and The Sea Hawk are some suggestions on swashbuckling. Sometimes I even go so far that I let the players write a name of a movie on their character sheet. Someone who writes Rob Roy will let the character fight differently compared to someone that wrote Rambo or Hero (examples for a more generic action game).

    ---

    I know that you should ask questions all the time, but you can also increase the effort for the players, by making them involve things in their descriptions. This already exist in the game due to the use of traits and keys, and you should never ever allow more than three of these things together. Never ... ever! What you could do is to put them in dangerous situations. You already got this kind of thinking when you write "reckless flying, chasing, escaping," but you can extend this. I always throw the characters out of something. Now it's their turn to describe their way out of it. Flood things, make things crash, make them loose control over mechanics, put them in a situation where they are about to be executed. Last time we played, I was going to be forced into marriage (again - I played Lady Blackbird), and the others saved me. The body guard teleported me away from the altar and then swung away with a wire connected to The Owl. No roll, only one short description.

    To come up with situations like this, I use two mantras. "Why do they fail?" and "Think big". It's not one goblin. It's a whole colony! The characters don't travel safely to one port, because an octopus will make them fail. No, "think big". A whole army of squids are chasing them Matrix style! "Why do they fail?" can be used anywhere. Ask yourself this question when they fail a roll in combat, so you can create more elements that they have to take into consideration when describing their next action. This creates a back and forth flow between you and your players.

    Ask yourself this question when you want something to happen. It can be as trivial as reading a book or epic like ... well, using the sun as an energy source. The latter could sound quite impossible, but remember: "they are action heroes!".

    \n Just wound up in a discussion like this at another forum, heh. Anyhow.

    Verbs over adjectives. Adjectives have a very poor color-to-time ratio. They take up too much gameplay time, usually, for the color that they add. That's because they're static tagalongs, and they don't create anything terribly new in their own right. Cool actions are about CHARACTERS doing THINGS to OTHER THINGS.

    The environment is dynamic. Encourage players to incorporate the terrain and environment into their description, and have it change through their actions and the enemies' actions! Kick over barrels, slash rigging, tilt the deck! Keep reincorporating these elements and letting them evolve as the scene progresses!

    Keep it punchy. You want to leave your players with vibrant but short images. A bunch of quick shots that stick in their minds are better than one elaborate "long shot" that they'll potentially get lost in. And remember--it's not a book or even a movie. It's a game, and a game has its own pace.

    \n One bit of advice I keep close to my heart is:

    You don't suck, he's just that good...or maybe just lucky. It's inevitable that a player is going to fail a roll during an action scene, and there's a strong tendency to narrate that as a PC blunder of some kind. Don't do that. Seriously, don't. Don't say he swings and misses: say that his opponent manages to block the attack. Don't say he slips and falls: say the rock he's pulling himself up by crumbles unexpectedly. Especially do this when it supports the PC's concept: the world's greatest driver doesn't lose the race because he's too clumsy to make a turn without crashing, he loses because some idiot backed a truck out of an alley and finding a way around him took too much time.

    It's such a little thing, but it really works so much better than harping on how terrible the PCs are at their jobs.

    \nIn terms of the pacing of a game--it has to be a lot quicker and snappier. That's for a couple reasons, in my mind.

    First, because you're sharing table space and spotlight time with a number of other players. If spotlight time is distributed fairly, then taking extra time on an action is multiplied by four or five. If spotlight is distributed unfairly, you're having fun at the expense of the others.

    Second, because table energy is a precious commodity. Description and adjectives tend to drain energy, verbs tend to boost it, because there's more engagement with the environment.

    And, when I think about it, books can afford to get into more description simply because your brain is able to absorb it better. When something is on a page, you can read each individual word, read the whole sentence, and unconsciously go back to review it all. When you're listening to something at the table, you get one shot at describing something, and everyone's attention is devoted to making sure they understand exactly what you're doing in the fiction. Details detract from that, because that's more for the brain to parse through.

    Obviously, it's not an absolute, and you shouldn't have Spartan action descriptions, but it's good to keep everything focused on what the active consequences of your move are.

    \n More action scene advice: never do the same thing twice. There should never be a round where someone says "I guess I try to attack him again" or whatever. Add descriptive flair to every manuever or event, so that even if mechanically your npc is "just attacking" again, last time she was "leaping behind the desk and popping off a round" and this time she "barrell rolls from the desk to the chair, firing wildly." The troll swings his club overhand at the paladin, but next time it will step back and jab like a lance, then go low for the feet with a wild swing... just mix up descriptions for everything, no matter if the rules engage those particular manuevers differently or not. Stuff like that keeps it fresh. Best case scenario, these fictional details add tactical depth. Worst case scenario, at least every round sounds and feels different, even if the same dice are getting rolled.

    \n I actually think it's the GM's duty to help the players differentiate their descriptions. If they fail, and the GM describes the failure like "She throws you on the ground and is just about to run her steam-driven arm through your body when... Oh, lets cut to someone else", its hard to do the same maneuver again. Frankly, the GM can do this with any kind of roll to make things interesting. It's never interesting if nothing changes.

    It's also the GM's duty to change the environment to keep things interesting (the players can help, of course). Over the years, I noticed that seven rolls are the maximum for a conflict if you want it descriptive. After that, the players start to focusing more on the mechanics ("I did five damage") than actually describing what the rolls did. I actually built into my own action game around that a combat can consist of at most five actions per player, and then the combat must come to a halt. Perhaps the ground is opening up or a ship is crashing down? Perhaps a third party is joining? Perhaps the opposition flees, or takes the characters captive? In my second game, I limited each conflict to five actions for the whole group.

    I would like to continue on what CarpeGuitarrem wrote about pacing. Why I wrote that you shouldn't allow involving more than three traits and keys is partly because of pacing. The maximum of elements that the player can involve in their descriptions are three, and three is the absolute most. Why? Because after that number, the players start listing things, and those descriptions are never fun to listen to. "Hmmm, the door is locked? Okay I'm going to try and open it, I get bonus dice because (checks list) I'm strong, and I'm clever enough to do it silently. And I wink at my friends, so I get another die because I'm doing it charmingly." (source)

    This is also why you should lower the difficulty for the rolls, otherwise the players can feel that they want to include ALL the aspects and keys every time. That leads to repetitive and long descriptions, which kills the flow and any kind of action feeling that you guys built up so far. (I understand the thing with high difficulties, and that's so the players will spend their free dice and therefore have reasons to play out slower character-driven scenes later on. I feel that, even if the idea is good, it's a bad implementation.)

    Summary
    1. Break up action filled sequences after five rolls.
    2. Include at most three elements in each description.

    \n Cool suggestions here Let me add:

    Light Some Fuses. Something important is going to happen soon! This provides pacing and encourages risky actions. It can include literal fuses (the airship is rigged with explosives and about to blow), time windows (the evil captain is about to reach the escape boat), and now-or-never moments (the ships are passing each other; your only chance to jump across).

    Provide Clear Goals. Make sure the players know what is at stake and where they're supposed to charge. Otherwise you get "what should we do now about this vague problem" moments that suck out the cinematic energy.

    Let Them Shine. Ensure that every PC has a chance to do what they're best at, whether it's piloting or fisticuffs or deception.

    Details, Details, Details. Add interesting and quirky details to everything. The enemies, the environment, the situation, everything. You never know what the players are going to pick up and run with.

    Once you have characters in a rich environment doing what they're great at under intense pressure while pursuing clear and important objectives, you've got great cinematic drama

    \n When I'm writing action scenes in scripts or running them in games, I focus on two things: geography and consequences. Both of these keep action scenes feeling 'alive' and real to the players.

    Geography: Keep a clear idea of where people are in relation to each other and what's keeping them separated (that they'd have to overcome in order to fight

    Consequences: every action has a reaction. If they shoot a window that's separating them from their enemy, there's glass on the floor. If you're trying to hide from someone on a beach at night, you leave footprints in the sand. If you're running through a city, you'll get tired.

    You don't have to play the consequences straight away, but it's really satisfying when you do play them--people tend to nod with appreciation and go "Yeah, that's right" (acknowledging that something should happen, even if it screws them).

    \n A little thing, which makes a big difference: You need to give players something to respond to when they're taking their actions, so you need to be careful to describe NPC actions too. Just as how talking scenes tend to build description upon description, where you'll say something specifically to push the character to say things that highlight aspects of their character, action sequences go the same way, only with action instead of dialogue. You'll build off of each other's descriptions, and that will give the action sequence a form.

    The main reason I mention this is that, in the role of GM, you'll have to describe much more action than the players will, so it can be easy to run out of ideas. I sometimes find it helpful to have a list of basic types of actions I can riff off of, for those sessions where I'm a bit brain-dead.

    \n As a very high-level, general advice: Action in roleplay is all about energy. It's been touched upon before, but I'd like to expand on it. I guess I use this/2010/09/flow-er-model-step-1-3.html definition of 'energy', but it doesn't matter too much. During an action scene you can track energy by how impatient and ecstatic people are, how loud they're talking, how big their body language is. Anything that makes the energy drop is bad, obviously. To a certain degree, you can create energy simply by showing it yourself; If you're ecstatic, you're players will become ecstatic. So stand up, don't whisper, make sweeping body language movements to supplement the cool things you're describing. More energy is created through feedback, through sending back what you receive. It grows the more it bounces back and forth. For this reason, I'm a big of systems that allow the GM and player (or player and player for PvP) to "duel on words", trade cool descriptions in rapid succession. It drops whenever there's significant time lag. When there's an awkward pause not filled with action chops. Dice and other such mechanics doesn't have to be damage the energy if they're quick and tense, like when there's a lot riding on every roll, but they quite easily can. If there's a lot of fiddling with mechanics and special powers, lots of interpreting of the dice, that can destroy the energy, and you have to start from scratch.

    That's not to say you have to have full steam all the time. Personally I love flashbacks, courtesy of 3:16, as a mechanic for interjecting some calm and characterization into a long battle. A flashback to where a character learned their ultimate technique (think Kill Bill 2) also has a natural platform to jump right back into the action again! So yeah, take breaks, whether it's for in-game socializing or real life air, but as long as you have fuel, keep up that energy!

    Also super important: Be a fan of the player (not of the player character as in AW, though that's not bad either). This means encouraging them and believing in them to do something cool. Some people have difficulty coming up with cool action stunts under the implicit time pressure roleplaying has and the energy requires, but encourage them even still. Nod and smile at them, compliment them them for trying, even when they don't quite hit the mark. And when they do, dammit say so, don't just think it! Again, feedback, they give you energy and you give it back again.

    \n I'll add my five cents: Whenever you're into action, keep the metagame really short. All the advice on this thread takes precedence over pauses for checking rulebooks. Don't take more that three minutes to double-check all the bonuses you're adding to the dice roll. Keep on the table one of those clocks used to play chess or a hourglass if you must. And it's not like you've got to keep eye-contact with the players on a staring contest, but each time you let your eyes wander elsewhere for too long, it's a cue for players to focus on something else. And even if whatever is distracting you is another aspect of the game, that takes a toll on the flow of the game, slowing down all the action you've been building.

    Of course, you can also play battles at a bit slower pace and still have fun going full powergaming tactical with miniatures, it may still feel like an action scene, but that's a completely different atmosphere to the one I'm thinking of here. In my group we enjoy the tactical miniature battles too, but personally I've had much more fun by far whenever we used them only for reference, hand-waved most of the specific rules and went for a broader interpretation of the general ones and went full cinematic on almost non-stop action.
