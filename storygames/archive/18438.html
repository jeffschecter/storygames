    \n I think the key element is not "nothing bad should happen to anyone vulnerable", it's more about watching for certain trends (and being aware of them) + making sure you don't trivialize the situation for the vulnerable party. For instance, if a certain character suffers some great pain and degradation, don't just treat it as a bit of backstory to gloss over: be sure to address the consequences, the impact on the person and those around them. What sucks is when, say, a male protagonist's loss of his favourite car is treated with more weight in the story than the death of his girlfriend. There are a lot of misogynist tropes which get included in stories and games "just because" -- it's an easy emotional grab for a male audience, or considered to be -- rather than using those situations deliberately and with care, as fuel for powerful, moving stories featuring characters we care about.

    Generally speaking, if a character in your story doesn't appear to have *any* say whatsoever in what happens to them, and doesn't get to make any important decisions (which influence the outcome of the story, or at least of that character's personal story), that's probably a bad sign. Take a closer look and ask yourself why you're writing (or playing) things that way.

    Edit: I saw that you watched "part of" the video. The best discussion (and a very well-rounded one) happens in the last 5-6 minutes: if you're going to watch anything, watch that part!

    \n I guess I'll stir the pot and play Devil's Advocate. These games seem to be firmly in the ultra-violent spectrum (I think Borderlands 2 is the only one mentioned that I've played). They feature the deaths of hundreds of people; but they're mostly just men, so that's okay. If even half (equality) of the mooks were women, it would be held up as an example of misogyny. The death of a woman is used to garner an emotional response and this is key; I saw Anita in one of those TED videos where she talked about some of the comments she received on some of her videos. Awful, hateful things that would make you murderous if said to some-one you loved. In fact, the same emotions that these games try to evoke. Her Kickstarter was over-funded within days.
    Just to be clear - these are not games that feature violence against women. These are games that feature violence. The (male) protagonist has only violence as an option. He can die, too, but at least the game gives you the option of saving him (that's the whole game-play; surviving by killing). Once you cancel out the gender violence, you're left with the objectification of the female. The point was made that she is a "prize" to be won. In some cases, not even that. She's the ball in a patriarchal game; a good metaphor that deliberately overlooks the emotional bond of love/caring that the games (in they're very limited, stilted way) try to engender in the player.
    So, I would conclude that what we are seeing is not some patriarchal agenda to disempower women; but rather bad, lazy writing.
    It's powerful stuff to see all those game cut-scenes of women being killed over and over. It's meant to be; that is it's function in a lazily written story. It's not the woman that is disposable - it's the plot hook that's a toss-away. Apply the same technique to lazy writing in sit-com plots. Hell, just limit it to examples of the dunce husband/boyfriend being pwned by his partner.
    We are shown a very narrowly focused symptom of a wide spread disease. Some of you can't remember a time when there weren't video games; let me assure you that violence against women predates them. And isn't that the real issue - real violence against real women. Doesn't make the video games right; just makes them poorly written.

    \nI'd like to mention that this hasn't been my experience. When you play a game in which they only way you can really force you towards an objective is through violence, that's what people will do. Maybe not every time... but much more often than they would otherwise.

    I mean, it's even a trope: "Dude, make sure your plots can survive the death of any NPC. Because you know your PCs will try to kill many or most of them, even if there's no good reason for it!"

    When you play something D&D-like, where the only real form of non-negotiated conflict resolution comes through hit point loss and death, when people don't get what they want, they turn to violence: it's the only hammer they have to advocate for their character. Someone doesn't want to give your character what you think she deserves? Ok, what are your options? Well, you can ask them for it. Didn't work? Ok, you can threaten them. Didn't work? Your only remaining option is to kill them and take it.

    Play a game where you have other tools at your disposal, and the difference in behaviour is shocking. For example, if you're playing Monsterhearts, when you can't get something you want, most players will try to get (or spend) some Strings on their opposition. That means they will flirt with them, offer them experience, try to sleep with them, or call them names in public. Because that's what's effective in that game, that's your "hammer" which you can use to push towards your objectives.

    Think about it:

    Here's a person who won't give you what you want. What do you want to do?

    Game A: If you shoot them, it's pretty safe for you, and if you succeed (a high probability, since the game is weighted towards you winning fights, more often than not) you will get what you want. If you want to negotiate, they can always decide not to give it you, ever. (This is D&D, Dogs in the Vineyard, hundreds of other games)

    Game B: You can try to shoot them, but your odds of success are about 5%, and even if you hurt or kill them, you might not get what you want. Or you can show them a little skin/a nice gift/take them on a date/out-dance them in a public competition, which gives you a 70% chance of success, and if you roll well, you will definitely get the thing you wanted. (This appears more often in non-mainstream or "story" games, in my experience.)

    In which game do you think you (hypothetical average gamer) will choose the violent option?

    Game design matters.

    So, yes, I think tabletop games don't suffer from the Damsel in Distress and similar issues as much as the video games Sarkeesian talks about (although it certainly pops up now and then), but they certainly do, as a category of games, suffer from the violence-is-the-only-form-of-effective-interaction problem.
