    If you want to capture the brutality of war for the infantry -- and I don't think any game has ever really done that -- I'd suggest taking any simple system you're comfortable with ( Savage Worlds, maybe?) and layering on a level of sheer random "you're fucked." GM fiat will do, but here's one experimental thought:

    1. Every player draws one card before a combat starts, then the players turn them over one at a time (in a random order so no one gets stuck going first all the time), so if you have three players, one turns over his card before play starts, one about halfway through the combat, one at the very end.

    2. If you turn up any card but an Ace, you're fine. Discard it. Don't reshuffle the deck. Ever. Not once in the entire campaign.

    3. If you turn up the Ace of Spades, you're dead. Right then. No recourse. Stepped on a landmine, stray bullet to the head, your best friend tried to clear a jam and blew your face off instead, whatever. Dead.

    4. If you turn up the Ace of Hearts, your best friend's dead. You nominate your best friend, but the group can override you by majority vote (GM gets one vote, same as anyone else; you don't get one).

    5. If you turn up the Ace of Clubs, you're maimed. For life. Right now. You live, but you're not in the Army anymore. Look at the last non-Ace card anyone revealed if you want details: 2 or 3 = spine broken, paralyzed from the neck down; 4 or 5 = spine broken, paralyzed from the waist down; 6 or 7 = arm blown off; 8 or 9 = leg blown off; 10 or Jack = castrated; Queen or King = blind. GM decides appropriate cause based on battlefield circumstances, but remember you could have always tripped and broken your damn fool neck.

    6. If you turn up the Ace of Diamonds, something good happens! The fight ends, just like that, and the enemy runs or surrenders. GM decides what pulled your ass out of the fire unexpectedly. For example: sudden arrival of air support (like that last scene in "Saving Private Ryan"); enemy leader hit by stray bullet and their morale broke; friendly artillery suddenly landed on enemy; enemy's own artillery suddenly landed on enemy due to forward observer error; friendly unit engaged enemy on flank; enemy had supply problems prior to combat beginning and suddenly ran out of ammo.

    Do this for every single combat you run, and I think you'll see your players get appropriately jittery. And of course you can customize effects for other cards.

    Here's a weird one!

    Albedo Platinum Catalyst is military sci fi with furry characters. Hard sci fi â€” aerodynamics, chemical bullets, no artificial gravity, radio communications, politics. With copious moral ambiguity. And don't forget that they're funny animals. The rabbits are fascist bad guys.

    I'm not crazy about the system for the most part â€” it's sort of what you'd expect for a 90s RPG despite its postmillennial publication. But there's a gem of a rule that I'm planning on using for a game sometime:

    You have buddies, called "supporting characters" who are your character's retinue. They've known you for some time and will do what you say, giving you multiple characters to use. But here's where it gets super excellent:

    From the book, in a sidebar (this only gets a sidebar and it should be the whole game):

    Why have multiple characters?

    In the dangerous setting of Albedo, much of the drama is derived from the constant threat of mortality. If characters aren't put in mortal danger on a regular basis, then the drama lacks sincerity. On the other hand, if Player-Characters spent most of their time in the informary or hiding in bunkers, the story would lack interest. Commanding officers aren't at the front of the party, or bearing the brunt of danger â€” that's why a command structure exists. With Albedo, the Players shouldidentify strongly with their Main Character, but also have respect for their supporting cast. Game Hosts should craft plots that challenge the Main Characters to do the right thing, with a level of danger that often results in grievous harm to the Supporting Cast, as a reminder of the seriousness of the setting.

    What that means to me is that you name your hit points. They have home planets, species and a history. And they die. You get assigned a new dude, but the new kid just isn't the same.

    A guideline in a sidebar isn't enough to fully support this, but it makes the game start to be about something meaningful â€” war is about watching your friends die.

    This one sidebar and the paragraphs around it are enough to seed an entire game for me. And if you can work with the skills and task resolution system, maybe the system's good for you, too. Plus, the comic is pretty neat and a lot of the artwork is either from the comic or done by its creator, Steve Gallacci, for the game.

    Remember that "grunts" -- or even junior officers in many units -- won't have much input on "find, fix, and flank": They often get told, "go attack that" or "here, defend this" without even being told the context. GM fiat or sheer randomness is honestly a good way to depict the kind of situations they get thrown into, and even what the odds are. Forget any idea of "balance" in the wargame sense of "these two forces are worth the same amount of points": Fights should alternate unpredictable between cakewalks and walking through the valley of the shadow of death.
