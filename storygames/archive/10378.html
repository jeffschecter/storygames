    \n I'm running a Burning Empires campaign. It's painful fun, and I find that the actual interaction & fiction generation we go through in a 9-hour play session is much less than what we do in a 3-hour Archipelago session.

    Still, it's sort of fun trying to find the arcane rules in the clunky rules text, not to mention trying to piece together the setting elements. Highlights from last session include the regular "how many scenes do we each get again" hunt through three different sections of the rules, none of which are completely unambiguous; and the question "what is the main religion actually about", which is covered in only two paragraphs in the book in addition to random information buried in lifepaths. (NOTE: I am not looking for long posts with answers to these questions, so please don't post them).

    The part of the game I like the best is that the players mostly run it themselves. And there is a perverse sort of fun in seeing how the fiction and the mechanics interact, like a burning weather balloon weighted down by a chunk of Big Ben's internal clockwork.

    \n It's worth noting that some games don't support a chronological order, aside from "prep" elements, because they are so open-ended or (for lack of a better phrase) unstructured in time. I have struggled with this aspect of GLASS in trying to begin layout and full rules write-up. At the moment, GLASS rules are setup so that one learns ALL the terms, ALL the Calls, and at least has skimmed ALL the Abilities and Limitations and Advantages and Defects... and the last (procedural) chapter is actual character creation. Why? Because it's a points-buy system and one needs to understand the scope of effects and how to tune them to suit a concept before one can, say, make a "Magic Missile spell."

    Similarly, the procedures for play are presented VERY early, so that one know when and how all the elements come into play... because one can totally control initiation of those procedures in most LARPs (i.e. open play). Now, WITHIN an given exchange or use of ability, there's a "procedure," but it amounts to little more than a script, of sorts, for what is done when to activate an Ability and then attempt to hit, after which there's a Response, which is in itself an Ability that might have activation requirements (though not usually) and its own resolution or attempt to hit. In actual play, it become second nature--which is key: these "exchanges" take place in seconds of real-time combat.

    Anyhow... long example to show that, actually, chronological order only works for some (highly structured) games. Others require at least some "info dump" of components so that, later, procedures acting on those components are comprehensible. Finiding the balance... or the "orientation" of top-down or bottom-up is definitely an art form. BE seems (to me) to get that backwards at time--procedures coming as info dumps without all of the components defined; as a result, it can be a tough slog (and, yes, requires two full reads to really grok).

    This might be way many games have "tutorial" bit near the front, to get you sort of playing and to teach you more and more elements in the order of core to fringe (or basic to advanced, or minimal and full simulation, etc).
