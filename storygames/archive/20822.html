    \n I think there are many positive features to more selective group discussions - you can speak more honestly, perhaps, without holding back, and have the conversations you want with the people you like. But it makes it incredibly difficult for anyone who is not already part of those circles to participate.

    Let's try a practical question:

    For instance, let's say I pick up a new RPG I like. Cool, I think! I'm gonna run this on Friday. But then I decide to ask for some advice about it, so as to do it right. However, I don't know a whole lot of people who are familiar with the game.

    In the "forum world", I could do a Google search of Story Games, first, to see if there have already been threads about that topic. I'll often find some good ones, maybe old, maybe new, of collected advice on the topic. So I can read through that first. Forum conversations often have well thought-out answers, or link to previous answers which are.

    If that doesn't bear fruit, or I have more questions, I can make a new post and see if the topic attracts any other people.

    But what do I do in the "social media age"? How do I get advice for my Friday night game? Here I am on G+, ok, but I don't have any friends - I'm new.

    \nPaul - YMMV, but I tend to stay in my home stream a lot. I subscribe to a lot of things and set them to post to my home stream, where I enjoy a constant barrage of diverse material. On the home page, posts with new comments will get a boost upward, but not continually, and I think there's a diminishing value on the rank boost per comment over time. I'm sure it's even more complex than that. Anyway, my home stream is a giant stream of very diverse stuff.

    When it comes to notifications, however, I'm more strict. I only receive email notifications on my own threads and when I'm tagged. When I hit G+, the first thing I do is click the little red bell icon to scan what's been posted lately (in those places I care to get notifications from). Once I've gone through that list, I turn my attention to the home stream and begin scrolling. I don't actually hop out to Communities very often; I tend to comment right there from my stream.

    By going directly to Community and Collection pages and clicking the little gear icons, you can turn notifications on or off, and also change their frequency settings to low, medium or high. So I do that. If you set them low you will miss some stuff. And you can set what you get email notifications for under your home menu "Settings".

    I actually do have a Circle with just me in it. Two, in fact. These are useful for things you will need later, but keep it very focused or it will just become another slushpile. I also have a Collection with just me in it. I'm testing to see which is better, and what the difference is.

    One way to organize your own public posts so you can find them later on G+ is by using #hashtags. With some people it has become the primary means of post organization, although it's less popular there than it is on Twitter. Hashtags can be searched, but like any search this can pull in false positives if you use common words, so you want to make your organizational hastags unique, usually by #UsingCamelCase and stringing several words together.

    It is odd that Google (of all companies) has such shitty search features on their social network.

      \n Not to derail, but that can be argued. Forums are a legacy of Web 1.0. What we typically think of as "social media" or "social networks" are a Web 2.0 phenomenon. There are core differences in the way such systems are built and used, mostly dealing with ease of distribution and redistribution, the idea being that content could be added to the network or viewed from the network regardless of what page you were on.

      It is perhaps ironic that although social media were built with networked distribution in mind, and although they possess APIs permitting all sorts of remote interaction with their content (which is what makes them Web 2.0), most people still go to facebook to read facebook posts, youtube to search for videos, etc. The great decentralization heralded by Web 2.0 failed to happen when it comes to the giant social media networks. Or rather, it only goes in one direction: people produce content in the center (and they mostly consume it there too), and then it gets pushed out in tiny chunks to other places. This gives it an almost "broadcast" quality. Then there's the vast scope and great speed with which such networks move, making storage of long discussions impractical for reasons explored earlier.

      Anyway, point is, what we're doing here is structurally and functionally different than what we're doing on FB, G+, etc.
