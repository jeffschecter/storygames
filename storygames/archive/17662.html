    One time a fate card really surprised you, and worked nonetheless!
    \nI think maybe once someone drew the chance card \u201cAmor Victor\u201d in a confrontation with the evil (?) Spider Queen of the City, resting in her moon tower.

    The card reads: \u201cThe power of love triumphs in this situation. Exactly how this happens is for you to describe.\u201d

    I think it was an interesting scene, with Nindra showing some of her more emotional sides. She\u2019s just lonely, the poor thing. \n
    How you and Martin Bull Gudmundsen managed to write the first draft together!
    \nThe basic concepts for the game emerged from a piece of automatic writing I did in 2001 (printed in the back of the book). I thought to myself that \u201cthis almost looks like the seeds for an RPG setting\u201d, and contacted Martin. We wrote back and forth to each other with snippets of texts on a Yahoo Group for a couple of years. Pretty early on, Martin came up with the Chance Card-system. It confused me at the time, I didn\u2019t really understand where he wanted to go with this. But we started a test play, played for 19 sessions, kept on developing the game, and finally in 2008 were able to release it in the Norwegian. \n

    What are the overlaps/differences in regards to Archipelago?
    \n* I think Archipelago also uses the resolution card system Matthijs invented. (Yes, but... No, and... etc).
    * Itras by also has a separate Chance Card system, which works in a slightly different way (see below)
    * Archipelago is GM-less, Itras by has a GM.
    * Archipelago is setting-less, Itras by has a specific setting.

    \n (An introduction for those not familiar with the game)

    Setting
    Itras by labels itself a \u201csurreal role playing game\u201d. It\u2019s also been called a retro-surreal urban fable.

    The setting is a city, reminiscent of Western cities in the 20\u2019s and 30\u2019s. In the city center, reality is relatively stable, but the further afield you get, the more it deteriorates, mutates, becomes dream-like. In the setting chapter you\u2019ll find descriptions of dreams which have become real, mad scientists, an outline of a city strictly divided by class, sea elephants, the Machine God who lurks under the city, a gentleman with a monster in his basement, a description of the structural cancer that haunts some of the city\u2019s buildings and much more.

    We heartily encourage each group to make Itras by their own by changing, adapting, adding and subtracting.

    There are pulpish elements, horror elements, creepy surreal elements, all together in a strange blend we hope will inspire.

    System
    The system is very simple and described in a few pages. The game pioneered the use of Matthiijs Holter\u2019s resolution cards (also featured in Love in the Time of Seid and the Swedish RPG S\xe4gen), and has it\u2019s very own Chance Card system. The resolution cards function as a resolution mechanic, and read things like \u201cYes, but\u2026\u201d, \u201cNo, and\u2026\u201d, \u201cYes, and\u2026\u201d etc. The results are interpreted by players after the cards have been drawn.

    The Chance Cards can be drawn once per session by each player, and serve to infuse the game with surrealism. Some examples:

    Cut Scene: Jump forward three hours. Describe what conditions the characters find themselves in. You\u2019re not allowed to describe what has happened in the meantime.

    Nemesis! This card awakens the character\u2019s Nemesis. In some way, this arch enemy affects the situation. Exactly how is up to you. Doesn\u2019t the character have an arch enemy? Well, now she does.

    What\u2019s in its pockets? The character has something in her pocket which might be useful. What could it be?
    These cards are interpreted by the player who draws them.

    Advice
    The book is chock full of advice on how to roleplay, be a gamemaster, plan a campaign, make an adventure, play with surrealism and more. I think it\u2019s as good as a beginner\u2019s game as for more experienced players.

    So Itra is Archipelago with GM and setting, or is Archipelago the Generic Universal Itras By?
    \nI think they're just two separate games sharing some similarities in system.\n
    That's Norwegian, Finnish, German and English down. Any other languages on the way?
    I received one PM regarding a possible French edition, but that's very far from final.\n
    If I have the Norwegian edition, do I need the English edition?
    Not really. There's a new sample scenario, some new illlustrations, some new sample characters, some new setting elements, a few clarifications here and there... I guess it's like a v. 1.5.\n
    Itras By has been in development and playtest for years and years; like most successful RPGs, the work for designers and organizers doesn't seem to stop at publication - the work of promotion, game organizing and support just keeps on going. What's it like to have a game around for that long?
    Today's youth are like Jesus: they live at home until they're 30, and if they do anything it's a miracle.

    Our 11-year old still needs loving support, and as with any love child, we don't mind.

    What's given me the greatest joy during the past few years is suddenly meeting or hearing about people who've played (or just read and been inspired by) the game, sometimes for years on end... That's both humbling and exiting.\n
    How can I become a famous game designer of sexy glamorous games like you? Does it involve pacts with dark and nameless powers from beyond the stars we know?
    Now that you mention it, I did actually sigilize for Itras by at some point, but I don't believe in that Chaos Magick stuff anymore (even though it eerily often seems to work).

    Dedicate, test a lot, play a lot, expand, revise, be open to other's ideas whilst trusting your vision... Persevere...

    What role did the additional translators play, compared to your role? How do you think things might differ if a third party had done the translation?
    I translated the main bulk of the text, Jimi translated the sample scenario (and helped proofread the rest), the publishers have helped with additional editing. Neither me nor Jimi are professional translators (or native English speakers), so that might make for some weird choices of words/sentences here and there. I'm eager (and a bit nervous) to read the reactions to the language of the book.

    On the upside, I'm very familiar with the book's content, and was able to do some editing in addition to the pure translation of the text.

    \n As you know, I recognize in myself some of what you're talking about. I don't think I'm bipolar, but I do have more ups and downs than most people around me; and it's wonderful to be in that phase where all my ideas seem great, they come easily, and I really just need to sit down and write them down and get them into the world NOW. (It sucks to be in that phase where the world has no color and I imagine I am worthless.) I'm speculating that the creative high is related to that thing where I perceive connections and patterns in so many things - "this idea from this field would be great fun when combined with this other idea from this entirely different field". However, seeing too many patterns can tip me over into semi-mystical and less rational moods. So, part of why I like Itras By so much is that it has all these things in it, things that I see and feel and recognize personally.

    \nA couple of short reviews over on Drivethru/RPG Now

    "The setting is creepy, majestic, and presents scenario ideas on each page. The rules are simple and clear, and focus on narrative control. Fans of "Over the Edge" will be impressed by this game, in both rules and setting. I highly recommend it."

    ... And a review over on RPG Net

    "Overall, I\u2019m frankly blown away by Itras By. If you like Gormenghast, Twin Peaks, Kafka, Mieville or even Roald Dahl, you will find something to intrigue you in Itras By. I would have liked a slightly more structured system, but the trade off is the greater narrative freedom granted to both players and the Gamemaster. In general, this is an intriguing and well executed piece of game design, and the background is both absorbing and inspiring. The material here will be useful in some way to anyone with an imagination."

    A collection of the reviews I've found to date: http://itrasby.com/reviews/

      \n We played at Modern Myths, in MA last night. I loved our game. @Evan_Torner ran it.

      The Moon Tower from the setting was central. Our characters revolved around Babelsburg (renamed Babelsfield) and the near-failing film industry there. With the help of a magical quest to the Moon Tower, captured on film as the star Brigitta (played by Evan) travelled through the world of the films, the studio was saved.
