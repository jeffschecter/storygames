    \n Huh. Hard to say, without pointing at a game like Universalis (and although I've played Universalis many times, it never fails to bore me in play a little each session). More than "games that exist out there", I'd love to see more people simply talk in a forum (like this one!) about how they would take on these issues in their games. This also cuts out the "read these essays" effect. Coffee talk.

    Frex, I've blathered on last year about how using some advice from With Great Power about having players play major NPCs when they were not in scenes made the game a lot of fun... and have extrapolated it into a style of play that I reproduce for just about all the games I play now, *especially* traditional ones: L5R, D&D, etc.

    Here's how I've been addressing "Avatars" lately (do you mean PCs here? The characters which each player has singular control over? Just want to make sure): So, playing one character, getting under the skin and all, a character which I have the most control over is really fun for me. It's what I love about roleplaying. So I'm playing mostly in games where everyone has one character which is "theirs", which they control just like I've done all the way back to the days of Star Frontiers.

    How things have been different recently for me: Everyone's got that one character... but there's ALSO a pool of NPCs which the players regularly play. It's a kind of shared control: Sometimes the mob boss will be Travis if Travis' main PC dude is not in the scene. Sometimes I (the GM) will take on the role of the mob boss because I have a particular plot point that I want to introduce. It got to the point where I had a 1-page list of major NPCs for our L5R campaign, and next to each was listed the player who had taken on the role the most, as well as the player who had taken on the role the second-most. Almost like a playbill, with "Actor" and "Understudy" :-)

    In any case, this has been badass. There's all sorts of living NPCs in motion which keeps things hellishly interesting for us, far more than my old "I, the GM, controls all characters who are not on the sheet in front of you players" style of play.

    Director's stance (if I'm remembering things correctly, I might be getting the terms mixed up) is easier to do: Someone will be like, "Oh, before we go raid the mansion, I'd like to meet up with the merchant's wife". And I'd be like, "Shit, ok. So it'll be you then, and (point at another player who won't have their PC in the scene) you're the wife. Go." etc, and watch what happens. As GM, facilitate dice rolls when they need to happen, but otherwise just let everyone get in there and roleplay.

    Again, though, I like to do this kind of "avatar sharing" deeply (with NPCs), but while still letting the players each have "their guy".

    Hope that helps and wasn't too far of topic. Did you have any specific questions or anything about these "takes" or what in particular you want to see or know more about?

    -Andy

    \n Games, games... these are the first example I remember

    I don't know exactly what you mean with "avatar", so I will use "protagonists" or "character".

    A game with protagonists played by more than one player, with no single authority over him/her: "it was a mutual decision"
    A game with protagonists played by more than one player, but one has always authority in any single moment: "Spione", "Dirty Secrets (not all the protagonists in this case)
    A game with protagonists played by a single player, but with major inputs from the other players that can't be negated: "Carry" and "Dirty Secrets" during character creation, "Psi Run" during the game
    A game with protagonists played by a single player, with major inputs from the other players that can be fought and bargained or negated: "Polaris", "Carry",

    About Director's stance, a stance is more a player's position to the game during a single moment of time than a game propriety, in every single game we talk here there are moments of each stance (for example, when you get excited during a D&D game and describe what happen to your foe after you hit him, you are enjoying a moment of director stance). More than that, the stance by itself don't say what you can change or influence outside of your character: the backstory? Or only the scenery? What happen right now? Or everything? These are very important differences much more important than the stance the player is playing in this half second.

    In short (and doing no justice to the theory behind it: if you prefer a more exact explanation I can give you the links ) there are cases when a player can change (or add to) the backstory (the content), or he/she can have a strong input to the framing of scenes and the situation , or he/she can have a say in the outcomes and plot points, or he/she can narrate the results of the rolls or the draw or whatever. Or all of this or some of this. What is exactly that interest you?
