\nCutter lay in a natural gully cradled by the roots of a big tree. His body crawled with perspiration and he was muddy from dragging himself into position on his belly. The rifle extended ahead of him like another limb.

The air was dead-still and laden heavily with moisture. At a range of three hundred yards there was bullet drop to consider, but at least there was no crosswind. The compound squatted in the deep V of a narrow valley, watchtowers at three stations, but the men inside wouldn't see Cutter where he lay in wait.

He glassed the inside of the compound with his scope, tracking silently with the rifle. Fat droplets of warm water fell from the tree limbs above and soaked into the jungle camo Cutter wore. Somewhere down there was Trang. When he saw Trang, he would put a bullet in the man's head. The crosshairs. One touch of the trigger. Done.

All he had to do was wait.

So last week I posted a discussion about simulating the complexity of bullfighting. At issue was how to replicate the subtleties of the form (or of something like that) without resorting to total abstraction or its opposite, the flurry of dice rolls. I think the subject may have been too esoteric.

Thinking about this topic further, and because FigureFour's working on a ninja game, I realized there's another situation where this kind of thing comes up: assassination.

In this case, the actual act is a matter of a single die roll. In the example above, it's a sniper firing a single shot that will either eliminate the target or not. When a ninja strikes from the shadows she's either going to administer a killing blow or not. What matters here isn't the intricacy of the act, but in everything leading up to the act.

The sniper has to find a perfect place from which to take his shot. It can take minutes or hours and then there's the long, agonized waiting. A ninja creeps through a moonlit compound at night evading guards and climbing through unguarded windows to be in the exact right moment at the precise time to make her kill.

Sure, these things could be simulated by a series of dice rolls. The sniper checks Perception, or whatever, to find a suitable spot, then the applicable skill to assemble a nest and then when the time comes he pulls the trigger and rolls to it. Likewise with the series of stealth rolls the ninja makes to accomplish her task. These things don't seem to invest much in the process and what ought to be the climax \u2014 the killing itself \u2014 is just another roll at the tail end of some other rolls.

There has to be a way to dramatize the steps leading up to the assassination, but I don't know what it might be. Hence my submission to you, hive mind.

    \n Hm, here's something completely off of my head. it assumes a skill system of some sort . Let's call these complex things we want to simulate extended tasks. There is a number of steps to the task, of which the player knows at least some of, and may know all of.

    For your sniper/assassination case, they might be: find spot, (avoid patrol), wait, take shot, escape. The step in parentheses is unknown to the player until the step before.

    Each step consists of two or three options (one is possible, but less interesting, four or more may be excessive). Each option is consists of a two-sided card (actually, cards don't have to be physically present, but they are a convenient way to talk about it).

    Each topside of the card has a solution or approach to the step (the bushes near the road, the top of a nearby hill, etc.), a skill or a set of skills, an approximate difficultly, a possible benefits (phrased in terms of bonuses to later steps).

    Each face down side of a card has the actual difficulty and the actual benefits, and maybe some additional narrative color.

    The player makes their choice, (which could include retreating to an earlier stage, or aborting the whole procedure (although some steps, such as those involving enemies) may not allow this), flips over the card and either makes a skill check or automatically succeeds (if it's easy enough or they have enough time -- take 10 and those sorts of rules).

    If they succeed, they get the benefits on the card (a good location will give bonuses to the assassination shot), if they fail, they either go back and try again, or there will be consequences on the card (guards alerted! You fall in hole!, etc.).

    Repeat until task is done. Don't forget to role-play each step (if you're not bothered by the"Dance, Monkey, Dance" problem", this is a good place to give bonuses for good description to avoid this process from degenerating into a purely mechanical step).

    In this case, the actual act is a matter of a single die roll.
    \nI take issue with this assumption. I offer you the following simple resolution system as my rebuttal.

    System
    1) The Sniper Player narrates all the events and preparations leading up to the moment of assassination.
    2) The GM secretly writes down no less than one and no more than five complications on index cards, one per card. None of the complications may result in the failure of the assassination.
    3) The cards are shuffled and laid out, face-down, on the table.
    4) The Sniper Player rolls a single die of any variety.
    5a) If the roll is odd, the Sniper Player has the choice of either resolving the assassination as a failure (no kill, end of conflict) or submitting to a complication. A single card is flipped, the Sniper Player and the GM cooperatively narrate the changes in the situation due to the complication. Return to step 4. If all the complications have been used up, an odd roll indicates a failure and the end of the conflict.
    5b) If the roll is even, the assassination resolves uneventfully. End of conflict.

    Complication Examples:
    The attack kills an unintended target.
    The sniper leaves evidence behind.
    This location is no good. The job will have to happen somewhere else.
    The sniper is injured.
    The sniper is spotted by guards.

    Posted By: Sam HThere has to be a way to dramatize the steps leading up to the assassination, but I don't know what it might be.
    \nIs it disruptive to say that the drama is not in the steps leading up to the conflict, but rather in either what the character must risk being successful or what the unintended consequences are of his success?

    Sam, you're familiar with Burning Wheel's intent-task rules, right? That's one way of addressing this kind of issue.

    The other big-picture problem I see is, how are the discrete tasks leading up to the crucial moment systematized? I think this was part of why the bullfighting example fell flat--not a lot of people have familiarity with what goes into a matador performance. If I'm roleplaying an assassin, then most of what I describe will be cribbed from action movies I've seen. Who's judging whether or not the preparation is on-target? It's not ridiculous to think of someone at the table having military experience and therefore incorporating things into his descriptions that would never occur to me, but are significant (at least to him) in pursuing success. But this could just as easily come up in some kind of crafting challenge, or survival, or an athletic contest. So how is this discrepancy handled, between character understanding and player understanding?

    Feel free to ignore me if I\u2019m missing the trees for the forest.

    \n To go along with what Daniel and I say above:
    \n
    \nThe process you outline above (a series of rolls with the final big roll just another roll) is, as you mention, boring, with no big pay-off. If you miss one roll in the series you just keep rolling until you succeed/utterly fail (death or give up). Same thing with the big roll.
    \n
    \nBW addresses this by giving choices to the player: exactly what do you want to accomplish with this roll? How much are you willing to risk? What failure consequences are you willing to accept? It also rewards you (ding!) for making the rolls tougher/taking additional risks/making your life complicated by giving you extra dice.
    \n
    \nWhat I love about those extra dice is you can chose to spend them where you wish. Want to save them up for the big shot? Fine...just means all those other rolls are going to be much harder. But maybe you don't give a crap about the big roll...maybe you've got a kick ass sniper skill...you want to spend it on convincing your father to loan you the gun you're going to use to kill the target. And there's no way in hell papa is going to lend you his gun, especially since he voted for the guy you're going to kill. So you chose to spend you dice there.
    \n
    \nWhat I like about this approach is it sets out a job for the player, and the player decides how to accomplish it, but he also gets to make statements about what's important to him by spending his dice on what concerns him the most.

    \n The system that my brother Mel and I occasionally use for pick-up Traveller actually does exactly this thing. I've started to write it up a few times but it's not a high-priority task. Nonetheless, you can hear us use something like it in this attempt to hijack a damaged space freighter with Mike Holmes and our friend Cary at Origins 2008. It's really rather exciting.\n
    You describe what you want to achieve, and you and the referee discuss the constituent tasks required to achieve it. The result is a task sequence of two to five tasks whose outcome determines the overall success or failure of the effort.

    For each task, the referee sets the requisite difficulty (Routine 5+, Hard 8+, Very Hard 11+) as well as the general consequences of success or failure. One character leads the effort in every case; this is the \u201cactive character.\u201d That character\u2019s player describes how the character conducts the task, \u201cnarrating in\u201d up to one trait, one skill, and one item of technology in order to do so. A trait gives a bonus or penalty according to its value (2, -3; 3 or 4, -2; 5 or 6, -1; 7, +0; 8 or 9, +1; 10 or 11, +2; 12, +3). A skill provides its value as a bonus to the roll.

    Other characters may be able to help, depending on the task. The player of a character who is helping another may narrate in one skill; the bonus is then applied to the roll.

    The referee begins the task sequence with 1d6 points worth of complications. This number may increase or decrease during play. Note that a player can use up the referee\u2019s complications by introducing them himself or herself.\n
      \n
    • (1-3 pts) Personal Difficulty. Describe how the active character\u2019s personal limitations work against him or her in the current task and apply a DM to the roll based on the value of the trait thus invoked; e.g., if the character\u2019s .
    • (2 pts) Mistaken Assumptions. Describe unforeseen obstacles emerging from misinformation or bad assumptions; make the player re-roll all dice and take the lower result.
    • (2 pts) Technical Glitch. If the player uses a point of technology to roll additional dice, describe the equipment malfunctioning in some way and choose which two dice the player keeps.
    • (3 pts) Catastrophic Error. Describe things going badly in a dramatic way. Roll one die and apply the result as a negative DM to the current task.
    \nGenerally, a successful roll means progress toward the goal of the task sequence. The immediate consequences of a successful roll may be:\n
      \n
    • Enabling the task sequence to continue (i.e., moving toward the goal).
    • Enabling an \u201copportunistic\u201d roll for some other benefit or gain.
    • Providing a bonus to a subsequent roll, a bonus to all subsequent rolls, or a pool of expendable bonus points for any subsequent roll\u2026\n
        \n
      • \u2026equal to +1 per 2 points by which the roll was made.
      • \u2026equal +1 for a routine task, +2 for a hard task, +3 for a very hard task.
      \n
    • Lowering the difficulty of a subsequent task.
    • Providing points of technology or data.
    • Providing a reward, payoff, or profit in units of sols, either a fixed amount or in increments based on success.
    • Specifying the truth of a certain helpful fact, the existence of certain helpful conditions, or the possession of a certain helpful item.
    • Reducing the referee\u2019s complications pool by a set amount, or by -1 per 2 points by which the roll was made.
    • Reducing some arbitrary measure of opposition, resistance, or adversity by a set amount, or by -1 per 2 points by which the roll was made.
    \nThe consequences of failure are the converse of success, with some additional possibilities as well.\n
      \n
    • Causing the premature end of the task sequence, or closing off a particular roll.
    • Providing penalties to subsequent rolls, or adding to the referee\u2019s complications pool.
    • Causing damage to equipment (which may result in the loss of technology points, penalties to skills, or \u201chits\u201d to equipment).
    • Causing the loss or corruption of data (which may result in the loss of data points, penalties to skills, and so forth).
    • Causing injury to characters (which usually takes the form of \u201chits\u201d to individuals).
    • Causing a loss of cash on hand or other financial loss.
    • Adding a new and not particularly rewarding task to the sequence (e.g., \u201csurvive an ambush\u201d).
    • Specifying the truth of a certain unhelpful fact, the existence of certain harmful conditions, or the absence of a certain helpful item.
    \nGive player\u2019s characters and vehicles three hits each; give other characters and equipment one hit each. A character or item that loses all its hits is mortally wounded or critically damaged; medical treatment or technical repair may be employed to save wounded characters and damaged equipment.

    In some cases, you may wish to allow players to achieve success by taking damage, loss, or injury on a one-for-one basis (e.g., the active player fails a very hard task, rolling an 8. The effect of failure has been defined as ending the task sequence\u2014\u201creturn to base,\u201d specifically. As the referee, you tell the players that if they take three points of injury or equipment damage, the action may continue).

    \n I think he's referring more to using the Raise and See process to reach a point where one Gives or takes Fallout.

    So the sniper is on one side, with various "Traits" that give him dice (sniper scope, find hiding spot, ghillie suit, spotter, etc) and the mark (the GM) gets dice from various complications or opposing elements (cover, moving, wind, visibility, etc). Then you can escalate for more dice ("stand off" escalate to "move into compound" escalate to "icepick to the ear") which would be bringing in complications as others have said, when you take fallout as necessary to keep the mission alive (you're IDed, you're being pursued, you suffer injury, you're killed by the guards who catch you).

    It would be its own system--it's not a DitV hack with different terms for color--but the basic pattern of dice handling and fallout risking would work beautifully.

    \n
    Chatting with a fan today, I was advised that there is a flaw in the
    \npersonal combat system: there is no way to totally get the drop on
    \nsomeone with, say, a sniper rifle, and assassinate them. I thought
    \nabout that for a while and considered a reflexive defense: sure you
    \ncan, with maneuvers and so on, but the more I thought about that the
    \nmore I thought it was a boring way to do it and more likely to fail
    \nfor boring reasons. Then I realised, assassination is actually social
    \ncombat.
    \n
    \nA successful assassination means getting the Means into place at an
    \nOpportune time while maintaining Secrecy. The actual violence is the
    \ntiniest fraction of the endeavour. And so I propose social combat on a
    \nbulls-eye map (for visual analogy as well as simplicity of the map).
    \nThe center is labeled KILL and the outside labeled FOILED. Three pawns
    \nare on the map: Secrecy, Means, and Opportunity. Two actors are on the
    \nmap: the assassin and the target, with their own skills and aspects
    \nand so on. A timeout is established (one turn per zone is good, so
    \nwith a bullseye of FOILED-*-*-*-KILL that's five turns). The
    \noperational rules are:
    \n
    \nGet two pawns and your opponent into your target zone (KILL for
    \nassassin, FOILED for target) and your opponent is Taken Out (if you're
    \ngetting close, maybe a concession is in order). If no one succeeds
    \nbefore the timeout, no one is taken out -- the assassination is a
    \nfailure or never comes off at all, but the assassin has escaped.
    \nNarrate these results based on pawn positions -- if Secrecy is in the
    \nFOILED zone, obviously the target has been alerted and raised the
    \nalarm. The assassin may even be a wanted man now. If Means is in the
    \nFOILED zone, maybe the target has managed to get the materiel
    \nconfiscated or even changed border management laws so it was
    \nimpossible to get it in! If Opportunity is in the FOILED zone, the
    \nassassin was never able to match up with the target's deliberately
    \nobfuscated movements.
    \n
    \nNo matter which way the map state changes and no matter how it ends,
    \nthere is a whole diverse story of several scenes all related to the
    \nwhole process of political assassination. Now the actual trigger pull
    \nis so boring and obvious that we don't roll for it at all -- a really
    \ngood assassin never fails when all the pieces are carefully in place.
    \n
    \nAnd isn't that really what you want an assassin character to be good
    \nat? The hard part?

    Posted By: HalfjackI posted this in a couple of places some time ago. Time for it again!


    Chatting with a fan today, I was advised that there is a flaw in the
    personal combat system: there is no way to totally get the drop on
    someone with, say, a sniper rifle, and assassinate them. I thought
    about that for a while and considered a reflexive defense: sure you
    can, with maneuvers and so on, but the more I thought about that the
    more I thought it was a boring way to do it and more likely to fail
    for boring reasons. Then I realised, assassination is actually social
    combat.

    A successful assassination means getting the Means into place at an
    Opportune time while maintaining Secrecy. The actual violence is the
    tiniest fraction of the endeavour. And so I propose social combat on a
    bulls-eye map (for visual analogy as well as simplicity of the map).
    The center is labeled KILL and the outside labeled FOILED. Three pawns
    are on the map: Secrecy, Means, and Opportunity. Two actors are on the
    map: the assassin and the target, with their own skills and aspects
    and so on. A timeout is established (one turn per zone is good, so
    with a bullseye of FOILED-*-*-*-KILL that's five turns). The
    operational rules are:

    Get two pawns and your opponent into your target zone (KILL for
    assassin, FOILED for target) and your opponent is Taken Out (if you're
    getting close, maybe a concession is in order). If no one succeeds
    before the timeout, no one is taken out -- the assassination is a
    failure or never comes off at all, but the assassin has escaped.
    Narrate these results based on pawn positions -- if Secrecy is in the
    FOILED zone, obviously the target has been alerted and raised the
    alarm. The assassin may even be a wanted man now. If Means is in the
    FOILED zone, maybe the target has managed to get the materiel
    confiscated or even changed border management laws so it was
    impossible to get it in! If Opportunity is in the FOILED zone, the
    assassin was never able to match up with the target's deliberately
    obfuscated movements.

    No matter which way the map state changes and no matter how it ends,
    there is a whole diverse story of several scenes all related to the
    whole process of political assassination. Now the actual trigger pull
    is so boring and obvious that we don't roll for it at all -- a really
    good assassin never fails when all the pieces are carefully in place.

    And isn't that really what you want an assassin character to be good

    \nBasically the idea is to avoid having each participant have to plan in detail an actual assassination so that it would work (because many players won't know enough to do that well and it's not all that fun anyway) but rather let any idea they have get mechanical representation and therefore narrative weight. All of Diaspora social combats can work this way -- you don't need to know how to investigate a murder, say, but any idea you have, however specific, is wedded to the story of the investigation. You can see them as pivot points, with the boring (but realistic) details unnarrated or glossed over.
