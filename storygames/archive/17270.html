\nThis is my poll on The Big Purple about what makes/breaks immersion for people.

You'll see straight away I managed to screw up the poll a bit by leaving out some important things mentioned by others in the thread.

My own precis of the interim conclusions I'd draw from the poll is on page 7 of the thread:-\n
* avoid dissociated mechanics, "author stance" and anything which regularly requires out-of-character thought processes to decide how your character acts or what happens in the game
* keep things believable - retain suspension of disbelief
* don't railroad the players
* make sure players start the game with solid character concepts with easy roleplay fodder
* avoid personality mechanics, at any rate dictatorial personality mechanics
* use 1st person direct speech in-character dialogue --- avoid social conflict mechanics, at any rate ones which interrupt the free flow of natural dialogue excessively
* cut out background distractions - make sure you turn up to the game smelling fresh, and leave your phone alone
and finally the one which I personally find most subjective:-
* avoid intrusive mechanics, including any mechanics which are "clunkier" than your group is comfortable with
\nPlease note this is specifically not intended to be advice for making a better game or a more fun game, and whilst some of it would chime well with a "narrativist" storygamer, other aspects of it are antithetical to "narrativist" storygaming as I understand it. It's simply a list of what, it seems to me from the thread and poll, helps to produce immersion / avoid disrupting immersion for the majority of immersive gamers. Specifically with the caveat YMMV - some gamers who experience / enjoy immersion may find that some of the things suggested in the list are actually counter-immersive for them. And obviously, in compiling the list, there is a risk that my own bias has crept in, as it nearly exactly reflects my own preferences. The only major difference I have to the majority is that my tolerance of "clunkiness" is far higher than most people's.

What do people think of my list?

    The need to make strategic decisions based on mechanics that feel heavily disassociated from what the character would be knowing and feeling can be seen as a challenge to the desire for Embodiment opportunities.

You can achieve flow through Embodiment - the act of making those character-based decisions can be the challenge that makes Flow happen.     I think deconstructing the "immersion in character" concept into it's component parts (as well as looking at other common motives), the way I did in my article would lead to a much more fruitful discussion than your tangled web of related meanings.

    When I look at your survey, I see it more as a survey about which of my 16 motives a player tends to value. Except with only half of them present and not looked at from the right perspective.

    Let me restate my definition of immersion:- feeling somewhat that you are your character, feeling like your character, feeling as if your character is an extension of yourself (or vice versa), a sense of identity with your character, [usually vividly] imagining being your character, seeing/feeling things as your character does.     \nFunnily enough, I long ago reached the view [see my Lulu essay] that railroading breaks immersion because it deprives people of a sense of ownership of their characters. It's the same phenomenon of immersion in character that I'm talking about which railroading breaks. This one unified sensation of feeling almost as if you are your character or feeling somewhat like your character (or somewhere in between those two ends of the continuum) can be broken by numerous different things. That doesn't mean that it's a number of different and separate, psychologically unassociated phenomena conmingled. The proof of this is in the fact that once immersion in character has been broken for someone for one of the reasons listed, the other things that can break immersion become almost irrelevant, save as further barriers to re-attaining the state of immersion. This is true for me at least, and it's consistent with all the conversations about immersion I've ever had, though I don't think that the specific point has come up explicitly in them.

    What I infer is that you've noticed that people complain about their immersion being destroyed by railroading and you've inferred that when they talk about immersion some of them are in fact talking about their sense of ownership of their character, which you call protagonism. But in my view you're fundamentally mistaken because they're actually by and large in fact (IME) talking about immersion in character in more or less exactly the sense I describe. What they're getting at is that a sense of ownership is a building block for immersion in character in the sense I describe, or more precisely, and more accurately, that the stark awareness of a lack of a sense of ownership of one's character (or a sense of agency), which they experience when railroaded, is, for them, a barrier to attaining/maintaining immersion in character in the sense in which I use that term.

    Let me break it down for you. You get the sense of immersion in character (in the sense in which I use that term) by feeling somewhat as if you are your character, feeling a sense of oneness with your character, that sort of thing. You can't [and this is not a universal thing, but true for most people, subjectively] feel as if you ARE your character if you don't feel that you're in fact making any [significant] decisions for your character. Why? Because it's by being the one in control of your character that you [not universally, but generally in many cases] start to feel the greatest sense of identity with him. By the act of making decisions for your character in the way your character would, just as by talking as your character, you are "being" your character in a concrete way. That produces the feeling of "being" your character. The one begets the other. Scupper the one, and you scupper the other. The cause is in-character roleplay with agency. The effect is a psychological sensation. But the cause is not the same as the effect. The cause and the effect are separate things. \n
    They think about exploring the setting through their character, which I call out separately as Exploration. You try to say that that's what you mean in your OP, but it still comes up in people's responses.
    \nObviously if you're immersed in character in the sense I describe, it follows that when you explore the setting you will be doing so through your character's eyes. [Subject to the rider that there is this thing called dual consciousness which Stanislavsky describes - you could be exploring the setting through both your own eyes and your character's eyes at once.] But that's not the essence of immersion in character, which is simply feeling somewhat that you are your character, feeling like your character, etc. as per the above. It's just incidental to that that if you are immersed in the sense I describe, you will also be, out of necessity, exploring the setting through/as your character, whenever you're exploring the setting at all.

    When people try to articulate their idea of immersion and talk about exploring the setting as/through their character, they are (almost certainly, in almost every case) trying to articulate the same idea that I'm trying to articulate, of being immersed in their character's perspective, mindset and feelings (the most innate aspect of what their character is - his mind).

    One example is when you have a group making characters. As a GM you may state that one of them must be a baron, and one other must be his servant.     \n@omnifray I know exactly what you mean by character immersion; it's like pornography, you know it if you see it. It amazes me that people dispute it exists, conflate it with other concepts or suggest other peculiar terms for it. However, like pornography I admit it's hard to define exactly. Stick with the idea.     My old group, untouched for many years by any other form of roleplaying does it and know perfectly well what it means. There are techniques I uses in traditional games which you can use to encourage it. For example, my group would be uncomfortable with creating content as players, but through their characters; no problem. So, I couldn't say to a player "there's a town nearby, what's it like?" but I could ask them as their character to design and build a town in character and deduct resources and they'd be all over it. Their characters are sufficiently powerful in the game world to justify this.

    Players might suggest content by offering up suggestions for content indirectly, so asking leading questions about the environment. They know perfectly well that I do make some stuff up as I go along, but there are also preplanned elements and I try to keep this seamless, and they conspire to keep it so. They aren't interested in building a story directly, though if one emerges from their character interactions, that's fine. Sitting round a table playing Fiasco would be utterly alien; the idea that it's in the same category of game would be strange to most of them. That said, this isn't true of all the players - a couple play non-traditional games, and yet they can do character immersion too, while understanding the techniques at a higher level, too. I am much more likely to discuss some of the inner workings of the game with them now, but still, we talk about the campaign as a real thing rather than as a combination of the creative input of the participants. The players know that at any time their characters can go off and do whatever they want (and they occasionally do) so it's up to me to provide plausible and engaging threats and opportunities. Most of my pre-prepared material gets used at some point.

    Another example - that of interaction between players whose characters are not present at a scene - "ghost voices." The pressure for this to happen is strong; it allows the participation of other players in individual scenes, but it wears at the edge of immersion. For a long time we forbade it, but an in-character solution was developed - the Lingua Enigma, a magical network which allows them to see and communicate with each other at a distance. This was ideal - the pressure to create it led to a great game session and it solved a system issue without breaking immersion.

      \n The sense I get is that the most immersion-breaking thing to do is to play with unfamiliar game procedures.

      Somehow, we can engage enormously detailed tactical combat mechanics, spend forty minutes discussing five-foot adjustments and to-hit rolls, and still feel that we're "in character". Honestly, the whole thing strikes me as a case of being used to your tools, and can see through them (or with them) to the fictional world.

      Any new tool - learning to ride a motorcycle, for example - feels strange, overwhelming the rider with new sensations. This all fades with time as the rider adapts. Before long, they can meaningfully start talking about the feel of the road (which of course they can't feel, except through a 100kg vibrating metal frame) and the tranquility of the road (despite sitting on top of a gas motor that's blasting away at a deafening 115dB).

      One of the most immersive role-playing experiences I'm aware of is psychodrama, which gets people into role so deeply that their hands go sweaty at the prospect of having a difficult conversation. This, despite the fact that - at a conversational level - the vast majority of what's said is out-of-character speech concerned with the mechanics of the procedure. "Stand here, stand there, would so-and-so have delivered the line this way? Did the person playing your sister get it right?" To top it off, those involved can be changing roles as often as every thirty seconds!

      If you take a poll to learn what techniques create immersion, I posit you're going to wind up sampling fairly traditional play conventions, because these are the ones that are most familiar to the mass of gamers.

      Games whose mechanics are the most self-consciously about trying to create a different play experience look the least "immersionist", because these are the most mechanically unfamiliar.

      If it was, say, Fiasco that had been written in 1974, I think this conversation would look rather different!

Any "your character could not do that's" are dealt with quickly, and we allow players to backtrack if their actions were relying on something working which can't. Introducing new players is not tricky; you simply make their characters unfamiliar with the world, so the player's ignorance is the character' ignorance.       \n@JDCorley, no.

      [First, to get a trivial observation out of the way. There's precious little difference between hit points in D&D and health levels in WW games IME, at least in MET-LARP versions of the WW games, which WW tabletoppers can be presumed to be reasonably familiar with.]

      It goes way beyond mechanical familiarity, and the simple proof of that is the way that out-of-character banter can ruin people's immersion in LARP - including people who are utterly familiar with the conventions of tabletop gaming including the relatively greater acceptability of chit-chat there. Thus, there is at least one way that immersion can be broken which demonstrably has nothing to do with mechanical familiarity.

      Another example which isn't quite as easily demonstrable but is nonetheless true is the general proposition that out-of-character thinking [e.g. "Author-Stance"] breaks/strains immersion for many gamers. This has nothing to do with mechanical familiarity, and rather more to do with the essence of immersion, though I accept that for complicated reasons it doesn't necessarily work in this way for everyone. The essence of immersion [in character] is that you feel somewhat as if you are your character or you feel somewhat like them. It's obviously going to tend to be easier to do that, subject to complex psychological factors, if you are thinking like your character. I have spent a great deal of time GMing, and a great deal of time mixing in-character and out-of-character thinking, for instance while playing NPCs, but also for a variety of reasons while playing. Probably the vast majority of the people polled on the TBP poll could say the same thing. Yet there is a strong vote to the effect that dissociated mechanics and "Author-Stance" break or strain immersion. The essence of those two things is of course out-of-character thinking.

      Thus, for many gamers, it's not merely the habit of purely in-character thinking which makes out-of-character thinking break/strain their immersion. It's something rather more inherent in out-of-character thinking itself.

      [The caveats include that if you've already visualised something in your head perhaps instinctively and without consciously dreaming it up, not being able to give voice to it in "Author Stance" may actually hamper your immersion. A minority of immersive gamers would seem to find this problematic.]

      Another example is switching characters. I found frequently switching characters in Montseguar 1244 to be repugnantly counter-immersive for me. I imagine many, many gamers would feel the same way. That's not due to some unfamiliarity with the concept of switching characters. I switch characters all the time when I'm GMing. Maybe I briefly immerse in those characters when I'm GMing, and my immersion is broken up when I switch characters. But I found that more painful, and I do mean painful, when I was playing Montsegur 1244, probably because I had gotten more deeply immersed than I do when I'm GMing, probably because when I'm GMing I have so many other things to think about and in short so much out-of-character thinking to do (see above). And I have known other immersive/trad gamers complain about having to switch characters for the same reason. In fact, come to think about it, there was a time in the past when a group I gamed with insisted on everyone running multiple characters in a game of D&D, and I remember I hated it and insisted that I only wanted to play one character. The reason is you can't get deeply into character with several characters at once. Or maybe some people can, but many can't.

      Where mechanical familiarity obviously does play a role is in the individual's judgment of what is believable (and thus suspension of disbelief) and what is a dissociated mechanic (and thus requires you to think in an out-of-character way). As I may have mentioned, my version of the test for a dissociated mechanic is:- does the rule lead you the player to reason along lines which fail to sufficiently closely model a reasoning process that your character could sufficiently reasonably have? Both instances of the word "sufficiently" there are subjective. And that completely covers the case of hit points in any event. So I'm not saying that there is no truth whatsoever lying behind what you say. But what I'm saying is that you are mistaking a small part of the truth for the whole scenario.

      I don't think it's constructive to insist that the *only* reason why immersive/trad gamers tend to find trad games more immersive is that that's what they're used to. Do you claim to understand their gaming habits better than they do? Do you claim to be a better judge of their psychology than they are? I would urge you to consider that it's inherently improbable that trad games should have evolved the way they have, and that story games should have evolved the way they have, without each having some inherent advantages and disadvantages, for some groups of gamers, over the other. A major advantage of trad games over storygames, possibly the single major advantage of trad games over storygames, is the way they promote immersion for a certain proportion of gamers. I don't mean for everyone. But by denying that that could be due to anything other than learned gaming habits you are essentially implying that a great deal of trad thinking is simply irrational.

      \n @ JDCorley, I think you are using "story" in a different way from me. By a "story-focused" approach to RPGs, you would seem to mean playing RPGs with an interest in the values, desires and goals of the characters, exploring their personalities, exploring character development etc., rather than physically investigating the wider game-world or seeking out challenge/power or socialising in the real world. That, to me, is not a "story-focused" approach, but simply part of a holistic approach to play, where you are interested in all sorts of interesting facets of the game-world and the characters, and not simply physical investigation, challenge/power or socialising in the real world. These things, which you seem to view as story-focused, are to me integral to many kinds of enjoyable trad roleplay. I do not view them as necessarily "story-focused". I can see that in a loose sense, a game which takes this holistic approach will tend to seem a damn sight more story-like in its outcomes than one where only combat, or only physical exploration, or only the real-world cheetos matter. But to me it is just a holistic approach to roleplay, and not really necessarily a story focus.

      Let me explain. People who enjoy this sort of thing may not actually care how the "story" resolves, or even whether it resolves. It is not the destination, but the journey which interests them. Furthermore, they may not see themselves as creating a story, but simply as exploring the game-world. They are exploring the game-world in a holistic way, which is just a fancy way of saying "with a broad perspective", and not with a narrow perspective limited to physical exploration or combat. Their character is, after all, a part of the game-world, and they are exploring their character's personality and the personalities of other characters as well as and together with other aspects of the game-world. This is just a natural part of roleplay if you don't limit yourself artificially to a particular perspective, which is by no means to imply that "limiting" yourself to a "particular perspective" is necessarily a bad thing or not as fun as a "broader" perspective - some of my most enjoyable roleplaying experiences have been in obscenely combat-heavy LARP.

      Suppose you're roleplaying your character and two choices are plausible - rescue the princess, or join forces with her abductor. Your character would probably be more likely to rescue her, but you realise that if you join forces with her abductor there is the potential for a story to develop of human tragedy and fall from grace, and the possibility that your character could later fall in love with the princess and seek to undo all his evil acts up to that point. In that instant you can choose to *bend* (not obliterate) the natural quality of your character-decision-making to accommodate what you see as the story-potential you have spotted in a slightly unexpected move. The fact that in making that decision you had one eye on the broader potential of the *story* means that you were in "Author Stance" and you were roleplaying by means of out-of-character thinking.

      To put it another way, if you roleplay fully naturally (not just in the sense that it *looks* natural to the outside observer, but in the sense that all your decisions are based on in-character thinking), and are interested in character development and your character's personality and allow them to happen naturally without consciously and deliberately making avenues for them, that's just part of the basic essence of trad roleplay approached with a broad perspective. Only by deliberately adopting a narrow perspective and shutting down opportunities for that kind of exploration can you avoid it.

      If on the other hand you roleplay in a way which outwardly seems natural but which is based on the out-of-character thinking inherent in deliberately and consciously choosing paths on the basis that they will expose opportunities for character development, that's going beyond purely trad roleplay into a slightly different realm, and that's what I think of as a story-focused approach to games of the RPG/storygame variety.

      In a way, what you are expressing is your own subjective view that the best story is one based almost entirely on what would naturally happen. That's a subjective view I'm inclined to share to a very great extent, perhaps entirely; where we differ in substance (rather than in language usage) is, it would seem, merely that it's my assessment that a substantial portion of gamers give rather more weight in that equation to other factors, such as which choice will give more opportunities to test CFK's commitment to his beliefs in future.

      \n@komradebob

      I summary, I think your issues with character immersion are:

      The time is takes to learn the system and setting, and expectation that it's you who does this
      Players not wishing to give out of character hooks to the GM out of character.
      Passive characters
      Poorly connected characters

      I'd argue that with the possible exception of the first none of these are problems relating directly games which support character immersion, but nonetheless, I'll address them.

      The first. Choose a light system you know or can master quickly. There are plenty of those. It's your players' job to know how their characters interact with the system. Character immersion doesn't mean a complex system at all. Then, choose a setting you know, or one everyone is already familiar with. For example, the real world in your city today.

      The second one: During character creation you ask them to create a character who is driven to do whatever the game's primiary activity is. Then, you ask them what their characters would want to do. Then you put that in front of them. Reward active play with in-game currency.

      The third; have them create all their characters together and have them be part of a group which does things your fiction encourages. Ask them why they are the sort of characters who hang out together and don't kill each other. It's only during character creation where they will be explicitly, as people not immersing in character and it's your chance to put the onus on them to tell you why they are a team.

      Many of these forms of behaviour are actually far more problematic in games with distributed GM authority. Try playing Fiasco with someone who is passive, unengaged or doesn't want to contribute to the fiction. I agree that trad games are harder to run, but they are usually a lot easier to play.
