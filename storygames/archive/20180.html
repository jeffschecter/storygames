\n I'm just curious, as a Scandinavian.

The Nordic scene, from Denmark, Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Iceland. If you are influenced by them, why are you influenced by them, and what made you read about them in the first place?

I'm asking about anything related to the Nordic countries, really, from Norwegian roleplaying poems, to Turku manifest, to Jeepform, to any kind of traditional gaming (or LARPing) that you have heard of. Why have you heard of it? Why did it catch your interest?

I'm mostly asking this because of what @AsIf wrote in this blog post.

I'm also asking myself so I can realize, and start to look at, these things from a different perspective. From a foreign perspective, outside of the Nordic countries.

    \n Speaking for Finland, I wouldn't go as far as 98% - unless we're talking about the entire population of the country, of course! For roleplayers, I'd say that something like 15% are more or less primarily oriented towards Nordic freeform as their primary style. Similar numbers as you'd get for Forge-oriented proge gamers, pretty much. Another, somewhat smaller slice seems to prioritize OSR gaming nowadays, and the rest are unreconstructed trad/mainstream gamers and/or WotC consumers, I guess. Numbers courtesy of my ass, of course

    I agree with your actual point, though - it's totally feasible to be gaming around here without caring a whit about auteurism, larping, immersionism, jeepform or any of that stuff. I do it myself, really - despite having a pretty intense history with the hobby, I've never really gotten into the Nordic stuff; it's pretty far away from my actual interests, it's more likely to see me playing Warhammer than sitting in a closet getting into touch with my feelings

    \n Tough question, really.
    I'm Danish, just for the record, but for many, many years, I played the new stuff at Fastaval, and only there. With my regular group it was GURPS. Always.
    It has to be said, though, that for many years I was very much against pretty much anything new. Couldn't see the point. Railed against Fastaval games that were suddenly written as free-form (before that was a term). Railed against games written with semi-live in mind. And the irony was that they just formalised what I myself had been doing for years. Don't know the rules? Forget them or flip a coin. Hey, it feels more natural to play out this scene. Let's do that.
    A few years ago, I decided to try something new. I'd choose a random jeepform game and play it with an open mind. And it was glorious! (Resan).
    After that, I became more open to novel experiences, and I began to see games with rules we didn't have to ignore to play the game we wanted. Where we didn't need to mutilate the rules to support the fiction, but where the rules work! They help!
    I will admit that I've passed on the more complex systems. While I adore what Dogs in the Vineyard does, and I love the world, the system is too complex for my tired brain. That's why I'm mainly stuck on Apocalypse World hacks involving monsters and PrimeTime Adventures.

    \n Most of my contact with Nordic stuff ( the more experimental kind) is very much at a distance, through discussions here mostly. I can think of a couple of aspects that made my ears perk up.

    1) It doesn't always have to be geared towards commercialism to count as success
    I loved discussions at The Forge when it existed, but a lot of that was geared towards turning your game idea into a viable product as a major goal. Nordic scene seems more geared towards getting the thing to a functional stage, then hitting a con and having people play it when you put it on. Big success there is in people talking about the experience and getting a rep as a scenario/game designer and runner-of-game. That's pretty damn cool to me.

    2) The scenario is the game
    Even though techniques are refined and borrowed from scenario to scenario, the goal isn't to build a core system and then tack on scenarios to it. Each scenario is a game is a specific event, and the build ( your combination of mechanics choices as a designer) is made with that specific game in mind. Holy Hannah, that mentality opens up a ton of options!

    3) A lot of it seems geared towards producing a unique one time experience
    The scenario-game might be played by another group of players and another GM, but the experience for a specific group is all their own. It isn't part of campaign play, and never expected to be such. Again, opens up new design possibilities.

    4) With all of that stuff, and designing for a short playtime, you can ( and should) cut out lots of mechanical cruft

    5)As a one-shot, you can develop a mentality of really playing things to the hilt among participants

    6) As a one-shot, you can really focus in to a very specific situation/set up/cast of characters

    Oh geez, just a whole bunch of stuff has been influential on my thinking, even at this long distance and removal from the folks more near the center of this kind of thinking.
