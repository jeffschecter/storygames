eruditus
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
So my wife and I were asked to run some new World of Darkness games at a local convention. At first I was hesitant. There are 100 games I would rather run. I mean with all the awesomeness we've experiences recently with games like Mortal Coil and Mountain Witch and the list goes on, WoD seems sort of trite.

But we agreed for some free crash space and begun the task of bringing life into one of the "big dogs."

I started adding concepts that we've been discussing from our Indie Game experiences - adding bangs to spur narrative control, using character backgrounds to drive hidden agendas and aggressive scene framing to pack in as much of the "important stuff" as we can in the four hours we have.

Joanna says something to the effect of "we're running a World of Darkness game, not one of our indie games."

That struck me. Sure we all talk about the mechanics of the game promoting these ideas but how much am I misrepresenting a game by including these ideas.

So I am not ignoring elements of the new Storytelling system. We plan on using Virtues and Vices and factions to add background elements and points of tension. Since it's a con game I'm not going to give out normal XP and instead award players for using bangs and plot interaction. Something that may be beyond the rules but again the rules are not specifically designed for convention one shots.

As such I realize that there are a great number of expectations from both veteren playes and those new to the World of Darkness. We obviously want to forward an agenda - exposing a wider audience to many of the topics we discuss here and show others that "you too can have fun again" (a bit tongue n cheek).

So what do you think are some of the pitfalls? What sort of resistance have you seen to some of these techniques? What games have you been using these ideas with and aanyone think WoD is a bad fit?

    Pitfalls will happen when you use words like "Kicker" and "Bang", get the blank faces, do the "Ohmygosh, you don't know what Kickers and Bangs are?" and proceed to explain them.

    Instead, just use them without giving them names. IIRC, when Judd runs con games, he basically reads off the kicker for each character, and that's it. People grab the characters based on the kicker. I don't think Judd calls them a "kicker" or anything.

    So, maybe just use the techniques without giving them names, without explaining WHY you're using them to the gamers. No terms/jargon, no elngthy explanations, just use them. See what happens. If they work, awesome. If they don't, then back it up with trad GM skillz.

    That's about all I can recommend. Kickers/Bangs/Say Yes Or Roll the Dice, etc all work demonstratively awesomely with WoD games (I used them in a Vampire one shot and two exalted games), and there's never a need to lift the veil to the gruesome gearwork of jargon and meanings behind actions.

    Good luck!

    -Andy

    I so have to agree with Judd here. Part of the problem with 'bangs' and 'kickers' is they have got associated with 'new-fangled Forgie ways' which puts some people off. Using the term tends to result in people saying 'I don't see anything wrong with the way I used to play'. I've seen people really resistant because they believe you are saying 'you are playing wrong'. See bang and kicker were just the terms Ron used to highlight a way of playing that had been around for a while. He didn't even claim to have invented it, just tagged it with a name. I am certainly aware of 'bangs' beforehand, though kickers were new on me.

    Setting + Story Now is really old. To be honest those old AD&D modules were, IMO, early incarnations of this: Setting was just 'The steading of the hill giant chief' and story now just 'The giants keep coming down in to the lowlands and raiding. The king sends you to go and kick giant butt' but it was the same principle at work. The Morrow Project is one I really remember as setting up their scenarios like this. Most of the module was the description of a community teetering on the edge of conflict. The last 4 or 5 pages of the module 'Play of the Game' were around how to use the material. It always began with 'wake-up' for the frozen team - always throwing a bang right at the players like 'the alarm sounds and as you wake up you realize your bolt hole is filling with water and if you don't get out you will drown' Then it discussed the cnonflict that the players were likely to meet when they entered the setting. It was clearly non-linear with direct advice from the reactions of playtest groups to what happened etc. Of course some were better than others and had a real 'story now' set up - the Mexicans are invading - while others allowed the players to wander at will a bit more. But still its the same principle. Create setting with opportunity for conflict. Story Now. Drive with bangs. I used to love reading those 'play of the game' sections, because I really saw how to make this material come alive.

    We've got better at refining the tools for this sort of play, but the philosophy is not new.

    What's perhaps more interesting is where the more railroaded approach started, but I think this is moving toward being a seperate thread.

    i think that Judd made a useful 'good sentence' on Sons of Kyros about this that went something like tell the players "There is nothing you ought to do, but I have things you can do". Judd?

    More specifically-- in reference to your excellent Mortal Coil post above-- That incident is familiar to me, too. I have a close friend, so old school that he prefers the term Maze Controller from Mazes & Monsters, to "DM" or "GM". Any reference to 'indie' or 'forge' or 'hippie' games as the source of an idea-- taints that idea in his mind. Yet, to his credit, that taint takes the form of applying critical scrutiny to whatever he's hearing and offering laser-sharp criticism of it. Subsequently, I talk to him more about new game concepts than anyone else. Often, I'd prefer if he'd just grasp an idea and share my enthusiasm: digest a good idea and move on to the next bite. But his killjoy critique does help me really see all sides of concepts, too.

    And I've come to see that he's like the Mortal Coil player you mentioned: he has a mental picture of rpgs as a process, like a complex machine with certain necessary parts. When you start tampering with or removing or replacing those parts; he tries to visualize how the machine will possibly work The Same Way afterward. And, of course, it won't. Same deal with 'letting everybody grab the steering wheel on the road', to extend the metaphor.

    So I've got to concede he's right. And the best I can do is show him that the machine will now function A Different Way.
    So now he dismissively talks about 'most of those story-telling games' in an over-generalized way. And now we're in the position of debating which machine is better: the old one or the newfangled one. Some of his points are legitimate: many narr games require players to bring more to the table, instead of just sitting back and listening until it's time to roll dice and kill something. Some of his points are ignorant: "all those games don't have a GM or dice and don't work for long campaigns only one-shots." Occasionally he's surprise me by adopting a truly strange new idea without hesitation. He'll even shrug and play new games... but they haven't displaced his affection for a Platonic Ideal of perfect old game that whirrs beautifully in his mind. (And he's a pretty good DM, too.)

    In conclusion, at least he's over the mental hurdle of the woman you described. She's still at the point where she thinks you're just breaking the game with Powergamer Munchkin tricks and Conflict of Interest between your agenda as a Player and your power to Narrate like a meta-god. And she's even in the right ballpark: those are things that would wreck the old machine she's envisioning in her head. The trick is getting her to envision a new machine. To lead a horse to water. And enthusiasm isn't always contagious.

    Although ideas like bangs, kickers, flags, aggressive scene framing and so on currently mostly come out of the indie movement, I don't see anything about them which limits them to indie games.

    They may work better with indie games, I have no view on that, but they are GMing techniques that would easily work with most trad games.

    As such, I suspect the issue may be one of labelling them, of making them a big thing rather than just GMing tools. There is no reason you can't run Gurps with flags, kickers, bangs et al and while Sorceror might get better results with that approach I think these techniques would add to a Gurps game too.

    In short, I don't think there is anything intrinsic to trad gaming which makes these problematic, I suspect the issue is more one of labelling or overemphasising them. If you just use them, without discussing them any more than you would discuss any other techniques you use in GMing I suspect there will be far less resistance.
