\n On May 9th, I released Gun Thief as a $3 pdf.
On June 12th, I started offering a $5 print+pdf combo.

At one point, Jason Morningstar whispered me that it would be interesting to hear about my experiences, publishing a little thing like that.

So, a brief history:\n
  • I originally wrote gun thief for the Design in 365 Seconds contest
  • So, Gun Thief was written on April 1st. I reworked the text several times over before its publication. In total: 8-10 hours of writing & design time
  • I playtested the game several times, and quickly realized that the Jagged Women needed more concrete moves. So I added them.
  • I spent 15-20 hours laying the book & pdf out. This was my first real project that I used inDesign for, after numerous not-serious experiments with it
  • I released the pdf, and then the print, and I first sold it in person at Go Play this weekend.
\nSo, the decision to publish for money!

Well, my playtesting and editing, all of that detail oriented stuff, that was motivated by the notion that I might sell it.
If it'd just been a thing, that I was making for me and my friends, I'd not have tightened the rules, or the text (as much as I did), and I would have given up on the inDesign portion after 7 hours, declaring it "good enough."

Now, John Harper was a ghost that haunted this whole process. He designs beautiful, tight stuff and releases it for free. Ghost/Echo, Lady Blackbird, The Mustang... I'm looking at all of you, here.
The decision to charge dollars for Gun Thief was definitely made while acknowledging the existence of his products.
In the end, I came to a decision: earning money helps me feel proud of my work, helps my work gain attention, and can make people value my work more.
It also allows me to create a physical product, and I like my rpgs as physical products.

The $3 price point was one that came about pretty naturally. At first I thought $2 was the sweet-spot, then I remembered that Paypal dings you and that I'd be making less than the cup of coffee each time I made a sale. And so I bumped it up to $3.

The $5 price point came about even more naturally. The physical books cost $2 each, for printing and shipping, when bought in a 50-unit order.
So, I tacked my $3 of profit onto my $2 of costs.

So, let's look at how the project has faired:\n
  • Over the internet, I've sold 17 pdfs.
  • Over the internet, I've sold 8 bundles.
  • At Go Play, I sold out of all the copies I brought by mid-day on Saturday. I don't remember exactly how many copies I brought, but it was around 15 for-sale copies.
\nPeople who've bought the game, weigh in! How is the print quality? how tight is the design? what did you expect from a $3/$5 product, and did you get it? how does this micro-publishing thing settle with you?

Things I've noticed about the process:\n
  • With a $3-5 price-point, tweeting and facebooking can generate immediate sales. On a social-media-network whim, people will buy your game.
  • With a $3-5 price point, you can make sales by just sharing a neat thing. Like that photo-op scene that me and my girlfriend shot for the print-sales thread. Like a single mechanic. Whatever.
  • People like buying stuff at conventions. Having products that cost less than lunch money means that people will buy it without reservation, on the spot. Both me and Joel Shempert sold out of all of our copies that we brought (of Gun Thief and the Dreaming Crucible, respectively. Both at $5.)

    As one of the people who bought The Gun Thief, and not only bought it but bought the bundle, I have to say that I have absolutely no objections to the product as delivered whether it be the PDF or the print version. For your first InDesign work it looks absolutely fantastic. Nice open layout, good structure, good sense of composition, and the choice to go with a comic book printer rather than a booklet or pamphlet printer was inspired. No, the comic book format will probably not last as long as a nice stapled pamphlet version on heavier paper -- but I don't really care, since I have the PDF and can print it on whatever I want whenever I want, so if I want to take it off to a high-wear convention, I just have a nice coil-bound copy printed for cheap and away I go.

    \n

    I'm actually rather surprised that you haven't seen more sales for the game than what you report here. Admittedly, it is a bit of a niche product but it's extremely well done and I think all you really need is to get it in front of more people to get more sales at this cost point. Anything that you can do to get it mentioned and especially put in front of people will sell it. That's really all you need to do.

    \n

    (I have to admit that I do take a certain pleasure in being responsible for at least two of your bundle sales, one to myself and one to my sidekick. I now have unquestionable early adopter credit.)

It's actually more convenient for everyone involved than, say, $4. This is super-important for selling street-style, in person and out of your satchel without a storefront or cashbox. Though it was super-fun at Go Play to see two people Paypal me from their phones to buy copies on the spot!\n

    Posted By: Mcdaldno

    Where do I go next, as far as generating sales?
    \nJoe - I'd say that for a low-budget advertising setup, social media is your friend. Make a twitter account for the Gun Thief and post links to AP posts and to your photos and stuff. Start a flickr pool, maybe - have people take or submit their own Gun Thief photos. Borrow a video camera and make a quick teaser "trailer" for the Gun Thief. Create some stuff that's easy for people who are excited to share the game to shop around.

    I've never made or sold a game, but I do work in marketing and I know that a good strategy, if you're a single-man operation, is to turn your fans into a big ol' hype machine. Look at how Luke and Jared pushed Freemarket - hell, I'm a prime example of it. I love the idea of the game enough that I'll retweet cool stuff they post, I'll talk to people and send links. Create what Luke called "your fellow mystery cultists" and you're good to go. As long as the PDF is easily accessible to buy, people will jump at it. A product like yours is totally an impulse buy, too - people who see some neat photos or get a half-decent pitch (enough to generate curiousity) will throw a couple of bucks at you just to feel like they're "in" on the secret.

    Posted By: Eero TuovinenI'm rather strongly inclined against buying a game that costs less than $10 myself.
    \nHm.

    I think my most immediate reaction is, "Then Gun Thief is not for you." I mean, if I'm selling the PDF for $10, I'm asking for a dollar per page. That's ludicrous.

    Honestly, I've tried charging lots for a small game (see Ribbon Drive). It leaves a sour taste in some people's mouths, even if I make it beautiful and add some neat value to it (see cool CD, featuring cool bands). That avenue is one I'm not interested in exploring further.

    And so, the current exploration is in tiny products. Honestly, I'm not interested in Gun Thief making it as a $10 or $15 item. I want to know if a tiny game can flourish, can be its own kind of market. So far, I'm hearing a moderate yes: I can sell out of the stock I bring to cons. I can sell PDFs and can chew through modest print runs pretty quickly. It's enough income to pay for some of my immense coffee budget, and that's a nice feeling. People like it.

    If the price point prevents you from saying "this is worth my investment" because it is so little, I'd expect the same reaction to its form (a dozen pages, roughly) and its gameplay (an hour, roughly).

    \n

    Okay, that's rather funny -- I've been to Nelson, British Columbia on more than one occasion. Or rather through Nelson, since I used to date a girl who lived in Prince George. Nice area, but as you put it "geographically isolated."

    \n

    Still, for a product like Gun Thief, as far as I can tell it in relation to other online-centric distributed products, the key for continuing to sell is simply that: continuing to sell. You can create a Facebook page, you can post on RPGnet, and you can represent at cons but your big win is going to be in continuing to do all of that, as much as you can. Someone else suggested creating a Twitter account; that's absolutely a great idea if you don't already have one for yourself. If you do have one, make sure that it's linked to from any of the webpages that talk about your game and that you post game related content (links to actual play sessions, links to threads where you discuss the game, etc.) on a regular basis. Same thing with the Facebook page, and so on. It's going to be an ongoing process, as often and as consistently as you can manage to present content.

    \n

    It's not like the PDF is going to spoil in storage; a copy of Gun Thief bought next month will be just as good as one bought this month, and so will the one in the month after that. As long as you keep presenting it, as long as you keep putting it forward, as long as people keep seeing it you will have more people buying it and a $5 price point ensures that people can do so on a whim and/or instantaneously when the opportunity presents itself.

    \n

    Show up on every RPG-related web forum that you can find. Get people to talk about Gun Thief as much as you can. Show up at conventions and run it when you can afford to. Find somebody willing to run an online version, maybe using Skype or TalkShoe, and link favorably to the recording of the game. Nothing sells a game like people playing a game. (That's actually one of the interesting things about the photo shoot you did with your girlfriend, that it came across like game-content, like play. Aside from looking cool, it gave an idea of what the game experience was like. That's a good thing.)

    \n

    The more people that see your game, the more people will buy it. The more people that hear about your game, the more people will buy it. You want to find ways to have more people see your game and more people hear about your game. Podcasts are good, interviews are good, forums are good, videos are good (as long as the sound quality is reasonable, which is a real problem with a lot of the promotional videos I see these days). Anything that you create that makes people want to share that material with their friends, via liking or blogging or tagging or whatever, that is a sale waiting to happen.

    Where do I go next, as far as generating sales?

    RPG Geek might be your friend (http://rpg.geekdo.com). It takes little effort to list your game and keep your RPGGeek entry alive. I'm no expert on this, but I have had some good luck with it. How to Host a Dungeon has won some good friends and fans through RPG Geek--definitely some people that never would have found the game otherwise.

    RPG Geek is about finding that dedicated group of people who already grok what your game and your publishing ethos are already about. They exist, so go find them.

    Here's how it works:

    1. Create a page for your game (if one doesn't exist already). Don't be a shill, just use this as a place to present your game to the world.
    2. If you've got some pics, Web links, or other useful stuff, post it. Having a few things on the page shows that the game isn't just an orphaned entry.
    3. Find some geeklists that you think are groovy and in the same vein as Gun Thief. For example, if there isn't a geeklist for "cool print games $5 and under", you should create one. Likewise for games about loners with guns. And so on.

    \n Cool, David. I'd happily share that information.

    Finding a Printer (30 minutes, plus a lifetime)

    I spend a lot of time (an hour a week, averaged out?) researching "publishing stuff" like printers. It's more of a compulsion than anything. I'd already heard about Ka-Blam a few months earlier, after Johnstone mentioned them to me. Props to Johnstone.

    So the time I spent scouting for a printer expressly for this project was about 30 minutes. I already knew Ka-Blam was the cheapest solution for this format, and I only sought out a cursory confirmation of that. Ka-Blam has an online price calculator, so I already knew roughly how much it would cost. (The total came to $90USD for 50 copies shipped to Canada.)

    Formatting for Printer (1 hour, plus previously mentioned time)

    The time I spent re-working my files into the right format for the printer (Ka-Blam Comics) is already included in my 15-20 hours of layout & design time.

    Ka-Blam is designed for comics, and thus it wanted image files of all my individual pages. After struggling to try to make that happen, I sent a couple emails back and forth with them, and they offered to accept a PDF instead. So... 1 hour customer service time, spread across two days?

    Proofs (1 hour)

    Living in Canada, shipping from Orlando is a bitch. They had the option to only accept a digital proof. I gambled, and didn't accept a physical proof. Thus, time spent checking a proof as such was very low. I mostly just scanned for typos. I didn't find any at the time. Since then, I've found an omitted word and one awkward line break.

    Inspecting the Entire Run (1 hour)

    When they arrived, that package of 50, I checked them all out to ensure that none were unusable. The cover is a bit streaky (it's pure black on glossy paper, so this was kind of expected). I made sure none were really streaky. I made sure the staples were in the right spot. I flipped through each book quickly, to make sure all the pages looked right. I spent about a minute on each, plus some time just staring at the stack as a whole, for no apparent reason.

    Marketing (5 hours)

    I've spent about 2-3 hours posting about Gun Thief here.
    I've talked about it on twitter several times.
    I spent about an hour making a Facebook page that I've updated occasionally.
    I've spent an hour or two fiddling with my website page for Gun Thief, most of which was trying to get that image slider for the photo drama to display correctly.

    The Photo Shoot (2 hours)

    One day, I decided I should dress up like a gun thief, Kaleigh should dress up like a jagged woman, and we should stage a little vignette story and share the photos online.
    If you know me and Kaleigh, you know that this is a pretty normal decision for us, as far as decisions go.

    So, we spent fifteen minutes dressing up and looting my brother's room for weapons, and then thirty minutes shooting.
    I spent about an hour selecting and tweaking photos. And then I uploaded them to my fileave account.

    Distribution (4 hours)

    I've emailed out a lot of PDFs, mailed a couple hard copies, and dealt with handling cash at GPNW.
    I'm not sure how to account for that time accurately, but let's say I've spent 4 hours doing that.

    Mailing List (4 hours)

    I added a page of names to the PDF, and sent that updated PDF to everyone who's on the mailing list (hint: if I've sent you the pdf, I've included you on it). This list of names (grouped into genre appropriate categories, each category containing 5-20 handpicked names) took me like 3 hours.

    I'm inviting those who've bought the game to join in on a super secret project (42 Problems). I spent an hour or two writing instructions and examples for that project. In the future, I'll spend 2-12 hours on this super secret project.

    Summary

    I'm probably nearing 50 hours of invested time. I've done better than breaking even.
    Much of the work has been a labour of love.

    The most draining parts were the last 4 hours of layout, the distribution, and struggling to make the website image slider thing work properly.

    \n Right now it looks like before taxes you are making around $2 for every hour you invested. If you sell...

    2 times more... that's around $4/hr
    3 times more... that's around $6/hr
    10 times more... that's around $20/hr

    These numbers are probably off but they give you an idea of what is involved.

    I think the game is to make more of these, see which ones have traction, and then reinvest in them. The low investment ideally means you can create more.

    I wonder at such a low price point how much you would benefit from advertising on sites like rpg.net in terms of exposure. At $25 for 25,000 impressions... you would need to sell 1 game every 2,500 times it is viewed to effectively break even (around 10 games). Advertising somewhere else may be better (depends on the game and the community) or even reinvesting the money into a video instructing people how to play that can be shared.

    I don't think it is about the money for you (and even if it was)... so the real trick is how to turn those 10 new people exposed to your game into more than people who buy it... but people that actually play it and then talk about it. I think that is where the true value is in comparing micro publishing to free publishing. Can the little money you make be turned into an opportunity to get even more exposure compared to just having a free game?

    A lot of marketing people try to sell silver bullet solutions but I don't think that works. You need to experiment. Put your game out in front of different people in different ways and mercilessly measure the results. Then try something new (or old) based on what you find and do it again and again till you have the right process for you. And even if you do find a successful process... doesn't mean it will be successful tomorrow. That's why experimenting all the time is so important! It's often not just finding the right people or the right game but the fight game for the right people in a way that is convenient for everyone involved.
