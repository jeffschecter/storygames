\n Hey friends,

We've got a problem. You know the constellation of gigawatt deuterium-fluoride laser satellites we all put in orbit as doomsday weapons? They appear to have networked and reached some consensus on revised operational orders. Now we are so screwed!

In short, they are pruning technology by firing on and vaporizing any EM spectrum emitter between certain frequencies. We think 1cm to 1km but nobody is rigorously testing the upper and lower bounds, if you know what I mean.

Our dumb robot masters are wiping out all advanced technology, all power production, all radio sources, everything cool and fun. What's left? I'm wondering what the practical implications of this are for we humans on the ground.

And, more importantly, how do we knock them out of the sky?

Typed on a manual typewriter,

    \n Thanks to communications systems, reflection from the sun, and electrical noise, the satellites themselves are nontrivial EM sources in the targeted radiation band. Thus, you may not need to shoot them down if you can get them to shoot each other down. You can likely accomplish that by disrupting their communications.

    Submarine-launched nuclear missiles are an excellent attack vector. Purely ballistic missiles such as the submarine-launched Trident-series ICBM have minimal onboard electronics, no active EM emitters such as tracking or communications systems, and existing electronics are well-shielded - these things need to survive sub-orbital re-entry, after all. Furthermore, even if detected, shooting down an ICBM in-flight is a very low-probability maneuver.

    The nuclear payload poses a triple-threat to the satellites: the explosion could destroy satellites directly (least likely scenario), indirectly destroy or cripple via EM pulse (very plausible), or promote satellite friendly-fire through communication disruption via EM pulse (unknown probability).

    Submarines themselves are essentially immune to orbital laser attack, provided that they do not foolishly surface. Seawater strongly interferes with any signal that might betray the submarine's precise location to an orbital sensor. Moreover, submarines are already designed for stealth, and thus already seek to minimize EM signatures. Finally, seawater forms a virtually impenetrable barrier to orbital laser attack. Since the missiles can be launched underwater and since modern submarines have a number of ways of communicating without surfacing, both coordination with remaining command and the attack itself can occur without risk to the sub.

    \n Sparks, as within the internal combustion engine, produce broadband EM radiation in the radio range as approximate point sources. But those satellites would need some damned sensitive detectors and a way to cancel ambient noise in order to spot a car from orbit. The EM emission is low power (though I wish I knew more specifics).
    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spark-gap_transmitter

    Likewise, leakage from a Faraday cage would have very limited range. Our robot masters could utilize ground-based drones to seek out sources undetectable from space and relay firing coordinates. Bear in mind that you can't call out of a Faraday cage either (well, not using EM waves).

    Running power from a shielded power source requires shielded wires. That's okay, people do that in the lab all the time, but well-shielded cabling can be expensive. Coaxial cable should do the trick to shield from orbital distances, though. Then you just have to worry about leakage at contact points. We could communicate between Faraday cages using shielded wire connections.

    First, though, we'd need to shield the very construction plants used to make the wire mesh, metal foil, metal plates and what-not that we want to use to make the Faraday cages. There should be enough surplus lying around to get started, but organizing a scavenge while dealing with panic and death from above? Not looking good for the human race.

    \nThanks for the source. With that question covered, let's discuss other satellite vulnerabilities and other ways to overcome our own.

    Our Communcations:

    Our communications issues can be solved with shielded cable, as Quentin detailed. However, as noted above, this requires demanding infrastructure investments if it is to be used over long range.

    Buried electric lines already have some shielding by virtue of being buried, and could be used to supplement such a network.

    Fiber optic lines are completely safe from the satellite targeting parameters. Intercontinental communications already occur via undersea fiber optic lines, and so that's a solved problem, and the communications backbones of industrialized nations are also mostly fiber optic.

    Laser-based communications provide another avenue: directional, secure, relatively high-bandwidth, simple (MAKE magazine had a build project on the topic), and capable of long range with adequate power. A diesel generator in an enclosed metal shed later and you're set.

    The LF band and lower bands provide another option, and this solution is easily implemented by modifying existing equipment.

    Their Weaknesses:

    Remaining in a stable orbit is a challenge for these satellites. A 1 terawatt beam fired for 1 second emits 1 terajoule of energy. This imparts a photon momentum of p = E/c to the satellite, or about 3,300 kg*m/s of momentum. This is equivalent to firing 5 kilogram mass (11 pounds) at twice the speed of sound. In other words, this thing kicks like an artillery piece.

    To compensate, the satellite must take action to correct its orbit. Shots straight down (or up) are of no consequence, but any shot in any other direction will perturb the satellite out of orbit. Most problematic are shots at a target in the forward 180 degrees of arc along the satellite's direction of motion. Such shots will serve to slow the satellite down, thus degrading its orbit. A shot at a horizon target along the direction of motion is the worst realistic case.

    This becomes a potential attack strategy when combined with the fact that these are satellites, arrayed in a particular fashion and in particular, known (or knowable) orbits. Deliberately providing targets in such a fashion as to bias the satellites to slow themselves down and knock themselves out of orbit is not difficult. Of course, the satellites presumably have maneuvering jets to correct themselves, but these have a finite fuel supply - a fuel supply that, given the large recoil of each shot, will be exhausted quickly. As we shall see shortly, correcting one shot with another opposing shot would not be a good strategy, nor would emitting half the beam in the opposing direction to make the shot recoil-free.

    Another weakness is recharge time. I assume these satellites are not solar-powered, as gathering a terajoule of power from solar cells is impractical for such a weapon: incoming solar flux on the sunlit side of the earth averages 680 W m-2. A satellite would thus require a mind-blowing square kilometer of perfectly efficient solar panels to recharge in a mere half hour. Clearly, an on-board reactor is required. However, the biggest nuclear sub ever launched had "only" 380 megawatt power output. A satellite launched with such an absurdly huge reactor system would manage a 1 second shot every 45 minutes or so.

    Thus, the satellite network can be swamped easily, and cannot viably use laser shots for navigation nor "recoil-free shots" due to the strategic cost. Before an attack such as a nuclear missile launch, simply switch on a series of decoys or actual real-value sacrifice targets around the globe. Either something gets shot at, creating a chink in the robot overlord armor, or nothing gets shot at - which means you are safe to operate, as the robot holds itself in reserve for defense against attack. Congratulations, you now have a Cold War scenario.

    Finally, going further afield: \n
      \n
    • the satellites exist in an orbit that is susceptible to plasma injection. Any plasma that gets injected into the magnetosphere tends to remain in fixed bands due to the "bottling" effect. A carefully calculated, large injection of plasma at, say, the north pole could flood the satellite orbital region with enough free ions to destroy both detection mechanisms and actual electronics.
      • \n
    • Various anti-satellite weapons have been researched around the world, and some could probably be pressed into service.
      • \n
    • Thunderstorms provide a large zone of safety, through which the robot cannot see with its antenna eyes.

      \n It occurs to me that the satellites might be operating under a crushing scale disadvantage if they can only fire sufficiently destructive shots every few hours per satellite. This means that they'll be knocking out only hundreds of targets per day. Assuming that high EM emitters are top of the list, this means that the satellites will be taking months in simply destroying radio antennae and large power plants (how much external leakage do those have, anyway?), and assuming that people make efforts to keep up some sort of electricity-generation, it'll be years before they might go for something as small as a car. We're talking hundreds of thousands of significant targets, and every target is smaller (and more replaceable) than the last. Anybody have any good numbers on whether those satellites can actually dent anything but the largest concentrated infrastructure installations, or are they doomed to being swamped by the largest 1% of EM emitters while we use the remaining 99% to keep the civilization intact and work on a solution?

      Of course losing that most important 1% still means utter chaos in major metropolises and other high-strung logistical centers around the world, but there are many things that won't be impacted by the satellites directly: military installations (not known for their EM output, really), laboratories (often have their own back-up electricity orders of magnitude smaller than anything the satellites would fire at, assuming they go solely by signal strength), my house (doesn't use electrical heating), and anything smaller than a major power plant or electricity network hub, really. It is likely that we'll lose the Internet and many other services highly dependent on EM output (radio stations, I imagine, would go up first) or reliable electricity, but what remains would still constitute the majority of the world continuing to work normally. For instance, any estimations for how long the satellites will take to get through blowing up all the major EM emitters in Central Europe? Could be that they'd take months or years to get to Finnish targets in their kill list, if they'd ever do.

      Even if the scale issue is only just about considerable instead of overwhelming like I paint up above, it still seems to me that unless the satellites can choose their targets intelligently or they have absolutely overpowering advantage, it should be possible to swamp their firepower with false signal operations. Powerful EM emitters are absolutely trivial to build when you get down to it, and if the satellites really can fire only a few hundred shots a day all together, it doesn't seem that impossible to simply manufacture more emitters than they have shots available. A single well-organized workshop (not talking of a factory, even) can already put out hundreds of these things a day, so we just need electricity, somebody to drive them to distant locations and some replaceable wiring - then it's just a matter of taking the hit and installing a replacement until the satellites lack the power to continue firing.

      I'd say that the conclusion here must be that the satellites have a higher-order intelligence controlling them, something with complex strategic goals and a more complex heuristic than simple EM signal strength. Considering that, I'd have to conclude that it must be possible to negotiate with whoever is controlling the satellites - if they have the intelligence for strategic priorization, they'll have strategic goals, and if they have that, then they have needs of some sort. Thus they should have something they need, and maybe we could provide that without such a destructive war. Even better, the odds are that the somebody controlling the satellites is here on Earth - so I'd say that the priority number dos (right after setting up the various Faraday cages, false signal interference, portable power generators and other logistics) is to find the brain of this operation and blow him out of the water. My money, for the record, is on the Morningstar - he knows suspiciously much about what's going on, and I don't find his cover story about a networked AI that believable, considering the facts on the ground.
