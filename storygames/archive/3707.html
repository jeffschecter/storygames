\n As mentioned in this discussion, I am working on an introductary story-game, intended to bring new people into the hobby and make it appealing to non-gamers. I think I'll be building it around James West's The Pool, since it is simple and has been thoroughly play-tested.

However, there is one significant problem: the dice. Not that I think there is anything wrong with the mechanics, it just uses too many of them for the purposes of an intro game. I don't think average people keep twenty to thirty d6's on hand, which is a major obstacle for the game being ready-to-go after a single read-through.

Decks of playing cards are ubiquitous, though. Here's what I came up with after some idle shuffling. In the following, "face-down" means the value of the card is unknown even to the person who draws it from the deck:

When a conflict comes up, the GM draws 1-3 cards from the deck and plays them face-down on the table.

The player then draws one card and plays it face-up.

If the player's character has a relevent Talent, they draw and place a second card face-down.

The player may then spend points from their Pool to draw further cards and play them face-down, one point per card.

Both player and GM flip over their face-down cards. The player wins if their highest value card beats the GM's highest value card, and things lead on to the Monologue of Victory or Pool increase as per the original rules.

I play it that face cards (Jack, Queen, King) are all worth 5 (giving a semi-bell-curve to possible results). I'm tempted to engineer some special bonus to them, but that may be unduly complicated and random. Haven't figured what to do with Jokers, but it always bugs me when a game calls for removal of certain cards from the deck, so I'd like to come up with a use for them.

Hows this look?

    \n Dude, seriously, if you are throwing down dice more often than twice a scene in the pool you have big trouble.

    Odds of rolling at least one one on 5 evenly-weighted dice: %60
    Odds of drawing at least one ace or king card from an evenly shuffled deck: %50

    The difference gets lesser the more cards you draw. Ten cards/dice is close to the tipping point:

    Dice: %84
    Cards: %84

    If you want easier, rather than harder odds, sure, use face cards, but take out the jacks.

    At 5, this gives:
    Dice: %60
    Cards: %63

    At 10, this gives:
    Dice: %84
    Cards: %87

    \n4 players = 60 cards = more than one deck

    You don't actually need all those dice. When you create a character, you assign bonus (pluses) to your Traits by spending dice from the "starting pool." So your actual starting pool is usually between 3 and 5 dice.

    And also, people are accustomed to sharing a deck of cards when they play card games. So probably all you need is one deck of cards. When a conflict comes up, you figure out how many cards you get from Traits, and how many you're spending from your pool (which you track as a number, rather than as a handful of cards) and the GM deals you that many cards. And then he deals you 1 to 3 bonus cards.
