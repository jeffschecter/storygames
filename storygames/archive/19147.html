\n I have a terrible habit: I start a game session full of secrets that I want the players to eventually discover, but I have a really hard time keeping them. I feel like part of the fun of so many games is discovering things and yet I have such a hard time holding off, keeping mysteries mysterious.

So what do you do in your games to help with pacing? How often do you do big reveals (or little ones)? What do you do to keep the players interested?

    \n If I have a secret, and I want everyone to be excited when I reveal it, then I'll frame it as an answer to a question, and work on getting the players to ask that question. Getting them to ask usually involves putting something at stake, hinging on the answer.

    Example:

    Secret: the mayor is a vampire!
    Answer: the mayor has been ordering the police to bring derelicts into a secret facility so he can feed on them cuz he's a vampire!
    Question: why have the police been bringing derelicts into a secret facility?
    Stake: we've been tasked by our spooky boss with finding out the truth behind the facility; or a missing derelict is a friend of ours; or our reporter buddy disappeared investigating this story; or something.

    Once the stake is set and the question is raised, you can speed or slow pacing by putting all the answers in one place or several. "You sneak into the facility and see the mayor feeding" is fast and centralized, while interrogating or spying on various cops to figure out where the orders are coming from and who's seen what at the facility is slow and spread out. If the game is going slow and the PCs are talking to a cop, have that cop implicate the mayor. If the games is going fast and the PCs are talking to a cop, have that cop implicate the mayor's aide.

    All this is my take based on the premise in the initial post -- that it's up to the GM's judgment when to reveal the GM's secrets. If you wind up not loving that, there are alternatives.

    \n When I'm about to play mysteries or intrigues, I do a fish tank. It's basically an adventure around one (or several) happenings and it got an implicit question of "Why is this happening?". All the factions in the adventure are connected through relations and for every scene I have, I reveal at least one relation to the players (as a reward for they taking actions). Through the relations that unravels, the players will be able to understand why things are like they are.

    \n Here's a tangential idea, but maybe it will inspire you with something:

    When you create your setting and the situation, come up with some secrets. These are all interesting and surprising truths, which most people do not know (like that the mayor is vampire, why not).

    In play, when the players pursue some knowledge about these secrets, do not hold back. Let each attempt to learn something give as much information as would be reasonable in that situation: if they investigate the mayor's bathroom, they'll see that there's no mirror there. If they visit his bedroom in the middle of the night, they'll see him dining on a victim. If they ask him directly, he reacts in a very powerful way, denies everything, and has them arrested. And so on: obvious clues, obvious consequences.

    But, for each secret, figure out how revealing it or acting on it would have major repercussions. For instance, maybe the mayor is responsible for supporting a PC's sister's hospital, and without his help it will collapse or be bought by the mafia. Or he is responsible for holding back the forces of evil. Or maybe it's just a matter of having the other potential mayor be an enemy of the PCs.

    So, the players will get the secrets easily (from your perspective; for them it will feel like hard, but exciting and rewarding work, since it'll still require a scene or so for each major clue or piece of information they uncover, and they'll properly get into some trouble or some adventures in the process).

    But with each secret they learn, they will face a difficult choice:

    * If they reveal that secret or act on it, there will be significant consequences.

    Now the game gets interesting, I think.
