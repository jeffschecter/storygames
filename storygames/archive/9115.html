\n OK, what are your guys opinions on a game with tightly structured length? What I mean by that is the sort of game, not necessarily with a real world time limit (although that could be something to talk about as well) but with more of a built in limit on the amount of scenes in an "adventure" for lack of a better word.

For example, in 3:16, each planet has a certain number of threat tokens, and once you use them the planets over. I'm thinking about something similar to that, but with even more restrictions, along the lines of "each adventure has four combat scenes and three downtime scenes and then it's over, play a second adventure if you want it to go longer" sort of thing.

Thoughts on the upsides/downsides of this sort of thing? Do you totally hate it? Think it's good, but only if used correctly to guide the flow of play in a positive manner? Have any specific thoughts or examples about games that use this kind of thing well? Did you think it added or detracted from the experience while playing those games?

    \n I'm not a fan. It works in 3:16, where the threat tokens don't really matter, because they've got no effect on the story. And it's different in something like The Shab Al-Hiri Roach, where the scenes are essentially background to the story.

    But I don't like them in things like Inspectres and PTA. I prefer to let the story draw to a natural close, rather than a pile of tokens telling me when to end. Otherwise, you get the problem that the story ends, but there's still tokens; or the tokens are out, so the story ends too soon.

    Also, I think designers shove things like this into their games without thinking about it.

    \n I think constrained scene economies are morally reprehensible.

    ...oh, everything I've designed has a constrained scene economy. Let me reformulate that.

    I think constrained scene economies are the best thing evar.

    In my experience, players tend to stretch things out unnecessarily, wander incoherently, and simply not use their game-time in an efficient manner. That is, if you look at a typical unstructured game and itemize the things that the players actually accomplish in terms of story and characterization (or whatever their CA values), a very small portion of time is actually used for what they play for. Most of the time is groping towards figuring out what they want and how.

    The way I design, the purpose of game procedures is to guide players to be more efficient with their time, hitting the notes they need to hit and then ushering them off to the next opportunity. Procedures that affect scenes do the latter part (usher them off to the next opportunity), while the rest of the game procedures do the first (guide players to hit the right notes). So in my experience, you really need those note-hitting procedures to complement the scene-ushering procedures. Otherwise the scene procedures needlessly jerk the players around (and you get Graham's complaint, where the scene shifts for no reason).

    \n In a game I'm currently co-designing, we've got very simple and tight rules about this kind of thing. The playtest feedback so far has been encouraging. Each scenario comes with a fixed number of "acts" (not as constrained as single scenes) of which the first has a fixed setting, with a pre-written "prologue" and a set of "roles" for the PCs which tie them into the prologue and setting. Also, the last "act" is usually contrained in some way, either in setting, or by inclusion of a named important NPC in it or something.
    In play, "acts" alternate with "breaks" in which the players are encouraged to discuss the ongoing game out of character.

    I've found that I have to very clearly explain the constrained structure to my fellow players before the game, insuring everybody got it just right. But as soon as they get it, they move with it and consciously drive the fiction toward a fulfilling ending. Lots of workshopping happen in the "breaks", but it never devolves into pure pre-scripting of scenes.

    This somehow resembles playing Geiger Counter: people need to know what the structure is and also need to understand they're responsible for their own fun. Then they cooperate to get maximum enjoyment within the limits of the structure, and the structure does not kick them in the balls.

    \n Whiteknife, since you seem to be interested, I'll expand a bit on what I said. With tightly constrained games it's even more important that the mechanics have the right "feel" to them (something more than just determining success or failure) and how they're represented.

    Take a countdown, for example. A game could just have a limited number of scenes and declare it over once they're done. That's fine if the countdown isn't the point of the game. Another way of doing it is to move a pawn along a track, so everyone can see that there is a time passing. A stack of cards running down throughout the game gives an even stronger visual cue as to time running out. For a lot of tension, use the same stack of cards running down, but have them run down extra-fast and have the players fight a losing battle to add cards to the pile to keep them from running out.

    Along the same vein, risk is evoked by gambling, but it is made better by having a concrete representation of what is being risked (e.g. chips), rather than points recorded on a sheet.

    It's not just representation, the process of it matters. For example, being able to save dice from one roll to the next gives a sense of building something, and possibly sacrifice for a greater goal.

    Sure, all these things are doable in a larger game, but tightly constrained games are often very good at focusing on one particular "feel," so you can really go wild in expressing that in the mechanics, promoting one behaviour in the story and discouraging others in ways that games with wider scopes can't get away with so easily.

    \n Whiteknife, I suggest that if you want to enforce a set "adventure format" you'd rather think of that as a hard limit and build all of the rules of the game around that. It's a design tool you've got.

    I consider it a pitfall of games such as D&D that they're built around an "ideal" format but not tightly enough, basically not enforcing the format except by backhandedly punishing you with diminished fun when you "fail" to follow it. Apart for a bunch of a mildly formulated suggestions, a DM's on his own to make the game work. I believe it's a consequence of a marketing constraint, really, because of how a majority of gamers think a RPG "should" be made... But it is, in my eyes, a pitfall to avoid: the 21st century game designer, armed with an understanding of roleplaying-game as - well - a game, has a whole bunch of other options which don't contemplate re-designing the shortcomings of D&D again.

    All of the above coming from a D&D fanboy, by the way.

    \n I realized it might look like I was criticizing Graham's group's Fiasco, which I wasn't, by the way. To the extent that a group tends to lose story focus, they probably benefit from constraints like time limits (Puppetland, Fiasco), a list of allowed moves for the GM (Dogs) and/or all players (Storming the Wizard's Tower is inspiring in this regard), or a reward cycle centered on story goals (TSoY, WoD).
