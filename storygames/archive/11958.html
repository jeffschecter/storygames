\n HISTORY
(skip down to 'THE GAME' if you only care about games, not comics)

I was in a comic shop recently, when a friend pointed to an issue of FemForce, in awe that the title still exists. Indeed there are well-over 100 issues.

How can I explain the significance of this? FemForce is a comic that was always just sort of -there-. My eye has passed-over FemForce on the sales racks for Three Fucking Decades without EVER lingering or giving it a second thought. (Ok, maybe one time, my pubescent-self flipped through it, was disappointed that it wasn't -more- pornographic, and then dismissed it forever from my thoughts.)

FemForce arrived in the early 1980's when upstart companies like Eclipse, First, Pacific, and AmeriComics vied for a 'distant third' place after Marvel and DC.
And the comics they published weren't all that far from Marvel and DC's fare. There was a pretense of being a tad more college-y or discerning, but we're still talking superheroes or sword-barbarians or space adventurers. 'Alternative' comics came later.

FemForce's niche was that it was a little more buxom than Marvel and DC.
Tame wank material for fanboys.

That's a blandly-solid niche, and it positioned FemForce nicely for when the late '80s b&w Indie glut arrived.

But it's downright amazing the FemForce kept an audience all the way thru the 90's, when shiny, spherical boobs glistened on countless Marvel and DC and Image covers; and actual pornographic comics arrived that made FemForce look limp. And thru the 00's, when FemForce's analog art style looks increasingly dated and clunky.

And yet, it remains. Go figure.


THE GAME

So this week an RPG.net review of Super Babes: The FemForce Role-Playing Game (1993) caught my eye.

I'd never seen this game. I've only read the review. The most interesting part is description of a mechanic called BIMBO POINTS:


"Next you get to \u201ca statistic that is thoroughly unique to the Super Babes system, BIMBO POINTS!\u201d This is of course the game's other main example of \u201cgenre simulation.\u201d On one hand it simulates those gray-area power stunts that \u201ccrunch\u201d games don't do very well; mechanically you can tell the GM to give you one Bimbo Point (BP) so that you can perform an action you can normally roll and have it succeed automatically. Agreeing to take TWO BPs means you can perform \u201c(a)ny action that is not covered by the rules that a character wants to perform\u201d. You can only earn up to 3 BP per session, \u201cthough why anyone would want more is beyond us.\u201d
See, the drawback is that at the beginning of each game, the GM rolls a d20 for each character and if the number is under the character's current BP total, she suffers a random Bimbo Event (BE). This is rolled on a percentile chart, with some events being both cheesy and predictable (character is sexually harassed by superior), some being a bit less predictable (character accused of sexual harassment) and some being potential threats to the character and her team, such as 'cult begins worshiping character' which could be a REAL problem if combined with 'cult decides they must sacrifice character'.
The book points out that in the comics, the limits of a game system wouldn't apply, only the limits placed on the character by the writer and/or editor, so again, the mechanic covers such \u201cspecial\u201d events while limiting the power this gives the player by forcing the PC to accept the consequences for going outside the \u201crules\u201d by means of another plot contrivance, this time going against her.
This is actually a fairly innovative mechanic in its own way-- in fact it predates the \u201cFATE Point\u201d mechanic by over a decade. The problems with the label being obvious; one, the mechanic is also used by male characters who are not bimbos (although they mention the mystic Green Lama as an example of someone whose near-omnipotent magic also ends up creating several complications for him), by female characters who are not bimbos (like Nightveil/Blue Bulleteer) and female characters who are not bimbos but who are objectified and/or the victims of comic misfortunes (like Yankee Girl).
In fact, Flare, from the aforementioned Heroic Publishing, is probably the best example of an actual bimbo in this sub-genre: She's highly sexual, and while possessed of high native intelligence, doesn't always seem to apply common sense, and both factors end up causing her problems."

Going only by this description, I think Bimbo Points might be more feminist that the reviewer allows. Thematically, Bimbo Points seem to say that "if you agree to play-along with society's sexist stereotypes when it's to your advantage; then it might become habit, and you'll keep acting like a bimbo, even when it's no longer to your advantage."

Or maybe I'm giving schlock too much credit.

    A bit of Googling reveals that SuperBabes: FemForce was an official Hero System product.

    So Bimbo Points aren't just similar to Hero Points, they're an intentional play on words and mechanics.
    (And possibly a subtle critique.)


    Google offers more illumination into FemForce rules:

    "To sum up; attractiveness and costumes were both three-level concepts. You were either normal (default), attractive (Com 12-15, +1d6 for Pre attacks), beautiful (Com 16-20, +2d6 for Pre attacks), or gorgeous (Com 20+, +3d6 for Pre attacks). Your costume was either normal (bodysuit, nothing very sexy; no bonus), revealing (usual "bathing suit" costume, +1d6 for Pre attacks), skimpy (lotsa cleavage, thigh-high boots, typical "fanservice"; +2d6 for Pre attacks), or scandalous (something only the "slutty supervillianess" would wear, or maybe Witchblade in some of her more scanty moments; +3d6 for Pre attacks). This means that if you're Gorgeous and wear a Scandalous costume, you get +6d6 to your Pre attacks. Not bad."

    So the game draws a distinction between literal "empowerment" via exploiting one's sexuality
    vs.
    crossing some line into Scandalous or Bimbo territory.


    In "Backlash", Susan Faludi describes the paradox faced by business-women who have to avoid feminine attire (to some ill-defined extent) so that they will be considered 'professional', while not crossing the (ill-defined) line into being considered 'too masculine'.

    At least, SuperBabes offers enough mechanical Crunch that such boundaries are clearly drawn, even if the basis for the boundaries is suspect. I bet the lines would be blurrier in actual play than on the character sheet.

    I'm thinking that playing FemForce according to the Good Girl Art conventions that spawned the game, would sidestep controversy. Just like playing Maid works as long as nobody questions the entire idiom.

    Playing FemForce as-written, but without a feel for Good Girl Art conventions would lead to disputes about the boundaries between concepts like Skmpy and Scandalous.

    However, I also think that those same rocky moments would be Thematic Gold for that breed of Narrativist who loves exploring the ambiguity of social/ethical/moral boundaries. The fact that the game could EASILY be played that way suggests that the rules aren't just plain stoopid.
    In fact, is there a BETTER game for exploring gender roles in superhero comics? This one has all the right details to crash into.

    It's a tool that could be used for (a certain kind of) feminist good, or for what (some versions of) feminism would consider evil. So maybe it's chaotic good.

    Or , again, maybe just schlock.

    \nShe's also a late-middle-aged matron rather than a young maiden. But that was all part of the joke. Y'see, Red Tornado wasn't meant to be taken seriously, she's really a contemporary parody of superheros.

    In the golden age, comic magazines would contain a mix of features in every issue (rather than just the single story they usually do now). Several short comic stories of different genres (detective, western, strange adventure), a couple short prose fictions, and usually some pages of gags or comedy strips with an eccentric cast of characters very much like those of Popeye or Lil Abner. One of those comedy strips was Scribbly, about a kid in the city aspiring to be a cartoonist and his relatives and neighborhood friends. After the superhero features started to eclipse the others in popularity, the writer of Scribbly had one of the supporting characters, ole' "Ma" Hunkel, overhear some kids raving about Green Lantern comics* and decide to deal with the local gangsters by taking the mystery man approach.

    *doubly cheeky because Green Lantern appeared in the same magazines as Scribbly, and even though he's spoken of as a fictional character Red Tornado would later meet him and the rest of the JSA in person.

    Red Tornado is pretty much intended to invert everything that had been established about superheroes at the time:
    *Superheroes are invariably young viral men / RT is a cantankerous mature woman.
    *Costumes are dashing and fancy / RT's costume is an old pot and long underwear.
    *Adventures are grand and happen in exotic locales / RT beats up swindlers in the local neighborhood.
    *Heroes have superpowers or are supercompetent / RT is just a big brawler.
    * Heroes have incredible secret lairs / RT lives in a tenement.
    *Superheroes are serious / RT's stories are comedic.

    To try and make this at least somewhat discussion-relevant, its arguable that despite its seeming innocuousness, Red Tornado is sexist in its own way, in that the idea of woman superhero (or least not a "pretty girl" one) was seen as so inherently ridiculous that it could only appear in comedy stories.
