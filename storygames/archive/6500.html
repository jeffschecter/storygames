[Thou Art But A Warrior] Spitting Curses, Bathed in Blood

\n At one point my guy says "I'm a Jew" and Clinton, as the Emir, calmly replies, "You mean you were a Jew."

Yeah, it was that kind of a game.

OK, so first of all, Anna Kreider facilitated her Polaris hack Thou Art But A Warrior (which I prefer to call TABAW, which sounds infinitely more bad-ass, like a futuristic weapon system). You play knights of the Islamic caliphate in Spain on the eve of the reconquista. Things are fucked and getting more fucked by the second. It's a perfect match for Polaris. For me it's better than Polaris, because it gives me something I know how to care about.

It was me, Rachel, Clinton, and Nick. Since it was a one-shot convention game at camp nerdly, we played the express version - two protagonists (me and Nick), two antagonists (Rachel and Clinton), and double the progress of calamity. I think this suits my play style well and I'm not sure keeping your foot on the accelerator harms anything.

So Nick's guy is a straight up Moorish knight, Captain of the Guard, and I'm a Mulatti from a prominent Jewish family who is the Emir's treasurer. We build up a solid R-map with the emir, his daughter (beloved of Captain Nasir, hater of Lamech the Treasurer), Marwan, Nasir's drunk apostate brother, General Ismael, commander of the army, that sort of thing. We pointed at each other as much as possible.

The ritual phrases in Polaris make the transition unscathed. They apply the same awful pressure that makes that game so fun. Bad things are going to happen to us, and we make bad choices. I had a hard time pushing my guy, lamech, into heroic and awesome activities, because I am such a glutton for punishment. Rachel and I worked it out though, and I think we pushed each other pretty hard. I ended up whipping my own father in public. Good times!

One really cool piece of TABAW is that as the situation deteriorates, you get these crazy-ass scenes where normal causality goes out the window. Anna kept encouraging us, when we crossed one of these thresholds, to go nuts - a beheaded General kept spitting curses, God covered my father's flesh with invisible armor, that sort of thing. It's brilliant and makes the supernatural elements pop in contrast to the rest of the game, which is as prosaic as the funeral elegy of an entire culture can be.

The game's baked; time to take it out of the oven and serve it up hot.
