\n Hands up if you have a problem with violence in RPGs. My hand is provisionally up -- I personally get weirded out when I find myself either playing in, or running, a game in which violence has become abstract and cartoony. Sometimes, occasionally, I can loosen up a bit if abstract, cartoony violence is, you know, in-genre. But the "cartoony" (i.e. unrealistic in both its execution and consequences) thing is important if I'm not gonna get viscerally upset by the violence.

But otherwise? Ew. No. Violence has its place at the table but it either needs to be built into the assumptions of the genre/setting/theme/whatever we're playing or it needs to be used very carefully.

I know this is a well-traveled path and I know a lot of folks pay lip service to saying "no" to gratuitous violence in their games, but for serious here: Can you enjoy a game, like say D&D (among others!), in which the execution of violence seems to be the very point baked into the game? What are your boundaries, if any? Is it "okay" to just cut loose and make-believe that you're killing shit left and right? If so, how is that "okay" but cutting loose and playing make-believe about other forms of violence (rape, emotional abuse, socioeconomic injustice, whatever) not-okay?

    \n I'll start with a story:

    I had huge problems with D&D in general, and aimless violence (every problem can be solved with combat) was just a tiny tiny bit of that. I decided I would never run the game again and that I would only play it under extreme circumstances.

    But then, recently we started playing Pathfinder (which is, frankly, just D&D with another name) and we decided to take it completely non-seriously. We actually stressed "No roleplaying!" as a rule at the table. It was all extremely self-referential and blatantly exploitative and all that jazz...and it was GREAT FUN.

    For example, whoever we killed (even the local shopkeep) turned out to have been the bad guy all along, so we retconned all our violence to have been justified, leaving our reputation as "heroes" perfectly clean. Our social contract shifted from "we are playing a game where we pretend we're elves" to "we're playing D&D". It was hilarious and it was liberating and it was a little bit scary.     \n I can't remember off-hand that I'd ever had problems with violence in rpgs, any more than I'd had problems with sexuality. The closest we come is a sense of disappointment and lack of control when I get into a game with new people and they prove to be playing a completely different game from what I'm doing. I can vividly remember a session of Dust Devils in 2005 or so; there were two players in the game who were just messing about without caring for the fictional situation, fictional consequences or who their characters were. This sort of thing can get ugly in a standard forgista game like DD, because the GM's job in this sort of game is to basically throw out Bangs and their consequences. In this case it meant an increasing spiral of violence and atrocity as the problem players answered every problem they could comprehend with violence, killing and brutalizing the poor citizens and then engaging the rest of the player characters in a tedious and meaningless siege/gunfight.

    I was annoyed by the above and uncertain of how to deal with it socially, as most of the group was new to me, but socially functional, while these two acted like 14-year olds and ignored all instruction and signals of how to play the game. I'd have to say that while violence had a role, it was not the violence that bothered me per se, but the inhumanly psychopath characters painted for our benefit by these two players. Their violence had no reason, and I was sort of stuck as a vehicle of the violence porn, being the GM and responsible for handling the consequences - a part of which was depicting the human costs of the choices made by these players, and trying my darnedest to make the NPCs somehow sympathetic and meaningful so they wouldn't shoot any more of them without good reason.

    It's easy to see that violence wasn't the crux issue in the above vignette, but the fact that we had a mix of players who communicated on different levels - the perpetrators here didn't realize that they were annoying and creeping out the rest of the group by creating atrocities in the fiction but refusing to carry the moral consequences for their characters by becoming clear-cut villains of the piece or otherwise acknowledging the human cost of what their characters did. The specific in-fiction violence would have been fine if we had understood why their characters acted. I've also had similar experiences that have not involved violence, but have otherwise flouted the fiction due to earnest but clueless players using the wide discretionary powers some games give to them.

    \n I guess I would want to distinguish a couple of different ways that violence has been problematic in games (or in fiction generally, for that matter) for me.

    1. Violence solves problems. In fact, ONLY violence actually effectively solves problems.

    This one is particularly an RPG thing, but it's very much out there in the culture at large, too. In RPGs, it probably comes from a long history of combat being the one arena of play in which there were hard rules and concrete consequences. I've often run into the attitude (and can remember having it myself at certain times) that the only way to be SURE of a "win" in play was the the Clear The Level strategy - kill absolutely everything that you can. Deals won't stick, prisoners are a huge headache, and enemies left alive will inevitably return with buddies & powerups later.

    The lesser version of this (violence solves problems, but it isn't the ONLY thing that does) provides much fertile ground for play (DitV, anyone?) but still flirts with some nasty ideas from time to time. It's all a bit fascist.

    Then again, I enjoy superhero comics, and you don't get much more fascist than that.

    2. Violence is both graphic/realistic and morally trivial, aka "violence porn"

    Possible to get right, but NOT easy. Played as black comedy, parody, and the like this CAN be fun and non-ugly, but it's rarely successful in my experience. Usually it's just empowerment/revenge fantasy or shock/gross-out.

    I like my violence either sufficiently cartoonish and steeped in genre convention to reach the "good clean fun" level of a good dungeon bash, or IF it's going to be serious, to be damned serious.

    It's like anything else "serious" in game: everybody's got to be more or less on the same page about what's acceptable. I think my biggest frustration with violence has been relative to other things - there are great kerfuffles and much rending of garments about the slightest hint of gender, class, race, politics, religion, what-have-you intruding into many games, but the baseline assumption that a game will inevitably contain problem-solving through unproblematized application of extreme force is as common as dirt.

    \n Paul, I don't think of that sort of thing as a serious problem, mostly because it's really rare. It must be about one in a hundred roleplayers who is so detached from social niceties and creative cooperation that they can just barge into a game and do their thing without trying to pick up the cues from the other players. I only remember meeting a few gamers like that, and the one I know best is actually an on-and-off mental patient. It's a fluke, the great majority of people I sit down to play something like Dust Devils with are perfectly able to look around them and understand what it is that the others are doing.

    The normal situation with game violence for practically all of us, I expect, is that we consider it purely in the game's own gamey frame. In a commando skirmish game like D&D, of course there's going to be violence, but it doesn't bother us because it's just pawns in a game, the same as chess. And then there are the various dramatic games where we do observe the violence as violence, but even then we're routinely protected by genre and Aristotelic story structures; the violence is detached because it's imaginary and formulaic in a way. Only when we encounter the violence as violence porn, detached from dramatic or strategic framework, do we get bothered by it. This is all pretty natural, I think.

    From the above viewpoint it seems that creepy violence in a rpg is actually really easy to replicate: just make the violence gratuitous in a way that lacks any justifying framework such as a strategy game or dramatic plot, and describing the violence or listening to it will rather soon start to seem sick. I've actually sort of made use of this phenomenon in various horror games to make the impact of the gore perfectly clear.

    \n A good angle that a couple of posters have already touched is that the most obvious way for violence to not be gratuitous in roleplaying is for it to not be gratuitous, not to remove it entirely. This is evident in D&D as well - the normal D&D narrative involves violence for heroic, justified purposes, in display of courage in the face of adversity, in defence of civilization against barbarism and weak against the strong. That sort of stuff is entirely fundamental to masculine identity, I might say that we as a culture need it to have our men grow righteous and responsible. This goes for roleplaying games as well as other media - we want violence in our narratives, but we want it to be righteous violence.

    Thus, one clear-cut answer to the original query about D&D is that violence in D&D is not bothersome because it is presented unambiguously within this established narrative of righteous violence. If you can accept that narrative (at least for dramatic purposes) in other media, it will prove no greater problem in roleplaying games, either. It is probably for this reason that the easiest way of making the violence in D&D creepy and disgusting is to break the rules of righteous war we know from our cultural conditioning - have the PCs harass non-combatants, initiate fights with a profit motivation and so on. This is also a likely reason for why the same game events can bother one person while they don't bother another; when you have slight differences in your acculturation to the use of violence, you read the subtext differently, and then one person's righteous war becomes another's human rights crime. I might hazard a guess that attitudes to colonialism are a major fault line in this regard when we consider D&D; in our society today it is entirely possible for one person to grow up with a keen awareness of ethnic minority rights and what conquistadorialism looks like in reality, while another grows up on a nationalistic narrative, perhaps with a goodly dash of the white man's burden mixed in. Put those two people at a table and ask them to play a game about professional mercenaries who strive to strike it rich by wandering the countryside and trying to "discover" a "lost" treasure, and you'll find that they'll interpret the subtext quite differently.

    It would be simplistic to exonerate D&D entirely of critique here, because societal standards change and what was politically acceptable in pulp literature during the '30s or '60s and what is acceptable today are different things. Thus, even if Gygax et all. originally intended their game to be politically neutral (which is likely to my mind), it need not be that anymore once the society's value landscape changes. From a modern viewpoint D&D has a pretty mercenary worldview, just like much of pulp fantasy - if you'll read Fritz Leiber, for instance, you'll find the same idea of an uncaring world and society, one in which protagonists make their way by their wit and sword, their foremost goal being to scrapple their way up from immediate poverty and into the tyrannical and arbitrary ruling class they find themselves arrayed against. This is where D&D comes from, and it can be seen in the mercenary way the game runs on gold coins and killing expertise. The most original core D&D experience does not involve defensive war against an aggressive enemy like it often does today; rather, the core experience is for a bunch of independent adventurers to delve into the wilderness to "find" treasure. This is proactive activity, the same as colonial exploration and exploitation.

    Still, the basic point is valid, that point being that D&D is intended to run within the framework of just war, even if those limits have shifted since the game was first written. Today a GM might need to motivate player characters more and make sure that the enemy they steal their gold from is really and truly evil enough to make the game work; this is all because our own value landscapes are different from what they were 50 years ago, most of us find it more difficult to accept the premise of enterpreneurial, conquistadoric slaughter the game originally offers. Even as it is fantasy, people sometimes don't want to get involved in such a fantasy. I could easily see how one might write an upgraded game that removed this tension altogether even today, though - you just need to make sure that the copious violence in your game all happens clearly within the framework of justified, chivalric warfare, whatever the audience understands with that, and you'll find that they'll have no trouble whatsoever with engaging with the game. I am rather certain that D&D would lose plenty of its glamour in this sort of process, though; art works on edginess and identity, and watering down the Conanesque "by the might of my thews" from the game would spoil something that's likely considered the best part by many. I know that I myself enjoy this literary genre and rpgs made of it, even if I am aware of the faults in the worldview behind the exercise when implemented in the real world.

      As to the basic questions of violence, robbery, and D&D, it might be worth noting how far D&D as played today has wandered from its origin.

      IIUC the evolution of the game, the earliest form was essentially a format to give context to a campaign minis game with fantasy flavor.

      The game was run with many players, like, a couple dozen. The goal of those players was to enrich and empower their characters, to be more useful to whatever faction they joined or created, once it was time for the big showdowns. Players were kinda on alignment based teams, but each individual player was acting a bit like a player in Diplomacy.

      The GM was mostly a neutral referee, and dungeons were mostly small side quests to grab loot ( pays for the armies and the castles which players would eventually build), and power up individually for the big battles. The dungeons were supposed to be kinda stupidly deadly, although not necessarily at tournament levels of deadly. After all, multiple parties might be going into and out of the same dungeons at different times, only partially completing it. Plus, it was good thing for some characters to die and others to outstrip even their (supposed, alignment bloc) team mates. After all, the campaign aspect needed to end at some point, or at least reach the level of the big battles being fought.

      Now think about just how far D&D has drifted/evolved from that initial set of circumstances.

      Play culture rapidly moved away from the entire many player, minis club/group context where a campaign in the wargamer sense is being played.
      Those side-quest things move from being interesting side-quests for loot to being the core of the activity.
      Character death moves more and more into taboo territory.
      GMs become less neutral referees.
      Dungeons become less of a circumstancial ruin to be partially plundered as players and characters are best able, and something to be fully completed.
      Without the wargame campaign context with its PvP, play starts haing other contexts (stories/worlds) being provided.
      The norm moves away from adenturers who can come together for a delve, then disperse and join other groupings of players/characters for the next delve to permanent teams of constantly improving dungeon-delvers.

      To sum up:
      There isn't gratuitous violence in D&D in its original context. Violence is core to that D&D.

      It is instead that D&D has drifted so far from its roots that we start to see gratuitous violence in it in the newer context that has become the norm. (And realy, that newer context has been there in one form or another for a very long time. I'd date it from D&D achieving fad status and breaking out of the hobbyist gamer ghetto circa 1980).

      Problem is, even with 4e, you'e still got that core animal that goes all the way back to the earliest incarnation, and that's where the weird taste combo comes from.
