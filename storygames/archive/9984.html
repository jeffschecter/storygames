    \nWhat does the new book do for the setting: the most important part is that it updates the crunch material for the Solar System. Mostly that's the sort of thing you could do yourself, of course, so it probably appeals just to my fans (yeah, I have some here in Finland; crazy). The second thing is that it adds crunch: there's roughly thrice as many cool Abilities, Secrets and Keys for each entry as there are in the original TSoY. It's made to my own sensibilities, of course, but I'm sure most anybody will find something to his tastes in there. The third thing is that it expands the setting with new ideas. The table of contents hints at what those are.

    Who wrote the book: I compiled and edited everything that went into the book, but most of Clinton's original prose is there. The Southern Initiative relies heavily on my forum discussions with Josh Culbertson from last year, you can read those at the Forge. I wrote the parts of the Southern Initiative that I didn't lift directly from those threads. The giants and dwarves originate similarly. Other stuff is mostly from my pen, such as the furry material, etc. As there are many sources of inspiration in the book, each chapter itemizes its sources separately.

    And yes, the trade-off of more vs. less material has very much been on my mind. I suppose the book will be funny for others to read in the way it bursts into sermons about how to use setting source material now and then. My own stance at this point of writing (read: after getting it done) is that it takes a bit of willpower, but I can pretend to complete ignorance of anything if I want to, and act like I didn't have that setting material. Also, there's the fact that unlike Exalted, TSoY does not do plot: all that setting material is only "setting" in that it gives a name for something that might or might not be pertinent to an individual campaign. It doesn't tie that material down to the map and say that this must be the thing player characters encounter when they go to those coordinates.

    \n Ah, yes. I've been calling the rule set Solar System lately (and using "TSoY" for when you specifically play in Near), so I understood that differently.

    What does TSoY give to the Solar System: The most basic part is that it's full of immediately usable crunch integrated with the fluff. Most places you can't get both at the same time. Another reason is that while Solar System is universal, it's not generic, and TSoY has been planned to take advantage of the things SS is good at: sympathetic, human stories. In SS I write that the game works best with a setting that is dramatic (people make choices), epic (great changes are possible) and adventurous (the aforementioned are resolved in the grand action adventure tradition); Near as a setting and TSoY as a genre are all of these.

    But basically, if the question is whether you need the book to enjoy Solar System to the fullest, I would say not. Only if you're looking for new inspiration, or are already in love with Clinton's haughty, agonized elves, sorry drunk goblinkin or the wicked Seven Houses of Ammeni is this book going to be worthwhile. (Or if you're just interested in the "metaplot" of what I changed and added, I guess.) If you're already up to your ears in setting stuff and don't find making your own crunch hard, this isn't going to be that important.

    What does WoN give to TSoY: If you already have Clinton's book, then you'll only want this one if you want updated crunch, new crunch or the stuff that is new to this book in comparison to Clinton's. I find that his book is still a perfectly serviceable introduction to the game, especially as it comes with the rules included.

    Somebody help me out here, surely there are better reasons for getting this book? I'm supposed to be selling it at Gencon. Perhaps there is a flaw in my carefully orchestrated "split system from setting and sell in separate books" plan that I didn't perceive when I went to it last year.

    \n I might not just be understanding Enligsh very well tonight, Adam. I worked 70 hours and slept 10 at the end there to get the book done, so not entirely recovered yet. "Try again later."

    PDF version: Yes, sometime after Gencon.
    Completely new things compared to Clinton's book: The Southern Initiative is entirely new material, as are those couple of races, as is the last movement pretty much entirely. All other chapters have new material, most of which is in the crunch. Most of it's been featured in the Internet in some form or other, but some is completely new, and all has been rewritten to work together.

    Backstory: I'm afraid the backstory is both more clear and less clear than before. It's more clear in that there is more information out of which you can build your mythology and cosmology, but it's also less clear in that it contradicts itself even more now, and still doesn't give an unified explanation for anything. This is one thing I actually like about TSoY, that it does not pretend to a canon. I have nothing against writing about my own understanding of what the "big story" is about, but I didn't feel comfortable doing it in this book, because it'd have been interpreted as "The Big Story" that all gamers, like sheep, are forced to follow by instinct.

    \n Ah, yes. So, what makes Near great?

    I really like the humanism. I didn't include my anti-fascist fantasy lecture in the book (I almost did, but relented), but basically the bit that I most like about Near is that it very much refutes the concept of heroism as an identity: you are not a hero because you are a son of a god, or because you're the right race or have the right upbringing: you're always and merely a hero because of your actions - heroism is not a state of being, it's a state of action. Contrast and compare with modern, post-Tolkien fantasy literature: it's very easy to get me to scream my hatred at yet another story where the protagonist realizes through a series of conflicts how very special he really is. Near is all about the opposite, about how even the most lowly slave or woman, one that would never be considered as a protagonist of a proper chivalric romance, can still matter and be a force for difference in the world.

    (It's ironic that I actually added prongs to the material to support the traditional heroic narrative; makes for more excitement when a player genuinely has a leg to stand on when he claims divine inspiration for his character. Doesn't help him a bit against the bitter widow knifing him in the back, of course, but that's the story taken to the extreme: the game doesn't just refute the idea of divine heroism, it actually allows you to bring in the divine heroes and beat the stuffing out of them. Raising the stakes, that.)

    Another thing I like about Near is how organic it is - was with Clinton and became even more so after he let the wolves rip at it a bit. TSoY genuinely does not have a genre, it just is. There is no bibliography in the book, and almost every chapter implies a different literary context in which it would make sense. The book is brimming with genre elements, but they do not form a context we would have to follow in play. I find this extremely liberating when compared to some games that live from and for genre convention. Here I don't have to work backwards from genre models: I can really just look at what the setting is in my mind and play according to that, whatever the consequences might be. (This isn't unique in comparison to many traditional rpgs, understand; it's just rare in well-designed Forgista games, which often draw heavily from genre expectations.)

    A third thing I like about Near is that it really, truly has no canon anybody would care about. I've grown pretty good at ignoring canon, but in truth I don't feel comfortable doing that with something like Middle Earth, to pick an example setting; the canon is just such a large part of the value proposal with those settings - the very reason I play in that setting is that I want to figure out how my new story fits in with the canon. I don't know yet what we're doing with Near, but whatever it is, it just might be a new thing in imagination, a thing for our Internet generation. We are postmodern, we do not need a one truth, we can sail in between possibilities and flexibly nail the facts for concrete play while keeping an open mind in the abstract. At the very least, Near is genuinely a setting, merely - it doesn't support, cannot support metaplot when it just barely supports a geography.

    A fourth thing I like about Near is that as a dedicated Solar System setting it really is remarkably advanced mechanically. This isn't objectively better than being light-weight, it's just different; when I want to play with material that has really been thought out by many people, material where I don't need to improvise, Near is an obvious choice. The more integrated systems in the game are so complex that they'd be nigh-impossible to improvise; the individual explosive ideas hidden in the crunch landscape so succulent that one stumbles upon such only with luck, and gathering them together in one place becomes a great chore. Having such a rich landscape to work from is a great value add to the game.

    The above reasons are almost exhaustive, but I think that they're not quite everything... I suppose that I also simply like the individual ideas in the setting. Or perhaps the simple, birds-eye view they're presented from. TSoY does not try to be atmospheric, it doesn't try to lure you in with immersive prose. It just gives you the tired saints responsible for atrocity, the hungry and short-sighted nobility, inhuman politicians, witch-women turned crazy from grief - and everybody else in the setting for that matter - and asks you to do what you will with the cast. I'm not saying that the quality of the mere ideas is not high - it is, I like them all - but it's not just ideas, but representation. My book has a huge amount of text, but it only names something like half a dozen characters by name, and half of those are dead. It's stark and solely concerned with your autonomy as a player, it refuses to get mired in dictating your stories or their style for you.

    Hmm... I suppose I'll need to make that a bit punchier for Gencon.

    You're right that using a +1 rating always means that your equipment is going to get damaged. I don't mind; it's just wear and tear, and it can be ameliorated with suitable Secrets or repairs.
