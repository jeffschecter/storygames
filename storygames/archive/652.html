How much should we get to see?

TonyLB
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Play Advice

Okay, so when we play we are all both actors and spectators, right? Players do things and also observe other players doing things.

Now I've seen two broad divisions in terms of what people expect as an audience: In some games, everything happens right there around the table, and people assume that they will get to see the whole shebang, from soup to nuts.

In some games, people go off into another room (often with the GM) and chat about stuff secretly. Other participants, obviously, don't expect to be witness to that secret stuff (though sometimes it is assumed that the GM [b]must[/b] know, or that secrets must be temporary (i.e. that the other players will eventually find out what happened)).

So ... is this something inherent to what we're actually doing, or is it a holdover from linear media like storytelling and television?

Thought experiment: Imagine you were watching a soap opera where thread of the story were being simultaneously followed on ten television channels. When one thread weaved into another (i.e. Jessica storms out on Roland, but bumps into Edward filling in what looks suspiciously like a grave in the back garden) you get to choose which thread/channel to follow next. But the broadcast is only happening this once, and you don't have ten VCRs, so you will not get to watch the whole thing.

That's ... it's totally unlike storytelling that I'm familiar with ... and yet ... and yet, it draws me. I feel strongly that, for all that is lost ("But what did Roland when I went off following Jessica? Where did he disappear to, and why are temporal clones of him returning from the future to destroy the past? I have to know!") there would be some powerful things gained.

Similar thought experiment: Imagine a game in which any two players could play, separately, without consulting other people. You get together with the GM and do GM-stuff, then you wander off to the other room. Bobby's there, so you play a scene between your characters. Suddenly Jake storms in ... apparently he needs a scene with Bobby in the worst way. So you head back to the GM and slide back into that scene. When it's appropriate, you dump some of the resources you got playing with Bobby, and everyone says "Oh, you sly dog! Have you been up to something?" but they all know that whatever it is, recapping it can and will wait until after the game is over.

I think that'd be cool. D'you think that'd be cool?

    Isn't it really a combination of personal taste and the needs of the kind of story you are telling? Many RPGs attempt to duplicate the experience of the character in the player, so when they choose to adapt elements of fiction where hidden knowledge is important -- mysteries, surprises and betrayals -- the players have to keep information from each other to maintain the same sense of surprise.

    It's not actually anything new. This approach to gaming is very old and occurs in three forms:

    1. secret resources: stuff on your character sheet or in the DM's notes are hidden. This began when the hobby began.
    2. secret experiences: the GM pulls you aside and tells you what you see that the others don't, or passes you a note. Almost as old as secret resources; I remember note passing being mentioned in The Fantasy Trip.
    3. diffuse experiences: the group is large enough to split into subgroups so that everyone's experiences are potentially secret, as in a LARP.

    Now, I object to restricted knowledge on two grounds: personal taste and experimental interest. Although I've played in and enjoyed games that relied on secret resources (which works well in a gamist approach, I only tolerate the secret experiences approach if it's kept to a minimum, and the whole idea of diffuse experiences turns me off. I had this hammered home in the first Burning Wheel game I played; it was the rat-people demo with a group of about 19 people, all split off into small groups doing several things at once. I didn't find it very enjoyable at all.

    But in addition to personal taste, there's the matter of experimental interest. Those three techniques are all very old; I'm not interested in creating new games that explore re-using those techniques. I'm much more interested in games that allow full disclosure and separate the feelings of the character from the feelings of the player. Or, better yet, games that emualte surprise or sudden shifts of fortune in a different way, perhaps even using retcon techniques; instead of the team leader in that cyberpunk game knowing in advance who the real employer is and lying about it to the rest of the team, what if a particular game mechanic determines after the fact that the team leader was lying? Then everyone is surprised.

    Something Neel said is, I think, relevent here. Because of that, I'm just gonna pop it up full:
    The games I run and play in aren't stories. When we play, me make this gigantic tangled mass of narrative. There's too much stuff in them to be a story. We make stories out of them, by taking a particular point of view, and highlighting some bits of the mass as important, and sidelining other things.

    When you take a point of view to get a slice of the game, you get a story -- protagonists, antagonists, and supporting characters emerge. However, you can slice a game in multiple ways, and get multiple stories. And in each slice, who the protagonists are is different. All from the same play session.
    To that I'd add a concept from Roland Barthes (oh boy, here I go...), the difference between a "readerly" text and a "writerly" text. In a readerly text you get a story that tells itself, that is pretty simple and straightforward and easy to understand. It carries all its meanings inside it, and simply requires you to understand its words to understand its story. In a writerly text, otoh, you have to tease out the meaning of the story. You have to bring yourself to, and you have to wrestle with it in order to get meaning out of it. As the man said: "Writerly text is ourselves writing, before the infinite play of the world is traversed, intersected, stopped, plasticized by some singular system (Ideology, Genus, Criticism) which reduces the plurality of entrances, the opening of networks, the infinity of languages"

    In terms of games, readerly texts are games that make a coherent story at the end of which you know the story and can be done with it. Writerly texts are games that when you are done playing you can start figuring out what they were about and what they meant to you.

    A lot of our work recently in RPG design and play has been geared towards making stories and narratives, and of that the majority seems to be headed at a readerly perspective. We make stories that we all understand, that are communal to the group, and that carry their meaning complete and fully encoded within the game and the narrative we create together. Everyone knows what is going on, if not in all the details, than in the main, and the story makes sense to everyone as it stands. We go out of our way to make things make sense, tie up lose ends, and make sure that when we are done, the story is done.

    Now Tony, I could be wrong, but I think one of the things you're looking at doing would be a possible technique for making a more writerly game -- a game where we produce a text in play, and figure out what it means later. If there are all these different streams going on, of which we have only a limited knowledge about their full extent, then it keeps us from having a stable and defined meaning about what the story of the game is, what the game means, or even if our character is a/the protagonist or not.

    As a note, because of the way that games are constructed, it would seem to me that we have to add an extra layer to Barthe's schema. We create the game as writers, but we also experience the game as text. So "writerly" and "readerly" don't mean "how much narrative power you have durring the game to craft the story" they mean "how much work you have to do afterwards to understand the story." I'd posit that how much narrative authority you have in game actually has little to do with how much the game text ends up being writerly or readerly in this context.

    In so doing it would very certainly make a game that would require us to work, to search for meaning both durring and after play, and that could be a very cool thing.

Do you want to do the work as you create, after you create, both? Create the game, then create the text, then create meaning from the text? Texts? Get all fully post-modern and destroy the very idea of the game as a metanarrative and get back to the point where all we have is a collection of disperate voices that may or may not even be telling the same story?

      So, all this post-modern, non-linear-time hippy crap aside (at least for the length of this post) ... here's a rough sketch of a situation in which this disconnected play is powerfully better for a certain theme than 'round the table play.

      Among other stuff that happens in a game, players can place side-bets on the achievements, choices and actions of other characters. They spend resources that they got by playing with the GM (Victories in the classic sense) and write down (for instance) "Tamahome loves me: 5" on an index card. Then when the bet is resolved the card becomes worth that many of either Happy Points ("He loves me!") or Misery Points ("He loves me not ...") depending on the results.

      People can pre-spend on the assumption that they're going to get one result or another. Like, if Miaka is totally convinced that Tamahome doesn't love her (even without evidence) she can start spending her Misery points. If it turns out that she guessed (or was informed) wrongly than the amount that she spent is added to the opposite pool ... if she spent 4 Misery points, but it turns out he does love her then she gets the original bet (5) plus her overspend (4) for 9 Happy points.       And now, straight into potentially ... I dunno ... heresy. Or, worse, stupidity. But here's my thought. The rules system can, in many ways, tell you what's important in the game.       Now I think one of the ways of alleviating the "Alex doesn't like the story that Bob and Caroline want to tell" issue is to break up the idea of story elements that are globally important and make it make sense with the asynchronous type of gaming that we're talking about here. A story element could be, explicitly and measurably, important to Bob and Caroline, but not to Alex.
