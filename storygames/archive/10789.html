    \n I liked the Mist. The book and the movie.
    \n
    \nSeems like you could really capture the tension and conflict of the situation by using loaded questions that put people in conflict with each other.
    \n
    \nOne person:
    \n- is attracted to that person.
    \n- hates this person.
    \n- respects that person.
    \n- depends on this person.
    \n- knows what's really going on.
    \n- knows that they know what's really going on.
    \n- is an outsider, living an alternate lifestyle that others disapprove in.
    \n- thinks it is the end of the world and sinners must be sacrificed to appease the gods.
    \n- is afraid and will do whatever the forming mob says.
    \n- is normally an authority figure but feels powerless.
    \n- is afraid and has a gun.
    \n- wants to make a run for it.
    \n- wants to keep everyone in where it is safe.
    \n- refuses to believe what is happening no matter how much they see,
    \n- must protect their dependents at any cost.
    \n- has loved ones trapped outside and want to save them or make sure they are still alive.
    \n- is a believer who knows the end is here and what happens now doesn't matter compared to what waits for them after.
    \n- thinks we all live in sin with our splitting the atom, stem cells, and abortions and welcomes the end time.
    \n- is an atheist but faced with this insanity, maybe they were wrong.
    \n
    \nUse the questionnaires to seed the idea that these are normal people. Lawyers, preachers, cops, teachers, parents, kids, store owners, blue collar townies. And some of them have the potential to discover hidden strength in extraordinary circumstances while others could display hidden madness and ugliness.
    \n
    \nI would never fully show or over explain the monsters.
    \n
    \nPut on constant time pressure.
    \n
    \nKeep challenging the questions:
    \n- should we stay or should we go?
    \n- what should I do before I die?
    \n- what will I risk to save my loved ones?
    \n- if one of us endangers the rest, should I just stand here and watch?

    \n I think the questions are a huge part of Dread. I love those of the loaded variety.

    Some questions I did for the last game where the PC's were looking for a missing young man were:

    -Why does Sam O'Donnel, custodian at St. Mary's Asylum, know you by name?
    -Why do you secretly hope you never find Brian Burnham?
    -Why don't you trust The Private Eye (another PC)?

    Tailor the questions to your specific situation. Stuff like:

    -Why do you have trouble staying put?
    -What did Jimmy Benson do to make you want to rip his guts out?
    -Who do you have to live for?

    \n Damn, I thought you meant Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium (Dread: TFBOP, not Dread Jenga).

    Having only played DJ a few times, I can tell you this much: it's a very fucking mean game for folks with the shakes, and I've got a bit of them (minor nervous disorder plus hard living). As such, be prepared to make liberal use of the rule that lets someone who drops the tower stay in the game, only to die horribly at some later, surprising moment. They won't get to really do much, not being able to pull (i.e. they can only do things with their skills that need only the one, automatic pull) but they can stay in the narrative. And you can have extra fun with the Sword of Damocles hanging over their head, a la Final Destination.

    Games with elimination, in general, can be tricky. You might want a stable of NPCs that can be picked up by an eliminated player, if only to keep folks from wandering off after death and, thus, potentially being a distraction (or bored).

    Whatever you do, don't let anyone bring this thing!
