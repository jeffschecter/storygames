    I don't know much about advertising, but it seems like the telephone game doesn't really correspond to the nature of viral marketing.

    If I understand it correctly, viral marketing is fake-grassrooting. It's blogs creating by marketing types, instead of actual fans. But if done properly, it seems authentic enough.

    Here's what I would do: I'd just ask people in the room to talk about what they like. I'd point to people and ask them to describe the best movie they saw in 2006. I'd ask them why. Good acting, good plot, good special effects? Then I'd ask what other movies they'd compare the movie to. Then I'd ask everyone who hasn't seen it to put their hands up. Then ask them to keep their hands up if they'd go see it, based on that testimonial.

    The point being made: a movie web site lists only those reviews that praise the film. It's only natural. But some guy writing about the movie on his blog, or mentioning it in his podcast, is merely sharing his experience. You combine that with the referential nature of most amateur movie reviews ("It was like Blade, only with monsters instead of vampires, and it was funnier"), and the average person suddenly has a frame of reference, and also the relaxation of boundaries. I mean, some movie trailer tells you that the movie is great, well, what else are they going to say? You can't trust them. But some guy on his blog, telling you what he thinks -- well, you might not agree with him, but you don't question his agenda.

    I may be way off, here, but that's probably how I'd approach it. I'd give them a crash course in the efficacy of viral marketing by demonstrating how unpolished it appears, and how convincing it can be.

    -- Rafael

    I really like Rafael's suggestion. I'm not quite sure the telephone game does what you want: the message being passed always gets mangled, which perhaps isn't what you want to convey: "Use viral marketing! It'll confuse the message!".

    There's a few warm-up games that might work. There's the one where you give people a sheet to fill in: "Find one person whose birthday is in August. Find one person who likes foreign films." and so on. The point is that you need to talk to do it and the information spreads virally: "No, I don't like foreign films, but Jess does, go and talk to her."

    Oh, and the one thing you might add to Rafael's suggestion is to ask how people heard about the films. You can bet no-on will say "I saw an advert": they'll say things like "A friend told me it was good".

    Like a dumbass, I left my essay at the office. I'll post it up here on monday so you guys can download it. Basically, I broke the ice by asking, by show of hands, if anyone has seen a movie they really liked in the theater this year. Luckily, the most opinionated guy in the office had raised his hand so I asked him to describe the favorite movie he saw this year. I then asked everyone else who hadn't seen that movie if based on that description, they would recommend it to someone they knew. The CEO was like, "hell no!" other people were "sure" and others were just "meh, maybe." Then I said...

    "That's viral marketing."

    No technobabble, no BS. Just people talking to other people. I then went into a whole schpiel about how "viral" stuff isn't new. I defined viral marketing as:

    "A controlled message passed from one person to another. The message survives as long as it is interesting enough for an influential person to notice and easy enough for them to pass along."

    The rest of the presentation broke down a viral marketing campaign by giving it three characteristics:

    Fidelity: How accurately a message is passed from person to person. I used the game of telephone as an example, but didn't actually play it with the group. I described some techniques that can be used to maintain the fidelity of a campaign.

    Hotness: This is what makes the message noticable and memorable. I used catching a cold as an example.

    Immunity: This is all barriers that could make a message die, I segued this into the three characteristics of an influencer. That is, the person who will become the self-appointed champion of the message.

    Exposure: The influencer's media environment and all the messages they've learned to ignore. As an example, I asked the group how often they've actually investigated a car alarm going off or even thought of calling the police.

    Bias: Kind of a scary word, but a valuable one. These are the prejudices that grant an emotional core that can reinforce an otherwise cerebral message. The key question to ask here isn't what will make the influencer cares about (that's Hotness). The question is: what does the influencer want other people to care about?

    Access: This is whatever communication structure that the influencer prefers to use when talking to our target audience. It's not always the internet. For example, if we're targeting wealthy retirees, we should investigate country clubs and wine tastings as potential viral marketing opportunities.

    (Yes, I gave stats to viral marketing.)

    In closing the presentation, I tried to make all of this stuff relevant to our clients. We're in a city where street-level ambient ads aren't necessarily going to work as well as in a densely populated pedestrian environment like NYC. Our clients aren't selling burgers and movies, so Subservient Chicken and personalized Samuel L. Jackson phone calls aren't necessarily the best techniques either. I called those campaigns "water balloons." They had a broad splash and reached a lot of people all at once, but evaporated quickly. For our clients, a "laser" approach would be more suitable: Focused, well-refined and persistent in the long-term.

    I also had illustrations and used "peanut butter and jelly" throughout the presentation as an example of a fictional viral marketing campaign.

    Unfortunately, the presentation was too good. Now I've been tapped to speak at another event, in front of strangers, later next month. Eek.
