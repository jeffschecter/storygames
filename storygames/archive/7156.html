\n
\n
\n
\n\nKevin Allen Jr \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2008 \n edited July 2008 in Directed Promotion
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In the past few years we \u2013as a community\u2013 have born witness to incredible innovations in the field of collaborative storytelling. The indie RPG movement ushered in a new generation of game designers with unique goals and motivations. These designers have pushed the boundaries of the storytelling medium, finding innovative ways to present collaborative fiction to new audiences.

It\u2019s time the boundaries were pushed even further.

It is with great pleasure that we announce Design Matters, a partnership amongst 6 game designers exploring new methods of marketing and sales. Design Matters is: Clint Krause, Gregor Hutton, Jonathan Walton, Nathan Paoletta, Epidah Ravochal, and Kevin Allen Jr.

Design Matters\u2019 focus is laser sharp. We exists only at and for Gen Con. If you aren\u2019t going to be in Indianapolis this year you will still be able to get a hold of our products, through the same means you always have; but if you are at the convention we hope you stop by and see what we\u2019re offering up. Design Matters has a simple, if lofty goal: to re-imagine the way games and gamers find each other. We aim to attract the person for whom role-playing is just one of many interests in their spectrum of entertainment. While our games are certainly accessible to the hardcore role-player, the array of games presented at our booth appeals to those who may not identify strongly with roleplaying, but who are interested in playing a low-social-commitment game with friends who share their interest in a specific genre or kind of game.

We invite you to participate in this brave new sales experience at Gen Con 2008, booth 1940. Come check us out and discover that yes, in fact, Design Matters.

For more info, come visit www.booth1940.com. Be sure to check back for many more updates in the next few days.

Please, feel free to use this thread for any questions or comments you may have.

    \n Yeh. While we are totally excited and happy to have y'all come by and check out our games (especially new releases - where else can you take a look at Annalise, Empire of Dust, Sweet Agatha, and 3:16 in print?), the main goal of the booth is to be a place where people who play any kind of game can find a storygame/RPG/fiction game that (a) fits into their existing life schedule and (b) is focused on some genre or theme that they're into, but didn't know there was an existing game for it.     \nNo problem, Keith. And I think this quote from you is really key, actually.

    I, personally, am pretty satisfied that our products are a good fit together, at roughly the same price point, and offer a range of diverse play and content that customers coming to the booth will find something that they like.

    I'm also really confident that my investment of buying into the booth will be covered by a share of the booth sales, since I believe that there is a market for our products (in some general sense). Now, I can't tell which particular books will sell well or not-so-well. But we are sharing the risk of buying the booth and we'll share the reward of any sales. The customers will determine what they like, but as designers we're saying we wouldn't be here without each other as a community.

    I think it is an attempt to be openly and honestly mutualistic in supporting each other's books, without the distraction of selling my book over someone else's (or vice versa) or trying to demo or promote one thing over another.

    I also think we'll be less about hard sell at the booth given our attitudes and personalities. Kevin and Nate have done a great job of organising the booth and I'm happy to give it a go and see if we can connect with the public and share our games with them.

    Some people have said they didn't like the "try a demo" culture (I'm thinking of Neil Gow's bad experience with it at last year's GenCon) and the loud selling of other more traditional booths. Well, here's a booth that's different, does the public actually want to engage with it? We can only offer them it and see if they do.

    \n I would like to offer an apology.
    \n
    \nIt's very obvious that I stepped on some toes with my first post in this thread. Unfortunately, I was very tired when I wrote that, missed the point of the opening post and didn't express my point very well.
    \n
    \nPlease accept my apologies if my post upset you. I wasn't trying to imply that anyone's products in booth 1940 were crappy or that anyone participating in the booth is anything less than a good person.
    \n
    \nWhen I read it in my weary state, what I got out of it was, "Wer'e sharing a booth at GenCon and this is a new and wonderful thing in marketing!" This upset me because banding together for a booth really isn't a new thing, and I really don't consider profit-sharing to be anything that is going to help with marketing.
    \n
    \nHowever, now that I have looked the post over a few times, I finally got what is actually great about this effort. Its a tough mission they've decided to take up and I truly believe that it's going to take more than a single GenCon to pull it off.
    \n
    \nGood Move #1: "We aim to attract the person for whom role-playing is just one of many interests in their spectrum of entertainment." I've found that most gamers usually have a wide number of interests, but most game publishers keep targeting the same audience, slicing up the same pie into smaller and smaller slices. They rarely try to bring new people into the hobby and thereby refresh the hobby. This tells me that they will hopefully reach for the untapped audiences.
    \n
    \nGood Move #2: ". . . who may not identify strongly with roleplaying, but who are interested in playing a low-social-commitment game with friends. . . " This reinforces my above assumption, and tells me that the intent is to gain the interest of those that don't want to spend hours and hours playing an RPG each week. The public view seems to be that an RPG requires big, thick books and hours of prep in order to play. With luck, this view will change over time.
    \n
    \nSo, to the six of you in booth 1940, I wish you luck and I will be sure to drop by to see what you are doing to accomplish this.
