\n Hello,
\n
\nI am a rather neophyte game designer, and am working on a game with the working title "The Waste".
\n
\nThe Waste is a post-apocalyptic, party-based (though solo play is possible) horror/survival RPG. PCs would encounter mutant raiders, strange monsters, and crazed wastelanders as they each strive to maintain their Hope and Humanity.
\n
\nThe setting is still very much in-development, and nothing is really set in stone yet. Thematically, my main influences are Fallout (the video game) + Rustbelt + Don't Rest Your Head, with some of the mechanics more informed by FATEs Aspects, and John Harper's Lady Blackbird (I use a modified Trait mechanic, but we'll get to that some other time).
\n
\nI wanted to post the skeleton of my resolution system here to see if I can gain some insights as to how it might shake out, primarily, the interactions between Hope, Humanity, Pain and Stress.
\n
\nHere's a few terms, and roughly what they mean:
\n
\nHope- this is represented by a number, starting at 10. There should also be a sentence, which is essentially a character goal. The PCs' Hope is the reason that they continue to press onward and travel the Waste. Without Hope, man becomes a Slave; he can fight the Waste no longer.
\n
\nHumanity- this is also represented by a number, probably a range between 1 and 20. Without Humanity, man becomes a Monster.
\n
\nStress- this is the GM's dice pool that he rolls against the PC's whenever there is a conflict. The number of dice is chosen based on both the difficulty, and relative importance of the task being undertaken.
\n
\nPain- this is a dice pool that the PC rolls against himself. Pain dice are earned and kept when a PC fails a conflict roll, and elects to 'Take the Pain'. You'll see how this works below.
\n========
\nCONFLICT RESOLUTION:
\n
\n1. Declare Intent - the Player announces a task for his character, like "I want to kill those 3 bandits", or "I want to hotwire this old Buick". The GM will then announce his own Intent for the scene, like "The bandits want to capture you", or "I want the Buick to backfire, and blow its engine".
\n
\n2. Gather dice - the Player gathers a dice pool based on the Lifepaths and Aspects on his character sheet. This pool will be between 1 and 10 dice. The GM will gather an amount of stress dice determined by the relative difficulty, also between 1 (very easy, or not relatively important) and 10 (very difficult of pivotal to achieve the character's Hope)
\n
\n3. Dice are rolled and compared. The highest die on each side are compared to one another, with person who has the higher roll succeeding.
\n
\n4. If there is a tie, compare to the next highest die. If one person runs out of dice to compare, the person with dice remaining wins the conflict.
\n
\n5. The degree of success is the difference between the two dice that are compared to one another. If one person runs out of dice to compare (due to ties), then they effectively have rolled a 0.
\n
\n6. If the player succeeds on the roll, their Intent happens. If the GM wins the roll, then the GM's Intent happens, unless the player decides to Take the Pain. The player can elect to take an amount of Pain dice equal to the degree of failure on the conflict roll. If he does this, his original Intent for the conflict succeeds (instead of the GM), but with some type of complication with severity equal to the amount of Pain dice he just took.
\n
\nNow, what are Pain dice used for?
\n
\nIn future conflicts, the GM will roll Stress dice against the player as normal. The player will roll two separate pools: one from his Aspects and Lifepaths (as above), and a set of different color dice for Pain. Pain will be counted with the GM's Stress in order to determine who succeeds on their Intent.

    \n When Pain is taken by a player, he writes down how it comes about. Here are a few examples, not all of which occured in physical combats:
    \n
    \n-Took a shotgun blast to the hand (2)
    \n-Bitten in the hand by a giant rat (1)
    \n-Thought I saw my brother's ghost (3)
    \n-Angry about being conned by a card shark (2)
    \n
    \nPain sticks around, until the player alleviates it in the game. Pain can be alleviated in some cases by doing things within the fiction, like finding healing (and making a roll against the those Pain dice) or tracking down the card shark that duped you. Things like that.
    \n
    \nOR players can elect to spend Hope, in order to alleviate Pain on a 1-for-1 basis.
    \n
    \nA PC can re-gain Hope points by pursuing whatever it is the listed in their Hope description. Hope awards should be sporadic and only given for successfully progressing their character's story. Hope should also be given for a PC helping another PC with their Hope. I am still working on more specifics here.
    \n
    \nIf Hope ever goes down to 0, the PC kills himself at the first appropriate opportunity. The player should have narrative authority over this.
    \n
    \n
    \nHumanity- At any time, Humanity can be sacrificed to aid in any conflict roll, even increasing the PC's dice pool above 10. Humanity is sacrificed on a 1 point for 1 die basis. However, this sacrifice should symbolize the character giving in to more base, primal instincts. If there is an especially brutal, malicious, or violent solution to the conflict, then the player must take it, to a degree based on the amount of Humanity he is sacrificing.
    \n
    \nAnother way to lose Humanity- Whenever a time arises when a character's Pain exceeds his Hope, make an opposed Pain vs Hope roll. If Pain wins, the difference is subtracted from Humanity. If Hope wins, nothing happens. If Hope is 0, then the roll is unopposed.
    \n
    \nIf Humanity reaches 0, the PC becomes a Monster, and is unplayable.
    \n
    \nPC can regain Humanity by showing Compassion for others, saving lives, and generally going out of their way to improve the lives of others in the Wastes.
    \n
    \n===================
    \n
    \nAll right, time for my thoughts and questions.
    \n
    \nThe intent behind this system is to facilitate the telling of very tragic, grim tales of survival and the moral choices involved. One thing of note is that there is no possible way for the GM to directly kill the PC without the player's go ahead.
    \n
    \nThe player can:
    \n1. elect to spend all of his Hope to commit suicide,
    \n2. keep going until all Humanity is lost and the PC becomes a Monster,
    \n3. fail a roll when the GM's Intent is to kill the character and elect not to Take the Pain
    \n
    \nNot exactly cheerful ends, but I personally like the idea of giving the player some level of control over the PC's demise, should it happen. I would like to hear people's thoughts about these options, and anyway to make them perhaps a bit more interesting.
    \n
    \nAnother thing that I will really have to get through playtesting is how many opportunities there should be to regain Hope and Humanity in order to allow for an engaging, scary, and dangerous game that isn't a hopeless downward spiral for the PC's (unless that is the group's goal).
    \n
    \nIdeally, stories should involve very strong moral choices where the PC's Hope will be at odds with their Humanity. I might even specify that Hope should be something that might even be a little selfish to ensure that this type of conflict occurs.
    \n
    \nOh, and 'Monster' is still ill-defined, but I kinda like it that way.
    \n
    \nQuestions:
    \n
    \nIs the above interesting to you?
    \n
    \nAre there any holes or things I seem to be overlooking?
    \n
    \nIs the currency between Stress, Pain, Hope, and Humanity clear?
    \n
    \nMost importantly: Are there other questions I should really be asking here?

    \n a) S-G is not the best place for game design help.

    b) Have you read or played Sorcerer? Because you have, in large part, outlined broad swaths of how Sorcerer plays. If you are aware of the similarities, what differentiates your game from this much more established game? What does your game do or say that Sorcerer can't?

    c) Lots of designers have tried to shy away from the word 'stakes' ever since somebody said something disparaging about stakes. If you're designing something with stakes, call them stakes. It does you no good, and in fact detracts from the plain-english utility of your game, to call them something else just because 'stakes' is presently unfashionable.

    d) Right now, you have three giant inputs that are up to GM discretion (which is a polite way of saying fiat) \u2014 determining Stress, affording opportunities for Hope refresh, and affording opportunities for Humanity refresh. This means that the game set up allows GMs to beat the living shit out of players by setting high difficulties that sap their currency and the denying them the opportunity to regain that currency. If that's the sort of social set up you're going for, great! Spot-on target. If you're not looking for a game where the GM clobbers the players with the story-stick until they give up, then you might want to consider giving authority of currency-refreshes to the players \u2014 so you decide when you refresh, or (better) you decide when other PCs refresh.

    e) I rather like your context-defined damage-alternative Pain. Do I always roll all of my Pain, or only the Pain that's relevant to the thing that I'm doing? If the latter, who decides what's relevant and what's not? Does the GM determine when I get to erase a particular Pain? Can I make 'heal up from that shotgun blast' part of my stakes in a conflict?

    \nI ask because games about "survival" have to be very careful about how they set up player incentives. The easiest way to survive, after all, is to risk nothing - and therefore be boring as all hell. This then drives the GM (or whatever source of opposition) to push harder on whatever they can, driving the players to turtle further, and so on.

    Your game has to give players - not characters, players - something they want enough that they're willing to go out on a limb. This is particularly important in post-apoc games where just about anything constitutes "a limb."

    \n Hi Charlie,

    Yes, Rustbelt does heavily stress Hope. But there's no Hope stat. Nor is there a Humanity stat. Both of which are intentional.

    There's no Hope stat because it's kind of a given. The very fact that the character is still alive indicates a certain degree of hope. Beyond that, every time he sticks his neck out for someone else, that indicates even more hope. You don't behave like that without hope.

    Why there's no Humanity stat is a little harder to explain. Playing the game is sort of a discussion of what Humanity is in the first place.

    But the reason for the absence of both of these is, you are what you do. The characters are defined through their actions and decisions. These have consequences, which is what the Price and the Psyche mechanics are all about, and sooner or later the cracks start to show somewhere. But on the level of moral judgment, it's all subjective and up to the "viewer," as it were. Different players can (and have) pass different judgments on the same character's morality. Quantifying this sort of thing with a number would defeat that purpose. Saying somebody has Humanity 0 means that we all agree that he's inhuman.

    (I didn't want that in the Rustbelt, but you might want it in your game.)

    And, yes, the Rustbelt system is very much on the crunchy and nuanced side. I like a complex, nuanced instrument to work with; others don't.

    If you want it, the ashcan is still available at the Unstore here. (The first edition should be out by GenCon 2010.)

    EDIT: Oh yeah, and, yes, the old magic rules were cut. The new ones are much better and even creepier. They're also in a section headed "Rules for Eldritch Shit," which makes me smile.

    \nHumanity- this is also represented by a number, probably a range between 1 and 20. Without Humanity, man becomes a Monster.
    \n
    \n
    \nIs there a compelling need to make these stats?
    \n
    \nMy inclination is that if there is a sufficiently interesting fictional payoff for the players that they will (as players and while playing their characters) exhibit hope that that that payoff is achievable in a way far more interesting than playing to a stat.
    \n
    \nSimilarly if the payoff is compelling enough that the hope of achieving it comsumes them (both as player and while playing their characters) they will exhibit their humanity, or their inhumanity in what they are willing to do to get it.
    \n
    \nIs there something you can set up in the game that will serve as this fictional payoff so you don't have to use stats to represent hope and humanity.
    \n
    \n
    Now, what are Pain dice used for?
    \n
    \nIn future conflicts, the GM will roll Stress dice against the player as normal. The player will roll two separate pools: one from his Aspects and Lifepaths (as above), and a set of different color dice for Pain. Pain will be counted with the GM's Stress in order to determine who succeeds on their Intent.
    \n
    \n
    \nThis strikes me as selling Pain short. I'd like to see it do more than increase the difficulty.
    \n
    \nHere's an idea freshly spawned from my brain.
    \n
    \n1) the player states their Intent / sets stakes / whatever...but the GM does NOT. The GM's stakes is simply automatically "no that didn't happen".
    \n
    \n2) Pain dice are rolled by the player and help the player win. But, if the player wins with a Pain die the result becomes a "yes, and...here's how things just got worst" result. So the player gets their stakes...but the GM then gets to throw an additional complication, condition, twist, whatever on top.
    \n
    \nThat would be a much more interesting death spiral to me:
    \n
    \nCurrent Death Spiral: as Pain increases...failure increases...
    \n
    \nSuggested Death Spiral: as Pain increases...success gets more and more complicated. Pain (game stat) begets pain (fictional state)
