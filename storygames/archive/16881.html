\n Hey Story Gamers! I am assuming you're all Americans who care personally about religion. Or who are atheists and pedophiles, you know, because these things go together. Or maybe you're Europeans who are REALLY godless.

Now that we've got everyone on board with that respectful intro: Have you played any games incorporating Jesus? I think there are a lot of interesting ways to approach Jesus in a gaming context - one way would be, as in "Heads of State", to make a series of mini-games highlighting very different aspects of the whole phenomenon. For instance:

- A short story-telling game where the disciples related the same episode through different eyes
- A debating game where different religious authorities tried to shape the biography of the Saviour
- A game about personal choices for Jesus - who do you save? how do you relate to your mortality?
- A game about the Second Coming, where Jesus arrives in our world and things happen from there
- A game about a historical, non-magical Jesus who is the leader of a guerrilla tribe somewhere in the Middle East thousands of years ago

    \n We played Toga Toga Toga, a scenario for Sci Fi Beta Kappa, which is a science-fiction frat house comedy game.

    At the beginning of the scenario, the GM was trying to steer us towards organising a toga party. We deliberately misinterpreted his hints ("Hey, guys, who are those guys who were around thousands of years ago?") and organised a Jewish-themed party instead.

    Then we decided that, to make our party the best party ever, we needed the most famous Jewish man ever. That, we decided, was Jesus, and so we transported him to the party in a time machine. The highlight of the party was when he danced into the middle of the floor and flirted with the Dean.

    It later emerged that the GM was a Christian and had found all this rather difficult. (We genuinely felt bad about this.)

    \nThe Last Supper is a really interesting one-session LARP/potluck dinner in which the various apostles generate the answers to the nature of the faith through discussion with each other and an inscrutable Jesus. All have secrets and agendas that influence how they approach these issues and one will betray Jesus. As the game progresses these beliefs are recorded and vignettes are told of the consequences and schisms these cause centuries later and the tasks and roles each apostle takes creates the shape of the church.

    To the best of my knowledge there has only been one actual game of it played but it was very interesting and influential on my thoughts about gaming. We had a range of people from various Christian denominations (some particularly observant, some not), atheists, agnostics, and pagans. To the best of my knowledge none of the Christians found the endevour offensive though I imagine that any Biblical Literalist would be deeply upset by the premise that the Bible and faith that exist are primarily shaped by the disputes and beliefs of the Apostles and not by Jesus or the possiblity that it could have had a different outcome.
