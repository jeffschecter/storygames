    \n What's so hard about it using cards? I really think you have a bug up your ass about this, and if you could put your wariness of that aside you'd find that actually the game is very easy to play.

    The game system is not about playing poker. Let me repeat,we are not playing poker during the game and you should put all notions of this aside.

    I really think you threw up a barrier about the system and didn't want to engage with it at all. Personally, I think this is stupid and you should just get on with it and play the game.

    You say that doing what other people recommend is not playing and I agree. But, Shevy, I think you have this weird notion that there is always a "right" thing to do or something? Or that there is some "winning" choice that can be found a priori and applied to any given situation. That is not the case. At each point of the system there are choices and you as a player have to make them on behalf of your character.

    As best, and as simply, as I can here is how Mob Justice works.

    (i) We say fictional stuff between ourselves and the GM.
    (ii) At some point someone disagrees and, if they are suitably interested in having their way, they stick a story chip in the middle of the table. This chip is being risked by the player or the GM, in the hope that they get their way and story chips back if they win,
    (iii) The player who stuck the story chip in gets some cards based on their skills and what they are doing. They then make a "poker hand". The other players don't get to see the hand yet but they have seen how many cards it was formed from.
    (iv) Everyone else then has the option to get involved in the conflict. Whether they actually do so is their choice. If no one chooses to then the original player gets their story chip back and gets their way.
    (v) If people want to get involved they stick a story chip in, which they will lose to someone else if they don't win. In return they get cards and the chance to get their goal. Again other players don't get to see the cards themselves but they do get to see the number of cards it was formed from.
    (vi) Play then proceeds between the interested parties in turn. They have the choice to
    -fold, i.e. give up and cut their losses, where they bow out of the conflict
    -call, saying "I've invested enough story chips, let's see if I win"
    -raise, make the other players put in more story-chips to stay in the conflict (this could be an attempt to "buy" a win, or to extract more story chips from other players if they are confident of their hand)
    (vii) The cards are shown and someone wins, they get their goal and to "set" what happened. They also collect all the story chips risked in the middle.

    Why you would want to fold, raise or call is something that you should only judge at the moment you have to make that choice in game. How good is your hand? How badly do you want to achieve your goal? How many chips do you have to risk? Is it worth the risk? Can you gain story chips out of this conflict? And so on. The answer will be different for each and every conflict.

    You say "I'm sure the system works really well when you know what you're trying to do, but you have to factor in that I know nothing about Mob Justice." That just blows my mind. As a player you say to the other players "Hey, I want my character to do this in the game. How do I do it?" And we say, "Well, if you want to do that then you do (a). You can also do (b) or (c), but you want to do (a)." Then you throw your hands up and say "doing what other people recommend is not playing."

    Really, I played AD&D in the early 1990s. I did not have a clue what I could do or not do, but I asked my other players and the GM. "Hey, I want to do this" and they said "Oh, in that case roll this and you want to get 15 or better" or "Cast that spell". So, I did. I just got on with playing.

    I don't know if this helps any, but needlessly worrying about the system is getting in the way of you having fun.
