Footprint vs. Pacing

TonyLB
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
Okay, people seem unable or unwilling to stop conflating two things, so I'm going to be very, very clear. It probably won't work, but it will let me feel justified when I wash my hands of any obligation to explain again.

Pacing is how much story, conflict, immersion, octopus-wrestling or whatever you pack into a particular unit of time. If you're going to meet in a tavern, journey through a random encounter, attack the overlord's castle and spend your loot on ale and whores, all in the course of four sessions of four hours each, that's your pacing. If you're going to do all that same stuff in one four-hour session then you've sped your pacing by a factor of four. If you're going to do all that same stuff in a one-hour session then you've sped your pacing by another factor of four.

Footprint is what size chunks of time your game is self-contained and complete within. If you're going to spend four hours doing all of the stuff above, and that's going to be your self-contained unit-o'-game, then your footprint is four hours. If you're going to spend four hours just chatting in the tavern, and maybe eventually thinking about going outside, and that's going to be your self-contained unit-o'-game, then your footprint is four hours.

Footprint and pacing? Completely independent. They have, quite literally, nothing to do with each other, unless you add some other assumptions into your mix.

Here's an assumption: "I can't have a self-contained unit-o'-gaming unless it tells that whole story, from tavern to dungeon and back to tavern again." If you make that assumption then, yes, saying that your footprint is one hour will have implications on pacing.

But, of course, that assumption is absolute bunk. So don't make it.

Are we good now?

    Just to riff on the metaphor...

    A footprint is the size and shape of a single "step", the smallest *whole* unit of discrete time that it takes to play "a complete session" of a game. When talking about multiple sessions, you're talking about multiple steps, each leaving its own approximately same-sized footprint behind in the sand.

    Footprints can be small (i.e., short) or big (i.e., long), and they can be wide (i.e., permitting little other social activity in concurrence) or narrow (i.e., cohabiting the same length of time with other social activities).

    Thus, Jungle Speed, which requires utter concentration and not much social byplay while it's being played, has a short, but quite wide, footprint.

    Roleplaying games tend to have longer footprints, but they can run the gamut from narrow (lots of out of character, potentially off-topic chatter) to wide (a strong focus on staying in-character and focused on the details of the game).

    I think footprints the way you're talking about them are operating at two levels.

    I think Tony's level is "In the immediate, for the day I have planned ahead, if someone says, hey, we're gonna play X, how much time is that going to take me" (and with my embellishment added in) "and is that all we'll be doing while we do it?"

    I think *your* level is "For scheduling and planning purposes, for the next month/season/year of activities in my life, how many days and how much of each day is that going to take me?"

    While I totally agree that both are important to the landscape of gaming, they're both trying to address very different things, in terms of the evaluation of time investment.

    The former answers the question, "given X hours, is it even possible to do Y".

    The latter answers the question, "given the long-term commitment represented by X, can I successfully incorporate this into my schedule?"

    IMO.
