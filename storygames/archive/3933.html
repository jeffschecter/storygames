\n This has to be the worst time in the world to start a new topic, with everyone flying to Gen Con, but still. I've been reading Ryan Dancey's series of posts on his blog, about the future of the hobby, and his ideas impressed me a lot. It seems like Mr. Dancey thinks Story Games are the future of the hobby!\n
The goal of most of the people in the hobby is not \xe2\u20ac\u0153play a role\xe2\u20ac\x9d. The goal of the hobby community is \xe2\u20ac\u0153tell a great story\xe2\u20ac\x9d. Roleplaying is a tactic, not a strategy. Some participants want to play roles, and that\xe2\u20ac\u2122s fine. Others want to provide narrative structure. Still others want to create systems for interaction and adjudication. And another group wants to generate environments. All of these people need to be made co-equal for the hobby to succeed long term.
\nThe posts so far are here:\n
  1. Climb The Highest Mountain
  2. Keep Hope Alive!
  3. Step 1: Redefine The Hobby
  4. Step 2: Redefine the Experience

    \n What I don't understand in the article is the obsession with "saving" the hobby. Saving it from what? He talks about the growth of the popularity of MMORPGs as this great threat, stealing players from the hobby. What's his beef? People do what they think is fun. MMORPGs are more popular because for the people who play them, they're more fun. Does he want to stop people from having fun?

    He's got this grand vision of ressurecting the hobby to the "glory days" when D&D was more popular than Jesus. Why? What's so special about these games that makes them somehow more important that MMORPGs, or TCGs, or whatever? If people are having more fun playing games on their computers, then let them. It's like he's anti-fun, or something.

    I think trying to "revitalise" the hobby is a red herring. From my perspective, the hobby is plenty vital. My problem finding people to play with evaporated as soon as I stopped defining people as "gamers" and "non-gamers". Everyone loves playing games, everyone loves telling stories. The secret is finding games that let people tell the stories they like, in the ways they want to. Everyone's a gamer just waiting for the perfect game for them.

    I think Ryan's right that there's a perceptual problem with roleplaying games that makes people balk at getting into them. But I think that problem is shared by most gamers. We think of our hobby as this unique, cultish activity, where recruits must be initiated and indoctrinated before they can be trusted to play. That's simply not the case. We've got to stop thinking of our hobby as this unique snowflake that must be protected, and start thinking of it as just another way to have fun. Everyone likes fun (except Ryan Dancy).

    I think he has a good point, though, in that we should be trying to distinguish ourselves from video games. If for no other reason than for good business. As he points out, the collaborative, emergent, "story" elements are the strong points for the RPG/Story-game media. We should emphasize that.

      \n I think its hard not to disagree with Ryan's assertion that MMORPG's are beating the tabletop for some people, in terms of what they want from an rpg experience. Simply put, what some people wanted out of their weekly D&D session they now get better from a MMORPG. I'd say that's mostly the people who liked the whole game aspect of D&D, but were not into the story side.

      The story side, and particularly the ability of the player to drive the story as much as the GM is the hardest part for the MMORPG authors to emulate, because that requires real-time interaction between two people. And that's a damn expensive service to provide.

      I'd say that Ryan's post is far more about how D&D survives than about story games as we know and love them. Looking at the D&D Insider announcment at the same time, it would seem that Ryan is pitching that focusing on shared story creation as a differentiator is how he sees tabletop gaming (and D&D in particular) surviving. What is D&D insider if not an attempt to mix the convenience of on-line play for many with the shared story-telling of the group. I'm not commenting on how successful this will be, but I would suspect this is the strategy being outlined.

      WoTC solves the problem of providing the cost-effective interaction that differentiates a TRPG from a MMORPG by getting the fee-paying public to sign up to run the game as well as to play it. It really is some sort of hybrid model.

      Story games as we care about them are probably only of interest to Ryan in that they reject much of what can be virtualized in a MMORPG in favour of shared narrative. As such they highlight what differentiates TRPG from MMORPG.

      I think what we should take from it is the warm feeling that others have realized what we have that story games capture the essence of what is vibrant and important about the TRPG experience.
