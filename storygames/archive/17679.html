\n I'm working on a game tentatively called TerrorForm, and I plan on using Apocalypse World to power the game. The basic premise is that the Earth is beyond hope and humanity (for reasons that each group will establish in-play) has decided to leave the planet for orbital stations to terraform it. The players will be those left behind. I want the game to play across three Phases, each Phase representing a different stage of the process. Abandonment (we've been lift behind, oh crap what do we do), Regenesis (the machine and bio-goo are coming, how do we survive?), and Homecoming (invaders from the sky! We must protect our home). The playbooks will evolve across each Phase. The Doctor becomes the Patcher becomes the Medicine Man.

Here's my current issue (now that the intro is done). I want to come up with new stats to use. Hot, Cool, Sharp, Hard, and Weird are great, but they're definitely AW stats. I want to have 4-5 stats that reflect the setting I outlined above and function to describe the characters like the AW stats. I've been thinking of using these:

Controlled, Wild, Connected, Removed, and Mournful. I'm worried about how I've got them set up like dichotomies right now. Having a high Hot doesn't mean you're not also Cool. Controlled and Wild seem to be too opposite of one another, though. Wild and Mournful are nice and punchy, but the others seem to be lacking something.

Do you have any thoughts on this? If you need more clarification on something, please feel free to ask. I'm just starting this process.

    \n The thing about AW stats is that they convey different aspects of the character's personality, with a special focus on the personality traits that most support ass kickery in the particular kind of post-apocalyptic fiction that AW represents. One way you might approach this is to ask yourself, what genre are you going for, and what fictional characters can you see the PCs emulating? And then asking, what makes these specific people interesting in this genre?

    In Apocalypse World, the characters are getting into tense situations, defying death, seizing things by force, manipulating each other, and so on. They succeed at these things because they're cool as ice cold, they're hard like boiled leather, they're hot as hell, they're just that much sharper than the other guy, or because they're just straight up unhinged bat shit nuts weird. To rephrase @Paul_T's point just a little bit, the moves in AW are all about what the characters do, but the stats are all about what the characters are, what specifically makes them so bad of ass.

    So, what you might do is, yeah, do what @Johnstone said: decide on the basic moves. What do the characters do in this game? Like, what are the things they're all generally going to want to be able to try? And then, for each move, say to yourself, what makes a person ready, willing, and able to actually pull this off? What personal characteristics are conducive to it? For example, in AW, all of the characters are going to be in situations where it's tempting to try to seize something by force, but the ones who are going to be most good at it (and therefore most inclined to it) are the ones who are HARD, the ones with hearts of boiled leather and brains like a muscle taut and ready to pounce. And everyone's going to want to get neck deep in the psychic maelstrom but the ones who are not going to get swept away are the skin-crawling freak job "We Have Always Lived In The Castle" creeps who make their home there.

    Anyway, the point is, the thing that makes AW so baller is the harmony between genre and what appears on the character sheet: genre is the most important (or at least the most abstract and "general") consideration, followed by who the characters are and what they do, followed by the specific personal characteristics that make them so bad ass in this genre. You could do worse than to approach your problem from this angle, I think! Hope this helps!

    \n In the spirit of giving you even more to think about, you might ask yourself, does your game need stats at all? I mean, in AW the stats convey quite a bit about the setting and its expectations, but they also affect the frequency with which the protagonists succeed at things they want to do. So they do double duty: they communicate why these heroes are bad asses, and they ensure that the heroes are bad asses more often than not.

    An unmodified 2d6 gives you a hit 21/36 of the time, or 58.3%. It's a strong hit 6/36 of the time, or 16.7%. That means, even rolling +0, you're more likely than not to succeed to some extent, and your chance of attaining the best result is not negligible. For some genres, you might not necessarily WANT the characters to succeed more often than that. For example, if you were making a Gothic horror game, you might want bonuses to rolls to be few and far between, and to stem from the PC's actions (helping, getting situational modifiers and the like) rather than being inherent to them. This might be something you want try out, just as an experiment, to see if it feels right.

    By contrast, a +1 modifier to 2d6 gives you a hit of any kind 25/36 of the time, or 69.4%. It's a strong hit 10/36 of the time, or 27.8%. That's a pretty considerable improvement. By the time you get to +2, you're getting a hit 30/36 of the time (83.3%), and fully half of those are strong hits. +3 means you're getting strong hits more often than any other result. That's fully appropriate for AW, and it's arguably appropriate for games like Dungeon World and Sagas of the Icelanders (because of the genres they're modelling), and even Monster Hearts (because of what the moves are for that game --- things that high school students in high school drama situations should be good at), but might not be right for all games, depending on the basic moves you have in mind.

    In other words, you should think about whether you need permanent standing bonuses to the basic moves at all, based on your expectations of how often the characters should succeed at the things they do without immediate complications or fallout arising from their own actions. There might be other ways to convey the personal characteristics of the characters on the sheet, other than stats.

    You might experiment with something like the following. In AW, each splat has one high ability score that makes them good at basic moves that use that score. Instead of doing that, you might make your splats so they say something like, "When you use [this or that basic move], roll three dice and keep the highest two to determine your result." Someone mentioned it in another thread as an option to replace flat bonuses. This is interesting because it does improve the odds of getting a hit, but it's biased toward soft hits. Now you have a hit 174/216 of the time, or 80.6%, and it's a strong hit 77/216 of the time, or 35.6%. That's almost as good as +2 in terms of getting a hit at all, but not quite as good in terms of getting strong hits.

    \n My suggestion is to read and play as many hacks as you can, and pick some moves that do sorta what you want and hack them until you have a few core moves that cover most of the basics of play (this could be 1 move or 6 moves, but probably not more than 6-8, because that's too many to keep track of, unless you've broken them down into categories). Then you play the game and see which moves people actually use and if the moves work in play like you want them too.

    I was working on a "Terrorform" thing for AW a while back, but never ended up with something in a format that I liked.
