On a thread on nerdnyc, Forager mentioned:
Here's my problem with D&D at low levels: the trappings of the game - the artwork, the color text, etc. - promises a game of heroic fantasy adventure. But playing D&D at low levels is more about building up enough power so that you can engage in heroic fantasy adventure. Chris Chinn (bankuei) has made the observation that on the cover of the red box D&D basic set is a picture of a warrior confronting a dragon. But the game inside doesn't give you the tools to make that picture "come alive".

That unlocked an ocean of memories! The first time I played a published RPG was in sophomore year in high school in the cartooning club. The GM was our art teacher. He gave me a bunch of fiction to read, I was amazed by the art and he kept saying think Lord of the Rings. They asked me to come up with a character idea.

"I am a young but promising magician whose town was murdered by a dragon. I threw spell after spell at it but my magic was of no help. And I had never used a sword before. But as the dragon tried to swallow me whole, I got lucky and plunged the sword into his eye and killed it. I now wear its skin with the names of my dead family written on it as a symbol of the day I became a man."

The GM looked at me and said ummm... you can't have a sword if you are a magic user and what you described would never happen. You would have never killed a dragon and you can't have dragon scale armor. And you can't fling spell after spell at the dragon. You only get 1 spell. So we will say a dragon killed your town and you ran away and swore revenge. But we're first level so it will be probably a year or so before you're ever even ready to face even a baby dragon.

I played the game. Out of 2-3 hours of playing, I only had fun 2 times. 1 time I cast a magic missile which was and is still more fun than it has any right to be. The second I used caltrops hidden in greek fire to stop a bunch of people from chasing us. After that, I stopped playing. I've never played war games. And I hated that aspect of the game. Even if I liked it, as a wizard I couldn't do shit.

The next game I immediately played a series of superhero RPGs. DC followed by Marvel. Marvel rocked! From what I can remember, it did exactly what it promised. I had some guy with UNEARTHLY strength and I was hooked! It was just like the comics. It felt right. I fell in love! I almost gave up on RPGs. This totally turned me around.

Then I played shadowrun. At the time I hated guns. I didn't want to use a gun. But every character I made got their ass kicked because I didn't have a gun. Then I found out about hackers and thought this way I can play and not be violent. I played a hacker which meant I did nothing for 75% of the game and the 25% that I did play, I played alone and everyone else was bored. I finally learned to love Shadowrun when I started making brutal gun happy killers. And then I had an amazing time.

Then we played Starwars. I was fucking pissed because everyone else had played for 2 years and had amazing characters and they made me start from the equivalent of level 1. And I couldn't do anything! It sucked! I was soooo bitter. So then I manipulated the fuck out of everyone outside the game into having a time travel episodes into the far past. Before that I stole all these plans on the Death Star which was already built. Then I conned the GM into letting me stay in the past. Then the next adventure I went from being level 1 to like level 20!!! With everyone else being level 10. The GM gave me all the XP I would have gotten for all the years that passed by between those 2 sessions. And on top of that, I became the inventor of the Death Star and was a billionair. Then I turned evil and made everyone pay for making me play a first level character at first which included me setting up event knows as the "Hoth Massacre" where... fuck, I won't even get into it. I was such an asshole. Eventually everyone ganged up and killed me.

Ahhh, highschool! Good times!

    The first time I played an RPG I was 5. It was D&D. My friends and I got stick swords and become adventurers and made our little brothers be kobolds. We beat the crap out of them all day long. It was 1979 -- and may have been one of the earlier boffer LARPS.

    Over time I settled down into playing D&D as a boardgame. Years passed happily like that. Then I got the idea (don't remember where) that RPGs should tell stories. Years then passed in which I had no clue what I was doing. I'd buy mountains of RPG stuff and sit there looking at it with the dread feeling in my gut that it had some obvious use that I just couldn't make heads or tales of. When I tried to use it to make stories it didn't work. I could make it work when I just played it as a game, but surely that wasn't the point, right?

    I finally figured out that the point was for me to tell a story and to bring the players along for it. Then followed years of the painfull teenage power-game angst of the Dungeon Master with his submissive players, emotionally and verbally abusing them into doing just as I pleased. College changed that, as I had to learn to be cool. So I became the Lord Illusionist, and learned to force my players along transparently. I got really good at doing exactly what I wanted in a game without the players feeling my hand on their back the whole time.

    Then someone started talking to me about Narrativism and this game called Sorcerer. I think it may have been Joshua BishopRoby. Fucker. Screwed up everything.

    I never should have stopped beating up on people with a stick sword.

    My first game was Gamma World when I was 10. I had seen the D&D books at my cousin's house, and I was soooo in love with them. But back home, my friend JT had Gamma World, so that's what we played. When I moved, I took photocopies of some of the GW charts and stuff with me and started up a game group in the new neighborhood using only those precious pages.

    You can do a lot with massive random mutation tables, weapon stats, and character sheets.

    The thing I remember most about those early days was our flat refusal of GM fiat. If the GM (which was usually me) said, "The Hoops catch up to you and throw nets over you," the players would all moan and wail. "No way! We roll to escape! We roll to cut the nets! We roll to scare them away!" Every single thing had to be thrown into the jaws of the mechanics and gnashed to pieces. It made for zany, madcap escapades as PCs failed rolls to notice the most obvious things or to manage a polite conversation without igniting a blood feud.

    The players were also fiercely independent. I would throw hook after hook to get them to "do my story" and they would ignore them all and go charging off to steal a town's water supply or something. I feel very lucky to have had players like this in my formative gaming years. The idea of the "GM's plot" just never took root, and I think my gaming was better for it in the long run.

    I was 10 or 11. I was a big game fan, my dad taught me to play Chess, and I had a huge collection of the roll-and-move stuff that was mainstream at the time (Payday, Careers). The local drugstore(!) had a couple shelves devoted to board games, and I was always on the lookout for the new thing. One day this purple-grey box showed up on the shelf that said "Dungeons & Dragons" on the cover. I was fascinated.

    To buy a new game required several months of scraping and saving, so while I was waiting I read the back of the box scrupulously and looked at the grainy photo of what was inside, trying to figure out what this might be. I was a fiend for Choose Your Own Adventures books, of which I had a couple, so I thought it might be something like that. It looked like it had cards with maps on them, and maybe other cards that bits of text that told you what happened, or at least that was what I imagined from the bad photo.

    Finally, the day came, I had the funds, and I went and bought the box. I don't believe they ever restocked it. Instead of what I expected, I got a HUGE rulebook, something called a "module" (at least it had maps in it) and some funky dice, and a crayon. I read the rulebook cover to cover.

    Then I got my parents together. They were pretty much the only people I had to play games with at the time (yes, my childhood sucked that bad). I got them to roll up characters. I tried to convince them to name the characters, but I don't think I succeeded. I read the flavor text box from the first numbered encounter in B1: In Search of the Unknown. They stared at me. I put the game away.

    About a year later I got moved to a different school, and actually made a few friends. A couple of them liked games as well. So I got out the purple-grey box. The module said 4-8 characters, so each of them (I had two players) rolled up three or four characters. They actually gave them names. I read the flavor text, and we were off. We'd play for the 30-45 minutes between the time school got out and they had to get on the bus (or my dad arrived to pick me up). The defining moment was a few sessions in. They got into a fight with a purple worm, and one of the characters got swallowed. The book explained that if you could kill the worm in so many turns, you could cut a swallowed character out before he was digested.

    The bus arrived. I started packing up. The players revolted!

    We round up rolling d20s in the parking lot next to the door of the bus as kids walked on. The swallowed character was rescued. We were hooked.

    A few months later, the school declared RPGs demonic, but that's another story.

    First time I played (well GMed actually):

    Fall of 1977 at my best friend's birthday weekend. He got the original blue box Basic D&D (with the geomorphs and monsters and treasure sheets, not a module) as a present. His older brother had played in school. I had looked at the D&D set in the store months prior, and turned this pencil and paper game down in favor of Tractics, a good wholesome miniatures game. I was rather skeptical about this imaginary pencil and paper game, so when everyone set up to play, I decided to just watch (this was probably the origin of my tendency to prefer to watch games before actually playing them myself). While they played, I looked over the rule book, and found out about the dungeon maps, and perhaps even the mention that you could play with miniatures.

    Suddenly, I was interested. I ended up staying up all night reading the rules. And changing them before we even played the first time (coming from a wargaming background, it didn't make sense that all weapons did the same damage). The next morning, the others created new characters, and ventured into the dungeon. We sat in the back of the station wagon (this was before seat belt laws) as we rode to Battleship Cove (for the main activity of the birthday) and played. By the end of the day, I think we had run through two or three sets of characters (getting them up to third level and retiring them).

    Certainly one of the things that sold me on the game was all this talk of not really winning, and the cooperative nature of the game. Of course I soon discovered that the only way to guarantee that kind of game was to be GM.

    This is a story about GM fiat, and just how far it goes.

    I was about eight or nine, so it must have been 1985 or 1986, when I went over to an older friend's house. I had just read the Lord of the Rings for the first time, so when he said that we could play a game kind of like it, I was hooked. I made a female human neutral good cleric and spent about an hour reading all the spells and going, "Whoa! Cool!" Then the party leader told me that I wasn't supposed to cast anything but healing spells, and I got really mad at him and told the group that if anyone was mean to me, I'd let their character die. That shut them up quick. We didn't actually get to play that session, though, so it didn't make a difference.

    I took that character to camp with me that summer, where the morning activities were voluntary and you could sign up for whichever ones you wanted. On the strength of that one not-quite-session, I signed up for D&D. We ran through what I'm fairly sure was "Desert of Desolation," though the GM never let us see the book he was running from. I had a great time running around, smiting things with my mace, and rescuing the party left and right.

    The other kids were all guys and were mostly eleven or twelve, and the GM was maybe fourteen, so a certain amount of raucousness was inevitable. Unfortunately, one of the other players decided it would be fun for his character to have sex with mine. I was eight, so I didn't even know what sex was - and then this guy describes his character sneaking into the tent and getting it on with mine. I got really angry and started shouting about how it was SO GROSS and how I wanted to have woken up when he came in and killed him with my mace. He started shouting back about how sneaky his character was. The GM stepped in and said that he was the GM, and that my character had (EW!) sex, and that's how it was. I said, "Nuh uh, you can't make me do that." He said, "Yeah, I can too." And that's how I ended up in a fist-fight with the GM during the very first session I ever played.

    For the record, he beat the crap out of me (being several years older and much more fit), but he decided that because I cared so much about it, my character could kill the other PC before having to have sex with him. So the GM and this other guy and I - with my black eye - sat down to roll up a new character for him.

    This might have scared other girls away, but fortunately I'm a feisty bitch and I came back the next day raring for more. None of those boys messed with me ever again!

Pretty quickly I had morphed D&D into a game where my response to player input was to keep things interesting and fair. If I didn't trust myself to come up with that on my own, I used a d20 to decide between positive and negative outcomes. If I recall at one point during that game I even made a geneology chart for the ruling family of a kingdom, and ran for several months using that essentially as a relationship map.

    I'm still not sure how I got from reading D&D DM advice to the idea that the DM should be responsive to players ideas, and should offer players opportunities, rather than force them into situations. That's pretty much affected my playing and running of RPGs ever since. Perhaps that is why when I first encountered Mo's post on push and pull I immediately thought: push = players, pull = GM.

    - Mendel
