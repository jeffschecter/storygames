    \n I Am Not A Tax Lawyer, but from the way I figured out taxes last year, that's not making things easier. See, charitable donations are tax-deductible, but only if you itemize deductions. So for us, for example, even though we gave a good chunk to charity it didn't make a difference for our federal tax return because even with the mortgage interest added in, the standard deduction was still higher. It did make a difference on state income taxes, though.

    So it's not as easy as just not reporting it.

    I assume you could create your game company as a nonprofit and then directly offset income and donations (and expenses). That still requires keeping track, and I don't know how much paperwork is involved in doing all this. Maybe Ryan (Finis was nonprofit, right?) or someone else can chime in on this with direct experience?

    \n Yeah, if the money passes through you as an entity, it counts as your income first, before you do anything else with it. Before Clinton left Lulu, I heard one of the initiatives he was pushing for was setting it up so that money from purchased books could be sent directly to charities, without having to pass through the author's hands first, but that never happened.

    I filed taxes once as One Thousand One. Sucked balls. I thought I did everything right and the government still said I fucked up and charged me like $180 because of it. If I was doing a commercial release again, I would certainly hire some tax folks to handle it for me. Even then, keeping track of everything is such a pain in the ass. Better to get a seperate small business credit card and back account and then do all your business transactions (including buying meals at GenCon) through that account. Doing anything else is madness.
