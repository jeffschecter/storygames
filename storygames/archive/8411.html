    \n Hi Lynne. Welome to SG.

    Tell us about your game? What's it about? What's the most enjoyable part of playing it? How many pages is it? How many times have you (or other people) played it? What's your target audience (what kind of people do you think would like your game)?

    On the publishing end, the easiest route for publishing a PDF (for money, publishing it non-commercially is even easier) is probably getting some free webspace (like a Wordpress blog), slapping a Paypal button on it, and emailing copies to the people who buy it. Depending on your goals for publishing and your target audience, there might be a few other things you might also want to consider.

    General advice for publishing PDFs:

    1. Tony is right that the hardest thing is marketing. Anyone can sell a PDF, but it's hard to get attention for it.
    2. The standard Forge advice for selling your game is: get people playing it. Go to conventions and play it with people.
    3. You might consider selling a print copy, too. At the very least, you could publish it on Lulu.com, which is a print-on-demand service. You could also do a short print run: say, 50 or 100 copies.
    4. The places people often sell things are: Indie Press Revolution, RPG.NOW, Drive Thru RPG,
    5. Jonathan is also right that it's easy to set up a web page and do it yourself.

    Other general things:

    1. Post in the Publishing section of the Collective Endeavour. They will be all friendly and give you advice.
    2. The archives of the Forge and the Collective Endeavour are good resources for publishing.
    3. Tell us about your game already, for the love of God.

    \n The idea of putting up a web page with a PayPal button and mailing people who pay your PDF works, but only on the back of some other form of marketing that lets people know that your game exists, otherwise they won't be looking.

    PDF retail stores (like RPG.now) get around this by putting loads of products in one place and hoping that people stumble upon your game, but it's not something that offers high volume in most cases, and there is always a non-insignificant cut taken out of the sale price.

    A combination approach works really well. If people are already looking for your game then they will often buy from your direct site, which maximizes profit and customer contact. You can get them looking by creating buzz (by running the game, or blogging about it, or sharing it on communities such as this), or by selling physical paper copies through places like IPR or at conventions (if you don't want to sell paper online).
