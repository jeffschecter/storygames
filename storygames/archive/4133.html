    I'm going to post on just one for now, with the caveat that I'm totally not trying to sell people on it:

    With Great Power
    Neat Stuff:
    * a pacing mechanic for the story, that basically forces the characters to lose a lot before they really start to succeed, and that pacing mechanic is represented by a sheet of paper.
    * "Love for Mary Jane" can be just as powerful, if not more (if the player wants it), as "Webslinging/Spidey Sense/Super Strength/Super Dexterity/Wbslinging/etc"

    Good for:
    * Games with no GM secrets: The GM has to have scenes which showcase the enemies to make them powerful.
    * Players who are open to play roles other than their character: There is definite character ownership, but the game pretty much makes the players ALSO get involved in other character's scenes as anchors, antagonists, random NPCs, etc. There isn't the "all NPCs controlled by GM" effect.
    * Stories focused on those aspects of the PCs that the players want brought into play. If the GM runs a game where the big bad is just some random dude who must be stopped, or where people are finding it hard to relate to the baddie, then the GM didn't read the book or setup correctly.

    Avoid for:
    * Games where the "big plan" needs to be a secret from the PCs.
    * Games where the players want to play their character, and don't want to play-act other random NPCs as much.
    * If the players don't like card-based resolution. Cause there's a lot of fucking cards in play. I love the game and yet it sometimes gets annoying to track them all.
    * when you really want to know how muxh your character can lift. I'm not being facetious here in the "Oh, because knowing that is so blase, and this gets past all that drivel...". But honestly, I'm running a WGP game right now which is hot as fuck. And yet, I know now that I want to run another supers game within the next year using another system, because I do want to do the whole "character building/see how much they can lift and how powerful they are In Numbers" thing.
    * Groups where it's often the case that a player or two can't make it here and there. Pretty much everyone has to be on board the whole time, otherwise the story changes without that person's input, and missing one week means that they show up to a game where so much shit went down they don't know how to fit in anymore.

    Other Concerns
    I haven't made an AP about my ongoing With Great Power: The Night Watch - Seattle game (which Matthew Gandy and Quintin Stone here are in), but a few things that it took me a while to get around with this game:
    * Antagonists need scenes, too. I had maybe 2 antagonist scenes in 4 sessions.
    * Make the players roleplay the roles of major NPCs, don't take them on all yourself as the GM. The first scene where the PCs' boss enters, I made the conscious choice to hand this role to one of the other players rather than play him myself as the GM (and gave the descriptors: "He's a large man, he's very powerful. He seems nice and reassuring, to new agents and to old hats. He's black, and he always wears a three-piece suit. Go!"). Magic happens when you do this. If the GM takes on all the NPCs, he will be talking to himself a lot, while 1-2 players sit there on their hands.
    * The main thing: DON'T RUSH TO CARDS. Give the players their roles, let them roleplay, dammit! Only step in when it's apparent that something needs to be resolved. Bask in the conversation that unfolds, don't sit there with sweaty hands ready to jump into the roleplaying, and break it up screaming "OK! Conflict! Let's Go!". My players have fucking astounded me in this campaign with their in-game in-character roleplaying, and as a GM I've been really nervous, like "Shit, I should be rolling dice or something now. Instead, I'm just sitting here not doing shit while the players talk to each other in character. I have to force a conflict now!" and getting past that nervousness is the first step to running an awesome WGP game. Not doing so means that you're pushing the game along without care to how the roleplaying is unfolding.

    I love WGP, but only for specific modes of play. I was lucky this time around. Next supers game I play will probably be T&J or H&S, just to play some supers in another mode.

    Also, I suggest you think about adding the recent Marvel Supers (with "Stones") to the pile, as you can find them on eBay for cheap, and as JD Corley demonstrated, it was a very overlooked game with a lot of coolness brimming under the surface.

    Does that help, man?

    -Andy

    \n Hey, you left out Wild Talents too.

    Neat stuff:
    - The One Roll Engine
    - Very pretty book, great full colour art
    - Fantastic chapter on supers world building by Ken Hite
    - A good default world, building off the timeline in Godlike.

    Good for:
    - Traditional type Marvel/DC style supers world gaming
    - Really nice, general power building rules

    Avoid for:
    - Combat is extremely deadly, even for high powered heroes if they neglect defensive powers
    - Almost no general 'how to run the game' advice
    - Prepping opposition is a lot of work for the GM, because badly chosen powers might make the PCs totally walk over the bad guys, or vice versa, or result in a stalemate.

    Other concerns:
    - My one game with this was abortive, because of two main issues. Firstly, I like to keep my before game prep minimal (preferably zero), which this sure didn't support in any way. Secondly, the campaign really dealt with issues that weren't supported (Cold War superspies, focus on trust and paranoia). So, very good for certain modes - if my spies had been cast more as James Bond types, I think it would have worked great.

    \n Heroes Forever RPG
    \n
    \nNeat Stuff:
    \n- Uses and expanded version of the D12 results table resolution system first introduced in the Dark Realms RPG (every and all die related resolution is resolved using a D12 and that table)
    \n- Point based system that lets players mix and match most any crazy set of skills and powers.
    \n- "Class Levels" which let the system represent supers and creatures of any power. Each class level represents a x10 factor to all powers, attacks, defense, etc. So while you average low powered Hero is a class one, some of the most potent DC style characters would run class 3 or 4. But the scale system keeps going up and one NPC detailed was a Class 12.
    \n- The "background" setting for the game system assumes that Supers and Immortals have been around since the dawn of time and as such, it is a completely alternate history setting.
    \n
    \nGood for:
    \n- Games that intergrate Magic, Super powers and ubber tech.
    \n- Playing supers adventures at both the mundane and world shaker power levels.
    \n- Also uses the Personality rules from the Dark Realms RPG, thusly rewarding character development & interaction (ala, rewards the role playing of the role playing game and encourages players to continue develope the personality of their character further)
    \n- Taking an oddball setting and plugging in powers at any level into the mix and still havng it make sense and not destroy
    \n
    \nAvoid for:
    \n- Fast to no Game Master prep time. NPC and creatures can take a while to develop using this system.
    \n- Fails to balance mixed class level characters well into the same campaign. Class levels 2 & 3 can interact ok, 3 & 4 also, but Class Level 1 characters will have a tough time taking a single hit from Class Level 2 and above NPCs.
    \n- Core game rules were designed to be low cost and provide game system and base powers only, so it fails to deliver the depth or cool factor of the alternate history setting the game is based on.
    \n- Needs to have a quick gen accessory to make picking and stating out NPCs on the fly quick and easy for the game master.
    \n
    \nFailings of the core system we are looking to "fix" through expanded materials:
    \n
    \n- Put out an "World Atlas" book that case serve to show the plenty of maps and highlight the settings alternate history and give game masters and players ideas on how to take the skeleton of the setting and really make it their own.
    \n- The "techie" type characters (ala, a Batman) really need a book dedicated to them, with tons of options at stuff they can use. Bruce already has the Billionaires handbook printed up, but it has to go through binding yet.
    \n- I need to make a NPC quick gen accessory set of some kind and get it out there cheap. Then pile on lots of accessible NPCs in each world and setting sourebook we do.
    \n- I think we're going to have to dedicate a book to supers with mental powers. They have some cool and powerful powers in the core rules and in some ways, the lack of other support materials make them both extremely powerful (ie, some of the strongest and most powerful supers might not also have mental defenses) but also big targets and fairly one dimensional characters. More options, lesser powers in that arena and some additional methods to get said characters involved in the traditional "physical" slug fests are needed.

    \nMutants & Masterminds

    Neat Stuff:
    The best-looking, most "supers comic feeling" RPG books ever printed, hands down.
    Requires only a single d20 per player.
    Hero Point, Power Stunt, and Complications mechanics are some of the better "story fudging" rules you'll find in any d20 RPG.

    Good for:
    The whole "character building/see how much they can lift and how powerful they are In Numbers," as Andy put it. M&M is basically what HERO would be if you cold pry it away from both the designers and fans who insist it remain virtually the same game it was twenty years ago, and who judge a book by word:penny ratios. It lets you get your Lonely Fun on, but without being so work intensive and page-heavy that you question why you're even bothering.

    Avoid for:
    If you have even the slightest inkling of getting your Nar on. This is still very GM-focused, traditional Sim play, and the published adventure product is standard "push you through the plot" fare. Also, if the idea of a spreadsheet being helpful during chargen scares you, you probably don't want to play M&M.

    In general, I'd say that M&M is the best trad supers RPG in existence right now. But if you don't want trad, avoid it.

    \n I'll can give you my opinions of The Supercrew. Keep in mind that these are not neutral in any way, since I'm the one who wrote the game.
    \n
    \nNeat stuff:
    \n-The game, including rules, examples and everything is a comic, and has the same size as a comic book.
    \n-It's short.
    \n-You play yourself, but with super powers.
    \n-It's mandatory to paint your super hero costume with crayons or similar on the character sheet.
    \n-The heroes may have any power level. A Batman-like character will contribute just as much as one with power on a Superman level.
    \n-It has a very swedish take on the concept of super heroes.
    \n
    \nGood for:
    \n-Fast character creation.
    \n-Adventures that lasts only one session.
    \n-People who like to use super powers in inventive ways.
    \n-Creating villains on the spot.
    \n
    \nAvoid for:
    \n-If you like to create detailed characters, pick super powers from long lists and combine the powers to make your hero as powerful as possible.
    \n-If you want the characters to get more and more powerful as they get more experienced. There are no rules for it!
    \n-If you want a detailed campaign setting.
    \n-If you like heroes who feel sorry for themselves a lot.
    \n
    \n/tobias

    Oh alright then. I don't know whether you'd call it a story game. The approach when I wrote it was for a fairly traditional system, but cut down to make things relatively simple and fast, in play and particularly in chargen.     \nCapes

    Neat Stuff: Click-N-Lock Modules for Chargen: Powers plus Personality, customize, Go! Quick and easy, plus visceral fun, like playing with a toy.
    Conflict cards with dice on each "side" to tell who's winning, plus poker chips to stake on the outcomes. Again, more fun, toy-like elements that are really grabby.
    GMless play, meaning everyone gets the same means to impact the story, without having to give up playing a character to the hilt.

    Good For: Superhero stories where the characters are challenged on what they believe in and what they value.
    Hardcore competition around the table: everyone contributes to the story, but you have to fight for the outcome you want! What makes it work is that everyone has equal armament.
    Starting quickly: chargen can happen in as little as two minutes, and additional characters whipped up like magic.
    Batman and Superman can be in the same game and have equal effect on the story.
    Games with lots of melodramatic narration (some mechanics even require it!)

    Avoid For:Games where your Hero's power level/how much they can lift/etc. are important.
    Games where a GM unfolds a plot.
    Games with more than 4, maybe 5 players.
    Games where you want to roll lots of different dice (only D6s here).
    Starting quickly without learning a lot of rules. The whole machine fuckin' sings once you know it, but there IS a learning curve.
    Games where cooperation is desired. To be sure, everyone cooperates on the highest level to make a story, but within that process is a brutal player on player battle. If you're going to get hurt feelings because someone blocked you from your goal in order to achieve their own, stay away from this game.
