Dungeons & Bananas is a game that owes a lot of its history to chance. I made a system for creative challenges many years ago and we decided to playtest it at a con. Problem is, there were only three of us, which felt like too few, so I hacked it into a GMless co-op game on the spot. And it rocked. Oh, how it rocked.

The game got played again and again at different cons in Sweden and was repeatedly the most played game in the Indie Room. People asked me to write it down, and my lazy answer was always "Yeah, I'll totally do that later, promise." Years later Jonatan Kilhamn wrote down an English version of the game for a Story-Games Christmas exchange or somesuch. But alas! he had misremembered some parts and the result was flawed! Another year or two passed, and finally Rickard Elim\xe4\xe4 asked me if I could send him the document, and I did, mentioning the errors in it. He took it upon him to correct the errors and made a great layout of the game. This game is now finally available for your enjoyment!

---

What's it like? It's a cooperative GMless game where the players team up to beat the dungeon. It incorporates short-term tactical risk management (how much Trouble am I willing to risk on this roll?) as well as long-term strategic resource management (should I burn my Spotlight Banana now or should I save it for later?). But the core of the game is the creative challenges it poses the players. How do you get past the guards using your Fancy Hat and Impressive Climbing Skills, and how to you get injured in the process? How to you escape from the Zorgon warship using your Alien Artifact and your Psychic Powers? These kinds of creative challenges are super fun to meet and just as much fun to listen to.

This is also a great game to play with children. I've played this with 8-12 year olds and had a blast, and I don't even like children! Their creativity will astound you and they'll have a heck of a time, too.

So go on, you know you want to read and then play this. It's super fun, and it's the only one of my totally famous games to be available in English, so seize the opportunity! The king of Sweden and also ABBA totally love this game, too, seriously. Time traveling Alfred Nobel said it was "Totally rad" and gave it 5/5 sticks of dynamite.

    If I roll high enough to go past the end of the list, are we are the last challenge?
    \nYes, that's right. You always play the last challenge, so if you roll to pass it, you stop there.\n
    If I get 1 trouble on a 1, do I roll d6 and we move to the location that many down the list? Or choose? Or +1? Or is it just color without actually moving on the place list?
    \nIt's just color. All the results on the "1" list, except the last one, are without mechanical effect. The parts in regular text is the color, what you need to weave into your description, and the parts in italics describe the mechanical effects. All reluts on the "1" list except the last one, as well as all "1" results on the other lists except for list number 2, have only color and no mechanical effects.\n
    Is buying a new item only for other people? By the phrasing I can't tell if it's "buy anyone an item" or "buy anyone but yourself an item".
    \nIt's "buy anyone but yourself". You can only buy new items and unlock items and abilities for other people. You can never spend your stones on yourself.\n
    It looks like each player must take exactly one turn, not more or less, during each full round, though a completed or failed challenge may cut off a round. Is that right?
    \nYes, that's right. Turns to face the challenge can be taken in any order, but nobody can take two turns in the same "round", and when everyone has taken a turn, you add some new stones to the Trouble pile and describe a change to the challenge.

Spending your points on others helps spread the efficiency around, especially since you tend to go "Alright, who doesn't have a +4 item yet? I've got some points to spend here".

    \n Hi, we've been playing this game last night. Nobody played before, and we've been trying new systems lately. This was the first game all our players got excited about during play. It shines!

    We started with a high fantasy tune, escorting a young prince through dangers to reclaim his throne. As we were unfamiliar with the maths at first we used waay to many blue dice and lost our first two challenges (one involvig fighting off huge birds attacking out hot-air balloon and the other about surviving in the harsh mountains after the crash land). We decided to start over and travel around a mythic Europe in search of the Holy Grail. We lost the final challenge in a forgotten tepmle of Solomon against Cain himself. The game was working fabulous, but we had some minor issues here and there.

    There were five of us, and sometimes it felt flat that five different people had to act in basically the same situation in a challenge. There were multiple attempts to avoid this, one "gm" effectively changing the challenge after each roll, we were urging others to change the situation ourselves and such. However, after the fact, I think the best way to handle this would have been to lower the round limit to four or three. Like a round ends after four people have rolled, raising the trouble limit only by four, and the fifth person starting next round.

    Maybe is was beacuse of our overcompensation on the liberal use of blue dice in the first adventure, but the trouble list was not used that often. Even when it came in to play, only trouble ratings of one or two were used, and these were not adding much pressure. We got through the challenges without risking a lot. Might have been a bit of a lucky string of rolls, though.

    We found the rule that you give stuff only to others wonderful to spark interest in each other's characters, however this was kinda lost during a challenge. I felt that some link or pressure was missing in connecting the actions of one player to the next's. It's like the actual actions and circumstances did not count that much if we were lazy. We were certainly tired at the end of the session, but i felt that some nudge or an arrow in the mechanics would have been nice. I'm thinking of a combo rule or similar, but then again, I have no idea on how can this be put seamlessly into the system.

    All in all, a great game. It will be our go-to game when nobody feels like GMing for a whole night.

      \n I've written up a simple summary of all the game mechanics, for my own purposes. I'll post them here, in case I got something wrong - or in case someone finds these notes useful.

      I used Rickard's suggested inversion of the Trouble pile; it seems simpler to explain and use (and you don't have to distribute the Trouble "evenly" this way). Otherwise, it should be identical to the rules as written (if I did it right.)\n

      Characters

      Three Traits, rated 1 to 3 (higher is better)
      Two Items, rated 1 and 2
      A Spotlight Banana (Secret Weapon)

      The Dungeon

      Make a list of encounters/challenges.
      10 entries for a short game (equivalent to 4 challenges on average)
      20 entries for a long game (equivalent to 7 challenges on average)

      The First Challenge

      Make two piles.
      Challenge pile: 4 x players stones
      -- Whenever all the stones are removed, you win the current challenge!
      Trouble pile: One more stone than the Challenge pile, in a different colour (4xplayers+1)
      -- If the last stone is ever removed, you lose the current challenge!
      (Even if you also emptied the Challenge pile on the same turn.)

      Each Round

      Each player takes a turn.
      At the end of the round, each player takes one Trouble stone from the pile.
      (If the last one is removed, the Challenge is lost.)

      Each Turn

      Take one die by default (red)
      Add yellow dice for up to one Trait and/or one Item
      (Used Traits and Items are marked, cannot be used again)
      Add as many Trouble dice as you want (blue)

      Rolling

      Each 4-6 is a success (take one stone from the Challenge pile)
      Each 1-3 is Trouble (take one stone from the Trouble pile)
      Yellow dice cannot generate Trouble.

      Trouble

      If there is Trouble, read the relevant result from the Trouble table. The red die determines which column you use; the amount of Trouble determines the result.

      Spotlight Banana

      You can use your Banana once in the game. If you do, mark it. During this turn, your dice are successes if they are 3-6, and Trouble if they are 1-2.

      Winning

      If you have won the Challenge without emptying the Trouble pile, you may move on to the next Challenge. Before you do so, each player can spend any of her earned stones to:
      * Unmark another player's Items or Traits (cost equal to value)
      * Give a player a new Item (cost equal to twice the new value)

      Losing

      If you lost the Challenge (emptied the Trouble pile), return all the Trouble stones to the Trouble pile. You can still spend the Challenge stones, as described above.
      If you lose three Challenges, or lose the final Challenge, you've lost for good.

      Sidenote: Unspent Stones

      If, after a Challenge, you didn't spend all your stones, write down the number you have left so you can return the actual stones to the piles. (Otherwise, you'll mess up the math for the next challenge!)

      Moving to a New Challenge

      Roll 1d6 to find out which challenge you face next.
      Collect the Challenge and Trouble stones into two piles again; roll 1d6 and add its value (in stones) to the Challenge pile. (The Challenge pile will get bigger and bigger as the game goes on.)

      The Final Challenge

      If there are no more Challenges remaining, this one settles all: the entire Adventure is lost or won here.

      \n Paul, I've had a similar experience at first, but I've been thinking of this a lot lately. I think that the statistics of the game is counter-intuitive. As an aside, I understand that a very significant part of the game is not here in these mechanics. I would just like to understand the statistical implications so that i can tinker with the rules.

      First, here's a thing.
      Winning the first challenge just inside of 1 round allows 4 trouble per player, and 4 per player per round. It's 8 trouble dice, 1 red and 7 blue. It yields 4 sucesses.
      Winning in 2 allows 3 trouble, 1.5 per round. 6 dice, 2 red and thus 3 blue. You need 2 white to get the fourth sucess.
      Winning in 3 allows 2 trouble, 0.66 per round. 4 dice, 3 red, 1 blue. You need 4 white to get the fourth success.
      Winning in 4 rounds is unlikely as you roll 4 red dice and it's 2 trouble and thus you lost.
      This of course is to win 50% of the time, without considering the banana and trouble table results. The latter is very harsh if you get more than 2 troubles. You might want to throw in 1 or 2 white dice to have a much more reasonable chance. Winning much faster with a lot more white dice is counterproductive as you get less XP so it does not help you in the long term.

      Harder challenges don't change the list above. The trouble part does not change, you just need to meet the higher success requirement. Adding more blue dice in harder challenges does not help, you need to add more whites to win those. So then, blue dice strategy does not depend on the challenge rating, one should do it the same all the time. The best way (statistically) would be to aim for a win just before the end of round 2 or round 3. That means 1 to 3 blue dice per challenge per player, and 3 or 4 white on the first challenge, increasing the whites by two by challenge.

      If you are behind the probability curve within a single challenge, you are tempted to take more risks to get even. However, the most obvious thing, ie. adding blue dice does not help you at all. On the contrary, you are worse off with more blue dice: you get 0.5 success for 0.5 trouble per blue, AND you get worse results on the table. You can only improve your chances with white dice and the banana. These are simple resources that you can basically burn to get successes with relative reliability and no risk. I can only see two instances when adding blue dice helps you winning a challenge: right before the end of a round that causes you to lose anyways or with the banana.

      If you look at the XP side, you can see the pressure. To be able win a 5-challenge game with a good safety margin (+2 white dice per challenge), you need 4+6+8+10+12 = 40 white dice. You start with 9. You might be able to refresh them once or maybe twice, let's say 15 more dice for 15 XP. But you need 16 more dice, that's 25-30 more XP. You earn 8+9+10+11=38 XP for the challenges AT MOST. So then the XP is barely enough to win a 5-challenge game. If you get one more challenge, you need 14 more dice but you get only 12 XP for the fifth challenge. It gets a lot harder when the game is longer.

      All in all, blue dice are just the opposite of what one might think of them instinctively. You need to add more blue dice when you are winning: they are of long-term importance, and don't help much inside a challenge. You should use them to exploit your good luck. But you need the bananas to mitigate bad luck.

      By the way, I don't really like this role of blue dice. I wished to see them as a short term tool to risk more in order to get even, but that's not the case in this game.

If you still have a lot to go before hitting the limit, you might want to roll more blues in order to get as many XP as possible from the challenge. This is especially important in longer games. There really is a big difference in the difficulty in games of different length. A 10-game is going to be really easy and a 20-game is going to be really hard. To compensate for this, one could roll two dice for adding to the challenge pile, choosing the highest or lowest to adjust the difficulty. I haven't done the maths properly on this, but this adds or removes on average about 1 extra stone per challenge. I think this is should keep the difficulty on about the same level for , say, a 10-game compare to a 15-game, but again, I really haven't done the math on it.

      Anyway, if you don't roll blue dice you'll drain your resources and fail, unless you roll really well or play a really short game. You need the blue dice for the long run, and, as way said, you should roll more of them when you're doing well, and fewer when you're doing badly. In the later, harder challenges, you'll need to be really conservative with your blue dice, but for that to be possible, you need to have amassed a good amount of powerful items to be able to bring those yellow dice to the table and skimp on the blue. Basically, use the blue dice to collect as much XP as you can in the early game, since you're going to need it in the late game. And, if you find the game is too easy, play a longer dungeon or increase the difficulty.

      (I just realized, you can probably play a longer dungeon and roll both the speed you go through and the challenge level increases with "roll 2 keep highest". That way it should be about as difficult as a regular long dungeon (you have fewer chances to fail but also fewer chances to recover), but not take as long to play through.)

      \n I've been hacking this game to my group's tastes, and played some to see how it worked. Please do tell if i'm hijacking the thread. Also I'd be happy to hear about your experiences or rules alternatives you've tried or considered.

      I got rid of the trouble pile, red/blue dice only yield the immediate drawback coming from the trouble tables. This has the "risk more to gain more" vibe instead of the careful balancing of blue dice. Every challenge takes two rounds, and is lost at the end of the second round. This simplifies maths, but takes away some of the thrill. Still worth it, I think.

      I introduced Advantages, things you can create if you achieved at least 3 sucesses. This gives two white dice to the next roller if he exploits the Advantage and is lost if he doesn't. Advantages can snowball, if he also achieves 3 successes, the next one gains 3 dice, etc. End of round nullifies the Advantage. This was a great change, players thinking about exploitable advantages and focusing their attention more to the things happening in the fiction.

      I introduced Overwhelming Success, 5 or more successes. This restarts a round with a fitting narration from the player. This was so-so. I wanted them to aim at these with blue dice, but it was more of an unexpected and unplanned for bonus if we got there. Also 5 was quite low at the later challenges and was not that hard to achieve, making late challenges actually easier. Or at least feel easier. Not good.

      There is no recharge of traits, everything must be bought at a price of one stone per bonus. Also not only items, but more generic traits can be created as well. No boring recharge stuff, but loss of some continuity of characters. I still like the effect overall.

      After a failed challenge you don't proceed with the chapters but someone creates a recovery challenge that is rooted in the specifics of the failure. Losing that means losing the adventure. Feels more like a story with consequences.

      I still struggle with some issues. We found giving feedback on not-so-good narration awkward. But I have no idea if facilitating should go into the game rules or not. I also don't know what kind of rule this would be. Any ideas? We find the prewritten list of chapters not so inspiring, I am toying with the idea of just figuring it out as we go and use a dice roll to decide if it's the last-but-one chapter. Any experience with this?
