GMliness Fuck (or, Question GMliness)

\n
\n
\n
\n\nRobert_Bohl \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2010 \n edited September 2010 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So, I was listening to the most recent episode of The Jank Cast, the one on GMless games, and it combined with a few other things swirling around my head regarding the notion of the GMliness. I came to the conclusion that we* need to adopt this attitude about the question:


(graffiti in my old home town of Northampton, MA)

Where gender = the idea of GMs.

So, instead of figuring out whether games have GMs or not, or how much GMliness they have, we have to break out of the thinking structure that says every game either has a GM, or doesn't. Instead, we should refer to how the game apportions responsibilities and authority.

For example, the role of personifying the antagonism is separable from being the rules adjudicator is separable from being the social organizer is separable from making mechanical or strategic choices for the antagonist is separable from the person hosting the game night is separable from who says how the plot of the game is structured, ad (quasi-) infinitum.

Take a look at How We Came to Live Here by Brennan Taylor as an example. There are two GM-like roles, the inside player and the outside player. When dealing with conflicts internal to the village, the inside player makes all mechanical and strategic decisions for the scene, but the outside player plays the first (and potentially only) non-hero character introduced in the scene. Personifying the antagonism has been split (partially) from making mechanical choices for the antagonist(s).

Now take a look at Shock: Social Science Fiction by Joshua AC Newman. Personification of and mechanical decisions made for the antagonist are made by a single person (the Antagonist), but the world (through Ownership of Shocks and Issues) and the plot structure (by defining how each scene begins, getting last choice on Intent for a conflict, setting the story goal) is heavily influenced by the Protagonist and by Audience.

Finally, take a look at my own game, Misspent Youth. Being The Authority is almost entirely about ensuring that the game's structure goes as it should and, most importantly, about personifying the antagonism in the most frustrating, maddening, unwise-actions-inducing way possible. No one can really make mechanical or strategic decisions for The Authority, except inasmuch as taking action as The Authority in fiction constitutes strategic action. And even then, the strategy The Authority is working with is a meta-strategy about how to fuck with the emotions of players and, through them, their characters. The Authority isn't trying to win, or to beat the clique. It's not possible to, nor is it what's called for.

--

Note that, crucially, I'm not suggesting that all games that refer to GMs be publicly burned, or even publicly scorned. This isn't about a battle over whether GMed, GMful, or GMless games are best. I just think that we should really move beyond this notion of there being a metric based on how GMed a game is. It's about qualities, not categories. Ask "What does this game do with authority and responsibility," not "What category does it fit inside of" or "What categories do we need to make to fit this?"

I'm someone in favor of tagging things with appropriate descriptors than categorizing things, generally. Maybe this is just an example of that.

* When I say "we," I mean me, and you if you want to.

    \n Sure! I've thought about this some over at Praxis, too.

    What I want to see (and make) are games where both authority and metagame roles are distributed. So in Medical Hospital, one guy is always responsible for assigning surgical procedures to everyone at the table. Somebody else has the job of monitoring attention levels and calling for breaks. These are fixed responsibilities (part of the system, one procedural and one social) tied to roles drenched in melodramatic color, and they can change hands effortlessly.

    Both of these things have to be done, and the normal setup would be for the GM to handle them, or, in an egalitarian equal-authority GMless setup, for everyone to handle them.

    I think it will be interesting and fun (and effective) to just hand those responsibilities to the people who want them. Medical Hospital is going to be sort of a baby step in this direction.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI want to see more games where authority is radically unequal but evenly distributed.
    \nOh man, yeah, but I think it's actually really easier to generate this from grassroots play practices than "de-volve" it from the existing player-GM responsibilities, which seems like what Jason is trying to do in Medical Hospital. I hope Jason can make it work, still, and if anyone can do it, he can.

    When Shreyas, Thomas, and I were talking about Four Nations, the idea was that each player took on the role of one of the great oral storytellers of four different ethnic groups and they've gathered together to tell the great epic that unites their peoples, the equivalent of the Illiad or the Sagas. The design conceit was that each ethnic group had completely different but somewhat compatible storytelling tradition. For example, maybe one told stories with masks, where another used shadow puppets, while another used a brush, bell, fan, and bowl of water. And the different traditions would interact with each other, during the storytelling process, both directly and indirectly, based on the rules governing individual traditions and the larger rules governing the telling of the epic.

    So something like that allows you to draw completely on the needs of the game, because they are so far outside normal conceits like GM and player. You wouldn't even think of issues like "what are the normal GM responsibilities and how do we distribute them to these storyteller figures?" What? That SO doesn't matter! Instead you start with, "Okay, how do the People of the Fated Threads tell stories?" And you'd develop four separate storytelling styles and then, in playtesting, you'd watch for the interactions between them and make adjustments until you found a near-synergy that still allowed for occasional disruptions or uncomfortable interactions that players would have to figure out how to resolve (those issues of intercultural interaction being part of the fun of the game).

    My group has been doing this for a long time, actually, since back when we played old World of Darkness games which gave the GM too much work and started to fall apart if you weren't the best GM in Tucson. We call it "backstage-model GMing." We divide the traditional model of the GM into a number of different roles:

    Producer: You wrangle all the players into showing up in the same place at the same time.
    Caterer: You bring food and drink to the session.
    Director: You actually yell orders, call for rolls, say what bad things will happen if the roll gets cocked up, etc.
    Set Designer: You describe the room we all just walked into, the street corner, the storm drain, etc.
    Casting Director: You help people roll their characters and explain what all the stats mean.
    Stage Manager: You know and explain the confusing rules.
    Fight Choreographer: You know and explain the confusing combat system, and run combat.
    Makeup: You describe what characters look like.
    Key Grip: No one knows what this guy does.

    \n My take on the options:

    Plan 1: Point out what ought to be covered, but leave players free to divvy responsibilities according to what they like.
    Upside: Flexible, responsive to moods, maximize specific players' likes and strengths.
    Downside: Unreliable. Who does the tasks no one is eager to do?
    Fixes: Maybe some tools for communicating coverage? E.g., laundry list of needed tasks and system for passing around the unpopular ones. James (Lula) reports something that seems in this direction.

    Plan 2: Direct a group to cover and divvy responsibilities in a pre-designed way.
    Upside: Clear. No tasks undone or done twice. Possible new type of experience for the group.
    Downside: Constraining. May squash/under-utilize some player strengths & preferences.
    Fixes: Provide play framework (goals, purpose of play, social dynamics) that make the specified roles fun in themselves?

    \n There's a lot I need to respond to in this thread that I don't have the time to right now, but I just wanted to talk about another idea I had.

    Assign player-equivalents \u2014 some indication of the amount of effort it takes to do a given job \u2014 to different roles, and let people choose up to a total player-equivalent of 1. You can also find ways that given roles synergize well together, and thus are "discounted." For example:

    Director = .55 player equivalent
    "Script Girl" (plot continuity) = .3 player equivalent
    Actor = .4 player equivalent
    Extras = .4 player equivalent
    Protagonist = .55 player equivalent
    Antagonist = .10 player equivalent

    These are off-the-cuff values and likely to be wrong, and only represent one way of sectioning things. You could structure these jobs differently for a different kind of game.

      Posted By: Christopher WeeksThe more I think about it, the more I doubt that it's a functional model. But it was an idea.
      \nShit, didn't mean to shoot anything down. The Uni economy could be used as a starting point - "role-playing an antagonist" - 1 coin to take control, n points to add coins, etc could easily add up to 10; "introducing a plot twist" - the various characters and components and traits in the plot twist could easily add up to 55.

      Some kinds of GMing I might want to get "paid" for somehow. If I'm providing adversity instead of getting to play my own character, for example. (And Uni kind of does this by giving you a coin profit when you initiate complications.) Or being caterer. (How about you bring snacks or drink - you get coins!)
      Or knowing a complex rules system. (I don't know how you can get paid enough for that.)
