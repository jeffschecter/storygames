\n What stuff should we watch, read, play?\n
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
\n
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.
\nRock,

    \n [D&D Encounters Video 2]\n
    Posted By: DanielSolisPosted By: jenskotD&D Encounters Video
    \n[1] This deserves a bump. This is the first time I've seen a video that accurately depicted average gamers and still made D&D seem like a fun, exciting activity. (It's so good that I'm setting aside my nitpicks on whether it's a storytelling game!) Compared to some of the previous examples of game company video promos in the past 30 years, it's a great leap forward.

    Yes!

    My only nitpick is I would like to see a player describe anything. Even as simple as, "I swing my sword." There are brief moments in the video where a player may actually be describing something but there is no sound, except when the GM describes something. To be fair, I know quite a few players, new and old, who prefer this style\u2026 player rolls, GM describes. It may be way less intimidating for new players. A common fear heard is, "do I have to know how to act?" At the same time, you get players who do what to describe something or roleplay in character and the GM at these events can squash their attempts and enthusiasm. But Encounters is meant to wrap up in an hour, and D&D 4E combat takes an hour, so there needs to be a level of streamlining to meet the time constraints.

    The video reminds me of a commercial for a casino. I actually wouldn't mind seeing D&D made into a casino game (speaking as someone who doesn't enjoy casino gambling). Maybe D&D a bit more like Castle Ravenloft / Wrath of Ashadralon where if 1 player dies, the group fails and instead of treasure, you have a money pot.

      \n [heavy metal 2]
      Basically, they are confirming what all gamers know but ignore - armor is heavy and takes energy to move. People didn't wear armor all the time, they put it on for combat (battle or standing guard), and then took it off. The key finding here is that armor that excludes the legs (I'm thinking Spartans here) are going to allow for more efficient movement and reduced fatigue. The n = 4 is pretty usual for this type of study but I would have chosen volunteers younger than 36+/-4 years, perhaps from the British military. The choice of having them carry a broad sword in both hands seems like it would be inefficient and might off-center gait; personally, I'd prop the sword on one shoulder like a soldier with a rifle. I imagine that non-standardization of armor at the time makes it hard to generalize. I'm curious what other measures could be looked at in terms of how much the armor unbalances you and how different styles of armor compare in terms of increased fatigue and ground reaction forces.
