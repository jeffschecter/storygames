    \n I think you're right, Tim. Doctor Who is a peculiarity, without a direct US analog for comparison. The show isn't to us Yanks what it is to Brits, but it is close insofar as our closest analog to Doctor Who is... Doctor Who on PBS or BBC America. Doctor Who isn't a cultural mirror for Star Trek or the Battlestars Galactica, it is its own thing and, with it, so are the Daleks, especially of late.

    Now, if you wanted to draw a parallel to US television series on a case-by-case basis, then the Borg were rather to Star Trek: The Next Generation and its spin-offs as the Daleks were to Doctor Who, in that they were deemed must-see villains (until they were overused) with lots of symbolic baggage and a dose of personal grudgery. Spike was Buffy's Dalek, I think that's apt (within the tolerances that such a comparison requires).

    I'll admit, my knowledge of Doctor Who prior to Eccleston is pretty weak, so I don't know how much the modern Dalek's relationship with the Doctor resembles that of, if I may, yore. So maybe my sense of the Dalek is front-list heavy. As you can see in this thread, the Borg were run into the ground. How the Daleks have avoided that fate, I don't exactly understand.

    Cheers,
    Will

    \n Star Trek is the only American sci-fi series to have anything like the iconic status that Doctor Who does in the UK. Klingons absolutely fit the well-beloved, popular aspects of what you're asking\u2014as well as the qualifier "you don't get XXX every week [despite Worf] but next week is XXX week and you must tune in because XXX are fun"\u2014but they were never scary the way Daleks were (meant to be*) scary. The Borg are much more like the Daleks in terms of scariness and they're fairly popular villains, but not as popular as the Klingons.

    No other American TV show has had the longevity, history, and popularity to compare. Original Galactica was on too briefly and was always a niche compared to Star Trek; rebooted BSG was on too recently to have had that kind of impact, plus the effect of the robotic Cylons was totally supplanted by the humanoid ones. Firefly is similar\u2014way too brief, niche, and too recent. Babylon 5 was too niche, and although PsiCop Alfred Bester comes close in "fun and scary", he's not beloved the way kids love the Daleks. Buffy had popularity, but at 7 seasons nothing like the longevity of Star Trek or Doctor Who, and nothing like the Daleks in the sense of being a recurring but only occasional threat\u2014Spike doesn't count because he became a series regular, as well as a (reluctant) member of the Scooby Gang. (Both Babylon 5 and Buffy also don't fit because they're aimed at adult, or at least teenage, audiences, while Doctor Who is aimed at adolescents/families.)

    The Joker is probably the best fit overall: even ignoring the comic-book origins, in TV and films he's been around about as long as Doctor Who or Star Trek, starting with the '60s Batman series and going through various animated series and film series; he's a recurring villain but used only occasionally, not as constant as Klingons or even the Borg; he's generally presented as a threat on par with Batman, in the same way that the Daleks are Doctor Who's ultimate villains; he's both fun and scary because of his criminal insanity; and as a result he's got enough popularity to match the Daleks.

    Zombies are disqualified only because, not having ever been occasional recurring serious threats on an iconic TV series, they don't meet Graham's later qualifier in post 9 "you don't get XXX every week, so next week is a must-see because XXX are featured." I also have a vague sense that zombies are way more popular with adults than with kids\u2014after all, zombie films are all aimed at adults\u2014so they also don't quite match up to Daleks in that sense. However, Jared is correct: if it weren't for the "must-see episode" qualifier, zombies would be exactly what Graham's looking for.

    (*For the record, Daleks were never scary until the new series\u2014but, I was already around 8 or 9 when I started seeing Doctor Who (Tom Baker era), so maybe I would've thought they were scary if I'd seen them when I was 4. That first Eccleston episode with the Dalek though was the first time I really got the idea that yes, they are scary implacable killing machines.)
