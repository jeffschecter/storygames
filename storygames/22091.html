Here are some quotes from the the \u201cTrad Reformation\u201d thread:

For me, the third option (OSR [and proto-OSR like the Knights of the Dinner Table] and Story Now games being the two other ones) has been neo-trad games a la Apocalypse World and Blades in the Dark.

and

Although I think the \u201cneo-trad\u201d games (like Apocalypse World, Blades in the Dark, maybe FATE, and others) seem to be mostly derived from the story game tradition, curving back towards traditional play rather than the other way around.


This something I\u2019ve seen said on this forum a few times, which is that games like Apocalypse World and Blades in the Dark aren\u2019t \u201cStory Now\u201d games, but something more traditional (or Neo-traditional).

So I guess I don\u2019t understand what counts as \u201cStory Now\u201d. I\u2019ve read Ron Edwards essays, I\u2019ve lurked on forums, and from how I understand it, Story Now is a style/agenda/whatever in which:

A.) The main goal of play is to create an meaningful narrative that at least approaches what could be considered a \u201cgood story\u201d (i.e. Characters do something and are changed by the experience, there is some kind of premise addressed, and the structure at least of sort resembles a traditional story with rising action/climax/denouement, and there is a satisfying conclusion of some sort, with \u201cmeaningful\u201d, \u201cpremise\u201d and \u201csatisfying\u201d being highly subjective terms.)

B.) The story created is in no way prewritten or controlled by one member of the play group. Player decisions control where the story goes, and there is no foregone conclusion or expectations of moving towards a particular outcome. You \u201cPLAY TO FIND OUT\u201d. It is shared creation of the narrative that happens organically.

C.) Decisions made (in play and in design) prioritize story-creation (as described in A.) over the immersion in/exploration of the shared imaginary world (Simulationism/The Right to Dream) or the overcoming of challenges by the players (Gamism/Step On Up).

So when I run a PbtA game, for instance my own fantasy hack (SHAMELESS SELF-PROMOTION!), I think it conforms to the above. The narrative is improvised on the spot, based on the characters\u2019 starting premisses and driven by their decisions, and the mechanics are designed around pushing the story forward (altering the narrative in one interesting/meaniful direction or the other) rather than \u201cbalance\u201d or \u201cverisimilitude\u201d.

I find this to be true of most PbtA games, Fate, and Blades in the Dark... or I think I do?

( I\u2019ve seen some criticism of Dungeon World on this front that it \u201cdoesn\u2019t do PbtA right\u201d, but in this case let\u2019s leave that for another discussion and take PbtA to mean games like Apocalypse World, Monsterhearts, and Masks - games I think most would agree \u201cget\u201d PbtA.)

So, what am I not seeing?

Am I wrong about what Story Now means?

If not, how do the games mentioned not qualify?

What games out there do qualify as Story Now? (And how are they different?)

    \n The terminology itself is rather arbitrary. For the record, I would never call something like AW a "neo-trad" game myself, despite using the term in my own rpg history writing; I reserve that term for games that are fundamentally traditional while distinguishing themselves from earlier stages of the tradition by adopting minor ideas and influence from progressive design. Generally published by mid-size houses like FFG, Mongoose and such. AW is in cultural history terms a straightforwardly Forgite indie game in comparison. You might as well call Sorcerer a "neo-trad" game. The argument for AW being trad basically amounts to it having character classes and a GM, as far as I can see.

    Apocalypse World is rather clearly a narrativist game, which maybe makes it a "Story Now" game as well? As people enmeshed in the PbtA movement should know, the "PbtA" label in itself doesn't mean much, so there's no guarantee that any given game under that rubric is also "Story Now" or "narrativist", but for AW itself I don't see any controversy - insofar as rpg theory even is a thing, such that any analytical claims can be made, AW is as narrativist or Story Now as just about any game you'd care to name.

    That said, Jason is of course right in saying that you don't have to play AW - or any other game - in a narrativist way. Games are only "Story Now" in the sense that they support and harmonize with the intent to play them in that way, not in a mandatory sense where it would be impossible to do something else with the game instead.

    In general, though, I would suggest that juxtaposition between an analytical theory term like "Story Now" and a cultural history concept like "neo-trad" will probably only get confusing if you insist on treating them like somehow equivalent categories.

    The fact that "Story Now" is something of a marketing label that Ron coined for his own narrativist game design doesn't help the confusion. You could understand is as strictly a synonym of "narrativism" in the Big Model sense, or you could understand it to mean "the particular kind of narrativist game that Ron Edwards makes", and I frankly don't know which way you should go. Is Prince Valiant a "Story Now" game?

    In the end it's just a stupid terminology squabble. Probably not worth worrying over unless you're doing marketing and need to establish cultural context.

    \n Here I am, your friendly neighborhood anarchist, reminding you that categories do not exist and objects in the world owe no fealty to any reductionist schema. To put it in philosophical terms: there are no Pure Forms, and objects are differentiated by exhibiting their own unique combinations of Qualia. But further: that these qualia do not adhere to any particular scale, field, or schema among themselves. Worse than comparing apples and oranges, this is more like comparing apples and flying.

    Thus, the approach taken to the classification of complex systems should be more molecular than categorical. There will always be multiple axes along which to parse these objects, many of which not be reliably mappable to the qualia of other objects in the sample. The resulting taxonomy will resemble a multi-dimensional collection of "tags" (which themselves will exhibit a lot of gapping and overlapping along different dimensions), and will still fail to define a universal and contiguous spectrum.

    And from a creative standpoint, that's good news.

    \n Neurotrash, you're right about Story Now except for (C) in your OP. The main distinction between the RGFA Threefold and the Forge's Creative Agenda (i.e., GNS) is that where the Threefold imagined Gamism, Dramatism, and Simulationism to be describing priorities for individual decision-making at given moments of play, the Big Model strictly speaking saw GNS as serving as a communal (group level) orientation to the game over longer arcs of play or "reward cycles." So an orientation toward Story Now didn't mean that individuals made decisions based on what was "good for the story"; rather, it meant what generated the most payoff or satisfaction for the group as a group was the sense that the arc of play as a whole was meaningful--versus faithful to its source (Right to Dream), versus challenging to its players (Step On Up). A group's Creative Agenda doesn't have to be all-encompassing; it just has to emerge as an overarching orientation toward the game.

    \n AW is definitely Story-Now-supporting (aka "narrativism!"). However, contrast it with something like My Life With Master or Fiasco or Polaris or Primetime Adventures. All those other four games have a lot of explicit structuring around scenes, scene framing, who has authority when, etc. And those rules are necessary to the way those games go about supporting Story Now play.

    Lady Blackbird, Burning Wheel, Shadow of Yesterday, Riddle of Steel, and a whole other strand of games don't necessarily mess with the GMing authority structure, but have whole economies based on character flags. Flags are basically things on the character sheet that tell the rest of the play group, but especially the GM, what the player is interested in exploring regarding that character.

    So, AW doesn't just "have a GM and character classes." It's also flag-less, has no explicit scene structure, etc., so it doesn't really require players to break actor stance in the way that earlier Forgite games did. That's why neo-trad is a reasonable label for it, though it's certainly very explicit in the text about rejecting certain of the most common aspects of trad play, especially GM fiat.

    Personally, I don't love the neo-trad label. I just set out to examine what was happening in RPG design *outside* of indie circles in 2000-2006 or so, and called that the Trad Reformation.

    \n I\u2019m with @AsIf on this one; there are trends and similarities between games that share cultural influences, but ultimately you can make an argument to draw the line almost wherever you wish.

    @Deliverator explains the reasoning for labeling something like AW \u201cneo-trad\u201d quite well: compared to \u201cstory games\u201d of the same time period, it has relatively few obvious markers of a typical \u201cStory Now\u201d game, a traditional GM role, and no explicit directions towards Theme and moral conflict, no explicit scene economy or scene framing rights, etc, etc - and it\u2019s true, I\u2019ve run AW for trad gamers and they don\u2019t \u201cnotice\u201d that they\u2019re playing anything different.     \n If you are rewarded in-game ... that is, IN the actual fiction ... for something, then it's an intrinsic reward. So if you like to act in character, the reward should be more opportunities to act in character, not a +1 to your skill roll or a point to spend later. Giving that kind of extrinsic reward does either nothing, or CAN decrease the excitement for acting in character. Not everyone sees the reward as a tool of communication; they actually see the reward ... as a reward separated from the act, and when they start to try to earn the reward instead of enjoying the act in the first place, the intrinsic reward for doing it decreases.

    The two exceptions of when extrinsic rewards influencing the intrinsic rewards negatively, and instead can affect the person's intrinsic reward positively:
    \u2022\xa0If the reward is a social one. Like, "I notice you". Likes in Facebook are extremely powerful.
    \u2022 If the reward is part of a competition. Like, a combat.
    \u2022\xa0The task is boring to begin with.

    People need to understand this, instead of just designing with rewards in mind because that's what a really successful roleplaying game has been doing for forty years. Where the designers who is designing rewards in the same matter haven't realized that that the said popular game's reward system is successful because the whole game is \u2013 in its mechanical design \u2013\xa0about competition.

    \n Ya'll, an intrinsic motivation means something very specific in the psych literature. Intrinsic rewards are internal to a single person's psyche. Satisfaction from a job well done. The joy of learning something new. The sense of belonging that comes from interacting with friends and community.

    The GM giving Alice a fate point is very, very, very, very much not intrinsic to Alice. You wanna say otherwise, you're making up a new definition for "intrinsic" and attaching it to a word with an established meaning, in a rather confusing way.

    The full story on extrinsic reward / intrinsic motivation is indeed a lot more complicated than "X destroys Y," and it's much more subtle than "americans vs other folks." The nature of the interaction is more mediated by a person's internal framework for motivation, and whether external rewards are seen as affirming internal choices or imposing external constraints. (Note that a single reward framework can usually be viewed both ways, depending on mindset.)

    But the fact is, the interaction between extrinsic reward and intrinsic motivation is A Thing. A Complicated Thing. "My game gives points that encourage behavior Foo" is often completely insufficient to actually encourage behavior Foo; for at least some people, it will actively discourage behavior Foo.

    Here's two mechanics:

    Every time a player is forced to make a hard bargain or ugly choice, give them Inspiration.

    When the players turn to you to see what happens next, give them a hard bargain or ugly choice.

    How are they similar? How are they different?

      \n Those are good questions. I think that the real reward here is mainly social, but, also, in part because of the way the XP feeds back into future play (by giving your character greater or different abilities, which allows you to pursue greater challenges in the future).

      The key, I think, is that the group has already established the value of a particular challenge (in this case, finding gold in death traps, let\u2019s say), and thereby challenged the player to attempt something risky. When the player steps up to the challenge and carries it off, the XP reward is a way to communicate achievement on the social level, as well.

      So, if there was a sense that there was no real risk involved, the power of the reward would vanish. If there was a sense that it was arbitrary - or given after the fact, without expectations being established beforehand - the reward would also lack meaning and serve, at best, as a very weak incentive. If there was a sense that 5000 XP wasn\u2019t legitimately ten times more dangerous or risky or time-consuming, then there wouldn\u2019t be any real difference between 500 and 5000. That would be another failure case.       I think that the points and the numbers only work and mean something when the players have all agreed to consistently connect them to real in-game events.

      I can easily imagine a player saying, for example, that they felt the challenge was too easy, and therefore they decline the award. The points mean nothing by themselves, in other words, but are a signaling mechanism for risk, challenge, success, courage, smarts, and social reinforcement.

      Paul_T said:

      @Eero_Tuovinen, I\u2019d love to hear why you think that AW is clearly a Narrativist game. (For what it\u2019s worth, I don\u2019t disagree at all, but I find the design fairly subtle about in a number of important respects, and the book isn\u2019t explicit about any kind of theme-hunting or moral choices in the way that, say, Sorcerer is. Traditional gamers seem to pick up AW and \u201crecognize it as one of their own\u201d, instead of squinting at it as a foreign entity built by swine. )

      \nThere is no inherent reason why a game couldn't be both narrativist and recognizable to trad gamers at the same time. They're perpendicular properties independent of each other. Specifically, being narrativist doesn't require you to have strange rules; well-crafted trad rules can work just fine, depending on what exactly you're trying to do.

      AW is clearly narrativist because the most natural read of what it's about on the immediate level is "how does your character slot into the post-apocalyptic landscape as a person performing a social role", and on the wider campaign level "what happened to the world, and what are we doing about it now". These are literary premise questions, the precise sort that defines narrativism. The game doesn't try to answer them in advance, and doesn't encourage any of the players to do so; it merely asks you to play to find out. Couldn't be much clearer about being narrativist.

      Whether this qualifies for "story now" or "story game" or "neo-trad" or whatever depends on what you mean by those words, but the narrativism bit is only in question if you read the same game text I did and somehow came to a different conclusion about its subject matter.

      And, of course: if somebody plays AW in a non-narrativist way, then that's what they do, and good for them if it works for them. My saying that "game X is narrativist" does not mean here, and cannot possibly mean, anything more than "I think that the natural creative interest in playing this game is a narrativistic one". I could possibly see a Sim read of the game, just barely, even if it doesn't seem nearly as natural as a Nar read does.

      \n Yeah, 2097, Deliverator and I are all on the same page. Exactly!

      Eero,

      I don't disagree with any of that! Thanks. I think what makes it potentially "neo-trad" (which is not a term I particularly recognize as meaningful, for the record, depending on who's talking and how they want to define it) is how less overt it is about its play priority.

      I remember when it came out that it was a shock to a lot of Forgite gamers. We asked, "where are the character Flags?", and "these rules don't resolve conflicts! Is this task resolution again?", and "where are the reward cycles for addressing premise?", and so on and so forth. Stylistically, it's a bit of a hybrid, including all kinds of things not typically associated with Narrativist story games, like hit points, weapon stats (gun porn), sandbox-like GM prep (as opposed to Kickers and Bangs or defined moral boundaries and escalation - compare to other Narrativist games from the period which usually instruct the GM to "figure out what the players care about and then prepare dilemmas for them and escalate, escalate, escalate! Force them to take a stand on the issue!"), and escalating character powers which increase their stats and damage output.

      Clearly, it works as a Narrativist game, but I think it marks a significant departure from game design up to that point, and started quite a new trend (games like Blades in the Dark seem to appeal to traditional gamers in ways that My Life with Master, Polaris, Grey Ranks, Dogs in the Vineyard, etc, never did).

      Even The Riddle of Steel had its Spiritual Attributes, which were a fairly overt "indie/Forge/story game" type of thing. Apocalypse World is remarkable in the way that it has no (or almost no) overt player-facing "premise addressing" stuff at all, and even on the GM side, it's kind of subtle and presented in different language. (For instance, we get "be a fan of the players' characters" instead of "the PCs are the Protagonists of the story; make sure the story is always about them".)

      I agree with you (you've said this before, I believe) that AW is sort of a spiritual successor to The Shadow of Yesterday; although TSoY has more overt "author stance" kind of business in it - especially Keys and BDTP - it has a lot of very traditional elements, from character building (Secrets!) to a traditional GM role.

      Back in 2007, John Harper said:

      "TSOY has the most beautiful alchemy to it. It straddles the traditional/story-game divide and delivers something so pure and good that it is now the thing I point to when someone asks me what an RPG is."

      In 2010, @jenskot wrote a nice mini-essay on the waves Apocalypse World made on the scene, and how a lot of "indie gamers" were confused by its design (I was one of them!). Although he is very tongue-in-cheek, his "lies" were all based on real people's reactions on the "indie scene" to Vincent's new game:

      http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/13081/apocalypse-world-lies/p1

      \n To this day, AW is the game I see causing the most performance anxiety among GMs who are considering running a new game. The hybrid nature of its design is yes, close enough to Trad to make sense on one level, but different enough (read: qualitative and ambiguous enough) to send you running in circles making sure you grok it well enough not to "botch it" and embarrass yourself. In a way, its lack of utter difference makes it harder to discern its subtler differences.

      In part - and I can speak only of edition 1 here since I haven't seen ed 2 - some of this trouble is due to the book's terrible organization. But that's just a sidepoint, since like most GMs preparing for their first MC performance, I remember reading and rereading and querying and googling for literally *days*, worried that there was some key structural element that I was missing, or that I would take the "wrong" artistic/philosophical stance, in short, that I would fuck it up.

      Of course, with help from Paul and others here and on other forums, I finally did it, and rocked it. But no other game has ever given me such trepidation.
