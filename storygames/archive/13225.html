\n I was writing another version of my Matrix Game rules the other day - trying to boil it down a little more (I've got it to 2 pages) when I thought to look at other games on my shelf.

I pulled down Hack Master which is basically 1980's D+D. I know how to play D+D but when I looked at it from the lens of a novice trying to figure out how to play I found the book baffling. Page after page of text on subjects with tables and pictures and nothing that said "When you play do this..." It read like a hodge podge of rules. I know it is a joke parody of AD+D so I didn't think much of it. But when I looked at my 3E Player's Handbook I was also flooded with text (but also more understandable rules). The old White Box D+D (that I started playing in the 70's) wasn't much better. There is one and a half pages that show a dialogue between the referee and the caller.

I work so hard to explain just how to play my game that it boggles the imagination that so many successful games don't. The only way a person could learn how to play them is to be shown. I wonder if Indie RPGs have that problem. I know that though I've written rules as simply as I can that has always been a weakness of my games.

I guess what I'm interested in is what people's takes on other games are. What games fail to tell how to play. Which ones work and why? Please approach it from the point of view of a novice reading the game rather than being shown how to play by a GM.

Chris Engle

    \n Dan,

    That's pretty interesting since I consider Dogs in the Vineyard one the better texts in this area. I personally kind of have a minimum bar for such things. My minimum bar is basically a clear instruction on each player's roles and goals. For example Burning Empires hits the minimum bar because it tells the Game Master (a) he should play to win the Infection mechanic and (b) his fictional contributions should challenge the PC's Beliefs. HOWEVER, it doesn't tell you HOW to do either of those things. There are few if any strategy tips for the Infection and there is ZERO discussion on crafting situations that challenge a PC's Beliefs. The reason that I don't fault the text for the latter omissions is because those are the RPG equivalent of strategy guides for board games. A board game tells its procedures and what the goal of the game is but rarely, if ever, goes out of its way to give you pointers on what the most effective way to do that is. That's left up to actual discovery through play and practice. I'm fine with that.

    So Dogs in the Vineyard tells you to (a) prep a town and gives you a process and (b) tells you to play the town and (c) tells you not to play God. That meets my minimum bar. It's pretty amazing how many games DON'T meet that minimum bar. Diaspora being a recent example of a text that doesn't meet my minimum bar.

    I can talk about going beyond the minimum bar especially for GM-prep/play heavy narrative games like Dogs in the Vineyard and Sorcerer but I don't want to derail the thread. If anyone is interested let me know and I'll start another thread or continue discussion here if it's not off topic.

    \n Over on the Roleplaying Rewards thread, somebody linked to a really interesting video about incentives given by an economist. He talked about 3 factors that go into people being satisfied with their endeavors, and one of those things was "mastery". In other words, people like getting good at stuff that they find meaningful (like playing guitar, or knitting, or whatever). I think certain RPG designs purposely reward/encourage a pursuit of 'mastery' (hell, Gary Gygax wrote a book about it), and that in these instances, the approach Jesse attributes to Luke makes a lot of sense. If that's the case with a game, figuring out how it works is part of the pleasure (as with the comparison given earlier about board games and strategy games).

With RPGs, you have the issue that sometimes 'lack of mastery' can make the game fall flat when everyone involved is a novice player, since so much of what goes on in the game is always necessarily left to the group (that's kind of the whole point, right?). For my personal preference, I like things that expect and require some agency on my part, some 'figuring it out' being necessary and so forth, but some strong procedural structures pointing in the right direction are awfully nice (so, yeah, I like the Dog's town creation system a lot, and I love the AW MCing rules).

    \nHi Chris,

    I didn't know how to play indie games* until last year, so I can tell you explicitly the games I had trouble understanding:

    + PTA -> I had had no idea, from the book, about when a group was prescribing too much story, and how to bring conflict into a scene.
    + IaWA -> I had no idea, from the book, that I shouldn't lay down plans (and that I should learn how to roll with the story).
    + 3:16 -> I had no idea, from the book, how I was supposed to frame non-combat scenes or how much the players were to drive the action.
    + Gun Thief -> I had no idea, from the book, how the Jagged Women character was supposed to tempt the Gun Thief.
    + Universalis -> I had no idea, from the book, that it wasn't a pile of crunch.
    + Lady Blackbird -> I had no idea, from the book, what 'hitting a Key' meant.

    On counterpoint, the games (from the earlier days) I understood (I was able to play these games and have fun, without doing any research):

    + Spirit of the Century -> The book offered guidelines about how to do what I was already familiar with, but in a new way.
    + Don't Rest Your Head -> The book had nice, long examples.
    + InSpectres -> The rules were easy, there was good reference, and it didn't as me to do something I wasn't already comfortable doing\u2014it didn't leave any glaring question marks or doubts.

    In a sense, the familiarity that games book provide, by using examples or by dancing cleverly on already trodden ground, gives me confidence to go ahead without research. When games ask me to remember too many new things or ask me to perform in a new way without telling me how, I get scared, and, therefore, lose my enthusiasm.

    And lately, the games I feel I've been able to follow, but haven't tried yet:

    + A Taste for Murder
    + Mars Colony
    + Montsegur 1244
    + Love in the Time of Seid


    * Come on, you know what I mean...

    \n So I'm thinking that playing RPGs is a bit like cooking. it's a mix of art and science, some people are more skilled at it than others, and it's generally wise to start with a recipe when you're trying something new.

    Some folks have a lot of experience, feel confident with their skills, or are just generally willing to experiment (and fail). Others like to follow the recipe to the letter, following exact steps in hopes that the cake won't fall flat. If it works, they can be bolder the next time and try throwing in more of their own style. If it fails, they can (hopefully, if the recipe writer can be trusted) assume that they missed something important and can go back to the cookbook to figure it out.

    RPG rules should support both approaches, just like cookbooks do. The science is in the original rules- tell them exactly what steps to take to reliably produce fun the first time. After that the art begins, when they start experimenting and bringing in their own style and creativity.

    Some people are just wired for the science, and need a clear process. Others are wired differently and inclined to throw in handfuls of random ingredients right from the get-go, just to see what happens. Those folks will still appreciate a clear process to use as a benchmark, but they will also like suggestions for new directions they can take ("using corn meal instead of flour isn't recommended" or"try putting bacon in your cookies!").

    Game designers are, by definition, of the experimenting mind-set. It can be hard to understand folks who don't understand a rule like "do this until it's enough" because that's how we do it. Some folks need to know what enough looks like, so even if you're inclined to write the bare minimum of rules and let the players suss it out for themselves in play, it doesn't hurt to show them at least one successful path\u2014maybe the way you did it, in your last game\u2014to give them a good starting point.

    \n A lot of trad RPG's are like cooking stew, you already know how to make stew, here are some new ingredients and new techniques, but ultimately it's the same stew you've had for dinner a hundred times before. And that can be great.

    However, baking is chemistry, and improvisation gets you objectively bad bread. Some story games are going for very specific things, and you when you get that wrong, the result is flat and unenjoyable. Most of Vincent Baker's games are like this, and I find it really helpful to read his games closely, follow the instructions closely, and read a lot of discussion about them to understand the implications. But when I do that, the results are very tasty.

I learned to play D+D by seeing my brother Nate run it. I think he learned out to run it from some news articles on D+D that we got along with the rules in 1976 - that and that one page example of dialogue between the referee and caller in the third booklet. After that most RPGs followed the same rough format. GM tells you the situatiion and anks what you do. The players say what they do. Each one gets to do this. The GM either says yes or makes them roll (a skill, a combat roll, against a stat, etc.) The GM says what the other guys do next and rolls for them (but we have to trust their rolls because they are secret) and the procedure repeats. The only game that differed from that format in the 70's was "En Garde!" by Game Designers Workshop. It was a lot more explicit (which makes sense since they published a lot of board games).

    Jason Morningstar said that not telling people how to play could be a feature. I'd like to hear more about that. I'm unclear on how vagueness about procedure is a virtue.

    I understand how you don't want to tell people the best strategy but I see that as very different than describing procedure. I tell people "Make up a scene" or ask the question "What happens next?" This can be anything so I'm not dictating what has to happen - anything they say is their scene - but the flow of what people do is clear. I like the point that if the play procedures are not clear that one feels uncomfortable enough to never play the game. It is like Temple Granden's Autism insights about cows and slaughter houses. She walks through the shute and tells them what cows will be afraid of so the remove those things. If the rules are a wall of text I'm scared off any more.

    I wonder how much the wall of text phenomena (far from a new trend in gaming) had to do with needing to flesh out a book so it looks like it is giving more even if it's fluff.

    \n This thread is a good example of the complexity of game systems.

    They have many different mechanics that converge on a given outcome from different directions. So a player can get her done lots of ways. That is like real life - and thus confusing. When you add in multiple rules complexity multiplies by much more. You can get some real unexpected outcomes. From Jason's perspective on exploration this could be the source of days/months of fun. From the point of view of the newbie it might mean the difference between trying or never trying a certain game.

    I'm thinking about limiting my initial rules to the two pages of very simple procedures and only introduce the variations (three pages) after they have played a game. I've observed that players have a strong dislike of 1. Remembering sequences of events, 2. Making decisions, and 3. Making mental gear shifts. So the more we ask them to do this (and switching from one resolution method to another is a gear shift) the more people are going to hit the wall.

    \n For a long time, I've favored style guides over strict rules when it comes to conveying how to run and play RPGs. It's not that I think RPGs shouldn't talk frankly and clearly about how to play them\u2014they should\u2014but that lots of good advice of the sort can be collected into books that are separate from RPGs.

    What I'm wary of, though, is the impulse to badmouth older games for not being as explicit in their GM instructions as we've since seen other games be. It's great when a game book can be explicit and insightful and innovative when teaching GMing, and we should learn from these games and get better at teaching our craft, but it's not as if teaching GMing is easy. We all have the luxury of hindsight and of learning from mistakes made by (and by playing) those RPGs that were less explicit in their advice or instructions.

    Teaching GMing is a little like teaching someone how to write a novel, design a video game, or run a TV series... in real time. A lot of what's important can be taught, but a lot of it can only be learned by doing. If it were easy to teach these things\u2014or to capture the formulas and conflicts of great drama, or distill genre down to dice pools\u2014we'd have caught and tamed the dramatic story a long time ago. I, for one, dread the day that we can bottle the great GM and sell it for $4.99 on the Kindle. I like talking shop, I like discovering new tricks and techniques, and I like tackling individual play situations as they occur and treating them distinctly. I like that it's an ongoing process and I fear the idea that we can nail down the magic. I'd rather see it move around that sit dead in a box like a pinned butterfly.

    Which, again, isn't to say that teaching how to GM is a bad idea. It's just to say that there's a place for the kind of GMing that just plain isn't easy to teach. I like that it's challenging, sometimes. I like my video games easy, but I like a bit of a challenge when I run an RPG.

    Maybe that's just me.

    Cheers,
    Will

    [Edit: I know I'm sparring against straw men, here, and I'm not saying that an RPG should hide the ball from anyone. I'm just saying.]
