\n I'm reflecting what could be a good dice-based resolve mechanic from a cinematic perspective.

The more I think about it, the more stats like skills etc. seem to be just marginal factors. They merely define what's possible or probable within the fiction of the setting, thus defining the context.
For example, an ex Navy Seal landing a helicopter is a plausible scenario. An ordinary nurse landing a helicopter is improbable and spectacular.

So, how do I define difficulty from cinematic perspetive?
1. Be honest: Do you really need a random resolve mechanic - or do you just avoid taking responsibility for the (collaborative) story?
2. Or put another way: Does the element of surprise make playing the specific scene more enjoyable than a straight decision?
3. Do you want something to succeed? Then just ditch the "fail" option and roll the stakes/price of success.
4. Give more space to the middle ground: partial success, concessions, hard choices

Basic parameters for difficulty from cinematic perspective:
- Scale of the scene (epic, minor) sets the stakes for success/failure.
- How long do you enjoy to see the character(s) struggling defines the probability of success.

Example 1 (ex Navy Seal landing helicopter):
I ditch failure completely. Instead, I try to come up with a fascinating *twist*.
Perhaps he's landing on a property he's not supposed to?
Perhaps thinks it a hostile chopper and starts shooting at it?

Example 2 (ordinary nurse landing helicopter):
She gets 20% for success (=helicopter wrecked, everyone survives).
She gets 20% for failure (=1 passengers dies, a few badly injured).
Rest of the 60% is partial success, possibly a hard choice.

What do you think?

    \n Rickard,

    If you want to keep uncertainty, then flipping a coin, rolling a die, or drawing a card are the quickest and easiest ways to do that. There are options that are potentially more interesting, like blind bids/votes, resource spends with hidden/opposed agendas, and all sorts of not knowing what the other players will do to a scene... but these tend to take longer and pull attention away from the flow of the fiction.

    For example: one player plays the Odds, one player plays Disaster, and one player plays Glory. When the nurse or seal tries to land the helicopter, Odds describes what's most likely to happen, Glory describes something much better, Disaster describes something much worse, and then they all spend tokens or cards or roll off to see which one of their visions prevails. Maybe some meta-mechanics about the story privilege or penalize different players at different times, a la the Tension and Survival tracks in Dead of Night. Neat, right? But a huge interruption from "we're envisioning the helicopter, it's getting close, OMG, will it land?!"

    I see nothing wrong with picking dice and working from there.

    This sounds interesting, but I see it only indirectly in your seal/nurse examples. If you define a concrete way to judge scale and length and a concrete way to translate those into stakes and probability, you might have something!

    One idea: have different resolution methods for different cinematic moments. So, like, rather than categories like "stealth" and "strength" or "physical" and "mental", you could have categories like "barely possible", "asking for trouble", "risking it all", "escaping certain death", etc. And then when you do something, we see which of these categories fits, and roll accordingly. So "barely possible" would have low probability of total success and all sorts of fallout, while "escaping certain death" would be guaranteed success but many different degrees of role reversal vs just barely escaping, etc.

    \n Here is an example of how I designed This is Pulp, which is a cinematic storytelling game.

    Design goals
    I want to challenge the players' creativity.
    I want to have the participants collaborate to gain a feeling of togetherness.
    I want the game to be short. 1-2 hours of gameplay.

    Requirements
    Uncertainty
    Challenge

    To achieve that, I use the participants knowledge about the tropes from action adventure movies. Movies like Indiana Jones, King Kong, Tintin, The Rocketeer, Sky Captain and the World of Tomorrow. Because if you know something, you're more keen on participating. Also, pulp lack prestige and can be silly and humoristic, which removes some of the anxiety that can come from being creative with other people (the thinking pattern that humor requires also enhances creativity, but that's another story).

    The game master set up scenes and create dangers in each scene. That person throws in a couple of tokens (the tokens are a pacing mechanics, allowing me to control how long the game takes). The players need to come up with as many ways of overcoming the dangers as tokens. The challenge is that they need to use their hero's abilities AND involve three side effects in their descriptions. So they need to take at most five different things into account:
    1. The situation itself (danger + environment).
    2. The abilities (also colored by the alternative 1930s world).
    3-5: the three side effects.

    It so happens that the player can choose to spend a resource to avoid some, or all, of the side effects, and a die roll is a required when they do that. The thing is, I mostly threw the dice in because some people like to roll dice. The game master then takes the player's description into account to tell how the situation gets worse, up until the very last token is taken for that scene. Then create a new scene with a new danger and new tokens.

    Did I need a dice mechanic? No. The uncertainty came from the game master's initial danger and the players' descriptions, and it certainly didn't break the flow because it was part of the gameplay.

    The tokens are, by the way, how I took @BeePeeGee's parameters into account.

    \n Rickard:
    It's essentially a differentiation between situations where I enjoy having dice and where I - when being honest - already know the story I want to tell.

    So (as Hopeless Wanderer suggested in a way), I don't want to be in total narrative control and enjoy the excitement of uncertainty, where it's reasonable.

    Reasons for dice are in this perspective:
    - (as Komradebob suggested): to choose betweeen two interesting options (i.e. burglary: what if I get away or get caught?)
    - to choose between stakes for a result I want (what will it cost me?)
    - come up with entirely random surprises (usually random tables)
    - creae a sense of peril (i.e. 5% chance I could be seriously wounded)

    David_Berg:
    I like hidden votes and resource management for a certain competitive style among the players (or their characters). For what I have in mind, a strong self-responsible collaborative approach is important. So, I prefer the group to come to a mutual agreement in conversation.

    David_Berg:
    The "length of suffering" is in my opinion very directly related to rate of success.
    To take the helicopter example: If you want him to just land and be done with, you don't roll and it's finished.
    When you roll for possible complications, you prolong the story arc with further detail ("wait, maybe he landed in dangerous territory?").
    I like your categories for the stakes. Each movie has different scenes where the stakes are higher or lower. This could fit in.

    phoenix182:
    Yes, it is really like an epiphany. I use very simple categories nowadays.
    For Supers, I like the "Worlds in Peril" approach of defining your powers simply as:
    Simple, Difficult, Borderline, Possible, Impossible

    \n The dice represent everything that is bigger than me, too complex to calculate, and therefore beyond my control. The dice represent Reality, which pushes back and tends not to go as you predicted, even when you thought you had considered everything.

    I might get hit by a meteorite today. What are the odds?
    I might want to rewrite every day so the odds are zero...
    but I can't because I don't control the meteorites.

    When I'm roleplaying I don't want to be a writer "up here". I want to be my character "down there", which means remaining within my character's consciousness and affective capabilities.
