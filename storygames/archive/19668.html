\n I wrote in my D&D AP thread:\n
In other words: we seemingly had a lot of choices, but only as long as we chose the pre-planned choices. As soon as we wanted to do something else, up came the rails.
\nTo clarify, here I meant the phrase \u201cUp came the rails\u201d as I\u2019ve often heard it in this context. Up came the restrictions and the strive to go to the prescribed goal. I didn\u2019t mean to stomp on the other thread and deliberately misuse their word \u201crails\u201d.

I want to clarify how I have used and heard used terms in the past.


This image is supposed to be wholly fractal: scenes themselves break down further (to subscenes, tasks, actions, intents) and scenarios themselves join up further (to sessions, adventures, campaigns). In other worlds, turtles all the way down and all the way up.\n
\n
Rail
The word rail is already polysemically used for both a railing (a barrier) and a rail (on which a train travels).
Hence \u201cup came the rails\u201d \u2014 up came the restrictions.
\n
Railroad
In the image above, the red line as it moves through several nodes is the railroad. If there are few or no other ways it could have taken, it\u2019s even more a railroad. If the scenes are pre-scripted, it\u2019s even more a railroad but that\u2019s not the defining factor.
\n
\u201cScene exit\u201d or \u201cscene out\u201d
My proposed term for what can end a scene and lead into the next. If I understand it correctly, these were called \u201crails\u201d in the other thread. These are the start of a single segment of the red line as it exits one node.
\n
Illusionism
Using deception about the way the game world is constructed (\u201cThe ogre is behind the second of the five doors the players open, regardless of the order they open them in, but the players play believing that other rules about the game is in place\u201d) or, more egregiously, about the way the game mechanics are used (\u201cThe dragon has no pre-set amount of hitpoints \u2014 it dies when I feel the players have been challenged enough, but the players play believing that other rules about the game are in place\u201d)
\n
Participationism
Players playing in the scenes as the GM presents them, not minding that they are following a preset sequence of scenes (with perhaps a few places where it can branch, or open up, and then go back on the same \u201cline\u201d). Perhaps they are doing this because some other aspect of the game (for example the combats, or the intra-party talking and joking) are interesting, or perhaps they are doing it enjoying the GM:s work.
\n
SAS, the Storytelling Adventure System Guide
The old White Wolf\u2019s system for writing up and interpreting pre-written scenes. They don\u2019t have clearly defined \u201cscene exits\u201d, just a \u201cscene goal\u201d, some issue that needs to be resolved.
\n
Fate Core
A version of Fate which on pp 225 through 249 lays out a guideline for a GM setting up and creating scenes in play, also based around resolving a question or goal rather than specified exits.
\n
Colossal Cave a.k.a. Adventure
An early single-player IF game (which evolved into Zork). \u201cRooms\u201d are described briefly and the most salient exits are listed along with, and this is interesting to me, the most salient and interactable objects and their state. Its brevity is fantastic. In the later Infocom derivations you can often get more detail by promting for such \u2014 including raising the saliency of objects and features otherwise mentioned in passing or completely glossed over.
\n
PMT: Porte-monstre-tr\xe9sor
A French view of dungeon crawling: \u201cDoor, monster, treasure\u201d. Interesting to me: it can be rephrased as a detailed and complex spatial location or social situation, combined with interesting obstacles with more than one way to overcome, combined with some reason for engaging with them. My experiences so far have been good and this is a way of play that I\u2019d like to explore further, and the reason why I have recently taken a break from the more scene-framing theories and styles of play (such as PbtA and even my once-beloved Fate).
\n
\nAgain, I can\u2019t know for certain that the DM in yesterday\u2019s session had a prescribed goal in mind, since I haven\u2019t looked at the written scenario \u2014 it could\u2019ve been an improvised sequence of nodes, something that is improvised but ends up feeling like prescripted sequence of nodes).

    \n The second meaning of the word "rails" (i.e. "railings") also comes up if you ever take a child bowling. They can raise the "rails" (railings) on either side of the lane, preventing the ball from falling into the gutter. One can see how the word became polysemic (noyce word, that!): in both senses, forward motion is governed by the rail(s). The difference is in how much play the ball has while traveling its course. Meaning 1 directs the ball's course absolutely, while Meaning 2 provides a "heuristic" system with impenetrable boundaries. This might be a metaphor worth mining.

    The kids' bowling example suggests another notion: We may tend to think of these boundaries as being a defensive line for the GM's purposes ("plot" the typical jewel being protected). But some boundaries might be placed to prevent the PCs from getting into trouble they're not ready to handle yet. "Don't confront the Boss before you get the sword" kinda thing. Not because of pacing or plot really, but simply because the GM knows that if they face that guy now, they're dead.

    \n This is the first time I hear about Illusionism operating necessarily with consent. While Illusionism with consent is possible (that's what games that encourage GM fudging advocate, insofar as the players are expected to read and believe those parts of the rulebook), I don't see how consent would be mandatory. Certainly discussion has generally not presumed consent in Illusionist cases, actual play cases have usually not reported consent, and my own experiences are also consent-less: when I've GMed or played in Illusionistic games, it has been without player knowledge of the specific extent of the GM's powers, and thus without them being able to grant consent.

    The term "railroading", on the other hand, has never been much used for accurate theory work that I know. If I were to give the term a precise definition, I certainly wouldn't say that it is force without consent, but rather that it's goal-oriented use of GM powers (force or otherwise), which is often considered non-consensual due to that type of usage being illegitimate (even if available) in the games where this becomes a problem. That seems to fit the archetypal use cases much better than the idea that railroading is necessarily non-consensual, and any use of force might apply. (As a clarifying example, I would be surprised if a GM who killed a PC because they didn't like the player was characterized as "railroading" in a natural dialogue; the term is usually reserved for situations where the GM is manipulating play to achieve their desired plot, after all, putting the PCs "on rails" in a metaphorical manner instead of letting them choose their own direction. Dragonlance is railroading, but surely not any and all force use is.)

    Let\u2019s talk about the black curtain here.
    There are a few reasons why a GM might hide information.

    Let\u2019s say they have a prepared map of a dungeon and its contents. Part of the mode of play is exploration. Exploration is enhanced by the players not knowing the map of the dungeon and where the items are, which items are there. It\u2019s also enhanced by not giving the stats for the monsters they meet. Let\u2019s call this mode \u201cMode X\u201d. \u201cMode X\u201d means that the location and its contents are defined ahead of time but kept secret to the players.

    Most players enter a dungeon crawl knowing that parts of this information will initially be secret.

    Now, \u201cthe black curtain\u201d and \u201cillusionism\u201d has sometimes been defined as GMs disguising things as if they were prepared according to one mode, for example as \u201cMode X\u201d, but in reality there are other factors leading to how those things operate.
