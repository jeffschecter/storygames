\n Been on my mind lately. If someone with better forum-fu than me could find the old threads and link them, that'd be great.

So, the quality of the GM / facilitator is probably the single most important factor in whether or not an RPG session will be an overall positive experience. But, the reasons why it's hard to get paid to GM are manifold and manifest:

1) Subjectivity \u2014 this is a problem for any form of performance art, but especially so with RPGs, where even the understanding of basic definitions and motivations is so controversial.

2) Lack of reproducibility. Again, this is true of any sort of performance, but most other arts and most other forms of facilitation have recognized credentialing or degree programs or what have you.

3) The sketch factor of paying/getting paid for what is supposed to be a purely social experience.

4) Relatedly, no established vector for marketing GMing services. (In particular, there's no way gaming companies would pay for this sort of thing... right? They make their money by selling books, minis, etc.)

5) Despite the importance of the GM role, the prominence of other factors in determining whether or not a session will be fun. Even if I could control for system content and only run games I like, there's still the issue of disruptive players, or even players who are just tired/cranky. (This may be the most important one!)

Still, there are some people who've done it successfully, and we definitely already have established traditions of GMs getting into cons for free, and many groups will, for example, play for the GM's pizza. Many groups here in NYC are happy to pay $10-15 / session for use of the upstairs in an eatery, though they also get the benefit of the food. My point is that it's not completely crazy... right?

Anyway, am thinking about giving it a shot, if only by posting in "Lessons" on Craigslist, since I have a fair amount of free time on my hands right now. Would love help crafting the ad. Am definitely going to include my "real-life" i.e. non-gaming credentials (I am a professional educator, went to an Ivy League school, etc.). I want to promote the idea, in addition to simply running sessions, of helping people get their own campaigns off the ground\u2014"teaching people to fish" etc. Also, feedback surveys, in order to get testimonials!

Goals:
-Will run for adults or teens or kids, but I don't want to run for huge hordes like Tavis does. ;-)
-Run full sessions, not demos\u2014at least two hours of play time.
-Set the hourly rate low, build up a client base. Was thinking about charging per player, but there are some perverse incentives here. Something like $5/hour/player for the first four players, $10/hour/player for players five and six.

And no, I'm not going to wear a mask. :-)

    \n How about crafting your project in the form of a course or club for people to take instead of a hobby activity that you just happen to pay for? The disadvantage is that you'll lose the people who are looking for entertainment (they won't want a "course"), while the advantage is that it's not at all weird to pay for a course on something, even a frivolous hobby you want to become better at.

    The actual content of what you do wouldn't need to change at all here, even. Compare with say Judo: a Judo teacher is often paid because his pastime is characterized as a course or club activity. If the Judo teacher asked you to pay to "do Judo with him", that wouldn't go over nearly as well, even if what actually happens during the session is exactly the same either way.

    This approach would be easier if you got in touch with some institution that arranges courses and clubs on hobbies. Here in Finland that would be "kansalaisopisto" or community college. Having your hobby club listed with them gives you instant credibility as a community college teacher on your topic, at least around here. Also, your activity will be considered a good thing for the community instead of money-grubbing. Of course, at least here the payments for doing courses through organizations like this are pretty standardized, so you wouldn't get an opportunity to set your own fees.

    \n I've been a pro GM since 1995, Oslo, Norway. Running six groups a week currently, each for sessions 2 hours and 15 minutes. Some of my experiences may help you.

    Here's my penny ...

    First; get a good game!
    - It should be much more effective in feeding the fiction than most classical game are ("more effective" = faster, easier to "read").
    - It should be simple to run, as in; lets you make scenarios on the spot (preferably).
    The One Ring may work, if streamlined a bit, and may be enticing to fans of The Hobbit ...

    Getting started is a challenge; no one wants to pay for something they don't know about, so you may have to buy yourself a market; make the price less of a threshold (cheap the first season, and then increase to an acceptable pay, as the players get fired up).

    Location is important; you are better off playing in free locales. I started up in after-school clubs (using classrooms for free), then privately (using around the kitchen table, in rich homes), and nowadays in a cultural centre for youths (using their premises for free). Paying for the venue cuts a large piece of the cake ...

    It is easier to sell this to children, parents paying. In my experience it is OK to start off with 9-10 year olds, and most fun to play with those 11-13, and sometimes hilarious with the older youths (14-19), depending on the dynamics you are able to create in the groups.

    \n (one breakfast later)

    The practical side:
    - have parents make payments beforehand, for playing 8 or 10 weeks. RPGs need some time to hook the participants
    - set regular times; the same time each and every week, barring school holidays
    - be very polite to your customers, and clear in your communications
    - make a blog with all info necessary, refer to it in your communication

    - be very friendly and welcoming the first session; shake hands with each and every player, say your name and ask for theirs
    - tell the players this is for FUN, and most fun will be had if all is playing along, treating their characters with serious intent, doing their best to survive
    - make it clear that they are meant to collaborate on the challenges you throw at them; this is a statement to establish group-loyality; a good thing for game-play
    - tell them you are meant to play both good AND EVIL characters for them to meet; this is a statement to draw them in, and to make them join up against "a common enemy" (group-loyalty)
    - help them along! You will get young and inexperienced players, and need to be helpful to make game-play fun for them; RPGs have some codes which is hard for some to crack; give them time by being patient and helpful as game-play proceeds

    - do NOT use a GM-screen; it detracts from your communication with the players, and anything you could put on it may be put on the table, or in a chair beside it
    - put a game-sheet before you on the table. Draw a large square on it for the table, names of players outside the square (as they sit around the table), and names of characters inside it, all in BIG letters; use it to note names of important NPCs too, at your end of the sheet, to help yourself in game-play
    - be sure to write down what happens during each session, on the back of the game-sheet; it makes it easier for you to keep track of the fictions in different groups
    - read your notes aloud at the beginning of every session, to remind players where they are and what is happening; invite players to correct whatever you got wrong

    - let game-play build and develop in liege with player initiatives, but be not afraid to make your mark on the fiction; look for dramatic opportunities all the time
    - have fun!

    - use the alarm on your bright new and shiny smart-phone to keep time, so you may engulf in the sessions as a focused and engaged GM

    - invite players to laugh a lot, and invite them to be serious and focused too. A good mixture will earn you their friendship, gratitude and respect. Use that to make the sessions a good time for all of you

    Good luck!

    \n The GM quality thing is probably a red herring here, I should note. People spend a lot of posting time discussing that in these topics, but to me it seems that it's difficult to measure something like whether you're a "better" GM than the other guy who does it for free. It's always going to depend on personal chemistry and the choice of games more than some hypothetical GMing skill that could be developed and proven by having a lot of nerd credentials. (Really, how much of the discussion around this topic is really about the biggest nerd penis?)

    What is it that people would pay for, then? The two values I would expect to be money-worthy are a solid social space for play (both physical space and a good schedule), and an active and committed group to play with. The quality of the organizator/GM is a third value, but it's ultimately just a secondary concern that supports the first two: if you could arrange say four other people who were motivated to play the same game I was, and a convenient social space for us to play in, I could pay you for that even if you didn't play yourself.

    A lot of ink has been spilled over whether being paid to GM actually decreases the quality of the experience, so it seems important to turn that aspect of the experiment into a strength. One way to view this is that having players pay for the experience means that they should be more committed to play, and therefore the session could be better than some game where the players are there just because they're friends and they have to be.

    A scenario that's often bandied about in this context is one where a group of players hires the prospective pro-GM to run the game for them. I don't think that's realistic at all; if you already have a group, you've already got half of the above value equation covered. Most roleplayers probably are able to take on the GM role if the game happening depends on that, I would expect. For this reason I'd say that it's important for you to focus on matchmaking - building a group out of separate strangers - than assuming that some conveniently-sized group of friends wants to bring you in.

    As Tomas says, the play space is crucial, and potentially expensive. Invent a good solution to that, and make the social space good as well: maybe your game runs weekly like clockwork at a specific time, regardless of the usual scheduling ballet - even that's worth something to gamers who're used to having to dance around every other possible social commitment every other player could have in their lives. (Really, it often seems that 95% of roleplayers consider the game-night the least important thing they could be doing. Anything else will have priority.)

      \n As a suggestion on pricing, I might recommend just make a flat fee for the session, and then defining numbers of players and time involved rather than going on a per person/hour basis ( and also what those hours of play mean: are there breaks, what happens as you reach the time limit, what about set up time, etc ).

      Don't under-price your services. Figure out what you want to make and then name that number from step 1. You can always offer promotional pricing or cut a deal, but it's hard to raise prices once you're up and running, since you'll be the first person setting expectations.

      Think ahead about pricing for add-ons. What happens if, at hour 4, people want to keep playing? Is there an upcharge? Are there limits? Will you have pricing designed to discourage certain behaviors? Sounds dumb at first, but do you really want to end up in a twelve hour session? You might want to think ahead about that.

      Hell, how much would you charge for hours 6-12? $100/hr for those add-ons? Maybe, under the right circumstances.

      Poke around and find out what other service jobs cost in your area. How much do birthday clowns charge? Face painters? Fortune tellers? Again, sounds dumb at first, but those are closer to what you're proposing than other sorts of jobs. It will give you more of an idea of what you should be considering and what other people are paying, even if it isn't an exact fit with being a roving mercenary GM!
