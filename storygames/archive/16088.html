    \n The character generation templates in both ACKS and the Player's Companion have packages of starting equipment, where rolling 3d6 for template also stands in for the range of starting wealth. For example, an anti-paladin might have (on a roll of 3) Gnawed wooden unholy symbol (mouth surrounded by eyes of Kaleth), befouled scourge, rusty ball-and-chain, filthy wool tunic and pants, dirty sandals, 2 small tattered sacks, 2 weeks\u2019 iron rations, or (on a roll of 18) Unholy symbol (all-devouring worm of chaos), black-shafted spear, polished sword, black steel shield bearing all-devouring worm symbol, black lamellar armor, black armiger\u2019s tunic and pants, riding boots, medium riding horse, riding saddle and tack, saddlebags, 1 week\u2019s iron rations, 3gp

    \n The ACKS equipment packages are designed to be roughly what you can afford with your 3d6 roll. So you could say "OK you rolled an 18, you can take package #18 with the lamellar armor and everything and have no money left, or you can take package #3 with the filthy wool tunic and have 18 - 3 = 15, x 10 = 150 gp left to buy other stuff you choose or just carry it around to make bribes with."
    \n
    \nIn New York Red Box I at least spend so much time having characters die and rolling up new ones that the inspirational value of going over the list and thinking of clever equipment tricks has long ago worn off. Forcing new players to engage with the question "what do you bring into the dungeon" in detail has its virtues, but I think there is a lot to be said for doing this after the first adventure. Using equipment packages lets you get into the dungeon faster, when you're there the GM can teach the importance of equipment by asking hard questions about who has a torch and what you are carrying treasure in, and then when the party makes it out with whatever treasure is to be had, going shopping for better equipment is part of the reward and not a pre-play obstacle to overcome.

    \n In our campaign we did the equipment purchase for the first batch of characters last summer, but ever since then new players and replacement characters have been done on a simple principle that's not quite purchase nor equipment bundle: the players just write down whatever they want, within the loose precedent of the campaign so far. For example, it's been hashed out a dozen times that yes, a 1st level character can afford a AC 22 armor (leather or equivalent), so everybody can just write that on their sheets if they want it. On the other hand, you don't get AC 25 (chain, etc.) unless there's some fictional positioning involved that would justify it. Lately the players have favoured playing members of Hans Kr\xfcger's (a 5th-level Fighter player character) mercenary company, as he's outfitted his company with mid-range armor, so logically the new player character will also have that

    I find that the above method encourages players to respect the fiction from the start. Our style of hardcore gamism relies on everybody truly caring about the fiction, which is why I find it useful to give the players certain freedoms and responsibilities from the start. It gets them used to thinking of the fiction seriously and making positioning decisions without mixing the executive branch (that is, the character's own greed) into it. I've yet to have a player who would interpret "write anything reasonable you think your character should have at the beginning of his adventuring career" as a free hand to put down a horse and plate mail. Wait, no - I did have a guy who wrote down a horse, but it was no big deal to correct it, and he did have the presence of mind to ask about it so I could correct him - because obviously, a horse's a pretty expensive thing, right?

    The reason for why we moved onto this method is that buying equipment is slow, and it gets old. The 1st level character doesn't really and truly have an interesting set of options available, and once the other characters have some money to loan him, a low roll on wealth won't be a problem, either. In reality the new character will be getting roughly what he wants off the lower end of material wealth anyway, so there's no reason to waste time with the equipment list. Similar characters will get similar things, which means that it's even further pointless to have the player read the lists again and again. Also, I should emphasize that the equipment list itself is questionable from my viewpoint: players relying on an equipment list for their ideas are not thinking for themselves, and they are sadly limiting themselves to the ideas on the list. Furthermore, it's not like the prices on those lists would hold, from the start our campaign has worked off the assumption that prices change constantly (more realistic, especially as any given static list is going to be hilariously wrong in some aspect): when a player wants to buy something, the GM (or some other player; lately I've subcontracted the haggling) throws out a price off the top of his head, and maybe a bit of haggling ensues if the purchase is so large that it matters.

    Interestingly I still have players roll for some spending money on top of their equipment list: 3d6 silver pieces (the default monetary unit) or 2d6 gold (when the character starts in the Imperium) to be held until the game actually starts and they need to pay themselves out of the inn or whatever when they leave for adventure.

    All that being said, equipment packages are a definitely competitive method! If I had time for building my own tables, I would go this way. Also, convention games and one-shots, definitely the superior method. One of these days I'm going to do this thing. Maybe make it an alternative to the freeform method we're using: it might be interesting for a player to risk getting a really bad equipment set, or one that includes stuff we don't normally allow for beginning characters.\n

    \nYes, all this. Also, we have a system where players get to retroactively add obvious things into their inventory by rolling Wits checks. A bad failure will indicate that the character actually forgot to take something the player had marked down, while a success means that even if the player forgot totally to take lamp oil, the character had his head on his shoulders. This is a logical outgrowth of the view we have on player-character relations: the player is not his character, he's just the guiding intelligence that provides the character's intent. The character is not going to hilariously underperform himself just because the player is not literally waging his own life and death on the character's antics.

    \n If you're really introducing new players to that era of D&D, buying equipment and learning how to best use it is part of the process of discovery. These seasoned gentlemen would be happy to sip brandy, chomp cigars and opine on their experiences, but I think experiencing the process for yourself is a better remedy.

    Character creation for that era of D&D is utterly painless. If selecting equipment adds 30 minutes, then the whole process will take 45 minutes -- which leaves plenty of time to begin your first adventure.

    \n Lightly armored specialists (whether thieves or elfs, or halfling)s are more likely to die if they encounter an enemy thy can't avoid. It's really quite tragic when that happens.

    On the flip side, a party that skips out on multiple lightly armored scouts is likely to be more capable of handling up on minor threats, but less capable of handling up on the broader. longer term threats, since they're travelling slower and with less overall information. The whole party becomes a blind, blundering elephant.

    Individual deaths are more likely with a number of light scout types, but TPK is more likely without them.

    In this regard, movement rate and the related exploration rates become yet another vital resource to be managed.

    \n I guess that the usefulness of scouting has a lot to do with the rules and procedures that the group uses. :)
    \n
    \nFor example, if you use the alternative combat sequence in S&W White Box, use surprise rolls for or against the party based on scouting and overall stealthiness of the party and reason that movement in heavy armor is difficult, you'll end up playing up the importance of scouting. It would affect M-U:s quite a lot, because you need information, so you can prepare the right spell. Heck, I know games where all sorts acrobatics, climbing and stuff are the key to avoiding monsters.
    \n
    \nThen again if the dungeons are pretty straightforward, surprise is something that you can't affect too much, armor doesn't hinder you in other ways except in regard to movement rate, if the turn sequence let's magicians fire their spells without preparation and if sneaking is something you'll resolve with thief skills, it's kinda insane not to use armor. It'll really become stupid if the thief skills are handled so that if you miss your roll, you'll alert the enemy, because the added security that two scouts would give, will make sure that at least someone will miss their check. :)
    \n
    \nYou can read the books both ways.

    \n It will also affect things when you're playing really old skool style and have multiple parties plundering the place on different game nights with different play group composition.

    Straightforward dungeon is tricky too.

    I wish Eliot W was still around here and active. He'd found a link to a discussion of old dungeon design that discussed linear vs. dungeons of other sorts. Any time you have a lot of looping or branching, having an ability to scout is very important. If the dungeon is mostly linear or only has a few minor branches or loops, not so much.

    Of course, you probably don't know which you're getting when you go into the thing.

    I do think the "Don't split the party" maxim has at least half to do with GM difficulty in handling a split party, and a quarter to do with player side non-game issues (like down time for mainbody character players). Since running thatis hard when you're supposed to be entertaining everyone as GM, perhaps it isn't so much of a shock that scouting has become considered to be either very deadly or fairly useless?
