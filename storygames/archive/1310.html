Okay, so this is going to be short, but I'll add some more later.

I was already planning to head down to my friends' new house and break it in with the start of a new game -- going to take down Bacchanal, Mortal Coil, PTA, TSoY, and see what folks want to do (while hoping for Mortal Coil).

And I get home from work, and the Shab al-Hiri Roach is FINALLY HERE!
... and... in other packet, The Princes' Kingdom is FINALLY HERE!

So... my co-parent* is babysitting our friends' six-year old daughter, and I have to swing by and drop off some stuff for our daughter anyway, so I grab TPK and a pile of dice, print some character sheets, go over, ask the girl if she wants to play one of those games like the grownups play all the time, and I sit down and, less than an hour after I get it in my hands, I'm starting up The Princes' Kingdom with two players, one of whom is a six year old first grader making up a princess named Rose with talking mouse named Ginni!

Her opening Struggle was convincing her teacher that she really could talk to the mouse and be understood, and vice versa. So awesome. You should have seen her beam when the schoolmarm stammered out an apology for not believing her. My coparent played the other princess, Rose's older sister, and loved the system too (never played or was interested in DitV).

Great night, and now the added possibility of maybe a one-shot of the Roach tomorrow... and if not that, reading it, finally, tonight!

(* - Coparent: an ex-spouse, when you both actually remain friends, act like human beings to each other, and do stuff as a family on the weekends.)

    Rose's Character sheet

    Older sister Isabella's Character sheet

    Dice/Struggle resolution I explained to both players like so: "when you want to do something that will get you want you want, you push forward two dice to show how strong you think the thing you're doing is, so big numbers mean a big strong thing you're doing. I have to try to match that with as few dice as possible -- if it takes me more than two dice to do that, it's going to bother me later, and if I can do it with just one dice, it's really good for me. Then it's my turn, and the same rules work the other way." The six-year-old got the mechanics of it, if not the tactics of it, and the adult asked further questions to understand how fallout and turn the blow worked.
