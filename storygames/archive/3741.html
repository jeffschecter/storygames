\n I'm looking for more Renaissance fiction to include as suggested sorces for my Principia game. I've done a fair bit of historical research, but my fiction list is very short. Here's what I've got:\n
    \n
  • Marvel 1603 by Neil Gaiman and Andy Kubert
    • \n
  • The Company of the Courtesan and Birth of Venus by Sarah Dunant
    • \n
  • The works of Alexandre Dumas
    • \n
  • The Drawing of the Dark by Tim Powers
\nCan anybody suggest any others? Some themes that I'd like to highlight are:\n
  • Renaissance intrigue, spies, assassination
    • \n
  • Swashbuckling action
    • \n
  • Artists and performers whose abilities go beyond mere art
    • \n
  • Steampunk and clockpunk (set in the Renaissance is peferred, but not required)
    • \n
  • Good historical storytelling of any sort, with or without the fantastical
    • \n
  • Stuff with a philosophical bent
    • \n
  • Characters who are astrologers or alchemists or who can predict the future
    • \n
  • Illuminati/conspiracy stuff
\nI've also got a few non-Renaissance sources which also work for this particular game:\n
    \n
  • The Girl Genius Comic and League of Extraordinary Gentleman (for the Steampunk concept)
    • \n
  • The movie The Illusionist (because artists and performers are a part of my game)
    • \n
  • Dune (for the noble intrigue and prescience aspects)

    \n How about Guy Gavriel Kay's Tigana? It's fictional late medieval italy, so I'm not sure it qualifies, but its high on politics, intrigue, assassination.

    Kushner's Swordspoint is also fictional, but has the right feel.

    Can't go wrong with Romeo and Juliette

    Wiki lists Paul J. Mcauley's Pasquale's Angel and Terry Pratchett's Discworld Series a being clockpunk set in the renaissance period.

    \n Michaela Rossner's The Stars Dispose is Renaissance fantasy centering around a cook's apprentice. Michelangelo also makes an appearance. (I especially like it because it has Renaissance recipes in the back, but anyway...)

    Gregory Keyes' The Age of Unreason trilogy is a little later than the Renaissance, but also interesting. It's a bit more adventuring than intrigue, although that factors in as well. It also involves a vast demonic conspiracy and Isaac Newton as an alchemist.

    In Lois McMaster Bujold's The Spirit Ring, the main character's father is inspired by Georgius Agricola, author of De Re Metallica.

    Mercedes Lackey has a couple of co-authored novels set in the Renaissance that I haven't read. This Scepter'd Isle, Ill Met by Moonlight, and Slanderous Tongues focus on Elizabeth I and the machinations of good and evil elves. The Heirs of Alexandria series takes place during the 1500's in an alternate timeline where the Library of Alexandria was not destroyed.

    \n Mary Gentle, Rats and Gargoyles, The Architecture of Desire and 1610: Sundail on a Graveand others, is exactly what you want. Her vision of Civil War England in The Architecture of Desireis particularly good.
