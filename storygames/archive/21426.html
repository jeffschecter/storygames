\n I ran a successful Apocalypse World 2e campaign last year, in what I would call the "default" setting, i.e. a hardhold in a desert wasteland. I'm considering running AW again, but I want to make sure it's still fresh and interesting. New players and playbooks will certainly go a long towards that. But I also have a desire to change up the setting significantly. This could mean everything from a "green" apocalypse (The Last of Us, Summerland) to a snowbound one (Snowpiercer). The idea I'm most interested in right now is taking a more sci-fi route, with the game being set on an alien planet or a decaying space station.

I'd call this less of a full hack and more of reskin, in the vein of a genre shift like Fallen Empires. Players are still the most interesting characters at work in a specific community in both FE and in what I'm discussing. Most PbtA hacks move away from the community-centric politics that I believe are at the heart of AW, but I'd hope to retain that in this reskin. I'd even keep the psychic maelstrom. That's why I don't necessarily want to utilize any of the many existing sci-fi PbtA games.

Running AW on an alien planet would probably work as-is, especially since Earth in AW is usually alien enough to begin with. My question, then, is what you see as the potential pitfalls and opportunities of setting a game on a post-apocalyptic space station? What would you change to support the genre?

The first things that spring to mind is the reduced significance of vehicles, assuming zero-g dogfights aren't a focus. This cuts the Chopper down into a rabble-rousing gang leader, rather than a mobile threat, and makes the Driver almost superfluous. But maybe selecting those playbooks would mean space travel becomes a going concern. I'm also wondering whether the lack of external arrivals might hurt the drama. After all, the new warlord can't invade a space station as easily as they might a landbound hardhold.

Does anyone have experience with running AW or any game in such a circumscribed environment? Maybe on a defunct cruise ship or some other non-standard setting? The only game I've seen which adapts a concept similar to this one is the Malandros-Drama System hack Gangs of Titan. Any resources or similar concepts would be much appreciated!

    \n The first thing that came to mind was Dead Space (unrelated to the video game of the same name), an old GURPS setting someone cooked up on 4chan way back in the day (there's a download link at the bottom of the article).

    Very, very bleak. Homeless people living in huge nearly-dead space stations, scrounging whatever they can and killing each other for the water in their bodies.

Now that I think about it though it could make for a really interesting AW hack; I always felt it was lacking as a GURPS setting, but some PbtA influence could really instill some focus and meaning to it.

    The first things that spring to mind is the reduced significance of vehicles, assuming zero-g dogfights aren't a focus.
    I'm also wondering whether the lack of external arrivals might hurt the drama. After all, the new warlord can't invade a space station as easily as they might a landbound hardhold.
    \nThe "station" can consist of many separate large, medium and small habitats of disparate origins, with internal connections between them a welcome exception: travel on the outside with small spacecraft (down to EVA spacesuits with thrusters and walking with tethers to adjacent habitats) might be the norm.

    Space vehicles could be even more important if habitats are physically unconnected and possibly distant: strung along the same orbit (e.g. a low Earth orbit like the ISS) or constantly avoiding collisions around a stable Lagrangian point.

    A typical hardhold would encompass a defensible part of a large habitat, a whole medium habitat or a few small ones.
    In large habitats, like a traditional O'Neill cylinder, Choppers with land vehicles like bicycles or horses could be a "mobile threat" even without traveling very far; they need not be related to the technologically different marauders who might or might not threaten the exterior of the station.
