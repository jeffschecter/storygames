=====
My verdict: Oh man, Pathfinder NPC Codex is bad ass if you play fantasy RPGs. I figure it's a real promising buy for 13th Age players as well. I've been going through my copy for a few days now.

I'm always disappointed in fantasy miniatures because I can never find a good one to represent my character, especially female characters. Well, this is basically a collection of a gajillion tons of Pathfinder art (so men and women, about a 60-40 split (!), featuring awesome, detailed, fantastical and cool wear) turned into hard, thick, cardboard character blanks plus plastic bases for use with battlemats. Games like FATE and 13th Age with "zones", you can put them on their bases, or just leave them flat on the table.

Going through each punch-out sheet of dozens and dozens of characters, I hit upon one character inspiration after another; tons of characters from "Evil Cultist Woman" to "Arcane Scholar" where half of me is like, "Oh, man, this would have been the perfect mini to use for that X game I played Y years ago", and the other half is "Oh, dude, I want my next character to BE HER!".

It's not as useful in a capacity of "I need 20 detailed NPCs that all look alike to use as canon fodder", this set has duplicates for combat, but maybe only 2-4 tops. Most of the time I can see using them with Halma Pawns: "These 5 pawns are cultists; but this one over here stands out (drops NPC)", stuff like that.

Lots of minis, almost a 50-50 male/female split, very little cheesecake.

Only drawback is that they're mostly all "badasses": Actiony, dark-hero types, most of them wearing SeriousFace or GrimDark reactions. So it won't work as well for, say, Ryuutama or Your Happy Honobono Adventure Game, etc. But 90% of D&D-like games, absolutely.

    \n Yep, starting with the art (minis in this case) first, then letting that inspire the character means that the two match up vastly better than trying to do it the other way around.

    It works for the GM creating adventures as well as players making their characters.

    Taking a step further on the GM end, Paizo makes tons of really great gridded location mats and such as well. A nice collection of those can inspire adventures the way the minis stand ups inspire characters.

    They can even be nice for that sort of thing when the game doesn't have mechanically fiddly rules involved. Inspirational art is inspirational.
