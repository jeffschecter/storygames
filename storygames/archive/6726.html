\n Hey everyone. All the cool kids are running 4th edition campaigns, so I thought I would too.

Like any traditional game, 4e has oodles of charts, some of which are essential to play, some of which are redundant. If I'm going to put together a DM screen/reference sheet, which charts do you all think I should have on it?

The Skill Difficulties chart from the DMG is a given, as are the standard numbers for monsters by level and role. I already have separate references for eveyrbody's use for what the effects of they various conditions are, and what typical Standard, Move and Minor Actions are, so that's covered too.

So what else should I keep at my fingertips during play?

    \n Personally, I never try to build a screen until I've run the game I'm building it for a few times -- unless it's a small enough game that I can just put *all* the tables and such on a screen. With that said, though, my initial guesses would be:

    DCs to break or burst common items (PH 262)
    a place to put the PCs' Passive Insight and Passive Perception scores
    attack modifiers (PH 279)
    a place to hang the current initiative order (maybe just the edge of the screen, using Post-It tags with names on them)

    \nI'd say there is lots to reference, but 4e is built so that most of what needs referencing properly belongs with the PCs and/or the monsters.

    I don't know if you've seen the "giant castle" DM screen that WotC has produced a limited number of, but it basically has two shelves on the inside, where you're supposed to put the cards from the D&D Miniatures game (which have the "regular D&D" version of the critter on the back). That seems like an excellent idea, and a little work with a photocopier or scanner and the Monster Manual would prevent having to buy the D&D minis to get the cards.

    \n I've never liked using an actual screen -- it makes it hard to reach the minis, and I don't like having a bunch of crap between myself and the players -- but something that helped me in 3.5 was making myself a customized index. Any time I looked something up and thought to myself, "you know, I had to look this up last time, too", I'd jot down the subject and page number on the inside cover of the spiral notebook I kept all my sloppy, half-assed "adventure notes" in.
