\n So, here we go again.

After looking through the comments in the first thread on Riskbreaker (and thanks to everybody who gave them!), I realized that my responses were basically going to be a new set of rules, changing several elements: the zero-sum Advantage, how Advantage would be used outside of combat, etc.

So, after a couple of ridiculously long train rides yesterday, I present: Riskbreaker, the Total Rewrite Edition. It's an attempt to work out the implications of the feedback I got.

Some caveats:

1. This version is even less stable than the previous one. Literally *no* playtesting has gone into it. It's more an attempt to just to get the ideas out on paper and try them out when I get the chance. Example: the values for Item Risk are pulled out of thin air. Proceed at your own risk (heh).

2. It's incomplete - the rules for how the GM converts Advantage to opposition for the party is left completely blank. There'll probably need to be some sort of cost table (like Item Risk) -- probably dependent on relative difficulty.

Actually, now that I think about it - there's a rule missing regarding initial opposition Advantage set against a party trying to achieve a Goal. I came up with it last night: it starts equal to the Goal Level. The party starts out with the deck stacked against them and struggles towards victory. That's the idea anyway. Plus, it gives the GM the ability to throw opponents and/or obstacles at the party even if their initial rolls are made of pure win.

Also, the way I envisioned it, the party can have several goals going on at the same time (something like a 'Current Quests' screen in an CRPG). Though, couple that with the previously mentioned rule and a party that's really splitting its attention can get themselves into *serious* trouble. Which is kind of neat.

3. Individual rule clarity might be at an all time low. I did the rewrite on a couple of all-day train rides. Not the best of circumstances, by an means. Please ask me what I meant if it's not clear.

Thanks again.

    \n - I think I was probably just reading in the "two sides" thing, having understood that from the previous version.
    \n
    \n - Frankly I think that any notion that AOE is any more "risky" or "potent" an attack form is largely just a statement of preference. I mean, if you want to emulate the feel of the fireball, it might make sense to do it this way. But there's no real right and wrong here. I could argue, in fact, that somebody could charge headlong into a group of bad guys and threaten to kill them all off in one sweep of their sword. Basically if AOE can do as much damage as you want, I could argue that any other attack could, if ramped up, do as much damage as you want, with commensurate risk.
    \n
    \nThe point being that I understand if you want to have some variability here between these things, as it's interesting in terms of tactical depth and exploration. But I don't see any particular way of stating how these things work as inherently better than another, thematically. Cap it at 3 per enemy seems to be just as effective at making this discrimination than not capping it. Again, if in fact you're trying to emulate D&D, which did cap damage done by a fireball such that one of them could not kill certain very powerful monsters (but could damage many of that monster), then your version doesn't do that well. And a cap is more rich tactically.
    \n
    \nSo I remain unconvinced there that an open cap is neccessarily better. But I think it's probably a pretty minor thing. The argument against my method is that mine requires a small math step to calculate the cap. If it's not really useful, then getting rid of that step makes sense from that POV.
    \n
    \n
    \n - I get the weapon change thing now, and how it affects durability. That said, it seems to suffer from several potential problems:
    \n - Is there ever any reason for a character to not use their best weapon ability? Mechanically? Why would I ever use my sword, if my spear ability was one higher?
    \n - Worse, can you actually see players spending points on having both sword and spear skill? What's the possible use? If it's to have a backup weapon, why not carry two spears?
    \n - If it's the ranged attack argument, then I'll arm myself with a bunch of javelins. A weapon that I can use both as a ranged attack and melee. Weapons all do the same damage, so there's no downside to this, right?
    \n - If you allow this, to make it mechanically sound, you have to be very specific about when the weapon skill can be changed. It might even become important to have a way to determine who gets to declare first. Again, all presupposing we can come up with a mechanical reason that this would ever happen.
    \n - In HQ, no matter what skill you switch to, you always use the same pool of points. Switching skills just changes your rolls, and doesn't affect your distance from losing.
    \n - And, in fact, a common criticism leveled at HQ is that basing your pool size on your skills is actually somewhat problematic. For a couple of reasons. First, it's a double whammy. Skill makes you both better at whittling down your opponent, and makes you harder to whittle down. Second, since the bids are relative, there's not a lot of difference no matter what size pool you have. Lots of people play where the pool is always 100 points, in every contest.

    Here's another way to approach it, using the Item creation rules to leave things open while pinning down mechanical specifics:

    Set/Create Trigger

    Effect: Create an if-then trigger where the result is an effect from the Item Risk table, and/or the established Risk in Kill or Skill Damage.

    Cost: The established Risk

    Skills: Any applicable to the situation: P:Observation, P:Scout, and skills such as W:Set Trap, W:Tactics

    Success: The trigger is created.

    Failure: As above, but the element or its creation somehow works against you, increasing the Advantage of an opposing side in the same conflict by the established Risk.

    A trigger has two parts, a state and an effect. Example: "If someone enters this area and fails a P:Observation Counter Roll, a one-time trap goes off doing [Risk] Kill damage." (You could up the number of times it goes off by paying Risk: +1 for each use, according to the Item Risk table).

    The trigger for the Psychology roll from the court case would be: "If my side makes a successful Law roll, it can do [Risk] damage to the opposing side in Skill Damage."
