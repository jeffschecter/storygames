\n Periodically, I ask everyone about any of their games that I haven't purchased yet. I'm a Story Games collector so I'm a pretty easy sell. Here are the type of games I like, but that doesn't mean these are the only type of games I'm interested in, so don't be hesitant to post your game and some info about it if you like. Here's the type of games I typically like:

- GMless / GMlite games in which the players don't just advocate for their players via their players POV, but also equally contribute to other parts of the story and tell it together. The players typically take turns framing scenes and play their characters while simultaneously directing the narrative, world-building, playing NPCs and taking on other roles that are traditionally left to the GM. They are the writers of the story in the largest sense and also its audience.

- I typically like narrative based story games rather simulationist RPG's

- Finally, I typically prefer simple rules and games that are played in a single session as one shots, or games that are played in a few sessions.

As I said, these are just general guidelines of what I typically like in games, but if your game doesn't fit this criteria, it doesn't mean I won't like it, so if you would like please tell me about it

Now, please sell me your games!

    \n Have you seen my recent game Three Dooms?

    In it you create a stone age town and play out the community's reactions to various events, to invent/explore their culture, build a history, and see if they can survive the three disasters that are lurking somewhere in the card deck.

    It looks like it hits your preferences pretty well:
    - Everyone takes turns in a GM-light role.
    - You're building the history of a stone age town (you'll play characters now and again, but potentially different ones each episode - it's more about families and history than individuals).
    - Focused on collaborating on the story, drawing a map of the community & surrounds, and simple resolution rules for when you need to decide if something works or not, as well as changes to the community between events.

    Key inspirations were The Quiet Year and the board game Pandemic.

    \n My Play Dead games meet all those criteria: GMless, narrativist (mostly) and designed for single-session play.

    101 Zombies is a gamist/parlour narration game about killing as many zombies as you can, while working loosely as part of a team.

    Pyramid is a classic pulp adventure about raiding an ancient Egyptian tomb: it pretty much plays like a boardgame except your imagination takes the place of the board.

    Mystery of Life is a darkly comic pastiche of Law & Order in which a devil & angel investigate a death in the first act, while the second act is a courtroom drama where the deceased's soul is judged and sent to Heaven or Hell.

    Finally Sawmill is a co-op horror story where strangers must overcome their suspicion of each other to escape a serial killer's death maze, but one of them must die in order to save the others.

    \n Hey Jeff! I have a story game called The Box that is in blind playtesting stages now. The rules, process, and cards are complete, I'm just working on layout, art, and small tweaks to wording.     You and your group will collectively discover a strange box, open it, and unpack an estoteric object from inside. Then you will go through a divination process to discover its true nature or purpose.

    If I have to trace some distinctions between Fate/FAE and FateLess based on what was expressed in the thread...

    Fate's performance varies depending on how the GM handles the rules.

    FateLess sears away this variance. There is no GM

    Fate can end up being about Wolverine's claws.

    FateLess is by design much more about "shit happening" which in turn makes the PCs change and evolve. It's cool that Wolverine has claws, but the focus of the game is on what he does with his claws, how he does it, what consequences this causes, and how Wolverine's goals and motives change because of that.

    Fate frames dice rolls as conflicts of some kind, where the only chance of "failure" is presented by a bad dice roll. This in turn is drastically reduced by the use of Fate Points and other mechanics.

    FateLess frames dice rolls as "critical tasks" ... this (I have seen at the table) tends to make things faster by reducing the scope of the dice roll, while keeping them meaningful.
    You still have tools, like Fate Points, to tilt the balance in your favour but:

    - it's much more common for PCs to not be in a group, so pooling resources and help is much less common

    - there are mechanics in place that have "bad" things happen independently of the dice roll, so you could succeed at impressing the girl at the bar, but something else might also happen to complicate the situation, or rise the stakes, or impose a price, or plain and simple reveal new unexpected info (which change the fictional positioning, so dice or no dice you might feel like riconsidering your actions and goals)

    - there is no Stress or Shifts or Boxes of any kind... PCs can only sustain 3 Consequences, and the 4th one sends them "KO" ... and all kinds of "damage" (physical, social, or else) translate into Consequences.
    So while it is possible to often succeed at rolls, it is also true that a single failure has a lot of weight.
    Not to mention that pure and simple fictional positioning is more than enough to inflict a Consequence. Of course the target PC would be allowed to act in some way to prevent it.

    The net result in my experience is that although it is not easy to mechanically fail and end up KO, the Players feel plenty of challenge.
    The danger is real, and it is clearly perceived, although the odds are stacked in favour of the PCs, if they are willing to put effort into it. Usually, this effort is less about spending points, and more about coming up with genuine ideas ... fictional positioning plays a huge role here.

    I personally find this is the case in Fate only under the guidance of a GM that is both skilled and has the right sensibilities and inclination. In FateLess it's the default.

    That said, FateLess is still by design a "pulp" game.
    The interesting question is not to know IF a character will be able to achieve something. They might fail, but it's more likely that eventually they will succeed.
    No, the point of the game is rather everything else around such event:
    - how will the PC choose/manage to get to the goal?
    - what is the PC willing to do to get there?
    - getting to the goal changes the situation, how will the PC handle it?
    - what will happen after the goal is achieved?

    . . .

    I don't know. Does this sound a bit more like what you were looking for?
