Context first: We are talking about Fast-Food Roleplaying here. It gets cooked up quickly, you snarf it in a hurry, and then you go back to the counter and yell "More! More!" and more is cooked up exactly the same way. Check out this thread if that's not clear to you.

So you're about to create a world and characters for ... say ... an hour of self-contained roleplaying. I think it's perfectly clear, in that context, that you won't choose to write up details about the arctic north unless you're planning to go there. You won't make a character write-up for Sir Not-Appearing-In-This-Story. Your time should and will be focussed on the stuff you're actually going to use. Everyone with me so far?

One further piece of context: This is for a teenage angst crisis game, and so people will explicitly be writing up the emotional stuff that's troubling them. Because we're going to use it in order to play, right? Wouldn't do to not have ever mentioned it in the beginning.

Whew! Context done.

So here's my question: What should the game require of you if you want to, for instance, say "There's freaky witch-magic in this world"?

I have a theory that by the end of the game any such statement should be linked to something about the internal life of the characters. If magic didn't become an issue for someone, somehow, then why on earth was it included in the first place?

Contrariwise, I think that by the end of the game any internal statement should be linked to something about the external life of the characters. If your mopey, brooding angst didn't cause you to do something then why on earth was it included in the first place?

And then ... urgggh ... slowing down.

I have more thoughts. Before I get to them, do people buy this overall theory? Does it sound like I've got a decent grasp on the necessities of the small-social-footprint, Fast-Food model?

    In improvese (the only language I speak these days) "There's freaky witch magic" is an offer, which everyone explicitly accepts. Then it is the group's responsibility to manifest that, and heighten it, and reincorporate it.

    Manifest: "Dang, there sure is some witchy-ass witchery up in here. Creepy."

    Tony, I think you are carting-before-horseing. Especially with the kind of set-up that you're trying to create, the story should be facilitating world creation, not the other way around. It's easy to think of setting as a sort of 'foundation' for story, but this is pretty much untrue. Setting is, at best, a pretext. It's only there to take the things that the characters do and care about and knit them together enough that the audience will believe that they all exist in relative proximity. There's only an Empire and an Emporer because Vader needed to have an evil master. If Vader was a rogue agent of evil, Star Wars wouldn't have the Old Republic / Empire thing in its setting at all.

    Abdul

    o Desperation

    Alice
    o Royal Heritage
    o Mysterious Ass-kicking Powerz

    George
    o Steam-Powered Mechanical Arm
    o Patriotic

    And then play.

    Joshua,

    Quite right! To remind anyone who's gotten bogged down in this flurry of stuff from me, Tony said:
    Which is to say, short-hand: The quality that you create by making two traits that work together as a pair is independent of their pairing. They are, individually, well-suited to connect with another trait. I find it very funny that it is easier to make two traits that will be grabby no matter what they're paired with than it is to make one.
    Which is to say two things, as I read it: first off is that a grabby trait is a grabby trait. If you think that giant robots are cool, then you think that giant robots are cool. If you think that giant robots are cool in conjunction with angsty teenage pilots, and you think that ninjas are cool in conjunction with fighting hard to improve themselves then you should also find giant robots with pilots fighting hard to improve themselves and ninjas who are angsty cool too.

    This seems staright-up true.

    Tony's second point is that it's easier to create grabby traits in pairs than singly. At first I thought this was crazy talk, but I have since changed my mind. The reason that this turns out to be true is that having two synergistic things makes it easier to sharpen each of them. So I may think that giant robots are cool. When I come up with a synergistic trait (such as angsty teenage pilots), I am forced to refine my idea of giant robots. What is it about them that is cool that makes them go together well with angsty pilots? This feeds back the other way, now that I've sharpened my idea of giant robots, what is it about angsty teenage pilots that is cool in that light?

    So it's easier to create that second trait because I'm riffing off the first, but then that second trait makes me sharpen and improve the first to make it even better than it would have been. That's dang cool.

    Now, it's not clear that this means that creating paired traits like this is actually easier than creating just one trait, but it does seem that creating a pair is easier than creating two individuals. And it's entirely possible that pairs just make for better traits, and that's cool.

    Thomas

    Okay, I've just run a playtest of the new Misery Bubblegum rules, and the absolute standout thing that I noticed is that making a conflict always involve an External Tension and an Internal Tension makes it dead easy to narrate the outcome.

    "Tracey and Kyle sneak into the producer's trailer and look around for evidence. They win, and are not caught before finding the evidence. The Issues at Stake are 'Kyle fears that his dad might be involved in something rotten' and 'Tracey returns to shake up Kyle's life.' The conflict is resolving this way because of the interaction of these two elements with each other. So, obviously, a guard comes in and Tracey grabs Kyle and kisses him, which is the perfect cover for what two teens were doing alone in a wagon they weren't supposed to be in, and while the guard is Aw-Shucksing they get away with some incriminating evidence."

    It was like the External and Internal were two perpendicular lines, and the point where they met was always the exact target I needed to hit in that particular moment. RPG Cross-hairs, baby ... it makes me happy to be a traditional "Describe all the outcomes" GM again, because it makes it easy and fun!

    More to come, but I just wanted to convey some of my enthusiasm. This idea has legs, folks!
