    \n I've used it for pregen PCs and it helps a lot, but the people using the types have to understand what they mean. I'm pretty well versed in the theory so it's a useful tool for me.

    Ways to use the tool:

    1. Straightforward. Assign one of the 16 types to an NPC and use it to guide how you play that character.

    2. Use the type descriptions on keirsey.com or socionics.com and cherry-pick phrases from them as fodder for creating characters.

    3. Use the type sorters (quizzes) as a way to discover your NPC's personality. (Which is more important to your NPC? being kind or being right?)

    3. Use the type sorters as a laundry list of traits to assign to NPCs.

    \n Michael Fiegel's RPG Vox uses a mirror-universe version of Myers-Briggs for a character personality-typing system. It's essentially a relabeled/reordered MBTI inventory that focuses on the negative aspects of each type. I assume that by focusing on issues associated with personality types rather than on self-examination, it's better suited for games.

    For example, under Myers-Briggs I would be categorized under INTP (the Architect). Under the Vox system, I would be a SMAR (Seer eMpath Anarch eRemite: the Tinker). The capsule description for this type is : "Detached and logical; critical of self and others; second-guessing; aloof." I will leave it as an exercise for those who know me whether or not this accurately describes me.

    Other types include: the Vizier, the Pastor, the Scoundrel, the Artiste, the Hedonist and the Tyrant.

    \n The quick-and-easy way to do it is to have four "buckets" of character traits; each bucket contains traits which correspond to one of the four facets of the Meyers-Briggs. Then you pick one trait from each bucket. It'd be like the MB Test, but you'd get traits that were more colorful.

    \n I'd take 10 points and split them (either choosing or rolling a d10) on each axis, producing results like this:

    Introvert - 3, Extrovert - 7
    iNtuiting - 4, Sensing - 6
    Thinking - 8, Feeling - 2
    Judging - 5, Perceiving - 5

    Hopefully when I need to know how this character would react in this situation, one trait or axis will pop out at me.

    Personally, I think Myer-Briggs is better than nothing, but not as good as other options like Belief/Goal, political orientation, two contradictory-sounding short descriptive phrases, etc.

    \n I use this all the time when prewriting characters and even organizations. The value of Meyer-Biggs absolutely isn't the numerical scores but the archetype summaries that lets you get inside the skin or recognize that person from your daily life. Time wise I don't see it as worth using unless you're going to play the character for an extended time.
