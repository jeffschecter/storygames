Is Fate Core resolution mechanic "bad" for emergent story?

Hasimir
November 2017 in Game Design Help
Once upon a time people sitting at an rpg table kind of expected to deal with a gargantuan rules system with tons of crunch. Anything shy of that would have been considered light and agile and a breeze to play.

Nowadays with the existence of minimal games that, with very few mechanics, are able to deliver deep and complex stories and experiences, the expectation has shifted. Especially in the universe of games with an emergent plot.

And so it happened that I ended up having a few discussions about how the core resolution mechanic of Fate (be it Core or Accelerated and even FateLess) was perceived by the paople talking to me as too heavy, too complicated, too slow, almost in the way of the story.
"The story is great, but whenever we roll dice, meh!"
The example they made to me is as follows...

Let's compare two different games we could nonetheless put in the medium-heavy area of the crunch spectrum. Fate Core and Apocalypse World.

In Apocalypse World the Player's description would trigger a resolution moment (aka: a move). What'll happen is that usually there will be a dice roll or some other specific thing as prescribed by the specific move. This would determine an outcome. Someone would describe it. And the game/story/fiction would move on from there.
Mind not that to resolve a single situation you often spend a lot of time performing a lot of not-so-decisive moves.
What people told me they perceive, what they feel, is that all of this move-doing is smooth sailing. It's simple. It's easy. It's somehow concrete and solid, thus logical.
Description -> roll -> outcome -> description -> etc.
Whether you succeed, or fail.

Compare this to Fate.
The Player's description would trigger a resolution moment.
This could mean any of 3 different resolution structures (Challenge, Contest, Conflict)
In each of those, you could opt to perform any of 4 types of action (Cr.Advantage, Overcome, Attack, Defense
Eventually you get to roll.
Let's say you roll poorly and get a negative outcome.
But wait, you could spend Fate to roll again... and to do so you have to highlight/modify (ever so slightly) the fiction by invoking an Aspect.
But wait, maybe it's not enough so you can spend another Fate on another Aspect.
But wait, your opposition maybe Invokes Aspects of their own and now your score is not enough anymore, do you spend more of your Fate? Do you have any more relevant and unspent Aspects?
Eventually you get a final outcome.
But wait, maybe you would accept to succeed anyway with a serious cost.
Finally the outcome is clear, and the results get narrated into the story, that can finally move on.

And this could happen for every single test within a longer Contest/Conflict.
This, they say, feels complex, convoluted, slow, and very abstract.

Now, the discussion I had was not even about Fate. It was about FateLess which compared to Fate Core or even Fate Accelerated is like a small feather. Still, it keeps the original fundamental structure:
- trigger
- roll
- invoke?
- outcome
- accept cost?
- final outcome

And there, as in Fate, the Invoke part could get quite inflated, and even more so if you are facing off against another PC doing their own invokes.

And... nothing... it got me thinking.
Is such structure truly conductive to fiction-first narrative-emergent gameplay?
I never had a problem with it. And know tons of people who also don't have a problem with it.
Hearing this critique, from different people, in different occasions, all in just a short time span ... it's making me doubt my assumptions.

Is the Fate basic structure truly this much cumbersome and almost "in the way" of an emergent story?
Am I unable to see it out of mere habit?
Or is it just a matter of personal taste? Of having played so much variations of PbtA that now anything actually different feels uncomfortable?

    I've been discussing this both on G+ and Facebook with Fate aficionados.
    I believe that, in the end, it really is about how the basic Fate approach to the game feels to you.

    Fate is very much a colour-first (or cosmetics-first, as @Paul_T would put it?) game of pulp action.
    The two main things are:
    - represent/highlight colourful details of the fiction
    - see what happens next, as in, the consequences of PC actions, rather than testing if such actions succeed or fail (although failure is still very real and possible)

    This, I believe, is just a slight deviation from what AW offers itself.

    Pulp means, in Fate, the ability to look a failed roll in the face and say "But I am a Depressed Mad Scientist and I will try to open this effing door by applying eldritch osmosis!" and possibly turn a failure into a success.
    It's about the ability to have a say in how the story goes, beyond your luck with dice.
    The whole economy of Fate points is there to create a relative scarcity for this, so that the Players will invest points (and time, and creative effort) only when it is important to them.
    Squander your points on every single unimportant failed roll, and the system self-balances by running out of such points, so when it actually matters to you, you are stuck with whatever the dice say.

    Here lays the difference with, for example, AW.
    In AW all the manoeuvrings and negotiations happen before the roll, to establish if/when/which Move to apply... but once a Move is selected, that's it. If the dice say f-you, that's that. And you go on to the next Move.
    You do often have to make additional mechanical choices in AW too, about Holds and tokens and picking effects and questions etc ... but I believe they feel less invasive because they are not about changing the outcome, just about seeing it through in detail.
    All in all this justifies, among other things, why AW would feel less complex and more fluid than Fate.

    Discussing with the Fate crowd, it turns out that the ability to change an important outcome is valued enough to make the cost in added complexity feel like it's worth it.
    Snd not only that, but they actually embrace such complexity, because it is exactly when you can most meaningfully represent the things you care about of your PC, all the "I prevail because I'm the son of a lost culture" kind of stuff. Even when, despite all your efforts, you end up failing no matter what.
    The important bit, the representation of colour, the cosmetic highlight, happened because of such mechanics, and that's what matters most.
    To them.

    I can see the value there. It's possibly the reason I chose Fate as a fundamental base for my own FateLess system.
    But I also personally prefer a much lighter crunch. With less numbers. And FateLess does this to a great extent. Compared to Fate all the numbers are much less arbitrary and instead come from the direct observation of the fiction, and are very few and far between, to begin with.

    Still... numbers or no numbers... the complexity we are discussing comes from the underlying structure:
    - describe
    - roll
    - outcome
    - put some element of the fiction in the spotlight to alter the outcome
    - final outcome

    I'm considering ways to make it lighter.
    Like, normally when you Invoke an Aspect you have to choose between 2 mechanical effects (reroll or get +2) and sometimes one is more convenient, sometimes the other is better, and this is 100% unrelated to the fiction. Streamlining this to be a single effect instead of a purely mechanical choice could help.
    Still, it won't change the structure: you DO always have the option to leverage Aspects in order to modify the Outcome of a test.

    Although I am noticing one thing between the people that speak in favour of such structure, and the ones (me included) that are expressing doubts about it.

    When "we" talk about the outcome-modifying part we seem to envision it in an awkward and unsatisfying way. We envision one action being tested, and then its outcome being pulled here and there, in more or less reasonable ways, until finally the resolution of that first action is concluded.
    I can picture in my head a PC swinging a punch, having it stop mid air, waiting and waiting until stuff happens, and finally being freed to complete the swing and hit the guy in front of them.
    I've played it myself like this when I first tacked Fate, and I've seen many people play it like this too. But it was a long long time ago.

    Instead (most) Fate supporters today seem to clearly see this situation with a completely different flow. Admittedly, such flow is only hinted at in the original Fate rules, as an aside optional note.
    (and in FateLess there is no mention of it, regrettably; something I'll soon fix)
    But long time Fate players all seem to have it internalised as the obvious way to play.

    The PC swings a fist towards a guy's face.
    The stake (more or less explicit) is to overcome him, to win this fight.
    [roll = fail]
    The outcome is narrated. It is real. It happened. Just, not yet stake-conclusive.
    The guy dodges.
    Now the question is, will you let this stand, or keep on pushing?
    If you let it stand, then this (and maybe some extra narration) adjudicates the stake and moves the fiction along; in Fate it's a GM task, in FateLess there are mechanics to take care of it.

    Otherwise, you move on, you describe more stuff, possibly more actions.
    "I am a son of a gun and have seen more bar fights that I can count, I keep throwing fists at him!"
    [pay Fate to invoke the son of a gun aspect = reroll = still fail]
    The outcome is narrated. It is real. It happened.
    You land a few blows but he is just bigger and badder than you!
    What do you do?
    "I am not the most honest person, so I grab a chair and smash it on him"
    [pay Fate to invoke the not the most honest person aspect = reroll = still fail]
    The outcome is narrated. It is real. It happened.
    The guy sees you reaching for the chair and kicks it away from you, and now he seems to press on to the offensive.
    What do you do?
    "I won't/can't spend more Fate on this, but I won't surrender either. I accept to win at a serious cost."
    [now this ends the conflict, adjudicating the final true stake]
    Seen like this it doesn't look like an awkward moment drawn out while the Player counts points and wrecks their brain to find a way to exploit Aspects in a way that doesn't scream powerplayer.
    Instead, it looks more like a dynamic action scene.

    In theory, and by the book, conflicts are just dramatic moments. Forks on the road. Opportunities to shine.
    In practice, according to my personal experience and a lot of friend's reports, normal Fate (Core/Accelerated) can be made very Trad and even railroady if the GM so wishes and the Players don't know any better. Even Fred Hicks offered advice that is 100% legit and effective but I could only see fit at a veeery gmLed table; it's just how Fate Core is built.

    But still, by the book it's a fork.
    So yeah. The game assumes not every conflict is worth pouring time and creative effort and Fate points into turning an unfavourable outcome into a favourable one.
    This, and the fact that you usually roll dice much less frequently than, say, in AW.

    About Aspects... they are both pre-set and spontaneously made.
    They define stuff about your PC, her items and relationships and powers and flaws and whatever.
    They are also discovered or even created on the spot, both through simple narration or with specific actions (Create Advantage).

    Discussing with friends... the main problem they seem to have with this approach is... you follow the fiction, you follow the scene, you follow the action. And at some point you get to this conflict. A cliffhanger. A fork. You have a picture in your head because of all that has already been established so far. You roll and... and if you want to modify the outcome you have to switch the mental gears used so far, take a different play stance, and put creative effort starting from what could be described as unstable footing.

    To describe it again.
    If you fancy having influence over the outcome, you then have to step back from the action and find ways to have one or more of your Aspects somehow fit into the narrative.
    Done wrong (how I usually see it done) you just point your finger to an Aspect and try to have the other Players see how it makes sense for it to help.
    Done right (according to a footnote in the rulebook and what amounts to a lot of "GM savvyness" from the community) you would have to come up with a negative but not decisive outcome for your failed roll, then describe a continuation of your action that leverages an Aspect.
    THEN you spend 1 fate, and have to make a mechanically relevant choice: reroll or +2 ?
    Knowing that maybe you will need to leverage one more Aspect anyway to get to the victory condition.
    THEN if things go wrong anyway you have to decide if it is worth, after all the points spent and the descriptions provided, to accept a Serious cost to finally win no matter what the dice say.
    This to me is not a chore. It is fun. It is interesting. It produces more fiction. It turns a simple Yes/No test into a dynamic action scene that showcases the PC's traits.
    It is what everyone in the Fate communities I visited loves and considers THE game. Not just a part of it, but the core feature.

    But I have to admit that I can see also the problem some people have with it.
    They go with the flow of the scene, and this procedure simply does not click with them; it breaks the flow, and then asks them to come up with more narration starting from what, to them, is now suddenly weak footing.

    I have a very bright and creative player that, even in the lighter and more cooperative FateLess, struggles visibly whenever she could and would Invoke an aspect... and gets frustrated when she sees how other Players, more keen to this system, effortlessly come up with descriptions to leverage 2-3-4 Aspects each.
    This produces that not ideal feedback where she enjoys the game, the story, she even enjoys most of the mechanics... but when she gets to the dice rolling part... meh! :P

    The point of contention is not dice-roll number distribution.
    The point it is:

    - Fate creates an outcome and then allows Players to change it multiple times by spending a mix of game-points and creative effort... this produces a highly representative fiction, drilling down into the details of an action and the aspects (literally) of how and why a Character faces it

    - other systems (for example AW) create an outcome... and that's it. End of the story. You now have to follow through with whatever that outcome dictated. Especially in PbtA systems this also moves the fiction either around or forward, while giving it a relatively specific "shape" as a by-product of how Moves work.

    Also... in Fate (at least by the book) you roll pretty seldomly. Each roll represents something big and important. And the roll, as extended and modified as it can be, will resolve the whole thing.

    On the contrary in PbtA you roll very often, as your fiction will trigger some Moves whenever you describe something triggering them. And a single move is usually not enough to solve a whole situation.

    . . .

    Starting from this, the original doubt was about the ability of the Fate structure to be in itself enjoyable, not in the way of creativity (is it defeating the purpose for which it was created?).

    The answer, as it happens, is yes... the Fate structure is perfectly fine in the opinion of the whole Fate-loving segment of players. I just bumped into a bunch of people that instead have problems with that approach, all at once, in rapid succession... which took me a bit by surprise and made me question the system itself.
