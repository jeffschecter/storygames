    Very good point. All the above (except one) are in my "vision". Basically my vision included all the above, but specifically excluded the following:

    * Game Design (ground-up style, not houserules and the like. The latter are fine, the former are best met elsewhere, or at least that was my idea)

    * Uh... and this is a hard one... Theory. I see a lot of "let's hammer this stuff out" style discussion. And I'm glad it's going on somewhere. But not always glad that it's happening here.

    * Design Diary stuff. I'm still waffling on my attitude for design diary stuff here, but I often see AP or general question thread stuff that seems more like "Here is my shit that's coming soon/Here are my thoughts on this thing I'm working on" rather than "I need some help on this. Anyone?" The latter is cool, the former is a huge grey area for me, but acceptable for now.

    And what I wanted to have more of is:

    * General gaming talk. Which is happening more these days, I think.

    * Gaming-related talk. Which too is happening more these days (SF/lit discussion, etc)

    * Directed discussion on how to make gaming better. Simple techniques, cool tricks that have worked, etc. That's sort of what I was aiming for (but didn't codify) with the Actual Play and Gamecraft forums.

    I think that there's definitely Design Talk going down, which is awesome. I want to get more General Gaming Talk going down, too. Talk about playing and GMing tricks and the like.Tips. Process. Stuff like that. It's just something I'd like to see more of, but of course haven't gotten around to posting more of myself. :-)

    Jason: Will get back to you on growth, but we regularly have about 2-5 new folks join a week.

    ===-=-= Now, some things I was thinking of implementing, and Why =-=-===

    I'm no where near any sort of "Things need to be changed! We're diverting from the goals of the site!" sort of actions. The last few weeks I've just been slowly rolling around in my head what is happening with the site, and what I want (because it's even hard for me to realize what I like and don't like, without seeing all sorts of general actions and behaviors).

    Here are some thoughts I've had.

    Theory. I'm thinking of creating a theory category. That way we can have a flood-valve for the inevitable (constructive) discussion on more abstract theory.
    But theory is always changing, evolving. Like a Heraclitus egg in a Buddhism blender. So to that end, and given this site's openness to thread necromancy, every quarter or so I'll nuke all these theory threads that have gone untouched for 3-6 months. Flush them away, no permanent record. Consider it the Sand Mandala treatment of theory discussion. If someone thinks that they're awesome enough to keep permanent, they'll flush out their ideas on them and keep them archived offsite; either in their blogs, on their personal sites, or in the Story Games wiki (I see no problem with date-archiving articles based on discussion there).

    Game Design. I'm waffling on doing the same for Game Design that I was thinking about for Theory. But for now, the group design threads are incredibly fulfilling for this site, and the personal "look at my stuff!" threads aren't NEARLY bothersome enough yet to go this route.

    What else was there... I'm forgetting something. More later.

    Can you maybe provide an example (even a made up one) about how it seems difficult to address one group without stepping on the toes of the other? I want to see what you are seeing so that I can perhaps find a solution if I agree.

    Sure, I'll make something up so as not to use names/posts. But in the end I'm sure the example will be about me anyway:)

    Let's say I want to talk about the marketing of story games and lament about why "we" (I refer to "we" as the community; I have no published paper-and-pencil games) aren't reaching out to a potentially wider audience with our games. I feel like some people take that as a dig on why they aren't doing more to market their own games or perhaps alter them to be more marketable to non-gamers. I feel like there are three different topics at play here:

    1. How I can make my game more awesome and push the boundaries of paper-and-pencil story gaming.
    2. How my game can more effectively be marketed to the existing customer base given the fact that this is probably a part-time hobby for me.
    3. How can the community as a whole grow itself past the "enlightened ex-D&D players" market and find greater commercial success.

    I feel that all three of these are very important, but it's difficult to talk about them all at the same time. I will say personally that as an experienced computer game designer, I am very interested in #1 as it is a new challenge for me. I feel like I already understand #2 pretty well and it isn't really relevant to my situation. And I am very interested in #3 because I want to see this genre see real growth and share the passion with many other non-gamers. But others will have differing takes on all three.

    And on this forum, I feel very comfortable talking about #1, I sense frustration from some game designers about #2, and I feel like I am barking up the wrong tree on #3. And so I wonder whether I should just shut up about trying to "popularize" story games and concentrate on trying to learn the craft of designing these games for my own non-commercial purposes.

    What do you think, Andy? Would a category called something like "Popularizing Story Games" be helpful here if the intent was not to tell someone else how to design or market their own game?
