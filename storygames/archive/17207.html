\n As I understand it, Ghost/Echo and Otherkind propose essentially two ways to modify a given roll: By adding bonus dice to select from when assigning goal and danger results and by adding directly to the goal roll result (I am explicitly excluding changes in narration as result of selected die color as it doesn't modify the roll, only the narration).

I'm eager to brainstorm other ways that can be used to modify an Otherkind roll.

Potential Otherkind Dice Modification Options
([Note X equals the count of goal + any dangers)
Bonus die (cancel a single penalty die or add to dice pool, roll, and then select only the highest X)
Penalty die (cancel a single bonus die or add to dice pool, roll, and then select only the lowest X)
Add + / - Y to goal result
Add + / - Y to any danger result
Add + / - Y to danger of specific type
Ignore 1 penalty die (but do not gain bonus die if no penalty dice)
Re-roll all dice
Re-roll Y dice (e.g. 1 die per point of a given currency spent)

Potential Triggers for Dice Modification
Spend resource to do [Modification Option]
Once per game do [Modification Option]
Take 1 [penalty currency] point to do [Modification Option]
When you do Z in the game, you may do [Modification Option]

What have I missed?

    \n Head over to rpgNow, download a free copy of FUBAR. It was basically designed as an exercise in playing with Otherkind (or Ghost/Echo) dice to give variable difficulties, and then tying the whole thing into a storytelling engine with a familiar RPG format rather than a die rolling engine on a double sided page. If you really want to explore it further, there are a few cheap supplements available for it.

    (In an abstract way, this list basically covers most of the options I've thought of though).

    I've also been playing with the notion of gaining automatic successes on (X number of dice) in certain circumstances. For example, you can "minimise your risks" in a field where you understand the theory and practice pretty well, this lets you merge two positive dice into a single automatic success. Less variability in the result, at the cost of less potential greatness (but you can only do this on a "class skill").

    \n I made a hack based on adding more math to Otherkind dice, Steampunk Crescendo:
    http://cid-7b2ca018d365f2e2.office.live.com/browse.aspx/Steampunk Crescendo?uc=1
    also
    http://www.lulu.com/spotlight/dindenver

    So, I played around with a lot of possible mechanics:
    Roll a bonus dice, but don't keep all dice
    Roll a bonus dice and keep it (In my version, this is determined before the dice are assigned, but it might be interesting to experiment with how the dice are assigned if the bonus is assigned after the dice are rolled)
    Roll a penalty dice, but don't keep all dice
    Roll a penalty dice and keep all dice
    Add 1 to one die assignment, subtract one from another
    Roll dice now, save them for later
    Re-roll one of the dice
    Add/Subtract to/from one die before it is assigned
    Add/Subtract to/from one die after it is assigned
    This is everything I had decided to use. I am sure there are others you can do to finagle it.
    We have played the living hell out of this game, so I can let you know how it affects actual play.

    Require lowest die to be assigned specifically.
    Require one die be rolled and assigned specifically (e.g. must be assigned to Narration), then roll the others and assign to the the rest.

    \n In the Yearbook,

    there are 3 categories to assign dice when you roll:
    - Are you successful?
    - Are you safe?
    - Are the people near you safe?

    The results mimic Psi*Run.

    You start with 1d6.
    If a Relationship is present or at stake, add 1d6.
    If an Accomplishment helps you, add 1d6.
    if a Regret motivates you, add 1d6.

    BUT:

    Advantages upgrade d6s to d8s.
    Disadvantages downgrade d6s to d4s.

    So when you roll, you may have a number of d4s,d6s,d8s.

    On a d4, you can't get the best result in category (but you can get a mixed success). On a d8, you are very likely to succeed at no cost but can still fail. D6s have an even chance.

    This worked REALLY well for the style of game and themes we were playing with.
