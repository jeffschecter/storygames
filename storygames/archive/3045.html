\n So I did a little actual play for my IRC persistant game, "Gears of Pain". http://gop.awardspace.com/gop.html
\n
\nI felt the game wasn't quickly moving enough, which is what I'd like to address with an added rule, the concept of "Prequels".
\n
\n"Prequels" are basicly color scenes that are controlled entirely by one player, written after one session of play, and mean to occur at the beginning of the next session that they play.
\n
\nPrequels have several purposes:
\n
\n1. To remove the need for a color or exposistionary scene in game-play.
\n
\n2. To provide a quick means for people to be invested in the other chracters in a given session.
\n
\nI have some concerns about the system, in that its very much like requiring players to play on a play-by-post forum(though not in a very interactive fashion, which I'd view as a weakness of play-by-post) and there's a possibility of a cognitive disconnect between the non-interactive prequels and the interactive game play.
\n
\nThoughts, suggestions for a better system, referalls to similar ideas?

    \n Sounds like bluebooking, Sean, which is an awesome technique in lots of games. Basically, players are allowed/encouraged/required to write out short scenes or journal entries for their characters in between the regular face-to-face sessions. This leads to some fantastic investment, although unless there is some mechanism to share the results, it can lead to some divergence and confusion, as I'm working off of an understanding of a bluebooking scene that you are unaware of.

    The term 'Prequel' seems to suggest that they happen before everything else in the game. If they're always between in-game action, may I suggest "interstitals'?

    \n For what it's worth, Full Light, Full Steam (my game) allows bluebooking between sessions. There is a slight reward to it: if the journal entry or whatever falls under the auspice of one of your thematic batteries, you can 'charge' it and use it in the subsequent session. So if in my journal entry I display how I'm a badass Veteran, in the next session I get a die-bonus when I do badass Veteran things. The thing of it is, this reward is actually pretty slight -- it's basically a modest bump to one die roll -- but it works, it gets players doing it, and more importantly to me, it helps focus those bluebooking entries to emphasize the characters, rather than being unfocused "and then I did this" sequences.
