    \n Depends. Sometimes its what people are saying. Someone saying something inflammatory and trying to get away. It's like "No offense, but that haircut looks stupid."

    But I think a lot more of it has to do with amatuer reading and writing. (No offense, that's just my opinion). What I mean by that is that formalized writing, at least in English, is taught to assume that you are writing your opinion. So, when a reader forgets that (or never learned it because only a few of us chumps took classes where we read a lot of essays) they can confuse defininitive statements with a statement of fact, even when it's clearly opinion: "Chocolate is gross." Since most forum posts I've seen are like that one, I'm blaming the readers. I'll back that up with: on this forum, there is a guideline to read charitably. And it works pretty well when its remembered. But in general, I think JD is wrong. It seems like quite a few people do not remember that everything is fundamentally opinion.

    But writers aren't entirely blameless. Aside from frank opinions, which sometimes are purposefully inflammatory or one sided, the writer's responsibility is to communicate clearly. In essay writing, this would be bolstered by analysis and references, something that generally isn't done (and probably is only sometimes warranted anyway). Other times its statements of opinion, disguised as fact. Like "Nobody likes chocolate!" means "I don't like chocolate, neither do most of my friends", or rather instead of "in my opinion" being omitted it's "in my experience." All of which is often not just subjective but hyperbole, even accidentally due to the way our memories are selective to reinforce what we want to believe.

    All of which is compounded by the immediate and casual nature of online correspondence in a medium that is broadcast without the subtle nuance or context in our usual immediate and casual methods of communication. Tacking on "this is my opinion" won't help. If the message arouses passion within you, it will look like a dodge or cushion. If you aren't particularly effected by it, it will look unnecessary. But reading charitably and writing with consideration will help.

    \n I don't think it's ever worthwhile to apologize for your opinion, whether it's in real life or on the internet.

    However, it's worthwhile to explicitly identify possibly-inflammatory statements as an opinion on the internet, in order to keep the flamewars down to a minimum. When a simple "I think" or "in my opinion" can get people to discuss something with you without jumping down your throat, it makes sense to use them. Part of writing clearly should always be to understand the needs of your audience, and on the internet, most of your audience really needs to see the weasel-words in order to sedate whatever part of their lizard hindbrain would normally leap to the keyboard to Prove You Wrong.

    \n I think it's a culture of followers that provokes this apologism. That culture doesn't automatically treat words as merely the speakers opinion, because they hope to find people who don't speak opinion and instead speak truth. If they were to treat each speakers word as just their opinion, they feel they would block themselves off from the 'truth speakers'.

    This means that to a degree they are always listening as if what they hear is true. So when what they hear clashes with their beliefs, they aggrivatedly blame the speaker for doing something 'wrong'. Because each time it adds another reason to not just believe what you hear, and the more of those reasons there are, the more they are seperated from the truth speakers they might find, or have found. It takes them awhile to flush those reasons away.

    It's not about just managing flamewars or writing clearly. When you pander to these audience needs, your line of enquiry gets damaged. Realise what your getting back is flawed because of all the consessions made in the enquiry and decide whether flawed goods are better than what you could get elsewhere (usually it's a choice of flawed goods or no goods at all). But don't bother thinking managing flame wars or meeting the needs of the audience perfectly will result in perfect goods, or your ending up in the same position as them - your not questioning what your getting. Being a well spoken little poster with lots of happy responces doesn't mean you get truth. And being a shit speaker usually damages the goods even more. I make no case for aggitation.

    It'd probably be good as the theme for a roleplay game, that whole situation. Add some fantasy element so theres a bit of distance and such, but otherwise good to go.
