    The democracy of pirate ships has been shown to be a direct precursor to the democracy of the US, what with it starting in port towns.

    So, pirates, like anyone else, mostly want to drink, fuck, and tell stories. Eating regularly is good, too. Presumably, they don't go around pirating unless they need something, or a particularly good score is on the way and their greed gets the better of them.

    As I understand it, pirate democracy worked something like this, though I'm certain it varied between ships:

    Someone has to be captain. They elect him. It's much less wasteful that having a mutiny every time the captain makes a bad decision. I doubt there are term limits, but I bet you can vote the guy down. Where it gets interesting is when there's a controversial decision and the crew is divided. That may be one place to take the game.

    You've got to have actual piracy in the game. Watch the food and gold dwindle, putting the pressure on, then go hunting when the crew's nervous enough. Pirates rarely killed anyone, believe it or not. Most people just aren't murderous enough, and if a crew gives up, that's better for everyone. Especially since sailor's have likely been pressed into duty, Shanghaied into service by their own government. It's a good way to get more pirates. But some ships won't give. A Spanish Man O' War is a floating castle, likely filled with gold, chocolate, and ransomable nobles. And, perhaps most importantly, wine, rum, and food. So you've got to have mechanics for stealing stuff and having sword fights.

    Me, I've always wanted to play a pirate ship that was a slave vessel, taken over by the slaves. Arabic is the common language on board, but patron gods line the gunwales. Religions clash, but everyone needs each other. Mutinous or otherwise antisocial crew are sold. Lots of seething crisis on a ship like that. Sometimes, the captain is Muslim, and rum is not allowed on board. Then they get a new captain.
