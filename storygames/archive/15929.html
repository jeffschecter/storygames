\n So we have a lunch hour Moldvay game that happens on Wednesday. I am looking for a better way of making random tables. I am familiar with the die drop tables and such. What I am looking for is a way to maximize the about of information that comes out of a minimal number of die rolls.

For an example:
Henchmen Table
I would Name, Occupation, Race, Appearance, etc.. but I don't want to roll more then a few times.
Any good ideas on how to handle this? Same goes for encounters etc...

Also if you know of some good resources for well design and/or unusual random tables please post them here too! Maybe with a little comment on why they are cool?

Thanks,

    \n Write a short program to do a bunch of random rolls and spit something out...

    Take a page from 1000 Blank White Cards. Give players blank cards and ask them to write either a name, notable detail, profession, or race on them (one on the top, one on the bottom. When you need a henchman, draw repeatedly from the pile and use either the top or bottom - your choice. Do the same for treasure (but with only one thing) but be clear that "only one person will get each card so don't put it on there if you'll be sad that someone else gets it" (to keep things reasonable). Tell them the setting (hills, underground, forest, swamp, plane of flames, etc) and have them write monster cards for that setting, while you're at it have them choose how much XP each monster is worth and you scale the difficulty to match (200xp for a goblin card, there are going to be bunches of them or one really powerful goblin). That way they design the setting and seal their own fate. When an awesome card comes up, give a couple XP as a tip to the person who wrote it.

    \n Use split-column format:
    \n
    \nWrite name/occupation/race/appearance etc
    \n
    \nacross the top, write the numbers down the side.
    \n
    \nHOWEVER: you can either roll once and read all the way across to get a henchman, or else roll once for each column. Or roll once and re-roll any specific result you don't like.
    \n
    \nExample:
    \nhttp://dndwithpornstars.blogspot.com/2012/01/split-column-assignment-generator.html
    \n_____________________
    \n
    \n
    \nOr use die-drop format:
    \n
    \nmake a table where you physically drop the die on the table--you read what the die lands on plus another table that is keyed to what number actually comes up on the die.
    \n
    \nExample:
    \nhttp://dndwithpornstars.blogspot.com/2011/05/quick-generator-for-when-you-need.html
    \n
    \nIn that example you extract even more information because you also read what kind of die comes up.

    \n It's worth putting some thought into whether you feel that the results of your tables A) make strong implications about demographics, physics, and other statistical data about the fictional world or if they are there more to provide surprise, variety, and independence of player action to GM decision. I am more of camp B but it seems to be basically a personal decision. Another way to look at it is does the table show what might happen on a typical day, or what might happen on an "interesting day" or under "heightened reality."
    \n
    \nFor example if you have a d20 table of available henchmen showing "20. 1st level Elf" do you take that to say "5% of the population looking for mercenary work are elves" or "while Elves are much rarer in this region, I do want to include the possibility of hiring an elf as an element that enters the game."
