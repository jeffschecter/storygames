    You didn't read any of my posts in that other thread, did you? I'm feeling unusually charitable, so here goes:

    For me, ethical includes the normative right and wrong mixed with the more complicated nuances of applied ethics.

    While Ralph comes close to maintaining that all decisions in an RPG are ethical (due to, I think, the creation of a fictional narrative in which we identify with the characters) I think we can also safely say there are other decisions going on: strategic, tactical, aesthetic (as GB Steve pointed out) and personal.

    I"m going to get in trouble for defining these because you're all going to quibble with my terms/definitions rather than trying to actually understand what I'm saying, but here goes:
    Strategic -- playing the game based on mechanical properties over multiple instances of play.
    Tactical -- optimizing mechanical play within a single instance of play.
    Aesthetic -- appealing to genre or doing what's cool, despite other mitigating factors.
    Personal -- recognizing that the other players at the table have needs/wants/preferences and addressing them over any other consideration.

    I think those four elemental decisions can be found in most game play.

    In Starcraft:
    I can ramp up harvesting early, and tell my allies to go zealot while I rapidly tech up.
    I can use a queen to neutralize your templar before I send in my guardians.
    I can choose to build an army of firebats in Starcraft merely because it's cool. Got a light? (x48!!!)
    I can choose to pull back from an attack and let your base survive because I want the game to go on longer or because you're screaming and frothing from the next room and have stated, "If you nuke me again, I will murder you." Sorry dude. It's just a game! ;-)

    You know what I can't do in Starcraft? I can't make an ethical decision -- AT ALL. There's no right and wrong in Starcraft. There's only winning or losing. When I sacrifice a small band of marines in a crystal patch to act as a distraction (and die), I am not weighing their needs against the needs of the base. There is no gut wrenching decision, tearful good byes or tragic ending for them. I merely made a tactical (or strategic, depending on the situation in the game) decision.

    It's a magical thing that when we play that game with so much blood and carnage, there are no ethical decisions contained within. Perhaps sitting down to play games about simulated war is an ethical decision. The decision to cheat in a game is certainly ethical. But both of those examples are above and beyond the act of game play itself.

    The magic of a roleplaying game is that space for an engaging, gripping ethical decision DOES exist right in the fundamental framework of the game. In a roleplaying game, giving the order to leave your friends behind because you need that dropship NOW is powerful and affecting -- there's situation, context, fiction, emotion and, ultimately, narrative. Your decision resulted in the death of one or more of your friends. You bastard! How could you?! To get that final holy grail of narrative, you start us off with an ethical decision.
