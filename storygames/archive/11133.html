\n An essential feature of monsters is that they have a weakness. Sauron's weakness is the One Ring; H. G. Wells' Martians are vulnerable to bacteria; the Death Star has a thermal exhaust vent.

But we rarely see it in roleplaying games. In investigative games, you might sometimes find a ritual to banish a monster. But you don't just banish it. There'll always be some threat; some twist in the tale; some reason you can't just tick the ritual box and save the world.

In fighting games, it's even harder. It would be really unsatisfying to walk into the dragon's lair, stab it with the ancient sword and see it die. There's got to be a battle first. We want to fight the dragon and, only at the last minute, when all seems lost, exploit its weakness.

But how could that work, mechanically? Is it even possible?

Graham

    \n A crunchy mechanical example from a hit point model would be something like the rules for staking Vampires in the Buffy RPG. You can *try* to stake a vampire any time you want - if the damage you do, multiplied by 5, would be enough to take the vampire to zero then they're dusted instantly. If not, they just take normal damage, and are probably upset with you. So, it's best to "soften them up" with conventional attacks before going for the big finish.

    In more story-gamey terms, maybe you have to build up a certain number of victories before you can exploit the weakness. Sure, we know where the thermal exhaust vent on the Death Star is, but getting close enough to attack it requires getting past the gun towers and all those pesky TIE fighters. Plus, every time you lose a dice throw on the way, you lose a wingman. Rest in peace, Porkins.

    \n Hey Graham,

    As I like to play it, traditional gaming is all about the interplay between fighting the monster and exploiting a weakness.

    Fighting games have a mechanic you engage to destroy the monster. So fighting the monster essentially comes down to rolling the dice until the monster (or you) dies. Exploiting the weakness means not engaging those mechanics and thus defeating the monster in a different way. To my mind, traditional games are all about this division. In fact, a lot of games now have this as an explicit part of the game.

    In Storming the Wizards Tower, every encounter has a weakness by design, period. It's there to give the characters an alternate path to victory. You will probably use the same mechanics, but the fiction of the weakness means you might use different actions, spells, resources, and so on.

    Poison'd has a set of "fight" mechanics and a set of "non-fight" mechanics. If, say, I roll sneakyness to steal the Bosun's pistols before I get into a fight with him, I'm creating a weakness to exploit. 3:16 similarly has a stat for fighting and a stat for everything else.

    In a lot of OD&D, you can easily encounter a monster that simply can't be killed normally. A randomly encountered wolf pack may as well be the Death Star for a first level character. The game then becomes all about looking for a weakness to exploit so you can survive. About half the time this weakness turns out to be "if you set them on fire with a flask of oil they die!" Also, many of these games have no specific mechanics for this, which can create a lot of problems. Ben Lehman's HQRPG is specifically designed to exploit the situation of the overwhelming threat to drive thoughtful play.

    D&D 4.0 has the skill challenge, which can readily be applied as a mechanic for exploiting the weakness. Mind you, because of the way D&D 4.0 is structured, you are less likely to need to do so unless the DM specifically sets up the encounter to require it.

    Exploiting the weakenss can still be difficult! A vampire is a pretty bad ass foe for a low level D&D character even if that character knows the weaknesses of vampires.

    \n Storming the Wizard's Tower has monster-building rules that actually require monsters to have a weakness (chosen from a list), so that a very successful Finding Things Out roll can uncover it. That underlines Storming's commitment to maps \u2014 which, in the game's terms, means any kind of information tool, like an orally-transmitted history, or a tapestry depicting a legendary battle, or a memorable, boozy conversation with a washed-up old adventurer at a tavern. The weakness is supposed to reflect the monster's theme, and discovering and exploiting it entails telling a story around that theme.

    Other games have said it's OK to defeat the bad guys by being clever, but then not provided mechanical support. Leave it to Saint Vincent to make a game that does what it says. I am so eager to play Storming that it hurts.

    \n Monster weaknesses are incredibly common in video games. Broad categories are

    Weak Point: requires the player master the game's controls and identify movement patterns or "tells" to time shots so that they hit the Weak Point.

    Damage Type Weakness: requires the player manage various ways of dealing damage, and then determine through experimentation or research what kind of attacks are most effective.

    Of the two, the second adapts most easily to RPGs since it's largely resource allocation and creative thinking, as opposed to a feat of dexterity.

    As a weird experiment in a D&D-like fight, you could have the GM and the players play a game of Mastermind during combat, each player making an attempt during their turn. When a player uncovers the GM's pattern, the monster's weak point is exposed that round and struck for massive damage or killed instantly or what have you. But standard critical hit rules are supposed to cover this stuff.

    "Getting under the dragon" would require only that the mechanics of the game make attaining position an interesting challenge, which is why you see it all the time in video games. The flanking rules of D&D 3e get at this a little bit, too. Abstract positioning, and you eventually get back to "hit points," which are just a count-down clock to monster death. Maybe 15 damage = got into a better position for your next strike in the game narrative, so in the end your character only hit the dragon once as far as fiction is concerned.

    \n I think what you need is the right mix of player skill and chance. Chance determines when the weakness is exposed; player skill is used to manage resources so that when the weakness is exposed, the character can take advantage of it.
    \n
    \nThat requires things like:
    \n- Being alive. Build your character right, and utilize his/her offensive and defensive advantages to stay in the fight long enough.
    \n- Being in the right position when the weakness is exposed: Stay close to the monster, make sure the monster can't run away, don't be behind the monster when its belly is exposed.
    \n- Having the right weapon/macguffin. You can't confront the Final Boss Monster before you've managed to find the macguffin, which requires thinking and skill.
    \n
    \nYou could roll dice every round of combat, for example, to see if the weakness is exposed. Then, if your character is conscious and in the right place and has the right weapon, they might be able to kill it. If not, more combat rounds, and the real threat of death or other severe loss. No chance of losing = no challenge.
