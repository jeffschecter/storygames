\n Ran my first game of Maid: The Roleplaying Game tonight. It was INSANE! Like, crackhead insane! It equaled and exceeded my expectations for leaving everyone breathless with laughter!

It was a oneshot for a group of players who were all meeting for the first time (two men and two women) and fortunately everyone was into the concept and the genre and really, really ran with it.We all decided to keep it rated PG-13 and despite some of the gutter level humor and innuendo, never had anything that squicked anyone out.

Chargen was quick and set the tone - once people started rolling stuff up everyone knew what kind of nutso game it was going to be. For the scenario, I used the Favor Race idea, coupled with getting through the daily schedule at the mansion. I decided it would be the day after the Master's birthday, so the house was littered with presents in every room (which could be opened by anyone for a roll on the extensive random items tables). So other than the various tasks on the schedule (make breakfast, clean up, etc) there was no scenario - I decided to allow the game to be entirely driven by the characters and what became judicious use of random events.

There is no way I could relate what happened in the game in sequence. Once it got started and the random events started going off and presents opened, it all just blew up! Some highlights I remember:

* The Master changing from a man to woman (because he accidentally was served the Milk of Fertility by one of the maids).
* One of the maids was actually a man - this coupled with the above led to all kinds of madness!
* All of the maids messing with each other in an effort to curry favor with the Master - lots of "accidental" trips, nasty glares, mops in the face, etc.
* The sex-changed Master being disguised as a man through the application of Ace bandages (wrapped tight) and a rolled up pair of socks...
* More double entendres, over-the-top slapstick, and crazed action than I can describe - I laughed so hard my face was sore afterwards.
* Whacko magical maid powers

For me, it was a real GMing workout. Every time one of the players initiated a random event, I had to figure out how to work it into the action. More than once when I looked up the results of an event roll, I was like "What the hell?!" The players said the look on my face was priceless. A couple of times I just had to shake my head and roll with it. It was all off the cuff and totally seat-of-the-pants, and really fun!

I'll be looking forward to running this again!

    \n Part of the issue with the game's accessibility is the thing that otaku culture, as a geek culture, depends a lot on references, and embedded in all the tables are references to stuff that went beyond even my level of knowledge. If you roll up a Plugsuit on the costume change table, or you roll that the Fist of the North Star shows up on the Post-Apocalyptic random event table, some people might wind up scratching their heads a bit.

    I don't think it would be too much of a problem to play the game, especially if you stick more to the basic rules. You can always work in your own references to whatever you do know. (Just ask Leonidas). Although my friends are all into anime, relatively few of them have actually seen much of anything in the genre proper[*], but it's mostly turned out just fine. You might might want to get people to watch/read some of, say, He Is My Master or Hayate the Combat Butler to better get in the mood.

    At least, that's what I hope. When it comes to otaku stuff, my perspective is kind of warped these days. Hardcore otaku will probably get the very most out of Maid RPG, but hopefully everyone can get something out of it.

    Filip,
    To me, Maid RPG is more of a slapstick game that has the potential to be pretty serious if you choose to make it that way (and some of the scenarios and replays show how to do this, in some cases in mid-stream). We got the designer to write a bit about creating the game and his intentions, and he said he wanted a game where the character had "romantic appeal," and then went on to basically say, "Just go do whatever you want with it."

    [*]There was the same thing when I playtested my Raspberry Heaven game. A bunch of my friends have barely watched any Azumanga Daioh. The heathens.

    \n When I got the game, I expected (for some reason, based on what I had heard about the Japanese gamer love of crunch) was a "we miss the point of humor by making too many systems" comedy game. What I truly expect was "Tales from the Floating Vagabond" to the nth degree...
    \n
    \nI am so glad I was totally wrong! I cannot imagine that there are people who can read over the basic rules, put it to work, and not get some really fun and funny play out of it. The game is a absolute and total delight and I actually got to the end of looking it over and said "I need some more tables". Those who know me know that the tables in the this book had to be something special (so special, I had previously thought that only "Doc" Cross and S. John Ross could write such tables).
    \n
    \nAdd to that the random elements system that reminds me of the curse system in "Dork Tower" (which is abused to great fun by my gaming pals). The game is and absolute delight and I intend to play this game at a pal's up coming Bachelor Party - so geek it hurts!
