    \nHow Not To Throw Up

    Some guidelines for public speaking, with some nifty advice that may be applicable to GM'ing, especially for cons and demoing. I liked this gem:\n
    Use silence as punctuation. My favorite trick in the book especially since I'm a fast talker. When you hear yourself gaining verbal momentum, stop. Count backwards from 5. Walk across the stage. Resume. These breaks are going to give both you and your audience a chance to mentally regroup.

    \n Some interesting insights on predictability in a script that seem to translate well to running a game. The key point that got me:

    In this case, I don't think "predictable" is the problem. What it is, is a symptom.

    What is really going on is that the bones of the structure are showing. We aren't invested enough in the characters; the scenes aren't fleshed out enough. So, alienated, we stand back and observe the structure. We have time to think about how predictable it is because we're not involved in what's going on in the scenes.

    -Rob D.

    \nJax Film Festival
    The Annual Jacksonville Film Festival has a documentary about a LARP held in Seattle.

    Here's the blurb on the Jax Film Festival site:
    "MONSTER CAMP is a rare and fascinating glimpse into the world of live-action role playing or \xe2\u20ac\u0153LARPing,\xe2\u20ac\x9d a real-life version of World of Warcraft where gamer stereotypes are simultaneously shattered and confirmed. The film meticulously examines the lives of die-hard gamers at NERO Seattle, one of 50 franchises worldwide that abides by similar principles, rules and ideologies. At NERO, for 48 consecutive hours, participants immerse themselves in a world completely unlike our own. Software engineers, department store managers, and high school students by day, for one weekend each month these NERO players transform themselves into warriors, sorcerers, dwarves, and lizard people. It is a place where people can be anything, dress any way, and have almost any power. Where players inhabit a world of fantasy, chivalry, and imagination, if for only one weekend a month. "

    I find it interesting that it is compared to World of Warcraft instead of D&D. I know WoW is much more popular and visible, but it is fascinating nonetheless.

    \n From one of the Game Studies mailing lists I'm on:\n
    I just wanted to spread the word about something new we have launched at
    Carnegie Mellon: The Game Innovation Database v2.0.

    You can see it here: www.gameinnovation.org
    .
    You may have heard about an earlier version of this project -- but the new
    version is radically different and vastly improved.

    The goal of the project is to document *every* innovation that has ever been
    made in the history of video game development. To do this, we have developed
    a graphical system of displaying how different innovations build on each
    other. Further, the system is designed to easily support both contributors
    (people who want to add new content), and editors (people who want to manage
    and clean up that content).

    Our sincere hope is to build a coalition of faculty from different schools
    to serve as editors, so that one day this database can grow, thrive, and
    become a shared resource the entire game education community has developed
    together.

    Also: We have found here at Carnegie Mellon that giving an assignment of
    "contribute three new innovations" into the database serves as an excellent
    homework assignment in Game Design classes -- it gets students to really
    think about the nature and history of innovations, and they get very
    interested in making a contribution that others can use. If you would like
    to use this method in your classes, we'd be happy to support you any way we
    can!

    If you are interested in joining the GIDb coalition as an editor or
    contributor, please sign up at the site, and/or drop me an email!

    We plan to have a list of all schools that participate in the coalition
    listed on the front page of the site -- so do let me know if you are
    interested.

    If you have comments or questions about the site, and how it works, the site
    has forums where you can post them -- we really would like your feedback --
    we are seriously committed to doing whatever it takes to make this site be a
    valuable resource for the game education community.

    -Jesse Schell
    Asst. Prof. of Entertainment Technology, Carnegie Mellon University

      In the not-related-to-sgames-but-related category: "The Great Pyramid of Agile":http://worsethanfailure.com/Articles/The-Great-Pyramid-of-Agile.aspx

      \n

      Backstory: There is a new/hot/useful/faddish/wankish philosophy for software development called "Agile" that has been getting more mindshare lately. It's a revolutionary theory / a proven methodology for great new success / more of a manifesto / just a regurgitation of common sense from ten years ago / nonsense.

      \n

      WorseThanFailure (used to be: "The Daily WTF") is a storied software-development curmugeonblog. The lampoon awful, dumb, badwrongfun ways of programming that lead to awful software stories, but lately they've also taken a more critical tone, asking questions like: Why does it have to be this way? What are we doing to wrong that we're engendering awful, critical failures in our field? It's an important dose of general skepticism and focus on good, basic practice - especially in the wake of all the new fads that pointy-hared bosses and insane venture capitalists try to push.

      \n

      So. Read the article. Sound familiar? I think it's an interesting and perennial issue among different kinds of people seeking innovation the field they care about.

      \n
