\nI don't know what Maddrjeffe meant when he meant that Rule Zero was useful in situations like this ("You fail"? That has its own sets of problems...). But, when I saw this problem, the first thing I thought was, "Oh, shit... time to stall."

Let me try to explain. I mean, yeah, if I find myself in this situation, it's likely that I overlooked something before the game began, because I get this feeling that players don't start dicking around with you on rules unless there's an underlying problem with the game you're playing.

But it's also certainly likely that players will try something innovative and unexpected that you yourself didn't figure. If that's the case, what do we do?

I've found myself in a couple of situations like these before: again, mostly because of lack of planning on my part. Fine, okay. The thing is, though, when I was in that kind of bind, the first thing I did was to ask as many questions as I possibly could about the situation that the players wanted to engineer. In other words...

How did they know that the tapestries had cyanide with them?
Do their characters know if the poison would remain in the silk, even after the silk had been washed and treated for display?
Do their characters know if the poison would be released as cyanide gas if the tapestry is burned?
How are they setting up the fire?
Do they know which direction the Tong guy is coming?
What do they know about the Tong guy's armaments, equipment and bodyguards?
Do they have an escape route?

That sort of thing. And at this point, while I'm taking down their answers, I'll be asking myself further questions:

Would the tapestry burn easily?
Would it release smoke if it burns, or would it too easily?
How would the gas behave?
What kind of gas would it be (would it still be lethal)?
Would the players have a good idea where the gas would go (or do they miscalculate)?
Would the players be able to control the fire?
Would anyone be alerted by the burning tapestries?
Does the Tong guy prepare for situations that involve poison gas?
Does the Tong guy know about this plan? How would he know about this plan? What would he do to counter it?

One way to answer this question would be to structure each and every one of these questions in some way as a conflict or a task to be performed -- basically, a dice roll and a lookup table. If you have a generalized conflict resolution method, this is useful. If not, I suppose you make it up -- Rule Zero again!

In any case, a suggestion or a plan that would throw off a GM has been turned into an interesting situation, or at least a hilarious clusterfuck ("He's got a gas mask! Shit! The fire's spreading! Geez, someone put it out -- why are you all lying down?")

Am I doing it right here? What other ways would there be to handle unexpected situations from your players? What do you guys think?

    \n Ben makes a good point, though. In the original post, Maddrjeffe says that the players were "meant to" have a throwdown with the bodyguards. Rather than structuring games around what the players are meant to do, you can just see throw situations at them and see how they respond.

    I think the questions you ask yourself are good. Some general rules-of-thumb, I think, would be:

    1. Try to build on any idea that a player gives you. So, if they burn down the tapesties because they're cyanide-laced, don't say "Yeah, there's no cyanide in there" or "Yeah, there's cyanide, but it burns when you burn the tapestries."

    2. Having said that, don't be a push-over. You're allowed to block ideas that seem lazy or wildly unrealistic. If a player says "Oh, I throw a rabbit at him, which claws him to death.", you're allowed to say "No, that doesn't work."

    3. And you're allowed to add complications, too. Just because they've released the cyanide doesn't mean the bodyguards can't attack as they're choking to death.

    (Um, I need to subtly plug my book Play Unsafe, which is all about this stuff.)

    \n Well, first things first: I like it if the game gives me at least a general framework for this sort of thing (many do--some don't).

    The second thing I do is think about what *I* believe would happen in reality (or in a book or movie) if this happened.

    Then, if that doesn't seem to yield results, I ask the players what their expectations are for the outcome.

    Then I adjudicate based on that input.

    In the tapestry situation I would probably do the following:
    1. See if there are rules for poison gas (or, failing that ... drowning--the most mock-worthy of rules, I'm sure!)
    2. I would be totally confused by this approach to generating poison gas--and I would ask the group if they had ever heard of deadly burning silk? I might google "silk cyanide smoke"

    (shockingly, I found this: http://carolinanewswire.com/news/News.cgi?database=1news.db&command=viewone&id=1763&op=t\n
    Fire smoke is a common source of cyanide exposure. Cyanide may be produced by the pyrolysis (incomplete burning) of common synthetic or plastic materials as well as from natural materials such as wood, paper and silk. Cyanide is increasingly recognized as a common and dangerous component of fire smoke, adding to the effects of carbon monoxide as a fire smoke toxicant. In fact, cyanide poisoning and carbon monoxide poisoning most often occur together during a fire. Prompt recognition and treatment of cyanide poisoning with an antidote can save lives.
    \nAnd from another link:\n
    Recent research has revealed that hydrogen cyanide present in smoke generated at building fires may play a more significant role in injuries and deaths of firefighters and civilians than once thought.
    \nSo it seems that, yes, burning silk can produce cyanide.

    3. However: reading the links, burning silk does not produce invisible, deadly cyanide gas--it produces cyanide laced smoke--and the deaths associated with it are the kind associated with smoke inhalation: this means that there's got to be a lot of smoke. So if creating a massive, smoky fire will kill the guy then some portion of that danger will be from the added poison.

    4. So okay: I would discuss this with the group and say that their plan will essentially involve killing him through smoke inhalation--not subtle and not fast. It'll also obscure the room.

    I would use the drowning rules (possibly modified) to run the encounter if they decided to go along with it.

    \n Oh shit, I have a story about this.

    I was playing Burning Wheel for the first time and Luke was running The Gift, in which Elves and Dwarves clash. We had a full table, four elves on one side and four dwarves on the other. As the game wore on, one of the players, who was turtling quite a bit, was looking more and more uncomfortable, stopped doing anything.

    Luke ran to the bathroom during a break.

    "Guys, I don't think this is what he intended for this adventure at all."

    He said it as if it was a terrible thing, as if we were ruining the GM's plans. He was so stressed out and upset.

    "I think this is the kind of thing that he wanted. He didn't have a hard and fast plan."

    The constipated player didn't look convinced but he did eventually loosen up and rock out for the latter part of the adventure.

    From then on, whenever I started a game at a con with gamers I didn't know, I'd say, "I don't know where this game is going to go but that's cool, we're all going there together and I'm sure it'll rock." This not only let's players know to go for it full throttle, it also let's them know what kind of gamer I am. I've seen players visibly sigh in relief and relax when I've said that.

    \n Tariq,

    Yeah. I sometimes don't know how to react to what players do. Here are some things that may help you:

    1.) Graham Walmsley wrote a book called Play Unsafe. The groundbreaking advice he gives in that book is: "Be boring". It is very hard to resist the urge to make things more awesome, more tense, more face-stabby, more glitzy. But push yourself to just say the most obvious and easy thing that comes to mind.

    The players light the tapestries on fire, pouring cyanide smoke into the room? My first thought on how to react is that the room fills with black smoke, then I decide that's not cool enough, and decide they start sporadic explosions and a little girl who's caught in the smoke poses a moral dilemma.

    Okay, that's stupid. That's stupid because a room full of cyanide smoke was intense enough. So I back up. I describe the black smoke. I describe the obvious thing, which is that the only pursuit possible is a clumsy pursuit at this point. And probably one guys gets through, and maybe the players kill him. the obvious and boring thing is that he dies easily and quickly, which totally doesn't give away their location to the blind chasers. And probably some of the chasers are smart, and the obvious and boring thing would be that they pull back and start looking for the long way out, totally de-escalating the current station because that's the obvious thing to do.

    And... that freakin' rocks. Now you have a squad of pursuers who are racing to cut off the PCs, and the PCs aren't sure where they are coming from... the first one went down easily, but he was caught by surprise. So we're still not sure if they are formidable foe.

    And then if one of the players asks about the dead guy, you COULD say that he is holding the Vial of Gladeron, but that's too interesting and surprising. You could say that he is the PCs' long lost brother/ex wife. Again, too interesting and surprising. So you do the obvious, boring thing, and describe him as being plain-ish, in his mid-twenties, and clean-cut. And you realize that maybe now is the perfect time to describe what they're up against, so you mention that he has grenades, a semi-automatic pulse weapon, and well-defined forearms. And you want some kind of visual marker to describe how he's a badass soldier, so you reach for the low-hanging fruit. The most obvious thing you can find, which is stolen right out of bad 80s action movies: He's got a big, grizzly scar running the length of his face.

    And bam. That was so obvious. So boring. So exactly typical.
    And it fucking rocks. Go and read it again. Not only does it rock, but because it's all typical and expectable, it gives players a lot of ground to jump from. If you'd described things in this innovative, intense way... it'd have closed many more doors.

    2.) "The Mountain Witch Trick"

    Leave the players to fill in the important/interesting detail. So, "most of your pursuants were caught in the smoke, and only one managed to make it through to you in reasonable time. Who was it?" Alternately, ask, "But as the smoke filled the room, it suddenly dawned on you why this course of action would pose problems for you. Why is that?"

    The tricky thing about solitaire poignancy is that it really requires you to be on your A-game and pander to everyone's interests. And sometimes, you are right there. But this thread isn't about the moments where your GMing pops. It's about when your GMing fails to pop. So, when you aren't sure what the shiny, make-everyone's-head-nod-simultaneously moment is, ask someone else to finish your sentence.

    The brilliant part about this is that no matter what the person answering says, it's correct. You've given their answer absolute authority, so that they can answer confidently. By phrasing that question, you've pre-loaded their answer with importance. By having it be a two person collaboration, you've proven that it wasn't pre-planned, and that spontenaity will help it rock.

    3.) Ask the players

    The simplest thing to do in this situation is say "Wow. That's a pretty innovative twist. I'm kind of stumped as to where to take this..."

    And then follow that up with any combination of:
    "Where did you see this going?"
    "How would the bad guys react?"
    "What will you be doing next if things go according to plan?"
    "What if the smoke doesn't catch?"
    "Who is doing what in this situation?"
    "What would happen next?"
    "Can any of you guys help me out here?"

    \n How do I deal with that sort of thing?
    First I go, "Shhwwaaaat?" Then I start nodding my head.

    Take, f'rinstance, this, from a game of the Rustbelt:
    In the previous session, PC Brown Jenkins, played by Steven, asked the NPC mechanic Mule-Ear Joe where to find the brakeline on a car so that he could cut the brakeline on a car that belonged to a known spy (who didn't know that Jenkins had made her for a spy). Late in the next session (well after the spy and her contract killer accomplice had been dealt with), Jenkins is being captured at gunpoint by a minor antagonist and his goons. He decides to stand down, so they're taking him to the car. Just before they put him in the car, he breaks loose from their grasp, and he rolls under the car. I'm thinkin, "What is he doing?" And so is Steven. He's not even sure why he did that. Until something clicks and he says, "I cut the brakeline!"
    And I go, "Shhwhaaat?"
    And he goes, "That is, if it's in the same place as the last one?"
    And I go, "It is NOW!"

    Then he gives himself up, playing it off like that was just a dumb desperation move, and they eventually run into an intersection because they have no brake fluid left and get hit by a truck. Jenkins survived the accident, as did one of the thugs, who Jenkins promptly strangled with a seatbelt. Thanks to the resolution system, all of this was easy to handle, so I just ran with it.

    This sort of thing is exactly why I love flexible resolution systems with some degree of abstraction. Everyone should have one, even if only to fall back on when the less-abstract mechanics don't apply.
