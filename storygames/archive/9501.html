\n So I've been listening to a lot of Clyde's old episodes, and his first interview with Ron Edwards and subsequent discussion of flags and bangs sprouted in me this thought: detailed character histories (or, in SoK speak, "playing before you play") are basically a (unconscious, mostly) coping mechanism for people who really want Story Now gaming but find their desires frustrated by the traditional systems they play in. That is, they want, in the course of play, to be able to play out all this really cool stuff but find that they can't, and so they make really complex backstories as a way to secondarily fulfull this desire.

I realize I'm not talking about anything new when it comes to RPG theory, so if you read this and go, "duh", well, that's cool. Just say that or skip the thread. I'm formulating my own thoughts to myself, and putting them up here in case they're helpful to anyone else.

    \n I once wrote this on this subject:

    After reading this post by a script writer:
    Character Descriptions In Pitches

    I felt drawn to these words of his on characters:

    1. What do they want? Why can't they get it? How do they go about trying to get it?
    2. What are they like?
    3. And only then, if necessary, the minimum backstory necessary to explain what they're doing in the story of the series.

    Which is where RPGs generally differ, since they tend to be inverted. We come up with a huge detailed backstory, figure out what they are like, and then completely ignore that first question. They don't want anything. They are static.

    Which then makes me think of this post on characters (by another script writer), specifically, Mary Sue characters:

    Twilight of the Innocents

    The quote I really digged on in her post was this one:

    To me, this is what most procedurals are about. Being plot-driven, a really compelling character would just get in the way. You want characters who will deliver the information that will move the plot forward. You want them to be super-smart, to not be tricked or fooled, to go up against the villains.

    Since so much of roleplaying is plot-driven (like a procedural), you need these heavy backstory, static characters, because characters with wants who are in motion (characters who can answer number one from above) would do their own thing, ignoring the plot the GM has prepared.

    Dynamic and static characters both serve a purpose and neither is better than the other (although most of us probably have a preference, I know I do), however, groups are in trouble if there isn't some kind of consensus about which type should exist in the game they are playing together.

    Because static characters in a game that isn't plot driven will just stand around staring at each other boring all the players, and dynamic characters in a plot-driven game will frustrated the players as they tug of war over the fiction.

    \n "the group" and "the system" (in the lumpley principle sense) are simply two faces of the same coin.

    Why that desire is frustrated with that group? Because all these cool things are not used/played/added to the SIS. Why? Because the system used by the group don't allow that use (because the system is based on GM's fiat, because the system include people laughing at anybody who would try to add things like these, or for any other reason).

    It's always the system used by the group. So, talking about "the system" INCLUDE the group. and include the books, the habits, the social pressure, etc. Everything.

    Changing it to "the group" only mean blaming a single factor for everything. And, seeing that "the group" include in this case "the victim", is another case of that common worldwide sport, "blaming the victim". After all, it's YOUR fault for following these books advice, right?

    \n Certainly many games have things that fulfill the roles of flags and bangs, but some inspire more background writeups than others. I don't think it's the failings of some games that push people to write more, but the degree to which their chosen game relies on pre-game preparation. A game that relies more on improvised story may discourage such extensive preparation by encouraging people to leave it abstract until it emerges during play. I don't think that's the biggest influence, though.

    The biggest influence, I suspect, is the presence of a powerful and inspiring collection of symbols/language within the game. People seem to enjoy exploring "charged" concepts, ideas which imply a great deal more (e.g. the way "knights" imply chivalry, glory, daring, etc.), and the way people explore such concepts is through creating story. A game that inspires people to write a lot before play isn't falling short. The players are so eager to get started that they've started early.

    I think that background stories can function like flags and bangs, but can also go beyond, revealing some things that flags and bangs have difficulty conveying, such as desired pacing and tone. It doen't mean that the game that results will be unsatisfying (or that previous games were). Just the opposite, the communication of such things can increase the chance that the resulting games will be satisfying.

    \nI don't disagree. I'm not talking about a failing of games/mechanics/texts, but rather a discrepancy between what a particular kind of player wants and what a particular kind of system delivers.

    My conjecture, reformulated, states: A player who wants Story Now at the table, but cannot get it within the confines of the system she's playing in, or the group she's playing in, will make a detailed character backstory in an effort to fulfill that desire. It's my belief, based on experience, that that detailed character backstory will not adequately fulfill the desire.

    Again, when I read that, I say to myself "duh!". But as with most things, I have to say them out loud to myself to really understand them. Such is the curse of the external processor.

    \n I would be leery of assigning one big motivation to people for writing huge character backstories. Some people like to do it and see it as part of their preparation for play, others are looking for something they don't get in play. Others don't write significant histories at all, and still others can't. They don't know who their character is until they have played them a bit.

    \n Another term for "playing before play" is "98%ing", a term I heard on the Forge somewhere, but that hardly ever gets used anymore, it seems.
    I'll always remember this game of Exalted I played in college - I played a creepy ol' Abyssal named Ex, and Ex was firmly convinced that death, on a worldwide scale, really would save us all from a lot of pain and suffering. Despite his good intentions, his undead masters decided they'd rather use their power to fight each other, and had none of the transcendent, watery-eyed zeal that Ex possessed, leaving him feeling like his masters didn't care about "the struggle". This made for great conflict!
    The rules didn't really get in the way of this actually, but my group sure did. Out of a huge group (five other players; huge for me), only one other player (also portraying an Abyssal, as it happens) cared about what I had going on with Ex. When Ex forsook his lords to join the Solars (who at least were fighting for something!), the GM gave me a puzzled look and went back to refereeing the last combat scene of the game. Sighhhh For me, it was about scenery-chewing, not writing reams of background, but my abnormal level of chewing set me apart from the group, for the most part.
    Hansel, I think I managed to locate an effective way of achieving Story Now for myself without a huge background - Ex was a battlefield looter before he was taken by the death-lords, and ... that was about all I had - that he'd seen a lot of horrible stuff, and death seemed more like a mercy than a terror. Like I said, the player who seems to be interested and energetic about something, but preoccupied, would be the person I would focus on - someone who's really latched onto something, but seems out of step with the rest of the group.

    \n Well, I hope nobody here think himself as able to understand perfectly the personal, subconscious reasons for the actions of a lot of people they don't even know (or even if they are talking about people they know, people usually go to professional help that take really a lot of time and efforts to reach that kind of understanding, not to their GM). I took what Hans said more as an "think about this possibility" than as a "everybody do it for the same reason".

    And, in any case, it' could be said that writing a lot of background IS the sign that the player want to express something about that character that he can't in play. But sometimes he can't simply because it is something that take a lot of time and concentration and can't be done at the table, or simply because he don't play enough to satiate his hunger to play that character. But the fact that sometimes it's not a sign of a problem, should not be a reason to forget that that problem exist and should be noticed when it shows up and be avoided.

    Sometimes, I get the impression that years of good, fulfilling playing has made a lot of people simply forget how it's "out there", what the common experience of role playing is, for a lot of people. I am not talking about "we are going to save these poor gamers", it would be silly. I am simply saying that the push for better game systems came up in the first place because problems as these were (and they still are) very, very common, in a lot of playing group. Saying "it's not a given that everybody do that for this reason" it's not the same as saying "the problem doesn't exist".
