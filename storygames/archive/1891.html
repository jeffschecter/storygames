Characters without goals... kinda ranty...

Gaerik
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Actual Play
So, one of my players went all ape-shit over the World's Largest Dungeon book which is 900 pages, 16 poster maps, and 1600 encounters of dungeon crawling goodness. He went out and bought the thing ($100) and plopped it in my lap. "We wanna play this!", my group chanted in unison and then offered the appropriate sacrifices of pizza and beer. I was a little apprehensive about running such a huge dungeon but I kinda like dungeon crawls too. Also, I started reading the text and was pleasantly surprised at how well the designers put the product together. They really thought about making the product easy to use without having to flip back and forth between 3 or 4 different sourcebooks. Maybe I'll review the product later but for the sake of this discussion, let's just say I liked the product enough on reading it to give running it a go.

Now, one of the things the designers say in the text is that the normal XP system outlined in the DMG breaks down if this dungeon is run as 1 huge campaign. There are simply too many encounters and the characters will find themselves leveling way faster than usual. So, they said to either just hand out XP at various plot points in the dungeon or to find an alternative XP system to use. Great! I thought. I can try out the Wyrd XP system that I read about over on the Forge. It works by allowing the players to chose goals for their characters, giving the players bonuses when pursuing those goals, and then rewarding them richly upon completing the goals. That rocks and I figured would shoe horn quite well into the game.

So I brought the new XP system up to the players and two of them basically said, "I don't like my character to have goals!"

WTF? I was blown away. It has never, ever in all my years of gaming crossed my mind to play a character without having goals. What the hell do you do with the character if you don't have any goals for it? I mean, if I can't think of some cool goal for a character then I just trash the thing and create a new one. This isn't even a product of playing these new fangled hippie games. This is from way back with AD&D 1ed, Vampire, Rifts, RuneQuest... whatever. All my characters had goals. Even if they were simple things like "Prove I'm a better adventurer than So-And-So."

I didn't know how to respond to such a statement so I just said, "Okay, how 'bout I just reward XP at appropriate times?" Everyone was good with this... well, except me. I'm a little worried now. How long can you sustain a game or player interest if the players don't even have a goal to pursue with their characters. Just getting through a dungeon is a good enough goal for a 1 session dungeon but it seems rather insufficient for a sustained game. Has anyone else ever run across this sentiment before in players? Does anyone here actually hold this sentiment themselves? Can you explain the logic of it to me? It is beyond my comprehension at the moment.

    I think you missed something when you were asking for character goals. You didn't just ask them if their characters had any goals, you asked them if they would like to declare goals and use those declared goals to determine how much XP they get. Declaring goals is fine (or not), but as soon as you start tying things like character advancement into the achievement of those goals, then picking those goals becomes a strategic decision (at least if you have a Gamist agenda). Not only is it a strategic decision, it could very well be the most important decision you make for the character, since it will effectively determine your rate of advancement, and therefore the effectiveness of your character. Furthermore, you have no basis for making that decision, since before the game you don't know what kind of goals will be easiest to gain XP with. From a gamist perspective, this doesn't sound fun.

    A LOT of misery in roleplaying has come from people trying to make D&D do things it wasn't designed to do. It evolved from wargames, it's about winning this fight so you can be better at winning the next fight, and all the 3rd edition stuff about feats and prestige classes reinforces this focus.     If your friends drove up in their brand-new Ferraris and Lamborginis and Porsches and said, "Dude, there's this amazing road out in the Midwest. Five hundred straight miles of nothin' ! Think how cool that would be!" and you say, "Yeah, but that could be a little dull, how about we stop every fifty miles and go off-roading for a while" -- well, they would look at you as if you were crazy. Your choice is to convince them to hop in some 4x4s to wander around in the mud and woods instead, or to convince yourself to get in a hotrod and enjoy the long, straight, fast ride. Trying to compromise by taking the sportscars off-road or the 4x4s racing is just going to get you a lot of breakdowns and no fun for anybody.

    This interests me, a lot.

    I love these newfangled story-games. They are a lot more fun for me than what I was used to in the earlier days of the hobby. My wife, however, shares the view that your players do: She prefers to react to things as they come up, rather than plan ahead. It's difficult for her to focus on a character before any of the in-game drama happens, she prefers to let the in-game drama temper and sharpen her character, basically "discovering" the character through play. She found the upcoming Sorcerer & Sword game we have planned difficult, because we haven't started playing, but she's already had to invest a lot of skull-sweat thinking about the character, and she hasn't been able to interact with the world enough to get a feel for how her character "should" operate. Character creation for her is stressful because of her playstyle preferences. She is, in all other respects, a good player.

    Since this is designed to be a glorified dungeon-crawl, I'd say compromise: Tell the players that, when they come across a goal they can get behind, declare it in-game. In addition to any XP they normally get, if their actions contributed to achieving their character's goals, they get an additional 10% on top of that. If they have multiple goals, and those goals are met, the 10% stacks. (Ex. three goals met=+30% xp bonus.) Be sure to let them know that "I want to improve my prowess at [character class]", "I want as much wealth and cool toys as I can carry out of here", and "I long for the thrill of combat" are valid goals; some people simply do not want more out of life than that. Just explain to them that those who have a drive beyond simple survival (which is a given) tend to advance quicker, as those are the types of people who are motivated to learn faster. If it's used as a carrot, rather than a stick, it should go down easier.

    Yes they may level up faster, but since this game has a built-in encouragement for them to seek out bigger and badder things, this should not be too problematic.

    Just a thought.

    Hi Andrew,

    I've been roleplayingfor decades and have spent much of that time longing for narrativist play (the style that wyrds, keys, muses, spiritual attributes, or goals all tend to support). When I first learned it was possible to play a game where narrativism was the focus, I was tempted to declare it the ONE TRUE WAY. What I've learned since then, is that there are people who've longed to have other agendas expressed just as clearly in play -- and have been just as frustrated as I -- and they too have a tendency to extreme responses when pushing for a chance to play their prefered style.

    I suspect the vehemence of your players' response results from an internal process something like this:

    Cool, the World's Largest Dungeon! Tricks, traps, challenges, tactical maneuvers, fights! There no way this can produce anything but the sort of player challenges I really had fun with that time back years ago. What? You want player goals? WTF? Why are you messing with a perfectly clear game agenda? No!

    I don't claim to read minds. My point is that WLD is the kind of setting where players in the past have experienced gamist play -- and which also strongly supports gamist play. Now here you go, looking to mess with the all important reward mechanism...grafting on something that rewards something other than player cleverness.

    My second point is that many players have been frustrated by not getting a clear reward from past play and they may work hard to get it when they see the opprotunity (and they should; you should too; not in game, but in the social negotation before play).

    My suggestion is to negotiate. First, take a look at your own needs. Are you trying to insert player goals because that's the only play you are willing to offer? Or because you're in the same kind of frustrated position I described above?

    If a hypotehtical someone else ran WLD would you play for what it is, rather than what you want? Once you know what you want, talk to your people. Some options on the table are: someone else GMs WLD. You run WLD for four or five sessions, then switch to (say) Shadow of Yesterday for a four sessions. (Limits are important -- if someone's been frustrated in a desire, they can jump to conclusions.) Finally, you can sit out WLD, or even meet separately (at another time) with people who're interested in another game.

    I'm in the club of people who would like to experience a good, clear narrativist agenda and who've rarely experienced it. I know what it's like to be frustrated and want something else. But I've also learned that I can be perfectly happing playing or GMing to support a gamist agenda, provided I'm clear that's what I'm doing.

    If you do run WLD, do the dungeon crawl whole hog. That's what it's best suited for. Enstate an XP system that rewards players for achievement and maybe even competition -- defeating an opponent, overcoming a trap, entering the 5th level, getting in the final blow, killing the most in a single battle. If the existing XP system means they level up too fast, then half the rewards.

    You might want to have a look at Tunnels and Trolls or Agon for ideas about GM attitude and approach.

    - Alan

      I've had problems with goals before (and, you know, I think player/character-goals are great in general)--but I'd stay away from being too hung-up on them.

      1. Pressuring players to take goals can create cases (I've seen) where the player adopts a goal but isn't excited about it. This is, IME, worse than "no goal."
      2. Before play starts I sometimes have problems coming up with goals. I may not be that familiar with the character or the world ... if other stuff besides character-generated drama is going on in the world (and this applies to games even like Sorcerer and DitV) then I'd rather my goals work more closely with that than simply be a goal that may be tangential.
      3. I may find that helping another player with their goal is more interesting than pursuing mine.

      For all these reasons, doing goal-based experience would be a problem for me--especially in a dungeon crawl scenario where my main point in being there is to "explore/clean-out" the dungeon. And I'm, you know, pretty hip to the whole goal-oriented, PC-driven play thing.
