    \n Neil, it was great fun for me too.

    I'll try to explain scene economy now.

    Sessions of Mouse Guard are broken into turns, The GM's turn and then the Player's turn. The GM's turn is when I was telling you guys what you needed to do and what to roll. You could make recommendations or offer alternatives, but I had the final say so. It continues to be the GM's turn for 2 obstacles. Basically, I present two problems and you guys deal with them. The Player's turn is when each players get to say what they do, which usually means rolling a test, doing a conflict, or a versus test. Each player gets to do this once. They can do it more times if they have earned checks through playing their Traits against themselves. After that, the session ends or you play through another turn.

    As far as scene economy? Well, the game is honestly closer to a standard adventuring rpg than it is to something like Primetime Adventures or even Burning Empires. I think using the term scene economy is more confusing when playing Mouse Guard. If I use board game terminology, I usually get more people nodding along. I would've loved to explored more of the game with you guys, but that would involve more sessions...

    \n Yes, "challenge" is a better word than "screwed".

    As for how the turns relate to the fiction, consider the time in the game we had where the bear was approaching the village. The GM turn ended with us discovering where the bear was going. One of the mice wanted to motivate the villagers into building barricades against the bear. Everyone agreed that this was a cool thing to do and that there was plenty of time to do it before the bear arrived. As it was, the player framed the scene to do the persuasion attempt as one of his actions in his turn.

    If he hadn't done that, much the same scene would (probably) have occurred during the GM's turn. So that raises the question of what's the difference between the GM's turn and the players' turn. The same things in the fiction get addressed by both: both the GM and players want to have scenes addressing items that have arisen from the fiction so far. The only thing I can really think of is to do with who frames the scenes, in terms of both situation and, indirectly, objective.

    Does that make sense?

    If you want to discuss this more, perhaps we should break it out to a separate thread.
