\n After the great time I had at this last Origins I've been thinking about something I've wanted to do for a while. I'm working with my local gaming Meetup group to plan a once-a-month taste test get-together. It should be a bit like Games on Demand in that I will have three games prepped to play, each with a different flavor, so when people meet I can run something for them. It should give them selection and give them a taste test of games they might not have tried before. If I can get an additional GM we can expand to more games but at first it will be three to pick from.
\n
\nKeeping with the taste test theme I want to rotate through the games that are available each get-together. I don't want to replay the same one over and over. Once a game has been played it will be off the list for a few months before it can be played again.
\n
\nWhat's everyone think?
\n
\nAny advice on running games in the GoD tradition?
\n
\nThanks.
\n
\nVictor

    \n Search the forum for "Indie by Storm" and you'll find my advice for running 4-6 games in a single four-hour slot. I don't think that's what you mean, though I may steal and repurpose the term "taste test" for my own nefarious purposes.

    I've done a fair amount of what you're proposing, though, with my local groups. We would play various games as one-shots or even short series (2-3 sessions). Games with a small ramp-up time work best. Burning Wheel takes an entire session just to make up characters (for me anyway) so that might not be suitable for a one- or three-shot.

    If by "the GoD tradition," you mean that your friends show up and you pitch a few games at them and they pick which they want to play, here's my advice: do read my "Indie by Storm" stuff to see how I prep it. You basically have to prep all the games you are going to pitch. Have all the materials ready for them. Make one-page cheat sheets with rules procedures to help them pick up the rules quickly.

    I also recommend nixing the "off the list for a few months" rule. What purpose does it serve? Are you trying to experience new games, even at the expense of great fun? If a game is a blast, and you're all loving it, why cross it off the list? Play it till you'd rather play something else.

    \n You'll find some discussion about how we run the New Zealand version of Games of Demand (the equivalent of what you're talking about), here: The Secret Plan.

    I'd also recommend asking the designers of any of the games you're interested in about whether they have any sales demos they can give you. These can make great jumping-off points if you're a bit pushed for time. For instance, Timothy Kleinert has a great demo for the Mountain Witch that can also act as a starting point for a session.

    \n At SGBoston, we ran different games every week for 3+ years. Ben Robbins does a similar deal in Seattle. Really, you don't need to bring multiple options yourself. Just get other folks to pitch various games and then play whatever gets people's attention, or (if you have enough people) split up and play multiple games. It starts out slow and small, with just you and your associates, but over time it can spawn its own thing. Now, Dev, Nathan, and I (the three SGBoston founders) are all no longer in Boston, and SGBoston is doing just fine.
