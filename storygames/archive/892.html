Who recruits for your GMless game?

Tony Irwin
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
In this thread http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=862&page=1#Item_0 about designing a gateway game, TonyLB makes what I thought was the most insightful comment of the whole thread:

"D&D is the gateway game that it is because people *want* to GM it for their friends. They want to do it enough to convince said friends to play."

I've finished a GMless game and I play it exlusively with non-roleplaying friends. I'm the recruiter for my own system. If I hadn't done all the rules, text, art*, layout, other headache stuff for it, would I still be motivated to recruit?

I'm play testing my second GMless game. Again, if it wasn't my game would I still be recruiting for it?

I play Universalis maybe once every 3 months. When I first got the game I was playing it 3 times a week. When I first started playing it was so much fun teaching the people the game, seeing the light bulb switch on above their heads, shaping play until they were ready to dive in and make the game their own. Is it possible that I recruited for this GMless game because it let me GMed exactly the way I enjoyed most? Now my friends all know how to play it, they don't need a GM any more.

Disclaimer: The three games above are all amazing fun. The only way for me to enjoy that fun is to recruit other people to play them with me. Please take all that for granted - I'm interested in the other sneaky and shadowy reasons for why Im recruiting for GMless games. For why I'm doing a GM task (recruiting/facilitating) for games that claim not to need them.

I don't have a point to make through this thread. I just want to listen. Here's who I'd love to hear from in this thread:

1) You play/have played a GMless game
2) You have recruited people to play it with you.**
3) You can talk about why.

Here's when you're off topic

1) Talking about "what is a GM and what do they do"
2) Explaining to me that I can't play by myself, that's why I recuit
3) If you haven't actually really truly played and recruited for a GMless game.

Thanks!

* When I say "art" read "some drawings I drew"
**Playing online is cool to talk about because you guys are *really* recruiting - actually going out and making contact with people you don't otherwise know.

  • TonyLB
    July 2006

    Well, I had lots of little play experiences where, when nobody was offering any direction, play stagnated and people got (frankly) a little silly. And then I had lots of play experiences where I went into the game with an idea in mind ("Remember honey! Dinner at the Jensens at nine o'clock, and don't you dare be late!" ... he's gonna be late) and whether that idea came to pass or didn't, it made the game better by providing structure.

    Sometimes people embrace my ideas ("My guy should totally be late! His family life is going straight to hell because of all the secret heroics") and sometimes they reject them ("No, see, it's not about meeting with the Jensens, it's about fighting the forces of evil! Forget the Jensens, we will never speak of them again.") But either way they aren't staring at (metaphorically) a blank, featureless plain, wondering which way they should drive. The prepared story elements are landmarks. Drive toward them, drive away from them, drive around them ... anything. You've got something to orient yourself to.

    Come to think of it, prep-work in a GM-less game serves much the same purpose that player flags serve in a GMed game. They give people an idea of what directions it's worth guiding the game toward.
