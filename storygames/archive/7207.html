Hi,

\n

Black Cadillacs is coming to GenCon Indy. I'm a member of the Ashcan Front (booth #2039), and I'll be demo'ing and selling the ashcan there. Here's a really swank promo shot of the book, thanks to my friend Meghann Gervais (Little Studio Photography and Jewellery).

\n

\n

Here's the back cover blurb, which is only mildly hyperbolic:

\n
\n

You play soldiers during wartime. It's bloody and heroic and tense and horrifying.

\n
\n

>

\n
\n

And then...

\n
\n

>

\n
\n

You tell the stories of what happened to you. As players you see everything, what
\n happened, and how it got told to family, friends, loved ones, and to fellow soldiers.
\n In the gap between what happened and how it got told is the most powerful humanity
\n you'll ever play.

\n
\n

I'm very excited about both the game and the physical product. The game is well-playtested and solid (but still needs that extra blind playtesting, thus the ashcan.) The product is a true labour of love. It's 104 pages, half-letter spiral-bound (like a steno pad), featuring a hard cover with rivets and stainless steel.

\n

In addition to the booth demos, I'm also running two scheduled games of Black Cadillacs at the Indie Games Explosion. If I'm reading GenCon's game registrations right, there are still some spots left for the Friday and Saturday games (both run 2pm-6pm).

\n

Personally, I'm itching to do something with the siege of Stalingrad in WWII, or maybe the soviet push westwards after they break the Wehrmacht.

\n

Oh! I'm planning on all kinds of after-hours gaming too, and could probably be coerced into facilitating a game of Black Caddies.

\n

Cheers,
\nD

    \n Hey Graham,

    Knowing full well that "tell me about the system" can mean a great many things, I'm just going to jump in with both feet. However, I'll work in chunks, which will allow you to interject and redirect me if the chunk I'm yapping on about isn't what you meant. I'm also positive that I'll gloss over some stuff.

    To give you a sense of where this runaway train is going, my intention is to start at 'the tip of the tree' (system-wise), which happens to be the conflict resolution mechanics. Underpinning these mechanics (progressing towards the bottom of the tree) are the conflict generating mechanics, the scene structure mechanics, the immediate situation generation mechanics and the larger situation mechanics. Continuing with the analogy, there are a number of garlands hung on the tree, that link the various layers together.

    Conflict Resolution\n
    • every player & every player character must be involved in each and every conflict.
    • the players are rolling against the GM, but may or may not be rolling for their characters. It's sort of like the self-improvement conflicts during initiation in *Dogs* in that regard.
    • a round (once around the table) is called a *Moment*, each Player turn is a *Go*.\n
      • Each Moment, there's a free and clear phase during which Players make declarations for their Gos.
      • Each individual Go is essentially a task which is being resolved in service to the larger Conflict.
      \n
    • there are 3 conflict tokens. Each time you win a Go, you claim one for your side (Players/GM).
    • get all 3, and your side wins.
    \nGo Resolution\n
    • pool vs pool
    • all d6s
    • read much like *Sorcerer* on meth (or so I'm told.)\n
      • high roll in pool vs high roll
      • if tied, proceed downwards
      • if tie unbreakable, add dice to existing pool 'til tie breaks\n
        • matches (say, a pair of 4s) beat non-matches (say, a single 6)
      \n
    • GM's rolls cheat: her 1s are wild and can be set to any value she desires.
    • Players rolls don't normally cheat. However, there are ways to claw back that power (that's much later, because it's a Garland...)
    \nGo Outcome\n
    • the winner (player or GM) of the go decides to assign strain (more on Strain later) or narrate
    • the loser does what's left
    • strain assignment comes first\n
      • points are placed into the character's three Strains: Horror, Valour & Hubris
      \n
    • the post-Go narration is constrained by the Strain assignment. (for instance: if you're assigning and put all the points into Horror, then my narration needs to have a horrible edge to it. However, it still has to be about the Declaration for the Go, too!)
    \nThat's a good start, I think. Please let me know if this is going in a useful direction for you. My next planned installment is the conflict generating mechanics & scene structure mechanics.

    Cheers,
    D

    OK. I thought that I might be going off into the weeds. I'll start over.

    Black Cadillacs is good at...\n
    • doing shitty, gritty war without doing macho bullshit Rambo.
    • capturing that random-death horror factor.
    • keeping everyone on-page with the (pardon the theory) SIS.
    • giving everyone a very real, very mechanically important opportunity to comment on the game-as-activity, and by extension, war.
    • weaving the soldiers' lives "back home" into the narrative.
    \nThat sort of thing?

    Oh, it's also setting-nonspecific. It's designed to allow the players to build the war they want to play, and to discover why.

    Hi Charles,

    \n

    The charcters (Troopers) in Black Cadillacs are normal folk. Unfortunately, they're stuck in a god-awful, shitty mess. Since the game transpires in cinematic time, the players see all of the exciting bits -- the killing, running and terror. On a character-level, it really is about normal soldiering (without having to delve into military minutiae unless that's what floats your group's boat). Some of the troopers emerge as heroes, some as cowards, and some just die alone.

    \n

    At a player-level, it's all about heaping untold amounts of shit on the Troopers. It's also very strongly about exploring war as a human reality as opposed to a military or political reality.

    \n

    I'm getting all shitty-poetical here, so I'll stop. Ask me some more questions and I'll try not to get all Chaucer on your ass.
