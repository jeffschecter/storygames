    \n
    \n Because designing a game is like getting into a fascinating conversation with everyone else who's designing and playing games.

    When I bring a design to the table it lets me engage players in a conversation about gaming in a way I can't seem to do any other way.

    When I experience someone else's design, and it influences my own, I've learned something from them and expressed what I've learned in a way that can be transmitted to yet another person.

    Which I guess is kind of like saying I design games because I'm lonely.

    \n\nSabreCat \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nOctober 2007 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Getting up to the elbows in a creative project I'm excited about is a transformation for me. The only experience I can liken it to is that of being in love.

    Baseline life is cool enough; you could even call it "contentment." If I've dragged myself above my occasional bouts of crushing anxiety and night-terrors, and I'm pleased with the work I'm doing at my job, and in the evenings I play video games or watch DVDs or go hang or RP with my circle, I'm fine. The days glide by and things are all right.

    And then the Idea comes along.

    There's this brief fluttering, faltering period where maybe it just fizzles out. But if not... if I get words of support here and there, and some momentum of one thought connecting to another, and make that effort of will that says "yes, I'm going to DO this," boom! I'm on fire.

    It's got all the symptoms of limerence: I can't get my mind off the project. There's a spring in my step. I bust into these giant stupid grins for no externally evident reason. I work harder (when the daydreams don't carry me away, at least), speak with more confidence, laugh louder.

    It's not all excellence and ninth clouds, of course. Like love, sometimes it flits away when I really need to draw on that energy. And as with love, a rejection can crush like physical force. But in that way that no one but a lover can grasp, somehow it's worth accepting those deep abyssal trenches for the sake of the peaks. It's one of those things that, even if I'm not in a spiritual mode, makes me feel like I arrived on this planet for a reason--and that is to create something.

    ...My last big infatuation like this was NaNoWriMo last year, which ended up being my first "win." This time it's writing a story game, also a first for me. I'm scared and uncertain, but I am pumped.
