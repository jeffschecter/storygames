I've heard mention of presenting pregens who lack only their guest appearances in novels and completing that step at the table, and also some talk of maybe reducing the number of Fate Points... but it's all fuzzy in my mind.     \n Give only 5 Fate per player. Then be very free with compels they will want to accept.

    Doing pregens is a very good idea for a short time slot, especially with six players! I would limit it to four players, personally, but that may just be my style. You could leave one novel blank on each sheet, and let the players fill it in. You might also leave a blank aspect or two that they can create.

    Be very explicit about everything you do, system-wise, to show them what's possible. When they arrive at a new scene, lay out the scene aspects. When the villains ambush the heroes, have the bad guys assess and declare aspects right off the bat and then use the free tags to lay the smack down. Talk about everything you're doing, especially the stuff that might be "hidden" from PC knowledge.

    And of course, get to the action immediately. If there's one failing of the SotC book, it's that the sample adventure is not a good model of a rocking SotC session (in my opinion). Don't give them a mystery right away, give them guys trying to kill them.

    \n Either that or consider dropping the Stunts for a one-shot. I feel like I know SotC forwards and backwards by now, but in the context of a one-shot with players unfamiliar with the rules, consider having some of their 10 Aspects suggest Stunt-like abilities and have them Invoke those Aspects (and pay a Fate Point) to "cool stuff". Contending with 5 players (for example) that are dealing with 25 Stunts for the first time is a handful. I'd probably still start with 5 Fate Points as suggested above, but hand them out very liberally.

    If you expect even two players to know the rules who can help out the other players, I'd leave the Stunts in. Stunts are cool.

    At Dreamation, Judd had the players fill out their guest roles in two other novels and name those two Aspects that went with them. That seemed to work pretty well. He also had the Stunts listed and cool artwork to accompany each character, which was fun.

    \n My last one-shot experience: I printed out the pages for each PC's stunts and talked about them briefly.

    I also started them with 5 FATE points, but I had a couple people who didn't feel encouraged to spend their FATE points, and I wanted to highlight the high-flying action of the system, so I gave them the other 5 FATE points in order to get them feel like bringing the awesome. It's a con game, so I treat it as me trying to sell an experience. I still hit them with compels, and the game worked out over-all.

    For the one-shot I participated inby at GenCon SoCal, I (think I) suggested we all read the stunt and write down in one sentence what it does as well as the page number. The "writing down in one sentence" thing, I think, helped us understand what our stunts did.

    \n Okay, I just finished the playtest with my Friday HERO group. It went absolutely nothing like I planned, and I mean that in the best way possible.

    First off, I'd planned a "teaser" to open the session; basically resolving the cliffhanger from the previous (i.e., non-existant) "episode." Basically, the PCs are in the cupola of a zeppelin that's heading straight for the Woolworth Building in NYC, the tallest building in the world as of 1926. Also in the cupola are Doctor Methuselah and his two killer robot servants (Great minions). On the nose of the zeppelin is the Negative Temporal Device; once it comes into contact with the metal spire on top of the Woolworth Building, it will make use of the building itself and the zeppelin's frame to create an arc of Negative Temporal Energy that will send New York city 10,000 years into the past!

    Then I had basically nabbed the "107 lb. quartz sphere of dragon-summoning" idea from the Tips and Tricks chapter of the book, and figured that would be the adventure "proper."

    As you can probably guess, we never got to the sphere scenario. Between explaining the rules, coming up with guest spots and Aspects for the two final phases, and dealing with the whole Methuselah scenario, we killed about four hours (the perfect length for a con event, thankfully).

    The start was a little rocky. I'd preempted our regular Star HERO game. One of my regulars was not nuts about this, and he also led off the night by proclaiming how bad he thought SotC mechanics were (having perused the SRD, I'm guessing), and how it "got pulp all wrong." With him, it's HERO or nothing, so I wasn't too surprised. Still, he's a huge pulp fan and a font of historical info, so he gave it the college try. He ended up adding a lot to the session.

    The other hitch was that it took a while to explain Aspects. They seemed a bit hesitant that they weren't concretely defined, and one player thought that compels were, in his opinion, "too open to abuse." The other hitch was that some of the Aspects I posted above didn't make too much sense to them, especially Katar Singh's. That I can't blame them for; Katar Singh was the PC I did the most research for, a mix of Punjabi war hero Matar Singh and some bits of my father's childhood in India thrown in. Ergo, the Aspects make pretty good sense... if you happen to be Indian.

    The final big blunder I made was that I didn't assign novels for them to use for Phases 4 and 5, which is what you normally do according to the book (i.e., hand out index cards with the existing novel titles). Ergo, it took a lot of time for everyone to come up with Aspects and figure out in whose novel they guest-starred.

    \nIIRC, as written, defensive spin only gives a +1 on some factor in "the next roll", period. So whatever die roll happens next. If you want to give someone a -1, you give their opposition a +1, that's it.

    Also as written, spin doesn't have an effect on non-defenses, unless you have a stunt or some other situation that adds a "spin condition" into the mix.

    There's also no "I put a penalty on him and it just floats around unused" with the +1 from spin thing. It's the next roll. So, the next time the dice hit the table, if you've got spin, speak up and apply it!
