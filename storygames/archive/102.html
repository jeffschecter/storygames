I've placed this discussion in Praxis! because I think it's a marketing issue.

I've noticed that, save for one or two big mainstream systems or settings, the majority of games mentioned here (what are you playing? what do you want to play?) seem to come from folks active in public design at the Forge.

That's fine: well and good. Hear, hear.

However, I've also noticed that there's a tier of small press/self-pub/indie games that don't seem to get mentioned as much. That, of course, is the tier I seem to be in. I'm talking FATE, Truth & Justice, Cat, InSpectres, and other such games.

I made a joke blurb for my company's games a while ago: "Too indie for the mainstream, and too mainstream for the indie." But now I wonder if that's really, honestly true. Many of the games I mention above are fairly mainstream in the way they handle narration, player empowerment, and play-style.

I've been wondering why these Other Indie Games aren't getting a quarter as much attention as, say, Polaris or The Shab-al-Hiri-Roach -- and that's no disrespect for these two games, not at all. I've been shocked to hear that multiple folks on this message board hadn't ever heard of T&J, for example.

Does anyone have any ideas?

As I'm both a socially compartmentalized sort and a syncretic thinker I spend most of my time in public talking with the community I am in about the things they want to talk about, and much of my time in private working out the ways in which the tenants of the different communities interact at my own table.

    This has also, in some situations, lead to me not participating in communities because I get tired of one set of "friends" talking constant shit about the other set of "friends."

    And to kick in on the side note: I've actually played Enemy Gods and Cat. I've never, to my remembrance, talked about playing either because no one in the blogsphere talks about playing either.

    The other problem is that I'm both to busy and to lazy to post a lot of Actual Play reports. Maybe I should start doing so, as I know they are awesome good for both theory and sales. Chad and Keith have both told me that I'm a fuck for not posting about my CoS and T&J games, and I think they're right to do so. Buzz is for shit, but talking about the game actually being played and how it works in real terms is a wonderful tool in many, many ways.

    Finally, I have to say that (fortunately or un) the reason that a lot of games kick harder at first and get better buzz to start with has much to do with novelty. I know many people whose heads turned towards Dogs because of the "pseudo-Mormon paladins in the old west that never was" aspect. Or grooved on Polaris because it was "just so different."       Really Chad, you won't come across as a jerk if you write actual play reports about your games. They're just as important as demos, in my opinion. Talk about the cool stuff that happened in your games and how the mechanics supported and created interesting situations.

      There's a huge difference between this and shilling. Actual Play is not about "selling" the game in the way a shill is. It's writing about the stuff that has actually happened in play that got you excited.

      There are a number of benefits:
      1. It will encourage other fans to post about their own actual play. The more AP posts out there, the better.

      2. It gives people another example of how to use your game. Certain mechanics may not have totally clicked for them, but when they see it in your AP, they'll suddenly understand why the rule works the way it does.

      3. It focuses discussion on your game and the cool stuff it does, rather than degrading into comparisons of your game versus other games, and other conversations that aren't about play.

      4. It gets fans who've bought the game excited about playing, reminding them what they liked about it in the first place.

      5. It lets more people know that your game exists.
