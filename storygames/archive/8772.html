\n In Jeff Colyer's Primetime Adventures: Cold Sleep game at Dreamation, I enjoyed playing my character quite a lot. But my most rewarding contributions to play were:
1) collaborating on stake setting
2) collaborating on scene framing

I just love throwing out ideas like, "No, wait, how about if you lose, instead of failing to fix the robot, you fix it so well that it can replace you!"

Maybe many Narr games provide opportunities for that, but my two best experiences in this direction have been the two PtA games I've played.

I wonder if that's because "TV episode" provides a wonderful reference point to constrain the infinite array of scenes that could be framed and stakes that could be set. Of course, the initial set-up plays a huge role too, but I think I've played other games with similar set-ups that played quite differently.

What games help you get your "and then how about THIS could happen!" groove on? How do they do it?

    \n Hi Dave! I think a big part of it is your group's social dynamic, because any game can benefit from this. But you need a degree of trust and flexibility as well as permission to throw stuff out and see what sticks - as well as permission to say "no". This last point may be important - games where authority is clearly defined handle this better than those where it is not. Like in Grey Ranks (just to pick a game we've both played recently out of the air) if you frame a Personal scene, it's all yours, so an atmosphere that encourages suggestions is fine and healthy, because you have explicit permission to ignore all of them.

    \n Nice, Jason, that totally adds up. Forge some agreements about the parameters of the fiction you're creating, grab or generate some facts and color for inspiration springboard purposes, roll out a dynamic situation, and use rules that clearly state who can/should make suggestions/declarations when. Voila!

    I guess at this point there's a fairly long list of "Forge-style" games that do this, huh?

    I've had this thought bouncing around for a while, for a game in which players posit competing new additions to the fiction, and then another player (or a group vote) determines which one is actually accepted, based more or less on "that would be the coolest addition to this narrative!" Is there already some game that does that? I know Universalis has some "put your money where your inspiration is"-type deal going on, which sounds similar in some ways (and maybe better), but meaningfully different.

    What if all the narrative competitors were GMs, and the judge was the one player playing a character? The GMs don't battle for what simply enters the setting, they battle for which of their setting entries is the one the chracter chooses to interact with. I guess that's actually a different idea, but might be fun...

    \n Jason, absolutely: if you make up something really cool, and it's deemed 2nd best, and then it effectively leaves play forever, that'd be a major letdown and the process would get draining.

    I was thinking that either (a) there would be some way in which unchosen contributions do resurface later, perhaps as elements (e.g. threats or assets) within the larger situation provided by winning contributions, or (b) initial contributions would be pure setting color, e.g. a description of a single item, and thus less burnout-inducing.

    I imagine (b) as kind of like interpreting drawn words in Sign in Stranger. It could even use a similar "inspiration springboard" procedure. I could definitely improv cool rocks, rusty gates, strange animal noises, dropped diaries, etc. all day. It certainly doesn't sound any harder than improvising compelling character behavior over and over! But maybe that speaks to your point about a particular skill set.

    Of course, if you're throwing out pure color with no plan, what happens when the character opts for your color? How does that contribute to a playable situation? Improvising an answer to that could get hard. This might work best in a cosntrained environment like a whodunnit or a dungeon...

    As for plan (a)... I could see the "winning" GM running with a situation while the two "losing" GMs think about how to work their respective contributions into it. There could be defined options ("threat", etc.) to give them something to shoot for. As opposed to plan (b), the "vote on contributions" phase would take longer (as GMs baked fuller contributions) and happen less frequently, and "winning" GMs would get a longer time to steer the fiction.

    \n I know it is PTA blasphemy (and I'll loose alot of dirty hippie love on this) but I usually set explicit stakes on both sides of the conflict in PTA, before narration is resolved.

    Its the way it was run when I first played, and it seems to mostly work for my style of GMing. Basically I want the collaboration before the conflict to help frame really good stakes on both the players and GM side, regardless of who get narration.

    While the narration adds color and can twist things a bit, in general stake setting before hand avoids two problems
    1) having someone buy in with fanmail, win narration and take story somewhere unfun for the player having the conflict
    2) automatically negate what the original stakes rather than take the story somewhere more interesting

    Both of these concerns are probably a result of running mostly convention games where folks will occasionally either freeze up when winning narration, or sometimes grief other players and the GM. If my home group ever gets back to our "Thin Blue Line" Chicago cops with superpowers PTA game, I'll have to be sure to take off these restraints and see how it feels.

    Regardless of how much I mangled the ruleset, that game was awesome on toast entirely because of the players.
