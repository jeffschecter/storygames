    \n Sorry to hear about your troubles Ryan.

    I've only run one BW Campaign which lasted about 10 sessions, but it was one of the best gaming experiences of my life.

    The system is very difficult, and the difficulty can of course get in the way of the actual gaming at times. A large way to curb the transition of learning the rules to print off scripting sheets for everybody. You will get better if you keep playing, I also wouldn't sweat missing rules as long as everyone involved in the conflict is enjoying themselves.

    One of the strong points of BW is the sub-systems and their complexities. Many people compare them to mini-games -and in such a context they can be downright fun. Unfortunately, sometimes I did find the scripted combat to become a bit tedious but my players enjoyed it.

    Concerning your 2nd point, I would consider talking to the players and asking them what you're doing wrong. You may be doing nothing wrong and they're just not engaged with the story. Perhaps they did not buy into the premise as well as they could have. Did you Burn up your campaign setting/situation together? Either way, I'd let them know how this is affecting you and try to solve the problem together.

    Posted By: sacredchaoOn a side note, if BW doesn't work out, any recommendations for a "simpler" but still story-gamish system I could transplant my world too?
    \nI ran BW for a year, on a near-weekly basis, and after that our group agreed to move on. After a year of playing, we still hadn't gotten our heads around the full-on combat system. If I were to start again, I would heartily recommend this advice (which others have already given you): Do not start with the full system; start only with the basic test format described in the first section of the book.

    Of absolutely prime importance is that you and your play group get strong familiarity with the Artha fate-point-and-experience system and get it woven into your play at a fundamental level before you attempt to move on to putting the more detailed conflict systems into play.

    By not layering the more complex systems on immediately, you will learn how the economic engine of the game works, and how to more effectively set stakes and consequences for conflicts. From reading the book (even though the book pretty much bluntly gives this recommendation itself), you may not believe this to be the case. BW provides these richly detailed systems for resolving conflict, so you think, what the heck: this is the full experience, right? With all these wonderful options, my players won't be happy resolving entire bloody conflicts with a single die roll!

    Unfortunately, that's exactly the fundamental matter behind BW: it's absolutely vital that you get an effective handle on setting stakes for conflict, and getting the Artha system to become second nature to you.

    Without that firm grounding, you won't get the approach required to use the more complex resolution mechanics in the best way: the advanced combat mechanics might seem very simulationist or tactical, but they are not. What they are is a complex set of options you can use to address conflict at a high-level narrative way: if you haven't already got a good handle on scene and stakes-setting, then your approach to these rules will be much harder.

    My players never got their heads around this and in the end it brought our campaign to its knees. (For what it's worth, our group's players still are uncomfortable with high-level stake-setting-based conflict systems: we moved to using Reign last year, and suffered very similar problems because our gaming group just doesn't seem to "get" narrative play.)

    What can you try if BW just isn't for you? If you want fantasy with a similar Sword-N-Sorcery feel, then I would suggest you try "Shadows of Yesterday" or "In A Wicked Age". Both of these games are much simpler to engage with than BW, and have the same narrative approach to gaming. I would also highly recommend HeroQuest, especially the forthcoming new edition of HeroQuest (which is more generic and not tied to the Glorantha background that some people quite like and others don't at all).

    Another bit of advice: Learn by playing not by reading. If you have the opportunity to go try out a narrative game session run at a convention by someone who knows the game and "gets" how it should get played, you absolutely should. This would be especially true of BW: if you're near to the north east of the US, it's not out of the realm of possibility that you could attend a con where the lineage of the person hosting a BW event at a con is quite close to the source, so to speak.

    I personally found HeroQuest impenetrable until I attended a con and saw how it was used in action, for four hours, by Greg Stafford. This was eye-opening: I immediately became enthused at how the game could work, turned around and dropped it on a group of newbies in my home town, and ran it for almost a year to great enjoyment by all.

    A huge number of what people describe as "indie-games" seem to be quite "descriptive" in their approach to rules, rather than "prescriptive". This can make learning how to play them quite difficult, because strong familiarity with the text still might not be all that helpful when it comes to actual play of the game. Because of this, learning by playing, or even learning by watching others play, can be helpful. I recommend learning by playing, because personally I find "actual play" reports very hard to get through: in many cases, I have the same reaction to them I did when faced with the little fellow at the counter in the game store who regales anyone who will listen about the awesome play session he had on the weekend.

    Oddly enough, I would also suggest DnD4, but with this proviso: try to design your encounters with a higher proportion of skill challenges: instead of 2 or 3 challenges per 10 encounters, try tuning that up to 5 or 6. Skill challenges are, essentially, a narrative resolution mechanic nestled within the framework of a more-or-less traditional tactical conflict resolution game. My experiences with DnD4 to date have been surprisingly positive, and from the point of view of the referee I can also attest that building encounters with the game is, to me, much, much easier than with Burning Wheel (I found prepping games in BW quite difficult) -- your mileage may be similar.

    \n While I don't have extensive experience running BW, I have been in exactly the same situation. My first game running BW ended disastrously with exactly the issues that were mentioned in the OP. The system was 'too complicated' and there was a lot of OOC chatter from those players not directly 'in scene'. Some of those players were positively vehement in their opposition to the game however, some absolutely loved it.

    That first debacle was purely down to me in that I leapt in with both feet using both DOW & Fight! in that first session. Big mistake! And one that I rectified for further games by taking the advice given on the BW forums and repeated above. I only used the basic ruleset as outlined in the first 70odd pages and built on it as players became more accustomed to the steep learning curve. I also provided loads more information to the players on what the basic system involved. This was a printout of The Spokes from the BW wiki, which is the first 25 pages of the BW MRB.

    Can I ask, are you the only owner of the rules, or does anyone else have a copy? I have found in subsequent games that more players went out and purchased the rules, and it has really made a huge difference in terms of what the players can contribute in terms of systemic knowledge. While my current game is still full of mistakes, it is faster and definitely coming together in terms of getting the system under out belts.

    It also may sound funny, but recording the APs was done as a means for me to learn from the experience and has been a valuable learning tool.

    \n My first experience with BW was returning to my 'home' gaming group after a few years to college and dropping in on an ongoing game set in post-Arthurian England over Christmas break. Being one new person in a group that knows the rules isn't bad, and while it is intimidating, when you see how everyone else uses the rules it's easy to deal with DoW, Fight, and whatever else comes up (we never used Range and Cover, oddly enough). Having even two people who own the books can make a huge difference (as is probably the case for most systems). If some of your players are still interested, maybe consider putting the game on hold and letting some of the more interested players borrow your copy (or if you're feeling generous, buy a copy for them)? If you can have a couple of other people who know what's going on and can help guide those that don't, it will probably move much smoother.

    And, to repeat the above: start simple. The basic rules for opposed tests and linked tests can get you a long ways into the game, then consider introducing Fight or DoW at a climactic moment, and start making it more common as needed. For example, my first session of Burning Wheel went something like this:

    My Character gets introduced to the group (no dice rolls)
    We embark on a mission (some simple rolls to set things up in various ways)
    In executing the plan we put together, my character runs up against his beliefs (and actually breaks one, since I didn't quite get the idea at the time). (a few linked rolls, beliefs and artha come into play)
    Some thugs sent by the opposition attack (full on Fight)
    We eventually present our case for a treaty with a local leader (DoW, one of the more experienced players as the main presenter, everyone else aiding)

    If you spread out a similar arc over a few sessions, I'd hope people would pick it up.

    Another thing to focus on might be showing how the rules are 'better' even if they are complex. In a Fight, maybe script an enemy badly to show how the scripting mechanic creates a cool chaos in combat. If you're going to do a duel of wits, have it play out in an area where other systems might not be as well equiped, maybe a court room or somewhere else associated with formal arguments that a single roll doesn't really capture.

    Finally, when in doubt, try to build a conflict that deals with the character's beliefs. I think the variety of roleplaying systems shows that if you put something interesting at stake, it doesn't really matter what mechanic you use to resolve it, people will be interested.

    \n Hi Ryan,

    Sounds like everyone has already given you some good advice. We also have a number of good threads for helping new groups cataloged here.

    Just to reiterate the stuff that should help:\n
      \n
    1. Set up an interested situation in which everyone is invested. Check out the Game Description here. That's an example of what I mean. It's a compelling situation with no neat answer.
      2. \n
    2. Go back over Beliefs. Everyone should have a Belief tied into the situation at hand. They can all be different, but they should all focus on the situation.
      3. \n
    3. Take the advice in the book and start with only the first 77 pages of the book. Focus on Standard tests, Versus tests, Linked tests, Advancement and Artha.
      4. \n
    4. Pay special attention to Intent and and Task. Fight!, Duel of Wits, Range and Cover, et al, are superficial to Burning Wheel. They are fun but not essential. Intent and Task (together with Beliefs, Artha and Advancement) is the beating heart of Burning Wheel. Remember that all tests--that is, anytime you use the dice--have consequences in Burning Wheel. Consequence is tied to Intent. If you succeed, you get your Intent. If you fail, your Intent is complicated or twisted in some fashion (which can mean failure, but not necessarily). Whenever anyone goes for the dice, make sure to ask yourself: Do I have an interesting consequence if this Intent is failed? If not, just Say Yes.
      5. \n
    5. As GM, it's your job to regulate the scale of the Intents the players put forward. It's ok and even beneficial to suggest that an Intent might be too big for one roll, and to break it up into a series of discrete Tasks. More tasks means more potential consequences means more potential interesting twists and turns in the story.
      6. \n
    6. Encourage the players to take risks in pursuit of their Beliefs. Risk is the flip side of Intent. Whenever you make a test, you risk having to pay the consequence. That should be embraced! Consequences are interesting! As the GM, it is your job to make sure consequences are always interesting, but not always so punishing that players fear to take risks.
      7. \n
    7. Begin introducing the systems from the Rim once the players gain facility with Intent and Task, tests, Artha and Advancement. Resources, Circles, and Duel of Wits are all good candidates to introduce first. Remember that Intent and Task remain fundamental concepts. They don't get superseded because you've added a subsystem. In fact, an action in Fight! or Duel of Wits is simply a more regulated version of a Standard or Versus test in which we specify the scale of your Intent for you. If you choose a Strike action in Fight!, your Intent is to hurt your opponent and your task is the Sword skill (or whichever weapon you're using).
      8. \n
    8. Encourage your players to take some responsibility for the rules themselves. That doesn't mean they have to read the books (although it would be nice). But if someone states the Intent of making a tapestry as a gift for the baron using his Weaver skill as the task, while the GM and the player are talking, someone else should pick up the Character Burner and look up the Weaver skill to give the sample Obstacles to the GM. This sort of thing goes a long, long way.
      9. \n
    \nHope this helps!
    Thor
