\n It's October!

Playtested the multiplayer version of How to Host a Dungeon last night with Tony, Ben, and some guys who's names I don't remember. Both the races I controlled -- plague elves and raksasha -- were non-standard, which made it a lot more fun for me and enabled me to be less uptight about following the rules. Tony, I think, is also looking for more ways for the game to be looser and create more spaces for player creativity, which is exciting.

I also played Race for the Galaxy, which seems to be more or less the same game as Dominion and San Juan (though earlier, I think). You spend resources to build farms that produce stuff that you trade for resources. And then there's this separate track that scores the points you need to win. I'm finding this whole slew of related games pretty unexciting, actually, since the strategies seem to mostly revolve around building synergies between productive card combinations and that doesn't interest me much, I guess.

    \n Last night was Mouse Guard! Every time we play, the ruleset reveals another brilliant facet to me. This time I got to see how the advancement system redirects the natural energy of metagame play into cool fictional consequences.

    During the GM turn, I made a hopeless 1d Persuade roll, one that I self-nerfed with my guy's Cunning trait, against Dack, another PC. In the fiction, my guy Sumer was trying to pull Dack away from a useless sideshow to our main mission. I did all this in service to Sumer's Goal ("Complete this dangerous late-fall mission as quickly as possible so I can get home to my weasel friend") and to my grubby, impure metagame need to improve my character and get myself some screen time during the Player Turn.

    So, I tossed my pitiful 1d, nagged Dack perfunctorially, gleefully dotted a Persuade fail and Cunning check, and figured we'd move on. But Wil the GM reminded me that failure can earn a consequence, and he threw an Angry on Sumer. Then, when Dack horked up his roll to complete his side mission, Wil threw an Angry on him too.

    Now Sumer and Dack are simmering at each other. We're bickering, behaving suboptimally, selfish prisoners of our emotions. It felt ... authentic. I love how the ruleset and Wil combined to prompt that. And I love how the ruleset will force both Dack's player and me to decide whether we ought to use precious Player Turn checks to get over that. Forced prioritizations are cool and revealing. Does this fester into long-running hate? Or is it the silly moment that brings them closer?

    Also, last night I realized that with the exception of the Weather Watcher rules, I have learned everything I needed to know to play Mouse Guard from its character sheet.

    \n D&D4E again last night. Did one encounter and had to knock off early.

    Despite being low on dailies and healing surges, the Crimson Blades decide that they need some closure on the current adventure NOW, so they ignore the cautious counsel of their warlord and storm Taer Shiri's fortress.

    Taer Shiri is ready for them. He has a teleport bolt power and several unbreakable, doorless birdcages hung up in his fortress. His plan is to teleport at least one PC (hopefully more) into the birdcages, holding them hostage while leaving at least one alive to go and kidnap a baby for him.

    Early on, it all goes my way. First round, the warlord is caged. Then I get some giant ants into a four-on-one with the ranger. I knock him down, he fails two death saves. The fighter sprints over, eats some oppies from my ants, and stabilizes him. Now the fighter's surrounded.

    But meanwhile, Taer Shiri's teleport power refuses to recharge. He has an avenger and a druid in his face and a caged warlord shouting advice to them. Taer Shiri takes heavy damage! FINALLY, Taer Shiri's teleport bolt recharges. If he cages the avenger, he's probably got enough monsters left to finish things. But the avenger is not in position to be teleported into captivity. He needs to move ONE SQUARE towards TS to do this.

    I think about it. You can count on an avenger to move towards its target, and they are together on top of a 1" wide wall. Seems like a pretty sure thing! So TS, who is down to just three hit points, readies an action to fire into that square when the avenger crosses it. I have you now, I think, gleefully anticipating the negotiations to come--

    Surprise! The avenger has a utility that makes him invisible for one move. If TS doesn't see the avenger enter the square, his readied action can't go off. So the avenger fades out, sprints across the battlements, reappears next to TS, and crits him in the face. Game over.

    It's actually kind of a delight when the players bust out a power I didn't know about, and it perfectly foils my plans.

    \n Last night we started Burning Boston. It was fantastic.

    We started out in Judge Dods sitting room, where he asked Ezekial (the praying Indian) and Samuel Maverick (son of the first Samuel Maverick) to get the confessions of the indians who burned down John Brown's house. Of course, the Wampanoag chief Wamsutta had mysteriously died just steps from Brown's door a few months before, but none of the colonists really thought about that.

    Maverick (played by the indomitable John Harper) immediately assumed the indian's innocence, and pushed Dods, then the local Constable, to try to find inaccuracies in their accounts. A few failed rolls and people were starting to look at Maverick funny, thinking he cared about this a little too much. Problem is, the first thing the only english-speaking captive did was proudly acknowledge what they had done.

    The 'interrogation' included Maverick allowing the warparty leader to tear up his confession to keep the peace a little longer, and Ezekial (played by the great Paul Riddle) having to deal with one of the captives who wanted to convert.

    Since Maverick allowed the first confession he transcribed to be torn up, he and Ezekial set to writing a new one, but with the intention of it also being a manifesto for colonist/native relations. One barely failed roll later, everyone took note of the document (which the nailed to a few doors around town), but assumed it was just an eccentric charity case of Maverick's. In light of this, Judge Dods decided to allow a full trial after all, but with Ezekial and Maverick presenting the defense.

    With only a few days to prepare, Maverick and Ezekial decided to head to Cambridge, the site of the fire, to look for evidence. Of course they had some other goals in being there as well: gathering supplies for the coming winter, as well as investigating the possible magical causes of Wamsutta's death.

    Maverick managed to detect and find Nekoti, the spirit that killed Wamsutta, and make contact with him in the woods. Ezekial fell in with an indian hunting party intent on keeping this spirit from taking over another soul. After a tense showdown with knives at throats and some Fear spells flying, everyone backed off with no clear winner: Maverick learned Nekoti worked for a white man named Digges and showed some power over the spirit, Ezekial learned that Maverick can do magic, and the Wampanoag now believe that Maverick has always controlled Nekoti (due to some failed rolls).

    We closed the session with Zeke and Maverick arriving at Mayor Little's house in Cambridge, since they had been invited to dine there. Of course they were seated between Little's eligible daughters...
