    Ryan, I'm really excited to see this. I've been thinking about something lately. It goes like this:

    \n
      \n
    • Internet fora are designed for debate. Like a debate, it works fine with two participants, acceptably with maybe five, and disintegrates into arguing after that.
      • \n
    • The way to show your appreciation in a thread is by posting in it, supporting whichever side of the argument you're on or introducing a new point.
      • \n
    • Because there's a rowdy ruckus going on, the way to be heard is to louder.
      • \n
    • AND because that so rarely works, it doesn't pay to make big investments of thought into a post which will probably be ignored anyway, so many smaller posts increase your chances of being post, sort of an intellectual r-strategy.
      • \n
    \n

    I furthermore noticed this:

    \n
      \n
    • When I put Human Contact up on Kickstarter, several people gave me a buck, just to say that they liked what I was doing but without having to explain themselves.
      • \n
    \n

    SO, there are a couple of things I suggest.

    \n
      \n
    • Make it so you can sign your name (username, Gravatar, whatever) where you like something being said. You can't comment there, you can just say that you like it. All you're saying is, "I'm listening".
      • \n
    \n

    That means that the remaining people are debating the issue or trolling. With the noise reduced, it should be easier to tell the difference than it usually is. A reputation system means that people who are new to the system need to think about a bunch of things before they can participate and need to ask questions offthread before they comment.

    Yeah, that's a black box for an actual rep system I've been thinking about. If someone's done real study on reputation systems, though, I'd really like to read it. I'd love to be able to design this game right.

    \n

    The problems I'm trying to address are:

    \n
      \n
    • A lack of institutional knowledge \u2014\xa0newcomers don't know who to listen to.
      • \n

    • Volume and repetition works better than quality to attract attention

      • \n

    • My reputation is not a simple "one man, one vote".

      • \n
    • The higher my reputation, the bigger the size of my reputation votes. This is dynamic. If I make an ass of myself, all my votes go down in value retroactively. I.e. if people like what I say, they'll like what I like.
      • \n
    • Similarly, the broader my reputation, the bigger my reputation. I.e. the more people who like what I say, the greater my rep; it's better if two people like one thing I said than if one person liked two things I said, and better if two people liked two things that I said than either.
      • \n
    • I gain reputation for giving reputation with a diminishing return. I.e. the more I raise the bar, the more I'm raised above it. If I give rep to everyone, we all go up together.
      • \n
    • I have a reputation for certain subjects that might be bigger than my rep for other subjects. I.e., the rep system knows that there's certain stuff I'm interesting about. One of these categories should always be "funny", I think.
      • \n
    • My reputation translates to legibility. Newbs are legible but low-key. Becoming downmodded makes your posts less legible. Respected voices are max legibility, probably having powers of layout at their disposal.
      • \n
    \n

    This last part, I think can work two ways:

    \n
      \n

    1. I lose reputation for clearing out a thread. When the population drops, if one person and I are still arguing while no one's reading it, we're soapboxing. We should do that in private, where we'll probably have a more constructive conversation anyway

      2. \n

    2. Such conversations automatically become private.

      3. \n
