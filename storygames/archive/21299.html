\n Okay, @Jeff_Slater said put my game up for feedback/play testing.

The (mostly) quick "sales pitch":

Sometimes it is hard to pick up a new game, learn it quickly, and facilitate effectively.

I'm going for super brief rules (with a little more detail for those who might want it.)

P & Q rules set is super brief.

Schism is the first play set. The instructions and conflict driving mechanisms are mostly rolled into the Prompts cards.

Really I wanted to see if I could create a generic story game with minimalist rules. It's not designed for replayability, I will publish it for free if I do publish it. It would be straightforward to add play sets (generic or not) in the future.

The biggest problems in play testing were problems of pacing: groups spent too much time on world creation and the first half of the deck. Some groups didn't get to the end of the deck.

https://drive.google.com/file/d/0B_3Y6aADWfWfNER1SjVpSjlSVTA/view?usp=drive_web

Thank you in advance for any feedback.

    \n Do I understand it correctly that the rules of the game can be basically summed up as follows:

    There is a card deck of prompts, plus some notes-taking paper. The players are engaging in a communal storytelling exercise in a conch-passing fashion. You always pass turn clockwise. On your turn you must, in addition to doing general chairmanning and maintenance of the story, draw a prompt card and follow its instructions. The prompt cards are numbered in order, and together they essentially act as a mad-libs frame into which the players slot their own detail. The cards chart the process of story creation top-down, from setting to character to sequential plot run-through, such that once the players have exhausted the deck, the story will be finished.

    Assuming I understood this correctly, a follow-up question: what kind of creative motivations do you envision a group to have when they play this? Is it just that the players want to create a story together? What kind of people are they - particularly, do you envision players who like creating stories and do it outside of gaming exercises, or are the players people who need the structure a game provides to support them in story creation?\n

    \nThis is the kind of game where I personally would favour the use of a timer: just say that every turn lasts two minutes no matter what, and if a prompt is left unfinished then that's the group's problem and not yours. Note that the players would presumably feel responsible for finishing their turns successfully, because playing an incomplete or bad turn accrues problems for the next player in line, and the group as a whole, so you'd be letting down the team by failing.

    \n Eero, thanks for your thoughts.

    Your summary is accurate, with the departure from "pass the conch" that play test groups usually chose to frame a lot of scenes, and that contributed to running out of time.

    One of the MDA aesthetics of the game is expression in the form of barely bounded creative options, which I attempted to at least hint at in the gameplay examples.

    That's almost an invitation for "blank page" syndrome, but I found when people had trouble most often it was fitting in the card Prompt (I added more low specificity options on the cards after that).

    The basic game idea is: what is a very spontaneous play style that could have tension and complications injected from the game itself (Prompts deck).

    Game written in reaction to House Of Reeds. Inspiration taken from In A Quiet Year obviously.

    Who would want to play this game? The intended audience is gamers who already play story games. A quick pick up free game when no one is in the mood to play _____ again quite so soon.

    In play test it was often players who had never played a story game before, but almost invariably with RPG experience. So possibly a game to introduce players to story games.

    Quality of the stories in 5 playtests most reminded me of the Follow games I've played.

    \n Hi Mongrel,
    Out of the three playtest of P&Q: Schism that I've take part in. The first playtest created the best story, the second the second best story, and the third the third best story. The third story in particular had less surprises and interest than the first two playtests. This may have just been that particular night but I feel as though something else played a role: by having four options to choose from the cards didn't lend themselves to having to interpret the prompts in a more limited way, and therefore the interpretions were less creative, original and unexpected. They didn't throw as many wrenches into the narrative. Just because players say they have to strain and be creative to interpret and incorporate a prompt into the story doesn't necessarily make it a good idea to add extra options. The worse that would happen if a prompt didn't work was that a new card would be drawn. So I suggest that you limit prompts to 2-3 per card. Especially if you see the pattern manifest again in future playtesting. I also suggest that you do an analysis of the prompts during play test, keep track of the card numbers and which prompts worked in the narrative and which didn't. Over a lot of play testing you will find which prompts create the best stories. Anyway, I think by constraining choices, the stores will actually come out more interesting, novel and creative. Just my two cents. I 100% believe that with time and with play testing the game can be great; it's already a good game. I definitely recommend that other story game forum members give it a try and playtest it. Thanks

    \n An interesting game, thanks.

    I'm not surprised that open-ended world-building questions take a lot of time.

    Overall, I agree with jeff's feedback: some of the prompts contain too many options or don't constrain the action enough. (I believe one was simply "the plot moves forward"!)

    The game seems to me to suffer from some hang ups drawn from traditional RPGs, like character sheets (why does this game even need PCs, for example?) and detailed world-building as a first step to story inspiration. (I think that most successful fiction starts with themes, conflicts, and strong characters, and then fills in details - if at all - to support those focal points. "This story is about a little girl who finally had enough...")

    This is especially true of the "secret" information which is generated along the way - I can see few reasons why making it public knowledge wouldn't improve the game.

    Some of the prompts are quite interesting, and flagging a die as an available option is a fun idea.

    I also think the game could use some more clarity on what you do on a given turn. The open-ended list of things you can do makes it pretty hard to narrow it down to a concrete action or style of play, which is not helpful.

I've toyed with ideas for different play sets in which there is one shared protagonist, or 3 shared "PCs".
