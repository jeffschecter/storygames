\n Heya,

I suck at writing blurbs on the back of my games. I really do. I never know quite what to say, and anything I write seems to just restate the introduction to my game or is really boring. I think my problem is because I don\u2019t understand blurbs very well.

When you read a blurb, what types of things stand out? What do you want to see in a blurb before you even consider buying a game? I\u2019d like to make my blurbs memorable, but I\u2019m really not sure how to do that. So, I want to ask you guys, what makes a blurb good for you?

Examples are always helpful too

Peace,

-Troy

    \n I like the blurb for Committee a lot, it was what made me want to review the game.

    This Meeting of the Committee is Called to Order...

    You braved crocodiles, Nazis, necromancy, and devolved ape men to find the mythical city and the secrets it holds. You tested your skills and resolve to their limits, driven by a secret desire that motivates you to greater deeds just as it blinds you to possible pitfalls.

    Now you have returned from your expedition and must report to your peers on your daring exploits, proving to the hardened adventurers, geniuses, and polymaths gathered before you that you are worthy of their respect in your every endeavor. You\u2019ve gone into danger and survived driven by your desire\u2014now is the time to bring it to fruition.

    The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries is a storytelling game of exploration and adventure inspired by the pulps. Playable in a single evening or over multiple sessions, it allows you and your friends to tell tales of harrowing danger, daring deeds, and furious action with freeform narration and pulse-pounding timed conflict resolution. No pre-game preparation is required, so grab your hat, your whip, and your .45 and get going!


    What's important to me is that the blurb stimulates my imagination. If the blurb makes me think about an exciting adventure or a cool story then it's doing a great job.

    \n The promise. The imagery.

    I really love the Dogs in the Vineyard and Truth and Justice blurbs:

    Dogs:
    You stand between God's law and the best intentions of the weak.

    You stand between God's people and their own demons.

    Sometimes it's better for one to die than for many to suffer. Sometimes, Dog, sometimes you have to cut off the arm to save the life.

    Does the sinner deserve mercy?
    Do the wicked deserve judgement?

    They're in your hands.

    Edit: the line where he talks to you calls me.

    Truth and Justice:
    A man in crazy pajamas atop a skyscraper rains laser death down on the streets. He's laughing as he does it.

    Below, fires bloom for the news cameras. Reporters duck debris as they yammer on about demands and manifestoes and terror.

    Meanwhile, in the background, the screams of scorched innocents melt into the wail of sirens.

    It makes me angry. My cheeks burn with it -- or that just might be from the wind.

    The madman grows closer and closer, impossibly fast. His eyes widen as he lifts his weapon in my direction. He won't make it in time.

    My hands tighten into fists as I brace for impact.

    I have a set of crazy pajamas of my own.

    The last line did it for me.

    \n I have a set of crazy pajamas of my own.

    Not a word of a lie, I find that line actually moving.

    I like blurbs to communicate the Big Three, more or less.

    * Who will I be playing? How cool are the characters? What's the social stuff going on - trust, honor, duty?
    * What sort of stuff happens? What's the situation? What adventures happen and why?
    * What are the big themes going on? What's the setting?

    God's dogs in a West that never was does all three, lickety split. Religion, smackdown, Western.

    Okay, so what I'm seeing so far is that:

    1. A blurb oughta put an idea for a campaign into your head right away
    2. It needs to promise that what is inside the book is fun
    3. Humor and wit can sometimes be affective as well
    4. The Big 3 isn't a bad place to start

    Is that about right?

    \n Troy, #2 hints at it, but:

    The good blurb doesn't try to tell you everything. Rather, it picks out a highlight and it engages you in that highlight. The Dogs back cover blurb is wonderful because it is addressed to you, and it asks you a question. The Truth & Justice blurb works because it feels like it's a moment in play - a brilliant moment that explains the theme of the game and involves "I" statements.

    Good blurbs highlight stuff and make it immediately engaging. Another example that is wonderful is The Prince's Kingdom:

    You are nine years old. Your brothers just got you a puppy for your birthday. The three of you are seeing the world from your very own boat. You are the sons of the King of Islandia!

    And only you three can stop the war.

    Seriously!

    A good example of how to engage people in highlights without making AP-esque, I/You statement prose is Moments Frozen In Time, from Polaris. It's not the back cover, instead it's the first page. It gives you like 6-12 quick snippets, much like Wicked Age oracle elements, which give you a visceral and sensory understanding of the game. It is lush and engaging.
