The Look Of The Thing.

Levi Kornelsen
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
So, I posted up this sample character for The Cog Wars in my journal...

http://members.shaw.ca/LeviK/Clarence.pdf

And Andy mentioned that to him, a good-looking visual bit, like a character sheet can make or break his impression of a game.

Is that true for you? Does the look of the stuff - the paperwork, if you will, as opposed to the book - that you use during the game itself make a big difference to you?
  • Matt
    April 2006
    As a promo, that initial spark of interest that it generates can be a real draw (look at what happens when White Wolf posts it's splat intros on its site).

    Once you get into actual play, less so on its pure visuals, but a really usable sheet is a big bonus. If you have to hunt around for the things you use regularly it can get frustrating (not unlike using a website).

  • Josh Roby
    April 2006
    Well, are we talking about the in-the-store considering-a-purchase scenario? Art, layout, and figures are probably more important than the copy for making a sale. I've heard lots of people many times say that the character sheet sold them on Game X; I imagine the character sheet, the cover art, and interior art towards the middle of the book (what's visible on a casual flip-through) are probably the biggest sellers.

    In actual play, I usually make my own character sheets, anyway. ;)

  • Eric Provost
    April 2006 edited April 2006

    The character sheets & other assorted visual bits do have a pretty big effect on me. If the character sheet draws my eye, then I read it. If I read it and have no idea what's going on, then I'll probably loose interest. However, if I read it, and wonder how everything fits together on the sheet, then I'll probably be interested in at least skimming the rules to satiate my curiosity.

    -Eric

    I think there is a big divide between games with one page character sheets and multi-page sheets. Its kind of like resumes. Multi page sheets don't turn me off, but they tell me (valid or no) that the game is crunchy combat rather than fluffy story.

    Also, the best looking character sheet I've ever seen (designed by one of our player/illustrators for the homebrew ancient rome game kleenestar and I run) is also the least functional. Its entirely in Latin. Three years in and players are still asking me what "silvicultrix" means.

  • kleenestar
    April 2006
    I apply the index card test: if I can't take the information that really matters and fit it onto an index card, I know the game is going to need some mucking about before I can be happy playing it. While I'm capable of getting really into the crunchy bits and the obsessive recording of character information, I find that the games I actually play have highly reducible or even memorizable character sheet data. Sounds a little silly when I actually say it, but there ya go.

    (Of course, I'm willing to chop left and right to get things to pass the index card test . . . I've got three or four different index-card-ified Ars characters, for example, and believe me when I tell you that was comic.)

    --Jess
