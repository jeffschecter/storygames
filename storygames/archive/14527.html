\n I ran my second Dungeon World session tonight, and ran into great and confusing things.

We played a quick "first session" I didn't make any fronts, just jotted down ideas and left a lot of blanks (I actually wrote down questions I was planning on asking the players).

First, the new rules are great. The subtle character tweaks all seem perfect, the equipment list makes encumbrance and gold super fun, and the big monster section was fun to read. Even better, the "first session" rules really helped me to get the idea of asking questions.

As each player made their character, I asked them specific questions about many things on their sheet, like "What went wrong at the end of your last quest?" for a character who's God sends them out on missions. The characters were rich and interesting. I started them in a tense situation this time, and it definitely made it fun to ask the characters why they were there and their relationship to the villains. So, the question-asking was a major success.

But... I still don't get combat. For background, I've never played AW, and every time I've played a game without turns, I get thrown for the same loop.

The 3 players crawled through a small tunnel into a cavern the size of a small room. They spoke at the 4 goblins for a few moments before the goblins attacked in a frenzy. All 4 goblins charge at once, serrated swords at the ready.

I ask "what do you do", and one of the players naturally pipes up and wants to attack. Hack and slash. Good so far.

I then ask "what do you do" again, and the next player wants to shoot an arrow at the oncoming goblins. They're nearly in melee range, so I say they must defy danger (because they are about to volley). They do so, and roll in the 7-9 range. I say that the extra goblin is coming at his head at the same time as the first dives for his feat (ugly choice?). He sends a volley at the one at his head, getting a 7-9, and not killing it. I say he takes damage from it (similar to hack and slash), and he is tackled by the one at his feet.

Looking back, that seems like an extremely poor outcome for such decent rolls. The characters seem like they should be better than that? Defy danger before a volley seemed weird, and it happened again later - a 7-9 on Defy danger followed by a 7-9 on Volley made for lots of problems for a small amount of success.

Moving on, I ask "What do you do" again, and the next player "goes". They start to gravitate towards "turns" at this point). Turns continue for the rest of the combat. I couldn't figure out how to avoid them. Players wanted to go and felt like turns were the only way to do it.

Later, a player hacks'n'slashes a goblin, taking damage from 2 of them (because there are 2 goblins "on" him). Even later, two players attack the same goblin and both take damage from him (because they both rolled a 7-9). See why I'm confused?

The thief player left combat feeling very useless, because he never rolled higher than a 9, and rolled one 6.

So, all-in-all, my problems are mainly

1) Too many failures. 7-9s feel like failures.
2) I don't get turns, or the lack thereof. How do you avoid them, or embrace them?

Thanks! And keep up the good work!

    A) Hang in there. It's worth it.

    Read the linked items above.

    C) Here are my observations. Your problems might not be the same exactly, but they sound similar:

    1) Remember that there is no GM turn. (Yes, I know you know, but remember.) You're not supposed to do anything with the monsters until the PCs fail a roll or stand there gawking at you waiting to see what the monsters do next. When they do that (wait for the GM's "turn", that is), hit them HARD (as in 'hard move'). The players are new, too, of course, so explain to them that they, having just stood there, gave you permission to smack them down. So, direct damage or nasty monster move. Danger to Defy at the very least. Don't wuss out with Signs of Doom, though. That should discourage the players from waiting for the GM's turn as well.

    2) Remember that there are no player turns. (Yes, I know you know, but remember.) Ask the players what they do, but don't hold them to that until one of them (your choice, probably whoever would have acted first) rolls, and then see if the results can spiral out of control ("moves snowball"). Then ask them what they do now. Maybe they ignore it and keep doing what they would have done anyway. Maybe they do something else.

    3) When you get the opportunity, "spring a trap", "turn the environment against them" and "separate them" can mix things up in interesting ways that might break up the "turn" structure.

    4) Hack and Slash can sometimes be confused with "general combat". Don't let that happen. Hack and Slash is for aggressive player attacks. If you get a move, and a monster attacks with it, "I hack and slash" is (probably) not a real defensive choice. "So, you're taking the hit and attacking it anyway?" Once the PCs start Hack and Slash, though, the monsters will mostly be on the defensive until the players miss. Also keep in mind that the monster's "attack" from a less-than-perfect Hack and Slash can be a monster move instead, or even just "It shoves you to the ground!"

    5) If your players' "soft" hits feel too punitive, remember that you're in control of how bad those "spots", "hard bargains", "tough decisions" and "unwanted attentions" are. If it feels right and makes sense in the fiction, you can totally softball them.

    I'm not an authority, but that's what's working for me. I hope this helps.

    (Sorry to pick on you, Ludanto, but that bit of advice is way off base)

    Of course there are turns! It's a conversation. You take turns talking, right?. Also, there are beats to the action in the fiction. The PCs are doing things. The monsters are doing things. Say what the monsters do! Don't wait around for the players to "gawk" at you and don't just wait for failed rolls to take action.

    Instead: Picture the fictional scene in your head. What's happening? What would the monster do? Say that. Look at the monster moves for ideas. Give everyone a chance to talk and take action, including yourself.

    Just remember: unless you're making a hard move (see the rules for that) say what the monster does, but not its effect. Then ask what they do. Sometimes the monsters will be moving around, getting in position. Sometimes they'll be swarming on top of the fighter, trying to tear his eyes out. Say what the monsters do, make it all dungeony and exciting and cool, put them in a spot, then ask what they do.

    As GM, you will make moves all the time. NOT just when they look at you. NOT just when they fail a roll. All the time! Remember: making GM moves means (so-called) "soft" moves, too. Say what's happening. Have your monsters take action! Be part of the conversation. Paint the picture of perilous dungeon adventure.

    Sorry for the mini-rant. I just get annoyed at this particular myth, that the GM is supposed to wait around and only make moves when a hard move is called for. That's not how it's done. Doing that will warp the game and send you down a not-fun path.

    \n Everyone's already said it, but: the thing here is turns as in a conversation and turns as in initiative order. DW has turns like a conversation ("never a monologue"), not turns like initiative order. Moves are what you do when it's your turn to talk. The phrasing we use might be a bit strong ("when everyone looks at you"), which might lead to some misconceptions, but the basic thing is that when you have to say what's happening, you make a move. That's where turns come in.

    You'll also notice that some of the moves give you natural segues to other bits of the action. For example, Avon casts magic missile, but his concentration gives the goblin backstabber a chance to spring from the shadows, landing on Avon's back. The goblin's riding Avon, trying to get in a good stab (Avon got a 7-9, I put him in a spot). I could stick with Avon, but this also gives me an opportunity to see what someone else is up to, with this as a prompt. "Castor, you're knee deep in sludge, locked in battle with the crocodillian, when you hear Avon's yell of surprise behind you. A quick glance and you see that there's a goblin on his back, trying to stab him, what do you do?"

    There's a turn there, like a conversation turn. I decided to switch the focus to someone else because:
    -I didn't want to turn it into a monologue, so I need to let someone else talk
    -I felt like fictionally Avon probably had a few startled moments before he has a chance to really react to the surprise attack, so fictionally someone else should act
    There's a rules reason and a fictional reason. If you take "turns" like that, I think you'll find there's more flow.

    Note also that not every "turn" is a move. When you ask a player what they do and they say "I push in the door" (unless there's a move that applies to opening this door, which depends on the exact circumstances) you just tell them what's on the other side. If what's on the other side might prompt a response from another player, you can just go to that player:

    Me: "Okay, you slay the last goblin, the hair on the back of your neck is still on end from the evil aura. What do you do, Castor?"
    Dan: "We're in a hurry, so I don't even check for loot, I shove in the door into the ritual room."
    Me: "The wooden door swings in, almost too easily, like the ritualatorium wants you to find it. On the other side you see Adarazaz, Omar, you've finally caught up to him. What do you do?"
    (Dan didn't make a move or anything, but I just followed the flow of action to Isaac, since his character has some bad blood with Adarazaz. We'll get back to Castor after we see what Omar does.)
    Isaac: "Right where I want him. Is he surprised to see us? Can I Backstab him before he notices?"
    (Ooops, I didn't actually give enough fictional information. Time to clarify a little, so we know what's happening.)
    Me: "Well, his back is to you, as his arcane movements stoke the flames of the sacrifical fire..."

    \n Here's the helpful way I think about it.

    Threat: There are only two states of being in the game. Either the PCs are being threatened, or they are not being threatened.
    If there's a bunch of gobboes waiting in ambush, that's a threat. If there's a trap up ahead, that's a threat. If a character has been poisoned, that's a threat.

    The primary thing you as MC need to do is keep track of all of the threats, which PCs are being threatened (some threats hit everyone), and how much harm each threat does.

    Once you have that in mind, you do 1 of 2 things.

    1) announce future badness (is the way AW puts it, DW might call it something else). This means in the fiction describe the situation such that any smart player whose paying attention can figure out that there's a threat of some kind nearby. Things like "This would be a good place for an ambush", or "after the snake bit you you can feel a burning sensation traveling up your arm".

    That's when you go "What do you do". The players may then perform a move that allows them to avoid the effects of the threat or do something that fictionally avoids it. That's fine...there are always more threats. If they do anything else...nail them with the harm. Keep nailing them with the harm everytime they do something that doesn't deal successfully with the threat.


    2) When you have the opportunity to do a Hard Move (like from a failed roll) spring some threat on them that you haven't announced first. If the players cry "holy crap that's frickin harsh"...then you're doing it right...that's why its called a Hard Move.


    Basically when you're doing a combat you say things like "The gobbo archers are preparing a volley", "the hobbo gobbo is moving around to flank your wizard", "the ogre just ripped a big boulder out of the ground" You say stuff like that for all of your monsters. That's your turn. That's the stuff John's talking about above. Each of those things is a threat that you need to identify (either scribble it down, or just remember them).

    How much harm does a Gobbo bow do? If there are enough of them the volley could threaten everyone.
    How much harm does the Hobbo do? If nothing stops him, that's the smack down he's going to lay on the wizard.
    How much harm does a big frackin' boulder flying out of the sky do (and probably AP to boot)...some fool is going to be eating that harm shortly.

    Then let the players decide what they're going to do. If the paladin moves to intercept the Hobbo and succeeds, great. That means the Wizard is spared that harm, the hobbo harm is now is targeting the paladin who, depending on what his move was, may eat that harm or manage to avoid it too.

    If the ranger dude starts to pepper the ogre with arrows, its possible that one of the effects is to deter the ogre from finishing his boulder hurl. If the ranger is really successful the ogre might eat harm from the ranger and be unable to attack back, its cool, you can always make more ogres later. But if the ranger's only partially successful, guess who that wounded and now enraged ogre is going to be flinging that boulder at...Ogre eats ranger's harm, ranger eats ogre's harm.

    If the fighter moves to start wailing on the gobbo archers, determine what he does. if he charges right up at one, he'll probably eat that ones arrow on the way in (trade harm for harm). If he maneuvers around the flank successfully, he might dish harm without taking any. If he figures out a way to distract the whole mob of them, he might spare the rest of the party from eating that volley...otherwise...everybody will be getting hit with some nice arrow harm.

    Once all the players have gone, announce more badness for your monsters. Maybe the Hobbo is now gearing up for a massive two handed strike against the paladin (which you've already decided means +2 harm). Maybe the gobbos keep lobbing arrows. Maybe the Ogre gives up on the boulder idea and just charges. Once again figure out the harm and who's threatened by it, and then ask the players what they're doing. Anyone whose action doesn't involve avoiding that harm...eats it.

    Rinse and repeat.

    \nDefinitely not how I do it. I think the hard moves should always follow from the fiction as already established. There's really no need to invent stuff right then.

    An important nuance, IMO, is that "hard move" does not automatically mean Harm, or disaster, or meanness. It means "the threat takes effect now." The severity is determined entirely by what's established already in the fiction.

    Sometimes it's just not that bad. And that's really okay.

    When you make a hard move you're following through on the threat as established. Not making up new stuff for the sake of bashing the PCs.

    \n The introduction of threats (monsters in particular) largely comes from saying what your prep demands. Not necessarily in a "this room has 5 goblins" sense, but maybe in the "this area is goblin infested" sense. Then they walk into a room, you know there's goblins in it, so you fill in the details and say "There's five goblins, crouched behind an overturned table, waiting for you."

They're fighting goblins with nets, so the thief gets caught and you separate them. The bandit leader has the map, so maybe he yells to his gang to mob the wizard him while he gets away (put someone in a spot).

    \n I played DW for the first time recently (after lots and lots of AW), with Sage running it for a group that included my brother (who hasn't played games in a long time) and somebody new to RPGs. They were totally fine, but, then again, didn't really have the baggage of turn-taking, unless it was from playing Castle Ravenloft. Clara (the newcomer) did ask in the beginning, "So how does this work? Do we take turns doing stuff?" but seemed to relax and go with the flow once Sage explained it would be a little more free-flowing than that, based on what was happening in the fiction.

    Sage's moves, compared to my previous play of AW, didn't seem all that hard, but that could have been for any number of reasons. Personally, I would have probably pushed a lot harder, even for new players. The whole point is triumphing over adversity and making interesting choices, right? And you can't really do that unless the GM brings the pain, so to speak. I didn't even end up using any of the Adventuring Gear I'd bought in the beginning, because it never really came to that.

    My sense is that "whether to deal damage" is a bad way to think about it. Try to focus on what your move actually is, fictionally, and then declare the results of what that move is, which could be damage ("spines shoot out from the walls and sink into the side of your face!") or a condition ("your foot's stuck in the trap!") but doesn't necessarily have to be. Like, going with the trap theme, maybe the ceiling begins to drop, but the players still have time to attempt something before they get crushed. The damage rules exist so that, when you need to deal damage, you know how to do that, but you shouldn't have to think about them until you need them.

    Anyway, just my $0.05, though other folks probably know a lot more about DW specifically.

    \n How I'd treat Charge! depends on the situation. It might just mean closing dramatically, getting in close where the goblins have the advantage, while the fighter's been trying to keep them at spear-point. It might mean damage, sure, to one person or more than one. Maybe even a mix: the goblin leads a charge, ending with his dagger in the fighter's gut, the others follow along and get in close (but don't make attacks).

    Something I've noticed recently in my GMing of DW: I rarely hand out damage. There's so much other cool stuff going on I usually pick up on something else first (remember, GM moves are just ways of picking an element out of the fiction to work with). I sometimes catch myself: "Oh, I'm working too hard to stretch this, it should just be damage." That's probably overall a good thing: it means my moves are diverse and tied to the situation. I just have to remember that yeah, this is a dungeon too, people get hurt.

    My experience, particularly at GenCon, is that even more traditional gamers are pretty cool with it, so long as you tell them up front how it'll be.

    Edited to add: Jonathan and I are on the same page. Even me taking it too easy on them, really, but the game still went pretty well. I think I backed down too much in the face of new players.

    \n If one character triggers a trap, everyone might take damage, right? Likewise, if one character draws a hail of arrows, other people could get hurt too. That's part of what it means to think about the fiction first. You consider what's happening, tell the players, and then figure out what that means. Maybe one character takes damage and the others have to Defy Danger to avoid the arrows (or they could decide to just take the damage and do something else instead). Maybe one character gets lit on fire and needs help from the others to put it out. Could be any number of things, depending on what the fiction demands.

    When I MC Apocalypse World I try to react like this, after I've picked my move:

    FIRST: Say what's happening, fictionally.

    "Aw, man, as you turn to hack at the other guy, you catch a crossbow bolt right in the cheek. Your mouth fills with blood and you have to lean over and let it gush downward just to be able to keep breathing."

    SECOND: Figure out what that means, mechanically.

    "So... that's [harm as established], mark that down. And it's Defying Danger to do anything except cough up blood and deal with your situation right now."

    THIRD: Ask the characters questions, to keep things moving.

    "So... do you pull the bolt out? Is anybody else going to do something about this, or are you leaving him to half-drown in his own blood?"

    If you handle things this way, what you do mechanically (#2), derives directly from what you said just happened (#1). So you never have to decide whether you should inflict damage or who you should inflict it to. Did you say that someone got hurt? Cool. Then deal damage as established. This is especially great if -- like me and you and Sage -- the MC tends to be a softy, because you have accountability to the fiction and what you've already said, so you can't weasel out of hitting them as hard as they deserve.

    \n What Jonathan said x10.

    I'd back up his first instruction ("Say what's happening fictionally") especially to include what you say before the players say what the PCs do. That's where the effects of your hard moves come from, generally.

    So, you go, "The goblins rush forward in a mass, spears thrust forward, shrieking, sheer evil hatred burning in their eyes. What do you do?"

    (That's the Charge! move. It's not my hard move, it's the thing I say that sets me up for a future, harder move. Like, "take damage from goblin spears and fall down.")

    Then, when the Fighter steps up to hack one down as it closes in, and misses his roll, your hard move is obvious. You don't have to invent something or try to judge what's too harsh or whatever. You just follow through on the consequences of the Charge! move.

    Also, even when the Fighter steps up and hacks one of them down with a 10+, you still follow through on the Charge! move -- unless a character has interfered with it. There are still 3 other goblins charging. Did anyone do anything about them? No? Then there's your hard move (an opportunity on a golden plate, you might say) so you follow through.

    The follow-through means bringing the full effect of your moves to fruition. What's the effect of a mass of charging goblins? The victims get shoved backward and stabbed with spears, probably. It would be weird if they didn't.

    (I say more about this idea over on my blog.)

    \n What Sage said. You always derrive new fictional content from what has already been established by the MC and the other players. Sometimes that means establishing fictional steps in between.

    \n Everyone nailed "be a fan" pretty well. Portraying a fantastic world is the big one for resolving your situations. In a fantastic world, are blankets reasonably found in the location? That's it!

    If that means making a judgement call, you have to make that with your principles in mind. You're a fan of the characters in that you're not here to block their ideas. "I didnt mention any blankets so there's none here" isn't really being much of a fan, right? That's not based on the fiction either. "No, the guild doesn't let members sleep in here, no blankets" is fair, you're portraying a consistent fantastic world. "Sure, they probably would, it's winter so there's a pile by what's left of the fireplace, but the flames are between you and them" is totally fine too, you're being a fan who wants to see how they deal with a situation. If you're not sure which one to go with you fall back on being a fan: if you were watching this on TV, would you be more excited to see the characters look for a blanket but not find one, or look for a blanket and see a challenge in getting one? Being a fan totally takes into account not being cheap, like John said. You're a fan of the characters, you don't want to just see them have solutions given to them.

    \nI just wanted to add my thoughts to Sean's initial query on combat and 'turns' and moves. I liken the DW conversation first and forefmost to the fantasy fiction your group is into. The mechanics can cope with a wide variety of fictional narratives, they are very robust. The disconnect can come when the conversation is at odds, or when (as Sage and John point out) someone 'hogs' the narrative limelight. Being a good DM means facilitating this conversation and asking provacative questions so that everyone gets their say, makes their stamp on the fiction in a way that makes sense to them. The moves (especially hard moves handed to you on a golden plate) just flow from great fiction.
    \n
    \nThis in turn develops great choices for the players. They want to fictionally make this happen (oh and it will on a 10+) but end up rolling a 7-9, so they end up with a difficult choice, having to accept one of many fictional shifts to their vision. This in turn opens up other players fictional inspiration and the moves 'snowball' from there.
    \n
    \nIn terms of the OP and combat, I like to ask my players how they want to tackle the fictional representation of the melee? Is it all close in, bloody,g ritty and individual? Or is it more abstract and strategic? What sort of stories do they like to tell? Note that sometimes its more than one fictional 'style' at a time! I had a fighter who was all about individual blows in Hack and Slash, trading Harm for Harm in brutal Joe Abercrombie style prose, whereas the distant Wizard was all cerebral and conflict avoidance (Ie:cowardice), narratively describing his emotional investment and thoughts rather than a gruesome expose of wounds suffered and inflicted.
    \n
    \nSo yeah, I'm not advising anything new, just re-iterating that if you feel a disconnect with DW and old habits of "turn-based combat', just focus on the fiction. Tell a good story first - your story, and the moves and mechanics will not only support folks turns in the conversation, but open up narrative possibilities and twists through failure. Twists that make much more sense because they are rooted in the fiction everyone has detailed, rather than just selecting moves and applying mechanics.
