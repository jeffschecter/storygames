\n Personally, I really enjoy it when I can get some reading satisfaction out of an RPG for its own sake. Anyways, what do you guys think of games that have "reading value" do you think it takes away from or somehow obscures the parts you need to play, or is it worth it for that extra entertainment value you get out of reading it?

    \n Stephen,

    I think what you're talking about is a book being entertaining. I don't believe that a book written in a "manual" style can't be entertaining. Hell, WEG Paranoia was pretty classic as a manual that was hilarious to read. As an editor for Misspent Youth, I helped Rob make his entertaining, attitude-laden style more consistent. He had these lapses into sesquipedalian doldrum that elicited my margin comments like, "Rob, you need to say 'fuck' here a lot more."

    As a writer, the tension is between saying clearly what you want readers to learn and entertaining the reader with prosaic wanderings away from the rules. I tend to get heavily procedural when I write down my rules. "You do this. And then you do this. And if this happens, you do this." Oh, sorry. Did I just put you to sleep?

    Game writers need to find a voice that can entertain while nailing down the procedure. The voice needs to go with the material, too. Rob Bohl's "fuck the police" voice in Misspent Youth was perfect for his neo-punk dark future game but would be out of place in -- fuck, I dunno -- let's say, The Princess Game. Use your voice to convey mood.

    Does this voice stuff distract a reader from the rules? Not if done well. Don't overdo it. Trim away the excess fat, but leave enough for flavor once the reader tosses the game on the barbecue of his mind. Use metaphors -- preferably not all about barbecues -- to make your points in interesting ways. Which are you gonna remember? The rule that says, "Remove excess florid prose, but leave enough to keep the reader's interest" or the barbecue analogy?

    There's also a point of salesmanship here. Like any good book, you are trying to hook the reader. Don't fill the beginning of your game text with soporific lullabies, like a dull "what is roleplaying" section. If you must write a "what is roleplaying" section, by all means, make it the most exciting thing in the game! Or hide it in a sidebar on page 12. The first page should be gripping. You are trying to convince the reader to read the rest of the section, then the rest of the chapter, then the rest of the book. You are trying to get your audience to pick up the book, read some of it, read all of it, and then play it.

    In the writing and editing process, pick up your book and see it through the eyes of a novice. Would you pick this book up? Would you read it? Read the back cover. Would you buy it? Read the first page. Would you want to read more or would you flip-flip-flip through to something more interesting? If you're flipping through your own material, cut it or rewrite it.

    I think we can do a lot to raise the level of art and craftsman ship in our game writing.

    Posted By: Adam DrayThat's a layout issue though. Graphic elements should enhance the text, never get in the way of it.
    \nI tend to be on the sneering-at-pictures side of this argument more often than not, but this is an excellent, excellent point that I agree with whenever I can muster up the strength to think about it rationally.

    When I'm flipping through a book, pictures are a navigation tool. I know that character creation rules are AFTER the picture of the zeppelin crashing into King Kong's face, I know that the advice section is on the same page as Tyrone Powers kissing Vecna with full tongue action, I know that if I see the illustration of a burning tent surrounded by shoggoths giving their equivalent of a thumbs up "we did it!" pose that I'm close to the table for falling damage...that sort of thing. On the level of me not giving a shit about what foofy (not to mention probably misguided and useless to me) atmosphere the art is meant to evoke, I still get value from the art, just in terms of having an instinctive split-second map of where I am in the text, so I can use my thinking brain for more important shit than memorizing page numbers and chapter headings.

    That said, I am less inclined to sneer at the pictures for their content if they are actually illustrating and enhancing the text rather than attempting to deliver a foofy, misguided, and useless sense of atmosphere to me. Fuck that noise. Show me what specific things look like in the setting: what do the spaceships look like, what does a city look like, how do people dress? When you say that the soldiers all wield man-portable assault cannons, what does an assault cannon actually look like? What does a Bigby's Expressive Finger spell look like?


    And on the topic of entertainment versus manual, manual trumps entertainment. I'm getting the book to make my own entertainment, to use it in a game: if the book's trying to entertain me instead of teaching me the tools and techniques to play the game, it is a huge, epic failure and a waste of my money, because I can get any great novel I want for well under $10 at a bookstore, or for free at my local library. I don't want a twenty-page short story at the front of the book: it's wasted space that could've been used to give me more tools and cool things that I could actually use in the game. I don't want three pages of funny jokes if those funny jokes can't make my game better.

    That said, a good manual uses entertainment to enhance the information it presents. Boring manuals are not as good as entertaining ones, either as instructional tools or resources. So once the "manual" part is firmly nailed down, then by all means there ought to be some entertainment value added on top of it. But, like art, it should be there to enhance the text, not get in the way of it.

    \n I don't even understand this distinction, frankly, unless we're discussing some sort of semi-game book with lots of bluster and not much in the way of shop talk. It boggles my mind that somebody who is presumably passionate about his hobby of roleplaying would find a text of instruction (a manual, if you will) about it boring - why do this thing if you don't like it? Or rather, how come your excitement about roleplaying does not translate into a curiosity about game texts, but you still buy and try to read them? Thus I'm firmly of the mind that having to bribe people to read your manual by adding parallel entertainment is just screwy - I'll write a book that is not a game instruction manual if I want to entertain you with non-roleplaying content. Something like... a Japanese-style replay, I guess, if you're still committed to pretending that it's the roleplaying that you're interested in and not just fantasy fiction. (Might not even be pretense, hypothetical play is a well-established phenomenon wherein people like the idea of roleplaying more than the actual act.)

    I do know many people who do roleplaying but don't care about it enough to buy and read expert texts, preferring to learn games and techniques from others by the way of instruction and example. In fact, these people are a 5:1 majority among roleplayers in my experience. It shouldn't be surprising, considering how non-readers don't buy games, if I choose not to make them my target audience when writing rpg texts.

    Looking at this from the other side, however (and proving that I don't really understand the whole premise of the discussion): it seems obvious to me that a good rpg manual, being a book of instruction on a practice advocated by the author, will do its best to paint the exercise itself in a positive, engaging and entertaining light. So ideally you'd find that manual engaging to read because you're interested in the activity it explains, all thanks to how it's been written in an entertaining manner - which is being done because the author wants you to become motivated to actually play. So this whole distinction between being a good read and being a good game seems screwy to me; how can it be a good game if it doesn't motivate you to play, and how can it not be a good read for a rpg hobbyist if it does?

    \n Well, yes, obviously -- that's the nature of putting "book" and "manual" (or anything else, for that matter) on an axis, that people will discuss only the extreme ends of it. When we talk about "good" and "evil" we naturally gravitate to the most overt, uniform examples for each and avoid the messy grey areas in the middle. It's just easier to talk about things that way.

    In this case, I think the real meat is in looking at this as a matter of priorities rather than as an either-or proposition: when you actually read a book that is (as all RPG books tend to be) in the grey area between book-as-entertainment and book-as-manual, which part of that mix appeals to you more? When you say "that's an awesome RPG book!", are you more likely to be praising its entertaining features or its instructional/reference quality?

    Because that's actually a preference that people have in real life: the things that you dwell on when you think about the RPG book that you love and all the things you love about it. I would go so far as to say that no normal person anywhere would say "I want this RPG book I spent $50 on to be a long and wonderful novel with gorgeous art that doesn't tell me diddly-squat about how to actually play the game" or "I want this RPG book I spent $50 on to be a scrupulously clear instruction manual for the game with absolutely no interesting or entertaining style to it whatsoever." You want it to be a mix of the two, you want it to be a GOOD mix of the two, but it's conceivable that one part of that good mix might be just a wee bit more valuable in your mind when it comes time to pick favorites.


    Me, I tend to lean towards the manual side, because I get so much entertainment from so many other things that I just don't see RPG books as valid competition in that arena. The novels I read, the movies I see, the TV I watch...they all tend to do a better job of pure entertainment than the best RPG book I've ever read. I'm not saying I hate RPG books that strive to be entertaining -- that's far from the case -- but I am saying that how entertaining the book might be is slightly less important to my "This is awesome!"-o-meter than how useful I find it in making an entertaining game happen when I sit down to actually play. Because in THAT arena, novels and movies and TV do a flat-out shitty job of describing a game to me: they might provide inspiration, but precious little in the way of instruction or advice, while RPG books can excel at both of those things.

    Manual-ness being equal, I'll give the nod to the more entertaining RPG book. But if the manual end is weak, the entertainment's not going to make up for it where I'm concerned. I'm assuming that for other people in this thread, the reverse would be true, that a less entertaining book will not satisfy them even if it is a clearer manual than most other RPGs they've read.

    I find it interesting that people give their opinion based on the false dichotomy, then point out that it is one. Really though, if you take two things that aren't the same thing, you can set up a continuum simply by placing them in opposition. Black and very, very dark gray, for example. It's not like black and white, which are more obviously not the same, but equally non-opposite.

    \n

    Anyhow.. I think some useful thoughts have come out of the thread, regardless. Some solid advice.

    \n

    Very few people are advocating for insufficient manuality. Most people have conceded that solid instructions are important.

    \n

    Very few people are advocating for a lack of entertainment value. Most people have conceded that dry reading isn't a bonus.

    \n

    So the answer, to me, is quite obvious. Hammer out the procedures until they're as complete as you can make them, and when you write the game text, make sure you include concise instructions on those procedures.. But don't try to write like you're trying to put your audience to sleep. Use a natural voice, digress as is appropriate, crack jokes, tell stories, whatever you feel should be in there.

    \n

    You're NOT going to please everyone. Accept it and be free.

    \n

    I'm not even going to address pictures. The OP didn't ask, so it's off-topic.

    \n It depends. If it's done well -- if the author's voice is likable to me and doesn't obfuscate things that need to be made clear -- I'm all for it.

      I like "punchy manuals". When I'm done reading a game book, I ask myself a few questions:

      1) Do I understand how to play? Can I make a character, arbitrate basic rules disputes, generate a story/adventure/game session?
      2) Can I explain how the game works to others? Can I say "generally you are rolling two dice and keeping the highest" or "you are rolling a pool of d6 and adding to beat 10" or whatever?
      3) Can I sell my players on it? Can I say "this game is about sky-pirates" or "pulp heroics" or "mice with swords" or whatever? If the concept is not one that they are immediately keen on, is there anything that -is- exciting/unique in mechanics or gameplay that will hook them anyway, a la Burning Wheel: "Yeah, it's fantasy with elves and orcs, but the character creation system is awesome, and I think you'll like how it rewards you for playing up your character motivation".

      Basically, this means I want my first read-through to be both stylish AND informative, but moreso the latter, since as the primary buyer of new RPGs (I mean new in the sense of "games that my group has not played before"), I have to be able to summarize and synthesize all the crunch AND all the fluff, and put it into a package that my group will be attracted to.

      I'm with deadlytoque on preferring punchy, informative manuals. Preferably ones that are quick reads, and that are amenable to skipping around.

      One quality that I think helps with skipping around is good organization: everything you need to know about a particular thing (say, skill checks) can be found in the same section of the book, covers all the important info, and is signposted/landmarked well enough to find it in a hurry. Bonus points if it's something I can pass across the table to someone else and know that they can read the section for themselves quickly and with a minimum of "wha...huh?" questions. Bonus bonus points if they already prep a cheat sheet section at the back that lays out the rules and game procedures clearly and quickly, so I can just steal it or maybe even xerox it completely and pass it out to the group.


      I used to read gaming books cover-to-cover, but as I've gotten older and more jaded and busier with real life, I tend to save my good sitting-around-and-reading time for novels or other "real" books. I don't have the patience to slog through a whole manual, or even a whole section, so manuals that let me swoop in and easily grab onto the good stuff and then go away to do something else are precious to me.
