\n Not going to GenCon? Bored? Let's bat this one around.

Player rolls a die in an inverted cup.
She calls odd or even.
All other players indicate thumbs up or thumbs down simultaneously.
The die is revealed.
If she was right, success. If she was wrong failure.

Now the tricky bit.
What is the difference between thumbs up and down? That's the degree of success if correct, or the degree of failure if wrong. Risky vs. conservative.
Thus, If everyone says yes and she's right, mucho successo

The twist: If everyone says no, and she's right, fabulous success. The opposite? Pitiful failure.

Is this totally broken? Probably. Is something else using something similar? Dunno. Am I ripping off that Pirates of the Caribbean dice game? I think so.

    \n One mechanic I keep kicking around is a voting resolution.

    Conflict arises, we establish intents, and then the table votes. Margin of success is the inverse of the margin of voting. So close votes are big, and landslides are no big deal.
    The twist here is you roll a die: on a 1, the result is the opposite of the vote, with the same margin of success.
    Vary die size for more or fewer unexpected turnabouts. A d4 game is way different from a d20 game.

    \nThis doesn't resemble Liar's Dice so much as it does Cho-han bakuchi, a traditional Japanese gambling game that I'm only familiar with due to Usagi Yojimbo.



    Contrary to what Mike said, I think the choices of thumbs up or down provides some interesting gambling to the system, if implemented right. If you're using Tim's Option #2 (or maybe a variant thereof), then having everyone agree to a thumbs up is really good when you roll a success, but really bad if you roll a failure (I assume). If you have a split between thumbs up and down, you get more median results: smaller successes and failures. How much do you want to augment the results of the die, when you don't know what that result is?

    \n It's one of those ideas that feeds my love of "risky. vs. cautious" (a mechanical concept I've loved ever since the original Usagi Yojimbo RPG,) in this case with the level of risk determined by other players, rather than the one whose ass is at stake here. You know, the dice can be your friend but if the others are lukewarm you could still miss your needs in successes. Likewise, maybe people start all going one way or the other, not just to boost your successes, but also to boost your failures, and in the parlance of the paranoid, "fcuk with you."

    Maybe this works in a "Success, BUT!" system.
