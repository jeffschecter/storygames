\n This is borne out of a bad experience, so please pardon me if it's reactionary, but I'm bringing it here for people's opinions.

What I want to know is, if there's a mechanic for handling a character's action, in what circumstances should you avoid that mechanic?

Like, when a player wants to know if an NPC is lying to them, when do you just tell them?

If a player wants their character to act on knowledge, and there is a mechanic for granting that character access to that knowledge, when do you just ignore it?

I'm not interested in dissecting the experience I had, because I'm not an impartial third party here, and I think the bigger question is more important.

When the game has mental and social stats, skills, and rules, at what point do you allow player behavior and player desire to supersede those rules?

Some game texts say "When both parties want the action to succeed, don't pick up the dice." That doesn't seem palatable to me. That's not a game mechanic, that's a social contract issue.
Some game texts say "Only roll the dice when there's a significant cost of failure." That seems more likely, but it still seems more like a social contract than a game rule.

Does anyone out there go by the game rules all the time?
Does anyone out there discuss what qualifies for an exception before play begins?
Does anyone out there negotiate an exception as it happens?

Are there people out there who change their policy depending on the game or the group, and if so, what's the underlying principle that remains unchanged?

    \n I always use the procedures of a given game's system. Most systems won't indicate mechanical resolution for everything that happens, though. I don't find the difference between mechanical and merely systematic very important, though, so insofar as I'm concerned it's all just playing to the procedure. Sometimes that procedure is all about freeforming the small stuff, sometimes it's important to roll dice for minor things to create randomized dissonance, sometimes the game requires you to actively avoid subject matter that can't be well-treated by the mechanical portions of the game. Lots of range in design out there, so I don't think it's very useful to generalize this as an universal question about using mechanics or not.

    \nIf there's some kind of rule that we all know is poorly explained or poorly phrased, yeah, maybe we'll talk about it beforehand, or try and agree on how to interpret it, or discuss how we've interpreted it in the past.
    Generally, when making exceptions, it helps to pause play for at least two seconds in order to gauge any objections to the change OR to simply confirm that we're all okay with the idea.

    As for this Social Contract stuff you're talking about... in my opinion, the only part of this that's Socially Contractual is explicating the expectation of house rules. But an individual game mechanic can be "freeform" without simply being part of the Social Contract.
    A "mechanic" is simply "the manner by which a given type of fact(s) is established through play." Doesn't matter if that means dice, or cards, or seeking consensus/agreement from the players and GM.

    \n Okay, so your question is, basically: "Dogs says to say yes or roll the dice. When do I say yes?"
    Well, when it'd be too time consuming to do dice for a given thing (logistics-level decision), or nobody REALLY seems to care about the event (social-level decision), or the outcome is quite certain (fiction-level decision).
    That first one might seem a bit odd, but I think it's important to err on the side of being charitable, and I think the order of priority COULD go like this:

    Logistics - we have X amount of time to play, so we prioritize accordingly with content exploration
    Social - we're kind of always checking in with each other through verbal and nonverbal cues about what's interesting and what's worth our time
    Fiction

    Obviously, these three categories are things I just made up, and the order could vary, but I think it's reasonable to assume that, at a con or during a one-shot, goal #1 is a tug-of-war between showing off the game's features and exploring what people find interesting. The internal demands of the fiction (exploration for its own sake) are gonna have to give way if there's any kind of sharp time limit on play.

    \nLet me put it this way: if it's a choice between honoring our social contract and honoring the rules-as-written, that's not really a choice at all. I will always, ALWAYS side with the actual real-life people around our gaming table over the cold, dead words of the rulebook. That's just part of the "I only play games with my friends" deal, really. If I favored the rules over our relationships with each other, I'd be a lousy friend.

    That said, one of the elements of our particular social contract is that no one thinks being shut down is fun, unless there's a good, explicit reason for it. So while it's often a no-brainer for a GM to skip a roll and just declare a character's action to be a success, it's less common for a GM to skip a roll and declare a failure; on the rare occasion when that happens, we ask why, and the GM either has a great answer for that or realizes, oh shit, I fucked that up, you really should roll for that.\n

    \nYes, and yes. We have even started doing things one way, decided that it sucked, and immediately stopped and did it the other way instead!

    Like Zac in Virginia, I think there's several reasons why we sometimes choose to set aside the rules, and different priorities for those reasons. I think our group would put "social" above "logistics", but there's obviously a lot of overlap between the two. (Something that takes an inordinate amount of time during the game to handle is often the same thing that some of us aren't interested in, so we'll just work around it instead.)

    \n Harlequin, it sounds like you don't want to use "social contract" as a guide in this, but in the end, it's all social contract.

    The agreement to use a certain set of rules is social contract, and so is who gets to break or ignore them and when.

    That might not be useful to you, so let me tell you when I choose to go around a mechanic.

    A) When the rules tell me to, hopefully with advice on when and how that should be.
    When it becomes obvious that it's going to completely destroy everything.

    Otherwise I like to follow the rules. It's comforting.
