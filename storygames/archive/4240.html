\n I'm having one of those "why is this so clear to me and no one else gets it" moments (or weeks), so I'm wondering if maybe I've hit a personal preference and mistook it for a general principle. So.

I need things externalized in my gameplay. I need my character externalized; I need conflicts externalized; I need development externalized. What do I mean by that?

Externalizing is this great American neologism that take a perfectly innocent adjective and implicates it as a verb. Externalizing is taking what is inside and projecting it outside. Which is uselessly abstract, so let's talk specifics.

Externalizing character is taking all that stuff inside your character and projecting it out into the world around your character. Your character is caught between two cultures? Then he's got two NPC friends, one from each culture, and they both want things from him. His being caught between cultures isn't something he cries into his pillow at night, it's a problem he deals with in concrete terms whenever he interacts with his friends.

Externalizing conflict is similar. Your character isn't going to decide which culture he "really" belongs to late one night after he's done crying into his pillow. He's going to decide when his two friends want things that are mutually exclusive, and he has to choose between concrete options.

And when he makes that decision, the consequences of the decision can (and should, in my august opinion) be externalized. The friend he chooses that one time starts making assumptions about later decisions; the friend he didn't choose gets distant. This mirrors the changes happening within the character.

Now, these things are essential to my enjoyment of a game, not just neat tricks that make the game more fun. I will not enjoy myself if the decisions that i make as a character are not expressed or elaborated out in the world of the story. I will not enjoy myself if my character exists in a vacuum where nobody cares about the decisions he makes. Seriously, I will feel like the GM is ignoring my creative input.

This is the problem that I've been encountering talking about Polaris and Mutual Decision, where it really bothers me that so much of the character, conflict, and development is "trapped" inside the character and does not get externalized into the world around them. At the end of the day, I don't care what kind of a person you are if the kind of person you were didn't change anything in your world-- because the answer is, in my mind, that you're a boring kind of insignificant person-- and why do I care about that?

Anyway. Resonance? Sound familiar? Or am I totally loopy again?

    \n I definitely think the first step is getting the important conflict on the table.

    The second is seeing what the player of the character wants to do about it. There are some conflicts in my characters I don't really care much if they're resolved, or put out into the real world in any focused way - instead, they inform all the other things he has to do, decide and deal with. If I want to keep a conflict so that it makes him cry into his pillow, that's okay! But I should be able to explicitly state what and why I want to do this.

    I grope for an example. I played a budding newspaperman in a Thief: The Dark Project game. He had great agony over his belief in The Public versus his cynicism in People. This informed but was never explicit in nearly everything he did. And it would have been a waste of time to introduce the conflict in more focused terms because no other players at the table really gave a crap about it. Still, the conflict, though primarily internal and never "explicitly" externalized, added to my enjoyment of play.

    So I think there's a sliding scale:

    At one end we have the conflict which the player never tells anyone about, even the other players.

    Further along we have a conflict the player tells some other players about but only brings into play through internal thinking and application to unrelated or near-unrelated areas.

    Further along we have a conflict the player tells the other players about but nobody really strongly pitches a situation right at that conflict.

    And still further we have a conflict that is fully "externalized." (Ick! Pthu! That word!)

    Make sense?

    We've talked about this privately and I'm beginning to suspect that what you're not seeing is a) the role of scope of externalization and b) the amount of time the externalization takes place.

    Scope, however, I think is the biggest issue. I am in 100% agreement with you that conflicts need to be externalize and impact "the world" but for purposes of any given story how large must "the world" be? How about just one person? What if my decisions and actions totally changes the fate of just one single person.

How much (for good and bad) of where they ended up were YOU responsible for. I also disagree that the mutual friends and family members in It Was A Mutual Decision are mere color. I and my character's inability to cope with being alone directly contributed to that.

    So I ask is one elderly character's death from neglect by her own son enough for you? Or in my second It Was A Mutual Decision game where an NPC brother decided he'd rather be fanboy than a brother.

    Which brings me to the topic of time. In the first It Was A Mutual Decision game the mother's death came about from exactly three points in the game. The first was simply my commitment during setup that the male PC have a sick mother. The second was when the male PC chose to stay with his girlfriend rather than move in to take care of her and indeed left one of his wererat brood to take care of her instead. The third was when we framed a scene directly to mom's funeral. That's it. A note at setup and two scenes in the whole game. Would that be enough for you? I know when Laura said, "It's the mom's funeral" *my* eyes lit up in horror given the state of affairs we last left the mom in.

    Even shorter was the role of the brother in the second It Was A Mutual Decision game. A note at setup that the female PC had a brother who was fan of the male PC. He showed up in just one scene. The scene were he blew off his sister to side with the object of his fandom. I admit I didn't have AS strong a reaction as I did to the mother scene. But the back of mind still thought, "what a punk."

    So, I'm totally on board that a character must face external representations of his personal conflicts and have an external impact. I just don't think that impact needs to be very large or far reaching. And in game setting, that it need necessarily happen over the long term. One or two punchy scenes will suffice.

      \nI pretty much agree with the previous posters. I have a hard time seeing why I should care if my actions had a real, lasting impact in-game. It's not as though there is some real stake in whether the villain is really defeated. For the most part I care about the journey more than the destination.

      Now, I like action to be occuring within the game -- interactions are what it's all about, in particular. However, those actions / interactions have an impact on the inner life of my character -- they're usually not a mirror of it.

      In a novel or play, you're identifying with a protagonist that someone else is controlling. Thus, the author needs to externalize in some way in order for you to understand the inner conflict. But role-playing isn't like that -- you're authoring your own character, so you don't need those same devices to understand your PC. That PC still needs action and interaction to have satisfying dramatic change, but it doesn't need devices like soliloquys or mirrored externalization or whatever in order for it to come across. The contrast of the two is something I discuss in an essay of mine:

      Story and Narrative Paradigms in Role-Playing Games

      That said, I did enjoy just fine doing a long campaign of Buffy where everything was symbolic externalizations of inner issues. However, I also enjoy campaigns that aren't this way.
