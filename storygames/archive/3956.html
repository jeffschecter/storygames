Industry Legend Wins Award 'For Excellence in Gaming'

The 2007 Diana Jones Award for Excellence in Gaming has been given to The Great Pendragon Campaign, a role-playing game campaign book by Greg Stafford, published by White Wolf.

The winner was announced at a ceremony packed with games industry professionals, from designers to publishers and distributors, held at Jillian's in Indianapolis at 9 pm Wednesday, August 16, the day before the opening of the Gen Con Indy game convention.

In terms of sheer scope, Greg Stafford's Great Pendragon Campaign breaks new ground, presenting almost a century's worth of continuous story with gemlike clarity; in almost fractal fashion, any given year can become its own campaign. Its greatest structural successes are those of Stafford's Pendragon: a superbly compact yet never sketchy adventure format, seamless hard-wiring of characters into setting and continuity, and unprecedented emphasis on epic, generational storytelling. Thematically, it is a triumph of Arthurian art , the roleplaying form's equivalent of Tennyson's The Idylls of the King or Wagner's Parsifal\xe2\u20ac\u201da brilliant personal engagement with one of the foundation myths of Western fantasy.

Greg Stafford has worked in the hobby-games industry since 1975, when he founded the publishing company Chaosium. His seminal game RuneQuest was released in 1978, and he is widely regarded as one of the most influential and inspirational designers in the history of the RPG field.

Also shortlisted for the Diana Jones Award this year were the pirate-based coin game Pieces of Eight designed by Jeff Tidball and published by Atlas Games; and Stefan Pokorny, the creator, sculptor and painter of the Master Maze line of miniature terrain from Dwarven Forge.

The Diana Jones Award is given annually to whatever in the opinion of its mostly-anonymous committee shows or exemplifies gaming at its best. Anyone and anything within the games industry and hobby is eligible to win the Diana Jones Award, including individuals, products, publications, publishers, distributors, retailers, clubs, organisations, conventions, events, trends, innovations and concepts. Previous winners include Peter Adkison and Jordan Weisman, the RPGs Nobilis, Sorcerer and My Life with Master, and the boardgame Ticket to Ride.

    \n I think what makes somebody like Josh or myself wary is that we may have trouble in terms of seeing how this could be collaborative. I'd like to give it the benefit of the doubt, but it just sounds sorta railroady.
    \n
    \nIt's hard to explain, so bear with me. For lack of a better term, the "Chaosium" model is to publish a plot in it's entirity, and then expect the GM to cope with the fact that it's a plot. Meaning that you have two options with a pre-set plot:
    \n
    \n1. Railroad the players through the plot. Oh, sure, the players can do dialog with Arthur as he passes through. But if Arthur is going to fight with Lancelot over Guenevre at some point, then the PCs can't be allowed to seduce her. Right? Because that would mess up all of that stuff in the book after that fact. So this makes it feel as though the role of the players is just to be there to view the events as they unfold, and have no real effect upon them. All Rosencrantz and Guildenstern.
    \n
    \n2. Allow players to affect the plotline, but then have to "wing it" in terms of modifying the ongoing plot, again, making much of what's in the book pointless.
    \n
    \nNow, interestingly, I think Josh, and certainly myself, don't mind "winging it." But then how is the book helping? Rather, how are the events listed pertinent? Why not just have a sourcebook of the characters and their motives? And even possible events.
    \n
    \nA lot of the problem with something like this is in jarring presentation. The text for many products like this is written instructing the GM on what "should" happen next. If those things are just suggestions, then they could do with a much better presentation. Further, the idea that Ian has - that' it's like WWII - is a fine idea. As backdrop (see Ron Edwards' notes on "Underbelly play"). But usually the characters are presented as more than backdrop in such products. Tempting you to allow the players to kill them or whatnot.
    \n
    \nAre the PCs supposed to interact with Arthur or not? Do they merely hear about his exploits, or do they get involved? If the book has no notes on this, what does it imply? Rather, given the presentation of the data, what's enabled by the data?
    \n
    \n
    \nNow, I think that I at least, and perhaps others, are leaping to some conclusions here. Perhaps Greg has come up with a way to present this information in such a way as to make it all really useful in play, not at all jarring, just suggestions for what might happen, no events that are predicated on long chains of previous events, etc, etc. Or something even more brilliant than this. Greg's certainly capable of such innovation, I think.
    \n
    \nSo we should hold off until we understand the product better. A problem with this, however, is that the size of the product is such that, barring understanding it well, and having misgivings about it, lots of us here are unlikely to purchase the book to get a closer view.
    \n
    \nSo, that said, if anybody actually has purchased the thing, or better yet, has played through some of it, I'd love to hear about it from them in terms of these things. Is it just a character sourcebook with some notions for potential events to occur over time to create events for players to respond to? Or is it yet another script that has to be deconstructed to be of any use to a GM who wants to empower their players to create real change in the plots in which their characters are embroiled?

    \n I want to talk (seriously this time) about the question Ian didn't. Specifically:\n
    Posted By: Mike HolmesAre the PCs supposed to interact with Arthur or not? Do they merely hear about his exploits, or do they get involved? If the book has no notes on this, what does it imply? Rather, given the presentation of the data, what's enabled by the data?
    \nReally, the "Chaosium method" of presenting a fully realized plot, is no different from a dungeon crawl module with a full description of every room. The players' decisions determine what parts of it they see next. Is there some expectation that the players will enter every room or interact with every part of the plot? No, certainly not, and this is extremely obvious. In fact, the worse-designed Call of Cthulhu scenarios clunk and shudder and clunk to a halt if players either refuse to go through a chokepoint or, worse, fail a roll that would lead them to one.

    What the data enable is a highly detailed version of certain events and people when they occur or enter the game. The "they have stats so we can kill them" meme was tired in 1992 and dead shortly thereafter. AD&D put a stake in its heart with modules that you simply closed and put away if the PCs killed everyone they came across. Killing everyone with stats was game sabotage just as much as refusing to go into the dungeon. Call of Cthulhu similarly statted out allies, contacts, and so on, so that is especially no longer an issue with "the Chaosium method".\n
    Posted By: Mike HolmesOr is it yet another script that has to be deconstructed to be of any use to a GM who wants to empower their players to create real change in the plots in which their characters are embroiled?
    \nThis is the real meat right here. If you don't believe, as I do, that you murder every author's idea no matter how faithful you try to be to it, it might be a matter of some importance how closely you adhere to the plot ("script" only really applies to a few avante-garde White Wolf modules from the 90s. By avante-garde, I mean shitty.) You may feel like your collaboration is fighting the plot, or ruining it, or going off the map, and that this is somehow "not supported." I consider that feeling loopy (of course nobody asked me!) At-table collaboration will always beat the designer, no matter how general the designer is, no matter how vague or "suggestion"-y the presentation is, and the collaboration will always be doing something that would make the designer stare in horrified disbelief.

    The super-detailed plot book is really not that different from the super-detailed setting book in that regard. Both can only be sensibly used as highly detailed targets for literary explosives planted by GMs and players. When you destroy them, the explosions are much more shrapnel-filled. (Liking or disliking shrapnel is a matter of taste, of course.) To say this is using them incorrectly is to assume that the only way to properly use them is to be an idiot.

    \n So here's a thing, there is no one story of Arthuriana. Like, even Mallory doesn't tell one single sustained narrative. There are all these things that happen, that as a whole develop into something greater than the sum of the parts in La Morte. In the rest of the corpus as a whole its really very fragmented, moving across about 1200 years, 3ish continents, and a dozen languages.

    Do characters in Arthuriana interact with Arthur? Well, sometimes. Other times he's really just a figure in the background, and the focus of the story is upon the knight (usually a knight) and the adventures they have through the magical realms of Romance. Owain goes and saves a lion, fights a black knight, marries a lady, proves he's a worthy husband, finally defeats the black knight, and returns married to Camelot to be titled "The Knight of the Lion." Tristan and Isolde are sometimes part of the Arthur corpus, and sometimes not, so sometimes they interact with Arthur, but mostly they interact with Mark -- their liege lord. Gawain gets heavily involved in the fall of Camelot, with Lancelot and the love affair and all that, but most of the time he's actually haring off about the country and the majority of his adventures involve fighting giants, fucking damsels, and being a generally human guying trying really hard (and sometimes succeeding) at being a good knight.

    Pendragon campaigns tend to follow a similar pattern. There is usually an initial period where the characters will interact with a couple of "names" from the early show -- a little Uther here and there, a little bit of Badon Hill where the PCs may die or may shift the course of the battle a little. Then after that there is a long period where the PCs are doing their own shit in their own world. Arthur is over there, and he's awesome and so say all of us, but the things the PCs are doing are like the adventures that Gawain and Owain and all the other **ains have. They romp about going to tournaments, fighting giants, getting screwed, or screwed over by faerie, and generally proving what kind of fellows they are by repeatedly being brought up against their traits and passions. Over time you get to know them in the manner you get to know most of the knights of Arthuriana -- by watching how they deal with situations over time. You then get to know their children, and watch the whole thing go generational as the first sets of characters die off, get old, retire or go to Camelot to be big shots and generally slip out of the game.

    By the time you get towards the end of the campaign, the second and third generation PCs are usually pretty bad ass. They then get involved with the last glory of Camelot, and in my experience at this stage do get directly involved with a lot of the "names" -- even more so than at the start of the game. Its also in this part of the game that the PCs influence can become telling upon what happens. The way the game tends to be set, its unlikely that the PCs will change the major events of the world -- Guinevere is still probably going to get caught in adultery, the war is still going to happen, Arthur is going to bite it.

    Now, I have seen games in which the players have managed to change some of these things. (Camelot always falls, but the details differ.) But even in the games where they don't, because of the structure of the game where the characters have long been involved in their own quests and their own lives, coming back to the "names" feels less like a "you will now watch their story" and more like a "oh shit, the whole world is falling apart, how does it effect me and my family, the generations of people we've watched for the past two years of play." It often happens in play that the characters manor being razed by Jutes is a more central moment of play than the Wicked Day.

    There is railroad and "watch the big story" potential in Pendragon. (Then, there often is in many sim games from pre-generated source material.) However, it mostly only happens when the group wants to watch the Big Named Knights do their thing. If they don't, if they want to tell the stories of their own knights, then they will. And suggesting that they cannot because they can't change Arthur's story is really odd to me. First, sometimes they can. Second, is carry railroaded if the players can't bring peace between American and the VietCong and make Vietnam the 51st state? So long as the world is the world and the characters story is the story, having the big names in the background is just setting, not plot.

    Now, as for the GPC itself, it does have moments where you can see rails. However, in the context of the kind of play that lot of people like, they are perfectly fitting rails. (And, as JD says, if you don't like them then blowing them up will be fun.) But the vast majority of the book is not "and then you watch Arthur say to Gawain, "Did you fight a green knight?"" The majority of the book is "so Arthur is a good king, and Camelot is really nice, now in your lands the faerie knight has kidnapped the woman you love, even though she may not have loved you and may have actually gone with him willingly, so are you going to go and save her from a life of wonder and ease in faerie, or are you going to crush the Saxon peasants who keep raiding your crops, oh, and what about your uncle who wants you to introduce your good-for-nothing cousin to the Bishop in order to get him a cushy job on the curia....?"

    Really, its a giant book full of situations, color, potential quests, lots of NPCs, and a huge amount of potential for players to make meaningful choices in the lives and stories of their characters. It doesn't do it by letting them retell Arthur's story, so much, it lets it do it by them telling the story of their knights in the rise and fall of the background.

    And yea, it certainly isn't for everyone. But there is a huge space between "this is railroaded" and "this is a totally protagonist driven narrative in which everything in the world is instantly responsive to the PCs", and talking about it like a simple binary isn't going to do this book justice.

    \n I think that having a prewritten plot is VERY different from having a dungeon description.

    It's the difference between "that house is red" and "you have to go to that house and knock on the door, or the adventure is finished!!"

    Said this, I see a big difference between a railroaded "adventure" and The Big Campaign, too. The difference is that everybody ALREADY KNOW at least the bigger points of the plot.

    So, you don't need to railroad the players to avoid them killing Arthur and taking excalibur. They already know that they shouldn't. They wanted to play that game to play in Arthur's legends, so they would not want to ruin that. This way,the big, pre-written legend become a limitation of the game premise (like the fact that you can't attack the master at the beginning of MLWM), and there's no need of a railroad. (it's still a very simulative way of playing, to "recreate" an already-know story, finding a niche for your characters in the sidelines, so it will not be everyone's cup of tea, but it's not railroading)

    \n Even with the threat of digression, I thought I'd not talk about what the book isn't, but instead mention how I think it would be better in the respects I alluded to in my previous posting. Something a bit more constructive to put forward a newer model for thinking about Big Campaign Books. I'm taking this direction because it was said that you had to do the work of integrating the characters and players into the campaign anyway, which is something I only partly agree with.

    So, instead of just providing a timeline, descriptions of significant events without huge player impact and various generic plot hooks, I propose something a little bit different. I think the book should have a section on integrating the characters in just the way I mentioned - in really investing them in the story. I don't mean half-hearted GMing advice either, but more concrete options players and GMs can choose to hardwire their characters into the situations.

    For example, maybe you have a set of Plot Points which you can spend, primarily at character generation, but also as the campaign rolls on. There's a list of different options you can buy with them, including things like "illegitimate child of Arthur." They could be from a wide variety of power/influence levels, so there could be ones where you basically only get a magic sword and ones where you actually buy the opportunity to be Lancelot to Arthur and Guinevere, essentially taking his part by being the one who has a secret relationship with her. As you can see, some of these possibly have greater impact on the storyline. For example, if you are playing the knight who is having a secret affair with Guinever, things could play very differently towards the end of the campaign.

    Now, there is a next, crucial step. The book would, instead of offering the typical generic plot hooks tied simply to the timeline, address these issues specifically. So, the issues that come up would each be associated with the different choices that the players might have taken. So, for example, the character with the magic sword might learn that it was originally stolen by his father and that there are still people who want it back. That's simple enough, but it gets more complex and should take inspiration from bangs. So, there's this woman who's telling you that you are Arthur's illegitimate son. What do you do? More bangs which tie you into the politics and prompt you to do something. Likewise for the knight playing the Lancelot role. Instead of just running through a retelling of the myth in railroaded fashion, certain key junctures would be converted into bangs that would really bring this story into the gaming table.

    In this way, instead of a book full of generated content, you would have a book full of content generators.

    Interestingly, a book written like that would have a very different flavor in its text. Even though most GMs customize and know how to work with things, Mike's point about the textual assumptions are interesting and in my experience realistic. While only a GM doing an extremely strict reading would go that route, the same can be said about the text in many places in the Great Pendragon Campaign.

This is especially because even after the specific criticisms I've leveled at the product, I still consider it useful and very well done. However, because of my concerns, I end up thinking about it much more as a reference book, than a campaign book. So, that said, the book is grand, but my beef is with the overall state and prevalent assumptions/standards of practice of published scenario/campaign writing in the role-playing industry.
