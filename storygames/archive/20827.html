    What makes the appealing or satisfying when the "heroes" lose? How did you design the game so that either outcome can feel like a satisfying ending? (It's a design problem I've thought about quite a bit, so I'm very curious to hear your thoughts.)
    \nGreat question, Paul. I'm planning to talk more about specific mechanics in posts during the Kickstarter, but one of the things that Follow does is mechanically distinguish between character desires and player desires.

    When we get to the end of a challenge, we put in stones to influence the outcome based on whether our character is happy or unhappy with the path the fellowship has taken (or has flat-out rejected the quest), but then as a separate step we put in stones based on what we *as players* think makes sense as an outcome. So your character might be totally gung-ho about overthrowing the tyrant, but as a player you look at it and think "oh there is no way what we did would work. We really should fail."

    I think that by encouraging that distinction mechanically, it lets us role-play our characters and be enthusiastic about their stories, but also reminds us that we're authors, creating a larger story as well. It sets the stage for us to appreciate and even encourage failure without feeling it's *us* failing.

    There are also points in the game where the mechanics may force us to lose characters from the fellowship, which again shifts our point-of-view re failure. But of course players are never eliminated -- you always play a character in the fellowship, even if your first main character is lost.\n
    Yeah...I think Ben would be the one to answer this one...I can't decode his secrets\u2026maybe he sold his soul to the devil?
