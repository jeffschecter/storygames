      \n Yes, that disturbed me more. I remember being terrified of Bloody Mary in the mirror when I was a kid.

      When I saw it I interpretted it as Bloody Mary - the corrupted version of Mary Mother of God - drowning baby Jesus in the public restroom sink. Which is even more disturbing to me. Did my imagination carry me too far?

      No offense, but if there is a revision of the book I hope these two get left out (to be replaced by new, awesome Jennifer Rodgers originals). There's abosultely nothing in the game text that evokes the atmosphere of those two works of art. I understand that the game was originaly a horror game and changed to a more generic fantasy game. I think it could have very broad appeal if the game reflected that and the rules were fixed/clarified - I would want to see it held back by an issue that isn't critical to the game itself.

      I'll step up and say that despite the fact that I love the art, I'd have a hard time giving an unqualified recommendation of the book to a friend because of the art. And it's not a "prude" or unaccepting position I'm worried about, a lot of people might not want to see that in a roleplaying book or want to worry about leaving a roleplaying book where their kids could see it - uncomfortable questions and horrible nightmares could ensue.

      I want to be clear; I don't regret my purchase at all, I love all of Jennifer's art, and I want to see the game do well. But I also think the warning in KODT was appropriate.

      \n The funny thing is, Negotiation happens in many traditional-style groups anyway; it sure did in the AD&D 2e games I've been in. "Here's the situation. What's your plan? Oh, well, there's this complicating stuff. What now?" The difference here is one of formalizing it and making it streamlined (to take minutes instead of hours of planning) and purposeful, with an added "selective fun function" (figuring out whether the challenge is worth going to crunchy mechanics for or not).
