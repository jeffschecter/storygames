    I'm not going to nuke Mendel here or burn him or anything, but this is interesting:
    Rhetorically, asking for an example is a request of clarification. But asking for actual play is an attack, an accusation that the play the theory references is non-actual, illusory and unreal, and by extension unlike the speaker's real play. And because of the impossibility in experiencing actual play that is not your own, it is a rhetorical attack that can never be fully met. No one can prove the legitimacy of their own play.
    Yeah, the policy line of Story Games (a site I built to basically take theory and craft and push it through play) is officially "This Above Paragraph is BS". You don't need to legitimize how you say your play went down (no one's gonna go: "I'm sure it didn't happen in the way that you described; try again"); but play has to go down, and Actual Play is the way to share it. Doesn't have to be the "4 pages of text graphic blow-by-blow", it can be a sentence or paragraph describing how something happened.

    I have smelled intellectual bullying in RPG theory, just as I have in philosophy (actually, my Master's thesis in Philosophy was how Socratic Method and dissertation/paper grilling by peers was a social form of hazing/bullying). I haven't seen anyone ever be an "Actual Play bully", doing this:

    "Well, do you have any Actual Play to back up your wild claims?"

    But I HAVE seen people taken to task for having their heads above the clouds, arguing for the sake of discussion, in a shifting, illusory realm that no longer has anything to do with nuts-and-bolts gaming on any level; Using Actual Play as a rallying cry to keep this hazy illusory junk tied to something that could actually be observed or used. But heck, I'm sure SOMEONE's used "requesting actual play" as a club to put down a theory in an intellectual bullying sense. People do creative, bizarre shit to make a point sometimes.
    The impossibility of actual play highlights the danger of depending solely on examples. Because no one can relate the full experience of their own play, no amount of examples, no quantity of minute details can be as real as actual play is said to be.
    I just got a telegram from 1600: It's Descartes, and he wants his "Mind Body Problem" back.

    You don't need video cameras and impartial judges to assertain "John was really into his character that night after he got to do that thing he wanted."

    So yeah, the whole argument is pretty much rooted on the supposition that when actually providing Actual Play examples, that someone comes along and questions the legitimacy of what you described. Probably with a "Oh yeah? I bet it didn't happen that way." or "Your puny detailed 5-page play report does not satisfy the general claim you made". I've Never seen that happen.

    But oh well, as the title says, it's just an "Editorial" on an otherwise solid link aggregator. I've read plenty of papers with solid content that had editorials that I didn't agree with. Like on Salon.com, where there was an editorial by a Democrat who basically wrote an article about why all Democrats want Hillary Clinton to run for President. I didn't stop reading Salon or anything, but I did stop reading through all the editorials. :-)

    -Andy
