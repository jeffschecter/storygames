\n Lately I've been reading the Encyclopedia of Fantasy, a big thick Tome of Wisdom at my local library, which had an interesting definition of the Fantasy genre. Tolkien and C.S. Lewis's definition, in fact.

In essence -

1: Something is terribly wrong with a universe that can't be our universe.

2: Someone trapped in the wrongness notices.

3: They heal themselves the world by reciting a powerful story - often by becoming the main character of that story.

Fr'ex, say that it's always winter in Narnia - something is fundamentally wrong with Narnia.

The Pevensies are trapped in the winter of Narnia, especially Edmund (that traitor).

Only by becoming the main characters in the tale about the four human kings and queens can they break the spell on Narnia.

Discuss?

    So that list, it is a good one for identifying some fantasy and what it is about that story that makes it fantastic. But they sure aren't the only things that do this.

    \n You might want to go away and read Tree and Leaf which contains Tolkien's poetics for fantasy (sorry books are in boxes from recent move, so this is from memory). As a poetics it does seem to inherit from works like Aristotle's 'On Tragedy'; Tolkien's trying to identify what makes a fantasy work rise into the realm of art as opposed to entertainment. Tolkien also explicitly rejects the poetics of drama as being suitable for fantasy.

    First the art of fantasy for Tolkien lies in world-building, suspending disbelief to construct a believable world in which humans interact with mythological or fantastical creatures.

    Second the power of fantasy lies in: recovery ,holding a distorted mirror up to nature so that we might more clearly see our own world in an unfamiliar setting, escape Tolkien sees the modern industrial world as destroying souls, and consolation to the reader through a happy ending.


    Like all poetics this says as much about Tolkien's values as anything else, and the experience he had with modernity. However I suspect that most of us might agree with world-building as being the art, and at least with recovery as being a disinguisher of 'literary' SF&F.

    \n Perhaps we could say "heroic fantasy" (if that doesn't already mean something else - perhaps it does) for the kind defined in the OP. Basically, the heroes are heroes because they have noticed that something is wrong and are trying to fix/heal it, not just because the story follows them around.     And while a Big Bad/Dark Lord is definitely an easy "something is wrong", it certainly isn't the only one. I don't actually find it that interesting any more, myself.

    The Hobbit would then be heroic fantasy (or whatever) because:

    - Something is wrong: Smaug has taken over the dwarves' home and treasure.
    - Someone notices: The dwarves, also Gandalf.

    Actually now that I think of it, Smaug is about as big a bad as you can get, physically speaking. But he's not the usual Dark Lord trying to conquer the world.

    Science fiction was once called "science fantasy", a term that applied to Jules Verne's work. It doesn't at all follow this arc you describe. So I suspect you're talking about fantasy of a particular lineage coming from a particular, post-D&D period, yeah?

    \n

    When I think of "fantasy" in the heroic medieval European mold, the core orbits around Beowulf, King Arthur, and the Song of Roland. These are stories of earth-shaking events that center around individuals and their astounding abilities, including their abilities as leaders (an artifact of a social system that required stories to be written of leaders, no doubt). More distant orbits might be the Enuma Elish, Torah, and Upanishads; The Journey West; Hamlet, Lear, et al.; the corpuses of Oz, Narnia, Imajica, and Middle Earth.

    \n

    Outside of that, I'm not too aware, but I suggest that definition will not lead to further understanding and, more importantly, will not lead to interesting new stories. Analysis of these stories very well could, but careful definition and adherence to that definition seems like it will only lead to reproduction and simulation, not creation.

    \n

    I mean, when you sit down to write a story, does saying it's a "fantasy" story help you say what you want to say? How does this definition help you to create?

    \n I think you have to view Tolkien and Lewis' definition as biased, that is, it expresses the type of fantasy they, personally, were interested in exploring. But in all fairness, I think the "definition" of fantasy has changed over the last 50 years or so.

    I think anything that has medieval or "magical" elements nowadays would be considered fantasy, but I'm not sure it was always that way in the past. It is my understand that Howard and other Sword & Sorcery authors didn't consider their work to be "fantasy", but rather surreal horror. I also suspect, but have no proof, that the rise of SciFi has caused a reaction to what we consider "fantasy".

    I want to think that there's some kind of tie-in to "myth", in fantasy, that's important. I think the OP definition points to this, with the whole "reciting a story" bit. But the rest of the definition seems to imply a certain arc, which seems kinda restrictive.
