\n OK, maybe this is a weird one, maybe I'm missing something vital by not knowing anything much about the D20 market.

The Fantasy Heartbreaker was a game created by it's author in an attempted to "fix" a perceived problem with D&D. Generally these games succeed in creating one or two awesome nugget of mechanics buried in an uninspiring system which is basically a tweaked D&D, often without the serial numbers even filed off.

Since the D20/OGL license appeared, how has this changed things? Are there now no fantasy heartbreakers? Do people now just release a D20 game with their house rules inserted and the rest just says "See page XX of the PHB"? Is there a new class of D20 heartbreaker, or does the fact that these games really are just attempts to mould D&D into something the author thinks is better than vanilla D&D take them out of the heartbreaker category altogether?

Or do people just still publish the same type of heartbreakers they always did?

    Posted By: Rich StokesSince the D20/OGL license appeared, how has this changed things?
    \nA lot.

    The Fantasy Heartbreaker was mostly a product of the last 80's and early-mid 90s. I've seen VERY few products that could be truly called "heartbreakers" (that is "remaking D&D with a few houserules, but 1-2 interesting things, and then taking out a loan against one's house to sell it) since d20. Perhaps even just one. I won't count "IMAGINE RPG" (I can't find the link to the website. Anyone?), which is probably the epitome of Fantasy Heartbreaker in the extent that it looks exactly like AD&D with like 3 houserules, and an interesting armor mechanic.

    Since d20, I can only guess that the reason we haven't seen as many heartbreakers is that now people had the tools to create their own d20/D&D mods and sell them, something they couldn't do under the days of "T$R" (and its aggressive stance against people posting houserules on the internet).

    I haven't seen a Fantasy Heartbreaker, something that didn't say "d20" or "OGL" on it and yet tried to mimic D&D, since. There's been a few generic fantasy games (Epic RPG, others) but each of those were unique enough that they were not really at all like D&D rulewise.\n
    Posted By: Rich StokesDo people now just release a D20 game with their house rules inserted and the rest just says "See page XX of the PHB"?
    \nYep. And they're not taking out loans against their house to do so. This is an awesome thing.\n
    Is there a new class of D20 heartbreaker, or does the fact that these games reallyarejust attempts to mould D&D into something the author thinks is better than vanilla D&D take them out of the heartbreaker category altogether?
    \nAbsolutely not. It's the creation of supplements for one system. There is no breaking of hearts, no "recreating the system from the ground up to sell as one's own without knowing what else is out there". It's utilizing an existing, strong system, plus one's creativity, to make supplements for an existing game.

    There are still fantasy heartbreakers out there, but not many d20-derived ones.

    \n The OGL has made it easier for people to publish their house systems and settings. This smacks of heartbreakery, even if the classic definition doesn't encompass the phenomenon.
    It still seems to be a case of people coming to think "My group thinks D&D isn't X enough, and plus, there's way too much Y! Considering how much they love the clever adjustments I've made of removing elves and making a cooler set of magic classes, and how cool our campaign world has grown, maybe..."

    I tend to judge heartbreaker status not by a fixed definition, but by aesthetic appeal to me personally. Which I find worrisome.

    \n Aspects of "Heartbreaker" I'm hearing called out:

    - System comes largely from an existing, commercially successful game. Especially D&D.
    - Game has 1-3 cool new ideas, but they are not the focus of the game.
    - Game includes popular design elements that do not work with what the game is trying to accomplish.
    - First published with a large print run and high production values despite lack of proven market.

    So I'm reading ARGNet, a news site about alternate reality games (you know, those things that are like I Love Bees - essentially LARPs that are so large-scale they practically can't happen without corporate sponsorship (that's not really true but aw, you know the ones)), and they're reporting on this game that had some trouble but looked really ambitious and promising: Ethan Haas Was Right is the name of the trailhead. It drew a following when some folks got it into their heads that it was promoting this interesting new movie project that JJ Abrams is being all secrety about, but it isn't connected to that at all. No, this would-be ARG is promoting...

    ... a tabletop roleplaying game...

    ... that isn't out yet...

    ... whose website looks like this.

    Holy shit it's the fantasy heartbreaker of the future.

    Where are they getting all that money from? Mind-boggling. Ron's gonna have to give up on that "mulch" punchline he takes so much relish in; the heartbreakers of the future will end up as thin air.


    I ... guess this doesn't have much to do with the thread, sorry. Except that, yeah, heartbreakyness doesn't really have that much to do with system. If anything really ended the heartbreak, it was PDF.
