    \n I've written about an adventure writing model called the fish tank. It takes about 30-45 minutes for me to write an intrigue with enough material for 1-3 sessions.

    There are a lot of games that have something similar, like Trail of Cthulhu, Spirit of the Century, Unknown Armies, Dogs in the Vineyard, Psychodrame, Svart av kval - vit av lust, and Noir.

    You can see the fish tank in almost every HBO series (Rome, Deadwood, True Blood), but an example of the simplest one is Kurosawa's Yojimbo (alt. A Fistful of Dollars, or Last Man Standing).

    \n You might also enjoy this thread - a "Game of Thrones" Apocalypse World hack, using Strings from Monsterhearts.

    A World of Ice and Fire

    In terms of what I would like to see?

    I think something like the Strings system in Monsterhearts has great potential to create intrigue, but all the moves available to characters in Monsterhearts are far too direct for proper intrigue. It would be interesting, I think, to create a game where most of the moves available to you (or, at least, the ones you want to be using!) need you to act indirectly - influence allies or enemies, create indirect pressures, and so forth. You can sway a person to support Alice instead of Geoff, but you can't confront Geoff directly, for whatever reason.

    After all, I think intrigue requires three things:

    1. A relationship web of multiple parties and factions who cannot simply go separate ways. They are, in some way, interdependent.

    2. A need for secrecy - your motives cannot be made public for some reason. For instance, a powerful overseer or power (the King, in many medieval intrigue stories) does not wish any individual below him to gain too much power.

    3. That indirect methods are more effective or less risky than direct methods.

    Intrigue generally exists in complex social situations where direct solutions are not allowed or frowned upon, forcing people to act "behind the scenes".

    For example, being a relatively physically "weak" individual or faction and trying to enact change against stronger factions or individuals may force you to seek indirect means.

    This could be a matter of playing women in a primitive society where "might makes right", but only men are allowed to bear arms, for instance. Create an interdependent group of people and then give some people strong aims they cannot immediately achieve, and you will quite naturally give birth to some intrigue.

    The hardest part about handling intrigue in an RPG, I think, is figuring out how to handle all the "behind-the-scenes" elements. We can't have everything happening "on-screen"... so how do we keep track of, and then resolve, all of these elements?

    \n So what does Littlefinger do to get what he wants?

    *****SPOILERS*****

    He finds a weak/crazy lady in a powerful noble house in Lysa Arryn, and engages in a lengthy seduction until she wants nothing more than him. He can now leverage that desire, combined with her irrationality, to get her to do things, while giving only the flimsiest justifications for why.

    Not that his justifications aren't delivered with aplomb! Tantalizing promises, hinted threats, pledges of love, claims of forces outside his control, and lots of personal charm.

    Wanting to be near the seat of power, but not having any highborn excuses to do so, Baelish looks for a place where the highborn cross paths with the lowborn, and seizes upon a brothel as the perfect opportunity. Here he will get some personal info, and maybe some personal dirt, on the leaders of the realm. His pitch to them is absolute circumspection, never letting on that keeping their secrets is itself a form of power.

    Having some genuine skill at accounting, and at identifying opportunities for profit, Baelish does very well for himself as brothel-master. When the realm's position of Master of Coin becomes open, he presents as a logical candidate:
    - makes huge money in his own business
    - vouched for by Lysa Arryn and by all the local nobles whose sexual secrets he's kept
    - diplomatic speaker, should be able to negotiate with various factions
    - not allied with any particular major faction who the crown wouldn't want to give power to, won't create appearance of favoritism or power consolidation
    - seems deferential and should obey the crown's orders

    Once you're Master of Coin, you're in pretty good shape to make powerful allies and lackeys. You control who gets taxed and who gets subsidized! Anticipating that a power struggle may be decided by the local security forces, Baelish makes sure to pay them well and get on their good side.

    Baelish invests his increased wealth and power in an information network. Eventually, he's as well-informed as anyone in the game.

    Aware that Robert Baratheon is a crappy administrator who relies on his Hand, John Arryn, to keep the kingdom intact... and aware that Arryn is on track to expose the king's "son" as a bastard, and his wife as a traitor, thus paving the way for a simple end to that matter... Baelish seizes the opportunity to destabilize the realm and to keep an illegitimate heir on track for the throne (which will aid future destabilization when the truth emerges in some fashion) by removing John Arryn, which he does by calling in his Lysa Arryn asset.

    Having told Lysa that he would now be hers, Baelish now has to keep her pacified with regular contact and excuses why he hasn't joined her yet. He continues the manipulation expertly.

    When the chaos eventually erupts, Baelish uses his sway over the security forces to determine the winner of the first clash, putting an accused-but-not-proven bastard on the throne to be challenged by the king's brothers and other claimants. The Lannisters appreciate Baelish taking their side, and award him a promotion to John Arryn's vacant position as Warden of the East.

    *****END OF SPOILERS*****

    Looks to me like the relevant personal skills are:
    - conceiving possible opportunities and hunting for them
    - recognizing opportunities that come your way
    - charm
    - seduction, manipulation, lying
    - diplomacy, negotiation
    - reading people
    - organization and management - keeping up many simultaneous ventures
    - accounting
    - entrepreneurship
    - memory (for all that info he collects)

    That "spotting opportunities" stuff can be further specified as spotting:
    - the winning side
    - weak people (gullible, lonely, slightly insane, etc.)
    - fragile systems
    - weak points in systems or relationships
    - useful people to pursue allegiance with
    - access to that which is otherwise out of reach
    - vulnerabilities or gaps in that which is otherwise protected
    - present or future openings in powerful positions
    - brewing crises
    - information sources
    - opportunities to create leverage
    - ways to apply leverage which will work

    Some of that may be redundant with "reading people".

    Food for systems thought, hopefully!

    \n Good summary, Dave.

    Perhaps something fairly formulaic could be assembled out of that:

    * Identify or Create a Destabilized Situation (You'll want to use your skills to pit people likely to work together against each other)

    * Create a Sense of Trust, Love, Dependency or Obligation in a Target

    * Leverage Existing Trust/L/D/O in a Target to Get Them to Do Something They Normally Wouldn't

    It's nice if the last step isn't deterministic: it's a gamble. You're betting that you've established enough leverage over a person that they won't see through what you're doing. This is where dice would be best engaged, I feel. (Or some other uncertain process, like player votes.)

    They might:

    * Immediately see what you're doing and turn against you
    * Immediately see what you're doing, but not say anything, go along with you anyway (as a trick)
    * Immediately see what you're doing, but not say anything, go along with you anyway (because they can see no way out of this situation, they feel trapped)
    * Turn down your request, but still feel favorably towards you ("I wish I could help!")
    * Happily try to help you

    \n In the best Intrigue campaign I've played, with Burning Wheel, we had an artifact which helped immensely: a whiteboard with a relationship map. All the PCs and major NPCs were on it, color coded and clustered according to their approximate spheres, with the standard lines-plus-words between circled names to indicate a relationship of some sort. So in the upper left to middle were all the nobility in blue, in the bottom left were the priesthood in red, and the nobles who were more tightly connected to religion were in the bottom left of the Noble area. When things changed we updated the map. This helped keep track, remind us when we e.g. hadn't played with the mages for a while, served as a nice record of some statuses like when someone died they stayed on the map but got little X's for eyes.

    It's Burning Wheel, so there were few player-level secrets. Not "you don't know the Prince's agenda at all" instead "prince wants to get out of arranged marriage and is currently lying that it's peachy to not upset the wealthy potential uncle-in-law, what do you do?" Dunno how well a giant public relationship map would work in a player-secret-intrigue game.

    \n I think Paul_T's analysis is spot on.

    Further to that line of thinking, it's worth asking what we commonly enjoy about intrigue stories and what, therefore, we want to get out of an intrigue game. What are the emotional rewards of intrigue?

    - empowerment fantasy: castrated (literally or metaphorically) characters using their brains to overcome humungous odds. David vs a whole society of Goliaths.

    - excitement: in a royal court/presidental office/whatever, family squabbles and work relationships like the ones we all live out every day have epic consequences, a ripple-out effect on the lives of millions.

    - amorality: it's tiring worrying about being a good person all the time, so it's relaxing to indulge in characters who give zero shits about this (this one might be just me?)

    - secret sharing: people like being next to power looking out, rather than outside looking in as we normally are in real life. that's why Underwood addresses the audience directly.

    - sex: it's unusual for a intrigue story not to involve a bit of seduction. i suppose it's not inherent to intrigue itself, but we might as well embrace it.

    What others? I'm wiped for now.

    \n@empowermint,

    That's a good list! An interesting take on this.

    I would add that one of the interests of intrigue which is missing from your list (and, in particular, the one that is generally not addressed by RPGs) is the vicarious challenge and thrill of participating in the intrigue itself. Bluffing, lying, relying on people's honesty, seeing through someone's deceptions, and so forth.

    This is hard to do between *characters*, but can be done between *players*. (e.g. the board game Diplomacy, or any bluffing game, even something like Poker) However, it tends to distract from the story/roleplaying aspects of the game.

    If there are particularly good ways to make this happen between *characters*? That would be a challenging but potentially very fruitful discussion.
