These Dice in the Stuff to Watch (Nov) thread inspired me to think of a simple storytelling system:

* You, as a PC, have three Modus Operandi (MOs):
-- Resolve, symbolized by Rock, which measures your ability to "grin and bear it" or otherwise bull through a situation.
-- Manipulation, symbolized by Scissors, which measures you ability to change a situation into something more amenable to you.
-- Flexibility, symbolized by Paper, which measures your ability to find alternate ways to approach and deal with a situation.

* Each MO has a rating, from 1 to X, determined by dividing Y dice amongst the three MOs during character creation. [1]

* In a given situation, you roll a number of Roshambo dice equal to the MO that you are using [2] and then apportion results:
-- For each die result that would beat the MO in use in Roshambo (Failure dice), you pass the die (and its result) to another player. [3]
-- For each die result that matches the MO in use (Success dice), you may roll another, bonus die. If its result would beat your MO, ignore it [4].
-- For each irrelevant die result (Neutral), pass it to another player of your choice, who may do one of three things:
*** Discard the die to introduce their own character into the scene, state his or her goal, and roll the appropriate MO. Repeat the above die apportioning steps for THIS new character in the scene. Note: Each MO may only be invoked--by ANY character--only once per scene; so if you started with Resolve, the next player may only introduce him- or herself and state goals using Manipulation or Flexibility (and the following player in the scene may only invoke the remaining MO). As such, a passed Neutral die to NOT be usable for self-introduction after the third character introduction into a scene.
*** Turn the die to a face which would be a Success for the character from whom this new player received the die. Use it to narrate a way past a Complication for you, taking into account the die result, on his or her turn--proceed to narration below.
*** Turn the die to a face which would be a Failure for the character from whom this new player received the die. Use it to narrate a Complication against you, taking into account the die result, on his or her turn--proceed to narration below.

* Narrate situation resolution:
a) Narrate one of the die results still in your possession, taking into account the MO to which its symbol relates.
b) Then, in order around the table, each player to whom you had to give a die result narrates a Complication (or a counter to a Complication if using a Neutral die result as a Success die, or the arrival of their own character) as a result of previous narration.
b.1) If you have a remaining die, narrate how that die result's MO would counter the unresolved Complication.
b.2) If you have no remaining die then the Complication thwart(s) the initial course of action with which you began the scene (your stakes)--narrate how the Complication brings your character low and his goal to ruin.
c) Repeat Step B until the turn order gets back to you or until you are thwarted (B.2). When the turn order gets back to you, go to Step A, unless:
c.1) If you are narrating a die result and there are no uncountered Complications then narrate how the MO of that Success die (the associated action, feeling, reaction, or conviction) brings about your goal in the situation (your stake).

Footnotes
1) Scaling of effect TBD.
2) Scene framing and driving TBD. Who can choose the relevant MO TBD.
3) Or GM? TBD. No GM needed, now.
4) Or DON'T ignore bonus die results that beat your MO?

Thoughts, anyone? I think this could be a fun way to complexify something like Pass the Stick. The most-obvious issues are noted in the Footnotes, but I welcome further ideas or any problems that you notice off the bat. Single-sided stakes are a possible problem (only the current spolight player really has stakes; everyone else just tries to thwart, if given a die). Maybe it needs to get away from the My Guy approach, taking on more of a "single PC game" notion or a "shared PCs game" like Universalis?

Anyhow... I think it's a sweet idea, and I want to see what folks run with from that kernel above!

Updated based on Ralph's killer idea for bringing in other players and making a sort of Prisoner's Dilemma.

    \n OK, I'm thinking hard... and sort of getting lost. I'm reposting the (fomer) state:

    * You, as a PC, have three Modus Operandi (MOs):
    -- Resolve, symbolized by Rock, which measures your ability to "grin and bear it" or otherwise bull through a situation.
    -- Manipulation, symbolized by Scissors, which measures you ability to change a situation into something more amenable to you.
    -- Flexibility, symbolized by Paper, which measures your ability to find alternate ways to approach and deal with a situation.

    * Each MO has a rating, from 1 to 5, determined by dividing 9 dice amongst the three MOs during character creation.

    * In a given situation [1], you roll a number of Roshambo dice equal to the MO that you are using and then apportion results:
    -- For each die result that matches the MO in use (Success dice), you may roll another, bonus die which is treated like any other die roll (Failure, Success, or Neutral).
    -- For each die result that would beat the MO in use in Roshambo (Failure die), distribute them evenly amongst other players (your choice who, if not evenly divisible) and that player must use it to narrate a Complication on his or her turn (see below).
    -- For each irrelevant die result (Neutral), distribute them evenly amongst other players (your choice who, if not evenly divisible) and each of those players may do one of three things on his or her turn:
    *** Discard the die to introduce his or her own character into the scene, state his or her goal, and roll the appropriate MO. Repeat the above die apportioning steps for THIS new character in the scene. Note: Each MO may only be invoked--by ANY character--only once per scene; so if you started with Resolve, the next player may only introduce him- or herself and state goals using Manipulation or Flexibility (and the following player in the scene may only invoke the remaining MO). As such, a passed Neutral die is NOT usable for self-introduction after the third character introduction into a scene.
    *** Use it to narrate a way past an existing Complication on his or her turn, taking into account the die result (Resolve, Manipulation, or Flexibility)--proceed to narration below.
    *** Use it to narrate a Complication against you on his or her turn, taking into account the die result--proceed to narration below.

    * Narrate situation resolution:
    A) You narrate one of the Success die results still in your possession, taking into account the MO to which its symbol relates.

    Then, in order around the table, each player to whom you had to give a die does one of the following:
    B.1) Passes--usually for those with no dice or who want to narrate a Success but there's no Complication yet. A player that passes may interrupt another player later in the turn order, at any time prior to that player narrating.
    B.2) Narrates the arrival of his or her own character.
    B.3) Narrates a Complication, if using a Failure die or a Neutral die result as a Failure die.
    B.3.i) If you have a remaining die, immediately (i.e. out of turn order) narrate how that die result's MO would counter the unresolved Complication--narration then proceeds to the next person in turn order.
    B.3.ii) If you have no remaining die, turn order passes to the next person with a Neutral die. If that person narrates his or her Neutral die as a way to bypass the Complication, you are still succeeding in the conflict (getting your stake). If that person chooses not to narrate his or her Neutral die as a Success, he or she may only narrate the arrival of his or her own character (i.e. Failures don't stack).
    B.3.iii) If you have no remaining die and no one with a Neutral die intends to narrate a Success, then the Complication thwart(s) the initial course of action with which you began the scene (your stakes)--on your turn, narrate how the Complication brings your character low and his goal to ruin.

    D) Repeat Step B until the turn order gets back to you. When the turn order gets back to you, go to Step A, unless:
    D.1) If there are no uncountered Complications then narrate how the MO of that Success die (the associated action, feeling, reaction, or conviction) brings about your goal in the situation (your stake).

    Extended Example
    Amy, Bob, and Chad are playing. It's Amy's turn to frame a scene and attempt to gain a stake.
    Amy: "I'm back at the ranch and I want to convince my mother that Bob is the right man for me to marry--I'm going to use manipulation, tweaking my mother's heartstrings."
    Amy has a Manipulation (Scissors) of 4, so she rolls four dice: Rock, Scissors, Paper, Scissors--Failure, Neutral, Success, Neutral.
    Amy: "Damn, looks bad for me; and I'm not rolling the bonus die--too much already out there. OK, I come into the kitchen and fix Mom with a mournful look, saying simply, "Mama, if you wanted me to be happy--TRULY happy--you'd let me follow my heart and marry Bob." Amy discards her Success (Paper) result, then passes the Failure to Chad (as he's most likely to come up with a good Complication), and passes one each of the Neutral (Scissors) dice to Bob and Chad. It's not looking good for Amy, but Bob's turn is next.
    Bob: "Pass--I want to marry Amy, too!"
    Chad: "Alrighty, you love birds"--he puts forward the Failure (Rock, Resolve)--"your mom sets her jaw, scowls at you, and says 'You can marry that good-for-nothing party boy when I'm in my grave, but not sooner!'" Chad discards the Failure die; it's Amy's turn.
    Bob: "I interrupt"--he puts forward his Scissors--"I walk into the kitchen, having heard that last line from outside the screen door, while walking up to ask Amy to go on a stroll. I'm using my Resolve 3" Bob rolls: Scissors, Scissors, Rock. "Woot! Two Success and a Neutral--good luck, Chad! OK, I say, 'Ma'am, you know we'll just elope--do you want to lose your daughter or gain a son?' My agenda is the same as Amy's" He discards one of his Scissors, and passes the Neutral Rock to Amy. Chad sighs; it's now Amy's turn.
    Amy: "I'm using this as a Success for myself and I say, 'That's right, Mama. I'll leave you all alone, like Daddy left us years all those years ago!'" Because Bob interrupted, it's now Chad's turn (Amy's last Neutral).
    Chad: "Damn, that's cold. Fine then, your mom bursts out in tears, clutching at your sleeve and saying, 'So you WOULD put me in my grave, then, 'cause I'll die if you do that to me--I... I'll kill myself'" (Recall that he's narrating a Scissors as a Failure for Amy's stake.)
    Amy: "I'm out of dice and yielding the stake... for now. 'Now, now, Mama... enough crazy talk. We won't elope; let's just drop it for now, OK?'"
    Bob: "But I still have a Success, so *I* can win without a possible Complication. Hmmm... OK, I pick up Amy and sling her over my shoulder in a fireman's carry. 'Yep, ma'am, we won't elope... I'm just TAKIN' her down tot he courthouse now and an little old bird like you can't stop me!' I rush out the door, Amy alternately laughing and beating on my back in mock rage."
    And the scene ends there, with Bob succeeded in winning Amy's hand, though with a very pissed off and affronted new mother-in-law!

    \nRules Adjusted for Fixed Dice Numbers and (Some of) Ralph's Ideas
    I) You, as a PC, have three Modus Operandi (MOs):
    I.A) Resolve, symbolized by Rock, which measures your ability to "grin and bear it" or otherwise bull through a situation.
    I.B) Manipulation, symbolized by Scissors, which measures you ability to change a situation into something more amenable to you.
    I.C) Flexibility, symbolized by Paper, which measures your ability to find alternate ways to approach and deal with a situation.

    II) Each MO has a rating, from 1 to 5, determined by dividing 9 dice amongst the three MOs during character creation.

    III) When it's your turn to frame a scene, choose an MO that you will be using to achieve your goal in the scene and roll five Roshambo dice.
    III.A) Each die result that matches the MO in use is a Success die.
    III.B) Each die result that would beat the MO in use in Roshambo is a Failure die.
    III.C) Each irrelevant die result is a Neutral die.

    IV) You may keep as many Success dice as are equal to your MO's rating.
    IV.A) Distribute the remaining Success, Failure, and Neutral dice evenly--you choose who gets which and who gets any extras, if they are not evenly divisible.
    IV.B) Each Failure die must be used to narrate a Complication: something which opposes your goal.
    IV.C) Each Neutral die may be used in one of three ways, on another player's turn:
    IV.C.1) Discard the die to introduce one's own character into the scene, state one's own goal (which could be the same as yours or in opposition or orthogonal), and roll the appropriate MO... with one restriction: Each MO may be invoked--by any character--only once per scene; so if you started with Resolve, the next player may only introduce him- or herself and state goals using Manipulation or Flexibility (and the following player bringing a character into the scene may only invoke the remaining MO). As such, a passed Neutral die is not usable for self-introduction after the third character introduction into a scene. Finally, do the above die distributing Steps IV and IV.A for this new character in the scene.
    IV.C.2) Use it to narrate a way past an existing Complication, taking into account the MO of the die result (Resolve, Manipulation, or Flexibility) not the original player's MO.
    IV.C.3) Use it to narrate a Complication against you, taking into account the MO of the die result.

    V) Narrate the scene flow, using all dice in play.
    V.A) You narrate one of the Success die results still in your possession, taking into account the MO to which its symbol relates. This is the Opening Volley, which should establish your stakes and line of '"attack."
    V.B) Starting with the player to your left (i.e. clockwise), the first player with a Failure die (or a Neutral die that is being used as a Failure die) narrates a Complication.
    V.C) Continuing clockwise, if a player with a Neutral die wants to self-introduce or narrate it as a Success for you, he or she may now do so.
    V.D) Once a Complication is bypasses (with your narrated Success or another player's Neutral-cum-Success), the next player with a Failure die (or Neutral-cum-Failure) may narrate another Complication. Note that this means that Complications can't "stack."
    V.E) If your Successes (and other's Neutrals-cum-Successes) run out while there is an Complication still to be bypassed, then you have been Thwarted: narrate how the unresolved Complication brings you low and your plans to ruin. Otherwise, you succeed in your aims and may make a last narration of that result (even if you are actually out of Successes, having used the last one up to bypass the last Challenge).
    V.F) All remaining players whose characters are in the scene continue to use their dice (for or against each other) until they have resolved their stakes one way or the other. The cycles of this entire Step V can become convoluted in two- or three-way conflicts; just keep cycling clockwise, offering each player a chance to Complicate or bypass (or self-introduce) until the scene resolves itself (or the dice resolve it, by running out!)

    VI) Whenever your character is in a scene, you may Burn one point of an MO (reduce that MO by one) to narrate a Success for your agenda, taking into account the MO Burned. This reduction in MO does not affect the number of Successes that you may keep, because that distribution (Steps IV and IV.A) happens prior to any Complication and, thus, prior to any reason to Burn.
    VI.A) A point of Burned MO may be recovered by granting a free Failure die (i.e. not one of the five you roll) to another player in a future scene.
    VI.B) Your MO may never increase above its starting value [1].
    VI.C) You may redistribute your nine MO points at any time between chapters [2].

    Footnotes
    1) I do not see a need for an "advancement" system, for this gamelet.
    2) Chapter structure could be explicit or based on a resolution of something like Best Interests, or just by group consensus that the current characters are pretty much either content or pretty firmly fucked.

    \n If I follow your most recent key change correctly, I'm not sure I like it. It seems to remove some of the strategy of play discussed earlier and replace it with a more loosey goosey pass the conch approach...is that what you wanted?
    \n
    \nMy gut tells me you don't want the die to be useable for anything once its given away. If you do that, then all success and failure will be decided by player whim and the dice are reduced to just an over complicated ballot system for player voting.
    \n
    \nIf the symbols are restricted to what they can be used for, then options are creatively constrained. If you have a Rock MO and give me a Rock die...I can only use that die to help you. I could use it to introduce my own character's complication if one is available, to hurt someone else's scissor complication, or either way against someone else's paper complication, but I can't use it to hurt you. I think that's a very important constraint and losing it would diminish the effectiveness of the system.
    \n
    \nYou'll definitely want to playtest having to keep your success dice vs. being able to keep any combination of dice you like. I think they'd play very differently from each other, but I'm not sure one is necessarily "better". Keeping successes first is a plus in the "character advocacy" column. But being able to choose any combination is a plus in the "gamesmanship" category (you might choose to give away successful rocks because you know they won't be useable to help a scissor player, for instance. Plus, narratively, sometimes being able to narrate the obstacle you face is more important than narrating how you overcome that obstacle. Either way could work well.
    \n
    \nIn III.B. do you mean to state that you can ONLY keep successes? Even if keeping successes first winds up being the way to go, I think keeping only successes wouldn't work well. It means that you'll often be keeping fewer dice than your Trait allows...which will make the differences between MOs rather moot. But playtesting here should help determine.
    \n
    \nI'm not initially fond of the idea of being able to buy spent MOs back with future failures. That would seem to me to motivate players to periodically invent conflicts they don't care about just to restore Traits. I'm thinking just having them refresh between sessions would be fine. If you were going for longer form rules I'd be tempted to have every group customize a refresh standard based on their genre...like "Spent Resolve refreshes whenever your declared Nemesis wins a Conflict with at least 3 net successes"
    \n
    \nDo you have to introduce your character into the current scene? Or can you do a "Meanwhile, back at the ranch" style scene. I'd vote for the latter because having a system that could seamlessly handle multiple conflicts occuring at the same time in different places would be pretty sweet. Also I don't think you want the new Conflict to be anything other than orthogonal to existing conflicts. If you want to thwart me you have to use the appropriate dice to reduce my success. You can narrate the obstacle as being your character punching me in the face, but if all you're trying to do is stop my action...that's not a goal of your own. If you want to roll additional dice to try and get the dice you need to stop my action you can introduce your character...but then you have to declare a goal of your own that is at risk while you hope to get dice to stop me with (2/3s of the dice you roll on average would be useable to stop me). If you want to support me...its just the reverse. But I would actually completely forbid goals that are the same or opposed to existing goals.

I can easily hamstring your chances simply by claiming that MO before you even if its not my best suit.

    For now, I'd like to table the notions of MO refreshing from Burn, but I like something like this model as a working framework for related discussion:
    Every character has Aspirations which are accomplished in Stages, via scenes.
    A given Stage might take several scenes to accomplish, however, based on its complexity or difficulty.
    A characters agenda in a scene is called a Goal, when that agenda is not success with an entire Stage.
    A player may redefine a Stage any time between scenes, with one exception: an failed scene outcome can not become a new/alternate Stage for the failing character. This rule could mean that previously accomplished Stages become moot, and an Aspiration must be completely re-planned or abandoned in favor of a new Aspiration.
    When an Aspiration is accomplished, the player may revalue all of his or her MOs, up to a total value of 9.

    Every two Aspirations that a character accomplishes increases the character's MO value total by 1.

    I have come up with two ways to deal with passed dice while still mitigating the problems of extreme results (all of one die result, I feel, will lead to dull play scenes).
    Passed die results are Successes or Failures or Neutrals with regards to only your Goal [original rules]; but any passed die may be discarded by the recipient to self-introduce.
    This "Permission To Intro" rule change keeps the original rules' feel that Fortune's first results matter, but it also opens up more opportunity to intertwine characters.

    Alternately, there's the "Chance To Become Neutral" rule change:
    Passed die results remain associated with your Goal (S/F/N), with one addition: If another player who received a Neutral die uses the Neutral to self-introduce, and the MO of the die you passed is one that the other player uses, then the recipient may use the passed die to self-introduce also (and he or she must, of course, use the last MO not in use already in the scene).
    Example: You use Resolve 1 (Rock), and roll Scissors, Scissors, Paper, Paper, Rock. That's 2 Successes, 2 Failures, and a Neutral.
    You give both Failures (Scissors) to Amy and the Success and Neutral to Bob.
    After you narrate your Opening Volley, Bob uses the Neutral to self-intro, choosing Manipulation (Scissors)--either of Amy's Failures (Scissors) may now be used to self-intro herself (with the only MO left, Manipulation/Paper) leaving the other Failure either to make a Complication for you (and only you) or to be discarded.
    This is slick in handling mechanics because, with every player having their own five dice of one color and with self-intro involving discarding a Neutral or Become-Neutral die, one never really has to keep track of what a Neutral becomes--it is always associated with the original roller's Goal. No dice side flipping; no flexing dice into re-targeting another player. Just straight-forward "what color is it, who's color is that, what MO did they use?" and your done.
    Additionally, the side always tells the current narrator using the die what MO must be used, remember. I may not have made that clear enough, but even with the (above) "passed dice can be used for anything" key change, the passed die MO constrained the recipient's narration to the die side's MO. I think I am OK with killing that rule in favor of one of these changes, but a passed die was never just a carte blanche, it always had an impact on whether Resolve, Flexibility, or Manipulation was to be taken into account in how it was a Success or Failure for the passer's Goal.

    So you see that I've reverted the original rules about die results, and have two ways to extend them. The latter requires an additional rule to mitigate the dull narration that I imagine will result from overwhelming Success or Failure with no Neutral results. The problem is rooted in the rules that limit scenes to only one active, unanswered Success or Failure narration--it makes moot extra Success or Failures. But these rule changes can break that up:
    D.2 Use the die to narrate a Complication\u2014something which opposes your Goal\u2014against you, taking into account the MO of the die result (Resolve, Manipulation, or Flexibility) not the original player's MO. A player may only create a(nother) Complication if there is no current, unresolved Complication in the scene.
    Pile on the Complications, in turn order, if you want! Fate can be cruel. Situations can spiral out of control. There's always another way around a problem, in a new scene.

    Also:
    IV.F (old F becomes G) Any additional Successes may be used to narrate follow-on bonuses or accomplishments or boons, as related to your agenda in the scene.
    Mama not only grants her blessings on my her daughter's wedding, she also give the young couple the family heirloom dining set and tableware, to help them start their household. Basically, you can accomplish more than one Goal, with slick narration of Successes after Bypassing all Complications.
    -----

    With regards to passing only Successes, what else can I do? What else makes MO relevant at all (disregard Burn here, for now)?

    I recognize a problem with being limited on Successes kept with no limit on Failures passed--it stacks bad odds. I have a COOL idea, I think:
    What if, similarly to how your MO limits the number of Successes that you may keep, it also mitigates how many Failures you must pass? I see two ways to do it, the latter I think cooler still:
    1) 5 - your MO = max passed Failure dice. This could mean either:
    (a) an MO maxes at 4 and you may only have a total MO value of 7, or
    (b) one rolls 6 dice, with current MO points distribution.

    2) Distribute 9 Success points and 9 Failure points between the three MOs

    The former makes more consistent character types: if you're good in something, you don't fail spectacularly at it (and even masters CAN fail, but only with 1 die). The latter makes for very interesting, dynamic characters: I could have Resolve 5S and 5F--I can succeed or fail wildy; my Resolve is at Fate's mercy. Or I could have a Resolve 5S 1F--I tend to succeed, and when I fail it ain't by much.

    Frankly, unless you see a BIG hole, 2.b is going to become a rule.

    In summation on all your points about the subject of "what do I keep"--I think simpler is better, both for my target demographic and for speed of play (it takes time to come up with constrained narration). I also don't like the idea of trying to explain why it could be FUN to narrate one's own Complications--I am more on the side of character agency than story uber alles. I could see a Designer's Note sidebar with that variation for experimentation, though.... Let folks figure out the "baseline" game of My Guy before branching out into a more authorial stance, guiding others with self-Complication.
    -----
    On that note, I am generally going to stick on the No GM side, unless there's some glaring hole in the structure of conflict framing and cycling that needs a "guidance" role. As it stands, players can build their own Aspirations and Stages, and everyone should easily grok how to push for themselves, against and with others as needs must (as dice drive opportunity).

    I also fear the game turning trad, with a GM providing opposition. I don't want players to ever feel like teaming up on a near-permanent basis is a best tactic, because a GMs is out there framing opposition to all their Goals. Similarly, I don't want to put a burden on one player to become the GM-as-fascilitator, trying to herd players into opposing each other and tweaking situations to keep everyone at odds.

    I really want a Diplomacy vibe: early, we players are sort of around the edges of the game, each aimed inward, but able to find allies and common ground for a time. But as Stages wither and Aspirations regroup, the knives come out, kings are sometimes made, and a story resolves and closes. A new one might begin (episodic, serials), but anything that turns players into permanent allies is bad. And I think a GM is as likely to do that as skillfully pluck and prod a situation to that point.

    It's Complicated, not Dogs in the Vineyard, to use indie shorthand.
    (Or Universalis, not Hero! ;^) )

    Self-introing to frame a distant scene could also be an "Advanced Technique," but it should always be knit to the source scene (which is hard for learning gamers, and even seasoned ones and skilled authors!). I don't want some random passed Neutral die to be a license to steal the limelight, dragging other players into YOUR (inserted spotlight) scene away from the scene of the poor schlub who gave up the Neutral.     \n OK, if you don't want to dig through all the above whys and wherefores, here's the updated rules:
    http://davidartman.com/systems/roshambo-dice

    For the two options on how to use passed dice, I went with the simpler one: use as it is (Success, Failure, or free to choose) or to self-intro. I think it's unlikely that a beginning gamer would grok the idea of a die changing what it can do based on someone else's MO during a self-intro... hell, it confuses me a bit, still. I don't think folks would recognize the opportunity often enough... but anyone can remember "it's a Success or Failure for the roller's MO... or you choose, if Neutral... and regardless, you may discard it to self-intro."

    Added basic Advancement system, too--it's simple enough to figure out. I also have a "regression" system that is kind of unique. Both together, though, means that someone could eventually hit 5 & 5 on every MO, which makes the dice totally in charge. That might be a feature, not a flaw--with experiences good and bad, one learns to follow Fate's whims (i.e. one can keep every Success rolled without having to pass it and risk someone using it to self-intro; and one must pass every Failure, with no discards due to low Flub rating).

    Barring glaring holes, I am going to "lock" this version down for purposes of playtest. We will use d6s, with 5-6 beating 3-4 beating 1-2 beating 5-6. I encourage others to give it a whirl and post APs (or just rule issues, here in this thread).
