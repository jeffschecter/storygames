    \n So, for custom tarot - that is, games with their own set of thematic, illustrated cards which are somehow "read" for inspiration in ways reminiscent of a tarot reading...

    @Wilhelm mentioned Witch Quest up-thread: it was probably the earliest such game published.

    The Fortune Deck in Everway is very much a simplified tarot. The instructions for reading it are simple and effective and translate easily to other games and different decks. Besides teaching me what little I know about card-based divination, Everway is an important piece of RPG history - though I don't think I'd much enjoy playing it as written these days (it's an quintessential textbook example of 1990s GM-driven participation-ist design).

    Ganakagok by Bill White is an oft-forgotten gem. Its custom tarot map to a standard poker deck, and the suits and numerical values are also used in play, but it does instruct to read the cards' imagery for inspiration in specific ways as well. I have heard all sorts of criticism leveraged at Ganakagok over the years, but I maintain it's a top-tier quality example of the sort of designs coming out of the Forge mind-space of mid-00s.

    Everway and Ganakagok informed all of my own attempts to design RPGs employing a (standard) tarot deck to my satisfaction - none of them finished and available at the moment, I'm sorry. I don't think I've yet read or played a RPG where the Rider-Waite deck (or the Book of Toth or other off-the-shelf "occult" deck) was used to my full satisfaction - but already this thread has taught me about games I didn't know or had not considered, so I'll happily look into them.

    \n Thanks for the nice words about Ganakagok, Raffaele. Ron Edwards recently told me that just upon reading it, it seemed to him to be a little "over-engineered," and that seemed like a pretty fair judgment. Still, for all its faults, it has produced some really nice play over the years. I think that having "In a Wicked Age" come out about the same time helped people understand what to do with oracular motifs as creative prompts; before that, that seemed to be one of the hardest things to get players to do.

    I do love card-based oracular resolution, but it is very hard to do well. In general, tarot-like cards work great as prompts for creativity, as in world-building and character creation. They are harder to work into conflict resolution, even though ironically the whole point of tarot reading is to tell you what is going to happen. (Interestingly, apparently tarot cards started out as game pieces and only later got co-opted by fortune-tellers).

    For example, for an IGDN slot at Gen Con this year I ran "House of Cards," which does this nice thing where you draw a card from the Major Arcana and use it as an archetype for your modern fantasy character caught between the world of magic and the world of the mundane. That works extremely well, in my opinion. Players drew two cards, picked one, and were able to use it to inspire character concepts very quickly.

    The action resolution mechanics weren't terribly satisfying to me, so I asked the players if they'd be alright experimenting a little bit; we did this thing where your hand was your hit points, basically, and you played cards to take action, narrating something in line with the action types associated with the suit of the card you played. The opponent had to respond in suit with a higher value, and the loser lost an additional card. Lose all your cards, and you're out of the scene; once the GM is out of cards, the scene is over and you've won. Something like that; it had a few kinks but the players enjoyed it and were able to do some clever things with the card-based special abilities in the rules. The game is worth taking a look at.

    I've actually been working on a game called "The New World" which I call a "Freewheeling Narrative RPG of Mash-Up Speculative History" that does something similar to what I tried to do with "House of Cards" at GenCon. "The New World" is a GMless game where the PCs are historical archetypes from different cultures in contention within a single geographical space, broadly defined as Natives, Newcomers, and Outsiders. It has a Ganakagok-like deck of cards with specialized motifs that are used for world-building and during both character creation and scene resolution, with moments of role-playing tied to the use of cards to take action in a scene. It's pretty much finished; I think it just needs some playtesting to fine-tune the rules presentation.

    "The New World" has been on the backburner for a while since the publisher I wrote it for passed on it, but if anyone is interested in taking a look at it--Raffaele, you probably want to see it--PM or send me e-mail at billwhite at ganakagok dot com.

    \n@Bill_White "over-engineered" sounds almost appropriate. Almost. I mean, it's really "crunchy", with a lot of moving parts... but functional. Successfully designed complex-but-functional games are very important historical stepping stones. One thing Ganakagok did for me was defining (together with a couple other games) the upper limit of technical complexity I was actually comfortable playing with - and that's quite the valuable lesson!

    OTOH, I have this feeling that, for better conflict-resolution in RPGs using traditional tarot cards specifically, we have to look toward greater simplicity... That we have, for example, to downplay the numerical values of Minor Arcana (which developed into the playing cards we're much more comfortable using as randomizer in games) and rely more on the oracular-pictorial value of all tarot cards even for resolution. This necessitates a shift away from our traditional reliance on numbers as the preferred vector of "hard" systemic-fictional content in our traditions of design.

    An interesting "trick" I've developed, but haven't deployed as part of a fully functional game design yet, is based on reading the numbers on Major Arcana cards strictly as ordinal numbers, expressing a sequence. This is employed similarly to an "initiative count", but of course takes on greater meaning due to being a fixed order - while the cards themselves can be physically scattered. This is what the "Cards speak" mechanism in my upcoming Everway remake is based upon, though the "fixed order" element was dropped from that particular design because Everway Fortune Cards don't have numbers.
