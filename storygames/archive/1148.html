So, I like Espionage fiction & cold war history. I have a little game idea, not more than an inkling for now, and I think it's basically in new gamespace, but I'd like to get a feel for what's nearby. It's going to be a game about (or perhaps more properly, set) in the world of real-life type espionage, but focused a lot on the political machinations and less on individual missions and such. Maybe there's already a game out there that can do the kinds of things I want to do, and I just haven't heard of it. So, I'm looking for all the spy games out there, maybe a few words on each, like:

Spycraft: d20, official setting is kind of crazy-James Bond-y, but it could be used for any kind of quick-small-team-covert ops type stuff.

Cold City - 1950 Berlin Monster Hunting. 'nuff said.

I know there's probably tons of these out there, and chances are some of you have played some of them, so let's see what you have to say about them. Thanks!

    Posted By: John HarperHey Fred,

    Can you talk more about how Wilderness of Mirrors works? I saw it at the con and now I regret not grabbing one. Begin the pimping.
    Unfortunately, I didn't get hold of a copy, so what info I have is second-hand from Rob Donoghue, who did get one.

    Basically the deal is this, as I understand it: The GM states a simple mission, like, 'The diplomat's daughter has been kidnapped by a drug lord. Bring her back alive.'

    The players then start planning their rescue effort, inventing all the details -- because, after all, they're spies, so they have intel. So they draw up the maps, envision the satellite photos, place inside agents at the scene -- all of that.

    For every 20 minutes of planning and/or play (as I understand it), the GM gets a complication point.

    Then, they start the mission, and the game works on the principle that no plan survives contact with the enemy -- the GM spends those complication points to make some part of the plan not play out as anticipated. Oh, you're supposed to take the third left? Problem: there are only two lefts. The third left appears to be a concrete wall.

    That's my "surface" understanding of the game -- but it sounds pretty hot.

    A couple more comments on older spy RPGs...

    original Top Secret - The horrors of random character creation -- including, by the official method, random selection of skills! The (sometimes) coolness of the "I pick a move, you pick a move, we check the chart" melee combat system.

    James Bond - The original "universal table" RPG.

    Mercenaries, Spies, and Private Eyes - Underappreciated. The martial arts rules are wonderful, given the T&T-style combat system. The first "use any attribute with any skill" system.
