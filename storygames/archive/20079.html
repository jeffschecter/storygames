\n Here's another one in my series of "Issues that could come up in any hobby, not just RPGs, but that's what I'm into so I'm asking you guys" questions!

I'm currently running a game that benefits from some amount of GM alone time prepping.
Of all the time I spend prepping, 98% of that is groaning, fearing the work, arguing on forums, listening to podcasts and playing video games and only 2% is actually trying to prep.
It's like such a creative block... I fear doing the book-keeping so much and the game suffers for it.
What are your takes on this?
Have you had similar problems? How did you overcome it?
Like, for four or five days now, my "task" has been "transfer the time-keeping records (of diegetic events) from index cards to a calendar". I have the calendar printed out with Zakharan names and dates. There's like five or six cards. The task will probably take less than 20 minutes. I've spent days and days just postponing it, dreading it, lying in the sofa staring at the ceiling...

    \n I have similar inclinations recarding prep for old school D&D. I cope with it by honing my improvisation skills ever keener, and by pre-designing campaign protocols before the campaign has ever begun. The latter seems to short-circuit prep-laziness somehow, probably because you can work on stuff whenever in whatever size blocks you feel like, rather than having a deadline in the next session or such.

    In the middle of a campaign the best cure for the prep-lazy seems to be a) excitement about the game and b) solid procedures that leave no ambiguity as to what I should be doing and when.

    \n I only prep what inspires and excites me. I don't GM games that require busywork. That said, I can sometimes find inspiration and excitement in the most mundane stuff if I look for it -- often by envisioning how it'll come up in play. Is there an awesome moment that will arise when the players look at the calendar? "Oh no, only 3 days till x!" Or, "Wow, we've been doing this for X months and no fatalities, that must be a record!"? If so, you could try focusing on that.

    When I lived in New York, I did most of my prep on the subway, en route to where we played. I see no need to prep early -- "right before I need to use it" works for me.

    \n Tips so far summary:
    1. Learn to break it down! Figure out a practical next step. Like sometimes I need to go to the "go get that box of index card and a pen, put them in your hand" level. Other times it's more of a creative struggle and I can go to a random generator (I have made a list of random generators I like, like pulling a random Everway card).
    2. Prep buddies, as an extra motivator and "audience" that makes you want to work.
    3. Get re-excited about the prep! I'm not onboard with Dave completely to visualize how/if the material will come into play, I'd rather keep it skeletal so I can throw it away if the players take the game's direction elsewhere (my goal with this campaign is to run a very player driven game. But figuring out why you want a piece of prep in place can be a source of motivators. "If I have this calendar, it will enable me to better keep track of rations, and when to do faction turns, and, and..." "The players have really eaten through a lot of the dungeons I started with, but they've barely touched the social conflicts in the village. Maybe I should prepare some more dungeons along with ramping up those town conflicts!" [hmm, or whatever else conclusion you as DM can draw of them eating through the dungeons.]

    \n Ok, so here's a model I've been experimenting with lately for writing and running DayTrippers adventures (this is OSR/trad play, although the GM takes an object-oriented stance rather than a plot-oriented stance, which is how I read Sandra's comment above). I call it a "RunSheet". It doesn't contain stat blocks because those are all in the book or in my notes. That makes it system-agnostic, woot!

    The RunSheet is intended to serve as a guide for the GM during play, but I find that the act of writing my module this way forces me to think in "object by object" terms, thinking about each object (character, location or event) in isolation, giving each its own little tracks and triggers - and yet still keeping everything together and fairly easy to see all at once. For some stories I only need one sheet. For some I need to break NPCs out from Locations or Plot Events. Depends on the adventure type and gameworld particulars.

    Write whatever works for you in each little box, then you can wind em up and let em loose.

    Here is a RunSheet for "War of the Worlds".
    Here is a RunSheet for Act 3 of "Romeo and Juliet".
    Here is a blank RunSheet.

    Notes: On the left are the primary narrative Elements: Plot/Acts, Locations, and NPCs. Each has some little square boxes that can be filled to with ID numbers to identify any Information available via that Element. See the WOTW NPC Sheet for an example of that. In the center are the Conditionals. They are linked on the left side to the Elements they may be triggered by/within. On the right are the Effects of those Conditionals, or what I call "Rails". Rails with ID numbers represent Information, and that's what the little squares in column 1 are referring to.

    \n I want to post before reading any more than the OP so I get this out before I want to modify it:

    BOPI (brainstorming, organizing, processing, inspiring)

    Not that they need to be always done in this order, but here's the rough sequence that works well for me:

    Inspiring: I make sure I'm inspired. It's something that you know when you are, but sometimes forget to notice when you aren't. Usually if I feel a mental block, it's because I feel like I "have to" make progress on something as I've lost inspiration. Now that I've recently learned this about myself, I am easier on myself but also make a concerted effort to seek inspiration when I start to feel bad vibes. I get most inspiration through reading.

    Brainstorming: Once I'm inspired, brainstorming can happen. This is assuming that I'm inspired about my subject matter rather than my processes (more on that later). My biggest personal sin is not properly brainstorming before moving into processing. I think a lot of analytical types like myself fall into this, and if you get more than 1 in a room you can really undermine a brainstorming session.

    Processing: This term is purposely vague because it totally depends on the creative activity. When I'm working on a game, often this is the act of judging what I like and don't like, and going over design issues.

    Organizing: Also pretty vague. But this is moving around processed or semi-processed ideas and content so that they are able to be worked on and ultimately consumed by someone else (or myself after I've forgotten all my design ideas!)

    About inspiration; don't assume that it only relates to brainstorming. I am just as often inspired to rethink an existing idea, or to reorganize existing materials because of something I read or heard somewhere. This distinction might mean nothing to others, but it's helpful to me because it helps me to remember that just because I feel inspired in the moment doesn't mean I'm being inspired to do the right thing, right now.

    \n Personally, I love making prep interactive. (To the point where it's hard to go back to doing it "the old way" - I don't know if I ever could.)

    It's like Apocalypse World "provocative questions": you let the players build the world for you by asking them questions.

    Except I like to do it in the prep phase (and not necessarily in play, particularly if it's a very traditional type of game group).

    I ask them provocative questions and then I add a little (often random) process on my end to jumble things up.

    For instance, let's say there's a castle and a royal family which is important to the game. I might ask: "Tell me: who is your most trusted contact in the castle? Why do you trust them so much?" Or: "Who is the most suspicious royal, to your eyes, and why?" (Name an existing NPC or invent a new one.)

    And then I might have a list of important plot elements, like "The secret portal underneath the throne room", and I've decided before I hear the players' answers that I will roll one die for each NPC, and on a 1 or 2, that NPC knows about the portal and is somehow intimately involved with it.

    So prep is now a mini-game. I anxiously wait for the players' reply, and then I roll the dice, and something cool comes out of it. It's fun! And, with relatively little work, I have tons of material for the upcoming game.

    \n I'm lazy. I've got burned as a GM and I didn't wanted to ever had to made intensive prep ever again. I tried several different random tables and aids and polished my improvisational skills. I was still unsatisfied with the results. My first campaigns were good and everyone enjoyed them, but required too much prep and carefully done railroading here and there, even when they went into the sandboxing stages. The games where I tried improvising gave the players more options but weren't as good.

    I had to find a commitment between doing good prep and improvising for the player's sake. The cognitive ability to do that was simply beyond my own brain. And so I found it there: I could use the player's brains! (mwhahahahahaha!)

    Now my method for worldbuiding goes for a series of provocative open ended questions for the players and dice rolling to give a scale to any variables (amount of population, how good is doing the economy of a kingdom, number of followers of a god, size of a kingdom, etc). Then I use impro and their help to relate their answers to each other, and add anything that helps to explain the situation or plain label it as a mistery. After the basics are established I tell the players that everything they have just created is public knowledge for everyone in the setting, but not necessarily the truth, which it's for them to find out.

    I'd still like to improve the way I create the character relationship tree, but so far things are going okey. Our current campaign is more or less sandbox and I keep asking the players for "places of interest their characters have heard of" as well as for characters and monsters they would like to find, either good or bad. For the rest, all I have to do is find out the problems arising in the setting and give them a face the players can punch or talk to if they want. Last 5e D&D session I totally improvised a monster out of nowhere (well the table for creating monsters in the DM book helped a bit) and only gave it a couple of special abilities when the players rolled a 1 when facilg it.

    The rest of the campaign is building up from the consequences of the actions of the party, which at some point went murderhobo enough to create three or four enemies of their own, which I only had to incarnate and level up into an interesting opposition.

    I'm also trying this method to bring depth to the story: Frame the scene (I use my good old Resourceful Deck, and here's how to use it) Come up with some random twist after players have interacted with it and something happens and if I can't come up with all the details about what's going on behind it (though even If I had I still do this to see if players come up with a better idea), I ask the players to make an intelligence roll (or use any other suitable skill). The one who rolls lower starts giving their deduction about what's really going on. The next one gets to find more arguments to support that version or give another. The same goes for the next and so until all players have spoken. The last can even summarize all and go sherlock holmes about it, pointing at something everyone else has missed.

    Players have surprised me more than once with their interpretations, which in turn have served to trigger another scene for the session or completely change my plans for it.

    You can also google "concept art" on the web and pick up anything you find to show the players and have their minds overflowing with ideas about why are their characters seeing that and what could happen next. Even strange noises could trigger that now that I think of it.

    Of course, treat this advice with as much honesty as you want to have in your games. If your players can take it, make it clear about these methods. Some players can't and demand that you do proper prep. It's up to you but I see no reason to not be honest about this, nor to make way too clear the details for the players, most care more about a good game that about how it was made.

    \n I also play Netrunner (not an RPG and I'm not claiming that it is an RPG, but it has similarities in the way we play it).
    I typically get my prep done because there are specific numbers that I need to fill out.
    The deck needs to have at least X cards, at most Y influence, and exactly Z or Z+1 agenda points.
    I can fill it out with things I think would be fun for the runner or painful or challenging or funny or frustrating... depending on our moods. I can feel creative but the constraints help against procrastination because I know when I'm not done -- when the numbers don't match up. I don't know when I'm done -- because I could tweak and tweak -- but I know when I absolutely am not done. I use a Emacs script I wrote to figure out the numbers for me.

    [As an aside, I don't consider Netrunner an RPG because you can [i]only[/i] do what's in the cards. Like, you can't go to your day job unless you draw the card Day Job. You can't stay up all night unless you draw the card All Nighter etc.]

    So that's what I want to learn, a prep procedure that's complete and sandboxy (a lot of suggestions for low-prep games are great, but I'm fallen in love with prepped sandboxy games) but that I know when it's done and that I know how to do.
