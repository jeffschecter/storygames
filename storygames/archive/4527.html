\n So, I'm tidying up some of my blog posts into a book. It's fun. I'm enjoying it.

The problem is, I'm stuck on the introduction. I can't write an introduction any way that makes me happy.

The book itself is about using improvisation techniques in gaming. So far, I've tried the following ways to write the intro.

Autobiographical: "Like many gamers, I started by playing Dungeons and Dragons. Then I stopped roleplaying and did improvisational theatre. When I went back to roleplaying, all the improv theory helped me..."

Inspirational: "This book is about improving the way you game: making you more spontaneous; making you play better as a group..."

True to its source: "This book is an attempt to adapt Keith Johnstone's theories of improvisation to roleplaying."

...and I don't like any of them.

Can you help? What am I missing that's obvious?

Graham

    \n Ah...OK.

    Justin, the reason I wanted to write this thing was because I was frustrated with most of the discussions of improvisation. Individual techniques would be mentioned, but out of context: so someone would mention "yes, and", but without all the background about blocking and accepting.

    So I wanted to go back to basics, really, and try and explain this whole chunk of theory - which really helped me, especially in GMing - from the beginning.

    Thor, well, the story is that I gave up roleplaying because it was boring; then I did improvisation for a while; and, when I came back to roleplaying, I used all my old improv techniques, and they worked really well.
