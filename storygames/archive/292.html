seeking the secrets of game design riffers

PaulCzege
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
Greg Stolze and I had a quick email exchange recently about riffing and grinding, the two creative processes he writes about in the Gamma World GM's guide. His condensed version:

"Neither one is inherently better: They're just different....A grinder takes an idea and (usually in the privacy of his own home) works on it a little, polishes here, adds there, snips this bit away, glues that bit back on upside-down, polishes a little more, shows it to some colleagues, nods politely at their comments and then takes it home for further refinement.

A riffer hears A and immediately thinks B (or 1, or 3.14156, or "purple"). Like a jazz musician, he can just pull stuff out of his ass with very little run-up time or prompting.

Almost no one is a pure riffer or a pure grinder."

And that totally clicked for me. I'm skewed way grinder myself, as a designer and as a player. And my wife Danielle is skewed way riffer. Which offers quite some insight into why she's stalled on the pirate rpg she's been designing. She's been trying to architect a system. And it hasn't been working. So now I'm thinking we need to figure a way for her to riff the system.

She has some stuff on paper, but it's all disconnected. My current thinking is that she should make a list of things she thinks she needs mechanics for. Fill that list in with the stuff she already has worked out. And then we should play, with her making up temporary mechanics as necessary for the rest of the stuff on the list.

So anyway, this is a question for self-identifying riff skewed game designers? What do you think of the plan? Would it work for you? What is your own design methodology?

Paul

    I'm a riffer.

    I also call it being an engineer rather than a dreamer. I don't do well at coming up things all on my own, out in the cold. But if you give me ideas I can spin off of them all day long. I can even do it in very high preasure situations, no problem. As long as I have the seed to work off of, I'm all good.

    Getting at the seed when it's buried inside myself though? That's brutally hard.

    The only consistant way I can do it is to go back to what I'm good at. I look at other games, other fiction, other whatever, and look at what I like and don't like about it. I compare what it does to what I want to do. I steal and retool, then I abandon to resteal and re-retool. And I do it again, and again, and again. Until finally I'm left with something that most people think is all cool and orriginal and aren't I just so creative.

    So maybe Danielle can find some inspiration in going over other games? In looking at what they do and figuring out what kinds of ideas she can spin out of them?

    If she's really riffing then trying to do it on her own, in the cold, creating out of nothing probably isn't going to work. Looking at other games, listening to other people's conversations, or having a Q&A like Tony suggests... anything that gets her to the process where she's engaged in part of something rather than feeling the need to create from nothing is probably going to help.

    Here's what I do to make up homebrews. It's totally not big up front design, but it works really well.

    1. Start with a game. It doesn't really matter which one, as long as it's in the same neighborhood as the ballpark.

    2. Run it with sympatico players.

    3. Poll the players, and find out what worked and what didn't.

    4. Change exactly ONE thing about the rules to improve the game's feel, whether to fix something wrong, or to make something good better.

    5. Run it again.

    6. If the change helped, then celebrate, and go back to step 3. If the change hurt, then undo the change, and go back to step 3.

    Changing one thing at a time means that you can isolate the effects of a change -- if you make multiple changes, and things get better, you can't tell what helped and what was dead weight.

    As you iterate the process, you drift away from the original rules, and it's actually surprising how quickly the game stops looking like the original and becomes its own thing.

    Riffer here too. I tend to collaborate, and I do best with structure, sometimes a _lot_ of structure. Give me a blank surface and I'm paralyzed - throw up three walls and I will _explode_ in the 4th direction.

    Easy to say, sometimes hard to address. Things that help include having to answer fairly specific questions or to frame fairly specific situations. These can be rules related ("How would the ship fight from master & Commander Play out?") but it is often the most innocuous questions ("What do the people of Paar wear as hats?") that are most powerful because their answers spawn more questions and can really get a lot of inertia. Tiny ideas become the irritant in the oyster shell,

    Now, that works for me, but it obviously may be of no use to your wife, but if it is, it's a concrete way that you can help her out, because questions coming from the outside are almost always more powerful because they're fresh.

    Beyond that, I'd look to things like Tarot, horoscopes, the cut up method or anything else that creates a pattern that the brain can latch onto and run with. Most riffers I know are just looking for the seed that Brand so eloquently describes, and crazy stuff like that can be useful for generating it.

    Anyway, good luck to you both.

    -Rob D.
