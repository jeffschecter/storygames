    \n I think this whole conversation is based on a perception of genre that's pretty superficial. Color does not determine content. Fantastic genres appeal to gamers because they offer more scope and more easily identifiable points-of-entry for the imagination than more naturalistic genres. While it is entirely possible to simply recapitulate the historically extant story content of these genres - thus recapitulating and perpetuating their less savory aspects along with their positive ones - it is also equally possible to mine them for raw materials to assemble in new ways.

    Pulp & Fantasy are cultural middens full of symbols. What we make out of those symbols is our responsibility, not the responsibility of the symbols.

    \n As an aside: even if you create a game that's not about geeky subject matter, there's still the issue of geeky process. Playing present RPG's usually involves an extensive set-up time, fairly complicated rules, and a pay-off that usually takes a couple hours to arrive. (I realize this paragraph isn't specifically on-point, but I feel it often gets overlooked.)
    \n
    \nMore on-point, it seems like RPG's involve a couple different tasks: (a) playing a game, (b), telling a story, and (c) pretending to be someone else, (d) anything else I've overlooked. So, if you're trying to reach an audience outside of the traditional gamers, good questions to start out with might include:
    \n
    \n1. What kind of game would non-gamers enjoy playing?
    \n2. What kind of story would non-gamers enjoy telling?
    \n3. What kind of person would non-gamers enjoy pretending to be?
    \n
    \nI think #2 and #3 could very easily involve escapist stuff--superheroes, wizards, etc.--if done in the proper style (though certainly it could involve other things as well). Everyone enjoys a little bit of escapism now and then. To me it seems like the trick is to make it more like a parlor game: rules that fit on a single sheet of paper, complete cycle of play lasting under an hour, etc.

    I think Jasper's got it right that the geekiness of things like D&D are somehow linked to the fantasy tropes, but the objections Daniel and Joe raise are important. too. Obviously, it's not just the trappings, because Harry Potter, children's books or not, appeals to a huge number of people of all ages. Why?

    I think it's not just the tropes, but the way they are used, the way heroic fantasy literature focuses on the mere things and not on the broad meanings behind those things. Harry Potter, despite being filled with magic, isn't about wizards at all. What the non-geek masses find so distasteful about heroic fantasy is the obsession with fantasy and medieval minutiae.

    \n

    D&D and most other fantasy RPGs are worse. The geekiness of D&D lies in its obsession on how dragons are able to breathe fire, how beholders are able to float, how medusae are able to mate, how the body parts of these various creatures can be used to recreate similar effects in potions or magic items. It's the obsession with fantasy tactics: which spell is the right one to use when encountering a troll? What combos produce a synergistic effect? It's page after page of medieval weapons and armor and their unique bonuses and penalties.

    \n

    There's nothing wrong with a game that focuses on these things for people who like that sort of stuff. There's nothing wrong with wanting to make a game that would interest nongeeks, either. I want to do both, myself. But not in the same game. That's madness.

    Posted By: Jasper PolaneThose trappings include misogynic and racist imagery. So why do we keep returning to these genres?
    \nBy way of one possible answer, let me tell you about what happened in the local game I'm running right now.

    I came up with this setting for a Fate game that was part sword-and-sorcery, part anime, part Arabian Nights. My girlfriend was going to be in this game, so one day we talked a bit about how sword-and-sorcery isn't very nice to women most of the time. She didn't have much experience with the genre, so we went out with a copy of Ron Edwards' Sorcerer and Sword and picked out a book or two from its bibliography.

    She went on to create a member of a semi-religious order of all-female gravekeepers, whose job it was to cleanly bury the dead, oversee funerals, and perform the rituals that would calm their spirits ('cause in the setting, the dead aren't going properly to the afterlife right now). Allowed to live off of whatever they can salvage from the corpses (i.e. abject poverty) these women wield a double-edged social influence - they are necessary in their communities, but they are shunned by most as a reminder of human mortality. Their role in society transcends political concerns, too, so the movements of kings and princes couldn't matter less to them, as all die equally and are equally in need of their services. Because the character's job involves defending gravesites against robbers and the occassional toe-to-toe with an angry ghost, she's also quite danger-ready.

    And I thought to myself, "Hmm... let's see... outsider from the world at large? Check. Fiercely independent set of motives? Check. Willingness to overturn established social orders to carve her own place in the world? Check." And so it turns out, her character is more true to genre than any of the others in the group, all without any of the imagery you're talking about.

    Then, we turn around and agree she has a twin sister whom she's estranged from, who fell into life as a courtesan for the upper class of the city-state where the campaign primarily takes place. Their relationship, and their commentary on their respective life choices, has become a huge source of thematic content in the campaign, and a big part of the reason why it has a lot of the emotional resonance that it does.

    To a large degree, I think we can return to these genres because we don't believe that their worst aspects necessarily define them. Or, for story gamers, because those bad aspects give us something to say.

And the resulting stories could have the "multivaliencies" that you're talking about.
