\n I'll be participating as a player in my first Fate session on Friday and am excited. I'm curious to find out if this is the generic crunch-lite system I've been looking for. I'm also going to be reading Savage Worlds and Cortex soon as well so I fully expect at least one of the three to hook me.

But I want to know your thoughts. As a player who values mechanics that aid the roleplaying and keep the flow of story going which of these systems do you think would suit me? I'm open to all genres, just looking for a system that helps me tell a story with my friends and have some randomizers involved.

    \n Fate encourages you to engage the story on a high level, and to keep things narratively interesting. Savage Worlds is good for just popping down resolutions to actions that characters take. Cortex is similar to SW in that regard, although its evolved cousin, Cortex+, takes the narrative action up a notch, and is a really interesting system. (The "roll and keep" dice pool with lots of dice is a neat mechanic.)

    \n I like FATE but I find it a bit too crunchy for my tastes. Essentially, I love the aspects mechanic but skills and stunts leave me cold. I've tried setting up a game with it, but we sort of got stuck at character generation (admittedly, I've learned a lot of the last few months and wouldn't dream of organising a game like I tried then). Is there really a reason why you can't just have aspects? And is there not an easier way to generate aspects? I like the fact that the standard rules of FATE 3 require fewer aspects; hopefully it will address some of my other issues as well.

    Cortex+ Drama/Smallville (along with FATE/Starblazer) were what first alerted me that games could be more collaborative than what I, as a lapsed trad rpger, had assumed was possible. It's certainly simpler than the other two. I'm itching to give it a go at some point. Any system which encourages players to think about their relationships with every die roll is good in my book.

    Of the three, Savage Worlds is the only one I have any experience of actually playing. I enjoyed it but found combat really clunky - mainly because my players didn't know all the options (I think this is a common issue with what Ron Edwards would call fortune-at-the-end systems). I ended up creating little cards, a bit like I understand how D&D4e is organised, but the game was short lived. I'm not sure it really met my needs as a storyteller.

    \n Cortex and Savage Worlds I know quite well and are both pretty crunchy task resolution systems. They have different takes on how to hit target numbers and the reward resources are either plot points or bennies but at their root they are both quite similar. I agree that combat is clunky and slow but think that may be because conflict resolution systems show off what true speed is. Both need thoughtful character gen to make effective characters and can leave you unable to achieve what you thought your character was about from poor choices.

    Cortex Plus in all it's flavours is a very different beast to Cortex, is much more a conflict resolution system (except for the Marvel flavour) and rewards playing the system tight to the roleplaying. There is a lot of crunch but a lot more leeway with using characters effectively. I don't think they've got it right yet so research what you like most before picking which one to go for, of instance Smallville is fantastic at pushing relationships but tough to get final consequences out of.

    Fate I am still to play so would be interested to hear about. I also have it as the shadowy figure that sounds like the answer to my games need for generally GM'd games.

    \nI've been flicking through the Kickstarter previews of FATE Core and FAE and I have to admit I'm impressed.

    They've really upped the collaborative nature of the game, organised the rule book much better and pared it down to the basics.

    FATE proper is still a little crunchy for my tastes (although I'd be tempted to give it a go at some point), but FAE deals with that. My only criticism of the approaches, um, approach is that I'm not fully sold on the six key words they've given them - but that's clearly the simplest hack going if you want to change it.
