\n So, I've got 10 songs that would make superb games. There's romance in a foreign cafe; sci-fi epic; teenage sex. Others, too. I'm listening to one of them now and, I swear, with no exaggeration, there are chills going down my spine.

Would you like one of them to write a game about? If you would, post here and I'll give you a song.

If you do post, though, you've got to write me a game. I'll ask you questions to make sure I'm giving you a good song, and we can talk if you're unhappy with it, but you've got to give me something.

I'm especially interested in hearing from people who like writing games about foreign countries and relationships. And that sci-fi epic. I need a game about that.

Graham

    \nso hard to beat


    so hard to beat was written for a challenge by Graham Walmsley to design a game based on a song. Teenage Kicks by the Undertones has an interesting and influential recording history in the UK, but the song\u2019s very simple lyrics only hint at the appeal and awkwardness and ease and strictures of sexuality during the teenage years.

    So I\u2019ve used the tune primarily as a jumping off point, focusing on the question in the first line: \u201care teenage dreams so hard to beat?\u201d

    so hard to beat uses playing cards and the game truth or dare \u2013 that loaded weapon, which I thankfully haven\u2019t played since I was a teen \u2013 as a kind of pseudo-sociological filter to see which characters will hook up with whom. The cards of the paired-off characters then inform players what the psychological tone and memories of the encounter or relationship will be like.

    Any game about teen sex written by and/or meant to be played by adults has the potential to be or to become creepy. If you\u2019re going to try so hard to beat, talk with the rest of the players to see what kind of game experience you are all comfortable with. A game about sex doesn\u2019t have to be sexually explicit. A game with some truth or dare moments doesn\u2019t have to be about your personal truths. A game with characters who are teens exploring their sexuality doesn\u2019t require players to closely identify with the characters, to speak as them, to relive your own teen dreams to provide authenticity. Or it can, whatever you prefer\u2026


    Setting up

    To play so hard to beat, you\u2019ll need two or more participants, paper and writing implements and two packs of ordinary playing cards.

    Each player puts three note cards, sticky notes or pieces of paper in front of them. These cards represent characters- teenagers who all attend the same school and/or live in the same area.

    Characters

    Each player decides whether they will have two male characters and one female character or vice versa.

    Write the gender of the characters on the cards: 2 Fs and one M, or vice versa.

    The suits of randomly drawn playing cards will determine which cliques or social sets the characters belong to. Before picking cards, decide as a group what four social groupings your teens will be part of. The groups can be four cliques in a school; four different schools in a city; four different neighborhoods in a town; four favored genres of music, etc. Characters can sometimes switch between groups so do not pick ethnicity/race or any other immutable characteristic as the cliques.

    Whatever you pick, two of the groups will be larger; two will be smaller in number. Decide which two of the four cliques/ groups tend to have more members in your chosen setting and which two tend to have fewer members.

    Take two decks of cards. Remove all the diamonds and spades from one deck. Combine the remaining whole and half deck, shuffling all the cards together. Jokers are kept in the decks and will be wild cards.

    Decide which groupings correspond to each of the four suits. Your two larger groupings will be represented by the hearts and clubs. Your two smaller groupings will be linked to the diamonds and spades.

    Deal each character one face up card and two face down playing cards.

    The suit of the face up card shows what clique the character is part of. The numerical value of the card represents the character\u2019s status and desirability. Characters will usually date/ hook up with characters of the same clique and with a value near their own.

    The face down cards represents the character\u2019s interest in and availability for dating and sex as follows:

    Hearts \u2013 desire for sex
    Clubs \u2013 internally or externally generated qualms about sex & obstacles to dating
    Diamonds \u2013 special circumstances (overcomes barriers and allows atypical pairings- each diamond eliminates one barrier)
    Spades \u2013 logistical availability for sex (has a car, parents away, can get birth control, etc)

    Knowing the gender, clique, status, and starting cards of characters, players can now write characters\u2019 names down on their papers. Also write down a phrase that describes something memorable about the character\u2019s appearance, reputation, behavior, etc.

    After filling in these very basic bios, players describe their characters to the other participants.

      \nso hard to beat (continued)

      Truth or Dare

      so hard to beat is played like the game truth or dare. Players decide if they want truth (to answer a question about one of their character) or to accept a dare (to try and hook one of their characters up with another specified character.)

      Because characters can only pair up if there are certain \u2018matches\u2019 between their face up cards and between the characters\u2019 hidden cards, players will need to use truth questions to discover which characters might be able to hook up. Sometimes suggesting a coupling that will probably fail though use of an early dare is also good strategy because the cards of the two characters involved will be revealed to their players. Once players figure out which characters can get together, they can use dares to make those hooks ups happen.

      Character can only have sex under if the five following conditions all apply:

      \u2022\tThe characters are in the same clique
      \u2022\tCharacters\u2019 face cards are within 3 values of each other (for example a character with a 6 face card could hook up with characters with ace cards ranging from 3 to 9).
      \u2022\tThe characters are different genders
      \u2022\tAt least one of the characters has a spade card face down
      \u2022\tAt least one of the characters has a heart card in their face down cards that is higher in value than the other character\u2019s highest value face down clubs card

      Each diamond held by either character in their face down cards can eliminate one of these conditions.

      So, for example, if the characters have three diamond cards between them, they can date and have sex even if they are in different cliques, are of the same gender, and lack a spade card (don\u2019t ordinarily have the physical opportunity to be together alone).

      Every turn, play starts with the player with the character with the lowest value face up card, then proceeds clockwise around the table. Players can request a truth or dare, to be posed to them by another player. The first turn, the person to your left will provide the truth or dare; the next turn the player two spots to your left will ask, and so on.

      Truth

      Truths in this game consist of two questions. A question is asked \u2018to a character\u2019 and answered by the player controlling that character. That player is also asked if their character has one of the four suits in their face down cards. When you get asked a question, answers for your character, then tell (everyone) if there is at least one of that suit of card in the character\u2019s face down set of cards. You don\u2019t reveal how many of that type of cards are face down. But you do say the value of the highest heart or club card, if you get asked about those suits and your character has one or more of them.

      or Dare

      A dare is phrased as a challenge to have a character held by the active player hit on one of your characters or a character controlled by a third player. You cannot dare a player to have two of their own characters get together. When the dare is made, the two players controlling the characters involved look at the face down cards of both characters. If the match is possible, then the two character have a sexual encounter and the players narrate a psychology/ memory scene. (see below) If the pairing works, both players whose characters connected get to draw a card from the deck and place it in the face down pile of their choice of one of their three characters.

      Getting new cards between turns

      After every player has had a chance for a truth or dare, the character with the lowest value face up card gets a new face up card to replace the one they have (trying to reinvent themselves). All players can also give one of their characters a new face down card or replace the character\u2019s face up card with a new one.

      Psychology/ memory

      When a pairing occurs, the two players involved look at the cards of both characters. The suit of the highest card or set (pair or more) of cards among all cards of the two characters (face up and down) determines the dominant shared tone of the date.

      The next highest suit, just counting the other character\u2019s cards (not combining with your characters\u2019 cards) feed into the pairing as a secondary, private memory of the date. (If the other character has only one suit of cards it is possible that your minor tone will be the same suit as the dominant tone).

      Hearts \u2013 emotional high
      Clubs \u2013 emotional weirdness or grief
      Diamonds \u2013 physical pleasure
      Spades \u2013 physical awkwardness or disappointment

      The two players describe the date, without necessarily being graphic. First each touches on something that captures the dominant tone of the encounter. Then each player shares their character\u2019s private memory.

      Ending the game

      The game lasts at until each player has participated in least one psychology scene.

      ...

      \n I have the most of the game in my head now, and scattered around on notes. I just finished writing the intro:

      Probability Warriors

      Who would have thought that we humans were special. When you look at the great vastness of space you would think that somewhere out there there would be a being like us. And certainly that was what we thought when we for the first time came in contact with a with a living being from an other world. But as it turned out, we were the oddballs.

      They had studied us for many years. There is something about us humans that they did not understand. We do not need a coherence generator to exists. For all other spices in the universe the problem was that when they reached consciousness, the state of being aware of oneself would destabilize the quantum state, and they would disappear in incoherence. The only reason that life could exist in the universe is that there happened to be a natural coherence generator somewhere near the center of the galaxy, that was possible for the aliens to clone to build a network of generator throughout the spiral arms to keep them stable. But being depending on a coherence generator also meant to be dependent on the ones who controlled the generator.

      But humans are for some reason not governed by the same laws. We can exists without a coherence generator. We have something that, for lack of a better word, is called the soul. No one really knows what it is, but it keep us in a coherent state despite our consciousness, and therefor make us independent of any external devise to keep us stable.

      After the aliens had studied us for many years, they could not stand anymore to just watch us, jealous of how our soul made us independent. They also wanted a souls, even if it meant to steel it from the humans. But do to complex intergalactic politics, a war broke out between species over who had most right to the human souls. A battle stated to rage in the universe, and here on earth, where species after species tried to take humans as theirs. This battle has pushed humanity close to extinction.

      But now we are fighting back. After studying alien technology, scientists have gained a far deeper understanding of quantum mechanics. They have found a way to push the quantum system of the human body into a uncertain state, which is only possible for humans, because our soul prevent a total breakdown of our atomic structure. In this state the human body and mind will get superpower like properties. The body get stronger and can morph, and the mind can morph things in the environment around it. The body can also, under the right circumstances, in an instance travel through time and space. But there is on downside with the uncertain state, and that is you will easily make irreversible damage to the foundation of your being, if you do not disconnect yourself from your humanity.

      A few people has been selected to be lifted into the uncertain quantum state, so they will gain power to save humanity. They will travel through space and destroy the alien's coherence generators, and in the end destroy the first coherence generator. This will forever free the earth from the threat of aliens.

      These selected few are called the Probability Warriors.

      (Mechanics follows in a day or two)

      - Anders

      \n So I've had the game finished for quite a while, but I wanted to do some layout for it. But I haven't had the time, and I won't have any in the nearby future, so here it is. Inspired by the Bowie song The Man Who Sold the World:

      The Final Piece

      A roleplaying game for three players or more

      The Seekers
      Each of you will play a Seeker. Take a piece of paper and write on it "I seek the Truth about" followed by the thing about which you seek the truth. Examples include: "I seek the Truth about my father's murder", "I seek the Truth about the government" and "I seek the Truth about love." The more abstract, the more of a challenge will the game be for you.

      Traits
      If you are four players, each of you will also have a single Trait. A Trait can be a personality trait, a skill or an attitide, or similar. Try not to pick something unique or highly unusual. If you are three players, or five or more, see the end of the document for rules, but finish reading the game first.

      The Truth
      Each player will also play the Truth that the player to her left seeks. Think of an answer to the search of the player's Seeker, and make sure that the answer is something that could conceivably cause the Seeker to lose her mind.

      Framing scenes
      Each player frames scenes for her own Seeker. The scene should be about the Seeker trying to get a step closer to her Truth. Although the scenes are framed by the Seeker's player, the Seeker's Truth is the one who acts as a GM in the scene after it has been framed. At some point in the scene, there will be a need for resolution to occur. Resolution should only be invoked when the Seeker does something -- it is never a passive thing, nor an attack of some sort from the outside. Rather, the cards are drawn when the Seeker attempts to find something out. Thus, it is only the Seeker, never the Truth, who calls for the cards to be drawn.

      Resolution
      When the cards are called for, the Truth will deal a single face-up card. The Seeker can call for more cards, one at a time, until she doesn't want any more. When no more cards are to be drawn, look at the following things:

      The sum of the cards are 25 or more: Face cards (jacks, queens and kings) are worth ten, and aces are worth fifteen. If the sum is 25 or more, the action that the Seeker made when he called for the cards to be drawn, is successful. If the sum is not 25, he suffers some misfortune (injury, scorn, loved ones shunning her, or similar).

      There are at least two red cards: The Seeker gains a piece of the Truth as told by the player playing it. One piece of the puzzle falls into place. In addition, the player gains another Trait. This Trait needs to be the same as one of the other Seekers has. Thus, there are only four traits in play, in total, and all are defined at character creation.

      For every spade, the character gains one point of Madness.

      When the Seeker doesn't want more cards and the resolution is done and narrated, all cards of the club suit are discarded, and the rest are shuffled into the deck.

      Becoming a Finder
      When the Seeker acquires the third piece of the Truth, and thus gains the final Trait, the Truth player will reveal the entire Truth that she was seeking, and the Seeker becomes a Finder.

      When it is a Finder's turn to frame a scene, she will frame it as a meeting with any Seeker who is not yet Lost (see below). The Finder will impart some wisdom or shed some light on the situation (if necessary, the Sekker's Truth player can tell the Finder what the Turh is). In the Seeker's next conflict (which will happen in her own scene; there are no conflicts in Finder-Seeker scenes), she will have the power to ignore the first spade drawn, thanks to the enlightenment provided by the Finder.

      Becoming Lost
      If a Seeker's Madness is ever two points higher than her number of Traits, the Seeker is Lost. If this happens as a result of a conflict that would make her a Finder, the Seeker is Lost instead. Being Lost is a descent into madness. Narrate it with appropriate flair, and leave that character for the time being. She can appear as an extra in any scene, but can have no more scenes of her own.

      Endgame
      Endgame is triggered when all Seekers have become Finders or Lost. At this point, every player draws a single card. The Finders sum theirs, and the Lost sum theirs. The result is compared, and a winner is chosen. In case of a tie, the Lost win.

      If the Finders win, the Lost are found and all is revealed. The Finders will know the final Truth of the world. Do not narrate this. Feel free to discuss what you think the final Truth is, however.

      If the Lost win, the Finders are dragged with them into madness. All is lost and dark.

      Playing with three players
      With three players, Seekers do not begin the game with a Trait. Rather, they gain their first in the first conflict. When three Traits have been named, all in all, Seekers will start to gain Traits already defined by others.

      Playing with five or more players
      With five players, there are five Traits in play, in total, and every Seeker starts with two of them, with three left to be discovered during play. With six players, all begin with three Traits, and so on.
