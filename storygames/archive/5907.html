[Nobilis] gets a revised reprint announced, plus the first section of Society of Flowers is out

\n
\n
\n
\n\ngamera_spinning \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2008 \n edited March 2008 in Directed Promotion
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Rebecca Borgstrom has released "Unlikely Flowerings", the first part of the long-awaited Society of Flowers supplement for Nobilis as a 115 page pdf at Drivethrurpg for $5. It's also available for free at Eos' website here, but purchasing it from DTRPG will show your support for the author, her efforts and improve the chances of seeing the rest of the book.

Nobilis is also getting a reprint by Eos Press. The reprint will be revised and twice as thick as the 2nd edition, sized at 8.5" x 11", contain new art, a new visual style, and content from The Game of Powers.

On the one hand, I'm overjoyed to see this game coming back into circulation, and I wish the publisher well in producing what is effectively a new edition of the book. On the other hand, Eos Press has a spotty history when it comes to delays in design, printing and distribution of their products, so we may not be seeing this for quite a long time to come.

Thoughts?

    \n That's awesome news that Society of Flowers and Nobilis will both see new light. I do worry a bit about it in the hands of Eos, but it really depends on who's doing editorial and layout work. It would be hard to top Bruce Baugh, Carol Johnson, and James Wallis. It's nice to hear that the art will be all new, since I didn't think the stuff in either edition (aside from the covers) was all that evocative of the game. And YAY for incorporating parts of Game of Powers into the core, since I always thought it made a better tabletop system and the tabletop version made a better larp. Moving to a standard size is kinda sad, but the layout of Game of Powers proves that it can work fairly well for Nobilis.

    All in all, great news for my favorite under-appreciated indie game.

    \n That's interesting, Mickey. I find the basic rules of Nobilis to be about the most straightforward of any game I can think of. The main difficulty, I think, is in a lot of the little subsystems that govern the supplemental Rites (especially the Rite of Holy Fire and Nettle Rite, which get used all the time). Hopefully, those will get a better treatment in the new edition. I'm sorry your GMs didn't enjoy the mechanics or think they could communicate them effectively.

    I totally sympathize with small press publishers trying to put out a book on a budget, but I still think there are ways to do that and still have a book that's readable and looks nice. I often get the sense from Eos that they take on these huge projects, several hundred page hardcover books that are full color or have extremely complex layouts (like Weapons of the God, Unhallowed Metropolis, Nobilis, etc.), end up with long delays, and in the end have to rush to get the books done, because they have a large audience demanding them. And because they print in large numbers, they have to wait for the books to arrive from overseas and have to deal with all the issues that come with printing from a distance, including long delays due to printing errors or whatnot. I mean, sure, it's totally up to them how they run their operation, but I feel like there are choices available that might make some of that easier. However, Eos is a small publisher that seems committed to publishing projects like a mid-range publisher (like Green Ronin or Mongoose), which means they're constantly straining because they don't have the capital, staff, or resources of a larger operation. And that worries me, because it seems like a complex balancing act and I want Eos to keep producing interesting projects. But as a small operation with big money invested in each of their products, it seems like one or two unsuccessful products could jeopardize their future. And that would be really unfortunate.

    Still, that's just my impression from a distance. Perhaps things look very different at ground zero.
