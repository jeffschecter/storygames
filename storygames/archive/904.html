Tactical Play and Fortune

Christian_Griffen
July 2006 edited July 2006 in Story Games
I'm reading through Iron Heroes these days. For those who don't know the game, it's a variant for 3rd Ed. D&D. It includes good concepts, one of them being tokens. See, you can gather different tokens depending on your character class (or feat), and then use those tokens for special effects such as attack bonuses, causing penalties, etc.

I immediately notice, however, that you have to expend the token before your attack roll (i.e., it's Fortune at the End). That kind of mechanic has always been frustrating to me. Here I am, building up a resource, then spending it on a potential effect, and if I miss my roll, it's completely wasted (most of the time; not if it was a bonus to attack, but if it was a bonus to damage, bonus v. armor, penalty, etc.). For me, that sucks.

I'd like it to be like this: I roll, then I can spend a token to bring about a special effect. In other words, a Fortune in the Middle type of mechanic that avoids the frustration of wasting precious resources on a bad roll.

My question is: what are the advantages of having it the FitE way? Why is the design like that? I can't think of any reason to allow a waste of precious resources other than maybe some risk/gambling involved (or maybe balancing concerns with regular D&D?), but for me, that would wear thin really fast after wasting my resource enough times. The combination of having very few tokens (at least at low levels) that do relatively little and then can potentially wasted just ruin the whole mechanic for me.

So again: what am I not seeing here? Does it work out better in actual play? And, more broadly, when you design (or choose) a game, why do/would you have a FitE mechanic for scarce resources?

    Gambling falls short of what's going on here.

    Choosing to spend a token before a roll means that I increase the cost to myself, but I receive no proprotionate (or, as may be more apt for gambling, disproportionate) reward or possibility of reward for doing so. The reward is no larger. I've increased my _likelihood_ of success, but I have not changed the nature of succes and, in fact, I'm paying more for it.

    In gambling terms, this equates roughly to buying more numbers at the roulette wheel. I increase my chances of winning with each additional bet, but I diminish my overall reward. Now, that's not to say there's no appeal to it - the same logic encourages us to buy multiple lottery tickets because the perceived value of a win is so much greater than the additional investment, whereas the roulette table makes the additional cost more obvious.


    I'm confident that everyone present appreciates just how compelling a true element of gambling can be, and it seems rude to assume otherwise, so the question is really, does the token system provide those benefits?

    For me, the question comes back to what the stakes are. If one perceives the token to be the stakes...well, that seems like a sucker's game. I spend tokens, and I do not get them back, not much fun there. On the other hand, if the stakes of the scene are the stakes, then I'm uncertain how the tokens tie into things - those stakes don't change. If the proposal is that it's actually some sort of currency-exchange of tokens and scene, I'd have to ask for an explanation of how that works.

    So, to my mind, spending a token before the roll has none of the thrill of gambling - all of the thrill that I can get from the situation already exists in the die roll I'm going to make. With that in mind, the gambling benefit seems non-existant, and by extension, can be disposed of without regret in favor of a system that provides some other benefit.

    Obviously, I think that there _are_ benefits to spending after a roll, but I don't think that needs elaborating. The question is whether those benefits are outweighed by something else.

    -Rob D.

    PS - For the record, if there is some "Double or nothing" or similar component in the expenditure of tokens that adds some genuine gambling component (which is to say, a little risk/reward goodness) that I'm unaware of, I would be delighted to hear about it.

    PPS - Similarly, this is a less broad defense of fortune at the end mechanics. Most of the better ones genuinely have a gambling component, and use it to good effect.

    - Some classes require spending actions to get them
    - Some Tokens are earned via Feats- actions, conditions, etc.
    - Some things let you spend Tokens for things that are a gamble (+2 to hit, bonus to damage, etc.)
    - Some things let you spend Tokens for a SURE thing (instant healing, move an extra square, etc.)

    But sucker bets? Those make up a LOT of game systems, particularly a lot of point-build games. Often you'll find that there's 1-3 winning strategies, and everything else is a waste of resources- and hardcore groups quickly figure out those strategies or produce their own houserules to make up the difference. A lot of "system mastery" we've seen is the ability to weed out sucker bets, and not actual in-play mastery of tactics.

    While it's related, the other big impact is on the economy of knowledge. Take the basic question of "How hard is it to succeed at this?" Sometimes a player knows, sometimes they don't, and their spendign pattern will depend heavily on these things.

    If the player does not know the difficulty, then the incentive is to spend your resources based on the importance of the roll, rather than the difficulty. In this situation, the advantage in spending after the fact is still present, but is not as pronounced because even after the roll, an expenditure can be wasted.

    If the player knows the difficulty, the incentive is to spend the resources when it will have the most optimal impact, which is to say, based on the _difficulty_ of the roll, and the likliehood of success with or without the bonus. The incentive to spend beforehand exists, but the incentive to spend after the roll is far more powerful as it usually creates a "sure thing".

    From a pure game-playing perspective, neither approach is really better - hidden information is as much of an appealling component of play as calculated risk. Certain other factors, like how difficulties are determined, can play into this - as an example, if the difficulties are hidden, but can be reasonably approximated by players with skill, you have an interesting situation where player knowledge/skill may transform the situation. However, keeping aware of the interplay gives the designer a good valve to adjust for how he wants player decisions to go.

    -Rob D.
