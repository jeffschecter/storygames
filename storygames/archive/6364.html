[IAWA] The World (House Rule to emphasize environment)

\n
\n
\n
\n\nRy \n\n\n
\n
\n\nApril 2008 \n edited April 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Sometimes, a character positions themselves into an advantage above and beyond what's represented by their forms. This kind of environmental advantage can be captured with a special Particular Strength, awarded by the GM.

Pick a d12 with an interesting color, that's the World.

The world is unique, so only one character can have it at a time. The World can be used with any forms.

The GM can award or take away the World from any character at any time EXCEPT during an action sequence where it's being rolled.

Note that, like any Particular Strength, using the World makes your character less likely to go on the Owe List. Also, only one die for a Particular Strength can be used at a time, so it cannot be combined with a Particular Strength. You can still describe actions in terms of a Particular Strength employed, but you won't roll the dice for it.

    \n I was going to start this at today's session but it got cancelled, and next week will be some new players so I'll hold off on house rules then.

    The reason for the rule is that IAWA doesn't have a mechanic for environmental or situational advantage. If I prepare really well for a battle, I'm using my forms. If I suck at that preparation, I'm using my forms.

    The World is supposed to give that well-positioned or well-prepared character an edge, and emphasize the environment. That (I hope) will help encourage things like "I run up the temple steps, using height as an advantage" which flesh out the scenery around the characters.

    \n
    \nWhat we tend to do in IAWA is have the dice rolls indicate to us how well our character used elements such as the environment to their advantage and then we fill in the blanks with the narration. If I roll poorly, depending on the character, I don't imagine my character looking like a fool tripping over himself. Instead I try to imagine what else could have gone wrong, environmentally, distractions, selling old injuries and so on and then justify the bad roll by fleshing out the scenery.
    \n
    \nThat being said, the dice in IAWA are all about inspiring improv and tension. So I'd love to hear how these changes affect the game.
