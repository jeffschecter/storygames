    \n - Roleplaying Games will be ubiquitous.

    - You will play RPGs at school, at work, with family, while doing homework, in therapy, in job interviews, and on dates.

    - You will play with people all over the world but it will feel like they are in your living room.

    - You will play with people who don't speak the same language.

    - You will create your own RPG rule systems on demand by selecting a series of techniques you can mix and match to achieve specific goals with a guide that will live playtest your custom system for you as you decide what rules to use.

    - Your game will be public to the world and anyone can join in.

    - You will play in public spaces dedicated for RPGs.

    - You will wear virtual costumes and alter your voice with modulators.

    - You will play RPGs as a professional sport.

    - You will be rewarded cash or prizes for in game achievements.

    - You will not use physical dice, pencils, or paper.

    - Someone will ignore all of the above and still play RPGs exactly the way we play now.

    Best,
    John

    \n There will be a renaisance in role playing starting in the mid 20's in elder centers. As the core D+D audience of the 70's and 80's start moving into retirement they will have time like they haven't since college. They won't have a lot of money and RPGs are cheap.

    We are also just tech phobic enough to not be totally dominated by electronic games. Now Gen X and Y, I'm not so certain about.

    Look at miniatures gaming as a paralell. It has not had any pretentions about going mainstream since the 70's. We are now in the golden age of figure availability. You can buy figures in many different scales for practically every period of history imaginable. It has become art. RPGs should be so lucky.

    I'm not certain if this is totally optomistic but I think it is more up beat that down beat. I'm hoping to be alive in 2050 to see how it plays out. I'll be real old then...

    \n Optimistically:

    * There will still be in-person roleplaying, either in actual space or virtually, but the person-to-person live improvisational quality of the experience won't die out

    * There will be some really terrific integration of truly useful, not just novel, automation tools into the experience

    * Many of the designs and ideas behind many game designs of the early 21st century will show up as training and thought exercise tools for more practical applications: corporate training, therapy, sustainability modeling, whatever. those models will highlight philosophical as well as mathematical differences in a more transparent way than games do today.

    * There will still be a huge, huge market for creating amazing, immersive synthetic worlds. They will of course have the advantage of actual immersion via direct sensory input or whatever. I think the right model could even incorporate collective/democratized design. But I feel like that'll be its own hobby, Sim City writ large. Will players with an interest in story/interaction be able to access your world/creation's API? That would be neat, or possibly disastrous.

    * Optimistically, MMOs will continue down a sufficiently different development path that the differences in experience with tabletop gaming will become even more obvious.

    \n In a world where convenience and high production values have become assumed and thus taken for granted, the first world will become obsessed with genuine, unscripted experiences instead of "fake" ones. RPGs will be the haven of luddites, spiritualists, hippies, revolutionaries, and some faction of the "we're too cool for you" ironic hipster crowd. This trend will pick up a decent number of imitators, and the hobby will produce multiple waves of New Hotnesses with mass reception.

    "I game with my friends in person" will be the new "I drive a hybrid car".

    Maybe this doesn't sound appealing to some, but the optimist in me assumes that the actual gaming itself will still be cool, so hey, the more the merrier.
