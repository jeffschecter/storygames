\n So I was watching The Tin Drum last night, which is awesome by the way. Even Graham would say it's awesome, it's that awesome. Did I mention awesome? Morningstar, have you seen this?

It got me thinking about antagonists, and how they usually get what they want...at first. In contrast, protagonists rarely get what they want, and their initial want has a serious penalty from the start(I want to stop growing, I want to leave Tatooine). Protagonists usually catch a glimmer of their destiny(joining the circus). It's a neat contrast.

How would antagonists fail, other then never being transformed by their want?
How do protagonists fail? Never realize their destiny?
Is this great for story?

    \n This is an interesting topic:

    Part of what makes a protagonist convincing is strong and effective opposition to their goals. Just like how you have to earn the right to be on the Owe List in In a Wicked Age...

    Dealing with antagonists as major characters in a story is trickier! For instance, if you have a villain who tries to enact a plan, but is thwarted every step of the way and ends up looking like an underdog... it often feels like they are actually the protagonist of the story, regardless of how nefarious their goals may be. A good example of this kind of subversion is Joss Whedon's "Dr. Horrible".

    \nTransformation? You're going to have to elaborate. Is it a process of character 'growth' wherein the antagonist becomes 'monstrous' and is consequently vanquished for his greed/hubris? If so, I'd comment that many villains simply don't have this 'transformation' - Ming is always merciless, Lecter is always psychotic and Vader even goes for redemption.

    Redemption being, in my mind, the failure of villainy. Ineffectiveness cannot be this failure; if a villain is ineffective at villainy, he's not a villain. An antagonist may be thwarted, but must always pose a real threat. As I see it, redemption or defeat are the only two options for antagonistic failure.
