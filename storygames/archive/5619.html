\n Heya,

I'm in a bit of pickle for a sci-fi game I want to run. One of my players is really into Techies. You know, the guys who are the mechanics and tinkerers in the future. I want to make up some Kewl Powerz for techies, but honestly I can't think of any. We're playing a game sorta like Mechwarrior if you remember that RPG from back in the '90s, but honestly the system doesn't matter at the moment. I just need some ideas for cool things techies can do.

Some examples of what I'm thinking is how Han Solo bangs the bulkhead of the Millenium Falcon in Empire Strikes Back (I think) to get the electronics restarted, all the crazy things MacGyver pulled off in his show, or how Mr. Scott always pulled off a miracle in the origonal Star Trek series.

If you guys have any experience playing these sorts of characters, share your ideas. Even if it's only just snipets of actual play where your character did something cool, or a summary of a passage in a sci-fi novel you read that sticks with you as cool "techie" stuff. I really appreciate your help. I don't want this guy to be left out because the starfighter pilot and pistoleer have so many cool abilities of their own.

Peace,

-Troy

    \n A lot of times, at least in the games I've played, the tech guys don't get the glory shots when combat rolls around. They hang out in the background while the pilots get all the thunder and lightning. That being said, here are some of my suggestions:

    * Critical failure override -- The mech (or whatever) takes a critical hit, and it basically flatlines. Your techie is the only one who can get it operational again.

    * Improvised weaponry -- Current munitions do nothing against this particular foe. Someone's got to come up with a special weapon that will destroy the enemy. Who will it be? The seductive space vixen? The cocky space captain? The enigmatic space chef? The... techie?

    * Crazy bullshit con artist technobabble -- On Stargate SG-1, Carter (the resident brain) is always spouting some high-tech mumbo-jumbo, and the rest of the team is all like man what. This is never exploited! Primarily, this is due to the fact that Carter is an ethical person. What tripe. I say use this stuff to hypnotize people! Your techie can spit out a stream of incomprehensible terms and jargon like an indie gamer explaining Simulatious Drift to someone who doesn't even know what OBGYNS (or whatever) stands for. This can be used to access secured areas, get into exclusive nightclubs, meet with people who don't want to be met, and to obtain special, uh, stuff. Laser gun parts, or stuff of that caliber. You know what I'm saying here, I don't need to spell it out for you guys.

    \n I work for VMF-25. We're a recce unit attached to General Pratt's I Corps. Ten machines, and they are thirty year old bastards on the wrong side of their design lifespans. On a good day we get two in the field at any one time, and it isn't the same two all day if you know what I mean. A few are little better than gutted corpses and I feel bad about that, but our supply lines are a little tenuous out here on the frontier, you know? I'm the internal systems guy - my job is to crawl into the guts of these things and keep the wiring straight, the avionics operating, all the sensor modules humming with 100% field uptime. Well, call it 90%, right? Anyway, it's complex stuff and the pilots all know my name, do me favors to tweak their HUDs or whatever. They like me because I used to be a pilot and I get them - everybody else thinks they are assholes, which is not an unfair assessment. When I'm not all up in their machines I like to scrounge what I can from the bone yard - the other day I got a sweet little PWT array from a smashed-up Hornet; I put it in Mech G and it runs great, although it's false-color and no LIDAR. Last year during the evacuation of Croesus I had the smart idea of rigging enemy IFF transponders on our mechs and it saved our bacon. I got those from some spooky dude working for S2; it's all about who you know and I know everybody.

    \n
    \n
    • Make a device more powerful at the price of either increased failure range or timed burnout.
      • \n
    • Cobble together working devices from broken ones. "The power supply from your laser will help me repair your overloaded life-support systems until we can get you back home." Having more broken stuff around can give you a bigger bonus to fixing things.
      • \n
    • Restore mecha HP, or give them temporary HP that lasts until the patched pieces get shot off.
      • \n
    • Convert one kind of equipment into another. ("I took the fuel from your rocket launcher and built your a jump-booster!")
      • \n
    • Create defenses against specific attacks at the cost of particular weaknesses - you paint the mecha silver so lasers are less effective, but now it's extra-visible on radar and easier to hit.
      • \n
    • Jury-rig power sources for mecha in the field that need to recharge. Solar, wind, 10,000 gerbils, etc. I love the image of an Exalted Warstrider sitting against a windmill with some cables running back and forth.
      • \n
    • The Main Villain Escape Plan effect, where he can spend his Awesome Points to retroactively be prepared for an unexpected event. "There's a door in the way? Good thing I brought my mini fusion cutter!"
      • \n
    • Make rolls to boost certain parts of the mech on a round-by-round basis. It's the "Scotty, more power to the engines!" ability.
      • \n
    • Find weak points in enemy mecha that result in a greater critical hit range or special effects. "Those models are weak in the lower back; hit him there and his whole hydraulic system will start leaking." Have the player state the fact before rolling, to see whether he was right.
      • \n
    • Let him work on his experimental stuff for several sessions in a row, and make them into advancements that many people can use! Real technology works that way, why shouldn't game tech?
      • \n
    • Have rivalries between different techies. "Bah, this is Von Puffenstuff's work! He might pack a punch, but he makes his power supplies out of used cardboard."
    \nAn excellent example of the technobabble ability is Lucca from ChronoTrigger, who puts her enemies to sleep with it.

    Tie the stats for the team's equipment to the skills and abilities of the techie.

    \n

    Random example pulled from Firefly and Serenity:

    \n

    Kaylee is a better mechanic than the surfer-dude she replaced. Therefore, Serenity's stats (speed, breakdown %, whatever) are better under Kaylee's care than they were under Surfer-Dude. Without a mechanic, Serenity is a lump of metal. It has no stats and does not get from place to place. In effect, the ship's abilities are just an extension of the mechanic's abilities and no more.
