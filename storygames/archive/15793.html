    Anyway, everyone sitting down and talking about their characters and the premise of the game and what the characters actually DO in the game is a great thing to do, and in your shoes that would be what I would do next session. No actual game, just working out what the game is going to be.

    The players obviously are trying to pull the game in different directions, but so are you: from the example you gave, when they tried to be proactive and deal with situations in ways that pushed the game towards what they thought would work, you took some steps to block them (your powers have no effect, you need to pass all these skill checks, the NPCs try to stop you, etc.). The only message that sends is, "I don't care how you want to deal with this situation, because the only way you will succeed is if you deal with it the way I expect you to." When I've done that to players in the past, it hasn't worked out so great for any of us. They feel (correctly) that they are not playing the game they want to play, and I feel (also correctly) that I am doing a bad job of steering the game in the best/most interesting/most fun direction.

    So that's my advice in a nutshell: don't play the game again until you've all talked this through and figured out what the PCs do in this game, and how they fit into the setting. I'm sure there's common ground between all of you where you can all have fun. Our gaming group has gone through this before and will inevitably go through it again, and taking a session off to establish that common ground has always paid off for us. I think it will for you, too.


    As far as unasked-for advice goes, personally, I like to have the first big event in a game be something that's well matched to the PCs' abilities, something that they could absolutely face head on if they chose to, because I'd rather that the first bit of feedback they get about how the game's going to work be positive (you can solve this and prevail) rather than negative (you are an insignificant speck who sucks at your job), even if the only idea they can come up with is brute force. I save the big guns, the things they absolutely cannot face head on, for later on. That way, when they try and fail against the Big Bad, it might be seen as a challenge -- we can find another way! -- and not as some kind of power trip to keep them down.

    I also try very hard to not form (or at least not get attached to) preconceived notions about how the PCs could solve a problem. If I'm doing it right, then I'll be listening to their plan and thinking about how that COULD work, not why it WON'T work. It's pretty rare that the people I play with come up with a completely terrible plan for anything, so I'm okay with just setting up the situation and letting them tell me what the solution to it is. Even if I'm using a situation where there is one critical action needed to succeed (put the gem back in the idol's mouth, save the princess), I try to avoid creating one critical path that must be followed to perform that action. (ie, I don't care HOW they get the gem back in the idol's mouth, they can come up with whatever scheme they want and if it's at all plausible I'm willing to let it work, even if it's not what I would have done.)

    I do think about potential failure, though, so that I can try and come up with something interesting and not utterly terrible to present to them if their genius plan is executed poorly or just wasn't genius enough. But again, if my goal is to have the players drive the story, I do my best to not get attached to any of those ideas; they are raw material that I can draw upon to respond to what the players gave me, not things I need to force the players into.

    \n One thing that doesn't seem to have come up yet.

    Explicitly discuss character mortality.

    "So, are you guys OK with your characters dying horribly because you made a bad judgement call? Are you OK with TPK?"

    The sort of game you seem to be aiming for often does feature a relatively high character mortality, and if the players have bought into it a TPK doesn't have to be a terrible downer. "Damn, we really messed that one up, didn't we?"

    On the other hand, if you want to tune mortality rate down, just take a page from Fate make defeat in combat mean whatever seems most interesting instead of death. (Stripped of valuables and left for dead, captured, etc.)

    Also:

    If the players have trouble picking up your hints of how dangerous something is, make it explicit. "You know about local gods like this. They're insanely powerful." You know: instead of trying to evoke a mood by describing a dark and brooding forest in detail, tell them that the forest is scary -- and then fill in the details. Subtlety is all well and good, but clear communications trump it 9 times out of 10.

    If a character tries a tactic that isn't going to work because of ... stuff, and you think their character should know it -- tell them.

    "Um, the chasm is 100 yards wide and a mile deep. When you say you jump it, do you mean jump across, or jump into it? Because you know, you can't jump 100 yards, and if you jump into it you'll die. You know this for a fact."

    "You start the exorcism, and then realize that it isn't possesed. It's a damn god. You can't exorcise it. You might be able to banish it, if you knew how to banish things."

    You need to be damned careful with this, though. As said earlier: listen and consider why it could work, instead of automatically thinking about why it won't. Also do your best to phrase it either as "are you really sure, 'cos there are consequences" or as a failure -- not "you can't even attempt it".

    \n It's possibly the dice.

    I've played in many Storyteller games, and we're often struggling with the dice.
    There are just too many stats, and too many monsters that have WAY better stats.
    It's difficult for players to understand how they want to or should spend their points, and even harder for them to really grasp what stats and skills they need.

    And it leads to (at least once in every game) having the GM ruthlessly or accidentally slaughter a character out of the blue.

    So we stopped hiding the dice. The GM would say upfront "Look, this creature gets to roll 13 dice against you if you do that, meaning you have roughly a 2.7% chance of not dying." We trusted the players to keep that separate from the character knowledge, and it solved a ton of miscommunication errors.

    This leads to the "so what do we do instead?" And that is, admittedly, the point of giving a monster so many freaking dice. Get them thinking about less obvious weakness.

    Just my two cents. Discard as necessary.

    A West March-style game is a deadly sandbox. You're supposed* to be a neutral arbiter, and not telling a story. It's fine to foreshadow and warn players, but if they tread into danger and die, so be it. They won't make that mistake again.

    I had to reboot my Hinterlands sandbox. After a few adventures and several near-TPK situations, I understood that the players weren't interested in a true sandbox.     * If you want it to be a West March game, anyway.
