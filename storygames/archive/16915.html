    \n Semi-pro designer (technical manuals, but also marketing collateral). I'll give it a first thoughts:
    * Too much margin--old school didn't have the money to waste mimeograph paper. They ran text to the walls. Especially, your header area is vast. Go back to the source--check out some old AD&D class-specific "orange sheets". [Full site] By that scaling and aesthetic, the information on your character sheet would fit about 1/6th of a sheet of Letter paper.
    * Too much black--that mimeograph fluid wasn't cheap, man!
    * Typeface choices are good--very much in keeping with the somewhat schizophrenic 70s aesthetics (modern, clear, san-serif typefaces mixed with blubbery, serif heading types--the latter come to dominate D&D sheet designs by AD&D).
    * I think I see where you're going with the tape effect. I wouldn't follow you there, but that's a purely artistic choice.
    * Try different background textures, ideally real or high-res ones. That painterly effect isn't jibing with what you're trying to do (which, I think, is to make it look like yellowing paper that's thirty years old). Whatever texture, go subtle--I see some of the blobs of that texture "pop" more than your headings!
    * I'd better-organize the rules reference (especially since you're going to have all kinds of extra space when you adjust margins!) Bullets are OK, numbers with parentheses after them are better (a modern technical design no-no, but AH, the look!). Tables and Charts, too! What an opportunity to have a "Table 1: 1D bonuses for:" and "Table 2: 1P penalties for:"! Hehe... table enumeration on a character sheet MIGHT be just a bit too twee, though! But definitely make the bonus and penalty dice bit a table, and the general rolling procedure an enumerated list!

    That's all I got, fast and dirty reviewing. As with anything aesthetic, YMMV, my 2\xa2, eye of the beholder... all that.
    David
