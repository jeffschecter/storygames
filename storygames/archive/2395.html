Awesome-o-fy... History!

tony_dowler

At risk of wearing out the therm "Awesome-o-fy"...

Since I'm writing an alternate history game, I've been doing a lot of brainstorming on alternate and secret history storylines. Lately I've been playing with a technique for awesoming up historical figures to use as game seeds. Here's how it works: take a real historical figure. Take some real facts about them and twist them so they become over the top adventure fodder. Here are a few I've come up with:

Thomas Hobbes is nearing completion of his magnum opus, the Leviathan, a giant steam-powere war machine powered by royal blood.

Shakespeare and his sorcerous collaborator Francis Bacon summon otherworldy spirits using ancient Greek theatrical magic. In return for having their stories told on the stage, the spirits carry out secret missions for the crown, helping establish colonies in the New World, sinking Spanish galleons, and rooting out French agents in the British Isles.

Hieronymus Bosch, a half-mad painter with second sight creates terrifying portraits that reveal the secret faults and sins of his subjects. His most recent work, a huge tryptich landscape of the City of Brussels entitled The Garden of Earthly Delights shows the locations of doorways to Other dimensions and additional Secrets Man Was Not Meant To Know.

Now it's your turn.

    Sounds vaguely familiar! I find that if you do enough research, you'll find stories that are compelling enough on their own. The thing that they often lack, or rather, the thing that they require that not every member of the audience will understand, is the context of their actions. Thomas Paine wrote Common Sense, and everybody knows that. He also was a bit of a tinkerer, working on early steam engines, and made a harrowing transatlantic journey to get to America because that's where the action was. Unfortunately, "working on early steam engines" and "harrowing transatlantic journey" don't really sound or feel as weighty as they were if you don't understand the level and rate of technological advancement or what sea voyages were like. So what I'm doing is taking the structure of history's stories and just punching up the specifics a little -- so Thomas Paine builds an ornithopter and flies across the Atlantic. You still get "tinkerer" in there, you still get "heroic transatlantic journey," but now the details are viscerally impressive, rather than just cerebreally impressive.

    Besides, Ethan Allen is cooler if the Green Mountain Boys ride moose into battle.

Heartbroken and by now quite mad, Pershing recruits eager young officers he encounters during the Mexican Punitive Expedition into his cult - junior officers like his staff Adjutant, George Patton, and a young turk named Douglas MacArthur.

    Colin, John --

    Some history does not need much to 'awesome-ofy' it. Seriously, the only additions I really made to Ethan Allen is that he and the Green Mountain Boys rode moose into battle and that he was a member of the secret society Sons of Liberty. The rest -- the independent republic of Vermont, playing the US Congress and the British off of each other, the inciting rebellions within the US just to make a point -- is right out of history. Ethan Allen is pretty straight-up and obviously awesome, so he needs less work. Others, like Thomas Paine, were awesome all on their own, but were not obviously and plainly awesome. You have to understand their historical context in order to understand their awesome.

    Now, in a long-form game where you immerse and get to know the historical background and cultural context and everything, the players can learn how awesome these elements of history are. For my purposes in this game, which is pick up and play and runs in one session, that's not an option. SoL is high-color, visceral, and crazy-go-nuts so that it can be immediately appreciated.

    (Now, as a tangent, if one effect of the game is to strike up a little interest in the players as to who these figures really were, and separating out the fact from the awesomofying fiction, that would be really neat. It's one of my second-order hopes for the design.)

    You could totally play a "straight" game set in the Revolutionary War and include and get to know the effect that, say, social networks played in the war (ie, they made the very war possible). However, one way to get across that importance of social networks is to ratchet up the intensity of the facts. Instead of "just" committees of correspondence, the game has this giant networking conspiracy that links together hundreds of patriots through diverse means including Freemasonry, Quilting Circles, churches, and artisan guilds. The irony, of course, is that the fiction often comes around to being very close to the truth of the matter. As another example, it's hard to get modern players to understand the impact of, say, artillery in the Revolutionary War (ie: this was the war where they kind of figured out how to use it effectively). They're just cannon, right, and smoothbore manual-loading versions -- low tech! How could that be revolutionary? So my solution is to replace them with freakin laser beams, and the "point" is not lost.

    To me, it boils down to two issues: time and taste. In the time corner, awesomeofying history gets across the emphases faster than explaining the context of the facts. In the taste corner... dude, George Washington in clockwork power armor on a clockwork horse.
