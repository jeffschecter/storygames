I frequently find myself coming back to my Sorceror books, and have indeed played it, but still get hung up enough on the problems that I just keep it aside. So, goods/bads:

* The die mechanic has been, in my group's experience, a bit high in the handling time. We did it as we should - just "What's your highest die? How many of yours beat my 7?" - but quite simply, in practice, for us, given our very subjective and finnicky aesthetics, IMHO, *it was a drag*. So I'm interested in a mechanic that doesn't have the "Sorceror/Donjon" kind of approach to the die pool; maybe I'd even prefer something without a die pool at all.
* Also: the die mechanic is really, really swingy, meaning both good (the little guy can triumph!) and bad (the big guy can continously be inept!). It's a balance thing, and I think I'd prefer a slightly less swingy mechanic, where earning bonus dice was a little more helpful on your side.
** w.r.t. the "inept" thing, it doesn't have to be a "whiff" per se using Fortune-in-the-Middle techniques, but even with that it wasn't quite right for our preferences
* I like the ideas behind the combat system (combining action roll + initiative with the tactical question of aborting for defense, etc.) although they were was somewhat high handling time in practice. (That might have just been experience though; I'd feel comfortable enough with Sorceror combat.)
* I do like the dice economy of score -> dice -> successes -> bonuses -> penalties. I do like the implementations of contact/summoning/binding/humanity to describe the demonic relationship. I like the very few stats!

This morning, I came up with a die mechanic I really like. It seems compatible with Sorceror and _somewhat_ keeping within its sprits; it also yoinks gracefully from HeroQuest, which had it coming.

*The Rules:* Wherever you normally roll N dice on your side, instead roll a single d10 [or a d12? I'm not sure]. (Just as you'll always roll at least 1 die, your N is always at least 1.) If the result is greater than your N, it's a "good" success; if it's less or equal, it's "great". Announce the number of your result and if it's good/great. (So I roll a 4 with my Lore, which is 5. I announce: "4-great".) Compare both sides' results:

* great vs good: the "great" result wins, and the winner's die result is the number of successes
* great vs great: the higher result wins, and the difference in die results is the number of succeses
* good vs good: the higher result wins, with 1 success
* tie: coin flip!!! (Or perhaps, simply give 1 success to the underdog.)

EDIT: Clever rephrase. Great results always beat good results. Otherwise, the higher result wins. A "good" winner only gets one success. A "great" winner gets their die roll in successes; if the loser has "great" results, these cancel those out.

And you proceed as normal.

_Bonus Dice:_ You increase your N by the bonus. If both sides have a bonus, cancel it out so only one side has a bonus. If someone has more bonus than is actually useful, that's okay. (So if you have Stamina 7 and 5 bonus dice, your N is 13, which basically just means any result will be great; that's okay.)

_Combat:_ Similar to the normal version:

# Everyone picks actions until everything is free + clear.
# Everyone rolls their die, and this will be their roll for their action.
# Proceed in order of highest-die-result to lowest. (Yes, ignoring whether it's "great' or "good" for you.)
## EDIT: Actually, great results must go before good ones.
# To defend before you've used your action, you can either:
## Roll a separate die with an N of 1 (keeping your current result)
## Abort your action and roll defense, using whatever score you want.
# To defend after you've used your action, roll as normal.

...

So I think this can actually work. The die mechanics will have a different economy, but at first glance it doesn't look like this is too radically different. I feel like the underdog can still win, but the better player still has an advantage; you can always pull off at least 1 success, but never more successes than N (the number of dice you would have been rolling). The choice of die type definitely does matter here, so I'm not sure about my choice.

What do you all think?

    I just got back from a playtest with a friend, and we liked how it worked so far! There are some things to work out, but I'll save that for a Forge post in the A.M. Let me address Andy's example.

    Firstly, we figure out our actions until we're free and clear. (We decide we're going to beat on each other this round.) You have a clever strategy (blow sand in my eyes) so your score will be Stamina + 1 (5). I'm going to try to use my Cover (professional soldier, 5) to give me bonus dice to this action. So with a score of 5 on each die, I'm going to roll a d10 for me (9-good) against a d10 (6-good). I succeed with a 1 success, and get a +1 for my role.

    Now we roll for our actions. You roll a d10 for your score of 5: 4-great. I roll a d10 for my score of 6: 8-good. You go first, attacking me with that result. I decide to abort my action and reroll d10 for dodging with my Stamina 5: 2-great. That's 2 successes you have against me. You're going to use those as bonus dice in the next round.

    (What if I tried to suck it up instead of aborting to defend? I'd be rolling with a score of 1, so my 2 result would have been a 2-good instead of a 2-great.) (Also, I am rethinking some of the abort rules. More on that later.)

    So the next round, you're going to try to smack-talk me, while I'm going give a command to my demon. For your Willpower 4 + 2 rolled-over bonuses, your score is 6. (My willpower is 3.) So we roll: you get 2-great, I get 7-good. You go first, attacking me with the 2-great successes and "I suck it up", rolling a single d10 with a score of 1: 10-good, so you get two smack-talkin' sucesses against me.
