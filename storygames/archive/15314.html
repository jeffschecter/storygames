\n I'm trying to come up with some milestones or big events that might happen to characters after a zombie apocalypse. For example, in many zombie movies and TV shows, characters fall in love or have sex, someone often dies, characters may need to kill family members that became zombies, kids may grow up, and the group may get closer to a zombie free zone.

Here's what I have so far:

\u2022\tFall in Love and Have sex as rite-of-passage (optional)
\u2022\tSuffer someone\u2019s death and come of age
\u2022\tPersonal growth created by some set of circumstance
\u2022\tFighting with zombie parents or loved ones
\u2022\tGet closer to safety and the zombie free zone

Any ideas?

    \n Bryan, are you looking for things that will be motivational for the players/PCs, or just plot directions?

    If it's the former, I think Nathan's got the right idea. We ran a zombie-themed TSOY game years ago and wrote (or rewrote) some helpful keys for it:

    Key of the Survivor - "I. Will. Survive." Your character is determined to make it, no matter what the cost.
    Key of Terror - "Oh sweet Jesus, help me!" Refusing to help because you are afraid, even if it might get someone killed.
    Key of Trust - Through everything that's happened, the one thing you can still depend on is each other.
    Key of Wishful Thinking - "They're still people. Maybe we can help them."
    Key of Obsession - "I don\u2019t care if he\u2019s infected, I won\u2019t abandon a God-damned child!"
    Key of Overconfidence - "Nuthin' I can't handle." For going into a situation with no plan or precaution.
    Key of Indifference - "Face facts, people - not everybody here is going to make it."

    \n Just to revisit a point that's already been touched on - the one where someone you care about is a zombie.

    I think the really crucial part is the moment when you regroup from an attack, achieve a bit of rest (because you've killed the last one in immediate view, or sealed off the big heavy door, or something like that), and then you discover that the person you care about has a bite wound. They haven't died yet. They haven't turned yet. But you know they're going to and you don't have a way to stop it. So what do you do now?

    I think that moment is HUGE!

    Also, the moment where you realize that one (or more) of your living companions is a liability because they're most likely going to get some living people killed if they keep doing the stuff they're doing (hoarding stuff, dragging behind, making too much noise, second-guessing every damn thing, making stupid emotion-driven choices, whatever).

    Those two types of moments have to happen to really hit the genre on the head, I think. And they can happen a couple times each, with some variation.

    \n Apologies if this is negative (genuinely) but the OP list look too nice for a zombie game.

    If I had to characterise the genre in one word that word would be bleak. This isn't a genre about personal growth, about becoming the man or woman you were meant to be, about finding a safe place (though it is about trying to find one, in part, trying and failing).

    This is a genre about pointless, and frequently random and undeserved, death. It's a genre about there being no safe place. It's a genre which typically implies (if it doesn't outright state) that this is our extinction event. The survivors are temporary. They're the nerve impulses still flickering through our corpse.

    I can't think of a single major (or indeed minor) zombie movie in which the characters establish some new hope. At best a handful escape to an uncertain future.

    Some of the suggestions are good though. With some repetition from above:

    First kill, as Jason said.

    First kill of someone you know.

    First loss of someone you care deeply about.

    First sight of the new ruined world (the camera pans back revealing devastation, corpses and burning buildings).

    Running out of food/fuel/water and having to take a hearstopping risk because of it.

    First time you let someone down and they die.

    Rob's one. First time you deal with someone alive who's infected.

    Rob's other (he's on a roll). First time you have to deal with someone alive who's a liability to your group. What do you do?

    Also, choices. Rob's are good because they involve choices. Ted's bitten. You like Ted, but he's going to turn. What do you do?

    Four new survivors just showed up, but one has a fever and they're not willing to let you inspect them. What do you do?

    Bob's an asshole, but he's become more than that. He's hoarding food and you just found out he'd been stealing from the central supplies your group has stashed. What do you do?

    Mary's pregnant. What do you do?

    Clara's got a life threatening illness which is easily treatable with meds. Which are in any major hospital. Where the dead are. What are you going to do?

    Choices. The genre is about slow decline and the terrible choices made along the way.

    Also, Felan's is a key scene. No real choices there but it is a common feature to these things.
