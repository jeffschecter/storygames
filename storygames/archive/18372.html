\n Hi there. I'm thinking about an article for the next issue of the Larp Symposium book (the little Italian cousin of Knutepunkt).
I'm considering the idea of writing something about the possibility of organizing larps/rpgs in a more seriuos context than a roleplaying game convention.
I know for example that All the President's Zombies was part of an art exhibition.
Any other examples of that?
Despite from the title of this thread, it doesn't need to be a serious (i.e. boring) context. I'm just curious to see documented examples of larps/rpgs shown *beside* more mainstream events.
(artsy larps from Nordic countries don't count, unless they were part of a bigger non-larp event).

See also: Juhana Pettersson, Role-playing and the mainstream.

    \n Brody Condon was paid to run Level Five at the Hammer Museum in Los Angeles and somewhere in the SF Bay area. The sequel, On Stellar Rays, was in NYC.

    Mike Young's The Road Not Taken has been used in college classrooms as a tool for psychodrama.

    \xd8sterskov Efterskole is a Danish school using larp as a focusing structure for instruction.

    Seekers Unlimited is a group in Los Angeles using larp to aid classroom instruction.

    Jennisodes episode 50 is about Pete Figtree using rpgs in the classroom.

    I don't know if you want to explore the use of roleplaying in corporate training. It's super common for things like teaching customer service or IT support representatives how you want them to interact with customers. And leadership training. And for things like sexual harassment training. But those tend to be thought of as roleplaying "exercises" rather than "games". Not sure if the difference matters to you.

    ---

    Question though - you said "*beside* more mainstream events".

    Does it need to be incorporated into a larger, unifying event? Or does placement count?

    Does it count when kids are playing an RPG at lunchtime at school? (cause that's been happening since the beginning of the hobby)

    Does it count when kids are playing an RPG at school as part of an official school club and the teacher sponsor is actively participating?

    Or does it only count when it is part of classroom curriculum? (I assume it counts then. If not, I have no idea what is going to count.)

    Do larps count when they take place in public parks? Do they count if they interact with the other park goers? At the last one I went to, we had a park goer see what we were doing and think it looked great, so he made a character and played. Does that count?

    \n Also, Aaron Vanek ran a Harry Potter family day at the Rancho Cucamonga library (near Los Angeles) that was basically a larp in disguise. They had people playing Hogwarts teachers and treating the kids like they were magic students and taking them through some interactive activities, like a simple chemistry experiment called Potions Class.

    \n I was involved with running a big art event call All Worlds Fair, a large interactive art/performance event which involved a large of number artist crew each getting a room and creating an individual experiences for their room "exhibit". Some of the artists used RPG gaming as a precursor warm up/rehearsal material for their crew and some turned their exhibits into games where the audience was roped into completing quests in order to "level up" and build upon the story.

    The games that were used as inspiration and warm up included: Fiasco, The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen, A Quiet Year.
