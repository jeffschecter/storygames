\n I had a list of questions that I've expanded about gaming logistics. This touches on "social footprint" stuff but I really think of the topic as logistics. Here's the scenario:

There are people. By whatever social mechanisms, the group decides to play a roleplaying game together.\n
  1. Do the members of the group currently have generally compatible schedules?
  2. Do these schedules allow most of the members to meet regularly in person?
  3. Can you expect the schedules to be stable or adapt flexibly while maintaining a regular meeting time?
  4. Does the group have access to a comfortable, semi-private venue, such as a player's home?
  5. Can the group regularly accomodate gaming sessions of 4 hours or more?
  6. Do all members of the group have the time and inclination to do game-supporting activities away from the table before the first session?
  7. Do all or most members of the group have the time and inclination to do game-supporting activities away from the table on a regular basis?
  8. Does the group have a supply of props, miniatures, tokens, office supplies, and so on, or do they have the funds and inclination to purchase such objects?
  9. Does the venue include a large table to play at?
  10. Does the group have the resources for all members to buy a copy of one or two hardcover game books, and do some members have the money to buy several more books?

    \n One-shot pick-up games kill most of these requirements dead, which is kinda why I like them. While most roleplaying games require you to participate religiously, as if you were a hardcore fan of a TV show, there is definitely room for games that are more like boardgames or DVDs, requiring a few hours of your time and no ongoing commitment. The challenge there, as it has been with Geiger Counter, is being able to get everyone on something-sorta-resembling the same page and create a really great play experience with as little planning, prep, and background knowledge as possible. We're getting better at helping people do that, I think.

    Another option is the In A Wicked Age, loose anthology style, where things can vary a lot between sessions and still maintain a loose continuity. I haven't done as much of that, so I'm not sure I have a lot to say in that regard.

    \n I was part of something called a "Gaming Circle" a few years back. It ran really well, and went like this:

    * We had a membership of about a dozen people. Their names were ordered into a list.
    * There was a game every Saturday afternoon.
    * Each member committed to a particular Saturday, when they would run a game--a one-shot. When you signed up, you would pick your Saturday.
    * Each Sunday, whoever was the next GM would send an e-mail to the next X people on the list, where X is the number of players he or she needed for the game.
    * If some of those people couldn't make it or didn't reply, on Thursday the GM would send out a general invitation to everyone on the list. Whoever replied first would fill in the missing spot or spots.
    * After the game, the GM would e-mail the next GM, telling them where they had "left off" on the list.
    * The next GM would send out their invitation, starting from wherever the previous GM had left off.

    This worked really, really well 95% of the time. Of course, there were instances where a GM was sick or had to cancel, but it was very rare that a GM couldn't get enough people for his or her game. If you were really gung-ho, you could play almost every week, since many other people made other plans unless they were one of the initial invitees.

    So, that's an example of logistics in action. For me, being way too busy at the time to commit to any long-term game, it was perfect, and because the selected GMs would generally spend a lot of time preparing their "best one-shot", the games were very high-quality.

    Ryan, how many people were in your IaWA "pool"? I would imagine that unless your numbers were huge, after three or four games everyone would know the mechanics, theoretically speaking, right? I'd like to hear more about the problems you ran into.

    \n Interesting!

    In that case, I'm guessing the "West Marches" approach would not have worked for you, either, huh? You have to provide the impetus for the gaming within the group.

    Some possible modifications or solutions:

    1. Figure out who the "core" people are, and, when they're not present, play something else, like a one-shot or a boardgame or whatever floats your boat. Don't be afraid to cancel a session now and then.

    2. People wanting to play but then not being able to keep up to the commitment could mean you set the bar too high. Maybe instead of a weekly game, you can set some level that they CAN and WILL commit to? Like, "Hey, I want to play this game. Let's all schedule three evenings, right now, that we all commit to." After the three sessions, you can decide whether you want to play some more, make it a regular thing, stop there, etc.

    Because some people just want to try something, or don't have the time to play every week. In the long-term, regular commitment-type situation, they may be tempted to say "yes" so they don't get left out. I guess my recommendation is to a) lower the commitment level necessary, but b) require that commitment to be hardcore. This might mean people are only up for one-shots without scheduling in advance. But then you're not building expectations that no one can meet and feeling disappointed.

    \n That's an interesting question. I think a lot of it comes down to the people. I'm the kind of person that if I say I'm gonna be somewhere, I move heaven and earth to make sure I really will be there. I'm also highly motivated by roleplaying - I'll turn up to roleplaying things consistently, because I'm pretty devoted to my hobby. Possibly also I just don't have a very busy life.

    I guess in groups there is always going to be a range of enthusiasm levels, from "always, every time, no matter what" to "maybe, if I feel like it, if nothing better comes along".

    I think Ryan's got the right idea, looking for games that work at a significantly lower level of buy-in. I've long said that one of the big turn-offs to the hobby is the percieved high level of commitment required. No one would play soccer if you had to join a professional team in order to play. The fact that you can just have a kick-about with your friends now and then is what makes the game popular - it supports a range of commitment levels.

    Now, as for specific games that support low buy-in play, I think that one-shots and pick-up games are a good place to start, but possibly the problem starts with the "prepare situation, learn rules, make characters, play for hours" model of gaming. Can we imagine a game that worked a bit like a soccer kick-about, where players can drop in and out, play at any level of ability and with any number of people, and don't have to show up more than once? That seems like it would be a pretty radical design.

    \n I've had the best success when I:

    - Email 3 times the number of players I need. So 12 possibles for a 4 person game.
    - 1-2 weeks in advance (any more and people tend to drop out).
    - I keep everyone on a BCC list so no one sees how many people I have actually contacted.
    - Include most of the info they need in the email subject.
    - If people see that the email went out to 12, they feel less pressure to respond.
    - I pick a central location to play (no more than 50 minute commute for anyone).
    - Usually a weekday from 7-11pm.
    - I try to give everyone 2-3 days to pick from. For example, Tuesdays, Wednesdays, or Fridays.
    - Fridays tend to work the best for non single people because they don't have work the next day and they still have the weekend for bigger plans.
    - I schedule dinner for 6pm and set that as the starting time so if people come late, they are still early for the game itself.
    - I send out a reminder email the day before and the day of.
    - If it is more than a 1 shot, I usually keep the commitment down to 3 games at a time.
    - And I don't schedule future games in advanced. I schedule them after each and every game.
    - I send out highlights of the game inbetween game to people via email, "wasn't it awesome when...!!!" to make people feel welcome and their contributions important and this also is a great reminder to keep the game fresh in their minds.
    - I don't require anyone to do any prep or work outside of the games themselves.
    - I tell people to give me 48 notice if they have to cancel.
    - And if they do, I ask why so that I know their level of commitment.
    - I try to tell as many people about my game as possible to build excitement and interest with people not playing in case someone drops out and we need a fill in asap.
    - It's awesome to have a fiance who games as she often acts as a last minute replacement if I can't find someone.
    - My friends who all have children will often help each other with babysitting.
    - I'm sure there is more!

    \n I'll add my experience. Every question in your list is wholly valid - but becomes even moreso by addition of a supplemental question "+(a) No fuckers, do you REALLY mean that answer YES?"
    \n
    \nI used to be part of a big gaming community but very few of them actually gamed. They talked about gaming, they bought the books, they discussed why a particular game wasn't right for them but when the chips came down they rarely ever played. If they did manage to get their arses to the table, the game inevitably lasted one session as it wasn't the orgasmic gaming experience that they had dreamt it would be. In the end I just laid down a line and said 'Only people that want to play bother coming!' Four did. And we still game together, every other weekend, eight years later.
    \n
    \nI think you have to accept that whilst some of your answers (the table, props and books) might not change, the time and commitment ones will vary across time. Flexibility is the key and commitment. If one of us is going to miss a session we let the GM know well in advance. The session may move to a more appropriate date. The session may be bumped a week. The story may change so that the character is elsewhere. We have developed a tolerance and an ability to roll with these things. However, overriding all of this is the idea that Gaming Comes First. An idea that you will not see your gaming as an insta-bump activity for soccer practice, gardening, going to the movies or painting the hallway. For five hours, every other week, you game.
    \n
    \nOur other thing is nigh constant communication - we have a messageboard and three active blogs which we all use. Many of us speak on IM or once a week at the pub. We are all friends. We are very open about our thoughts on the games and helping each other improve (and giving props when things go amazingly). So we know when someone has a paper due, or a conference to speak at or a work project looming. We are aware of birthdays and holidays and anniversaries and conventions and we build them into our calendar.
    \n
    \nOverall though, my experience is that you will only get committed gaming from committed gamers. For people who see it as just another possibility on any given night for something to do, you will get minimal gaming.
