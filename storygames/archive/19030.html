\n [Edited: This thread started as a discussion about the house ruling of skill in this post. Below, I open it up to Classic Traveller skills in general.]

The Classic Traveller resolution is skill based. There a list of skills. The skills are written up like this:\n
MECHANICAL: The individual has skill in the use, operation, and repair of mechanical devices. The person is considered to be handy in this field, with a talent similar to that of a green thumb. This skill specifically excludes the field of engineering; it does include non-energy weapon repair.
Many of the devices of civilization are strictly mechanical in nature, and the need to repair, replace, or simply use them pervades life. Mechanical expertise allows a character
the ability to operate mechanical devices easily, as well as to repair them quickly and efficiently. Any situation requiring a knowledge of mechanical aspects of devices or equipment can be affected by mechanical skill.

Referee: Specific throws for specific situations must be generated. Obviously, the throw to fabricate a new main drive bearing as a starship plunges into a flaming sun would be harder than the throw to repair a broken air lock hatch while in port. Success in any mechanical enterprise is also affected by such variables as tool availability, personal strength and dexterity, education, and situation.
\nOr this:\n
ADMINISTRATION: The individual has had experience with bureaucratic agencies, and understands the requirements of dealing with them and managing them.

When contact with officials is required, understanding their needs and motives will assist in dealing with them. A basic throw of 7+ will successfully resolve normal interaction without further problems (such as avoidance of police harrassment, insuring prompt issuance of licenses, approval of applications, avoidance of close inspection of papers, etc). DMs to be applied: no expertise, - 3; per level of admin expertise, +2.

When serving in a bureaucratic organization, admin expertise allows competency in the eyes of superiors. DMs to be applied: +1 per level of expertise. The exact throw to be determined by the referee under the circumstances.

Referee: Admin expertise should also affect the quality of work and potential of success for an organization which the character is controlling or managing.
\nA couple of things: There are Characteristics in the game (Strength, Dexterity, Endurance, Intelligence, Education, Social Standing) which are kind of forgotten after character creation. And over the years many folks have tried lots of tweaks to make the skills use more uniform, inform the rolls more logically and so forth.

But, basically, the GM sets a difficulty number, the player rolls 2D6 + Skill modifiers. He hits the number to succeed.

In other words, it's straight up skill checks. And I'm not really fond of straight up skill checks. I like the feeling that the Character is working against someone or something -- not just rolling against himself. I also find simply setting a skill number and rolling kind of dry.

So working off a skill check variant I found online, here's what I'm going to try:

The Need to Make a Roll
When do you roll? When something is at stake, conflict is on the table, and the outcome is uncertain.

If the use of the skill is something that the Character could probably handle given his skill and background, there's no need to make a roll. For this reason, few skill checks will ever start at Easy or Moderate. There's little point in making a roll that the Character should be expected to make in average circumstances.

Referee Decision Making
As with most CT task checks, the player will be rolling 2D and must try to equal or exceed a target number created by the referee. The referee decides on the difficulty of the task on a scale from easy to virtually impossible. He rolls 2D with the appropriate modifier based on the difficulty. This provides the "Rolled Difficulty."

Easy: 2D-4
Moderate: 2D
Difficult: 2D+2
Formidable: 2D+6
Virtually Impossible: 2D+8

This provides a bit more elasticity in results, as the target number is weighted along the difficulty scale with averages of 3, 7, 9, 13, and 15 respectively. But it provides elasticity to the results: with two random numbers being rolled, everyone has to lean to the dice to see who rolled what and compare. I like that.

I also like the fact the Character is not rolling against his own skill. He's rolling against the circumstances of the fiction. If he's trying to get an airlock door open, he's now rolling against the fictional details of that airlock: The make, the model, the tools he's got fitting or not fitting, the ship's AI realizing it's being breached and overriding with a new code, and whatever.

[The bit with the referee making a 2D roll plus modifier is the part I am specifically curious about. Normally in Traveller the GM just sets a difficulty number along a range of values. I'm adding the weight dice rolls for the opposition, for reasons listed above. Does this work for people? Is it crazy in some way?]

Positive DMs
Next the player rolls the 2D and adds the value of the relevant skill.

He also gains a bonus of +1 if his relevant characteristic equals or exceeds rolled task difficulty. In fact, if his characteristic is double the rolled difficulty, then he gains a DM of +2 instead! When the DMs are added up, a result equal to or exceeding the referee\u2019s difficulty number is a success\u2014just like in Book One.

Unskilled Penalties
Many of the skill descriptions in Book One give hefty penalties for attempting a task while untrained (i.e. with no relevant skill levels). Default skills are those skills that the characters are assumed to be familiar with if not fully trained, skills like Vacc Suit, weapon skills, ground car, etc. As in Book One, default skill gives a DM of 0.

For other skills the referee must decide on their value (how rare or specialized they are). Is that skill you\u2019ve requested fairly common, or is it rare? Examples of the first might be Steward, Electronics, or ATV (skills quite familiar to most characters through everyday experiences). Examples of the second include Engineering, Pilot, Forward Observer, Streetwise, Leadership, and so on (specialized skills requiring specific education or experiences to understand and attempt).

Apply a DM of -2 for lack of a common skill
Apply a DM of -4 for lack of a specialized skill

Using Two Skills in Combination
Some tasks call for skill in two different areas. Turning a missile into a drone might require both Gunnery and Electronics. If the referee calls for two skills that must both be possessed, then he must also double the difficulty he just set and allow the main skill to add a DM of +2 to per skill level, and the secondary skill to add the usual +1 per skill level. If the character lacks just one of the two required skills then assume to be at a default level for this task (receiving no untrained penalty). If you lack both required skills then you receive only one untrained penalty (whichever is the harshest). For example, if Pilot and Vacc Suit are required, you'd get a -4 penalty for having neither skill, not the full -6.

Fictional Positioning
The more the Player works and pushes on the fictional situation, the more he can improve his chance of success. Just like OD&D, the Players are encouraged to create work-arounds the need to roll. Making a roll means you might fail! So, avoid them if you can! And if you can't avoid them, add color and details to the Character's circumstances to improve, fictionally, the Character's odds. What is the Character doing? How is the Character doing it? As the Details get added, the GM knocks down the Difficulty one or more levels.

[This last part is something I'm adding as well. Thoughts?]

    \n Some thoughts:

    I do not like adding a GM roll just for the hell of it - what does it accomplish? Instead of rolling 2d6 we are rolling 2d6-2d6, which increases both bell curve height and extreme values, but does little else. Aesthetics are of course aesthetics, I don't want to go against that; I don't myself feel like having the GM roll for "the semiconductor chip resisting your attempts to fix it" is an elegant touchpoint between the mechanics and the fiction, but some do feel otherwise. It's also questionable conceptually whether a task resolution system should have a random factor in the difficulties like that - does it mean that the GM doesn't know or doesn't care to determine how difficult this task is, exactly, or what is it that we're attempting to gain here conceptually?

    I like how you bring the Characteristics into the system, it's mathematically interesting. However, it's also quite a bit of handling time; unless you're literally intending to abstract the system into a conflict-resolution system (instead of a task resolution system, which it used to be), and thus reduce the amount of rolling by a magnitude, that's likely too much rolling and comparing and calculating bonuses.

    That mechanical solution for using two skills in combination is convoluted as hell from my viewpoint. It is possible that e.g. a Traveller background might make that seem really pleasing and elegant because the game uses similar hijinks in other parts, or whatever, but for me as a Traveller-outsider that doesn't parse as anything I'd want to use. Have you considered something simpler, there are plenty of ways that this particular conceptual issue has been resolved in rpg history. For the sake of the example: determine difficulty normally and roll against it with the primary skill relevant to the task; if the secondary skill is more than half the value of the primary skill, add +2 to the result.

    In general your logic here straddles the fence between a robust task resolution system and a robust conflict resolution system in a way that I find uncomfortable; I understand how to utilize a good task resolution system e.g. as a part of a conflict resolution procedure, and I understand the value of a system that resolves conflicts explicitly, but this one feels like it's neither a fish nor a fowl: on the one hand randomized difficulties and formalized unskilled penalties scream "abstract conflict resolution system", but then again the text clearly concerns resolving individual tasks. How is the logical gap between a task and a conflict crossed here, aside from the ideal that task resolutions should only be made when a conflict is also up in the air?

    (In case that question is not entirely clear, an example: in some games that utilize task resolution to resolve conflicts we first determine the stakes of the conflict so we are being explicit and clear about the creative interest the group has in the scene, then we determine the turning-point task on which it all hinges, then we resolve that turning-point task, and creatively assume that all the other minor details of the scene fall out in a way that accords with the results of that individual task resolution. The Shadow of Yesterday is a schoolbook example of this type of conflict-resolution-via-task-resolution.)

    We can also discuss alternative treatments if you'd like, but I won't go into them now in case you're already committed to these particular concepts and are just looking for minor fine-tuning.

    \n Digging into the rules, I find I'm reading them with an eye I did not posses when I was 17. (Good!)

    So, let's examine Classic Traveller skill resolution as it stands.

    Here are things I find:\n
    ELECTRONICS: The individual has skill in the use, operation, and repair of electronic devices. The person is considered handy in
    this field, with the equivalent of a green thumb talent. This skill includes the repair of energy weapons. An advanced technological civilization depends heavily on the use of electronic devices. The need to use, repair, and replace electronic devices is ubiquitous.
    Electronic expertise allows a character to use and operate electronic items; generally the skill is a DM applied to the throw to understand, repair, assemble, or operate. Complex items would also require a certain level of education or a very high intelligence; many devices may also require some degree of dexterity.

    Referee: Specific throws for specific situations must be generated. Obviously, some throws will be harder than others, and many will be impossible without an accumulation of DMs based on expertise, education, dexterity, intelligence, and the availability of parts and tools.
    \nLook at how many variables the referee is responsible for adjudicating! How does the referee judge the specific throws? Which DMs should be applied for expertise, education, dexterity, intelligence? Given that some skills are described as giving DMs of +1 per skill level while others are a +2 per skill level, exactly how crazy making is this?

    Or does it actually work? The referee, along with the players, sort of figure out the right throw to be made, bringing to bear the appropriate DMs? I'm assuming this is why everyone has been trying to reconfigure the skill system for years. But like looking at OD&D fresh, maybe it does work as is. (In other words, you don't change the starting HPs for a character in D&D because it makes them too fragile -- because the point is they're supposed to be fragile.)

    And this, in the Self Improvement section of the rules:\n
    Education: A character with an education characteristic lower than his or her intelligence characteristic may improve education level through the use of correspondence courses and tutoring. In general such courses (or tutors) have a base price of CR 50 per week. Generally one session per week is taken, though 2 per week are possible. After 50 sessions are completed the character's education level is increased by one. In one four year period, it is possible to increase the education characteristic a maximum of 6 levels.

    Education increases gained are permanent.

    In addition, any character may, once during his or her life, take a sabbatical (for 4 years) for the purpose of specifically gaining a skill through education. Such activity is the equivalent of a technical school or college education, and allows the acquisition of one specific non-weapon skill with a level of 2. Cost of this education is CR 70,000.
    \nSo, clearly education is supposed to matter. But, again, in the rules how education is supposed to apply is never defined more clearly than in the Electronics rule above. Again, it seems as if the group at the table is supposed to (expected to? have to?) grope their way toward a method of determining the process for resolving task rolls -- whatever their nature.

    There are 2D6. There are characteristics, skills, and fictional circumstances. And the referee (along with input from the players) has to cobble together the throw and the DMs on an almost case by case basis.

    Has anyone worked games like this? How do they play? Do they work? Part of me can't imagine they do. Other parts of me imagine that the only way they can work is if the players add the bits of fiction/color to add in the DMs they want to help them succeed. Which is cool!

    \n It occurs to me that having a complex system of modifiers for various situations - of which actually having skill count to different degrees in different situations is one extreme example - could be good if you've got a table that is honestly interested in correctly factoring the difficulties. Our D&D table is like this, for example: when we settle down to figure out how e.g. a given combat goes, everybody is doing their best to apply the rules correctly and communicate the fiction well to make the resolution accord with it. The opposite type of table would be one where a Doylean interpretation of the fiction reigns, all modifiers to checks only stand on the strength of GM fiat, and players lie to get better odds. "Of course my character was standing here, in this beneficial spot."

    Even if you're not afraid of the latter type of group, it is worthwhile to consider handling time and system elegance. We make this stuff work on D&D via "rulings, not rules", which reduces handling time to exactly whatever amount we find desirable on a case-by-case basis. A group that wants a firm set of rules is stuck checking the rule book every time for whether you were supposed to get double bonuses from this skill in this situation or not.

    Looking at what you've presented here in terms of tools (I'm not familiar with Traveller, although I probably should look into it - looks interesting), my own inclination would be to clarify how difficulties are set, determine a few standard ways that roll bonuses are derived, and then leave the rest up to an OSR-like common law process.

    What I say about setting difficulties is something very specific: I don't think that a trad rpg "difficulty table" is useful at all. Having a table that tells you what an "easy" or "moderate" task is numerically is a subtle tool of trad gamemastering, as it relates task difficulty to supposed proper character skill numbers instead of anything observable in the fiction. If I was considering an old school game (and note, I don't know if you want Traveller to be that, maybe you want it to be new school), I would for hygienic reasons attempt to discover sharper and more objective tools for this crucial part of the equation.

    As an example of what I mean, I'll tell how I deal with setting difficulties in my D&D homebrew: a task at difficulty 20 is moderately challenging for a demographically average human without special skills, such that they are equally likely to fail or succeed. Difficulty 30 is a task that is practically impossible without talent or training. Difficulty 10 should be automatic success for everybody in ordinary situations. These are fictional touchpoints against which individual tasks are evaluated, so that their difficulties may well end up in between these numbers - I usually use 5-point increments. Almost all difficulties can be established well with these simple touchpoints.

    That might seem like almost the same as the difficulty table you presented, but there is a crucial difference: I can be called on my numbers because they are defined by fictional benchmarks instead of likelihood of PC success (which is what these "medium difficulty task" type lists really amount to), and players can double-check them easily enough. Is the task I'm trying here really so difficult that it deserves DC 25? I would have said that almost anybody can do this thing reliably all the time, so why is the GM giving me such a hard time? I better ask him to show his work - explain the logic of the decision and draw my attention to the fictional details upon which he determined that number.

    Developing the theme of setting difficulties, regarding randomized difficulties: I understand the notion of providing for random detail by randomizing resistances in resolution, but I don't think that this is the best approach for doing that in a task resolution game. (It could be good for a conflict resolution system, many such use it successfully - just look at HeroQuest!) The problem is that you do not know at this stage how elaborate the fictional details concerning individual tasks in the game actually are going to be. When a character is trying to jury-rig a broken computer, it's all well and good to randomize how badly the computer is broken, and determine difficulty on that basis. However, what if we already know that the computer's power source is broken, but the computer is otherwise fine? We could well know this before setting the difficulty has ever come up, assuming that we're playing with organic task resolution procedure. Is it really fair to randomize the difficulty in a situation where we know perfectly well how difficult the task is in fictional terms? I would say no, and would rather use real benchmarking of some sort to determine the difficulty.

    Putting the difficulty side of the equation aside for a moment, I'd like to speculate a bit about how the system might be pushed. Am I correct in assuming that the 2d6 is a cornerstone mechanic that should be retained, no matter what? (In my OSR musings I call the mechanics that I want to retain for aesthetic and historical reasons "cornerstones".) How about the dice + skill against DC formulation, is that also a cornerstone that cannot be abandoned? The Characteristics and Skills are surely cornerstones, the system has to use those purposefully for it to make any sense as a revised Traveller skill system.

    I probably do not need to tell you that one thing you could do with these mechanical components is to simply play with Apocalypse World dice mechanics, achieving a robust conflict-resolution-via-task-resolution system with very little work: 2d6 + skill, roll 7+ for partial success and 10+ for total success. -1 for being untrained in the skill, -1 for difficult circumstances, and most circumstances trigger extra checks instead of factoring more modifiers. Roll under the relevant Characteristic to get xp or whatever, there are many ways the Characteristics can be mixed back in once the skill resolution is clear. Of course this would mostly obviate the Traveller skill-specific rules for situational modifiers and such.

    \n And thinking about it more...

    In my youth I know I saw the role of the referee to guide the PCs to challenges to use their Vacc Suit or ATV or Electronic skills... which seemed really dull.

    But I do see, now, that the skill list is really just there to say, "Do things kind of like this. These are examples. And when something challenging needs to be tested, then cobble something together."

    I originally saw the skill as limiting the options for the PCs. Especially since, for the initiated, the skill are doled out randomly in character creation and the PCs might be built with a rather limited set of skills.

    But really, the skills are only a part of how they'll try to get things done -- with the characteristics playing a part as well, as well as the "roleplaying" (adding details, color, fiction as they maneuver and poke the fictional world) to sort out how they want to solve a problem.

    This goes back to the Afterward from Book 3 of Dungeons & Dragons:\n
    There are unquestionably areas which have been glossed over. While we deeply regret
    the necessity, space requires that we put in the essentials only, and the
    trimming will oftimes have to be added by the referee and his players. We have
    attempted to furnish an ample framework, and building should be both easy and
    fun. In this light, we urge you to refrain from writing for rule interpretations or
    the like unless you are absolutely at a loss, for everything herein is fantastic, and
    the best way is to decide how you would like it to be, and then make it just that
    way!

    Not only is this a distinctly different connotation, it draws specifically from the historical/miniature/war game roots of RPGs that Bob covered in his OSR/History thread.

    Looking at the quote above in many ways the players are working hand-in-hand with the referee. In fact, if you squint and look at it a certain way, the players are setting their own target and DMs, making their case to themselves and fellow players by talking it out loud. The referee, in the miniature war gene sense of referee, thinks upon the statements (fictional elements meeting mechanics) offering judgment, suggestions, discussion. It's almost the opposite of how we normally see the process between GM/Players in trad games.

    Of course, the process goes both ways. As you say above, the referee can make a judgement and the player can question it in turn. But the point stands that in many ways the players are creating their own challenge in this kind if play and referee is there to provide pushback/more things to think about. The referee can provoke more thought and fictional color than the players might think if in their own as well as more "fog of world" to crest more tension, mystery, and sense of "world" beyond the PCs.

    What I find fascinating, in regard to all this, is that the Classic Traveller text assumes one can play the game "solitaire" -- either alone or with the players all playing PCs. But that the game is "better" with a referee. I think this lines up very much with the spirit (but not the letter) of adjudication described in the quote above.\n
    These game rules are as complete as they could be and provide for solitaire and unsupervised play. But the main thrust of the game is the refereed or umpired situation. An independent referee allows a large degree of flexibility and continuity often not possible when players themselves control the game. A referee inserts some measure of uncertainty in the minds of the players as they travel through the universe. Finally, the referee deals with new situations that these rules may not cover; after all, no set of rules can totally define the universe and how it works.

    My own experience with this kind of thing (mostly through Stars Without Number and World of Dungeons) aligns with Eero's.

    The process of determining a difficulty number or deciding when to roll or determining the stakes of a given roll are all done by interrogating the fiction for the necessary details. This also involves making judgment calls, which serve two purposes: 1) They make the game unique to our group since our judgment calls are specifically our own and reflect the aesthetics, values, and preferences of our group. And 2) They allow us to adapt a simple mechanism to an infinite variety of fictional details and situations, without accounting for all of them exhaustively a priori.

    Since it's the GM's chief job to understand and present the fictional space as accurately as possible, setting a target number or calling for a roll is a wonderful opportunity to do just that. Like Eero says, DCs that are based on fictional qualities become another tool for better describing the world. They're a game tool that represents an in-fiction thing -- when my character looks at that DC 20 wall, she knows that she should ask one of the skilled climbers in the group to tackle it.

    This also means getting rid of the notion that more dangerous or consequential things must be more difficult to accomplish. The fictional state determines these things, not our notions of how the situation "should" be. Avoiding the death gaze of the monster might be absurdly simple if you know the trick and thus a very low DC (or, in a "roll under your stat" system, only calling for a roll in extreme circumstances), but the stakes are still very high. Setting the stakes and difficulty independently based on the fiction is a powerful tool that makes all the hijinks of fictional positioning come into play.

    (Edited to add, for completeness sake: The reverse is also true. Sometimes things of very little consequence can be extremely difficult to accomplish. By changing their fictional positioning, players can try to manipulate the various resolution components -- achievement, danger, difficulty).

    I've just been reading through the combat system in Traveller Book 1. It's kind of strange -- in a good way!

    Before encounters begin you roll to see if either party has the drop on the other (maybe one does, maybe both spot each other at once, maybe neither do). Then you roll to see at what range the involved parties are at with mods for terrain (in a building you're more likely to spot each other at Close or Short range than Medium, for example). Surprise attacks are allowed until something happens to alert the unaware group. Or the group with the drop can try to move on without being spotted. Once the other side is aware of an attack, you go into standard combat phases.

    There are four choices for movement at the start of a round (Stand Ground, Close Range, Open Range, Evade). Then everyone announces attacks (attacks are only viable if you stood ground). All movement happens simultaneously and then all combat happens simultaneously. There's no initiative at all.

    I'm assuming then, that there is a Free and Clear stage, like Sorcerer, where everyone gets to announce what they want to do in each of those phases, and then change their announcements as other people make their announcements.

    And I can assure you that Adolescent Me had no idea what to make of this. If everyone can announce at the same time and everyone takes action at the same time how do you herd these cats? Again, given age and other games under my belt it all makes sense now (and seems cool).

    Also, the rules use what are called Range Bands. You use a lined sheet of paper to mark each characters relative distance to each other (not position, just distance) with the characters marked in the bands between the lines.

    So you get this:

    RANGE BANDS
    Close, Short: 0
    Medium: 1 to 2
    Long: 3 to 10
    Very Long: 11 to 20

    This matters since different weapons have different effectiveness at different ranges. And, of course, you need to be at Close range to get to someone with your fist or knife.

    The thing is, Adolescent Me looked at this and kept seeing stand up fights. Which had no color and was dull. But the fun comes in when you actual add positioning and terrain. While the lined sheet of paper is efficient, a wipeable hex map (with one each equalling one band) where you can draw terrain and allow cover, flanking and stuff is really where this kind of challenged based play is going to shine.

    Again, there are abstractions in the system that need to be unfold to concrete color and situation that is not provided. I think that's why OD&D spawned, in part, the Tolkien-Pulp-Fantasy-Kluge. It was the concrete imagery people had. Instead of saying, "Oh, what don't I see that I want to see, and how do I use this loose tool set to make that?" people grabbed onto the color that already had rules stapled onto them.

    \nChristopher, I'm curious -- have you played this way before? If you have, and this thread is just about resolving to play Traveller that way, then what I'm about to say is probably old news. If not, though, hopefully this will be useful to you.

    When consulting the fiction for answers to (e.g.) "what should the difficulty be", there are a bunch of steps where the group can either cohere or flounder, and I've found that it's super important to have (1) a rationale for the process, and (2) some consistency in the process itself.

    1) That whole "reflect our aesthetics, values, and preferences" can be united or contentious, and in my experience the difference-maker is an up-front statement of intent. If your group is united in "we consult the fiction only as a reality sim, based on physics and causality and nothing else", then you can create a process for doing that. Likewise if your group is united in "we consult the fiction through the lens of 'What would be cool in Star Trek', wherein dramatic convenience trumps realism but only as long as nothing is too unrealistic". I suspect any orientation can work. The thing that often doesn't work is when one player reads the fiction like a scientist while another player's reading it like a cartoon audience.

    2) At some point before the dice are rolled, the GM and each player at the table form ideas about what's happened in the fiction, what possibilities exist, how likely they are, the probable consequences of acting on them, and more. My ideal is that, before a roll is made, we all communicate on the fictional states and possibilities relevant to the roll, without getting bogged down in other stuff that doesn't matter. The most efficient way I've found to do this is to have players verify their assessments before committing to actions. "I'm thinking of jumping onto it. That'd be, what, difficulty 6?" The reason many players don't do this is they're afraid the GM will alter the fiction via their response ("It's difficulty 8 because of blah blah new content!"), so just establish that that won't happen in your group, and you should be good.

    I hope all this stuff isn't beside the point for this thread; if so, my apologies. I mention it partly because that's where I look for the answer to "how do we arbitrate this Electronics roll". Once we agree on what the fiction is and how to read it, the rest is cake.

    \n The way I phrase "realism" in the D&D context that we've been doing over the last few years is that we are engaging in a constructive dialogue to discover challenges and solutions; getting things "right" is not important per se, but rather the important thing is to delineate the constraints of the current situation clearly. Thus any given issue of realism (say the penetration capability of a winch-bow against proofed plate mail at 300 feet) is only important insofar as the issue is raised according to procedure at the game table, to be acknowledged and factored into decision-making.

    That might sound like exactly the same as traditional realistic simulation, but there is a difference: we do not care about the supposed realism of the way we handle situations, we only care about considering the striking, interesting and mention-worthy aspects of the situation as we perceive it. Realistic considerations are part of the bricolage of constructing a fictional challenge, and there is no necessity or duty to attain a consistent level of simulation in the way there would be in a game that was truly concerned with realism as a creative goal. I might characterize the difference like this: in a truly realistic game air friction is technically speaking on the table all the time, and it is a procedural error to forget to account for it at any given time; in our challengeful old school D&D air friction is only ever relevant when a player brings it up and it is accepted as relevant on a case-by-case basis, to enhance the tactical complexity of the situation currently under consideration.

    Perhaps the most striking technical difference for this distiction that I'm drawing is the "rule of three" that we tend to apply to resolving situations: at most three-ish situational factors will be considered for any given task resolution cycle, so everybody better focus on bringing up the most important factors first so as to not waste time. Although the fictional situation and our understanding of how it would "really" play out is our primary source of material for play, it is only that - material that has to be separately acknowledged, raised on the table, mechanized and resolved as a challenge of decision-making ability, not a challenge of knowledge about real history and physics. Any realistic issues that do not contribute to establishing rich and interesting challenges may as well be constructively ignored on a case-by-case basis, assuming that "it did not become a conclusive factor in this particular engagement". Very different from an attempt at consistent simulation.

    I hope the above explains how I can be talking about organic realism on the one hand and refute realism as a creative goal of play on the other. There are differences, although I will say that old school D&D the way we play it (that is, strictly fiction-first) goes as far towards realistic considerations in its creative interests as it is possible to do while actually having a gamist creative agenda.

    I'm not sure if this is the phrasing you'd use, but this is in my head and seems consistent with what has been discussed over the last couple of weeks:

    Each challenge for your group seems to be a unique puzzle or board game of sorts, built up either with some planning or on the fly, with unique factors available to the players to deal with the problems at hand. There is no specific solution, nor should there be (that would defeat the purpose of this kind of play).

    Imagine Avalon Hill's Panzer Leader. It introduces many specific elements to engage players in a multi-faceted set of challenges based on lots of details of tank combat that, in fact, excludes pretty much everything else about life and war and the planet earth. There is no way to simulate all of reality at the table -- and that is not Panzer Leader's point. It is a unique experience of problem solving with the elements at hand designed to make a manageable, intriguing puzzle solving/challenging experience.

    I consider my reference to the Appendixes of Dune in much the same train of thought -- but for use in the elasticity required for an RPG's "enveloping fiction." Frank Herbert lays out the details of religion, politics, history, economics, et al and says essentially, "These were the pieces on the board, the rules of world, that produced the situation that arose on Arrakis." He is defining the pieces that matter to the tale: religion, ecology, and so on. But he is also excluding a bucketload of material. How the fuck does Spice actually work? It doesn't! Not really. But it is one of the "situational factors" of the sort Eero refers to above. Again, is it real? No. Does it matter? Yes. Are there a bazillion other factors Herbert could have introduced in creating a fictional setting in the future involving a star spanning empire? Yes. Did he introduce a bazillion factors? No.

    Bringing it all down to the focused level of a conflict/situation in an RPG per Eero's comments:

    It sounds like Eero's group is doing exactly this kind of work. Picking the "reality" of whatever has been established and is at hand -- the biology of creatures, the powers of dark cultists, the kinds of weapons vs. the kinds of armor, how the duke is predisposed to one thing or another -- whatever is most interesting to focus on to build a series of moving pieces that will offer the Players an interesting set of options and challenges to manipulate and build on.

    There can be no simulation or reality because -- well, life is reality and that's that. But there can be fascinating selection of fictional material which draws on both reality as it is known (a set of facts useful to building a challenge) and the fantasy as it is know for the same effect. This selection of material works at the macro level (setting building), to mid-level (situations and encounters, whether they be with creatures, magical metaphysics of potions, or whatever), to the micro-level to a specific set of "rules" for the use of a skill to determine success and effect (Eero's "rule of three" above).
