\n I'd like to get people's critical feedback on a game mechanic I'm calling "Action Cards"

The idea is that players have a hand of five action cards that limit but also structure what they can do in the game. They play a card and point to a location in the game and say what happens there. If no one challenges them the action happens and the turn ends. The card gives them a certain number of victory points so at the end of the game there is a winner - of sorts. Players get one challenge per turn to try and replace the action - and get the victory points for themselves. That's the idea in brief.

The action deck is a 54 card deck. Each card has a suggestive word or phrase and a number of victory points it gives. Examples of cards are:

Charge - 1vp
Innuendo - 3vp
I've got a plan - 1vp
Betray - 3vp
Look whose in trouble now - 2vp
etc.

A player plays a card and uses the words to launch their ideas about what to do. More interesting story elements (like hurting your own character or talking rather than shooting NPCs) give more victory points. What I hope this does is help players through that awkward part of story games where players just don't know what to do next. When you have total freedom - indecision takes hold a lot. Players redraw back up to five cards after each turn.

If a player makes a good story in their scene no one will challenge them so good story telling is rewarded but if someone doesn't like what was said they may challenge and make up a different story for what happened. The two players then roll 2d6, the high roller wins, re-roll ties. This means the dice only stomp on player's creativity when competing players want to stomp on one another. Everyone gets a one chance to do this every turn but they don't have to and it is possible that games could happen where no dice are rolled.

The game continues until the story is completed or players run out of cards (which ends a game session - you can pick the story up again later if you want). So the game is time limited and because of victory points it yields a gamist winner.

I have some other ideas on how to move stories along but this is the basic mechanic.

Oh, the game context is that players have a character they champion in the story but they can make other players act. Each player is an author more than an actor.

What do you think? What are the holes that I'm not seeing? Is there anything here that just kills fun?

    \n 1) If every other player can challenge every turn, there's a strong skew towards pre-play negotiations. I have to be fairly sure the (frex) three other players will like what I do, because I basically need unanimity: one player disagreeing means I have a ~42% chance of failure, two others jacks that to ... a number I can't figure, but it's HIGH! So my card doesn't matter nearly as much as how I sell it before I reveal it.

    2) Why wouldn't I always challenge an opponent who has a VP lead, if I want to "win?" Why wouldn't EVERY player who's not in the lead challenge EVERY other player?

    3) What happens on a challenge roll tie? "Yes, but...?"

    4) What happens, mechanically, if a challenge roll (or rolls? See #6) is the high roll? Loss of turn? Only loss of card (i.e., "Try a different way")?

    5) Can that same challenger(s) re-challenge? (I'd guess no... which DOES open up an interesting bluffing mechanic--I play a high-VP card I don't really want, knowing everyone will challenge it to be sure to get the high roll against me, after which I play with impunity my lower-VP card, smugly smiling.)

    6) Simultaneous challenges or sequential? The former seems better, to me, both for the bluffing in #4 and to avoid dog-piling a leader per #2.

    7) Any cost to challenging? (If not, why not? Free challenging incentivizes challenging everything, either for gamist or narrativist reasons.)

    It's an interesting "story driver" idea... but the "game' part of it seems lopsided, with perverse incentives and imbalanced resource expenditures in conflicts (at the moment).

    \n I wouldn't toss out the whole idea, but I think it needs symmetry. It costs me a card to attempt to contribute, it costs a challenger nothing. Challenging is sequential, so there's an incentive to try to "play chicken" (I think bluff is better, but your current model doesn't LET the original narrator "go again" if successfully challenged; rather, it seems that they are just skipped over).

    It wasn't clear to me on first read that the challenger had to offer an alternative narration--and pony up a card of their own?--in the course of challenging. So if it's card-for-card, then (again) I challenge everything because I want MY VPs (as challenger) to go to me, not yours to you.

    A good mechanic should present choice, not automatic play: it either presents a risk/reward decision or a trade-off or otherwise presents a tension of incentives.

    I suggest you flowchart the whole turn sequence out, and it will be more apparent to you where perverse incentives lie. (Also, the whole timing and sequencing will be more clear to us, minimizing back-and-forth as you elucidate rules details.)

    Further, if your playtest experience is different than what we anticipate from the RAW, then you need to tease out what parts of satisfying play are coming from your table culture and what are from the RAW. Anything from the former either needs to be codified into rules or advised as a "way to play charitably" (though the latter way is weaker than having the RAW drive "appropriate" play).

    \n I really like the concept of 'too much choice', I've definitely had that feeling for myself and others during play. But I feel like the VP element really muddies the waters.

    For example, one of my gaming group is keen to join in but (coming from a lifetime of Warhammer and board games) his storytelling isn't so strong. Perfect for Action Cards! Except... his competitive nature means he's going to be more focused on winning over telling a good story. (It doesn't help he's the joker of our group and will laugh off any nonsensical narrative choices he makes for the sake of scoring).
    Someone else in the same group enjoys using RPGs to express themselves, but doesn't have much experience with shared storytelling and sometimes finds it difficult to get their voice heard. Perfect for Action Cards! Except... the VP mechanic means she's going to be challenged at every turn, because it's in the other players interest (especially Mr competitive, above) to do so.
    Neither of these people are SOBs, they're fine folk with roughly similar goals towards RPGs. It's just their approaches to gaming are very different.

    Maybe instead of a 'challenging' mechanic, players could have a chance to show their approval of a scene, instead? (I'm thinking of something the reverse of contempt tokens, from the Quiet Year.) Something like this:
    - A player narrates an action from a card as described. Every card has a VP score on it as you describe (more interesting options have higher scores - I really like that idea, too.)
    - After a player narrates an action, everyone else must either approve or disapprove it. Every approval earns the narrator one more VP. Provided they get at least one approval, they also get the value on the card. If no-one approves , they get nothing that round (not even the score on the card.)
    - After the game, everyone cashes in their VP for sweets (or just play with smarties or something!)

    Obviously the idea's not perfect (especially if you have a small group) but I hope this is useful nonetheless. (One final aside - I recommend Braggart - it's got a similar concept, but played differently. I think you and your group might enjoy it!)

    \n If a challenge is 2d6 vs 2d6, high wins, ties reroll, then the odds of winning a challenge approach 50%, notwithstanding the fleetingly improbable "infinity tie". More strictly: If the roll is not a tie, the odds for the defender and challenger are equal: 630/1,296. The odds of having to roll twice are 36/1,296, which is 1/36. The odds of having to roll three times are 1/1,296. The odds of having to roll four times are 1/46,656. For any given roll, there's a 1/36 chance you'll have to roll one more time, but the odds of having to do that indefinitely are fleetingly trivial. At the limit, that possibility approaches zero, and of the remaining cases, exactly half are for the defender, half for the challenger.

    The odds of winning a second challenge (to the same play) also approach one-half, which means that if two players challenge me on the same play, I only have a 50%x50%= 25% chance to get my way. Half it again for each additional challenge.

    If there's no cost to challenge, but you can only do it once, there is some incentive for the other players to either take turns challenging you, or to line up challenges if you play a high-point card. That means it might be a challenge to score any point at all, but the incentive seems to me to be for the player to play all cards in hand during a turn. There's not really any reason not to, right? And I can imagine two strategies, in addition to the bluffing trick that was mentioned earlier: play your cards in either ascending or descending order of point value. In either case, your opponents have either to blow their challenges early, letting you slip something in at the end of turn, or save their challenges to prevent that, which means you might nab some points earlier.

    If you could only play a certain number of cards from your hand each turn, that might change the strategy. It might also make it more interesting if the cards included things like, "You get +2 vp, and one opponent of your choice gets +1 vp", or "You take -1 vp and so does everybody else", and so on. Those kinds of cards would influence your opponent's incentives for challenging.

    \n I met with my group of local designer friends and we talked about how to add wagering into challenges.

    One idea Boco suggested was to have the player play one of their action cards to challenge but also set aside an additional card as a bet. If they won the challenge they would keep their bet card but also get VPs from the card they played. If they lost then the winner would get the VPs from the card they played but also get to pick up the bet card. That would leave them with 6 cards in their hand for the next turn and the loser would be down to 3 cards. This could work but it seems a bit clunky to me. It might be hard to explain it.

    Chet suggested that if the challenging player loses, that they lose their next turn. Boco and I agreed that this would be hard to remember because the challenger might not be the next player up - who would remember it then?

    Early this morning I woke up thinking about it. I always want the simplest procedure possible.

    Why not have the challenger play an action card to make a challenge. If they win, they get the VP's from the first player's card (they pick it up and put it in their pile of won cards). The card they played would be discarded and thus be cycled back though the game. Their card would need to be discarded to avoid creating a perverse reward that incentivised playing a big VP card every chance you had. If the challenger lost then the winner would get the VP from their card AND the VP from the card the challenger played. So the card played is the wager card. This doesn't add any new components to the game but makes the challenger risk something real when challenging. It might be a 1VP card so the cost isn't too high while still being real.

    There is a local gamer here abouts - Bob - who is one of those highly competitive players. When I imagined being him - and having to risk rewarding the player your trying to stop - it seemed that every a small cost would be enough to deter frivolous challenges. This will mean I need to include an action deck with all my games - a bit more work cause they all need some kind of card art - but doable.

    Thoughts?

    \n Chris,

    It seems to me you don't want competition so much as refinement of the narrative, so I suggest changing the language and procedure to not be a challenge, but an amendment.

    For example: When a player wants to amend the initial player's action, she suggests changing one part of the original description and keeping another, both should be mentioned - which turns this into a "constructive criticism". The initial player can accept the amendment (netting both players +1 VP) or refuse it. If he refuses the amending player can discard an action card to force the issue, turning it into a roll-off as you describe. And the winner gets +1 VP and gets their way in the narrative.

    \nMatrixgamer, I hope this won't derail your thread too much, but I wanted to talk about this for a second.

    I've had that same problem with similar designs as well, Paul_T.

    I ended up developing an untested mechanic to deal with it. I think I posted somewhere around here about it at one point.

    The rub of it was this: After the game is ended, a deck of cards is dealt around to the players. There are more cards than players, like maybe 15 cards total. they're all dealt out and it doesn't matter if someone ends up with an extra or not. The cards themselves are set up to make a nod to the game being a movie or TV show and ask questions about different aspects of the fiction just created and its reception by audiences.

    Some of them are frankly meant to be silly. Each player looks at the cards they have in hand, then picks one they feel somehow applies to contributions made by the player to their right. They read the card aloud and then answer any questions associated with it, relating it to the contributions of that player.

    After that round of cards and descriptions based on them, everyone simply votes one player Most Valuable Player for the overall experience of the session.

    The idea here is to encourage/acknowledge lightweight competition in an essentially co-operative story makng activity. Everyone gets at least one warm-fuzzy ( acknowledgement) from the first round, while the actual victor is determined by group vote at the end. As it is an MVP award, it really is just a first among equals concept and really does rely on working well with others.

    Not sure if you can work this concept into your game or not Chris.

    I would point out that there are some successful mainstream games that have a very lightweight approach to victory conditions. Apples to Apples come to mind. Competitive in theory, but in practice not terribly so. For something revolving around the fun of co-operative story making, that might be the best way to go.
