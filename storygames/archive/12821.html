    \n Here's one way that occurred to me just based on your example of the GLC. Take your organization, figure out what the defining characteristic of somebody in that organization must be, then have the players explain how that defining characteristic comes out through the lens of their character.

    For instance, the Green Lantern Corps requires that one be fearless. Above all else, the rings themselves require that a potential Lantern never let fear stop them. So what does this look like when it\u2019s played out among some of the biggest names?

    Hal Jordan \u2013 Fear is a drug to him, he\u2019s an adrenaline junkie. He gets into planes that barely work to go faster than humans ever should because it is only when he\u2019s freaking out that he\u2019s happiest. This is probably because, after losing his dad, he figures he doesn\u2019t have much to lose. He\u2019d be right if he didn\u2019t continue to amass loved ones.

    Guy Gardner \u2013 Guy strikes me as the kind of guy that was afraid a lot as a kid and developed a lot of bluster to cover it up. At some point, the bluster became his actual personality and so whenever he starts to feel afraid he just becomes more Guy-esque. He responds to fear with anger and being obnoxious; he essentially makes his personality bigger than the fear.

    G\u2019nort \u2013 Obviously too damn dumb to realize there\u2019s anything of which to be afraid.

    Sinestro \u2013 Literally and honestly thinks he\u2019s the biggest bad ass in the universe. Nothing in Sinestro\u2019s world is scarier than Sinestro. The worst part is, he\u2019s pretty close to right.

    Soranik Natu \u2013 A neurosurgeon, she manipulates the smallest and most sensitive parts of her patients and holds their lives in her hands every single day. She\u2019s calm, cool, utterly professional and handles her fear by putting it behind that wall of professionalism.

    Also, once you know what the real defining factor is (in this case, fearlessness) you know where to twist the knife. Hit Sinestro in his ego, lay out obvious trails to follow for G\u2019nort, put Guy in situations where delicacy is important, and endanger Hal\u2019s loved ones, that kind of thing.

    Thoughts and comments?

    \n I've got this game called Hex Rangers that's about a secret government organization fighting supernatural forces in the Old West. All the PCs are members of this organizationg (the USHR).

    The first thing I do when sitting down to play it with new people is explain the baseline of that a Hex Ranger is. They are all trained to shoot, ride, and survive in the wilderness, as well as to work magic. So you don't have to put anything on your character sheet about these unless you want to be particularly good at it or do it in a special style.

    The second thing is to start actually creating the character, which begins with a discussion of who the character was before he was chosen to become a member of the USHR. To be a candidate for the USHR, you must have had an encounter with a supernatural evil, and comported yourself admirably (just surviving is admirable). I ask the player what they encountered and what happened. (There's a lot of stuff that happens in this step re: getting everyone on the same page as far as what the supernatural looks like and how it behaves in our game, too.)

    By the time that's done with, the player's gotten pretty well into the character's head, and then it's time to stat them up. They've got two different kinds of stats: one is a set of betting limits (for the poker-style resolution) in four categories (vigor, wits, touch, sand), allowing you to personalize your character's approach in the most general sense. The second kind of stat is freeform traits that describe what about your character sets him apart from the Hex Ranger baseline.

    \n The other thing that immediately comes to mind in media franchises about groups with similar powers is that the medium tends to emphasize the particulars and idiosyncrasies of each individual member's abilities.
    \n
    \nFor example, in the GLC, each lantern's constructs, flight, and fighting style are all somewhat different, depending on their personality.
    \n
    \nIn G.I. Joe, each individual is a competent and skilled soldier, but each also has other talents which help the team to succeed.
    \n
    \nIn Power Rangers, all of the rangers are awesome martial artists, but each has certain tricks they can pull with their weapon or particular style which helps them to stand out from the other rangers.
