\n At your ideal convention, would you want games to run strictly on time? (Here, strictly to time means that each game finishes when it's meant to finish, with no exceptions). Or would you want leeway: so that, if a game was good, it was allowed to overrun?

What about if it was a small gathering: 12 people, say? Would you still want it to run strictly to time?

What if it was a playtest session? If a game needed more time, should it overrun? If everyone enjoyed it, should it continue longer?

Graham

    \n If you've scheduled an event, then you should feel an obligation to bring it complete on time. But that's assuming you've scheduled an event. Which is to say that I think that everyone will agree that having a scheduled event over-run another scheduled event is awful. But if you don't schedule events, then that's a lot less problematic, no?
    \n
    \nI can't imagine scheduling events for a 12 player convention. That's two or three - maybe four - concurrently running games. At that rate you can probably just communicate from "table" to "table" to figure out who wants to do what for how long.
    \n
    \nIn any case, if the players all agree that they'd like to play longer in a scheduled event, then why not? The issue is forcing a player to have to choose between half an hour of this cool game right now, and losing out on the potential fun of the next four hour slot (which they may miss entirely if they can't show up on time for it). As long as that's not an issue, then no problem usually.
    \n
    \nTo whit, when starting the session ask everyone if they have a session that follows in the next time slot, or generally how long they're willing to play. And then push to meet those deadlines.
    \n
    \nIt's not an easy skill, but it's important to learn enough pacing to ensure that you can fit the game into RL time constraints.

    \n Just to make my own feelings clear: I think that groups who overrun should be lined up and shot.

    This applies also to 12-person mini-conventions: where it's easier to overrun, because you take advantage of the fact that everyone's friends, but you still make me wait to start my game.

    And I think time management is an essential GM skill. It's not hard: you keep an eye on the clock; with an hour to go, you begin to drive things towards an end; with half an hour, you push hard for an ending; you aim to end with fifteen minutes to go; and, if unexpected shit happens, you've got 15 minutes to cope with it.

    Graham
