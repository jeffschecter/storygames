\n In the traditional GM role, I can spot five distinct sub-roles. There's:

1. Facilitator: Like a meeting facilitator, you make sure everyone gets to speak and push the group along.
2. World-describer: You get to describe the world.
3. Arbitrator: You make judgements based on the rules.
4. Adversary: You present opposition.
5. Actor: You get to play characters, like the players play their characters.

There's also, arguably, a sixth role of "organiser", which is more of a social thing. You get everyone round the table, perhaps you arrange the game.

Did I miss anything?

Graham

    \n What I've seen other people do:

    1. Author - person who writes the in-game scenario stuff such that some kind of twist is going to occur and thus we'll all be zazzed. {This is probably a bad idea.}
    2. Social Focal Point - the GM is a person of relative social desirability whereby socializing with them is of a significant motivation for many members of the group {Again, probably a bad idea.}
    3. Teacher - The GM teaches other people the rules.

    What I do:

    1. Cheerleader - I provide added enthuasiasm for the game, people's characters, people's play styles. {This is usually a good thing. Its not really a choice. I'm just really pumped sometimes. }
    2. Mr. Warny Man - I warn people when something they want to do might make them dead/a rotten bastard forever.

    \n I'll take Sean's warny man and extend it into Rules Advisor: You make sure the player is aware of the options provided by the rules, as well as the relative worth of the options in a given circumstance.
    \n
    \nScribe: This is frequently tasked to players individually or collectively, but often, it's the GM's responsibility to remember what happened before, and provide a summary as necessary.
    \n
    \nReintegrator/Recycler: Take the players previous contributions, and bring them back to light. NPCs that the players obviously liked/loathed/found interesting, themes introduced intentionally or by extension of their decisions, set pieces from scenes set up by the players, etc.

    \n Director seems an importantly different one. I'm wondering about things like Record-keeper and Planner: I'm reacting against those for some reason. They seem to go in the direction of "Guy who keeps the character sheets": they're important roles, but seem less connected to the game itself. But perhaps I'm missing something.

    There also seems to be some sort of "Storyteller" one, combining Wolfe's Reintegrator and Burr's Signpost. We haven't defined this exactly, yet, but it seems to involve weaving people's contributions into a meaningful story.

    And then Soviet's Continuity Manager seems, rather fascinatingly, to sit between Arbitrator and World-describer.

    There can't be more than seven, because there are always seven of these sort of things.

    \n I'll make a note that when I'm Mr. Warny Man, I'm rarely giving people -rules- advice per say. Usually its about informing them of elements of the in-game fiction that they might not be aware of, or are overlooking.

    Example:

    Me: The demon runs past a crowd of inner city school children.

    Player: I unscreen my gatling gun arms and go to town on him!

    Me: Uh, that might be a bad idea what with the school children.

    Player: Oh.

    \n One way in which record keeping can become a GM role is when the record is held as truth beyond what has merely been described so far. I.e., when the record represents the game. The GM might delegate this authority, but if he can say, for example, "Give me your character sheet," then it is ultimately part of the GM role.

    But for that matter...

    Delegator: you decide who fills what roles at what points in the game.
