    \nLet us suppose that you have made an accurate assessment of the situation as it currently exists, and thus that your statements about Vincent Baker's development abilities relative to your own are essentially correct. In this case, the stated evidence leads to two possible and mutually compatible interpretations:

    1) He is much smarter than you are, or otherwise more naturally talented.
    2) He is much more skilled than you are, or otherwise more practiced.

    Each of these, in turn, has a sliding scale of possibilities whereby they may come about. For example, if option 1 is true, then it may be that you are very stupid, that Vincent Baker is very gifted, or some middle possibility. Likewise, if option 2 is true, then it may be that you have only been able to acquire a meager skill set, that Vincent Baker may be extremely well-practiced and skilled, or some middle possibility.

    I will assume that you wish to rectify this perception of imbalance. You may find it difficult to raise your baseline intellectual capacity in order to neutralize the deficit indicated in option 1. Regarding option 2, sustained and focused practice under the guidance and criticism of a substantially more advanced mentor may serve as a remedy. Given that you admit to being a poor designer by comparison, it may be a wise strategy to assume that you are severely lacking in basic design skills, including the ability to accurately assess your own design skills, and seek a mentor accordingly. The value of this strategy is that it strongly targets the set of circumstances that you are most able to address in practice, regardless of whether or not option 2 obtains as a dominant factor.
