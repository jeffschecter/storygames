  • Lxndr
    September 2006
    Fastlane does this. Each conflicts leads people with a whole lot of chips, which they can spend on each other in any conceivable form. If one person spends more chips than another specifically against that person, they win that particular contest (the Fastlane term for these sub-conflicts). So if you have person 1 and person 3 working against one another, and person 2 wanting to help person 1 and harm person 3, person 2 can still easily be on their own side.

    And if person 2 commits more to defeat person 3 than person 1 does in a single contest, person 2 probably can get a 'favor' from person 1 as well (getting that character to mechanically acknowledge that they were helped, and that they "owe).

    So I guess Fastlane's solution is to allow multiple stacks of chips to be created to stand in for different contests or sub-configurations, and you tend to wind up with no more than 1 winner per contest (though if that winner was helped, they might owe). Then the overall conflict has as many configurations as there are contests, and there can be one obvious winner, or not.

    Yeah dude. Traditionally in games (TORG rings a bell here) you do this:

    1) Boil the action not into confilcts, but right down into TASKS.

    2) Order the tasks in CHRONOLOGICAL ORDER. Even if one task (Andy shoots Tony) is just an instant befor or after another one (Rick shields Andy; Joshua shoots Andy). You have a nice layer cake of tasks, in chronological order.

    3) For those single tasks, those single chronological intentions, you will always have two sides: One dude on one side, one dude on another.

    4) Sometimes for those single tasks, you have more people on each side. That's simple, you have all the others "support" each other somehow. Most trad games have mechanics for support/help (again, TORG is a good example).

    5) You sort out who did what in chronological task 1. Then move to 2. Then to 3. By then, either the overall scene is still clean, playing out as thought, or the entire landscape has changed because of the results of tasks 1 or 2.

    So, here's my proposed solution for dealing with the issue for Conflict Resolution:

    1A) Narrow the conflict down to tasks. Voila!

    4) Use the solution presented in step 3 in your own design.

    Tony, take a look at Nine Worlds. Its conflict mechanics come into play whenever any set of players come into disagreement about what to do next. Each player says what they want to happen, and then you figure out who is in necessary conflict with who -- with two players, it will be A versus B, but with three it can be A versus B and A versus C independently, or A versus B versus C, or basically any undirected graph of the players. Then, the conflict resolution mechanics decide who gets the most narrative authority, who gets second-most, and so on all the way down, and you can overrule the narration of anyone you beat (and anyone who beat you can overrule you).

    It's really, really nice -- Muses are the flashiest part of 9W's system, but I think this is maybe the most elegant mechanic in the game.
