\n Tell me about a song and why that makes you think about a game.

Here's one:

I'm No Heroine, by Ani DiFranco, is clearly the soundtrack to the Serenity game as run by my sexy, gothy, geeky, feminist, Serenity fangirl friend Kate. The song sounds like something that would come from a woman who has left the Core Worlds for the moons out on the rim... one who knows that the Alliance won, and can't ever settle down in a world without brown coats. Not to mention the instrumentation has a lot in common with the Ballad of Serenity.

    \nTim Hecker - Dungeoneering

    This song (and, really, all of Tim Hecker's work) make me think of Ghost/Echo like crazy. They are both vague and paint the same sort of weird setting in broad strokes. There are certain Tim Hecker songs where faded TV or radio samples emerge and sink into an ocean of glacial static - this is what I imagine divining the Ghost Field to be like.

    \n Well, I don't have a song that reminds me of a game, but back when folks were doing the roleplaying poems, I experimented with a little boardgame/story game hybrid inspired by The Green Fields of France ( Willie McBride), called Nameless, Faceless ( Remembrance Day)

    Well, how do you do, Private William McBride,
    Do you mind if I sit down here by your graveside?
    And rest for awhile in the warm summer sun,
    I've been walking all day, and I'm nearly done.
    And I see by your gravestone you were only 19
    When you joined the great fallen of 1916,
    Well, I hope you died quick and I hope you died clean
    Or, Willie McBride, was it slow and obscene?

    Did they Beat the drum slowly, did the play the pipes lowly?
    Did the rifles fir o'er you as they lowered you down?
    Did the bugles sound The Last Post in chorus?
    Did the pipes play the Flowers of the Forest?

    And did you leave a wife or a sweetheart behind
    In some loyal heart is your memory enshrined?
    And, though you died back in 1916,
    To that loyal heart are you forever 19?
    Or are you a stranger without even a name,
    Forever enshrined behind some glass pane,
    In an old photograph, torn and tattered and stained,
    And fading to yellow in a brown leather frame?

    The game was basically meant as a learning excercise for folks who find minis use to be too constrictive on creativity, rather than a jumping-off point for it.

    It involved moving small groups (3) of plastic WW1 figures across the board, representing a unit going over the top. As the groups of figures moved into each new space, the player would flip a card, calling for some sort of backfill of story related to one of the miniatures. The cards involved some personal bit of backstory about the character's life,basically either calling for info to be created or a short vignette to be created. The card was then resolved, puncuated, by something game-fiction current in the form of a roll against some fairly arbitrary battlefield event. The game ended when either the player managed to get 3 or more of their 5 3 man units into the enemy trench, or when it became clear that they could never achiee that goal.

    \n When asked to combine music and roleplaying, people usually just dump a lot of Dragonforce and Rhapsody youtube links, so this thread is a bit of a surprise. (then again, this is Story Games, the home of dirty indie hippies)

    Tom Waits has a bunch of tracks that make me want to run games, both in terms of atmosphere and lyrics. Singapore is like some Over The Edge game run by your local William Burroughs affecionado who had read The Sailor Who Fell out of Grace with the Sea while watching Pirates of the Carribean the previous night. Downtown Train is like a bunch of unsuspecting friends taking a road trip through the american countryside before they get stuck into what is essentially a cross between Twin Peaks and Silent Hill and the CD suddenly skips to God's Away on Business.

    One day I'd like to play/write/run a game based on the music of Xiu Xiu.

    Oh yeah, and Black Sabbath games almost write themselves.
