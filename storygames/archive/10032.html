Posted By: aeonite"OK you guys have to negotiate with the orc king or he will attack you. You need 5 successes before 3 failures. What do you do? Ed you go first."

"Well Intimidate is my best skill so I'll try that." (rolls)

"Ok that's one success. The orc king is impressed by your bravery. Who's next? Fred?"

"OK I'll try Diplomacy since that's my best skill." (rolls)

"Ooh, sorry, that's a failure. Diplomacy is not on my list of approved skills for this challenge. The orc king gets mad at you. Ted?"

"Hmmm. Well I guess I'll try Knowledge: Local History." (rolls)

"Ok that's two successes. The orc king is impressed that you know about his people's history in this land. Ed, what about you?"

"Is it my turn again? OK, well I'll try to Intimidate again because that's my best skill." (rolls)

"OK three successes. The orc king is even more intimidated now. Fred?"

"Well Diplomacy is my best skill and we can't use that, so I pass."

"Ok, Ted, your turn."

etc.

The argument I always hear when I bring this up is "Well you have a bad DM and/or a bad group of players and/or a bunch of novices" but that is patently untrue. It's occurred in 5 separate instances with 5 different groups and both experienced and inexperienced GMs. Something about the way skill challenges are described eats away at the very soul of role-playing and seems to turn what used to be role-played scenarios into a half hour of die rolling.
\nI'm pretty unfamiliar with the details of 4e, so I didn't know this was the primary mechanic for skill challenges. Mechanically, I think it's pretty clever if it can find the right kind of story or theme.

So what is it good for?

    \n Duty and Honour uses a similar mechanic at a larger scale for Missions. Usually you've got two deadlines for a mission. The first is a Story Deadline (You've got to take and secure the fort before reinforcements arrive). The second is more akin to skill challenges. You've got to have X successful tests before Y failed tests or the mission fails.

    So you may need 4 successes to take the fort. (D&H is a game of Soldiers in the Wellington's Army in the Peninsular Campaign)\n
      \n
    1. The first is getting your group there undetected. Success! You sneak past a patrol and make your way to the town.
      2. \n
    2. Next recruit local help/ intelligence. Failure! The locals, while sympathetic to the British, are not willing to risk their necks.
      3. \n
    3. So you have to scout out the Fort's defenses your self. Success! Skulking around, sometimes in disguise, you find the forts strengths and weaknesses.
      4. \n
    4. Next you set up traps and ambushes to take patrols from the fort. Success! You capture two patrols and question them about the fort's defenses.
      5. \n
    5. Sadly you were unable to prevent a French soldier from escaping. Failure! While you were wooing a local Spanish lovely, a tenacious Frenchy snuck out of the make-shift prison and is hiding in the woods.
      6. \n
    6. And the Search for him alerted the Fort's Commissar that something strange is going on. Failure! You actions looking for the escapee find him dead in a creek, but all the activity looks suspicious to the Commissar.
      7. \n
    7. The time to attack is now. the reinforcements are coming and the Commissar is suspicious. There not a moment to loose! Succes of Fail?!
      8. \n
    \nI don't know how much this address your question, but it struck me as an interesting example of parallel design.

Instead of just having to win over the king, I'd spread the thing out over the entire evening. Players decide who they want to approach, and you need to change the opinion of the court as a whole rather than one guy.

    The way I'm familiar with pre-4e skill rolls coming into play is a bit different:

    Jimbob (DM): "You enter the throne room of the orc king. From atop his throne, he glares at you.'Tell me why I should not kill you where you stand.'"

    Ed: "Uh..."

    Jimbob: "He raises his hand to summon his guards. You guys got 5 seconds."

    Ed: "Grungar the Barbarian gets in the face of the orc king. 'I have killed bigger and uglier things that you, little king. Do you really want to tangle with GRUNGAR THE BARBARIAN?!' "

    (perhaps a few minutes of RP ensues)

    Jimbob (DM): "The king sits back, obviously shaken. Give me an Intimidation roll at plus 10."

    Ed: (rolls) 17 plus 10 is 27!

    Adam: "The orc king cowers before you. 'You may pass through this room without harm,' he says."

    Key differences for me:

    1) The RP sort of drifts in very naturally from the encounter without it being called out as a Skill Challenge situation. If the DM wishes, he could easily resolve the situation based merely on the RP, or a secret die roll. If he already knows he wants the situation to go in a specific direction, he can do so. If he wants the king to be shaken, then the king is always shaken.

    2) But if he does want it to be based on a die roll, he then assigns an appropriate bonus to the roll based on the scenario as it unfolded. This can happen in 4e as well, BUT because of the sense that there are at least four more die rolls coming, I think there's less willingness on the part of the players to invest in the situation. Especially if the situation comes around to Ed a second or third time, where it starts to get ridiculous...

    Jimbob (DM): "The orc king is shaken but still not convinced. Ed?"

    Ed: "Uh... I intimidate again?"

    Jimbob: "Role-play it.."

    Ed: "I already did. Twice. How many ways can I intimidate a guy?"

    Jimbob: (arms folded, eyes narrowed) "Role-play."

    Which comes to...

    3) The mechanics of my preferred method are all wrapped up in one die roll, with mods appropriate to the RP, and possible mods added from other players assisting. Here, the one die roll becomes important, and once resolved it dictates outcome and you can move on. Whereas a Skill Challenge that (RAW) requires a series of die rolls so you can stack up successes makes each individual role less meaningful, and possibly meaningless. The DM could always rule after the first Intimidation that the Challenge is over because Ed was so awesome, but that's not how "it's supposed to go."

    \n If it were proposed as a skill challenge, then the DM thinks it's something that will require a number of different approaches. A combination of things, not just one player hammering Intimidate over and over. If it's not that kind of thing, then a single Intimidate check might be fine.

    As a skill challenge, if Ed keeps saying, "How many ways can I intimidate a guy?" my answer would be, "I don't know, Ed. If you can't think of any more, then you better try a different skill." Social encounters require some player skill in D&D. You have to at least justify the skill you are using with some kind of believable cause-and-effect in the fiction. It's why Ed can't say, "I use Arcana to get past the orc king." Every player at the table needs to know /how/. Every skill use has to be justified. If you keep hammering at the same skill, you better be able to back it up with role-playing and justification in the fiction. Perhaps Ed resorts to threats that are scarier and scarier. "I'm gonna hurt you, little man." "I will gouge out your eyes with my knife and fry them with some trail bacon." "HRANGAR cracks the muscles in his back and tells the orc that he's going to break him in half and suck the fluid out of his spine." Ed is just being a baby, in my opinion.

    If Ed role-plays something awesome, I give him a +2 bonus or something. Yay.

    But this challenge wasn't set up as "You guys have to /intimidate/ the orc king to avoid fighting him." It's open-ended. Skills that seem reasonable to me: Intimidate, Diplomacy, Bluff, Stealth. Skills that are sketchy or might be good "aid another" skills: Athletics (run past him), Streetwise (use contacts to know something about the orc?), Insight (understand that the king just wants to be loved?).

    \n Yep, could say the same thing about DitV, too--the Call BS Rule is important when the right to use an in-game ability is constrained by providing narrative rationale for its use. (I am facing the same issue with ASCII @HACK, and I wish I could find a mechanical way to enforce invocation rather than rely on group consensus.)
    -----
    I think "roll X successes before Y failures" could work for a caper-style story.

    But the real thing I see missing from skill challenges is a mechanical feedback into the X/Y system. A success should not only get one closer to X, but should have some knock-on benefit for subsequent checks, as appropriate (and vice versa, for failures). For example:

    Seducing The Princess
    * The princess is always has the first dance and is ever the eyes of the entire court.
    -- Success with Dancing: +2 to Seduction check; failure: \xb10
    * Proper manners only, for a proper lady.
    -- Success with Etiquette: +1 to Seduction check; failure: -1
    * She values a subtle mind, and will brook no blowhards.
    -- Success with Diplomacy: \xb10 to Seduction check; failure -2
    * "We are not amused."
    -- Attempting any Carousing or Gamboling: -2 to Seduction check
    * She has had the best tutors... and so rarely has challenging conversation, unfortunately.
    -- Attempting any History or Knowledge: +1 to Seduction roll

    And, of course, the Seduction roll must be the last roll. In fact, this could be done without even having a "roll X before Y" setup; you could just let the PC(s) keep trying different conversational and social gambits, either building up a huge bonus (sweeping her off her feet, so to speak) or getting enough rope to hang themselves (possibly never even attempting to Seduce, as her aloof attitude slowly perverts to chilly disinterest, contemptuous disdain, and finally affronted fury!).

    In fact... why don't more games do this sort of thing, inherently, with skill checks? I *think* Burning Wheel does something like this with Extended Challenges (success = an extra Helping die, maybe?); and I think the old James Bond Role Playing Game did this very thing with seduction (I know it took multiple checks, one for each stage of the "dance"). But why don't more games actually build + and - into extended task resolution, factored by the choice of action. Maybe it would make for too many Charts and Graphs, just to design an encounter (as above--and what if they wanted to, say, get her to leave the castle without doing a seduction? What +/- for THOSE related skills?)?

    A simpler method would be to just let the roll-over of a success add some amount (say half of the roll-over) to the following roll; and vice versa for the roll-under of a failure. That, however, can make for a virtuous or vicious spiral, hinging largely on the first roll. So maybe any success is a flat +1 and any failure is a flat -1? That seems almost irrelevant, on a scale of 1 to 20.

    I dunno... it seems like yet another D&D system which depends almost entirely on the GM/module to make the challenge interesting and narrative and not just a roll-off.

    \n Long thread. Too long for me to bother reading all of it, so apologies if this has already been mentioned.

    The problem with "X successes before Y failures" from a story point of view is that most stories we're used to succeed or fail at the last moment. If the movie has a tragic ending, it's not when they're halfway to the goal. They almost get to it, but then they fail, so close to their goal.

    If you want that sort of tension, "X successes before Y failures" might not be the way to go. You could have a conflict tree, or whatever it's called, instead, where each roll affects your chances on the final roll. So there's five rolls and then a final roll. Every success gives you an extra die and every failure gives you a higher target number (because making it linear, so success and failure cancel each other out, is boring). Or something similar. That will keep the tension, as you won't fail until the last minute, and no matter how successful you are, the final roll always has the possibility of screwing you over.

    Yeah, I didn't so much answer "What kind of story is 'X successes before Y failures' good for?", but I said what kind they're NOT good for, so extrapolate from there.

    \n Skill challenges are an awesome part of my group's toolkit because:

    1) D&D has never had built-in scene-framing tools before - just rules for overland travel and random encounters and all kinds of simmy stuff. Suddenly, there's a system-approved way to generate a vignette that glosses over 200 miles of overland travel through the Fire Swamp.

    2) They let each player add some color by showing their character doing something cool, without the bulk of the full combat system. I adhere pretty strictly to "Each player must take a turn before any player can take a second turn". I don't really know whether that's actually in the 4E rules or not, or whether I just cribbed it from DitV. Either way, each player has vastly different skills that they try to apply to any given challenge, and it gives everyone a little chance to step on up and flex their creative-bullshitting chops. Almost like a built-in Baron Munchausen minigame.

    3) Even if nothing is really at stake in a skill challenge - ESPECIALLY if nothing is at stake - it keeps up a steady stream of points of contact with the character sheet - and thus with the player's build choices. For me, it makes the social transitions from non-combat to combat much less jarring, because it means that d20s are constantly hitting the table no matter what is going on.

    4) I've never had a Skill Challenge take more than ten minutes. So the fiction stays intact and running during that little window of time when one player hits the restroom, a second person grabs a soda from the fridge, and a third is wiping down the battlemat and clearing off the dead minis. And soon enough, we're ready for the next elaborate set piece fight scene. Which is why we play 4E in the first place, because everyone at the table has already beaten Final Fantasy Tactics multiple times each and we're tired of waiting for sequels. So we're rolling our own.

      Posted By: Simon_PetterssonLong thread. Too long for me to bother reading all of it, so apologies if this has already been mentioned.

      The problem with "X successes before Y failures" from a story point of view is that most stories we're used to succeed or fail at the last moment. If the movie has a tragic ending, it's not when they're halfway to the goal. They almost get to it, but then they fail, so close to their goal.

      If you want that sort of tension, "X successes before Y failures" might not be the way to go. You could have a conflict tree, or whatever it's called, instead, where each roll affects your chances on the final roll. So there's five rolls and then a final roll. Every success gives you an extra die and every failure gives you a higher target number (because making it linear, so success and failure cancel each other out, is boring). Or something similar. That will keep the tension, as you won't fail until the last minute, and no matter how successful you are, the final roll always has the possibility of screwing you over.

      Yeah, I didn't so much answer "What kind of story is 'X successes before Y failures' good for?", but I said what kind they're NOT good for, so extrapolate from there.
      \nThis reminds me totally of how Donjon works. If you succeed at sneaking up behind someone, rather than declaring that you sneaked up, you add those dice to your following attack roll, meaning you're going to end up doing massively more damage. That points to a good design for this kind of thing, versus D&D not having a good design for it. If you have to think this hard how to make it work, it's not that useful of a mechanic. I suppose with a game as popular as 4e it might be necessary to figure out how to really introduce them well, but part of the issue I see is that it just shifts probabilities.

      If you need to do a skill challenge with 5 successes before 3 failures, and the skill you're using has a 75% chance of success each time, you're taking a 75% chance and turning it into close to 100%, since averaged out it'll be success, success, success, fail, success, success. Even if you start with the fail it's fail, success, success, success, fail, success, success. With a skill that has a 75% chance of success against the target difficulty, with 6 successes, 2 failures, you've changed it to a 50% chance of success overall. Does that end up really making sense? 'Cause when you have a system that doesn't make sense mathematically, it's hard to justify roleplaying with it when the outcome isn't likely to be what the numbers on your character sheet suggest they should be.

      \n I've had Skill Challenges go across the board from really awesome scenes or a series of scenes that move the narrative forward to mechanical speedbumps that just sort of throw everyone off. The discussions regarding Skill Challenges remind of the discussions around HeroQuest 1.0's Extended Contests back on the Forge. And to a degree a lot of the advice from one bleeds over into the other.

      Not everything should be a Skill Challenge. Or needs to be. Skill Challenges should, perhaps, be the 4e equivalent of Bringing Down the Pain in tSoY. Skill Challenges are also easier to comprehend in the context of broader spans of time. For example, my Skill Challenge to break into an ancient tomb would've probably been best served as a single roll. But my Skill Challenge to seduce a noble woman was spot on, primarily because each "round" in the Skill Challenge was spread out across a more organic timespan than round-to-round.

      This is all stuff I'm still learning by trial-and-error. And I agree that the what-to-do-with-it portion of Skill Challenges in the DMG were pretty weak. But there's bones there that can provide a framework for fun. Especially for the story-gamer types.

      Some things I've tried to do that have met with success...\n
        \n
      • Adam's advice about prompting the player's to roleplay what they're doing before announcing which skill they use is spot on. My players know, or should, that a good enough roleplay is grounds for an automatic success. And a bad one an automatic failure. Players only roll when the outcome is in question. And the DM decides the skill. But backtracking is fine.
        • \n
      • The Aid Another rules work great in combat because you are effectively sacrificing a standard action to give someone else a +2. In a skill challenge, there's no real cost for that. So I've been using a different mechanic in Skill Challenges for aiding someone else. Mainly, the DC is set as usual. When the player rolls a success earns a +1. Every +5 points above the DC gives them another +1. Every -5 points below the DC is a -1. But failing by less than -5 doesn't do anything. That's worked a lot better than the bog standard +2 for rolling over a 10
        • \n
      • I like the idea of using successes and failures in a narrative fashion ala Donjon. But I haven't gotten a D&D group to bite on that one yet.
        • \n
      • I also like the idea of rising/falling tension suggested by Morningstar's flipping of success/fail. In my experience, though, I think the best way to do that would be to have each success increase the DC of subsequent rolls and each failure lower them. Maybe +/- 2. That right there could go a long way towards giving Skill Challenges a tension that they sometimes start missing in play.
        • \n
      \nThat's all. Sorry for the long post.

      \n I just had another thought for dice mechanics where it would somehow seem less contrived in a more fixed skill challenge like is being talked about.

      Go to a dice pool system that uses poker dice, with the trick to get a hand of a certain value with a certain number of tries. You start rolling, and maybe you get a pair and everything else is unconnected, you then decide if you keep the pair to try bringing it to 3 of a kind and 4 of a kind on retries, or if you re-roll entirely. If you're sticking with your hand and only re-rolling a few dice, it suggests you're going to keep going at the angle you've started with, if you're completely switching gears and re-rolling everything, you have to say what you're changing about your approach.

      You could also do it with cards, but have the GM take the discarded cards and see what kind of hand he can make with it, so the trick is to make your hand of a certain level or higher, before the GM can construct a better hand from the cards you threw away.

      It puts something of a game back into it, and makes things more alive, rather than asking for a bunch of rolls (which could really be handled, if it's 5 successes before 3 failures, by throwing 8d20 and seeing if 5 or more beat the skill challenge rank, and be done with it).
