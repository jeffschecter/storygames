\n The last two games I've played (Misery Bubblegum and the Venetian Tragedy) had some rather long conversations where the two actors were butting heads verbally but no declarative statements were made that someone could say "That might not be so easy" or "No you fucking don't" or play a card on, sort of like:

"I'm going to demote you"
"But you can't, because ... "
"That's not true, I'm going to demote you anyway"
"But you shouldn't ..."
Etcetera.

And it either goes on and on, or someone steps up and says, "I think you're trying to convince him of this and That Might Not Be So Easy" - which works, but feels like it breaks up the flow, things grind to a halt where we all try to figure out what exactly the conflict is, what statement we're making that is open to question...

And we noted that this doesn't really happen in Apocalypse World - if, in Apocalypse World, someone said
"You can't demote me, because ..."
the GM would probably say, "Cool, so you're trying to manipulate him?"
Plus AW's got reading people and going aggro and other ways it engages with dialog.

How do we bring that to Archipelago & In a Wicked Age & Misery Bubblegum where controlling narration is the main game mechanic?

    \n I don't know if you've tried it yet, Jamie, but this is something Polaris does very well. With practice.

    I haven't played Misery Bubblegum but I can tell you that the flow gets much better with Archipelago II with practice and with players who've played together before. I didn't provide enough example before our Venetian game and may not have explained the Resolution card protocols as well as I have previously with LitToS, a game I've played several times now. I noticed fewer declarative statements in our game than I've seen in other freeform-ish games and it was often me jumping in to point out the conflict. And because I wasn't saying "That Might Not Be So Easy" immediately after someone saying something like "And then I (Lucia) seduce Stephano and he wakes up in my chambers," we had to then break up play a bit to fix who was the actor in question. If there's no declarative statement you have to figure our who the "I" is before "Yes, but" or "No, and", for example, can be interpreted without confusion.

    In repeated Love in the Time of Sei\xf0 games I've seen myself, by way of example, and then others add a forceful intonation to a narrated sentence that I/we fully expect to be challenged by TMNBSE.

    Also it's so fun watching guys like Jackson or Johnzo chew the scenery that I may have not stepped in as facilitator/editor as quickly as I might usually.

    Not trying to negate/de-rail your query. There's lots to be discussed here, I think.

    With Archipelago hacks, it might be as simple as having a rule or guideline in the text, something like: Every couple-few lines of dialog, make a declarative statement about what you're trying to accomplish with that dialog.     And here's an embryonic idea - what about a game where anything you say in dialog, to another player, they have to accept as truth or agree to, unless they engage mechanics. Something like -
    Player A: 'You're a piece of shit!'
    Player B: I roll to disbelieve. (Rolls, whiffs.) I feel the icy hand of guilt upon my heart. Um. "I may be a piece of shit, but you made me what I am!"
    Player A: I roll to disbelieve. (Rolls, makes it.) "We all make ourselves in this world, son. Go be a better man."
    Player B: I roll to resist manipulation. (Rolls, whiffs.) Fuck. "Well, maybe I will! I'll show you!"

    \n Something cool about Apocalypse World is that one player's PC can be talking with another player's PC and then when/if the conversation moves towards manipulation or violence, the player that reaches for the dice and says they're trying to Manipulate or go Aggro on the other PC has an advantage for simply moving towards conflict before the other player does. The other PC can maybe hinder if they know (Hx) the more aggresive PC well, but they're probably at a disadvantage for having not taken the initiative in the mechanical part of the conflict.

    That might be impossible to emmulate with straight freeform but with freeform + resolution cards it might be difficult but possible to do in some way. Like maybe some portion of the cards have two possible outcomes and the player that provoked the TMNBSE statement gets their choice of between the two outcomes.

    Or maybe you silently take a chip every time you provoke conflict in a scene and you can turn in two chips for a resolution card re-draw if the first one doesn't appeal.

    Still pretty freeform, but adds some conflict incentives. These thoughts are more in service of driving towards declarative statements than helping the flow.

    LitToS has some pretty good scene examples. I should have looked at those before our game and read a couple. I've done that every time I've played LitToS but the Venetian Tragedy playtest document doesn't have any yet, I think.

    Posted By: jdfristromHow would you write a rule or guideline for a game text that expresses this?
    I don't know that you can make a game rule for this. You can make a social cue, though, because it is predicated on trust. I was writing about this for (cough) a thing I'm working on just yesterday:

    You need to be confident that your table-mates are bringing the same enthusiasm, energy and devilish creativity to the game that you are. Everybody wants to succeed, and shared fun is the metric of that success.

    You also need to be confident in your own native genius. You know how to tell a story, you know how to work with others, take turns and follow rules, and you know what you like. That is more than enough!

    So you trust your table, you are brimming with self-confidence, and you are primed and ready to have fun. The rest is gravy.

    If you trust the people you are playing with, you know that as brilliant players themselves they are going to introduce really juicy stuff to the game. Crazy, unexpected stuff that is absolutely going to throw you for a loop.

    This is called generosity. You had ideas for your little dude, and even better ideas for their little dudes, but all that is going to change, fast - you've been given a gift. The wild ideas that have upset your own apple cart are the building blocks you get to use to make things even more fun. Pretty soon you'll be giving gifts of your own.

    If you're confident in yourself, you know that you can roll with the punches with the best of them.

    This is called agreement. That crazy, unexpected stuff? You're going to take that, build on it, and give as good as you get. "Yes," you'll say, "and..." Pretty soon people will be agreeing to your contributions, and ... you see where this is going?

    \nFor what it's worth, I had this problem as recently as yesterday in a game of Fiasco where several of the scenes were social discussions of plans. One way we approached it was to say, "Okay, let's take this scene further, past where we've talked about this and made a decision." If there was a problem with that, then there was a problem with the words - the proposed decision - which led to us resolving the scene. If there wasn't, then we could get to whatever it was that this scene was actually about. What you might be less happy with is the notion that you're breaking character here to do such a thing. There's not much that can help with that. I think it's like coming up for air. You take your bearing, figure out if this is the end of the scene, and then if not, dive back under for more role-playing. Sometimes one OCC question is worth ten minutes of in-character arguments.

    Also, I sincerely hope you got to use the phrase "Why are you arguing what's already been decided?" in your scene. :P
