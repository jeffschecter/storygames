\n In the thread on indians and culture, Brian ("brayo") said something that I would like to make into a new thread. He is referring to my use of orcs as natives in a conflict where the players are settlers.
Posted By: brayoTomas is tricking his players, yes, but that's what GMs do.
No, actually I'm not tricking my players. Most GMs I know don't trick their players.

If you want to understand my way of playing, and the results I get, this is a very important point.

I am feeding the players interaction with elements that are volatile, laden, provocative or shocking, to have the players experience that the situations are hard to control, and choices hard to make. And when choices are made, I follow up with consequences that makes the world come alive around them.

This is not trickery; it is working the imaginative field, deepening the theme and strengthening the conflicts, by simple and effective means. The players never have any reason to doubt my intentions. I will support their characters in scenes of harmony, in dealing with their everyday life, to make it easy to build a social base for their personality. But I will challenge their characters in scenes of conflict, to see how their personalities plays up in crisis. Through the characters, I will also strive to challenge the players themselves.

The point is to make players engage in the game, and to have them produce an emotional content that is in support of the game theme.\n
Posted By: brayoBut a better question might be not whether orcs are truly evil, but: does the range of attitudes towards orcs map to the range of attitudes towards Indians?
No, that is not a very interesting question at all; the orcs are not indians.

They are orcs, humanoids caught in a similar situation to that of the indians (and other people that have been victims in such conflicts). I want them to act somewhat like humans in that situation, invested with feelings of their own. The orcs should, being a fantasy-race, be sprinkled with exotic elements, of course. But ultimately; having orcs (or other NPCs) behaving in ways which are recognizable, helps us trigger emotional responses in the players.

That's where such game-play come alive. When we acknowledge feelings in others, it triggers feelings in ourselves. This is strengthening the emotional content of the game.

Doing this in fantasy games is great fun. It makes the games ten times as entertaining, and infinitely more interesting.

    \nI'd argue that it is only trickery if the information that can be sought out by the players (in or out of character) points only to X, and then along the way you reveal that it was really Y all along. If, however, your initial set-up leads them to believe X but it is possible for them to seek out information and realize that it's really Y before they've made an irrevocable decision, then I wouldn't call it trickery.     \n Tomas, the only thing that concerned me when I read what you intended was that it wasn't quite clear how the "these are orcs" part was going to be presented, and what your players would make of that statement. The way I could imagine it going wrong was if your players somehow read it as a cast-iron guarantee that these beings were objectively evil.

    To put it another way, at what level in the game is this going to be asserted as truth, from "it's just what some people in this world call them" upwards? If it is flagged too heavily as "just a name in the world" then it might give the game away too soon. But if asserted at too high a level, your players might legitimately feel misled later.

    I don't know if that's clear, so I'll give an example from a LARP I played which is one of the few times I've ever felt cheated, although the GM acted in perfectly good faith. The person who played the murder victim of course died fairly early on, so when a police inspector turned up to investigate the crime, no-one was particularly surprised to see this player reappear in a new guise. The problem was that the GM had decided to make a virtue of a necessity, and make the resemblance between the murder victim and the policeman part of the plot (they were related). But we all deliberately looked past the resemblance in game, and never commented on it, because we didn't want to flag up something that might damage people's immersion. You see the problem? It arises from confusing in-game vs out-of-game truths, I think.

    Edit: cross-posted with Troels who is making the same sort of point about how this can become problematic if done too crudely, although I don't see you as likely to fall into this trap.

    Posted By: Dave Hallettit wasn't quite clear how the "these are orcs" part was going to be presented
    They've been presented in various ways, always as an implied fact of the world,
    depending on who is talking:
    - prejudiced rumormongers: Orcs are filthy, thieving, murderous beasts! Kill'em!
    - old trappers: Orcs are peaceful, family-oriented, highly spiritual nomads.
    - fairly neutral: Orcs are green and fanged. They inhabited this land before us, but withdrew as part of a treaty.
    - prejudiced, but sympathetic: Orcs are primitive, and communicate in guttural grunts, but I believe they are generally peaceful.

    and then, when the time is ready;
    depending on what is happening:
    - encountering young orcs in "heat": It's a band of orcs, painted in red and black, with spears, very agitated, possibly angry! What do you do?
    - encountering orcs on the run: The orcs see you. They stop. Looks hither and dither, and then they make a run for it! What do you do?
    - encountering adult orc in intimate situation: The orc sniffs. It bends over, pisses the grass, growls and touch your arm with a curiously soft hand ...
    - etc.

    These are examples from actual play.

    Posted By: Dave HallettTomas, the only thing that concerned me when I read what you intended was that it wasn't quite clear how the "these are orcs" part was going to be presented, and what your players would make of that statement.
    They are presented as an in-game fact, Dave. As you may see from the last answer I gave you, the in-game presentation varies, so as to create an image that is both tense with conflict, and open for interpretation by the players.

    I really love to go about "facts" of the fiction this way, too serve the players with elements that hints on complex context and unpredictable consequences. It makes it easy for players to portray their characters on opposite sides of a discussion, and it makes it easy to reach a challenging depth in how conflicts are handled.

    And it opens the field for the choices made by the characters; as the field has been hinted at in all its width, any player-choice makes it is easy to close in on the details with the point of view of the characters in mind.
