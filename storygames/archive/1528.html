Dice Crisis of Faith

shreyas
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Play Advice

So, today, I asked myself, "To what extent do we need these baroque dice mechanics?"

One of the things that, historically, I thought was cool about the Forge was that everyone had his own cool dice trick. But these days, in the sort of carefully constructed story games people are building, I'm wondering if dice mechanics need to or should be as grabby and attention-getting as they are.

HQ is gonna be my whipping boy here; I feel like a very similar behavior could be generated a lot more cleanly, without the wackiness of augmentation and masteries and bumps and on and on; the choices I make when applying abilities to a contest are enough, without the rather intricate processing and output I get from the mechanic.

    "Make the mechanics as simple as possible, but no simpler."

    Can HQ really be done in a simpler way? I don't know the game system enough to tell. I'll take your word for it, though. And if so then yeah, simplifying it down is a clear win.

    But bear in mind that one of the things HQ does may be to focus your attention in certain places by making you spend time going through the mechanics in a particular way. So just getting the same mechanical outputs doesn't necessarily mean that you're having the same effect on the players.

    For instance, I could imagine a game where I required, by the rules, that people roll six d6s one at a time, pausing and making a notation after each one. That wouldn't be any different (in mechanical outputs) from having them roll the whole pile, but it would get across an entirely different emotional tenor.

    Shreyas,

    The professional statistician in me screams that cool/unique/baroque dice/card/whatever mechanics are critical - that to believe anything else is heresy.

    Alas, I think the reality is that the axiom of "Keep it Simple" is as true for story-games as it is for anything else. That said, I have a much longer and baroque reply.

    I think, fundamentally, the root question is "What makes a game?". By creating OGLs, we essentially say, "The game is the mechanics. And we own them - so you can't use them without our permission." If this is the case, then game designers must design their own mechanics so as not to put themselves in danger of IP violation. I think we all 'understand' the danger of creative license violation (ie., don't write a Star Trek game without a license from Paramont) and in this modern age of IP ownership, there is a natural concern as to how far that extends.

    Does that mean we should all make games based on the basic mechanics that someone else has done before and released under OGL? Not at all. If you can make an improvement on an existing mechanic, you should.

    More importantly, if your mechanic supports the story (for instance, a poker-based mechanic for a story-game related to Vegas or the Old West might be very appropriate) I think using a custom mechanic is as important as setting material.

    But in the end, your mechanic is only as good as how people use it. For instance, growing up, I played "D&D" with a couple different "GMs" who essentially ran everything with a single die - a d10 in at least one case. To me this makes a huge case for my original point, Keep It Simple. Unless your cool mechanic supports your story, maybe a simple die roll is good enough.....

    I go with preferring simplicity. A good bit of my own fooling around with dice systems was motivated by wanting to get rid of the chart lookups and other goofy math of HQ, as well as having a fetish for the D6--the working-class die, by God! Of course I ended up drifting a good ways afield, but the curve is simple, just small pools of d6, take the highest--there's only a couple curves to know and after that it's all shifting with straight-up sums.

    That said, if the dice wonkiness ties right in, I like it. Don't Rest Your Head is an excellent example. There are two readings on a roll necessary: you have to read how many 1-3s (successes, determining who wins) and the highest number picked from up to 4 sets (pain, discipline, exhaustion, and madness). But these do a lot more than determining success; they determine who wins, *and* at what cost, in one roll. That wins for simplicity and coolness.

They should serve a specific purpose not just get used because they are funky or 'all the cool games use them'. The three categories of impact are:
    (A) Color: Contributing to the thematic space. Generally by the type of randomizer chosen such as using cards (playing or Tarot) or Bacchanalâ€™s colored dice.
    (B) Focus and Feel: What influences success and determines consequences. Is it luck, training, inborn talent, passion, or thematic features (DitV escalating conflict and DRYH exhaustion and madness).
    (C) Pacing: How quickly can the result be read, understood, and implemented into the game. Referencing charts, rerolls, calculating modifiers, and choices or further rolls based on first result.

    In Shreyas example, if the same result could have been reached through the same interactions while focusing on the same things then I would say the rules are unwieldy and should have been written differently, if it would have played out differently then the rules serve a purpose (However lacking any actual knowledge of the HQ system or your group I won't comment on whether this purpose is a net positive or negative influence).

Baroque dice mechanics get more attention than the corresponding baroque rules systems because they are bite sized examples of how 'This Game' does things differently than 'That Game'.     I think HQ is designed to fill a couple of needs.
    1) It is a way for players to indicate what they are interested in and where they are at (the freeform skill naming system is a very intuitive way for players and GMs to gauge where everyone is and what they want from the game, e.g, do they call their skill Sense Motive, Savoir Faire or Mother told me not to trust a stranger).

    Shreyas: you're right.

    The functionalist purpose of most dice mechanics is to create a ritualized sequence of actions. For instance, in Dogs the ritual sequence of actions is

    1. Player one narrates something, and makes a Raise.
    2. Player two pushes forward dice, compares, and based on the comparison either Sees, Takes the Blow or Gives.

    The purpose of the ritual is to create a choice point where player two has to explicitly refute what player one says, explicitly affirm it, or to give up on the conflict. This means that when they repeat this ritual, the players repeatedly take each others' narrations into account. This has all sorts of good consequences for the strength and integrity of play, because the basic payoff of roleplaying is having the other players engage with your ideas, and Dogs makes you do it over and over.

    But: why not just say "Explicitly affirm, refute, or give" as the basic mechanic?

    Now we know that dice mechanics (or resource mechanics like Plot Points) can be helpful relative to pure freeform because they can be parasitic on our moral understanding of games. If breaking the ritual sequence of actions in a raise is cheating like cheating at cards, then the players are less likely to break that because they take it to be immoral. So a positive structure is enforced by our expectations about cheating and fair play.

    But relative to more structured freeform? That's a horse of an entirely different color, isn't it?
