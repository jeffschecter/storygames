    \n Are most of your scenes conflict driven?
    No, about one third are.

    If a scene doesn't have conflict, what do you do? Push for one? Skip it? Something else?
    Just let the scene unfold naturally.

    Before you play a scene, do you define the scene's Focus, Agenda, and Location?
    Yes.

    If not, how do you define a scene before play?
    Just like the book says.

    Does the Producer have stakes?
    Nope, but when I'm the producer my overall goal is to stir shit up.

    Can Protagonists have conflicts against each other?
    No, I can't figure out a way for that to work with the system.

    If yes, can they have Stakes that contradict each other?
    How would this work?

    If yes, do the Protagonists compare their cards against each other or against the Producer?
    I don't drift the rules this far, since I don't think that's the game's intent.

    If against the Producer, what happens if both Protagonists beat the Producer?
    They both win their stakes.

    How do you handle narration? Does the person with the highest card get to speak for the other characters? What do they do?
    High card narrates until he or she doesn't want to anymore. And yes, they speak for the characters.

    Does the Producer take the lead driving the plot?
    When I produce, I don't have a grand scheme, just things I'd like to do in individual scenes. But I try, and hopefully, so do the players.

    How do you define your stakes? If I win, this happens? The stakes are over X? Something else?
    I ask: "What do your characters want?"

    Do you describe how you are trying to win the stakes? If yes, at what point in the resolution process do you describe how?
    No no no, never. The high card describes how. ALWAYS, or there won't be much for the high card to narrate.

    Do you have any house rules for PTA? If yes, what are they?
    Lets see, our current season? And these may just be the way I produce:
    -Pitch session only lasts a half hour.
    -During pitch, if you say you don't like something that another player has come up with, you have to offer an alternative.
    -We don't play if all the original players can't make it to the session.
    -If a player forgets to give fan mail during a scene, we allow them to do it retroactively
    -If a player has an idea for a scene, I let them frame it even if it's not their turn. We then skip them when it is their turn.
    -We strictly adhere to including all the "Next On's" as a kind of goal for each episode. It kind of works as an interesting pacing mechanic to use in parallel to budget.

    Posted By: jenskotAre most of your scenes conflict driven?
    If a scene doesn't have conflict, what do you do? Push for one? Skip it? Something else?
    \nIt varies. Sometimes scenes make sense without a conflict in them. Sometimes they don't. Sometimes we're happy to just deal only with set-up or pay-off for other scenes. \n
    Posted By: jenskot
    Before you play a scene, do you define the scene's Focus, Agenda, and Location?
    If not, how do you define a scene before play?
    \nAt first I was very strict about defining Focus, Agenda and Location. I wanted to make sure that everybody at the table was clear about what was happening in the game. But I noticed that many of the people I played with had their own lingo to get some of the things across. And now we rarely actually name the Location of the scene. I still make sure that everybody pays attention to a scene's Agenda when the game starts, but once the players have included it in their pre-scene exchange.. I rarely have to mention it. It's the same with Focus, really. The less familiar the players are with TV conventions or articulating character-driven stories, the more often we make sure to include it. \n
    Posted By: jenskotDoes the Producer have stakes?
    Can Protagonists have conflicts against each other?
    If yes, can they have Stakes that contradict each other?
    If yes, do the Protagonists compare their cards against each other or against the Producer?
    If against the Producer, what happens if both Protagonists beat the Producer?
    \nThis also varies a lot. In some conflicts it makes sense for the characters to compare cards against the Producer. Most internal conflicts I'd say. In some cases it makes more sense for the players to compare cards with one another - explicitly antagonistic conflicts, like physical fights. It really has a lot to do with how the players look at the characters and the fiction. In some scenes conflicts are merely character issue projected onto the outside world, in others it's an outside conflict coloured by a character's issue. \n
    Posted By: jenskot
    How do you handle narration? Does the person with the highest card get to speak for the other characters? What do they do?
    \nAgain, it varies. Most of the times Highest Card = Veto Card. Character consistency is very important to me, more so than "my guy"-ism. Sometimes the highest card does overrule everybody else's opinion at the table, but then it's usually a question of "trust me guys, this is going to work". Sometimes it does, sometimes it doesn't. But we usually just want the game to carry on and not get bogged down by hairsplitting arguments that would make Trek fans blush. \n
    Posted By: jenskot
    Does the Producer take the lead driving the plot?
    \nThat depends on the show. If it's a character-driven drama, the Producer is mostly just making sure that the episode doesn't meander or leave to many questions unanswered. In a more plot-driven or franchise-heavy show, the Producer usually makes sure that the episode doesn't stray off the formula too much. Although single character spotlight episodes can sometimes choose to go a little off the rails. \n
    Posted By: jenskotHow do you define your stakes? If I win, this happens? The stakes are over X? Something else?
    \nAgain, it varies and depends mostly on what the players need to visualise the conflict. Sometimes it's as simple as saying "your issue independence is at stake". Sometimes it's about clarifying what the character wants to achieve. And sometimes sketching out some rough ideas of what a win and a loss means is needed.
