    \n I actually like the most recent iteration of Savage Worlds, but back in the day when Deadlands first came out it was very gimmicky. While playing it I felt like the "real" wizard in The Wizard of Oz. My character was looking all cool and stuff but behind the curtain I was pulling levers, punching buttons, stomping on foot pedals, etc. I was so busy looking "AT" the mechanics that I wasn't "IN" the fiction. The mechanics all worked okay, but they were too much and the same results could be achieved with a more streamlined approach. SW is still on the edge of acceptability for me, from a mechanics and crunch standpoint, but it's better than its ancestor was.

    \n Like "pretentious", I take "gimmicky" with a big grain of salt. Sometimes it's a reasoned critique, but often it's just a way of saying, "Other people seem to like it, but I don't."

    If someone designed an RPG using an Uno mechanic, and the mechanic didn't work well in play, I'd just call it unsuccessful. If they then went on to advertise the hell out of the game based on the Uno mechanic, then I might call it gimmicky.

    \nTHEN DON'T BE BUMMED

    Some games use a really unique, really strange, really obvious mechanic... and it works.
    See Dread, and how it re-conceives the Jenga tower as a tense horror pacing mechanism. Brilliant. Fucking brilliant.

    If you're using a Uno-type mechanic because that's right for the game, then rock the fuck out.

    I tend to ask myself:
    Am I being more elegant than clever, here?
    If yes, proceed.

    Posted By: Neko Ewen(I'm a little bummed because I'm working on a game that does in fact use an Uno-type mechanic, albeit because I felt it was legitimately the best thing for the game...)
    \nDamn! I knew that Uno thing had slipped into my subconscious from somewhere. But if it's the best thing for the game, it's not gimmicky.

    It's like this. If you read the Forge's First Thoughts forum, you'll often get threads like this: hey, guys, I've designed a game that uses die pools of d20s. Or: hey, I've designed a game that's based around Snap. Or: hey, I've designed a game based around spinning a coin.

    And then everyone says: wait, slow down, tell us what the game's about before you tell us about the cool mechanic.

    So that's the gimmick thing. (I apologise, in advance, if you have designed a game based around d20 die pools, Snap or spinning a coin. Your game is awesome.)

    Graham

    \n When I say 'Gimmicky', I mean that it's an action or gameplay feature that has no practical or mechanical effect upon the way the game works, but serves only to make the game different or gets in the way of play. There are very few games that I would describe as having Gimicky features, to be honest, and I can't think of even one off the top of my head. But that's me. I'm very open to new ideas and odd systems that other people might describe as gimmicky just because they aren't set in a traditional mould. So long as there is some manifest advantage to a particular mechanic, I generally don't regard it as gimmicky.

    -Ash
