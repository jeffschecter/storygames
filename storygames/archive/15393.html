\n My story: I've got some projects on my plate (both at work and at home), so I stayed away from Launch Day. Last week on Friday, I figured, "Hey, it's just a matter of time before I buy it anyway, let's do this" and got it from Steam. Cut to two nights later and I had already racked up 24 full hours.

Trying to mitigate my OCD-level of Skyrim obsession with the Fitocracy "Do ten pushups for every hour you play Skyrim!" challenge. I've done hundreds of pushups, and still about 200+ pushups in the hole. :-)

So, what parallels or common ground to tabletop gaming have you found?

For me, the main thing, as always with this series, is the wonderful graphics, views of beautiful fantasy stone structures and landscapes that I can "mentally take snapshots of" and later pull them into my games as GM to describe places, objects, sights and sounds.

An interesting thing: Right before I got into Skyrim I went through the great PS2 era action-RPG Odin Sphere on PSN. It has five main characters, 8 or so "areas" that repeat a lot, but between the scripting, voice acting, tie-ins between one character to another (I think I even shed a tear at the end of Mercedes the Fairy Queen's story when she revealed her True Name). So while it is nowhere near as gorgeous, open, varied as Skyrim, I found the story of Odin Sphere gripping.

Comparing that to Skyrim, I find the stories... not so gripping. I walk into SomeTown, I see that the Jarl is arguing with someone about some complicated neo-Tolkien medieval fantasy political shmutz that I don't particularly care about ("Let's just get to the point where you tell me who I need to put arrows into..."). The world/setting is deep (with TONS of books about the history scattered throughout the game) that I don't particularly find interesting. Maybe it's the fact that my character has no voice. The only time she talks is to Yelp In Pain and Shout Dragon Words. She has no history (she's a "wandering murder hobo!") or background. I guess it's up to my imagination - similar to those old ZORK games where we have to imagine everything in our heads - to give her a background and personality. But to me, it makes it easier to get into a character with a Voice and a Past.

However, having said that, I have to say that while I don't particularly care about the World At Large, I do find the small-scale stories that happen within it actually interesting. Like when I go into the Dark Brotherhood and see this little commune and see how there are individuals with their own interests and drives and conflicts, and work within that, that "hits a tabletop roleplaying button" for me. Or when I go to Town X and it has a bunch of people struggling with some particular issue or other related to other NPCs, a little "conflict microcosm", I feel connected to that. I don't really care about the overall plot of the game, but they did a really good job of making me care about "these four guys over here", "those six guys over there", "those two people who seem to hate each other yet need each other"... that stuff comes out pretty well, and I dig that.

How about you? What do you think?

(my character: A 39th level Redgaurd female sneak-and-bow type. An honorable fatalist (on one face she's honorable and 'good'; on her other face she's the leader of the Dark Brotherhood because That's The Way Things Have to Be)

    \n Modularity; The nice thing about Skyrim is that each system was developed independantly. From a design standpoint, the system looks as if it were developed and finished a long time ago, and the content was designed based on that system. Some might call it a bottom-up design structure. My major problem with skyrim is just that. Modularity. Maybe i've been doing this too long, but I take a look at the design of some of the quests and i start to see the seams in them. Some look really polished -- like the golden claw quest. Others look really unpolished like the magic college quests.

    Some mechanics were implemented that I used once and only once, like rotating the claw to see a key combination in the menu screen.

    I expected a little more from the game, and from a design standpoint, I saw fallout 3 with Oblivion content. I've played this game before and i'm not happy about it.

    \n I love how every town feels different from every other one. The changes in height and architectural style mean that I always know what town I'm hanging out in, even if I'm just fast-travelling in to sell off loot.

    I love how rich and detailed all the quests feel. I agree with you Andy, this game doesn't feel like it's "Here's one big epic story where you get to save the world". It's more about "Here's one little story about love, power, betrayal, etc. And maybe it turns out to be much bigger than you first thought. And here's another. And here's another."

    I like how the named vs misc quests are sometimes misleading. Some of the named ones are pretty simple and straightforward. Some of the misc ones are surprisingly deep and involved. That reminds me of a tabletop campaign game - sometimes little things become major elements because the players grab onto them and make them important.

    I like good crafting systems. So far smithing and enchanting have been fun. But as with previous games, the alchemy system makes me want to use the Internet. I wish they would put lots and lots more recipe sheets out as random things to find. There's alchemy labs showing up in dungeons in nearly random locations - do all these alchemists just keep all their knowledge in their heads and not take notes? Also - potions are really heavy in this game - self-made potions don't save you weight, the way they did in previous games, which further decreases my desire to spend time on alchemy. Which is sad, because I want to care about it. But it's a minor gripe, overall.

    Another minor gripe - it's neat that there's so many people you can get as a follower, but it results in having less rich dialog with each one. The followers in New Vegas were amazing - a major highlight of the game. In Skyrim they're enjoyable, and they definitely have a different feel from each other. But I think having too many choices meant that each one was less well done.

    Which is sad, because the locations you visit are extremely well done. Over and over and over again, I visit a new place, and it just feels so right. The people are having their various problems. And they're interesting ones. And sure, they all seem to have issues that a dangerous looking stranger would be perfect to solve, but for the most part the dialogs and interactions are great.

    So I wish there was that extra element where your follower would chime in and become a more interesting part of your explorations. And I don't just mean as part of an up front "follower quest", after which they lapse into silence once they become your actual follower.

    And it needs to be done in a way that isn't just "Oops. Can't do what I want to right now, because ___ is with me and they'll get mad and leave my party if I do that."

    I think having richer follower interactions would help decrease that sense of "I'm a wandering, backgroundless, murdering hobo that people in trouble have a bizzare habit of trusting instantly with their embarrassing secret problems." Because at least your followers would have stronger backgrounds that would interact with these people in interesting ways. And, of course, there's tons of potential for replay value that way.

    So I would have voted for a much smaller group of companions, each more strongly developed. Dragon Age, New Vegas, Mass Effect each did a better job with that element.

    But with that said, I totally agree that each of the little quest stories is exceptionally well crafted. This game does exactly what I want a crpg to do - it gives me tons and tons and tons of stuff to explore. And as I explore, it keeps giving me new and interesting things to see and do that haven't gotten repetitive at all.

    Another minor gripe - dragons are a little too easy to kill. I had much more trouble with a frost troll and with The Skinner guy in the Silver Hand. Maybe they'll level up with me and get harder (because I'm crafting and I don't feel super optimized). That being said, they're still pretty darn neat, the way they show up all over the place and not just is super scripted fight locations.

    I'm very ambivalent about the game. The game looks very pretty but is mechanically disastrous. Not a single bit of the mechanical subsystems work in a satisfying way. Some of the dungeon designs are interesting, an occasional quest is good. I hate almost every NPC I meet within seconds and I hate how I can't even interact with them in the way I want. I don't even think I can consider this a roleplaying game since roleplaying is so far removed from what I do while I play it. Every time I even feel an urge to Play My Character As If I Am In Their Shoes the game ties my hands. Racist asshole threatening to invade the homes of foreigners? I haven't found out how to trigger the quest involving him so I literally cannot interact with him in any way. Wrongfully accused some wizard of multiple murders? The game hasn't decided that I am allowed to present new evidence I found until arbitrary point A. Blatantly evil character? Can't just kill him and skip 3/4 of the magic school quests.

    \n

    Combat feels like two boxes rubbing up against each other until one of them runs out of points, stealth feels so disconnected from the game world that I can't believe anyone would even bother. Archery feels tepid. I feel spoiled by Action RPGs with good combat.

    \n

    I've played the game about 7 hours and I'm around level 25 I think. I have one good story. I saw a guy on the road collecting ingredients so I robbed him blind. 3 hours later, I am being attacked by Hired Thugs and Dark Brotherhood Assassins, hired by this guy. He really hated me.

    \n

    Regarding the setting, it's disappointing. I was very fond of Daggerfall and Morrowind's Lore, especially since Morrowind did really cool things with how the lore related and influenced the game, tying the color in with the mechanics. I loved how weird everything was in those two games. It should be no surprise that I hated Oblivion because of it's total lack of interesting lore, good mechanics or a decent plot. Skyrim tries a little bit to be interesting, and even shares some of the better writers from Morrowind but it's spread a bit thin and I keep getting the feeling that all the interesting stuff is outside of the region of Skyrim.

    \n Skyrim is an ankle-deep sandbox, with no real consequences for your actions outside the immediate and no living world. You can do a lot of cool things, and unique experiences through random chance but the game will not respond to your choices in any meaningful way.

    Breaking point: I did multiple quests for Dark Gods, and no-one cared not even my goodie-twoshoes sidekick. I've sacrificed countless people, and no one cares. No cultists are coming up to me, no witchhunters are after me, nothing-nadda-zip.

    I had fun until I realized how shallow and hollow the game was, after that my enjoyment took a noise dive. However, if you just want to explore some scenery, kill monsters, and organize bodies in funny poses the game can't beat (though you will have to crank the difficulty up after awhile, I swear I've doing the same damn tactics for the last 20 levels).

    At least in Fallout New Vegas your reputation could change based on your interactions with people, thus opening new questlines and such. There is nothing like this in Skyrim. The Witcher II, while being a more linear game, has a more tactical and satisfying approach to action-rpg combat, and far...far better writing.

    For my money, go with the New Vegas w/DLC or The Witcher II.

    \n New Vegas gave me 1 main storyline, accompanied by a big chunk of little side quests. The little side quests were good. I enjoyed them.

    Skyrim doesn't give me 1 main storyline. It gives me 2 really big quests - dragonborn quest and war quest. Plus it gives me a whole bunch of faction quest lines - Champions, Thieves, Assassins, Mages, etc. Plus it gives me Jarl quest lines. Plus it gives me "hey buddy can you help me" quests, like the Redguard lady in Whiterun. Plus it gives me "hey, you found this thing, now follow up on it" quests, like the Galdur amulet ones. Plus there's power grinding quests, like looking for more shouts. And then, after all that, there's the relatively generic "There's vampires in Bloodmaw cave." type quests.

    I've put in like 70 hours and I feel like I'm just getting my feet wet.

    So not every single one of the, what, 1000 named NPCs? remembers me and reacts in accordance to what all I've done.

    When the game gives me that many things to choose from, and does this good of a job of fleshing them out, I can live with that.

    Some games force you to do more of a "pick a side" thing. And people get whiny because they are forced to play the game through multiple times in order to see all the content. Other games let you pursure lots of different angles all at the same time, but people get whiny because they feel like the game doesn't react logically to their decision to be the paragon of good and champion of evil at the same time.

    Personally, I love it when a game lets me explore as much as possible of the content in a single pass through. I don't restart games and play them over again very often. So I am grateful that Skyrim is designed with a few limitations as possible. Sure, the result is that the game doesn't always react to my actions in a deep way.

    But here's the thing - in the games where the world reacts in big deep meaningful ways - that's because the game forced you to make a choice - generally a binary choice - at some point.

    And those games where you can complete a mission in like 7 different ways all the time - that's because those are pure linear mission-based games, rather than an open world where you can wander around do whatever damn thing you please whenever you want.

    Having worked with IT development teams for several years now, it blows my mind how good of a job this game does of keeping track of so much different stuff in order to present me with a world that feels so wornderfully persistant.

    Sometimes a little too persistant. I wish mudcrabs would respawn a little faster. They're my favorite way to capture petty souls for enchantment grinding.

    New Vegas wasn't made by Bethesda, which is why it has good writing and dynamic NPCs with personalities and opinions. It's also why it has a believable setting that doesn't fall apart as soon as you try to interact with it. New Vegas with the Dead Money DLC especially is the best bethesda style first person open world fuck-arounder gaming has. Probably what makes New Vegas work is, like so many classic 90s/early 00s rpgs, your choices matter to you and to the world around you.

    \n

    The problem with the NPCs in Skyrim. You are given at most two ways to interact with them from the following three: kill them, ignore them, do their 10 second fetch quest. At least 80% of the NPCs can only be ignored or killed, because they not any good for anything else. 10% can't be killed but can be ignored because they're tagged as plot important but don't actually have any bearing on any quests. 9.9% have 10 second fetch quests that they expect a legendary hero to do for some trifling gold pieces. The remaining dozen NPCs have questlines half as ambitious as those in Morrowind and probably three times as idiotic. That's not good enough for a game in 2011, especially not one that promises role playing. Cave Story, a japanese indie action game from 2004 has more elaborate NPC interactions on average.

    \n

    Re: the leveled monsters in Skyrim. They went with a modified Fallout New Vegas system. Normally, the first time you enter an area, that area will only have creatures and bad guys at your level and they will stay at the level they were when you first entered the area.. However, many of these areas have both a maximum level and a minimum level. So for instance if you are level 22 and first enter a level 1-15 area, everything will be at level 15, if you first enter a level 20-30 area, everything will be level 22. If you enter a level 30-50 area, everything will be level 30. It's an ok band-aid on a bad idea.

    \n

    It's still flawed. If you made the mistake of investing perks in non-combat skills in the first ten levels of the game, you will be horrifically outclassed by everything until you grind for a few levels and put points into a weapon skill or destruction magic. Conversely, if you focused solely on combat skills for the first ten levels, you will be blessed with never having to deal with the terrible combat system again. You can just relax and kill every enemy you encounter in one hit and so long as you drop a perk into a combat/destruction skill once every few levels, you'll keep such a massive lead on everything you encounter that the game never becomes a challenge. Don't make the mistake I did of assuming the skill number is used for anything except what perks you qualify for.
