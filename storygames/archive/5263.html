    \n To add a bit more content to that:
    \n
    \nOne of the things that makes Battlestar Galactica impressive is that it took an old, hokey TV series with little relevance, and--using the premise for allegorical purposes--turned it into a very relevant show about ethics and war. The "reboot" isn't about making Starbuck a hot blonde, or having some dude hallucinate about other hot blondes. You could keep all the little details of the original series completely intact, but if you changed the mood and manner of execution, you'd still have something worth watching. (Cf. Star Trek: Next Generation, which wasn't really a reboot at all. Though the proper nouns all changed, and the budget was a lot bigger, the general outlook is entirely consistent from show to show. It pretty much explores the same emotional space, with better or worse results depending on your taste.)
    \n
    \nSo, I think the idea would be: identify what's irrelevant and/or stupid-ass in Lord of the Rings, and deconstruct it.
    \n
    \nI think prime candidates are the role of women, elitism, and militarism.

    Posted By: Darcy Burgesscanon characters in a wholly new situation to serve a new artisitic purpose
    \nWitch-King, You So Cra-Zay is looking better and better. But I'm curious, Darcy: what "new artistic purpose" did you have in mind? Because it sounds like what you're looking for is a High Fantasy, Epic, set more or less in the recognizable parts of Middle-Earth, featuring the canon characters. Without some specific articulation of what you're looking for, this begins to sounds a lot like just doing Tolkein straight.

    (The fairly obvious contender for doing Tolkein is Burning Wheel, though I could see Shadow of Yesterday working too, or maybe In A Wicked Age for some of the smaller stories out of the Silmarillion. Midnight d20 is also an option. There may be a bunch of relatively newer games I know little about, too.)

    I think if you want to reimagine Tolkein, it makes sense to step back and look at typical Situations in his work. Which probably means revisiting a lot of northern European folk tales - the Icelandic sagas, the Niebulungenleid, Wagner's Ring Cycle, Beowulf, that sort of thing.

    One of the troubles with Tolkein-as-relevant-to-Western-Culture is that the stuff Tolkein is talking about resonates very deeply with how our civilization approaches things--and as a result, the sort of archetypes Tolkein conjures up get appropriated by our culture all the time. Do you remember how the movie version of The Two Towers was used as rhetoric in favor of invading Iraq?

    So, to some extent you've got exactly the opposite problem with the Battlestar Galactica guys: they had a dopey science-fiction story with ideas taken from fringe lunatics like Erich Von Danniken, and they tried to make it resonant by deconstructing it. Tolkein is already resonsant. Deconstructing Tolkein necessarily involves deconstructing parts of Western civilization.

    Actually one of the better attempts at this is Judd's Hateful Orcs game, which, aside from being fun to read, is maybe trying to work within Tolkein's racist framework to show a complex and (at times) admirable culture.

    Reboot ideas:

    Make Gollum more of a shadow. Much like with Baltar and his imaginary Cylon on BSG, only Frodo should see Gollum. Is Gollum even real? We don't know.

    The Witch-King of Angmar has to have more depth. He is the will of Sauron and should enact that will with brutal coldness. He is, of course, one of the ancient kings of men, though, and is an echo of what Aragorn could become. Aragorn and he should be mirrors of one another.

    Gondor's stewards should be in the most precarious of positions. Saruman should help stir up sectarian violence and the people should live in fear.

    Every character should have a mirror character: Frodo : Gollum, Gandalf : Saruman, Aragorn : Witch-King, Legolas : Galadriel (who should be horrible) - the rest aren't as easy, but could be figured out. Who is right and who is wrong should not be nearly as clear-cut, and should reverse sometimes.

    Boromir should be a woman. All the stewards of Gondor should be women. Aragorn taking the throne is a dire affront.

    \nThere is no such thing as Magic--it's all technology from an earlier age or sleight-of-hand.
    -This might get you a grittier feeling, or could allow magic to be fallible, or could open the door for other widgets. It also could setup Gandalf as more of a "power-gamer, l33t l00t" guy who's only interest in The Fellowship is to use it to get Frodo as far as Mordor... so that Gandalf can gank him and swipe it while keeping all the "blame" on Sauron (see below for why "blame" is quoted... recall that history is written by the victors!).

    Every race other than Elves was created by Sauron (or other Maiar) in the previous age, not just orcs.
    - Cuts right to the racial divide: it's not Good versus Evil, it's the newest creations trying to do what older creations did: expand and be fruitful. The Dwarves retreated and were eventually ruined; Hobbits (an earlier race) made their peace by accepting serious reproduction controls; Man... well, hell, they've gone bonkers and are spooging babies all over Middle Earth, they're a frigging *cancer* in the other races' eyes (especially the orcs, being a race that is routinely and summarily executed whenever Men find one of their colonies).

    The Race of Men are predominately ignorant, racist, homocidal morons; the handful of "wise, noble" Men we see in the Trilogy have had extensive PR "massaging" of their records by Elves.
    - It's probably true in the backstory anyway, and it fits with the one above well. And I like the idea of Elves sort of "revising" the History of the War of the Rings to make their hick allies more appealing (presumably so the Valar won't laugh them out of The West for hitching their horse to Men in the War). And what's that make the Elves?
    ...Utterly distant, uncaring participants in a "game" played in Middle Earth (as mentioned earlier). In fact... you could treat this as a LOTR meets Matrix Reboot! That would cover the "magic is tech" thing, too--the Elves and Maiar are actually cognizant that it's a game, but would be booted from the system indefinitely, if they ever said as much to any "NPCs". Their "magic" is just Neo-like Matrix code manipulation.

    Woah... *fritz sizzle* Head hurting.....

    As for system... hell, I dunno. What system do you like to play which has some capability of being setting-hacked? I could do all of the above with anything from Hero System to Universalis. Or even Middle Earth Role Playing--much of the above is situation or color, so setting hooks and a system that works for you is all that matters (as I see it).

    HTH--it's a fun concept!

    \n A little late to the party, but I've always wanted to try this from an entirely different angle: Take everything Tolkein wrote straight and then play the bad guys. You're ugly, quarrelsome, and doomed, and on some level you know it, because, hey, every time the bad guys seem to win, the gods (Valar) hit the game-reset button and rearrange the continents under your feet. But, dammit, you're powerful and bitter, so at least you're going to do some damage before you go, and maybe even have some fun along the way.

    Two approaches:

    Play it as tragedy. The player-characters are among the "nine mortal men doomed to die," the "men and kings of men" granted Rings of Power by Sauron centuries before the time of Frodo. You can wield awesome power, but every time you use it, you fade away a little more until you become a wraith-slave to the Dark Lord. Play with Ron Edward's Sorcerer.

    Play it as dark comedy. The War of the Ring is on, the Dark Lord's armies are mustering to crush Gondor and Rohan once and for all, the Nazgul are walking abroad -- and, dammit, why the hell can't all this firepower catch four freakin' midgets who might as well be carrying a damn homing beacon, considering how the One Ring operates? The player-characters are Ring-Wraiths, orc generals, Saruman, and other lieutenants of the Dark Lord: mighty beyond human ken, and yet ultimately just Minions, scrambling and squabling as they try to keep out of trouble with their Master. Play with Paul Czege's My Life with Master.

    \n I was disappointed, mainly because the Return of the Hobbits (and the ass-kicking they dish out) is *critical* (to me, and to Lit Crit theory) for the epic quest story arc: The previously weak and timorous adventurer(s) return home, stronger and wiser, as evinced by the fact that they Set Something Right. Another ten or so for the Hobbits' Return, and we've got a mere, oh, thirty minutes added onto the 12 hours. Big whoop. But it would have meant at least two more locations (and probably two sound stage locations: in the Barrows and in Tom's house).

    But even lacking those (to me) key scenes, the Trilogy is still 9.5 out of 10!
