Different Experience Fields

Christian_Griffen
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
So, is there any game out there that gives you experience in different fields?

I know some games like Burning Wheel give experience to particular skills that are used; I'm talking a bit broader here.

Say, you fight a monster that's all combat, and you get combat XP. You fight one that has magical powers, you get some magic XP as well. And if you want, social conflicts give you social XP.

Set up like that, monster design instructions would determine the XP that a monster gives by which powers you grant to it; so if you face a monster with "floating eyeballs of doom rays," you're saying "Oh shit," but also, "Hell yes, if we beat it, that's 50 magic XP!" Or they could even "unlock" your abilities in that you can't get level 5 magic feats until you've slain a beast with a level 5 magic ability. (And, in the spirit of Beast Hunters, the players could establish level limits of monsters (say, magic 3, combat 5...) they'll face so that they determine their own challenge level, but also the XP and abilities they can earn.)

    Christian - I've got most of the stuff for Cold Iron here.. But the way Cold Iron traditionally handled the broad groups of experience was that characters had levels in one or more of fighter, mage, cleric, passivle magic (defensive only), humor (social), and sometimes others (scout, thief, etc). Level 2 was 2000 XP, double for each subsequent level. GMs would hand out XP based roughly on what you did, specifying how it was divied up, plus many GMs would hand out XP you could put any where you chose. Of course the social mechanics were almost non-existent, but people would get humor XP for doing talky and leadery type things.

    My feeling was that it generally worked better than the individual skill type systems since it kept people focused. Also, since Cold Iron is a hit point type system, it helps to have a broad XP category to attach hit point gains to (can you imagine buying additional hit points for a few points in something like GURPS...you'd have some tank with 100 hit points, and not much else, serious turtledom). It also allows D&D type spell levels (again without having some dude have some massive spell as his only spell).

    For my current Cold Iron campaign, I've cut it down to fighting and magic XP because the rules for any other skills were just so floaty, and it encouraged players to create non-combatants that just didn't fit the actual play style (back in college, folks were better attuned to the combat heavy gaming, and you could trust that the thief or scout could still fight). Two divisions does lose some of the flavor of the system, but until I can come up with a way to guarantee players create PCs that groove with the actual play style, I don't want to open those other doors.

    Frank

    The Middle Earth version of Rolemaster had this sort of system - you'd get various sorts of points for achieving things: fighting, gaining treasure and the like. I'm hazy on the details now (I last read MERP about ... oh ... 10 years ago) - but it's something to look into for portability.

    One of the things I find interesting about such a setup is the possible combination of different experience points to pay for character advancement. Let's say you're playing an ambassador-type character, but you find yourself in a lot of fighty situations (let's say you've accompanied a group of adventurers to an ogre enclave to help in negotiations that were a no-starter). Instead of saying 'well, you can boost your fightiness, but not your ambassador skills', why not say 'Ok, you can use these fighty XP to improve your ambassador skills ... but what does that mean to your character?'

    Does your character start to adopt a high-handed approach to diplomacy? Is he more likely to resort to threats and intimidation instead of compromise?

    We tried the separate experience thing with Battleaxe. In fact the current design still uses a heavily patched version of this where players earn points to advance skills or attributes.

    Ultimately, we found that giving players a pool of points which they could use to buy upgrades to enhance their character the way they want to was the best and most streamlined solution.

    I think that earning separate experience for skills/atts/abilities used enables a higher resolution system, but I can tell you from experience (hah) that the cost is burdensome recordkeeping on the player's behalf.
