    \n Note: I'm friends with Luke so anything I say will inevitably be biased (even if unintentional). Please take it with a grain of salt.

    I payed $8-10 a month for D&D Insider when it was Character Creation tools, Adventures, Articles, Updated Content but most importantly...

    1. I played D&D weekly
    2. The Character Creation tools could be used offline
    3. The Character Creation tools could be used, without updates, even after you ended the service (or D&D stopped the service, say if they changed editions)

I also like to create characters on the subway, airplane, on busses... lots of places without internet access... even many conventions don't have reliable internet access. That's a challenging hurdle for me to jump.

    When I think of character creation tools, I think of apps. And many of the apps I use are either pay once or pay per use.

    But I would likely pay a yearly or monthly subscription for a steady flow of adventure content.

    On the flip side, I pay for Netflix which is highly unreliable, and I don't watch it all the time, but I pay for bulk content. There is so much content, there is probably something I want to watch. Although as Netflix content gets crappier I've contemplated dropping their service and have started using Zune and iTunes more where I pay for content as I use it. My friends have HBO but they only use HBO for specific TV shows. When a Game of Thrones is done, they cancel HBO and then resign when the next season starts.

    \n In terms of PDFs:

    Generally now if I don't receive a PDF free with my game book purchase, I hesitate to buy the game (with exceptions).

    This has kept me from buying games when they premiere at conventions. If the retailer can't give me a straight answers about the PDF, I'll often wait.     I also really like buying PDFs for games I'm not entirely sure I will play but would like to read and support the designer.

    That all said...

    If a designer doesn't want to sell a PDF, for any reason (disclosed or not), I completely 100% support their right to do so and will try not to bother them about it.

    RPG publishing is a part time job, and a poorly paying one for most people. I feel lucky that they spend the time making awesome stuff that I can play at all. I don't want to add more stress to their lives by pressuring them to do things my way.



    Edit: I would pay extra for a PDF if it came with features beyond what the book offers.

    \n It seems to me that real question that can't be answered by a survey, is how many people would resubscribe after their first year ended. My gut feeling is the first year would get a lot of backing from fans wanting to support the game, whether they needed the tool or not. But if people haven't' actually used the tool except to play around with it, they may find themselves fairly reluctant to resubscribe. This is particularly true for the upper end of the price range.

    Myself, I'd have to say I wouldn't be inclined to subscribe for the following reasons:
    1. I play lots of RPGs. If they all wanted me to subscribe, it'd get silly.
      2. \n
    2. Flexibility. I have spent enough time playing around with the BW lifepath system to realize that it encodes a lot of setting decisions, many of them extremely arbitrary. In an actual game, the GM and table may wish to customize them for their setting. Even if the tool allows for entering your own additions and changes, sharing a particular set of changes among a particular set of characters belonging to a particular set of players seems non-trivial.
      3. \n
