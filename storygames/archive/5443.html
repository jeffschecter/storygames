A crazy idea I had while I was in the shower this morning:

\n

One thing (of many) I dig about The Pool is the way its dice gambling system lets players mess with probabilities. It lets you assign importance to any given conflict -- lets you say, this conflict is important to me, moreso than others. I also dig the game part of this mechanic; is this conflict so important that you want to risk dice on it, given that, if you fail, those dice will be unavailable to you for future conflicts? This kind of thing is awesome, and it's not there in the perennial favorite, D&D.

\n

So, here's the crazy idea:

\n
\n

Hero Points

\n
\n

>

\n
\n

Each player begins with a certain number (what number is good?) of Hero Points (let's call this number n). During play, before a player rolls any die, Hero Points can be wagered on the roll. Each Hero Point gambled adds +1 to the player's roll. If the roll succeeds, the player keeps all the Hero Points he gambled, and gets one (or more?) new one. If the roll fails, the player loses all gambled Hero Points. (Maybe the DM adds a complication, too?)

\n
\n

>

\n
\n

At the end of each session, Hero Points can be spent to upgrade each player's character. Some areas of upgrade will cost more than others. Skill ranks might be bought at a cost of 1:1; base attack bonus upgrades should be more expensive, as should feats and ability score upgrades.

\n
\n

>

\n
\n

At the beginning of each new session, players with fewer than n Hero Points reset their Hero Point total to n.

\n
\n

What do you guys think? Has anyone tried this before?

\n

Thanks!

\n

-Will

    \n I'd be wary.
    \nThe reason it works with the Pool is that, no matter how many dice you spend, there's always a chance of failure.
    \nWith a D&D-like system, you can push the bonus high enough so that failure is not an option - at which point, the pool develops a life of its own, unstoppably growing and crushing all before it!
    \n
    \nAn anti-pool like system might work better - where you only spend the points if you succeed, and get a bonus point if you succeed. I'm still wary, thouigh.
    \n
    \nI think for most D&D style dice systems, a "points refresh every now and then" approach is the simplest and safest.

    \n With the gambling mechanism this is interesting. Games without that have tied advancement to left-over conviction/hero/action/drama points generally just result in people hording the points and not using them. On the other hand, the lure of advancement might be so strong that, instead of using them on rolls that matter, they'd instead be used solely on roles that are sure to be won. I think you would need to do something to make risk more attractive. One thought is to make the yield determined by how much of a difference the points make or how difficult the roll was to encourage serious gambling.

    \n We played through a couple of short D&D modules using Mark Whithers' anti-pool variant. When you succeed you lose your risked dice and when you're out of them you have to rely on your traits (e.g talented swordsman) for an extra die to help you succeed - it's like D&Ds niche protection - ultimately you'll end up relying on what you're good at. So you need to gamble on whether it's worth using up your risk dice throughout the adventure or acting to your traits and saving the dice for the big finale. Dice are refreshed at the end of the session or scenario - whichever you prefer.

    \n You aren't actually risking much with these hero points though. So what if you blow all n early in the session? Or never risk them at all? You still have all your front loaded mechanical ability to resolve tasks. Still have stacking feats, spells, skills, attack bonus, etc.,. It's a cool idea, but I think there's just too much other mechanics present to make gambling a few +1s really matter. The smart players who are looking to kick butt are going to gamble when they know they can win, get the +1 Hero Point, wash, rinse, repeat. Then at the end of the session, they'll upgrade their characters and prepare to do it all over again, this time backed up by stronger regular in-game stats.

    I think this idea might work in a more rules light edition of D&D. Like Basic D&D, maybe 1ed AD&D, BFRPG, Labyrinth Lord, etc., where there's not so much other mechanics to rely upon for every conceivable task that needs resolution. Even then, I'd want to put in some sort of friction where the more they use the points, the more the GM gets villain points or something.

    \n I have to second the comments made so far...

    I think a better way to look at the Pool mechanics is not as a "system where you gamble dice for bonuses", but a "system where you gamble dice to set the difficulty of tasks".

    As an add-on, it would _have_ to be Anti-Pool to work. I'd be up for that, though--especially if you want the feeling where PCs succeed on most of the most important rolls, but there's still an element of chance to it.

    (Also consider that rolls in D&D tend to vary wildly in terms of their significance. Getting a bonus on one of ten Climb checks is not at all the same thing as maxing out your dice when using some sort of save-or-die power against the Big Bad.)
