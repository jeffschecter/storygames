\n So, this thread about cultural appropriation in IGC games went its own way, and I'm being a bit unsatisfied by the lack of substance, because I think that the basic idea is interesting. Specifically, I've been only peripherally aware that "cultural appropriation" is a thing with a name that people use routinely, and that there are moral viewpoints involved with it. Live and learn, and here's a chance for me to learn more. Specifically, if I'm doing something where I should morally speaking be doing better, I'm interested in knowing the arguments and judging for myself. (Or having somebody else be the judge, whatever - as long as I'm convinced as well, otherwise it's not going to do much good to me practically.)

I'll tell you how I understand this thing, to begin with. First, the definition: cultural appropriation is where you "take" something that "belongs" to some culture you are not part of. So making a computer game about cowboys and indians is cultural appropriation, because you're using the IP (not literally in the legal sense, but it's the same idea - cultures should own their heritage) of native American tribes to make money. Building a sauna is appropriating Finnish culture. That sort of thing. I understand that it's especially considered evil (I'm in total philosophical mode here, so saying things as I understand them) when your appropriation is shallow and you're taking from people in a weak position, who are not in a position to exploit their own culture themselves.

Also, I figured that this has to have a Wikipedia article on it. I see that it has a more nuanced view on the matter. There are even arguments there about how cultures can learn from each other, and how appropriation of influences enrichens us all. The alternate viewpoint that occurs to me while considering this idea is that creative work is largely appropriating cultures. How could that be bad? Can you appropriate your own culture? What is the difference? How about, can you utilize your own culture's understanding of a different culture, or is that just more appropriation?

If you're into this thing, let's discuss cultural appropriation in roleplaying, both play and game texts. To start off, I'd like to present exhibit A, an excerpt of text from a D&D adventure I wrote last year; it's being published in the Fight On! magazine soon. The text describes GMing instructions for how to treat an imperial Chinese city a bit before the Opium War in the context of a one-shottish D&D adventure.\n
Posted By: Foo Dogs of the Heavenly Jade TempleThe referee may provide a map for the city, but for the purposes of the convention environment outlined above the city is more of a distraction than an opportunity: the referee happily describes Amoy and its adventures, allows the players to get lost in its exotic mysteries, and closes the session with utter failure for the HEIC mission.

The PCs may opt to wander the city and verify for themselves that it's a modern yet exotic locale of a culture with a rather different history than their own. My take as referee would be to keep orientalist glasses on all the way through: emphasize the inscrutability of the citizens and cast a wide mix of suspicions and fears at any European PCs, who will see fearsome arts and mind-boggling ignorance mixed up, inviting totalizing interpretations that cast the Chinese civilization as a whole in the role of mysterious super-people or reprehensible degenerates, or both at once. The cultural context of the PCs makes this place look like Menzoberranzan to any without experience of Chinese locales.

The reactions of the locals to any "foreign devils" will be magnified, they will point and gape as PCs travel the streets. Many are hostile or fearful, as the imperial creed has demonized foreigners for a long time; others will be curious and seek out the foreigners for their supposed amazing powers or superior knowledges (or entertainment value, as the case might be). The party may make purchases of most types of equipment in the city with the preferred amount of folderol regarding currency exchanges and haggling and widely variant prices offered to the exotic customers.
\nSo, that's appropriation, I think. Furthermore, I'm not just appropriating Chinese culture, but rather I'm appropriating the culture of 18th century European orientalists (who were in turn appropriating from the Chinese) and using it as a instructional backbone in advising a GM on how to treat an exotic locale at their table. Good stuff? Bad stuff? Racist stuff? For myself I was very satisfied at my take here - I got the idea for the adventure first, and then had to think for a couple of minutes about how to transmit a functional picture of the city of Amoy in a very tight space. Then I realized that actually, the most true to life and flavourful way of doing this would be to go to town with the way this era is traditionally depicted in European sources, as that would then contrast beautifully with the types of exotism you usually see in D&D.

Considering my piece and my reaction to it, should I be ashamed, or in general, is there something that I'm obviously not considering here morally. Am I using my arts for vile purposes without even realizing it myself?

If you have any thoughts on cultural appropriation, or your own examples to consider, or comments on my specific example, feel free to discuss! Also, please: if you feel that you need to disengage from the discussion and say something meta about how even having to discuss this is a travesty or things like that, do it in that other thread where we're currently hashing out whether it's cool to act like a political commissar of the red army on a discussion forum.

    \n I don't know if this will be helpful, but I'd like to offer a point of contention with your definition. You define cultural appropriation thus:\n
    cultural appropriation is where you "take" something that "belongs" to some culture you are not part of
    \nI submit that this definition can be improved upon in two ways, that will help focus the discussion better on the problematic aspects.

    First, when someone raises a complaint of "cultural appropriation", it's not really that they're claiming that their culture belongs to them. I think it's more like, these are cultural practices that someone identifies with, that they are important for their own understanding of who they are and how they live. In other words, it's not "This belongs to me", but rather "This means something important to me".

    Second, the problem with appropriation is not that someone else is "taking" those practices. It's not Western cultural appropriation when Indians wear business suits, or when Arabs go to a Finnish sauna. The problem is when someone emulates a practice without understanding it, or represents it in a distorted way. The appropriator uses a symbol or practice as a superficial way to denote a culture or a way of life, but by taking the thing out of context, without much concern for whether they understand the practice or are doing it correctly.

    The more precarious a culture's position relative to the culture of the appropriator, the more damaging appropriation can be. It can be damaging even when it's well intentioned, because it can still have the effect of belittling others and trivializing their identity and their way of life. That's why the "I'm not racist" defense isn't particularly credible. Cultural appropriation is lazy racism; it's not that the appropriator has negative feelings they ought not to have, but that they don't have the bona fides they ought to have. It's a kind of moral negligence.

    I dunno if this helps, but a European example might be, when someone wears a head scarf and hoop earrings to signify the romantic stereotype of Gypsy life. Because Gyspsies are cooooooool. Except, the actual lives of actual Romani people can be pretty hard scrabble and dangerous. To this day they're the targets of forcible assimilation tantamount to genocide in some "enlightened" Western countries. But they also have their own art and other cultural enterprises that speak to that history and to their contemporary condition. What it means for a non-Romani to play at being a Gypsy has little connection to what it means to a Romani to be a Romani, to be part of a history of Romani in Europe. And that's something people who dress up as Gypsies when they go to costume parties generally don't give the first crap about.

    \n So I was going to run a Victorian adventure game. Actually I've written about this challenge before. Here's the thread. The key thing to take away from the thread was that I wanted to have a game in which the characters were somehow outsiders from "normal" society's strictures. This was a very common Victorian adventure element: Sherlock Holmes was a weirdo druggie, Professor Challenger was a kook, Phileas Fogg was a lunatic suspected bank robber, Raffles was a thief, and so on. Even among heroes who were not outsiders, they were placed into situations where normal society was torn apart (War of the Worlds), distant (50,000 Leagues Under The Sea) or ineffectual (Les Miserables' Inspector Javert couldn't advance because he was incorruptible), or even quite overtly the problem. (The main villain of Sexton Blake stories was a corrupt Scotland Yard official.)

    This was one way I felt that my fairly light-hearted game group could engage with a time frame and a set of literature that often had racist or misogynistic elements or themes - sometimes pretty central, too. Their characters could defy those structures without erasing them. It was a FATE based game - I required that one of the Aspects of the characters be "why don't/can't you turn your remarkable abilities to the support of the normal organizations or governments of the time". The examples I gave were: "As a Muslim, they will never accept me here" and "'A female mathematician? Bosh, I say!'" The characters that were made included a ridiculed spiritualist/medium, a foreign freethinker, a Bohemian theatrical type, and a female academic who had made powerful enemies in the Church.

    So already before I even got to designing the opposition or other elements of the game and setting I was already setting up lines by which the player characters would be excluded from Victorian "civilization" with all its assumptions and lines and hierarchies.

    I share a birthday with Sax Rohmer, who always claimed (moronically) that he based the character Fu Manchu on real Chinese criminals he knew when he was a newspaper reporter in San Francisco in the last part of the 19th century. Because I shared his birthday, I was interested in his books at a young age and devoured them urgently as a teenager. I remember these books as the first time I understood that books could be racist (as opposed to just characters in books). Yet they were often exciting and action-packed, and one reason that they have persisted when so many other racist anti-Chinese books of the era have faded into obscurity is the compelling nature of the central villain.

    Rohmer is completely in love with this villain, completely fascinated by him. In one novel he has a page-long purple prose description of the guy, elaborate and exact....and in the next novel he uses the same description word for word. He wrote a "letter" to his creation by way of introduction to a collection at some point where he outright states that a world ruled by Fu Manchu would be pretty okay. But the books (begun in Edwardian times but rooted obviously in long-standing prejudices) are very different from many of the Victorian novels I described above.

    In Rohmer's novels, the main "heroes" are Establishment men right to their core, and most importantly, they have almost no agency. They virtually never do anything. Entire novels pass where they take no action whatsoever. Things are done to them, near them, and around them, and they have many daring escapes and moonlight pursuits, but the only person in any of these novels that takes any action to get what they want is Fu Manchu himself (or sometimes his daughter). He is already just a hairs-breadth from being the protagonist of the books by the time I even considered adapting him to my Victorian adventure game.

    I already knew I wanted to create adventures which would explicitly reverse the assumptions of Rohmer's novels and put the establishment hierarchy as irrelevant, blinkered and ineffectual to deal with the threats of the game world, as they are in Sherlock Holmes stories, for example, perhaps a bit moreso. So reversing course on Fu Manchu himself was natural - as I say, he was almost already there.

      \n So I wanted to have Fu Manchu around - what's interesting to me (and kind of makes me feel that whatever the outcome maybe it was pointless for me to do the work I'm about to talk about) is that I found him to be very iconic of a particular type of mastermind but most of the players had no actual knowledge of the character or the stories. Like, they kinda knew about the stereotype and the mustache, but didn't know anything else.

      Basically when I was thinking about what Fu Manchu would be in my game there were certain problematic elements I identified and wanted to reverse or contextualize in a way that would make our experience of the character less problematic. Here's how I went about it:

      Anti-Exoticization: As mentioned in the other thread, one of the very bad elements (there are so many bad elements I hesitate to call one the worst) of the Rohmer stories is that Fu Manchu is described and identified in terms that are almost alien in their exotic-ness. "Inhuman" is an adjective thrown around a lot in those stories. Like, this isn't just the normal (but still objectionable) titillation associated with "wow, they really do things differently there", this is seeing the character as completely beyond humanity. So I firmly determined that there should be no trait that Fu Manchu (or any other Chinese character working for him) has that a non-Chinese person doesn't also have. FATE is actually a pretty good match for this because it's stunt system (in the Dresden Files incarnation, which is what I was stripping down for the game) doesn't have really highly specific styles or traits, instead it has (for example) stunts that increase your damage with your fists, and that can be whatever you want it to be, from boxing to dumb luck to the blessing of some fist-oriented spirit to some form of martial art, you decide. I drew up a quick list of Fu Manchu's abilities and traits (genius naturalist and scientist, mastermind of secret society, ultra-educated, Mr. Polite, racist, covert war with scheming offspring) and I made sure that there was some other NPC in the setting that wasn't Chinese that had that trait - and Fu Manchu would not come onscreen until I had established that. He might be unique to the degree he had certain traits, and he might be unique in the combination of traits he had, but I wouldn't be saying or even implying that he was the way he was because of his racial origin. Again, FATE's Aspects really help here. Being a mastermind isn't an "Oriental" thing in this game, there's Professor Moriarty. Being a genius naturalist isn't an "Oriental" thing in this game, there's Jacques the apothecary who is secretly a nature-worshipper and is probably (quelle horreur!) a nudist. "Martial arts" isn't an "Oriental" thing in this game, being "Mr. Polite" isn't an "Oriental" thing in this game, etc.

      More Legitimization - In the Rohmer stories, Fu Manchu is portrayed weirdly ambivalently in terms of politics. This is an area where Rohmer's admiration for his creation often seems to win out over his natural loathing. Fu Manchu opposes fascists and communists, for his own reasons, but he is an anti-imperialist from the very first novel. His primary motivation in those early novels is something the modern reader (and the outsider player characters of my game) is really quite sympathetic to. So if I didn't want to make him a straight up good guy, I needed to give him provocations and rivals that would push him too far, and demonstrate that as tied up in his battles and scheming as he is, he is not a champion of the people. It's not so much that he opposes fascism, it's that he opposes anyone else edging him out of his position as ruler of the world. This is more of a change I made in his resources and methods than in the character himself - being anti-imperialist nowadays is a straight up good thing! - but I gave him more followers who had legitimate beefs with governments, companies and factions, gave him associates who struggled to achieve similar short-term aims (the protection of Chinese immigrants or the advancement of the Chinese people) but who would battle him as his sinister plan was revealed, and so on. Politics actually turned out to be a big deal in this game, so once we started going down that road, I was able to really put some characters in some dilemmas. Again, here I'm changing the context of the character more than the character himself. Rohmer's novels take place in a world where anti-imperialism is garbage pushed by fascists, communists and other would-be world conquerors. By changing it in my world to something that has legitimacy, I give the character more weight, and also make it seem like he's something that could really happen to a brilliant person who took things too far in what was originally a good cause.

      Okay, at about this point I started thinking "christ, am I really doing myself a lot of favors by trying to fix up this terrible character, I know I love it, but maybe it's too far gone, there may be too much shit to shovel, ya know?" But I was also kind of excited by the challenge at this point, AND it was actually helping me work out the setting of the game more.

      \n A side note, on the issue of agency: one of the great things about Forge-style Narrativism is that it places player (and character) agency front and center, so it makes it much easier to deal with problematic material than maybe it is in other styles of play. The core question of Narrativism is something like: "Now what are you going to do about that?" And then we find out what kind of person a character is by how they engage with the issues present in the game. It's not just "play to find out what happens" but "play to find out who you are."

      If you're playing something that's less Nar-focused, when racism or sexism or other issues appear in your game, it's not necessarily a viable option to really dig into them and explore them, because that may not say anything about your character or the world, the way that murdering random folks in D&D may not really say anything about violence (even as it arguably reinforces a culture of violence in other ways). If a D&D PC decides to enslave a bunch of goblins, if the other characters start treating that PC as a horrible criminal who needs to be murdered or at least imprisoned, the game isn't really set up to deal with that. But if the same thing happened in a more Nar game -- even something like Apocalypse World -- it might actually be interesting to explore what you do when your companion turns into a bloody slaver, because those are the kinds of questions the game is about.

      That's part of what I meant by setting up the game in a way that the problematic material can actually be addressed and isn't just color for another activity, like dungeon exploration. If morality and hard choices aren't really on the table, dealing with immoral behavior and problematic situations becomes more difficult and potentially dysfunctional, especially if different members of the group have different levels of comfort or sensitivity about certain issues (even stuff like violence).

      Posted By: WordmanA last question for Eero, having learned whatever was learned from this thread.
      \nDifficult question. I don't fear or regret reactions from audience segments per se, so just because some unrelated people think that the material is inappropriate wouldn't be enough to sway my inspiration - it never should be, or no honest attempts would get made.

      However, the notion that people who might otherwise enjoy my work would find it viscerally unpleasant because of technicalities concerns me. That is, I don't mind if people who don't like D&D don't like it, or people who take offense on principle (nothing wrong with being principled about your offense, it's just that I only care about formal complaints on a formal level, and not as a practical matter). However, if somebody would have liked the core virtues of the work (likes D&D, appreciates the framing and tools of challenge, appreciates the setting detail and color), but I messed it up by writing my meaning in a way that made them unnecessarily comfortable... that's a concern for me. A real one, as like I said early in the thread, I didn't really think about my audience here in international cultural terms at all. I just wrote what I myself would consider a well-written and relevant D&D adventure.

      The difficult question is obviously about the core values of the work, and about what exactly causes these cultural hangups. If a nuanced reading and well-considered research concludes that the fundaments of the work are incompatible with say non-western audiences, then I'm in a pickle with that - it's not that I wrote it badly or that it could have been written better, it's that the substance of the work is not universal to humanity. That, then, goes into some deep stuff: is art that is not universally relevant valuable? If it is, what do I need to care about its validity for non-western audiences, as long as it punches as hard as it may for its real audience? If the alternatives are for a work of art to be non-universal or non-existent, which is better and more responsible? Depends on your answers to some questions about communication, society, art and other such generalities. I'd lean towards the notion that even Europeans have a right to reflect on their past, even if the very topic would be uncomfortable for somebody else. Sort of like how I acknowledge a person's right to enjoy pornography without having to acknowledge enjoying it myself.

      On the other hand, if my adventure would be essentially fine if I only wrote it in a slight different manner, that's a question of craft and a flaw in my skills. Something to be fixed, surely. But this is not easy either: for instance, you can't create a work by second-guessing yourself, so to achieve such universally appealing language I would have to either internalize a new taste or get an editor committed to both the purpose of the work and a more inclusive aesthetic. Both are difficult steps to take for an artist, for I like my own work: I find the style punchy and fun, and the meanings nuanced and multi-layered. I would not be satisfied as an artist if the only thing I could manage would be to dumb down my work. One might ask whether I'm full of myself in saying that I like my work too much to fix it, but I don't think that this is a matter of skill, but rather of work ethic: you craft until you're satisfied if you're a real artist, and your satisfaction may only be measured by your own taste against your own gauges of style. It's entirely possible that my work is objectively sucky, and I would still have the same problem of not knowing where to stop and what to do if I could not trust in my own taste as a measure. That's writing to order, and ultimately you end up just fulfilling a rote work order when you work like that.

      Going through the above evaluations now, I'd say that I probably would have written (and certainly played) this adventure anyway, as I think that it has interesting and inspiring things going for it. After that I would look at the color elements really hard, trying to decide whether to transform everything into culturally neutral fantasy; based on this discussion, for example, I would know that for some people it's going to be really important whether the adventure depicts an encounter between Brits and Chinese or "Talkumerans" and "Hero'ese" peoples. This would be a really difficult choice, I don't even want to think about having to make it: putting on that make-up of inconsequential entertainment would, I think, make the substance of the adventure pretty much entirely comfortable for anybody who's comfortable with D&D in the first place, but it would feel so shallow to myself as a creator, I don't know if I could be happy about publishing the work in that form. Very difficult to think about which way I'd go, it's entirely possible that I would decide to not publish at all because both options are unacceptable.

      Actually, I'd probably see about asking some of these people who seem to have self-confidence and philosophical certainty about racism to edit the piece. Then I'd see whether I'd hate the outcome as much as I fear, and if I were positively surprised, I'd publish the edited version. Then I could tell anybody who asks that I'd used wiser heads than mine to ensure that my most poisonous ideas never made it to print

      Posted By: ivanGoing back to Eero's game, I was thinking that the reward mechanics are embedding the structure of privilege. Due to no merit, some characters get higher rewards.
      \nI think that this is a potentially powerful thing. It's actually a really neat thing that games can do\u2014model power structures in society and make them easier to critique.

      But the powers of privilege in society work best when they are invisible. So if you want to critique them, you need to bring them out into the open. When the players run up against them, they need to be explicitly stated and described. JD's comment on being transparent to the players is crucial in more ways that his personal foibles\u2014because when these rules aren't made explicit, even if some people recognize their irony they still function as part of the racism that they are trying to critique. They penetrate the game and make their racist content read as normal in this intimate, constructed space, hiding their own artificial nature. They may also make some players feel unwelcome.

      I think that the single most effective way to improve on these pieces is to make an explicit statement, right there in the same sentence. This carries the extra bonus for an OSR audience that a hacker DM will have more tools available for changing them, because she'll understand why the rules are the way they are. Instead of saying merely that mixed parties can suffer an XP penalty, and leaving it for the DM and players to figure out why that would be, it should say: "Because XP in part represents a character's reputation, hence their ability to gain followers, tell the players that behaving in that their racist society doesn't accept will give them a penalty, unless certain steps are taken."

      Instead of incentivizing a non-diverse party, tell the players that the system's racism is an obstacle that they can get around by gaming the rules, incentivizing anti-racism.

      I don't know if it would be enough to make the text entirely palatable to me but it would help. Plus, I'd be empowered to say: "in my game, that's not how XP works, so we'll just toss that whole section."
