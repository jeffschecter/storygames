\n I playtested Left Coast with Mike Sands last weekend. Basically, you play a science fiction author in the 1970s (like Philip K. Dick) who's also a character in a novel being written by your friends - and your friend is screwing up your already difficult creative and family life by introducing weird plot twists to make their novel more exciting.

(Here's the latest version of Left Coast. I wrote an intro to the game here.)

It was the first time I've run the new version of the game, and a few people have been circling around playtesting it, wondering if it works or if it's broken. The playtest was cool and I was happy with the results: Within about 30 minutes, Mike and I had created our two science fiction authors (J. Oberon Eldritch and Robert M. Curtis) and surrounded them with a cast of characters that very naturally led into two very different types of story:


J. Oberon Eldritch
Oberon\u2019s story was caught in the middle of kitchen-sink realism meets
LSD-induced enlightenment. He was simultaneously a bum who was
crashing at his brother's house, while also leading a group of occultists
and university professors in the consumption of drugs. Oberon was trying
to open his mind up to a higher plane of reality and (hopefully) understand
what the Psychic Voice in his head was telling him.

For me, the highlight of Oberon\u2019s story was seeing him arguing with his
straight-arrow banker brother, trying to scam $5 off him to afford postage
to send off his manuscript. The amount of venom and resentment that
came out of that simple conversation was pretty amazing.


Robert M. Curtis
Bob Curtis becoming enmeshed in a paranoid noir where his parole officer
was blackmailing him for money and Bob's doppleganger seemed to be
planning a crime to frame Bob and put him put back behind bars.


We played through three rounds of scenes:

- Bob meets Patricia Pennington, his new neighbour who seems to be spying on him.
- Oberon tries to scam five bucks off his brother.
- Bob\u2019s parole officer, Michael Gleisner, confronts him at the diner where Bob works.
- Oberon invites his friends and associates around to his brother\u2019s house to take drugs, while his brother invites his boss from the bank home for dinner.
- Oberon writes a story about the Vietnam war spreading across the planet. The story is set in Southern California, and one of the characters is heavily based on Oberon\u2019s brother
- Bob and Oberon meet up for a drink at a science-fiction convention, and learn more about each other\u2019s frustrating lives.

In terms of the rules, that was three Widescreen scenes, a Family scene, a Money scene, and an Ensemble scene.

(Mike's take on the session is here.)


So here\u2019s what I thought worked:

Making up non-player characters (NPCs) really worked for me. It asks just enough questions to give the NPCs an attitude and an initial spark of life. As a result I found them easy to play in scenes. I\u2019d be interested in how Mike found it. I also found it really satisfying to get to know the NPCs through conversation. I enjoyed the process of slowly figuring out what they really wanted and what they were willing to resist or insist on.

The process for introducing \u2018Weird\u2019 elements into the game (at least in the first few scenes we played out) seemed to work really well.

And best of all, I just enjoyed exploring the setting and the characters. Through play, I became interested in the characters who were being talked about but who hadn\u2019t been seen yet. For instance, I started the game being afraid of my parole officer and then realised I absolutely needed to have a scene with him.

I also ended up being really curious about what was going on with all the other NPCs in both stories: Oberon\u2019s father; the university professor using Oberon\u2019s drugs; Bob\u2019s ex-cellmate Kenny; my ex-wife new husband (the insurance investigator).


What needed work?

I think there\u2019s lots of stuff I need to tweak and lots of procedures I need to simplify or cull, but the game's basic structure seems sound. Now I\u2019m getting ready for a second, more extensive playtest, and trying to figure out how to present the rules more effectively, in a way that inspires people to want to play it (I've got a thread about that and the tweaks I think I need to make over at the Forge: here.)

During this next playtest I want to play through one or two chapters and take the game out for a real road test to see:

- what happens when you hit 7 on the Story rating and end a chapter?
- whether all the types of reset scenes work?
- what the game's like when you get into lots and lots of conflicts?

The goal is to get a new draft together that's massively simplified: more focused on the bare-bones of the procedures you need to play. (At the moment, there\u2019s lots of editorial direction, and I\u2019ve tried to cram EVERYTHING in the rules.)
