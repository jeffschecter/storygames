Unfortunately, all I know is photoshop, adobe and word. What other programs - or websites, for that matter - could you point me to that would help me professionalize the appearance of my work?

    \n I used Scribus, which is a free layout program, to lay out Play Unsafe. I wouldn't necessarily recommend it: too idiosyncratic.

    I'm now using PagePlus, which costs ten dollars, and which, so far, looks good.

    Having said that, the software is less than half the battle. The reason John Harper's work looks good is because he's good at layout. Layout is a skill of its own. The bad news is that layout is difficult. The good news is that learning a little about layout will improve the way your stuff looks a lot.

    Graham

    \n If you want to learn layout, pick up these books:

    The Non-designer's Design Book and The Non-Designer's Type Book, by Robin Williams

    The Fundamentals of Typographic Style by Robert Bringhurst.

    Then shamelessly imitate stuff you think looks good, while trying to see what things in the books above apply.

    Page Plus is pretty good for cheap to learn the basics. You can use Word to create something that looks okay as long as it isn't graphically heavy. Even with the tips you learn in the books I listed you can make Word-created docs look decent.

    Also, FYI I paid a lot for that art. I may have provided a lot of direction, but I didn't draw it. Spending money is really easy. Too easy.

    \n I'll go ahead and chime in with OpenOffice, Inkscape, GIMP, and Acrobat (to really funk up a PDF, if I have to). One of these days I'll master Scribus, but I rarely need the ability to do page-by-page manipulation (Scribus's strong suit)--RPG books aren't a lot like catalogs or magazines.

    I've also used FrameMaker for about a decade, but not for game products (other than Interactive Magic PC game manuals back in the 90s). At one time, I was 13th in the world on BrainBench for it (can't tell if that rank held, as now BB costs). I can make that puppy SING; and it's the only thing I'd use for a highly structured book spanning several chapters, with a variety of Styles and page layout requirements, and weighing in at 300+ pages. Unless, of course, I was doing layout for someone who wanted to self-maintain, in which case see above (FM is about $600, last I checked). Plus, the FrameUsers listserv has some of the most knowledgeable and helpful folks in the world.

    InDesign is the pretty-but-dumb bastard child of FrameMaker, but it's better supported, if you're a paying customer (Adobe wishes--in vain--that FM would die). It is sorely lacking in book-level controls and single-sourcing flexibility, last I checked (which, admittedly, is long ago). Then again, most RPG publishers do not require a lot of single-sourcing or content reuse, beyond standardized table and page templates.
