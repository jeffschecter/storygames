    \n I'm thinking of creating a thread asking people to recommend a game that they think is underloved, and at least 3 years old. From there, we take like 3-5 of them and make them into kind of a "Oprah's Book Club" thing for the year. No one has to read them, however if folks are waffling on something to play, they could pick it up, play as is or hack it, and post their results. Sort of a long-running challenge. No one would be forced to participate. But I'm thinking it might be a way to talk about and explore older games that only come up from time to time in nostalgic threads.

    Ex: I'd make sure that Blue Planet hit the first year/round. Others that I was thinking of would be Talislanta, Castle Falkenstein, Rifts, Over the Edge, etc. They could be played as-is, slightly modified/hacked, or totally renovated (new system, etc). Indie/small press would be up for rec, too, as long as they meet the 3+ year requirement (in other words, I want to look at some older games, not just the new hotness).

    Hmmmm.

      Posted By: Jarrod[2] Okay Troy, fire away. *waits*
      \nHere's what happened. The time frame was during the months of August through November of 2007. I had submitted the last two books of DL-Quarterly to RapidPOD in early May expecting to get them before my June release date. Months passed by and I received no word. I noticed that Rapid POD's site wasn't being updated anymore and remembered back to their problems the year before during the Origins-GenCon convention season. June came and went, and I was pretty upset that I never got my books. I both emailed them and called in July and got assurances that I'd get them soon, but never did. August rolls around and I call again. I think it was late August by now, I'm not for sure. Anyway, the nice lady on the phone told me that the company (now called Vixen Printing) was under new management and things were a little behind. That's understandable, and I was actually kind of glad because the company was just too much for Ken to handle. It's a good thing he moved on to other things.

      I get a call back a couple weeks later about Standoff (one of my two games). Here's what I get told. They'll be ready in a month. The price was nearly doubled, and I had to resubmit the files. That really made me mad, but I was already SO far behind schedule that I went along with it anyway. I waited about another month and didn't get my books. I call again. They were having trouble with the layout and had put the project on hold. They never called me about it. Never emailed me about it. Just shoved the books they had printed in a corner and forgot about them. So I redo the entire layout from scratch and resubmit it. Two weeks pass. No books. I call again. Now something was wrong with the cover. I redo that over from scratch and submit it.

      Some time in October I get a box over stuffed full of Standoff books. I ordered 25 but there were something like 50 books stuffed into something about the size of a shoebox. Inside there was a note that basically read, "We couldn't get the pages right, so here is what we printed. Hopefully there will be enough good ones in here for you to use. If not, let us know and we'll print some more." 30 of the books were un usable. The pages were cut off at the top or bottom or were only printed halfway on the page. I had never seen such shoddy work. I had barely just enough decent copies to send to all my customers in November, and those weren't the best of quality either. Shameful quallity really.

      Later in November or maybe early December I call to inquire about my last book, The Holmes and Watson Committee. They never heard of it. And they told me if I wanted to order anything from them the price that I was paying under Ken would be more than doubled, I couldn't get the paper thickness I had ordered in the begining, and I'd have to order at least 100 copies. I literally laughed at the woman on the other end of the line. They lost my files. Delivered my books more than 6 months late, and were telling me how the terms of the original deal would now be??? I said no thanks, hung up the phone, and looked up Lulu's website. I had my books from them a little over two weeks later and was very happy with the quality.

      The experience was so excruciating for me that I have pretty much sworn off publishing a book ever again. I'll stick to PDFs. It was just a miserable and painful emotional experience for me that I don't ever want to repeat. I cannot convey just how disapointed in Vixen Printing I was at the time. It ruined my trust in printers forever. Go to whispers if you have any further questions about this experience. I don't want to violate Andy's (3) rule.

      Peace,

      -Troy

      \n\nCalithena \n\n\n

      \n\nMay 2008 \n
      \n

      \n
      \n
      \n Hi all -
      \n
      \nThanks for the interest in Fight On! Judd, I picked lulu because it was easy, but I am starting to see some upsides there for a magazine publisher. Low risk/overhead as for any publisher, your stuff is out there for people to see and get easily, etc. The one downside is that if your magazine is small there's less of a margin for reasonable pricing, but since this is essentially a fanzine (the mag makes $1 or less per print copy, a lot of which gets rolled back in) that's not such a big deal for us.
      \n
      \nPaul's article is definitely on the edge of the envelope where 'old school' is concerned, but my interpretation of 'old school' is actually pretty broad. Back in the seventies we didn't know everything people know today, and systems were limited and klugy, but the cool thing about it is that everyone would just roll their imagination into what was there and it could all kind of be played, table by table, according to people's preferences. There were indeed some wild west coast campaigns, sometimes influenced by Arduin, that got into the epic/psychedelic range that Paul's contribution was in, where the rules often kind of receded into the mirror (characters could do almost anything) and the play often became either about a kind of crazy one-upsmanship in terms of surreal effects or else about demigod-level...dare I say it...narrativism? Anyway, I don't know how many FO! readers are going to get or use Paul's contribution, but I know I did, and I think the magazine wouldn't serve its roots or its fanbase well if it didn't push the envelope with at least one or two articles an issue dealing with weird situations, rules mods, playstyle variants, and so on. I actually have a few more 'indie designers' in mind I'm going to contact or have already contacted to write things that I think would be useful at least to some in the 'old school' framework. So there you go.
      \n
      \nIf Story Games people have stuff they would like to submit - either old D&D notes from three-ring binders that they're not likely to use now, or newer, pushing the envelope type stuff that they think might be usefully ad hocced onto older style games, I encourage them to submit - iggyumlaut@gmail.com . All our writers and artsts keep ownership and all other rights to their submissions, all Fight On! asks for is the right to publish the article/artwork in the version it's originally published in in the issue it's originally published in in perpetuity. (Judd, if you ever wanted to publish an old school version of some Mu's Bed stuff, just put it in the mail, I'd find a place for it.)
      \n
      \nSo anyway, thanks again to all you folks at Story Games for the interest, and Fight On!
