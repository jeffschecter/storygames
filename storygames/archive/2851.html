\nThis thread finally made me post something I was thinking about for some time now.

Why this tendency to force more BDtP into the game? I know people who dislike BDtP because it breaks the usual "flow" of TSOY by forcing the intentions on people, which some feel as a straightjacket to what can happen in-game.

So, in the thread mentioned above, couldn't the shouting-down-storms and stuff be handled as complex actions where one collects bonus dice for a simple ability check? That should allow for the same amount of in-game to-and-fro, without the hassle of BDtP, where you're forced to passive actions to change your intentions, which draws out BDtP (unneccessary). Or is it just a matter of people not setting their priorities (-> "intentions") correctly, and forgetting that it's important to give early, give often?


PS: Personally, I'm fine with BDtP as is (with a fix for group conflicts provided by the finnish translation), but as I want to convince people, I'm looking for supporting arguments.

    \n The slightly off-topic bit first:

    Looking at Eero's version... well, I'll be blunt, I don't see a different version, as such. There aren't any rules changes, per se; things are explained using different terminology, and different aspects of how it all works together are emphasized. Same system, same basic execution, looked at from an angle that's rotated 134 degrees on the horizontal plane and 32 degrees on the vertical: in cliched terms, it's just a different way of looking at it.

    My impression comes from the fact that I've run a six- seven-character BDtP very recently in which, during a given round, there were never less than three different goals amongst the participants, and only two three characters didn't change their intent during the whole conflict (largely because they didn't realize that the other Goals were present until after it was too late to do anything about it, and they'd been abandoned or become irrelevant) -- during that conflict, using only the English version of the rules and applying them logically, we did exactly what Eero's version implies.

    Which isn't to say I don't like Eero's version/wording -- I think his phrasing might be clearer to some of my players, though we all seem to get it already.

    The on-topic bit:
    I'm entirely in agreement with Jason on this: in my experience, BDtP doesn't happen so often as to drag down play, and when the player's call for it, it gives the scene more weight and electricity and (as one player put it) "mechanical heft." Generally lasting Harm is enough of a pain in the ass that people seriously consider whether they want to stay in, everytime they take a hit, and have clear opinions on when it's 'too much.' Then again, I set smallish stakes when I can.

    First edition BDtP used to be a not-often-fun PitA -- BDtP in the 2nd edition is not significantly more complicated than simple conflicts where people are rolling 'secondary' skill rolls for bonus die -- we're still driving along at the same rate, we're just using more of the gears, because the road got all curvy.

    But the curvy roads are the fun bits anyway, right? Provided the WHOLE ROAD isn't curvy -- that's tedious.

    \n Here's one thing about BDTP that I see misused sometimes:

    The book explains that the players have the right to ignore BDTP. They can take out the big baddie in one (successful) hit if they want to without going to BDTP. They are told that the GM doesn't have to kill off that baddie; they can escape, "come back", etc.

    However, some folks interpret that to mean "the big baddie can ONLY be shut down with BDTP". That's not the case.

    The GM basically has that "ability" to bring back baddies if BDTP isn't used, as a stopgap measure to make sure that the players don't just buffer up their ability into one big blow and take out every baddie with the intention, "My Intention is that "I kill him"! Gimmie all your gift dice! Hooo, I succeed! Another baddie dead!!!" The GM has the right to bring those guys back.

    But the GM doesn't HAVE to bring them back. And just cause the players don't use BDTP doesn't mean that the big baddie ISN'T put down for good in that one blow (physical or social). Sometimes, the GM is totally fine with that, and it works out better/cooler for everyone to finish the big baddie with that one hit. But the GM, if dissatisfied with the outcome, has the ability to bring that dude back at a later time. Another adventure, another campaign, or even "...twenty minutes later".

    If everyone is totally cool with it, no one ever needs to start the BDTP process.

    -Andy
