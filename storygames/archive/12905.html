\n I want to improve play by building up the world outside of the game session. I want to take advantage of the slower pace of "out-of-game" time to think out interesting locations, challenging GMC's, strange events, compelling hooks, and other pleasant surprises for the players to discover and interact with. Call this my Sandbox instinct.

At the same time, I want to improve play by giving the players the initiative. I want to encourage their input, let them take the lead in defining the fiction and always keep the stage open for a chance in the spotlight. Call this my Improv instinct.

The problem is, I find these two instincts are often in conflict.

Viewed from my Improv instinct, acting on my Sandbox instinct risks bad GM'ing by way of railroading, forcing the players to see the fiction as I deem it, and overpowering player input. Viewed from my Sandbox instinct, acting on the Improv instinct risks bad GM'ing by way of lack of support, failure to challenge the players and indifferent world-building.

I want to offer both the joy of exploration that comes with a pre-defined world and the rich drama that comes with a character-responsive narrative. Is it possible to deliver on both at the same time?

    \n Smitty,
    \n Well, I tried to strike a balance and usually it works pretty well. Here is how I setup a game with my friends:
    \n1) Agree on a game
    \n2) Agree on theme, play style (meaning pvp, good.evil play, etc.) and any others "I wont this"/"I don't want that" issues
    \n3) while the players make characters, I immerse myself in the details of the setting. Often I will make lists of factions and figure out why they do what they do and who would be political/philosophical allies/enemies
    \n4) Players come to the table with cool characters and I come to the table with a cool world. then I try and figure out the characters' motivations and hit them. Letting the world react organically to their mis-adventures.
    \nSo, the world is free to explore, but the improvisation is driven by my knowledge and understanding of an established setting (even if the setting is established by the group like in PTA).

    Posted By: E.T.SmithI want to improve play by building up the world outside of the game session...

    I want to offer both the joy of exploration that comes with a pre-defined world and the rich drama that comes with a character-responsive narrative. Is it possible to deliver on both at the same time?
    \nIs the rich, pre-defined world you are looking for something that has to be done without the players? The first several years of play for me involved hours and hours of out-of-game discussion. It was an Ars Magica inspired world, built by the players of the group as a whole, with major portions focused on by individuals. I came to it late, so instead of reading volumes of canon for play, I got to have the pleasure of talking with my friends about the world they'd built. What was the history of this political faction of mages? Why did these others specialize in that form of magic?

    That particular game had a long, slow build and was focused on letting us all play many characters and explore the game at leisure. If that is what you'd like to bring in, I can't recommend the colloborative world building and discussion enough. If you'd like that, then share the Sandbox.

    \n I think these two things can actually reinforce one another\u2014you just have to remain unattached to the consequences. For example, if you think out interesting locations, challenging GMC's, strange events, compelling hooks, and other pleasant surprises for the players to discover and interact with, your enthusiasm and example may well encourage the players' input, while you let them take the lead in defining the fiction and always keep the stage open for a chance in the spotlight. But the game cannot live or die by your interesting locations, challenging GMC's, strange events, compelling hooks, and other pleasant surprises\u2014those have to be there for the players to take or leave.

    It sounds like you have lots of fun building up the world outside the game session\u2014and that's totally okay, so long as you know what you're getting into ahead of time. To run a player-driven game you have to remember that those things not the plot and main characters, but the set and backdrop and costume closet, things for the players to play with and on if they so choose. Many of them will get ignored, and that's also totally okay.

    Cultivate non-attachment. Touch lightly with your strong hand.

    \n For a traditional fantasy game I ran decades ago, I set up an email list. I was working a summer temp job in an office, so I had a lot of free time - so every day I'd email a snippet of history, a legend, a folktale, a song. A few of the players got into it too. By the time we wrapped the game, the world was a big, complicated, self-contradictory mess that felt real.
