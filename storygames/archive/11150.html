\n I having second thoughts about a game term I'm using for Tokyo Rain.

Predators (like John Rain) are defined by three characteristics. Operations is general aptitude, skill, experience, etc. Assets are external aids, e.g., weapons, contacts, etc. The last I've been referring to "Psyche", but I'm afraid it's more opaque than I'd like. It's supposed to represent the agent's ability to mentally withstand the rigors of the operator's lifestyle, i.e., being able to kill people for a living and not end up in the nuthouse.

So is "psyche" okay? Thoughts for something else: resolve, willpower?

    What does flubbing a check on it look like in game?
    \nA session of Tokyo Rain represents a single mission. The Predator is trying to complete the mission as quickly and cleanly as possible. He "wins" every scene, but if he's not careful, the Prey builds up glitches and, ultimately, friction. At the end of the scene, the Prey rolls for the number of glitches he accrued in the scene, and the Predator rolls his Psyche score. Depending on the roll, some of these glitches are converted to friction. After the final scene, there is a final roll between the Prey's accrued friction and the Predator's Psyche score. How that roll turns out determines how the Predator holds up: Maybe he hesitates and let's the target live. Maybe he kills the target but falls into a deep depression. Maybe he is able to let it roll of his shoulders without a care. The result of the roll drives the narration of the denouement.

    The interesting thing about the books is that it is Rain's humanity, his ability to keep a hold on what kind of person he really is, that keeps him going strong. From Barry's perspective, the cold, detached hitman might look like he's holding up just fine, but he's really a powderkeg waiting to go off. It's a more "realistic" take on things than the cartoonish badass hitmen you often see. So, something like "detachment" wouldn't work well at all. That's sort of the opposite of what I'm going for. (Though it would be perfectly fine for a different type of game.)

    It's supposed to represent the agent's ability to mentally withstand the rigors of the operator's lifestyle, i.e., being able to kill people for a living and not end up in the nuthouse.
    \nPsychology provides a deep well of possible terms, some of which you will see below. A lot of it depends on what psychological process you're interested in modeling, and at what level. Functionally, the way things work psychologically when people become inured to things is they don't allow themselves empathy on a given range of activities/witnessed events. This can metastasize into a generalized lack of emotional display (affect).

    A human who kills other humans needs to dehumanize his victims, might lack the structures necessary to have that empathy in the first place, or might do a constant and high level of compartmentalization between his lives.

    My instinct would be to say something like Empathy or Callousness or perhaps both, with one capping the other or something. If I knew the books better or if I knew what kind of psychological process you're suggesting I might be able to come up with better suggestions.

    \n I should also note that I like Eric's suggestion a lot, especially if you want the nominal experience of the game to be like Rain's. I've now re-read the thread carefully and I see, Justin, that you see Rain's humanity as vital. Therefore, the coping mechanism that feels strongest to me is compartmentalization. A brief definition: think of how people separate Bill Clinton's job performance from his fucking; that's the masses engaging in compartmentalization, for example.

    Compartmentalization is fun from a game design perspective. The model I understand has a lot of moving parts. It depends on how deep you want to go into it. Since you want to see how someone will handle killing someone (question about that to come, mechanically), and what the result will be of this, I'd suggest something like Callousness, which raises slowly (or Empathy, which ablates), to represent whether your Predator can keep the feelings in this safe space or if she suffers a break of some kind.

    You might even give situational modifiers to Prey for being able to tap into the character's "safe zone" psychologically. For example, for the Predator, if she only kills hitmen, maybe this Prey-lady suggests she's not actually a hitman (woman). Maybe she uses a key phrase which reminds the Predator of her niece, etc.

    Now the question: How can you find out if you killed your Prey after the last scene? Did I just misread your post?
