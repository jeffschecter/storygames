How much character creation is not enough?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nDavidC \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2014 \n edited March 2014 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So, I had this great idea. I was going to make combat fast, fun, exciting and tactical. That way, we could RP for like 30 minutes, do an enjoyable combat for about 60 minutes, then get back to RP (our typical game structure) for the rest of the session with possibly a 2nd very short combat (15 minutes) in there somewhere. I wanted to separate out "crunch" from RP, so things like race, origin, personality are not part of character creation. (Elves don't get +2 dex).

I... I created a monster.

I got everything I wanted out of combat. RP is dead. It's like without hard lists to choose from, my players have lost all direction. People no longer pick out character quirks, wax poetic about their backstory or any of that. Every player has become a carbon copy of each other except in purpose. Think the board game Descent. It's like Numeria, except instead of _____ _____ who ____, players have been stripped down to just a (warrior, mage or thief). What's interesting is I play with a bunch of people who do a RP heavy larp that's ruleset mostly is about combat, so structure-less RP is their forte.

I've been modifying my formula for this over the past few months, but I wanted to know what you think? How much character creation is not enough?

    \n There's something about elevating the "inconsequential" details of race, origin, personality to the stature of stats and rules by giving them specific space on the character sheet and specific space in play - even if the game is combat-oriented. So even if there aren't modifiers, you should consider making the players write this info down and refer back to it in play Surely, race or origin must be useful in any logistics phase - "You're an elf, your racial affinity for bows gives you the edge when buying new weapons for the team," and so on.

    \n I worked on a game for five years -- it was called Acts of Evil -- that had basically this same problem. I'd carefully prep grabby situations, but players would just bomb into scenes, drive uncreatively for a conflict roll, and then flog the conflict mechanics. And they'd admit during and afterwards that they weren't having fun, because they weren't getting into their characters or roleplaying at all, but they also couldn't stop themselves from being consumed by engagement with the conflict system. The problem was because the human brain is cued to look for patterns and figure them out, and the conflict mechanics were a pattern they couldn't ignore. I never solved the problem with that game. I ended up creating a different game that solved it -- it's called The Clay That Woke. Essentially, what you have to do is extend the pattern of the conflict system out into the roleplaying. The Clay That Woke has outputs from the conflict system that impact the roleplaying, and things players can roleplay to affect their options in the conflict system.

    Paul

    \n Oh, and in case I wasn't completely clear, it's not about the sketchiness of the player characters at all. Starting characters in The Clay That Woke have very few characteristics that distinguish them from each other. They don't even have names. What's important is the way you connect outputs from the conflict system to the roleplayed fiction, and inputs from roleplayed events to the conflict system.
