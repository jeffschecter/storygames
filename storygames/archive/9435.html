\n Thought folks might be interested in a new variation on the Mafia game: Are You The Traitor?

There's a review link, but the guy isn't exactly a great explainer of the game (as far as I can suss out). But it's definitely unique, in that it uses rounds of play, no elimination, and some randomness beyond just role distribution (FatE--which I'm kind of against, if it's what I think it is by reading the review).

I'd put this post in Stuff To Watch, but I recall quite a bit of interest in a "new Mafia that's not a reskin," and so I suspect we'll get over three posts rather quickly.

    \n Yeah, what Mike said. It's not a re-skin; it's a re-design. Most significantly, it keeps all players in the game at all times (if I read the review right).

    As for the price-point, a couple of facts about Looney labs (disclaimer: I am a Pyramid game designer and have been a Looney Labs Demoer).
    * It's all made in the USA, which (obviously) makes it a tad more expensive relative to Chinese-made products.
    * Their form factor (that box size) has been chosen based on retailer requests vis a vis visibility on the shelf (i.e. they have made card game boxes bigger to help drive sales--no altogether altruistic, but they definitely have love for the FLGS).
    * They are a very small company (four employees?) with many products, and thus significant warehousing costs (all their products are voluminous--no stacks of 1000 rule books in a one square foot of floor space). Couple that with the production run sizes they have to hit to get the best prices, and you can see that their "rent' is a MAJOR factor in their operating costs.
    * They will not retail in "big box" establishments, because they want the smaller, owner-operator businesses to survive the "Wal-Martization" of the US.

    So, all-in-all, it might be more expensive than that new Magic Starter Deck, but that's the nature of a cottage industry. The most significant way in which they could cut costs would be to use production facilities (and even warehousing and fulfillment) with which they do not ethically agree. Believe me; I have spent a LOT of words, on and off their mailing lists, trying to find a way to enhance their market penetration by reducing costs or courting major retailers. They would rather stay small and stay "good," as they define it.

    Worth an extra 15% to 25% out of MY pocket....

    \n Yes, what Mike said. Hi, Mike!

    I don't think there was any particular NDA, and it's almost out. Jeff Abramson has a review here.

    In short, there are four key roles: the Keyholder (good), the Traitor (evil), and two wizards. The wizards look, sound, smell, and I presume feel and taste, absolutely identical. Only they know which one is which. All of the other players are Guards.

    The roles are dealt out. The wizards "go to sleep" (shut their eyes). The Keyholder reveals emself silently. The wizards get their good and evil roles randomly assigned, face down so only they know which is which. The traitor is not revealed.

    The good wizard wants to find who the traitor is so ey can kill em. The evil wizard wants to find out who the Keyholder is so ey can take the key and destroy the world. The traitor wants to find out who the evil wizard is so ey can tell em who the Keyholder is. The guards want to find out who the traitor is so they can point em out to the good wizard.

    Essentially, everyone but the wizards knows who the keyholder is. No one knows who the traitor is except the traitor. Everyone knows who the wizards are, but no one knows which one is which except for the wizards. There's a lot of "I'll bet you're the traitor!" and "I'm not the traitor" and "You must be the evil wizard", lots of paranoia and table talk until someone actually tries to identify who their quarry is.

    That's it in a nutshell. Jeff explains it better than I do, I think.
