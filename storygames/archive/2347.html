    From the publisher side of things, there is a lot of hassle involved with making your product more than "a book." Believe me, I've been thinking about alternate artifact forms for months now, and the sticking point is always in making your product available to be sold.

    Lets look at the simplest case: including dice with your game. This involves:

    *Buying enough bulk dice at cost so you can sell them and ship them and still make some margin (assuming you want to make money off your sales - this is not universally the case).

    *The time involved to sort dice into whatever quantity. This may be significant, actually, especially if your game uses a lot of different kinds of dice.

    *Packaging the dice. Do you do a box set with your game book at the dice inside (box sets = another round of production costs and research and assembly time)? Put your dice in a plastic baggie? Heatshrink and tape it to the book? Ship them seperately?

    *Can you afford to have all of your product pass through your hands on the way to retailers, distributors and resellers, assuming you use any of those services? If you're doing short digital print runs, is it worth the extra shipping and delay to have your printer ship your books to you, you attach the dice sets (or box them, or whatever), and then reship all of that to (for example) IPR or Key20?

    None of this is impossible, but it's a lot of work and probably significant expense, especially when your game is marketed to a community that has dice ownership as a kind of assumed thing.

    That said, look at Paul Czege - he sells dice sets for Bacchanel (or at least he did). He has the advantages that he sells (as far as I know) entirely direct, which cuts out a lot of the expense and hassle.

    Now, I'm saying all of this as someone who badly wants to be able to sell a book and have it have all you need for play in it, not as someone saying you shouldn't ever think about this. But, hopefully this can shed some light on the situation for you.

    OK guys, a few points. I'm not alone in having explored this in a fairly serious way. Robert, you're talking about "set-up boxes" (that's what they are called).

    For reference, here's what creating a game that shipped with 40 simple cards added to my life:

    1. Getting my designer to design an extra thing peripheral to the game text, which made him weep.
    2. Sourcing production for the extra thing, which involved either paying a lot or dealing with liars and rogues. Or both.
    3. Getting that extra thing printed, cut, collated, and packed to spec. These last two we did ourselves, exchanging money for time for our initial and second print runs. Now we gladly pay for somebody else to do it all.
    4. Coordinating production of two components, originally from two vendors, and making sure we didn't run out of either. Which we did in October anyway, even with the same vendor for both, for a month. This particular problem will never go away.

    So all this talk is nice as talk, but in practical terms it adds a huge burden that any sensible publisher will want to think very carefully about. I cannot imagine what this process would turn into with four or five different components. I'm sure the chaos scales exponentially. Sorry to come off like a curmudgeon, but my experience has been sobering.

    Robert, here's some quick numbers, gleaned from net searches.

    Indie roleplaying books in the UK cost, very very roughly, between 7 and 15 pounds. So, if you were selling a boxed game, you'd want to be somewhere around that range.

    Let's say you wanted to do it as cheaply as possible.

    Boxes start at about a pound. For that, you'd get a cheap, corrugated cardboard box, good for sending through the post and dull to look at: think Cheapass Games, but a bit bigger. You'd want to put labels on it, or a little cardboard sleeve, or something, to make it look professional, but it'd be OK.

    Now, if you're using a box, let's assume you've got dice or counters or something in it too. Dice and playing pieces are, very roughly, 10p each. A griplock bag to put them in is about 5p. So, a couple of dice in a bag is 25p; a bag of 20 counters is around two pounds.

    Then there's the book. Perhaps you'd want to print it. Since it's in a box, you could probably get away with just paper, folded in half and stapled down the middle: like a board game instruction leaflet.

    Then add a bit for shipping all of that stuff to you so you can put it together.

    It's possible, I think, to put the game together for about five pounds, and it'd look okay. And you'd be able to sell people a boxed game, ready to go. Of course, for that price, you could have a book which looked much slicker. But still.

    (This is all speculative, so experienced publishers are welcome to rip my figures apart.)

    Graham

  • Josh Roby
    February 2007
    See, I put "POD" in there as shorthand, and I knew I shouldn't have. Books are cheaper to make now than they ever were, thanks to a host of new technologies which is commonly and inappropriately called "POD technologies". One of the larger differences is in creating plates directly from the computer, rather than shooting film. There are better ink regulators now. There are others. Point being, new printing technology makes books lots easier to make than boxed sets today; back in the early 80s, the difference wasn't so extreme and thus a boxed set was a more viable alternative to 'just a book'. Today, the margin is wider, and it's a greater financial risk to do a boxed set as opposed to a book -- a risk that some folks are willing to consider, sure, but a risk, nonetheless.

      Ok, since I'm actually in the middle of doing this, I'll talk. First, see the game-in-a-container. You've got three books, 20 custom dice, 100 stones, a bag, and some character sheets, with room for pencils and other stuff.

      There's plenty of reasons to do this:

      1) You're not making an RPG. This is a great reason to do this. When I try to explain to other people that I make games, they see my books and think they're the instructions to the game, and wonder where the game is. Only role-players have a built-in expectation that a game comes in a book.

      2) Your game requires custom components. My dice are not available in stores, so I have to include them.

      2a) Your game requires a lot of components. I have three books.

      3) You want a distinctive look.

      4) You're not planning on making a ton of these. In my case, I plan to produce 50 sets and then see if it's feasible to make more. Each set will probably retail at $65 or 70.

      To make something like this affordable, you have to think of new ways to produce. My dice are wooden blocks, bought in bulk super-cheaply and then painted through a revolutionary new process I invented.

      I wonder if we're overlooking something here. The value of something like Ben's proposed Bliss Stage: Perfect Collection box might be in the way it enables "influentials" to increase Bliss Stage's penetration. Here, in a box, is everything you need to play. Your game club, anime club, whatever, spreads the cost over multiple people and over time.

      This is how it works for wargames today, mostly. This is how it works for miniatures gaming. There are a few people who spend a LOT of money to have the cool thing. They are the evangelists for the hobby, because people see the cool artifact and want to play with it. They may never buy the high-price-point toys themselves, but they buy the $15 book, etc. so they can play with the other guy's toys. Clubs and the like purchase the toys out of pooled funds, so that they have a draw to recruit people.

      $75 is competitive with lots of things people spend hobby money on. You just can't market it like you would a book. You market it (a) as a cool artifact in its own right, something that people will notice and want to ask about/play with, and (b) something that's meant to be purchased/used by a group.

      More data points on box costs: Pieces of Eight boxes (4 color + coating set-up boxes, roughly 6" x 9" x .5") eventually cost about $.66/ea, not including set-up costs (about $500 for the print run) and freight (widely variable depending on the distance from the manufacturer to you).
