\n I was just thinking about this today, and I'm sorry to go all "Livejournal" here (Mood: jubilant. Currently listening to: Hearts of Space: Mediterranean Echoes).

So I forget where it was, but recently there's been discussion here and there about "crappy single-concept thematic RPGs" (the RPG resolves around one concept and one concept only: MLWM, Breaking the Ice, etc), and how there's just this endless fucking flood of them hemorrhaging out of communities like this one and The Forge, Game Chef, etc*.

When playable, they're fun. No doubt.

I'm far more a traditional gamer kind of guy, though. I like My Guy, and Being the GM, and all that jazz. I love to throw some Story Games Sauce** all over the place when I run or play them, as setting up situation, focusing on relationships and emotional mechanics, and even a little lack of total character control really light me up.

However, I think I came to the realization that while I like these games I see around me, I'm really biding my time for the Better Games that will come out of this dearth, that proudly ride on the backs of their predecessors and peers. The 300-page Star Trek-like game that will use Best Friends style character generation. The martial arts game that will play out like a game-y board game, yet whose stories consistently leave the players teary-eyed at the table. The 5-page-long Dungeons and Dragons (ish game) that uses simply playing cards, has a setting of mythical India, and can be busted out and completed in hour-long sessions.

While I pretty much keep my fingers on the pulse (or alternately "mouth on the pipe") of what's coming out in both mainstream and indie (regardless of affiliation: I'm talking JAGS, Starcluster, and Key20 titles as much as Forge or IPR titles), I've been coming to terms recently, especially seeing all the crazy buzz here, on LJ, and on Gamecraft (like the stuff that is applicable to Your Game Right Now and doesn't require its own system), with the fact that when I see all sorts of new games coming out, I'm really looking for those few diamonds that stand on the backs of those games.

Seeing that process taking place right before my eyes has been really magical.

That's all.

-Andy

* Game Chef left me absolutely drained and devastated this year, seriously. Out of all the games I read and reviewed, like 90% of them I wanted to play right then and there. I was actually afraid to read other games for fear that I would want to play them to, and that was really kinda twisting me up in knots, even moreso than the last three years.

** Not to be confused with the kind of sauce that appears frequently in Takeshi Miike movies.

    \n Hmm. Interesting. I've been kind of thinking about this since getting back from GO PLAY NW. It seems to me that I maybe favor the opposite. I like a game that is focused, concise and about one thing. I think that one thing can be enough to provide a lot of interesting play and (asuming the game is good) make me want to come back to the game over and over again. I may be misreading you, but it seems to me that what you're saying is that you want something that fits in with a more traditional game theme that you might enjoy (Star Trek, Martial Arts, D&D), but uses the same awesomeness that you love from Breaking the Ice, Roach or whatever. I can completely get this. When Panty Explosion came out there were a lot of people who said "I would really like this if it where Samurai or Naruto or Star Wars". When you see awesomeness, you want it to be awesomeness in something you like, right? Isn't that why we all love Spirit of the Century? It's awesome like Dogs in the Vinyard, Shooting the Moon or Contenders, but it uses a "safe" genre. We all like high flying pulp action. We can all get behind that. It pushes a button that this community really likes having pushed, in a way that some other games don't.

    Here's the thing though. The more I play these games though, the more I feel like a more directed and focused "one idea" game is what I want. In the same way that when a buy a novel I'm looking for something very specific and not something very general, or when I go see a movie I want the particular experience that movie creates. I like playing Contenders because Contenders does something very, very specific that I really, really enjoy. I enjoy Contenders enough that I really, really want to keep playing it. It doesn't matter that Contenders "only does boxing". What matters is that it's the right game for me. I don't need to play Contenders as a side game to my normal game or as a one shot. Contenders is the game for me.

    So the thing I was wondering about in general was, do we want these "diamonds that stand on the backs of those games" because we want better games, or because we want games that do these awesome things but cater more to our personal tastes? If it is the later... well, there's nothing wrong with that. We want what we want. But what I'm finding is that more and more what I personally want isn't Star Trek, Martial Arts or D&D. Or maybe it is. What I reall am interested in is a personal vision. Because if I want to play Star Trek, or Martial Arts or whatever, I can. I don't need someones rulebook for that. I don't need 30 pages of feats, or a life path or some fan mail. But I would have never thought to play BlissStage. BlissStage is a very personal vision. It would have never occured to me to play something like that. Once I saw it I wanted to try it. I didn't want to go home and try to make Fate or D20 work for it. I wanted to buy into Ben's vision completely, in the same way that I wanted to experience Zodiac in the theatre earlier this year instead of just browsing the internet looking for old news articles. I bought (or borrowed or played) Perfect, Dogs in the Vinyard, Sign in Stranger, It was A Mutual Descision, Burning Empires, Death's Door, PTA and a number of other games for the exact same reason. In each case it was the designers personal vision that attracted me to the game. In each case I didn't know I wanted to play it until I saw it.

    I know I'm flying away from the topic at a dramatic speed, so let me try to circle back around. What I suspect is that becasue we prefer fantasy (or pulp, or sci-fi or whatever genre is safe for us individually), we're going to be unsatisfied (to some degree) with games like Breaking the Ice, Contenders or Roach. They don't give us what we want. On the other hand, If what we want is a game about romance, relationships, sex or close friendships, we're going to be... well, Burning Wheel, Spirit of the Century or any other number of games arn't going to work for us, but we'll be very happy with some of the other games I've mentioned here. I think a lot of the games that we've seen released over the last few years from this community (and i'll throw my own games in here) are not actually intended FOR this community. Rather, these games are created for a different audience, with the hope that this coummunity might enjoy them as well.

    I know I'm going on and on, so let me get to my real point. I've recently realized that I don't actually like fantasy games. I mean I do, but it's not my favorite thing. But because I'm interested in roleplaying games I've been "forced" to play fantasy games for years. I also have realized that I prefer a narrow focus game to a game that allows me to do "anything I want". If what I want is to go on a series of three dates, then I don't need a game that allows me to also shoot people or plot master crimes. Breaking the Ice is the perfect game for what I want.

    So really, after all that, all I'm saying is that I've kind of been thinking about the same thing, and what I've decided is that I do like a narrow focused game, and that I prefer to play something that isn't fantasy. Which doesn't really addres what you were talking about to much at all, does it Andy?

    Oh well.

    I do tremendously enjoy trying new games and exploring new mechanics. I can play very specialized premises for a handful of games in order to explore them. Other times I expect a game to allow a much vaster scope and I find it's in fact very focused, like Burning Empires; I feel disappointed.

    This is not that different from the feeling I get from traditional RPGs that have very detailed settings with lots of canon material; the more fenced-in by the system or setting I feel, the less interested I am. I like to have lots of margin to scribble in. A game that is solely about breaking up, or about boxing, or about dying, or what have you, feels like a board game to me. And although some days I like playing board games, ultimately I find them unsatisfying. The games I keep returning to have lots of possible story modes and structures.

    Posted By: AndyHowever, I think I came to the realization that while I like these games I see around me, I'm really biding my time for the Better Games that will come out of this [flood], that proudly ride on the backs of their predecessors and peers.
    \nTo quote something I wrote a while back on Vincent's site:\n
    Yes, screwing around with new mechanics purely for the sake of being innovative is Not So Great. But! Ever heard of the Burgess Shale? It's a particular rock formation that has a staggering variety of fossils in it -- more fundamentally different ways of arranging the body (phyla) than exist in all subsequent evolution. (I'm cribbing shamelessly from Stephen J. Gould's Wonderful Life here; I'm no paleontologist). The key thing about the Burgess Shale is that it preserves fossils from the "Cambrian Explosion," a very early period AFTER the basic principles of organizing a multi-cellular life form were evolved but BEFORE there had been a mass extinction to weed out the designs.

    Now, combined with the fact that two years is a fast cycle time -- D&D splits off from miniatures gaming say, circa 1974-1975? That's only 30 years ago -- one generation! ANd much of that time was spent, as it had to be, thrashing out "uh, what is this new thing anyway?" and running basic variations on the original wargaming structures, rather like Precambrian evolution throwing out lots of slightly different ways to be a single-celled organism.

    I don't know the hobby history too well, but the earliest conflict resolution/fortune in the middle design I know of is Story Engine from, what, the mid-nineties? (There are probably earlier examples).     Yes, screwing around with new mechanics purely for the sake of being innovative is Not So Great. But! Ever heard of the Burgess Shale? It's a particular rock formation that has a staggering variety of fossils in it -- more fundamentally different ways of arranging the body (phyla) than exist in all subsequent evolution. (I'm cribbing shamelessly from Stephen J. Gould's Wonderful Life here; I'm no paleontologist). The key thing about the Burgess Shale is that it preserves fossils from the "Cambrian Explosion," a very early period AFTER the basic principles of organizing a multi-cellular life form were evolved but BEFORE there had been a mass extinction to weed out the designs.

    \nIt occurs to me that most games are construction sets, at least to a limited extent. Burning Empires is a construction set for playing games set in the (as you pointed out) Sheva's War storyline/setting/whatever. It is narrow, but if that's the game you came to play then the construction set tools are certainly there.

    I've been wondering more and more if the tendency of gamers to prefer a construction kit is due to a kind of natural desire to tinker with, modify, improve or otherwise change games to fit their desires. A kind of answer to the bit inside of us that wants to make games? Or, is it maybe because we just haven't found the game we want? Have customization and tool box games arisen from the fact that for the last 30 years most game settings have sucked? That most games have done a poor job of emmulating the fiction of Tolkien, Howard and other genre authors? That most games have done a poor job of giving us rules that allow us to play the games we really want to play? So we have to fix these games to make them what we want them to be? If we had a game that did what we wanted it to do, would we still feel like we needed a toolbox?

    I'm sure some of us would. There's a ton of appeal to the tool box, and certainly many people like many games just for that reason. But it seems like you can have a narrow focus game that still provides a lot of tool box goodness. I'm finding more and more games that give me the play experience that I want without leaving me feeling like I need to modify them to get there. Using my own game as an example, Panty Explosion has the narrow focus of psychic Japanese school girls and social relationships between teenagers, but still contains plenty of tool box elements. Sign in Stranger is (I think) pretty tool boxy, but still carries with it a narrow focus that I find appealing and a lot of unique flavor that I wouldn't get in Traveller, D20 Future or some other more "generic" sci-fi games. I'm saying I don't think that narrow focus and tool box are mutually exclusive. It seems to me a game can be both.
