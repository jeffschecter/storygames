Baby, bathwatere and setting the stakes

Ian Cooper
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
I had not seen the debate on 'setting the stakes' here until it was linked below, but I can''t help feel that there is a danger of throwing the good out with the bad by railing against the use of 'setting the stakes' in games like HeroQuest.

We have started to refer to 'setting the stakes' in our HeroQuest game of late. When we use it we tend to mean 'agreeing the conflict of interest'. We make sure that everyone is on the same page about what the player is trying to achieve by their actions in the conflict as opposed to the task. We agree what the levels of defeat broadly (complete) broadly equate to. It's not a forked choice, its certainly fuzzy and narrated post-event, but we understand the magnitude of what is happening. In the same way we might set the stakes of whether complete defeat will result in death in a fight.

Now maybe its training wheels for conflict resolution. But it has had real benefits in players being less inclined to fear defeat and spend hero points to prevent it, because they can see that the story outcome of defeat will be interesting in the same way that success was.

So we can rail against it, but I think there are some folks doing it for positive reasons. Perhaps for where they are now in terms of gaming maturity, and perhaps they will take the training wheels off one day, but it feels like a valid way to explore conflict resolution to me.

    No, conflict resolution doesn't need stake-setting.

    All you really need is an understanding of what the conflict is, and an understanding that, if the mechanics are applied, the conflict will be resolved. A lot of the latter is a social contract thing.

    Setting stakes helps to achieve both. Everybody knows what the conflict is about, and what the outcome will be. Assuming you set the stakes well, it also ensures that everybody's happy with the outcome.

    However, you don't need to predetermine the outcome. As long as you know what the conflict is about, a functional group can use almost any methods to resolve the conflict. (Winner narrates. Loser narrates. Back-and-forth narration. GM fiat. And so on, and so forth.)

    I suspect that one will find that, whether or not stakes are specifically being set, most of the time defining the conflict will implicitly define at least the basic stakes.

    Stakes setting and conflict resolution are like chocolate and peanut butter, they go very well together, but they aren't the same thing and don't always have to be together.

    It is possible to have stakes set for a task. (We talked about this in John Harpers "stakes resolution" thread). It is possible to have a conflict resolved without specific stakes preset -- or at least without any predetermined results of the conflict set. (This is what Ron is talking about).

    Its also possible to have both stakes and conflict resolution, and have them work out together into peanut butter cuppy goodness.

    There isn't need to toss anything out, because we can keep it all. But if we realize that the host of related tools are actually different tools working together, rather than one massive monolith of mechanics that must always be used the same way, we'll be able to get better results and more types of results.

    Ah, clever. Now we have to get back to the subject until such time as the Event is resolved.

    So, anyway ... I like the general point that Stakes-setting is a communication tool (getting people all on the same page about what the possible outcomes are) and that conflict resolution is a social dynamic (that people are going to disagree, and that those disagreements will be addressed rather than avoided).

    Yes, they go great together. I wonder, though, whether they create a blind-spot for us?

    I'm not going to offer alternatives to conflict resolution ... my blindspot on that one is large. I want a CR system for getting out of bed in the morning ("I win ... I stay in the comfy, comfy bed"). I leave that theorizing to someone else.

    But I will say that I think that stake-setting is only the simplest way of getting people on the same page about what the possible outcomes are. There are other ones that would rock, too.

    For instance, you could say "Okay, whoever wins this conflict gets to write down something they want to have happen later in the story on an index card. At any time after that they may apply that index card to any roll that leads toward their desired outcome."

    That very clearly doesn't set stakes. It's not "If I win, X, if I lose, Y" it's "Whoever wins? Write your own ticket." Nobody knows what will happen if someone else wins, but everyone knows how much mechanical impact it will have. Does that put them on the same page? I think it does, but I could be wrong. Haven't tested.
