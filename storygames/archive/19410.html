\n This is an example of how possibility thinking and probability thinking can play out in real life.

In a previous thread I described how playing story games lead me to have a mental shift to what I called looking for possibilities rather than concentrating on probabilities.

First Probability thinking: Like many people I played traditional RPGs that focus on task resolution. Events were broken down to a series of tasks - each with a probability roll. To succeed in a task you needed to pass each roll. This usually meant playing to your strengths and avoiding your weaknesses because failed rolls were fatal. If the odds of success were not 66% or better, there was no point in doing the action. So in effect this world view says don't take chances, do what is tried and true. It works out in the game because you are super humans. In real life it works out differently.

So in my real life, my assessment of myself was that not only was I not a super human but that I was in fact weak in soooo many ways. So what were the odds that the most beautiful woman I met to go out with me be? Very low, so don't try. (Actually in college I intentionally tried anyway, met three beautiful women and learned I had nothing in common with them. Interesting experience.) Applied to game making, I could ask myself "What are the odds that I'll make the next D+D?" Very low in sooo many ways, so don't try.

Now Possibility thinking: My early experiments in story game showed me that almost anything could happen in a game if the player took the time to lay the ground work for success. So jumping on top of a building in a single bound was very unlikely to happen. Walking up a flight of stairs was very likely to happen, going to the elevator was easy to do, and these action made making it to the roof inevitable. If you missed a roll, no sweat, try another approach to building the next step. Persistence rather than brilliance creates success.

Applied to life I could look at where I was and think about what I wanted. I wasn't there, so what could I do to lay the foundation to get there? I knew the games I was working on were very new and that that meant they would not be easily accepted or understood. I also knew that if I was willing to do the foot work, that they would succeed. So the needed virtue was not brilliance or awesomeness but was rather persistence and patience. All you had to do was look at the possibilities in front of you and pick the ones that lead to the long term goal.

***

So for me probability thinking focused me on the short term and really limited me. I'm not saying it would do this to everyone, it didn't even do it to me because I did do my experiment meeting beautiful women. It didn't even stop me making other games because if it had I would not have done what I did. What it did do though was suggest that success must come in one big move or not at all, which isn't the way the world works. Possibility thinking was useful because it predicts success if you put the work in and focuses thought onto what you need to do next. It also says up front to have an end goal in mind and not to be swayed from it by short term gain.

***

What I'd like to hear in this thread are how the mental shift story games create has played out in your lives. I hope that possibility thinking is part of it but if focusing on the odds has been helpful for you I want to hear that as well.

    \n Focusing on the odds is tremendously helpful to me. I analyze situations and fine-tune my models of reality as I go, often by creating quantitative scales for qualitative things, and building a sort of utilitarian matrix with them. Two important points must be made about this.

    First, it would be childish and superstitious to believe that a single-order calculation is going to be very useful as a reality-predicting tool. X percent chance of ME marrying the most beautiful woman on campus? That can only be determined after the fact. But the chance of this step leading to that step leading to that step... or the chance that this type of person is swayed by that type of language, getting me to that desired step... that sort of stuff can be fuzzy-calculated within heuristic parameters and adjusted as you go. We're talking about complex multidimensional webs of very simple calculations, not happening at once but drawn out upon each other, each containing its own probabilities and potential vectors, which enables/forces you to take a stance similar to "cognitive dissonance" or "Schrodinger's Cat". When playing the odds, you remain in an uncollapsed wave-field. You must remember that these are models, and continually adjust them. The slippery slope lies not in creating models, but in believing them to be literal representations of reality, rather than the useful fictions they truly are, designed to help us jump fuzzily from one point to the next.

    Second: Isn't this Possibility vs Probability division you're drawing rather like the division between what we call "left brain" and "right brain" thinking, but applied to predictive judgments? And if so, then obviously the answer is that we need to use both in balance and allow each to feed back into the other.

    Contract your left tailfin muscle. Now contract your right tailfin muscle. Repeat. Again and again. Faster. Are you going left/right? No, you are swimming forward.

    Yes, this is what I mean. The unexpected is not just ways around a given challenge but of redefining what the challenges are.

    Trad RPG rules unconsciously guide play by defining ways to do things. They prescribe a set of procedures and those defined paths are the ones we walk down. I like playing like this at times but it never opened my mind up in the way looking at all the possibilities did.

    For example: Take The Lord of the Rings. Frodo is pitiful. He has no skills or anything that would predict success, no matter how much he steps around challenges. He ditches his competent friends and ends up with Sam (simple, loyal, Sam) and Gollum (curse your predictable but unexpected betrayal). Events build on events and Frodo's purity looks like it will win, but no! Gollum saves the day. You can't predict what will happen beforehand, which makes it fun to read. It could have gone in a lot of different directions. Evil could have won, it is not a straight line story like the Narnia books. Each moment is electric with many different possibilities.

    In my life I make games. At first I wanted to make a hit and be famous (who doesn't) but I'm a pitiful Frodo. I do what I can to increase my abilities but in the end I'm unlikely to succeed based on them. There are a lot of people who are much better and smarter than I. But I see possibilities in front of me. I make decisions and make games. I see the feedback they inspire and learn from it. I watch what other people do and don't copy it (it doesn't tell my story - no one remembers who built the second airplane). I know I won't be famous or a wunderkind but I like what I've done. Looking at the electric possibilities in front of me and actively engaging them has made for an interesting life.

    The games that helped me develop this world view put the players in the role of author rather than actor. Where you write whole scenes rather than get lost in the details.

    I hope this isn't the sim vs nar debate. That was always so full of "My way is better than your's is." It made for great flame wars on RPG.net but was so pointless. I'm fully aware that this approach isn't for everyone. I'm just interested in seeing how many people have made this mental shift and how it's changed them. And for those who don't find it useful, what the juxtaposition of this kind of game next to a more trad game makes you think of.

    \nI've gone back and forth on rather or not to reply to this thread. I've started at least three different replies, looking for a way to say what I was actually trying to. Hopefully this time.

    First and foremost, I don't share any of the experiences or conclusions that you bring up. That doesn't mean either one of us is incorrect, only that we have had different experiences, or have opposing warrants or perceptions that have led us to differing outcomes and beliefs. So when I talk about these different takes on what you post, understand I'm not 'coming at you', merely offering a different take on the same themes.

    Ok, so right away I have to say that story games created no mental shift for me. They were focused on different aspects of gaming, or used different techniques relating to those aspects, but for our roleplaying they were nothing truly new. Since our first games we were focused on rich characters and telling stories, and explored infinite possibilities. In fact, I could argue that much of the heavy mechanics encourages 'possibility thinking', but that might be better in the other thread.

    Roleplaying in general definitely impacted my life, and perhaps encouraged certain shifts in thinking overall. I would also agree that two of these factors were probability analysis and creative problem solving (what I would equate to your 'possibility thinking').

    If anything I'd say probability analysis is the more common, and more impacting aspect. Unlike what you describe, low odds doesn't necessarily mean not bothering for me. It's just acceptance of facts, and a way to analyze value. In roleplaying what action I take is dictated by my character, not the mechanics. If my barbarian would attack, then I attack. That I have only a 15% chance of winning isn't relevant. However, perhaps my military engineer character would alter how he attacks based on the odds, but ONLY because that's how that character actually behaves.

    Conversely, high probability doesn't necessarily encourage me to do something. What I think I should do does. I'm fairly intelligent, and have an enormous amount of knowledge of and connection with law enforcement. As such I feel I have a high probability of success in criminal endeavors. However I don't generally participate in those things as I have other objections (morals, unintended consequences, value vs consequences, etc).

    I'd also like to bring up the idea of 'pot odds' in poker. For those not familiar with it, basically this is looking at the payoff vs investment rather than merely the hand odds. If I need one of 10 cards to make my flush and win (I presume) I know I only have a 20% chance of success (from a full deck). However, if the pot is $100 and I only need to pay-in $10, then a 1:10 bet has a 2:10 chance of success - situationally making it a valid call. What's important about this is that you have to be able to calculate those odds to compare them to know rather it's worth calling. Possibility thinking MUST (statistically) cost you money at the table (unless you include expert reading and numerous other factors which aren't always controllable).

    Certainly creative problem solving has had value as well. Especially in tactical situations (I've spent more than a decade in military and security work). Looking for hidden ways around, taking the time to plan, using one's environment, being aware of the behavior of others, etc. It's all valuable.

    Roleplaying taught me a lot more than just those things however. Teamwork, social integration, friendship, numerous academic disciplines (history, sociology, economics, psychology, etc), creativity, time management, self-confidence, theatrics, public speaking, etc. It has been the single greatest impacting facet of my life in fact. However, nearly all of it came predominately from those early half dozen or dozen traditional games (D&D, Shadowrun, etc). The only impact of this new independent gaming age (story games or otherwise) has been knowing I can focus on different aspects to different degrees, seeing some interesting unusual techniques to accomplish old tropes, and the possibility of self-publishing.

    \n I will talk about synergy and emergence. Emergence is the result from when several parts in a system interact. I will continue using possibility and probability but keep in mind what I mean with it.

    Imagine a locked door and you want to get through. Imagine the door being in a wall. Now you increased to possibilities to enforce the obstacle but you also present two different probabilities. Add a guard patrolling outside the wall with a key to the door. That's a third choice but offers a lot of probabilities. But look what happens if you now try to climb over the wall\u2014you will risk getting seen by the guard. This is synergy, when one thing is connected to another.

    Probabilities just stack, where a wall with a guard creates two obstacles that decreases the chance to move on. Probability never creates synergy like possibilities can. The more things that live in synergy together, the more obscure the final outcome will be, which is what emergent complexity is about. Create how each part in the system affects other parts and you will have a living organism that will react when being tampered with. Tamper with the wall and the guard will react.

    Compare this to, for example, bonuses and powers in D&D4.

      \n This is actually a fairly interesting distinction, from a philosophical perspective. We can establish probabilities for a wide range of actions and reactions around us, even if some are fairly vague.

      We can also establish or at least estimate the probability of an average human taking certain actions, and generalize that to groups. (For instance, you can't usually meaningfully estimate the probability that your gaming group will stay together this year, but you might be able to estimate pretty well the probability that, of all the gaming groups in Berlin, half stay together.)

      However, it's very difficult (and sometimes impossible) to apply probabilities to a single individual's decisions. What is the probability that your wife will cheat on you in the next six months? In most people's lives, such a question is useless - we know it's a possibility, but we (usually/often) have no sense whatsoever of the probability involved.

      The same applies to the hypothetical random encounter table:

      After playing with this particular table and this particular GM for a long time, we might be able to make a guess at the probability of, say, a Dragon-turtle being encountered. But if this is a one-shot adventure and we've never used this table before, we don't have a clue - even the GM herself might not know.

      \nHere's my take on probability vs. possibility thinking. I don't know if it's the same thing or a different thing from what you're getting at.

      When I first started playing RPGs as a wee lad I would see great scenes in movies or read them in books and want to see them in my games, so I would convert them into RPG scenes in my head. So many of the best scenes are so highly unlikely. Siggurd kills a dragon with one blow. Bard shoots Smaug with the black arrow. The Karate Kid catches a fly with chopsticks!

      Let's say the protagonist walks a tightrope from one building to the next, picks the lock on a window, sneaks in without being heard, takes out the guard walking down the hallway from behind, shoots a dart into a wall and swings across the courtyard on the cable that's attached to it, sneaks to the vault, dispatching another guard...what's the probability of success in this mad scheme? Awful. This is a terrible plan. It can go so wrong in so many places. You have to be an expert acrobat, climber, assassin, shooter, lock picker. If I'm an expert in all those skills, say my character started in the 20% range and now has 75% in all of them. That's a 25% of falling off the tightrope, or a 25% chance of being stuck outside the window, or the guard seeing you, or missing your shot, or falling during the swing...One wrong dice roll, and it all goes horribly wrong. So, you don't do that. You do something safer, like just attack people with swords.

      And that's really how I thought RPGs worked. How they had to work. They modeled probabilities. If something was nearly impossible in real life, then it should be nearly impossible in the game.

      But, if you're playing Fiasco, what's the probability of that plan succeeding? 100%! If I'm narrating, all of that just happens. It may turn out to be the worst thing ever, but it happens. If I'm playing Fate? Pretty darn good. I may have to succeed with a cost along the way, but hey, that's cool. Or Burning Wheel? Well, it's different. The GM can say "Yeah, you walk the tightrope fine and the lock's no problem, the guards on the upper floor aren't going to be a problem...But, roll stealth plus a die for your climbing against their notice, and let's see if their waiting for you inside the vault..."

      And suddenly, games can be all kinds of things. They don't have to model how bad your odds are any more. I like books where protagonists do improbable things, why wouldn't I want that in my games?
