\n We all know we're status monkeys, and we treat people differently based on who they are. Gender, race, perceived rank, etc, etc, etc. Here's a game you can play by yourself if you like; it's not very new or original, but could be fun and interesting.

First, think of three different people in the S-G community:
- Someone who is an authority
- Someone who is very different from you in background (if you're a white european man, pick a black american woman, for instance)
- Someone where you can hardly even remember their name/nick

Now pick the three threads at the top of the front page (except for stickies & this one), and imagine how you would react if they had been posted by these three people.

    \n As a new user, I approach this from a significantly different angle. I don't know who anyone is or from what background they're approaching a topic and most other people don't have that information about me. I have to figure out what people are up to and how they think so I can express myself clearly and fit in (due to aforementioned status monkey behaviour.) Since I have been posting for a total of two days, I use a system of clumsy generalisations to read into what is going on, which I will now list as best I can for the interest value of the general public.
    \n
    \nThings That Are Cool:
    \n- Apocalypse World System
    \n- Being creative
    \n- Structure mirrors story
    \n- Being relaxed
    \n- Using standard punctuation
    \n- Being nice
    \n
    \nThings That Are Uncool:
    \n- Being belligerent
    \n- Aggression
    \n- Spoiling others' fun
    \n
    \nAuthorities (in my social model / gross generalisation) are people who post a lot, and people whose posts frequently receive positive replies. Basically, any regular is an authority. Other people typically fit into three categories: lurkers who occasionally post, unidentifiable new people (who have no grounding in the forum's social world), and 'cool new people' who probably know a regular or have a tangible link to something on the forum's Things That Are Cool list. (Fyi I don't compile these models. I am telling a folk story about what I think my brain is up to.)
    \n
    \nAt the moment, my thread-reading behaviour is driven largely by whether or not I think I will learn things about the community from the thread, or whether I will be able to participate in it. Both of these things increase my socialization to the community and allow me to accumulate cultural capital (at the risk of losing a lot of cultural capital if I do something Uncool.) Even presently, I am assessing whether this post is excessively weird or if I'm meant to post in this thread. It's pretty strange analysing it as I do it.
    \n
    \nReading what's interesting definitely plays a big part, because if I didn't think that what people post here is interesting and valuable then I would have no reason to be status monkeying, and I just proved to myself that I am doing so. o.0

    \n Thing I've learned, indirectly through this experiment:

    If a man is presenting some piece of design theory, or telling us about his "innovative new gaming idea," I am more likely to be critical. I am more likely to demand expert knowledge and execution. I am more likely to judge the contribution to be unworthy.

    If a woman is presenting some piece of design theory or an "innovative new gaming idea," I am more likely to think "good! We need this kind of thinking and design in order to keep growing as a hobby" but also less likely to think "this person is an expert."

    I tend to subconsciously classify mens' design ideas into an expert/unworthy dichotomy, and womens' design ideas get labelled something along the lines of "valuable, unfinished idea."

    ...so now I feel really uncomfortable having (A) discovered this about myself, and (B) reported it back to the group. Erg.

    \nThe first gate: Am I interested in this topic?

    Yes: I click in to read it, and only then do I see who wrote it.
    No: I move on without checking to see who wrote it.
    Maybe: Okay, now it really matters who wrote it. Proceed to the second gate.


    The second gate: Do I think that this person has interesting things to share on this topic?

    Yes: I read it.
    No or Maybe: Proceed to the third gate.


    The third gate: Do I think that this person has an expert, unique, or different perspective on this topic, even if it isn't a major interest of mine?

    No or Maybe: Game over.
    Yes: If I am really slacking off and killing time and there are no better threads jumping out at me, I read it.


    So the relevance of authority here is its role in convincing me that you have interesting or expert things to say on particular topics. What will convince me?

    - Having said interesting things in the past, obviously.

    - Having tangibly succeeded at the topic in question. Even if you suck at explaining stuff, I'm still game to hear you try, because you've found a way to do something I want to do!

    - Claiming success at the topic in question. "Everyone loves my GMing," for instance. I tend to believe such claims initially. But if you don't then say anything interesting, I will gradually get un-convinced.

    I, uh, am afraid I can't really tell what part of all that counts as authority.
