On the definition of "Geek"

Eric Provost
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games

I'm sitting here, reading over all the geek-oriented threads of late, and just out of whim I headed over to dictionary.com for a little grounding. I do that sometimes. What caught my eye was an American Heritage Dictionary definition;

A person who is single-minded or accomplished in scientific or technical pursuits but is felt to be socially inept.

Of course, in the case of the gamer geek, I'd imagine replacing "scientific or technical pursuits" with "arcane game knowlege".

Which was funny and illuminating. See, from years of context, I'd always disassociated the socially inept from the geek. I was internally using a definition where Geek = Arcane & Trivial Acomplishments, with lack o' social skills being a coincidental side-order. But now I see that some folks (probably most folks) define a geek by their lack of social skills. Heh. Didn't know that.

So, back in another thread, when I said I was happy with gaming being geeky, I certainly did not mean that I was happy with gaming being about social ineptness. I did mean that I am totally happy with the amount of time I invest in the hobby.

And now that I know the difference between how I'd been using "geek" and how others might use the word, I imagine that this may just be a little bit of a culprit for miscommunication in those other threads. But that's just me guessing.

    But it is the origin of the term. That is, "geek" meant a circus performer who would bite the heads off of chickens. It's come to mean what it means because of the implied ostracization of a person with such an odd talent. I mean just how do you relate to that?

    I find it hard to imagine that somebody could think of the term geek without thinking of the notion of somebody socially inept. I believe that the term began to be used to replace "nerd" in the 80's. Which was the term formerly used to refer to precisely what the dictionary reference above refers to. For some reason nerd became passe, and geek took over.

    C.F also "Pencil Necked-Geek"

    An assumption about "geekiness" is that it goes along with physically small or obese individuals. The idea being that these people were often outcast simply because of their body image (caused by either real or imagined taunts). Unable to compete with individuals more in the normal range of body size for the attention of the opposite sex (or, again, feeling that way), the notion is that they compensate for the lack of self-esteem by finding some technical area in which to excel.

    The enthusiasm for this technical field, then, is seen by those who don't need such as pathetic. Geeky.

    Dork means simply socially inept for whatever reason, though, yeah, it's often conflated with geekishness or nerdiness. Basically somebody who is unable to express themselves well because of their self-consciousness.

    There have been some speculations of late that the phenomenon is somewhat caused or actually synonymous with Asperger's Syndrom and other similar high functioning forms of autism. I've known two "gamers" who have been diagnosed with Aspergers, and most "Gamers" seem to exhibit at least some of the symptoms.

    As we move into a more technologically dependent world, the specialized technical knowledges of some "geeks," most obviously computer skills, have made these individuals capable of bringing down rather good salaries, which suddenly propells them into relative sexual attractiveness that they've never had before. This helps self-esteem, and "geeks" are slowly losing some of the self-esteem problems that they had.

    So there's this secondary effect where there is a populace who has technical knowledge, and previously lacked self-esteem, but who now do not. These people think of themselves as geeks, but they no longer exhibit the key ingredient, the lack of self-confidence. Instead they cling to the term so that they can indicate that they are somebody with that sort of now valuable specialized technical knowledge.

    This is phenomenologically associated with this particular period in history. That is, the rise of computers and the internet is what's caused this shift. Note, too, that many "geeks" hold onto the association as such, becuase a common form of defense mechanism against the self-consciousness caused by being ostracized is to assume that "everybody else" is simply bad. "I'm OK, you're not OK" to use the morphology from the pop psych book. Like, "If I'm a geek, so be it...you just don't see my value...my mom said so."

    Turns out that, because of Bill Gates, lo' the geeks are validated in this belief. But it doesn't make the psychological methodology any more healthy.

    And, yes, I am to some extent merely speaking of my own experience with the phenomenon. Meaning that in High School I felt ostracized, and resorted to RPGs are my form of technical knowledge (and computers). I was a pretty weak case, in that I tended to merely be cripplingly shy, instead of one of the uber-geeks who manage to actually maneuver through life looking as though they are not bothered by the fact that they are thought of by others as being inferior (didn't hurt that I was only a "geek" and not a "pencil-necked geek"). And I've grow out of it as most geeks do - it's not just "geek chic" but that experience is a good teacher. You learn not to be geeky, to have real self-confidence.

    Anyhow, for a primer on what my life was like in the 80s, see the show "Freaks and Geeks" which speaks directly to all of this, including the RPG play, and does so with absolutely starkly frightening authenticity.

    Mike

    Mike's got a lot of important points, up there. I think there's a little confusion between "social ineptness" and "social esteem" (or self-confidence,) though. Although the results for both are the same, there's a big difference between people who have problems making friends because of shyness, avoidant behavior, or lack of self-esteem, on the one hand, and people who have problems making friends because they miss social cues (because of borderline autistic traits.)

    I wouldn't bring this up, except that I think when we say something is "geeky," we're talking about interests that are usually associated with the borderline autistic group than with the low self-esteem group. Science fiction is not inherently geeky, but there is a stereotypical "sci-fi geek", obsessed with science fiction details, who fails to communicate well because all his conversations are really monologues about his obsession. The true sci-fi geek treats all of the surface details of their favorite books, movies or shows as equally important and wants to discuss all of them, in depth, while simultaneously not grasping them on the abstract or thematic level. It's all hours and hours of arguments about warp technology or elven history.

    I wanted to say something about this in the other threads, because I thought people were kind of circling around an important concept without approaching it. The general public, as several people have already noted, like science fiction and fantasy just fine, so it's not the science fiction or fantasy elements in RPGs that they consider "geeky". It's something else. It's the way "roleplaying geeks" latch onto the superficial details and pile one on top of the other... and it's the way they take these details from everything they have ever enjoyed and bundle them together. So yeah, both lasersharking and spell and equipment lists are symptoms of this kind of geekiness. Ordinary people who might like RPGs don't play them because they would have to become just as obsessively well-versed in those subjects as the geeks they would play with.

    As a side note, this is what I took Matt Snyder's rant about coolness to mean, too. Not that people actually think they will be cool if they buy game X, but that they will be ostracized unless they like all the canonical "cool things". That may be a complete misinterpretation on my part, but at least it explained to me his seemingly random mention of Jungle Speed -- because, yeah, I've seen people bring up Jungle Speed in threads that had nothing to do with that game, with a feeling of "I am mentioning Jungle Speed here not because it's relevant to the topic, but because I think it's cool, and I want you to think it's cool, too. And I will shun you unless you like it." I see a similar thing with mentions of Firefly, anime, and numerous other things, any one of which would seem perfectly normal in threads where they were relevant, but which get dragged into any discussion where "coolness" is somehow in question.

    I'm not immune. I deliberately avoid posting very much, because I don't want to unknowingly drift into monologues about my own "cool obsessions". And I'm proceeding very carefully with my game designs, because I know I may unknowingly pile on details in my games to make them "awesome". But the paradox of awesomeness is: jamming together as many different things as possible into your game does not actually broaden its appeal, it narrows it. So, proceed with caution. You can create monstrous chimera, but think of them as guilty pleasures. Don't be surprised when you attract only a small audience.

    Eric, I like this thread. I hope that you do get something new out of re-reading, because I think so far you're pretty reasonably representing what I was getting at.

    You'll note in those threads that I soon avoided using the word geek and started using the term "sub-culture" and specifically meant it as an indicator of what we generally understand (at least I do) as "gamers" and gamer culture. To spell it out: I do not identify with "gamers." I do not have an interest in the sub-culture, the in-jokes, the auto-reactions about what's cool. Generally, I find I'm really less and less excited about things that I recognize gamers getting very excited about. I probably did all that at one time. I no longer do. I don't find, for example, the elf tatoo thing appealing at all. It does nothing for me either way. I don't think collecting dice is worth my time. I'm never excited about the news of any of my formerly favorite RPGs being re-released in new edition (or whatever Teh Awesome release). And so on.

    These things are not having outgrown gaming in any kind of "when you you stop playing with toys" kind of way. These are, merely, changing tastes over years of time. The "ugly" side is that this change is also having observed repeated dysfuntional behaviors among game groups that I find needless and wasteful. I do not believe any and all members of the sub-culture have such dysfunctions. Simultaneously, I find these to be common enough that the problems are too widespread to maintain my interest.

    Meanwhile, I maintain an interest in the actual activity of role-playing games, of playing the games themselves. I design them. I enjoy playing them. I enjoy playing them with people who eagerly communicate not only about the game itself but about their social lives. I no longer wish to play with people because they are gamers. I wish to play with people because they are interesting people who demonstrate two things: A common interest in a particular game and the ability to share their personality and social events with me in a happy, healthy way. Not flawlessly, not in touchy-feely group therapy, but just good neighborly common sense.

    I have found over the last 3-4 years that, too often, members who express a pretty strong affinity for the gaming sub-culture do not meet one of two things I just explained. This, I hasten to add, is not universal. I have many happy, healthy friends who do indeed identify themselves eagerly as members of the sub-community (i.e. who let their geek flag fly). I enjoy playing games with them, despite no longer having any desire to join the hobby on their level of enthusiasm.

    And, so, that's that. I hope to be able to find, out there, additional people who share similar outlooks to mine. Right now, I feel very much on the border between cultures, despite that being a really silly, inocuous thing (it's nothing like, as John Kim expressed, being ignored because of one's race). Because I do have a handful of individuals whom I identify as in a like position to my own, and who have expressed sympathy with my views, particularly in the last week, I remain hopeful about it.

    Not surprisingly, perhaps, I share Matt's sentiments down to the letter.

    When I played AD&D in high school I was also collecting comic books and read SF (although, ultimately, I didn't care for the SF much!)

    Was I a geek? Sure. Was I socially awkward? You betcha!

    But... I had no idea there was such a thing as a geek, or I was part of a group of people "like me." I didn't feel like people were hating me. I did feel I was "wrong" somehow -- but that was just depression talking -- not other people.

    Because the outside world kind of liked me.

    Half the players in my AD&D group were women. Three were cheerleaders. We didn't play down in the basement, but at my parent's dining room table -- with plenty of snacks. We had a fun, social time. Gaming was part of going to movies, debating in the Model Congress, acting in plays, writing articles for the paper... and so on. Not everyone did all these things. RPGs were just one point of intersection for several of us.

    Years later, back home for Christmas, a mother of one of my classmates referred to me as a "Big Man on Campus back in the high school days." Damn, I wished I known that. All I could think back in high school was, "Man, I'm never going to get enough sex."

    So, was I geek? Sure. Were there people hating me for it? Maybe -- but I never noticed them. I was just too busy trying to keep my own demons and depression at bay and having fun with the things that gave me happiness. So, this whole, "Those mundane people who have no imaginations unlike us," thing was a concept that never occurred to me. I had no interaction with the football players. They did what they did. I was afraid of sports. Whatever.

    I bring all this up to get to this:

    I go to college. I have no contacts, so I meet up with the folks at the RPG club.

    I enter a new world. A whole vocabulary of inside jokes. Snippets of lines for "Holy Grail" out of context but always getting a laugh. Ten hour game sessions on Sundays. Insular and an almost siege-like sensibility against the rest of the campus socially. I feel weird with them. I do not fit in. I try for years... But I don't.

    I stop gaming. Because if that's what I have to be to game, I don't want it.

    And here's my specific addition to Matt's post...

    RPGs for me are not part of the "I'm imaginative / you're not tradition" or "I do this so you mock me tradition" or the "delight in the fringes of pop culture" tradition.

    When I said in High School to some friends, "Hey, I got this game you guys might like," I was excited because we were all into literature and art. And that's what RPGs are to me. They are an extension of storytelling and communal acts of creativity stretching back to the Greek Tragedies I was reading about in High School. I read the Hobbit after I started playing. I didn't read The Lord of the Rings until four years ago. What RPGs meant to me was the activity itself -- and the exploration of how we get together to make things up. It was just another form of stortelling -- just as a play, or a novel, or a movie all tell stories, but all in different ways.

    The minatures were a chance to paint. The handouts were a chance at design. The plots and improv of the characters all mattered in that they gave the players a chance to make hard choices for their characters. The choices of imagery -- the fantastical and larger than life -- all tapped my love of old fashioned stories of adventure and the supernatural. My source material were the Greek tragedies, the Homeric epics, Shakespeare, Edgar Allen Poe, Robert Louise Stevenson, Grimm's Fairy Tales... comic books like Tomb of Dracula, Dr. Strange, the utter weirdness of Howard the Duck and Marvel's terrific Conan adaptations.... Michael Moorcocks Swords Trilogy, Jules Verne, Robert E. Howard, H.G. Wells... the books and plays I was reading in high school and on my own that were part of the shared language of my social group.

    But not Fantasy novels published by Tor. Not because I looked down on those novels -- I just never read them.

    When I got to college all that got swept away and I found myself with a group of people who didn't make sense to me. We didn't share a common background of fiction. And how they played was very different. It was about the shared, secret language of RPGs, fantasy books. And socially it was all about "We're these people, and we're not like those people."

    Was I trying to be cool? Did I think I was cool? No, and no. I never thought in those terms. (And I'm sort of surprised to see that issue still on the table for some people around here.)

    Whatever happened to me growing up, whatever it was that turned me on -- or whatever it was that didn't turn me -- all conspired to make me not share a lot with the people who play RPGs and identify themselves as "gamers." I'm not saying better than. I'm saying not part of.

    I see no particular attachment of RPGs to geek culture. I can seperate them quite easily. I like the over-the-top storytelling technciques of Fantasy and SF -- where you turn the thematic dial up to 11 and have the hearts of the characters revealed in the monsters and machines of the world around them. But, again, that to me isn't geek. It's a kind of tradition of storytelling that goes back a few thousand years -- and that's what I thought RPGs were about.

    I could happily play an RPG with a bunch of football players. And I know they'd have a good time.

    Christopher
