\n I need an interesting date for an apocalypse to happen. It's got to be in the 1930s.

If we put the apocalypse after March 1st, 1936, then we've got the Hoover Dam. That's a wonderful thing, because it's one of the few power-generators that will keep running after an apocalypse. So we can have Las Vegas in the middle of a postapocalyptic wasteland, fully lit.

If we put it before December 11th, 1936, then King Edward VIII never abdicates. This is an interesting thing in the United Kingdom: Edward VIII was only king for a year, before abdicating to marry an American woman. So, if we time it right, he stays king throughout the postapocalyptic game.

What other interesting dates have we got? What I'm really looking for an equivalent to that Edward VIII thing, but for the United States. When can I end the world to make things interesting? Can we get a President Landon or something equally fun?

Graham

    \n Ooh! Check this out: Eleanor Roosevelt's "My Day" newspaper columns.\n
    Eleanor Roosevelt, January 15 (appeared as a January 16 column)What is it about going to a play or a concert, if you have a cough, which always bring on a tickling in your throat and makes you cough five times as badly as you have at anytime during the preceeding hours? This morning I went to Mrs. Townsend's Wednesday morning musical at which Mr. Schelling and his string quartet played. My guests were two ladies from the Diplomatic Corps, and two wives of members of the Cabinet.

    We sat down and I had almost forgotten that there was such a thing as a cough. With the opening bars of the music, the wife of the Mexican Ambassador began to cough and tried to stifle it and then she and I kept up a duet. We were horribly embarrassed, and I became acutely anxious between the gasps of the annoyed glances from neighboring boxes. I found myself choking as children do with whooping cough in my efforts to keep quiet. I should have left the hall, but I knew that would probably create a sensation. Mrs. Hull handed me a little box of lozenges and whispered that she always had them with her because they were made in Nashville, Tennessee! Madame de Castillo Najera and I each took one and gradually our coughs ceased. All was quiet during the entr'acte. I talked easily and had no desire to cough. The lights went out, the music began and with it that everlasting tickle.

    I do enjoy Mr. Schelling's playing, but I never was more glad to get out of a concert. Madame de Castillo Najera and I agreed that it was probably largely nervousness and from the point of view of diplomatic understanding, I think we achieved a bond in discomfort which will always make us feel more at home together. But even to achieve this, I would not like to go through this morning again.
    E.R.
    TMsd 15 January 1936, AERP, FDRL

    \n OK, regarding the Texas President, have you read Lucifer's Hammer? The die-back isn't 99.9% in that novel; but the "President" and "government" in (IIRC) Denver are lucky if they can transmit on radio with enough power to be heard in California. BUT, they ARE the grain of sand around which the pearl of a recovered government could grow. Many, many years after the fall and consolidation.

    During the fall, the President's just a guy with a piece of paper--and if I'm the guy with a loaded gun, *I'm* the President in *that* particular room, at *that* particular time. The authority of a person starts and stops with their capacity to enforce their will. Presidents and Kings have police and armies; post-apoc, they got little more than a nice suit.

    -----
    BUT, even more interesting revelation... it would seem that we don't have a "canonical cause" for this apoc? Am I sniffing something to do with situation generation? If so... GOD, there's a TON of ripple-effect things to consider.
    * An evenly distributed die-off means, as others have said, that nothing which requires major infrastructure continues to operate. Power generation, whole cities, plumbing, etc.
    * A population-density-related die-off could leave whole towns (and their coal plants and sewage treatment facilities) untouched.

    * A die-off with a specific inception point will cause migration and border conflicts and all sorts of shit to do with mobility and escape.
    * A die-off that's evenly or randomly distributed will cause holing up and isolation (if the cause is something that can even be avoided! Speaking of which...).

    * A non-sentient source of the apoc causes a certain "feel" to the apoc--random, human-induced maybe and thus a source of guilt, but nevertheless capricious and ignorant of suffering. Man v. Nature, in a nutshell.
    * A sentient agent gives a-whole-nother feel--predation, "evil" in a classic monomaniacal sense, enslavement, harvesting, worse. It could still be "capricious" as in unfeeling or remorseless. But, generally, it's an enemy at which characters can be aimed and it can be a point against which survivors rally. Man v Man (or v Himself).

    So I guess I have a new follow-up: Are we trying to help you find "setting nuggets" for a prescribed apocalypse? That's not too tough to deduce, and it basically makes a laundry list.
    Or are we trying to provide points of divergence which can be considered in the light of ANY apocalypse? THAT's going to be tougher, as it requires more of a consideration of cause and vector and speed of the die-off. It might just be too much to systematize (or it might be something that can just be rolled off a gaggle of charts with a hand wave at "logic" or "historical fidelity"?).

    \nFunnily enough, the bulk of WWII weapons used ammunition that was designed for WWI or earlier. So the Germans have their famous MG 34 firing a round first produced in 1888 (but not their infamous MG 42 if the world ends in the 30s). Americans have their 30-06 from 1906 with the Browning machine guns (1917, 1919) and maybe even the M1 Garand (introduced in 1936). The British had their .303 ammo from 1889 and the Russians were using the 7.62x54mmR in rifles and machine guns since 1891. It wasn't the ammo that changed in WWII, it was the weapons that used them.

    Posted By: Quintin StoneIt wasn't the ammo that changed in WWII, it was the weapons that used them.
    I said "maybe WWI stuff" to mean "maybe there would be enough surplus of WWI ammo at this time to find WWI ammo." The "maybe" is there because I'd think the stockpile would be small, a decade after the war ended and mere years after The Great Depression. But, hell, I dunno--maybe the Armories of the US were chockablock with Browning and M1 ammo in the mid-1930s?

    Either way, there's no way to speculate without more of an understanding of the pace, vector, and scale of the apoc emergence and spread. Not to mention its cause. Not to mention how long it's been since the apoc when the game begins (one day is a LOT different from one year). From what we know at the moment, the apoc could have left behind anything from The Road to Road Warrior. It being the 1930s just puts an even more fragile economy and society in the gun sight of the apoc.

    (Which make me wonder: what level of technology becomes immune to planetary apocalypse? But that... is another thread.)
