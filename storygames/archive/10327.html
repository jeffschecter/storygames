\n Last night we all showed up to game night, ready to continue our rockin' Lady Blackbird mini-campaign, and found out we had no dice and exactly one pencil to share among the six of us. So we made a laser out of a pig's cornea, some copper wire and an old cactus!

Actually we did some thinking, and realized that the resolution in Lady Blackbird is, by die, completely binary. Greg rounded up some pennies and we were off to the races. We had to flip a lot of pennies, so we shook them up and tossed them in a large bowl. It worked great and our evening was saved!

Have you ever adapted to an unexpected gaming circumstance and carried on in the face of adversity?

    \n I've played a dozen sessions of various games where we drew lots because we didn't have dice handy. Perhaps the most hackish was the time in college where we raided the (closed) cafeteria for ten plastic spoons, the skinniest of our number reaching through the grate over the entrance. We engraved numbers on the ill-gotten utensils with keys and played Call of Cthulhu at midnight.

    You might ask, why not just have each player tear a corner off his charsheet to make a paper lot? Or why not just walk back to someone's place and grab some dice? And in truth, I don't know. It seemed like a good idea at the time.

    \n Once, long ago, I was at a camp, about to run D&D... and discovered I had no twenty-sided dice.

    \n I feel like I've told this story a bunch here, but when I was a kid I'd heard of D&D but wasn't allowed to own the books (there's devils in'em) so I made up my own game that involved flipping pennies for success or failure, and we took one of the tablets of paper that we got for classwork and filled it with maps of the different places the characters went to.

    So that was me gaming in the absence of having any game stuff at all, just an idea of what gaming was supposed to be like.

    \n I have a VERY vague recollection of flipping through a book quickly to randomize 1-10 (or, rather, 0-9). I think we just ignored values that were out of range (e.g. flipping a d6 would ignore values 7-0). Can't recall how we did d20--probably two flips (odd/even for the tens value [0 or 1], then the 0-9).

    Ditto using the tenths/hundredths of a second value on a stopwatch or watch timer function.

    A favorite technique (albeit a prepared one) of mine is to put character sheets into sheet protectors and use wet-erase markers for tracking highly variable values (HP, Artha, Stun, whatever). Not technically MacGuyvering, unless you do it with cellophane or by sticking your sheet under a glass tabletop (hey...!).

    Posted By: Bret GillanSo that was me gaming in the absence of having any game stuff at all, just an idea of what gaming was supposed to be like.
    \nMy first gaming experience was similar. My friend Matt and I had no actual games; all our information about how RPGs worked came from a) the Saturday morning D&D cartoon, b) our friend Mike's older brother's AD&D books, glimped only rarely at the school bus stop, and c) the novel Bimbos of the Death Sun, which we both read repeatedly (and which presented a truly weird version of gaming).

    So we ran each other and our younger brothers through various "adventures", with all the game mechanics being invented on the spot, and mostly of the "If you roll a 5 or a 6, you kill him!" variety.

    That was some serious MacGyvering.
