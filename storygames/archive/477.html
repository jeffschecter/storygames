So, I've been pondering about TonyLB's "Are you Muy Macho?" and also by peaseblossom's "A Very Special Gender and Gaming Conversation". Anyhow, I thought I'd bring up a companion credo here. This is emphatically not the opposite of being macho, but rather an orthogonal category. Anyhow, here goes:

The Queer Credo

Being queer means being out. You chat with your friends and co-workers about your gaming; you read your game books openly at cafes; you invite people who aren't "gamers" to your games. You're not afraid to tell your friends that you played a manly barbarian last night (if you're female) or an elven princess (if you're male).

Being queer does not mean being butch, or being girly, or any other affect. You can be macho and queer, or femmy and queer, or geeky and queer, or sporty and queer, or any other affect. You can wear any other labels and still be queer, because those labels are not who you are.

Being queer means experimenting. You'll try new things free from preconceptions. You'll try freeform play, live-action, mainstream tabletop, and indie story games. You'll come to each of them with an open mind, and see what it does for you.

Being queer means expressing your feelings. You'll tell your fellow players what you really like and not be ashamed. Real people like different things, but queer people embrace their differences. If you like playing a manly and violent barbarian with bulging pecs and exposed nipples, you do so without people making negative judgements about you.

Being queer means saying no. If you're not comfortable with something, you'll say so immediately and without fear. You don't go along with something just because that's what your friends are doing. By doing this, your fellow players are more comfortable, knowing that you will make your boundaries known.

Being queer means not pidgeonholing. You don't play to win, or to immerse, or to imitate a genre, or to create a story. You play for play. You follow your feelings and see what you like, rather than conforming to a predefined concept.



So that's my (highly derivative) shot at a credo. All props to TonyLB for the style, of course. Comments welcome, as well as answering the question.

    I'm so queer that I play RPGs with my mother in law. I'm so queer that I've shown school principals and the parents of my students the books I've written. (Well, not the ones with the dancing demon sex -- but the nice ones.)

    I'm so queer that when I go to a party with cool arty folks they will say to each other, "That's Brand -- he's the one that writes those theatre game books" and they will think it is cool, because I am obviously not ashamed of it.

    I'm so queer that I've done everything from improv GMless LARPS to D&D as hack-n-slash boardgame. I'm so queer that when people told me that they had more fun playing with dice, I tried playing with dice, and when they told me they had more fun playing without dice, I tried playing without dice. At this point it's hard for most of my RPGing friends to name a game I've not played at least once.

    I'm so queer that people at the table often tell me "you keep saying things that I cannot unhear" -- but they do it laughing because the truth is they have accepted it about me. I'm so queer that when my best friend or worst friend (I don't game with enemies, I guess) pisses me off or crosses a line then we will have a talk about it -- to the point that it's become a running joke in my group.

    However, I am not so queer that I don't know what I like in an RPG. I do play for specific things much of the time. Not because I fear the other things, or don't see the value in them -- but because they are not the thing that I like most. My #4 and #5 trump my #6, and so while I won't pigeonhole anyone else, I will not play a game that is not the game I want to play just to maintain my queer image in my own (or anyone else's) eyes. I've played around a lot, and while I still like to do different things from time to time, I'm also queer enough to know that not every kink works for me.

    Oh, but I'm queer enough that I also don't judge others fun because it isn't the same kind that I like. Most of the time. No really. I know I come off as non-queer in that regard online all the time, but it's just my ass-voice, and not how I really feel.

    ---

    Also, I like the word queer rather than the more often used "closeted" or "out" or "gay" because queer (in my experience of the word) has a much more open and flexible rhetorical position in the language.
