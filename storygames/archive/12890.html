Conflict resolution without disturbing flow

\n
\n
\n
\n\nRobert_Bohl \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2010 \n edited August 2010 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n The main thing I cherish about conflict resolution is that the intent of the person who's rolling is preserved. If you want something, you're rolling for what you want, not what you do.

The problem is that often, pausing to set up stakes or whatever method you use to get to conflict resolution can a) result in pre-play and b) more substantively, blow the fictional flow of the game, where you step out and have a conversation about out-of-character things, then go back into it.

So here's what I'm thinking as a system to deal with that.

Throughout your and others' scenes, you're taking notes and updating a limited list of goals. If your goal changes, you just write the newest version of the goal. These x goals (let's say 4) would need to be thematically tied in to the game in categories (for example, in a werewolf game, you might have a goal for prey, a goal for sex, a goal for self-control, goal for maintaining relationships, etc.).

When it comes time for conflict*, you've got these notes, maybe on 4 note cards, and when you have your crucial resolution moment (I'm thinking cards right now), you play the resources you have on the card you want. Therefore, you're making your goal clear without breaking flow.

Now if you have a bunch of little handwritten notes, it might be difficult to read and it might be hard to take notes and still pay attention to scenes. Instead, I'm thinking I might want to have these cards preprinted, with fill-in-bubble or -text-field choices, maybe at multiple levels. For example: Sex goal > to have sex with someone > Name. Maybe there are multiple choices on the "sex goals" card.

Of course, this subsystem isn't complete without figuring out resolution, the details of the goals more firmly, or a thematic notion to pin this to. But I think it's a strong idea.

What do you think of this system? How do you route around the flow-breaking tendencies of many conflict resolution games?

* It occurs to me that it might be cool to have separate systems for conflicts you initiate, and conflicts that arise out of a "fuck no" situation in response to your actions, and a system for you to respond to both of these conditions from others.

    \n Rob,

    Okay, I'll answer this way. One way is to do two things (1) scale conflicts down to the action level (not the same as a task) and (2) demand more description rather than explanation or justification.

    "I pick him up by the lapels and slam him against the wall shouting in his face, 'Tell me where the girl is!'" It's pretty clear what the intent is.

    Even "I stare coldly in his eyes until he's quivering with fear." is pretty clear.

    "I ram by elbow into him and flip him over so he lands prone on his back!" Still pretty clear what the intent is.

    Basically when the action itself includes the intended result you don't need to quibble about stakes. The point is to understand that these declarations are *provisional* and pending the the actual result of the procedures and mechanics.

    Some people are hung up on "If someone said it then it must have actually happened and there's nothing to be done about it." So to prevent this people provide incomplete descriptions of their action, "I ram him with my elbow." They feel like they need to know what the mechanical outcome is before they can complete the thought.

    Jesse

    \n Rob, when you played with the moves in AW, did the MC let you narrate your own success, or did he narrate it for you?

    Here's a mechanic idea. I was inspired by the moves in AW where you get to hold some points and then spend them during a conversation with someone in order to get them to do what you wanted or find out something about them that you wanted to know, such as "is he telling the truth?" I wondered if I could apply that to something more physical too, so I made up this mechanic with fudge dice, but you could totally change it around to use any sort of dice or card pool, where a certain result can represent a kind of currency for you to act, another result is currency for your opponent to act, and another is a currency to add some story element you can use later. You choose the type of move you're making, which defines what sorts of outcomes you can narrate, and then for every point you get, you can narrate the actual outcome.

    So in this example, rolling 3 fudge dice for a fight move, every + is something you can do with the story elements that are already established, while every - you roll is something the MC can do with the same story elements. The blanks (represented as = here) are points either the player or the MC can use to establish a new story element, but they have to take turns with this -- you can't use a blank and a + one after the other, unless the opponent has no other resources to use.

    The idea is that when you choose the move "fight," you set the stakes as anything a fight would normally encompass, and then you roll the dice the same way you would in D&D, except that instead of saying "I attack... and I hit!" you state the general action, roll the dice, and then narrate the specifics, like this:

    ---

    PC: I attack... and....
    Fight roll 1: + = -
    PC: I slash with my sword, slashing at the goblin from above (+).
    MC: The goblin blocks it with his short sword, and grimaces at you (-).
    PC: I look behind the goblin and see an open door behind the goblin, maybe something I can push him into next time (=).

    Fight roll 2: - - =
    PC: Oh no, um... Okay, I notice the room behind the door is filled with dangerous animals in cages (=).
    MC: Okay, the goblin takes a mighty swing and hits you for lots of damage. You're hurting now (- - both together, an attack and then extra damage).
    PC: I don't give up though!

    Fight roll 3: - + +
    PC: Good, I attack the goblin again, with a hard swing to his left (+).
    MC: The goblin snickers and deflects the attack (-).
    PC: I kick the goblin and knock him into the room, where the animals and their roaring are a huge distraction for him (+ gives the player a bonus die on the next roll, like you have in Shadows of Yesterday).

    Fight roll 4: + = -
    PC: I attack the goblin again with my sword while he's distracted (+).
    MC: Okay, you hit him (2 damage) and he's out. But as he's hit, he stumbles back and hits a cage door hard, grasping at it and pulling at the latch with his dying breath (= the MC can use this blank too. Now the PC doesn't have any other goods or blanks to use, so the MC goes again).
    MC: The strange dinosaur-like thing with talons and a sword like beak stirs, opens the cage door, and looks at you, ready to pounce. Now you're distracted! (- the player gets 1 penalty die on the next roll. Here the MC could theoretically do damage with the animal jumping out right away and attacking, but he prefers to strike some fear into his player first, and give him the choice of what to do. His job isn't about ripping the PC to shreds after all, but giving him interesting choices).
    PC: Yeouch! I'm in no shape to handle that. I'm out of here.

    ---

    In a conflict about something else, you just choose the type of move you want to do, such as "argument" or "interrogation" or whatever, as specific or as general as you please. You can only narrate elements that pertain to this move, and only within certain parameters defined by the move, perhaps one attack per roll, one bonus, one extra damage, and so on.

    \nYeah, I can't really figure out why, but I find staking narration to be too dry. And I do like the fictive-directional-thrust of the goal cards system, where there are different categories of things the system says you must set goals for. I like how it requires that you keep attention on other players' scenes as they're playing.

    See, goal cards might also address some problems I have with spotlight. Spotlight is mega-important to me. I desperately need to design to fix idiots, like me, who can't let go of spotlight. Besides, (gently*) forcing spotlight on people who don't think they're creative is a great way to show them that they're creative.

    I like "party" style RPGs because it's easier for me to apportion spotlight that way. If everyone in the fiction is together, I know very well how to design to make sure no one's sitting around, bored. The problem with party RPGs is that keeping the party together can get contrived and annoying.

    While many indie games address the contrived and annoying problem easily by splitting people off into their own scenes, I want something that will make sure that people are as consistently involved in other people's scenes as they are in their own. This is probably going to be impossible to do perfectly.

    The system I've outlined above helps to address part of this. As you take notes (or fill in boxes and change a text field or three), the way the note-taking system is structured would (hopefully) keep your mind on things like what this means for the world, for your character, for important relationships, etc. For whatever the categories say the game cares about.

    * By "gently," I mean a) without being hostile or threatening and b) with creative constraint that is easy and fun and gamey and teaches you (without being didactic about it) that creativity is fueled by these constraints.

    \n It sounds to me that for the purposes of this thread, Rob, we're talking about...

    "Flow" = the steady stream of in-character, in-fiction descriptions of events

    "Conflict resolution" = ensuring the player's creative intent is addressable by some procedure, everyone involved agrees the intent is possible with the boundaries of their shared fiction leading up to resolution, and the intent is resolveable by some means outside the authority of a single player.

    So some thoughts, assuming I'm basically on-track with what we're talking about.

    * Is there a way to reframe a player's creative intent into the in-fiction descriptive "flow"? Is this just an illusion of high-level system mastery? Ritual phrases might maybe do this but...they're ritualistic, which to me is a rather out-of-fiction type of procedure. The players are playing the authors of the tale, not the characters within the tale.

    * Maybe every call to action is naturally comprised of two things: what you want to do and how you want to do it (intent/task per BW, sort of). And maybe the system gives you a budget with which to determine how much of each half you "win". Example: I slam Ruglok to the ground (how) to show the clan that I'm in charge (why). You engage the system, and the system gives you enough currency to either take control of the why or the how, but not both. Or on a very strong success you get both. Whichever part you don't win passes to another player at the table -- i.e. the clan knows I'm in charge; Rob, how did that come to pass? Or vice versa! I've slammed Ruglok to the ground; Rob, how does the clan react to that? In both cases, the part you *don't* win becomes this new point of tension in the fiction, which can lead to new resolution opportunities. The player tasked with resolving the part you didn't win is encouraged to add a detail (yes but or yes and or whatever) that adds a new twist, thereby continuing the fiction.

    Obviously super-duper rough idea. Hopefully the skeleton is clear enough.

    Anyway, just something off the top of my head.

I mean, in school, they teach us the first thing you do when you write an essay is to tell everyone what you're about to tell them, as a kind of introduction, to give them the expectations they need to know what they're about to read, then go through your supporting arguments step-by-step.
