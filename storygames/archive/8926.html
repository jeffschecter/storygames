Posted By: Jonathan M
It's difficult for me to look at the universal desire of gamers to play not only heroes but supernaturally powerful heroes who go about murdering people and imposing their will on the world by force as anything other than an expression of some kind of inferiority complex or oedipal power fantasy. As Deleuze and Guattari once suggested, everyone wants to be a fascist because everyone wants to fuck their mother.

I'd say that the indie scene was as much a part of that mindset as mainstream gaming.
\nI think it may have been Judd who, in the tone discussion for Flaming Taft, said, 'I don't want to be in another game where I fuck my mother.' And one of my wife's professors once said to her that 'repression is good - otherwise we'd all be fucking our grandmothers.' (I paraphrase in both cases).

That said, I also don't see as there's anything particularly wrong with the occasional engagement with your fantasies, power or otherwise. Isn't it the hallmark of liberalism to be able to do so? It seems perculiarly catholic to conflate the deed and the thought.

I'm pretty happy to accept that much mainstream gaming is associated with power fantasies (although probably not Call of Cthulhu much at all or Traveller quite so much) but it seems to me that one of the strengths of Indie gaming is the variety of experience it produces. Steal Away Jordan, Dogs in the Vineyard, Grey Ranks and others give rise to much more complex character motivations

But is it ever possible to play Heroic Fantasy without it being a fantasy of murderous heroism?

    \n Who knows? Seriously, this is one of those arguments that's consumed literary criticism for decades without being settled, and we won't settle it here. Is heroic narrative inherently fascistic? Maybe.

    Fro my own purposes, though, I'll say that I don't find it necessarily so - at least not in a fatal way. The appeal of the heroic fantasy is precisely its unreality: because we live in a world in which good and evil are not simple binaries, and violence rarely (if ever) solves more problems than it creates, where the individual is usually powerless to effect meaningful change... the fantasy of a world in which this is not so is profoundly consolatory. Mistaking that fantasy for a representation of reality - or of how things "ought" to be - is probably a pretty bad idea, but I don't see the enjoyment of the fantasy as problematic. I fantasize about plenty of things that would be really bad ideas in the real world.

    \n There is a weird and somewhat distressing way of coming at this, at least on the thread that presumably led to this one.

    D&D is about killing monsters of various sorts, who are clearly presented (in most games) as being obstacles to overcome in a game, using the resources of your dude. So someone does this, and then reads on the internets that they are a facist and a racist engaging in psychotic power fantasties. This upsets people, as it rightly should and leads to conflict instead of discussion.

    It's a good thing to discuss the sociopolitics / power dynamics of our games, like D&D. And you can clearly identify problematic themes in these games. But the responsible way to discuss this is to focus on the implications and unacknowledged subtext. So it's "Hey, we should talk about what this means" as opposed to "You are a sick person, and I am not because I don't play D&D that way".

    Posted By: GB SteveBut is it ever possible to play Heroic Fantasy without it being a fantasy of murderous heroism?
    Consider movies, television, fantasy novels, and so on. Start counting how many badguys are overcome by the goodguys taking pointy things and stabbing the badguys until they're dead. You may be surprised by how rare that "solution" actually is. It is far more likely that badguys are run off, tricked into the jaws of their own monsters, redeemed by seeing the light, or trapped within the mystic crystal prison. Or my personal favorite, fall to their deaths because while dangling from the parapets, they refused to take the goodguy's hand and try instead for one last stab. It's almost distressingly rare that the goodguy actually kills the badguy.

    Except in RPGs. I suspect this is for two reasons.

    The first is that most RPGs give the PCs a hammer, and then every problem looks like a nail. If the only mechanical option I have for solving problems is by killing the people behind the problems, well then, I'm going to be doing a lot of killing, aren't I?

    The other reason is less of a design flaw and actually somewhat positive: RPGs are more honest than mass media. RPGs allow their protagonists to judge the antagonists and act accordingly. Mass media does not allow its protagonists to do anything objectionable, which includes righteous execution of evil-doers, because, you know, killing is always reprehensible*. By contrast, your game of Dogs or whatever is for you and your table's entertainment, so nobody feels the need to water down what happens for the benefit of children who might sneak into the movie theater. This results in, I believe, a far more genuine and honest address of theme.

    Now then, for my money, you get the best results when you fix Reason One while retaining Reason Two. As long as there are, generally speaking, viable alternatives to violence while there is still the possibility of violence being used, you end up with a result where, when violence is used, it creates a powerful statement. You step away from "all badguys must die" and get to the golden stuff: "this guy was so bad he needed to die," "this protagonist was too filled with rage to use a non-violent solution," "there wasn't time to solve this other than messily," and so on.

    You also get repercussions and consequences of those statements \u2014 contrast with a game where violence is the only possible solution, where those "consequences" are pale, limp, and pathetic baiting on the part of the GM. There, it's not a consequence of what you chose to do, it's a consequence of playing the game. How much cooler is it when you face a consequence of your choice, rather than a consequence of participation?
