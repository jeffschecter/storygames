    \n As a complete product, I feel its unfinished (needed another editing pass, a bit more commentary/procedures on how to structure sessions), a lot of my complaints were resolved in the sister game Blood & Honor (samurai tragedy), however that still suffers from needing an editing pass as well. The Domain rules especially need fine attention, since...well, to run a campaign you must use those rules to provide structure. I'm guessing Wick was going for a Pendragon-ish vibe, but I feel the Domain rules need tightened up a bit.

    The games yearns for a tighter structure, and some sort of relationship map or sorcerer-style back sheet. A list of important NPCs (player-defined of course) and such, unless you are playing a pure character on character conflict, then just mediate the conflicts and make interesting connections.

    I ran a short game of Blood & Honor, and to make it cool. I had basically had to keep a careful eye on the domain rules, figure out implicit characters/conflict, and also remember that as narrator, I can just say things that are true. So the beginning of each session, I would do a preamble of events and such.

    \n Houses of the Blooded is probably the most quintessentially John Wick game he's ever written. Everything everyone has ever loved about his past books or his writing style in general is in it, but everything everyone could consider a flaw is in there too. In my opinion John Wick is a good idea man but very weak on execution; his games constantly make promises that they are ill-equipped to deliver.

    My group played HotB for about a year, and we unanimously agreed the game is basically a first draft. It needed much more editing to fix its very poor organization and bring in all the information for any given topic into the same section instead of scattering them around the book, both rules AND fluff. This might have also made him fix how much of it directly contradicts itself but maybe not, because sometimes HotB contradicts itself within the same page.

    I think it also needed a lot more playtesting, because we were encountering rules giving weird results or flat-out not working as described from our first session; off the top of my head, the aging rules that "bring [PCs] closer to the inevitable doom of Solace. Unlike other games\u2014where the characters are perpetually stuck at the vague age of 'about 25,' never growing older, the passage of time never ravaging their youth" completely fail to make creeping age a meaningful factor because the ven lifespan is 240 "years", PCs start around 20 and age 1d6 "years" every four in-game seasons... each of which spans 1-3 game sessions.

    HotB is attractive and eye-catching because it just has so much stuff in it, but when my group tried to actively interface with that stuff on its own merits we found the the rules clumsy and the book frustrating to reference. I would call it "unfinished" without any hesitation, because the game just doesn't seem to work properly out of the box. The basic "invoke aspects, wager dice, and establish facts" engine is brilliant (if a little challenging to GM), but almost everything else in there is more trouble than it's worth and by game's end we'd stopped using any of it. \n

    \n The game's main strength is that the only thing that matters is the tone.

    You all get together, around the table, and play this game. And the game has a specific agenda. And so long as you appear to gel with the agenda, you get Style points and everyone has a good time. And when someone steps out of line, they lose Style points and we are all reminded how not to act.

    The game's main weakness is that the only thing that matters is the tone.

    There's really a lot of fiction, and a lot of "conversational style" rules in the book. And once you read and absorb it all you get the tone that it's trying to enforce. But only as a whole. The individual facts are mutable against the tone. And tone is difficult to convey.

    The result:

    Everyone needs to be distant enough that they don't offend the "tone" set up and enforced by the GM (and the rules at large.) But everyone needs to be proactive enough to drive things forward. This involves players deliberately sabotaging their characters by simply acting at all. It's poetically tragically wonderful, in a way, but intensely frustrating if you relate too heavily to your characters. Throw them in the grinder and let them get destroyed, or don't play this game.

    It's how the Ven live their lives. Cheat, conquer, and don't get caught.

    \nMy problem is that the mechanics look interesting, but just don't hold up during long-term play, the kind of play they are explicitly intended for and supposed to support.
