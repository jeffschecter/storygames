    \n This has been an amazing decade for movies, up there with the 70s and 40s in terms of pure excellence. Most of us would find this exercise extremely difficult at ten movies and those listed might easily change from day to day. Naming only three seems a lot like Sparta.

    I'll focus on movies some might not have caught, going with Brick, Lost in Translation, and Before Sunset. My paragon roleplaying session would have the tight plotting, razor sharp dialogue, and the Sisyphean Noir protagonist of Brick, the quiet moments, visual splendor and reflective character studies of Lost in Translation, and the romance, beautiful human frailty and realistic ambiguities of Before Sunset.

    All three of these movies have great dialogue, each for very different reasons. Dialogue is really important to me in cinema, and I get really excited when it occurs organically in role-playing, as it's particularly hard for me.

      Posted By: TeataineOh, and far from it being my favourite movie but Pirates of the Carribean is the best D&D game never played.
      \nThe PotC movies are character-driven adventure romps, a style that D&D does rather poorly, ever since AD&D 2e started to push in that direction. Early D&D was about exploring unfamiliar environments in confined space alternating with monster encounters in those environments. Later editions have de-emphasized the dungeon-crawl elements, but not the monster encounters, most of which include hordes of goons and mindless animal-like monsters. None of that is particularly prevalent in PotC, which instead displays the kind of inter-character conflict, swashbuckling action, and failure-with-a-twist that is far better served by Burning Wheel (and especially Mouse Guard). Primetime Adventures, 7th Sea, Feng Shui, Lady Blackbird, and Otherkind Dice (see Ghost/Echo) would all be better choices than D&D to run this game.

      Instead, I submit Spirited Away as the best D&D game never played. Skeptical? Compare it to the how-to-play sections in the Moldvay Basic rulebook, you will see it conforms perfectly. Also: Pan's Labyrinth. Watch those two movies before you play the Red Box, it can only make your game better.

      \n Although I loved Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind, Memento, Gladiator and pretty much anything and everything from Pixar (The Incredibles, Finding Nemo, WALL-E... great decade for that company), I'll stick to mentioning three films no one else has, as many others have done:

      * 21 Grams

      Dark, gritty, intense, well-filmed. Also, Naomi Watts and Benicio del Toro are two of my favourite actors.

      * Les Triplettes de Belleville

      Such an unusual mix of elements, and yet it seems so coherent. A lot of great art that all fits together with a touch of fantasy and a touch of reality. Also: it's not that easy to make a good movie without any dialogue.

      But my top "no one seems to know about this movie" film is:

      * Bandits

      It's not a world-shaking, paradigm-changing work of art, but it's a movie where everything is just that good. Not deep and soul-shaking, but an incredibly well put-together film, where all the details are just right. The dialogue, writing, script? Stellar. Acting? Fantastic. It's got drama, comedy, clever plot twists, and lots of memorable lines, and it's sad and funny in turns. There's something for everyone, without feeling like a stretch--a light entertaining movie that's about as good as it gets.

      You can watch that one many many times and get something out of it every time.

      As for a runner-up, I would include Russian Ark. It's an amazing piece of work--a visual trip that consists of one single uninterrupted shot, moving through rooms, crowds, events, and through time. I can't imagine coordinating something like that. And the symbolism runs deep. But you need to understand what's going on in it in order to really enjoy it: I only "got" it because my grandmother was sitting next to me as we watched it, and she could explain every little detail and every little reference that came up on screen as the film played. Otherwise, it's hardly very accessible.

      So, a heavy drama, a cartoon, a light film, and a (perhaps overly) serious art film. Just for contrast, why not?
