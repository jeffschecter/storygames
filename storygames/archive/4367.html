\n I was just doing some RPG reading. A friend dropped some old stuff he didn't want, including a box set, <a href="http://home.flash.net/~brenfrow/dd1/firstquest.htm">First Quest</a>.
\n
\nFQ is the kind of ambitious product we don't see anymore. You've got absolutely everything in there to actually DM. Screen, dice, minis, maps and <b>plenty </b>of books and booklets with rules and adventures. Then you also have the audio CD. All in all, this product has big production values. On a sheer bulk level, it beats the shit out of the attempts at beginner's boxsets WotC released for 3.x.
\n
\nI have only skimmed through but I found something interesting. After rules and everything are explained, you get to the actual four adventures. They are supposed to be played in order and the first one is <b>The Tomb of Demara</b>. They take place near a town that has (explicitly) <i>no name </i>and only three NPCs. One is a wizard who can identify your items for gold. One is a cleric who will only heal you for money or magic items and the last one is a store owner who can sell you stuff and exchange gems for gold. I'll skip all the classic advice to the DM, which include reading through the adventure, making sure he knows the rules and so on. Then we're told how the adventure starts. All the heroes are hangin' out at the Red Dragon Tavern in "the town". And then the town cryer calls for adventurers and inquiring minds are supposed to follow him to the wizard's tower. What follows will be direct quotes from the book:
\n
\n**********************************************************************************
\n<b>Ask the players:</b> "Are you going to follow the town cryer?"
\n-<b>Yes:</b> Play CD track 3, resume adventure
\n-<b>No:</b> Tell them they go back to the tavern and do nothing for several hours. Then ask them if they want to go to Nethril's Tower. Keep doing this until they realize that nothing else is going to happen until they follow the town cryer
\n***********************************************************************************
\nEDIT: There are no guidelines anywhere, no sidebars on how to handle inquisitive players. No contingency plans for the rookie DM if they refuse. No guidelines if they want to interact with the folks at the tavern, or what to do if they want to explore the town-that-has-no-name-and-even-worse-no-map.
\n
\nI must confess this has to be one of the lamest RPG setup I've ever seen. And it is scary to find this in a product aimed at beginners. I don't care if you're a simulationist, gamist, narrativist, immersionist, fabulist or occultist. There are thousands of ways to setup an adventure, each drastically different and each one better than this. It's like they put the worse option possible.
\n
\nWhat the fuck were they thinking?

    Imagine an hour later. The adventurers are in the dungeon.

    GM: "There's a door in front, do you open it?"
    Player: "Well why don't you step into my character's shoes and tell me what happens, smartass?"

    There's this way of handling characters that is simply not built in AD&D. The game is designed for players to control a character and is ill-equiped to share it.

    In short, I strongly disagree with you. Not only is this not a fine intro to roleplaying, it's possibly one of the worse thing I have EVER seen.

    Posted By: Consonant DudeWhat the fuck were they thinking?
    \nWhy didn't they just start the adventure with the start of the action? I think because we humans have a very ingrained sense of how stories are supposed to go. To whit:

    Once Upon a time there was a _____.
    And every day _____.
    Until one day _____.
    And because of that ____.
    And because of that ____.
    And because of that ____.
    Until finally _____.
    And ever since that day, _______.
    The end.

    We're dealing with the transition from "and every day," to "until one day." Graham's talking about this right now over on his blog in this entry. The designer wants to show how the characters are transitioning from "not on an adventure" to "on an adventure," and the method for this transition is the town crier. Now, multimedia authoring is hard, if for no other reason than there isn't a lot of great prior art out there to loot for ideas. There are probably better ways this could have been executed, but I think the thought process behind why the adventure even starts this way at all is understandable.
