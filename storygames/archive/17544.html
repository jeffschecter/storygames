\n Escapism is a central element to many a game, and to the game-play of many players. Ordinarily this is all about escaping the dullness of everyday life, having a fictional action-adventure.

There are so many of them, in all canals; TV, movies, games ... all over the place. The action-adventure is a dominant cultural phenomenon. And I am part of it. As a professional GM I lead rpgs on a daily basis, giving action-adventures to young players, entertaining them that way.

My situation is that the action-adventure bore me. It is too shallow. There are hardly any emotions to it. No real exploration of the human condition.

I feel a growing need to escape the action adventure. I do that by initiating games focused on everyday life, modern games and classical ones, as often as possible. My greatest joy in gaming comes from these forages into "dullness" nowadays. Only thing is; these games are far from dull. They are ripe with nuances, human drama, emotions, relationships ... the rich realities of the human condition. They are everything but dull. Ordinary humans are everything but dull. I love to walk the shoes of another ordinary human being, in a true role-playing game.

So, my question is; do YOU understand what I am talking about? Do you share some of these sentiments? Please respond in earnest. Thanks!

    \n It feels like I played everything. Nothing really comes as a surprise for me setting-wise (I'm still surprised by game styles sometimes). Therefore I'm open to play almost any setting. It really doesn't matter what I play - it's the experience I feel when I play that's important.

    A buddy of mine introduced Psychodrame on a gaming convention. French social realism in a contemporary environment. It felt dull, just by the pitch. And then he continued with giving an example of a scene, where a person could just sit and sigh for a couple of minutes. Seemed really boring, but I'm always open for new things.

    And the game blew my mind.

    Sure, the game is about dysfunctional people so that's a little different, but for a guy who played high-paced action games the last couple of years ... it felt like I was being reborn. The slow pace, the game mechanism, the ordinary problem, the scenes where nothing happens on the outside but explodes in the inside - all that created a really cool sensation. I discovered a new way of playing games. I still like high-paced action games, but now I can flip between that and a really down to earth kind of games with "real" humans.

    Last time we played, we played While the World Ends. It's normally takes place in a Sci-Fi setting, but we changed that into a New Years Eve party 2014 with a bunch of old friends coming together for one last party. It was about New Years vows, friendship, drunkenness, betrayal and loneliness. I don't think that we would've picked a setting with that theme if it wasn't for Psychodrame.

    \n I'll try to answer you, Terwox.

    Like, in this game I have made you play a bunch of friends. They have been friends since childhood. Now they are old. The game start with them burying one of them. You play the scene where they share a beer, after the service, talking about him. Before the next scene you roll a die; to determine who has died. then you play a similar scene, with him as the dead one. He is allowed to play on, as memories talking to the others, while they drink and talk. And then you roll for death again ... etc.

    There is no GM. You play these old friends, burying one friend at a time, talking about old times. And then at the end, there is a special scene, all of them young and happy, once, and that scene brings it all together.

    It is a simple little game, deeply moving, thought provoking.

    But I'm not surprised that you have failed to see such scenes weave a spell for you in classical games. If you want to experience things like this, you have to make games to cater for it. It's like; if you want something to read like Hamsun, a porn-magazine won't do the trick. ;-)

    I call such scenes "reflective scenes". They are all-important for a game where you want to key into the deeper emotional content of a drama. And you have to be able to sink them; kill the tempo, shut your mouth, let it drip ...

    In relation to the average gamer, initiating such scenes the right way can stir feelings he/she never dreamt of having in-character, in a game.
