\n Just readed about FU and It's finally what I was searching for. However there's still a bit more of hacking to do to have a system to suit my groups playing style and my lack of prep time as a GM. First I'll need to make a long and suitable enough list of descriptors, fitting for the setting we would like to play. Then I'll have to decide if I keep the same descriptors groups used on this game or the AW variant I used on my last hack (which worked wonderfully)

And then is the GMless (or GMfull) part of the deal.

I'm still going for something with cards with 4 different descriptors/conditions and an image on it. Image will be related to the magic system used on the setting (hacked from the Magicka PC game) and the four descriptors/conditions are for the players to frame scenes, using the elements of the setting created a la Burning Wheel (but this time a shortened version that only takes 20 minutes to create a full operational setting)

As you can see, I'm not doing anything original. Probably somebody already made the game I'm looking for. The question is this: Have you heard of any game like this?

-Gmless
-Short prep time: character and setting building in less than one hour both.
-Praparations doesn't spoil the surprise of what elements can appear in the story or how's gonna end.
-Character relationships do matter in the game
-Players doesn't need to be accomplished storytellers
-Mechanics help players come up with ideas
-Story created by the players input keeps coherence no matter what twists the story takes
-Mechanics helps the fiction go far beyond players expectations.

For what I heard Fiasco is closer to what I'm looking for but I haven't been able to get a copy/get a good look at the rules/play it with anybody who owns the game

    \n Fiasco is pretty close to your list there. The biggest deficit is that coherence isn't mechanically enforced at all. If you want to get gonzo it won't stop you, you have to stop yourselves. To be honest I'm not sure any game is going to handle this requirement procedurally - somebody has to be willing to say "yeah, no." Fiasco follows a very predictable arc that is mechanically reinforced, though.
