There's gonna be those people at GenCon:

"Can I have a review copy of your game? Please? I promise to review it on my Livejournal, with at least three to five viewers..." etc.

In this day and age, who would you give a copy of your game to review?

Back in 99-2000, there was the "Justin Bacon" rule:

"Can I have a review copy?"
"Are you Justin Bacon?"
"...uh, no"
"Then no."

Recently a lot of people seem to want to give Ken Hite a copy of their game, in the hopes that it makes it to his journals.

Who are the New Blood to give review copies to?

For me, I think the people to best be giving your review copies to are:
1) Judd
2) Paul Tevis
3) Steve Darlington

Judd may review your game, but he'll certainly play a session of it, talk about that session, and that alone will drive about 50 sales flat out.

Paul will give it a thorough reading and fine review on his widely read podcast.

Steve D is a great pimper if he believes in your game.

Who else are some solid figures to give review copies to?

Also, do you have any feelings towards review copies? ("No one gets a review copy"; "If you buy and review my game, I'll then refund your money, whether the review was positive or negative"; "I'll subsidize half of your purchase if you review the game within 3 months", etc)

I waffle on Dan Davenport of RPGNet: His reviews are extremely thorough. Unfortunately, each review could be a book. And I don't think I've actually read through a single of his reviews completely, ever. But if you like "reviews that are themselves books", he'd be a good catch.

-Andy

  • ptevis
    August 2006
    I'm a player of games, not a shill.

    I'm both.

    I mean that fairly seriously. I totally respect what Jeff and Judd do, because it really fits with their show. One of the reasons I love hearing about games from them is because I know their experience with games is battle-tested. If it didn't happen at the table, they're not saying it, which is really cool. For me, however, there are games I read and can see that are awesome, but that I know I'm probably not going to get a chance to play, or at least won't have a chance to play for a while. I still want to tell the world about these games, so other people can see how awesome they are and play them. So yeah, I'm a shill, but for awesomeness.

    I have had people send me review copies of games. I've always tried to make it clear that I will only talk about games that I think are worth talking about and to set expectations appropriately. I'm not going to give games free advertising just because I got a copy, and I've had to tell people that I'm not going to review their games because they wouldn't like what I would say about the games. Similarly, if I know that a product is likely not the sort of thing I'm into, I'll let them know that before I let them give me copy.

    Basically, it comes down to this. My show is "Paul's views on gaming." If someone wants to give me a copy of a game, I'll take it, I'll read it, if it's good hopefully I'll have time to play it, and I'll tell people what I think about it, because it's now a part of my gaming world. There are a number of great games that I probably wouldn't have picked up if people hadn't give me copies that I then discovered were awesome. These are the games I want people know about.

    So for me, it's about expectations. Does that make sense?

    --Paul
