\n My personal roleplaying history lies pretty firmly in the camp of RPGs in the traditional style. I confess that I'm not the best with terminology, so if I get some of the terms wrong, please do correct me, but hopefully I can articulate what I mean to get across. For the purposes of the discussion at hand, I'm thinking of games like D&D; RuneQuest; Traveller; Big Eyes, Small Mouth; Stars Without Number; Tunnels and Trolls; and other such games, with a clear GM role and players in direct control of a single character in a simulated world.

I've seen a *lot* of pieces in this arena, and I'm working on one myself, but to me, the difference between a good, clear trad game and a murky one (a heartbreaker?) is one that I have trouble putting a finger on, except for that old definition of pornography, "I know it when I see it". My primary goal is to avoid the pitfalls that make a game hard to play or facilitate. So I thought I'd just put the question out there directly:

What are some qualities of a good traditional-style game, and what are some qualities of a bad trad game? And possibly, for bonus points, what are some qualities that don't make a difference one way or the other? (One standout in that category to me is complexity; you can make a good complex game, and you can make a bad simple game.) I'd also love if anyone could point me at any good discussions that have already been made about this topic.

    \n Personally, the relatively "traditional" games I've been digging lately are super short, like 4-10 pages, because -- since they draw on the participants' history of playing lots of similar RPGs -- they don't have to spend 100+ pages explaining how to play these types of games. They just give you the meat and let you go at it. This also lets them boil down the things we love about these games into a super-concentrated stew that is very exciting and accessible. The thing that's currently blowing my mind is Michael Wight's World of Warhammer hack. SO GOOD!

    I think this is a great model for "traditional" game design, especially if you're aiming primarily at an audience who already knows how to play these types of games. Or at least assume that the GM will know more-or-less what to do. There's still plenty of space to offer more specific instructions or tell the GM to focus on specific things, but you don't have to reinvent the wheel.

    \n David, that's an excellent question. In the interest of clarity, "making the game I want to play", the piece I'm working on (a game intended to replicate the moment-to-moment feel of being a protagonist in an action movie or TV series) does have advice to the GM on scoping and flow, but doesn't have hard-wired mechanics that dictate the pace of the mission (I don't say, for example, "when you reach Heat Level 4, these guys come onto the scene"). I guess in that vein it's not dissimilar from some of the latter-day games Mr. Walton was referring to, in that there's a defined "mission" and way to scope it, a preferred form of mission planning in the style of Dungeon World and its ilk (impressions, setpieces, enemies, but not a predefined sequence of scenes), and rules for the action and interaction of characters, but no meta-level mechanics.

    I won't be bold enough to say that tightly-focused games are "good" and toolbox games are "bad", or even "less good", but my personal preference is the latter of your two options, a game that does tell you how to play and should provide good results (at least more often than not), though I know that isn't everyone's cup of tea.

    \n To me, traditional RPGs are as much about what you do away from the table as what you do at the table. There's a very strong element of "I'm going to make this thing and then I'm going to show it to you." The GM would make this world and maybe this story and the players made characters. Then, at the table, everyone got to show off the cool thing they made and then the rules said how the things that they made interact with one another, lubricated by the fiction. The good traditional games gave you interesting processes for generating worlds/adventures/characters on your own and those processes were as fun to play with by yourself (like a mini-game) as the 'mechanisms' of the game were when you got together with others to game. Additionally, they generated characters that were easy for everyone else to understand. The bad ones, on the GM side, gave you a lot of setting information and told you to tell stories set in that world but didn't give you processes to go through. The bad ones, on the players' side, gave you way more options than were interesting (too many flavors of +1) and generated characters that had so many nuanced details that they presented a wall of text that other players wouldn't appreciate, couldn't remember, and ultimately didn't play to.

    \n Jim, thanks for the clarifications.

    The first thing that comes to mind is the synergy between characters and missions. What is the GM stoked about running, and what directions are the players stoked to send their characters in, and how do we make the two match up? A good "tell you how to play" mission-based trad game oughta either do that work for you, or tell you how to do it yourselves in a pretty foolproof manner.

    I'm thinking mainly of "this mission is tangible progress toward something the character cares about", but equally vital is "this mission involves activities the player enjoys".

    I think the options roughly break down to:

    Scenario First - GM makes a plan for the content of play, then gets players to make characters who will fit into that content.

    Character First - Players make characters designed to work well together. GM then designs scenarios based on those characters and how they work together.

    Material First - Everyone gets on board with a setting, genre, references from other fiction, worldbook, canon, or any sort of inspiration that can be agreed upon. GM and players then derive compatible characters and scenarios from this agreed-upon material.

    Whichever path you want to take, how easy the game makes it for the group to succeed will be a make-or-break feature. No one likes the trad game where the impassioned liberator of the underclasses gets shoehorned into corporate espionage missions, or where the tactical player spends all their time just roleplaying chats with NPCs.

    \n Another thing that's important to talk about is which of a number of "traditional" styles of play is actually going on. Or if multiple viable approaches exist.

    - Is it a sandbox game where the PCs follow their inclinations and the GM "follows them around" (to quote AW)?
    - Is is a game where the PCs pursue formal agendas, creating new ones when the old ones are complete?
    - Is it a mission-based game where NPCs assign broad tasks and the PCs attempt them, at least at first?
    - Is it a game structured by the environment, like a dungeon-crawl, going room-by-room? Or a flowchart of scenes?
    - Is it an "illusionist" game where the GM plans a bunch of challenges for the PCs to overcome?
    - Is is some combination of the above or, crazier still, something completely different?

    I've definitely sat in on some sessions where I assumed we were "playing to find out what happens" but, in fact, folks were playing mostly to experience being their character or find out what the GM had planned next. While I don't think GNS is really helpful here, "creative agendas" are clearly a thing and it's important to have a sense of what the game is about, even if it's determined by individual playgroups rather than the text of the game.

    What are some qualities of a good traditional-style game, and what are some qualities of a bad trad game? And possibly, for bonus points, what are some qualities that don't make a difference one way or the other?
    \nGood and bad are variable dependent mostly in the eye of the beholder especially when it comes to rules lite vs crunchy. Complexity can lead to nuance and players finding niches they enjoy, but it can also lead to confusion and frustration if it is not received well particularly by the GM. A GM that really "gets" a system can support players that don't get it at all even if the system is very crunchy. On the flip, even if the players are really into a setting, if the GM stumbles through it, they can destroy any story telling that the system was intended to do.

    Lite systems are nice for broad brush strokes. Crunch is good for detailed illustration of the story. A great story can be told by either one lite systems depend on their color, crunchy systems depend on a fine eye and a steady hand (metaphorically). You can like either or both but few GMs are good at both.

    One way to get a balance of both is to use a simple system 80% of the time to fill in large swaths of the background but then the focus becomes detailed. Because the mind's eye focuses on one thing, It assumes the rest of the setting is just as detailed as the focus. This is what a good game does.

    A bad game assumes the reader knows what to do. This is where I differ with what was stated above. Don't assume. In general, as I've found, if a 9 year old can read your game and follow the instructions, you've done a good enough job with writing.

    A more squishy concept is, does the game inspire? That depends on the reader to an extent. I'm totally not interested in zombie games. You put one in front of me and I'm not interested. So you can't please everyone. But you can make a game uninspiring by not explaining the core conflicts clearly enough. Players (including the GM) should instantly know what their job will be when playing the game. Even if they get to choose that job each time they play.

    \n Having read this thread, I see a lot of great advice, but there is one thing that still concerns me: when you say action movie / Tom Clancy genre, are you concerned at all with the structural conventions of that sort of fiction?     \n@Deliverator: You make a good point there. To be honest, this thread kind of diverted from "What makes a good trad game" to "Hey, let's discuss my personal project!" -- the nutshell version of the message I'm getting from my original question is kind of "eye of the beholder".     But since you ask, I am putting thought into mission flow and ramp-up; fight the weenies first, explore the space (since action follows the heroes), figure out the real problem, get to the baddie's lair, and mess his day up. The "mission planning" I intend is pretty sparse, *somewhat* in the vein of Dungeon World and its related games; figure out the first scene, figure out the baddie's lair (the final scene), and then lay out enemies, impressions, and a handful of explosive setpieces, with no sequential order to anything. More or less, the GM follows the players' action, dropping the pieces he has in his toolbelt in the players' way where it makes sense. Again, I'm weak with terminology, so forgive me if I step on the toes of any definitions here, but it's kind of "illusionist" in that way; the players will get to the Big Bad when it makes sense to the flow of the action.

    Oh, and the players don't die. If someone wipes out, the enemies simply don't kill him; they capture him. If he ends up in a nuclear explosion, however unlikely, the PC will survive and find himself in big trouble. And if the entire team is defeated, they're captured or left for dead, and end up at a pretty serious disadvantage. By rule, the mission never fails unless time runs out, or the PCs are wiped in the final scene.

    What do you do if the PCs genuinely and legitimately figure out a way to make the BB go splat right quick?
    \nGood for them, right? I've seen stories where the antagonist bites it in Act I, and the rest of the story is how the protagonists get away with their skins. I also make clear that anyone at the table can call bullshit if something the GM (or another player) does doesn't make sense.
