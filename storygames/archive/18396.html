\n I've noticed that, as the years go by and I get deeper and deeper into this hobby, I think about games a lot differently now. (Being around a lot of super smart people who really care about systems and how they work has clearly rubbed off on me.) I don't just play games anymore, I try to figure out how they work. I've been playing a lot of computer-based roleplaying games recently, mostly due to lack of people to play tabletop games with, and have been going back and forth between Torchlight and Fallout 3. At one point I turned on Fallout 3, looked at the long list of attributes on the screen while trying to figure out where to put newly-earned points, and I asked two questions out loud to the room (I'm an only child): "How are both of these games roleplaying games when they're nothing alike?" and "How is this the defining feature of what makes a roleplaying game a roleplaying game?" I know a lot of you are gonna think I'm really dumb for not crossing the finish line sooner on this one, but it was at that moment that I really internalized that advancement and roleplaying are different things. And yet, especially in the mainstream, these terms are seen as one in the same thing.

I found this thread over on the RPG stackexchange that talked about a system someone created for a competition (one of you?) that involved no mechanical advancement and whether or not such a system could sustain fun over the long term. What seems to fall out from that discussion is that the only thing really necessary is change, though I have a feeling like that's a little too simple.

FYI, the question is rhetorical (sort of). Mostly I just want to watch you smart people discuss this so I can learn all of the things.

    \n You learn about the concept of "roleplaying" from either the tabletop culture, or the video game culture. Age demographics have a massive influence on this: those of us over 30 years old have most likely learned about roleplaying from the tabletop culture (this is just a demographic guess in the basis of what was being played when I or you were 12 years old; obviously does not apply to everybody), and therefore find it pretty strange to equate advancement and roleplaying - that's never been the defining feature in the tabletop rpg culture. However, the younger a gamer, the more likely it is that their childhood happened over an era when "roleplaying" was this computer game genre.

    Personally I don't even use the video gaming term "roleplaying game" unless necessary for clarity, it's just so jarring to have the same word used for two entire different practices that share only the most superficial features. I mean, it's not even historically inaccurate to say that what defines a computer roleplaying game and differentiates it from just plain adventure game is character advancement - that's the only feature of Dungeons & Dragons that remains in its CRPG off-shoot, so if it weren't the defining feature, what would be?

    Of course, I regularly meet people for whom there is no sharp contrast between a crpg and a roleplaying game; they talk of strange, wondrous and insane things like solo roleplaying and how they immerse in a crpg's story and characters. I'm probably pretty hardcore myself insofar as the crpg genre goes, but this is so far from the emotional relationship I have with this type of game that I can't really even imagine the process in any but the most lurid way. Does explain how anybody could like Final Fantasy games, I suppose.

    If we set the crpg genre aside, for which "advancement = roleplaying" is actually the bald truth, then for the tabletop hobby this is a pretty tired cliche. I mean, I'm sure you can turn over some stones and find a bunch of people who'll believe it (really, you'll just need people who haven't played or heard of one of the dozens of roleplaying games with negligible or non-existent character advancement), but that doesn't make it true. We might as well attempt to have a discourse about how having a GM defines a roleplaying game.

    \n Roleplay is focused on role - on the differentiation between the character and the self.

    How the role differentiation manifests depends on the nature of the game.

    For one-shot events, character advancement almost never makes sense or is important (though I'm currently developing a live combat larp game based on XCom, where players will advance their soldiers' abilities mid-game through some very limited options, much like the Firaxis game.)

    In a text writing game, like an online forum, the role differentiation manifests primarily through use of voice and the possibility space that makes sense for that character. As with most fiction writing, character change happens extremely slowly if at all. Consistent portrayal usually trumps change.

    In things like Nordic larp, character differentiation tends to be minimal compared to many other forms of roleplay because the goal is often for players to feel things through their character actions, so the character and player should tie together tightly. Also, these tend to be one-shot events, so again, character advancement would rarely makes sense to begin with.

    However, in long term games with a strategic element, character differentiation usually takes place at the strategic level - in the abilities that your character has. So in a game where strategic choices matter, changing the strategic choice context a bit over time by changing your character's abilities is commonly something that interests people. Otherwise, you're working within the same possibility space for a long time, which can get dull and boring and repetitive and repetitive and repetitive...

    In long term games with less focus on the strategic, there will be a focus on other things, like relationships between the characters. So tracking change in those areas becomes more important. We can see this with the strings in Monsterhearts or the changing relationship dice in Smallville. By emphasizing change in those areas in the mechanics, the system reinforces the emphasis on relationships as being central to the ongoing story.

I don't quite know what a game that doesn't change over sessions would look like - it'd presumably be like a play, repeated unchanged from showing to showing.     As for what sort of change is minimally necessary from session to session, I'd say that merely a decision to play differently from the last time seems to go some distance, provided that the game has sufficient scope.     \n How did "advancement" become synonymous with "roleplaying?"

    You got me thinking. I like that.

    Advancement isn't synonymous with Role-Playing; it is, however, synonymous with Game. It is a reward system that is well known in Computer Game Design theory but the idea predates our hobby and even computers. It feeds a compulsive need many of us have for goal completion/ task resolution. "Just one more level/turn/build phase, then I'll go to bed. Okay, maybe just one more..."

    One of the more recent iterations of this theory is Badges. Collect 50 Golden McGuffins to gain 50 xp and the Golden McGuffin Collector Badge! Fallout 3 has this in the form of hidden Perks (kill enough giant ants and you'll get one).

    It creates a (false) sense of achievement. I've felt it myself. After completing a particularly difficult game/level/map I look around, half expecting some material award or at least a parade.

    Browser games are particularly bad for this; they actually are DESIGNED to be addictive. Ask any "Garden of Time" fanatic (I know a couple. I still don't get the appeal).

    \n Really quick point here, especially regarding your observations on Fallout vs. Torchlight...

    The term "roleplaying game", especially in the computer sphere, is a marketing term. Sorta like what Vincent Baker said about nano-games vs. game poems, how those were two marketing terms. The main point is, when it comes to dividing things out, the label doesn't matter so much.

You have a character, and you can modify things about that character. Leveling up and other advancement just come part and parcel with that, especially as a means of keeping things fresh and letting you start humbly (and accessibly) and end powerfully (fighting the big bads, etc.)

    \nAlthough, as mentioned, it's hard to untangle video games from the influence of D&D, you can look at the ways that other games feature changing scenarios or advancement. I don't know how much intentionality there was over the three-way meaning of "level" in D&D (character level, dungeon level, spell level) but I think it's really important. A few sandboxes and sims aside, pretty much all non-rpg computer games feature the Dungeon Level, constantly changing the site of play. This kind of game (so platformers, pac-man maze games, and fighting games at the simplest, but puzzle games and shooters too) doesn't have character advancement but is built around another mastery and player advancement. Discovering the next Level, and realizing that you totally can't handle it yet, is a pretty neat feeling.

    I think a lot about fighting games especially because of the way they build a society around this advancement and construct a kind of athleticism.

    So yeah, the purpose of the Level of either kind is to make changes in the scope of play possible, to present goals that a beginning player can't do, to reward continued play, repetition, and mastery. Some of the addictiveness of character-advancement games comes from layering the Levels on top of themselves\u2014grinding to catch your character advancement up with your player advancement is a technique that draws out anticipation for the goal-success, and produces a complex system part of which takes place outside of the game's code.
