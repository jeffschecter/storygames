\n Hey Matthijs,

I like it. But the Down's Syndrome part is fuzzy for me. The situations on the cards are so non-specific to Down's Syndrome, or rather, are so broadly applicable to the raising of any child, that (unless Catherine's player is as familiar with Down's Syndrome as you are) you've written a game about raising a child, not one about raising a child who's "different".

I think if you leave the game as is, what you'll get in play is either:

1. Scenes about ordinary childhood issues (perhaps making the point, accurate or not, that children with Down's Syndrome have the same issues as children who don't).
2. Scenes that mischaracterize or over-characterize Down's Syndrome as something it isn't; perhaps an honest expression of parental fears, done privately as you've written the game, but not a particularly useful exploration of parenting, unless all you care about is the emotional catharsis of irrational fears.

If what you want is really a game about raising a child who's different, my recommendation would be to ditch the Down's Syndrome specification entirely, and to generalize Catherine simply as being "different". Then, implement some cards that gradually define her difference over the course of play. For these cards, draw broadly from such sources as The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Nighttime, articles on ADD (or rather, maybe one of Thom Hartmann's better books), your reading on Down's Syndrome, The Highly Sensitive Child (by Elaine Aron, I believe), Beggars in Spain, Ender's Game, etc.

And then Catherine will be differently "different" for each game, depending on the cards that come up.

Paul

    \n Thanks for your comments, Paul & Jason!

    Paul, one of the major unspoken questions posed in "Catherine" is: What are the differences between raising a normal child and a "special" child - and, in particular, what are the differences between the lives lived by normal and "special" children? I want this to be answered by the players, through play. I believe this will highlight the players' conscious, unconscious and semi-conscious beliefs about "special" children, child-rearing, and - last but not least - the relative values of lives.

    In Norway, there's a debate about whether or not it's right to abort a foetus with Down's. This game can also be seen in that context.

    Jason, having two children in play is very interesting. It will complicate the game, and bring some more issues so that it's not as tightly focused; however, it will probably add a sound "reality check" as the normal sibling demands to be treated fairly - for a whole range of values of "fairly". I think I'd want to play the game as written first, and then perhaps expand it with a sibling - and possibly an additional parent. Quite possible to do within one evening of play.

    \n Hey Matthijs,

    A child who can't sleep, one who's angry at her father, who's dealing with meanness from peers, a father who has an irrational concern that something's wrong, who's in love, but hasn't yet told his child, etc., all of these are ordinary parenting situations. You know this. You couldn't possibly have written so many ordinary situations if it weren't a conscious decision.

    With these situations the only thing to take the game into the realm of "difference" is your statement that Catherine has Down's Syndrome.

    I'm not suggesting you specify "differences between the lives lived by normal and 'special' children" in a way that telegraphs a "relative value" answer to your premise. I'm suggesting cards something like this:

    "No matter what Catherine does with make-up or clothing, she cannot hide facial characteristics that reveal to others that she's different."

    "Catherine takes everything she's told at face value."

    "Catherine is easily overstimulated, and as a result, avoids or sticks to the margins of group activities that others would find pleasurable."

    \n Hey Matthijs,

    Let's say Jason and I were to play it. I'm Catherine and he's Chris. We turn over a card. Catherine can't sleep. So I'm thinking, well, I could have Catherine screeching unintelligibly and slamming her head repeatedly against the wall in frustration, but I'm doubtful of my understanding of Down's Syndrome. I'm clueless about it really, and I know I am. So I'll just play it safe. "Dad, I can't sleep."

    So, Jason could have Chris say, "Did you remember to take your meds?" But he knows he's just as clueless about Down's Syndrome as I am. So he plays it safe too. "Do you want to talk about it?"

    And so what you get is rather pedestrian scenes of the ordinary challenges of child rearing.

    Unless one of us decides to push the envelope. But we're modern guys, right, we're conditioned to steer clear of even the perception of insensitivity. So we might push the envelope, but it'll never be about "difference". It'll be, "Dad, Mr. Drozdalski has a pig fetus in a jar on his desk."

    How is it not going to play out that same way for everyone?

    Paul

    \n So I finally played a short game of Catherine - four cards plus the end card.

    As has been suggested on this thread, it was harder than I expected to know how to portray Catherine at various ages. We talked a little about it between scenes, and tried to figure out how Catherine would feel and act. I felt like I'd want some sort of chart saying what sort of skills, attitudes, cognitive level she'd have at three, five, nine, eleven... Perhaps this could be written on the cards. However, people with Down's can develop in many different ways and at many different speeds, so perhaps the game should start with a discussion of what sort of child Catherine is?

    We switched roles along the way, alternating between scenes who played Chris and who played Catherine. I'm not sure that was a good idea. It would have been better to alternate between games, I believe.

    I made one big mistake: I didn't warn the other player of the end card, and it came as a surprise - an emotional shock. It's allright, we talked about it and worked it out; I apologized for my stupidity. However, this clearly needs to be stated up front, and perhaps there should be different optional end cards, so players can select what sort of story they want.

    Now that I've got the game more at a distance, I'm liking some of the suggestions on this thread a lot more. Having different cards that make Catherine different from game to game is a good idea - however, in real life, the parents would probably know her diagnosis from early on in many cases; if a child is born with Down's, for example, they would know. So perhaps Catherine should have different "life paths" - in practice, a set of different decks.

    I'd like to add the "normal" child to the game as well, for a three-player game - I think a two- and three-player game would be two very different experiences.
