But I'm trying to bring a bit more exposure of indie games to a local con that's taking place March 2-4th.
I'm going to be running 5 games, with a couple other indie games being run by friends of mine.

The gaming group I'm currently a part of is doing a bit of self-branding right now. We have a Yahoo discussion group and are basically attempting to advance indie and/or experimental gaming, gain a larger playtesting net, show people new Forge-y ideas, and stimulate the flow of creative energy in Vancouver. I'm thinking about having a handful of pamphlets with our gamer bios, our mission statement (yeah, we have one), our name/logo, and a list of games we hope to explore in the near future. Has anyone done something like this for an individual group?

I'm running a session of Perfect, two sessions of Polarpunk (my game in playtest), and two sessions of The Shadow of Yesterday.
Kirk Mitchell is running a session of his game in playtest, Kin.
Daniel Wood is running a session of Dogs in the Vineyard.

The game pitches I posted for my games are:
Polarpunk: The Hunt for Chicago (3 of 6 spots remaining)

In a post-apocalyptic future, the second ice age has come. Humanity has been living in isolated bunker colonies for generations, while polar bears have claimed the earth as the new dominant species. As advanced scout's for your bunker-colony's reclaimation efforts, it is your job to scavenge the ruins of fallen cities, make first contact with the other tribes, and to start the fight of reclaimation. Using Clinton R. Nixon's award winning The Shadow of Yesterday system, I plan to run a different kind of dungeon crawl: One focused on tragedy and humanity and what being a hero means. If you've never encountered The Shadow of Yesterday before, the designer sums it up quite well: "Set in a post-apocalyptic fantasy world where the creation of a moon has ravaged cultures and thrown the fate of the earth into question, TSOY is like a Weird Tales contribution written by Emily Bronte."
Perfect: A Well Placed Dagger (FULL)

Upon request, I'm going to be running my own published indie title, Perfect. Set in a dystopian, Victoriana world, Perfect is a game about criminal passions and rebellion. Can you hold onto the things that you care about, or will you be forced to sever ties? "It's like Kafka and Charlotte Bronte had children, and those children wrote Blade Runner." Malcolm Craig (A|State, Cold City) "Folks, Perfect is an excellent game. Your character's gonna sweat, cry, get tortured, flip out and commit crime, and you will love every damn minute of it." (Remi Treuer, of the Durham 3 podcast)
TSOY: The Earth Trembles in an Uncharted Language (just put it up, 5 of 6 spots remain open)

As Knights of New Empire, it is your job to tour the borders of New Empire, collecting taxes and pledges of fealty. Everything is going according to protocol, until you reach the home of the mud people. The mud people refuse to answer your demands. Worse, they refuse to let you leave. They have secrets that are worth killing to protect. The Earth Trembles in an Uncharted Language is a scenario of dark fantasy and horror, pitting the Knights of New Empire against their deepest, darkest fears. The game is run on Clinton R. Nixon's The Shadow of Yesterday system, and is open to beginners.
Polarpunk: The Aeon Machine (5 of 6 spots remain open)

[same description as the first Polarpunk session].

    I thought about doing something like this a while ago and came up with the following tactics:

    * Get as many people as you can to run games of the type you want to see.

    * Make sure you're running game that can take large numbers of players (5-6 as a minimum). This might mean changing the games you run so you're running the ones which can support large groups easily.

    * Try to half fill as many games as you can by getting people to come along who you know who are already converts. That way you are running all those games even if nobody else is interested. I realise that the point is to get converts to the story gaming cause (which sounds a bit nefarious to me), but you'll look like a right plonker if you turn up with a game to run and you don't get enough players. I was at a Con last weekend and there were a couple of games which were announced and which nobody signed up for. That made people think that either the game being run or the GM are a bit rubbish.

    * Make lots of noise, so that it's clear you are enjoying yourself. As Jason points out, you will end up enjoying yourself, and the masses will see that this is good.

    * If you are playtesting at a Con, try to get some decent feedback. I found this somewhat hard when I ran a playtest of UmlÃ¤ut lately, because everyone who played was really enjoying themselves and having a great time. So when you try to pick out what didn't work, you generally get the response "Nothing, it's totally awesome!!!" So take notes as the game is running.

    TBH it sounds like you're doing most of this already though.

    Do. Not. Wander.

    The way you'll be able to form community outside of the sessions is if people have an idea where that community is located geographically. If they want to go talk with some indie folks, they should know where to go.

    So set up a base-camp for your group, and make sure that it's "staffed" as best you can.

    Wandering around and drawing people into discussion? That's something to do when you are surplus at the base-camp. Otherwise, trust that people will come to you, as long as they consistently know where it is that they should go.

    The impression I got, at Conception, from being a Collective Endeavour groupie, was that people are more interested in the games than the group. There were posters up everywhere about the Collective Endeavour, but what really generated interest were the games: they were booked up well in advance.

    So my impression is that you should start with the games: get people to play, show them a good time, then tell them about the group, rather than hitting them with your gamer bios and mission statement straight away.

    The game write-ups look great. Personally, I like the ones that start with the setting ("As Knights of New Empire, it is your job to tour the borders of New Empire...") rather than "Upon request, I'll be running my own system...". On the Polarpunk one, I'd find it more grabby if you started with what I'll do in the game: i.e. scavenging alien cities and making contact with tribes.

    I fully support Rich Stokes' idea that you should be as loud as possible. I'd suggest some sort of PA system, with pumping hip-hop. That'll get people's attention.

    I have conquered and unconquered cons many times before and this is all good. Let me add one more thing:

    Use "their" marketing. That is, find the mailing lists for your local RPGA or LARP or whatever has the signficant con presence (what? You don't know who has the significant con presence? You think you will conquer without intelligence??) and send out an e-mail specifically inviting them to specific games that will appeal to their interests (horror if it is a Vampire LARP that has the oomf, science fiction if it's a Trek club, dungeon crawls or fantasy if it's the RPGA.)     I'm late to the party, but, in addition to what everybody else has already said:

    1) Yes, make the focus on the games. People, uh, don't care who you are. They care what games they get to play. Then they notice that you and your friends ran the best games at the con. And then next con they seek you out.

    2) Seed your games with players. A game with no signups gathers no signups. A game with one signup gathers more players. At the strategicons, we did this by signing up for each other's games. That, and we wanted to play each other's games, anyway.

    3) Base camp is nice, but not strictly necessary. On the other hand, we're hopefully getting some base camp action this next week, and I'm looking forward to it. From past experience, it's hella convenient even if not necessary.

    4) I touched on this above, but invading cons works best iteratively. The first time out may not be super awesome. The next time will be. The time after that will rock on toast.
