Programmed Instruction and Choosing Your Own Adventure

Jason_Morningstar
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
It just occurred to me that these books may be of interest to somebody - I'm re-playing/re-reading Combat Team: The Captain's War (I went through Infantry Combat: The Rifle Platoon and Armor Attacks: The Tank Platoon years ago). These are all a weird combination of choose-your-own-adventure and tactical how-to manual - barely accessible to armchair warriors at all, terribly unforgiving, a little corny but fascinating as well. There's a pretty deep tradition of programmed solo adventures (even solo games, a la Fighting Fantasy), and Antal's books certainly have enough game-ness to them to pique my interest. After exploring the good (and bad) decisions and outcomes, I feel like I've got a better idea how to handle myself the next time I have to mount an armor-based counter-attack against North Korea, anyway. I wonder how this format could be re-purposed to, say, teach somebody how to play a new game, or how to behave in a role-playing game group, or how to set stakes or something.

    --------------------------------------------------
    GAMEMASTER QUEST

    The Players sit down at the table to play, and begin the light talk and snack-eating. Today is the day that you were to bring AwesomeQuest with you and run it for the other players. This is the First Night. You know the rules back and forth. You have 1-2 quick-play scenarios at your disposal that came with the book that you're familiar with, and some pre-generated characters. Unfortunately, none of the other players are currently familiar with the rules or setting of the game.

    Soon, the talking quiets and stops, and all eyes are on you. What do you do?

    If you give them a breakdown of the setting and rules before play to get them fully into what the game is about, turn to section 51.

    If you jump right into a scenario, handing out characters and explaining the rules as needed to kick off the game while easing them into play, turn to section 74.

    If you brief them on the gist of the rules and setting and ask the players for what kind of campaign and characters they want to play, turn to section 94.

    -Andy

    Here's an interesting attempt to revive the programmed adventure format:

    http://www.darkcitygames.com/

    ...these guys are selling a number of PA style modules, inspired by the old Fantasy Trip modules which were also done in a PA format. They have a (mostly playable) download of their rule set, which is an attempt to be TFT compatible but not pure TFT (since there are rights problems), and they have a short adventure you can download in the PA format in addition to the ones they sell.

    The most interesting thing about the old TFT adventures and these new ones is that they were designed with the idea that you'd have several players working together to go through the PA, with either no GM, or a GM whose only real task was to play the opposition in set piece battles. I ran the free one a couple weeks ago, and a one player said, "it's like playing Gauntlet on the tabletop."

    I also bought one of their for pay games and its very good quality POD.

    I loved those things, and have to agree that they had (and have!) application in teaching RPGs. The mid-80s (Larry Elmore cover) Red Box D&D set did have a short one to help teach the game, but it was loaded with typical bad D&D adventure silliness -- no logic to the dungeon setup whatsoever. And it suffered from the emphasis on "shoot 'n loot" dungeoneering.

    The original version of 3rd edition GURPS also had an introductory programmed adventure, though it was stuck in the back of the book as an appendix. I've played it a couple of times -- it was better than the D&D one, with a more logical setup and structure, but still had a "shoot 'n loot" style.

    Circa 1990, D&D had a "big black box" set, with a system for learning the game that was essentially a series of programmed adventures. However, the set was meant to take a newbie from knowing nothing about the game, up to being a GM. I seem to recall it having some cards that were "programmed GMing", with the player being asked how they'd decide certain kinds of things... I think I'll dig that out tonight and post more about it later.

    And on the DVD front, there's already a D&D-branded DVD programmed adventure (although it doesn't teach how to play, and is more a pick-a-path thing): Scourge of Worlds. Reviews I've seen have been very mixed, depending on what people were expecting.
