    \n Most of the games I play these days feature a lot of conflict but very little violence. Sometimes the threat of violence is involved, or sometimes the aftermath of past violence, etc. But the heart of the matter is, while a majority of roleplaying games model a narrow strand of action-adventure fiction, a much wider variety of conflict types is featured in fiction and equally fulfilling in role-playing games.

    Conflict-less games are trickier. Typically, these are actually about some kind of inner conflict: an inside struggle over a difficult decision, one's identity or a difficult relationship, etc. See for example Ribbon Drive.

    \nSign in Stranger is about exploring and discovering an alien world, usually in a situation where failing to do so is risky. I would call "Oops, we thought we were supposed to eat this thing, but we weren't, and now we're mutating" danger, but not violence. I guess if the only thing inflicting harm is non-sentient, there ya go.

    There are plenty of internal conflicts possible in Sign in Stranger, as the characters adjust to the radically unfamiliar. The primary conflict I've seen, though, is about learning and understanding. "We'd really like to know what to do with this widget the aliens gave us, but we might not be able to figure it out!"

    The failure conditions vary a bit, and there's certainly more drama when failure means starvation or being trapped or caged or something, as opposed to just frustration, embarrassment, and some wasted time. I can't claim that every session I've played has included high drama. The arc of learning and discovery has always been satisfying to me, though, with or without high drama.

    My own draft conspiracy game, The Eye in the Pyramid, follows a similar logic. GMs can introduce violence if they like, but really play is about encountering unknowns or secrets, forming theories, and eventually finding out the truth.

    \n I think that in gaming, traditionally, rules have focused on violent conflict in a major way. Character creation, advancement (e.g. XP rules), and resolution rules all pointed towards fighting and killing each other. (Consider, for instance, that D&D's hit points, for all the abstraction they represent tell you when someone is dead but not at all who won the fight or who got what they wanted.)

    This meant two things:

    1. If you are playing a character in such a game, and you're not getting what you want, the only way you can enforce your will is by going to the sword. Someone disagrees with you? Your only leverage is to attack and fight them.

    Combat, in this setup, becomes your only lever against the fictional reality you're playing in and all its other inhabitants.

    (You can see from this why so many D&D players, for an example, would get incredibly angry if they found out their GM was "fudging" combat results.)

    When all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail.

    (The spells in old-school D&D are a really interesting counterpoint to this - an exception to this general rule - and, for that reason, I've always been sad to see them becoming more and more combat-focused with each edition.)

    2. If you've invested so much time and effort in learning a game system, creating a character, picking powers and weapons, and so forth, you're going to (quite naturally) want to be rewarded for all that work.

    There's all this *fun stuff* in the rulebooks, right? I want to see it in action!

    Players (and GMs, to a lesser extent) quite naturally want to gravitate to this material. It's something you've thought about, it's probably something designed to be exciting, and it's something you can really dive into to find you have some agency in the game when it's lacking.

    From the above two points, I think an expectation has been created among gamers to always expect and seek out violent conflict - combat - in their games. I've seen it go so far as for people to say that "roleplaying without combat is like pizza without cheese", or even to say that a game without a combat system is not an RPG at all.

    I think there's absolutely no reason - other than the history of roleplaying game - to assume anything like that. There are now a myriad games out there which create intense, powerful experiences without any fighting at all (and sometimes without even the *option* to include any fighting!).

    The things which make those games engaging are all the same things which make any game engaging: interesting characters in interesting situations, a cool premise, escalating conflict, emotionally-driven stakes, mysteries, surprises, game rules you can use or manipulate to create uncertain outcomes, ways for the players to compete with each other, ways for us to imagine and experience unfamiliar people and places, and ways for us to surprise each other.

    When I think of the games which are typically considered "story games", I'd say MOST of them achieve this goal, and with panache. It would be easier to list games (in this genre) which focus solely on fighting.

    However, even among "story games", there is often a focus on violence and violent conflict, particularly - to take a popular example - in the games of Vincent Baker.

    Violent conflict makes a powerful statement about human desires and our limited abilities to reconcile them with each other. It is the final stage in any two human beings' attempt to control each other.

    For this reason, a lot of "story games" do feature violent conflict, but often with a subtly (or not-so-subtly) different focus: rather than looking at the "how" of fighting and violence, let's try to look at the consequences of violent conflict.

    \n RPGs are rooted in genres that feature romanticized violence, like sword&sorcery and space opera. And games of any type often involve violence because it's the most direct expression of conflict.

    Subtler types of conflict can be hard to represent in game mechanics. Many games have extrapolated combat mechanics to "social combat", but this runs into trouble with narrative -- you don't have to be a good swordsman to say "I run it through with my sword!", but a social conflict is largely based on talking, and it's awkward to separate the character's supposed silver tongue from the player's actual words. If the player just says "I talk the countess into letting me visit her bedroom tonight," that's boring.

    Successful nonviolent games might be ones where there's little serious conflict at all, like Ryutama (the closest thing you'll find to a "My Neighbor Totoro" RPG) or Breaking The Ice, or where the conflict is pretty much unwinnable, as in the more truly Lovecraftian horror games like "Lovecraftesque".

    \nI'm not at all convinced this is true. Perhaps from a typical "gamer" mindset, modeling other kinds of conflict is unintuitive. But it really shouldn't be! Just look at card games and board games: they have no problems at all making proceduralized versions of financial competition (Monopoly), exploring a new land (Settlers of Catan), etc - with no "fighting" in sight. Pick your theme and go at it.

    From the other end of the spectrum, any story games with "generic conflict" mechanics (like Primetime Adventures, the Pool, etc) easily run the gamut of any and all conflicts.

    Recently, there's been development in more subtle game design, too, which is fun. Three games with a pretty interesting take on non-violent conflict (and in very different ways):

    Kagematsu

    Monsterhearts

    \n One thing I read recently was Beginning Idol, a Japanese RPG where (as one might infer from the name) you play as members of an idol singer group, trying to balance work, training, and interpersonal relationships. The challenges in the game are primarily (in the core rules, at least) based on determining how well you do your job or nail a performance, rather than being competitive/oppositional.

    Mechanically it does this by being a rather firmly-structured resource management game that forces you to make choices in preparation for a final big performance that's determined by a lot of fiddly dice rolls, wearing appropriate outfits, using special items, and/or invoking your friendships with your co-idols for various benefits while trying to avoid a total breakdown due to stress.

    \n This is the concept I'm trying to address directly with the game I'm currently developing. It's based around intricate description and imagination instead of characters and narrative. Characters and narrative are still present, and can be developed by players if they wish, but 90% of the mechanics center around description and imagination, so it shifts the whole mental mode of the game into a realm where violence doesn't even occur to the players, and there's no specific way to address it.

    To build tension in such a game, I'm trying things like keeping certain pieces of information at least partially hidden from players until a later point in the game, or restricting the kinds of conclusions that can be drawn until a certain stage of the process. The tension is driven more by mysteriousness and curiosity than fear or danger, which also helps downplay violence (and even conflict in general) while still holding the players' attention.

    \n Boy, all this conversation and NO mention of Golden Sky Stories? For shame! This game is far, FAR more "Totoro the RPG" than Ryuutama aspires to be. (C'mon, Ryuutama's battle system really isn't that unusual)

    The game is about playing animal spirits in and around a small Japanese town. And you... help people. With everyday problems. Help two friends who had an argument reconcile. Help someone who is frightened of taking the next step in their life come to terms with it. Ensure that the beloved local ramen shop has someone to take over when the current owner retires. It's diceless, with any sort of competition being settled by comparing stats and spending Feelings (A pool of points that you earn by having relationships with others!) to raise that stat for purposes of the test. "XP" is rewarded by other players in the form of "dreams" - rewards points that are supposed to be given out when another player does something that you feel helps someone, or that seems cute or heartwarming. Dreams are spent to strengthen your relationships, which gives you more Feelings (and more Wonder, which is similar to feelings, but used to fuel your magical powers).

    It technically does have a section on fighting, in which it tells you not to, how to resolve it if you do (just like any other test, with the loser being knocked down or run off) and how you are penalized for doing it (it harms your relationships).

    GSS makes me happy just to read it.

    \n This has been a very interesting conversation, and I feel like I've learned a lot from considering both sides of the discussion. I'd like to offer, if I can, what I think the misunderstanding is here. I will preface this by saying that I am not familiar with Golden Sky Stories, and am only attempting to address the examples given by Rickard.

    I think any situation in which time passes (and possibly even situations in which time does not pass) could be framed in a way that implies conflict. Even a tree standing in a field has conflict between its limbs and gravity. I think the search here is not for examples of situations in life or games in which conflict is categorically absent, but for situations in which resolution of that conflict is not the object of the exercise. Whether or not Golden Sky Stories is focused on resolution, I do think that Rickard's examples are adequate framings for situatiosn where resolution is not part of the exercise.

    If the object of a hypothetical game is to explore the thoughts and feelings of a man in an empty apartment waiting for a call, we could still point to some conflict in the situation, but the resolution of that conflict has no bearing on the game. Whether or not the call eventually comes, and what that might mean for his life, make no difference if the game is only about that time in which he is waiting. The same applies for the other examples as well. I think Airk has done a good job of pointing out that conflict is going to be at least implied in any situation, but maybe what Rickard is talking about is situations that aren't concerned with the resolution of that conflict.
