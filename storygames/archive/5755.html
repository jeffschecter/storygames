\n Over in the monster thread of doom I wrote:\n
I think one of the issues here is "when and where is the best place for criticism to happen?" In my mind, if criticism happens after the game has already had several hundred copies printed, that's far too late. Revisions and addendum and the like are all great, but not as good as having the criticism happen before games get commercially released. However, when people are not writing or designing their games in public, or when open playtests have only moderate participation, it's really hard for that to happen, yeah? I can think of a couple of really cool games in playtest right now, Anna's Thou Art But A Warrior and Brennan's How We Came To Live Here, and it seems like they've only got a few folks actively involved in playing and offering criticism before the games get declared "done" and go to layout and commercial release.

Honestly, that's where the tragedy is for me, not that criticism doesn't happen after games released (because then it's almost too late), but that people aren't taking it upon themselves to offer criticism before mistakes or oversights become harder to fix. For my money, I'd rather be involved in and see more criticism at that stage in the game.
\nSo what about actively organizing folks to do critical reads / playtests of games that are mostly finished but not yet published (or published games about to go in for a reprint, to help fix any remaining issues)? Prevenge instead of revenge, yeah?

    \n Brand, in the other thread, wrote...\n
    Sure, for those it works for that's awesome.

    But, and here is the rub, not everyone likes to work in public. Whats more, not everyone will produce their best results in public. Not everyone will get good feedback from "everyone." Which is one of the reasons, I think, we're seeing the fracture. As we get more games that are designed for Not You we will get more games about which your opinion or playtesting isn't going to be helpful. As we get more games designed for Not You we'll get more games that we just can't agree about.

    Like, IAWA has such a place in this thread because, I think, its a big dividing line. I loved it. I've actually played it. I've had actual rocking play with it. I also have something not always great to say about the layout of the CR section, but that's an issue few others have talked about or seem to care about. Others have played it and liked it, but only after a hard time. Others have played and it and not liked it -- sometimes for exactly the reasons I did play it.

    Should IAWA have been written better? Designed better?

    I dunno, better for who? Better in what way? Better for which committee of the community defining what quality is for which population?

    And no, I don't mean that as snotty. Because the design in public has had a very important function in the history of Indie RPG development -- it has led to a certain amount of community standard. It has lead to a kind of social vetting process by which members of the community let each other know before publication what they will and will not take. It has thus lead to a good standard.

    But that's breaking down, broken down. And now we've got games being designed in smaller pools, and possibly for smaller pools, but a lot of folks still want the rule and the standard of the old group.

    But just because we had it then and don't have it now doesn't mean its gone forever, or that returning to "everyone design in front of everyone else" is the only, or best, way to fix it. Still designing for review, still designing with others commenting and playtesting and pushing, is still good and still possible. It just happens in different groups, with different standards. And, for some people, missing the old group and not having found a new one, it doesn't happen at all.

    Which sucks. Like, especially when it happens across old tight lines. But I think the answer is not to try to rebuild the one community with the one core value, but to ride the wave and distribute it across lots of different communities. Accept that NerdSoCal and Nerd NYC and Toronto Indie are going to have different standards, and build up those community standards where you are (even if that is virtual), rather than looking to be excited and involved in every game from every group that has "indie" attached to its name.

    \n Jonathan, you have just made it clear to me that Paul and I have done a LOUSY job of explaining, to you at least and very likely to others, the purpose, scope and function of the Ashcan Front.

    So, let me quash that notion spit-spot. I repeat: It is what you are after here in this thread. That you don't think so dismays me greatly. It's a symptom that our project is failing.

    To whit:

    The Ashcan Front is NOT a project to examine mechanics exclusively. Of course it can also examine texts.

    The Ashcan Front is NOT a thing that exists a GenCon and then only again at the next GenCon. It is a year-round project of examining games-in-production, or pre-publication.

    The Ashcan Front is a lead-by-example kind of thing that says "Hey, gang, we think we don't do a good job of critiquing games and making them the best they can be.     In short, I see absolutley no distinction between this thing you're calling "prevenge" and this thing Paul and I call "Ashcan Front." The problem is obviously one of our poor communication and promotion. But, some of the problem is also with people unwilling or unaware they can get involved, for example, on the forums. We'd welcome it.

    Posted By: Matt Snyder
    In short, I see absolutley no distinction between this thing you're calling "prevenge" and this thing Paul and I call "Ashcan Front." The problem is obviously one of our poor communication and promotion. But, some of the problem is also with people unwilling or unaware they can get involved, for example, on the forums. We'd welcome it.
    \nOn the one hand, I was and am really excited about the Ashcan Front. As a testing-only participant, it's let me down in that what feedback I've given seems to have boiled off into vacuum (to whit: there's a superbly dominant strategy for chargen in A:.o:.E:. that I mentioned on the Front's forums and that Paul didn't acknowledge.) So, while, yes make the Front do more, make it reach out to more designers and all of that, also realize that there's different expectations.

    Also, the Front is publicized as covering a very special part of the lifecycle of a design: 90% done. Where the remaining 10% will take the other 90% of the time. I think there's a lot of utility to the sort of cross-pollenization that JR is talking about. Presenting a 30% done idea to your friends, and testing it in person to see what of it is worth keeping is one thing. Sending a 70% done design to other designers to test blind is another. Ashcans are something very else. They're all valuable processes.

    The other part that I'm objecting to as I see it (and maybe it's not even showing up quite in this discussion) is the idea that criticism after publication is too late. That's bollocks. It might (emphasis on "might") be too late for this book. But if it encourages someone else to do a further cycle of testing, or to have another pair of eyes or a new table take a look at their game, it wins.

    \n Hey Jonathan,\n
    Posted By: Jonathan Walton"Hi Brennan! I saw your post about playtestingHow We Came to Live Here. I mentioned this to people on the Ashcan Front forum and it looks like there are three folks interested in reading and commenting on your text and two folks who are interested in running playtests with their home groups. Are you interested in coming and talking to us about your goals for the game and how we can best help you?"
    \nThis would be possible if we had a community of folks just champing to do the hard work of reading and playtesting and critiquing games, and for someone like me or Matt to read Brennan's post about his game or half-formed rules write-up and somehow be more effective at representing it to folks and creating enthusiasm for it than Brennan himself. I don't think either of these things is generally true (though I'm sure the latter happens sometimes, via serendipity). The Ashcan Front is actually founded on belief in the reverse. That the landscape of games-in-development is crowded, increasingly so, and so you're going to have to dial up your efforts and fight for reading, playtesting and critiquing of your game if you're going to get it. You need to package your game-in-development in a way that helps people realize they want you to succeed and that they're willing to put time into making sure you do. You need to present your game-in-development in a way that helps them help you. And you, not me, are best able to do that.

    I spent a couple of months contacting folks early last year. And it was with that message. (Ask Judd. Ask Clyde.) I wish it were the other way around, and we did have a community champing for doing the hard work. But I think the reason we have this conversation every year about too many half-baked games is because efforts built on what we ought to be doing as a community (providing honest critique, doing more pre-publication reading and feedback) haven't been successful. Well, The Ashcan Front isn't going to eliminate that in six months. But I'll say this, I will be quite surprised if any of the ashcan games from the booth last year is pilloried as a half-baked game once it's done and published.

    When Matt says The Ashcan Front isn't a Gen Con project, what he's saying is that it's a year-round cultural project: You don't have to wait until Gen Con to do your game as an ashcan. You don't have to wait until Gen Con to use market forces and presentation to fight for reading, playtesting, and critiquing of your game-in-development. Ask Eric Provost.

    Paul

    \n I'm going to point out an obvious thing here.

    I've championed for better editing and structuring of text for years, and I've come to believe that many - perhaps most - designers don't actually want external criticism. They want to playtest with their friends and spouses, have other friends and spouses* read their games, and then put them out there. This is what people do. It's not for lack of people willing to read and criticize their work prior to publication. There are other reasons; I don't know what they are.

    During the last three years, I've offered plenty of people help - both in public and in private. Some were designers who'd published games that badly needed editing, and who were about to start work on second editions. The only person to ever take me up on my offer was Adam Dray, back in 2005.

    Being a writer is to accept that your text - all raw text - needs rewriting. Being a serious writer is to accept that you can't do that alone. And, in this case, "alone" includes "with your best friend and their best friend". As long as people don't want to be serious about what they do, there's not much to be done.

    * A lot of spouses there, yes. I'm not sure if that's even legal.
