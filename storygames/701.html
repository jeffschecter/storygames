An observation about art and gaming

CPXB
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
I had a thought about gaming as art.

I mostly accept that role-playing can be an art. But it seems to me, then, a lot of people seem to confuse changes with the medium with changes in the art -- that new rules allow people to create better art.

I disagree. It's like saying that CGI is objectively better than a Renaissance oil painting based on the fact that a computer was involved in the first. I think that it is important for gamers to remember that folks playing blue box D&D are creating as legitimate an artistic experience as someone playing in Polaris -- that changes in the medium allow people to express themselves differently, but that those differences do not constitute superiority in an artistic sense. That Polaris isn't better than blue box D&D, it's just different -- just as computer graphics are not inherently artistically superior to oil paintings or TV is an artistic improvement over stage theater.

Which might not apply to anyone here, but I felt the urge to say it where someone else might actually hear it. ;)

    CPXB:

    Improvements in technology and technique lead to improvement in the quality of the artistry, across the universe of portrayal. Each new medium is not so much building higher than the first, it's building in a new direction.

    Let's get out of the static arts and get into the lively arts, because roleplaying is a lively art.

    Let's say you want to put on a play, let's say, Cats.

    On the one hand, you have a Grecian amphitheater, masks, drums, trumpets, and the human voice. No sets, no costumes besides featureless robes, no lighting besides sunlight. It'll be really cheap to make though.

    On the other, you have a modern stage, makeup, costumes, lighting, orchestra, and sets. You'll spend lots of money on it.

    If the experience of the first is as "good" as the experience of the second, why do they go to all the trouble and expense of using all the modern tools? After all, art transcends such worldly concerns, doesn't it?

    The fact is, people DO want art created with the most modern tools and techniques. Producers and directors want to have the entire toolbox available to them. Yes, they put on plays using less than the whole kit of tricks, but they usually take at least one or two of the best tools out at some point.

    Art people like is better.

    Art people spend money on is art people like.
