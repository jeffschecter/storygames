So in one of Matt's threads, he talks about the perception people have of gamers, and bringing new people into gaming, etc.

I'm going to tell a story about getting "outed" as a gamer last Spring, which was really humiliating. But first I want to say my purpose of the thread is as follows:

1. For people to discuss being "out" as gamers, being outed, etc. based on personal experience. (I'm sure everyone has an opinion on this, or can invent one with a few moments' notice, but I don't want empirical signal to be lost in theoretical noise.)

2. To discuss attemps to bring new people into gaming, which actually worked based on personal experience (ditto.)

By the way, my underlying theory here is that I don't care who games, with whom, what games they play or how. Basically, the reason I want to bring new people into gaming is that I enjoy it, and I wish I could play more often, and had more people to play with. Whether this dilutes the Elite Awesome Cult of Gaming Hobbyists, or whether it has economic impacts for designers, or is good for POD publishing: for me, personally, as a gamer, I just want to play a good game once every other week, you know? And I don't want to get weird stares about doing it.

=====
Okay, so this is me getting outed. Late April 2006.

I'm about to turn 30. I've been out of college several years, finishing up my first year of law school. Most of my classmates are a couple years younger than me, and, by and large, most of them are pretty cool people once you get to know them. (There are a few raging pricks, but nowhere near as many as I'd feared, considering that it's law school.)

So, my whole life, I've been the nerd with Charisma 13: just enough to be get along with everyone, but still unmistakably a member of the nerd race (+2 to saves vs. STD's, -2 to spot checks without corrective lenses). Coming to law school, I'm like, "All these people are over-achieving dorks. I'll be among my own kind."

But no, it turns out that even among nerds, there are nerds, and I'm one of 'em. I like talking to people if we've got something in common, but I'm no good at parties, and prefer to get to know strangers over time. (It turns out people notice this about you!) Also, apparently I know more stuff than the average bear. (It turns out people notice this too.)

Anyway, so I'm chatting with people during a smoking break. We've got Goldberg, the class president, who's openly gay; Fernandez, the Scalia-worshipping goofball; and Shlimmy, on whom I've got kind of a casual crush in the already-in-a-wonderful-relationship sort of way. These are all people I get along with, and are probably my best acquaintance-friends in law school.

GOLDBERG: James, did you ever play Dungeons & Dragons?

ME: (Fuck. Fuck fuck fuck.) Um--

GOLDBERG: --'cuz it seems like you would have been the type.

ME: Yeah, as a kid. It was fun. (don't mention that I've spent most of the last six classes of Civ Pro flipping around in the on-line SRD--the mounted combat feat tree is awesome)

SHLIMMY: (smokes; gazes at us; gazes into the distance)

GOLDBERG: I remember hearing about that from other kids, you know? That it had, like, twenty-sided dice.

FERNANDEZ: All kinds, man. Four-sided, eight-sided. (to me) I think the box had a twelve-sided one too, right? With the little crayon?

ME: Yeah... But it was kind of stupid. (Don't mention that in 8 hours I'm going to be playing Sorcerer with Jon and Eric, and I'm super excited about it)

FERNANDEZ: Oh, I don't know, it was kind of fun.

GOLDBERG: Twenty sided dice.... (shaking his head)

SHLIMMY: (snubs out cigarette)

    Levi Kornelsen wrote (I think on rpg.net somewhere) once about how most of the stigma was, in effect, self-inflicted. This is plainly visible to me in James' story. So when I changed jobs in February 2006, I decided I was going to be totally open about the fact that I game. Now, granted, I'm a software engineer, and although none of my immediate co-workers were gamers (see below), they're all computer nerds, and several played World of Warcraft or similar fantasy computer games. So I admit it wasn't as threatening an environment as, say, law school.

    Point is, when people would ask me what I was going to do over the weekend, I'd flat out tell them I was running an RPG, and then we'd talk about what that was and their experiences with people who they knew who shared the hobby. This led to curiosity, and two of them recently joined our game nights. Intercube talk is now RPG-geekery in addition to all the other geekery it was before. A couple of other people around us are weird about trying it with my other players they don't know, but we stopped by a game store at lunch, looked at a bunch of RPGs, and they settled on Spycraft 2.0, which will have its first game in a conference room at the office (after working hours) sometime after the new year (I'll GM, with at least 4 players who are either totally new to the hobby since they met me, or haven't played in 15 years).

    Maybe this is the exception. There will always be the creepy guy who wants to tell somebody who doesnt care all about his character. I was a missionary in LA for two years - I think I can tell when somebody doesn't care about what I'm trying to say. So be honest about the fun you're having, inclusive and nonthreatening, and treat it just like you were talking about fantasy football or golf; treat it as though its a totally normal hobby to bring up. If you're awkward about it, or creepy, that's self-inflicted social stigma as far as I'm concerned. If they're awkward or weird about it, maybe they'll change their minds when they see somebody 'normal' who's not afraid to talk about the hobby.

    The idea that its only for weirdos can only persist if only weirdos admit to the hobby. Thats my thoughts, and my story.

    So, like, I used to have trouble outing myself about anything. I was afraid to be a gamer, a nerd, a feminist, a queer, a good citizen.

    Honestly, I would cover up my tracks when volunteering, because at school I'd hear people say "man, 30 hours of community service, this blows. Stupid graduation requirements." And I'd be like, "Hm. I better not mention that I am planning to spend roughly 30 hours THIS WEEK ALONE doing volunteer activities. Keep that hush, hush."

    Because, you know, when I mentioned shit I was involved in I was called a goody-goody two-shoes, or an overachiever, or I was told that I was "doing bitch work for free". ...Similarly, I was afraid to admit that I was queer, because I'd get the shit kicked out of me. I was afraid to be a gamer, lest I be ridiculed. To summarize: high school is a ridiculously vicious place.

    The thing is, I hated being afraid to own my identity. It was stupid. The kind of people who were going to judge me are the kind of people I'd prefer to single out and avoid anyways.

    So, here's how I approached that conversation before:

    Him: So, are you like a D&D player?
    Me: Um... urr.. no. I mean, I was once. Boy, that was dumb.
    Him: Yeah. d&d is for snivelling losers who don't get laid.
    Me: *snivels, like a loser who doesn't get laid, all the while dying on the inside a bit*

    And now, the approach I take:

    Him: So, are you like a D&D player?
    Me: Yeah. I'm huge into games. I played D&D for a long time.
    Him: Oh yeah. ... Cool. (note that this "cool" is lukewarm)
    Me: *nods* I play a lot of different games now though - both roleplaying and like, card games and such.
    Him: Cool. (note that this "cool" is cool)

    So, here are the things I do when someone talks to me about games:

    1.) Build on what they say. Note in the example above, even though I no longer play d&d, I used to, so saying yes is a building block. It bridges what they are (at least, marginally) familiar with, with what I want to talk about. "Yes, and..." tactics.
    "Do you X?"
    "Yes, and a lot of Y and Z too. More Y and Z than X, but all the letters in the alphabet are cool."

    2.) Go with their lead. Sometimes someone asks me about d&d and RPGs, and then uses that as a diving board to RISK. Even though the two conversations are entirely different and they just went into a gigantic tangent, my immediate response is, "oh, man, RISK! I used to play that game all the time with my friends." And if they started talking about tactics, I'd talk about tactics. If they talked about conquering nations, I'd bridge back into the discussion of RPGs, with this AP-kind of narration of a game I'd played that focused on conquering nations and war.
    "Yeah, but the games I play are nothing like d&d" or "yeah, but that's not the same at all" are both conversation killers are dividers. Thus, I avoid if possible.

    3.) Remember that too many details can kill inquiry. Answer in a way that feels honest, like you want to talk about whatever is being inquired about, but don't go into great length unless you know people are genuinely interested in that. If someone says, "...like d&d?", my general reply is "yeah, in the sense that we are doing collective storytelling and roleplaying. I mean, the focus and the social dynamics are different, but if you understand how d&d works then it wouldn't be foreign to you." The answer doesn't feel 100% to me, but the 100% answer would kill their inquiry dead. They'd be like, "mhmm. Okay. Right. Let's talk about something else, because you shut down my attempt to connect with your experience."

    So, yeah. That's what works for me, both in sharing my hobbies and cultivating interest and inquiry. This isn't an RPG-specific thing, it's part of how I communicate with people about my interests in general.
