\n I was thinking today about large monsters in D&D and other RPGs and how D&D kinda set the standard for "more bulk/size/flesh means more hit points". Or at least "can take more hits".

In breaking that assumption in design today, I decided to play with the idea that bulk means adventurers simply need to figure out what means or capability will let them wound such a creature. How to get past the dragon scales, or the dinosaur hide, or whatever; the creature might actually take the same "number of blows" to go down as most things once it's been smacked a good one.

And that led me to this idea about problem solving in a fantasy sandbox; adventurers acquiring the means to do the things they want. Because I was thinking "if I put a dragon to guard the treasure, I should include in the notes what's needed to get past its scales." and then my next thought was "do I really?"

I mean, is there anything wrong with throwing a bunch of scary crap in the dungeon and letting the players try to figure it out for you? (And letting them, obv)

    \n It's a wonderful technique, and, in my opinion, one of the driving forces of successful OSR play.

    However, as a GM/designer you need to consider very carefully how you will deal with "edge cases". Dropping a block the size of a Buick on the dragon should probably deal damage to it, sure. How about just throwing a brick at it? What if it's thrown at the head? What about dropping that same stone block on its tail? Etc.

    How you deal with those situations and what calls you make should be handled in a principled way, and informs how the game plays.

    \nThey take a similar approach but there is structure to it. So for example the lycanthropes handbook has a number of different kinds of lycanthropes, but also goes into things like giving some of your were creatures unique weaknesses (i.e. a werewolf that is not hurt by silver but some other substance specific to its personal history). The books basically give you tools to customize your monsters.

    This idea of tying the monster's weakness to its past plays out in many of the books, which in my experience was an excellent premise for adventures. So if you have the party trying to deal with a flesh golem menacing a small town, they might find it nearly impossible to kill unless they do some investigating into its background or into local legends about it. This works well for ghost adventures, vampire adventures, mummy adventures (The Guide to Ancient Dead is particularly good), liches, etc.

    \nI think this approach can work. Some players dislike it though if you allow them to effectively create the solution (so know your players). Another option is to devise the solution, have some idea of how they might discover the solution, but also just be open minded. My rule of thumb in any investigation is if the PC pursue a course of action that would plausibly yield a particular clue, let them have it. This comes up all the time when I run police procedural stuff because even though I may know what evidence is out there and the details of the crime, players have so many resources to draw on and can triangulate clues in ways you hadn't imagined before play.

    \n I generally think that not figuring out a solution in advance is a fine design practice for challengeful sandbox adventuring. The only lurking issue lies in protecting players from arbitrary loss to what proves to be an unsolvable challenge, and that won't be a problem as long as they can opt out of the adventure when they conclude that it is unsolvable. Alternatively, making the unsolvable adventure a procedurally random event also helps, as then it's the luck of the dice rather than the GM determining your fate.

    For a concrete example, let's assume that encountering a vampire at night proves an impossible challenge for a given party in a given campaign setting with a given sort of vampire in it. My stance is that putting that vampire into a dungeon the adventurers are not forced to explore is fine, and putting the vampire in the random encounter table is fine. However, it is not fine to just decide to force the vampire encounter on the party; at that point you've essentially taken the responsibility for the game being fun on yourself, which is a pretty shitty position to be in if it proves that the vampire cannot be beaten. That's where the worry about encounter challenge ratings comes from, a GM considering themselves responsible for the outcomes of encounters. Don't put yourself into that position of being responsible for an encounter, and you don't need to care about whether it is winnable or not.

    \n I'll expand a little more. So what I'm talking about here is a design where when the GM wants to prepare a monster, the sum of her preparation is to name the monster and write about it in plain language, along with some bullet point facts. There would be no "stats" per se because the rules explain what to do with facts, and the GM is aware of her remaining assumptions about her creations. Example:

    Dragon
    The dragon is very old, gigantic and a decrepit green color, covered in slimy patches. It has one horn on its head. It's about the size of the longhouse in the village the adventurers come from. It is paranoid about its treasure, and will only speak with intruders long enough to eat them or scare them away. Nobody remembers when it moved into the cave; to the people nearby it's always lived here. Anyone examining it will notice the blood red veins in its wings. Someone with nature or animal lore will realize it means the wings are possibly not in good enough shape for flying.
    * Can eat people in a single bite
    * Can grab with talons
    * Can't fly anymore (will not share this secret with others)
    * Can fill a quarter of the cave with poison gas which causes a retching and writhing sickness that ends about a minute after leaving the gas
    * Has a single layer of scales that are impervious to swords, axes and arrows

    \n Yeah, this is the direction my own D&D development has been going for several years; if the specific mechanical procedures of the game cannot engineer the relevant statistics out of the particulars of the fiction, then there's a problem with the mechanics.     \n I find that simple descriptive action resolution systems and a flexible set of character stats work best for this type of approach, enabling you to easily determine the type of roll or action necessary to adjudicate an action, no matter what sort of weird idea the players come up with.

    The more flexible the core mechanic is, the more applicable your resolution system is to any type of action, the less planning you need to do, and the more freedom the players have in making up responses to fictional difficulties. This not only increases the feeling of character proficiency, but it increases the chances of surprising each other with creative solutions.

    I will add:

    I often think that there's a subtle psychological effect at play when a player (any player, but in this case particular a GM) already has a certain "solution" to a challenge in mind.

    That person is much more likely to undervalue, reject, or be skeptical to other solutions.

    I feel, as a GM in this style of game, that coming up with a challenge (and not thinking about solutions at all) is a good hygienic practice (as Eero would say), and try to do so as much as possible.

    When adjudicating possible approaches and solutions, then, you can afford to keep an open mind and allow yourself to be biased in the players' favour (in cases where you don't feel total impartiality is possible; that's the goal otherwise) and everyone gets to play a fun "solve the puzzle through creative thinking" game and gets to be surprised by the outcome.

    Much more fun than the dynamic we sometimes see, where the GM is sitting there bored (because the players aren't making the "right" guesses) and the players are frustrated out of their skulls because they can't read the GM's mind and don't know what to do.

    \n I disagree that you should not come up with a solution. I think that you should ALWAYS come up with _A_ solution. Not "the" solution, but you should always come up with something that would work and is not completely batcrap insane, just as a way to verify that there IS something. Designing a sort of "sample" solution to a problem can help avoid this, and also means that if the players "punt" and go "Okay, we want to go to talk to the ancient sage who knows all about this " then you have some details to give them.

    \nI don't know about Simulationist, but I tend to think of sandbox as meaning players are dropped into a setting that they explore freely from the point of view of their characters. The GM's role is to breath life into the setting, create a sense that they are somewhere real and that their choices matter. I also think of investigations as contained sandboxes.

    I too find stuff like fail forward doesn't really jive with how I like to run or experience sandboxes. To me, while I know lots of people who enjoy fail forward, it suggests a storyline progression or an inability to truly fail, which for my taste is important. I don't want to play India Jones or feel like I am literally a character in The Temple of Doom or Raiders of the Lost Arc who is destined to get the treasure and the girl. Rather I want to feel like I am a character in a living breathing setting whose destiny is something of a blank canvass.

    That said, I think keeping the game interesting (not failure itself) is totally compatible with sandbox play. All that means is you have active NPCs, groups, other things in the mix that keep the game enjoyable.

    \n I'm sortof going to disagree with your assertion that fail-forward implies a story, but the methods that don't imply a story tend to screw with the simulationism (yes, I'm sticking to this) of a "sandbox"; Namely, in a proper sandbox, if you are picking the lock to a door, you can work on it for some amount of time before the guard patrol comes by, and the GM has figured that out because it's part of your sandbox. To me, this seems like strange space to be in, but it's one that many people playing "sandbox" games seem to land themselves in.

    \n I've interestingly encountered this puzzle paralysis phenomenon in investigative railroad gaming (Call of Cthulhu, to be precise), but never in a sandbox. I believe this to be the case because of the way a sandbox GM takes responsibility for the setting conceits: they know who built this endless starcase, they know what manner of themes and cosmological constants are behind its magical (?) operation. They have a failure-mode at the ready, which in this case means encouraging the group to give up and starve to death so the game can continue with new characters doing something else.

    For what it's worth, that infinite staircase is, to me, a completely everyday occurrence for a challengeful sandbox game. That's the sort of shit you encounter with crazy wizards and capricious gods. Knowing that there is a solution in advance would do nothing for the way we put sandboxes together around here. As Paul says, the only questionable things I see in this scenario are that I'm not certain the GM knew enough about the underpinnings of their trap to judge solutions appropriately, and the group clearly didn't know how to execute a failure mode - give up, describe a blood-curdling end as the water runs out for the desperate adventurers, scribe a final warning message on a wall, and die.

    (Incidentally: if I were a crazy wizard building an infinite staircase, I wouldn't include a "solution" either. Chances are that you're simply just fucked if you fall into something like that, intended to hold you. On the other hand, if it's an incidental property of a multi-dimensional mansion or whatever, perhaps there is hope yet. The GM's gotta know these things to be able to judge what might work and what might not.)

    As for "failure modes", let's consider that for a bit. I've been using that term to describe what happens in an adventure game when the adventurers lose. The failure mode of the game begins to initialize when things start going awry, and it properly starts when the players acknowledge defeat. The failure mode consists of the way the players construe their feelings for defeat, what they do as a follow-up in terms of the play process, and how the game goes forward after the failure, if it does. As part of my personal conviction that there is something rotten in rpg land, I happen to think that many traditional games have awful, horribly dysfunctional failure modes, and that contributes hugely to the double-think behind GM cheating and railroading and all that stuff.

    So, the basic question with failure modes for me is, what does a good failure mode look like? The kind of failure mode that enables the group of adventure rpg players to actually have failures in their game, instead of shying away and having the GM overtly or covertly cheat out of trouble. There are different strategies, but here's what the failure mode looks like in my more-or-less by the book old school D&D homebrew:

    * Character generation is fast (as in, it takes a couple of minutes), and a high rate of fatalities is acknowledged from the first overtly with an amused attitude of "morituri te salutant" and all that. Character stables, character retirement, episodic play and all the other adaptive measures that support high rate of fatality are in full effect.
    * The same objective rules apply to NPCs and PCs, and there is an universal saving throw mechanic for that little bit of extra uncertainty in the face of certain death. Judgements are fast, precise and lethal: fail that save, and in the lava pool you go. Everybody knows the rules, everybody applies the rules, the GM has no shadows in which to prevaricate. You know when you are supposed to die.
    * The question in ambiguously dangerous situations is quite explicitly whether win or loss is to be had, not "how you win". This means that play is goal-oriented, and ambiguity is reduced to resolution processes. Everybody is engaged by the challenge, the possibility of victory or defeat.
    * If a character is rendered un-adventurable by e.g. a case of early death, the group commiserates with the player, they smile and laugh bravely to prove their valiance, and generate a new character to join back in the fray. New characters are introduced without fuzz or muss, as the party has a lively revolving door anyway due to the changing player line-ups from session to session, and the high mortality rate. You'll soon be in play with a new character.
    * The few successful adventurers inspire the group, who know that success in this endeavour of heroes and fools is indeed possible for the lucky and the brave. Such characters may retire from play specifically to enjoy their fictional largesse, and to avoid a horrible death in the next adventure.
    * Should the entire adventure fail instead of mere few fatalities on the way, the party acknowledges this and the GM crows about in mock satisfaction at their failure. Then he offers new adventure hooks, and integrates the failure and its consequences in the sandbox setting, creating more depth and detail from failures as well as successes.
    * Should the entire party die to some total fuck-up, everybody laughs uproariously and goes into an immediate post-emergency analysis of what, exactly, went so very wrong. The campaign might end at this point if we are satisfied with our exploration of this milieu and there aren't any on-going plots or operative characters left, but more likely we just start again with new characters, possibly tackling the same adventure that killed the last batch.

    As can be seen, the failure mode in our homebrew D&D consists of a quick character generation cycle, acknowledgement of the possibility of defeat at all levels, a certain attitude of machismo, and the idea that the best way to get over defeat is to get back in the saddle and try again. I'd like to specifically highlight that the expectation of defeat combined with the goal-oriented play causes the entire group to move head-first towards the imminent resolutions of situations instead of shying away and fiddling about, trying to postpone the inevitable. The morbid tone itself helps prepare players psychologically, and derive joy from both success and failure. For this type of game psychology the idea of cheating death is loathsome, as it casts a stain on the honor of every achievement ever made in the game - you have to valiantly take the blows of defeat for success to truly mean anything.

    Maybe an example is in order. Here's an adventure I wrote for last year's OPD contest. It was inspired by a dead character, as described in the adventure text. I think you'd agree that it is quite puzzle-heavy, insofar as such things go.

    However, the primary consideration while writing that adventure was evaluating whether the gauntlet described in it fulfills the personal standards and goals of the NPC who developed the challenge in the first place. I never attempted to evaluate the difficulty level of the adventure against an ideal player's capabilities; it is what it is, and it'll mostly depend on the skill level of players whether it is "easy" or "hard" for them. I would expect hard, but that is not a primary concern for me in writing the piece.

    This example is somewhat meta in that the adventure itself contains a NPC who is creating a puzzle, so in that sense there is indeed a puzzle in there. However, I write adventures without explicit in-fiction puzzles the same way, merely figuring out what makes sense for the setting and what is the genuine challenge proposition that the fictional environment has to offer. An adventure needs to front the challenge it offers, but that challenge does not need to be calibrated to be a good challenge for a sandbox adventure game.\n
    The whole "we play our game different" thing is actually pretty much irrelevant, because we're assuming that everyone mostly agrees on how the game "should" go. If that involves 40 sessions of level 1 character death, and that's fun, enjoy. It sounds awful to me, but I'm not playing, so my opinion on that really doesn't matter. The point being here that you should use this simple check to verify what sort of "puzzle" you have created.
    \nIt would be an awful misunderstanding of my position to think that I want or plan for a game to feature e.g. 40 sessions of character death. It is a thing that may occur, but that occurrence is an outcome of our dynamic creative process at the gaming table, and emphatically not something planned for by the GM. The mind-set is entirely different, the GM comes to the table with no preconceptions about what should happen when the rubber hits the road - when the other players hit his scenario, that is.\n
    Edit: I guess it's possible that you might just not care what kind of puzzle it is, but that seems like it's the least efficient approach here, since you're not even checking whether it makes sense in the game world.
    \nI do not care about predicting the course of events at the game table before the game is played. I do care about creating compelling and internally consistent fictional material. I believe that we can agree that it is possible to care about making sense in the game world without also caring about whether players might be able to overcome the challenge.

    \n Airk,

    I think Eero's point here is not so much that "how we play our game differently" (as you put it) is relevant to your argument (although I'd say it is), but that it is *absolutely* relevant to what this thread is asking about. He's responding to the general topic moreso than your particular point. The OP is clearly asking about this very issue.

    As for figuring out solutions to puzzles, I'll say that my own experience has been the opposite of yours:

    * Coming up with a solution ahead of time tends to make it more likely that the GM (or any other player) is a) less open to other solutions (this is a subconscious effect, note, and probably operates even if you're aware of it, but is of course even worse if you are not) and b) is more likely to cause the kind of un-fun situation in play that you're describing from your own game experience.

    * Rather, it is better to spend time thinking about the fictional details of the trap or puzzle itself. Who put it here? How is it constructed? How, exactly, does it operate? What are its strengths and what are its weaknesses?

    This is what we're missing with the "unending staircase" trap. We have no idea how it operates, what its purpose is, or, really, any details about its function.

    In my opinion (and I think Eero was talking about the same thing - correct me if I'm wrong, Eero), the GM's fault here was not in failing to think up a solution, but in failing to think up any fictional details about the trap itself. It's the equivalent of saying, "There is a room, and if anyone enters, they die." Without having some idea of what causes this to happen, what triggers it, whether it can be reset, and so forth, the GM has no way of judging a potential solution (e.g. what happens if someone sends in a illusory person first, and then walks in while invisible?).

    However, you do make a good point: if there is some kind of absolutely unbeatable trap or puzzle in a game, AND your game is premised on success always being possible, perhaps it's a good idea to give a thought as to whether the trap is solvable at all.

    I can see this coming up, in particular, with someone using a pre-designed module. For instance, if I was handed an adventure with an unending staircase, AND I didn't want any "certain failure" scenarios in my particular game (which is a very reasonable constraint for most games, I think, hardcore-play as described here notwithstanding) I'd want to give it some serious thought before I put that module into play.

    * Who put this here?
    * What is its purpose?
    * How, exactly, does it function?
    * What are its limitations? (For instance, maybe it works by "teleporting" anyone who reaches a certain point on the stairs back to the bottom, and this teleportation uses up energy from a finite source.)
    * Did the creator intend for it to be a hopeless deathtrap, or did she have a backup plan for what to do if she ended up caught in it herself? (i.e. is there a "backdoor"?)
    Etc.

    Otherwise, you need to give a thought about WHY you're so interested in including an "unsolvable" puzzle in your game in the first place. What's the purpose of this game-piece?

    In this sense, of course, we are in complete agreement, just approaching the issue from different angles.

    But, overall, "think about how your puzzle is constructed in enough detail to be able to judge potential solutions, telegraph danger level in a way which matches your players' expectations, and be overly generous in assigning odds of success to any player idea" is a very functional and robust way to approach this kind of thing.

    \n On a sidenote, I ended up chatting with Tavis Allison today, who is the creator of ACKS and a well-known sandbox expert. He rarely posts at Story Games, but has given this style of gaming a great deal of thought. Unsurprisingly, he had some very smart (and economical) things to say on this subject. He said he wouldn't mind if I shared them here, so here's another take on this whole thing:\n

    * I'd frequently use problems without knowing the solution, but I also give a lot of leeway for improvisation and flexibility in adjucating a situation. An unsolvable problem would only be a no-no if there was no latitude to work it out in play.

    * To look at the most common case, I think it's widely accepted in the OSR that if you put a monster that can only be hit by magic weapons in a dungeon, your obligation is not to place a magic weapon elsewhere in the dungeon - rather it's to ensure that the campaign is not a railroad that will be halted by the inability to get past that monster.

    * Extending that analogy, I think most people would feel that it violated a sandbox contract to put in a monster that could only be defeated by "I don't know what, I'll choose what works when the players seem like they're getting frustrated by not being able to beat it" - this negates the meaningfulness of the challenge, and is basically part of railroad thinking.

    * I'd be OK with, for example, a monster that could only be defeated by interrupting its ability to draw magical energy from its environment - I wouldn't feel the need to work out in advance exactly how the players would do that, it doesn't feel like baseless fiat to decide whether an attempt to do so would or wouldn't succeed.

    I'd put more thought into making sure there were ways for the players to learn how this monster works - I feel it's more important to make sure that the players have informed choices than to predict a decision tree at each point.

    * I'm totally OK with "if you stick your head into the demon's mouth it will be annihilated, no saving throw." The same caveats about latitude and railroad apply. It would be poor form if the player says "I put a stick in the mouth" and you say "It sucks you in, you die!" - there needs to be freedom to investigate. And the campaign needs to have rolling up a new PC be a valid option, not a derailment.

    * I'm with you on wanting to signal that sudden death is on the line. Once you put a few annihilators into a campaign the players will start looking for hints, but I enjoy play more if there is a way to mutually engage and dis-engage pixel-bitching mode without breaking immersion.
    \nI mentioned that we'd been having a discussion about these issues on Story Games, and briefly summarized some of the questions that came up in this thread thus:

    "One poster brought up an example of a 'neverending staircase'. Once you get on the stairs, you can't get off, you just keep climbing or descending and you never get anywhere.

    What do you think of something like that in a game?

    The group played for hours and couldn't solve the puzzle, so they all left frustrated.

    We had three potential solutions identified (not necessarily compatible):
    1. Play in a style where total failure is acceptable. i.e. the group decides, "Ok, we failed. Let's roll up new characters!"
    (This should be clearly communicated before you even play, of course.)
    2. The GM should give some deliberate thought to the fictional details of the trap/puzzle, so as to be able to judge possible solutions (and should judge very generously when potential solutions are suggested), so as to keep the game interesting for everyone.
    3. The GM should think up at least one specific solution to the puzzle beforehand.

    I'm curious which seems more functional or more familiar to you in your gaming experience."

    His responses:\n

    * The general rule is don't be a dick. Playing for hours while stuck on a gotcha trap, I gotta think the DM failed this rule.

    * Your solution #2 seems most functional in this case. It's a staircase and we all know some stuff about staircases; does climbing the walls get you off the magic escalator? Could a 50' rope get past the part of the stairs that magically repeats? Etc.

    As long as the situation is concrete, like stairs, I'd feel OK figuring out these details in play.

    * #1 I'd want to use in a situation where it's clear that you failed - this trap is bad for that because if you narrate "you climb stairs until your water runs out and you die" the players endlessly want to say "wait before then I try this and that!"

    * #3 seems to me to lead in the direction of a railroad, better to have leeway for many solutions because the tendency will be to steer players toward the one you pre-determined.

    * I would totally put this into a dungeon, but for publication I'd want a sidebar talking about how to keep it from being un-fun. The Swords & Wizardry starter dungeon was good at this.
    \nFor what it's worth. I thought his points about "giving the group latitude to work out in play", "making sure the players have meaningful choices, rather than attempting to predict every branch on a decision tree", and, of course "don't be a dick" (i.e. remember the social context of the game, and make sure everyone accepts the responsibility to keep it fun) were particularly interesting and on point.

    Tavis has a knack for explaining this kind of thing in simple terms. (Not surprising, given the amount of time he's spent thinking about these issues, as an OSR advocate in general and a professional publisher of a top-tier game in this playstyle in particular.)

    \n I'm curious:

    Is that something you've had come up in a gaming situation? (@Deliverator? Maybe you have?)

    I'm only asking because I've never had that experience; it sounds very foreign to me.

    When an "unsolvable" challenge has come up in a game I'm playing, it's been generally due to:

    1) a GM trying too hard to block off all possible avenues of tackling it ("Oh, and the walls are anti-magic, too, of course!") during the design stage, or
    2) the GM is concerned with the difficulty of the challenge ("it must be hard enough!") and blocks particular solutions the players come up with during play itself (the scenario Tavis references above, near the top of my last post - the GM is unwilling to accept possible solutions because she feels the timing isn't right for the challenge to be circumvented just yet), or
    3) the GM has a particular solution in mind, but the players are unable to guess what it is.

    (#1 can be another unintended consequence of trying to come up with solutions, in my experience: "Oh, crap! My puzzle can be circumvented with a 'Stone to Mud' spell. I'd better add an automatic 'Dispel Magic' enchantment, then... oh, and I guess I'll need a creature who's immune to normal weapons so the fighter can't just lift the gate...". This kind of thinking is dangerous, in my experience. I much prefer an approach where the GM devises logical challenges without taking into consideration specific character abilities, and lets gameplay progress naturally from there.)

    There is, of course, also:

    4) The challenge is solvable, but the characters do not have the mechanical leverage to do so. (For example, a dragon with 500 hit points and an AC of -10: the gang of 1st-level characters will almost certainly not be able to defeat it, but of course the challenge remains solvable in principle, just too difficult for this particular bunch.)

    It seems to me that most sandbox-style games accept this without a problem.

    It's never happened to me to come up with a challenge that sounds doable and then only later realize that it has no solution; that I've accidentally created an unsolvable challenge. (Particularly in a sandbox situation; of course there's nothing wrong with a challenge that is too difficult for the players - the issue here is about a challenge which is unsolvable in principle.)

    I suppose that the advice to "see if there is any possible solution at all" only makes sense if it takes into account the tools available to the players: "Can the players, given these characters and these particular powers, solve this problem?"

    That, again, sounds like a slippery slope to me. I much prefer a playstyle where that isn't a consideration. (But, of course, it will be for some people and some games.)

    However!

    I should admit that I find the very idea of a truly unsolvable challenge to be a fantastic proposition:

    In the real world, there is no such thing as an unsolvable challenge. (For instance, you cannot build a prison that no one, given unlimited resourcefulness and attempts, could not escape from.)

    Unless we're dealing with quantum mechanics, a philosophical conundrum, or something similarly far afield (perhaps a time travel paradox?), any logically-coherent puzzle will have some kind of solution, even if it comes to down to simple brute force destruction.

    This is especially the case in a roleplaying scenario (a la D&D), where there exists a precedent of allowing a roll for the success of even incredibly unlikely efforts (like a hobbit stabbing a mighty dragon with a knife, when a "natural 20" will allow damage to be dealt). This implies to me that any remotely plausible solution might be assigned a (very low, sure) probability of success, making any challenge ultimately defeatable, given enough resourcefulness and some luck.

    This is my objection to the "inescapable staircase" puzzle: it seems here that the designer has simply decreed that it has the quality of being inescapable, rather than explaining how it works, what triggers it, and so forth.

    I'd love to hear from anyone on what this actually looks like in play. Can you relate a story from an actual game?

    I don't doubt that it can happen, but I'm having trouble imagining what it looks like.

      \n Also, this bit from Dave is worth repeating, I think - he describes this whole process very well with few words:\n

      When I do this [arbitrate the resolution of a difficult challenge], I work with the players. Even if they don't solve the challenge now, they'll have a better chance of doing so later, and they are learning potentially useful information in the process.

      Referring to "Airk's Tower":

      I may disagree with Eero here, and I wonder if I just haven't run across the consequences yet. I see this tower as not being sandbox-friendly. To me, a sandbox shouldn't have railroads unless they lead to the only appropriate inevitably, which is PC death (or insanity, corruption, etc). I would handle this tower obstacle/puzzle in different ways; here are some examples:

      1) The tower has a door that leads to a treasure. If you can figure out how to get into the correct door, you access the treasure. Otherwise you can leave.

      2) The tower slowly fills with water. If you can't solve it in time, you die. Usually though in this case I wouldn't have used a no-solution puzzle. Or, if I did I might save this puzzle for a higher level when the adventurers possess more open-ended magic items that they could use as tools in creative ways I can't predict.       \n It was a direct reply to Matt's text adventure reference. Text adventures, also known as "interactive fiction", is a type of computer game. Many modern text adventures aren't written as specific scenes prompting for specific inputs. Instead, they're written as descriptions of places and the items therein, and the properties of those items.

      Here is an example of Inform 7 code:\n
      The Bar is south of the Foyer. The printed name of the bar is "Foyer Bar".
      The Bar is dark. "The bar, much rougher than you'd have guessed
      after the opulence of the foyer to the north, is completely empty.
      There seems to be some sort of message scrawled in the sawdust on the floor."

      The scrawled message is scenery in the Bar. Understand "floor" or "sawdust" as the message.

      Neatness is a kind of value. The neatnesses are neat, scuffed, and trampled.
      The message has a neatness. The message is neat.

      I think that while it is attractive to ask the GM to make sure that their challenges are "just right", it is not a practically achievable goal. The GM cannot know what level of challenge the players are capable of tackling, and he cannot in fact even guarantee that they will relate to the provided material in a constructive manner. I'd go as far as to say that even looking for some sort of moral justification is a mirage: you cannot even provide an adventure with which you could say that it's the fault of the players if they don't get it.

      This is why pre-negotiation of the challenge is so important: the players themselves have to participate in constructing the context and approach vector towards the material, and in this manner create their own challenge; only in this way we guarantee that a challenge has, in fact, been understood and accepted, and thus hallowed even in failure.

      As a practical example, let's consider a goblin camp in the wilderness. A GM who believes in their ability to pre-create challenges relies on the assumption that the adventurers will want to kill the goblins - that's the challenge, to kill the goblins. Therefore he determines the number of goblins and their weaponry in a way that provides for a fair fight - maybe he has a precalculated encounter pointbuy like modern D&D, or he's eyeballing. Whatever.

      Then, adventurers come upon the goblin camp, and we see the epistemological problems with this entire arrangement:
      * The GM cannot guarantee success: The fight was pre-balanced to be "fair", not a sure thing. Thus the adventurers might fail. If the adventurers fail, was it because the GM misjudged the difficulty, because the rules for balancing encounters are broken, or because the players under-performed? How do you know?
      * The GM cannot guarantee constructive approach: What if the adventurers attempt to parlay with the goblins? We're assuming for the sake of the argument that the GM has not considered this avenue of approach in advance - if he had, we could just assume some other truly unexpected approach vector. How likely will this new challenge, parlaying with the goblins, be fair and well-considered? How likely is it that the goblins are trivially easy to parlay with, or practically impossible? If it will in fact somehow be a fair challenge, even without the GM having pre-balanced the consideration, what prevents the initial fighting challenge from being balanced in the same way, without pre-planning?
      * The GM cannot guarantee moral high ground: The adventurers fail against the goblins and the evening is ruined. Was it the fault of the players for not getting that obviously they were supposed to sneak up on the goblins and attack without warning instead of announcing themselves like they did, or was it the fault of the GM for not having prepared a sufficiently fair encounter? How do you know?

      My alternative to the seemingly intractable morass that challenge balancing presents is to let challenge emerge naturally from the beliefs that players hold regarding the fiction of the game: encounter goblins, determine whether you want something from the goblins, determine what gives you the best odds of success, and then execute. It might be that the combination of goblins and tactics proves entirely trivial, in which case we can pass over this material quickly and move the game forward; it might be that the goblins prove impossible to conquer, in which case we'll know better than to try next time. Whatever the outcome, the procedure of the game is not reliant on the GM somehow divining in advance what kind of material will be construed as a challenge and tackled in a satisfying manner.

      If it seems a wonder that my games ever encounter any challenges at all, keep in mind that the GM is mandated to bring interesting, strategically provocative stuff to the table, and therefore it is not actually that hard for the players to find something worthwhile to do in the game. The default proposition is attempting dangerous commando hijinks in hopes of finding treasure. While I would not trust myself to judge whether the party can beat three kobolds, I certainly do trust myself to figure out with the players whether they dare to try it, and face the consequences if they fail.

      In summary: while I don't think that you can pass off anything whatsoever as sandbox material, I do still think that worrying about challenge balancing at the content design level is far, far premature. The time to worry about whether a challenge is present is when you've got characters on the ground, trying to accomplish something - only when you know the position and direction of the characters can you possibly know whether they are facing a challenge or not. Trying to predetermine challenge is like trying to set up a cliff-face obstacle without knowing which direction the party is coming from; either you railroad them to the cliff-face in your planned orientation, or you accept that the cliff-face will only become a challenge if and when the party wants to go over it, and not before.

      \n Here's a brainstorm for a GM tool:

      Whenever your players are approaching your content as a challenge (or are about to), quickly check this list:
      - Might they suffer meaningful loss?
      -- NO -> Might they achieve meaningful gain?
      ---- NO. Fast-forward through it; there's no challenge here.
      ---- YES. Play on! You're good to go.
      -- YES -> Are any of the following true?
      ---- 1) They have reason and means to learn what they face before it's too late.
      ---- 2) Once they're facing it, they can learn what they might lose before they've lost it.
      ---- 3) Once they've suffered their loss, they will know how to avoid an exact repeat.
      ------ NO -> Pause and make one (or more) of these 3 conditions true.
      ------ YES -> Play on! You're good to go.

      \n For what it's worth, I should note that I'm totally cool with any phrasing that acknowledges the plurality of approaches and values in approaching this question. As is often the case, I only object to normative umbrella claims that are clearly disproven by personal experiences of this or that gamer. In this case, my claim is that a sanity check heuristic is apparently not strictly necessary in challenge design because I've been getting by just fine without one for soon to be a decade myself

      I'll also emphasize that my position is not exactly a purely naturalistic one when it comes to conducting a sandbox: I do not believe it to be sufficient for the GM to simply simulate a world and let things fall as they may. The reason for this is essentially in efficiency of depiction, the way humans react to boredom, and the fact that most settings do not provide interesting content 24/7. Saying that it suffices to simulate steady like a train is like saying that you'll get good television by bringing a camera and broadcasting everything, unedited.

      Because of this editing issue my position on GMing is very focused on challenge discovery: the group needs to work together to actively abstract out of boring routines and zoom into the actually pertinent bits of the fictional fabric. This is seen in a simple form when downtime passes for the adventurers, but there are a lot of elements in the game that have nothing to do with realism, but everything to do with bringing players face to face with challenges. Random encounter procedures, the way adventurers miraculously hear about adventure propositions, the timing of adventure opportunities - everything is geared towards actually achieving interesting situations. Given a choice between the PCs missing the adventure train or just making it as the train leaves the station, the agenda of the game mandates that they make it so they can die of something slightly more interesting than old age and boredom.

      One way to think about this selective reality - a way we've often discussed at the table - is to say that the player character adventurers have the privilege or curse to be the exact people to whom interesting things tend to happen. If there is exactly one vampire in a city of 10,000 people, hunting one person a night, you know I'm gonna roll like 1/6 (15%) about whether it'll be one of you guys - at most a 1/20 if it seems like we don't need this magnified probability for trouble right here and now. It is simply necessary to magnify the probability of adventure if we want exciting opportunities and hazards to happen ever.

      (Why is the probability of that vampire attack not 100% to ensure an exciting vampire fight? It's because a golden mean is necessary between realism and adventurousness, and because the GM does not want the responsibility for the outcome of the encounter - particularly not when the realistic deduction really is that the vampire is rather unlikely to attack this particular PC over the thousands of NPC alternatives. By setting a reasonably low probability for events that realistically would be extremely unlikely, we can enjoy those events occurring within the lifetime of a campaign while still keeping them unpredictable and unlikely enough for players to discount them in their planning.)

      All this is relevant to the question at hand in that this challenge-oriented parsing of the setting probably affects the likelihood of "unsolvable challenges" occurring in some complex manner when compared to a hypothetical "pure simulation". On the one hand we might speculate that because everybody's on the lookout for some setting-appropriate adventure tropes, we might end up de-emphasizing boring and stupid possibilities like tripping and breaking your neck, and thus we get less unsolvable challenges than we "should". On the other hand, maybe this insistence on interpreting all evidence towards excitement would cause the GM to actively introduce elements that prove in hidsight to combine into an unfair challenge? Difficult to say whether this kind of relationship to the setting actually increases or decreases the occurrence of utterly unfair challenges.

      For my own purposes, though, I'm happy with the two observations from above: unfair and unsolvable challenges are pretty rare even without me being on guard about them, and the facts of a lethal playstyle make them not all that dissimilar from situations where the players simply fail due to their own incompetence, and therefore not all that remarkable. For these reasons I believe it better to not privilege teleological filters in choosing what kind of content gets into the game. Less GM burden, and a less artificial and predictable playing field.

      \n What I do:

      In prep, I do pay some attention to the difficulty level of things. That is, I make sure there is enough low-level adventure locations available that I don't have to worry about the rest, which do tend to be low-level also, but might not be. An occasional deathtrap or whatever would be okay, but I rarely see those in published adventures and rarely have the occasion to make them myself.

      Also in prep, I don't read the tables I use through, but rather skim them a bit to see if they are interesting at all. So, whenever the player characters encounter something in the deep underworld, it might be that I roll a Dungeon world monster and it might turn out to be the apocalypse dragon. Or it might as well be a bunch of kobolds from Basic fantasy rpg, if the dungeon level is small (as BFrpg has different tables for different dungeon levels).

      In play, I follow the prep and random rolls faithfully. If the result of a random roll is utterly impossible (a quarry or other fixed location where none was just before, or a monster that does not fit inside the location at all) and I don't figure out a way of including it within a few seconds, I reroll; this has happened maybe once or twice and I have refereed a fair number of sessions. I may or may not tell the players what the impossible event that I decided to reroll was, depending on pacing etc. I tell if they ask.

      The necessity for following prep and dice is that otherwise I would be tempted to steer the course of play; for example, to make player characters survive. And if I were doing that, than it would not be the players who are responsible for the fates of their characters. Their successes would be worth less, and their failures my responsibility. This would remove the point of play.

      For example: A bunch of 1st level D&D characters. Encounter is a storm giant and 2 chimera. Reaction roll indicates they are not overtly hostile, but not too friendly, either. They are encountered near the Death frost doom mountain. Quick improvisation: the giant demands the puny mortals to find its ancestor inside the mountain, or it would let the chimera feed.

      I had no idea this would happen. If the reaction roll would have been different, then the giant would have slain the scout and others might or might not have escaped. If the giant would have been hostile and the party would not have had a scout and surprise rolls had indicated that the giant or the chimera notice the party, then everyone would almost surely have died. Not quite a deathtrap, but an acceptable situation.

      Grinding gear contains pretty much a death trap. LotFP adventure with crystal heads contains such a combinatorial explosion of possible outcomes that I have no idea what would happen if characters entered it. It is a deathtrap in some situations, there is the possibility to start building an entirely obedient army or to have everything with tens of kilometers destroyed due to an alien invasion, or an outcome that would likely lead to technological collapse within the entire civilised world. There is probably more and I might misremember things. Both of these adventures are within the sandbox and they might trigger during any session now.

      Given this sort of sandbox, what could possibly be a ridiculous edge case? I'm asking because I can't really envision such. This is an honest question, not an attempt to bait you into an argument or to say gotcha.
