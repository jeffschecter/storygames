\n Dreamation game.

I had decided in my head that the way to stop the curse was to destroy the tapestry. And I had decided that the tapestry couldn't be destroyed until moonlight had weakened its connection to the demon it housed (demons don't like sunlight or moonlight), and the moonlight would only work when the moon was high in the sky, after 11pm. I gave the players a riddle plus lots of circumstantial evidence to indicate the moon solution.

So, they found the tapestry and figured out that it was bad news. They tried to slash it and stab it, to no avail. I narrated the tapestry wriggling toward the darkest corner of the rooftop. (I didn't say "Away from the moonlight!" because I didn't want to make the riddle too easy.) They read the riddle.

"All good!" I thought. I wasn't even going to worry about the "11pm" thing. This seemed a good moment to shine moonlight on the tapestry and chop it up and end the adventure.

And then they decided to bolt the fuck away from the curse effect which was sapping their strength, and from the skirmishing townsfolk nearby.

Uh oh. I turned to my "steps of doom" for the scenario, and saw the last two: "air become nearly unbreathable" and "demon escapes from tapestry". Well, we didn't have enough time left in the session to fight a demon. I described the tapestry bucking and thrashing around, but the PCs simply tied it up and continued with their plan to take it elsewhere.

Even if I ditched the emerging demon, this still seemed bad. They'd get to safety and then figure out what to do via leisurely experimentation -- major anticlimax. We were already in a nice, dramatic scene -- I wanted to make them do it now!

So, I just sped up the asphyxiation and made it clear that they'd never make it over the fence and away from the curse before they passed out. That seemed to do the trick. They latched onto the riddle, figured "something about the moon!" They theorized, "Try moonlight?" I backed this up: "When it wriggled on the roof, it was moving away from moonlight!"

So they threw it in a patch of moonlight! I narrated the magical transformation into a normal tapestry. "Yay!" I thought. "Now they'll hack it up and we're done!"

Nope. "Is the curse gone?" they asked. "Nope," I said, still figuring they'd destroy the thing to be on the safe side.

They didn't destroy it. They just worried about asphyxiating and what else they might have missed. One player latched onto a piece of the riddle, "past eleven". She theorized, "Maybe at 11pm, the curse will break!"

"Okay," I thought. Now I knew what to do. Build a little drama as 11 approaches. I narrated a panther monster approaching. I said that it was somewhere around 11 now; if not yet, then any minute!

Fortunately, no one checked my math. Their original plan for "after dark" in late spring, plus the amount of time they'd spent since arriving, would probably have left the hour around 9.

I was content to give the panther one or two swipes to cause injuries before I declared it 11. But one player came up with a clever way to keep it at bay, so I ruled that swipe had been lost. Then I narrated the dramatic end of the curse.

    \n The GM is supposed to seem like just a physics engine in play, definitely.

    In reality, he also has to make good puzzles. This involves either:
    a) prepping more thoroughly than I do -- for instance, I hadn't determined whether the curse travels with the tapestry or not, or
    b) ad-libbing "outs" when the players hit a "bad puzzle moment"

    A "bad puzzle moment" is when you have no idea what to do or how to get more info. This results in:
    1) doing nothing
    2) methodically trying everything with no rhyme or reason

    Personally, I find anticipating every contingency that's likely to arise in a scenario to be no fun. So I make puzzles that I hope are good, but I'm willing to ad-lib a bit in case they're about to cause a "bad puzzle moment". In my experience, even expertly-designed puzzles can cause "bad puzzle moments" because it's hard to read each other's minds. My buddy Al stays in physics engine GM mode and the players run out of ideas and then play just winds up sucking (unless there's other stuff to do besides this puzzle -- but in Vatndale, there really wasn't).

    As for pushing drama, it was a tough call. I'm more likely to do that when showing the game to people for the first time. For my home game, I would almost never* do that, because they all know going in that if the villagers all die because they took too long to remove the curse, well, it's just a bump in the road for their adventuring career.

    *the exception would be if we're running out of time and someone really has to go home

    Knowing nothing, I just have to say that when I do a puzzle in your random average D&D game, it's either completely optional (just some extra reward, say, and not necessarily obviously present, even), solvable with sufficient expenditure of resources (say, spend time getting hints via lore checks until you figure it out) or its outcome is fun even if it's never solved (a trap that goes off if you don't solve it, say). This way there is no danger of getting stuck in the morassy bog of puzzle-solving failure. That demon situation seems to me like it'd go solidly into category three: why couldn't the demon just get free and go on a rampage, aside from concerns over playtime?

    \n So, OK.

    What you're describing is a railroad. There's nothing wrong with that, except, perhaps, that it's a particularly step-by-step railroad: to break the curse, they need to shine moonlight on the tapestry at a particular time, then cut it into pieces. It's difficult to figure all that out. And, of course, you had to massively railroad the players to get them through that sequence: stopping them from escaping, telling them about the moonlight.

    So...would you like tips on how to railroad the players better? Or tips on how to run the thing in a less railroady way?

    Interestingly, it's worth nothing that the players offered you a couple of good endings. Fleeing with the tapestry was a fine ending: let them take it to a museum, then see what happens there. Fleeing is generally a good ending: you could just let them run away from the entire horror, letting them be responsible for the re-emergence of the curse. So, what I'm asking is: would you like to accept the players' endings? Or would you like tips on steering the players' actions towards the ones you had in mind?

    Graham

    \n The challenge-based adventuring stuff Paul's referencing is basically this; I've been writing about D&D-like fantasy adventuring now and then in my blog as I've played games in that style. The basic theme is mostly paraphrased in this post from a couple of years back. If I had to characterize the thrust of my writing in hindsight, I'd say that a half of it is (hopefully constructive) complaining about the clumsy bits of the D&D rules and setting, while the other half is about how I've learned to run gamist, fiction-heavy fantasy adventure: setting first, explicit challenge, heavy scene framing, clarity in player-to-player communication and so on. It's sort of like a hippie take on old school renaissance, approaching the particulars of system and setting with heavy skepticism while essentially glorifying the parts I like about old-school D&D.

    Speaking of dead ends in puzzle-solving, my current solution on the matter is to have the GM be rather relaxed about his role in the puzzle-solving interaction. By this I mean that he shouldn't be a tense and carefully non-committal robot; it's much more fun if he can taunt the players and put pressure on them like a quizz show host. Don't let the momentum disappear and make sure that chairmanship of the party is firmly in hand, in your own hands if necessary. (If I'm going to write a fantasy adventure rpg text at some point, it'll include a specific party leader character - the job of that player at the table is then to make sure that the party decisionmaking procedures run fluidly. I've found that it's very good if I can complain to a specific player if the characters are not doing anything interesting, and then expect him to direct the conversation to conclusions and out of the analysis paralysis.) The key point is this: you have to know when the party is not going to solve the puzzle - and however you find it out, knowing it, you can give them more hints or frame forward to the consequences of failure. I mentioned a pretty generic and fiction-respecting technique upthread: offer the players more hints in exchange for fictional resources. The simplest way of doing this is to offer them a lore ability check (and a potential hint) in exchange for the characters sitting around for a few hours wracking their brains. Of course this isn't interesting if there's no time pressure, but then you have to always be clear on where the challenge lies: if nothing is preventing the characters from sitting around until they get a hint, then you should already give that hint to them and narrate the time-skip, no need to make the players ask for it. And if it should prove that you've already given them everything and they still don't get it, just pressure them to quit: "Seems like you're not getting it, shall we move on?"

    \n Paul, easy: just create a setting that's full of satisfying adventures. I've defined the broad strokes of Lendrhald with that in mind, and I define each adventure with that in mind too. The key to avoiding visible contrivance with "dead end here" is to avoid red herrings, really. If players don't find stuff to pique their interest, they'll turn around before too long.     \n David, seems to me that Paul's point is that the players wouldn't find it very fair if you killed their characters in a situation where they're just replicating adventure fiction memes. It's not uncommon at all for a group to have different expectations about the diegetic framework (oh my, didn't expect to get to use that word in a valid context) in which their fiction resides: it is equally reasonable for a roleplaying game to exist towards the teleological goal of creating stories as it is for it to exist for causal purposes of exploring the consequences of actions. Both types of roleplaying games are out there, and many games are drifted to both directions from an ambivalent starting point. Considering this, it's not far-fetched at all that a player or an entire group would march their characters to a certain doom, trusting that the GM will conspire to ensure that the result is an exciting story instead of a short and unceremonious death.

    Do note that "the place is dangerous and you'll likely be killed" is mere setting flavour for a good adventure fiction protagonist. He'll grit his teeth and do what he has to do for king, God and country regardless of any danger to his person. It would be severely out of character for a player to shirk from that sort of challenge if he thought that the point of the game is to take up the GM's story hook and see where it leads. Conan doesn't get killed for going to a dangerous place, so why would his character?

    This is a pretty big thing in traditional roleplaying, this difference in fundamental models. The striving for unity in '80s game texts made it so that the mark of a good GM came to be that you'd be able to fulfill both goals at once, somehow; the rules system would take care of the routine causality, while you as the GM would cheat your way into a satisfying story whenever the causality would fail to produce such. In this more enlightened age we luckily have more vocabulary and design cases to compare and contrast, so we can see clearly how different games can be. Thus it's a simple matter to recognize whether you're running on causality or teleological narrative imperative in your GMing, which you can then tell the players. It's very fine for the high and mighty GM to drop hints via NPCs about impending death, but this is not the sort of quality communication I expect of players nowadays; if you want to say something to the players, then say something to them instead of dressing your message in in-fiction flavour. It'll serve communication much better if you'll just tell the players that this game's going to be realistic and you're not going to save their characters from a frustrating death if they insist of taking on an overwhelming challenge.
