\n I have said for years that Greg Stafford's King Arthur Pendragon is a stranger game than most people realize.

In a recent podcast, Luke Crane goes on about Burning Wheel Gold for about half-an-hour -- and then goes on a prolonged, frothing love-letter rant to the enjoyment he's getting from Pendragon as he plays it with the gang at BWHQ.

You can find the interview here. The Pendragon stuff kicks off shortly after the 30 minute mark.

Luke, specifically, had no encounter with Pendragon before his gang picked it up to play. And yet, two things are true: a) he absolutely loves it; b) he's finding it extremely, extremely strange.

Have people listened to the the second half of the podcast? What did you think of Luke's summary? What have been your experiences with Pendragon, if any? If you've had none, does Luke's excitable rant make the game something seem like something you'd want to play? If you've played, how did the weird features Luke talks about work for you? If you haven't, what do you think would be your response to the strange mechanics Luke's talks about with such glee and lover's touch of frustration?

    \n I listened to the whole podcast last night. I always (ALWAYS) thought that the winter phase in Mouse Guard was inspired by or an hommage to Pendragon, funny to learn that Luke didn't even know the game when he wrote MG.

    King Arthur Pendragon is my most treasured old roleplaying book - but I've only played one campaign (which I ran), and it was back in the late 1990s I think. My roleplaying friends at that time were also rather shocked, especially by the personality mechanics, which were the part I liked the most. Everyone did enjoy character/family creation, and the idea of running an estate/family rather than just a character. I would love to play it again - as per the rules.

    Some of us stole the personality mechanics and adapted them to an FBI campaign than ran for years, though the rolls (and effects) were voluntary and not imposed, which was a bit wiffy. Meaning, you could be brave as hell according to your character sheet and then act cowardly as fuck without any penalty. I found that less than fulfilling. I mostly used it to inspire my character's actions - like rolling for truthful or whatever before deciding whether to tell the truth or not.

    \n FYI, Luke and BWHQ's feelings for Pendragon are more of a Love-Hate relationship that depend upon where the GPC took us on any particular night and how the dice were treating them.

    I think at last night's game the pendulum swung to the hate side after the knights got brought up on charges of treason for giving aid to Merlin (who was stealing Uther and Ygraine's infant son), only to discover that the trial was a sham used by a faction of Christians as a powerplay to discredit Merlin and the Pagan religion. The knights don't know what Merlin is up to and he doesn't tell them. The scenario as laid out in the GPC is a pretty hard railroad and even ends with a note acknowledging it. Pretty much the only way to avoid it is to decide not to help Merlin when he asks you for it at the very beginning of the scenario.

    I could tell the players were feeling railroaded and helpless because normally a hard situation like that would see them swing into action with some sort of crazy scheme. Instead they were mostly passive, concerned only with spending the time between the accusation and the trial to go home to their manors and try to make heirs with their wives.

    To cap it all off, a part of the advancement in KAP is random. You roll to see whether any of the traits, passions or skills you used successfully in the course of play go up. In the normal course of play, you have to roll the rating of your ability or less on a d20 in order to succeed. But when doing experience rolls, you have to roll above your rating. The idea, or course, being that as you advance an ability it becomes more difficult to advance. But Luke managed to not advance a single ability last night, even the one rated at 5. It was a down note to end a down session.

    Hopefully next year is better. I find myself very much looking forward to the Anarchy Phase, which is fast approaching, in that the campaign becomes far less scripted from that point on.

    \nThe one change that was requested was some kind of anti-whiff factor mechanic. I think my group is fond of points to spend to keep failure from happening. What we settled on was giving each player a coin; during the game, they could spend the coin to improve a result (critical failure -> failure, failure -> success, success -> critical success). If they spent it on themselves, nothing extra happened. If they spent it on another knight, and narrated some justification, they got 50 Glory.
    \n
    \nNeedless to say, this was a popular move. Some of the players have had terrible, terrible luck with rolling that d20.

    \n All time favorite game EVER. I own every single edition (except for the second) and I've been trying to complete my collection of the Beaumaines fan-zine for years.
    \n
    \nLOVE IT LOVE IT LOVE IT.
    \n
    \nThat said...its BRP...with all of the bullshit broken nonsense BRP brings with it.
    \n
    \nWe had a couple of key hacks that we did to make it work. First, we trebled the skill points received in the non knightly skills at start of play. There's a bunch of fun variety between the different knightly backgrounds but when that variety results in a skill of 2 or 3 in something (roll under on 1d20) its utterly pointless. At least with a 6 or a 9 it was worth trying to use.
    \n
    \nThen for advancement we checked each skill EVERY time it was used, not just the first time (once per battle, not swing for fighty skills). Each extra check on the skill then gave a +1 to the d20 advancement roll which helped some of that.
    \n
    \nI'll have to check out the interview. We played the hell out of that game back in day. Ran a campaign from Uther all the way through the fall of Camelot and another one around the grail quest (in the first campaign the player knights were all too practical to engage in such silliness).
    \n
    \n
    \nThe most important thing to know about Pendragon is never to use a published adventure as written. The published adventures all suffer from the usual rail road and not understanding the rules of the game problem in a big way. Use the plot hooks scattered throughout the books, but the actual adventures need to be hacked for ideas not run as written.
    \n
    \nIf you run published Pendragon adventures they will tell such stupid things as "Make a Lust check to see if you're seduced by the maiden". This is absolutely NOT how the rules are supposed to work (its actually a violation of the rules in the rule book, particularly the earlier editions before the sim heads got ahold of it and blurred the line). The virtues and vices are meant to record your behavior not dictate your behavior. Only above a score of 16+ (where you start earning glory for your traits) can the GM call for a roll to force you to act a certain way (and the Glory reward is there to compensate you for that). Below 16, you just earn checks for your choices...so if you allow yourself to be seduced, check Lust, if you refuse check Chaste. We used the same multiple check hack here as we did for skill advances.

    \nIt was the late 1990s, so our standard mode of play was "systemless" (GM fiat, loads of hand-waving rules, fudging rolls or very few rolls at all, pre-made plot, the lot), so I think this game provoked us by saying: you need to do this and do it by the rules.     We didn't play pre-made adventures/campaigns, but I made it up as we went along, but still in the pre-planned encounters by the GM kind of style.     I guess I'm saying: we tried to play it exactly as we played our other games, and it was less than fulfilling, except the character generation and winter phase bit, which was awesome. One player, which was new to the group and I didn't know him that well, never came back to the second session.

    \nI can't speak for Luke, but I can give my impression. Trait bumps, Passion bumps and Skill bumps are a very big deal.

    First, as BW players, we're conditioned to forgive a lot of hardship and heartache on our characters' parts for the sake of advancement. We're happy to jump into a horrendous situation with both feet, knowing full well that something awful is likely to happen to our character, if there's a test toward advancement as a reward. In the face of that, the random nature of advancement is very jarring. And pretty unpleasant from the player perspective, from what I can see.

    Within the context of the game itself, remember that the quest for Glory is the fuel for the entire game. You seek Glory to become a legend to rival Balin, Gawaine, Lancelot, etc. And to get that Glory, you need high traits and high passions. You need to succeed -- or better yet crit -- on important skill rolls in important situations. You need to defeat dangerous enemies. And let's face it, you need to survive long enough to have an heir that can succeed you. And that requires performing heroically in battle so you can win rewards from your liege and plunder so you can live superlatively to give yourself a better chance of having children every year and to give your children a (still pretty lousy) chance of surviving to the point where you don't have to make survival rolls for them every year. And to perform heroically in battle on anything approaching a regular basis, you need passions and combat skills rated at 20 or higher.

    \n I recommend a stable of characters for PD, and a frequent change of scene. It helps to understand the game as a big tapestry on which individual PCs are pictured. This one goes down to a Saxon, that one becomes a Knight of the Round Table, that one conquers lands in Ireland etc. You will never get to the end of The Boy King playing in this mode, let alone the GPC, but it works very well for me. Players can come and go, using the Winter Phase to update their chrs if necessary. Embrace the randomness. One PC covered himself with Glory at Badon, and the next week got splattered by a giant with a club. One PC never did anything but the Winter Phase and became immensely Glorious.

    PDis certainly a strange game if you want to see it that way. I credit PD with making me broad-minded about possibilities and hence, later, open to the flood of indie games. I don't think it's Glory that's at the heart of the game, I think it's the sense of being guided into a different world.

    \n 4th Edition is basically the 3rd Edition pasted into a big book along with Knights Adventurous with a magic system tacked on.

    3rd edition was very clean and lovely -- and, again, that's what you're getting with 4th. Knights Adventurous allowed one to have knights from different parts of Briton and Europe.     The pill folks have to swallow before they swim in the river of Pendragon is that having high value Traits and Passions is fucking meaningful.     \n I've not studied the Great Pendragon Campaign in detail. We played off of the Boy King. Its basically arranged year by year as a timeline of "historical" events in the King Arthur story, where he was, what battles were fought, high level goings on. Each year includes several "rumors you hear at court" (stuff like "hey that new French knight is showing a great deal of promise" or "I wonder what the king will do about X") There's usually a number of plot hooks, some details on a key event or two that players could get sucked into (like fighting at the battle of such and such, or competing in the tourney at Glastonbury which featured these famous round table knights. Its interspersed with a gazeteer of places and cool little mini adventures that could happen there...like nasty "oh shit" moments like encountering "Black Annis" or being there when some donkey kicks some famous dude down a well. The nature of the events and tone they're presented in really give flavor to the different phases. Each phase steps through a different period in history...so in phase one the castles and armor and methods of fighting are very 6th century, by phase 5 (like 100 years later) the castles and armor and tournament pagaentry are very 13th/14th century...which all fades back to historical norms with the fall of Camelot.

    Glory's cool and all, there's a mini level up feature at different Glory thresholds IIRC. Its primary use is establishing a pecking order among knights. Your starting Knight will have 1000+ Glory (cuz he's a knight) maybe a little more depending on your father and such. Unobtainable King Arthur level is like 100,000, with most of the famous knights being in the 10-30K range IIRC (I think Lancelot was 80K in his peak version). You'll get glory from defeating baddies, a nice bit (10% of their glory I think) for besting a knight in a tournament. You get automatic glory for having strong virtues and passions (but that's like 16-100 points a year for scale purposes). I think also for maintaining yourself in lavish fashion. If you survive one of the big battles (like Arthur vs the 5 kings in the north) you can get beau coup glory all at once so there's a certain motivation to engage in such things.

    I find the game really takes off in the second generation of characters. Sons inherit a portion of their father's glory, stats, and reputation, which means the next 21 year old has a big head start over the first one. But its a lot of fun to watch them develop differently in the different Phase. Dad was a brusque somewhat barbaric warrior who took no prisoners and smashed people who crossed him with a big sword. Son like to sing love songs and recite poetry and prefers tourneys to muddy battle fields...lots of fun which son encounters Dad's old friends (and Dad's old reputations). We had a couple of players get to third generation characters just in time to participate in the end times. It was interesting to see how that generation of new characters in our game hated and despised the second generation characters who were still in play...blaming them for letting it all go to shit.

    As GM watching some players accumulate more glory, get granted lands and titles while other players fell behind due to character death or what not, and the fairly wide disparity in ability / wealth between the second generation characters of those whose father's were more successful / famous and those whose fathers were less so was really enjoyable...not sure how enjoyable it was for the players...there was definitely some inter player rivalry between the haves and the have notes...my rose colored nostalgia glasses may be glossing over some player disatisfaction there. But I definitely thought that added depth to the game.

    There's lots of solutions to the BRP whiff factor. Ours was to bump every skill up by 5 points at the start and then use multiple checks to ensure that they actually went up from use reliably. We also toyed with rolling 3d20 and keeping the median result as a way of introducing a bell curve but wound up ditching that. The real solution to the BRP whiff factor is to make ample use of Inpiration (I think that's what its called)...basically make a check against a passion to power up your skill check before making a roll (like Riddle of Steel Spiritual Attributes) at the risk of falling into Melancholy if you fail to be inspired. If you've got a skill of 5 and no passion to power it up with...that's pretty much the game telling you "WTF". So it worked out in the end once we learned to game it.

    \n The GPC is essentially the same thing as The Boy King except it goes from 485 (under Uther) to 566 (the wrap-up after Camlann).

    Uther (485-495), Anarchy (496-509), Boy King (510-518), Conquest Period (519-530), Romance Period (531-539), Tournament Period (540-553), Grail Quest Period (554-557), Twilight Period (558-566).

    The technology updates each period. In the Uther Period you've got mail and open helms, and a might makes right attitude. By the Twilight Period you've got gothic plate and full-on chivalry, romantic love, etc.

    Each year gives the location of court, gossip, news, intrigue, a major event of the year, some information about Christmas Court and details about the weather if pertinent. There's plenty of room to improvise and weave in your own stuff, as well as material that you can weave in whenever it's appropriate.

    You can only earn a maximum of 100 Glory per year from your lands, so that doesn't really help you. If you want your knight to be worthy of a story in the Morte d'Arthur or another romance, you need to go out and do deeds (i.e., play the game).

    We've had five or six episodes of madness so far. As per the table in the GPC, we roll on the table every six months of game time to see what has happened to the knight. In every case so far, the knight has returned to his senses within the six month period.

    The players were pretty nonplussed the first time it happened, but were much more accepting of it when they realized that knights always receive a number of experience checks based on what they were engaged with in their period of madness. One knight even spent his time wondering the Camlann Woods spouting prophecies of the coming of Arthur.
