Heya,

It's been a while since we've had a good awesome-o-fy old DnD setting. I'm interested in doing one for the Raveloft. Of all the cool bad-A setting back in the mid 90's I played in, this one was one of the scariest (as far as hoping against hope my character lived). As I look back, it now just seems not as full and engrosing as it used to be. So, let's make it awesome.

If you were going to make the Raveloft the way you wanted it, what would it look like? It can be any eddition, even the new Castle Ravenloft setting released by WotC last year. What would an awesome Ravenloft look like?

I'll get things started with this. Things that made Ravenloft Cool for me:

=Vampires
=Lycanthropes
=Paladins
=Great Mansions

    I've only played the first two Ravenloft modules. What I recall about them that sounded like it was going to be good in terms of creating mood and such, were the dream sequences. And they did do that to some extent.

    The problem was that the dreams were either one of two sorts:

    1. Color text where the DM was just supposed to read them. Or
    2. A whole second life for the character that didn't seem to be all that different from their normal life i.e. not at all dreamlike.

    I think that it would be interesting to have some mechanic in the dreams where the player sometimes has control, but other times does not - and then has some control of the entire scene. Somewhat like in real dreams where the dreamer is out of control of even his own actions, but other times his will controls not only his own actions, but the events that occur as well.

    Make the dreams more dreamy, I guess, and give the players some interaction with them. Along with some rules on how the dream outcome impacts the waking world. When are they premonitions? What does that mean? Are they visions of a parallel spirit world? Etc.

    Ravenloft was my bread and butter when I was a teenager. When we played AD&D we mostly played Ravenloft. I took those experiences, good and bad, and ran a game of Conspiracy of Shadows with it. Here are the notes I put together for that game:

    The Pitch
    There is something wrong in St. Archard but it is like a puzzle missing pieces. People have had accidents, but letters arrive in their names well after they have been buried. Soldiers are sent to eliminate rebels in the outlying villages, but only a few return and their memory is hazy. And the Marquis St. Archard has parties at his manor, inviting astrologers, macabre artists, alchemists, taxidermists, and others who's profession is a mystery.

    The Kicker
    Late one night as you work to put these pieces together, a woman wrapped head to toe in swirling robes places an infant with mismatched eyes into your arms and a note pinned to the cloak it is wrapped in. A sudden gust of wind comes and when you look up she is gone. Only her shimmering cloak remains, twisting in the wind.

    Mechanics Note:
    When you reach a Taint Rank of 6 you will have become a darklord and set up a domain of your own.

    Figures of Note
    Henri St. Archard is the ruler of St. Archard and the surrounding region (seen in red on the map below). He is famous for holding parties with odd guest lists at his summer home in the countryside. The common people believe he has dark dealings with the Fey.

    Adrien Beauvais is the cardinal of the Church of Ezra in Illes de Paynes. The common view that he is the most powerful man in the kingdom and rules through the weak king, Francois I.

    Francois I is the king of the Illes de Paynes, the country in which St. Archard resides. The common view is that he is a puppet of Cardinal Beauvais.

    Setting Material
    There are no demi-humans. Only humans. Also, the fantastical elements are not overt. No wizards in towers or clerics running around with healing spells. We stripped all that out.

    We also leveled the technology playing field and set it at around the sixteen/seventeen hundreds. Cannons, muskets, pistols and rapiers.

    Also, the horror is under the skin. More in the vein of Gothic novels and less like D&D. As a group we felt that in order for the fantastic/horrific to have any effect it had to not be common place.

    Empire of Darkon: is modeled on Czarist Russia. Azalin doesn't rule. Instead he is a sort of Rasputin character ruling through the wife of Czar Peter.

    Kingdom of Falkonia: is pretty much the same as in the books. Vlad the Impaler if all the rumors are true and he is constantly duking it out with Darkon.

    Iles de Paynes is like France. The country has two major conflict going on. Lamordia in the north, which it recently conquered (Germanic feel to it) and Invidia in the south, which just recently gained its independence.

    Invidia is like the Kingdom of Navarre. Think of it as if the Basques had won their independence. However, it is a cultural mix of French and Slavic groups. It is ruled by the Dukar, who is the reason they won independence.

    Barovia is like the former Yugoslavia. A bunch of Balkan like states held together by a dominant personality. It is ruled by the Devil Stahd through the proxie of his mortal decedents.

    Badjoz aka Nova Vaasa, is like the Ottoman Empire. Across the Nocturnal Sea the majority of the empire sits. It is the great threat from the East.

    The Shadow Rift is just a big canyon no one can explain, or really is willing to deal with. It is dark, creepy, filled with mists, and stange weather rolls out of it from time to time.

    Church of Ezra: is the primary faith of Ravenloft. We are using the structure of the Albinican church from Conspiracy of Shadows for this game.

    The Modified Map of Ravenloft

    Troy,

    A combination of both mystery unveiled over time with some player created input. See in Conspiracy of Shadows, players get to use what is called Destiny Points to add shit to the game. But it is a resource so it ain't like they are making up the whole thing... Just parts of it. It is basically a tool for the GM to be able to tailor the mystery to the players interests at the table, while still having the leeway to throw in what interests him.

    See back in the day, I was the only one with all the Ravenloft crap, so shit came to light over time and it worked out great. Part of the fun for everyone was seeing what was going to surprise them next. Fear of the unknown is a big part of that, and you can't get that without some level of mystery.

    I don't think the mists coming to take you away should be under total player control. I think the following would be cool.

    A stat called "Doom" or some such. It starts out high. With high Doom, the Dark Lord (Strahd, Lord Soth, whoever) has mega-bonuses to everything and is totally in control. As the players uncover more and more of the mystery the Doom score goes down. The Dark Lord has less and less advantage. However, at specific points along the way (perhaps when players take certain actions) you roll to see if the mists come and whisk you away. The roll is based on the party's Doom score. High Doom has almost no risk of being whisked away. Low Doom gets whisked away frequently.

    Anyway... that's my little idea.

    Gaerik,

    Clint Krause did something pretty similar to what you are describing. He took the Doom mechanic in Conspiracy of Shadows and turned it into a really awesome community mechanic for his game Roanoke.

    However, I think you are going in the wrong direction. Ravenloft for me has always been about the tragic struggle that you can't win. It is the realm of evil, not good claimed by evil, just straight up evil. If anything I would start with the Doom being managable in the beginning and getting worse as time goes on. The Dark Lord doesn't have less of an advantage but more of an advantage over time. The oppressiveness of the place slowly eats at you over time. Strahd or Soth or whoever the fuck has had more time to study you. They are ancient evils who have been gutting better men than the characters for centuries. Time and planning and the very land is all on their side. It is when you realize this at the end that they get their final victory, shattering any hope you might have just before they rip out your throat...

    Monsters...

    I suppose if we remove all of the other monsters, except for the vampire, that we'll have created the Dracula RPG?

    I suppose that part and parcel of Ravenloft is encountering monsters that, to real folks would be terrifying, but who, to D&D adventurers cannot be a thread. I mean Owlbears aside with their five hit dice, what about bugbears? Defined as seven foot tall goblins? That should be some scary shit. In D&D it's just not.

    How's some vampire going to be scary? Oh, I see. By giving him metagame powers so that he has plot immunity.

    I mean it sorta works. Other than that it leads to railroading. But is the monster slaying really all that key? Do we have to have it? I guess so.

    I think this is a case where you have a setting that comes about because of the game system. My usual suggestion is that there's a core of these settings that can come out with another game system. But I'm trying to see this one working with Hero Quest for example (the one I usually apply). And I'm imaginging the PCs having to fight all those monsters... And it's just silly. The best I can get is some Buffy the Vampire slayer vibe. With Strahd having the sort of plot immunity that Spike has.

    To the extent that Ravenloft is "D&D-ified Dracula" it's going to be hard to improve without just putting it back to being Dracula. And not because it's good.
