    \n I picked up a copy of Jaws at the UK Games Expo last week and finished reading it a couple of days ago. A very sharp and thoughtful version of PDQ, expertly tailored to bring out the themes of sword and sorcery fiction, it's also particularly well explained and engagingly written. One of the best-known features of PDQ is the damage / failure system, where traits are reduced and the first one to be affected (chosen by the player) generates a story hook. This has worked superbly for us in Truth and Justice but in many ways I think it's even better suited to the S&S; stories of Jaws, where it can sometimes be tricky to keep an "adventuring party" together. The story hooks let players add subtexts and themes to the game far beyond the old "kill the evil wizard and loot his tower" routine with little GM effort. I was especially impressed by the magic system, simple and flexible, yet maintaining the thematic restrictions appropriate to the genre: this is not a game where Magic Missiles are going to be flying left and right and power definitely comes with a price and a risk. It's a terrific little book, bursting with game ideas.

    \n I just realized I forgot to chime in: I picked up this game about a month ago. Won't be able to play it for a few weeks more, but I gave it a light read. System's solid and all, but I think the coolest thing about the game is the foundational elements table thing: A six-part table which shows the relations between colors, emotions, elements, and that chart is basically the foundation of the setting.

    What is it with all these awesomely crafted fantasy throwback games coming out? First the Barbarians of Lemuria completion, now this... 2009's been a great year for it.
