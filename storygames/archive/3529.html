\n Yesterday we played Memoir '44. The Americans won our first game handily, in the spirit of the day. For our second game, we hammered out some rules for zombies. There are already zombie rules, but they are sub-standard. Ours are better:

1. Any living unit that is completely destroyed is immediately replaced by a unit of four zombies. You will need a bag of zombie figures or tokens to play.
2. Zombies have their own turn, going third after the Axis and Allied sides. They are administered communally.
3. Zombies do not have command cards. Each zombie unit moves one hex toward the nearest living unit of either side. If two or more units are equidistant, determine their direction randomly. If adjacent to a living unit, they remain in place.
4. Zombies can only attack in close combat. They can move and attack in the same turn. Their attack strength, in dice, is equal to the number of remaining zombie figures, so they begin with a four die attack.
5. Infantry, grenade, and retreat symbols all count as kills when attacking zombies. Zombies do not retreat.
6. Zombies obey terrain restrictions and penalties. It's possible for a one-figure zombie unit to uselessly claw at the base of a hill. This is considered cool.
7. Units destroyed by zombies do not count toward victory conditions.
8. Informal alliances and temporary truces are encouraged.

One delightful tactical side-effect of this set-up is that an enemy unit reduced to a single figure becomes a "zombie bomb". If you can destroy them from a distance, you can unleash a horde behind enemy lines. Since you want to eliminate enemy units to achieve victory conditions, this adds a level of complexity to decision-making on both sides.

    \n This looked like fun, so I tried out a solo mission last night. I used the first scenario in the basic book, the Pegasus Bridge one. The rules work really well and project just the right feel. Here's what happened.

    The allies moved in on Pegasus Bridge. Slowed down by the wire, they only got off a few shots at the Germans before all Hell broke loose. The British started dying in the wire, and the zombie horde quickly snowballed on them. The Germans kept firing on them, knowing they were safer in their fortifications, and hoping to soften up the Brits. On the British right flank, a smaller scale version of the same thing was happening.

    This was a meatgrinder for the Allied forces. Literally every time Zed had a choice between them and the Germans, they went after the British. I don't know if my dice were off true, or if it was just fate, but they just couldn't catch a break.

    Soon, the Brits were down to a single unit with only 1 figure left! He was cowering in the corner up against the Caen Canal, quickly being surrounded by four zombie units with 12 figures between them. Two lucky card draws served to forestall the inevitable; first Behind Enemy Lines gets him out of the corner (and into the center of the board. Yikes!), then Medics & Mechanics returned him to full strength.

    The Germans realized that they had to kill that last unit in order to eke out a victory, came back across the canal, and were set upon by the risen corpses of their enemies. This is where terrain really came in handy, there were few units left to order, and that one die reduction for attacking into a town or forest was very helpful. Sadly, that last British unit was eventually cornered and eaten (by the last unit of zombies, no less).

    Curious to see what would happen, I kept playing. They rose up, shambled across the board, and swarmed the last of the Germans, who had taken cover at Benouville. Thus began the zombie apocalypse.
    _________________________________________________________________________________________

    A few reflections:

    Part of my brain wanted the zombies to interact with towns in some way. Halting when they walked through them, maybe regenerating a little bit. Obviously, this is totally unnecessary.

    It's great that infantry can stay just ahead of Zed, firing off one die a round and still staying out of range. Until, that is, they run out of room. Then they're just meat. Just like in a zombie movie.

    A 'fresh' unit of zombies is a frightening thing. Always use terrain to your advantage.

    I really want to see how armor and hedgehogs work with these rules. I think I'm going to do a beach scenario next time.
