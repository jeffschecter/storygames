    \n Hey Nathan, I think what's been said so far in this thread is just one step removed from answering your question, with that step being the "ruin" part in your thread title.

    "Okay, so maybe my tycoon portrayal doesn't matter to the rules in Monopoly the way it matters to the rules in Apocalypse World. So what? It can still matter to the group who enjoys the portrayal for its own sake."

    That's your point, right? I think it's a good point, and the only way to argue with it is to say what is ruined, very precisely. So here's my best shot:

    1) If I am portraying my tycoon, and that portrayal has no bearing on my progress in the game (winning, changing my character, being awarded MVP, addressing premise, whatever), then it is less likely that any given player at my table will bother portraying their character or listening to me with interest when I portray mine.

    2) It is often not possible for the actions I portray to have the consequences that I might want them to have -- like, if I'm portraying my tycoon as a ruthless bastard who will burn down your hotel when he stays in it, in hopes of costing you money. In Monopoly, I can't actually cost you money or hotels that way, because the game rules don't care to support a persistent fiction. So in playing my character, either (a) I have to alter my portrayal and not play my tycoon the way I'd like, or (b) I have to suck up the cognitive dissonance of having my tycoon do something that I know won't work regardless of how it might look in the fiction.

    "I burn down the hotel!"

    "Okay. Now it's my turn, and the hotel is still on the board."

    Ka-thud. Most players won't bother to burn down the next hotel after that.

    Do we then narrate how insurance paid for it and it was quickly rebuilt? We could, but at that point, why are we playing Monopoly?

    3) If I wish to do more than just act out my character for the group's entertainment -- if I wish to put myself in their shoes, think as them, and make decisions from their perspective -- then I want these decisions to matter. I want them to work out well, or work out badly, or go as I planned, or surprise me, or I want to get new info so I can choose differently. I want to be playing my character when the feedback comes in, reacting as them, in horror or anger or exultation. I do not want to go, "Aha, in this situation, my character does this!" and then have the result be, "Yeah, anyway, moving on..." How do I keep investing in my character after that? I made a big decision and I didn't get to impact or experience its consequences.

    So that's my answer. Those are the things I think "rules that ignore the fiction" ruin. If you don't care about those things, or you play with a group that enjoys doing fiction for fiction's sake on top of game rules that ignore it, then I imagine these costs aren't relevant to you.

    - Here's what's boxes:
    AW: I just rolled a 10+ on my go aggro.
    Monopoly: Your piece is on Madison Square. It's my turn.

    - Here's clouds:
    AW: There's a gun on the table. My character had a really mad look in his eyes when telling you to back off. No-one in Bud's gang has ever crossed that line before. I'm on the roof.
    Monopoly: My tycoon has a really ugly moustache. I always give a dollar to the homeless man on Madison Square when I pass. I carry kerosene to burn down your hotels.

    Now, 'putting your clouds before your boxes' doesn't just mean that you talk about the cloud stuff first and the box stuff second. It's that the boxes reference the clouds. Looking up who knows the street you landed on (that info is a box) is required before you know whether you can buy the street (another box). In AW, looking up what this guy's house looks like (typical cloud info) is required before you can roll Defy Danger to sneak in (a box).

    In contrast, none of the info in the clouds are ever required to use the boxes in Monopoly. I can always just say 'okay, give me the dice, my turn to roll' without caring about how my wonderfully roleplayed dinner with the mayor went.



    So how about 'Just talking' influencing the game? That's outside the game state the clouds and boxes represent. If my character just yelled 'you'll never invade Poland!' so that your character heard it (even I just yelled it and didn't say 'my character tells yours \u2026') is different from me yelling it at you. In the example, both happened. In AW, the fact that my character told your is now part of the fiction, the clouds, and may affect the dice. In a Diplomacy-style game, any boxes you could call upon to invade Poland before - you can still call upon them. Maybe you don't want to, now, since you know I'll be really upset about it.     Anyway: so I played Gloom on Saturday night. It's the new hotness, a sort of Chez Geek or Munchkin style game where you try to kill off your family in the most horrible ways while having good things happen to the other players' families. And it's horrible. For exactly the reasons being discussed in this thread.

    Bad enough that it's got that Munchkin thing "be the last person to be fucked" in the "strategy." But what really got me is that it tells you it's a storytelling game and you should add descriptions to the cards you play. For instance if you play "scarred by scandal" on one of your family members you have to say what the scandal is. And then the next person is supposed to build on that narration. But it's completely irrelevant to the outcome of the game.

    The mechanics are "boxes to boxes" (appropriate for a board game, no problem there). But then the instructions are like, "Narrate cool stuff!" (clouds). Which is terrible, because there's no interaction between the two.

    There are some good mechanics-first RPGs. I would cite both Contenders and Spellbound Kingdoms, at the moment, as being mostly mechanics-first. But they work fine because the mechanical stuff is both a) evocative and b) changes the fictional situation such that the players (including the GM, in the case of SK) have to consult the fiction to decide what to do next.

    I think Vincent's point can be read not as "clouds first is alwayz bettar!" but as "there needs to be back and forth between the clouds and the boxes"\u2014that is, there need to be a healthy amount of both rightward and leftward arrows.

    And while rules heavy games like Exalted (@DannyK 's example is stellar), 3E and 4E, etc, have the "no rightward pointing arrows" issue, games with no leftward pointing arrows are also terrible! I would argue the lack of leftward pointing arrows is actually a bigger problem for "trad" games than lack of rightward pointing arrows! There are plenty of things in those games (I'm thinking of WW and GURPS here) that tell you, based on the fiction, when to roll. But outside of combat, it's almost completely unclear how a successful or unsuccessful roll is supposed to actually change things.

    A good middle ground is found in FATE, Burning Wheel, and Dogs. Take Aspects in FATE: if you want to justify the use of an Aspect, you have to justify it with narration. But this feels natural and not tremendously arbitrary, IME, since the use of Aspects flows easily from the fiction and it doesn't have to be too crazy and elaborate.

      \n People play games to have fun. But generally they can't just determine what actions would lead to the most fun and do them -- if people could easily discern that they wouldn't need games. What they do, when they engage with a game, is the same thing they do in any complex system -- try a bunch of stuff, observe the results, choose a goal, and choose actions that seem most likely to get them to their goal based on their observations.

      The job of a game designer is to make sure that the actions people choose are likely to produce fun. This means they have to identify people's likely goals and make sure that doing "fun" stuff advances you towards those goals. That way, when people advance towards their goals, they'll also enjoy themselves. If you don't do this, you'll see people doing stuff that they don't enjoy and that upsets other people and blaming it on you. The classic example of this is grinding for experience or cash in World of Warcraft. People don't do it because it's fun, because it isn't fun -- but they view it as the most efficient way to accomplish their goal, so they do it while simultaneously complaining that they're doing it!

      Vincent believes -- as I do -- that imagining a story together, and paying attention to all the details, and making it feel real, are fun -- maybe the most fun you can have in a roleplaying game. So he designs games where the most effective route to accomplishing your goal is to imagine a story, pay attention to details, and treat it as real. One important way to do that is to require people to refer to details of the fiction to use the mechanics. (Dog Eat Dog does this with Judgement.) I think Vincent goes further and argues that your game should fail to resolve entirely if people don't pay attention to details of the fiction. I am not sure this is necessary or meaningfully different.

      You don't HAVE to do it this way, but if caring about the story isn't on the action flow, then many people won't do it -- and even those who do actively do it, because they like it, are more likely to forget when they're under pressure, because in moments of crisis their eyes will turn towards the choices that will advance their goals first. Lots of people try to roleplay in D&D sometimes, but when push comes to shove, will they talk, or pull out their wand of fireballs and chat afterwards?

      \n Yes, of course. It's not all or nothing, black and white. It's a spectrum, from very tightly bound to the fiction to very loosely bound. These points on the spectrum are game design options, not political statements or categorizations of game play.

      \n I agree that "fictional" vs. "real world" is not necessarily a great way to think about the clouds/boxes distinction, but I do think the clouds/boxes thing at least raised some interesting questions and is talking about an interesting and important phenomenon in games. I just finished reading a psychology paper, Construal-Level Theory of Psychological Distance, which suggests the notion that certain "distance"-related factor in human cognition are related and tend to be linked with each other, such as abstractness vs. concreteness, social distance, physical distance, distance in time, etc. They say, for example, that "how" tends to be close while "why" tends to be far, and "lots of categories" tends to be close while "few categories" tends to be far, and providing examplars of a category (e.g. a type of soda is: cola) is closer than categorizing (e.g. soda is a type of: beverage). I see a lot of parallels in RPG stuff here: e.g. the way that explicit stakes-setting games (focus on "why") can sometimes result in seemingly losing track of the "present" of actually experiencing the scene (abstract knowledge of events instead of concrete experience of them), a larger skill list tends to feel more "grounded" than a broader breakdowns (e.g. mind/body/social), going from the list of AW GM moves to a particular fictional event tends to push you into in-the-moment thinking while things like "who knows me better?" tend to push you toward more a more abstract mindset where you might notice themes, etc. I haven't worked through all the implications yet, and there are other papers I want to read as followups, but I think there's some interesting and fruitful stuff here for game analysis and game design.
