\n I've been benching a lot lately about KickStarter and pen and paper rpg.

We've come to find something interesting and I would need some your help/thoughts about this.

Some projects are "kickstarting" a campaign/world/setting with a system. That said, they declare on their KickStarter to be "compatible" with FATE, Pathfinder, etc ...
Here's two examples (but there are others) :
- Red Aegis
- achtung cthulhu

Now, you might even notice that sometimes the "module that allows compatibility" with those systems are not even in the starting goal, but in the stretch.

Is claiming "compatibility" with FATE, Pathfinder, 13th age, dungeon world really worth the trouble? Does it bring in "natural customers"? Or is it too much work for it (I'm talking ROI here)? Where should it be on the stretch (far below or really among the first ones)? Or should it be included in the first goal? Should we do only one or all of them?
You might also notice that those "rewards" are usually only provided in pdf as rewards.
Also, I would guess it would possible to "advertise" on forums, addressing those communities.

Anyone has an idea on this? Some data? Experience to share? Advices to give me?

    \n Seems to me that whether it's worth the trouble depends on how much your setting's natural audience (that is, the people for whom it would be artistically relevant) overlaps with the pre-existing audience (the people already into it) of the given rules-system. I guess sometimes there might be first-mover advantages (first horror setting for Pathfinder, or something like that) or such, but that'd be rare. Other things being equal, something like FATE compatibility will only be useful insofar as there exists an audience of people who like both your setting, and play FATE exclusively (that is, so much that they'd care about having FATE in lieu of something else).

    One thing we do know: this is very well-trod ground in the rpg publishing culture, and the idea that people want new fluff with the convenience of compatibility with their old system is not new; things like GURPS exist pretty much on this premise. There are people who definitely see value in one-system-\xfcber-alles in their personal hobby, and then there are people who are actively disinterested. Myself, for example - if you present your rpg product as "arbitrarily mechanized", so that it just happens to have rules set X bolted onto it, it'll be a big turn-off for me, simply because it's proof positive that your project is really just an exercise in creative writing and not rpg design. (If it was rpg design, you'd presumably have designed it for some specific game, instead of being willing to whore it out under whatever brand name happens to be hot at the moment.)

    So yeah, different audience segments, different reactions. A small press publisher can't hope to hit all audiences at the same time, and no publisher at all may hope to make everybody happy at the same time in a highly specialized market like this, so it all depends on the particulars of the project.

    One pretty clear-cut situation occurs to me: if you've got a popular IP that you're turning into a roleplaying game product, it might not be a bad idea to position the product as system-neutral, with conversion notes for half a dozen popular systems "in the back" or in the Internet or wherever. The reason why this might be smart is that with a pre-existing IP you're basically trolling for fans of the property, and your sales argument is going to be that your product is full of property-related content. Presumably these fan customers will buy it for the fluff and then adapt it to their game of choice, or they might even be completely uncaring of systemic issues - in either case you won't lose much if you alienate the system-afficinados in the audience by attempting to cover all bases.

    One more thought: I suspect that whether compatibility branding brings customers depends heavily on the specific brand, simply because the different games appeal to different sorts of people on the average. A simple "compatible with X" claim might be very appealing to a fan of a game that in itself claims that system doesn't matter, or that their system does "everything", or similar obfuscatory claims about the roleplaying process. On the other hand, claiming compatibility with a game whose audience prizes very specific operative interactions of setting and system might seem like a ridiculous claim to that audience. Thus it makes more sense to claim compatibility with GURPS or d20 than with e.g. Sorcerer or old-school D&D (unless of course your thing is, in fact, designed to work with the latter), simply because a fan of GURPS will have realistic expectations about what such "compatibility" means: they'll be expecting a book of fluff with mechanical interpretations of the narrative elements tacked on. That's something achievable for a generic setting product, unlike the highly specialized sort of interfacing that some other type of game might require.

    \n It occurs to me that this would be an excellent market research topic for anybody considering a publication that has leeway in what system to use for it. Think up your research plan well, and you'll get both pre-marketing for the project (as you troll for answers to your poll, and present facts about your product alongside the questionnaire), and useful data to base your system choice on. For example, if you find out that 20% of your sample group play FATE and 100% of those would only buy your thing if it had FATE stats, while 60% play Pathfinder, but merely 10% of them would find Pathfinder compatibility mandatory... that's the sort of data that might well help you choose which system to present your thing for!

    \n Personally, I make adventures and stuff for both Dungeon World and Labyrinth Lord because the vast majority of my gaming over the last 4 years has been old school D&D and Apocalypse World games. So I'm comfortable with both systems. I can port rules back and forth for specific purposes, or write up the same character, monster, or situation that I have in my head for both systems, and while they won't work exactly the same in both, they'll still be interesting in both and make sense in context (also, in my experience, it's entirely possible for two different old school D&D games to be just as different from each other as each are from any particular Dungeon world game, so there's kinda that to deal with as well).

    If I knew how to make decent 3.x/Pathfinder versions of my stuff, it would absolutely make good financial sense to do it. But I don't.

    I'm also pretty leery of the Dungeon World rules ports that are becoming more popular lately. Basic Roleplaying, 3rd edition D&D, Savage Worlds, and even Fate have been around for a while. Lots of people have lots of experience with them, and it's not all that hard to find (or be) someone with that level of familiarity. Look at Jason's firefighter thing for Fate: he knows how firefighting works, he explains it to some dudes who know how Fate works, game happens. Is the same thing going to happen with all these kickstarters and their various versions? Apocalypse World is pretty new and most people are coming to it because of Dungeon World, which is even newer. Is it even possible to have the same kind of expertise with Apocalypse world as the guys doing those firefighting rules have with Fate?

    \n Ah, yes - we're talking about Avalanche! I certainly remember the project, and agree that it is a very good candidate for multiple-systems treatment. I have my misgivings about it (mainly about how a lack of dedicated system makes the game fundamentally incomplete, no matter how high the quality of the campaign material), but I do agree that a proactive GM willing to do a bit of legwork should find the Avalanche material very useful for FATE, TSoY, Burning Wheel, and other similarly story-oriented games that can deal with a highly ambitious fantasy adventure campaign with a strong metaplot. Dungeon World would also make sense, I believe - Avalanche has certain systemic features that mesh well with AW-style GM prep responsibilities.

    (For those who are not familiar with it: Avalance is this unique and crazily ambitious high fantasy setting/campaign that Pells has been working on for a decade already. Imagine the scope and ambition of the Dragonlance campaign, except no rules-system, no plot railroad, and the material has all been arranged into a reference system consisting of time-lines, dramatis personae and geographical atlases, so as to make it feasible for the GM to handle the high density of information. The big Achilles's heel of the project is that Avalanche doesn't have a truly natural game system to exist in, so the threshold of commitment is pretty steep for anybody who considers playing it - you're either stuck with D&D-by-default, or will have to do some major hacking on your favourite fantasy adventure system. Very unique due to the novel timeline-oriented campaign arc, well worth the time for anybody interested in high fantasy adventure rpgs.)

    Assuming that Avalanche hasn't changed much since we've last discussed it, I still think that your best bet for making it "accessible" is to get in touch with suitable designers who can help you develop system backing for your creative writing; I think it's a mistake to think of this as merely a superficial matter of "converting numbers" from one system to another - it's ultimately a highly unique question as to how each individual game (especially modern games!) should treat something like Avalanche. Multi-system may well be the way to go; as we've discussed, it's the sort of project for which it makes perfect sense to put the material onto a website, which in turn makes it trivial to provide mechanical notes for several systems without compromising usability. Also, crowdfunding seems like a fine addition to your plans: you could arrange in advance for designer X to work out the Avalanche treatment in system Y, and then put it up to the people to determine whether that's something they want to pay for. (Could be multiple separate crowdfunding campaigns, note: first you fund the initial system-free release in whatever manner, then run rounds of funding in cooperation with well-known designers to have the material fitted for whatever systems seem likely to attract funding.)

    On the other hand, all that's going to absolutely suck if you don't believe in system development yourself. If I had to choose between getting the material in the way you believe in it, or getting something developed just because everybody else wants to have rules, then I'll go for the first option. Ultimately I believe that Avalanche already has about 60% of an implicit system contained in it (it's impossible to do elaborate campaign prep without implying much about the type of game the prep is for), so the ideal choice would be to bring out that system, but this is such a specialized case of rpg-related creativity that I'm very much keen to see what your ideal publication looks like, and to hell with what anybody else thinks.

    \n As for the simple question: assuming that you decide to attempt to launch with multiple rules-systems, which systems should those be? I recommend the following, off the top of my hat:

    Pathfinder: Your original literary inspiration is D&D, so this adaptation is ultimately very simple and natural. So much so that I could see Avalanche as a Pathfinder-only campaign, if you only were more excited about 3rd edition D&D yourself. The big thing here is that ambitious campaign products are Paizo's flagship strength, so you're setting yourself up for a comparison - could mean that you've got an already-established audience that wants a campaign product, or could mean that Paizo fans don't want it because it's not structured and written exactly like a Paizo campaign path.

    Dungeon World: If you can find a competent designer to do the adaptation, I think this would be a very natural fit. Trickier than Pathfinder, but not too much so, because the game totally fits that same mold of adventuring parties and missions and whatnot. A solid choice if you feel the need to attract audiences for which Pathfinder is hopelessly old-fashioned bullshit. DW doesn't have traditional big-setting stuff for it yet, so I could see many DW-enthusiasts checking out your thing if you could convince them that it's not a hack job.

    Swords & Wizardry: I hate myself creatively for this suggestion, but it has to be said that Frog God Games has been doing some pretty good business on trad D&D stuff disguised as old school (if that didn't make sense, pay no mind - it's about different types of D&D). This tells me that while I'm personally not too fond of a plot-and-character-based middle-school D&D product myself, there seem to be plenty of people who play old school retroclones like S&W, yet desire more modern campaign material. It's a somewhat tricky market, as it seems sometimes that nostalgia and superficial name-dropping are the main determinants of success, but it could potentially be an easy addition to your line-up. The big advantage is that adaptation for old school D&D is very, very easy mechanically, much easier than any other options; it does nothing to make the material truly suited for old school play, but as I indicated, it seems to me that there is an audience for all sorts of things out there.

    (An idea, off-hand: if you decide to do Pathfinder and S&W both, then it'll make plenty of sense to actually get in touch with the FGG guys and see if they'd like to work on the conversions with you, and perhaps partner up for the crowdfunding. I'm suggesting this because they've run some pretty successful crowdfunding campaigns over the last couple of years, so there's a lot of relevant expertise there for what you're thinking of doing.)

    FATE, TSoY and Burning Wheel are all creatively exciting options as well, but they'd also require more adaptation - old-fashioned game-design basically, somebody would need to figure out exactly what it is that this game can do with material like Avalanche. I would not consider these to be priorities for Avalanche in the first phase, simply because there are more pressing issues like basic publishing model and Pathfinder adaptation (really, it's likely to be the single most important system for you both commercially and creatively) to consider. I could see you doing FATE or TSoY after you've already gotten to the market and figured out how you're dealing with the D&D / Pathfinder opportunities.

    If your goal is to simply scrape for customers without any particular artistic agenda, I would suggest saving yourself the effort of attempting the highly technical design challenge of adapting for the above modern drama-oriented games. Rather, if you just want to have many systems for obscure market positioning reasons, start with Pathfinder, then Savage Worlds, and then whatever trad-style point-buys have been openly licensed lately - the D6 system would be a good candidate along the lines I'm thinking here. As long as you limit yourself to what I like to call "traditional" games, meaning ones without anything particular going in the campaign-structuring department, it's relatively simple to just stat up Avalanche for that game and be done with it.
