Slaying the Big Three

1of3
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
Welcome.

You are here to see the end of my support of Forgian principles of design. Hereby I declare - through my very own arrogance - the Big Three dead.

These questions are the very centre of what has been taught at the Forge. Although it is true that some very important principles of RPGs have been first formulated at the Forge and presented to the larger public, the conclusions drawn from these principles are often biased.

The Big Three tell us that it is important, what the characters do and what the players do. The first question is kind of fuzzy and usually hard to answer. Often the answer summarizes at least some parts of the answers to question two and three. We can therefore say that the actions of charcters and players are aspects or at least symptoms of what the game is about.

The problem with these questions is that one aspect which is very important in traditional design is completely ignored: The setting, the playground as Levi Kornelesen described it. Setting is usually considered an important aspect of RPGs. Often I hear, that people play RPGs because of the setting. Can they be wrong?

No, obiously they can't. At least not, if there is no inferior way of play. But, you might say: "Look at the Power 19. The setting is right there. Question 4. It is not that bad."

Well, let's read it again:

4. How does the setting reinforce what the game is about?

As you see, the phrasing differs significantly from questions two and three. The difference is that this formulation discourages one answer: "You see, my game is about that setting, really."

But this ignorance of one important part of RPGs is not only the problem with the Big Three. Although we can be sure that every game played by any gamer will contain characters' actions, players' actions and a setting not every RPG book contains all three and often some parts are changed by players. We therefore have to ask:


1. What do you imagine the players to do?
2. What do you imagine the characters to do?
3. How do you imagine the setting?
4. Which of these answers will you present in your game and which do you consider most important?
5. The inclusion of which techniques have you deducted from answers one to four?
6. Finally, how do you summarize, what your game is about?

    I think I see a problem, here.

    When design aids like the Power 19 are asking about Setting, they do not mean setting in the convential sense. It's not "Middle Earth" or "the Star Trek universe" or "a pseudomedieval fantasy world with lots of stuff in it." Let's call these conventional settings Scenery. They're part of what the Big Model calls Color.

    Setting is a one- or two-sentence summary of the conflicts implicit in your world, stripped of most of the Color elements. It's equivalent to what Hollywood calls "high-concept"; it gives you an idea of what to expect from the story (in movies) or from play (in RPGs.)

    Scenery: Middle Earth
    Setting: an overwhelming, corrupting source of Evil threatens to enslave or destroy everything, and only a few brave souls believe both that there is a danger and that they have a chance to make a difference.

    Scenery: Star Trek universe
    Setting: an optimistic, scientifically-curious interstellar culture with a strong belief in the power of reason and a policy against meddling in the affairs of others is expanding through space and encountering other people's problems.

    Scenery: pseudomedieval fantasy world with lots of stuff and adventurers
    Setting: in a rich, diverse world, the best way to survive is to improve yourself, and the only way to improve is to kill monsters and take their stuff.

    These are my own interpretations of those Settings, but that, as you will see, is part of my point. But see how, in each of these Settings, you have an idea of what to expect, given that you design Characters that tie into those settings? This is what Ron means by "Character + Setting = Situation".

    Now, then: "my game is about Middle-Earth" (or any other Scenery) is wrong in the same sense that "my game is about swords" is wrong. Your game may include Middle Earth or swords, but it's not *about* them. Games aren't about things, they're about what happens in an around those things. Plus, you can't even say "oh, I mean the Setting implied by Middle Earth", because you're really ducking the question... because the deep, dark secret here is that games are *always* about Setting. What people want to know is what your Setting *is*. Not your Scenery, that's just window dressing. They want to know what might happen if they play in your interpretation of Middle Earth, or Star Trek, or the pseudomedieval fantasy world filled with lots of stuff.

    Also, I'll nominate Talislanta, which I only recently began to delve into after finding a cheap copy of the 2nd edition handbook. The game seems an ideal setting-as-playground. You've got all this *stuff*, in the form of a map, a jillion PC types, and some suggestive material about the past and present of the world, all supported (at least in the edition I hold in my hands) by a light-as-meringue system. Maybe I'd take the system and export it into another campaign. But I have a theory about why I'm digging Talislanta after avoiding it for so many years. It's that the boldly alien weirdness of the setting "decenters" the reader, prevents judgment based on preconceived notions of plausibility, and lets you believe that the system really is "how the world of Talislanta works". (Okay, maybe the inhabitants are unaware of the ability level mechanics...) Result: a game that basically begs you to fill in the blank spaces, in the course of play--either through GM/player improvisation (my favorite: player-directed GM improvisation), or if you must, via the sourcebooks.
