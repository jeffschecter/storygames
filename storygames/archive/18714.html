\n Have you played World of Dungeons for any significant period of time?

If you have, think back on your time spent playing. What kinds of things did you roll for the most, and what were typical outcomes like?

Now imagine that you're looking at a transcript of that play history. Which of those common types of rolls could you group together into "basic moves" of sorts, and what would they look like?

For example, I'm pretty sure John Harper wrote at some point that, in combat situations, if you rolled a 7-9 you'd probably accomplish whatever you were trying to do and get hit by your enemies. That's a typical outcome on a 7-9 for some kind of "in physical combat" move. But what about the miss and 10+ versions?

(If you have any GM/MC moves that aren't covered by the original AW/DW MC moves, tell us about those too!)

    \n When you try to find something that is possible to be found: it might take a long time, you might find more than you wanted to or something else might go wrong. Roll+Wis(often?). On a 6- the DM will tell you how you're in hot water. On a 7-9, you surely find it but there are consequences. On a 10+, you have nothing to complain about and on a 12+ you find it in favorable condition/position or along side something else of use or value.

    Is that the kind of thing you're looking for?

    \n WoDu's die mechanic as written is highly context and outcome dependent, and doesn't encourage the kind of constrained choices typical of AW/DW Moves. In Dungeon Girls/Kids, I've tried to steer toward the most credible and interesting outcome or complication in a given situation for a Failure/Mixed Results outcome. Successes constitute a payoff, with Crits delivering payoff with extra heroic advantage or oomph. I use the Die of Fate to inspire interesting parameters ("How many orcs ARE there in that cave?") or answer questions like "Is there really treasure here?" I don't use it as conflict resolution at all.

    Combat is the one area where more standardized Move-like methodology could prove useful, but even that isn't necessary. I don't always deal damage on a Mixed Result, but frequently try to pose an immediate threat or disadvantage, or a bigger complication the character can see start to unfold. For me, a Failure means "Hurt 'em now." A Mixed Result means "Turn up the threat or suspense," which could mean "Hurt 'em now" if doing so seems the best way to ratchet up the dramatic tension. This principle isn't that different from non-combat situations.

    It seems like you're asking for examples of use cases that might distill into useful principles. That's cool, but I like the free-flowing judgment calls that go with WoDu. Moves are fun because they drive the player to choosing among constraints, balancing advantages with costs. WoDu doesn't do that, but drives the question "What's the most interesting outcome that could happen in this moment?" Maybe most or all die mechanics purport to do so, but WoDu makes it much clearer.
