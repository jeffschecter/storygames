    \nFeng Shui is a strange game, because it's not really that much that simulates the action genre. Sure, it got rules for mooks, the named characters can take heaps of punishment, having skill value of 9 is considered a pro when the character's got 13-15 in their skills, it's bad to wear kevlar, and the damage is increased by one if you do a KA-CHINK sound before you shoot with a shotgun. That said, most of the genre simulation is made by the players by 1) telling them that it's an action game, 2) letting them play typical archetypes (like maverick cop, ninja, big bruiser), and by 3) letting the players describe their actions using those tropes. Star Wars d6 accomplish the same thing with the same three tools.

    \n My game Final Hour of a Storied Age is built to emulate the genre of Epic Fantasy novels. The stuff that's carrying the most weight in emulating the genre is my story-structure/plot subsystem (for example, it's a single-protagonist story rather than a multi-protagonist character drama), but I also do some other things to evoke the right mindset for the source material (for example, play is broken up into "chapters" and the action revolves around a single "viewpoint character" in each chapter so that things feel more like a novel than a movie or TV show) and the mechanics have elements that encourage genre-appropriate stuff (for example, there will often be adversity from "the environment" since not everything in Epic Fantasy is a conflict with another character, you'll frequently encounter situations like the Fellowship's attempt to cross the snowy pass at Caradhras).

    \n Both Empire games (Duty and Honour and Beat to Quarters) are really good at emulating Napoleonic military fiction and Napoleonic naval fiction respectively (so, Sharpe and Hornblower, basically). Character creation involves not only a clear location in the rigid social classes of the time, but also card drawing that fleshes out interesting spoils of war and histories for characters, and makes reputation an up-front-important part of the game, just as much as Skills and Traits (you can risk your reputation on important conflicts). Conflict / task resolution also uses a neat skirmish mechanic which feels like the "everyone together, doing different stuff to help" really well. It's also been hacked for sci-fi naval / military, too.

    \nHot Guys Making Out.
    You get dealt standard playing cards at the beginning of the scene. In order to take any action, you must have a card in your hand that is a valid play (based on what you were led, and some other simple stuff). But the type of card you play dictates what kind of action you can take. And each character has a different list of actions that can be taken.

    Effortlessly teaches and reinforces genre tropes. It's really brilliant in it's simplicity.
    Feedback from one player was along the lines of "I really really wanted to tell you how I felt in that situation, but I didn't have the card, so I had to do that [pompous overhanded gesture] instead." Perfect.

    \n Greg Stolze's A Dirty World really worked for emulating the film noir genre / style for me.
    The stats are always in flux and represent what state the character is in right now. Metally, physically and emotionally. They are pretty low at the beginning and lead to the characters losing and getting beat down at the beginning, but as they rise over the time of one session the characters get more sucessful. Emulating the hard luck protagonists of many noir stories and the way personalities change for better or worse.
    It also has the same rules for emotional, social and physical confrontations, which allows for much of the underhanded threatening and only hinting at violence that Hays Code influenced film noir had. It also allows player vs. player conflict without deadly consequences. Because the stats only shift when attacked you can even modify your character in a way you like by losing confrontations.
    Each character has a secret another player knows and those can be used as "weapons" in social combat. Making secrets something you want to protect but also use as leverage against other characters. Thus encouraging the tension and distrust between protagonists in many noir fiction.

    ... Huh... the best genre games I can think of are all from the 70s and 80s. Maybe that's because the Big Generic Push came all through the later 80s and 90s (I count systems with scores of "splatbooks" as "generic").

    In the 00s and Teens--YAY!     \n Alright. Danger Patrol succeeds right from the start, with characters made from pulp SF-nal descriptors to produce archetypes like Robot Detective, Atomic Daredevil, Two-Fisted Scientist, Alien Flyboy, and so on. The mechanics create a dance between bonuses for daring pulp stunts and the "Danger Point" currency to encourage over-the-top actions and derring-do, as well as cheesy dialogue. The resolution is abstract enough to create the Indiana Jones/Star Wars style slugfest without worrying about the gritty details of injury (though you could surely narrate gory results, the game presumes a social contract that adheres to a clean depiction of events).

    Ars Magica pioneered a detailed magic simulator that combines Latin verbs and nouns to produce effects. In tandem with individualized side effects, magical mutations, and a split between formulaic and spontaneous magic, the magi are powerful, unusual characters, but not superheroic. The necessity for study and research over time creates its own subgame of long-term character construction. The medieval world is emphasized in the interplay of magical and divine forces, the impact of character aging as the seasons pass, and in the way wounds and combat are a real threat. The game doesn't have much of a metagame in terms of currency like Hero Points, so there's a little old school vibe in that you can't fudge die results. In similar wise, magic is powerful but limited, and much less reliable when cast off the cuff. It all adds up to a gritty package. My experience has been that really powerful magic is earned over many years of in-game time, and that the differences between the long-lived magi and the mayfly grogs over long-term games adds a consistent thread of pathos to Ars stories. If anything, it's the project management grind of deciding on seasonal research, study, and enchantment that informs the game with a lot of its sense of the long count.

    It's certainly true that you can set the dials to make a more light-hearted game. I've been in a number of wacky sessions, filled with oddball humor, but these don't subtract from the overall grit produced by Ars Magica's system and setting.

    \n I view "hard fantasy" as a genre, but that may be an affectation on my part. There's not a lot of support for it in movies or TV, but there's a lot of it in literature, from Guy Gavriel Kay's later work to Kate Elliott's books. Brandon Sanderson considers his stuff hard fantasy. You can make a case for the Earthsea series as well. Like hard SF adhering as much as possible to the laws of physics, hard fantasy puts forth worlds where magic follows definite, consistent rules, whether the protagonists are fully aware of them or not. The side that understands these rules most deeply tends to win the overarching conflict. In any case, I think Ars Magica qualifies.

    \n The hands down winner for martial arts cinematic action for me is Wushu Open. You can literally use the terminology of cinema to add details to your dice pool. On top of that, the rule of narative truth means that what the player says happens, this can make every player a 'goddamn action hero'.

    If you consider 'action movie' a genre, I would so use Wushu for The Princess Bride, the Matrix, any John Woo gun ballet, or swashbuckling adventure. If the players like beating themselves up, the system works for cinematic survival/horror too.
