    \n Arguably, this is a distillation and slight morph of the Campbellian "monomyth"; I find it works for some stories, but isn't essential to all stories. The "return to the normal world, bringing change" is very much in line with the alleged "Hero's Journey". (The validity of it as a universal source of myth and story is something I think is at least up for debate, but that's for elsewhere.)

    That being said, if we're talking about the "reader" (or in this case, the player), a lot of roleplaying games can indeed enable such a return, at least in the ones that are very involving in some way. You play the game, have an impactful session, and then return to your regular mundane life, having picked something up from the game. In fact, that's technically one of the major points of "bleed": you experience something in the session that you then take back out of the game and into your normal life. A lot of American-based games are just non-specific and not explicit about the concept.

    Still, even D&D has this effect. Players have memorable experiences in the game, and they bring those experiences back out into their normal lives in some fashion.

    If we're talking about letting the characters return to familiarity with change, I'd argue that it's a stylistic choice that many RPGs don't enable this. It's because, in a story, a lot of people like to see characters move on into new situations. You find new digs, new circles, new ways to see life. That being said, Apocalypse World absolutely does this: since it centers around a community, you're constantly returning to your community as a source of familiarity--it's a home base.

    \n That story circle seems great for a particular kind of story, one focused on a single character (or sometimes a few). A lot of RPG stories are not really about the characters, they're about solving the problem, e.g. saving mom/the town/the world, or getting what the characters want, e.g. fame/riches/xp. In these kinds of stories most of the steps in that diagram are optional. In fact everything but 2 and 3, which are requisite of most stories anyway.

    So why don't RPG stories follow through with 7 and 8? Most haven't laid the groundwork for that, and aren't going that direction. Even if they have, the story usually wraps up around 5 or 6 (perhaps skipping back to 1 with an implied 7 and 8, perhaps not). Of course, that's generally up to the table, and sometimes the return and showing the changes in (all of) the characters can be done to great effect. Other times it's superfluous or not applicable.

    Example: Fiasco. This is ostensibly focused on the characters! But what happens in most games regarding this story circle? Everyone starts with needs, so 1-2 might be discussed during set-up but scenes usually start with 3 "entering an unfamiliar situation." Now all these characters have powerful ambition, and we don't have enough scenes anyway, for them to adapt to a situation much, so 4 is entirely optional and likely to get glossed over. The second act is all about number 6 "pay a heavy price" - whether they actually get what they want (number 5) is a crapshoot, and may not happen for anybody at all! Once we find out who got what they wanted and who didn't, the scenes are over. So, the aftermath - a lot of ways to go with this. Whether you got what you wanted or not, you CAN use the aftermath to show 7 and 8, returning to regular life having changed. But more often in my experience, players choose to show 6, showing the heavy price they paid (or extending it if it was already shown). Others go with 5 - they finally get what they want after all, or 4, they got screwed but end up adjusting and carrying on. Sometimes, the character hasn't changed at all, or their previous familiar situation has been obliterated. And importantly, in most cases all the characters had a satisfying ending anyway.

    That story circle is great for some kinds of stories (centered around 1 or a few characters, especially in a repeating cycle like TV). I just don't think most RPGs are aiming for that kind of story.

    Hey, can you say a little more about what you hoped for?

    I mean, there are so many possible answers and avenues of discussion. My first reaction is to look at how the world outside of the PCs often has little meaning outside how it impacts the PCs themselves. So while the "having changed" thing can happen, there's no real impact on the "familiar situation" - because the "familiar situation" never meant anything except a stage for the PC to do stuff in the first place. For this cycle to work, the readers (players, in RPGs) have to be invested (and have a reason to be invested) in something more than just the character (their PC). In RPGs, that can be both emotionally unfamiliar and practically difficult to accomplish. Every RPG includes character creation as a readily visible, understandable and relate-able feature. Few include "situation creation" as anything other than a hidden, implied consequence. Even fewer do anything to help make the advancement/alteration/evolution of the situation relate-able. I mean, it surely CAN happen; "with a good GM", "when everyone gets it right", "if we get lucky." Some help might be nice, though ...

    But I'd be able to focus that a bit better if I knew more about what you were hoping for.

    \n Yeah, the monomyth is blown out of proportion, and Campbell was kind of an intellectual conman. That said, though, it's not incorrect to notice that most narratives have a structures that can be discerned and often seem to follow the same patterns.

    Boiled down, the Harmon thing is basically:

    Here's the hero, in daily life
    Something happens! Hero has to deal with it.
    Doing so exposes the dark side of live as the hero knew it.
    Situation gets dealt with, one way or another (losing is possible). Life finds a new kind of normal, but the hero is not the same person anymore.

    That covers a lot of story-telling. If we accept the idea of a less literal homecoming, the "return to the world" thing is pretty important to most stories. I mean, obviously, the Lord of the Rings does this explicitly, but you can see the same structure in, say, The Crying of Lot 49. (Gravity's Rainbow sort of does it, although I'd argue it's about becoming unmoored in a world that itself changes, which complicates the picture.)

    Even less epic, less deliberately myth-making, and shorter works do this: in "A Small Good Thing" (the longer version, not the one from What We Talk About When We Talk About Love), the parents have a normal life. Their kid gets hit by a car. They deal with grief. Their relationship strains. Someone keeps calling their house asking about their kid. Finally, the boy dies, and they find out the baker they hired to bake his birthday cake is the one who's been calling. They go to meet him in the middle of the night, he realizes his terrible mistake, and consoles them. Life starts again, but changed.

    How you do this in a RPG...is trickier. In my Masks campaign, I'm going to emphatically require the players to think about it, and have a real coda after everything plays out where they return to the world, utterly changed by their experience. And I may even pull it off!

    Building a mechanic around this, or a game, would be a very interesting challenge. I'm going to have to think about this. How do you design a system so that it gives weight to the conclusion?

    \n One way is to have the conclusion as a sort of endgame, an explicit goal. Brian Engard's Becoming RPG pits a single Hero against the Fates who bid and bargain to corrupt the Hero; the Endgame is where you tally everything up, and somebody gets to deliver an epilogue. Everyone knows that's what it's gunning towards.

    Ben Robbins' Kingdom has countdown "time bombs" in the form of the Crossroads and the Crisis. When they reach the end of the track, something happens. This can often make for an excellent conclusion, as it did with a session I played.

    The main trick seems to be to remove some of the open-endedness from the game. Establish that there will be a specific conclusion, and then advance the onset of that conclusion, as you try to figure out what the conclusion will look like.

    \n Yeah, I was thinking about that last night. I tried to ask myself, what question does Indiana Jones ask in Raiders of the Lost Ark? And I didn't have any answer; the only one possible seems to be "How will he get out of this?"

    So, that's a strongly procedural hero. But incredibly compelling! (In passing, I'll note that Marion does ask a question--"Will I be able to renew a relationship with Indy as an adult?")

    Laws has observed that RPGs have been strongly procedural since their inception. Story gaming is often an attempt to push into stories built on drama, sometimes hiding it inside procedural mechanics. (This seems to be Vincent's stock in trade; "Dogs" is a game about relationships dressed up as a police procedural, for example.) The true revolution of the AW mechanics is to give dramatic effects a procedural grounding. The move from a resolution engine to a consequence engine certainly changed my view of what RPGs could do or be about.

    How that plays out in the broader swath of gaming...remains to be seen. If, like me, you are mostly immersed in gamers with older, procedural expectations, moving towards a more narrative-focused, dramatic experience can be problematic. I had an email exchange with FP from my campaign last night; he didn't like the potential consequences of a narrative event I was exploring in the game, and told me flat out that he didn't like mechanical consequences for narrative effects. What can you do? It's not my game, it's theirs; I apologized, and mentally recalibrated my thinking about the game with a character who was intentionally going to remain dramatically flat.

    I guess what I'm saying (besides working through my churn of feelings) is, to what extent can you have procedural and dramatic storytelling working together, and how can you facilitate it? I mean examples exist--it's the entire rationale of the TV show "Burn Notice", for goodness sake. 13th Age and Gumshoe reach towards this. Apocalypse World...well, here's an issue; if you read Apocalypse World as purely procedural, it will flat-out not work the way it's supposed to.

    I know Hamlet's Hit Points addressed this, albeit with the intention of making a dramatic work, not a hybrid or procedural narrative.

    Yeah, I was thinking about that last night. I tried to ask myself, what question does Indiana Jones ask in Raiders of the Lost Ark? And I didn't have any answer; the only one possible seems to be "How will he get out of this?"

    So, that's a strongly procedural hero. But incredibly compelling! (In passing, I'll note that Marion does ask a question--"Will I be able to renew a relationship with Indy as an adult?")
    \nI disagree strongly with the idea of a procedural / dramatic divide. Yes, some films have more action in them. These generally externalize the conflict, but in all the compelling action stories, the external conflict is symbolic of the point. The monsters that the hero fights are symbolic of non-physical challenges. Such stories require more understanding of symbolism in order to fathom than some.

    As I see it, Raiders of the Lost Ark is all about putting one's faith in the divine. This is repeated throughout the film in nearly every scene. Indy starts out a skeptic - he scoffs at the local's superstitions (this is repeatedly emphasized in the jungle, and he later repeats it to his class at the university). We see this symbolically as he tries to substitute a bag a sand for a holy idol, which ends poorly. This comes up over and over. The climax is particularly notable because it breaks so far from tradition. The hero doesn't defeat the villains at all - he chooses to surrender, is tied up, and then closes his eyes. The Indy who does this is unquestionably a different Indy from the one in the beginning, in my opinion.

    And here's a key point - many people wouldn't be able to articulate this symbolism, yet despite that, I think it is responsible for the power of the story. I do not think that Raiders of the Lost Ark would be more compelling if this point was made more overtly - such as by a narrator talking about the importance of faith or somesuch. \n
    Laws has observed that RPGs have been strongly procedural since their inception. Story gaming is often an attempt to push into stories built on drama, sometimes hiding it inside procedural mechanics. (This seems to be Vincent's stock in trade; "Dogs" is a game about relationships dressed up as a police procedural, for example.) The true revolution of the AW mechanics is to give dramatic effects a procedural grounding. The move from a resolution engine to a consequence engine certainly changed my view of what RPGs could do or be about.

    How that plays out in the broader swath of gaming...remains to be seen. If, like me, you are mostly immersed in gamers with older, procedural expectations, moving towards a more narrative-focused, dramatic experience can be problematic. I had an email exchange with FP from my campaign last night; he didn't like the potential consequences of a narrative event I was exploring in the game, and told me flat out that he didn't like mechanical consequences for narrative effects. What can you do? It's not my game, it's theirs; I apologized, and mentally recalibrated my thinking about the game with a character who was intentionally going to remain dramatically flat.
    \nI don't know about you, but my experience of traditional RPGs is that they are generally quite dramatic. Given our opposed analyses of Raiders, though, I suspect that we're seeing different things. I suspect you might look at one of my old GURPS games and think that it was all just about procedure.

    One of the common problems I have with recent story games is that they are unwilling to allow for or trust in symbolism. Instead, the attitude seems to always be for making things more overt - like having stats for relationships or stats like "With Love" or "Self-Loathing". In a story game of Raiders, the attitude might be "unless you have a 'faith in the divine' stat, then your story isn't about faith in the divine". \n
    I guess what I'm saying (besides working through my churn of feelings) is, to what extent can you have procedural and dramatic storytelling working together, and how can you facilitate it? I mean examples exist--it's the entire rationale of the TV show "Burn Notice", for goodness sake. 13th Age and Gumshoe reach towards this. Apocalypse World...well, here's an issue; if you read Apocalypse World as purely procedural, it will flat-out not work the way it's supposed to.
    \nAs I said, I think that most compelling procedural games are compelling because of the drama. Trying to be too analytic about it doesn't necessarily help, and definitely trying to explain those analyses doesn't necessarily help.

    \n I don't mean to interrupt so I'll slyly and subtly steer the conversation back to the low-brow topic of RPG's ; )

    Perhaps Indy isn't the best example of literary character development but he's not a bad example of RPG character development, if you consider each movie a stand alone adventure in an on-going campaign. Bear in mind the vagaries/necessities of a Hollywood production; in particular: the second movie actually was a prequel but we (the audience) are seeing Indy for the second time and expect more from him. So -

    At the beginning of Raiders, Indy is stealing a native artifact because he thinks "it belongs in a museum". At the end of the second movie he is returning a native artifact to its tribe. Yes, chronologically out of order to character growth but to the audience Indy has grown.

    The third movie shows Indy connecting with the only family he knows; his father. In a flash back, we see the distance between them and the obsession both have for artifacts. By the end of the movie, his father has let the Grail go and urges Indy to do the same, and the connection between them is cemented.

    By the fourth movie, Dr. Jones senior is dead and the Son becomes the Father. The care-free adventurer becomes the family man and connects to HIS son (but, of course, isn't quite willing to pass on the torch/hat just yet). A single line brings this home: "This is intolerable!". When Indie says his father's catch phrase, we see the connection between generations.

    Okay, so not exactly To Kill A Mockingbird (and don't be dissing Atticus or you're gonna start a flame war!) but not bad for an RPG character over the course of a few action adventures.

    So, what can we take from this? One thing I've noticed (and utilized) is the use of self-reference with-in a story; that is to say, taking something from the past and bringing it up again, and shining a new light on it. It creates a connection from past events to present. And it can high-light character change/growth. It can give substance to an otherwise seemingly random series of events. It's why "you can't go home again", because even though home may not have changed, you have.

    How you can incorporate this into your game is system specific. But I think this is a case where the mechanics of any game might reflect the fiction (instead of vice-versa). Something your character does changes their character sheet and there-by changes the way they interact with the world. If your barbarian gives up his Barbarian Rage for some sort of Diplomacy advantage - well, either that's a story worth hearing or you're the WORST min-maxer ever.
