\n Over in this other thread (over here), we were discussing how to handle unusually difficult actions in Apocalypse World or *World games of all sorts. Lots of interesting approaches and solutions have been mentioned so far, and I feel like I have a number of tools as an AW MC to handle this kind of situation.

However, I'm surprised no one talks much about the opposite problem:

What if you feel the character should have a better chance of success?

What do you do then? How can you adjust the difficulty of the action?

I've seen two "solutions":

1. Make a softer move on a miss if the character is well-positioned fictionally.
2. Don't ask them to roll at all; just let them "do it".

However, I'm not sure this necessarily does the trick. For one, the rules tell us that "when you do it, do it", which means that under many circumstances you wouldn't want to use option 2. And option 1 is not much consolation for a player character going up against serious opposition. (A very soft move isn't terribly believable for a very dangerous opponent, after all.)

In my very first game of Apocalypse World, I played a Brainer with -2 Hard.

I never went aggro on anyone in that game, because I was too scared (the odds of outright failure being in the 72% range, and the odds of full success only around 3%). I would have enjoyed some way for the MC to give me a better chance to succeed which didn't break the whole system. Instead, I found myself roleplaying my way around potential Hard-based moves, kind of like how a D&D thief tries to avoid ever having to roll their thief skills.

I'm not sure this is optimal for the system in question.

Imagine that my character had a rival or an enemy: a hard-ass bad dude that he was committed to taking down. The Brainer couldn't just walk up to him and go aggro: he'd probably get killed. If he wanted to take him down, he'd have to set up a situation which allowed him for an automatic kill. However, if something went wrong and he didn't manage to get the drop on the "bad dude", I would still want his preparation or leverage (fictional positioning) to matter.

Let'say the Brainer got the dude cornered in a room and opened fire on him with an automatic weapon (go aggro). Would he really still only have that 3% or so chance to hit and kill him?

How do you handle this in your games? Do you have an intermediate solution between option 1 and option 2?

    \n This seems to me to be a complicated question with many aspects that smarter people than me will do a better job answering. At first pass, I notice two things.

    First, the consequences of failure should generally be interesting enough that you're still tempted to do things that might fail. This is on the GM, and it's part of making the world interesting and being a fan of the PCs. The thing about failed rolls in AW is that they are opportunities to push the plot along. Some of the best action scenes in the movies could be represented as one or two failed AW rolls.

    Second, cornering a dude and opening fire isn't go aggro. The rules are pretty clear that in cases like that, a roll is pointless. You're just doling out harm. I don't see what's not satisfying about that, when you've put prep into fictional positioning that puts you exactly where you want to be.

    \n So, preparation!

    There are several ways to get a bonus to a roll in AW, which have been mentioned here already. Help, reading a sitch and taking +1, some character moves, etc.

    There's also fictional positioning, which is a big deal, and well-understood 'round these parts, I think.

    There's a link between the two, though, that maybe isn't always obvious. I see people struggle a bit with this sometimes. It has to do with reading and then acting on the answers.

    So, first, let's think of the read moves as workspaces, because they are. The player asks 'how could I get x?' and the MC tells them the requirements. Take your Hard-2 brainer. She decides to go murder a guy with her shotgun, and that guy is in a position to fight, and she doesn't want to fight because fighting is bad news for her and she's not throwing her life away just yet thanks. So, she goes out to his compound, hunkers down and she reads the sitch. And she asks "what's my best way past his ability to fight me?" And the MC answers, "Well, he's holed up in there pretty good, with his gang, too, but everyone has to sleep right? When they're out on a raid, if you hid in his room, you could murder him when he came back and went to bed."

    That exchange is a kind of roadmap now. You want to make a preparation to get something (in this case, an enemy at your mercy) and the MC has told you the best way to do it. Sure, yeah, later you get +1 to a roll when you follow through, but that's small potatoes. You probably won't even have to roll once you achieve the position.

    Of course, getting into position could be simple ("everyone leaves, roaring out on their bikes into the burn flats. there's no guard because, seriously who would go into that place on purpose?") or it could be dicey, depending on the fiction ("crippled Roarke is left behind, clutching the chains of the guard dogs in one hand, sucking on a bottle of that rotgut with the other"). But, because of the asked-and-answered structure of the read move, you know that hiding in his room and waiting for your victim to fall asleep is literally the best way to get him at your mercy.

    (Aside 1: Are there any other RPGs that give you such a gift of effective preparation like that? The GM literally tells you the best way to do something. I can't think of any off the top of my head.)

    (Aside 2: At PAX, I asked Vincent if there was anything he'd change about AW now, and he said he would tweak the 'read' moves. On a miss, you still get to ask one -- and the MC makes a hard move, as normal. I've adopted that as an official rule and I love it.)

    There's still so much to discuss. But that's enough wall-of-text for now.

He may not be the hardest badass in town, he may not be the coldest or the hottest, but his wits are a tool that can give him an edge, and he'll use it.     Which is why I don't find modifying rolls very compelling. If its take a +1 forward because you had a conversation, or heard a thing in passing, or because you examined the door before opening it, I think the game loses something.

    I also like that the MC isn't concerned with how difficult something is beyond whether the outcome is certain or uncertain. I like that the MC is occupied with how difficult something looks, rather than how difficult it actually is. I'd rather the dice tell me if it was too difficult after the PC makes the attempt, rather than figuring that out in advance. Sometimes something looks more difficult than it is. The MC doesn't know. The MC is playing to find out.

    \n Rolling 3d6 and keeping the best two is a pretty steep advantage. And, as we've discussed in this thread, giving a bonus to a character in a beneficial position is not necessarily the best way to go or in the spirit of the rules.

    That said, this thread got me thinking about dice combinations a few days ago, and I worked out the probabilities. Here's my favourite solution:\n

    When your character has taken the time and effort to secure an advantage which should improve their chances, the MC will give you an extra die to roll. The MC can choose one:
    * You've earned a meaningful advantage. Take a d4.
    * You've managed to garner an unusually strong advantage or assume a extraordinarily strong position. Take a d6.
    * You've positioned yourself in such a way that you have a truly overwhelming advantage over your opposition. Take a d8.
    Roll all the dice at the same time and add the two highest dice together to get your result.
    \nThis is nice: an extra d4 gives you a little less than a +1 (on average, +0.7 or so), but tends to turn misses into partial hits without increasing your odds of a strong (10+) hit very much. Seems perfect for what we're trying to model.

    The extra d6 is roughly equal to +1.45, and the extra d8 is more or less like adding +2.3.

    So, if you want a "when you have an advantage" kind of mechanic, the extra d4 is a nice touch, meaningful (reduces your miss chance by about 14-15%) but not too strong.

    Once again, I'm not sure this is necessary or desirable. But the math is nice! I'm putting together a sort of Apocalypse World hack using this right now. I'll post it when I have the time to write it up!
