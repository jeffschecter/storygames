\n I was hoping someone could provide some advice about a game I played last weekend. It was a lot of fun and in theory used the RuneQuest III system, but in reality the GM just narrated the outcome of events as they saw fit, loosely guided by a few skill checks. It struck me that the system the GM had chosen wasn\u2019t really supporting running the game he (and the players) wanted. My question is can anyone suggest a ruleset that might be more suitable?
\n
\nBriefly the details of the game are as follows. It was set in Atlantis which was an early Bronze Age culture. The PCs were members of a noble family and the plot focused on their struggles with a group of politically powerful librarian-priests who were trying to stop Atlantis sinking by conducting mass sacrifices of barbarians to the sea goddess.
\n
\nIt was very much a traditional RPG, with all the scenes framed by the GM and little player authored content. However after the scene had reached a conflict the GM would call for a few appropriate skill checks and then narrate the outcome accordingly (with plenty of input from the players). For instance we had a fight scene where the fact that we would win and all survive was not in doubt, and the only question was how we acquitted ourselves. My character who was a distant relation trying to gain acceptance in the family failed his skill roll and injured the head of the family by mistake with a poorly aimed arrow.
\n
\nThe GM kept apologising for not running the game strictly by the rules and I think he would have been much more comfortable with a system that would have guided us through setting stakes, determining who won and then assigning rights to narrate the outcome. I discussed this with him and he agreed, however he asked me to suggest a better system, which I couldn\u2019t do. I thought of Primetime Adventures, which we had all played once before, but this didn\u2019t seem to fit given the heavily scripted plot.
\n
\nI would be grateful if someone more knowledgeable could suggest a more suitable set of rules. Thanks in advance for any help you can provide.

    \n This is perhaps a shot in the dark, but perhaps a minor hack of John Wyck's Houses of the Blooded would work? It's a game where you play nobles in an Atlantis-style era. Your opponents tend to be other movers and shakers, but you cannot touch them directly - you have to do it through intrigue, deceit or the strict rules governing warfare. The resolution system seems to work for your purposes - if the player wins, s/he gets to decide if and how their character succeeds or fails, and if the GM wins they get to decide. It's intended for playing tragedies, which your game sounds like it is. Finally, it's Fate, which makes it rather easy to mess with if there are features you don't like or would like to see. Fate is traditional enough that most gamers should find it recognizable, but flexible enough to handle story rather well, IMHO.

    Houses of the Blooded proper has a bunch of setting-specific rules governing marriage, seasons and so on, which may not be suitable for your game. Check out the preview PDF - it may be that all the rules you need are in there, and if not, the whole game is $5 as a PDF from IPR.

    \n I wouldn't discount PTA entirely, or at all. Remember that in many conflicts, the point isn't "will you succeed", but "will you do something positive or negative in relation to your Issue.

    For example, a scene:

    For the purposes of the plot, you know that the characters in the scene need to find a clue that leads them closer to the killer. There are three characters involved. The conflict for #1 is "do I impress character #2 with my knowledge of forensics?" The conflict for #2 is "do I let go of my staid personality a bit and show some imagination?" and for #3 is "do I assert my authority as The New Boss?"

    None of the people involved in the scene have, as their conflict "Do we find a clue?" Finding the clue is assumed.

    ---

    From that point of view, it sounds VERY much like that final scene that you described in your game.
