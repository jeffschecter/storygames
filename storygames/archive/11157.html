    \n I was playing Donjon. The GM is steeped in RobinLaws-ism and tends towards putting story ahead of everything. I'm not unhappy with that mindset at all, but we've been struggling a bit with the fudging thing.

    We were in an underground beehive fighting Giant Mutant Devil Bees. We rolled some massive dice pools but kept getting 19s and 18s, while the GM kept rolling 20s and beating us by a hair. The amount of whiff was uncanny. It didn't help that, for every bee we killed, another one appeared in its place. In the end our level 1 party of 5 people had fought 5 bees, each level 2.

    After the third flurry and about 4 hours spread across two sessions, we broke off and peeled out of there. After the session the GM admitted to fudging quite a bit to the PCs benefit (i.e. reporting lower numbers than he actually rolled) and that if this had been a more traditional game, he would've fudged even more because of his incredible luck.

    Why did he do it? Simple: because he wanted us to have fun, and getting our asses handed to us by a bunch of goon bees didn't sound like fun to him.

    In actuality, what happened was this:\n
      \n
    • Everyone had misunderstood the rules about fact stating. The rule is, when you get successes, each success translates into a Fact you can state about the fiction. The losing side will now narrate how those Facts came to be. We played this in reverse - when a player or GM succeeded, they just narrated something which contained some Facts. This means that the PCs, who tended to be on the losing side, never got narration rights and thus couldn't do anything in the fiction to prevent the ass-whupping from continuing.
      • \n
    • The fudging resulted in fewer successes for the baddies, which resulted in less extreme results. Had more extreme results been allowed to happen, the combat would've been over sooner - which would've indicated to the GM that this group of characters are not well-built enough, and their players aren't skilled enough, to handle 5x level 2 baddies on their own. It also would've gotten us back on track sooner, resulting in less "lost" actual playtime. The system wouldn't have killed us - it's just not lethal enough - and if it had, new characters are quick and easy to roll up.
      • \n
    \nDonjon is a very whiffy system. Much like Sorcerer (whose system is pretty much shares) it doesn't seem very concerned with accurate modelling of reality or even a gameable, predictable system. Rather, its mechanics give you unexpected results you have to deal with, and results which inform both fiction and mechanics in tangible ways. By compensating for the whiff through fudging instead of following the procedures of play to the letter, I believe our GM actually inadvertently prevented the story from proceeding instead of allowing it to proceed in the direction the system was pointing.

    \n I fudged several die rolls running D&D; 4E, 6-9 months ago.
    \n
    \nI did it because the encounters were too difficult and people stopped having fun. I ran 4E for 2 groups who have never roleplayed before and another group who played 2E and Vampire.
    \n
    \nWe ran several pre-made official Wizard encounters. I started with easy encounters and only scaled up when people became comfortable. The problem was that sometimes the easy encounters were deadly and the hard encounters were a breeze.
    \n
    \nThe encounters also seemed designed for maximum boredom. Monsters with high defenses so players whiffed a lot and waste their special daily powers, high hit points so combats would take forever, and monsters generally dealt small amount of damage making the players feel un-threatened until some mixture of condition combos, terrain, and luck quickly escalated the encounter to total party kills.
    \n
    \nWhen I fudged the dice frequently and adjusted hit points, defenses, and damage on the fly, these problems went away. I'm ok with fudging when people are learning to play or the group decides collectively to alter the game to customize it to our needs. So after they were all comfortable, I stopped fudging and let them know what I did fudge (they knew I was going to adjust the game on the fly to start).
    \n
    \nWhen we played minus fudging, it sucked. Everyone was frustrated. Having someone miss 5 times in a row over a 1-2 hour time span, literally doing nothing was more than ridiculous. But no one wanted to go back to fudging. A lot of these people played heroclix, magic, and video games. They felt if the games was so inconsistent, why bother. There were other games we could play. I left it up to them what level of difficulty we would play at. Same level, lower level, or higher level. This difference often felt inconsequential. It was way too random.
    \n
    \nI definitely feel the system was directly at fault.

    \n Couple weeks ago at 4E, I rolled 15 dragon breath to-hits and hit with two of them. Looking back, I wish I'd fudged those in my own favor.

Our fey pal was set up to inflict some sweet badness with his at-will teleport bolt, popping the PCs into a set of inescapable birdcages. He wanted them to kidnap their friend's baby for him, and figured they'd be easier to negotiate with if they were all in those cages.

    So I'm locked and loaded -- but when the time came to pull the trigger on the plan, I blinked. Some impulse told me that the encounter would be more gripping, fun, and appropriately challenging if the teleport bolt were a Recharge 4+ instead of an at-will. So I nerfed my own guy, downgrading his power.

    And it sucked ass. The first teleport shot imprisoned the warlord, who got to watch the whole encounter, occasionally using a ranged power when the action moved in range of his cage. And then the power failed to recharge until the last round, when it was too late, so I was unable to bring the encounter to the quick, decisive conclusion I'd originally envisioned and wound up with the degenerate case I specifically designed around, where 1-2 players would be bored in cages.

    Lesson learned: in a challenge-based game, don't tinker with carefully designed encounters because of some weird spasm that says that the players can't handle it. They'll find a way, or they will be defeated and we will mouse-guard up the failure and move on.

    \n Well, surely Jason can accept that everybody else understands the word differently from him. It seems pretty obvious to me that the term "fudging" is at its most useful when it's used for intentional breaks with the game's procedures in an effort to improve the experience. Under this definition Judd's dice-cheating is clearly fudging, while Jason's pregenerated NPCs are not. More significantly, we'll note that while a rules-set can give its blessing to fudging, it can't really change the fact that the fudge is always a break in the normal procedures of playing the game. I wrote about this in the other thread, I think - the reason for why I find cheating at dice annoying is that it leaves everything in the game up to my fiat instead of providing legitimate procedures. Whether the rules permit me to fudge is a secondary concern - if they don't, then I'm breaking trust, but if they do, I might still be breaking trust (with the games that advocate lying to the players, like the new Warhammer), or I might just be playing a badly designed game that has an unfun procedural break in it, forcing me to exercise fiat in random points. It's inefficient design, pure and simple.

    (A word more on those pregenerated WoD NPCs: while they are not fudging in any way I understand the term, they are a sign of pretty primitive auteuristic GMing. Predetermining which conflicts PCs can win and which they can't is not usually something I consider fun.       \n Fudging is not illegitimate, it's just boring to me. Also, a game written to be used with fudging, but without specifying where and why it is to be used, is badly written. Furthermore, even a game that cares enough to explain where, why and how you're supposed to use fudging can be a pretty awful thing - not in any logically absolute manner, but as a simple aesthetic judgment on my part. For example, I don't find the new Warhammer a very fun game. It is theoretically defensible, but even then I'm almost certain that nigh-on anybody will be better served by either scrapping the rulebook and only using the GM guide, or vice versa - the two parts are only usable together by wilfully engaging in an elaborate ritual of pretend-play wherein grand game-mechanical structures are pre-empted consistently by GM fiat. That sort of thing can theoretically serve a purpose, but only if we assume that the gaming group gets their rocks off by a combination of ritual pretense and GM-authored storytelling.

      (I just read the Warhammer rules a couple of days ago, which is why I'm using it as an example. The GM book includes lengthy treatises on why and where the GM should fudge. It's very logical and easy to follow, and rather insane from where I'm standing - why the fuck am I supposed to waste all that time picking the right powers for my dude if the GM's job is explicitly to ensure that whatever I do, the outcome is that my character triumphs after great struggle?)

      But still, most of that is my own preference, experience and understanding, which doesn't particularly need to be justified. My experience is that fudging is boring and stressful, because it only makes sense in a context wherein I'm responsible for actively overriding the game we're playing to ensure that everybody has a good time. This is almost a polar opposite of what I find interesting in games: a good game should be structured to provide clear choices, oracular supports, interaction and interesting constraints for creativity. Fudging is none of these, it's just a nasty glitch that pops up when the game is ill-designed and needs to be hacked on the spot to allow it to continue. I don't know if there is anything more for me to say on the matter.

      I guess I can see how somebody who is not me can enjoy fudging - as I speculated above in relation to the new Warhammer rpg, it seems like fudging is a fine tool for exercising GM fiat: you have an outcome you want, and you're going to get it even if it means breaking the game arbitrarily. I guess that if you find your goals more important than the rules of the game you're playing, then this makes sense. It's been a long time since I've had that sort of creative drive going into a game, though; nowadays I just go with the flow and work within the system and its goals, swapping games if a given game doesn't suit for what the group wants to do. I also find it a peculiar attitude to have meta-goals like that in playing a game.

      Setting aside my own preferences, In the end the only illegitimacy I can find in fudging is that I'm not convinced that it has a healthy purpose in fulfilling gaming goals. Every example of fudging that I've seen seems to me like they're just the result of a flawed game design (flawed in relation to what the given group is trying to make the game do). Even GMs who swear by fudging as the only reliable way of running games mostly seem to me like they're just deluding themselves about the necessity of a physical-model rules system; if you just drop the act and stop rolling dice where the outcome is already determined by GM fiat, then there's no need to fudge. If you think that the players need to roll dice and feel like there's an impartial rules system controlling the game (this is what the Warhammer GM's guide says), is that really a healthy motivation? You're essentially saying that the best way to entertain the players is by lying to them about the internal workings of the game they're playing. That's a quite interesting claim, and I find it a shame that we don't get more people in the real world, outside GM guide sections of rpg books, defending this viewpoint. Lying to make the game better, really?

      Seems to me that the only way to accept fudging as a valid design solution is to posit a game where the players are gaining value from the rules set even while it is only advisory for the GM, who is supposed to override the system any time it gives outcomes that do not serve his goals. Is this a reasonable position? It might be, but I've never enjoyed games like that. It seems like uneconomical design to me, to first build a rules system and then declare that anything the system does has to be vetted by the GM anyway. It's a lot of hassle, dice-rolling and immersion-breaking for the players just to create advisory data for the GM to use in his decision-making; total freeform seems much cleaner in this regard - you could even have the GM run some dice-rolling oracles if he really needs those to arrive at his decisions.

      \n There's a hell of a lot of ground to cover between Jason's view, which is "fudging is to make sure everyone enjoys the game by compensating for rules when they're bad", and Eero's view, which is "fudging is to uphold the illusion that cause and effect are due to player choice and impartial mechanics". There's also the fundamental difference of "my good friends are here to have fun with some game or another" and "here's this game, who's interested in playing it".

      Our "group" plays both traditional-style games and more focused-design story-style games (I hate terminology - you know what I mean). "Group" is in scarequotes because it's more of a pool of a dozen or so players. Literally everyone have wildly differing needs and expectations. It is simply impossible for me to say "Oh lookie here, I have this copy of Best Friends - let's play that next" to a group who just came off DnD 4E. Rather, if I want to play it my best bet is to say "Okay, look, I want to play Best Friends. Here's the pitch and the caveats. Anyone else interested?" This yields me a group of players who have bought into the theme, concept and mechanics of Best Friends, rather than a group of people with pre-determined notions of the techniques and procedures of play and what they'll get out of it, requiring me to put on a song and dance so they won't feel let down.

      Or not. Most of the time my pitches fall flat and the game continues to sit there on the shelf. That's fine, too - better, in my mind, than me having to wrangle with system design while under pressure at the table to ensure all players are fully satisfied.

      Sometimes I wish I could get Adam, Beatrice, Charles and Dagny in the same room together, have them explain to me what they'd like to do and experience, and put together a rule system on the fly using some generic rules that'd let them enjoy themselves. In that situation, I'd totally conveniently forget to apply certain rules sometime, misapply them other times, or bump the table with a grin when someone loses a roll they really wanted to score. But I don't do that because it puts undue stress on me as a host for what amounts to merely an enjoyable social event - I'd rather supply freshly ground coffee and tasty snacks, and not game if I can't get Adam, Beatrice, Charles and Dagny to agree on what works for them.

      Not everyone splits it down that line. Some members of our "group" has a third mode, which I can only describe as "I'd like to explore this setting/concept with this bunch of friends". They put the setting or conceit first, pitches that to the group of people they'd enjoy that exploration with, and thinks about system last of all. In play they tend to apply rules as they see fit - not change them per se, or alter dice rolls, but simply omit them if they feel they'd be distracting from the point of play at that time. Taken to its extreme this definitely leads to the "we played DnD and didn't roll dice at all, it was awesome" effect. I can't quite wrap my head around this approach myself.

      Still, personal preference and conscious decisions.

      \n For this post, by fudging I mean fudging on the dice.

      Basically, I don't like doing this. For me, if it's not open it's the GM abrogating the chosen system's results in favour of his selected results, which tends to lead to GM-driven story, deprotagonisation and crying kittens.

      If it is open, I prefer a mechanic for it, lest we fall into freeform diceless madness. Drama points or whatever. Oh, and everyone should get to do it.

      Anyway, I do fudge on occasion. Where I do so is when I have statted an encounter or opponent, but have made a mistake in doing so that doesn't become apparent until the dice hit the table. We're playing, and my hardass mercenary turns out mechanically to be a wuss or my effete courtier turns out to be a bad-to-the-bone duellist.

      Now, if I planned it that way, fair enough, but if I actually intended the courtier to be a wimp but due to my misunderstanding of the combat stats I've actually made him into Schwarzenegger, I'd then fudge. The reason is to fix an error of my own making, a rules error committed during prep that isn't apparent until play.

      If that happens more than once or twice, clearly I'm struggling with that ruleset, and will likely switch to another game.

      Last time I remember fudging was with Heroquest. I didn't understand from the book that a starting character was like a first level guy in D&D, the term masteries for a start made me think characters were serious people. The PCs created their characters, who by and large were portrayed as being experienced and competent people. We started play, I used the resistances suggested in the book and the PCs had their asses handed to them in every encounter. They were supposed to be experienced, but the dice said first level D&D guy. After a bit, I fudged every roll by the same factor to get the PCs to where we'd all intended them to be. I didn't run HQ again though, I was too annoyed by the mismatch between game fiction and game mechanic that it created and the lack of clarity in the book's descriptions of how stuff worked (plus I hated bidding wars, which I understand aren't in the new edition).

      Anyway, another data point for the thread.

      \nPerhaps sometimes, I think often though it's fear of consequences.

      The GM rolls the dice, or asks the characters to roll the dice. They've not thought through what may happen. The dice roll, the result is unacceptable for the "GM's story", or would plainly be unacceptable to the group. The GM fudges.

      There's a few problems there though, firstly if a GM is fudging because otherwise the adventure can't continue, that suggests to me railroading is happening. Secondly, the GM is abrogating the players' right to have an input on the result, if you want to roll the dice but there's a tiny chance of a dumb outcome - and that chance comes up, I think it would be better to openly say "let's ignore that", many old school GMs instead just fudge and so decide for the group which is problematic.

      I guess I'm suggesting it's not a sophisticated narrative technique, it's a very crude one which mostly serves to prioritise GM story concerns over group story concerns, by misleading the other participants as to the actual mechanics being applied at the table.

      \n So, this is what I mean.

      I played Traveller: we were infiltrating a spaceship, which had been hijacked by terrorists.

      When we missed a roll by one, we would sometimes succeed, but get a negative consequence. We'd get through the door, but set the alarm off.

      When we succeeded a roll, but only just, we would only just succeed.

      When we missed a roll by a long way, we would seriously, seriously fail.

      Although you could call this fudging, it seems to me that the system, as it was used at the table, was pretty sophisticated. It allowed for succeeding-but-with-a-consequence. In short, it did everything we do in indie games, but without explicitly naming it.

      So, however much we talk down fudging, I think it's a pretty good technique when used well. I also think that we've developed a strawman idea of fudging, in which thousands of GMs shamefacedly ignore failed rolls. In fact, I think most GMs know what they're doing, and fudge die rolls in a pretty interesting way.
