    \n Here are the rules for multiple protagonists in a scene:

    The scene concentrates on one protagonist. We'll call this one the primary prtaongist. That protagonist's Heart, like in ordinary situations, can use key phrases.

    There is another protagonist present. We'll call this one the secondary protagonist. The secondary protagonist is treated like a secondary character in all ways, with the following exceptions:
    1) The secondary protagonist's heart always controls the secondary protagonist.
    2) The secondary protagonist's heart has veto power over any statement made about the secondary protagonist, by either the Heart or the Mistaken or the primary protagonist.

    That's the rules.

    \n I'll take a stab at trying to explain this, as it's not really straightforward (and I've played Polaris many times!). "And so it was..." is the phrase used to frame a scene, so the Heart (or Mistaken) framing the scene can actually frame it at the beginning of a conflict in which two characters are involved e.g. two characters are fighting a horde of demons. In this case, the group now has to figure out how the conflict will work, since the conflict resolution system is designed to be specifically between a single Heart and their Mistaken. It then gets complicated because, as Ben pointed out, a Heart always has control over their character's actions. So you now have the Primary Heart (Heart1) in the main conflict with the Mistaken, fighting alongside the Secondary Heart (Heart2), who controls the actions of that character. There are many ways to deal with this: switching viewpoints, as mentioned above, is one way, Heart2 having veto power over any action that is narrated regarding their character is another. What if it's two characters fighting each other? Then you have the Mistaken as well as Heart2 providing an opposing force to Heart1: a completely different situation than the previous example!

    There are too many cases, too many possibilities to put down in rules. Polaris is a story about these Knights' tragic ends and the rules presented support that incredibly well. The reason this scene-framing technique is described as advanced is because it should really only be attempted after the people involved have played the game several times and have a good, intuitive sense of the flow of the game. It's for people who are comfortable breaking outside the established structure of the game (where the Hearts don't interact and do not even be of the same generation). There is no "right way" to run conflicts with multiple Hearts involved, so there are no rules for it.

      I think I figured out a neat way to handle multiple protagonists in the same conflict and it doesn't break the rules and it effectively uses "and so it was" in a slightly different way. At least I have a frame work of where to start when it comes down to multiple-protagonist conflicts:

      The key is to continue to play the conflict over multiple scenes. Since the position of heart/primary protagonist will be changing with each scene, the mistaken will also be changing and the same opposition could be handled by a different mistaken from scene to scene. The conflict is "the same" but the focus may change dramatically as different protagonists become hearts. So, each has plenty of opportunity to contribute. When the heart or the mistaken had enough of a specific scene they say "and so it was" and another heart and mistaken take over.

      The only thing I am not sure is when to finish the conflict.
