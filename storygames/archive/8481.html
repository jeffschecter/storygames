The GM is also constrained to outcomes of rolls in a couple mechanical ways: dice don't hit the table 'til everyone agrees to outcomes, and outcomes are absolutely unbreakably binding once the dice hit the table.

    Also, per your definition of "game" (which I agree with), the real kick in the pants is this: Don't Be A Dick. It's a rule! I believe it's actually printed in BE, but it's also an overriding, absolutely do-not-break rule in BW play culture. So if not being a dick is a rule, the only way the GM can do dickish things (i.e. fiat that touches the bathing suit area) is by breaking the rules.

    In short, BW is built around the GM following the same rules as the players, with the addition of special GM-only rules that give him the practical flexibility to administer the game. But there is explicitly no version of "rule zero" (where "rule zero" = the GM is above the rules) anywhere in the game text, or in the game culture.

    \n Here's an exercise.
    \n
    \nYou're playing D&D;, you're in a dungeon. In the course of the description of the hallway the DM mentions a door on the right. You go to that door. The DM says "It doesn't open". You say "I pick the lock". The DM says "It's not pickable". The M-U says "I cast 'Knock'". The DM says "it doesn't work".
    \n
    \nWe can all agree that this is the DM exericising fiat to declare that this particular door at this particular time cannot be opened, yes?
    \n
    \nOk, you're playing some computer RPG, you're in a dungeon. You noticed a door nicely rendered to your right. You go to that door. You hit the "open door" key. It doesn't opened. You pull up your skill list and select "Use Locking Picking". Nothing happens. You pull up your inventory list and double click your Open Doors scroll. Nothing happens. Now, if nothing happened because there was some random chance to open the door and you failed that's one thing. But if nothing happened because that "door" was just a rendered graphic and there is actually no coded door even there and nothing coded on the other side, its just decoration that is completely non functioning...is that not effectively fiat?
    \n
    \nJust as the DM decided, and had the authority to enforce, that no you could not open this door; so to did some programmer / programming team decide (and had the authority to enforce) that you could not open this door. The fact that they made that decision 1000 miles away and months or years of development time ago seems not to matter one whit. The decision in any PC game as to what crates can be moved, what chests can be opened, what chairs can be sat in, what windows you can break, what walls you can climb, what trees you can chop down...and what of all those things you can't is fiat. It was a decision made "and that's the way it is".
    \n
    \nYou might say "that sucks, I should totally be allowd to bypass the guards at the door by going around back and climbing in through the window". In the computer game you can't do that because the window around back isn't coded to be functional. In the table top game you can't do that because the DM will come with a never ending string of excuses to prevent it. In both cases its because some Plot Master has decided that you can't get into that building until you complete a quest so the guards will let you in, period. In both cases that decision is being enforced by fiat. Fiat on the spot by a live DM or fiat by programming is irrelevant, the effect is exactly the same.
    \n
    \nPC games are far far fuller of fiat than even the most railroaded table top game ever has been...at least until we get to petabytes of memory space and processing power...
    \n
    \nPlease. This thread continues to ramble around in circles because Filip chose to call this select list of things he's looking to avoid "fiat". It would have been infinitely better if the thread title was "Help me think of a game that has these properties". As it is there's just a whole bunch of unnecessary back and forth going on.
