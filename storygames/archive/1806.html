    The answer may simply be that it evolved from the FUDGE system, and that's certainly valid. But if that's the case, my follow up question is - do you see any specific drawbacks to shifting to a different mean-zero mechanic?
    None, really, so long as you stay in the same *approximate* result range (-4 to +4).

    I've heard d6-d6 endorsed with the idea that 1-6 or 6-1 is also taken as a zero as being a decently close fit. But don't fear -5 or +5 if you prefer it -- result ranges in Spirit/Fate are broad enough that it can weather the extra bump of one.

    Yes, the d6-d6 method produces a "Pyrimidal curve" if that oxymoron makes sense. As opposed to the bell curve. Which means that you'll tend to have wilder results. a +4 with FUDGE dice occurs 1:81. A +5 with the d6-d6 occurs 1:36.

    Which is why I think that the d6-d6 might actually be superior. If you like radical things to happen during play. Which I do.

    Truth be told, the statistician in me loves the idea of the fudge dice, and their nifty curve. And one can argue, too, that lots of radical results dillutes each - rather that rare radical results can seem more "special."

    What's interesting is that you can actually get something in between by simply rolling more FUDGE dice. This gives you the potential for more radical results, but they're still somewhat rare. Consider that with the FATE ladder, that if both sides roll 7dF, you can get the full range of things all able to affect each other. With about 10dF, you get to where any result can occur from any combination of opposing abilities. With 14dF, you can get any ladder result regardless of starting level. And with 28dF, you can get any result on the ladder with any roll from the opposition that's in the normal range.

    OK, I'd say that about 10 is probably more than enough. But then you can increase the scale to allow more values on the ladder to allow more dice to be viable...

    Mike

    The other thing of note that 2.0 did and SOTC doesn't, which takes specific advantage of fudge dice, is "flip" dice, so a "-" became a "+" when you used an aspect. SOTC grants a flat +2 bonus, which is more open ended, but also a little less tactile. It's a good change for Pulp (the old method limited the best outcome to +4, SOTC has no such limt), but I'm still not sure how I feel about it in general.
