\n My RPG group is having a tough time lately. I always GM (dislike playing) and we typically do gritty low-fantasy games (lots of Burning Wheel - currently doing Burning Wheel Ravenloft). We've played most Indie favorites like all of Vincent's stuff, Sorcerer, Dust Devils, Lacuna, etc. but lately we're a little burnt out.

The problem is that my players really want to do some high fantasy as opposed to our typical game. They want magic to be present and not something out of the ordinary. I'm not completely comfortable with this aesthetic (I prefer to Tolkien-esque take on magic) but I'm willing to give it a shot. Additionally one of the players expressed a desire to do something a little lighter than our typical deep-engrossing drama style play. If we don't find a game to suit this style we will probably drop RPGs for awhile and go back to Boardgames/Miniature games.

The help I am asking for is identifying a high fantasy game that rocks. These actually don't seem very common on the Indie scene but if there's a mainstream game that you think is awesome please let me know.

In doing my own research I came across the game Spellbound Kingdoms which seemed very interesting. The revised version is due out in a matter of a couple weeks and some of the mechanics were fresh and different. The only problem is that magic is snuffed out by those in charge so that they can keep it for themselves. The group doesn't want to worry about being persecuted for being a magic user (this happens way too often in our games historically).

Another possibility is an old-school retro clone, maybe. Lamentations of the Flame Princess seemed awesome but the more I learned the more it sounded like it was low-fantasy horror and again, not what the group is looking for.

Any help or suggestions would be appreciated.

    \n RPG's are all very much "to your taste" so take my advice with grain of salt.

    For mix of grim with high fantasy, Warhammer is a good setting. There's magic and elves and daemons, but it's a grim, dark world where you're not going to end up living in a white castle with a beauitful spouse and happy peasants singing your praises and there is no such thing as resurrection, dead is dead unless it's undead. If that's the kind of high fantasy desired, then it's not the one to pick. Captain Jack Sparrow is a good model for what a successful character in warhammer ends up as - slightly loopy, in need of a bath, prized possession is cursed, 2 out of 3 women you meet smack you in the face, somebody else got the girl.

    Warhammer also has a "tradition" of feeling a bit beer and pretzels in its sense of humour built into NPC's (all orks talk like low class Brits) and setting (e.g., you're going to the town ruled by the von Sapontheim family' - get that, the "once upon a time" family) though that can be tuned up or down at a table.

    3rd edition Warhammer is simple to play once you get hang of it, very little rules referencing at table. Scenarios can be combat or social heavy, you can run a horror-themed adventure in it (attacking players mental stress pools).

    Downsides - the price point to enter is higher than an indie game but on par with a traditional one, the rulebook in the boxed set is not the best organized, the ruleset is designed to blend in with and support the setting, particularly in the magic side of things. E.G., it is intrinsic to magic use that it is risky and may generate miscasts as it is using Chaos energy.

    \n You are looking at is the free version: Dungeon World Hack, which doesn't have how to write adventure details. Not sure of the state of the pay version or what it has in it. (Sage or Adam can probably give us an update.)

    You could use Apocalypse World (AW) fronts, but you probably don't need to buy the book. An AW front is really just a clearly thought out threat, which details what the antagonists want and how it progresses over time wrapped up in a cool way. I have AW, but when I play Dungeon World I don't write out fronts, I just use realistic feeling enemies that have real wants and needs and that adapt to the players actions.

    Given the type of game you are looking to play you could just grab any free fantasy adventure and adapt it to Dungeon World as you go. A good start is Tony Dowler's Purple Worm Graveyard as it was written for an earlier iteration by Tony called Apocalypse D&D. Link: http://apocalypse-world.com/forums/index.php?topic=210.0

    Other advice also on the Barf Forth Apocalyptica forums, although I think most people also post here.

    \n Hey everyone - thanks for the recommendations of Dungeon World. Sage and I are super proud of our almost-done little beast and it's nice to see people playing it.

    Dungeon World comes, as Justin said, in two flavours - the free-for-all Dungeon World Hack (that you'll either need Apocalypse World or a really solid understanding of said to play) and Dungeon World proper. The latter isn't "out" yet but will contain a fully fleshed out chapter describing how to create Fronts (both Dungeon and Campaign) and tie them together for short and long term play.

    As it's been said, you can "get by" with a copy of Dungeon World hack and an idea of what you want your campaign to be like, but the full version will have as robust a toolkit for building adversarial game elements as Apocalypse World proper contains.

    As for the state of it - well, the content is just about finished now - we're doing editing passes and shoring up the layout now. If you want a sneak preview, give DW hack a try, write some notes about your game and post an Actual Play report. That'll get you in to the Adventurer's Guild - where you can score sneak previews of the print PDF and bonus classes / adventures once the game itself has been released.

    My suggestion? Find one of Tony's microdungeons from his blog and give it a shot. Try it as a one-shot for your group to play-test what Dungeon World might look like in their hands.

    You can grab DW version 11/03/06 here - http://www.latorra.org/2011/03/07/dungeon-world-version-11-03-06/ and if you have any questions about the game specifically or rules or anything you'd like to yell at us about, drop an email to GM@Dungeon-World.com

    Legends of Anglerre also has everything necessary to pull it off, and even has a high fantasy base setting to use.

    I was going to suggest Dragon Age, which fits on everything except the theme is a bit dark (although magic is still pretty wide spread).

    Reign is pretty good for high fantasy, the magic system isn't gonzo but could certainly work. I much prefer Reign for straight combat though.
