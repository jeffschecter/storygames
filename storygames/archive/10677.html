\n I've responded in several recent threads about changes I hope to see in the state of indie RPG publishing.

This thread is to specify, as concretely as possible, what I'm talking about in these replies, what I think could change, and even some early and minor ideas on how I might go about that, possibly giving others ideas, too.

So far, I'm not communicating very well. People are upset or vocal about my criticisms. Some of what I have to say is counter to traditions of the indie RPG scene. They are challenging things. Some are my opinions which others don't agree with. So, bad communication, especially here online, isn't suprising to any of us. I ask you to read more charitably. I'll try to understand your concerns better in return.

Essentially, what I'm proposing is a new business model. I'm not saying the current scene has to adopt it, or even pay much attention to it. I truly, genuinely hold no ill will to anyone, anyone's game, anyone's goals as an indie publisher. Parts of the business-model-thingy I'm exploring here will challenge assumptions this community has about design and designer ownership, however. It's not meant as an assault. At my best guess, my intentions are to branch off from the current model in a new direction, and let the current model continue onward and upward.

First, I'm going talk about the evolution of my thinking to date, how and why I "got here." Then, I'm going to talk about some ideas I have to try something new in light of the conclusions I draw about creator-owned publishing.

Indie RPGs to date

I do not define "indie RPGs" not as a style of game design. I define it as a publishing model. Specifically, I define it as creator owned publishing.

That is, the person (or people) who create the game own their intellectual property and make key decisions about publishing their game, including pricing, printing, distribution, and so on.

Another key defining attribute is that each publisher sets his or her own terms for success. This allows for a vareity games, formats, models, etc. This is great. To date, creator owned RPG publishing achieved fantastic things. This includes: \n
  • winning awards
    • \n
  • pioneering publishing techniques
    • \n
  • inventing new distribution models
    • \n
  • trailblazing new marketing techniques
    • \n
  • creating a fun new subculture/audience within the hobby
    • \n
  • redefining and dismantling assumptings about creators and players
\nI'm very happy to be a part of this activity since about 2001 or so.

Hitting the wall

Last year, I ceased publishing my indie RPGs. The primary reason was that my games' sales levels did not meet my own self-defined levels of success. I faced a choice. Either I devoted a huge amount of time and resources to significantly boost sales of my games (approximately double over a couple years), or I would stop. I found the current level of sales and effort unacceptable for my goals as a publisher. Once I determined that, yes in fact, the level of work required to achieve my goal was probably unworkable for my lifestyle (full time job, wife & two kids, graduate school), I chose to cease publishing.

Since then, I've spent a lot of time thinking about this particular barrier. I spoke with friends and fellow publishers. I gathered up bits and pieces of sales information that others were willing to share.

I realized that there was a specific flaw in the creator owned model that I wanted to investigate. The flaw goes like this:

Creator-owned publishing, by definition, means that a publisher sets his or her own goals. However, to date, it means that setting certain reasonable goals is extremely difficult, maybe even impossible.

The reasonable goal I wish to see accomplished, or accomplish myself, is 1,000 games sold in a year. Think of it as a kind of goal to challenge, a call to raise the bar of publishing activity. If not for others, than at least for me.

I base this 1,000 games number on those publisher conversations and that gathered-up data. Several games have sold over 1,000 over longer than 1 year. Very few have sold 1,000 in 1 year (or, 2,000 in 2 years). Gregor Hutton appears to have accomplished this with 3:16, for example. (And, by they way, that rocks!)

I think the 1,000 is arguable, but I chose that number. Is 1,000 unreasonable, but something more like 600 or 750 sales reasonable? Maybe so. That'd be ok. The number I consider "average" for a "good" game is about 300 (Grey Ranks, for example, is at 300 over a couple years). I'd like to see "good" games routinely double that number, and exceptional games at least triple it. I'm obviously not expecting all indie games to do that. I just would love to see such growth happen.

    \nWhy not now?

    Ok, so I identified and explained some concrete numbers about what goals I'd like to see realized, either with games I publish or even with games peers succeed with.

    But it didn't really answer an important question I had about this flaw I'd observed. Why is selling 500+ games a major accomplishment? Why can't more games achieve at least that number more routinely? What causing this fuzzy limit and prevent people from regularly selling around 1,000 games if they give it a decent shot?

    And, from all the things I've gathered and the many conversations I've had, I conclude two things:

    1) Broadly speaking, indie RPG publishers have insufficient marketing or resources to reach that 1,000 games sold. All conduct some level of marketing to varying effect. Extremely few (fewer than 5) successful reach that number.

    AND/OR

    2) Broadly speaking, most indie RPG publishers design games that not much more than, say, about 500 people want or demand. Let me qualify that some: Not more than about 500 accessible people want or demand.

    Now, at this point, the jury's out. Am I off my rocker concluding points #1 & #2 above? I suspect some will find my conclusions here wrong. That's ok. I've been wrong before, I'll be wrong again. But, this is where I'm at currently. I stand by the above, but this isn't to say I won't hear other ideas about it.

    The implications of marketing and market

    If my conclusions above are close to right, here's what I think the implications are:

    If #1 is close to correct, then in theory I could do better marketing or acquire more marketing resources and reach 1,000 sales. This is no small feat. In fact, it's huge. But, plausible.

    If #2 is roughly correct, then I could be well and truly fucked. I have three options in this case:\n
    1. Stop publishing now, and save myself some time and effort.
      2. \n
    2. Proceed with games, work hard, and not succeed on my terms (i.e. not sell 1,000 games).
      3. \n
    3. Design games differently for a different market (i.e. different than the current indie RPG market).
    \nHow to proceed?

    I reached these conclusions and implications pretty recently -- about the last month or two. I talked these over with a few pals. (By the way, because this is pretty new thinking for me, that's a big reason I'm seeing concerns from peers that I'm not being very clear. I'm still thinking through this stuff. Please consider that.)

    First, #1 from above: The implication is that with superior marketing, my goals might be achievable.

    But, that puts me right where I ended last year! Nothing's changed. To to "superior marketing," I'll need more time or resources. Damn. That requires a very creative solution. Or, it means I stay out of publishing.

    Second, #2 from above: I have to make a decision. Assuming for the moment the decision is not "stop now," what's required? Well. differently designed games for a different market. Again, a creative solution's required. This one's fun to do, but also hard. It also requires resources, especially time.

    \nCreative solutions

    There must be dozens of ways to confront these issues. I don't claim these are the only or even the best. Hell, I don't even have a firm notion of what they are just yet, in practice.

    solving #1: A cooperative
    First, I have a problem. I can't do this alone. My proposed solution is to create a co-operative imprint. A unified label that "publishes" the members' games, which is actually to say it markets the members' games.

    In essence, the members would organize and share duties in a mutually beneficial arrangement. The equation results in increased resources across the board for each member. One example: Rather than each publisher setting up a website, a blog, etc., the co-op would set up one web presence with a shared platform and cross linking among members. We'd bring search traffic and eyeballs to one place instead of 6 places (or whatever number of members).

    solving #2: Games FOR demand
    Next, designing different games for a different market requires a few things. \n
    • First, the cooperative would need to identify and target the market. I've got one in mind. It basically boils down to active, but not especially hard core, gamers, and not the current recognizable "indie RPG" market.
      • \n
    • Second, we'd need to identify games that will meet this market's demands. Again, I've got a couple ideas for that. I'm sure others would, too.
      • \n
    • Third, and most controversially, the games are vetted in some way.
      There is some kind of approval process, editing or filter that assesses a game design, and decides yay or nay whether it should be published and marketed.
    \nThis last one could be a fucking nightmare! I also believe it's vital for the success of this concept. Without it, I believe we'd be right back where we started as individuals in terms of sales levels for various members' games (i.e. it's kind of a crapshoot who has 1,000+ while everyone else has 300 or so sales).

    I don't know who would be willing to participate in such a set up. Maybe no one. I'm interested seeking some people out, though.

    Think of it as a VERY ROUGH comparison to becoming the Dark Horse or Image of RPGs. Neat.

    Goodbye indies?
    The model I've sketched out here move closer and closer to traditional publisher. I'm basically suggesting a development & marketing team, new games with business plans, and a new market of "old" gamers. Well, hell, that's damn near a traditional RPG publisher.

    I think the key difference is co-operative ownership. Each member either owns his own material OR the group collectively owns all games in a kind of shared IP set up.

    Working for the man
    I have a similar notion of setting up a non-co-operative set up. A couple owners (me and pals, say) own the imprint, and we bring on board publishers to become their marketing face forward. I don't think this is very workable short term. I don't have the resources to do it, by which I basically mean the money.

    Done!
    Ok! That's where I'm at today. There's a lot of stuff here, written and rewritten. It took me several hours to compose this. I suspect there will be criticisms, questions, and more comments. I hope to keep up with those. If recent threads are an indication, it will be TOUGH to keep up with folks' questions and replies.

    \nIf designing for an audience is a goal, pre-qualifying an audience is equally important.
    \n
    \n- Do they currently roleplay?
    \n- Would they be interested in roleplaying?
    \n- Do they have existing interests that would naturally extend to roleplaying?
    \n- Do they have time to roleplay?
    \n- Do they have friends to roleplay with?
    \n- Do they have money to buy games?
    \n- If you gave your game away for free, can you sell them something else?
    \n- Do they have their own magazines or conventions?
    \n- Do these magazines or conventions reach 30,000 people (reaching 1% will yield 300 interested people)?
    \n- What products currently advertise to this market through these magazines and conventions? What products does this market buy?
    \n- Contact the people in charge of these magazines or conventions. Ask them what they think of your product?
    \n- Identify the leaders in this market. Contact them. Can they help you?
    \n- What venues exist to reach this audience?
    \n- Is this the right audience for you?

We have a pretty clear division of labor, with one person doing the marketing, a collaborative web presence, throwing out game ideas we don't see as being marketable (or reduce them in scope to a cheap PDF), and put one person in charge of marketing, one person in charge of numbers, etc. We're also pretty clear about trading our skills with each other (editing, layout, design, illustration) when necessary.     \n Hi Matt,
    \n
    \nI agree that game publishers in general, large, mid tier and small press and indie operations should be exploring alternative business models and distribution methods. It can only be healthy for the system. I see it both from the publisher and retailer perspective how screwy the 3 tier system can be and how dangerous an over reliance on it (from either side) can be. I am not advocating its destruction or revamping. It is what is it and it serves a useful purpose for many manufacturers and retailers. But it is not the end all be all of the games market. Indie, these days most small press and indie publishers hardly use the 3 tier system.
    \n
    \nBut that does sort of bring us back to your issue with moving a 1000 games a year. In retail, to move quantities of anything, you have to have access to the market. That is achieved in 2 ways (though there can be infinite approaches to get to either) and that is, a whole lot of retail points of distribution (ala, on the shelf available for purchase in many locations) or available online with an aggressive and well established marketing system to bring folks to the site to purchase.
    \n
    \nThe 3 tier system does not have the funding or capacity to intelligently distribute the vastness of games that are available. So they've engineered systems to allow them a fair chance at good service for top tier products, a modest chance at ok service at mid tier stuff and a small chance at catching some of the dollors from the more popular small press and indie stuff. Any company so designated in that lowest tier might partake in the use of the 3 tier system, but it is by design going to be too eradic and unstable to be forged into a reliable distribution system that you could hope to build a company upon. A tool perhaps for use, but not at the core of the business.
    \n
    \nBut lacking effective distribution through the 3 tier system, most independent companies fail to achieve getting broad access of their product to the public. This is the principle reason for failure to achieve strong and steady sales.
    \n
    \nWide access to the public can also be achieved online, but its not easy that way either. Through a whole lot of work, someone aggressively marketing and promoting a game can get it a fair bit of attention and achieve some modest sales and occasionally hit the right chord and get some strong sales. But all too often, these sales only stay strong for the duration the designer/publisher are agressively promoting the item. That is because that sales approach is push marketing. Sales only happen when the product is being pushed. In general it is less about brand power, networking and distribution points and more about salesmenship and word of mouth. It fails when attention slips from pushing that particular item to pushing some other particular item, or if insufficient time is available to keep the push going strong. Most small companies don't have large budgets to drive brand power and awareness and most often slip into a push marketing strategy for their online presence. Hence why an indie publisher can come out strong, sell a few hundred and/or upwards to a thousand books in their first year or two, but eventually those sales die off to a great degree once that designer moves onto start push marketing towards their next project (which eventually they get an inkling to tackle) and they may or may not be success in pushing that next product, but they achieve not much of an accumulative affect of having more items in total to sell.
    \n
    \nBe it offline or online, to build up a strong sales base and to get steady sales beyond what can be achieved from pushing on item, distribution points must be established (offline for the physical goods, online for the distribution of marketing materials and entry points into your store) and a pull marketing infrastructure put into place. By infrastructure, I mean, a system of articles, links, advertising placements, brand building efforts, licensing, resellers, search engine and directory placements, etc, that are existent and always working for you and do not require ongoing active "pushes" to remain in effect.
    \n
    \nTo that end, there are lots and lots of ways to achieve that. Yes, a cooperative could be one of them. Lots of indie publishers working together to dual brand their goods. Branding the cooperative as a whole and their games in specific. Much the same way a company might brand itself and its individual brands. You would merely be using the collective power of the cooperative's members individual promotions to brand the cooperative, rather than the traditional more capital intensive approaches that larger companies might use. It can work. If done right. Frankly, if you think about it, its much the same how IPR built up its presence, even if it weren't quite so deliberately conceptualized to work out that way, it is effectively how it did.
    \n
    \nThat all said, there is no magic in selling 1000 units in a year, much less year after year. I think since 1996 and after a couple hundred publications, we've had maybe 5 of them peak over 1000 sales in their first year. All of those back when we were using the 3 tier system almost exclusively and frankly, it simply worked a whole lot better back then (for smaller publishers anyways).
    \n
    \nAlso, 1000 sales sold that way is not necessarily more profitable or better for brand building either. The numbers game goes a lot deeper than raw units sold.
    \n
    \nExample:
    \nLets us our recently released Empires of History: WWII board game as an example. It has a MSRP of $47.95. At a 60% discount selling thrugh distributors it would net $19.18 per unit. If we sold 1000 in a year, with 900 through distribution and 100 through our website and conventions our total gross sales for the item would be $22,057, less cost of goods sold (including free shipping the distributors) of approximately $5500, leaving us a gross profit of $16,557. Now, on the other hand, if we sell just 400 units but all direct, our gross sales would be a bit less at $19,180, but our cost of goods sold would only be about $1800 and mail order sales have the customer pay shipping, so our total gross profit would be $17,380. You see how those 400 games sold turned out more profitable than those 1000 of the same game sold?
    \n
    \nThen there is the arguement that more games sold is still better because it puts more games into the hands of actual players. Well....1000 games sold into distribution is NOT YET 1000 games into hands of players. You might have up to 50 or more on distribtion shelves and a couple hundred on retailer shelfs. So its more a case of 400 vs 750 in players' hands. Then you have to look at the communication levels you get with the end consumer. They trend much higher with direct sales, where you can get direct contact capabilities and you can market your website, other products, your support forums, etc, etc. I would argue 400 sold direct vs 750 sold 2 channels down river will get you more stable communication with your customer base with the direct sales. Keeping that base informed and excited about the game, add ons, variants and simply other products you might eventually offer can make those 400 a much louder voice than the 750 disconnected buyers.
    \n
    \nWhich is not to say I am arguing in favor of direct, web only sales, as the ideal course of action. I think it depends on the game and the company. Just trying to illustrate how raw sales figures are just a surface look at the viability of a product and its prospects for continued profits and success in the future.
    \n
    \nTotal unit sales does NOT equal profits. TSR sold what, 1.5 million Forgotten Realms campaign box sets back in the day, each at a loss of $1 for a total loss of $1.5 Mil? Extra profitability on a product can be achieved both by expanding distribution and sales, changing distribution methods so they favorably impact your gross margin, or even by changing manufacturering processes to drop the cost of goods sold. GOB Publishing's top selling game in 2008 sold a bit over 700 units. That was about tied for its sales in 2007, which was its strongest unit sales in a year since its first edition printing and initial release back in 1998. The key difference there was in 1998 it was a $20 product almost all sold via wholesale ($8.00 ea) and a higher cost of goods sold vs recently the new edition being a $34.95 product mostly sold direct with a slightly lower cost of goods sold. That makes the game, as currently sold, vastly more profitable and more ably to contribute to supporting our company and its staff. We sell less of it now than in 1998, but its a MUCH more successful product for us.

    Posted By: TomasHVMThis is a myth amongst players, and an extraordinary one; how on earth didanyonestart playing rpg's in the first place???

    And most indie-games are easier to learn and more fun to play than the classical rpg. It should give you all the more potential.
    \nI agree partly, but not with the "most indie-games are easier to learn" bit (and of course the "more fun to play" bit makes me chuckle mordantly.) The chief advantage D&D has for new people, and the reason it has historically been able to drag thousands on thousands into the hobby - and whatever else it may lack, Fourth Edition is extremely strong in this area - is concrete choices. There is never a moment where someone is smoking a giant d12-shaped bong and says "do whatever you want, maaaaaan". It's "Here, pick from a list of cool, evocative classes, races, and abilities. Get a name and think about who they are while we start playing." Coming up with a name is the hardest part because it has the fewest boundaries! Writing a trait is far more difficult than distributing some stat points.

    That's not to say that many indie games have excellent "concrete choices" that make them easy to grasp. DitV's bidding structure is one. IAWA's Oracle pulls are another. The best trait in Dogs for new players is your coat because it's cool, evocative and it's a coat. It's not "a piece of clothing" or "a symbolic thing you carry your family made for you" or "a special item important to the Faith". You don't have to decide what it is, it's a coat, you just decide what your coat is like, and "tattered" is enough if that's what you want.

    Other things are harder for new people - Beliefs in With Great Power are so wide-open as to be hard to think about. Traits in games with freeform traits are like this. And if I "get narration"? Yikes! Can I give it to someone else?!? Not newb-friendly at all. That's not to say they're not good ideas or good design or that they don't reward the new people who get enthused about it and work at learning it, which are all things that keep someone with a game session after session. But they are hard to learn and do.
