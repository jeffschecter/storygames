\n Nathan and I came up with this at GenCon.

The problem with Story Games is that there are too many games about hard choices. I want a game about easy choices. Choices where one option is simply, clearly better than the other option. Like those crappy feats that no one ever takes.

With that in mind, I give you: Twelve Dollars or a Punch in the Face

1) You play the game.
2) When you get to a conflict, either the GM pays you twelve dollars or gives you a punch in the face. Your choice.
3) Then you keep playing the game.
4) Play until at least two people need to go to the emergency room, or are broke.

    \n I love the game Dread (the "First Book of Pandemonium" Dread), and am shaking at the knees until my copy is in my hands.

    It is loaded with Easy Choices.

    GM: "There are signs that at that abandoned factory warehouse over there, yeah the one with the skinheads patrolling it with shotguns? There's a demon in there. But it looks dangerous, what with all the skinheads."

    GM: "...so, do you want to infiltrate it?"

    EASY CHOICE.

    Player: "Should we try to subtly infiltrate the warehouse? Of should we hotwire an SUV, and drive it through the gates shooting at nazis with our revolvers while Pawn throws molotov cocktails at them from the roof of that building over there?"

    EASY. CHOICE.

    It's really fun to get behind the seat of an Easy Choice game, where the roleplaying and narration is all about how you go about those easy choices.

    Christopher, come play in the Sons of Liberty playtest at Gateway. The game works, not by having the players make hard choices in character, but having the players make contributions to the fiction. It's all easy choices, although sometimes it's tough to figure the optimal contribution. It's a really different, but very fun, game.

    \n There's a couple ways to answer the question, Ben:
    a) It's not a hard choice, as Mikael says.
    b) It's not an in-character choice.
    c) It is a choice, but the focus of the game isn't on making those choices, it's about the awesome contributions that result.

    Or to put them all together: the game isn't about making hard choices in character; it's about making easy choices in character and complicated choices out of character, and doing that with as much style as possible.

    \n ...you did, Christopher? You sure? I thought we lost you long before I started in on Sons of Liberty...

    The 'thematic statement' that comes out of SoL is embedded in how you portray these legends of history, what emphasis you put on them, how they approach problems, and indeed, what they see as a problem and what they don't. It comes out in how you articulate the game objectives, and what you present as acceptable solutions to those objectives. Because the game mechanics just tell you if you win or lose; they don't say what that means -- that's something that the players all create in the game.

    ...but that makes the game sound really heavy and serious, which it really, really isn't. It's much more like, say, Monty Python's Life of Brian commenting on religion: it's fun and funny, but that doesn't make it any less a commentary on religion.

    Ben, yeah, I can concur with the "what's next?" thing, which is why I have lots and lots of mechanical guidance for "what's next" which allows the players to, lego-like, choose which flavor of awesome they want to contribute. So you look at your hand and see that you have a 2 of Hearts, a 3 of Clubs, and a 5 of Hearts, you just need a 4, but then your sheet says you can claim a joker when you're using electricity, so you narrate how Ben Franklin picks up the lightning rod from the collapsed building (thus getting your joker) and throws it harpoon-like into the menacing British zeppelin (playing the Hearts for patriotism and martial prowess), thus channeling lightning from the storm into the airship (playing the clever Club card). Which may sound complicated, but the game is really just prompting you: do something with electricity, that's heroic, and is kind of clever. Players, I find, have no problem taking it from there.
