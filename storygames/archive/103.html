Dice: Resolving by "hand"

Levi Kornelsen
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
Let's say that we had a resolution system where you and your opponent each rolled five dice, always, and then used your traits, resources, etc, to reroll some of them, going back and forth - likely combined with risking or paying something at each reroll.

Now, let's say that you weren't just seeking higher numbers. Let's say that you were looking for, in effect, a better "poker hand" than your opponent.

Has this, or similar, ever been done?

    Verge does this, sorta. Both sides roll a pile of d6es. Each side chooses one of the values 1-6 to be their 'signal.' The number of dice with that value is the success value. So if I roll 11222234556, I'll choose 2 as my signal and have 2222 = four successes. All the other dice (1134556) are 'noise' and I narrate-in traits one at a time to reroll that noise to improve my count of 2's and increase the signal.

    It's not exactly 'poker hand' mechanics but it's similar (N of a Kind, but not Full House or Straight).

    If you're gonna do poker dice, maybe use different colored dice for different traits, and use the colors as suits. Even if you roll a giant pile of like 15-20 dice, it might be hard to put together a straight flush.

    Why not use cards though?

    FVLMINATA did something similar to this for effect rolls, let me see if I can remember the rules now...

    First off, you had to modify your dice in order to make them "tali" -- which were the knucklebones used in many Roman gambling games. Some of us freaks used real tali, because we were broken. Most people used modded d8's.

    Anyway, you'd roll 4 tali and then look for sets of dice that followed rules based on old Roman gambling games. The possible results were a Seino (one six), one pair (note that 6, 4, 4, 1 is not a pair -- it is a seino), two pair, three of a kind, a Venus (one of each number), and Vultures (four of a kind of anything but 1s), or Dogs (four 1s).

    The hand you got then acted as a multiplier to your base level of effect, determining the final effect of your action.

    It wasn't anything stunningly new or different, but when played with real tali it certainly did contribute to mood.
