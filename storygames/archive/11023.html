\n I was talking with FigureFour on #playnow (find a pickup game today!) about 3:16 and the ways it can be hacked. I'd been looking at using it for this, that and the other thing and also browsing the various hacks I'd seen posted. This led to talk of the WWII variant and from there to other wars, like Vietnam. And then things took a darker turn.

At this point I'm going to direct you to the lengthy blog post I made about using 3:16 to play out the neo-Nazi fantasy of a book called Devil's Guard. After this point I'll assume you went there and read it so I don't have re-explain the point.

:: intermission music ::

Okay, let's continue.

As you can see, I'm building off my other thread on playing a terrorist, this idea of challenging players' base assumptions by encouraging (I don't like the term forcing) them to take on roles and attitudes they might under other circumstances find discomfiting. I continue to wrestle with the sometimes fine line between productive transgression and gratuitous transgression.

What's the difference? It seems to me that the strongest case for tackling challenging and/or unpleasant subject matter is whether or not there's something to be learned. As we got from the (admittedly minor) controversy concerning the how to punish a slave discussion, what's informative to one set of players can be just plain squick-tastic to another. In the latter case there's not a lot to be learned except that some things are out of bounds. Getting into a game where distinctions are drawn between ideology, reality and man's treatment of other men... that seems worthwhile, assuming it can be pulled off.

A lot would depend on the GM in this instance, posing situations that present three solutions: one based on the players' likely reaction and one based on the characters' likely reaction and one based on some kind of amalgamation of the two. How a player threads the line through these goes a long way toward making such an exercise worthwhile as a learning experience instead of a hootin' and hollerin' celebration of the \xdcbermensch.

But get right down to the brass tacks, as I did in the blog entry, and what you have is a demand on players to take the role of human monsters. There's no two ways about it. And potential learning experience or no, is it worthwhile to even tackle that sort of thing in a roleplaying environment?

    \n Not for me, no. I won't even play Nazis (or the Confederacy, for that matter) in board games.

    You might be able to work out some interesting individual stories in short sessions or as part of a broader plot, it's the reason Hans Landau works so well in Inglorious Basterds, but I think that playing such characters over any length of time will either be depressing or get reduced to parody pretty quickly.

    The other unspoken huge problem with these scenarios (Nazi-style death camps, chattel slavery, etc) is they almost always involve the sexual domination and sadism, not to mention violent rape, that comes as part of the territory with such totalitarian power structures. Not a fun campaign for any women at the table (or homosexuals for that matter), unless you pull punches in a way that subverts your original question, I think.

    IMO, there are just too many ways for a game like that to turn suddenly and perhaps irrevocably unpleasant that it's not worth whatever lessons might be learned; I think any of us that are socially-functioning human beings have probably already learned them. Getting folks to think is nice, and challenging a few ideas is not necessarily bad, but making the game into an ANTI-social situation is where you have to pull up.

    -Jim C.

    I played a game of Cold City with Malcolm Craig (and Steve Dempsey, his wife, whose name escapes me right now, [Edit: It's Paula. I can't believe I couldn't remember it before.] Robert Bohl, and Tim Kleinert) a few years back in which I played an ex-SS officer. He'd contributed to this mind-shattering horror, only to discover that he had a Jewish uncle, a fact he'd kept hidden until the end of thwar. His secret agenda in the mission was to find him and get his forgiveness.

    \n

    The character, Felix Baum, was a terror. He wore little black spectacles and a dark grey fedora, walked without a sound, and carried a pair of silenced Mausers. He did magic at one point that literally undid a victim completely from time, so thoroughly did he kill him. He had a clockwork heart with which he'd replaced his real heart so he could do what he did. He solved problems like you'd imagine a horror fiction Nazi assassin would: he killed them, thoroughly and efficiently.

    \n

    But he'd developed a sudden realization that what he did was awful, so he was trying to find his heart. He did, eventually \u2014 it was this shriveled, jerky-like thing in his ex-boss' (now Stasi) desk drawer. And he found his uncle, a horror of machine and man grafted together, cowering in the sewers of Berlin. Felix brought him coffee and they played chess together as the credits rolled.

    \n

    Now, what's interesting to me is the guy didn't stop being a monster at any point. The very reason I played that character was so that I, a Jew, could get in his head a little bit as a person. He was contrite, at least in theory, but mostly, he murdered people. OK, his reasons were pretty good, but nothing he did, or could do, made up for what he'd done. He killed some people who'd done evil things in the process, but that sure didn't make things better for anyone. And it's not like apologizing to this one guy, just because he suddenly saw him as like him, made him good.

    \n This is reminding me of the hard core grind-house game I've been trying to work on over the past couple of years.

    All the players take on a role from one of society's seedier aspects (pimp, drug runner, corrupt vice-cop, hitman...etc.), all looking for one last big score to get out of the dark gritty world they know. But everyone else is also after the same big score.

    None of the character's has led wholesome lives, but they've been forced into it for a reason that seems noble to them. They've always thought that the ends justify the means, but now that they are reaching the end, they'll need to re-evaluate whether the means were actually worth it. If they pursue their goals, they'll suffer collateral damage in the form of dead friends, injuries to their physical and mental faculties, and probably end up dead. If they turn their back on their goals, they'll probably end up dead..."capped in the ass" by one of the people they've crossed in their life of crime and desperation.

    Being grindhouse, it would play on all the worst stereotypes of ethnicity and culture (rednecks, negroes, KKK, mafia, jews, whores, etc.)...but I'd like to think that among all the cheesy lines and carnage, there would come a point when players really have to think for themselves..."What is this guy doing?"..."What has brought him to this?"..."Is it all worth it?"..."Is it better to fit in with society, or play the part of an outside rebel?"...that sort of stuff.

    Still needs a decent chunk of work though.

    \n Interesting, in our game, set amongst the Red Cow clan of the Cinsina, one of the players requested to play an Ogre.

    For those who don't know Glorantha, ogres are cannibalistic uber-mensch. They are indistinguishable from humans, just better looking, stronger. They regard people as 'cattle'.

    The presence of ogres as a threat was integral to the Red Cow setting, they are a mythical enemy of the clan.

    I decided to run with it to see what would happen. We discussed how to play such a character in a way that was sympathetic. I suggested looking to Dexter or Lecter as archetypes here. The killer gains sympathy if he acts in ways we wish we could, standing outside convention. If the audience hates the victim, then the killer is a sympathetic character because he acts simply as others, constrained by norms, would wish to. Provided we want the killer to triumph or at least evade detection, then the character can be a 'hero'.

    I have some mixed feelings about how successful it has been in play for two reasons. The first is the inevitable problem that many of the murders do not go unnoticed, and so the authorities must choose to hunt the killer down. Of course revealing the player's as the killer, effectively ends the game for him, apart from some sort of dramatic swan song. This makes a lot of contests high stakes because, frankly I can only think of so many ways to narrate failure, that don't end up with the authorities on his tail. Second the player is cautious because of this, and so avoids invoking the 'psycho killer' aspect too often.

    As a result I feel we get a lot of 'deferred' play, with us not really confronting the issues of what it is to be an ogre in Glorantha.

    i'm still working on getting the right feel for it, but I'm not encouraged to try it again.

    \n Playing an apparently evil character isn't as special or offensive or objectionable as you make it out to be. There's someone who does it at practically every RPG game, the gamemaster. Do we make moral judgements on the GM for playing adversarial characters? No. Do GM's make moral judgements on themselves for playing such monsters? No. So why is it taboo to play such a character? Perhaps as players we make more effort to identify with our characters.

    Now, here's a question, can you design a game in which your character does not morally reflect you as a player, put that right up front so that players feel more comfortable exploring what they may see as villainous behaviour. I'd like to propose that there are some out there. World of Darkness dips a toe into this pool, and Legend of the Five Rings does a beautiful job with the Scorpion clan, but I think Houses of the Blooded does it best, particularly the larp rules. An example:

    At the start of the game, you get another player who will play your enemy. You approach that player, say "hi" and start a discussion as to how your characters will be enemies. You're making friends with the player all the while making enemies with the character. When that other person does something against you, you the player don't take it personally because you spent a great deal of time working with that other player to arrange such an event.

    I want to see more of that in gaming, where the game does as much to create and reinforce friendships between players as it does to create conflicts in games. When games do that, we can open a lot more doors to gaming possibilities, including exploring monstrous behaviour.

It's fine for that to be a surprise as long as it's OK with everyone for there to be real surprises.

    Posted By: Sam HIan, would it totally derail your game to have the authorities start to figure things out and put some pressure on the group?     \nJust wanted to note how this turned out. Following divinations that revealed the murderer was hiding amidst the clan I had the authorities charge one of the other players - a Humakti - with tracking down the murderer. Perversely making the conflict one between the players seemed to ease the tension of beginning the hunt, I think because the ogre's player began making trades between his fun and the Humakti players fun. We already have a good social contract for PvP conflict without concern, it's all in the open and based on maximising fun for all.

    As it should in Glorantha it ended up with competing and interwoven heroquests with the Humakti using the Summons of Evil to flush out his foe, and the ogre trying to convert more clan folk to Devil worship by spreading heresy. Both players seemed satisfied with the conclusion.

    I think the lesson I learnt most was that if a player builds a highly-charged background like this then we have to agree beforehand that we will not defer but play it to the hilt, or not do it all. It is the deferring of the essential conflict that caused me the greatest concern here
