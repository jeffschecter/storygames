Using Flags in Play

Linnaeus
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
So after a lifetime of playing "traditional" RPGs, I'm preparing to be Story Guide for a The Shadow of Yesterday Campaign in a few weeks. The social contract will be fairly traditional in order to ease the transition to the new style of play (eg. player narration and scene framing will be an option rather than an expectation).

I've been reading bits and pieces of the Forge, and have subscribed to various Forge-diaspora blogs over the last few months, so I'm fairly dialed in to what this style fo play is supposed to be about (Story now, fun now, tough choices, conflicts that move the story forward regardless of whether the PCs win or lose a conflict), but there are a couple of things I wouldn't mind getting some advice or confirmation on from more experienced hands.

The big one is about using Flags effectively as part of the game. I can see several ways that I can use them, and I was wondering if some of you would mind offering feedback about which are good, which ones aren't, and which ones I've overlooked totally. While I'm primarily looking for TSoY specific advice (the systems for what Flags do seems to vary a fair amount from game to game), more general discussion is fine.
  1. At the start of the campaign, take a flag from each of the players, and try to weave them together into a start-of-game bang that has a bit of something for each of the players. This seems like the best way of getting all of the players engaged right away, but could be impractical when playing with a larger group.
  2. During play, when I'm at a loss about how to crank up the tension (per Paul Czege's advice), grab a flag from one of the character's that is in the current scene, and add a twist that plays to that Flag. Seems like a reasonable technique, provided I'm quick on my feet.
  3. If a player seems to be losing interest in the current story line, add a new element that plays to one of his flags.
When I thinking about starting up this topic, it seemed like I had more ideas than that, so I imagine I'm missing some fairly obvious ideas here.

So what do the "pros" have to say?

    To take Judd's point a bit further, you've got characters with the Key of Conscience, Key of Justice, and Key of Redemption.

    Some of your Bangs for the character with the Key of Conscience need to offer a desirable choice that involves ignoring Conscience.

    Some of your Bangs for the character with the Key of Justice need to offer a desirable choice that involves committing an injustice.

    Some of your Bangs for the character with the Key of Redemption need to offer a desirable choice that involves turning away from the path of Redemption.

    Flags are big, red, glowing signs that tell you and the other players to test and challenge this character over his belief or ideal.

    This is my sig for the Burning Wheel boards. Burning Wheel uses Beliefs as flags, but substitute Key for Belief and this should work fine:

    "Here's a rule of thumb you can use. When a player gives you a Belief, you should be able to say to yourself: "Oh REALLY?! Let's see if you still believe that if THIS happens.""

    Here's how I'll be doing things for the Burning Wheel game we're starting:

    1) Nail down a central idea ("Warring States China, peaceful kingdom about to fall into war, one of the generals is a traitor")
    2) Players build characters as a group, playing off that idea and each other's Flags
    3) I prep a bunch of NPCs designed to hit on the player's Flags, either conflicting or supporting them.
    4) I make a reference sheet- on one column, all the PCs, their Flags (Beliefs, Instincts, Traits, Relationships, etc. For you, it'd be Keys). On another column, I put the NPCs and their "Flags".
    5) In play, I'll frame scenes based on which Flags seem the most pressing and interesting. If I get stuck, I'll scan down both lists and try to see if I can connect 2 or 3 characters either in conflict or common motivations and put them into a scene together. Basically- all the NPCs either want to help, harm, or use the PCs, pick one or more NPCs, and play them like you would a PC.

    We finished step 2 last night, so now I have to apply step 3 and 4 by next week, and we're good to go.

    Don't forget Secrets! Those are Flags, too. Generally they're of the "I want this cool stuff to happen" variety.

    And here's a tip about setting stakes: talk about clear results and consequences, not just events.

    - "If you lose, he gets away" is a decent stake, but not a great one.

    When I say the game is about x, am I talking premise (my guess is no, since that smacks of railroading) or just a general concept like "go after badguys" or "try to overthrow the corrupt warlord."

    You are most absolutely talking premise, which you most absolutely should be doing. First of all, you are a participant in the game, even if you're the GM, and the premise should be interesting to you in some way, shape, or form. You aren't doing this as a favor for your friends, you're doing this because it's fun. For you, as well as them.

    What you aren't doing, and most assuredly should not do, especially in preplay, is addressing premise, which is sort of like answering the question posed by the premise. It's the difference between saying "Looks like you're a bunch of do-gooders, wandering around trying to do good," and "Looks like you're a bunch of do-gooders, wandering around trying to do good, which is exactly the correct thing to do and you would therefore be "right" in your playing of this game." See the difference? Talking about the premise is great; setting up the questions is great. Answering those questions before you even start roleplaying kind of defeats the purpose of playing in the first place.

    Will the system tend to drive this conflict to formal, rolling dice against each other conflicts, or will it tend more towards the players wanting to stay away from each other (in the game world) and pursue their own goals seperately? ...
    Should I explain as part of the presentation of the game that this kind of player Key conflict is a viable option for play (obviously it is), but should be agreed to by all players, or should I wait until I see it as a possibility before I bring it up? When I point it out, how big a deal should I make of it?

    Depends on how you and your players want to play it. Talk to them and ask if they're comfortable with PvP conflicts or not. If they are, it's relatively trivial to set up conflicts where one player is on one side and another player is on another side. (His sister killed a man and you're the law in town. What do you do?)

    If they are not comfortable with PvP conflicts... well, you can try and set up conflicts where they'll be on the same side -- but in so doing, you are coming very close to deciding which side of the conflict is right and which isn't. (If that doesn't make sense, say so and I or someone else can clarify.) This is something to talk over with your players, as the question of PvP versus no PvP will determine large swaths of how your game will play out.

    Hi,

    Support Key of the Imposter? Easy- mistaken identities is a classic way of starting good drama. "I'm the famous doctor from the East!" (wink wink). "Oh, doctor, there's a special case you have to attend to. Um, the King has an... illness of which we cannot speak and no one can know about. If you could cure it you'd be well rewarded..."

    As far as my game, we've got a PC with the Belief, "Treat your enemies with respect"- which is easily enough to build NPCs with, since a major part of the Warring States stories deals heavily with honorable and dishonorable folks on any given side of a struggle, so naturally there's got to be at least one adversary who's a really stand up guy, and one who's truly dispicable. But that aside, I'm also going to make an ally who abuses his foes, cruelly, brutally, and unnecessarily- because that also plays off this one Flag.

    When it comes to my Flags to Framing method, it's real easy to work with because the players have built their PCs and Flags, and I've built all my NPCs to "fit" with those, like puzzle pieces.

    For example, using that PC, I know I can frame him in any scene with an enemy and things will get interesting. The honorable foe supports his flag, the dishonorable foe challenges it, and the brutal ally also challenges it. If, they capture the honorable enemy and then the ally starts torturing and humiliating him, what does the PC do? I don't know, but I know it will be interesting.

    Likewise, if any of the other PCs have flags that might link to the situation, or be at cross odds, then things start getting more interesting. One player has, "Win at any cost", so what happens if he's convinced torture IS a good way to go for victory?

    The key to this is to think about it like you would an episode of Jerry Springer, what groups of people can you put together that will result in chairs getting thrown? Or, for a deeper tradition, this shows up in Greek tragedy and Shakesphere- you already know that once Oedipus isn't killed, it's only setting things up to get totally fucked up.

    When framing scenes, don't think, "How can I convince the player to take his/her character to X location", ask yourself what plausible situation/location you could expect these PCs to be at, and the NPCs can also be at, and frame it from there. Like above, I could fret and fuss about how to make sure the enemy gets captured and not killed, or, I could just frame that someone else captured him and now they're at his jail cell.

    Once you know which characters ought to go together, and have a general idea of how it could happen- just scene frame it and see what happens.

    Is this the thread folks were looking for?

    "GM opportunism"? You're giving them what they want, not screwing them over

    Players show up to the table not knowing what's going to happen, but they improvise just fine- is it opportunism? Of course not, it's them improvising, as guided by their character concepts and the focus of the game. What we've missed out on is giving GM's the same tools and techniques so that they, too, can improvise just as well without having to write every possible event down.

    The players look to their character sheets- you look to your list of NPCs and the players' Flags, and you improvise from there.

    When the player has the Key of the Imposter, they're saying they want situations where they can take fake identities, so you set up a group of NPCs and situations where it'll be easy, enticing, and FUN to do just that. It's just like when a player in D&D takes a Rogue and loads up on trap skills- they want to encounter some traps, so you give it to them. Here, you should take the other Keys in play, and make lots of "openings" for that player to jump in.

    For example:

    The PCs stumble upon a dead man, having encountered some terrible misfortune upon the road. He's carrying official documents identifying him as the tax collector.

    A particular merchant family always bribes the tax man so they don't pay quite as much as they should- in fact, they aren't afraid to brag about what a smart business practice it is. The other folks in the community, though, are the ones who end up paying higher taxes so the tax man can make the quota.

    The Conscience player might be concerned about reporting the dead man (but also concerned about the community and it's problems), the Justice player obviously is going to be torn on this as well, and then the Imposter has room to present himself off AS the tax man.

    The whole problems and situation of the community, the NPCs, you've got that set up beforehand, though there needs to be about 3 more subplots of problems going on so that even if the players pass this one over, there's probably another one they'll find grabby.

    Set up 3-4 nasty conflicts, interwined between the various NPCs (and/or NPCs to PCs) and have all the NPCs trying to hurt, help, or use the PCs and triggering their Flags. All of it together is the equivalent of your GM's "character" sheet- when you don't know what to do next, you look at all the problems you've set up, the NPCs, and you figure out how to make things interesting. Players look at their sheets and try to figure out what skills or abilities they might be able to use- you look at yours and try to figure out what NPCs, conflicts, and scenes you can put together.
