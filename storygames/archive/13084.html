    \n Interesting point of view. In my experience a game, no matter how cool, generally isn't enough to make people new to gaming actually play. People curious about this whole tabletop business are mostly just that, curious. They see the potential of an activity being a lot of fun and very different from what they've ever experienced, but they need someone to show it to them.
    \n
    \nAll the newcomers I've seen who tried role-playing and who chose to stay were introduced to this hobby by a dedicated group of experienced gamers who created a great campaign/story/whatever together.
    \n
    \nI think that especially applies to people... I dunno, over 18? People who are not teenagers. Maybe that's just me, but if I was introduced (or introduced myself) to gaming today, I'd be much more careful with how much time I dedicate to this strange new thing those strange people want me to do...

    \nThat totally describes me, I was just saying the other day how I sometimes get to the end of an RPG rulebook and secretly wish that there was a sequel so that it didn't have to end.

    I have to think, on some fundamental level, there is a split between people who found RPG rulebooks, read them, and then introduced them to friends and people who were introduced to games by someone else who taught them how to play before they read the rules. I was very much the former, reading and interpreting the rules on my own and then trying to teach the game to friends.

    I am curious how the self-taught vs taught and read/create vs play spectrums interact/correlate.

    \nYou know what else reaches a huge number of people? The New Yorker . . . I'm waiting on this week's issue to arrive in order to read this story.

    My experiences have led me to conclude that the reason character creation is more appealing than playing is because, when creating a character, one has a singular vision for a him that that is disrupted (traditionally) by the game master and often by the other players. It\u2019s more appealing to imagine what transpired in the character\u2019s past and what he\u2019s capable of in the future than to deal with having those concepts derailed or unfulfilled in play.

    When I realized that, in certain games, my character\u2019s background and what I envisioned for him didn\u2019t matter, I started playing other games where those aspects were relevant--if not indispensible--to play.
