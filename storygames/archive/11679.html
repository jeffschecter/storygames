    \n In Frederik's Dreamation Jeepform, Courtny and I both based our (evangelical Christian) characters on people we know. We were both conscious of the borrowing, and it helped to shape our decisions, what we said, and how we reacted to each other within the fiction, especially given the fast pace of the Jeepform medium. It does feel a bit odd to secretly borrow someone else's skin and wear it around for a while, and I'd be uncomfortable if our unwitting models ever found out. They're both people we genuinely like and consider to be friends, so it makes me wonder a bit about us given that the characters ended up pigheaded, stubborn, self-righteous, and-- eventually-- victims/perpetrators of domestic abuse and a murder-suicide. Granted, that was the point of the scenario, but it still reinforces the fact that I'd never be able to share the experience with the people the characters were based on. Even if it hadn't ended badly, I still feel that it is a certain kind of invasion to base a character on someone you know; rendering someone into a fictional character is an odd crime, to be sure, but it definitely has a tinge of the criminal about it. That said, it's a great way to lend realism and authenticity to the characters and the story, so maybe it's OK in the service of the greater good?

    Also, I'm pretty sure Ignotus and I once named our D&D characters after two girls of our acquaintance from college: his was a sneak-thief, mine a swashbuckling prostitute. No, we never told them.

    \n All the time!

    On my Dogs in the Vineyard GM sheet next to "what do the NPCs want from the Dogs" is a column for "inspiration" where I enter names of people I know or famous people or fictional characters from other media.

    I've also played characters based on a combination of the personality traits of the other people playing!

    A common GM technique I use is to take character traits from PCs and make NPCs out of them by imbuing those NPCs with the same traits but to the extreme or the exact opposite. If there are no clear character traits to draw from or if I don't think the players are invested in those traits, I will draw from the players themselves.

    That said, I stay away from anything too personal. I don't want to create any unintended drama.

    \n I played my dad (a former project engineer) as an InSpectres agent in a game I played in a few years ago. It was kind of perfect. He had been working in his field for his whole life and was laid off. Rather than get a low paying job or sitting on his duff all day he answered an ad looking for technically-proficient people. He's not crazy about having to dispose of ghosts and monsters and approaches his job as a problem to be solved rather than as a potentially dangerous profession. Best of all, he was the oldest member on the team by a good twenty-something years so he'd go into "grumpy old man" mode. Next time you see me ask and I'll do my impression of my dad.

    \n I rarely take a whole person and transition them full into a character.

    However, I very often do take parts of a person I know and use them as the basis for some aspect of a character. Usually transitioned or blown out of proportion, the logic of their actions taken to an extreme form.

    Most often I do this with folks I know, but am not terribly close to. When I do it with folks I'm close to I've found it doesn't help me make the model as well, as there's a bigger tangle between how the person acts and how I feel about how the person acts. Which can be rich ground to explore, but often moves more towards the "couples therapy" area than "creating fun drama" area.

    \n I was once ran a Unisystem game where the players did not know what the game would entail. I just told them to stat themselves in Unisystem, no chargen points, just freeform statting. Then when everyone was done, I had them each pass their sheet to the player on their left.

    In the end, it was an interesting experiment but in general didn't have too great an impact. A few times they'd stop and say, "Hm, how would so-and-so react to that?" overall they seemed too busy worrying about the fact that they'd all been kidnapped by aliens.
