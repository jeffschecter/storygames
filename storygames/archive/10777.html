\n So I'm a bit over halfway through The Elfish Gene: Dungeons, Dragons, and Growing up Strange, the memoir of Mark Barrowcliffe, a British former gamer whose intent seems to be ridding himself of shame by writing this book. Then again, British humor is subtle, and reading his blog has made me realize he's proud to be a nerd. Huh. My American lenses didn't get that from reading the book.

Anyway, the most curious, and saddest part, of the book so far is this paragraph on page 139-140:

"D&D is, I believe, something virtually unique and unprecedented in human history. It's a story you can listen to at the same time as telling it. You can be surprised by the plot's twists and turns, but you can surprise too. It's more interactive than any other sort of narrative I can think of. If its subject matter were more serious then it would be considered a new art form, and it's probably surprising that nothing beyond murder mystery dinners has ever been evolved from it. This is why D&D is so addictive when it's played right. It's like the best story you've ever read combined with the charge a good storyteller feels as he plays his audience."

The part that's kind of sad is, If its subject matter were more serious then it would be considered a new art form, and it's probably surprising that nothing beyond murder mystery dinners has ever been evolved from it.

First: I wish he'd known about Forgie-ish* games.

Second: I wish he'd done some research before writing that sentence!

*bad form, I know. Can we not debate how inappropriate/false this term is in this thread? Thanks.

EDIT: posted on the author's blog, pointing him to Grey Ranks, Breaking the Ice, Steal Away Jordan, and Dogs in the Vineyard.

    \n I just finished The Elfish Gene. I thought it was a sad book, but not because of the author's attitude toward D&D, but because of the adolescent tragedy it contains: wrongheaded self-delusion, losing your best friend over a game-related argument--all the self-involved high drama of being a teenager. When I read it, I was reminded of The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao: both are stories about desperately unhappy teenagers whose obsession with role-playing games is depicted as an ultimately debilitating coping mechanism for their alienation from the real world, which can be recognized by the presence of girls. It's like the Flashlight Brown song, "Ready to Roll": We may not know any girls, but we got graph paper guiding the way. Of course, "Ready to Roll" is redeemed by a chorus that I choose to regard as sincerely if somewhat self-mockingly felt: That was by far the best time that we ever had!

    What other examples of this kind of geekish self-loathing are out there?

    \n D&D is sold in Borders and Barnes & Noble. I'd say that's somewhat mainstream (never mind that D&D 4E might not qualify as "role playing" to many).

    But the participatory nature means that it will always have trouble being called an "art form," because no one (typically) observes it as an audience member or, generally, takes away something without putting anything in (other than time, money, and attention). RPGs would be better off going "mainstream" with a sports model and its clubs than with an art model and its studios.

    I also think there's a viable market for televise LARP, given sufficent production budget and qualified actors and martial artists (or marksmen)... but that's another thread.
