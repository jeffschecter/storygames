\n Here are some hot tips on playing Fiasco at a breakneck pace, the way God intended. I assembled these into a little video, too.\n
Be sure playing fast is what you want. If you have a great time with four hour sessions, that\u2019s cool.
Be prepared. Bring everything you need and have it ready.
Be intentional. Say the words. \u201cWe\u2019re going to play this Fiasco in two hours.\u201d
Be aware of time and pacing.
Facilitate strongly - pretend you are a GM.
Fewer players = faster game. If you only have an hour, play a 3 player game.
With new players explain the game\u2019s sequence, why die choice matters, and what establish and resolve mean. Don\u2019t demonstrate, don\u2019t belabor it.
Have multiple copies of playset, make sure the next guy up is studying his options while one is deciding
Relationships first, then everything else
Don\u2019t allow analysis paralysis, suggest two choices when you see it creep in. When in doubt be obvious.
Clarify situation and jump in with an aggressive first scene - better to have a friend you can trust do it, putting yourself in the middle of the rotation.
Reiterate that everyone has only four scenes and you need to make them count
Edit ruthlessly, yourself and others
Be obvious and be active. Model the behavior you want to see.
Always be thinking of scenes for other players, to suggest when they ask to resolve.
Be alert for \u201cwe can\u2019t continue scene until we know outcome\u201d dawdling
Be alert for \u201cI am enjoying this so not deciding my outcome\u201d dawdling
Encourage color scenes, take color scenes, identify color scenes. Strongly encourage them in act II where they are often very appropriate.
Take a very short post-Tilt break, like 5 minutes tops, for serious
Omit the post-Aftermath montage, ask each person to describe their characters outcome.
\nDid I miss anything?

    \n Something i was playing around with last week with a nod towards some quick-gen playset was the idea of only rolling 1/2; 1/3; or a quarter of the dice at a time. Last die in each set is magic. Then roll next batch. Creates a new set of hard choices and interesting box of limitations. That can go good or bad. But the one concrete thing it does is speed up choice.

    I haven't tried this yet live.

    [edit: you'd want to do this in a way that the wild die doesn't end up at the same player at every batch. so maybe just a random handful of dice. "Ha Ha, seven "2s" best of luck Fiascites! Everybody is disgruntled family members with stolen firearms. Except you. Next roll..."]

    Posted By: GB SteveIt is really quite so satisfying playing games at this speed without taking the time to develop character interactions?
    \nMy experience has been that the faster paced games are more satisfying than the slower paced ones. The faster paced games means everyone is focused and pushing hard for their goals (which means more active, better defined characters). A slower game usually means people are meandering in scenes that don't really go anywhere or add anything to the game.

    But experiences and opinions differ. The same may or may not be true at other people's tables.

    Posted By: GB SteveI'm not so much saying don't do as asking if it is quite so satisfying.
    What Remi said, but also: One really nice thing about short games is that you can hold all the details in your head at once. I'm not averse to bigger fiction, to be sure, but there are wonderful things that happen when the story is of a convenient size to be manipulated, rethought, and reframed, all as a unit. I think that if you want to do certain kinds of story, like a whiplash con (think Lucky Number Slevin or CONfidence), keeping things short and simple is the only way to get there in an improvised framework.

    When there's time to spread things out and do something grander and more Faulknerian, that's cool too. But max-speed, direct, intense play can indeed be very satisfying, and has become the preferred mode of play for a lot of groups.
