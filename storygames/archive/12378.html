\n I'm writing a new game and it uses asymmetrical player roles, one thing i'm having trouble with is what to call these roles. the game is traditional in that there is one player who handles the all the characters and actions that oppose a few players who all control one fictional character each.
\n
\nWhat makes me hesitate to simply call the opposition a game master is the amount of baggage that word has that does not relate to this game.
\n
\nThe opposition player is just simply another player and subject to all the same rules as the character players. They are not demigod game designers, they are not responsible for preparing the session's story or events, they contribute to the play about equal to the character players.
\n
\nThe board game decent calls this player an "overlord" but that kind of thing feels too fiction dependent for what i'm going for. so what would you call this player?
\n
\nI was thinking of calling them the "rival" and maybe calling the player's "leads". i like "protagonists" and "antagonist" however these are two large words that are hard to fit where i need them to fit.

    \n The Buffy: The Vampire Slayer boardgame has one player take on a role like this. I think they're referred to as the "Big Bad", which is pretty genre specific. Betrayal at House on the Hill (a very fun board game, in my opinion) calls this player "the Traitor", because they start out as one of the group, then become the antagonist midway through the game.

    Note that in both of these, though, the bad guy player is controlling a particular token in the game that still represents them. So there's still an identification of the player with a particular 'character', even though they're also in charge of a bunch of other monsters. I can't tell from your description if your game has this same sort of 1:1 mapping. Is the rival player "in" the game as an individual character?

    I think "Rival" works well. How about "Enemy"? If the bad guy doesn't have a character of his own, maybe "Mastermind" or "Controller"?

    Posted By: TylerTthe game is traditional in that there is one player who handles the all the characters and actions that oppose a few players who all control one fictional character each.
    \nPeople are going to call that a GM.

    Your game should tell people how to play it, especially things that may not be obvious. For example, in a game with a reputation mechanic and a clear winner, this may not be obvious:

    "NOTA BENE
    It should be noted that "winning" the Shab-al-Hiri Roach is like "winning" a mustard gas barrage - your character may still be standing with a great Reputation, but there will be a lot of corpses on the ground. And chances are good that your Roach-bound peers will have had a great time making an utter mess of things, freed from the pursuit of victory. Don't fixate on victory, fixate on crafting scenes that will be outrageous, memorable, and fun to play."
