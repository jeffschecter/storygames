    \n I second the nods for all of Flannery O'Connor and a smattering of Faulkner -- O'Connor arguably invented the genre, and Faulkner took it into a more stylistic/less revelatory direction. "Light in August" is a good example of all the Faulkner you'll need.

    I also second Allison's "Bastard Out of Carolina" -- be forewarned that it is NOT a happy book, at all.

    The novels of Tim McLaurin are also good -- flawed in many places, but for themes and characters involving the decline of the Old South and the transformation into the New South, he's an excellent read.

    There are lots of others, but those are good for a start.

    And maybe someday I'll finish my novel about snake-handlers in Appalachia, and I can arrogantly add my own name to the list.

    \n Hey--- I don't want to start a whole controversy here--

    And I'm not well-versed enough for my opinion to have authority. But I have an -inkling- that...

    Southern Gothic is being reduced to Southern GOTH in all this talk about Swamps and Cemeteries and Tom Waits. ...and in much of the music mentioned.

    Aren't those superficial elements, which don't add up to Southern Gothic; without something more.

    AND-- isn't it possible to subtract all those retro 'Halloween down South' bits, and still have Southern Gothic?

    I've only read one Harry Crewes novel, "Body", , about a female bodybuilding competition. To give you an idea of the flavor : The contestants are named Shereel Dupont, Dorothy Turnipseed, and Marvella ["a big, black woman who is Shereel's nemesis" --Amazon.com]

    It's all mullets and monster trucks and Florida sunshine. ["Redneck antics" says Amazon] Stuff that would irritate lovers of darkness and spanish moss hanging in misty swamps.

    But there's still Southerners Trying To Cope, and off-kilter imagery, and the grotesque.

    There's a scene where a male bodybuilder and a morbidly obese woman are sexually fascinated with the differences in their bodies. And when the fat woman leaves her trailer park and stays in a big city luxury hotel room for the first time-- she looks at the toilet beside the bidet and thinks she's seeing the most romantic thing she's ever envisioned: two toilets, side by side, no partition. She daydreams about sitting on one, while holding hands with her muscle-bound lover; as he sits on the other.

    I have a feeling that Southern Gothic characters live in their present, not the past. But audiences tend to conflate the characters with the very-same Past that the characters are struggling Against. (In contrast: Goths and Vampires and Low-Fi retro bands--- seek to retreat into the past).

    'Body' was written in 1990, and it was a very up-to-date sketch (or unflinching cartoon) of southern life. But 20 years later, it is hard not to perceive the unvarnished imagery as the opposite: stylized imagery. It would be as easy to fetishize the cheezy spandex, oily bodybuilders, and big hair-- as it is to fetishize Old-time New Orleans, or Johnny Cash's drawl.

    But the whole point of New Orleans, is that it was a swinging up-to-date hotbed in its day. And jazz was cutting-edge music. Marlon Brando was a new modern actor, not a throwback like his character in Streetcar Named Desire. Tennessee Williams and Truman Capote were au currant New York socialites.

    And Johnny Cash didn't just sit on his farm and sing to the mule. He was on Elvis's record label when that was the newest, hottest place to be. Then he moved to the West Village and joined the beatnik folk scene/ right when Bob Dylan first appeared. He hung out with Sonny and Cher in the 70's. He went to Branson Missouri in the late 1980's-- when that was contemporary country's place to be. (And of course he made his way over to Trent Reznor and Soundgarden in the 90's.)

    Like I said, this is just a half-baked impression. I may be way off the mark.

    \n You make some good points, but I define Southern Gothic as Gothic that takes place in the South.

    If you get rid of the old creaky house, the visit from a ghost, the old family sins which are visited on the children, you don't have Gothic anymore. And when I'm talking about Gothic, I'm talking about the early 19th-century definition. Gothic novels were over the top and sensational, and certainly weren't considered high literature. A lot of what defines Gothic are the superficial elements. What we think of as cheesy, stereotypical Gothic from the old Hammer Horror films comes from the novels. We shouldn't pretend that Gothic is some high-falutin' literary movement that we've appropriated the vocabulary from but have misunderstood. It's 19th-century pulp. It was about mood as much, or more than, plot. If anything, Southern Gothic is much more literary than the original Gothic was intended to be.

    As for the Halloween-down-South bits...

    Having grown up in Alabama (and back in Alabama now), it's hard to escape the ghost stories about slaves beheading their masters, racism, spanish moss, swamps, Indian burial mounds, the feeling of inferiority to "more educated" northerners, white guilt, having friends die in hunting accidents, finding dead bodies, your neighbor's son stabs his girlfriend to death, wandering around abandoned antebellum mansions, religious hypocrisy, the "good old boy" mafia, families torn apart by nothing but stubbornness and pride, and the fucking oppressive August sun. Those were all part of my childhood, which while miserable, was pretty normal for someone of my age where I grew up. That shit is everywhere, and it's hard to escape it. I could tell you stories from my life which are as Gothic as any Tom Waits song or Flannery O'Connor story.

    Part of the reason a lot of contemporary Southern Gothic takes place in the past is that the authors (like Dorothy Allison and Donna Tart) are writing about their childhoods. There's been a literary shift from fiction to self examination.

    Also, the entire world is becoming more homogenized. There are tons of well educated Germans and Koreans, not to mentions tons of immigrant workers from Central and South America, all of whom are managing and building the new automotive industry in the area. Parents are more educated, less racist, and their kids grow up playing MMOs rather than getting lost in the woods or killing things with b.b. guns.

    \n Hey, so, the movie Black Snake Moan.

    It's about an old black farmer stumbling across an unconscious, skinny, white-trash girl... and deciding that the lord has chosen him to save her from the path of wickedness and sin. He subsequently chains the girl up in his living room, and attempts to fix her.

    The farmer is played by Samuel L Jackson. He used to be the greatest blues guitarist in the county, but divorce and despair have killed the music inside o' him. The trashy girl is played by Christina Ricci. The victim of sexual abuse, she throws her body at men in an attempt to feel something. Justin Timberlake is her boyfriend, who's gone away to fight in the war, having decided that his dream of opening up an auto-body shop is unachievable.

    There's blues, fundamentalist religion, a crazy man trying to punish the wickedness of others, a woman chained up in a stranger's living room, messy race relations, hot hot heat, troubled pasts, and unhealthy relationships. It takes place in the South.

    Southern Gothic?

    Posted By: GrahamThe heat is the most surprising one. When you learn everything from films, you forget about the heat.
    Graham, I dare you to visit us in Alabama this August.

    I disagree that you have to be from the South to write Southern Gothic. It might help with the authenticity, and you might be obsessed with they mythology and imagery from your childhood, but the most important things are your imagination and a love of the genre. I am a jazz and blues musician, and though I am Southern, I am also white. I don't think this detracts from the authenticity of my music. I not a practitioner of voodoo or worshiper of Satan, but both of these elements work their way into my original songs. If Graham pretended to be a Southerner and wrote a Southern Gothic masterpiece based solely on his knowledge of the genre and his imagination, then revealed himself to be English, his book wouldn't suddenly cease to be Southern Gothic.

    Here are some things from my life that could go into Southern Gothic fiction:

    Relatives: mill workers with missing arms, confederate soldiers, hobos, trailer dwellers who decorate their homes like outsider artists, grandparents who live forever but go insane sometime in their 70s, an uncle who was arrested for owning 22 dogs withing city limits.

    Places I've been: confederate graveyards, slave graveyards, abandoned shacks deep in the woods, abandoned antebellum mansions. swamps, Indian burial mounds, bluegrass festivals in tiny little towns, Paradise Gardens, Pasaquan (Saint EOM divined the names of my mother and her two brothers from a deck of ordinary playing cards), shitty little family-run circuses and carnivals, roadside attractions with a giant catfish as their main draw.

    Things I've eaten: rabbit, turtle, deer, rattlesnake, alligator, chitterlings, pig feet, pig ears, grits, gumbo, jambalaya, crawfish, collard greens, poke salad, blackeyed peas, desserts somehow incorporating marshmallows, jello, and pretzels.

    Folklore: slave narratives, folk healing (like rubbing a penny on a wart, burying it and dancing around it three times), keep a pocketknife with you to keep evil spirits away (wearing red and keeping cracker crumbs in your pocket do the same thing), local ghost stories.

    Religion: the horrible way the little country baptist church explained the human fossil record to my grandmother, poison-drinking and snake-handling going on in my hometown, being one of a handful of goth kids in Opelika during highschool and dealing with persecution from the religious, constant street-corner evangelism, Hell House, the slow war against Hallow'een.

    These things, and many more, are part of what I grew up with and they influence my writing and world view a good bit. However, I think someone like Graham could take this list and make a pretty good story out of it. The hardest part would be realizing that sometimes the stereotypes are actually whitewashing the truth.

      Posted By: Brennen ReeceYou make some good points, but I define Southern Gothic as Gothic that takes place in the South.

      If you get rid of the old creaky house, the visit from a ghost, the old family sins which are visited on the children, you don't have Gothic anymore. And when I'm talking about Gothic, I'm talking about the early 19th-century definition. Gothic novels were over the top and sensational, and certainly weren't considered high literature. A lot of what defines Gothic are the superficial elements. What we think of as cheesy, stereotypical Gothic from the old Hammer Horror films comes from the novels. We shouldn't pretend that Gothic is some high-falutin' literary movement that we've appropriated the vocabulary from but have misunderstood. It's 19th-century pulp. It was about mood as much, or more than, plot. If anything, Southern Gothic is much moreliterarythan the original Gothic was intended to be.

      As for the Halloween-down-South bits...

      Having grown up in Alabama (and back in Alabama now), it's hard to escape the ghost stories about slaves beheading their masters, racism, spanish moss, swamps, Indian burial mounds, the feeling of inferiority to "more educated" northerners, white guilt, having friends die in hunting accidents, finding dead bodies, your neighbor's son stabs his girlfriend to death, wandering around abandoned antebellum mansions, religious hypocrisy, the "good old boy" mafia, families torn apart by nothing but stubbornness and pride, and the fucking oppressive August sun. Those were all part of my childhood, which while miserable, was pretty normal for someone of my age where I grew up. That shit is everywhere, and it's hard to escape it. I could tell you stories from my life which are as Gothic as any Tom Waits song or Flannery O'Connor story.

      Part of the reason a lot of contemporary Southern Gothic takes place in the past is that the authors (like Dorothy Allison and Donna Tart) are writing about their childhoods. There's been a literary shift from fiction to self examination.

      Also, the entire world is becoming more homogenized. There are tons of well educated Germans and Koreans, not to mentions tons of immigrant workers from Central and South America, all of whom are managing and building the new automotive industry in the area. Parents are more educated, less racist, and their kids grow up playing MMOs rather than getting lost in the woods or killing things with b.b. guns.
      \nIf that's true, then it sounds to me like HPL stories about isolated towns of inbred cultists would be good supplementary reading.
