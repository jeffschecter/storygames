Fate/Hero/Happy Points. Do they work for you?

Andy
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
Hey all, I'm a big fan of systems that contain some mechanism where players get a reroll every now and then. I wanted to get a list of a few of these mechanics, and good/bad points of each.

Force Points: You get them by following the guidelines of the Force and succeeding in adventures. You spend one beforehand, and you can double your die roll. Good = It's like a die explosion of pure power, which is awesome.

"Eberron Points" (I forget the name): Each one you spend gives you an extra 1d6, which you can add to a standard 1d20 roll. Good = player gets a boost without forcing bizarre or system-breaking results.

Other systems (for die pools)

Spend a point, reroll one of your dice.
Spend a point, reroll all of your dice.
Spend a point, add a die to the pool (before or after you roll them).
Spend a point, take one of the dice and change it to its highest face.

Questions for the crowd:

1 Have you added a hero/fate point system to a game that didn't inherently have one? What were the results?

2 Have you played a game that had a hero/fate system built in that just didn't seem to work with your group? Why didn't it work?

3 The players get these Hero Points somehow ("End of session for succeeding in goals; end of session for Good Roleplaying; In the middle of session for entertaining the other players; In the middle of a session for following some mandate on your character sheet" etc). Which methods work for you and your players, or don't work for you and your players? (maybe one 'good', one 'bad')

4 What are your general thoughts on these mechanical widgets that let players change their dice rolls?

-Andy

    1) No.

    2) I ran KULT for my early group of friends, and it didn't always work. Sometimes it broke the coherency, the mood, or the purpose of the fate points. Though the point where it really broke down was not the mechanics' fault: we played a one-shot in which the three characters were Irish terrorists. They took over an office building and fought off the police (i.e., the "collaborators with the Occupiers"). Without the other two players knowing, the third character was a mole. When he betrayed them, led them into an ambush, and put a gun to his "friend's" head, the victim's player used fate points to reduce the damage from lethal to a scratch. That was a point of social contract breakdown, I think, because it was an act of player defiance, but the ability to do this brought the whole game to a screeching halt at a point where it would have been cool and dramatic, in the fiction, if the guy had died at the hands of the mole. But even without the player issues, having the points meant that the character was not in mortal danger in that moment unless I overrode the use as a GM. So basically, KULT is supposed to be a very dangerous and lethal game, but piling up fate points takes a lot of that out of the equation. That can be countered by GM, but I found that that was yet another burden on me.

    3) It depends on the type of game. In KULT, we figured out that having more than a couple of points destroys the mood. That means awarding them during the game doesn't work well. I awarded them at the beginning of the "adventure" so that the players knew how to ration them. I also always gave everyone equal amounts based on the idea that that was fair; they all would get the same chance of survival.

    4) I am a big proponent of "positioning" dice/points. I could have handled it better in my early years. I think that for various styles of play, these points allow the players to both indicate what's important to them, avoid deprotagonization, give tactical options, and enable one or more players/GMs to, as Rob said, reward cool play. But the amount and usefulness needs to also fit the mood of the game: many in cinematic games, few in dangerous/creepy games.

    I also strongly propose the use of the points or tokens as a guaranteed improvement, contrary to how they're used most of the time in Iron Heroes.

    I'm a big fan of these types of mechanics.

    1) Way back in my illusionism days, I added a point system to my Dark*Matter Alternity game that gave the players points they could use to get flashes of intuition, hunches, and other hints during the investigation by asking me a "Yes-No" question that I had to answer honestly. Looking back, this was basically a cover for the problem of failing forensics rolls and not knowing what to do to keep the story moving, but the players seemed to enjoy the ability to step outside the normal resolution system and just discover something cool with their creative questioning. It was by no means a perfect fix, but it solved the problem of botched rolls well for us at the time.

    2) I've really only had positive experiences with such mechanics.

    3) I like a combination of gaining these points as social reinforcement and as a result of smart play in using the mechanics to the best advantage. I definitely prefer games where there is a steady flow of such points, rather than only receiving them in clumps at the end of an adventure or when leveling up. The clumping tends to lead to hoarding behavior and using the points only in emergencies in my experience, instead of using the points for enhanced kewl when the time is right for it.

    4) Again, I love them. My current design project, "The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries," is basically inspired by the old Hogshead Game The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen with liberal doses of extra crunch in the form of many ways of using Acclaim points to alter your dice rolls (each kind of alteration requiring certain types of narration in your tale).

    One issue with such systems is that the points often have various uses. For example, Heroquest using the points to alter rolls and improve your character over time. Such tensions in the various uses seem to bother a lot of people who assert they can fray the system and create weird incentives for the players to do things the designer didn't expect. I personally don't see it as such a problem, so I'll let others weigh in on that issue if they like.

    I like games that have Hero Points because they facillitate drift.

    Take M&M, for instance. It's true that, as written, Hero Points in M&M are there to be "participation lube", but I've never used them as such. In fact, when I first started playing with the Philly group, one of the players complained, "I *want* difficult plot developments and setbacks to happen to me. You shouldn't have to pretend to be guilty about it," and we nixed them right there.

    Instead, we used Hero Points much the way Restrictions in Nobilis (or, as it turns out, Aspect Compels in FATE) are designed to work: I would introduce characters' Complications in such a way to further the story I wanted and pay the players off for it, or the players would introduce their own Complications and point out that they should get rewarded for them.

    The difference between what I was doing as an M&M GM and what (I think) the game text expects the GM to be doing is just that there was no pre-set narrative or path of scenes. If I brought in a character's crippled aunt, fear of spiders or fondness for moonshine it was all about making this scene, right now more complicated and challenging, not about steering PCs back towards "the story".

    I did also give out HP to give the villains extra oomph, but that's mainly because M&M characters can have rather a glass jaw without the ability to reroll the occasional save now and again, and IMHO players don't like the feeling of easy victories. For GMs who think that such a method is too transparent, it's also easy to give the villains points up-front (as M&M 1st edition did) or use the Villain's Luck Feat to build in extra points (effectively "resources", "toughness", "luck" and "story oomph") as part of a villain up-front.

    My point: M&M Hero Points don't need to be about participationism unless that's your favoured playstyle. When I ran 7th Sea the Drama Dice weren't really about "lube" either. They were much more about rewarding "teh awesome", and/or greasing players' palms for building characters with entertaining flaws which made for denser, richer, more entangle story -- right now.

    Well, it's hard to argue with hate. But I'll try anyhow. Here's the problem. If you don't have an alternate use for Hero Points, then the only disincentive you have to using one in a situation is that you might want it later. This is what I hate. Because it's really no control at all. Not knowing what the GM is going to throw at you later, and whether or not it'll be important, you really don't know whether or not to save them. So the only real criteria is some "average" interest of the current situation. Making the question of whether or not to spend really rather invariable. There's really no question being asked to answer.

    For me, in games like this, I feel as though I might as well roll a die to determine if I spend the point. It's just a further randomizer in the process, not part of player participation. Can't count the number of times I've either "run out early" or didn't spend any points at all waiting for the exciting stuff to come. That's fine itself - I like "wallowing" effects and such. It's just that when it happens it feels meaningless. What did I give up to get where I am?

    With something else to spend HP on, I now have another constraint to consider. "Damn, I'm hoping to buy into that new grimoire soon, but it would be cool to win this contest. Which do I choose?"

    This makes HP spending, for me at least, a real choice each and every time it comes up. Not that I harangue about it or something, but every HP spent is a statement of how important this contest is to me. Not vis a vis what other contests "could" happen, but vis a vis using the HP instead to indicate some character growth.

    This also means that it works best when HP are in good supply, because it's a more balanced choice to choose between "spend a lot on something" or "spend less" and the contest at hand. If, in fact, you're burning through all of your HP every session, yeah, that's a bummer that the character can't grow at all. It should be more of a question of "how much win" vs "how much grow," not a binary selection.

    HQ ain't perfect here, too, I'll admit. I'm sure a better system can be built. But it's got enough complexity of design that the spending of points is relatively meaningful.

    FP from FATE actually have an alternate side, their use as "GM Points." While I don't like to limit players this way personally, I do like the fact that at least there's some choice with a FP to either use it to engineer victory, or engineer events. So you can allow your character to fail, say, but use an FP to make the failure very interesting.

    Oh, one more cool things about HP in HQ. Sometimes they have a potentially cool effect, and at other times they don't depending on the vagaries of the die roll. This is cool because, since you spend the HP after the fact, you get to guage when it's "effective" to do so. So you never get the situation where you spend the point and it fizzles, and yet having a HP on hand is no garuntee that you'll be able to force the situation to be what you want.

    This is a problem, too. In some games you always have points on hand, and they can always allow you to win. Meaning that the die roll is "Do I win, or do I have to choose to win or lose?" I find it preferable that you don't know this going into a roll.

    Mike

    Thomas T,

    Good point. I'm gonna cautiously say that I think a number of systems do address this concern already though.

    F'rex, returning to Mutants & Masterminds, a Hero Point can be spent to:
    * Re-roll a d20, adding +10 to the result if it's below 11 (ie. guaranteeing a result in the 11-20 range, which is often an auto-success, but not always)
    * Use one of your powers in an abnormal way as a "power stunt" for the rest of the scene. (You can do this automatically, although you'll have to roll as normal to see if your new power use does its thing or not.)
    * Perform minor plot editing -- introducing an NPC or other "dramatic editing". This just works.

    Similarly, in HeroQuest you're almost always getting something from your Hero Point:
    * A bump in a contest may not guarantee victory, but at the very least it will reduce your level of Defeat by one whole category, which is significant in and of itself.
    * Adding to your Ability scores is obviously something you can keep and is automatically successful.
    * Adding new Abilities, particularly relationships and other stuff, just happens and gives a pretty nice mechanical boost right there.

    Personally, I don't think Hero Points should be "magic bullets" that let you auto-succeed. In games where using them bears a very heavy cost (say Doom in Conspiracy of Shadows, or Paul Czege's new Niche system) then I think that's neat, but otherwise they just generate uncertainty which == boredom to me. My personal preference is that they are generally useful, help make things less bad, and give me a decent shot at success without just guaranteeing it.

    For instance, my big beef with the Cinematic Unisystem games is that the effect of Drama Points is so great that there might as well not be another system underlying them. If I'm fighting a Big Bad who has N drama points and is committed to using them in this fight (let's say it's the season finale) then I either
    * have N or more drama points of my own, in which case I negate their effects and the fight plays out just like we didn't have them
    * have less than N drama points, or choose not to spend them for some reason, in which case I am in a world of hurt and open to things like x5 damage attacks

    ... all of which comes from the fact that in a game where stats and skills range from 1-5 (okay, a bit more for supernatural beasties, but still) a +10 bonus is just too much. It's like M.A.D. -- if you "go drama" then I've got to match you or else you can pull out a decapitation attack and still have a net +5 to your d10 roll, making it a virtual certainty. Which means that (to my mind) the Drama Points overpower the system, rather than gracefully integrating with it.

    GM: Angel goes first. He moves to drive his sword through Buffy's heart. That's a "decapitation" attack, meaning he takes a -5 penalty to hit and does x 5 damage if he does so. He's spending a Drama point (10) + sword (16) -(5) for decapitation +(6) die roll = 27.

    Buffy's player: Ouch. So what are my options?

    GM: Well, you have Sword +17. You could just roll the die. If you get a 10 then you parried okay.

    Player: And ... if I get a 1-9 ...?

    GM: Well you take 36x 5 = 180 points of damage and die instantly.

    Player: Okay. I'll spend a Drama point. So what do I have to roll?

    GM: Oh, nothing. Your Drama point gives you an automatic +10 to your roll, so you totally succeed.

    Player: Whew. Okay, I guess I'll try that too. I'm totally going to sword-stab him through the heart. I'll spend a Drama Point.

    GM: Great. You're rolling sword (+17) - (5) for decap attack + (10) for your drama point = 22 + your die roll.

    Player: Cool, I roll a 9. That's a total of 31. Take that, Angelus! I do ... um ... 160 points of damage.

    GM: Um. Okay. His defense is (16+6=) 22. So I guess he'll spend a Drama Point. That bumps him to 32. So he parries.

    I don't, in general, like hero/fate points unless they are somehow tied to the theme. Luck Points in, say, Warhammer are a fringe thing - they are kind of tied in to the gritty fantasy feeling of the game; you can get out from some situation with luck, but your luck is going to run out. And you can see it happening. Flipping and flopping rolls in Unknown Armies is also kind of fitting because they are tied with the meaning of things and the nature of Magick. You do something, a ritual, as a symbolic action and it gives your character and edge. With Hero Points, I'd like to have a reasoning of why a character suddenly has a greater change of doing something big or succeeding because of a past success. If there's a justification ("you're feeling self-confident, you know you can handle this one as well"), it's good, if it fits to the theme.

    Without ties to the theme, the points tend to become irrelevant in our group. Players don't really know what to do with them since they don't understand (or rather, want to understand) why they got them in the first place.

    I dislike such points as means to reward a player for doing something; they are usually just pavlovian mechanism used to encourage a certain kind of player behaviour with a superficial tie to what's happening in the fiction. I have a problem with a lot of Pulp genre games because they tend to have a lot of these mechanics are a still tied to the over-the-top -theme. On the same note; handing out experience points or whatnot based on how a player performed, and the straight out rating players with such systems, is right out.
