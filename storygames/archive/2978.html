\n I really, really want to play in games that are as amazing for each of these settings as Agon is for Greekery or Ganakagok for the frozen north. PTA might do, but I think there could be more than that. I haven't found systems worthy of them yet, and don't know what I'm doing well enough to write a system myself. Here they are:

1) Napoleonic War. I'm talking Sappers, Grenadiers, Curriassers, "Form Square!", Sharpe's Series, awesomeness. I don't want a wargame where I'm simulating battles with minis. I'm talking about soldier drama, fighting Boney's armies across Portugal and Spain and loving every minute of it.
2) Proto-Rastafarians in Jamaica, ~1655-1750. I'm thinking Maroons + Rastafarianism before its time + Capoeira. Tell me you wouldn't play that game.
3) Treasure Ships of Zheng He. Ming dynasty china + Islam + a powerful eunuch captain + some of the biggest wooden boats ever + Treasure. I want it all.
4) A good spy game. I'm running Spycraft 2.0 now and its jacked-up on system complexity - Maybe Wilderness of Mirrors if such a thing were available, or Spione with the same issue. I want to play Sandbaggers/Queen and Country, and I haven't found a game that helps me do that yet (maybe PTA).
5) A beautiful Steampunk story-game. Brass, leather, top hats, morning coats, penny farthing motorcycles, oh yeah you know you want it too everytime you read Brass Goggles


Gimme your 5. Whisper me if you have a solution for any of my 5; let the thread be for posting of lists and cries of 'Me Too!' and suchlike.

    5. Steampunk Star Wars- Controversy aside, those pictures were inspiring. If only such a mash-up were possible.

    \n 1) I am with you on the Sandbaggers/Queen & Country thing -- to which I'd add Spooks (AKA MI5 in the US).

Too big, maybe, to cover well in a single RPG....and b) There's almost no love of the genre among the US RPG-buying public, so you're dooming yourself to niche-yness of a possibly unsustainable level.

    3)Hammer Horror. To hell with "the monster within" -- I want evil monsters and stalwart heroes driving stakes and saving generic european villages (with, of course, the pulchritudinous burgermeister's daughter).

    4) Time Travel. I want aTime Travel game with consequences for altering history and its own internally-consistent mythology. I suppose what I'm really looking for is Doctor Who with the serial numbers filed off.

    5) Elizabethan -- add horror or magic if you must, but it's the time period that interests me.

    \n Most of these are tied to specific system demands, but are what I think of when I desire 'setting'.
    1. A world where battle rapping is the equivalent of Duel Monsters in Yu-Gi-Oh. "I summon the sun to burn a hole in his chest!"
    2. Hardcore modern political character drama a la The West Wing, where your character has fewer constraints on using a nuclear weapon than punching that guy he hates.
    3. A Die Hard-style setting where the PCs are the terrorists, trying to keep everything together while the police, the military, and random action heroes try to stop them.
    4. A seriously complete Peloponnesian war setting for a 10-year ongoing campaign, with great maps, a guided timeline of events, adventure seeds, and write-ups of all the major and minor politicial, military, and economic players (some fabrication would be necessary, of course).

    I didn't include much fantastic stuff, because that's covered in most ways by a plethora of games. What I'd look for in a setting is access to something I couldn't just run off the top of my head. As I mentioned, in my mind this is almost always tied to a system that foregrounds a particular aspect of the setting.

    Posted By: FlynnPosted By: Brand_RobinsJebus god I love the Silk Road
    \nBrand or Dave -

    Any recommendations on fiction or non-fiction (of the readable, not too dryly academic type) you would recommend on this subject?

    I don't have a lot of fiction in the area. However, there are a handful of non-painful non-fiction books I'll recommend. (There are many more good but painful non-fiction books. Its such a specialized area of study that much of the scholarly work on it is horrible to read.)

    Life Along the Silk Road, by Susan Whitfield is probably the best single source for an RPG reader. Its got accounts of life along the Silk Road from the 500s AD until the 900s AD, and comes from translations of documents found in the region. It deals with the daily lives, homes, activities, and politics of many people from the region and is full of material easily and directly usable in game.

    The Silk Road: A History, bu Irene M. Frankck and David M. Brownstone is another good, fairly easy to read source that deals not just with the Silk Road but the "discovery" of it by the adventurer archaeologists. It's good for games on the ancient Silk Road or for a brief bit of goodness for pulp games of archeology in the area. (Some of the material here is a bit dated, especially the stuff about the Tibetan Empire, but it isn't horribly so.)

    Religions of the Silk Road, by Richard Foltz is a short but generally well done book about the history of religion and cultural exchange along the Silk Road, focusing most heavily on Buddhism and Islam, but with good sections on Nestorian Christianity, Zoroastrianism, and Manichiesm. Gives a good sense of the dynamism and importance of the area in determining the shape of the world's religious geography.

    Those three should get you started, and all have pretty good bibliographies if you want more.
