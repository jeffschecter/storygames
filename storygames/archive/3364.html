\n Ive been doing some thinking about brain damage.
When we talk about brain damage in relation to rpgs, what we are really discussing (or so it seems to me)is conditioning. Being stuck in a pattern of thought. Thats the only arena where brain damage in relation to rpgs makes sense to me, at any rate.

So traditional games condition gamers to think in certain patterns,and this force of habit is hard to break; but breaking it can be fruitful, in that it opens up new vistas of enjoyment and fun.

The norwegian game design trend is tearing itself away from numbers and resources. Games are being designed to revolve more and more around improvisation and cooperation, and less around competition and resource management.
This has got me thinking.
The indie-culture as a whole seems very focused on the same things rpgs have always been focused on: Numbers, resources and competition.

Now Im asking this:
Is this a form of brain damage?
Does this focus (I would say overfocus, but thats just me) on rules and system, and manipulation of the two, remove fokus from the very core of roleplaying: Play?

    \n Temple, please don't drag baggage from one website to another. If you have issues with the stuff people have been saying over at Knife-Fight, please try to engage the people there.

    That said, I disagree with your conclusion for a couple reasons. First, ALL improvisational systems utilize structure---aka system---to varying degrees. I've written an article for Push on the topic, and I could share it if you want. The deal is that most of the structure in traditional improvisational systems is implicit and/or informal. This gives the impression of "free-form" or near "free-form", but most improvisation is actually very structured. RPGs can utilize overt system---aka mechanics and all that---because they don't have an (traditional) audience to impress. I view this as an advantage. As system influences play, the fact that RPGs can utilize structures that normal improvisational systems can't, means RPGs can produce fiction that a more "traditional" improvisational system can't. That's the long way of saying that I think your distinction between "improv & cooperation" and "numbers & resources" is a false dichotomy.

    Second, you're wrong in saying that "most" indie rpgs are obsessed with resources and numbers. In the better indie rpgs, mechanics serve to focus play. Are you familiar with the idea of the Fruitful Void? The basic idea is that the heart of play, the thing that really matters, the thing that players are really obsessed with, is NEVER systemized. Rather, the rules focus play on that "void".

    A great example is resolution in DitV. It's a common misperception that resolution in DitV is "about" resource management, even though it does involve resource management. The purpose of rolling all those dice is to limit PC action. Because PCs have a limit, they have to choose vary carefully what they are willing to fight, and what they are willing to let go. If the GM raises with, "I shoot the innocent bystander", the player has to decide whether its worth blowing all their good dice on saving the bystander, or whether its more important to let the person die in order to win the conflict. In other words, the resolution system forces thematic decisions, not tactical ones.

    And your other point: Conflict between players or conflict between characters? IMO the former is "bad," while the latter is"good."
    \nI don't like the brain damage thing because it implies a negative where I see simple human nature working as designed. Humans have been conditioned through millions of years of evolution to enjoy play, puzzles, and challenges. If someone has an innovation in game design that breaks the mold, by all means they should bring it forward. If people like it, they'll play it. If not, it is not due to any damage.

    Player on Player conflict is can be good and bad. When its good we call it competition. When its bad, its annoying. When its really bad, its an argument.

    Character on character conflict (in the absence of conflict between players) is really cooperation. The players are cooperating to create adversity for each other in the shared fiction. It can also be both good and bad. When its good, its character development. When its bad its a distraction.

    Good and bad above are defined by what it does to the game that the group is playing.

    \n "Brain damage" is such a loaded term for the reasons indicated above that its utility in constructive dialogue drops off a cliff since debate tends to polarize over the term itself. If you're after Socratic deconstruction of conventional thinking, using it might have a point, because you'd want to invite such a spirited discussion to surface and override preconceptions. Otherwise, "brain damage" is distracting to the point you're trying to make. To the extent any learned behavior becomes rigid enough to increase the difficulty of adopting or even apprehending alternative behaviors, it's legit to bring up conditioning. But I don't think you can't call mere opportunity cost "damage" per se.

    I had a boss once who liked to chastise people using terms like "you fucking moron," but when people got pissed, he'd say he wished people would just hear his words as "content neutral." What he meant was just what you appear to say above, that people should somehow ignore the denotative and connotative meaning of a term and look at the speaker's intent. However, I think you have to pay attention to the actual language being used, especially when that language happens to be polarizing. Intent is also important, but to get to the intent you have to interpret the language being used and the paralinguistic cues involved. Since we don't have face to face interaction here, raw text has to carry a lot more water in conveying meaning.
