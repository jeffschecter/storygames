\n What stuff should we watch, read, play?\n
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
\n
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.
\nRock,
John

    \n About that episode of COMMUNITY about D&D....\n
    \u201cThe studio and network\u2026were so upset about the crime of this episode having been written. The note session as a whole was preceded by a 45-minute period of them walking around the lot whispering to each other. They told me they would come up to my office and meet me privately. When they came up, I had the director and all of the writers in the office with me, because I was terrified. They sat down, and they said, \u2018Look, where do we start?\u2019 I couldn\u2019t believe this was happening. I was like, \u2018This is opposite of how you should feel right now. This is a great episode. We\u2019re going to get a 1.7 no matter what. We will build our ratings in other ways. The episode is not about credit cards; it\u2019s not about Hilary Duff. It\u2019s going to get the same numbers. There is a cultural build to a hit show. We have to prove to people that we\u2019re capable of good things so they can trust us, so that we can have a relationship. One day we will either be a highly rated show or we\u2019ll be cancelled. It will not have to do with this moment. This episode is good, the story is good, these characters are good. Anyone who doesn\u2019t tune in because the commercial says they\u2019re playing Dungeons & Dragons, it\u2019s not my fault. It\u2019s not on me.\u2019

    \u201cIt was such a depressing note session, because they didn\u2019t even have any notes on the story. They just didn\u2019t want it to exist. I took a photograph of my eyes driving home that day at 3 p.m. because I was leaving work early. I looked in my rearview mirror, and I was crying. More than crying, I was red-eyes, tears streaming, weeping. And I was weeping out of self-pity and frustration, like a child weeps when he doesn\u2019t understand his parents\u2019 rules. \u2018Why can\u2019t I have ice cream when I ate my liver?\u2019 I took a photo of it, so I could show it to them between seasons, because as I told my girlfriend when I got home, \u2018I think I\u2019m going to have to quit my own show, because I can\u2019t operate under these circumstances. I can\u2019t be this proud of something that the people paying me to do it are this ashamed of. It will never work. We\u2019ll never achieve anything. It\u2019ll never connect.\u2019\u201d
    \n\u2014Community executive producer Dan Harmon, explaining to The AV Club how the Powers That Be almost killed one of the best episodes of TV all year.

    You can read more about how the episode got written as The Onion's A.V. Club interviews Dan Harmon about each episode of the second season.

      From watching the trailer, I'm fairly sure this team isn't trying to make the 'RPGs make you go crazy' movie.       I think the trailer looks cool, but everything is going to depend on how the script depicts the motivations for why the villains decide to commit sexual assault. Handle it badly and people won't be saying "RPGs make you go crazy", they'll say "That's bad writing; I didn't believe it for a second." Handle it well, and people won't be saying "RPGs make you go crazy," they'll say "Yeah, that seemed pretty plausible in that situation with those characters."

      \n So in case you're no longer reading my super-long Smallville X-Men thread, here's something I did for it - a "minigame"/flashback.

      Hilariously, the subgroup of players I had for this military-based scenario was almost all female. So the story is mainly about female soldiers. Pretty cool.

      Afghanistan, Five Years Ago

      The events are basically:

      * Nightcrawler attempts to assassinate the President. The Army goes on high alert, and tensions in the ranks explode as mutants are singled out by other soldiers.

      * Taliban-backed insurgents take this opportunity, when things are in disarray, to attack. In their attack, they free a mutant from a local temporary detention center. He was mistreated by the town the PCs are protecting and comes back for personal revenge.

      * Isolated and cut off from support, they have to get civilians out of the valley in order to get them to a hospital (and also call for help), under attack from IEDs and rockets.

      It's relatively short, and emphasizes my mission-based/leader-based hack for Smallville rather than Wedges, but there are enough tensions over the mutant issue (and the fact that one just tried to assassinate the President) that there should be a bit of back and forth too. The Values/Relationships aren't on the list because I want to train the players that those are written in pencil.

      \n Cool excerpt about creating and the creative process, which I thought was relevant and inspirational for game design. Yea, I'm a little sentimental now that Origins is over.

      The poet Donald Hall, age 83, on the sculptor Henry Moore:

      I interviewed the sculptor Henry Moore when he was the age I am now. A magazine had asked me to interview him... I arranged to go to England with Jane [wife] and get out to his house in the country and talk about being 80. Henry was a wonderful man, affable, funny, not ironic. I asked, "What is the meaning of life." He answered me, without irony, saying that "you must have something you want to do more than anything else, that's at the center of your being, the center of your life, the one thing you really want. The most important thing about this desire is that it must be incapable of fulfillment."
      He woke up every day wanting to be as good as Donatello or Michelangelo. Every night he went to bed knowing he hadn't done it that day. The next day he would wake up with the same ambition in his mind, with total absorbedness. Always beginning again. Always knowing he'd fail. Always beginning again.

      from an interview with Donald Hall in American Poetry Review, May/June 2011

      1. I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me.
