    \n Only a handful of tabletop RPGs I've seen that do this sort of teaching through play. Which is unfortunate, because it's a really powerful technique. D&D actually does it very well. Your character starts out with only a handful of abilities, and as you master those you go up in level and going up in level gives you new techniques and abilities to master. It's not as tightly constructed as Mega Man, but it's there.

    Dogs in the Vineyard's Initiation conflict and interpreting Tarot cards to build characters in Ganakagok also are similar arenas where the game teaches you the skills to play through playing them.

    \n A lot of the good points about teaching concern foreshadowing:

    - Show the player a problem they don't need to solve now, but will need to solve later

    - Show the player exactly what the awesomeness they can later achieve will look like

    - Show the player* a thing they can do, which they don't need now, but will need later

    * in this case, it's not actually "show the player" but rather "stick them into a situation where they will necessarily do a new thing"

    \n Another good example of an RPG teaching you the skills of the game through play: Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple has a cute little tutorial level. It shows you a lot of important stuff about the setting and tone and how to play, before the number of goal words starts making your decisions difficult. By the time you finish that level, it's been half and hour and everyone understands how to play. Then you can explain the changing of characters and Temple Destiny vs World Destiny and then start them facing more difficult decisions from there.
