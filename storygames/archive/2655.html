    \n Part of the problem is that the organizers haven't really ever stated how they plan on organizing the games. It's implicit in the wiki but never stated.

    The other part of the problem is that in order for the system to work, people have to post games they want to run (in fairly large numbers) and people have to sign up for the games they want to play (also in fairly large numbers).

    We did the "Hey, who wants to play 'X'?" thing at Camp Nerdly zero. From my point of view it was suboptimal. Games started and stopped according to no real schedule, we wasted a lot of time figuring out what was going to get run, and I got a lot less play than I would have liked. I don't think the situation will be much better with more people; we'll be scattered all over the camp, with no way to notify them that something new is starting up and do they want to play.

    I *really* don't think it's a good way to organize the games.

    Let's say that I want to run my Amber playtest. It's second on lots of people's lists. they really want to play. I pick a timeslot I think would be good for it... say, saturday afternoon. It just so happens that the games that are FIRST on everyone's lists ALSO end up scheduled for saturday afternoon. Guess what? Noone comes to my game.

    How do I feel? Betrayed. I had all these people telling me they wanted to play, and it got ignored.

    How do players make out? They get one out of the top two games on their list, when they could have had both, if anyone had thought to reschedule the Amber game.

    So yah... I've got some personal feelings bound up in this, and it came out in my post. Maybe that was a mistake, but that's how I feel.

    How about this as a compromise: Reserve one building for pick-up play. The people who want to use the organization of the wiki can use it, and the people who don't can go to the pick-up play building or scavenge slots in the organized games when they get there. People who announce their games are more likely to get players, and people who sign up for those games are more likely to get slots.

    \n So here is my take on the schedule thing...

    1. It helps GMs and players plan out their days. I for one would like some time to wonder in the woods and do some camp-like stuff. But if that game of "Chimpmunk Commandos: SG Style" that I had been waiting for all year happened to get together while I was woodsing it I would be annoyed.

    2. Similarly, it helps me gauge what people want to play. I, as a GM, know what to prep. I am working on Artesia, a TSOY game, and two BE games. If it turns out that no one is actually interested in my MezoMecca game then that's one more thing I do not have to bring. And lets, face it. I'll probably get another 5 mph outta my car by not packing the Burning Empires book.

    3. It's a wiki. I put my name on stuff now and avoid the rush. As new items are on the schedule I can make a decision... do I want to play something different? It's content is mutable. Sure, it would be rude to step out of a game at the last moment but on the other hand that's why there is a "People that may want to play this if they have time" slot.

    4. It gives people a head's up on popular GMs/games. If we see that all the Dogs games are full then organizers and participants alike can offer a second game, etc. Looking at Dreamation there were a bunch of games that I saw people bumped off and the only thing that got some folks on was a sign up sheet - otherwise you would have had games like Mu and SotC be first come first serve or "I only want my buddies to play my game." (Of course that last bit is another conversation entirely).
