    \n I just took a quick look at the preview. I think the biggest problem is that your choices of font say something like "formal, classic" rather than relating to your subject matter. Take a look at the free fonts on blambot.com, which you can use in non-profit projects like this. The key thing is to give it some character without compromising readability, which can be a tricky balancing act. But I would definitely go with something that looks hand-drawn for the main headers, and something no more formal than a sans for the main text. If you can find a hand-drawn-looking font that allows pages of text to be read in comfort then great, but that might be asking too much.

    It would also be nice if your page layouts did more to reference the imaginative use of frames in many comic books, but I know even less about layout than I know about type, so I'll shut up at this point. Hopefully you'll get better advice than mine sooner rather than later!

    Edit: That thing about the body text looking weak? Some fonts out there on the web are poor digitisations of print fonts. They tend to have many problems, but one of them is that metal type printing spreads the ink slightly as it impacts the page. If a digital font adaptation doesn't allow for this, the result will look weedy, especially in PDF form. That might be what's going on here. Try the list at http://www.fontsquirrel.com/ for some quality fonts that are also free.

    \n My first impressions: I really disliked the cover illustration. I was tempted to stop right there, but I didn't want to literally judge a book by its cover so I tried to see what the text was like. The first page comes across as a wall of text, and scrolling down gives me the impression that the entire game is like that. It seems like it would be tiring to read. I think your combination of font size and column width is putting too many words on a line. If I were you I would try different page layouts to get shorter line lengths -- if you want to stick to an 8 1/2 by 11 page size you could try a 2-column landscape layout (it gives an effect very similar to a digest-sized book). I would probably also put blank lines between the paragraphs and lose the paragraph starting indentations so that the pages were a bit easier on the eyes. (Also, watch out for paragraphs spanning page transitions -- the two lines of a paragraph at the bottom of the first introduction page look kind of sloppy to me).

    \n Just taking a stab here, but is this layout done in Latex? I've tried doing good design layout in Latex before and it's really tough. Don't get me wrong, I'm a huge fan of it for certain uses, but even when trying to be creative it always reads as an ACM paper to me.

    I also think you need to up your leading and mess with your justification settings.

    The art also was turning me away. Maybe mess with something indicative of comics, but not relying on much artistic skill? Inset sound effects or speech balloon shapes if you have to. Or find public domain work and use it.

    As for the rules, I had the same response as Jamie: You had me sold with the first two concepts, but then the detail just slowed me down.

    Maybe one solution would be to make multiple, more focused games? Right now Kapow! is about all kinds of superheroes, which means you have to spend a lot of time on different scopes, examples, etc. and the text can't carry much flavor. Maybe if there was Kapow: Vigilantes and Kapow: Galactic or whatever instead each one could be shorter, more focused, and with more flavor. The Vigilanties one can have text written in nearly-noir style, while Galactic is full of space-bending strangeness. It could all still be the same PDF: cover the rule of three and the Primary rule first, then have a chapter each for making characters in each style, then the resolution system.

    Overall it feels like you started with this wonderfully clear concise core then accumulated stuff around it and not all of that stuff needs to be there. I'm completely behind the two core concepts, but had trouble beyond that.
