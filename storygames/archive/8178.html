    \n 1. Fingers on the Firmament is one I'm currently working on, but no game text has yet been written, so I hope it qualifies. It's about people who discover that they can reach out and grab the stars, hurling themselves through space. Actually, the game is really about being alone. And about death and why it terrifies me (how it slowly picks apart everything you've ever done until all of your accomplishments come to naught). It's also about the good stuff: community, creation, meaning.

    2. The Severance of Heaven-Earth Confederation is an idea that I've talked about, but can't quite pull off yet. It's a game that exists purely as a practice (not as a text), a living storytelling tradition or, really, several living storytelling traditions that evolve in parallel and come together in the act of roleplaying.

    3. Man, I'm not sure which one to pick here. Honestly, it would probably be a game that teaches people more about real life human societies, one that can be used in classrooms but also for fun, to learn more about people that we might never get the opportunity to meet. Something like A Day in the Life or Shock: Societies in Motion.

    Honestly, these will undoubtedly change as I both 1) learn more about what's possible in game design, by exploring and inventing new techniques, and 2) learn more about what's most important to me in my life.

    Posted By: Paul T.An easy and accessible, prep-less, preferably GM-less murder mystery game I can play in an hour or two with family after dinner--old folks, kids (of a reasonable age), strangers, etc.

    Not sure how to go about it yet.
    \nBeen puzzling on this. Had a thought.

    Think of a card game, general, based on creating sets and runs. Except that a small stack of cards are set aside - exactly like the "who did it" in Clue. Everything else is shuffled and dealt out. Sets and runs are "alibis", you must narrate your investigation to play a card. There are other cards as well (like the "reverse order" in UNO for interactions) with other investigators; again, these must be narrated.

    When your hand is empty, you can start guessing about the culprit. If you guess, anyone with evidence that contradicts you can point to it on the table if it's been played (rosie was over there, see?), or hand you that evidence if it's in-hand (if you guess that Rosie did it, and I have Rosie in my hand... I get to hand you the card - I'm one step closer to making guesses, and you now have at least one card to play).

    If nobody can prove you wrong, you should be the winner - look at the stack and find out.

    That might be a way to come at it.

    \nWhile You Were Surviving

    An episodic game, where you play through tight, high-prep scenarios.
    It's mostly systemless.
    The GM starts an episode by putting down an image, or reading boxed text, or putting a map down in front of the group. "Alright, so here's where you are when the avalance gets triggered. Mark, your pack gets swept up in the snow. It's the one with the comprehensive first aid kit. No one sees Amy. What do you do?"
    The team is involved in planning and executing an escape option from that very moment.
    When they get out of the avalanche situation, the GM congratulates them.
    There's a debriefing and "what went wrong? what went right?" feedback, probably with ritual phrases.
    And then the GM says "While you were surviving that... armed forces invaded the native village at base camp. Their situation is this:"

    In this way, you would go through disaster situations, disaster relief situations, hostage negotiation situations, exploration (dungeon crawling!) situations, and the like. You would sometimes be acting as a team. Sometimes not. Between situations a character might be swapped out for another.

    It'd be quick, the game would start IMMEDIATELY with a situation and a "what do you do?" question, and the mechanics would be minimal, situational and drive tension behind decision making.

Hmm... the only other different-ish design i've seriously been toying with is game that does classic adventures; like, actual classic fantasy, tomb-raiding, treasure-thieving adventures - not monster fighting! Perhaps something like the original Thief CPU-game in play content, where fighting is always/usually a bad idea, a last resort.
    \nHmmm... Basic/Original D&D? (Possibly with no leveling-up.)

    Ah, well, i am sort of riffing off of playing Red Box Hack with this, i think. At least a little. (And reading over the Storming the Wizard's Tower design page.)

    But i just don't think Basic D&D would generate the right feel, the engagement with the non-combat exploration and problem solving action, that i'm looking for. Now i say that never having actually played D&D before AD&D, but from what i understand it ends up playing like a rouge-like game with pen and paper - you send your pawns into a random/semi-random dungeon, try to avoid monsters, traps, and hazards (all of which consume your resources or kill your pawn outright) while searching rooms for treasures you can haul back to the surface to turn into XP or use.

    What i want is a game where the action of play is stealing "the treasure" - and, almost, working to mitigate risk? The composition and inter-relationships of the thieving crew should be important, so much that the principle form of injury / stress should be damage to those relationships.

    Um... so, not so much Tomb Raider as Ocean's 11 or The Lies of Lock Lamora...

    Really, i want a game which is an exaltation of the Thief archetype so beloved in Sword & Sorcery stories and novels, but supplanted in gaming by the Hero, Barbarian, and the Sorcerer... (Maybe think Sorcerer the RPG for Thieves? )
