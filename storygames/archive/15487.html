\n I've been watching this show about zombies (you've probably heard of it), and enjoying a lot more than I expected. (I'm not generally a television person, nor a zombie person.)

One thing jumped out at me:

This series looks like it could be gamed very well as a Dogs in the Vineyard scenario. All those issues of authority and violence, gender roles, etc, just scream Dogs to me.


(However, that's about the interactions between cast members. You'd probably need to think carefully about what to do if fighting zombies is going to be a part of the game, too. Either you need another, simpler/faster subsystem for that, or you just stick to fighting/guns for zombie conflicts. In this case, escalating to guns presumably is dangerous because it could attract more zombies, making it an interesting choice.

On the other hand, I do like the idea of taking relationship dice with a zombie: we see that a lot in the series, and no one ever seems to get bitten by the zombie of someone they knew, they tend to "win" those conflicts. Relationship dice could model that pretty well, actually, couldn't they?)

    Two Hour Wargames has a game that is pretty much The Walking Dead if it were an RPG/wargame hybrid, and I always get tonnes of positive feedback when I run it at cons or local shops because of a mix of the visual and the RPG-ness.

    \n

    All Things Zombie

    \n

    The mechanics are straight-ahead, the resolution system reasonable (2d6 vs Reputation, roll low -- except for ranged combat, but it's a simple Reputation + d6 / shot), you can play it online with a group with only a simple board/map-tool (like MapTool or your favourite), and there are expanded rules for establishing and maintaining your own base of operations, fast / Rager / smart zombies, and if you dig around a bit, crossover mechanics for their WWII game Nuts! with Nazi zombies.

    \n

    Who doesn't like Nazi zombies?

    \n There's a big difference between things like "we're arguing about our personal drama, but we need to do it quietly because if we scream and yell then the zombies will hear us and attack, so we have this extra pressure going on that's affecting our interaction" and "well, the writers decided to give us this totally safe zone to work with, so now we can scream and yell all we want and the zombies aren't really important anymore".

    Does that make sense, as the difference I'm getting at? There's a big difference between having interesting inter-character drama that is affected by the overarching situation of the show, and having character drama that starts to ignore the show's initial setup.

    It's great when you have an overarching situation and then you have the inter-character drama, and the character's ability to deal effectively with the overarching situation is complicated by their drama. That's awesome stuff. Love it.

    But when you set up an initial situation, and then you stop engaging with that situation and just wallow in relationship drama stuff that stops having anything to do with the initial situation, then you lose that exciting tension and now you just have people being stupid to each other.

    For example, I'm finally going through and watching Battlestar Galactica. I won't hide spoilers because I'm pretty sure I'm one of the tiny minority of geeks who is interested in the show and hasn't gotten around to watching it yet. But I'm at the stage after they settled on a planet for a while and now they're off again and looking for Earth supposedly. But the dynamics regarding the initial setup - with the Cylons hunting down the Humans, who then have their own internal problems that make it hard for them to deal with the Cylon threat effectively sometimes - have changed so many times that the tension I was talking about is totally messed up. So now, when I see Starbuck and Apollo going through their marriage/affair/whatever drama, most of the time it's not working for me. It's okay when it's intermingled with the Cylon attack at the Temple of Jupiter, because you have that extra tension thing. But later it's just them mucking around and wallowing in drama cause the Cylons aren't in play.

    So just because they aren't fighting zombies doesn't mean that the threat of zombies can't be used to add an extra exciting dimension to the interpersonal conflict.

    \n I prefer the comics. That's not saying much as I didn't really care for the show up until the last few episodes of Season 2.

    I don't know whether it's the script or the acting but I'm not really engaged by the characters as much. Aside from Darryl and Shane, I'm not very curious what happens to them next. The show's made some great creative choices with Shane's character relative to the comic, obviously, and that last episode was dynamite so I'll keep watching.

    I revisited the first Walking Dead trade (HC) recently and it's definitely the weakest twelve issues of the series. It got better over time and I find myself amazed by Kirkman's creative energy to keep the whole thing going all these years with few let ups. The courage in his writing and characterization choices too. The show is really just getting started.

    One thing about the comics is that Kirkman is a master of the cliffhanger. Picking up the series from the beginning and having to wait a whole month to see what was going to happen next was wonderful and agonizing. You can't really get that from the trades now as you can sit down and read 12 issues straight through.

    edit: reading single issues also, for me, kept the horror of the awful things that happen to some of the characters to a level where it did not overwhelm me to the point of not being able to continue.
