\n What makes a good oracle and/or element?

So far, from looking at those in the original text:

At least one character, who we know enough about to form an immediate opinion- but not nearly enough to completely define the character. They must come to life in play.
A second character with a relation to the first character, defined by their relation to the character.

And possibly:
And organization, perhaps defined by a specific member.
A prop, and its relation to a character.
A place, and its relation to a character.

Not every entry can have or should have all of these, but they seem to be a big thing. In the text's own sample play, the Exorcist, the Spirit, and the woman going to marry all have lots of explicit detail built in. The herders are looser, but still offer many threads, especially when connected to the other elements.

What else is out there that makes an oracle reading pop for you?

    \n In a thread over on RPG.net where we were all drooling about the imminent release of the game, and discussing the making of oracles, I posted the following:
    \n
    \n"The other thing I was thinking about this morning was trying to define what constitutes a really good oracle entry. (Mostly because some of my pulp ones I think are pretty weak.) I was musing about putting up a 'Lame Oracle' at Abulafia, where people could post all the bad ideas...
    \n
    \nAnd then this happened in my head:
    \n
    \nExample 'Lame Oracle' draw:
    \n
    \nA pie sitting on a windowsill.
    \nA man going fishing.
    \nA kid with time on his hands.
    \nA meeting between two strangers.
    \n
    \nYeesh. That's about as bad as I could come up with. No flavor, no indication of setting, background, or possible interests. Nada.
    \n
    \nOK, identify the characters: The pie implies someone who baked it -- a homemaker, since it's sitting on a windowsill and not in a bakery or something. The fisherman and the kid are explicit, as are the two strangers... Homemaker, man going fishing, kid -- this all looks like 'The Andy Griffith Show' in my head now: 50's Americana. Again; yeesh.
    \n
    \nBut what if the players really grab this and try to wring 'To Kill A Mockingbird' out of it instead? Ok, one player grabs a stranger, and makes him the 'other man', the man the homemaker cheated with once, who's back in town to pick up the affair. And someone else makes the other stranger the town gossip, a nice old biddy with her nose in everybody else's business. And what if the kid tried to steal the pie, and saw through the window what was going on when the 'other man' and the homemaker were alone in her house while her husband was off fishing...
    \n
    \nThis is going to wind up with betrayal, poison and murder in a small town, you just know.
    \n
    \nWhat I guess I'm saying is, I'm not sure there are 'lame oracle entries'. Some are better than others, of course, but if the players really push, anything can be turned into a good story with this game."
    \n
    \nIn the few days since then, I've refined my thought about good and lame entries: My 'lame' examples are lame because they have no flavor -- they add nothing concrete to the setting. They lack the 'explicit detail' that you mentioned. You can make something out of them, but you have to work at it. Looking at what people are putting up on Abulafia, the ones that I think are the best are the ones that are 'grabby', that have some detail that immediately inspires a thought about a character or situation that I can derive from the entry.
    \n
    \nWhat makes a good oracle is harder. An oracle's individual entries should all work together. Vincent's oracles are one big oracle, divided into themes. Stick within one of the four, and you get bits that more easily go together. (I tried that with the pulp oracles I started, but rather than dividing them up by story-theme I divided them up into 'types' of pulp, so that you don't have to get your weird science in with your gritty detective work, unless you want to.)
    \n
    \nOne trick might be to create a few entries that are actually too vague to be 'good' entries on their own, but that can be read as modifiers to whatever else the oracle delivers. 'A site where wicked ghosts gather and dance.' Just making it 'a site' is vague read by itself, the entry would be better if there was a concrete type of site -- but if the oracle also gives up 'A prestigious monastery, home to an order of warrior ascetics.' as another of the draws, then bang, it's obviously a haunted monastery, and the imagination is off and running.
    \n
    \nThese are my current (rambling) thoughts on the subject. Hope they help.
    \n--
    \nDainXB

    \n For me a good oracle entry is something that conjures an idea, a character, or a situation without actually laying anything down in stone.

    For example, "A company of desert horsemen, hiding a woman amongst them" is great because it gives me an image: desert horsemen, the beginning of a situation -- the woman, what is she doing there, why is she hiding? -- several possible characters (horsemen, woman, who they are hiding her from) with none mandated.

    The point, for me, is to get the juices of the people around the table going. Give them something archetypal without being generic, give them something kinda cool and cheesy, give them something to work from -- but don't actually give them a situation or a pre-set setting bit to use without modification. The whole brilliance of the game is taking these bits of stuff that get caught in the mesh of the mind and turning them into a story together.

    So far, I find that the thing that works best for me is pictures. Like, going through the Rifts MRB, I got most of my best Oracle ideas from the pictures. Who is that? What are they doing? Where are they going? Why is this cool? Now fit it into one sentence, and no fancy double clauses, and slam it into the oracle.

    Boom.

    \n Collected Oracular Wisdom:\n
    Posted By: Dave YounceThink of 5 broad categories for your Oracle things. For my cyberpunk oracle, those were City, Corporation, Technical, Virtual, and Criminal. In the Cheap & Cheesy Fantasy Game that gave birth to IaWA they were like City, Country, Ruin, Military, and Magical I think. Now divide up a sheet of graph paper into boxes like this, with your five things in place of the numbers, leaving at least 6 lines in each box if you can:

    Write at least one thing in each box, being sure that most of your places, events, and things still somehow suggest connected characters. Try to put at least 4-6 in each of your People boxes, and don't stop till you've got at least 50 entries altogether. Then make a new oracle on Abulafia, or if you're not ready to share yet, write your entries on index cards or old business cards, and you're set!

    PeoplePlacesEventsThings12345



    \n
    Posted By: Paul T.For making your own oracle, Vincent has his own suggestion here:


    TO MAKE YOUR OWN ORACLE ON THE FLY

    1. Have everybody write down two elements on 3x5 cards. If there are people around who're going to go to bed instead of playing, get them to write down two elements too anyway. Put them in a bowl. We had 10.

    2. Draw four as usual, play as usual.

    3. At the end of the session, the player whose character's up next goes through and chooses one, as usual. Her selection is limited but that's okay.

    4. After the session, have everybody write down another element or two. Add them to the bowl. I think that for it to work long-term you'll need to add more than 4 elements per session - but I don't forsee your friends having any trouble with that.

    Maybe when I'm home this afternoon I'll post the elements that we created for this game.

    There you go! My big idea here is to play In a Wicked Age Over the Edge using these oracle-on-the-fly rules.

    My big concern is that the events of the session will unduly narrow the new elements you create. One of the best things about the In a Wicked Age oracles is the curves they throw you, the nonhomogeneity of the elements.

    Posted By: John HarperOracle making:

    Open the comic book/novel/dvd that is your inspirational material. Find a character. Describe that character in one sentence, using this format:

    A former freedom fighter, the captain of a ragged starship, keeping one step ahead of the law.

    Make a bunch of characters that way, then change up the format a little bit for variety:

    A mysterious crate with strange markings, jealously guarded by a secretive aristocrat.

    Be sure to do some locations and events, too.

    The betrayal of a long-standing alliance, sudden and inevitable.

    So there's three to get ya started. :-) Once you start writing them, they'll take on some momentum of their own.
