    \n This being an SG thread, Judd's Dictionary of Mu should really be the first response.

    There's also the Dying Earth rpg, for Vancian pseudo-planetary fantasy, Matt Snyder's Nine Worlds is planetary fantasy of some kind, and I think GURPS Mars had a Burroughs section? Could be wrong.

    For other straight-up Burroughs, there's the Mars rpg for Savage Worlds (and a 3.x version), and two different supplements for original D&D. There's some other old school games spanning science fantasy, planetary romance, and swords & planets: Carcosa, Tales of the Space Princess, X-Plorers, a version of AD&D reskinned for Dune whose name I can't recall, and maybe Humanspace Empires unless its not finished? That Thomas Denmark guy who is doing the new Sorcerer covers has been working on a game called Warriors of the Red Planet for a long time now, I think it is basically D&D.

    There is also Tekumel (Empire of the Petal Throne), which is conlang/fictoanthro pr0n of a decidedly swords & sorcery/planets bent, has explanations for why dungeon crawling happens, and directly influenced the controversial virgin-sacrifice stuff in Carcosa. Not sure how much of the stuff you'd really like has been published, though.

    I promised somebody a basic playable draft of the revised World of Algol this month, but then I started writing science fantasy stuff for Dungeon World instead, so as to that stuff, who knows.

    The best recommendation though is probably the wikipedia articles on Planetary Romance and Swords and Planets. They give you a decent overview of the genre tropes.

    I'm interested by the characterization of Carcosa as sword-and-planet. Without being too high-school-y, can you say a little bit about what for you makes it fit that genre? I have seen the supplement, but my brief review left me with a sense of pulp macabre or horror. I'm obviously missing stuff. Is there something that for you really cries out sword-and-planet?
    \nWell, the typical touchstones of the genre are swords, laser pistols, aliens, mixing primitive and advanced cultures, adventurers putting ancient superstitions and fascist space dictators to the sword, and generally all sorts of sword & sorcery, except IN SPACE!! That's pretty much Carcosa right there.

    Carcosa is not quite as grim as its reputation suggests - it's just written in a pretty laconic manner that belies its nature. The facts on the ground are that it's about D&D adventurers stranded on an exotic planet full of dinosaurs, neolithic tribes, crash-landed UFOs and nameless horrors, which could just as well describe John Carter. The book doesn't go in depth into what it's like to play, but that's because it's a D&D setting sourcebook, so you're sort of supposed to know, already - and as it happens, applying D&D to what is presented ends up with basically John Carter, as the PCs romp around collecting laser guns and slave girls, and putting evil sorcerer-kings out of their misery.

    \n If you push the needle a little more towards "science fiction", then GURPS Planet of Adventure does the usually comprehensive job summarizing Jack Vance's excellent four-volume Planet of Adventure series, and distilling and transforming it for roleplaying. You are free to ignore the GURPS system crunch, or not as you wish. These are fantastic alien races in typical Vance fashion-- the Chasch, Dirdir, Wannek and Pnume could so easily be transported into any other game and provide a really unique playing experience.
