Form Follows Function

nitrospectide
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
I was recently reading through the archives on John Harper's blog, when I cam across the Dream Sheet from the Stranger Things game he's designed. I am a real fan of graphic design that is functional, and not just a coat of 'pretty' that someone has painted on. What struck me about this, was that it's a visual representation of a game mechanic. It shows process and flow.

What similar sorts of things has anyone seen in other RPGs?

Having taken a long leave of absence from RPGs over the years, I've found myself recently interested again, but this time from the perspective of game design. I've had a few ideas myself in this vein, and am curious what sorts of clever things people are doing with this approach.

For the game designers here, have any of you ever looked at the books of Edward Tufte?

    Jay, to more directly address your point--

    It's something that we're working on. I can see inklings of it in the hands of other designers; there's a deeply visual and tactile way to approach Agon character generation, for instance, by literally using dice until you settle on values you like. Harper's got it figured out.

    Some of the r/e games are more optimised toward their sheets, and some of the sheets more obviously intended as play tools (as opposed to the more traditional conception of character sheets as a recordkeeping device), than others. I think it's very illuminating to look at them with this in mind.
