    \nI see what you did there. That's how it's usually played out in these stories, and it got me thinking. How about this?\n
    Revelation - tying things up
    When all suspects got at least two facts and a motive, it's time for revelation. Put the hat upside down on the table. Each participant writes it's own name and a suspect that they want to be the murderer. Put the note in the hat, and the last one person putting a note in the hat shakes the hat draws a name, call that person out and who that person suspect.

    That participant gives a description of why and how the suspect could have murdered Mr Crow. New information may be added during this explanation.

    Continue with drawing names and give more explanations of how it could have happened, taking the previous information into account, up until there are only two notes left. The next drawn note is going to reveal the murderer. That participant may do the whole explanation by itself or demand assistance from the others. If the same suspect appears more than once during the draw, feel free to involve other suspects in the plan.

    <- The game is over. Discuss what went well, and what needs to be improved until the next time.->

    \n I like that better, Rickard.

    And effectively voting for the potential murderer is not bad. However, you may lose the "surprise" factor which you usually get in these stories: ultimately, you want one or more people to be heavily accused, but then someone who has NOT previously been suspected to be revealed as the murderer.

    How to do this? It's not entirely obvious.

    I had a game where a similar mechanic was implemented thus (reframed for a detective scenario for the sake of this thread):

    * When you make an accusation against someone, deal them one card from the deck, face down.
    * Everyone takes turns doing the same, until everyone has been accused.
    * Each player turns over their hand of cards and discards all but the lowest card.
    * The player with the highest card remaining is the real murderer.

    What this does is it makes the person who was accused only once much more likely to "win" the contest than someone who was accused repeatedly, and less transparently so than it would be with, say, a pool of dice that you roll.

    It's not quite right for your game, but you may wish to give this aspect - the endgame - some serious thought, since that's where the payoff of the detective crime novel reaches its climax.

    \n I did a playtest this weekend and I will now share some of my design ideas and how they came out in play.

    The vision I had was to make a game similar to The Quiet Year, MicroScope or A Thousand Years Under the Sun but, instead of a world, creating a scenario together. Not playing it out, creating it, like how I would create a murder mystery to Call of Cthulhu or similar games. I'm pretty sure that this is a unique take, even though I played A Taste for Murder. It's a unique take because it strikes a totally different WHY. It's not about playing out a character or trying to frame someone else\u2014it's about seeing how something you feed with ideas is growing. It's Easy Fun, if I use one of Nicole Lazzarro's terms, in comparison with Hard Fun or Serious Fun. The game also has a smaller People Fun. It's a light-hearted leisure game.

    MAKING PEOPLE WANT TO CONTRIBUTE
    I tried to make the light-heartiness stronger by a playful layout with pictures and, despite the theme, choosing names like Mr Rat and Mrs Cow. I did these decisions to make people more comfortable in participate, where Hard Fun (competition) or Serious Fun (do it for a higher purpose) comes with obligations to contribute well. I wanted the game to focus on the group. On a sensation of creation. Of an insight of where things are going. An exploration of where things are going. This is why I added that the group should discuss what they have accomplished, to get a sensation of what they have created together from nothing.

    During play, I wanted people to bring own ideas based on what other people said. I want people to help each other out to make the game easier for everyone. One thing I did was to make a rule physical. At first, I said in the rules that people could ask for assistance on several places, but I then turned that into a role. By saying "Assistant, please" anyone could take that role and give two suggestions where the detective chooses one of them. This wasn't used much in the beginning but later on, possibly because I asked for the assistant a few times to show how it was done, it felt like people needed it. It's great, if the player gets a blockage, to always have the assistant to fall back to.

    Another thing that contributes into bringing comfort is, according to my own theories, the fact that the game has a hard structure. A structure that is clear and showed up into everybody's faces in how the game should be played. You have to ask questions, you have to put on a hat (a gimmick that enforces the light-heartiness), you have to ask two questions, each suspect must have a motive at the end, and the suspect must agree with everything. We had a beginner in our group and she said that she was terrible at making things up. I noticed this in the beginning of the session but hardly at all after half the game.

    DEVELOPING THROUGH NARROWNESS
    And here's another thing I've been thinking about these last five years: to make things strict and small so people want to break free from it. Some of the comments I've been receiving on two fora have suggested other themes, and I wonder if I really have it in the game. Obviously, by using a narrow theme, I made people think of how to change the game. At the end of the game, I write that people should discuss what can be changed upon the next time they are playing it, and here is where I leave a space for someone to suggest another theme. I'm actually thinking of removing the paragraph that says that you can come up with names of your own, just to enforce this behavior even more.

    There are other tight structures that can be broken too. The questions were followed in the beginning but towards the end, people started to play out conversations in form of questions. This is something I guessed could happen because when you got a clear structure, it's comfortable to break free from it. If it doesn't work, you can always fall back on the original structure. I think this is really interesting in roleplaying game design and I wonder if it has been explored at all before.

    My thought with discussing what could be changed is to make every group develop the game in their own way. This would make the game stay alive.

    LEADERS SHOULD LEAD, NOT WAIT
    Another thing that this game plays around with is a different take on initiative. It did just what I thought it would do. The ones playing the detective picked the participants, who had sat there the longest doing nothing, to play the suspect. We had an equal share of playing the detective, without the game telling us to do it. I wanted to use the existing social rules, and it's possible that it did just work because we were strangers playing. But does it matter? This is a new kind of initiative system where the active players takes the initiative but where the inactive players get to play the suspects. I discovered, and that's also what I had in mind, that the suspect and the detective had almost equal share in how they contributed to the facts. So it doesn't matter if one takes the initiative. To take the lead is a good thing and should be encouraged, instead of having that person sit and wait through a segmented turn taking rule.

    WHAT WENT WRONG
    When I create mysteries, I use a relationship map like in my fish tank article. This didn't work at all with five players. The relationship map was hard to grasp after a while, with all the lines going everywhere. So I will instead write the names in columns and have one player writing facts - possibly the last one who played detective - while the others question each other.

    ---

    That's pretty much it. The game took about 1,5 hours and the beginner thought the game was one of the funniest games she ever played. And who was the murderer? Mr Rat, who had been conducting alien experiments together with Ms Unicorn. The late Mr Crow found them out. Mr Rat had also sexual relation with Mrs Cow, wife to Mr Crow, and were started to get feelings for her. So he thought he could hit two birds with one stone by killing Mr Crow. It was fun to reveal the motives one by one and finally hearing who the murderer was. The including of other suspects also worked great.

    \n I realized that I missed one rather big design thought.

    CREATE ACTIVE LISTENING
    What I think is one of the hottest things the last years in roleplaying game design, is the creation of active listening. When people that aren't in a scene wants to listen to what is said. A lot of games, like Zombie Cinema and Dogs in the Vineyard, achieves this by letting people that aren't in the scene affecting the outcome, by giving the ones in the scene rewards. If you read any of my thoughts on this forum, you know what I think of tangible rewards. So when I thought about how only two players were in each scene in The Murder of Mr Crow, I wanted to create active listening but without the use of rewards. "Assistant, please" is one of my ways of creating active listening. If people know that they can be included in the scene, they will probably listen to what has been said. Also, because everybody are going to find out a motive and because what has been established has to be taken into account, everybody must listen to what's going on. That the whole motive section is rather short (1-3 minutes of play) can also contribute to the active listening, where a 10-30 minute scene would instead loose interest after a while.

    \n ASSIST INSTEAD OF HELP
    I'm a kind guy who often offer a lending hand to someone else, but I noticed that if I ask "May I help you?", people are less bound to want it, compared to if I ask "May I assist you?". That's the reason behind why "The Assistant" got it's name. If you want help, it's because you can't manage it on yourself, and I don't want that kind of feeling in a collaborative game. If someone however assist you, that person places itself lower than you. So this role is a way of playing with status within the group, but also making the group more accepting to asking for help.

    YES CREATES ADVENTURE
    I'm currently rereading Impro by Keith Johnstone, and even if half my playtesters said that they wanted an option to say "no", I didn't change my game. I will now translate from the Swedish translation of Impro, page 153:\n
    There are humans that prefer saying "Yes", and there are humans that prefer saying "No". Those who says "Yes" gets rewarded through the adventures they will experience while those who says "No" gets rewarded by the security and safeness that they will achieve.

    [Ideas that he got from Arthur Couch and Kenneth Kenison, from "Yessayers and Naysayers", Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychologyol 160, No 2, 1960. My remark]

    /.../

    There is a connection to status transactions here, because low-status players have a tendency to accept while high-status players block. The high-status player blocks every transaction, if they don't feel that they can control it.
    \nSo why did some of the playtesters want to say "no", and why did I refuse them? (As a side note, why am I tired of the default state in roleplaying games is failure [=to be told "no"]?)

    \n CONFLICT IS UNCERTAINTY
    It's accepted that conflict, in our Western society, is what drives a story. You can see the result of that thinking that in all kinds of roleplaying games\u2014especially Forge games. The Chinese and Japanese has a non-conflict narrative form called kish\u014dtenketsu. It consist of four acts, where the first two introduces something, the third introduces a second element and the fourth connects the previous acts. You find an example here in form of a comic.

    If I step up a level, what conflict and kish\u014dtenketsu both have in common is uncertainty. Both can bring suspense or curiosity. I said in the previous headline that "yes creates adventure". What that standpoint also does is avoiding conflicts. This game that I've created has no conflict in it. The plot is already there, and the suspects can never lie, although they can half of the times answer in an evasive manner.

    I'm kind of tired of the heavy focus on conflicts that most storytelling and Forge games have. I wish there were some alternatives.

    \nFrom another thread.\n
    * Why is it important to roll the die in the open? Just curious.
    \nI couldn't let go of this comment. It bothered me somehow, and a discussion on another forum made me realize why.

    MAKE PEOPLE GROK; THROUGH THEME; THROUGH WHAT THE GAME RESULTS IN; THROUGH DEMANDS OF THE PARTICIPANTS
    To grok something is not only to understand it, but to also to realize it's purpose. I failed to explain that in this game, where I think it would be obvious that the die should be rolled in the open in a game where the sole purpose is, not to compete with or frame each other, but to take each others' ideas, listen to them and help everyone, in collaboratively way, creating a scenario together. For me, it was obvious that the die roll should be made in the open, so others can take that into account when they improvise together with the person who rolled the die. The game shouldn't create any uncertainties of why people are there together\u2014of what purpose they are there for.

    To be able to make someone grok the game's structures, I think that the author must first present the theme of the game and what the game will do. I did that, but what I didn't realize was that I also need to tell what's demanded of everyone playing the game. What I then should had done is to make every structure strengthen either the theme, what the game is about or what's demanded of the participants. I think that if I had done that in a good way, that question about the die wouldn't had been asked. I will take this insight with me into the next game.

    \n Hi Rickard,

    Yes, it makes sense. We play it and as I mentioned in the other thread, have "modded" it to make it more of the setting (1950s post-war England, though we sometimes go 1930s) and style we want to play. No changes to the mechanics, but we have done the following:

    - We use titles + first name(s) + last name = Name. Mr. George Halliwell and Sir James Edwards come to mind.
    - We create a number of "professions" (roles) appropriate to each game's setting. Gardener, banker, lawyer, vicar, gentleman, Major, lord or lady of the manor, author, actress, artist, gadfly, Aunt, &c.
    - We brainstorm three objects/heard/noticed per player that are appropriate to the specific game we want to play based on setting each game. We've called these Importants. Importants that work in a blue-collar seaside village murder won't necessarily work in an country estate outside Oxford.
    - We sometimes bring one character (or their relative) from the previous mystery into the current one. The connections add a neat twist.
    - We sometimes have the detective played by a single player the entire game (often named Inspector Albert Swinton, the name of a character I write narrative RPGs online in Storium).
    - We re-typeset the PDF (I'm a designer of 25 years) to make it more suited in terms of visual look to the setting we play.

    As I said, we like the game a lot over our five plays and find ourselves creating quite imaginative murders. The last one involved the tragic murder of Emily Millbridge the village librarian, a rather stunning diamond, the theft of a seemingly innocuous book, and a dead crow. Everyone was aghast when it was discovered that the murderer was none other the Lady Olivia Howard.

    \n Crow, not cow. :-) A dead black bird delivered in the night as a warning to a local councilman, it turned out. Though a dead bovine would be funnier and cooler.

    We create a fixed number of Importants (we couldn't come up with a better name) each session; usually two or three are suggested by each player. So they change from game to game, depending on the setting of the murder. We have some that have repeated a few times (overheard phone conversation, a delivery of goods, a missing letter, the theft of something important) but for the most part we decide on a Setting in a few minutes and then start the process of naming Suspects, creating professions/roles that fit the Setting, and then Importants.

    We do not necessarily use all of the Importants we come up with, no. It's nice to have extra though because we sometimes use them as "red herrings".

    I've sent our PDF along via email to show you. It's a sort of 1950s-styled "Penguin" cover book, taking our cue from Graham Walmsley, but only a handful of pages.

    Our play sessions, while not "dark", three of the five games have taken place in the early 1950s so rationing, the breakdown of class, and a general "greyness" and post-war weariness are common themes we tend to include. The next one might skirt into the political realm of the "Red Menace". The other two set in the 1930s were much lighter and one was quite full of comedic elements and situations.
