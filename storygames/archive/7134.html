    \n "Why are you playing with bad ones?"

    The same reason I'd put up with a glib answer like that - because all people have warts and lumps, personality wise. In fact, it's often the smaller warts and bumps that make a person actually interesting (to be with and to play with). Advice which relies on the beautiful, perfect people playing, is flawed. Beautiful, perfect people are boring. Also, with a little humility, I realise I have warts and bumps. I would not fit in a group of beutiful, perfect people. I would actually disrupt their oh so perfectness. Advice which relies on me grouping with people I in no way would fit with, is utterly flawed.

    On the other hand the e-mail might have been from one of the many thousands of gamers who play with people who are really beyond their acceptance threshold. In which case, yeah, why are you playing with them?

    \n Is that "bad players" as in "not so good at supporting the gameplay with inspired roleplay", or is it in the meaning of "destructive players"?

    If it's the first, I heartily agree with the mailer: any advice given should take into account that players are in fact amateurs.It's the games job to make them the kind of amateurs who fill the gameplay with love for the drama and the group.

    If it's the last, I support John's question: why play with them? However: destructive behavior is often based in dysfunctional social frames, and those may spring from a bad game too. If you play with players that are destructive only in the game, then you need to think the game through: is it right for your group?

    And as ever in such processes you may end up with answers you don't like. Your friend may be destructive in other situations too, and then you may need to rethink your "friendship": is his/her destructive behaviour really worth putting up with? The best answer may lay in how good it will be to game/socialize without him/her ...

    \n Folks, you're looking at it backwards. Consider the speaker, who thinks he has reason to call some guy a "bad player." The problem is with the speaker, not the players. He might be playing with people he shouldn't be. He might be expecting this player to do things that the player doesn't find fun. He might be expecting skilled and experienced behavior from a new player and isn't providing any guidance or support in gaining those skills and experience. Whatever he is doing, he should probably stop. That's why John's advice is directed at the speaker, and not the "bad players."

    And now, having explained it top to bottom, I believe I have killed the joke. Ta-da!

    \n Here's what Gary Gygax said about it in the "Dungeons & Dragons Dungeons Master's Guide" first edition, by Gary Gygax (copyright 1979). Thanks to John Kim for excerpting it on his website.

    Some players will find more enjoyment in spoiling a game than in playing it, and this ruins the fun for the rest of the participants, so it must be prevented. Those who enjoy being loud and argumentative, those who pout or act in a childish manner when things go against them, those who use the books as a defense when you rule them out of line should be excluded from the campaign. Simply put, ask them to leave, or do not invite them to participate again.
