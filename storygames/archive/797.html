An Open RPG Text

wyrmwood
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
In planning revisions to some of my game chef submissions, I've been hitting a design question which I'm somewhat wary about. One of the key ideas design criteria behind most of the games was to construct a multiplicity of meanings. Essentially to ensure that a broad space exists for interpretation of the game, while the underlying dynamics remained largely unified.

The key problem with that design goal is the need to ensure that the game does not inadvertently tell the reader how to play, and at the same time communicates that this freedom is intentional, removing interpretative authority from the text, while retaining dynamic influence of play.

The first possible solution seems to be careful explainations, but I fear that these are limited by the need for examples. In that view I've considered multiple distinct, and ideally contradictory examples of play. In essence these would map out the potential space of the game. The third possible solution is building into the design a sort of during play synchronization of interpretation. I figure some combination of these might suffice. How ever, I am curious if there might be other options I have not considered.

Ultimately I'm not sure if it is possible to entirely remove the RPG text's authority in interpreting the dynamics of play. But, I believe it is worth trying.

- Mendel

    Thomas,

    This is a bit more complicated than a game that does it well or not. Especially because there are two different ways for a text to fail to be explicitly silent: by being quiet but interpretable as vocal, by being explicitly vocal. The point is, I'm trying to make a text that is silent about the value of play states and dynamics, and unambigous about that silence.

    Most RPGs are a mixture of all of the above.

    Take a look at Dogs in the Vineyards:

    Dice on a character sheet - the text is pretty close to being explicitly silent on this, not valuing the presence or absence of dice.

    "Say yes or roll the dice" - the text is quiet on this, but not explicitly so. This causes ambiguity as to when you shouldn't say yes: importance to youself, importance to the players as a whole, potential for a fun conflict, significant in some objective sense.

    Being Dead - Since being dead puts you out of play, the text is pretty clearly judging this as negative.

    It is quite possible that no text can be explicitly silent on everything during the game, but I want to increase that expanse of play for which that is the case. I am very interested in communication, and I want to reduce or at least confine the influence of the text on the connotations of that communication.

    This is a difficult task, and it is a subtle distinction, but I believe that it is an important step in expanding the potential of RPGs.

    - Mendel

  • DevP
    June 2006

    I think in Dogs, some of the most important value judgements - Is this just? Is this fair? Is my character a good person? - definitely happen entirely at the table, with some but not much influence from the text.

    So as to removing that influence from your own text, clearly stating your intentions and some clear and contradictory examaples could both help. There are two things that could still be tricky, though.

    Firstly, default meanings. Being dead, for example, is almost always a bad thing, especially in protagonist-centered fiction; if your text doesn't say anything one way or another about character death, is it implicitly accepting the value judgement that being dead is a playstate to be avoided? So the game needs to consider what fiction elements have a really strong default, and whether it's important to explicitly justify the alternative.

    Secondly, emergent meanings. Regardless of what the text says, your mechanics have very strong power to reward play. If the player who gets the most in-game currency is the one who has the most fun/impact/attention, then even if you say "don't feel compelled to have or lack tokens" the players will tend towards certain interpretations of play. So I think you need to judge each rule, and determine if its effects are desirable; if not, you need to make sure that all the choices are ones that empower the players, so that they don't feel obligated to use a certain one to enjoy their game.

    Judson,

    You underestimate the relationship between values of game states and values of game dynamics. Since game dynamics move between game states, if certain dynamics are more or less valued, then the dynamics will acquire value by that same means. But at the same time, if certain dynamics are valued over others, the states will have an induced value structure. You can't eliminate that link. The point being that a game text that clearly imparts that there are no inherent values to game states (such as death) will impart that same to the dynamics.

    Certainly as you play the game you will find some states more valuable than others. But this is fundamentally emergent, it is not a value derived from the text, rather our search for structure in play. I don't want to reduce that form of emergence, indeed, I want it to be as viable as possible.

    Also, at no point have I been saying that designer embedded values cannot be good things. I'm simply saying that they are not always good things, depending on your design goals. Challenging is one way to do things, but it is hardly the only way to foster communication and allow internal beliefs to emerge.

    - Mendel
