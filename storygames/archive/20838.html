\n Sorry, this may be slightly off topic...

I was sitting down to play Swords without Master (as Overplayer) with three players all probably in their late twenties or early thirties. I was talking about my influences and of course said "Fafhrd and the Grey Mouser". Nothing... blank looks all around. Never read it. So then I said "Elric. Drug addicted prince of a decayed empire. Cursed black sword." Nope never read that, any of them. I said "Surely you've read Robert E Howard, Conan the Barbarian". No all around the table.

All three of them are avid D&D players and have been for a while (I assume since college or even earlier). So did I just happen to find the three that haven't read these or do people of that age have other influences? Are Leiber, Howard and Moorcock not widely read today?

Of course, on the bright side, I thought that my job might have gotten easier because if I ever get stuck, I can totally just port something in whole cloth and they will never know.

    \n That's a problem I often meet with, too, and haven't solved. When I meet people eager to play a "swords & sorcery" adventure, usually their cultural references are... something else entirely: manga & anime, a wide range of D&D spin-off fantasy, some computer games, Game of Thrones, and/or Conan the Barbarian and a bunch of 1980s movies. And I'm dissatisfied with that.

    \n When I was playing regularly in my teens and early twenties (I'm fifty-ish now), I certainly knew who they (Leiber, Howard and Moorcock) were, but I was a Tolkein fanatic and to a lesser degree a King Arthur devotee. Moreover, the two sets of contrast being drawn here between pulp and high fantasy on the one hand, and pulp and "big weapon" anime on the other would have been meaningless to me, not because I couldn't see or understand them, but because I didn't care. I was much more interested in the characters that I was creating myself. I was also interested in mythology, medieval history, and miniatures war-gaming. These were enough to propel me forward. About two years into my early role-playing, I got into a group playing Runequest and was swept away by the magnificent world-building that created Glorantha (as with Tolkien, world-building has always interested me). So, Greg Stafford et al. would be my next strongest influence when it comes to fantasy fiction. Later, I started playing Pendragon.

    I've had long periods of reading and enjoying science fiction (space opera, cyberpunk, and other sub-genres) but I've never liked SF-themed games. I'm not sure why. I've read and enjoyed many of the great writers, but as for role-playing in that genre, I couldn't care.

    I came back to role-playing after a long hiatus because I started reading original fairy tales, hundreds and hundreds of them--I can't remember why I started. I don't know what it is about them, but I'd rather design and/or play a good fairy tale themed game than anything else.

    Now, I would rather read Kelly Link. I wish there was a game that capture the Slipstream genre well. I hear she wrote a Fiasco playbook, maybe that would be good to look at.

    This is just a meandering, anecdotal experience. I'm not sure if it's helpful to the topic.

    Genre-purity, genre-knowledge--for some people, these things aren't as important, I guess.
