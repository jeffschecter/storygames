\n
\n
\n
\n\nWarriorMonk \n\n\n
\n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I've really enjoyed the way indie games have evolved in the last years. I've been surprised about how this evolution actually began like 30 or more years ago. I've seen a lot of mechanics designed by unsatisfied players and concerned GMs to make playing more interesting. I've seen also a lot of mechanics to make the GM work easier, start the game with no prep or make whole prep with the players in one session or less.

So I'd say yes, now RPGs are more fun to play and easier to GM. But what could make them more fun to GM? As a GM, where's your fun? What would possibly improve it?

Please, let's just start with answers for now; I'll clarify where I'm going with these questions in a few posts.

    \n Hanging out with friends, talking in funny voices, playing the bad guy, hatching nebulous-but-undoubtedly-nefarious-plots, cheering at characters' successes and commiserating over their failures -- all these are the fun things that keep me perched in the GM's chair.

    Thinking on the above, I think the best part of GMing for me is playing NPCs. There's some kind of transitional point where an NPC goes from "Petty Noble with Green Houppelande" to "Lord Veldik of the Rampant Marches," acquiring a scratchy voice and a tendency to glance away from whoever's talking to him - when it happens, man. It's just the best feeling. Improving GMing for me could be quantifying what happens to make an NPC gel and generating a toolkit or process to support that.

    \n My two absolute most favorite things to do as a GM:

    1. Sit back and listen to the players talking/arguing with each other in-character. This is awesome. I know gaming is supposed to be an interactive thing, but sometimes it's just so much fun to be the audience for a bunch of talented people.

    2. Play lots of different NPCs. As said above, getting to be a versatile character actor rather than a lead = fun.


    I also like prep work (having an idea, thinking of all the different ways you could use it, and polishing it until it shines is always a good thing), and I like getting to commandeer the most comfortable chair in the room. The rest of GMing is okay, I guess.

    \n There are many joys to the gamemastering thing but here are two big ones:

    1 Our D&D game is my primary fiction outlet right now, so its world and NPCs are chattering to me 24x7. That constant inner narrative builds up a crush of ideas. It's extremely gratifying to release those ideas into the wild, see them react with the PCs, and transform into surprising fiction.

    2. I love being put on the spot and challenged to be provocative and entertaining. In A Wicked Age really lights me up this way. Staring at a tangled r-map, having to come up with that first scene while everyone's waiting, is a fun brainsprint.

    When it works, when the first scene reveals itself to me and I start blurting out the ideas and then I get momentum and my delivery starts to get smoother, and then the other players start to react and then the game lurches to life -- that's the purest satisfaction for me. That is like pulling a big knife switch of happy.

    Anyway, I was wondering about this because while I've seen a lot of games that make me want to play, pretty few make me want to GM. Among these are the ones that offer no need of prep at all, simple engaging mechanics and an interesting setting that players can build over and thrash a bit without ruining it. I've seen interesing designs, where just the possibilities implied in the mechanics are enough to give me the thrills of what could happen in the game. Other designs offer out of the box solutions for GMing in general, but fail to provide enough creative resources for players to handle the creative stress of coming up with something amazing right on the spot.

    So I'm thinking if there could be a way to make GMing attractive to unexperienced players, instead of making them think it's something troublesome that one of the players have to do if everyone wants to have fun at all.

    \n I really like:

    - Hosting & organizing games.
    - Helping everyone have a good time.
    - Making sure the experience is fair.
    - Influencing pacing and spotlight, ensuring everyone gets their chance.
    - Combating "survival of the fittest" or "loudest".
    - Running games where I don't have to roll, require minimal prep, NPCs have minimal statistics, failure doesn't mean nothing happens, the fictional situation and reason for playing are very clear, player characters are empowered to go after their goals.
    - Thinking on my feet, improvising, leading by example, increasing the energy level of the game, listening and being surprised by what everyone does, getting complimented for running a fun game, the player's surprise when they ask "how did you do that" and I reply "I try not to be awesome, I try to help you show everyone else why you are the one who is awesome."
    - The pressure to be creative, my mind runs a thousand miles per hour and GMing helps calm and focus me.

    I really love the social aspect of GMing. I love people. It's one of the reasons I have trouble with campaigns. Playing long term games means playing with less people.
