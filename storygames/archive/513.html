[Drowning and Falling] Creating Challenges

Jason_Morningstar
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
One big sticking point with Drowning and Falling has been the creation of challenges, which come in three flavors - easy, difficult, or deadly. The original version had these decided by card draw, with an ace being deadly, a face card being difficult, and a number card being easy. After that, there was a lot of ambiguity about how to translate things like falling onto spikes or drowning in self pity, which is a problem. So I'm proposing a slightly more complicated way to create challenges that sweeps away all the ambiguity. Cards are worth points (2-14, an ace is 14) and you buy extra features (like dice of damage, trait zapping, or increased difficulty). In this system you need at least an eight to get a difficult challenge, and a ten for deadly. You should be tempted to make a painful, but easy, challenge or a mild, but deadly one (deadly meaning you are likely to fail and lose a level). Thus:

INJURY
+2
Damage is equal to the difference in the roll and the target number.
Thus, if the target number is 6 and you roll an 11, you take 5 hit points of damage.
+3
Damage is equal to the difference in the roll and the target number, plus a die of damage to each failure.
+4
Damage is equal to the difference in the roll and the target number, plus two dice damage to each failure.
+6
Damage is equal to the difference in the roll and the target number, plus a die of damage for each roll, even on successes.
+8
Damage is equal to the difference in the roll and the target number, plus a die of damage for each success and three dice of damage on each failure.

CHALLENGE
+6
Amp up challenge to difficult.
+8
Amp up challenge to deadly.

TRAITS
+2
Increase related harmful trait you specify by one when you fail instead of taking hit point damage, increase related helpful trait by one when you succeed
+6
Increase related harmful trait you specify by one when you fail instead of taking hit point damage.

Questions, especially for those of you who have playtested:

1. Is this no fun? It is a little more complicated on the front end but way less vague.
2. Are the point spreads accurate?
3. Did I miss likely combinations of penalties?
4. Should die penalties be stackable?
5. Do I need to rename "deadly"?

Thanks...

    I really need to go over the most recent document, as it's been so long since we've played, but here's my not-up-to-date stand.

    1) Simple rules only. Trash the complex stuff. It's a simple & silly game and it should stay simple & silly. IMHO even optional complex rules are no good. They don't reflect the flavor of the game properly.

    2) Danger + Monster + Treasure = Room. You use a card to 'buy' the danger level of the room and maybe a card to represent the power of the monster & the value of their treasure. Tied into point 1, there are only three values; low, medium, and high. (Labeled with more sillyness)

    3) Lists = awesome. But, using the playing cards, let's reverse the order on how things are picked from the list. Let's say that there's a list for Falling Challenges, like so;

    Easy (to avoid)
    Falling from a short height (like a step)
    Falling down drunk

    Falling for a trick

    Medium
    Falling from a medium height (like a balcony or window)
    Falling in love
    From an extra dangerous place (like onto spikes)

    High(ly difficult to avoid)
    Falling from a silly height (like a bottomless pit)
    Falling into Debt

    Now, instead of picking something from the list and then 'paying' for it, you've got your cards in your hand, and you're like; "Gee, I've got the cards to make two Low-level Falling dangers and one High-level Falling danger, which ones look kewl?" So, Cards + List = Your Options.

    Oh, and the character sheet, while having room for half a dozen characters or so, also needs to have the short-hand version of the list on it. Maybe it'll have to be a 2-page character sheet, I dunno.

    -Eric
