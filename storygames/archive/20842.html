\n I ran a game last week, and I am now getting ready for the second session.

It's a GM-run RPG with simple rules and strong character empowerment, and Keys (like The Shadow of Yesterday/Solar System/Lady Blackbird). There are three players.

I threw together a tense, tricky situation and a handful of NPCs with strong drives but not the ability to get themselves out of trouble. Then I told the players to come up with characters who fit into that and want to change it: the most important players in this mess.

My approach to this kind of gaming is to create a mess, and then follow the PCs trying to accomplish their goals. (With a game system which allows them to do so, but at a cost, some interesting drama follows quite naturally.)

What I got back from them was a little... passive. Two, in particular, are basically just trying to preserve the status quo. They're not powerfully pursuing goals, or unsure of what to do.

As a result, when we ended the first session, I realized:

*My NPCs are far more interesting and active characters right now than the PCs.*

That felt really strange, and uncomfortable. I can do the thing where the GM brings the fuel to the table, and pushes the game along, while the players react... but I haven't done it in many years, and I don't really *want* to. I'd rather be reacting to the players, as I have generally been doing for the past some odd years.

Do you have any clever advice on how I might approach the second session of this game?

* The players want to be passengers in your story. You should take the game in firm hand, come with a plot, and drive it forward full-on.

* Put away everything you have and throw the game in the players' lap; tell them nothing happens except for what they pursue.

Anything within that range or beyond it, along with a practical method of putting it into practice, is welcome.

What would you do?

What have you always wanted to try (even if you never got the chance)?

How would you go about it?

Feel free to ask for any details you'd like, as well.

    \n What I do in this situation is that I continue play and bring the fuel, but I do it in a way that puts the PCs on center stage, even if the players insist on being passive sideliners. This is not uncommon in certain types of fiction, e.g. fantasy literature - many well-known heroes of the genre are specifically passive people who experience things done to them, and perhaps rise to the occasion at the very of the story. Consider Bilbo and Frodo Baggins, or Thomas Covenant, or all the children in all the Narnia books: they're all just like your player characters.

    So what you do is, you let the NPCs set the agenda: Gandalf tells the PCs what he wants them to do. Then you bring complications, dragons or whatever. But then, Gandalf dies midway through while fighting a balrog, and now they're on their own, and new information emerges - Gandalf's plan is now impossible or undesirable. They have to make their own choices!

    Or, Gandalf #2 comes in from stage left, and he wants them to do the very opposite of what Gandalf #1 wanted, because we're shades of grey now. Note that this is an entirely ordinary narrativist arrangement, the PCs being passive doesn't change anything, really; it just means that the NPCs are slightly more proactive about demanding action from them, instead of merely hinting at the possibilities. A knight protector who merely wants to protect the status quo can still be the center of dramatic action when they are forced to choose between their loyalty to the king, and their loyalty to the queen.

    Note that you don't need to prep a plot, just the NPCs who want things, and who need the PCs to do these things for them. It is interesting enough, speaking of play content, if you have three interesting NPCs come and beg the PCs for help in turn; it lets the players participate by answering a multiple-choice question, and it's drama when you portray those NPCs asking them for help. After the choice you simply portray the consequences of the choice, and that's the game.

    This all actually smacks a lot of Fables of Camelot. It's a sort of a beginner game, so I see a lot of player passivity when running it. Hasn't been a problem, really; I just need to be interested enough in the subject matter to portray lots of NPCs and let them do stuff to provoke the players, and sooner or later they start to make their own stands - either I hit a compelling button (often something the player didn't realize would work in advance - flags are a flawed tool), or the player gets comfortable and confident enough to start actually reacting and doing things.

    \n Some players sit in one spot and let things come to them. That can work fine, if they're in a spot that's ripe for continual drama. And some people are stoic. That's okay too, if they dig that, you just have to play for subtle drama and a slow-build. My Apoc Vegas crew was a lot like this. What eventually drew them out of their safehole was realizing that offscreen, the world was moving and Fronts were taking up position and making alliances all around them, and they were about to be the only faction on the map who didn't have their hand in the regional power game. But it unfolded very slowly, taking six sessions before they made a move on the city map. That may just be their speed. If so, you might play into inter-character drama (even P2P drama) and let it become more of an emotional tale than a physical one.

    If you're still itching to pull a move, here are another few quick thoughts, although I don't know your players so I can't advise on which might work better...

    - abandon them. Now they have to think on their own.

    - approach as another NPC who plants seeds of doubt about the first NPCs, or have the PCs discover something that implies their NPCs are not what they seem. Why are they *really* hanging with us? Foster conspiracy theories.

    - attack with a powerful force or disaster that even the other NPCs are unprepared for.

    *The Background Situation*

    The general setting is a vaguely Dark Ages-fantasy-Europe place, just outlined in very broad strokes. (It's been hinted at more than actually defined.)

    There is a town named Verhoven, small, distant, isolated. Originally built as a military outpost, it survives now only because of its iron mine (which is operating still, just barely). It is far from the capital of the kingdom, or the King's rule, on the edge of a swamp.

    Twenty years ago, it was ruled by the Varga family. However, the head of the family became involved with one of the King's bastard sons. The King was none too happy about this, arrested the Varga, and threw him in a dungeon.

    The village has been run, since then, by a small group of Royal Guardsmen, who stepped in to replace the Vargas. They also brought in some Tchegarans (dark-skinned folks from the South, who are considered unclean and rumoured to consort with the devil - typical medieval casual racism, quite nasty) to work at the mine.

    At the present time, however, the head of the Varga has been released from the King's dungeons and is en route back to Verhoven, along with all his other Varga relatives.

    The problem? There has not been any kind of official statement from the Crown about, well, who's supposed to be in charge now.

    This means that the town is effectively separated into four groups:

    * The Guardsmen. They've been ruling the town for twenty years, and aren't exactly keen to see the Vargas return - the scrupulous few stay at the Keep, but many have been living in Varga mansions and spending Varga wealth to give themselves a very comfortable lifestyle for two decades now.

    * The Villagers. The locals mostly want segregation: to keep their lives separate from the Tchegarans, and have forced them to make their own burial grounds in the swamp (so as not to defile the town's cemetary with their dead). Some side with the Guardsmen while others look forward to the Vargas' return.

    * The Vargas. They are coming back and expecting to run the place once more. Of course, they'd like to see everything back to "normal" and the Guardsmen turn into their servants or sent home.

    * The Tchegarans. Horribly oppressed, they tentatively see the Vargas as potential saviours, and want to share in the wealth of the town (rather than continue being ostracized) as well as to see the Guardsmen out of here. They are forced to live outside the town in a little village made up of huts, and aren't supposed to be found in the town after dark.

    Curiously, I run into one particular problem all the time with NPCs connected to my PC during character creation; something about the combination of being asked to invent an NPC on the spur of the moment combined with how someone else plays that NPC in the actual game usually creates a character I really don't enjoy talking to or interacting with.     edit: oh wait, I have a piece of actual concrete advice to offer!

    Do everything in your power to avoid having an NPC start off with a big ask, it rarely works well. They should ask for help with something easy first -- something very much in the PC's wheelhouse, something that takes little effort and poses little risk, something that doesn't have a lot of strings attached to it. It's not just about giving them some room to establish personality and a relationship with the PCs instead of coming off as an immediately aggravating, problem-spewing quest monkey, it's also good psychology: just as in real life, getting a small favor from someone makes them much more likely to do a big favor for you later on. (Plus, the low stakes and negligible risk of an easy task can give hesitant players a chance to try things without feeling like they're ruining something.)

It turns out that this was pretty intentional: the player explained to me that he thinks people "always play the same character" in RPGs, and so he came to the game with a very specific goal before the game even began. He wanted his character's determining feature to be a strong enmity towards another player's character (Luca, in this case).

    The whole session, I started putting more power and more decisions into his hands, both good (information, deferrals) and less good ("Boss, what do we do with this stolen treasure?"). He enjoyed coming up with clever and self-serving ways to take charge of the situation and arrange deals which benefit himself, while skirting the edge of the law. (For instance, with the stolen Varga treasure, he told his men to separate it into two piles - the valuables stamped with the Varga crest, and the rest. The valuables that are clearly stamped or marked he will be returning to the Vargas, as though they were stolen and he fought hard to get them back. The other pile, of course, will go into his own pockets. This way his men are happy - he is not punishing them - the Vargas should be impressed with his "hard work" - and he gets a good chunk of change for himself.)

    Indeed, this seems to be the player's fun in this game: to see how much this character can get away with while still appearing like a lawful figure working for the good "of the people".

    However, I have thrown two wrenches into his plans, and we will see how he deals with them:

    * The Vargas have brought some of their own armed men with them to the town, and plan to force him and the Guardsmen to stand down while they take over entirely.

    * His favourite son is among them.

    I wasn't sure if the player would find this distracting or inspiring. Throughout the session I threw bits about his family into the mix (he received a letter from home, spotted his son in the crowd, and so forth), but he tended to ignore those, leaving them "for later" (for instance, he still has not read the letter). However, he seems quite excited by his son's presence.

    Questions for you, dear readers:

    1. What do you think of Giancarlo's situation now? How would you engage with him? Any other observations on how this has developed so far?

    2. How would you deal with the conflagration which threatens to spread through the town? After all, it's a dangerous and wild fire, and it's only the NPCs who are tending to it. How can I resolve the situation in a fair but interesting way, if the PCs are effectively pretty busy doing other things? (Although pulling them back in some way isn't out of the question! I just don't want to twist their arms to get involved, so the ideal solution would work well no matter how they choose.)

    3. In general, the session finished in a very hectic, panicked, high-tension moment. The town is on fire, Sergio is on the run, and all the storylines are coming to a head. There are a lot of "moving parts" in terms of the town's factions all being active, and I often find that kind of thing (larger-scale actions by different groups of people) hard to handle. Any advice on this front?

    * I'm tempted to try to start the third session in a calmer, slower pace (with the fire burning in the background), by zooming in on some small-scale actions and character interactions. (e.g. Giancarlo having a heart-to-heart with his son) Do you have any tips or tricks for developing a session in this direction, while maintaining the sense of crisis and tension?

    * How would you handle the actions of all the other movers and shakers in this scenario? There is a lot happening, and, aside from Giancarlo, the PCs are happy to be doing some things in the background, chasing after their own interests. This suggests to me that I might "plan out" the potential events of the next day or so ("what happens if the PCs do not interfere"), and see how much of that survives contact with the PCs' actions.

    \n Here are some thoughts on why a player may appear overly cautious. I don't know which ones apply to your situation, but perhaps I mention something that you haven't considered.

    * Some people prefer to be in audience mode and watch the other players and GM play. Even though we all in some way look up to people who take center stage, not everyone wants to be that person. They may be uncomfortable having all eyes on them that judge their moves, and may gain a greater satisfaction from observing others who prefer being the center of attention. You can gently put these players center stage and encourage their contributions however small, but they might never turn into someone who takes the initiative themselves.

    * There's existential pain involved in making choices, and some people don't like that. Some RPGs thrive on putting impossible decision in the player's lap to see what they do, but for some people the pain of making the "wrong" decision (and they may all be wrong from different points of view) is greater than the satisfaction they get from making that choice. Having to decide whether you save your wife or your daughter from the burning building might make a great scene in a film or novel, but may obviously be painful to act out. Other decisions may require your PC to turn into a bad guy, someone who lies, steals or murders, and you may not like to play that character. You have your pure motivations and morals, but suddenly you have to compromise them to get things done in game, which means you rather not do anything at all if the choice is between murder and doing nothing. You can help players like these by picking a game that doesn't require hard choices, or by tuning down the angst and shift focus from "if you pick A you don't get B!" to "do you want to get A or B first?"

    * After playing RPGs for some time, you may have learned that anything you show affection for in game is threatened or taken away by the GM. If you decide to have a daughter in game, she's kidnapped. If you decide to have a horse and wagon in a fantasy game, the horse is shot and killed by an arrow in an ambush and the wagon is burned. Any time you show the GM that something is important to your PC, or to yourself as a player, the GM sees that as something to go after. But if you play coolly, your relationships and stuff are safe. It's like in war movies when they tell the protagonist to not get attached to anyone else, because they might get killed tomorrow and then the pain is greater. This is especially a problem for veteran roleplayers, who have seen this happen before. You can help these players by declaring some fictional stuff off limits, or by giving the player veto power over things they control.

    * The player may be in damage-control mode, seeing GM prompts only as problems to solve and dispose of as quickly as possible and not as a starting point for their own contributions. As GM, you can't force the players to go after something themselves and if they try to play someone who wants to avoid "adventure", they can deflect direct "attacks" and ignore carrots waved in front of them indefinitely. If the fictional situation deteriorates (their daughter is kidnapped or their wagon is destroyed) it's not *their* fault, it's the GM's. Maintaining status quo is a very reasonable approach in real life, but in an RPG the PCs risk becoming non-viable protagonists when order is restored. If their daughter is kidnapped and order is restored by getting her back, this will end the RPG story (or film), which means we have to stop it from happening too soon. If we're making a film, we can pace the story to last 90 minutes, but in an RPG we want a great plan that is successfully implemented to be successful without an artificial time limit. This situation can't be resolved with in-game solutions, as the problem is on a player-and-GM-expectations level. Only by discussing the game and how you want it to play can you (perhaps) move away from just maintaining status quo.

    * This is linked to damage-control mode, but sometimes players may see their responsibility not as searching for conflict and escalating, but as avoiding conflict. Same as maintaining status quo, this is useful when you're part of a group or in the workplace, but doesn't make for very interesting stories. "Ruth always ate my sandwich from the office fridge, so the next day I poisoned my sandwich to get back at her" would be horrible in real life, but interesting in a film. If some or all players always pick the reasonable choice, avoiding intra- and inter-group conflicts, then not a lot will happen. If their daughter is kidnapped, the real-life reasonable choice would be to call the police and just wait, which may not be very interesting. In a story, we want them to chase the kidnappers or otherwise engage with the conflict. If the players see a potential conflict and go for it, the GM's job is a lot easier than if they're trying to avoid conflict. The GM will then spend a lot of time trying to engineer conflict, which the players can dodge by saying "My PC doesn't really care about that thing you threaten" or "My PC complains about their lot in life, but won't do anything about it". Again, a real-life talk about what the actual content of a session should be and how the players and GM can help each other get there is needed to improve the game.

    Perhaps something of the above is relevant to your situation, and can help you identify how to move forward? Do you recognize anything of what I said from the game, or did I miss the point?

    I could guess and pick events that you have described and try to assign a motive, but you know the situation best and it's always easiest to ask the players "Why did you pick A instead of B?" Listen to the in-character motives if the players give them "My PC would never do B!" or "My PC didn't pick B because of this potential consequence", or out-of-character if given "B sounded boring!". The "would never do B" response is obviously interesting, because then you can challenge that belief in game, but that risks disengaging the player if the answer was really "B sounded boring" or "B sounded really disturbing". Always try to get an out-of-character response as well.

    I've had a hard time with this, because many players will *only* cite in-character motives and will claim they themselves had no choice but to stay true to the fictional character they just created. I see choices as decisions on what future events to include and as a way of defining the character, not only presenting a finished character, but I think I'm more of an author than an actor. When a player says "I could only pick A, B was out of the question", I could see it as a failure as GM when I set up the situation (and I usually do). I've learned that some players (and people in general) just see fictional situations in this black-and-white way and it's not much I can do to change it. The best I can do is try to identify and amplify conflicts and dilemmas, but whether they make for a hard choice is out of my hands and with the players, depending on how much they engage with the content.

    \n I'd like to add that I have a preference for audience mode as well, both as a player and GM, so audience-author rather than actor. In impro theater, a basic tenant is to make the other actor look good which really suits me. I like to input stuff, then lean back and observe my co-players be awesome.

    Perhaps this is what the experience roleplayer and game master is trying to do? Perhaps they don't want to steal the spotlight from the newcomers, and instead tries to avoid becoming the center of attention? Since they have their own sense of pacing, you may become baffled when they engage some conflicts and not others without any reasons observable to you?

    I realized that you can also look at this from a group dynamics perspective. In a group you have leaders and followers, but also mediators that resolve conflicts rather than stoke the flames and other roles. Look at this link: https://web.stanford.edu/group/resed/resed/staffresources/RM/training/grouproles.html

    These roles apply both to the players and the PCs. One of the hindering roles are Withdrawing, which may happen at a gaming table, and with suggestions how to handle it. But a lot of the functional roles such as Encouraging, Harmonizing, or Compromizing may at the table look as if the player is not engaging with the conflict, when in reality they are trying to resolve it. You want the players to assume certain roles to drive towards conflict, but without clear instructions to do so, they might default to other group roles.
