    If you decide to release your work under a license that allows more than a standard 'all righst reserved' license - and I am big fan of that, although I can understand that there are reasons not to do it - you have several options: public domain, the various CC-licenses, the GNU Free Documentation License, and of course many others.

    The most important question when choosing between these options is whether you wish to ensure that derivative works based on yours should be as free as your own. That is: do you want to force people to release their derivative works under as free a license as your own?

    If you do not, public domain is a good choice. If you do, the GFDL is a good license, or maybe one of the ShareAlike-versions of the CC-license. The main problem with the CC-licenses are that there are simply too many of them: you cannot combine Attribution-ShareAlike documents with Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike documents and ShareAlike-document, because all of these three demand you to release the result under the license they themselves are licensed under. I consider this a real and grave problem, and the CC-people should either cut down the number of licenses or invent a hierarchy. The GFDL, being widely accepted, might therefore be a better choice.

    Nevertheless, I can definitely understand people preferring the NonCommercial CC-licenses over the GFDL; I am tempted to use one of them myself. So if it is important to you that (a) people can modify your work as they see fit, but (b) only you can actually sell it and make money out of it, then something like the CC Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike-license is worth considering.

    Posted By: Stuart RobertsonThe nice thing about a CC license is the line that says:

    Any of these conditions can be waived if you get permission from the copyright holder.

    Well, while other licenses may not explicitly state that, it remains true regardless of the license -- a copyright holder is free to put different conditions on different people. Indeed, there is software that is released both under the GPL and under other licenses -- e.g., Trolltech's QT libraries are available GPL for those who wish to make GPL applications, but are also available under a commercial license for those who don't want to share their code.

    OGL, GPL, etc. and other such "viral" licenses only become "restrictive" (i.e., you can't release using another license) if you don't own the copyright on everything you're passing on. E.g., if you use parts of the d20 System Reference Document, then your game can only be released OGL (unless you can persuade WotC to let you do otherwise). But if you simply choose to use the OGL for your own original work, then you're free to license it under other terms as well.
    If you had considered the Public Domain, I think the basic Attribution license is better. It's basically the same as public domain, but your name has to be included in the credits of any games built using your contribution. Which I think is very reasonable.
    I tend to agree there -- with a true public domain work, people are legally free to, say, simply replace the author's name with their own, thereby claiming authorship. Indeed, without any solid documentation of when you wrote something, it's possible that someone could take it, replace your name with theirs, and submit it for copyright... then turn around and sue you! (Unlikely, to be sure, but possible.)

    As a final note, although it hasn't come up in this thread, I'd like to point out that the (in)famous "poor man's copyright" of mailing a copy of your work to yourself in a sealed envelope and leaving it sealed holds no legal weight in the US. The Post Office doesn't verify the contents of envelopes, or even that they're sealed, so there's no good way to prove that you didn't just mail yourself an unsealed envelope and put something in there at a later date.

    [Edited to remove errors pointed out by JDCorley below]

    Creative commons and its relatives are the nice, formal ways of telling people 'this stuff is here for people to use, please use.' It is a contractual statement, with use constituting implied agreement to its terms. It's like a permissions agreement you would get from a publisher saying 'you may reuse this material in x place,' except it is pre-emptive.

    You cannot take public domain works and simply attach your name to it. That is not allowed, it's called plagiarism. If you are implying authorship of a work you did not author, you are flirting with fraud and its legal kin. Now, you can release a new edition, call yourself an editor or compiler, etc. and apply for copyright. But you will not get copyright for the public domain work, only your work.

    As for pre-empting someone else's work, well, that is a real sticky thicket of legality. More likely than not, unless they just blatantly cut and pasted you, you are out of luck. Using your mechanics, using a setting like one you had envisioned, but with modifications, will not bring down the wrath of the law. You may be able to bring a case, but unless you have a lot of cash to gain from winning, the cost of pursuing the case will outweigh any gains. And, of course, that assumes you *win* which is definitely not a foregone conclusion.

    Which is why I think Jason's CC-centric approach is very sound. It lets people know that they can re-use the work, but if they expect to turn a profit, they are expected to drop the copyright holder a line and work out a contract. It also protects those people (like fellow indie gamers) who just want to share some mods of the game with their buds on story games.

    The latter may seem like nothing to worry about, but imagine a case where a well-meaning designer gets burned--they see their work being used without recognition and the person doing it refuses to acknowledge it. Said designer, frustrated, hires a lawyer and that lawyer convinces the designer to broaden their case to include non-profit infringements. Sure, you can just stop distributing, but that kind of sucks if you had a wicked cool mod that everyone loved.

    There isn't one, really. The public domain is (Wikipedia):
    WikipediaPublic domain comprises the body of knowledge and innovation (especially creative works such as writing, art, music, and inventions) in relation to which no person or other legal entity can establish or maintain proprietary interests within a particular legal jurisdiction. This body of information and creativity is considered to be part of a common cultural and intellectual heritage, which, in general, anyone may use or exploit, whether for commercial or non-commercial purposes.
    Once you've published something under CC-Attrib, you no longer can establish sole proprietary interest over the work, and neither can anyone else. The only real philisophical difference is the second part: that you feel that your work is simply part of the fabric of the culture and deserves to be returned to that pool of knowledge. In practice there are things (like mathematical formulas) which aren't even attributable because they're trivially (or wholly) derived. Game mechanics have been established as being public domain for just this reason.

    But the nature of the written RPG (to date) is such that there's always an attributable element to it, making it identical to CC-Attrib in practice.

    As long as the legal consequences of "losing rights" are being mentioned, I'll go ahead and say this: here in the U.S., the most important (IMO, and I am not a lawer) fact about IP rights (especially IP, in my eight years of second-hand experience obtained by developing software for the practioners of that branch of law) is that if somebody with more money to spend on lawyers than you wants to fight you over your IP, you will most likely lose your IP. Or maybe you'll win, but become that bitter old man in the years or DECADES it takes you to do so. It sucks, but it is the way such things work in the courts. "Interesting" cases only occur when both sides bring roughly the same amount of money (and/or the same willingness to spend) to the dispute.

    I say this not to discourage people from doing what they think the "right thing" is regarding their IP, but to remind us all that the right thing can and will run smack into practical reality. For what it's worth, I'd be (and am) much more concerned about pissing off my friends and colleagues than about the legal "protection" *any* step would grant me, because that protection is always only as good as the lawyers I hire to back it up. On either side of the coin: I want my work to be "open", someone with a ton of lawyers can always dispute that I had the right to do so; I want to keep my rights, those lawyers can say they were never mine to protect in the first place.

    Just another angle to keep in mind,

      These are the advantages that I can see to releasing into the public domain:

      1. By releasing into the public domain, you give up copyright entirely. No one owns the copyright to the material... and so no one can, frex, release the material to someone else under a different license. Everyone has to play by the same rules.

      2. Since the public domain is not a license, there are no worries about the possibility that the license you choose could someday be found legally invalid.

      3. Since you no longer own the material once you release it into the public domain, there is no need for you to associate your name with it. If you want to release a game anonymously, then, the public domain may be a superior way to go.

      ... and that's all I can think of.
