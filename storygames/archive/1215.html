As I've mentioned previously, I'm using Clinton Nixon's Solar System (as in The Shadow of Yesterday) as the system for my quest game Errantry.

I've gradually become aware that I need to do a complete rewrite on the rules, though. In part this is because Clinton's writing style and mine are very different, and the seams between the two show (he is colloquial and chatty, I'm more formal and concise), but I think there are also opportunities for the rules to be clarified by a new presentation.

I'm not implying any criticism of Clinton or his game here, by the way. I obviously like it a lot or I wouldn't be using it as the basis for my work.

So, some questions for everyone familiar with TSOY (and especially Clinton):

1. If you could add just one thing to the rules, what would it be?

2. If you could remove just one thing, what would it be?

3. If you could change just one thing, what would it be?

4. If you could clarify just one rule, which rule would you pick?

Obviously anything directly to do with Clinton's default setting (the World of Near) is off-topic - this is about the system, for use in a different setting.

I look forward to the collective wisdom.

    What I'm hearing here so far that I haven't yet considered is:

    * Replacing Fudge dice with D6s. I'm not sure about this as it would make a big difference to the mechanics/numbers of the system. I'm inclined to agree with Per that it may not fly, but I'll give it more thought.
    * Not converting XP into Advances. I agree that this is a simplification but it may be a difficult simplification (if you know what I mean).

    I'm adding a couple of mechanics, notably Boons and Obligations. Boons are things you win by beating the characters you encounter in a contest (using BDTP), or convincing them that your quest goal serves their Key. You have to get a certain value in Boons to complete your quest, and narratively this works by using the items/help/information you get as Boons in order to advance towards the goal of your quest.

    Obligations are what you get if the encounter character beats you in BDTP - they're like temporary Keys that give you XP when you fulfil them (instead of when you break them).

    Oh, and I'm thinking of renaming XP as Renown (or possibly Repute).

    So far Boons are in a separate "currency", but I am likely to combine the two.

    OK, so I came up with another idea which uses five ordinary d6s and gives the same outcomes as rolling three Fudge dice, though not quite in the same proportions.

    Two of the dice have a different appearance (size, colour etc.) from the other three and are the "Gate" dice. Between them they define what is a minus, a zero or a plus on the other three dice you roll.

    A minus is any result below the result on the lower Gate die.

    A plus is any result above the result on the higher Gate die.

    A zero is anything between the two Gate dice (inclusive).

    So if you roll, for example, a 2 and a 5 on your Gate dice, 1s are minuses, 2-5 are zeroes and 6s are pluses. If you roll two 4s on the Gate dice, 1-3 is a minus, 4 is a zero, 5-6 is a plus.

    This gives about 50% zeroes (actually about 49%), rather than the 1/3 that Fudge dice give. You can make it almost even if you do a lot of fiddly stuff with "1s and 2s are minuses when they match the low die, 5s and 6s are pluses when they match the high die", but it would be easier then just to say "1-2 is minus, 3-4 is zero, 5-6 is plus" and be done with it.

    I don't know if Gate dice are a useful idea in themselves but they gave me another thought. How about you have two numbers which are "pillars", and they move according to events in the game? If you roll under the low one it's a minus, above the high one it's a plus, in between it's a zero. A bit like the Trollbabe Number idea but with two numbers instead of one.

    If you made them black and white and called them Jakin and Boaz you could use them in a Masonic-themed game, for example.

    OK, I came up with what may be a sensible way of using this for the Solar System. I think it simplifies the die mechanics, but you'd have to try it out and see.

    You have an "injury level" and a "fatigue level". They begin at 1; 1 is good.

    Harm increases injury - your injury level is your highest current "harm" level. It affects all three pools for 1-3 harm, but only the specific pool for 4-6 harm (as per basic Solar System). When harm "shakes out" at the end of Bringing Down the Pain, your injury level will probably drop accordingly.

    Spending pool points increases fatigue. Each pool point below your maximum increases your fatigue by 1. This is always pool-specific, and operates regardless of what your maximum is.

    On each task resolution, you roll 3d6 (or more if you have bonus/penalty dice applicable).

    Any die result that is below both your Injury and your Fatigue (for the pool of the related ability) gives you -1 to your skill.

    Any die result lying between Injury and Fatigue (inclusive) has no effect (0).

    Any die result that is above both Injury and Fatigue gives +1.

    You don't receive penalty dice for harm.

    On the one hand, this increases the consequences of spending pool points considerably. On the other hand, it reduces the number of penalty dice in use, and increases the number of times that the dice rolled give zeroes and so have no effect on the success level - that is, your success level is your ability level. (The average result over the long term is always zero in any case.)
