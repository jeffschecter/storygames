Hey hey, Story-Games!

\n

Playtesting of Black Cadillacs has consistently yielded two questions:

\n

1) Dude, what's with the title?
\n2) Does it matter if we don't know anything about the war we're gaming?

\n

Answering the title one is fun. I enjoy that.

\n

Answering the second has been, for lack of a better word, weird.

\n

I've gamed several wars that I know next-to-nothing about (Viet Nam, Korea, Boer, American Revolution), and the game has stood up remarkably well. However, I'm beginning to think that the book will need some sort of appendix of "useful starting off points" or interesting tidbits about a broad cross-section of historical conflicts.

\n

This is the sort of stuff I mean:

\n

The Battle for Crete (WWII)
\n* The first-ever completely airborne invasion.
\n* The indiginous population was as instrumental (arguably they were moreso) as the Commonwealth (New Zealanders) troops in staging a defence of the island.

\n

The Western Front (WWI)
\n* Late in the war, entire German offensives stalled out because the men were raiding British stores rather than pressing forwards on empty stomachs.
\n* In some battles, casualty rates from falling off of the duckboards and drowning in the mud were not insignificant.

\n

So, my question to you, Story-Games is this: If you were going to game a historical war, which one, and why?

    Hey Everyone,

    \n

    I've been re-treading some of my favourite ground -- the battle for Crete. I'm re-reading Kiriakopoulos' Ten Days to Destiny: The Battle for Crete 1941, and I'm hitting on some excellent setting-nuggets:

    \n
      \n
    • Hitler was more or less ambivalent about attacking Crete or not. The entire operation was essentially a chance for Student's Fallschirmjaeger (paratroopers) to distinguish themselves, and to further his own career.
      • \n
    • Oh, those crazy Germans. Their "lighting war" military doctrine emphasized speed and surprise. Therefore, it's a good idea for your paratroopers to jump with nothing but the lightest weapons. Their heavy gear was sealed up in canisters that were dropped with them. WTF? I'd rather have my shit, thank-you very much!
      • \n
    • A really strong argument can be made for the fact that bothering with Crete was one of many decisions that doomed Barbarossa (invasion of Russia) to failure. The entire transport wing of the Luftwaffe was tied up with Crete, and it delayed the launch of Barabarossa. How close to Moscow did the Germans get before stalling out? That's right, Jimmmy!

    \n I'm from Greece so I'd be cool to base a game on the Greek war of Independence. Especially the siege of Messolonghi.
    ''Messolonghi revolted against the Turks on May 20 1821 and was a major stronghold of the Greek rebels in the Greek War of Independence. Its inhabitants successfully resisted a siege by Ottoman forces in 1822. The second siege started on April 15, 1825 by Kioutachis whose army numbered 30,000 men and was later reinforced by another 10,000 men led by Ibrahim Pasha, son of Muhammad Ali Pasha of Egypt. After a year of relentless enemy attacks and facing starvation, the people of Mesolonghi decided to leave the beleaguered city in the "Exodus of its Guards" (The Sorty) on the night of April 10, 1826. At the time, there were 10,500 people in Messolonghi 3,500 of which were armed. Very few people survived the Ottoman pincer movement after the betrayal of their plan.

    Due to the heroic stance of the population and the subsequent massacre of its inhabitants by the Turkish-Egyptian forces, that followed, the town of Mesolonghi received the honorary title of Hiera Polis (the Sacred City), unique among other Greek cities.''

    The wikipedia entry is pretty blah, but this is one of the most inspiring stories in Greek history. In the end, those with weapons decided to make an exit against an army that outnumbered them 10 to 1, instead of surrendering. They had previously eaten every cat and dog in the city.

    Posted By: Darcy BurgessJoe, you're teasing! Dish a little about thewhy! I'm especially interested in your take on the Easter Uprising, since it seems to have a personal hold on you.
    \nMy apologies.

    The Easter Rising was deeply, deeply unpopular with the vast majority of the country. It was messy. Bloody. And tugged strands of labor, socialist
    and nationalist movements together. In fighting, the labor and socialist movements lost a great deal of credibility. And the leadership was primarily middle-class. So there's a meaty moral question there - what do you give up for a chance at freedom?

    And more pragmatically, it's an urban war. The only substantive action outside Dublin was in a small country town. Most happened in and around the cluttered city streets of the centre of Dublin. So it's guns through broken windows, improvised explosives and chokeholds. Awful, close stuff.

    Hi Dev,

    \n

    The issue of not taking things lightly comes up. A lot.

    \n

    Interestingly enough, the state of being concerned about it seems to be sufficient to prevent it from happening. In playtesting thus far, a lack of knowledge has not resulted in trivializing the important (read: human) stuff that goes on in-game.

    \n

    To distill, what I've begun to believe is the following:

    \n

    1) to trivialize or not to trivialize is a function of social contract
    \n2) ease of situation creation is a function of historical knowledge

    \n

    In hindsight, those points are hardly surprising. However, your comments resemble ones I made about a year ago; I had just started putting the game on its feet.

    Hi Dev,
    \nOk, I see where you're coming from now. That makes a lot of sense, and I think that I've got your ass covered in that regard. Here's why:

    \n
      \n
    • let's assume that you're playing with 2-3 other folks, and that they don't hold socio-political views that are carbon copies of your own. If you are in fact Jango Fett, and so are your mates, then you're probably screwed.
      • \n
    • Item: Although the system assigns stewardship over the protags to the players, it does not assign ownership. This is hardly groudbreaking stuff in the land of indie rpgs, but it's critical. There's no "my guy" going on. It's strictly troupe play, and when the system assigns speaking rights to a given participant, they hold the mighty directorial power stick over everything.
      • \n
    • Narrative constraints are essentially twofold: 1) The "sniff test" - does it make sense given what's gone before? 2) There are thematic and win/lose constraints derived from the fortune-based resolution.
      • \n
    \n

    When those three points are couple with the fact that play essentially proceeds in a reasonable facsimile of "around the table", each given player is never in a position of privileged authorship long enough to run away with the evolving situation. You're constantly responding to the last player, as well as to the system. It's really, really hard to put "the overwhelming stamp of Dev" on the game.

    \n

    And the really cool part is that even if you do (stamp your opinion all over the game), there's a check and balance for that too. But we can talk about that next if you like. However, I'd like to pause here and give you a chance to respond.

    Hi Dev,
    \nCool beans! Checks and balances? Sure!

    \n

    In fact, this is the part of the game that excites me the most. And what's important to note in the context of your question is that this mechanic isn't trotted out as a check/balance for the issue you've raised; rather, it happens regardless of what you're worried about. It also happens to act as a check and balance for that kind of behaviour.

    \n

    At the end of each session, each particpant chooses one moment of the fiction that's just been roleplayed. The important criteria here is that the moment moved or touched them as a human being.

    \n

    They then re-tell that moment in whatever format (I've seen letters home, after-action reports, photographs, etc) they choose. In the re-telling, they are not allowed to simply regurgitate the moment; the moment must be changed in some way (the degree of change is at the discretion of the teller).

    \n

    In practice, this has provoked some wonderful moments of drama as well as some hard-handed commentary. I guess that my question to you is, do you see how this could be used to also pull an errant player in-line?
