\n So, in another thread we were talking about game texts that fail to tell you how to play the game. I brought up my "minimum bar" which is that a game text should tell you what the roles, goals and agenda of each of the players is. I mentioned, for example, that Burning Empires meets my minimum bar because it plainly tells the players that they should try to win the Infection mechanic and try to fulfill their Beliefs and that the GM should try to win the Infection mechanic and should challenge the player's Beliefs. It's also clear that the text is confident that the procedures in the text, "just work" and that while adjudicating the rules is part of the GM's job, changing or breaking them is not.

I went on to point out that the text of Burning Empires doesn't go any further than that. It doesn't for example offer much on strategy for winning the Infection mechanic. It doesn't really talk about what exactly "challenging a Belief" means. It seemed to me that much of the discussion in that thread was turning towards texts that move beyond my minimum bar. I offered to talk about why I thought moving beyond that minimum bar is so difficult, especially for more narrative focused games like Sorcerer or Dogs in the Vineyard. There was interest so here it goes.

The "how" people are talking about is basically the equivalent of a strategy guide for a board game or video game. Most of those games tell you what to do and why you're doing it but don't tell you HOW to do it most effectively. They, rightfully I believe, leave that up for discovery via play and practice. You play a game, it works out okay but it peaks your further interest. You play AGAIN and discover more about the game and consequently like it a bit more. You keep playing and learn more and more and slowly you build up the skill and knowledge to master the game.

Something I want to point out is that a lot of strategy guides work on patterns. Even master class chess guides are largely about patterns. I remember playing the Icewind Dale computer RPG and out of curiosity I happened to be using the strategy guide. It had an example party and the odd thing about it was that it recommended THREE fighters ALL specialized in bows. I thought that was weird, until I tried it. Holy CRAP! NOTHING, and I mean NOTHING got within 5 feet of my party. I was a walking machine gun of death. But here's the thing, the guide still didn't tell me HOW they figured that out.

Even when you're dealing with something fairly discrete and mathematical explaining how you get a certain result is really hard. Making the jump to role-playing games most D&D strategy advice is about recognizing optimal build patterns. Making the jump to more narrative focused games the problem is even worse. How do you recognize a conflict (and not merely a lot of excited talking)? What makes for good Stakes in stake setting games? How do you recognize good moments for the characters take action (this includes NPCs for the GM). What's a "minor" vs. "major" compromise in a Duel of Wits?

The truth is, it's easy to teach someone how to read music and play an intrument. It's nigh impossible to teach them how to compose music from scratch. At that point you're making the jump from mechanical musical instruction to musical composition theory. First of all, aesthetics is partially personal (some may argue completely personal). There is absolutely no way to craft a piece of music that is going to hit the exact same emotional chord in every person who hears it. It is impossible. The best you can do is shoot for some basic principles and all that will do is get you something that isn't completely discordant.

I've tried to write the "strategy guide" to Sorcerer more than once. I call the project Sorcerer Unbound and it is my white whale. You can see the essay version of some of the content on my website. I have two or three half-complete drafts on my computer. Every damn time I try I run into these walls where I'm really no longer talking about Sorcerer in specific but rather the more fundamental principles (and perhaps assumptions) of dramatic writing it's based on.

Which is why I was really glad that Christopher Kubasik was taking up the mantle with his Play Sorcerer book. He's a professional screenwriter. He knows the princples of craft better than I can ever hope to know. Better yet, he has the self-discipline and focus to see a personal project through that I lack. I assumed he'd be done in under a year.

He's still working on it. This is partially due to the fact that his career took an unexpected and much deserved upswing. But it's also partially due to the fact that the book is nigh impossible to write. He and I have exchanged numerous emails and even had a couple of face-to-face meetings where we've talked at length about this and each and every time we come up with a NEW set of issues that really should be addressed. Christopher says things like, "Well, that's another chapter." Or "Hmmm... that's another 10 pages."

My original goal for Sorcerer Unbound was 32 to 64 pages. Little more than a "missing" chapter from the core book. Christopher, I think, was shooting for somewhere around 128 pages. And both cases the project has overgrown and overflown the original scope.

Going beyond minimum instruction: "Do this." is just really hard and actually aquiring the know how through basic practice and repetition really is much easier. But for basic practice and repetition to work the game has to have a focused application to begin with, so you can recognize when you're getting better at it. That's why it's so frustrating when a game text doesn't even meet my bar for minimum instruction.

    \n I think there's probably a lot of value in finding that perfect balance between explaining a game's procedures and thoroughly deconstructing their application.

    Example: I think one of the reasons the Burning Wheel scene is comprised of such strong advocates is that it takes a lot of experimenting to figure out how the game works. IME you need to play just tons and tons, talk about playing, read about other people playing, and just generally commit yourself to a lot of extracurricular activity. The Adventure Burner goes a long way toward encapsulating that information for BW, but it took five years of ongoing discussion before anyone figured out the game's best practices. But all those fans -- and I count myself among the hardest of the hardcore BW crew here -- have a profound investment in the game that probably would not have arisen if the AdBu stuff had been included in the original text.

    I think the same thing happens a lot with Vincent Baker's games, which are designed to create all sorts of emergent behaviors based on (perhaps) inobvious inputs and decisions. If he just came out and *said* how he intended, say, escalation to work in DitV (not just mechanically but dramatically and emotionally), then that conversation would probably not have taken place over many months and years, and fans of the game probably would not be so deeply invested.

    And the D&D hardcores, of course, have generated millions of words of analysis, looking at mechanical synergies as well as huge, epic discussions amongst various factions of players as to how best just run the damned thing. D&D would be a very different phenomenon if it had been built around specific procedures, like Apocalypse World. When you DIY, your investment level skyrockets.

    I guess I'm not sure that an RPG *should* go beyond its minimum instruction. Or that, like the fruitful void left behind by what the mechanics don't address, there's probably a fruitful void left behind by what the game's instructional text doesn't address.

    \n I think one of the issues folks run into with this is a similar issue to teaching writing.

    It is impossible to teach someone to be a good writer. Or next to it. The only way someone becomes a good writer is to write and write and write and probably also read and read and read and probably also have some talent for it.

    However, at the same time, you can teach people all sorts of things about writing. You can, for example, lay out how to write a spectacularly terrible but probably entertaining pulp novel. Or you can lay out the rules of formal structure and the application of Aristotelian plot.

    So when we're talking about telling people how to play a game I think we need to find the gap between "I can teach you how to write a cut and paste pulp novel" and "this game must teach you how to write Midnight's Children in order to be a good teaching text." Mostly because I have a feeling people sometimes look at the later, realize the impossibility of it, and so don't even try to do the former -- which is quite possible.

    \n It seems to me there are rules - bare bones procedures that you do again and again in the game - that can be summed up pretty quickly.

    For example: In D+D - The game master describes a situation - The players say what their characters do - The GM may have them roll a twenty sided die to see if their action happens or not - The GM says what the bad guys do.

    That pretty well sums up the basics of play but it absolutely doesn't tell you what you have to do and leaves continents worth of room to explore emergent play.

    I like the idea of using sidebars to explain things more. I've included the following side bar in my most recent set of Matrix game rules (now up on my web page http://hamsterpress.net ) It points people to the guidance the game materials give on what to do next. But I find in the PBEM I'm running now that I should tell people to do more in a scene than one thing. They are rivaling for the affections of a beautiful peasant babe and not moving closer to the space ship of the bad guys - It's a Star Trek game).

    SIDE BAR \u201cWhat should I make happen?\u201d

    It is sometimes hard to decide what to do. There are so many possibilities. To help people out, Matrix games includes materials meant to inspire players. The map shows locations like for instance a mysterious mansion \u2013 what should happen there? Locations have suggestions of what might be encountered there \u2013 a threatening house keeper. Maybe there is an overt question \u2013 What clue do you find? Character sheets describe people and their motives \u2013 how do you carry out their plans? Character sheets also list skills they have \u2013 what happens when they use them? Some games will have a plot track that suggests the sequence of events of a given type of story \u2013 so you find clues before arresting a suspect. The story opener tells players the general situation which begs the question \u2013 what happens next? The same question comes up after each turn when one or more scenes become facts. The game materials include lots of pictures that also can inspire action. The important thing is to pick something to inspire you, look at it and then make up a scene based on what pops into your head.

    Chris Engle

    Feedback on my sidebar is welcome - and greatly desired!

    Posted By: JesseHow many layers do you personally expect the author/designer to go?
    \nI think this is a key question. Sure the author can explain his game to you, but how much is actually required for productive play? And how much is too much. It is possible to overwhelm a new reader so much so that he actually ends up confused.\n
    My main point, was simply that going from core rule to "how to" elucidation IS a separate step from either design or raw rules presentation.
    \nYes. I agree -- to a point.

    On the other hand, most games, in actual play, break down into a certain set of procedures and patterns. You might have to look at play from a top-down, big picture point of view, but, in a successful game, the players are repeating certain general techniques. I personally think that we, as game designers, need to become stronger in identifying and explaining those patters in our game texts.

    Let me give an example. In Hero's Banner, when I run the game, I follow a pattern. I take each player in turn, examine his character's Influences, and frame a strong scene loaded with potential conflict based on one or two of those Influences. We play though the scene. I try to push towards one of the conflicts that I had in mind when I framed the scene. We arrive at a conflict, resolve it, and take care of any fallout afterwards. Scene ends. Go to the next player. Repeat.

    Yes, there are times when I break this pattern. In general, however, this is the procedure I use for successful HB play. Now, does the HB book adequately explain this procedure? No. The book isn't terrible. It sort of explains around the procedures needed to play. But I think we need more procedural explanations in our game texts. If we focus on clearing up procedural confusion, many of the problems surrounding emergent behaviors will go away. It's one of the things I attempted to do better in Mars Colony.

    Explaining how to play a narrativist game is difficult because we are attempting to explain how to create theme and art within certain system boundaries, but it is possible if we look at things procedurally. I will not, for example, be held responsible for the success or failure of the art another group creates with my game so long as they are playing the game according to the procedures I lay out. As designer, I have to be able to expect a certain level of creative effort from potential players (which includes emergent behaviors to a certain extent). I will take responsibility if the game procedures are incomplete or the game itself is just a bad set of rules for creating the themes it promises.

    \n Sounds like it, Jason. And you can see then why it might be useful for Player A, it would seem like bloat to Player B.

    Here's one way I'm trying to handle the tastes of both players. There is a section explicitly called out as non-procedural "advice" text. It is set apart from "instruction" text by a variety of layout conventions (smaller font size, leading, number of columns, etc.).\n
    Example: You're in a part of the book where there are are a number of branching options, some of which will call on you to describe a scene. Rather than repeat the same advice in each branch, there is a preface titled "How to Describe a Scene." It is set in an 8pt sans serif while the main instructional text is set in 11pt serif.
    \nThis is distinct from a typical sidebar because the content isn't quite so tangential to the flow of reading. It's something that is just as important and deserves as much attention to the instruction, but can be visually skipped for those readers already familiar with those details and are now only using the text for reference.\n
    Example: When you were first learning the game and reading the book, you read the instructional text and the advice text as a single continuous flow. When you go back for reference, you can easily skip the advice because it is so visually distinct.
    \nThat advice text is split up into a number of subsections concerning particular issues that frequently come up in play. Sections about long-term strategies, when it is appropriate to choose Door 1 or Door 2, optimal decisions to make here or there, and all supported with a peek under the hood of the game system.\n
    Example: "How to Describe a Scene" is split up into sub-sections like "Describing a Scene Using the Five Senses" "How to Build on Another Player's Contributions" and "When You Should Save or Spend Plot Points."
    \nNot all this advice is relevant in all branching paths of play, but it is relevant enough that it's useful to have it available all in one place. That advice established, you can continue into the branching paths of instruction and back-reference to specific advice where necessary. This method also allows enough space to write advice text that really is only relevant to branching path 1, 2, or 3, without burdening Branch 1 with advice that is only relevant in Branch 3.\n
    Example: You're reading a later part of the book concerning one particular branch of play procedure. The instruction says "Describe a scene where the antagonist and your character have a heated argument." The advice section calls back to "How to Build on Another Player's Contributions" and "When You Should Save or Spend Plot Points." It does not call back to "Describing a Scene Using the Five Senses" because it is not especially relevant in this part of play. Furthermore, this part of the book has very specific advice like "How Antagonists Argue" and "In Case Things Turn Violent."
    \nHaving general advice in one place also allows you to work back references into an example of play.\n
    Example: Ted is describing his character Tarak getting into a heated argument with Lord Bonecrusher. He's having a little bit of creative block, so he refers back to the advice on page XX. Specifically, he's having trouble deciding the nature of the argument without it turning violent. He sees in "How Antagonists Argue" that the antagonist may have a subordinate do the arguing. That subordinate doesn't have the authority to start a fight, but may shout and gnash teeth viciously. So, Ted describes Lord Bonecrusher leaning back in his throne, shrouded by shadows while his lackey Squick Anklebiter rattles his saber at Tarak.
    \nThis is one way we might be able to reconcile the amount of space required to go beyond minimum instruction while still being economical with our word count and page count.

    \n Piers,

    No need to apologize. I'm not that easily offended! And I appreciate your response. What I'm ultimately trying to get at is this: when does a minimalist game text work, and when is more explanation needed if at all?

    During personal and opposition scenes, Mars Colony relies exclusively on drama resolution, which pretty much means that the players have to agree on how the whole scene works out. I can see how that could cause some head-scratching when you read the book. In practice, I personally find that it works better than expected, especially in a two-player setting.

    To bring this back around to the topic at hand, I will say that, from the author's point of view, it's difficult to know what sort of mechanics the potential reader will be familiar with and what will require extra explanation. Even if I do provide lots of play examples, etc., I still have to be aware enough to include examples of the "correct" concepts.

    \n Tim's right that the example I posted above is definitely not the only way to do things. It's just one solution to the persistent problem of trying to appeal to different audiences. As elegant as your game rules might be, there are a lot of emergent properties you could discuss about it. In doing so, you increase the number of words you're putting in a player's eyeballs before they get a chance to play.

    I feel like I'm presenting a false dichotomy between "casual player who won't read anything" and "studious player who wants to know a game entirely before playing." Perhaps I am, but only for the sake of discussion. Everyone sits along a different point in the spectrum between those two poles. As a game designer and game writer (and a game layout-er) you have to decide at a certain point whether you want to devote ink and paper to appeal 1%-50% of that spectrum.

    Surely there is some reasonable way of saying, "Look, this subject is getting way too specific to spend money putting in print. Let's take this online, where text is free."

    It sounds like a copout in a book to say "See more discussion on dual-wielding folding chairs on Blog X" or "Visit Forum Y to ask any questions about enchanting hammers." Am I wrong? :P

    \n Daniel,

    Briefly, and I think we might actually be on the same page here, but, just to be sure, I think the question isn't always about cost to print. Rather it's about making a conscious choice as an author to present more or less text. Frankly, if I thought it would help, I'd spend the money to print a bigger book regardless of how small-time I am. Publishing less text is actually more nerve-wracking because there is an expectation that rpg books be long. There is also less room for error. If I'm doing less text, it's because I think it's the best way to present the game in question.

    \n My approach seems to cut right through at least one problem: reference.

    GLASS is going to be BIG: multiple examples of every Attribute, Ability, Modifier, and Play Rule (Option/Toggle); sidebars explaining the likely emergent play (or "tone") of rule choices, all of which are optional/modular; at least three and maybe as much as six ready-to-play Game Types (sets of Play Rules, some Packages and Templates, some setting "fluff"); and a whole section on advice for running a LARP in most environs/venues, prop and set making, promoting, logistics, bookkeeping and acting (viz CoC's LARP rules for the best such section I've ever seen). This book will be fat, and it will be pricey as a result (I estimate $50+).

    shotGLASS will be the same book, BUT with anything not a literal, use-this-this-way rule hidden using conditional text. Purely reference, for costs, verbals, effects, and basic math. Enough to actually play, though... if you "put in the work" to build a Game Type, know about LARPing in general and "boffer/Airsoft" LARPs in particular, and have had some exposure to HERO System or similar modular and highly configurable games. It will be a free PDF. (And it's basically already "done" with my System Test Document Gold Master Candidate.)

    I figure GM and grognards will buy the book, with the VAST majority of players just getting by with shotGLASS.

    And, yes, I will still have forums for follow-on advice and community, AND a Game Type and Character Builder database (which I might end up monetizing, if it becomes too pricey to host). So there's all that support post-read, pre-play AND there's portable reference for use in-game AND there's more advice, examples, and explicit procedural spelling out than you can shake a stick at, if you pony up. Seems the best strategy (and, no, not all that unique to me or GLASS).
