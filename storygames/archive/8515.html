    \n Wasn't this conceit a part of the recent Murderland design challenge Jonathan Walton cooked up? It had something like 30 submissions, should keep one occupied for a moment.

    My own game for the challenge handled the lack of character sheets by using dice to keep track of pertinent data, and by embedding non-numeric stuff in the fiction of the game in such a manner that it would only need to be referenced to the same exact amount of detail that any other fictional stuff would be - in other words, if you forget it then it wasn't important to begin with. The game also uses print-outs and one look-up table.

    \n I did a game called Forbidden City, which had coloured dice in bowls, in the centre of the table.

    When you narrated specific things, you'd take a die: for example, if you narrated showing your moral superiority, you'd take a transparent die. If you narrated needless violence, you'd take a red die. Then, in conflicts, you rolled the dice in your hand, and the different colours represented your attributes.

    A Taste For Murder does something similar. It does have a character sheet, but it's just a repository for trading dice, back and forth. Win a conflict, steal a die from someone else.

    Graham

    \n Hey. I must be stupid to have not realized it immediately, but my own, real, published and not at all sketchily theoretical Zombie Cinema very well doesn't have character sheets. So with Bacchanal we have at least two finished games like that. Sometimes the future gallops with such immense speed that all this stuff discussed on the forums gets behind. This isn't even the first thread here that I've found to consider something that people are already very well doing all over. The funny thing is that for some reason I never seem to remember that "I wrote a game like that once" myself. Must be because the game doesn't in fact seem all that strange and exceptional after it's been written, character sheets or not. Because I didn't specifically try to get rid of the character sheet when designing, I never internalized the idea that my game belongs in the "no character sheet" category.

    \n Several of the games I've designed (Land of Nodd, Musette, a couple of others) have a "character sheet". It's a piece of paper with information about all the characters, kind of listing all the folks involved in the story. So, it's just one piece of paper, with all the information for the game right on it. The characters also do not have any "stats" or numerical components--just their names and some stuff about them, like a relationship they have or something they want. I don't know if that qualifies those designs as "no character sheet" games or not.

    I really like this approach, compared to individual character sheets, because we can all look at it at the same time and see what's involved in play all at once.
