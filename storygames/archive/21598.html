    \n The Forgite term you're looking for is "task resolution", by the way. "Action resolution" sounds like a synonym to me, but it's clearly not the typical term for the concept.

    There's a wide variety of terms to select from, depending on what precisely you're talking about. I have one that I use regularly and that means almost precisely the thing you said:

    Traditional or "trad" roleplaying games are those with direct historical influence from the second wave rpgs of the mid-'80s. It's a concept of art history characterizing a school or style of design, so it's more perceived as a cluster of traits than a binary. (It's not defined by those traits, either - the definition is basically that your game is trad if you made it within the cultural context of the movement, following its stylistic trend.) The traits you listed are all typical of traditional games, among others.

    The hick-up with this term for some purposes is that it's not directly analytical in nature, you need to understand the game's design history to use it - we say that a game is "traditional" because it predominately belongs in this tradition or school of design and not because it hits 10/10 from a list of requirements. Therefore some traditional games don't have e.g. task resolution, and we still call them trad because they're demonstrably created within this historical movement, and have most of the other qualities typical of trad games.

    If that doesn't bother you, though, and you specifically want to talk about the historical school/movement/tradition of rpg design that "all those '90s games" belong to, then I think trad is a great term. You can even zoom into sub-currents and eras with various modifiers; for instance, I personally like to speak of "early trad" encompassing Dragonlance to Ars Magica, "late trad" from Vampire to the OGL era, and most recently "neo-trad" for the flavour of design that Internet era traditional games have become.

    \n Also, Emma's not alone in wanting to say "old school", I've encountered that phenomenon before: the pre-eminent Finnish designer Ville Vuorela likes to call his trad style of tabletop rpg "old skool". (This obviously made more sense before the old school renaissance.) He picked the name in the mid-'00s out of a hat specifically to contrast with Forgite progressive design; he perceives himself as continuing in the predominant tradition, making games that are normative and "the way roleplaying games should be".

    Overall it's not a terminological habit I like to encourage, though, to call both early D&D influenced refereed wargame style games and '80s style GM-story-with-complex-rules games with the same name. "Trad" has been around since the early days of the Forge, and it doesn't involve any major confusion so far that I know of - people usually tend to understand what kind of games one talks about when discussing "trad games".

    \n Whatever you decide, it needs to function well as adjectival modifier, because that's how it will be used fairly often--compounded with "game", eg. [viking hat game], [railroading game]. Nouns can function as modifiers in compounds perfectly well, but some abstract noun suffixes, like {-ism} seem to be disprefered, and so probably won't be picked up readily by the gaming or game design community. Concrete nouns like [hat] in [viking hat] work and so do abstract nouns with {-ist}, and gerunds using {-ing}. There are others that work, of course.

    If you want to use [auteurism], people would probably transform it to [auteuristic] when compounded with [game]. It suits the morphonotatic rules of English compounds better.

    This is just a (trifling) thought to consider, if you want your neologism to catch-on.

    I'm down a language rabbit hole, now, all my life, forever.

    \n It does make certain sense to not call old school D&D "trad" despite it being arguably the oldest major school of tabletop roleplaying. I mean, is it a tradition in the sense of having been passed down as an influential form through several generations of designers? Really - it's not rather something invented in 2008? And if it it is, is it the most influential tradition in the rpg-dom, or might there perhaps be another, much more popular style independent from old school that has a longer undisrupted pedigee?

    (Which reminds me: I am entirely happy with calling "trad" style games simply "mainstream", although this is obviously just a snapshot judgement in a given time and place. At this writing trad style games are the mainstream in sociological sense, so the two words refer to the same style of games.)

    Of course old school D&D is the oldest, but it's also something that may justifiably be called a revived playstyle. While there are individual groups out there who have played in this style continuously, and obviously the "trad" style itself emerged in response and interaction to the old school, the fact remains that there existed a long era in roleplaying when the central precepts of the old school were not acknowledged and the playstyle was only rarely recognized as something real (rather than an outdated atavism exercised by the children and the simple-minded). In that sense, while old school may be a tradition, it's not The Tradition in any sense that the majority of roleplayers would recognize.

    (I would also argue that both of the two other major schools of tabletop roleplaying, freeform and Forgite formalism - or whatever you like to call it, "indie games" - are at this point traditions in the sociological sense, on top of being movements and (at least in the Forge's case) artistic schools. Both involve plenty of newer gamers and designers who operate within those schools as their natural context. How the time flies...)

    \n Also, a clarification: I am aware of there existing a demography of gamers who do not recognize the old school / trad distinction as being real. These are generally older gamers who self-identify as old schoolers, yet mostly play with game texts generally considered traditional, yet do not consider the differences significant. So it's not like it's a generally accepted truth.

    (Hey, have we lost @JDCorley somewhere? He used to be notorious for holding that Forgite indie games are essentially similar to trad games and that there hasn't been a significant break in the tradition there. I guess no matter how you break down the variety of rpgs in cultural history terms, somebody will disagree.)

    But then again, most of the really vocal OSR people seem to very strongly hold to the idea that the old school is very different, operating under fundamentally different assumptions than mainstream modern games (trad style, that is). I like to at least give credence to people's subjective perception in matters of art history, so if OSR hobbyists generally consider themselves an artistic movement or a school of rpg design or a distinctive type of roleplaying, then by all means. I see no difficulty, OSR is certainly more distinctive than most movements considered in the history of the arts.

    I\u2019m OK with calling it \u201ctrad\u201d actually iff there is some consensus around that. (If not, there\u2019s just gonna be more confusion.) I don\u2019t really use the word trad for anything else so it\u2019s free to assign to this. Or, well, I kinda use trad as a superset/union of both 90:s-style games and old school sandbox games. I.e. task resolution games with a GM/PC split, whether linear or sandbox.

    For me the important ingredient of a 90:s-style game is a lack of alternative to the linear plot with force techniques. Like a lack of info on prepping place/scenario/characters, a lack of info on how to actually prep (except you\u2019d sometimes see flowcharts and other \u201clinear\u201d or \u201cnode graph\u201d:y setups) overall. And modules that were super linear. For a game to qualify as 90:s style, it doesn\u2019t have to have the force techniques in the GM\u2019s material (although that definitely instantly qualifies it).

    And my theory has been that people that grew up with like B2 and B4 for D&D, they knew how to prep a city mission for a 90:s-style game, so they didn\u2019t see any problems with the games. And those who were into the linear force techniquery, they were golden as well. Like, the German game DSA have some of the most linear modules of all time but they also have super detailed world books for when things go off the rails, and maybe they were onto something.

I particularly like to discuss monolithic One True Way-ism as a definitive cultural feature of the traditional rpg culture. I don't mean here the forum debate behavior where people accuse each other of playing wrong because they play different, but rather a much more internalized, deep form of the same: the true follower of this school of roleplaying genuinely believes that all roleplaying games are the same in their essence, and there is actually a wide swatch of generalizable rpg theory to be had.     \n@Jeff_Slater I was only half-joking. Experimental archaeology was one of the "new archaeologies" that came out of the desire to make archaeology more scientific and less art history. It was a trend in the late-50s, 60s and early-70s. The same thing happened in linguistics and anthropology at roughly the same time--probably other social sciences as well. Anyway, experimental archaeologist create "artifacts", like weapons, from authentic materials using authentic methods (if they are known) and then test them. I used to know this experimental archaeologist who could recreate a perfect facimilie of a paleolithic spear point in about 10 minutes with the end of a reindeer antler and the right kind of chert. Perhaps you've heard of the archaeologists who brewed Sumerian beer from a 6000 year old recipe. In much the same way that myth-busters tests lots of `myths' and urban legends. Experimental archaeologists collect data on weapons effectiveness (your two-handed sword comment triggered me) by, for example, shooting a recreation of an arbalest at a donkey carcass and measuring the penetration of different kinds of bolts, or building a trebuchet and trying to knock down a recreated castle wall. I honestly think that the birth of modern wargames (1960/70s) with its emphasis on granularity and physic sim owes a lot to that general trend in the social sciences, espcially experimental archaeology. So there is a geneology: experimental archaeology-->(miniatures) wargames (like Chainmail)-->OD&D-->modern sim RPGS.

      \n For myself, a major defining feature of "traditional play culture" is, as Eero nicely describes above, the idea that there is One Way to Roleplay, and we're all just hoping to get closer and closer to it.

      "Traditionalists", in this sense, are comfortable giving overarching advice on "Good GMing" or "adventure design" or "dice pools are better than linear rolls". For instance, there is the Lawsian idea that the GM's job is to please the players, and, therefore, figuring out what "type" each player is and then making sure they get some of that in each adventure is the mark of a great GM.

      The more "modern" school, influenced by Forge thinking, denies that attitude and tries to figure out what each game needs and how it should be played. It's the players' job to get on the same page and play the game together. It's a very different approach to thinking about roleplaying!

      Some people in the "traditional" school of thinking about RPGs don't recognize differences between RPGs as meaningful (like our beloved JDCorley), while others draw hard lines, and any games which don't fit their idea of "what roleplaying is like" are dismissed as "not roleplaying".

      For example:

      "That's not a real roleplaying game! It doesn't have rules for combat!"

      \n My recollection of the games of the later 1980s/1990s was that they loved mechanical confrontation. They were full of rules and rolls and were about confronting the GM's world and beating it rather than playing along within it.

      I am talking of games like Aftermath, AD&D (of course), Space Opera, Phoenix Command, GURPS, Hero system and so on. By confrontational, I mean that each problem required a roll on a skill of attribute to solve, even if the player was deeply into his narrative of the situation at the same time.

      Naturally, any of the listed games could be played more openly and interactively, but my experience and memory of the games I played was that they were definitely mechanics-driven.

      My suggestions, therefore fall into the 'Mechanics-driven', 'Mechanics/Confrontation' style of descriptor. I don't see such descriptors as pejorative and would be a fool if I did, because GURPS is my go-to system for gaming.

      \n I never played them so I can't comment. I'm commenting on the games I played so I can't/won't comment in depth on ones I am not familiar enough with.       It is significant that games like Fudge moved the goalposts and arguably set the foundations for narrative games. There was also the White Wolf series which further encouraged storytelling over mechanics but, again, I never played.

      The 1990s was probably a very significant time of change for RPGs. The buds of freeform storytelling were there but the era was dominated by rules-heavy mechanical systems and the inevitable AD&D (also rules-heavy, of course). Like most generalisations, plenty of exceptions exist but they are in minority.
