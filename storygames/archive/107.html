    In my ongoing Truth and Justice game there are several characters that became marque characters durring play, but none of them existed before the game started.

    The first thing we did when playing the game was make the PCs and their city, then looked at the "supers issues" they wanted to deal with and started off with them giving a couple of suggestions for NPCs that they might like and the types of villains they might like to face.

    I then went and stole lots of characters from other RPGs. This "stealing" mostly involved me finding a cool picture of the character, glancing briefly at what the character was set up to be, and then remolding it into the game. Following that mode we ended up with the following marquee NPCs:

    Alice, The Queen of Hearts = Stolen from SAS. Amusing because she's almost the evil twin of one of the PCs, and just happens to be sexually attracted to said PC. She's also the marquee type of every roguish mercenary ass-kicking gun-toting feme fatal in comic book history, with obvious and specific nods to Zealot and Silver Sable. Those with more knowledge of comic history can tell me where those characters came from.

    Protonic = Stolen from M&M, redone so that he really is Superman without any of the Superman canon. He's become a favorite of the PCs because he lets them get into the "what makes a hero" and "what is our responsibility to the world" issues in ways that put them very visibly and directly in line with him, or directly opposed to him. And really, if you want to take on the iconic image of the superhero, its hard to beat Superman for it.

    Gaius Janus = Who started off as Janus from SAS, then became Lionell Luther from Smallville, then was turned to the side of the angels by the PCs, then became a god, then died, and is now back from the dead. He's really a slightly sexier, updated Lex Luthor who walks a line that's far more grey than black in response to the fact that many of our players are corporate managers and scientists, and so have a slightly different take on the "evil corporation and mad scientist" issue.

    The Dark Prince = A Brain in a Jar, as all brains in all jars. Except that this guy is a dolphin brain. Yes, a dolphin brain in a jar. He was made to be a one off to let us have some goofy DOLPHIN BRAIN IN A JAR TRYING TO MELT THE POLAR ICECAPS action, and instead he became one of the most formidable and compelling villains in the entire game. He is, at the structural level, quite clearly become Magneto. (In fact the players refere to him as "Magneto-dolphin-brain" and "The disembodied dolphin Jesus Christ in a hover mobile.")

    Atavist = Conan the Freaking Barbarian meets the Increadible Hulk in the universe of the X-Men. The boyfriend of the sexy femme PC felt inadiquate, got exposed to radiation at the lab he worked at, and gained the ability to change into a manly-barbarian warrior of unstopable strength whenever he felt jealous or helpless.

    I could go on, but I think the point is made. All of my NPCs can be traced pretty clearly to pictures and iconic images taken from supers RPGs, who were in turn taken from big name characters in comics. However, by putting the PCs first and building to suit them and the themes the group likes we've managed to combine a lot of iconic images with a lot of personal connection.

    So, I think my short form answer to Andy's question would be: I don't know if you can build a generic setting that will fit that well -- but you can take elements from settings and reimage them into your games pretty well. Really, its not too different than Marvel's "Ultimate" line.

    Chad,

    There are a couple reasons to do it as a publisher doing a precanned setting.

    1) Iconic images and cliche. If you have an ersatz Superman then everyone knows going in what that character is about, what to expect from them in a general way, and how to use their relationship to the character to push their story.

    2) Because I want to play that world and don't have the liscence. Let us not forget that one of the big draws of the Marvel games of all types has been the ability to play Rogue or Spiderman and to make out with Storm and date Silver Sable. So what to do it you don't have the legal rights to it? Make it close enough that the urge can be satisfied without violating copyright.

    3) It's easier. You can make up your own themes, or you can draw on 75 years of the living Superman mythology that permeates every aspect of American popular culture.

    4) We're geeks.

    Are ersatz versions of Big Name Supers necessary to garner attention? If so, why? If not, why do comics companies and RPG companies do it?

    Well, honestly, to show that their heroes could beat up the other guys heroes, especially before the big money in crossovers was found.

    Squadron Supreme (which has been re-imagined as Supreme Power) existed pretty much solely so the Avengers could fight the Justice League (Which lead to one of the few good points of the recent JLA/Avengers crossover, when Hawkeye complains that these guys are just a Squadron Supreme knockoff). Similarly, the Shi'ar Imperial Guard seem pretty much created so the X-men could fight the Legion of Super heroes.

    Beyond that, there's obviously something to be said for using familiarity to illustrate something new (as Supreme Power illustrates). Note that comic companies are even doing it to themselves with things like DC's else-worlds and Marvel's Ultimates. Ripping off something iconic lets you take all the good parts (specifically those that make it iconic) without the cruft (White Kryptonite kills plants? WTF?!?!).

    I think there's a bit of a bias in perception here. Actually, there are tons of comic book universes which don't have those characters. Prince Valiant, Tintin, Asterix, Finder, Strangers in Paradise, and so forth on endlessly.

    But for something to be called a superhero comic pretty much by definition is imitating DC and Marvel. If it doesn't look like them, it's not called "superhero". It seems you're looking for something which imitates spandex, general superpowers, and other conventions -- but not those particular characters.

    Of your examples, I'm only familiar with Astro City. Your implication is that the parallel characters are necessary to sell the universe, but I don't think that's quite true. Astro City is very deliberately similar to the classic comic books. It's not imitating simply to sell. It's imitating because that's what it's content is. It is expanding on, exploring, and deepening the superhero myth. And icons are a part of that.

So given that by definition they are imitating, it is only natural that within this set we see a lot of iconic characters imitated.
