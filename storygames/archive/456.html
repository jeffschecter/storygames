Ready made out of the box?

Nir Shiffer
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
Following the first draft of Jonathan Walton's A New Anthem (about a month ago) there was a sidetrack discussion about gaming groups taking systems and adjusting (or "fudging") them to their liking. This is obviously a common phenomena, and always at a group's disposal, but the question remains how desirable is this phenomena?

My position was that given what I'd like to do with a game, I prefer a system that does this while requiring as little alteration as possible. The ideal system, then, would be one that I wouldn't have to change at all.

(This is not saying that I'd only ever use one system because it was "the best", but rather that a system would be best suitable for a specific style and gaming experience if it provided them with as little alteration as possible. This seems to be an obvious statement, but it's still important to make)

Chris Tatro's very wise response is hereby italicized:

So long as the designer and the gm/players are different people, I don't think you will EVER see a game played unfudged straight out of the box.

My personal philosophy on rpg rulesets is that they are toolboxes. They're full of stuff for me to use when I need it, but I sure don't need all of them every time. And sometimes, for some simple stuff I might not need any of the tools at all.


Which makes me wonder. I mean, I know how I play it, and I know how my friends play it, but that's a very limited sampling. So:

Do you play games unfudged straight out of the box?

What are your views on altering a ready-made system?

Are these views different when it comes to "mainstream" vs. "indie" games (I can imagine someone would be more willing to mess with GURPS then Polaris, or vise versa)?

Where's your limit? Which is to mean, how hard will you work to change a system you see potential in rather then just trying something else?

And finally, what are your views on the toolbox analogy? Do you believe that your game is best played whole and should kept like that, or do you agree that players who know what they're doing should just pick and choose the ones they feel serve them best (let's assume these players already have some familiarity with the game, and are not working blindly)?

    Now, as we go along, we come across stories that we want to tell but which don't quite fit the rules we have. What do we do? Simple, we start playing with the rules to make them fit our new premise. Sometimes, this works quite well, when the rules are relatively well-suited to the (new) premise. On the other hand, sometimes the rules are very poorly suited to the premise and you end up with a franken-game, a monstrosity of ad hoc rule-premise grafting. I personally began digging into indie games for just this reason--look, build the rules around the premise!

    Shit, Josh and JD are agreeing on something. Must be true. That or it must be evil.

    So, to put what they've already said in my terms:

    You can play Ticket to Ride right out of the box. This is because there is nothing in Ticket to Ride that requires any investment from the players other than about an hour of time. You take it out, you count the trains, you deal the cards, you play. Play Ticket to Ride with just about anyone one earth, and you get about the same results.

    You cannot play Dogs right out of the box. Besides the fact that you have to make towns and characters -- adding something to the game not already there -- you (more importantly) have to add your own issues to the game. Furthermore, you have to bring your own judgement to the game: both about the fiction and (through the most critical player rule) about the way the game itself should run. Part of Dogs says "run this the way your group runs this" -- which is something you don't see in Ticket to Ride. Playing Sorcerer makes this even more obvious, as unless you have a strong group definition of humanity (and demons) play isn't going to go all that well. Sorcerer needs you to bring the things that squick you, or else it isn't gonna be fun. As a result you'll almost never get the same results from Sorcerer when playing with different people.

    That is why the "out of the box" issue gets weird with a lot of RPGs. What you can do with boardgames, you can't do with many RPGs because of the very element that makes many of us love RPGs: personal investment.

    (Of course, if you're playing a D&D game with pregen characters and a pregen dungeon like the mid-period boxed set, you probably could play right out of the box. That's the point at which the boardgame/RPG divide starts to crumble.)

    Now, unlike JD, I do differentiate this from changing the system or "fudging" for one simple reason. When, in Dogs, you make a town, make moral judgements, and have the most critical player decide on specific interpretations you are doing what the game tells you to do. You've added something, yes, but you haven't change the fundmental procedure of play. Ditto Sorcerer and deciding humanity. You're not out of the box anymore, you're making your own thing, but you are doing so according to the procedures of play laid out in the book.

    This is significantly different than when you fudge a rule in a game in order to change the procedures and methods of play. If you were to play Sorcerer without humanity at all, that would be a fudge. If you were to play Mutants and Masterminds without capping powers at level, that would be a fudge. If you play GURPS by replacing the dice with tarrot cards, that is most certainly a fudge. When you do something that changes the way the game works, rather than just adding material in a manner the game already intends for you to do, you are fudging the rules.

You can parse the "out of the box" or "fudging" thing as either:

    (a) We used only the rules presented in the book and nothing else
    (b) We used all of the rules presented in the book without changing anything (but added in our other-stuff rules)
