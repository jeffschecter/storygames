\n My first thread. As the topic said: what is your favourite narrating technique at the moment?

Mine right now is to describe environments from an emotion. If the winter landscape hates the PCs, then the cold bites them in their faces. I can also give strength to an environment (or a person ... or even a personality) by describing a contradicting emotion to what the PCs sees. The dark empty halls may give a calm sense, the torturer are really a nice chap or someone who's angry tries to control him/herself and can even give away a smile.

Cheers!

    \n That's a cool rule. I still remember one of my players way back who wrote long descriptions about his characters, and after hearing about his cold blue eyes, the fine fabric in his clothes, bla, bla, bla, I kinda had enough. So nowadays I tend to just describe what's standing out from something. Perhaps a blue hat on a woman, a golden door in a house or one old voice in the market that stands out from anything else.

    One thing I do have noticed though, and give more attention to, is that the more you describe something as a GM, the more focus the players will give it. So important persons get longer descriptions than faceless mooks.

    \n I tend to float about in techniques, using whatever suits the scenario at hand.

    Nowadays I've led some scenarios in a dark cityscape, a fantasy-city so big a special race has evolved in the depths of it; Mould. The moulds live in the sewers, and cowers themselves in mud to protect their delicate skin from scars. They ooze of mud and muck!

    The characters of two weekly campaigns have been close to these humanoids the last weeks, in the dark. I've really enjoyed making splashing noises (moulds walking), and deep whale-like sounds (moulds "emotiocating"), and describing the pungent smells emanating from the dark ...

    The characters have avoided direct contact each and every time, so they have never really seen the moulds! LOL

    \n I like playing with the other players perceptions. Have my character rush into a situation that most likely leave the other bewildered. When they wonder where I'm going or how I going to have a plan, I state OOC that I have a plan, but don't explain it. That create an OOC suspense and stake "What is Elins/w176s plan?".

    Then I'll bide my time, work towards my plan and then hopefully manage the a great reveal and/or plot twist and/or awesome thing I'm been building towards.

    Knowing that something going on, but not knowing what either in character or out of character, and especially the latter is a great way of building expectations.

    Jared's rule of three
    urdwell.blogspot.com.au/2006/09/technique-jareds-rule-of-three.html
    \nOf similar use, my favorite method of generating character descriptions ever remains Over The Edge's Signs. Basically, for each trait, you figure out some way in which that trait impacts your appearance or easily-observable behavior: so a trait like "fighter" might have a sign like "well-built", "always carrying a weapon", "lots of scars" or whatever, as long as it made sense for the trait: and you would, since you had four traits, end up with four awesome descriptors which, like Jared's Rule of Three, nicely alleviates the "height weight eye-color"-style details.

    Explicitly cinematic camera tracking shots.
    \nCare to elaborate, perhaps with an example? I've been playing a lot of action RPGs these last few years, so this sounds interesting.
    \nIt's mostly about making an effort to be mindful of including more dynamism and movement in my static descriptions. A lot of what others have said about describing the viewpoint of an observer shifting to pan and zoom to give what would otherwise be bog-standard ten-foot room descriptions some sense of happening in time and leading directly to whatever action is going to happen there. As a PC I try to describe my actions similarly, with the 'camera' in my head doing the same thing a camera does in a film, which is direct our attention to what's important in a scene with ongoing movement.

    \nOh, nice. During combat, when playing action styled RPGs, I explain to my players that they should see a combat round as the camera is shooting their character. Each new action with each PC is like the camera is cutting between them. So when the camera isn't pointed against their character they may do something else in between. Perhaps climbing up the mast so they can have a duel there, moving to the stairs to fight off the queen's royal guard or discovered something in the scenery that they may use. As you can see, it's mostly about moving (I don't play with rules for movement at all in any RPG, except tactical ones). I've discovered that it's a technique that's quite common in action movies.

    \n Upstart, I tend to create systems where the rule mechanic and what happens in the fictive world must be connected through a description. It follows this simple list:

    (0. Tell your intention.)
    1a. Say what you use in the roll.
    (1b. Roll the dice.)
    2. Describe how the roll takes effect in the fiction.

    Lets say you want to roll for the skill/trait/whatever Cake Baking. You roll and succeed (or fail), and if you don't describe what you did then the roll was pretty pointless. Nothing happened because you gave no input to what would happen (and so didn't the system either). In some games (most games?), the GM usually wants to give some modification to the roll. In that case, you use number zero on the list.

    I usually don't care what the players roll for, as long as their motivate how they use the skill to achieve what they want. "You want to unlock a door with Pie Baking? Sure, as long as you can describe it." How did Odysseus escape the cyclops using a riddle? This is something you can use with any dice mechanic or with no randomness at all, as long as you or the system gives something to the players to include in their descriptions. My name for this sort of thing is \xbbcreative challenge\xab. Been playing with this for the last five years.
