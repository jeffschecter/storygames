\n From the "What did you play over the Holidays?" thread, there was this:

(GBSteve) "Tales of the Arabian Nights, a board game which would work fine as a solo RPG"

My experiences with this game have been mixed:

I played it with my hippie RPG group who love both games (hippie, traditional, and board games of all kinds) and we loved it, had a ton of fun watching the fates of our characters.

I played it with more of my boardgame-oriented friends who have also enjoyed RPGs, and the MST3K-ing of it just didn't stop: They didn't like the goal-less-ness (impossible to "game to win"), the amount of handling, and the fact that it could be all scripted out (half of them are programmers).

Didn't stop me from loving it, but I did realize one thing: With the latter group, we had 5 total players as opposed to 4 with the previous. 3-4 players is really the cusp of "entertaining" for TotAN I think: Any more and half the table has nothing to do for that much longer.

So, in general, I gotta say that I love this game. While there's no "point" to "winning", you can have a lot of fun basically having your character have horrible and awesome stuff happen to them in equal amounts.

I remember that Matthew at our table was Scheherezade. Her first quest was to get married to his love. Upon completing this quest, the next quest card he picked up was "Now go find the lover that has been haunting your dreams". What a great situation, "Sorry honey, this OTHER guy is my fated guy. Keep the light on." Then on the way to the man of her dreams, she was seduced by an efreet.

Mark's Ali Baba (IIRC, might have been someone else) was struck Insane and Ensorcelled, which meant that other players moved his character around and made decisions for him at random.

My character in the second game went on a DOUBLE HAJJ. Completed one Hajj, only to turn around and do it again.

It really feels like the intersection of an RPG and a boardgame, save for the kind of total randomness of effects of what you do vs the results. But very fun if you like that sort of thing, and can stand playing a very story-oriented game without a focus on "winning". One suggestion though: Keep to 3-4 total players. With 5, the handling becomes a little too much, too much downtime between turns, etc. I might even try a 4-player game with two teams of two characters who alternate, just so all the craziness happens to just two characters in order to see what happens.

Another game I found like this was Betrayal on House on the Hill (aka "Betrayal at the House of the Dead Heroes who Never Win"). The sheer brilliance of the 50-some-odd scenarios and the way the "traitor" is picked makes it feel equal parts role-based and boardgame. However, you won't become as attached to your character-as-person as you will with Tales of the Arabian Nights.

Anyone else play this game? What are your thoughts?

-Andy

    \n Since this is about other games, too: It was suggested to me that Arkham Horror can be "roleplayed". I recently bought the game and played one match solo and then three more with my friends. It didn't have a speck of roleplaying in it. It's all about resource management and damage control and there is no reason why anyone would ever just "play their character".     I don't think the game even provides any kind of story, beyond the vaguest of arcs. So I'd love to hear from someone else who played and if they had a similar experience.

    That said it's a damn good boardgame. It's just very obviously not a rpg.

    I think the same goes for HeroQuest. It's got characters and a dungeon and a story and all the trappings of early D&D. But when you play it it's so clearly a boardgame it couldn't be anything else unless you systematically started to throw all the rules away and leave just the characters, their stats and the dice. Trying to roleplay just makes it boring and the story is a railroad anyway. At least that's my experience with it.

    \n I love Tales, it's a great game. I've played it several times as a two player with my wife and it's worked really well.

    We only made one change, and oddly it is linked to the question. We dropped the character names.

    The problem was when we started playing, if I had Aladdin and Emma had Scheherazade we felt we should pick skills they'd have and take decisions as they would, which quickly came to clash with the events we encountered.

    When we just took a counter and decided on who our guy was ("I'm a scholarly white mage, I'm a beautiful young street thief") it worked much better.

    I still wouldn't call it really rpg-like. It's more that there's a small rpg element in it. Really it's what the name of this site suggests - it's a story game. It's a game that generates stories.

    One of my boardgaming mates is really keen to play it. He's also an rpger and mostly on the trad side, but willing (like me really) to try story games - they're just not where his preferences lie. In trad rpgs though he's all about the story and in-game exploration and all that fun stuff so it's no surprise he's tempted by this. The more pure boardgamers I could immediately see wouldn't like it because it's not really a game in that sense.

    Re Arkham, I love the game but I'd never use it as an rpg. It has more complex rules than Call of Cthulhu. Why would I want a clunkier system to run something an existing rpg system already does so well? It's a great boardgame. If I want Cthulhoid roleplaying though I play CoC (and if I didn't like CoC I'd play something like Trail which is also less complex than Arkham).

    For those who do use it as an rpg though I'd love to hear more about it. What's folly to me may be brilliance in another after all. It's funny how often that's true in life.

    \n I first played Arabian Nights in a group of three. So we were always taking part in one way or another, and the game was relatively short. I enjoyed it. An interesting thing that I noticed was that the two other players had wonderful stories about other games they had played: with drama, derring do and amazing turn arounds.

    When we played, it was a surprise to me, coming from an rp background, to have all of the story come from the texts. The main choices we have are our responses, which have a subtle (?) effect on the character by giving you a slightly different range of outcomes and rewards. As we played, we overarching stories arise: I was married and so became tethered to my ugly wife, another character rose to vizier and might have become sultan, one suffered all the indiginities of ensorcellement, curses and finally died: then won the game by appearing at Bagdad at a crucial ending moment of the game. But these were all things dictated by the text, and I think they became story through the process of the annotative comments we made in between. They felt more like reading stories than playing them out to me.

    But then, when we finished, the other players regaled others with their tales of highs and lows, as though the choices and turns were things they had experience or originated--what I associate with playing an rp. It still strikes me as an odd sort of of way that 2nd or 3rd person narrated art can become a 1st person experience through identification. Like watching a sports game, or movie and feeling the experience having happened to yourself. But in this game you do make choices. It is more like a choose your own adventure, really. One you share with others.

That card is the single thing in the game that can turn someone's interest in it into absolute indifference or hate. That quest has GOT to be Nerfed.

    Basically, the quest is "You are accused by a Vizier" (kinda like an Alladin story), "So you wander off for 3 turns. But starting on turn four, you have a (high) chance of being captured and returned to Baghdad. If you have a treasure with you when you return to Baghdad (which /may/ happen if this is the 2nd or 3rd quest you take in the game, but UTTERLY impossible if it's the first quest you pick up), you succeed and gain riches and status, otherwise you are locked in the dungeon and have to escape somehow (usually through the use of various esoteric skills, most of which you simply won't have if this is the first quest you pick up.

    ...oh, and the dungeon is nigh inescapable. You'll be in it for at least 10 rounds (perhaps an hour of actual play) until you find a way out. Maybe."

    I'd houserule to either:

    * Remove that quest from the deck entirely. It's just not well thought out, and impossible to resolve.
    * If it is drawn as a First or Second Quest, return it to the Bottom of the quest cards and draw again. If it is the third or higher quest, then play as normal.
    * If you draw it and end up in prison, on Turn Three you "escape" (perhaps drawing a Fugitive or other status) and discard the quest, drawing another.

    I totally understand that in general it is a VERY polarizing game, heck I saw the same effect in my test groups. However, that quest you drew (and me) is just plain borked if you drew it as a starting quest. It looks really story-rich, and is, but it's also broken and frustrating if you haven't had time to build up your character yet.

    Posted By: AndyIt really feels like the intersection of an RPG and a boardgame, save for the kind of total randomness of effects of what you do vs the results.
    \nLike when my cleric hits a monster and suddenly my buddy is allowed to feel better?

    It strikes me that these games will continue to be difficult to categorize as long as our definition of roleplaying includes pure metagaming avatar play, like I've seen in a lot of tourney D&D, as well as play where you constantly engineer the game to your character's detriment in spite of his/her fictional wishes, like you can in, say, Fiasco.

    Sorry if that's a threadjack. It's not supposed to be.
