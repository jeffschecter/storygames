\n Ok, it sounds like some people are interested in following the design process of House of Spiders, as well as discussing it and giving feedback, etc... so I will add anything of interest, as far as the design, marketing, publication process, ect. as it becomes available (and as my busy schedule permits).

House of Spiders is a collaborative storytelling game about power, courtly Intrigue, Machavillianness, etc....here\u2019s how Ron Edwards describes its driving aesthetics:

-Influence: trust and distrust, loyalty and conscience, promises and payments
-Privilege: perceived virtue, authority through violence, social stability and instability, status vs. wealth
-Control of information: unwelcome truths, withheld truths, lies, compartmentalization
-Patience: the tension of the right time vs. too late, the contradiction of preserving a passionate impulse for future ends

Thanks


In another thread Paul_T mentioned that it would be interesting to check out my House of Spiders design process and see some of the feedback and suggestions I receive from other designers in public rather than keeping the process behind doors. HoS is a collaborative storytelling game inspired by Game of Thrones.

I\u2019m open to doing this in public if people are interested in either checking out the design process or participating in giving feedback, perspectives, etc.

Anyway, let me know if you are either interested in seeing the design process or participating by giving your feedback, your perspectives, etc. If folks are interested I will start posting HoS stuff and going through at least some of the process publicly.

Thanks all

    \n In case this has never come up: what you're proposing is the default indie process, Forgite best practices if you will [grin]. I personally think that time has proven it as the best approach for a small press game: you gain friends and attention for your project long before the game is finished. You're essentially marketing simultaneously with designing. The only real reason not to do it is if your process doesn't bend naturally to the needs to public work - having to fend comments from others, having to maintain a regular update schedule, etc.

    The one thing I'd particularly like to highlight as an advantage of the public process is the possibility of attracting like-minded "advocates" for your project: people who hear about it early on, like what they hear, check out the design, appreciate it, and then go on to not only help you finish the game, but also to help in marketing it.

    New designers particularly need all the advantages they can get, plus the nature of the advantages you get from a public process (a wide variety of double-checking of your work, potential to attract advocates) is particularly useful for new designers.\n
    Jeff_Slater said:

    I\u2019m open to doing this in public if people are interested in either checking out the design process or participating in giving feedback, perspectives, etc.

    \nI feel like this observation should be sort of obvious: you're much more likely to get somebody interested in doing any of those things if you first post your draft and then see if anybody cares. I mean, that's how we decide what we care about: you post your draft, we fall in love with your ideas. It's a sure sign of an insane commitment to game development for somebody to say that they want to do critique on a game before they see what sort of project it is [grin]. The good stuff obviously attracts a lot more attention.

    \n Hi everyone,

    I\u2019m very excited to post this consulting session video with Ron Edwards for House of Spiders. For those of you who don\u2019t know who Ron is, he is the author of Sorcerer, the founder of the Forge, and the main reason we have such a thriving \u201cindie\u201d RPGs scene. I highly, highly recommend that you hire Ron to consult on your game. I\u2019ll give more details of what he offers and pricing below.

    I suggest that you skip to 19:00, when viewing the video, because until then, Ron and I, are largely talking past each other or about stuff other than my game. He is basically asking me what the setting of House of Spiders is like, and I\u2019m misunderstanding the question.

    House of Spiders is less about the setting and more about tones, themes, and certain types of characters. It is kind of like Fiasco in that it doesn\u2019t really have a specific setting, rather it is trying to tell a certain type of story. In the same way that Fiasco is trying to tell a Coen brothers like story, House of Spiders is trying to tell A Game of Thrones-ish type of story (in the best possible sense)...it\u2019s more about setting up a story with those types of tones, themes, characters, conflicts, etc., and it mostly lets you fill in and establish the facts and color of the setting during setup and to flush it out as you play. I very much hesitate to use Game of Thrones as a comparison because it\u2019s not exactly accurate, it\u2019s just an easy way to invoke the idea that courtly intrigue, Machiavellianism, power, ect is a central part of the game, by referencing something people are familiar with. But it may also sell the game short, and give people the wrong idea about it. Here\u2019s how Ron describes the essence of House of Spiders; I think it gives a better picture of what the game is actually like, and its driving aesthetic:

    -Influence: trust and distrust, loyalty and conscience, promises and payments
    -Privilege: perceived virtue, authority through violence, social stability and instability, status vs. wealth
    -Control of information: unwelcome truths, withheld truths, lies, compartmentalization
    -Patience: the tension of the right time vs. too late, the contradiction of preserving a passionate impulse for future ends

    So Ron was asking me a simple question: What\u2019s the setting like? And I was giving him complicated answers that weren\u2019t directly answering his question. Long story short, save yourself some time and frustration and start at 19:00.

    The consultation gets especially good towards the end, as I explain my game in more detail and Ron gets a fuller understanding of it. Ron, provides some excellent insights that are very valuable and are without a doubt going to change my game for the better. I wouldn\u2019t have come up with a few of these paradigm shifting insights by myself, and my game wouldn\u2019t have been as good as a result. Anyway, I hope you enjoy the video.

    Here is what Ron offers for $100 for indie designers:

    -Reading
    -Playtest: external or with you
    -One solid consult session, by phone or email or hangout as you prefer
    -One strategic publishing discussion, recommendations, and networking
    -Promotion and public discussion on social media at a designated time

    This is an insanely generous deal by one of the top designers in the profession. If you don\u2019t take advantage of it there\u2019s something wrong with you! Go to his site and contact him right now!

    Thanks for your so much for your interest in House of Spiders, and for any feedback you provide!

    Best,
    Jeff

    \n We had a extremely productive playtest session of House of Spiders last night. Intrigue upon intrigue upon intrigue. The group in large part enjoyed the experience and felt more positive about the way the game played than I did (yet at the same time they were very perceptive about the areas that could use improvement. I want my game to be extremely solid and to precisely and thoroughly meet the goals of my design, and it still needs a lot of work to do so. The game may be fun but it is very slippery and needs mor structure. The group\u2019s feedback and constructive criticisms where extremely productive and insightful; I\u2019m very greatful to them and indebted to them. I appreciate their time and willingness to help immensely.

    I want the game to solve the problems in the GMless Collaborative Storytelling Game design space, as I see them. This playtest session gave me more information than I\u2019ve ever received from a playtest. I can see clearly where the opportunities lie in order for me to achieve the aims of the design

    I\u2019m excited and reinvigorated to improve the game and I have come up with some great ideas about how to do it; it\u2019s going to involve a sea-change and radical restructuring the game (which it should, this is a sign of real progress). Burn the dead wood off, and then burn it off again; if you want to create a great game you must kill your darlings, and you must do so gladly.

    The changes will differentiate the game, and likely solve a lot of problems, at least from my perspective, that have yet to be solved in this design space. I\u2019m extremely happy with all that I\u2019ve learned from the playtest and the solutions that have presented themselves. Just because a game is fun doesn\u2019t mean that it is achieving its goals or is even solid. I want House of Spiders to be built upon a rock.

    When I rewrite the game I will share it; I apologize for how much time it is taking to give you folks something more solid than a discussion or video of consultants and I discussing a game in abstract that you\u2019ve yet to read or play yourselves. I\u2019m very greatful for your interest and don\u2019t take it as for granted and as a give. Thank you for being patient with me.

    P.S. Zack, Mahony, Carrie, Roy, and Scott (who is a new member of Story Games SLC\u2014this was his first session)\u2014thank you all for your generosity and for you wonderful and insightful feedback! Please let me know the next time you playtest, or would like a studio critique of your games, I\u2019m there.
