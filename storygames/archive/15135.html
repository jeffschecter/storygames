\n The Question
\n
\n"My friend wants to run Mouseguard but without using the Mouseguard system. Thoughts on alternatives?"
\n
\n
\n
\nDo you folk have any?
\n
\nHere were mine:
\n
\n1) Pick up some iteration of Fate, and run it straight
\n- borrow Dresden Files or use the SRD
\n
\n2) Pick up the Solar System and prep a little campaign specific set of choices for advancement and narrative control:
\n- http://www.arkenstonepublishing.net/solarsystem
\n- http://rpggeek.com/rpg/826/solar-system
\n
\n3) The Questing Beast: a radical narrativist game about heroic anthropomorphic animals. It is genius. The generic version ("The Pool") is free, but tQB is a gem and quite cheap
\n- The Pool: http://www.randomordercreations.com/thepool.rtf
\n- The Questing Beast: http://www.randomordercreations.com/rpg.html
\n
\nBut these are heroic little mice suffering setbacks and making deliberate choices about when they will risk it all, when they will shine, and when they will take consequences for the good of the team. There need to be explicit narrative-influencing mechanics and reward mechanics to bring that story out to the fullest. I don't think a modified tactical game (D&D, Savage Worlds) will really deliver that Mouseguard feel.

    \n I've noodled around with a Trollbabe hack. I call it Mouse Patrol.
    http://rpgsystems.wikidot.com/trollbabe
    http://theunstore.com/index.php/unstore/game/63


    - The three traits are Action, Reason and Social. Action succeeds when you roll below your number, Reason when you roll above. Social's range is whatever range is shorter, plus your number.

    - To start out, each guard mouse has a Specialty associated with each Trait that allows a free re-roll when doing that thing. Mice also get free re-rolls when doing stuff that is in their Nature (running away, hiding, foraging, etc.).

    Example Guard Mouse

    Sadie, Number is 6
    Action succeeds on 1-5, Specialty - Fierce Fighter
    Reason succeeds on 7-10, Specialty - Seasoned Scout
    Social succeeds on 6-10, Specialty - Mission Oriented

    Advancements let the Guard Mice add new Specialties and possible change their Number up or down by one.

    \n I've been working on an alternative to Mouse Guard ever since my ~4 month game of it died due to a player revolt. The final straw was when I read back over the conditions rules and we all realized they are even harsher than we had thought. I can try to reconstruct the various complaints over the course of the game about how the systems just didn't work for us. One thing to note, we may have had consistently weird dice*. Most players had double digit fate points stored up because most often when they failed a roll, they had no 6's to re-roll.

    Meanwhile, I'm hacking Drift to give me a simpler alternative - since it already has belief-focused characters who change because of their experiences and challenges, but also supports quick, interesting mission design and a communal record of the world. We've already done character creation, although currently that group is playing through an Iron Heroes story arc (yes, enjoys Irons Heroes, Fiasco, and Nobilis, but cannot get Mouse Guard to work for them...), so after we finish that I plan to test out my Mouse Guard hack.

    - Mendel

    * I am the guy who played 1001 Nights, rolled almost 20 gems, but still died before I got a turn.

    Posted By: wyrmwoodThe final straw was when I read back over the conditions rules and we all realized they are even harsher than we had thought.
    \nThis is possibly completely unrelated to your experience, but I found that conditions were too harsh when I...

    1. Made the GM turns too long.
    2. Didn't include the PC's relationships who can alleviate their hungry/tired conditions for free.
    3. Used more conditions instead of twists when players failed rolls.

    #1 was especially a problem. It can feel unnatural creating missions that only start with 2 obstacles (with more obstacles introduced with twists). But going with shorter missions means more player turns, more Persona / Fate points, and more players giving the Prologue and thus alleviating a Condition or Taxed Nature (which also means Nature is used more, and players succeed more).

    I still occasionally make missions too long, especially for 1 shots. But with 4 hours, it is possible to complete 2 GM and 2 Player turns, thus alleviating many of these issues (for me at least).

    \n John,

    I rarely had more than 2 initial obstacles, and on at least one occasion I ended up only using one during my GM turn. Persona and Fate points were next to useless (only once in the entirety of the game did a player succeed on a roll they would have failed thanks to fate points), and I ultimately house ruled that you could spend a Fate point to get an extra player turn action so they would have more general use. But even that didn't help much, since extra actions don't let you try to recover the same condition more than once.

    A one important source of conditions was the weather rules, which seem to fairly aggressively toss out extra conditions. Combine that with how hard it was to remove significant conditions and how incredibly frustrating that made the player turns.

    If you want to talk more about this, perhaps we should whisper?

    But you don't need house rules. You need Checks. Are the players earning enough Checks in your Turn?\n

    \nConditions are great. It's nice to have a mouse guard tired and thirsty and maybe angry with someone. Conditions are story hooks and motivate good performances. Anyway, I don't see this problem in my gaming experience. Two obstacles per GM's Turn, one Turn per session, three to four hours of play. Maybe one or two Conditions in the entire patrol.

    But as jenskot said, you don't need to make the characters angry or thirsty or sick or whatever. You should to establish Twists instead. That's how the story goes somewhere. That's how interesting things happen. And remember: Conditions are not punishment. Conditions are interesting ways to move the story and the characters to new and enjoyable situations.
