    \n Great question, AFT. Here's how I see it. It's not primarily about the nature of game design from a numerical or procedural point of view. It's more social and psychological. It's that these designers *are* my friends, or at least acquaintances towards whom I feel warmly. So why would I want to change what they wrote when we had a perfectly good time playing the game according to the book?

    Now, sometimes there will be something that is unclear in the book, that becomes clear when playing with the designer. That is the downside of this phenomenon, and can be eliminated through better rules-writing.

    Another way of putting it is this: by playing with the designer, in person, it becomes at least slightly easier to grok what the game is capital-A About, what the designer's vision for the game really is, and so on. Sure, with good online support or just good texts this extra bonus isn't necessary\u2014I understand Dogs, L&L, 4E, and Riddle of Steel quite well, for example\u2014but I think having several years of getting to play games with their designers* has made me, in general, more desirous of directly emulating what the designers are doing at their own tables\u2014more trusting that there is, in fact, great value in such emulation.

    Matt

    *I didn't list all the games I played that were being run by someone who was a close friend or spouse of the designer and who was very close to the design process, but that can be cool too and in a similar way.

    \n That's honestly a perspective on things that had never really occurred to me. I like it, it makes sense.

    Gaming with the designer sounds sort of like putting on a stage production with the playwright: you don't have to guess how a particular line should be read or what the set should look like or whether you're misinterpreting the script, because you can just ask and get an answer. And the end result is the closest thing anyone's going to get to seeing the play as it was conceived in the author's brain, which is kind of cool. (It won't be exactly what's in the author's brain, of course, because a good playwright will collaborate with the actors and the director and the crew and incorporate their best ideas into the actual production; I assume that a game designer playing his/her own game would do much the same thing, and use the best of what everyone brings to the table.)


    I still think there's a lot of value in doing your own thing with the script -- tweaking your production to speak to the themes that you want to address, drawing out the elements that resonate the most with you, and so on -- but in the process of finding and following your own vision of the work, you give up the chance to fully share the author's vision of it, and I can see how that's a big deal.

    \n Instead of just a list, I'm going to try to see what I remember from the various times:

    Agon: GenCon 2006... It was fun, but I don't remember anything in particular about John or even who else was playing.
    Annalise: JiffyCon Boston sometime, with Shreyas and Elizabeth and maybe Dev? A castle rose up from out of the forest and Elizabeth had a cloak made of crows
    Bliss Stage: late playtest in Boston with Alexis and some local friends, Ben and I argued too much about rules, my mecha Ultraman got crucified by the aliens
    Burning Wheel: local NC con, Luke glossed most of the rules (aside from Duel of Wits) but I was blown away by his energy, only time I've screamed at someone IC
    Danger Patrol: last year I ran this during an off-week of another game, John played a robot with detachable head, there were void sharks
    Dance and the Dawn: I've played this at least twice with Dev, though not since the final revision and publication
    Dogs in the Vineyard: Ben ran this for Vincent, Alexis, Meg, Julia, Emily (?), and me; Vx laid hands on a demonic fire, Meg shot everyone
    Escape or Die: played this with Fred, Rob, and someone (James Brown, Mark Majcher?) at GenCon 2006; I was really out of it and slowed things down, we died
    Freemarket: Jared ran a pretty finished version at SGBoston one week, they had stuck custom stickers on blank playing cards, it was zany and amazing
    Misspent Youth: JiffyCon Boston a couple years back, I remember being super impressed by character creation but not sold on the overall resolution
    Mist-Robed Gate: 11-players in the Bakers' side room, Shreyas, Elizabeth, Vx, Meg, Joshua, Julia, Emily, Nathan, Dev, etc.; the whole W. Mass crew + Boston
    Ribbon Drive: Joe was down from Vancouver one weekend, so we broke this out; surprised by how touching it was and how minimal the rules were
    Shock:: JiffyCon Boston again, playing with Boston folks, Joshua, and my friend Perich; I remember it being way easier than playing from the rulebook
    Sign in Stranger: JiffyCon Boston, pre-ashcan game with Emily and who else? The aliens gave us a gardening instructional video that was a year long.
    Steal Away Jordan: JiffyCon W. Mass, pre-publication game, I played a wetnurse / nanny with the ability to make the baby cry or quiet it.
    Universalis: GenCon 2006, Games on Demand, this game was all over the place but I don't remember what it was about

    Looking over this this, I'm noticing that it really depends on:
    -- who is local to you, building friendships with them
    -- going to local cons, where designers are most easily accessible
    -- to a lesser extent, playing with folks at big conventions (GenCon) when the chance arises
    -- but I didn't really go out of my way to make these happen, just stumbling into most of them

    Additionally, none of these games -- in comparison to other times I've played these same games -- were particularly striking or memorable, aside from playing them with the designers. And since I don't tend to think of Dev, for example, as "the designer of the Dance and the Dawn," since I used to see him every week, I had to wrack my brains to come up with many of these. I'm sure I've missed other games for the same reason. Have I ever played Polaris with Ben? I'm not sure. Does it matter? Not really.

    In general, I think my most memorable game experiences would have been impossible if the designer had actually been there. Ben may or may not have enjoyed playing in the Bliss Stage game I ran a few years ago and Luke might have pulled his hair out over our Mouse Guard game, which freely ignored his advice on structuring the game in several major instances. Vincent would probably dig our current Apocalypse World game, but it might be really different if he was playing.

    \n Jonathan - interesting observations.

Harper looked in on a Danger Patrol game/playtest I was running at GPNW (just a couple times and at a super respectful distance) and I don't think it phased me much.
