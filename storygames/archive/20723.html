\n Assume that 1) we're designing for an "incoherent" game system, 2) we want this game system to accommodate combat, and 3) we want the system to temporally organize combat by dictating the order in which characters may act.

I like the concept of initiative, because in a confrontation it's possible to take control by forcing your opponent to "play defense" \u2013 you have possession of the ball in soccer/football or basketball, or you're laying down suppressive fire, or you're making attacks in melee that can't be dodged except by stepping back. With skill and luck, you can create situations where your opponent has to overwhelmingly focus on reacting instead of acting. Or you just get the drop on them.

But I don't think that the initiative paradigms I'm familiar with \u2013 individual initiative, group initiative, reverse declaration, and free-and-clear \u2013 address this element of a confrontation. AW can model this very nicely, but not in a formalized way.

One game mechanic that I've been mentally playing around with assumes an initiative queue that is determined at the beginning of the conflict. The character at the top of the queue is the one that gets to act first (although they can choose to delay and possibly interrupt), and taking action moves a character to the bottom of the queue. When someone takes direct action against you and you have the opportunity to react, you can choose whether you react or not. If you do react, you get to ameliorate whatever consequences your opponent's action would cause for you, but then your position in the initiative queue drops directly below theirs; i.e. you're now last and they're one position above you. If you don't react, you take the normal consequences but get to keep your initiative position (assuming that the consequences don't affect it). Sort of like you're "acting under fire" if you choose not to react.

Thoughts on this idea? Thoughts on existing game systems that might be of interest here? Thoughts on ways that you would model this sort of thing?

    \n In Matin\xe9, all combatants had a fixed initiative value. That value determines who were going first. When doing an action, the current initiative holder lowered its initiative.

    Free action: no movement.
    Simple action: five steps.
    Complex action: ten steps.

    The thing is, the initiative was shown on a circular board that had 30 steps. There were no rounds. Attacking and defending were both complex action, so if A attacked B, they would move the same amount of steps. So A would always be the attacker over B, unless:

    \xd7 A failed with its attack roll and B succeeded with its defend roll.
    \xd7 B did a fast action (-5 on the action but only moving five steps).
    \xd7 B didn't defend at all (no defense roll).
    \xd7 Someone else attacked A, which lowered its initiative.

    The system had some downsides, and it had some good sides too.

    \n That's a nice idea, no gripes about it - depends on the rest of the system whether it could be improved upon.

    Two systems from my recent games that might or might not be of interest:

    In the first system "Initiative" is defined in dramatic terms as an arbitrary opportunity for a given character to impact the events, so it doesn't particularly depend on character stats or anything like that - the matter who gets to act when is intentionally an issue of luck and willingness to take risks. Whoever declares action first starts the combat and has the first initiative. After the first actor the initiative goes around the table in sitting order. The GM takes initiative at his turn, but only for the single most appropriate NPC at a time. This regular initiative process is disrupted by the fact that characters have a right to "respond" to actions pertaining to them (an enemy attacking them, or attempting to flee, or whatever); if they do, the initiative jumps to that player, and then continues around the table normally. The reason for why you may not wish to react every time you could is that every action carries an inherent risk of loss, and the more you act without reorienting yourself (only possible with non-responding actions at your naturally occurring initiative), the more likely such failure becomes. The game also assumes pretty heavy "passive presence" (that is, the characters do all sorts of things by assumption, without the player needing to actively declare e.g. defending oneself and continuing to engage in melee) for the characters, so a player in a winning position does not necessarily need to take actions constantly to continue pressing their advantage.

    My second example is supposed to be a modestly realistic and tactically interesting reinterpretation of the D&D initiative system, particularly 3rd edition: every character begins combat by rolling initiative with a d20+Wits attribute check, and initiative is counted down to determine who gets a turn when. Monsters and NPCs have their own scores. The core conceit is intentionally familiar, but the concept of "initiative spending" takes this to a new direction: the Init score is interpreted as an active measure of personal combat awareness for an individual character, and therefore characters spend points from it during combat to activate various effects. An active defense (as opposed to relying on your static AC) such as a dodge or block cost a few points, reacting to orders by someone else at a different initiative does as well, and so on. A character 10 or more points up on an enemy can spend 10 points of initiative to outright take a second action; a 3rd ed. style opportunity attack costs 5 initiative. Characters can take combat actions to confuse and confound each other, damaging each other's initiative scores in addition to their hitpoints. You only get one normal action per round, even if your initiative drops during it so "your number" comes up multiple times, of course. It is very well possible to drop to negative initiative, which inflicts confusion, hesitation and such difficulties on the character; it is therefore important for characters to take the time to reorient themselves occasionally during combat, rerolling their initiative scores and observing their environment. In general your initiative level determines how detailed tactical information the GM gives you, so whether you're at <10 or 20+ Init at a given time may come to matter in subtle ways as your character miscounts the number of enemies or whatever. What I like about this system in practice is how it retains the simplicity of D&D's answers to "who hits first" and "whose turn is it", while also fully supporting the idea of real-world initiative that you mentioned: superior initiative enables characters, and inferior initiative disables them, all in flexible ways that are easy to add to and expand on further.

    \n I like an initiative system with some tactical choice and trade-offs for acting sooner vs. acting more deliberately. Here's an idea for one such system:

    Each character has a pool of dice they can use to enhance their rolls during combat. At the beginning of each encounter (or each round of actions if you want to get more granular,) everyone involved blindly bids a certain number of those dice. Characters act in the order of the strength of their bids (ties roll their bid dice, plus relevant bonuses.)

    So, bid low: have more bonus dice to use during your turn. Bid high: act quicker with fewer dice.

    \n This is a pretty interesting topic.

    Traditionally, "initiative rules" simply indicate what order actors make their "moves".

    However, there are many other important factors in "initiative". I'll try listing a few here:

    1. When is it your turn (you, the player) to make a meaningful decision?

    * Traditionally, this coincides with the turn order. When it's your turn, you decide what you're doing. Of course, this is not the only possibility.
    * We can have systems where all actions must be decided before initiative is determined at all.
    * We can have systems where determining *what* you do is coupled to *when* you do it in other ways. For instance, the "reverse declaration" method, where the last person to act must announce their action first.
    * We could also have a system where you can choose to act first in exchange for limiting your options. Yes, you get to react right away... BUT you don't get to choose HOW you react, or your choices are limited.

    I designed a system like that once but never playtested it. In high stress situations, you could hesitate or you could carry out one "trained" action. Your range of "trained" actions was very limited; experience and skill meant you could have a larger list of possible actions to choose from than your opponent.

    2. When does your character have a chance to make a move?

    * As above, this does not have to be linked to your choices as a player. Why not explore some other possibilities? Perhaps we know that your character acts first, but you get to decide what exactly it was they did after the others' actions are known?

    Or what if decide on our actions in a certain order, but all our *characters* then act simultaneously?

    In that case, initiative really means better "move" selection - the loser has to do so blind, whereas the winner can make a well-informed decision.

    Eero makes a really important and usually overlooked distinction on this next point:

    To what extent is a character active or passive throughout the process? The game assumes certain things about their actions throughout the process:

    3. What is your character doing when it's not "their turn"?

    Some games assume that everyone is actively participating all the time; in other games, your character might as well be standing still (shocked, stunned, hesitating) until your turn comes up.

    That implies very different processes, both for determining what happens in-fiction and what happens at the table.

    Give a thought as to what characters are assumed to be doing during other players' turns or declarations!

    Each little step in your process implies things about what's happening "on screen" and how the characters are behaving when we aren't consciously manipulating or describing their actions. (The "attack of opportunity" is a perfect example of this.)

    4. Which actors are initiating the action, and which are responding to it?

    This can also be uncoupled from the other options. Perhaps you have the initiative, so I can only choose defensive or reactive "moves"... but, as the player, I still make that choice first.

    I might need to declare that I'm putting up my sword and moving over to block you from getting to the door, for instance, even before your turn comes up!

    5. Which actors are limiting the options of other actors?

    Sometimes "having the initiative" means that I can dictate the terms of the confrontation, limit the movement of my opponent, or press an advantage.

    6. When I make my choices, as player or character, which other actors' choices do I know?

    We can play with chronology here in various ways. Perhaps I can win a chance to act first in exchange for *ignoring whatever is going on around me*. That has some interesting implications; in this model, the most careful and thoughtful actor has the best information, but therefore acts last. (The opposite of the "reverse declaration" method I mention above.) The least informed, most reckless, acts first, but is making their choices blind (or practically blind).

    7. Are the number and sequence of "moves" intended to be balanced or equivalent?

    In most D&D-derived games, there is an attempt to give each actor an equal number of opportunities.

    There is no need for this to be the case, of course. Perhaps seizing the initiative means I get to act, act, and act some more. My opponent doesn't get a chance to act at all, unless they can reverse the situation and seize the initiative themselves.

    Or perhaps we wish to play with the dramatic balance of actions. (For instance, I've seen games where a group of PCs attack a monster, and the sequence of turns is PC 1 - GM - PC 2 - GM - PC 3 - GM - PC 4 - GM, and so on. In practice, from a dramatic perspective this feels like a good "back-and-forth". However, it means that the monster is taking four times as many actions in a given period of time as each PC. This could vary based on the number of actors involved instead of being based in any kind of simulation or "game balance".)

    8. Does anyone have the option to decide who acts and when during the process itself?

    Sometimes the "initiative" is a static process we go through; in other rules, it is something we manipulate during the actual play - in this variety, my "turn" might not be about doing something as a character, but it might be about making sure that I delay the opponent's move long enough for my friend to get in their own move...

    Hopefully that gives someone some inspiration. It's off the top of my head, so I'd love to hear what other variables can be uncovered and then mutated...

    You and David both seem to be inclined toward a simultaneous-action system, but I'm not sure how I would model something like "a fighter on your tail" in such.     The big change with simultaneity is that the pursued fighter has to choose maneuvers, flares, etc. on the assumption that the pursuing fighter will continue to pursue/attack. If the pursuing fighter's strategy is to instead make their target waste fuel so they'll eventually be forced to land (so I can steal your ship's secrets without blowing it up first, mwahahaha), then you do your power roll just as I back off to cut off your obvious escape route, and maybe we're both a bit surprised.

    I look at it this way: fictionally, either side can change their choice of action at any time; what the rules tell us is when we look for choices of actions.

    If you look at both combatants simultaneously, they're still each reacting to the last thing the other guy did, they're just no longer doing it one at a time.

    I think I can change my mind faster than you can react to me changing my mind, so I actually like simultaneity fictionally too -- just because I start shooting at you doesn't mean I must continue to shoot at you while you react to that.

    \n An approximation from (old) memory of the initiative system in the Swedish game Eon, which I played a truckload of years ago:

    First you divide the combat up into duels. People don't actually run around attacking first this guy and then that guy; if you're engaged with an opponent, turning away for just a fraction of a second is really dangerous. If there are more opponents on one side than on the other, one or more will have to take on multiple opponents by themselves. That's pretty hard, and if you're not super skilled, you might have to just try to survive long enough for one of your friends to finish their guy and come help you.

    Next, each duel rolls some dice to determine who gets the initialtive. That side is the attacker. The attacker is the only one who can attack! The only thing the defender can do is to keep defending and try to gain the initiative, unless they have some sweet special abilities. This gives rise to a back-and-forth between the opponents and jockeying for position, getting the initiative and using it to deal some damage. As an attacker, you can push on, making sure to keep the initiative at all costs, but that might tire you out. Or you can make one powerful attack, knowing that you'll probably lose the initiative. As a defender, you can take chances and cut corners in order to take back the initiative, or you can be conservative and careful and hope the attacker will make a mistake before you do. And so on.

    Unfortunately, the above was accompanied by a bazillion die rolls, but I think there's a lot of merit in the system and it could be made fun. And for a dogfight, it could work, too. If someone's on your tail, all you can do is try to shake them and gain the initiative; you can't attack until it's YOU who have THEM in your sights.

    * You start with a pool of dice or points.
    * Taking action costs you some number of those.
    * You regain dice/points when OTHER people act.
    * You decide when to take your next turn; it's up to you.
    \nThis could be extra cool if acting first and acting with maximum dice/points have different advantages that need to be weighed against each other. I think there's some of that in Simon's examples, but here's one that's maybe clearer:

    Roll with max. dice/points = "Insta-kill" attempt option available
    Roll with any dice/points = "inflict Disadvantage" option available
    1 Disadvantage means nothing; 2 Disadvantages curtails 1 Action option; 3 Disadvantages curtails 3 Action options

    So the longer you spend "charging up", the more your opponent can incrementally push you into a bad spot.
