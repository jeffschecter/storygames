I ran across this discussion on RPG.net, Adventurer Conquerer King System versus Astonishing Swordsmen & Sorcerers of Hyperborea, when I came across this quote:

\n

ACK doesn\u2019t understand the point of race as class, so it doesn\u2019t do it right.

It occurred to me that I\u2019m not sure I understand the point of it so I thought I\u2019d throw the question over here: what do you think is the point of race as class? Additionally, do you like that aspect of the old style of game or do you prefer the separation of race and class?

    \n Race as class means each type of non-human functions as its own distinct archetype (or stereotype, if you want), and makes the setting (or at least the PC party) more human-dominated.

    Alexander Macris also wrote in that thread:\n
    As designer I don't like "race as class" because I think it makes the demihuman races seem monocultural and bland. I don't like "race separate from class" because it results in min-maxing ("If you want to be a mage, be sure to choose elf"). The solution we adopted for ACKS has neither of these issues.
    \nThis is true! But I like that demihumans become monocultural. I don't think they necessarily become bland, though. At least not much blander than the four human classes. If you want some variety, you could just use a random table to determine what your elf character bleeds (Sean's elf bleeds hummingbirds and you can guess what my elf bleeds).

    With race being separate, humans almost never get any real specialization, so for whichever class you choose, there's some non-human race that does it best, usually. And so you get fewer human PCs. ACKS does it the old Talislanta way, where instead of one race being better at a specific class, each race has their own set of classes.

    \n I don't know about boring, but I prefer race-as-class for the aesthetics of alieness: I have difficulty relating to the liberal humanist modern fantasy world where things like elves are just people like you and me (what's the point if you're just going to make them human ethnicities, that's what I don't get), and making non-humans into classes unto themselves provides just sufficient distance. It's like the game is saying that these beings are so unlike humans that we could not even relate to their internal social processes; the best we can do is to group them all under this one label. Very true to life, that, and in an amusingly ironical way, as it's not just us as the audience who are in the dark about the internal nature of the elf; we the creators, and even we the game designers have chosen to leave it as an unanswerable conundrum at the point where we satisfy ourselves with this simplistic archetypal gloss. Anything we'll come to know about the elf will ultimately have to come from actual experiences in play. It also makes for some really cool character classes: you don't need to choose whether your character is a dwarven priest or a dwarven fighter, he can be both or neither, all within a single class.

    Of course, not playing with non-human PCs at all is also a fine option. In practice my games tend towards extreme humano-centrism, with the technical possibility of playing some sort of non-human characters as well on the occasion. I do avoid what I see as the supreme thematic weakness of trying to have both non-humans and having them be socially viable player characters - if you want to play some thing from the wilderness beyond human ken, why the fuck would you expect the local sheriff to just nod his head happily as you stroll the main street? (Obviously this logic only works for the sort of fantasy world where non-humans are exceptional. But again, if they're not, then we're back at the question of why not just play with human ethnicities if you want to treat your non-humans as human in all ways anyway.)

    \n But seriously, it seems to me (and this is just anecdotal data) that when players have race as an additional element beyond class with which to differentiate their characters, they are more prone to stick to the usual stereotypes (of elves and dwarves, etc). Somebody else is playing a halfling thief? You can make an elven thief! And then both of you play the exact same kind of halfling and elf everyone else plays except they are also thieves. Which tends to be what a lot of people want.

    On the other hand, when your party is comprised of two dwarves and three fighters, there's only so many ways you can differentiate your characters, and actually portraying some semblance of culture (or difference of cultures) is one way to do that. If both dwarf players go with the stereotype, their characters are exactly the same. If they were playing a dwarven cleric and a dwarven fighter, they could both feel absolutely fine about being hearty, ale-guzzling beard-bearers because they have different classes to make them seem different.

    The human-centric idea makes sense and it also makes sense that, over time, as settings became more exotic and less implicitly or explicitly human-centric (and/or the rules sought to be more generic) that there arose a need or desire to split races from classes.
    \nThis is possible, but would have happened before D&D was first published. In original D&D, race and class IS split, but dwarves and halflings can only be fighting-men, while elves can switch between fighting-man and magic-user from session to session. In Supplement I: Greyhawk, Gygax introduced thieves, who could choose any of the four races no problem. The Holmes Basic Set (77) continued this setup, AD&D (78) expanded the number of races and classes and how you could combine them together, and then Moldvay's Basic D&D created race-as-class as we know it in 1981. There's not a linear progression from simple to complex in the published rules themselves.

    \nNot in the way you mean it, no. The classes are largely socially determined according to 19th century European framework (a big part of the campaign is about dealing with the challenges and possibilities of traditional patriarchal societal roles in a strategic context), so they're things like "Gentleman Adventurer", "Expert Commoner", "Exotic Savage" and "Exceptional Female". The latter two are particularly wide and customizable because while the campaign parses identities in terms of simple labels, abilities are determined in more realistic terms; basically this means that the Female class ends up as a bit of a catch-all, there being no particular reason why a woman couldn't do most anything a man can in the setting.

    But the real reason I don't want AD&D 1e or 2e or their simulacra is that I want race as class. Shocking, I know, even I was shocked when I realized that.

    I want all Elf PCs to be the scions of the dying Emerald Empire, who, after a decades-long adolescency of honing their skills at both swordsmanship and wizardry alike (like their merciless forebearers who crossed the Emerald Portal from Arcadia to Tellus, and dropped half the world to its knees with sharp swords and sharper spells), journeying forth from the crumbling spires of the Isle of Mists in their century-long Sojourn to the Lands of Men. Most return to the Isle after their sojourn, disillusioned with the fleeting lives and the ephemeral works of the Young Races, to spend the remaining centuries contemplating the timelessness of Elven art and philosophy.     I want all Dwarf PCs to be envoys from Crownstone, the last citadel of the beleaguered folk who once ruled the warrens beneath the Elves' colonies, all magnificent palaces and temples and plazas carved from the very bones of the earth, bedecked with sparkling gems and precious metals, until the Cataclysmic War with the Elves, turning the dwarven race away from magic and towards their current, unyielding theocracy, after which the humanoids descended on their marvellous citadels and their own Dominion of Khazad crumbled into dust. The Holmes Basic Set (77) continued this setup, AD&D (78) expanded the number of races and classes and how you could combine them together, and then Moldvay's Basic D&D created race-as-class as we know it in 1981. There's not a linear progression from simple to complex in the published rules themselves.

    \n Games aren't the only place this happens; Star Trek, for example, although having every possible reason to use species, frequently refers to race. I suggest it's become a widely used term, for good or ill, in any context which makes a thing of having lots of weird and wonderful "types of people".

    Indeed, with a slight squint it's possible to see the division of race from class as being a negative thing in its own right. It presents a model of tolerance; sure you have a bar in which elves and dwarves and humans mingle, and it all looks happy enough, but what it's also doing is asserting that if people look different then they really are different. Maybe this isn't not supposed to provoke any animosity and all such difference is "tolerated", but to tolerate something implies that it is in some way provocative or disturbing but which you must make an effort to accept. That's actually quite different to seeing a universal humanity in which differences (in the real world) are trivial and insignificant.

    As such, I don't think that the splitting of class from race really does lead to a better arrangement, and my approach has instead been to sidestep the apparently insoluble probem and use human-only settings. The only other way, I think, would be to put elves and dwarves and whatnot back into the realm of "monsters", genuinely supernatural, alien beings. None of this, however, is fantasy-as-we-know-it (although it is arguably quite good Sword & Sorcery).

    Or, you just have to day fuck it, my game is not intended to say anything about the. real world, it is should not be read too deeply and over-analysed, it is just a bunch of genre conventions strung together.

      \n I think race-as-class works best when you want to emphasize that race's unique approach to combat, magic, or what have you. If dwarves are just fighters with a saving throw bonus, you don't need to worry about it. But when, say, elves uniquely combine the abilities of fighting-men and magic-users, and have unique rules for how they progress in both of those skill sets, it helps to treat them as a distinct class. Ditto when you want to emphasize the guerrilla aspect of halflings, or what have you.

      I think ACKS does this aspect of race-as-class really effectively. Dwarves aren't fighters and clerics and thieves, they're vaultguards and craftpriests and delvers. In the ACKS context, demihuman racial limits also have a demographic meaning, and they have implications for how large demihuman domains can be.

      Keeping them separate is good when you want to treat each race's approach to classes as a matter of combining general skill sets. Then you can just list the multiclass combinations they're eligible for.

All elves are x.
      For a similar reason, I'm not a big fan of the D&D racial ability bonuses where all elves are nimble and wise, but not so strong. It also, I feel, limits the (Although I really dig the 13th Age, which has an interesting perspective of that mechanic. One ability bonus(from a choice, often one physical and one mental) and a trait is given to the race, then one is given by class from a choice, to represent training.)
      I also quite like the ACKS, which does give the races a mechanical cultural distinction, which can be built on in play. I discussed a similar method for a low-ish fantasy earth analogy setting, too, with a set of core classes (Fighter/Rogue/Mage/Priest/Minstrel) that could only be actually taken within cultural archetypes, and so worked very differently (Such as Bard/Skald/Rhapsode/Ashik).
