\n In another thread, Jeff said:\n
We played a simplified form of Polaris for one-shots; it didn't go great. I think that some of our players just didn't appreciate the "but, only if" mechanic...but I think the real issue was unfamiliarity with the mechanic and not knowing how to use it well. With the variant rules we played with, I think it was important to use some restraint when employing the mechanic. In one story the mechanic was basically used to demolish another's character in every scene...their was no real tension and no real conflict, and it just made for a bad frustrating story. In the other story the players lost interest in the mechanic; I think it was due to a learning curve issue and some misunderstanding of the rules...we just weren't able to use it, in a consistent enough way, to improve the story.
\nI want to hear all about this! What were the variant rules?

In Polaris it's super-important to start with a firm understanding of what everyone is supposed to be trying to do. The antagonist has to know that they aren't trying to defeat or even stop the protagonist, they're trying to make their life interesting and introduce a cost to the things that protagonist wants. Otherwise it's a disaster.

    \n Yeah, Polaris is collaborative - like most similar Forgite story games, it presupposes that you're using the rights and privileges of the rules to help chair and regulate the various perspectives and creative impulses of the players for a common goal, and not so much to create conceptual "free range" provinces within which the players are each individually free to do whatever they wish. Just about every privilege in the game has an implicit "only use this liberty to advance your role in the cooperative" tag to it.

    Bringing the latter type of rules-understanding to a game with the former type of presumption tends to lead to degenerative play, as the game is designed to provide "liberty with responsibility" rather than "absolute liberty"; the game is able to give freedom and options without excessive fencing, but only by assuming that the players will utilize those freedoms for the common good. What fencing there is has a normative and pedagogical function rather than being a systemic attempt at security.

    For a contrasting example of what a rules system with consistent "free range" thinking looks like, consider Universalis: it does not presume per se that the players have any sort of creative cohesion to their play, and in fact the covert negotiation over the creative coordination is much of the point of play - you only get to the point where Polaris starts after you've been playing Universalis for a while. In the meantime, play is maintained by a set of cold, soulless and abstract rules about point-buying fictional elements, voting down disruptive initiatives, and so on. Technically speaking it is not a procedural problem for Universalis if a player is a jerk; you just keep parrying and counter-parrying until the cows come home, and a play will have been had, roughly in the way the game text envisions.

    I personally find it very curious that regardless of the seemingly great difference between the "power and responsibility" school and the "free range" school of thought, both types of games are best played by soulful, observant individuals with curiousity and respect towards the emerging fiction. One would assume that a proper free range game would suit superficial players who could entertain themselves and each other like a proverbial herd of bulls in a china shop, but ultimately it seems to be difficult for such players to be interesting. It's not that a free range game breaks down so much as it never goes anywhere if the players don't listen to each other and work together. In this regard I suppose that although Polaris may be misplayed due to a misunderstanding about the modal nature of the conflict rules, the fundamental problem is not that somebody's a "free range player" - the problem is that they're so insensitive that they keep doing the unfun thing again and again, instead of examining their assumptions a bit and discovering a different way of collaborating with the other players.

    \n That's actually really common on blood opera games, Paul. The issue is rather universal, and pretty much my only cure for combatting it is to try to get people to play less like a director and more like they were their own character.

    This only happens with players who are wholly on board with the storifying creative agenda, by the way - the issue is technical and perhaps psychological. The problem would also not occur if the game was not an advocacy game, but rather something that genuinely had room for the players to relinquish their advocacy role.

    Polaris, though, is not that kind of game - it fully requires the players to claim their roles and play them. Even a player who hopes for a tragic end to their character needs to act like a GM, bite the bullet, play their role and hope for the other players to play theirs. If the antagonist is doing their job, they will bring on the tragedy, while you try to avert it. You might not try too hard, in which case you'll get that tragedy, but you still need to try for the game to function.

    Because advocacy-based games are so common, this problem is also pretty common. The only cure seems to be to make it clear to the players that they don't have the option, the luxury, of giving up before the story's over. Your job of advocating for your character is not a privilege, it's a duty.

If you want to die, I'll fight to save your life, so that you can contemplate your own misery. If you've embraced your grand tragedy, I'll try to turn it into some petty failure or bittersweet ending - so that to have your tragedy the way you want it you'll have to earn it, to fight for it.
