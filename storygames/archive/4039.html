\n Most of you don't know anything about unWritten, so I'll briefly describe it then I'll go into my first playtest. (Its actuall not my first playtest, its my first Actual Play)

unWritten is a game that allows you, the players, to create a setting, determine the games premise, and create the protagonists in the story. Game play consists of the players using the hero's journey to structure play, with a rotating GM each scene. Within each scene the hero's journey indicates the types of conflicts to push.

A couple of weeks ago my son, girlfriend, and I sat down to play a game of unWritten. My son, Julian is 15 and compares everything to D&D, my girlfriend hates RPG's for the sake of hating them.

So sue me, I twisted their arms.

This version of the game (I made changes as a result of this playtest) starts out asking you to define the genre and the narrative constraint, then it goes into several questions that help you refine the setting. The questions focus on (in this order) environment, history (natural and social) society, and key setting characters. That may seem like a lot, and it can be, but the players are encouraged to give brief stereotypical answers. It'll show in the playtest.

Then you define the games premise, answering more questions that this time focus on key the game elements that the characters will be dealing with, and then you define the cues. This portion of setting creation builds off of the things you did in the previous section...

Needless to say these have been mightly streamlined!

Now you create the characters, answer 3 questions about the group, then three about each character. Now you define the characters Traits, of which there are 4, expertise, physical, social, and link. The first three are self explanitory, the fourth, link, is a trait that represents the characters connection to the setting. This can be anything from a sword to a memory.

Now we are ready to begin...
The genre we were after was "Dramatic, Mystery, and Realistic." Despite loving Lost, my girlfriend hates sci fi... go figure.

The narrative constraint was "Dramatic," which is to say that the game was to focus on melodramatic conflicts, stuff that deals with interpersonal relationships and such.

Now keep in mind that we weren't going for historical accuracy, we were going for an interesting and engaging story.

    \n This game didn't even get past the 1st scene. What I got out of this was seeing that I could create a setting in a very mundane environment with mundane characters. I would love to see that premise played out, Inuits struggling with tradition in a modernizing society (which happened to be several hundered miles away... but thats a minor detail) in an RPG.

    Maybe I'll get to actually play this actual play.

    I was actually able to figure out how to streamline the process of setting creation. Everytime I've done with my regular play group it would take a long time, but it was always so much fun and by the end of it we were totally excited about what we going to do in it. But by strong arming my girlfriend and son I saw some where things could be changed for the better.

    Now the setting questions optional and less cues are required, etc.

    This is my first Actual Play, and I feel like I just wrote a novel. Can i get some pointers on what to focus the writing on?
