\n Hey folks, I'm looking to put together my first official playtest of a game our company is developing. We have a few people signed up and ready to go, that part wasn't hard at all.

I come to you because, well, I've never done this before. I've run games, many games, but never a prototype game and never for the purposes of exploring the limits of the rules as much as possible.

What methods, techniques, and advice on session flow and administrivia would you folks suggest for a first-timer?

    \n Ask everyone to take notes.

    Prepare to not have fun; this might very well suck.

    Prepare to have fun; tihs might very well be AWESOME.

    Pay attention to what you do at the table; very often people use GMing techniques of various sorts to paint over holes in the game's text. That's fine, just pay attention to what you do or, better yet, ask a player to pay attention to that.

    Spend time after the game with your players just sitting and talking about what worked and what didn't.

    Take criticism not only gracefully but gratefully.

    Good luck!

    \n Also of note, especially in the early stages of playtest, you do not have to playtest the whole game on the first go. You'll need to playtest the whole game at some point, but you can start with just the basics and then add the elaborated rules in later iterations.

    If you can record the session -- video or audio -- that's good stuff.

    I tend to go for hardcore playtests and tell the players to try and break the game. However, there is a catch to this in that some games require a certain level of buy-in that gamebreaking is totally at odds with. Figure out if the game really needs players to invest and care on their own before asking them to consider the game in a dispassionate and analytical fashion (dispassionate and analytical can happen after play, anyway).

    \n [Edited to note that this cross-posted with Christian and others]
    \n
    \nBe prepared for people to try to break the game - in fact they may even ask if it's OK to do so. For a certain sort of geek like myself it's fun to stress-test the rules and see what taking the extreme options does to play. This is actually pretty useful, but can make play suck, like Judd says. The goal of this is to tell you where you may have to shore up limitations and such, or create different ranges of options.
    \n
    \n
    \nThis one is difficult, but... try not to "fill in the blanks" for the players. That is, if you're one of the designers, then you know how the game is supposed to play. Try to only deliver information that's actually in the book. Even if this is a tad stilted. People playing will not have you or any of the designers there when they play. So they won't have their gaps filled in by somebody who knows the intent of the game. That has to come from the text.
    \n
    \nSince this is so difficult (impossible to do perfectly, really), this is why you also must do independent playtesting where everybody running and playing have only the text to work from. Yeah, sure, if you're available to people online you can fix some perceptions. But the quality of the text is precisely in how little they have to contact you to figure it out.
    \n
    \n
    \nLike Judd says, getting feedback is important. That said, a good technique (one I've only used a couple of times, but works well) is to have people write down comments on paper right after the game ends. Some folks will find this to be a bit of a bore, and won't want to do it. But see if you can get them to do so.
    \n
    \nThe reason for doing this is the same reason that police take statements from witnesses individually. Which is that if you do it in a group feedback session, players perceptions tend to get altered by social patterns. People start to agree with each other, and not state differing perceptions.
    \n
    \nOnce you have gathered their separate feedback, then you can put them together to get group feedback. This actually has it's own benefits. So do both.
    \n

    3. After a bit of testing the rules, then we'd start from scratch and create characters together.
    4. If there is time after that, I want to try and run them through the mini-scenario a second time, this time with the new characters.

    At this early stage, what the players say they have problems (while still worthy of attention) is less important than what you see them doing at the table. What comes naturally to them? What do they naturally do that you don't want them to do? What do they struggle with? What strategies do they adopt to help them play the game? (I'm talking nitty gritty, down to stuff like note taking or taking turns.) Is that stuff in the game? Should it be?

    \n Are the people you're playtesting with friends? You are using "we" a lot, do you have more than one writer? Are the other writers playing the playtest? Are the people playing the playtest already familiar with the rules?

    I think once I know some of these answers I can share better some of the things that have helped me.

    Generally:

    Don't apologize before you play. If you say, "I know this is gonna suck, but . . . " you might set people up to agree with you. I think it's fine to make it clear that it's rough, but that you hope to have fun.

    Design the scenario or guide the game toward testing the parts of the game you think might be weakest or that you have questions on. Since this is your first test, use every part of the buffalo (see if you can get every part of the game played). Joshua disagrees but I think maybe you can touch on everything. If you can't, if the game's too big, don't stretch things and make an unfun play experience to achieve the buffalo-usage. If you don't get to use every part, try to make sure the parts you didn't use get used in the next playtest along with all the stuff you changed and are excited about.

    Solicit feedback in a structured way. Have a series of feedback questions to ask ahead of time rather than just "what did you think?" You might want to dump those questions in this thread and get them critiqued. Also if anyone is quiet, ask them what they thought. If they're uncomfortable talking, ask them to email you. Invite people to email you anything they'd rather not say in-group.

    I think it might be more useful to record the after-game bull session than the game itself (if you had to choose, which you don't, which is just to say I think that's the more interesting part to listen to later and the part you're more likely to forget).

    On the recording front, pay attention to what you tell people and if the game is successful, make sure that whatever you said gets textualized and used in the rules. Judd says a version of this above.

    Don't be stubborn about killing your babies, but don't assume that everything that everyone says is equally valid. My second playtest had a very vocal person who had ideas for the game that were very different from mine and it was hard not to get demoralized or add badwrongstuff to it. I managed, though, I think.

    I do not think you have to bring a functioning text to the game. I'm still playtesting from outlines because I'm not sure enough of the rules and I don't want to do all the work of writing up a bunch of text that I am likely to ditch.
