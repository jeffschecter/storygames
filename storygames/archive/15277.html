\n I'm working on a game about a Generation Ship, sailing through space for over a thousand years. It looks at the changes that occur and how things get weird.

I'm including a d66 list of Decay stuff, which is simply a list of systems and sub-systems which can have things go wrong with them. This includes the people on the ship as well as the ship itself

The broad categories at this point are:

Life Support
Automated Systems
Supply and Storage
The Human Body
Society
Repair and Upkeep

So what are some things in those categories that could suffer failure, decay or simply need to be monitored more extensively than usual? Do those categories seem good to you? Are they too broad or too narrow?

Some examples:

Life Support:
Oxygen and Atmosphere, Heat Management, Waste Management

Supply and Storage:
Food Supply, Water Supply

The Human Body:
Reproduction, Degeneration

    \n Society:
    Linguistic drift \u2014 changes in the language and meanings of words over time. Samuel Delany's novel "The Ballad Of Beta-2" is a great exploration of this. (Of course this is only significant if the ship is contacted by people from the original culture. But you could have fun making up a language to use in the ship.)

    Human Body:
    Mutation of pathogens. It doesn't take much more background radiation to increase the rate at which bacteria and viruses mutate, meaning that new diseases could pop up rapidly.

    SF writer Charles Stross has written a couple of lengthy essays on just how difficult it would really be to run a starship; you might find them useful. A couple of them:
    http://www.antipope.org/charlie/blog-static/2009/11/designing-society-for-posterit.html
    http://www.antipope.org/charlie/blog-static/2009/11/the-myth-of-the-starship.html

    \n Life Support
    - Fire. Filter failure. Severe artificial gravity fluctuations. The ship's plants die. Heating issues. Insulation failure. Widespread fungal infection and no antifungals in sight.

    Automated Systems
    - Computer crash. Door sensors are off. Reactor issues.

    Supply and Storage
    - Theft and misuse.

    The Human Body
    - Unanticipated baby boom (or infertility). Long-term genetic damage from radiation causes mutation, and not the X-men kind.

    Society
    - Mutiny. Dealing with the elderly. Serial killer goes after a particular profession. Younger generation stops believing in the destination.

    Repair and Upkeep
    - Out of replacement X (filters, lightbulbs, heating elements, diapers).

    \n So, it depends on how gravity is generated on your ship, but over 1000 years there's crap that can happen to you neurologically and physiologically speaking from living in artificial gravity. You might not notice it until you get off the ship though. For example, if the ship is a giant rotating torus dealie, then there's a slight inner-ear thing that can happen where you get dizzy if you move too fast. What would happen is, in the first 10 years or so everyone gets dizzy frequently, potentially stumbling and falling over. In the next 100 years or so, people earn not to rush around everywhere. In the next 1000 years, depending on your theory of how fast people evolve (let's say fast, because it's more fun), people's bodies adapt and they stop getting dizzy. Then they arrive at their destination; now they get dizzy when they move fast because the destination isn't a rotating torus.

    I'm sure there must be lots of little "humans not designed for this type of environment" effects of this kind. Effects of prolonged odd diet, for instance.

    Other stuff: what about wider knowledge, technical knowledge? If your best engineer dies and a virus deletes all the engineering textbooks, you could be left with an inexperienced apprentice working from his class notes running the whole ship. (Maybe that's tools and skills.)

    What about classic star trek type stuff? Spores get on board somehow. The ship gets hit by a meteor. A bacterium mutates and everyone starts getting sick. Stellar radiation causes some weird genetic abnormality. And so on. Maybe these fit into your current categories, I dunno.

    \n Systems and subsystems
    * Propulsion
    ** power supply (connection to power system)
    ** reaction mass (collector if a Bussard system, stored mass otherwise)
    ** thrust system (the actual thing that throws out the mass)
    ** shielding
    ** control
    * Power
    ** fuel
    ** heat management
    ** exhaust/waste dumping
    ** shielding
    ** control
    ** transmission (getting power to other systems)
    * Life support
    ** power supply
    ** expendables
    ** recycling systems
    ** heat management
    ** transmission (getting water/air/food to people)
    * Navigation
    ** observation (telescopes, &c.)
    ** computation
    ** power supply
    ** interfaces
    * Command and Control
    ** interfaces
    ** power supply
    ** security maintenance
    ** automation
    ** monitoring
    ** logging
    ** diagnostics
    ** access control
    * Entertainment
    ** power supply
    ** interfaces
    * Automation
    ** power supply
    ** interfaces
    ** software maintenance
    ** transmission (automating other systems)
    ** access control
