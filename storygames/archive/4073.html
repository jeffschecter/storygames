\n I certainly do like game sessions and game systems that lend themselves to over-the-top, loud, gonzo actions. I'm a big fan.
\n
\nI'm also a fan of sessions and systems that lend themselves to quiet action. I enjoy playing a game where people can clearly hear the things that a character can't quite bring themselves to say, and where a whispered confession bears more weight than a shouted monologue.

\nI know a lot of systematic ways to encourage the former. I don't know a lot of systematic ways to encourage the latter. Any suggestions?

    \n Hi Tony,

    What occurs to me immediately is the reward mechanic. Some systems, as Shreyas says, are all about "faster, harder, more violent!" But not all.

    Primetime Adventures     is opened ended in this regard. Fan Mail is rewarded for -- well, anything that hits another player's fancy. If you take the time to establish the focus of the content up front, it should help people get on the same page for delivering that kind of content.

    Bonus dice in Sorcerer, likewise, can be awarded for anything. Describing how the PC is remembering the murder of his son as he draws his gun, focusing on a simply touch in an encounter between two PCs (I'm thinking here of the moment in Silence of the Lambs when Lector is in the big iron barred cell and she passes him the files and he touches her fingers lightly -- the first human contact in years.)

    I"m sure there are others, but it's 6 in the morning for me right now. But the key is, at least these two systems can easily reward the quiet, the half-spoken. Remember, we're out of the land of Players Only Speak in Character, so you can lay out the dialogue and then add, "But all he's thinking is that he doesn't want her to leave."

    I'm not sure I'm seeing your concern about being "noticed." If someone points at you and says, "What's your guy do?" at that moment everyone is going to notice what you're doing. Right? I mean, it's not a matter of not being "noticed." People at the table might not care. But that's a completely different issue.

    As Matthijs points out, establish style and genre as a focus. And if you need to pull focus when your turn comes, take the time to set it up and see if you sell it well and get the reward from the group. Also, think more in terms of the "internal life" of the character found in novels, and less in terms cinema and comics books -- which usually reveal life only through external deeds.

    There's no guarantee you'll get the reward, of course. But, again, as Matthhijs points out, that comes down to the players and the set up for the game.

    If this has been a problem, can you give specific examples? Or is this one of those times where the human brain starts worrying about something that could happen, instead of just pressing forward and seeing what happens?

    CK

    PS a tangent from some cross-posting from the last few posts:

    On the matter of system: There's a trend growing/coming down the road, in my view, where the rules of reward are so clearly spelled out as to guarantee there's never miscommunication, disappointment or lack of clarity about what is good.

    While I admire the impulse, to me, it's like asking all the participants to wear foam padding suits at the table. It starts removing the risk of creative endeavour from the players. And that risk is, in my view, one of the things that makes Narrativist play Narritivist play. (Some folks don't care about Narrativist play, and I know and respect that, so please know I'm not schooling you to play Narrativist play; I'm saying what I like about a certain kind of play.)

    Just as in Gamist play the competition is, "Will I successfully use these rules to win?" in Narrtivist play it's, "Will I use the tools and techniques of story well to reveal what matters to me?" If there's no risk -- if we're all wearing foam padded suits -- I'm just not that interested. It takes the sport of it out.

    That's why I specifically referenced PtA and Sorcerer. In both cases the reward comes from nothing more than the group -- instinctively, as audience members and peers -- responding to the whatever the fuck causes aesthetic pleasure that makes us go, "Yeah, that was good."

    So, it's a function of system. But not a completely, "Here, do this and you get and extra die," kind of system.

    To each his own. But I wanted to point that out.

    Why "shamefully"?

    I'll be blunt -- and I guess I can be blunt, since the example is fictional:

    I'm not buying Ed's line. That is, if we were playing Primetime Adventures, he's not getting Fan Mail from me. The details are lacking, the "sell" isn't there. Ed's making a play at something -- but it's not clear, not particularly creative. It clearly means something to Ed -- and I buy that. But Tony's point is Rachel is supposed to notice/care. And all I can say is, Ed needs to find a way of phrasing the beat with more poignancy/impact/something.

    Just because we think something is important doesn't mean that importance we've communicated that importance to other people at the table. Fan Mail in PtA is all about that -- did your peers get it.

    Sure, sometimes the joke or the fun-bit-of-violence is (maybe) easier to get the the Fan Mail for. But why is that? Is it because that's what this group responds to? Is it because a kick-to-the nuts is easier to communicate than a soulful moment? There are a ton of questions to be asked here.

    In Tony's example, Rachel apparently is only focused on fighting. She got what she got from Ed's plaintiff sigh. Okay. In one view, that's fine. But Tony, I'm assuming, is assuming that Ed is disappointed that Rachel is only interested in taking his character's plaintiff sigh as a kick off for a charge. Not only does her character apparently miss the beat, but so does Rachel. And that, apparently is a problem.

    My other question is: Why is this a problem? Ed has his moment. As Tony points out, no one's going to notice everything. So, why is he carrying around his disappointment Rachel didn't get more from it? I mean that. Really, what's the deal?

    And all I can say is, if Ed really wants to turn the direction at the table ("how the session hits us") he must make that beat clear. Right now, not clear.

    Unsubtle fictional counter-example:

    In The Iliad, when a character is cut down outside the walls of Troy, Homer often does this quick flashback, where he'll describe how the character was raised by his mother and father. He describe how the character, as a boy, played with his dad, and how he laid his head in his mother's lap. And then he'll describe how the father is already dead and the mother will never have her son lay his head in her lap again. And then we cut back to the kid hitting the ground outside the walls of Troy.

    Now, in my view, if Ed wants to actually get the group onboard with his sigh, he's going to have to unpack it if he wants people to notice it. He's going to have to do some work and not expect everyone to just come along for the ride because he means well and he's doing something emotional and significant.

    I means, describing, in that sigh, what his childhood was like with his PC's brother. It means eliciting incidents that have impact on the other players at the table. It means grabbing the reigns of the narrative for a moment or two and earning the Fan Mail.

    We often think people should give us our dues because we mean well or something is a good idea. But in fact we have to communicate it and make it clear.

    Ed might have his guy sigh -- but I don't know what that means. It's an indication of something, obviously. But it's a blip.

    The issue is not about the "size" of the moment. It's about the clarity, the specifics and the drama of the moment through concrete details.

    Is Ed willing to put himself out there and take a risk to have the other players come along with him through some clear, creative effort. Because, as it stands in the fictional example, I'm not surprised Rachel rode over that moment. Give me a Homeric flashback. Give me a St. Crispin's Day speech. Give me Ed's character drawing his sword, and have Ed describe his character remembering how the two brother's once promised, when they got the swords as boys to go on adventures together. Give me something concrete and detailed and clear -- or just give me the damned fight.

    \n There's been a lot of talk about how to use the carrot, but that's only part of the "training tool" dichotomy. There's also the stick.

    If you make the loud, gonzo, and over-the-top actions painfully dangerous then people are more likely to look for alternative outs. People avoid gunfights when the rules are set up so that only 5% of people that enter one end it without being maimed or dead. Likewise if it's impossible to start with the amount of driving skill necessary to pilot your motorcycle up a spiral staircase, people are more likely to stick to the road.

    If you couple that with a rewards system that does things like offer bonus experience for a session without violence or playing through a dialog where at least one member of the group is impressed by the dramatics, you've got a system with clear markers for how its meant to be used.

    Okay, so here's how lamentation and suffering work in MSKR.

    \n

    When you hurt someone, they accumulate suffering. Suffering reduces your effectiveness. You don't want to carry around suffering.

    \n

    The way you get rid of suffering is to have lamentations, where you go to some friend or family member and talk about your problems. This turns suffering into revenge chips, which you can cash in to hurt the originator of that bit of suffering extra-hard. But the revenge chips just sit on that other character; you have to interact with them in some third context to actually capture them and arm yourself for your next clash. In fact, whoever interacts with them net gets the opportunity to claim those chips.

    \n

    So there you have an escalating spiral of violence, but it's modulated by these interludes of lament and conversation which are necessary for the escalating spiral to continue, and every such moment gives you a chance to reexamine your involvement in the struggle, and jockey for a position in it. You may pounce on an opportunity to carry water from the well with Draupadi, because it lifts a very real weight from your character's shoulders, or arms you with new tools to hurt someone.

    \n Damn, Mark beat me to it. While, of course there's a lot to be said about technique, Tony's looking for mechanics, I believe, and I think that it's doable.
    \n
    \nIn Universalis, in fact, during development, we at one point had a mechanic to meter out progress of plots. You created a plot idea, and then put in X amount of coins into that plot, and then when they were gone, the plot ended.
    \n
    \nIt wasn't a good mechanic, so that's why it's not in the game anymore. But it's indicative of what you can do to meter out events. Basically have something be like, for lack of a better term, "long-term stakes." And then, like saving in your 401K, people invest toward it's end. If you try to withdraw the results too early, you get a "substantial penalty." If you will.
    \n
    \nWhat's good about this is that the player can choose to rush to a conclusion if he feels that it's neccessary by making the decision to burn the extra rewards he'd get by otherwise waiting. This makes each decision on the path one with a mechanical repercussion, informing the decisions all along the path. You'll tend to get longer paths to results, but, where especially appropriate, you'll get the quick dramatic conclusion.
    \n
    \nYou can even spike this by making it a "Tragedy of the Commons" situation. That is, say you have a pool for a certain outcome building, where it goes up by the number of scenes played, each scene (so at the end of the first you have 1, at the end of the second 1+2 = 3, at the end of the third 1+2+3 = 6, etc). Any player can take the pool at any time by moving to resolution. But, of course, the longer you wait, the larger the reward.
    \n
    \nJust a thought. Adding rewards to incentivize moving to a resolution over time can be thought of as the "Puerto Rico Prospector" method. Doing so with penalties for early withdrawal can be called the 401K method.

    \n Tony, Perfect does this in semi-system ways.

    First of all, you commit a crime. You automatically succeed at your crime, be it stealing patterned fabric from the tailor or burning down the Chief Inspector's home. For some people, this is big incentive to go big or go home, because your crime will always succeed regardless of difficulty or scope. For others, it takes the "Oh, I have to play to the hilt" edge off, surprisingly. I think that this is because when you give limits to what's achievable, people will always push for achieving those limits. When you widen the playing field and say "do whatever you want, just make it matter to your character" then you end up seeing some really low key and personal crime rather than crazy-gonzo-all-the-time.

    That said, the phenomenon I most often see is escalation, escalation, gonzo escalation, totally low-key counterpoint that brings everything down to a really symbolic level all over again. Rinse, repeat.

    For example: someone steals a locket from an ex-lover (low key). someone busts into an art gallery and replaces a sanctioned painting with their own (unsanctioned) work (pretty big). someone torches a building for revenge (big). someone puts the bible back into print and circulation (crazy gonzo!). someone breaks curfew to deliver a love poem to a girl he's never introduced himself to (low key).

    The juxtaposition between epic gonzo and understated simplicity that exists within game sessions of Perfect is great.

    Now, how is this dealt with in a more mechanical sense?

    Well, the economy of the scene (how many points the GM gets to spend, what the reward level will be, what the consequences might be) is all determined by two questions the GM asks... one of the player and one of himself.

    Question to the player: On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does this matter to your character?
    Question to oneself: On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does this upset the balance of society?

    So if you say 6 and I say 9, I combine the two and have a total of 15 points to spend on adversity this scene.

    The important thing about the questions is that they don't address scope or magnitude of a crime. Sneaking out after dark to see the lunar eclipse might be a 10 for a character. Torching the Inspector's Yard building might be a 4. Similarly, maybe you marking your grey suit with an undetectably subtle pink tint would totally earn you a 10 from the society if I were GMing that game. Killing a man? Well, that could be anywhere from a 4 to a 10, depending on who and why.

    The mechanics give even playing field to setting the dials at 4 or 11, in my mind.

    \n I think TheCzech just asked the same question I was going to ask, so let me look at it the other way.
    \n
    \nIf what you want is subtlety at the gaming table, where the subtle nuance is being introduced by a player and not the GM (and are therefore denied the opportunity to use a pause, quieter voice, different mood setting tools, or a description that draws attention to the fact that this little quiet detail is nevertheless a big thing), then your problem is not that you can't have a quiet scene, but that a quiet moment in the middle of a noisy scene will almost inevitably be swallowed up by the action going on around it.
    \n
    \nNow in a movie such a moment usually gets marked out in ways that go beyond the moment itself. The music stops or changes, the camera focuses closely on the character, we get a shot of the glance, then a closeup of the sigh, then the shake, then a longshot in which his words are the only things we can hear. In a manga we'd get 7 different frames for all of this before the big one where the fight starts. In other words the quiet moment gets a huge frame around it so that we can't possibly miss how important it is. Its like a little drawing in an art gallery, which almost always gets a huge white mount around it, so that even from a distance we can see that there is something there, even if its far smaller than the 10 by 10 oil painting next to it. (The other gallery option is to put the little pictures in a room of their own, where they are all big compared to each other, but that's a quiet scene full of quiet moments, which is different.)
    \n
    \nSo how do you get all that framing on the gaming table? One option is clearly to use system to do it. When you lay down the card, or chip, or spend a point you are saying to everyone "Look, this moment is important to me, and I think it should be to you as well, now pay attention". then you get to pause, wait a moment, and introduce your poignant moment. In this case System is like all the movie tricks, giving importance to something that can't command attention by itself.
    \n
    \nThe alternative is just to tell the other players, as an aside, or out of character, that this is important. Your fellow players, after all, are just human. They aren't inside your head, knowing what matters to you, and your gaming table probably doesn't come with a score, and close in camera angles. Sometimes their just going to miss things, just as they miss the fact that someone was trying to take a combat action before them, or that you had to pop to the loo when they weren't looking. So if you don't have rules to make it obvious that this little moment really counts for something, you can always tell them.

    But again, in actual play, I've seen it (from systems like The Riddle of Steel, The Shadow of Yesterday, Polaris (!), With Great Power, etc) ALWAYS play out like #1, and never to #2 or #3. The "turning down" was needed because of a problem of perception, namely one player jumping ahead, without understanding the player's intent.

    I've even been Rachel. Frex, in our current With Great Power game, I've at least 2-3 times in about 6 sessions jumped in screaming:
    GM: Conflict!
    Matthew: ...Um, I'm just not feeling it.
    or
    Matthew: What's at stake?
    GM: ... ... ...uh... Well, you guys seem opposed right now... Tell you what, keep on going and let's see what happens.
    (scene resolves, without conflict)

    In a way, me as GM jumping in to find conflict was just as "jumpy ahead" as the Rachel above trying to get to the fight scene. When I backed up and realized that everyone was on the same page But Me, it was easy to say, "*Oh*, yeah my bad. Continue!"

    Again, though, that's all social stuff. Well, a piece of social advice you could write down, and thus codifying it into a "rule" without having a Number or Mechanic attached.

    -Andy

    \n Heya Tony,

    I've got this thing, this idea bouncing around in my brain. You know how DitV gives mechanical weight to words by assigning dice to them? Like, words can be stronger than guns in that world? Ok, so what if we had a system that gave weight to "Something Implied". Your character shrugs his shoulders and turns his back on my character. He's not saying anything. He's avoiding saying things. He's implying. That's step 1.

    Step 2 is communicating, player to player, that your character is hitting me with the weight of his implications. I wanna know that this is a clear situation. I mean, if I call down the katana-rockets after just plain missing the implication, well that won't be awesome for anyone. But then, the communication between you and I should probably be quiet too, right? Hand signals might work. I've got this image in my head of us having a small selection of thought-bubbles on sticks that we can quietly hold up to our temples. That would be cool.

    Step 3 is to reward me for "Interpreting Implied Things". Ok, so you've hit me with your quiet gunshot of implication. The only way I can respond to your character's implication, without suffering the consequences of the mechanical weight of your implication, is to make an interpretation. Not necessarily a correct interpretation. Any interpretation. Then maybe my character gets to steal the mechanical weight you levered when you brought down the implication. Or something. I can't help but imagine that one player's interpretation of another player's quiet implication will also be quiet. Subdued.

    Now Rachel gets to know what you're up to while you're doing it, can consider that you're willing to reward and/or punish her for responding or failing to respond. Not only that, but if Rachel starts punching people in the brain at that moment, you can know that she didn't miss what you were doing, she just chose to respond to it differently than you wanted her to. Which I imagine will be way more satisfying than assuming she just didn't hear what you were saying between the lines.
