What the hell... Board Game Design Challenge?

DanielSolis
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Directed Promotion
I've been trying to think of a way to get some traffic to Luchacabra after a summer-long hiatus, grease up the old board game design gears, and see some more RPG designers tackle the issues unique to board games as they did in Vincent's game-game contest. To promote Luchacabra, the competition would follow the methodology of the Luchacabra board games: being cheap, using common props, and getting as much emergent stuff out of as few rules and props as possible. The rules would be something like:

* Entries must be playable with two players but multi-player options are acceptable. (Shannon's my only reliable playtester and getting any more people would be like herding cats.)

* Possible game are limited to what I've got around the house. This includes a chess board, a mancala board, a backgammon board, checkers, a standard chess set, 3 sets of 36d6 (red, blue and green), four sets of 10d10 (red, purple, blue and green), six sets of about 20 glass beads (red, purple, blue, white, clear and gold) and a deck of playing cards. Not all props need to be used, and in fact, the fewer props used the better.

* Games would be judged on clever use of the game props, mechanical elegance, and emergent complexity (seemingly simple rules that lead to unexpectedly deep gameplay).

None of this will happen until I get the bugs fixed in Luchacabra's css n' stuff, but I just wanted to gauge interest. So, what's your interest?

    I do have a fairly limited set of props listed, but I was considering something even more restrictive. I've gotten a lot of mileage out of just d6s and a chess board.

    Me personally, I'm not so concerned with theme in board games. I'm looking for a fun diversion that takes up about half an hour or so. That having been said... I do often come up with a mechanic first, I then look for theme that best suits that mechanic, then take inspiration from that theme to flesh out the mechanics.

    In essence, the theme is a often just mnemonic device to better understand the rules. The rules may also better help understand the theme, but that's a more difficult design goal that often leads to a heavy-handed "message" at the expense of playability and fun. It can be done, and when it's done well it's amazing, but it's very hard.

    What if it weren't a thematic constraint, but more of a constaint of premise of the challenge itself? Something like:

    Sir Leonard Wooley was overseeing excavations in the ancient mesopotamian city of Ur when his team discovered two board games, what would later be called the Royal Game of Ur. It was only years later that the rules were discovered on a cuneiform tablet that dates almost 2000 years after the dating of the games themselves. Until then, there were few clues as to how the game was played or what insights it may provide about the culture that created it. Sooo....
    What if we were architects from the far future? We have recently recieved word that the following artifacts were excavated from the site of a very ancient culture:

    * A board with an 8x8 grid of alternating light and dark squares
    * A small pouch containing...
    * Eight red cubes with dots etched on each side, numbering from 1 to 6.
    * Eight green cubes with dots etched on each side, numbering from 1 to 6.
    * Four red glass beads
    * Four green glass beads

    There were no written rules found anywhere near the artifact. The rest of the excavation uncovered a complete burial chamber of an ancient royal couple and their child, who all died under unknown circumstances. The four walls of their chamber bore murials depicting the sun, moon, stars and astronomical phenomenon. The game itself was found on a pedestal in the center of the room. The board was situated on this pedastal in such a manner that the board's corners pointed towards the walls of the chamber.

    Each of us have been given replicas of these artifacts and challenged to reconstruct how the game was played and what significance it had in this culture.
    Would something like this be cool?
