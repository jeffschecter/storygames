\n For the Make Game$ Fast contest I'm writing a game about Vegas.

There are myriad parallel worlds, and Vegas exists in all of them.

At the start of the game, each player shares a memory of Vegas. Those memories are used to compose the plane of existence in which the game takes place.

But I've never been to Vegas!

Please share your memories of Vegas to help inspire me to write this game.

If I get enough, maybe I'll add them to the Story Game Situation Book to.

    \n All I know about Vegas I've learned from CSI. And Swingers (Vegas, baby!). And Oceans 11, 12 and 13. So my memory is secondhand.

    Apart from that layover when I had an hour to wander the predawn streets. I walked down the Strip a little. I could see cold, clean light on the desert horizon, but a constant dirty warm pulse of humanity surrounded me. And it wasn't the Elvises, the drag queens or the young couples that caught my eye. It was the workers finishing shifts, making no eye contact, weaving expertly between gawkers as they made their way to cabs and cars. Crumpled polyester workers from casinos, shows, theatres and theme bars.

    \nAround that hour, there's also the complusive gambler moms pushing baby carriages around between casinos, the crush of sex industry workers finally getting their chance for a drink just before the sun breaks the surrounding peaks, the bitter glances that minimum wage earning hotel and bar staff sneak at tourists who push wealth and entitlement in their faces every day, the strange emptiness that settles in when someone finally turns off all the neon, the cops made so skittish by encroaching gang migrations that they're apt to shoot you for looking at them the wrong way, the wary look you'll get from any stranger who's trying to figure out what your angle is... even if you're just asking for the time.

    Away from the center of things, there's the obligatory strip mall on every corner, nestled between blocks upon blocks of cookie-cutter frame stucco houses that They are mindlessly filling up the desert with, despite the fact that they don't sell for the most part, stand empty, and gather economic and literal dust.

    Vegas is capitalism's Vatican - everything glorious and terrible about the rule of the Almighty Dollar is reflected in its streets, its architecture, and its inhabitants. It wears a history of lawlessness, murder, and vice as a badge of honor. Its mayor, now in his third term, was a defense attorney for Mafia gangsters.

    I love it here.

    \n I sat at a poker table at MGM for 14 hours one day, playing 1-2 NLH. Since they bring you free drinks, I had four consecutive Red Bulls, then nearly fell out of my chair from the shaking. When I got back to my room, my friends were drinking vodka and cran, and poured me a drink. After downing the first one in a gulp, I asked for another, then made a bet that I could stay conscious for ten minutes. I lost, with seven minutes to spare.

    I watched the biggest (semi-regular) poker game in the world being played in Bobby's Room at the Bellagio. Sam Farha and Jennifer Harmon were there, Phil Ivey showed up.

    One day, having not seen anyone I knew for days despite being there with about 12 guys, I ran into some of them on the floor of the casino where we were staying, and they asked if I wanted to go to a basketball game. I didn't have much else to do, so I went. We got stoned in the car on the way to the Thomas and Mack center, and then watched Team USA demolish Puerto Rico. Watching Dwayne Wade, Lebron James, Gilbert Arenas and so forth in person was really cool - I'll probably never get another chance. Since I was sitting on the outside, they sent me for food. I had to stand in two different lines, one for the massive hot dogs, one for the beer. By the time I got back to my seat, I had finished my food...and someone else's.

    I've always wanted to go to The Gun Store. Still haven't done it.

    My memories of Vegas, in bullet form:

    \n
      \n
    • Learning that the desert is no place to forget your sunglasses.
      • \n
    • The smell of cigarette smoke, long embedded in the walls.
      • \n
    • The endless ringing and clanging of slot machines. First it's noise. Later it becomes music.
      • \n
    • The luxury rental place that was right next to the Taco Bell. You could get a chalupa and rent a Ferrarri on the same block. * I did one, but not the other.
      • \n
    • Before they shut it down, you could feel the heat from the fireballs at the pirate show.
      • \n
    • I've never met a friendlier stripper, or a crazier one, than the ones I met at The Crazy Horse II.
      • \n
    • Overhearing some guy mumbling "lucky bastard" because he saw us making out as a threesome. Swingers? Yeah. It's so easy in Vegas.
      • \n
    • Standing on the strip and watching the planes land at McCaren.
      • \n
    • Watching the sun set on the desert, while enjoying a steak that cost as much as I'd normally spend on a week's groceries.
      • \n
    • 36 hours of consecutive inebriation. But being careful not to go so far as to risk loosing any memory.
      • \n
    • Drinking a fishbowl of rum while a friend is acosted by Ferengi. "This is why women should not be allowed clothing!"
      • \n
    • Related: Being exactly drunk enough to be completely shocked by the teleporter trick.
      • \n
    • Coming to the realization that, at the end of the Freemont Street Experience (which is mesmerizing) is the hotel that they used in the climax of Cool World. I'm always looking to the top of the building for the "Spike". Always dissapointed when it's not there.
      • \n
    • The drive between Salt Lake and Vegas is an adventure of it's own. The LINE that's drawn between Arizona and Nevada. Arizona = tremendous vertical mountains. Nevada = vast horizontal desert. Don't forget to stop at Wendy's [in] Beaver.
      • \n
    • It's the place where steak and lobster is what burgers and fries are to the rest of the country.

    \n Looking down at the Strip from an unfinished ballroom in the Venetian. It was dark and empty except for the lights from outside, and the floors were still covered with newspaper and construction trash. We weren't even staying at the Venetian, but we'd somehow wandered into the pool complex, and from there we took an elevator up to the highest floor we could get to, where there was a desk for a concierge who wasn't there. In fact, the entire place seemed empty, so we wandered around as quietly as in a church, oggling their collection of jams and the maids' closet with shelf upon shelf of complimentary slippers, and then found a stairway heading up. Once we came back down from the ballroom, an officious security guard with a mustache asked us to leave, which is just what we were doing at that point anyway. I guess his job must get boring -- something about his tone made me think that stopping us totally made his day.

    \n Danielle and I were in Vegas in late August, 2005, for her sister's Jimmy Buffett themed wedding.

    My stand-out memories are:

    The men and women handing out prostitute cards all along the strip. They were in groups of two to six, standing apart on either side of the sidewalk, each with a stack of six or eight individual cards ready to hand to a passer-by. They never said a thing. They just snapped the little stacks of cards in their hands. Snap, and then hold them out for someone to take. Snap. Again. I was collecting the cards with plans to play Pimp. Each hand-off was an economical and polished exchange. I collected 120 cards in the day and a half we were there, just in our travels up and down the strip.

    Ditching the family (who all ended up at Jimmy Buffett's Margaritaville for the third time) on Saturday night to wander the casinos and ultimately discover a duelling piano bar in the Paris. The duellists were fun, and wild. The tables were full at first, so we watched from the doorway. And then when seats opened up we had a good time at a cafe table for an hour, sipping cabernet, and putting in requests.

    Paul

    \n My main memory of Vegas is from many years ago, flying in, at night, in a little plane which we'd taken from the Grand Canyon because our booked flight had been canceled. Miles and miles and miles of dark, empty desert, with the pilot following the river in order to find his way, then suddenly Vegas in the middle of nowhere, lit up like some kind of lurid neon-covered drag queen. We came in over a night baseball game and it felt like we were going to land on the home plate.

    That, and the slot machines everywhere. I've only ever passed through on the way to somewhere else, so I haven't seen any of the entertainment there.

    Mercifully, the couple times I had to walk by the place this past weekend, they were blaring an '80s rock soundtrack rather than Buffet.

    The memory I always like to relate is similar to MikeRM's, and it's of my first view of Vegas. When my friend Jason moved to L.A. in 2000, I went with him for the drive as far as Vegas. Crossing the border of Arizona, just past the city on the border, you can see a bright glow on the horizon, which is Vegas, 140 miles away. After a while, you can make out a distinct white beam stretching straight up from the midst of the glow: that's the light on top of the Luxor, still 100 miles off*. Finally, there's a point where you're clearly up in foothills above the city, but you still haven't seen it, and suddenly you round a curve and spread out below you as far as you can see is a vast valley full of golden lights, and that is Vegas.

    A memory from this weekend: stumbling out of the Excalibur in a daze at 1:30 in the morning, having just spent the last hour and a half walking about 4 miles from Treasure Island down to Mandalay Bay and back, and realizing that the good-looking woman off to the side who is calling and waving is in fact trying to get my attention, and is probably a prostitute (although not dressed particularly slutty).

    Another memory: discovering to my delight that while the casino and hotel exterior of the Aladdin has been converted to its new Planet Hollywood theme, most of the mall is still the colorful "Desert Passage" theme rather than the cold, stark and soulless "Miracle Mile" theme they're changing it to. Vegas is always changing and soon all traces of the Aladdin will be gone, but I'll always have the memory and the photos I took of what it was like.

    *Edit: apparently the border town of Mesquite, NV, on I-15, is only 90 miles at most from the Luxor. But still, you can distinguish the Luxor's beam amidst the general Vegas glow at that distance. I expect to be roundly mocked by Harper for correcting my own story.

    \n You can add mine to Eric's as I was there for all of them. :P

    1. Being in bed between two people, drunk enough that the room was spinning and asking Eric to put his foot on the floor to make the room stop spinning and it working.
    2. Being called Lord and Lady Provost at the Excalibur and not feeling at all odd about it.
    3. Being accosted by a littel green man at the Excalibur.
    4. Being told that this woman would be our wench for the evening and not even thinking twice about eatting a cornish hen with our hands. (Jousting at the Excalibur!)
    5. Dragging a woman nearly topless through the Riveria because she was too warm to keep her shirt closed but her bra was still on and no one thinking anything of it.
    6. Being so drunk upon waking up that my breakfast order included bacon, eggs, toast and a Mai Tai!
    7. Winning $160 on a $1 craps table and having only walked up with $20.
    8. In the same day having the hotdog special for $1.50 at the Westward Ho and then having a $100 steak at Circus Circus.
    9. Two-fers! Oh the mighty two-fer. You can do pretty much anything in Vegas... two for the price of one.
    10. Being able to carry your drinks on the street, and even leave your glass on the curb. It WILL get picked up.
    11. Dancing with a group of people I've never met while waiting to cross the street.
    12. Realizing that 95% of the people you see are not from the area but still know everything about the area.

    I love Vegas!

    \n Flying into Vegas at night, seeing all the lights and facades shining up at you like a big mirage, blink and it's gone.

    Shivering in the Nevada sun in a heated pool in March, watching skinny people sunbathe at the poolside despite the cold.

    Waking up and looking out my hotel room window onto the Strip. It made me think of what it must have looked like when the Devil showed Jesus all the kingdoms of the world.

    Being in Vegas another time for a professional meeting, alone and between jobs... walking the Strip and realizing what a horribly grim place Vegas is when you're not in the mood.

    During the same walk: suddenly coming upon an enormous Buddhist altar set up in a paved area between two hotels. There were Asian people praying there. A real-life moment of Zen. I never found out what it was about.
