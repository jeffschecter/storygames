    \n@Jason - Thanks, I'll keep posting more maps as we go.

    @James - Basically -- the Regiment text will be standalone rules-wise. How you play the Regiment has become distinct enough from AW that we'll have to thoroughly explain everything you need to play it. So, yes, you'll have enough to understand how to play. However, there's a lot that Vincent covers in AW, particularly about storytelling, that I doubt we'll get into.     @Chris - My Thursday group is scattered between Washington, California, and Massachusetts, so it's online or nothing. We've been doing the online pen&paper thing for about 3 or 4 years and have been jumping on whatever new, free tools we can find to make the experience more workable. Currently, we use the Skype conference call feature for audio and we use Infrno for everything else. Infrno has audio and video chat (but we've found Skype to be more reliable for audio), a whiteboard, a dice roller, customizable character sheets, and other handy features. Drawing in Infrno is a pain, so I usually sketch out my maps by hand, scan them in, and then upload them to the Infrno game table in advance of the session and have extra maps scanned and on hand when needed. All in all, we're pretty happy with the system.

    \n We tested fight the enemy out and had a good experience with it. Even the heavy machine gunner, who had to spend 2-gear each time he made the move felt it worked well. Like John said, this is just the first prototype of the move. First impressions: it promotes scarcity in gear and supply in a good way. Previously, few moves actually prompted a gear expenditure and in the first couple sessions of the game, the players - as airborne soldiers - were able to stockpile it. I think there's actually a better balance now with scrounge for spoils.

    I wouldn't say that gear expenditure became a distraction - not any more so than marking stress or wounds. Because of the move, Smith ran out of ammo and had to get more during the fight. Because the fight move now requires gear, there's a strong mechanical tie between the gear you carry (which, in part, represents ammo) and the move that consumes the ammo.

    \n There's an element of redundancy across the three - harm - to varying extent.

    There's an element of confusion between close assault and attack the enemy that a number of play test groups have encountered. Attack the enemy is just about doing harm while close assault is about driving the enemy from their position or forcing them to surrender etc. However, sometimes, folks have wanted to "close assault" the enemy, meaning attack them at close quarters in a way that fits with the name of the close assault move; but, mechanically, should be attack the enemy. Suppress the enemy has always lacked teeth.

    There's also a move count issue, in general, with the Regiment.

    Those three factors, and others, led to our recent initiative to reduce unnecessary moves and to consolidate like moves. We felt that attack the enemy, close assault, and suppress the enemy could be combined into a single move with the effects of those original moves as possible outcomes. It needs work; but, I think it will become an elegant solution.

    \n Would love to read your AP Matthew!

    I'm pretty happy with the casualty rate for the game and I (personally) wouldn't want to soften it for the final product. Franklin / Barnes both ran straight into the fight pretty much all the time. Franklin died as the result of a very risky scouting mission and some unlucky dice rolls. Barnes could have been moved the safety of a field hospital. Instead, he chose to stay behind and fend off the German assault by himself and he died because of that. Amherst should have died several times; but, didn't. Often, I'd throw him some crazy problem to overcome and he'd come up with an even crazier solution. The harm dice liked him and wanted him to live on. Smith, however, mostly tried to keep his head down and, despite the harm dice hating him, he managed to live through it... barely.

    The real source of the lethality in this game is the GM, because it's the GM who says when a character is exposed to fire and how bad that fire is. So, even though the system is pretty brutal when dice are rolled, a GM can pull their punches by exposing them to fire less frequently or by using direct or concentrated VOF sparingly. That is a way; but, it's a way that detracts from the game. Part of the experience is having risk and consequences. The correct way to put the gloves on would be to tell a story where the risk of death in the fiction is low. Maybe the characters are rear echelon or maybe they are policing conquered territories, etc. Conversely, you could pull a Stalingrad and kick the meat grinder into high gear. In this way, the GM can set the tone and expectations for the game.

    The replacement character creation time has not been too bad. The disruption is fairly low and I've been able to get the new character back into the game in the typical spotlight rotation. I try to keep enough involved NPCs around that the players can just take them over if they want to. That's what Franklin's player did when he started playing Barnes. That kinda takes care of the back story and personality for them. John and I are restructuring playbooks in a way that should make the process of creating a new / replacement character super easy.

    \n Chris, I guess it depends on which session you're referring to. If you mean the end of Session 4 in Eindhoven where we decided how the larger battle played out, that was an improper use of engagement moves that illustrated the need for a GM move to allow dice to determine how the big picture plays out when the GM does not want to dictate a solution. It was very suspenseful. We were all on the edge of our seats waiting to see how the rolls panned out. Each result I saw led to consequences that ultimately resulted in the destruction of Best Bridge. Since we were all very invested in the outcome, each roll was significant.

    In general, I use engagement moves frequently; but, I usually only use one - not multiple in series - then follow with narrative and basic moves. I use engagement moves to set up the next shot in the movie. In that sense, it's a prelude to the action that follows. It sets the stakes. So it's not a formality or an absence of action, it's a thing that creates suspense as we determine whether the unit's start to the engagement is good, bad, or ugly. That's a cue for me in storytelling. I'm using "engagement" on a much smaller scale than, perhaps, John is - where any significant shift in the tide of battle is potentially a new engagement move. The scope of an "engagement" is vague by necessity, so using it once or twice a session at a macro level is just as valid. It may mean the posturing on the micro scale is determined more through narrative than dice. I like to use the dice to make the decision for good / bad / ugly - so I don't have to - and then I tell the narrative from there. That's just my way.

    A lot of the historical detail did come out into play; but, it's a little different. In play, I'm telling them what they see and hear and know based on what they say they do and how well or poorly they roll. Of course, there's also the meta game level where I fill in historical context, so as players, they understand the big picture better and can appreciate more fully just how screwed their characters are. In the background, men and materiel are moving around to these historical timetables and I try to guess at how the characters' actions might affect the balance. Then that affects what they see and hear and know and so forth. Session 5 is a really complicated example of that where I used a AW-style clock to keep track of things - something I don't normally do.

    \nYeah, attack the enemy and close assault look like two sides of the same coin, and should probably be one move. Like, for example, if you use them both at the same time, you get this:

    When you make an attack, roll+battle. On a 10+, the enemy must break and flee, surrender, or take the harm.
    On a 7-9, the enemy can instead choose 1 of these:
    * Fall back or take cover (and they can't effectively fight anymore).
    * Trade harm for harm.
    * You inflict harm, but it takes a lot of shooting (-1gear).
    * You inflict harm, but not accurately (-1 harm).
