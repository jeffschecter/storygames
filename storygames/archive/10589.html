Posted By: Jason MorningstarIs anyone else interested in discussing technique? I can start a new thread, but I have nothing to contribute - I'm interested in the outlines of a postmodernist game, even as a thought experiment, if it encompasses new ways of approaching the fiction or social experience. If you've thought deeply about this and think it isn't what we do currently, maybe you have some ideas.
And we did have a few things that came up in that thread that I think are interesting starting points for games and tables looking to produce a highly postmodern experience.

Bill mentioned playing John Tynes' Power Kill, a "meta-role playing game" that sits on top of your existing game and deconstructs it \u2014 that is produces a critique of your game's violence with respect to what the consequences would be if you engaged in sociopathic "kill goblins and take their stuff" behavior in the real world.

I followed up with a design observation: Seth Ben-Ezra's game Showdown has that strain of deconstruction baked right in. St Vincent's game "kill puppies for satan" deconstructs even that deconstruction; in the "real world" of kpfs, satan rewards you for your evil (puppy killing) behavior with evil points which you can use to do evil things, which makes people hate you, which motivates you to do more evil stuff.

So, deconstruction. How do we expose the assumptions of foregoing games and get rewarding play from inverting them?

Simon, among others, was interested in the interplay of parody vs postmodernism. The former is frequently employed by the latter as a technique, but not every parody is a pomo production (or is it?). How do we make parody serve postmodern ends?

Matthijs brought up the Hippie Method, and suggested that it has nuggets to be mined for play as a postmodern production. What can we do with this?

Nick, among others, suggested that all RPGs are postmodern, that you can't avoid it because of the nature of the medium. To what extent is that true? And what does it mean for our understanding of our play and designs?

    \n A big part of how I approach the concept of postmodernism is the deconstruction of universal truths.
    Put differently, things cannot be known, only seen & interpreted.
    Given that lens (which, granted, is a limited one within pomo discussions), I want to look at game design.

    Does your game codify who a character is, in a permanent and fixed and universal way?

    Dungeons & Dragons gives you a Strength stat, and further it tells you that your Strength stat is a determinant in hitting someone with a sword. This design feature is antithetical to postmodern thinking, in that it asks you to construct a codified, rigid, universally-structured way of understanding the world.

    Dogs in the Vineyard also has stats, but much more importantly it has freeform traits, like "My Weapon Is My Salvation 2d10". Suddenly we've kicked the door open to the notion that people define their own strengths, that how we view our own experiences is vital, and that our interactions are rooted in our perceptions. My dude might be an excellent rider, and an articulate fellow, and a compassionate lover and a good lay... but if I don't codify those things, they are only colour. I decide what matters in terms of how my character succeeds in the world, and in doing so acknowledge my character's priorities, worldview and self-conception.

    Flags are a step further in this direction. Flags exist to say "this is what matters to this character's story." It explicitly assumes that narrative worth is rooted in individual goals and experiences, not in some cosmic scheme. Saving the world from Zarlok the Necromancer? It isn't necessarily more important than taking my half-elven daughter to see the Fountain of Memory, or building a mercantile empire, or hooking up with the virgin princess. It deconstructs the idea that there are universal measures of worth, and it says "what I want is more valid than how the world works".

    A much bigger pomo leap would be to stop attributing (mechanical) worth to a character in terms of the meaning we've assigned them (Strength 17, "My Weapon Is My Salvation" 2d10, flags, etc) and start assigning worth in terms of what we experience of them. Another way to say this is: systematize action over classification.

    One mechanic that does this is fanmail - giving narrative/mechanical power to others on the basis of actions taken (actions including narration of any stripe, here).
    Another mechanic is trust mechanics.
    Another mechanic that does this is Wushu's take-a-die-for-narrating-useful-action mechanic.

    These break free of the notion of prescriptive power, and in doing so take down the idea that power is fixed, permanent, universally consistent or even codifiable.
    It puts everything in flux, and makes power relative and dynamic.

    \n There are a couple of sense of postmodern here which I think are both legitemate. What Joe says above is 100% spot on IMHO. Post modernism is deconstructing the idea that the game has authority to state absolute truths in the fiction.

    But in those games, system still has a level of authority over the fiction. There's another sense of postmodernism, I think, that's about taking apart the possibility of even entering into that sort of authority relationship at all. In D&D, the rules define the absolute truth about my character's strength. in Dogs in the Vineyard, I define my own strengths and we interpret them and see them change in the fiction. To take it one step further would require a game that takes apart the entire notion of the rules or system having any authority at all. I think Kill Puppies for Satan and Powerkill are both in this area.

    Also, WTF (http://imago.hitherby.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/wtf.pdf), mentioned in the Things to Watch the other day looks like a beautiful postmodern deconstruction of RPGs to me. Even the GM's authority to state that they are in fact the GM is up for grabs. WFT indeed!

    \n So here is my list of the "elements of postmodern rpg design" based on what I've gotten out of the conversation so far.

    Reflexivity. The game invites introspection, deconstruction or criticism of play on the part of the players. PowerKill, kpfs.

    Pastiche. The game mirrors, mocks, or imitates another game. Drowning & Falling, Hackmaster.

    Bricolage. The game borrows from, incorporates, or plays off of other games to achieve its effect. PowerKill.

    Diegetic Relativism. The game is not committed to absolutist or realist methods of referring to the fiction. UA, DITV.

    Irony. The game text means something other than it says. WTF. Maybe all of them.

    Recursivity. The game involves players playing players of a game. 1001 Nights.

    I offer this as a tentative taxonomy; maybe the categories overlap, maybe there's something missing, maybe a category label is a misnomer. Let me know if you think I've misrepresented anything.

    \n Okay, so on reflection, I'd modify my list to look like this:\n
    Reflexivity. The game invites introspection, deconstruction or criticism of play on the part of the players, or the game-text can be read as an act of introspection, deconstruction, or criticism on the part of the author. PowerKill, kpfs, WTF.

    Intertextuality. The game mirrors, mocks, imitates, or plays off another game in order to achieve its effect. Drowning & Falling, Hackmaster, PowerKill, Mazes & Minotaurs.

    Diegetic Relativism. The game is not committed to absolutist or realist methods of referring to the fiction. UA, DITV, Snow Day!

    Recursivity. The game involves players playing players of a game; more broadly, one part of the game is echoed in other parts. 1001 Nights, Fortress of Woe.

    Dislocation. The game plays with or otherwise renders unfamiliar typical conceptions of player, character, game, or story. Sweet Agatha.
    \nI took out "irony" because it seems like it's too broad a category, squeezed "bricolage" and "pastiche" into one category, and added "dislocation" to accommodate Sweet Agatha per gbsteve's comment in the other thread.

    Fortress of Woe is clearly recursive and intertextual. You play players of a D&D-like game crawling through their own RL "fortress of woe" high school or other suburban nightmare. I'd be interested to see how it plays out, to see whether it generates any kind of reflexive moment. Accounts of kpfs that I've read note that it is the act of reflecting on your attitude toward your own PC that gives it its impact--can I forgive this hateful loser I've created? What kinds of players will people create, and will they be able to reflect upon that act of creation as having some bearing on their own play in other games? Or will they just play it for laughs, like a tabletop "Big Bang Theory"?

    \n Jeepform frequently focuses on these things, Bill.

    For example, many jeepform games encourage bleed (the blurring/disruption of player/character divide), especially in horrific scenarios. This is clearly an effort at dislocation.

    Ghosts has two of the players take on the role of lovers who kill one another, and later re-appear as ghosts, urging a new set of lovers in the same hotel room towards repeating their violence. Doubt tells the story of lovers, who are also actors playing characters in love - with different players playing their onstage and offstage versions. These are pretty phenomenal examples of recursivity.

    The Upgrade! relies on our understanding of game shows, and teaches us many jeepform methods (aggressive cuts, flashbacks, flashforwards) through comparison to how game shows operate. In other words, the teaching method is partially "we're doing what they do". This strikes me as intertextuality.

    Further, several scenarios are structured so that different players (or sets of players) tell the story from different, non-reconcilable (or at least non-reconciled) viewpoints. This touches upon Diegetic Relativism.

    \n I didn't want to have to engage in neologism, but I think there's a difference between games that play off other games for their effect and games that make reference to other genres, like The Upgrade! This narrower form of intertextuality is closer to the kind of self-referentiality that strikes me as quintessentially post-modern, since we are all intertextual now. So I'm going to coin the term "interludicality" to refer to the quality of referring, often ironically, to other games in order to achieve its effect. Alternately, we could scrap the category and simply regard inter-game reference as a kind of reflexivity; the two are perhaps linked. On the other hand, a game can be interludic without being terribly reflexive; imitation is the sincerest &c.\n
    Reflexivity. The game invites introspection, deconstruction or criticism of play on the part of the players, or the game-text can be read as an act of introspection, deconstruction, or criticism on the part of the author. PowerKill, kpfs, WTF, Violence.

    Interludicality. The game mirrors, mocks, imitates, or plays off another game in order to achieve an ironic effect. Donjon, Drowning & Falling, Hackmaster, PowerKill, Mazes & Minotaurs.

    Diegetic Relativism. The game is not committed to absolutist or realist methods of referring to the fiction. UA, DITV, Snow Day!, Misery Bubblegum, many jeepform games.

    Recursivity. The game involves players playing players of a game; more broadly, one part of the game is echoed in other parts. 1001 Nights, Fortress of Woe, Ghosts, Doubt.

    Dislocation. The game plays with or otherwise renders unfamiliar typical conceptions of player, character, game, or story. Sweet Agatha, jeepform "bleed."

    Posted By: Mr. TeapotPosted By: Jason Morningstar
    Bricolage is the only one on that list that seems weak to me - every game after white box D&D does this to some degree, right?
    \nFrom where I'm sitting, every rpg since white box D&D does all of those things to some degree.

    Right--"to some degree." It is absolutely a question of degree. Compared to other forms of literature, roleplaying is extraordinary in its blurring of boundaries and so forth. But I think it's certainly possible to imagine "philosophically modernist" roleplaying, characterized by (a) earnestness (i.e., the suppression of reflexivity: "listen, we're just here to play; it doesn't mean anything"), a heroic auctorial sensibility (i.e., the suppression of interludicality: "this game is revolutionary!"), diegetic realism ("the game mechanics are the physics of the world"), linearity (i.e., the absence of recursivity: "you are not your character"); and contextualization (i.e., the suppression of dislocation: "the game is not the fiction"). In some cases, those are hard-won design insights.
