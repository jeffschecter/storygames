    \n Ok, so "story game", here, means "GMless roleplaying game", right?

    This is where you have to consider what the stance of the players is in the game. When I'm playing, what am I trying to accomplish? What are we playing to find out?

    Am I trying to create the best story? Am I trying to gain something (perhaps a game resource) by creating fictional opportunities for other characters? Am I trying to advocate for my character and try to achieve their goals?

    You can't really answer any of your questions until you have some sense of what the interpersonal dynamic at the table is going to be. Once you have the interpersonal dynamic is decided upon, what are our creative goals when we play this game? What does it look like when we're accomplishing those goals, and what does it look like when we fail? Then you can try to work out procedures which operate coherently with those goals instead of getting in their way.     \n One thing I find interesting about Game of Thrones is that power is often carried by supporting roles/advisors as much as the rulers themselves. What if in the GMless version, you play as advisors instead of the leaders/rulers?

    In the world-building, each player could create a hidden piece of information about the ruler/territory in which their advisor begins the story. This information would only become public and activate if a battle or other operation takes place involving that ruler or territory. Then there could be mechanics for discovering the hidden information in other areas. So the rulers (essentially NPCs at this point) stay in their given territories, and possibly overtake others, but advisors could possibly visit other territories, have spies, work deals, etc. to learn this hidden information. Forming alliances could involve sharing at least some of the hidden information about each place. Advisors could also switch sides mid-game as well. You could also have players create some true and some false hidden information, and when they share they cover up the part of the card that indicates if it is true or false, creating the possibility for misinformation and double-crossing.

    You could have a "Loyalty" stat that suffers when switching allegiance, but it could be worth it to be on the winning side. There could also be a mechanic for generating more hidden information throughout the game so you never could know every possible factor. And maybe the battle doesn't even take place, the game is only an information-gathering game.

    I don't know enough about your game to know if any of this fits with what you're doing, but I think if you're going to make a GMless variant, you might as well make it wildly different than the RPG version. Best of luck!

    \n Jeff,

    From your description, it sounds like these "secrets" are only "secret" from the characters, not the players. (Although your hints about "players leaving the room" suggest otherwise...)

    Is that right? That's not complex, from a design perspective. But *player secrets* (secret information about the gamestate)... that is the challenging part.

    The only games I know which handle "player secrets" effectively either have clear conditions for how they are recorded and revealed (for example, in Diplomacy the secret information relevant to the game is written down and then revealed simultaneously; whereas in Musette the secret information is simply handled as cards face down, and a clear rule about when we can turn them over) or have rules which make sure that at least two players know any given secret.

    At this point, I'd encourage you to take little parts of your game (like a subsystem, or a single scene) and play through it with some people. You can even try some of this on the forum, as a thread. See what dynamics it creates and how much fun it is.

The point-buy economy combined with the public elements of play could constrain play in a meaningful way, and record-keeping would enable everybody to scrutiny the secret plots later in a meaningful way, to ensure the legitimacy of the proceedings.     \n I called my game Zombie Cinema a "story game" on the back of the box (in 2008, was it). I did that mostly as a covert action, to make the game more appealing to boardgamers who might have identity issues with playing something called a "roleplaying game". It wasn't a difficult choice, though, as the game is pretty much precisely the sort of game that people around here tend to mean when they say "story game"

    Historically, "story game" was just something Andy thought up when he was starting up this forum - he wanted something that'd refer to Forgite drama-oriented games without being technical or exclusionary, I think. For years it used to be explicitly the case that "story game" (in the title of the forum) was not intended as a subset or alternative to roleplaying games - it was more like a synonymous shibboleth, an alternate term one could use to emphasize a certain subculture of roleplaying, without particularly defining it in separation from other kinds. Kinda similar to how certain web-based roleplaying communities call them "RPs", which simply isn't a term you'd see tabletop gamers use.

    It seems that over time some people have become more comfortable with a separate "story game" identity, which makes them more eager for a real definition. Others have mellowed out even further, seeing such differentiation as meaningless. I can only imagine how this might make things peculiar for folks at times: on the one hand we have a person who can't wrap their head around D&D discussions occurring at a forum called "Story Games", as if it was an appropriate topic, but then we also have people who don't understand you when you say that you want to "change my roleplaying game into a story game" as if the two terms were not synonyms.

    As regards my own opinion, I've largely lost faith with being able to establish terminology - it doesn't matter what I think words should mean, somebody else's opinion will catch on with the masses anyway

    In the rare situations where I've used "story game" in a substantial way, it's always been as a synonym for the Finnish theory term "drama game", which indicates a game that is structured with narratology and dramaturgical tools, with a concern for story creation. It's best understood in contrast with other technical agenda framework concepts like "adventure game" and "war game"; those are all potential descriptions for roleplaying games that draw upon different conceptual frameworks to achieve their aims. This is useful (for me) because drama games in this sense have many similar technical concerns (e.g. protagonists, scenes, literary themes) that are not captured by ideas like "Narrativism" or "GM-less". (Many drama games are, indeed, Simulationist - even Gamist - and very much GM-led.)

    This sounds very interesting Eero! What if for the most part the PCs adversaries were primarily NPC's and the PCs were mostly aligned out of common goals and interests, but might have different individual personal motives for doing so. In other words, what if the game was only Gamist in part. How do you think you would handle something of this nature?
    \nActually, I'd totally go with Rune's overall structure for something like that - that's exactly the situation in Rune. The core idea is that the player characters are very much competing with each other, even while they're not struggling against each other. This is an entirely normal set-up in many sports and such, and also in some roleplaying games. Agon is another obvious example of a D&D variant that explicitly forefronts the inter-character competition (although it has a GM).

    (Also, speaking in GNS terms, this is still totally Gamist all the way, no partiality involved. Gamism as a creative mechanism is much more varied and subtle than people often give it credit for.)

    So we've got the following tools/ideas to build with:
    * Rotating GM: we don't need to use him all the time and for all purposes, note. He just might be useful at times. For example, you could have players provide adversity functions for others in situations where they would otherwise be largely sidelined, because their own characters are not involved.
    * Point-buy intrigue: characters can "do stuff" in secret from other players by the player paying for the privilege.
    * Competitive/Cooperative Gamism: the player characters are not for the most part in a zero-sum conflict against each other; rather, the key conflicts in the game are NPC-derived, and victories are joint victories against adversity, or achieving dominant stature among the PCs by exceeding the accomplishments of others. Very much the same as normal D&D, all in all.

    The game I see in this works something like so:
    1) The players create a playing field for the scenario cooperatively, or get it prefab from the game designer. The "playing field", this being an intrigue scenario, consists largely of social mapping: who are the power players, what are their goals, what are their pre-existing relationships.
    2) Player characters are either already inserted in the scenario, or the players create them, depending on the particulars of the game. The PCs are ostensibly on the same side for the most part, but the game does not mandate this structurally, and there are uncertainties (secret orders in prefab PCs, or random/arbitrary elements in chargen), which means that the PCs do not trust each other quite implicitly. A good idea often seen in similar cooperative boardgames might be the "Traitor" - the players know that almost everybody is fundamentally on the same side, but they also know that somebody is not.
    3) The players start maneuvering, which might be regulated in terms of turns or fictional resources (time, money, etc.) or whatever suits the game best.
    \xa0\xa0a) Each maneuver consists of public and private elements. The public element is what your character is ostensibly doing (visiting relatives, partying, going to church, participating in a state function whatever), while the private element is something you may wish to actually achieve (form secret connections, activate agents, spy/rob/murder somebody, whatever).
    \xa0\xa0b) Either, both or neither of the character's maneuver elements may be effective in some way: your public appearance may have great political implications, for example, or your seemingly suspicious visit to a brother may be just that and nothing more. There might be rules mechanics that derive from a character's willpower stat or whatever, in fact, and mandate that the player insert a certain amount of "meaningless" activity in his character's maneuvering.
    \xa0\xa0c) The players can, should they wish, spy upon and watch for each other's activities. Presumably dice rolls and such tell us whether intelligence operations are effective. As a result a PC might capture another's secrets by e.g. witnessing their secret tryst.
    \xa0\xa0d) The "secret reality" that forms over time basically consists of events and facts that individual players know about, but the rest of the group does not. The trick to consoling these with each other is that "secret facts" only have probable confidence levels as long as they have not been brought into light. For example, you might think that you killed someone in secret earlier, but if another operator was secretly protecting them, the reality of the situation may only become clear once the body (or living person) is produced in public. The rules mechanics resolve conflicts between contesting realities, obviously.
    4) Because there is no GM providing hard and dynamic resistance from NPCs, the players take turns doing so, or the system mandates them to do so communally on occasion. This is probably directed and curtailed by a sort of a point-buy system as well; for example, each important NPC might have an "adversity value" that the current GM player expends to produce complications. It is probably not worthwhile to simulate NPC intrigue in any great detail; rather, the NPC actions produce "disruptive wedges", events that force the PCs to adapt their plans and take risks.
    5) The game probably benefits from some sort of an exhausting resource spiral; once the resources are exhausted and the social situation stultifies (no more significant moves are possible because the new king was already crowned, or whatever) the scenario comes to an end, with the player characters ending up more or less satisfied depending on their success in primary and secondary goals.

    \n Yes, but think of it this way: if you're committed to not having a GM, then who's going to do the thinking for those NPCs? Intriguing is tough work, the players will have their hands full just intriguing on their own behalf.

    So what you'll likely want to do is, you approximate NPC intrigue instead of actually doing it. You still have full mapping of the terrain (social terrain in this case: customs, relationships, political issues, whatnot), and a full record of what the PCs do in the scenario, but instead of keeping track of everything NPCs do, you abstract most of their activities into dice rolls.

    This is, incidentally, how many wargame-ish rpgs reduce the burden caused by the opposition. D&D has random encounter rolls, for instance: instead of the GM having to track the particular positions of each monster in the dungeon at each time, he simply figures out their frequency, and rolls random encounter checks to see when and what the adventurers encounter. From the perspective of the characters themselves the outcome is about the same, but the method used saves 99% of the work.

    Applying that principle to a GMless intrigue game, you don't want to have to keep track of 20 NPCs and what they're doing every day. While this would surely produce the most intricate and dynamic scenario, it's simply too much work, particularly without a GM, to figure out every day whether this is the day the countess realizes her husband's infidelity, or whatever. Much easier to e.g. roll dice every day for an "intrigue event", and maybe the countess' thing comes up at some point, or not.

    What this scheme looks like in practice is that the players will have to intrigue against the NPCs actively, which even with the presumption of total NPC passiveness still requires quite a bit of intriguing to get anywhere. Totally passive NPCs would be boring, though, which is why you'll want to get the most bang you can for the attention you put into the NPCs and their actions; you don't want to spend more playtime than you have to in the minute detail of how the NPCs prepare their plots, as that's already what you're doing for the PCs. Instead, you just roll some dice and abstract away the unseen bits of the NPC actions until all that's left is the part that the PCs encounter.

    For an example of what I mean here, let's say that the PCs have entered a hostile court under false pretenses so as to spy on enemy royalty in their natural setting. Let's also say that there is a clever cardinal in the court, a NPC who suspects the PCs of nefarious purposes. How are we going to actually conduct the gameplay here? I suggest that the group does not have the time to run "daily actions" (or however player character intrigue is organized) for this cardinal NPC, not with a bunch of player characters to run as well, not to speak of the other NPCs, too. Besides, if the players had to run this NPC in as much detail as they do with the PCs, what would differentiate between the PCs and the NPCs? Wouldn't it just practically be players playing two sides against themselves?

    To avoid the difficulties with that, we'll instead use an abstracted system. For the sake of simplicity of example, we could say that there is a 1/6 chance per day that a "major problem" occurs for the PCs. (In a real game the probability would surely depend on what the PCs do as well, of course.) We do not know in advance what that problem will be, but once it has been established that something bad is coming, the players can figure out (probably aided by the rules system) that hey, apparently it's the kindly old cardinal, and hey, it seems that he's been collecting evidence against us, and hey, apparently we're all going to jail now. Note that we never made the decision in advance that the cardinal would start looking for evidence against the PCs, and we never tracked his progress, and so on - we're simply back-projecting this intrigue at the moment it is revealed to the PCs, so as to minimize the workload of tracking NPC activities. We know that the cardinal is doing something with his days, but we don't know what until the proceedings of the game make it necessary for us to find out.

    Of course the fact that we never went into the detail of what the cardinal was doing with his days doesn't mean that the PCs couldn't peek: maybe spying upon a NPC could have revealed a covert danger like an impending criminal investigation in advance, allowing the PCs more time to react to it, or derail the investigation in some way. Just like in the above situation, we still do not know about the cardinal's nefarious purpose in advance: we simply invent this particular danger (probably aided by the game system) at the moment when we actually need to answer the question about what the cardinal has been doing on the sly. The answer might as well be something else, though, because nobody is planning the cardinal's activities in advance.

    In truth the big, fat question here for me would be why ever would I want to remove the GM from a game like this - having a GM makes all of this so much easier, plus the feeling of having a consistent, antagonistic world operating around the PCs is priceless, and ultimately impossible to replicate with clever GMless tricks. We basically played this game (well, Gamist Paranoia, which is close enough) a year and a half ago in Helsinki, except it was a GM, and it worked excellently; I believe that something similar is possible without the GM as well, but it does require more careful regulation, and it's impossible to achieve quite as much uncertainty and paranoia when the players know that the cardinal isn't really plotting anything before we take a peek and roll the dice and see how things stand.

    \n As I said earlier, this is a very ambitious and challenging prospect for a game. I think that, if successful, it might even be truly groundbreaking (and possibly even spawn a new genre of gaming). However, from the "pitch" I still have no idea how it would operate.

    Ultimately, I think you have to go to my earlier question:

    Who or what provides adversity in this game? What are the players and/or the characters struggling against?

    I've seen that come from: a) other players (head-to-head competition), b) a GM (or a rotating GM position), or c) the system itself. a) is a competitive boardgame, b) is a relatively traditional RPG, and c) is a cooperative boardgame or puzzle. (Are there other options?)

    My own design efforts often concern merging or combining those factors in different ways. For instance, in my game Land of Nodd, the players collaborate to create each others' stories, but are rewarded for doing so well by gaining resources they can use to give their characters a better chance of success. The adversity a character faces is provided mechanically by the system, but given life (in fictional terms) by other players. There also a tacit competitive element, however, where players who reach their goals earlier make it harder for those who follow them.

    Some more ideas:

    * One way to establish sufficient "distance" for players to act as adversity for other players is to keep the stories of the various PCs separate.

    If you're trying to overtake the throne in King's Landing, but my character is away in Slaver's Bay, I'll find it much easier to provide "impartial" input to your character's storyline.

    The more separate they are, the easier this is to do. When they start to merge (e.g. both our characters now are jockeying for the throne), you have to design very carefully, and introduce procedures or mechanism which allow the players to navigate some awkward tradeoffs. (In Land of Nodd, it's accomplished by opposition players a) not having any mechanical input into resolution, and b) being rewarded in terms or resources when they do a good job.)

    * David Berg has a game coming out called Within My Clutches. It features a "secret master plan" for each character, which is revealed at the end of the game. It works well because it has little bearing on the mechanics of the game (so we have no cause to bicker about it), and all the players are on the same side about it: we want to support each other in developing this plan, and hope to see it revealed at the end.

    The clever thing he has in his game is a mechanic where we get to find out a little bit about the plan at certain points. For instance, if I get a certain number of points, I get to ask you to reveal certain elements of your plan.

    It works largely because it's not a competitive game, though, note.

    * Eero's idea of having an overt agenda and a covert agenda is pretty interesting. I could even see a system where a player declares an overt goal, but also prepares a secret goal (as Eero suggests). Meanwhile, however, his opposition (another player, or maybe the group in toto) prepares a secret "consequence" to his actions.

    Some kind of resolution process then tells us:

    -Which outcomes take place, and which do not.
    -What the immediate outcome of the scene is (does the overt agenda succeed? - possibly the least important or interesting part here, but it could be if it has repercussions on future actions).
    -Which outcomes are revealed, and which remain secret.
    -Which parties or players are given access to that information.
    -Possibly: which information is public knowledge *in the fiction*, and which is not

    For instance, we might find that:

    -Celia's visit to the Temple ends in an argument, and she is thrown out, earning a bad relationship with the Temple (overt outcome).
    -This is public knowledge at the table (all the players know, of course), but secret in the fiction (the people in the City have not heard about it, at least not yet).
    -Celia's covert goal (to slip a bribe to the head priest) was successful, and all the players know this.
    -Celia's actions have a player-authored repercussion, which is that the Temple will start hiring assassins, disguised as priests. This is known to the other players, but not Celia.

    This could be an interesting position to design a game from. You'd have to be very careful about the possible outcomes to avoid game-breaking combinations (like something happening but no players being aware of it).

    * Your best friend in design for this kind of game, in my opinion, is balanced mechanical tradeoffs.

    If you can create balanced decisions - so that either way you choose is interesting, and doesn't screw someone over - you don't have to worry as much about who is making the decision and whether they have a bias.

    For instance, you cannot have two players competing with each other for victory, and then give one the authority to decide how challenging the other's tasks are (e.g. the "difficulty number" for their rolls). But if you add in a counterbalancing incentive, you can sometimes make it work. For instance:

    -When your character tries to do something challenging, every other player decides whether they think it's Hard or Easy. For every player who says it's Hard, your chances of success drop. However, if you succeed, for every player who said it was Hard, you score one point. These points are how we win the game, or get bonuses to future actions, or something similar (experience points, Pool dice, new abilities, more resources you can use to make MY life more difficult, etc).

    I do something like this in Musette, where I want balanced conflicts, but also for players to take sides in the conflicts. In Musette, your desired outcome is to win the conflict, but against as much opposition as possible (the more cards against you, the more it contributes to you winning the game).

    This means that a player has an incentive to contribute both towards and against one of her goals. Player goals are secret, which also contributes to an interest in "hiding" one's motives by contributing evenly to various "sides" in conflict.

    \n As for "story game", I think you won't find any kind of common definition. Eero is on the ball about this one: it depends on the particular "crowd" using the term.

    Personally, I use "story game" as an umbrella term for any game which deals with the imagination and seeks to create a narrative. An RPG, then, is a subset of "story game" (although I can imagine some RPGs which I would hesitate to call 'story games', if they are really not interested in creating a coherent narrative).

    A lot of other people, though, like to use "story game" as a subset of RPGs: roleplaying games which focus on "the story" more so than they focus on each person's character and their advancement.

    Some people consider "story game" to be a design movement (originally referred to as "indie games", but now that term seems silly, with so many games on the market being published independently). In that case, we look for certain design features and say "hey, that has Character Issues and it's GMless, so I say it's a story game!" In this, much like with pornography, you won't be able to draw strict lines, but any given person can easily say, "I know one when I see one!"

    In some parts of the "trad RPG world", "story game" or "narrative gaming" are pejorative terms used to lump together new games not to the liking of a particular community. The term is used, then, to distinguish "games I don't like" from "real RPGs". For some people, this goes so far as to label a sinister conspiracy which seeks to destroy gaming for everyone...

    The latter two groups sometimes use the term in what I can only consider a sort of political sense: to label something as part of a group I like or dislike.

    (Has anyone seen other uses of the term? I think those four categories roughly cover usage I've encountered.)

    \n I'll second the recommendation to read In A Wicked Age... It's not GMless, but its GM role is very player-like, and its ideas apply nicely to GMless games (or "GMful" games, as I like to think of them). At its core is a conflict resolution system of dice vs. dice, and the winner dictates the consequences to the loser. However, there are only three possibilities: two are mechanical (dice penalties) and one is freeform. That is, you can negotiate something that happens in the fiction, but if you can't come to an agreement about that, you have to fall back to one of the two mechanical penalties (winner chooses which one). It's a carrot-or-stick negotiation.

    Also Polaris. Polaris rotates specific GM responsibilities with specific players (based on player seating arrangements) during each scene.

    Somewhere there is a list of roles or responsibilities that GMs often take on; it would behoove you to look at that list and decide if specific responsibilities are concentrated in one person at a time, or everyone shares them. Specialization makes sure that someone is thinking about the things that need to be thought about, rather than just assuming that someone is thinking about it.

    The Shab-al-Hiri Roach is a GMless PVP game. Like many of the story games of the early 2000's, it's a "scenes and stakes" game. The spotlight player puts a goal out there for the scene and the rest of the players bid coins for or against it. Winners double their bid out of the kitty; losers forfeit their bid to the kitty. This seems a fruitful ground for some kind of resource management for your game.

    Annalise is a GMless game, but it isn't PVP usually. It's usually a group of people cooperating to notice and ultimately defeat an evil stalker of some kind (ostensibly, a Vampire). One of the really cool things it does is bring multiple stakes to each scene. The player might want Stake A; the player's temporary GM (each scene has a "story guide") adds Stake B; another player adds Stake C; a fourth player adds Stake D. Then you roll 1d6 for each Stake and assign a die to each one. Basically, a high number means the stake will come to be, and a low number means the stake will not come to be (not exactly, but go with this for ease of understanding). Then the spotlight player struggles to fiddle with each of the dice, spending game currency to tweak a die up or down a point or trust to fate and reroll it. But the other players fiddle with the stake dice, too! This really forces you to 1) take some bad things with the good, and 2) decide what is the most important thing to you. In case it's not obvious, each of the stakes must be able to come true independently ("orthogonal" stakes).
