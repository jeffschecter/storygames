The Underlying Worldview

Jasper
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
Assumptions:

Games create models of existence. Their rules define causal relationships within the imagined world. Games select only a few important ideas to include, and by doing so define the play experience. Only some facets of reality are useful, and just as important are matters psychological, social or philosophical. Every game must have both (contra the old idea of games being physics simulations) and, in fact, every rule probably has a social or otherwise "mushy" aspect to it.

With all these rules, the internal logic of the game world is created. Of course, this happens through the thinking of the players, meaning that the players' thinking is influenced by the rules of the game. If someone plays a game a great deal, perhaps it will even affect his thinking outside of role-playing. World-views might come simply from the author's own perspective, or might be inserted more purposefully into a game.

Question of Interest:

What unusual, interesting world-views (logic) have underlain RPGs?* Tell me about your own game(s).

* I'm interested in ideas that are incorporated into the rules — perhaps subtly — not in philosophies present in the game's setting material, even though the two may, of course, be connected.

We might need to talk about what typical world-views are, first, in order to discuss what's atypical. That's fine.

Example:

In D&D, people are defined strongly by their material possessions, and the accumulation of possessions mirrors a person's internal evolution. Contrarily, many recent indie games have no place for possessions at all: If mentioned during play, they are temporary and serve merely as props. E.g., The Roach. We could say that this reflects a world-view that sees objects as having great or little ultimate importance.

    This is true, but Jasper still has a point. You can strip away that "Western game about..." bit and still play with D&D rules. And, those rules, still generate the item-centric sort of worldview Jasper is talking about.

    That isn't a tautology--it owes a lot to a model of interpretation that has had some (to my mind) nifty things to say about the way in which simple rules support (even where they do not directly determine) certain kinds of habits. As long as we don't collapse the worldview onto the rules, reduce one to the other, this can be a very interesting discussion.

    To take the D&D example: those rules, esp. 3.5, support and assume characters will accumulate objects in order to meet the challenges set before them. Those objects are as important as the basic character stats in their way--so, it becomes such that one of two worldviews tend to occur, both supported, but not determined, by the rules:

    1. Characters are just stuff, a running tally of resources with which challenges can be met. This leads to the very fiddly, small unit wargame-iness of some D&D.
    2. Stuff reflects who the character is, so players spend a lot of time tailoring their items to express how they see their character, to provide them with tools that encourage them to interact with the game world in certain ways.

    Paying attention to the rules, rather than the color, also gives us a nifty way to think about how we can hack games, using the rules in ways other than to play out a certain sort of campaign. It tells us something about what the rules are *capable* of doing and what they are *less* than capable of doing.

    And, moreover, it allows us to group games around certain kinds of shared worldviews--think about the many games that, regardless of mechanical differences, basically converge with D&D in terms of the 'stuff really matters' dimension. Think, too, about how those games often just feel like D&D when played, even when they are very different in terms of 'surface' mechanics.

    I am really curious to see what people will say about their own games...

    You raise a good point, Jason: that "protect/save what I care about" is indeed a goal, and a worthy one. And like I said, I'm not down on Superhero stories if done well. I mean look at games like Capes and With Great Power: they tap into that rich, rich well of "and what will you sacrifice to protect/save them" and similar questions. It's just funny that all the possible stories and drives have been (mostly) narrowed down within the genre to just the one, the defensive, status-quo-preserving, bad-guy-stopping one. Is it possible to have more proactive world-changing in a superhero story? Or even just a more complex landscape of personal ambition and moral positioning? My musings on, f'rinstance, Dr Doom are hardly original; Stan himself said that the whole hook for the character was that he was a world-conqueror who honestly thought himself best for the job, and rather had a point. That's hot stuff. But in their execution, characters like Doom and Magneto end up falling as bit short because they can never be truly vindicated within the story: "when will you realize, Victor, that for all of your nobility, you're just wrong?" It's kind of the illusion of moral complexity. But what if we took the concept and really ran with it? What kinds of stories (non-deconstructionist ones) could be told when the lines of right and wrong are not constrained to the labels of "Hero" and "Villain"?

    I confess I have no idea how to relate this directly to the fantasy paradigm in general and its D&D iteration in particular (AKA the topic under discussion), except perhaps to say, "all that stuff up there? goes for D&D fantasy too." Actually, Claire, I'd love a synopsis of the problems and your "villain-worthy" solution to them, so that we've got something more concrete on the Fantasy side to consider (and just for my own curiosity). But I'm starting to worry that I'm derailing the thread. It's late and I can't honestly figure whether this relates to the OP's topic. Except that the Forgotten Realms does represent a (presumably) "interesting worldview" that has "overlain games." So there you have it.

    One factor in the "preserve the status quo" thing in comics came from the Comics Code. Not depicting authority figures in a bad light was explicitly part of that code: 'Like the previous code, the CCA prohibited the presentation of "policemen, judges, government officials, and respected institutions ... in such a way as to create disrespect for established authority." But it added the requirements that "in every instance good shall triumph over evil" and discouraged "instances of law enforcement officers dying as a result of a criminal's activities."'

    Although I note that "Females shall be drawn realistically without exaggeration of any physical qualities" was also part of the code, and that one was definitely more honoured in the breach than the observance.

    See, my thing with deconstructionism is, it's an essentially critical, cynical, even reactionary discipline. Not that I don't enjoy it; Watchmen, for instance, still rings my bells. And Brand's scenario, which sounds pretty deconstructionist, made me sit up and go "awesome!" But when I said "whould't it be great if. . ." what I was thinking of was a more sincere and straightforward approach: not desconstructionism (for instance, Ozymandias' famous twist on the "Bond Villain speech" in Watchmen--it's a great moment, but essentially relies on the "straight" version that it's mocking), but rather just doing it. Saying, "here's a superhero who has definite goals and pursues them," then playing that out. And though I was primarily thinking of actual comics fiction, my wording in the last sentence makes me realize this would apply well to gaming, as well. Not that a lot of gaming doesn't already DO this, but it's worth pointing out, especially in light of Claire's description of a gamer mindset essentially opposed to such play.

    On another note, good point about the Comics Code, Mike. Comics have been an essentially straitjacketed medium for a long time; even now we're just starting to see a breakout (a mature and innovative breakout, that is--"The Ultimates," as opposed to "The Savage Dragon*.") from the superhero norms that have stood for decades. Even in the absence of the Code, I think there's still a mindset that prevails in superhero stories, though it's certainly evolved from the early Code days. Similar to the "only Villains do that" fantasy mindset that Claire encountered.
