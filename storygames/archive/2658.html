\n I already had the PDF of Seven Leagues, and I'm scheduled to run a game at our local gaming club in three weeks. On Sunday, I found a print copy at our our lovely FLGS (The Dreaming in Seattle's University District), which had just received a shipment from IPR. So today I was going through my book and musing a few ideas and possible tweaks.

First, using 2d6 instead of 1d12. I really dislike wide flat curves (a.k.a. uniform distributions) because the die roll always seems more important than the character sheet. On the other hand, I do realize that we expect crafty, talented players to stack as much as they can in their favour, but let's look at typical expected rolls.\n
  • A typical static Roll 13 might mean 4 or 5 for Virtue (range of 1-7), maybe a +1 or +2 circumstance and/or narrative modifier, so assuming a default difficulty modifier of 0 (pretty hard), 5-7 (range of 1-9) plus 1d12.
    • \n
  • A typical conflict Roll 13 might mean 4 or 5 for Virtue and +2 to +6 for narrative modifiers (say 2-3 Embellishments), so say perhaps a total of 8 or 9 plus 1d12 versus the Antagonist's roll.
\nAlmost half the time (roll of 8-12), the die roll is contributing more than the character and player. With 2d6 instead of 1d12, the high rolls would be less frequent (as would the automatic failure and automatic success -- snake eyes and boxcars, 1/36 odds each instead of 1/12). Based on experience, would this skew the results too much?

Second tweak I was interested in: how about having the players agree on the narrative bonuses for Antagonists in conflict rolls, based on the GM's narration? Do you think this would slow the game too much?

And how about giving special treatment to conflicts where the loser rolls a 12? Perhaps this would give the winner a Pyrrhic victory, or the loser would retain some unexpected advantage.

Does anyone have any recommendations on the preferred number of players, based on experience? It seems like this game might lend itself to very small groups, even single-player games.

    \n With respect to 2d6 vs 1d12, one concern would be the weakening of the 1 failure/12 success feature of the current system. More significantly though, a bell curve favors the strong. In a case similar to the static example you cite, let's look 4 Virtue, +2 for narration, no mods. On 1d12, you get success on 7+ (exactly 50%, no coincidence BTW; that's how I devised the "base" difficulty of 0). With 2d6, you increase probability somewhat (since 7 is at the "hump" of the bell curve; 58.3%). So in the midrange, barely any change; none so's you'd notice. Now let's look at 3 Virtue, no mods. Success is on 10+, or 25%, with a d12. For 2d6, though, 10+ is almost 17%. And in cases of "impossible" odds, it gets even worse: under 3% for a natured 12 on 2d12 vs. over 8% with the dodecahedron.

    2d6 Roll/Percentage
    2 = 2.8%
    3 = 5.6%
    4 = 8.3%
    5 = 11.1%
    6 = 13.9%
    7 = 16.7%
    8 = 13.9%
    9 = 11.1%
    10 = 8.3%
    11 = 5.6%
    12 = 2.8%

    The upshot is means that characters with low Virtues or narrative mods are at a progressive disadvantage compared to high Virtue characters. I feel this runs counter to the genre, where children escape ("Defeat") witches.

    But look, the proof is in the pudding. I say: try it! Let me know how the recipe turns out.

    On to your second point. I don't think the players voting on GM mods is too much of a time problem. Rather, it's a fairness issue. I think the GM has (or ought to have) the interest of the *story* at heart. Players, on the other hand, tend to get attached to individual characters. I think players would be less fair than a Narrator. It boils down to responsibility. I thought my solution of a generic mod for all GM narrations was a good compromise; but again, give it a whirl.

    I haven't given much consideration to losers meting out Defeats; I've toyed with "loser narrates", but that's not quite the same. I'd be curious to hear more about how you see a Pyrrhic Defeat might work, mechanically.

    Finally, I have found that 7L, oddly, does NOT tend to work well for single-player games. 3-4 players (alas, that old spread!) seems to work well. Players need each other to narrate cool Embellishments to get the creative juices going. Good narration tends to feed on itself. In fact, 7L does well for mass play (5-6 players or maybe more) for this very reason, plus the rules are so light lots of players don't bog things down.

    (BTW, years ago once tried to run a Champions game with a dozen players. What a nightmare! It took a whole evening and several pizzas just to play a single round. The upcoming Super Leagues will have none of that, I assure you).

    Thanks for the great questions!

    \n(1) Odds of 2d6 vs. 1d12: OK, I see what the thinking is. In most games I would find it counterintuitive because if a player sank all her/his points in a given ability, s/he should be able to kick ass at it and not be run over rough-shod by Lady Luck and the dice. But fairy tales are very much about the triumph of the small over the powerful but undeserving, so why not! I'll try it your way before I mess with the system. :-)

    (2) Player voting on GM mods: I do believe I will try that one. Seven Leagues strikes me as what Chad Underkoffler calls a "high-trust game." If the players are drawn into this shared construction of a story (as I expect the typical group at our club to be), then I don't expect selfishness problems. Besides, the GM would still give the PCs' bonuses so there is the lure of reciprocity.

    (3) Pyrrhic victories/Partial defeats: I'd like to borrow some of the narration ideas from octaNe or DitV to make defeat as conducive to a good story as success. I think it's important to show the players early in the game that "losing" means a setback for the character, not the player, the story, or the group. In other words, I want the players to relax about the bad die rolls which are bound to show up. I'm not quite sure what I'll do, but it will probably simply be along the lines of "OK, your character failed, tell me what happens."

    (4) Ideal group size: The player synergy makes perfect sense, now that you mention it. It's also true in other open-ended games like Wushu, octaNe, or PDQ. That's good, because I was planning on offering four spots to begin with, and accepting up to 6 if needed.

    Large groups: sheesh, yeah, Champions with a large group can be a bear. At the other end of the spectrum, we've been doing great with 8 players in Wushu: Roanoke (see our thread in the Actual Play section). :-) I had 7 players for Truth & Justice last Saturday and that was straining my capacity as a GM. And I certainly prefer light-weight games when I'm running.

    \n Continuing with ideas generated by reading and prepping for Seven Leagues, I'm going to do some partial cross-posting with the 7leagues mailing list. Hieronymous discussed on a recent S-G actual play thread how he had used the phrase "velvet box" to thematically link the player characters, asking the players to find some way to include these words in each character's background, to help solve the classic "why are we all together?" and "why do I care?" problems. I'm a long-time fan of thematically linked PCs and group character creation and I was musing on ways of fostering these links.\n
    • The trick used by Hieronymous: the phrase or keywords that unite the character concepts. I particularly like the juxtaposition of seemingly unrelated words you used, the cut-up or "exquisite corpse" approach that can jolt new ideas out of surprised players. here are a few links of Web sites that randomly generate such strange phrases or even verses.
      • \n
      • Language Is A Virus
        • \n
      • Exquisite Corpse
        • \n
      • Cadavre Exquis
      \n
    • Imposed or negotiated theme: Selecting a cohesive requirement such as "Your all Fables and it's 1750" or "You are the tsar's three beloved children" can greatly improve cohesion. For added fun, the entire group can take the first hour or so of the game to decide together what kind of story they want to tell: "I want tales of cleverness and saving the realm;" "I'd like this to be set in mythic China;" etc.
      • \n
    • Images: As an Everway fan, I was considering bringing cards (such as Atlas Games' "Once Upon A Time", Z-Man Games' "Fairy Tale", or Jody Bergsma's beautiful playing card decks) and having the players use them to tell each character's Legend; that would be a good opportunity use a collection of linked cards and asking that each player pick one. Picture books (e.g., collections of Arthur Rackham's fairies, Ivan Bilibine's Russian folklore, Maxwell Parrish's 1001 Nights images, etc.) would also work.
    \nI would love to hear other suggestions on how to encourage thematic party cohesion.

    \n I love the notion of cut-up (and have been a fan of the Dada-ists since the early 80s, when they came back into fashion. For some truly amazing and bizarre visual inspiration, I highly recommend the two collage novels by Max Ernst, La Femme 100 T\xc3\xaates and Une Semaine de Bont\xc3\xa9.

    In the rules I suggest kinship as one possible Troupe motif. Here are some others, perhaps more pedestrian than Anemone's:\n
    • All the Protagonists inhabit a Domain; the Dominant requires something of them;
    \n
    • "Characters may also be granted Luck as ... a consequence of aiding another being, whether bestowed intentionally or not." A being who bestowed a gift at one time or another to the Protagonists is threatened, endangering the Charm formerly bestowed;
    \n
    • Conversely, the Protagonists have all suffered a Defeat at the hands of a common Antagonist, who now requires a geas of them;
    \n
    • This one's Bradbury-esque: all the Protagonists originally arose out of the imagination of a Mortal author. His (or her) last extent manuscript is threatened by extinction.\n

      \n More ideas: setting jam. When I read the the section on the denizens and Provinces of Faerie, I think of other games from which I could borrow setting material.\n
        \n
      • Earthdawn - Although I love the setting, I never took to the system, which is both too crunchy and too erratic for my tatstes. Our group played it for nearly two years before giving it up. But when looking at the trolls and elves of Underhome, it's hard not to dream of Obsidimen and Windlings and high-adventure games that don't get bogged down by the system...
        • \n
      • Mechanical Dream - Another system that loves to mix and match die types and pool size. I could very well see exploring Kainaas and absorbing the setting in small manageable doses, stepping through Seven Leagues' Clockworks.
        • \n
      • Supers - Hieronymous mentioned his new project, Super Leagues. That made me think of another supers game that has some subsystems I love, and some I don't enjoy: Capes! Why, I can see using Capes' fabulous click-and-lock character generation, treating the abilities of the persona and power modules as Seven Leagues' Charms, Tabboos, etc.
