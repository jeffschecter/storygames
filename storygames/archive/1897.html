Bang!: A Halfway/Gateway Game?

Mcdaldno
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games
So, there's this wicked game called Bang!

It's a card game, where you play out a spaghetti western shoot-out.

Every player has a character. Each character has a special ability and an amount of hit points (most are 4, a few are 3).
Each player is given a role card. These are Sheriff, Outlaw, Deputy and Renegade. There is only one Sheriff, ever. Although everyone knows who the sheriff is, all the other roles are kept secret.

You play Bang! cards to shoot people. You play miss cards to dodge bullets. You play Panic cards to steal from other players.
There are caravan cards which give you extra draws. There are barrel cards that decrease your odds of getting hit.
There are special cards like Dynamite (basically a Russian Roulette disaster. You light it, and if it doesn't blow up in your face you pass it on.)
There are Indians that descend from the mountains and attack everyone when you play the Indians! card.
There are different guns, and different horses, to put into play.



So... I have a game group at school. before some Arts One class, we gather in the Arts One lounge and play games. Usually, we play Jungle Speed. Occassionally we've tried Fargo (a die-gambling game), Mao, and Gin Rummy. I bought Bang! last week, and sent an email out to people telling them to show up extra early for today's class.

We played. And while we were playing, I noticed a few things:
-We all had specialized characters with hit points, weapons and tools, who were making very strategic choices about how to manage resources (cards and alliances).
-A few players were getting "in character". Megan mentioned that "Yeah, the reason I missed is because this corset is too restrictive", pointing at hte illustration of her character, with its tight corset.
-Most of the roles were secret, so people were trying to feel out who was on their side and who wasn't. Similar to citizens trying to establish who is Mafia in... Mafia. It almost felt like the Trust mechanics in tMW, except all done socially.
-When the Dynamite card is played, there is a chance that it'll blow up in the face of the player who played it. Thus, there was a lot of decision making. Do I want to go out with a bang? It feels fitting for the renegade to throw dynamite into the mix, don't it? (It was more than pure statistics, too. People didn't want to play it too early, because the dynamics weren't right for dynamite laying, etc.)

And, I started to feel like this was almost a roleplaying game. If we'd had the opportunity to pick our character and role, it would definitely have passed as a RPG in my eyes.

So... I'm interested in story games. They've never played them, but are interested in Bang! (with its characters and special cards and strategy and such).
I want to introduce them to story games, in a non-pushy, non-hard-sell kind of way. Just, "hey, you might also like this. and then this."

Bang! has a premise (spaghetti western shootout, ripe with hilarity and genre trope), characters (although no character narration), hit points and weapons and armour (and by armour, I mean barrels you hide behind), events (Indians descend from the mountains!).
Where do I go from there?
What game do I suggest?

They like visual elements. Tactile elements. It needs to be playable in 30-40 minutes, with 3-8 players.
We like getting excited and riled up. Jungle Speed is thus a default game. (and not only that, but when it gets down to 5 minutes before class we tend to crank it up to quadruple speed and play while yelling at each other to go faster, and get full of adrenalin.)

Ideas?
Misery Bubblegum feels like it might work, if the rules are still similar to what I played at Camp Nerdly.
Tony, if you are reading this, what's the current state of affairs with that game?

    This is a great thread, Joe! At a previous job, there was a small group of people who liked to game together at lunch, and if the game was cool enough we could rope additional people in to play. Bang! was quite popular for awhile because it was quick and could support 4-9 people pretty easily.

    The Mafia-like aspects were pretty popular with people once they fully understood the ramifications (usually during/after the first game). I thought the secrecy/backstabbing was a really interesting and simple metagame that fit well over the basic game mechanic. My experiences were a lot different than Jason, but perhaps that was the large groups we played with or the group dynamics.

    But this brings up a much wider question about "gateway" games for non-gamers that I've been thinking about for awhile. I think the big difference between a more normal card/board game and a story game is that in the former, the elements of the story are already there. No one is going to ask you, "Ok, Bart. the Sheriff is having a smoke in front of the bank. How are you going to get your banditos in there?" The situations are already setup, usually in a stylized format. When players add color to a card/board game, it's because it's safe to do so. You aren't taking so much of a scary creative leap and the color isn't going to make someone "win" or "lose". I saw the same thing when we would play fast-moving 9-10 person games of Formula De and someone would miss a braking zone and describe their car careening with smoking tires into the turn.

    I feel like story games are something different, because in essence you are playing without a net. There's no board, there are no cards or miniatures to fall back on. In the end I think a story game so much more rewarding, but it's also an acquired taste.

    There's also the time commitment. Most people will sit easily for a 30-40 min game, and maybe a 2 hour game if it's really interesting. But if you think about the dynamics of non-gamers and casual gamers, usually if you are getting together for an evening to "game" you are playing several games.

    I would also disagree about Dogs even though I love that game. The dice mechanics take some getting used to, and the moral choices and subject matter make it not for everyone. And in a 2-4 hour session you are only creating characters, testing them and maybe getting a few scenes in.

    I think this quote was really telling: "They like visual elements. Tactile elements. It needs to be playable in 30-40 minutes, with 3-8 players." Honestly, the closest I've seen to this so far is 'Once Upon a Time' (storytelling card game), but I just wasn't satisfied with the interruption-based mechanic.

    Joe, the game you describe might not exist yet. But when it gets made, I think we would have a great gateway game. BTW, I think buffing out a game like Bang! is pretty awesome.
