    \n When I was a callow youth playing AD&D in high school and frustrated by too-slow XP accumulation, I would occasionally "round up" XP where I thought I could get away with it; this was usually towards the middle or end of a campaign, when some characters had died and been replaced or resurrected, some players had missed sessions, etc., so that differences in XP totals were expected. Usually this was done via creative arithmetic, but a few times I took out an eraser and just awarded my character a few hundred XP at home. And then, surprise surprise, when that weekend's 514xp award came along, I just happened to be only 428xp away from a level, and was as happy as a clam.

    I would also "forget" to apply some modifiers from time to time, so that if no one spoke up my chance of hitting the bad guy could be just that tiny bit higher.


    I actually still feel kind of guilty about all of that. I know it didn't actually harm anyone at all, but still, I probably shouldn't have done it.

    \nThat's interesting!

    I used to play Call of Cthulhu masters tournaments 2 times a year and smaller linked adventures at smaller conventions across the US. And cheating was rampant. The GMs were leading everyone down a specific path and needed players to succeed to move forward. The amount of times I saw... make a skill check... ok, you failed... anyone else want to try? ok... another failure... make a knowledge roll.... make an idea roll... make a luck roll... anyone else?

    It's unclear to me why rolls you can't fail were rolls at all. Even if you wanted to roll for it, there are rules in later editions for skill modifiers (doubling your percentage chance if something is easy or having it if it's hard) but no one used them. GMs I asked said they learned how to play not from the rule books but from the modules and many of the modules would often call for skill checks that many would normally assume would be an automatic success. It became part of the culture.

    Quickly, players realized that succeed or fail, the story was going to be the same. And the GM wanted you to succeed but didn't want to just give it to you as if to preserve the illusion of choice or chance. So players started doing the same. If it was obvious GMs needed you to succeed, players would cheat so they could move forward down the GM or module's plot. This was much less disruptive than going down the path of skill check -> group skill check -> knowledge check -> idea check -> luck check.

    And GB Steve nailed it exactly. Since so many skills in Call of Cthulhu are about what you know, it's hard to justify failure unless there are outside complications. Why does my University Professor who's the top 10 authority in Latin Language studies keep failing rolls to read a letter written in Latin? If the GM doesn't provide an outside reason, and keeps making you roll... it destroys any suspension of disbelief. CoC games often turned from horror into comedy. And you didn't care if your character died because a bumbling professor who can barely function at their specialty ad nauseum loses sympathy.

    So CoC players started cheating the way a D&D DM would cheat behind their DM screen to keep the adventure from falling apart. It was amazing to watch!

    Why did we keep playing? CoC games seemed to attract the best roleplayers. After getting past the above, it was almost always a guaranteed good time.

    \nThat's really interesting. I'm not sure I agree with it entirely (I'm enough of a nerd to enjoy talking about whether the rules are working or not and what we could do to make them work better), but it does make me realize that I really don't care if anyone at the table is cheating, so long as they're doing a good job of it. People in our group roll out in the open all the time, and do I ever look at what they're rolling? Fuck no. They can pretty much say whatever they like, and I'll never know. I don't look at their character sheets to see if they really have the skill bonus they claim to, I don't check their math unless they're doing it out loud, I don't keep track of how much experience points they have or what their character is carrying...none of that matters to me, as long as I'm never forced to think, "There is absolutely no god-damned way that makes any sense at all."

    Which means as long as they never roll suspiciously well-timed natural 20s one right after the other, or claim to be masters of seventeen different areas of expertise, or anything else that brings down that particular pretend building, I'm cool with it. Give yourself extra XP if you feel like it. Add a dot of something if you need to. Claim a success when you should have failed. Work on your trick dice-throwing skills, go dice-bowling, whatever. It's all the same to me as long as you're not being ridiculous about it. I'm not going to try to catch you cheating, so as long as you don't make a big deal out of cheating, we're good.

(You draw a certain number of cards, and if one's a joker, you come back to life with awesome abilities! And a demon inside you!)

    As it happened, another player shot my corpse in the head so he couldn't come back as any kind of undead. So at that point, I breathed a sigh of relief (I was starting to feel pretty guilty), and shuffled normally.

    Card three when I drew up my new character was a Joker. I would have gotten to be Harrowed naturally. On the plus side, the character I ended up playing is one of my favorite characters ever.
