OK, it's that time again. Just to recap, here's where it began.

To recap the Intention:

You all know what happened with Battlestar Galacrica. That corny period-piece got not only a facelift, but an entire re-imagination almost from scratch. Most of the character names were the same, and the Cylon Threat remained, but after that it was basically put through some really creative gymnastics, and what emerged was awesome, pure awesome. Well, much of that is the screenwriting and direction, but still, you get my point.

Here's an art project where someone Awesome-O-Fied HE-MAN (the anime/action figure line), and was such a jaw-dropper that it directly inspired this line of threads.

Anyway, I was thinking that maybe the same can be done to old D&D (or other) campaign worlds. Take the core ideas that are there, and shake them up. Reimagine them.

To recap the Rules:

If you're tangentially familiar with the setting, join in the brainstorm session.

Just throw out a few ideas (or one) per post. You don't have to write paragraphs, even a sentence or two is ok.

You do not have to build upon yourself (you could say "There are Elves, and they are badass!" and "There are NO Elves!" in the same post, just to throw out some ideas). You can build on your own ideas if you want.

You do not have to build upon the ideas of others; you are absolutely free to contradict or ignore them. You can build on others' ideas if you want.

It's not "group setting creation" so much as simply brainstorming.

Also, feel free to pull things out of Dark Sun that you liked and talk about them (and why you liked them), which can maybe set off some cool ideas.

-Andy

    Yay, first post.

    I was a huuuuge dark sun fan as a kid and teenager, and I remember wanting to change a couple of things to the setting even then to make it full of more badassery. So, as I remember:

    When the sundering came, all of the peoples of the world were left with nothing. Magick robbed them of their lands, their food. Many women found their wombs unable to hold child. Milk curdled and seeds turned to dust. But there were people left behind. Many people, and those many people that wanted to fight and live found themselves and gathered together. They created a city-state, Tyr, and lived together, fighting against a dying world for life. It has been thirteen generations since the sundering of the lands.

    The old races are still there: Human, Dwarf, Elf, Halfling, Thri-kreen. The common tongue that has developed over the years is a polyglot of all of their languages. Indeed, there are very few culutural boundaries left between the races.

    All of the races, excluding the Thri-kreen, can have children together, that's why their called races and not species. Most of those children are born dead, sadly. Some rare few do live on, sickly and twisted. All of these half-breeds are called Mul, from the dwarven tongue meaning "bent". Oddly, the dwarves seem to be the most capable of interbreeding, their children coming out strong, hardy and healthy. It is rare for Mul to make children, but it has been known to happen on occasion. The less said of these, the better.

    No gods. No clerics empowerd by gods. There are many different strange faiths on this world, but none grant mystical powers. There is no proof of any god existing. You are all alone.

    Magic users could syphon life energy from anything that was alive. Plants, sure, and animals too. But they could also take from their own bodies, or the worst amongst them would steal lifeforce from other people. The energy could in no way be stored. It was through the syphoning and weaving of this energy that magick was created.

    One gigantic city-state. Huge, sprawling, monstrous. It's impossible for many people to live in the far-off desert for very long, so the city-state, let's just use Tyr 'cause it's a cool name, must be gigantic. It houses most of what is left of humanity. There are plots of land inside of it that have, over a couple of generations, been nursed back enough that they can hold resilient crops like wheat. Legionnaires guard this land constantly and any caught stealing from it are seen as the worst of criminals, akin to rapists and murderers. The city is a loud, bustling, vibrant place, full of weird music and alien art and architecture from the many different cultures that were forced to live together when the magic burned up the fertility of the planet.

    Why go outside of the walls of Tyr? Because there are fortunes to made on the rare bits of gold that can be found there. There are the remnants of the old civilization to be found. There are creatures that threaten the borders of Tyr, horrible monsters that attack the farmlands and water supplies. Because there are still small outposts of people out there that need supplies and may be rich in rare supplies of their own. Because of those rare people that come to Tyr selling mysterious objects and claiming to come from another city-state well south of Tyr. Because, in your heart you know there is more.

    Just like in Rome, Gladiators are celebrities. It is foolish to waste expensive slaves on death in the arenas, so many Gladiators are free men searching out fame and fortune. Indeed, most Gladiators do not kill in the arenas, though they may beat and break their opponents quite readily.

    Lose the psychic powers. It dilutes the power of magick.

    More about class culture. Show more of what it's like to live as a slave, a freeman, a merchant, artisan, etc. Rather than having the rich, the slaves and a vast and muddy middle, discuss more of what is in that cream in the center of the oreo.

    I like what Blue said about the halflings. Keep that shit around.

    That's all that I've got for now. Off to work.

    Little things:

    Kalak the Sorcerer-King (who dies in the first metaplot adventure).

    The leader of Tyr is Prince Ahmad Ibn Kalak al-Tyr. He is seen as beautiful, generous, trustworthy, cruel but fair, often compassionate, and also secretive and mysterious. He is recognized as a celebrity ("Did you see him? Who was that lovely woman on his arm? Did you see what meal he ate? The restaurant he ate at will certainly become popular now!" etc), and in fact has lived for several hundreds of years, forever young, and often goes among the people (to meet them, to go out to eat, to rest in various parts of the city, etc). His father Kalak is the True King of Tyr, but rarely emerges from his palace and is even more shrouded in mystery.

    Of course, this is all an elaborate ruse. How deep it goes, at what lengths Ahmad goes to retain the peoples' loyalty, and what dark, so utterly dark arts he engages in back at the palace... The Prince's True Form... that may all come out one day.

    Dragons

    There is only one dragon, and it inhabits a spectral dreamtime-plane that intersects with the material plane. People can only ever encounter the Dragon in Dreams, while Unconscious, or more often when under a heavy Soma-trance or in an elaborate Dream-Ritual. The dragon is a cruel being, but also wise. It teaches those it finds worthy. Every once in a long time it makes its presence felt in the world, and that's usually when horrible disasters (natural and unnatural) occur.

      Thinking about the idea of magic-as-radiation, what if it was but wasn't?

      Magic makes stuff happen that wouldn't otherwise. It's like directed human action, but moreso. You chuck out all these spells, creating all these spectacular effects and they have to go somewhere. They dissipate like smoke into the air, but they don't vanish into nothingness. You could look at a spell as a temporary burst of order, then the after effects are an equal and opposite contribution of disorder to the world. If you mix all the colours of the rainbow, you end up with brown. The use of magic increases the tendancy of the world towards entropy. That's why the world is a bleak wasteland.

      Bonus nifty: by implication, any order-creating action driven by free will (magic is just the uber example) will eventually slingshot back into an equal amount of entropy. The cosmological principles of the dark sun universe are actively hostile to life. The system is rigged to make itself hostile to life in response to life's existence. That's one hell of a metaphysical truth to have lurking in the background - and worth highlighting because it's so relevan to the foreground.

      Anyway. I swear I was thinking about radiation.

      Take as a given that the world is a bleak wasteland because of entropy - the tendancy of chaos to average out over time. As the amount of order (order is probably a bad term to use because it implies stasis, when from the human PoV stasis is the consequence of entropy) in the world decreases, entropy acts more and more on those remaining pockets of order. Which is where the radiation thing comes in. The world arrived at its' current through the averaged effects of cnstant change. As there's less stuff to affect, the effects become more pronounced and noticable when they occur. Hence mutations and weirdness and scary random things happening in the night and "my baby why won't you show me my baby?" are a part of the setting.

      Moving on, you can also look at that in another way too. Magic is fuelled by increasing entropy (not a straightforward cause and effect, but definite correlation). From a certain point of view, any instance of order - living things in particular - is a magic battery.       And that was the genesis of defiler magic. In a world that's constantly slowing down, the regular kind of magic was growing less and less effective (because there was less stuff to act against). And by properly I mean actively shortening the life of the world by 30 seconds every time they want to know "who's the fairest of them all?".

      Yes, I understand that the DS setting is to some extent an allegory for environmentalism concerns. And you can focus on that in a simple "Save the World vs Wasters" dichotomy, in which both sides are simply rational, and one side is just not altruistic enough to realize that they're destroying the world for future generations.

      But I think that's going to get old pretty quickly, in part because it's too simple an equation. Probably fine if what you're looking for is just a place to stage miniatures battles. But if you're looking to get at some more serious introspection about the relationship of cultures and beliefs and their intersection to environmental matters, I think that you'll have to add some things to the setting.

      That is, D&D settings are often quite thin in these departments because all they need to function in terms of setting up conflict is the alignment rules. They are evil, we are good, let's fight! But that voids all real discussion in game about the potential subject matter. To make it have any depth again, you're going to have to chuck alignments, and start digging deeper into the belief systems that the cultures have in order to find the more complex conflicts that lie beneath.

      That "digging" may mean, in fact, "making up stuff." There's a point at which you'll look at the culture at hand and it should occur to you that it's far, far from detailed enough to be like any real culture. At that point, it's fine to start adding details not only to make it realistic, but to create sources of conflict.

      For example, to me the most interesting thing isn't to try to make the conflict between the major sides at all more grey. But it's to factionize each of the big sides themselves so that there's a lot of potential for conflict.

      An easy one, for instance, is the "Ends Justify the Means" argument. That is, there'll be some rebelious types who feel that it's OK to damage the environment a bit in the course of overthrowing the regime. Then there'll be those rebels who feel that doing so is throwing away the potential legacy that they're fighting for. And what of other rebellions in other city-states? Is it worth overthrowing one Dragon-King if that merely means that another will take it's place? What values to the rebels in that place have? Are they compatible?

      The mere existence of "Preservers" as opposed to "Defilers" says that such philosophical differences appear on an individual level. But somebody taught this magic to each of these types, they didn't learn it from nowhere. Who does this teaching, and can they possibly avoid being involved in the politics that swirl around them?

      Are all of the Druid spirits all friendly with each other? Or are there separate groups of Druids based on how their spirits interact? How do they relate to the spirits? That is, who teaches a Druid how to speak to a spirit? And what lessons do they tell in their stories about the spirits in terms of what to say in order to placate said spirit? What values does that transmit to the individual?

      Even if the world has a large amount of materialist thinking, I think we can see that there's a potential of complex inter-relation of belief systems that you can develop here. You don't have to make them whole cloth, there are the seeds of these things right in the setting material. All you have to do is to develop them into something deeper than just a source of power, but discover the groups' and characters' relationships to those powers.

      I like the bit about water, but that's a lot of dice. .. ... ... and yet, I've bought Koplow dice in the hundreds, so I'm not one to talk. :-)

      If you want to play the survival parts of the setting up, here's a possible way:

      Throw a group of bonus blue dice in the middle of the table. This is Water. When you use them, it symbolizes dehydration. Imagine them as Bonus Dice from TSOY (but if I were using TSOY, I would completely remove Gift Dice and replace them with Water Dice), or those bonus 1d6es in Eberron (but for d20 I'd replace them with 1d10s). So an average group will have about 20 or so of these dice.

      These dice only replenish once per scenario/adventure completion. Not every session, but rather at the completion of an "Adventure", at which time the game world advances no less than One Week, and the Water dice are replenished.

      A player can pull and roll as many Water Dice for a roll that he wants to. No limit. Though a social example of, "it's probably more polite to only take one or two max unless the others envourage you to take more" would be helpful.

      When a player really wants something, they take a Water die, roll it and add it to the result. They can do this for an action, a conflict, an attack, a damage roll, etc. If they want to, they can pull dice Before their initial roll, or after their roll. They cna then add an Adiational water die to the roll, and so on.

      When a player uses the last of the Water Dice, SOMEONE DIES. A character, that is. Player death is not encouraged.

      The Discussion: The group members look around. Someone may volunteer: "I want to die, guys". In that case, the GM kills off that character (see below). In most cases, no one will step forward to die, or will find it more fun to go with the Call to Death.

      The Call to Death: The players should engage in as little open debate as possible. There's no reason to convince the group of Who Should Die, in fatc it would be best to explicity not allow such talk. Instead, pieces of paper are passed around. The players have to write down the name of the PC who Should Die. They then fold it up and pass it to the GM. If someone accidentally posts the name of a Player instead of that player's character, then that vote paper is ripped up- The GM is encouraged to give that chosen player a disdainful look as he does so.

      The GM silently counts the votes, finds the "Winner", nods in affirmation, and destroys the votes (preferably by fire). If there is a tie, the GM may call for another vote (requesting that the players think about changing their vote, but that they don't have to). Otherwise, the GM can just choose between the two chosen characters. If after the second vote there is a second tie, the GM should just choose one of the two characters.

      The name of the Chosen Characer is not revealed.

      The Death: Within the next session (any time during that session), the GM will create an opportunity for that character to Die. There is no saving throw or dodge roll. It fucking Happens. The Death has to follow the guidelines set below that fit the mileu of the Dark Sun World. The GM is encouraged to create a scene of chaos, and lead the players to think that perhaps Another character had been the Chosen Character (if you've watched enough House MD, it's like the scene before the opener: You see someone obviously sick, but then person next to them ends of having convusions and being the Big Sick Patient that episode).

      The death has to come about from one of the following things:

      The Harshness of the Wilderness - (being caught in a sandstorm, falling into a sand trap, being preyed upon by Fire Murder Ants, etc)
      Random Violent Act - (bandit crossbow bolt, rampaging monster, noble's carriage rolls over you, etc)
      Fury of the Sorcerer King - (If you have done actions that would have angered the Sorcerer-King, then she finds out, and kills you, either herself, through her templars, or by some combination of torture and execution - Tossed into the obsidian mines where a guard shivs you after your first day, etc)
      Treachery - (poison elf tea given to you by an assassin who was long on your trail, stabbed in the back by an NPC you saved, etc)

      After the Death, the Water Dice are automatically returned to normal. One of the Water Dice is replaced with a die of the color Red*, and are called Blood Dice.

      (and if it were my game, these Blood Dice would be higher stepped than regular water dice. Like, if all Water dice were d6es, then these dice would be d10s or d12s).

      Thinking a bit about a specific dying desert world fantasy game system.

      So, we start with Andy's idea of the common pool of Water Dice, which everyone may draw from to boost their actions.

      Now, I would want to make it so the player-characters aren't just some jumble of adventurers, instead they are a community. A ragged little desert town (&/or tribe), a noble house, a caravaning merchant guild (or gypsie family), or some such.

      In any case, the Water Pool belongs to this community - it is the community's water supply. And, since water is power, both literally and figuratively, it also can be seen as your people's powerbase.

      Also, it'd be nice to have the water dice that are used turn into other kinds of dice, i think. (The pollution idea Daniel Solis mentioned.) This changes the tone a bit: you aren't using up your water supply, your world, you're corrupting it. The transmutation colours should all be uni-directional, morally dice that harm, only harm, dice that heal, only heal to keep the pollution unambiguous.
