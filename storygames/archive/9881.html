    \n Traditionally Call of Cthulhu starts with a hook. You might call this a bang although it's often more gentle than that. It's basically where the PCs discover that something is up. This might be at the reading of a will, the disappearence of a friend or a trek into the jungle to find some ruins.

    Often this part of the adventure is written after you've decided on the nature of the threat. And it is almost always a threat. The players might want occasionally to play cultists but the usual heroic mode is to be trying to save something from the Mythos threat. And in the the manner of Liberty Valance, the way to defeat the Mythos is to use its powers against it and thus become tainted onself (see The Man Who Shot Joseph Curwen by Ken Hite).

    Once you've decided what the threat is, then you can work out the ramifications of the threat and work backwards as to how the PCs become involved and forwards as to what the threat will do if not stoppped (a bit like a Dogs game).

    The Mythos game is almost always plotted because it's a mystery game and part of the fun is in not knowing what is going to happen. You could use the Dogs method to generate a scenario, all you'd have to work on then is a plausible hook.

    There are ways of impovising such games (here's one) but you'd need players who are well versed in the Mythos to make it happen.

    \n Trail of Cthulhu is quite good for scenario structure. It goes something like this.

    The Hook: Something happens to draw you into the adventure.

    Scene 1...which leads you to a Weird Place where you Find Stuff Out.
    Scene 2...which leads you to another Weird Place where you Find Stuff Out.
    Scene 3...which leads you to another Weird Place where you Find Stuff Out.
    Scene 4...which leads you to another Weird Place where you Find Stuff Out.
    Scene 5...which leads you to another Weird Place where you Find Stuff Out.

    Final Scene...which leads you to the final Weird Place where it all comes to a head.

    The Final Scene needn't always be a confrontation. It can be a ritual or, well, anything, really. My Cthulhu scenarios tend to end with the Investigators confronting the monster but unable to do anything about it.

    Also, it needn't be that linear. Scene 1 can give you several clues, which lead to Scenes 2, 3 and 4. I like that structure much better, actually. Essentially, you dump players in A Weird Location, like a village, and say: "OK, in this village, there's a church, a pub, a library and a monastery on the hill. Where do you investigate first?".

    One thing to keep in mind is that many CoC adventures are run as one-shots, perhaps with characters that survive and re-appear in the next one-shot. Or not. Also, CoC players tend to come into the game without any real adventure-avoidance issues. CoC doesn't tend to be played sand-box style, where players hare off and start a bootlegging business or something.

    The thing about that is: You can rely on the players to muddy things up from simple mis-understanding, and tracking down red-herrings that they've created in their own minds, so keep it simple.

    The Haunted House is a classic CoC adventure for that reason. It's really only a couple of pages long, but with new players it's almost always memorable.

    \n Bret, so you know what Steve was saying about onion layers? Steve knows what he's talking about and does actually smell strongly of onion.

    The adventure starts mundane and becomes weirder. So you don't start with "This big eldritch thing has been seen shambling outside town". You start with something like "This guy has disappeared". And, when they investigate, they find creepy things: like "It looks like he was torn savagely apart" or "He's wearing an amulet with a weird symbol".

    And then libraries are often used, so perhaps there are records of people dying in similar ways, throughout history. And the amulet is used by some sort of cult.

    And so on, weirder and weirder, until you get to the monster's lair, and finally the monster itself.

    Is one way to do it.

    \n I re-read CoC (several editions, in fact) last year, and what stayed with me was this sense that the game's writers actually presume many different forms of campaign; most significantly, it seems that the default campaign form should actually not be adventure-based, that adventures are only something you run as special events at conventions or such. By this I mean, it is more important for individual adventures to paint a total picture over time, as the characters come to be steeped in the lore and ultimately form some sort of organized mythos detective / mythos defence agency. Individual missions are not that important.

    From this viewpoint, it's not that important to make your individual adventures ultra-elaborate or fraught with danger, as your goal is more to make a string of seemingly separate events that slowly develop into an organic campaign. It also gives you context for failure: it's OK for the characters to not solve an individual mystery: they can just take the weird artifact home, and sometimes later the GM brings it back in a new context, in which it makes sense anew.

    This second, non-adventure-based model of CoC: that's what I want to write into a clearer, more playable form some day.

    \n HPL's "The Dunwich Horror" would be where I'd start. The generic picture you get of a CoC party, with one dude each wielding a spellbook, a scientific/technological implement, and a shotgun? That's the good guys in "The Dunwich Horror."

    I started improvising CoC scenarios only after running a dozen published ones. They're good practice. The adventures in Chaosium's "The Stars are Right!" are good, as are the ones in the Delta Green sourcebooks from Pagan Publishing. At the next level of prep/difficulty, there's Pagan's hard-to-find "More Tales of Terror", a book of situations with three possible explanations each, usually Mythos, occult, and Scooby Doo (a few T of Ts are printed in issues of "The Unspeakable Oath", too). The high-wire act is (apologies to Baker) Doggy-style, where you don't know The Answer going in. The key to success in that style is big-time player buy-in; if your players are Lovecraft fans, or don't mind reading a few stories as prep, that should be easy, since the genre conventions run so strongly.

    \n I think one of the key elements to stress in CoC scenarios is tragedy; for an (effectively) old-school game - the rules haven't really changed since its first publication - it makes for some dashed expectations. Published scenarios are the way to go, even if you just read them for the feel of it. The sanity system basically means that, no matter what short-term goals might be met, in the long term all the PCs are doomed. But read some scenarios to see how this happens - in Secrets of Kenya, for instance, there's a scenario about the PCs being captured by troglodytes and climbing a massive underground wall to try and escape - only they can't. That's it - no hope, no way out - just lots of horrific ways for them to fail. It's a game about tragedy, really, not just horror (although in every scenario I've ever played, and a few that I've ran, I've been genuinely scared).

    And I don't see how my little seed is "bad Cthulhu", anyway. To me the crux of the Mythos is that it is so close to our world, except for that one crazy thing that cruelly reminds us of the universe's ability to go on without us. Tentacles are an accident. Secrets and revelations that drive their viewers to insanity are the essence. I see lots of potential here for secrets and revelations both.
