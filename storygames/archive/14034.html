\n For those games where you come up with your setting and possibly even your genre at the table ... or maybe in e-mail before the session starts ... what's out there? What are the pros & cons of each?
Here are some I know about:

Sorcerer - the "one sheet" ... GM has the most authority ... means the players might feel disenfranchised

Universalis - take turns coming up with tenets, building on the result, but you can challenge - often brings awesome (leonardo da vinci: demon hunter was an awesome premise from one of my games that we sadly never finished)

PTA - spend an hour doing some very freeform negotation with some pointed questions about premise / tone - I haven't played this one, I've heard good and bad things about the setup process - an hour is a long time for a one-shot

Archipelago - choose a setting and genre, with "try a different way" in effect. make a map.

Geiger Counter - style sheet possibly, structured pre-production: premise / directorial style / sketch of the map / trailer and working title.

Microscope - big picture; bookends; add & ban list

I think most of these suffer from a "who speaks first wins" problem - and you get a setting/genere that the other players are okay with (that's why they didn't veto, that's why they "yes, and...") but not super-excited about.
Settings can also end up kind of bland or cliche - nothing making them special / unique.

What else is there?

Do you have any house techniques that your group uses to decide what setting/genre to play next?

    \n Fiasco's playsets give some setting creation, even when the playset itself is a bit constrained. But what details are relevant or present in the Manna Hotel differ from game to game, depending on player choice.


    Ganakagok has everyone add details one at a time to the physical map and the social relationship map.


    I don't know if it is still in the current rules, but I know that Jason Godesky's game the Fifth World used to have a very interesting setting creation setup phase. It is a post-apocalyptic primitive utopia game, so each player picked a real local landmark to be important in play. Then each other player proposed how the location changed in the next 400 years. The player who proposed the location picks the idea that they like best, which becomes the true story of that place.

    \n I think you can definitely play "steampunk space" Fiasco. Actually, you can play "steampunk space" Mountain Witch without changing any of the rules (just writing different traits and going off to kill the Babbage Queen or whatever), so that might be opening the door a bit.

    I think, however, that the "punchy consensus premise" is both mythical and kinda a red herring. It works way better, in my experience, for the GM/facilitator to come in with a concept/playbook/something and then for the players to decide which parts they are interested in and drive towards them.

    Like, I come in saying, "We're playing Mountain Witch, but it's in steampunk space and you're all going off to kill the Babbage Queen. Draw your Darque Fates. What kinds of total badasses are you and where do you meet up, in the process of journeying into the heart of the Peerless Babbite Empire?" And then one player says, "I'm the captain of the Arc Regal, a piratical steam-barque which runs on the flames of a captured elemental." And then suddenly we have pirates and elementals, stuff I never expected, but I'm still down because it's still steampunk space Mountain Witch.

    That kind of thing, rather than starting from nothing, is generally much more gripping and effective and that's why, following IAWA, both Geiger and Fiasco went more in that direction. My sense is that the upcoming 3rd edition of PTA is also pushing more along those lines.

    Posted By: jdfristromDo you have any house techniques that your group uses to decide what setting/genre to play next?
    \nOur process is pretty traditional, I think.

    First someone gets the urge to run a game in some particular setting and/or genre, they psych themselves up for it, and then they pitch it to the group to get other people interested. Nothing gets played if someone isn't already interested in running it: there's no "you need to run a game, like, right now" pressure and no "one of us should totally run this game" nonsense. Interested applicants only, no conscription.

    Then there's at least one session where everyone talks about the game, focusing mainly on what sorts of characters will be in it and what kinds of things they will be doing, plus setting- and genre-specific questions ("Are there zeppelins?"). This is really the most crucial part of the process, it's where the person who's running the game and the people who are playing in it get on the same page with each other. While it's not explicitly a collaborative effort the way a PTA pitch session might be, there's a lot of feedback running in both directions, and people aren't shy about advocating for particular things they want or pushing back on things they don't want. The person running the game obviously starts out more invested in the setting and establishes most of the details (in whatever way they typically like to do that), but it'd be ridiculous to think that the players aren't getting a lot of input into everything.

    During the game, many times players will introduce setting elements or otherwise push the envelope of what's already been established (some more than others, based on personal preference and how comfortable they feel with that particular setting), and most of the time the GM just picks that stuff up and runs with it. If for some reason that doesn't happen, there's the expectation that a reason will be given ("No, actually, this is after the Hindenburg burned, so there aren't really that many zeppelins left and there's no big zeppelin pilot union" or whatever).


    In the past, we've tried out other ways of doing things, but it never worked out for us.

    Having the group sit down and brainstorm a setting together was fun, but a complete disaster in play; it just led to a game where everyone just kind of liked the setting, but no one LOVED it, and we've found that for our games to really hum, we need at the very least a GM who LOVES the game. We have a lot of slow-burn players who need a few sessions to get a handle on their character and the setting and really connect with it, and without someone enthusiastic to sit at the head of the table and encourage that, things bog down.

    Taking a setting from someone else (trying to use a prepackaged setting without any modifications, using a "shared world" setting with other groups, etc.) runs into the same problem; the GM rarely loves the setting, is visibly less enthusiastic about running the game than we're used to, and it drops the energy level of the whole table.

    On the opposite side, way back when we were younger and stupider, we had a few experiences with overly-autocratic GMing. Not surprisingly, a GM who loves his setting too much to ever let the players affect even the tiniest corner of it turns out to be a recipe for a terrible, terrible game. No feedback and collaboration is perhaps even more deadly to our fun than too much collaboration.

    Generally, we tend to do best with games run by someone who has a clear idea of what kind of game they really and truly want to be running, can share that idea with everyone else, and can leave enough room and flexibility to incorporate the very best ideas brought forward by everyone. We want a strong and committed director who knows how to listen, I guess.

    \n We tried an experiment inspired by Shock: tonight - the first one, where we all wrote down premises or settings and then tried to mash them up, didn't work, because our settings were all incompatible. We couldn't figure out how to mix "Haight/Ashbury in the sixties" with "Turn of the century - gaslight."

    So then we tried writing one or two evocative words on two flash cards and see how many we could get in the setting.

    Our words:
    Orgone
    Espionage

    Submarines
    Lighthouse

    Submarine
    Volcano

    (Yeah, my friends both picked submarine(s)...what's up with that?)

    So ... it nearly could have been Spy Who Loved Me, but "Submarine" instead of "Submarines" made us think of underwater civilization, and Orgone made us think of a whole different species. So we zeroed in on a nuclear sub (with a russian spy on-board) on a diplomatic mission to visit the Orgonians - a race of orgone-infused fish-men that had just made violent contact with the USA at a particular lighthouse. The lighthouse keeper was on-board as well, the one human who had met Orgonians and lived...

    So, success, I think. After we'd set things up, one friend said, "I could just do this over-and-over all night."
    So after we finished playing we had time to set another one up just for fun. That one turned out to be a Victorian-era story about a mining company that was causing sinkholes in the neighboring area, opening up a network of caverns ... and unleashing some kind of phantom (or maybe the phantoms were invented by the company to scare people away, so they wouldn't realize the company was culpable ... scooby doo) ...

    \n HeroQuest suggests using questionnaires to create the background for the PCs community, and from those choices determining the resources the community has in addition to its history. There is an example in the rulebook. Sartar, Kingdom of Heroes has a clan questionnaire for setting up the background for an Orlanthi clan in Sartar - the PCs make choices about the clan's mythic history, ancient history and recent events. Earlier versions of the same idea appeared in Hero Wars and the Gloranthan computer game King of Dragon Pass. It's a way to allow the PCs some kind of choice around what stances their community holds to setting elements, rather than setting elements. We also find its useful for running through the background for the PCs in a way that is more interesting than reading it aloud, or creating handouts.

    Diaspora has 'cluster generation' where each person at the table (including the GM) gets to design one of more systems within the cluster in turn. You can use anything from random dice rolls to whim to figure out system characteristics, the creativity comes from setting aspects on the system and creating some kind of narrative that explains that system and the whole cluster. Works well in play and we came up with some interesting ideas that meshed together far better than I thought they might.

    Houses of the Blooded gives players the right to narrate 'facts about the world' for successes in contests - these leads to 'on the fly' situation and setting generation. As a GM you really have to roll with the punches on this one, as you might have been juggling a whole set of facts that have happened in game into some shape in your head when someone introduces a dramatic twist that turns that perception on its head.

    \n Smallville's Pathways system creates the player characters, all of their supporting characters and locations, and significant non-player characters and lays them out in a relationship map. The system itself is inspired by flowchart-style lifepath chargen such as that in Rogue Trader. The relationship map is entirely collaborative and the GM (Watchtower) does not, by default, have anything to do with it other than to advise or coach new players on the process. In a sense, like PTA and to a lesser extent Fiasco, the players at the table are creating their own spinoff campaign and identifying all of their key conflicts and characters in the first session. Watchtower takes the map and uses it during prep to determine what characters to create that can drive wedges between PCs and drive up the conflict.
    \n
    \nDresden Files, of course, has a similar process whereby the group creates the city in which the campaign will take place, determines important aspects that make the city interesting, which leads into actual chargen.

    \n Reading Annalise, I like that the First Player gets to decide the setting. I mean, you can talk about it all you want before hand, but the minute the First Player sets the opening scene, that's cannon. It's nice to have the rules set so clearly.

    Remember Tomorrow you get to establish whatever you want, and a few times we've had seemingly cotradictory cannon, but when you get them introduced and established, they smash into one another and gel into something nice. You can let the dice hammer out any arguments there.

    But my favorite way to introduce setting is still The Mountain Witch. The Dark Fates give you retroactive continuity and flashbacks. There's a wicked chess-match of "oh no, you may have believed her to be your sister, however..." etc. Admittedly, I'm partial to it probaby because it was my introduction to this kind of stuff. Before that, it was all pre-established fiction.
