\n Been playing GM-less games for perhaps 10 years now and these last few months, I've joined a group who play a massive amount of GM-less games. I love it!

But I noticed something.

It doesn't really matter what kind of game you play, the build-up is pretty much the same if you're playing GM-less games with the same people over and over again. It's the same kind of drama with different characters in a different environment. But I remember when I played Archipelago (GM-less) with some other folks and it was an amazing session, but I wasn't that sure if it was thanks to the game or the gaming group. The structure for how we prepared the game, framed scenes and used the rules was similar. So yes, both groups understood the game.

Now follow me on this thought.

In traditional games with a GM, people usually say that it doesn't really matter what the game is; if the GM is good, it will result in good sessions. And of course, it's the other way around as well. The thing is that after a while you (read: me) start to predict how the GM is steering the game. You learn the build-up.

My reflection is as following: because traditional games with a GM only depends on one person, you can get several different build-ups in a gaming group with several GMs in their own games, even if all the GMs are using the same game.

In GM-less games, it depends more on the group as whole which means that different games will pretty much result in the same stories.

GM-less RPGs played with my regular group
Archipelago
My Life With Master (sort of GM-less - the players frame their own scenes)
Polaris
Psychodrame
Svart av kval, vit av lust (we've played this a lot)
The Coyotes in Chicago (sort of GM-less - it's one PC played by one player and 3-4 GMs)
While the World Ends
Zombie Cinema

PS. Just a disclaimer: I don't see this as a general problem for all players. The problem lies probably within me, because I tend to notice build-ups. I can't enjoy certain movies for the same reason. "Boy meets girl, boy looses girl, boy gets girl back" as a simplified description of a build-up for RomComs, for example. DS.

    \n I was going to suggest that you try a GM-less game where you just play your characters and don't worry about dramatic arc, and where the starting situation and rules ensure that evolution and escalation will happen through play. But I don't know if such a game exists.

    In Dead of Night, the system actually codifies and tracks a "tension level", from which the GM is supposed to derive (among other things) the fictional tenor of the moment. So you probably won't wind up doing exactly the arc you would have done anyway. I bet such a system could be used in GM-less play.

    Another random idea: come up with a list of broad types of Bangs for Sorcerer, list 'em on a table, and roll on it whenever the group needs more scene fodder or a player "feels like something should happen now". Interpret the results in context ("An antagonist expands the scope of their threat" means the corrupt cop is now going after your mom!). It wouldn't be as quality as a good GM's bangs, but it might be playable. As for demons, one or more players would take those on as their primary characters.
