\n Some newbies in the local scene have had a bad time trying to jump right into level 1 characters.

Does anyone know of good rules / options for slicing the 1st level powers into smaller chunks, so it can be learned in pieces instead of all at once?

I'm thinking of something like...

Level 0.1 - Constitution HIt points and stats, basic racial features
Level 0.2 - Other Racial features / powers
Level 0.3 - Class Features (but not powers)
Level 0.4 - Feats
Level 0.5 - At-Will power
...
and so on so that by the time you hit level 1, you're a level 1 character and you've learned all the pieces individually.

    \n I see this as being more than just the fractional level rules thing and more a scenario that teaches the parts of the game.

    Child I
    Stats: None
    Get: A blank character sheet, a name, choose your race (but don't read the rules).
    Play: Introduce yourself as a kid, by name. Describe yourself.
    Learn: Speaking in character.

    Child II
    Stats: All 8s, 8 hit points
    Get: race features, racial traits EXCEPT stat changes
    Play: Hide & seek
    Learn: Rolling initiative, Moving on the grid, rollin' a d20 to hide

    Child III
    Stats: Choose your 6 stats, hit points change per Constitution
    Get: racial stat bonuses, train starting skills
    Play: You guys were horsing around and *smash* you broke a vase. Adults will be mad. What do you do?
    Learn: In-character social interactions, making stuff up, coming up with ideas for how to act, maybe working as a group.

    Child IV
    Get: Class features;
    Play: A gang of teenagers picks on your party.
    Learn: Making attack rolls, taking damage, beating minions.

    Child V
    Get: gain 1 feat; gain 1 at-will attack power\t
    Play: Interlude (training montage)
    Learn: Describing actions you take over time

    Child VI
    Get: gain 1 encounter attack power
    Play: A trip through the woods (angry animal attack!) with an adult bowman not too far away
    Learn: How to use powers (at-will vs. encounter). Possibly getting butts kicked.

    Child VII
    Get: gain a second at-will attack power
    Play: some kind of group rite-of-passage
    Learn: Skill challenges

    Level 1
    Get: Your daily power
    Play: Start the game
    Learn: Not my problem anymore.

    \n Not wanting to be difficult, but I just don't see the psychology here. I used to do this sort of hand-holding myself earlier in the decade, but it never ended up well: the only result I got from excessively simplifying a game and then re-introducing the real game in steps was that I stringed happless newbies along for quite a while with false pretenses on what the game was really about. I mean, just look at what your suggested character generation method implies about the game: were I to start in this game, I would assume that we're going to play some loose, light and fun story-telling game thing; it would be quite jarring to realize slowly that actually naming my character and whatnot is just spinning my wheels.

    Or I don't know, I suppose this could work with sufficiently young children. Not too young, though, as they'll need to be able to grasp the full rules soon enough. You know your target audience much better than I do, to be sure.

    \n I think that Eero's point is that, if the first session with a group of players new to RPGs work as I think it would given your "Child I" setup, the session would likely play out as a kind of freeform acting game, which may lead the players towards a type of play which D&D simply is not meant to be used for. Then, when they enter "Child II" phase and are told to move around a battlemat, they're likely to still be involving a lot of acting, and probably wonder "What's this grid meant for, anyway? It serves no purpose in my story." And so on.

    Worst case scenario, this may lead to a very conflicted type of game, with players not quite sure what it's all about and what play they're meant to engage in.
    Best case scenario, they'll learn all kinds of useful role-playing techniques (characterizing, story weaving, min-maxing, tactical thinking) and utilize them all in the end to great effect - and then each 4 hour session can handle only about one encounter because of all the other "fluff" that doesn't have anything to do with the 4E "system" (my own experience).

    Totally paraphrasing what I read from Eero's statement there, I'm actually not sure what I believe would happen with this approach. Eero, did I understand you about right?

    ...BUT, if you break down Ryan's "classes" into something like, "What we're going to demonstrate next", I think everything will still work out.

    Start with Level One characters, full sheets. Try to use the sheets that have the Final Values, and not the Workbooking (those you printed out work fine).

    If anything, color-code the character sheet so you can do things like say, "OK, now let's look at the Light Blue Area of the sheet. Those are the skills. Trollkiller, see if you can find the hidden door; roll perception. Here's how that works..."

    \n Also, the beginning to all recent Final Fantasy games do this as well (other console RPGs do it, too, but FFX and FF12 are great examples). They have you go into combat, they tell you EXACTLY what to use. Then, they have you use another ability ("Overdrive", etc). But they have you do it step by step. This might be good for your intro game: Tell each player exactly what to use on their first round of combat, then the second... then something happens in the game (a mini-boss appears on the map) and tell them to use their daily or encounter power, etc.

    Then extrapolate that backwards to perception cheks, skill checks and so on.

    \n 1st level D&D characters are not exactly complicated. Especially if you build them not to be.

    I'd be looking at slow introduction, rather than simplification. Introduce matters with the most bog standard monsters you can find. Pitch the difficulty low and let them try their at-wills out. Then introduce the Encounter powers. Then when someone gets hurt, explain the healing powers to the leader. A few encounters in, throw in a slighter stiffer encounter and explain the dailies.

    Start next the next session on a new day, and let them explore what they can do.
