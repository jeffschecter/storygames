\n We've had a few discussions of "railroading" recently, and it's clearly that everyone has a slightly different idea of what it is. To some, it's an unethical and dysfunctional practice; to others it is an artistic/creative tool like any other. For the purposes of this thread, you can use your own definition, so long as it suits my criteria (below).

So, in this thread, let's collect Actual Play experiences of railroading, and how they were positive for you and/or your group.

Tell us about a time when 1) you were a player in a game, 2) the GM (or whoever) railroaded the game in some way you weren't aware of OR did not consent to, 3) you found out about it later, and 4) you see it as a positive experience which enriched the game. (And explain why!)

Criteria #2 is the important part. Some people say that being deceived is what makes railroading bad; others say it's not the deception, but the fact that it is done without consent. However, I think everyone can agree that if both happen, it's railroading. For the purposes of this thread, either will do, however. (But a situation where you were aware it was happening AND were happy it was going on doesn't qualify for these purposes: that doesn't sound even potentially problematic, and would be considered Participationist play or something similarly benign, or at least easily treatable, by 9 out of 10 RPG theorists.*)

So, please make sure your AP fulfills those criteria, above. (And let's leave the GM's perspective out of this for now; that's going to be quite different.)


*: Names of theorists withheld for confidentiality purposes.

    \n I played one Vampire session in 2001 with a GM named Sean. He loaned me his coat, and then disappeared forever. I still have the coat.

    Sean was an excellent actor. He had a setpiece planned where he introduced a creepy villain, and that villain had us all as a captive audience to give us a creepy speech.

    I don't recall the player characters doing much of anything besides reflecting on the hooks we were handed and moving between locations. There was certainly no fighting. I can't remember how I felt about that at the time; I vaguely remember just enjoying acting out characters in a room full of new people. No one ever told me "there won't be any meaningful decisions for you", but I don't recall feeling disappointed. I'm a solid actor myself, so merely expressing my vision of my guy felt meaningful.

    Anyway, we went where the GM funneled us, and then he got to deliver his creepy speech, and the speech was great. He was clearly inspired by Bob from Twin Peaks. I was the only person in the room who got the reference, and Sean was grateful that I gave him the hat tip rather than "you're just stealing this, I've seen this before".

    Anyway, most of what I just wrote is guesswork (this was 10+ years ago), but the one thing I remember for sure is Sean as Bob lurching and snarling around the room.

    I feel pretty sure that there's no way Sean would have done that without first making sure he had control of the scene and wouldn't be interrupted by some "I attack the bad guy!" shit.

    \n I've got a ton of things I'd like to collaborate with, but they fail the first criteria, so I'll refrain from dropping them. However, I think I can contribute to the thread explaining why I have so few experiences that meet the first criteria. You see, I have studies in advertising and also studied to be an art critic at colleage. I learned to carefully read people since I was a kid, and as a comic writer, I'm familiar with thousands of plots and twists from many kinds of forms of fiction. So, GMs have actually a hard time trying to play illusions on me. As most writers will know, it's kinda easy to foretell how it's gonna end an story or at least what's gonna be the biggest issue coming, based on carefully looking at the small details introduced and their presentation. If the details aren't subtle enough and no red herrings obfuscate them, the ending can be guessed at first glance. If these details aren't presented at all, the story feels bad written since "unexpected" twists will be more like "unreasonable" or "unexplainable" twists.

    So, while I despise being manipulated in real life, I can take it in the fiction as a natural part of the game. "Hey, it's getting late! ok, let's move foward, your characters arrive at the dungeon at sunset, who's going first?" Is a barely memorable piece of railroading, but one that responds to a problem as players getting distracted with things outside the game. "No, Buttercup isn't interested in you and is in fact ready to tell his father about your advances, so better get out of town for now" Is valid if you know the player enough to be sure this is him trying to make the game go gonzo, while the rest of the players just wanted a serious adventure. At least is valid railroading for certain gaming style and certain groups of players, and one kind of railroading I've almost always been grateful of.

    So basically, most of the best railroading I've seen as a player hasn't been too good as an aesthetical experience to make them memorable, but they have been good enough that I don't remember much of the sub-optimal playing conditions that triggered them.

    In a separate note, asking one of my players if he remembered one time that I railroaded him, he couldn't pinpoint some specific moment. Either I was a terrific illusionist then or haven't otherwise made memorable railroading scenes, though I remember making illusioninsm left and right on every campaign.

    \n That's fascinating, WarriorMonk and AsIf.

    If I were to answer the same questions, I would say the same thing: "I'm pretty sure I always know when I'm being railroaded, but I used to do it for years and the players couldn't spot it."

    It seems unlikely to me that both of those things could be true simultaneously.

    WarriorMonk, in your examples, was there any lack of consent from the players?

    The reason I'm asking is to tease out the difference between:

    1. "Hey, it's getting late! ok, let's move foward, your characters arrive at the dungeon at sunset, who's going first?"

    - Everyone smiles and nods. "Great, I draw my sword and check for traps!"

    This is, from a certain point of view, not railroading, but not that different from, "Hey, it's getting late. How do you all feel if we skip ahead to when you get to the dungeon?" "Sure, sounds good!" (Which I'm pretty sure no one would ever call railroading.)

    and

    2. "Hey, it's getting late! ok, let's move foward, your characters arrive at the dungeon at sunset, who's going first?"

    - Players complain or try to "run off the rails", perhaps changing their travel plans or trying to arrive at the dungeon later or earlier, or setting out in the opposite direction.
    - The GM subtly changes things so that the characters end up at the dungeon at sunset anyway, or invents a huge storm which forces them to take shelter under the cliff... which is where the dungeon is, etc.

    This is the one that I consider railroading, personally, and I'd like to hear if anyone has positive experiences along these lines.

    I suppose a simple, "I resisted at first, but then I realized it was actually a good technique, and we used it with the group from then on" is a potential benign use of this technique (to overcome existing biases or habits, force the group into a different type of play which it turns they enjoy after all). But I don't have any good personal experiences with this.

    \n Condition #1 says "When you were a player" so that rules out being a GM, which rules out 99% of my experience. The other 1% has seen some very artless things. The question in my mind is not "railroading versus not railroading" but rather "artful, purposive use of the technique versus clueless, aggressive, or ego-driven use of it." Some GMs are artists, and I'm looking for them.

    From observation of random samples I'd say the majority of GMs are pretty mediocre, artistically speaking. So if given a choice between a railroad and a slow-paced, bumbling mess of random details interspersed with "ummm" and page-flipping, I'll take the railroad. At least that GM has a strong sense of what they're presenting. Such clarity increases my faith that I'm in the hands of an artist, not completely wasting my time playing what might as well have been a random solo game.

    \n Ok Paul, I think I finally came up with an example, although I had to go outside of TTRPG to do it (since I'm rarely a player in TTRPGs). This was in a LARP called "Cosmic Conspiracy" which I participated in around 1991. The game was set in a rather typical Illuminati!-type world. I believe one hour equaled one year of gametime, though it may have been one month of gametime. All players had a profession but secretly belonged to one or another secret society (of which there were many, all running at cross-purposes to each other of course). Early in the game the "President" (an NPC actor) came on the short-circuit TV to tell us that the US economy was in peril, terrorists blah blah blah. We were told that we should spread our money around and spend it freely, or else the economy might collapse. Several hours went by. Fortunes were pursued, secrets were discovered, etc.

    After the lunch break, the second act began. We all resumed character and play continued, only to be interrupted by another TV message from the "President". He was saddened to tell us that our efforts to save the economy had failed, that the US economy was being ratcheted down, and that from here on out, all currency would have exactly 50% of its previous value. Our savings were halved. Thank you and have a nice day. Groans of disappointment went up from all over the building. Everything we had worked so hard for now seemed paltry and stupid. But after a short time we acquiesced to the new conditions, and play resumed at a slightly slower pace. By the end of the game we had adjusted to the newly-set currency values.

    When the game was over I talked to the GMs. They told me that the halving of the dollar's value was something that was set in stone, it would have happened no matter what we had done in the first act. So the "don't let the terrorists win" thing was really just a ruse to get us spinning our money around like crazy. There was nothing we could have done to prevent this.

    Although as a player I was among the groaners when the announcement came down, as a designer I was intrigued by the social experiment they had performed. They had demonstrated that within our simulated economy, the face value of currency is unimportant as long as everyone's money is equally effected, and that given a little bit of time, people can adjust to radical devaluations of currency without the whole of society losing its momentum completely. This may seem obvious from a distance but it feels very different when you're inside it, and invested in it.

    Obviously this was a far cry from being a truly empirical economic experiment, but nevertheless I found it fascinating - much more so than my previous (illusory) notion that the value of the dollar would somehow be pegged to the total amount of all transactions in a game year. The fiction itself was ephemeral (I don't remember my character's name or profession, nor a single thing he bought or sold), but the lesson I drew from the metagame - the moral of the economic experiment - was permanent.

    Today I would agree with that. But at the time we didn't even know the word "larp". Cosmic Conspiracy was called a game. I had no idea what to expect because at the time, it had the air of a one-of-a-kind experience.\n
    As a player, did you feel that a line had been crossed? To what an extent, for example, did you believe that your spending had an impact on this second-act event?
    We didn't know the second act event would happen, obviously, but we were given to believe that our spending was causally linked to the state of an economic metagame (i.e. a simulation of the GNP or Consumer Confidence Levels of a real national economy), which it turns out, it wasn't.

    Did I feel a line had been crossed... Tough question. My first feeling was a simple, selfish "Aw, FUCK!" - but that was mostly in-character.     I do remember feeling that it could have been handled better, but by "better" I really meant more illusionism (perhaps periodic announcements showing the dollar in decline prior to the second act, etc). It happened so fast that it felt heavy-handed and caused the game to stall for quite a few minutes. I remember thinking afterwards that if I was the GM I would have foreshadowed it more. But underlying all of that, there was still the understanding that the GMs were the GMs, and we were in their world, not our own. No right to complain on an epistemological level. Only in character.

    \n Here's an interesting AP example of something that is somewhere in "railroading" territory:

    Coming back to an Apocalypse World game after a hiatus, we found that the MC had written each of us a "love letter". They didn't take away agency from our characters, but they did suggest certain things had happened. Most of these things (e.g. my character looking for a certain other person) had been already established in prior play, but, still, the MC was taking some liberties with the direction in which we were headed.

    And, of course, the outcomes of the roll (and the stats chosen) were carefully designed to limit outcomes within a particular sphere. (Perhaps a love letter based on a stat someone had at -1 and a sole positive outcome on a 10+ hit suggests that bad things are going to happen to that character.)

    This was an effective and fun way to "fast forward" a bit and resume the game at full tilt.

    Arguably, a "love letter" falls somewhere between railroading (a good love letter, I think, doesn't presuppose certain actions of a character, removing player choice) and the regular domain of an MC - making moves in the fiction. The grey area, I would argue, has to do with the scope of the love letter (e.g. "Two weeks have passed, and you're still looking for her...").

    Would this be a valid MC move in normal play? Perhaps. But the MC is certainly placing a firmer hand on the tiller when she writes these letters than in typical AW play.

    \n I played in a Dark Heresy scenario (hacked to a Renaissance Europe setting and a different ruleset) that involved almost literal railroading. That is, our PCs were packed into a carriage driven by our patron's lackey. This carriage carried us from scene to scene until we got to the main adventure location. Our patron also, annoyingly, accompanied us the whole way, giving us orders and preventing us from sticking our noses where they didn't belong. By the time we got to the final, climactic scene, I got the sense that no matter what choices we made as investigators, the story would have ended with the same set-piece encounter.

    BTW, I'm not sure I buy either lack of awareness or lack of consent as necessary criteria for railroading. In general, I think it's more that the players are not aware of any alternative to railroading & illusionism, and consent is given tacitly, because most players just want to play a game and, again, aren't aware of any alternative or don't really care to pursue one.     \n I think ruling out consent or knowledge precludes the majority of railroading memories for two reasons:
    1) if you see the rails and decide it's worth following or at least not fighting, this is consent
    2) if you didn't see the rails, the GM won't volunteer this information later.

    I just can't really think of a positive railroad situation that I wasn't aware of simply because the GM wouldn't reveal it. But I digress. I have an example of a kind of railroading that players wouldn't consent to but would be thankful for when the tables turned.

    I played a lot of 3.5. Came up on crunch roll-playing and build optimization. We always expected a cool story, but badly wanted to show off what our characters could do.

    There was always at least one player who would get out of control, use some odd ability to mess up the whole party and narrative path, and then laugh and chuckle as he watched things crumble. The GM would then threaten "blue lightning" or some other railroading effect, and we'd thankfully get back to the game.

    Everyone consented to the railroading except the target player. However, with the tables turned, that player would be thankful for the railroading when someone else got out of control.

    But that was just a bunch of immature kids trying to make sense of a game above our pay grade.

    \n Whenever you're told that your character is doing something you don't agree with, or can't do a thing that should usually be able to do with no problems and there's no explanation nor a way to take it back -but you're warned that there's an explanation behind it that requires you to renounce to your preconceptions about the world and the rules that limit reality- it's natural to feel uneasy about it. Certain horror games benefit from this to create a particular atmosphere.

    Think of this scene: A player stablishes his character as a normal mental healthy person. But then small details of his world begin to warp as a warning that reality isn't what it used to be. One day, the character wakes up and without reason, buys a weapon. The GM imposes this scene and then allows the player to act his reaction. Let's say he disposes of the weapon, returns it to the vendor or something. Then the GM tells him that on the next day his character has an argument with his boss, goes back to his office, opens a drawer and finds there the weapon again. Then he gives the player back the control of his character so he's free to dispose of the weapon again, try to hide it, etc. The next day on the morning, the character hears noises on the basement. When/if the player goes to investigate, he finds the weapon on the floor and his boss tied inside a box. And the guy completely freaks out from the PC as soon as he comes closer.

    So, in this case, the railroad has been used to create this atmosphere, as if the PC is losing his mind and doing things behind his own back, or he's maybe the victim of a complex and insane practical joke or... well, possibilities are endless and all you need is to ask the player what he thinks is happening to find even worse and more interesting options to keep the story rolling into suspense. A GM used a lot of tricks like this for a magnificent one-shot suspense/terror game. After the first three empty rooms we found, we started to fire our weapons like crazy at the first noise we heard, which turned to be a rat. We almost killed ourselves on the process, but it ended up being an awesome scene, as we were more than grateful that it was only a rat after all.
