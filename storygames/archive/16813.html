\n This is a really long AP, but I wanted to share and I am hoping that the wonderful people I played with will be up for discussing it with me here. And of course, comments and questions are always welcome.

Fabricated Realities 2012, Saturday the 16th of June, 2:00 \u2013 5:30

This session was handily facilitated by Caroline. She graciously let me be Shakespeare. I had only been Shakespeare once before, in my first game. It\u2019s great to let someone who has played before be Shakespeare so they can work on filling out their questions while the facilitator explains the rules to everyone else.

We decided that this would be played in modern times. We allowed no table talk and were careful in enforcing it. I explained to players before the game that sometimes I would seem curt. We took two 10 minute breaks.

    \nShakespeare\u2019s Questions
    Morgan, played previously \u2013 William Shakespeare, 56, playwright and father

    I had already typed up answers to Shakespeare\u2019s questions. I had been carrying this around for a couple months, since I noticed that Shakespeare gets to immediately start filling out questions without input from others.

    In prior games it seems that the majority want to blame Shakespeare for bad parenting and the strain on the relationship. Of course, it takes two to estrange, but even when the daughter is old enough to make sound decisions, the parent always gets judged more harshly. I think the friend\u2019s questions are skewed this way, which is great. However, I wanted to break that. I mean, I know I did the right thing.

    The answers to the questions on my sheet revealed that my 25 year old daughter, Genevieve, was usually truthful to a fault. However, her step-mother, Melinda, caught her stealing the presents from under the Christmas tree and loading them into her car. I had pressed charges and Genevieve was awaiting sentencing. I didn\u2019t reveal until the end that my secret hope was for her to be safe, and since it would always be my job to protect her, that it might be best for her to straighten out in rehab and jail.

    She was old enough to know better, but had made a choice which should be reviled. I was certain my drastic action would be justified and my friends could help me discover what was at the root of her terrible crime.

    Shakespeare\u2019s Friends Questions
    Caroline, facilitator, played previously \u2013 Angela Shakespeare, 43, ex-wife of Shakespeare
    We learned that Genevieve\u2019s mother writes letters to her daughter and that they are getting closer. She also felt that Shakespeare is right about Genevieve\u2019s habit of truthfulness and that she is only confused and lonely. In her opinion, her friends are incapable of being bad, if they were to cross her, they were not her friends to begin with and Shakespeare is shortsighted, hot tempered and brash.

    Jess, played previously \u2013 Oliver Bell, actor and prot\xe9g\xe9 of Shakespeare
    (Jess, I don't have your answers sheet. Help me fill this in.)

    Noah, a new player \u2013 Vincent Price, public attorney for Genevieve the first time
    Vincent always took Genevieve\u2019s side and believed her incapable of doing wrong. (Noah, I don't have your answer sheet either. Help me fill this in.)

    Dana, a new player \u2013 Embeth Marlowe, 40, friend of the family.
    Embeth and Shakespeare are friends since he thinks that her husband, Chris Marlowe, is his greatest rival. In fact, Chris Marlowe is a worthless drunk and she writes all his material using his name. Embeth has grown closer to Genevieve recently. She feels that the young woman is looking for guidance, but that Shakespeare is deceived about Genevieve\u2019s true nature. Embeth knows that a true friend is supportive, but thinks that Shakespeare is pathetic and grasping for fame at the cost of family. He\u2019s neglected Genevieve, while she lavishes attention on her own 25 year old daughter.

    Marc, played previously \u2013 Constance Trudeau, 25, daughter of family friends
    Genevieve\u2019s best friend from boarding school had an awkward teenage fromance, but were friends afterward. They have been out of touch recently, but went to visit her. Constance feels that it is important to console your friends. She knows Shakespeare as a hard-headed traditionalist, who can\u2019t see anything but his own way. She doesn\u2019t have children, but thinks it must be hell to have a daughter.

    Characters who were not also actors
    Genevieve Shakespeare, my daughter
    Phill, Angela\u2019s new partner
    Chris Marlowe, Embeth\u2019s sot of a husband
    Melinda Shakespeare, my current wife.

    \n Boy, do I have a lot of feels about this game!

    First, a few answers from my answer sheet (some of which were basically rendered moot):
    He thinks Shakespeare is wrong about Genevieve, who can be conniving and deceptive, that friends sometimes have to dish out some tough love, and that Shakespeare is too wrapped up in his own life. He is already estranged from his own family.

    For me, this game was an object lesson in being specific. I said that Oliver had once been very close to Genevieve, but had mysteriously not been speaking to her for "a while". (ETA: Because they had a secret relationship and a bad breakup, which I kept secret from the other players.) In my mind, that while was months, not 8 years, especially since I had already said (out loud, even!) that Oliver had only known Shakespeare for 6-7 years.

    However, in the very first scene after we played the estrangement, Shakespeare framed me into a situation where I'm sneaking into her boarding school 8 years ago, which was also the time when Gen and I fell out. So right away I am suddenly creepy statutory-rape guy. I sorta sat back and didn't grab a character in the scene the first time or two that we played it, and by that time it was established that Oliver's rapeyness was more than the 'statutory' variety. About the only thing I could do at this point was to play Gen in the scene in such a way that she enthusiastically embraced whatever relationship we had. It also turned out that this was the only scene with Oliver in it, so that was the only information we got about his character.

    This re-characterisation threw me off quite a bit for most of the game. I managed to talk myself out of being mad about it (hey, I wasn't nearly clear enough in the first place, and besides which, just because Shakespeare says that's how we fell out, doesn't mean that actually was how we fell out), but rethinking my character on the fly, while playing other people's characters, unmoored me from any strong feelings I had about Shakespeare, Genevieve, and their estrangement, so I found it really hard to show Oliver as having any point of view about the characters he was playing.

    Conclusion? Have a shorter, clearer character concept next time, and base it around the character's opinions of Shakespeare & daughter. Secret motivations are all well and good, but you never get to bring them out in play. And for FSM's sake, be specific when telling other people about the character!

    \n The game places strong, explicit limits on communication between Shakespeare and his friends concerning the play, and implies some limits on communication between the friends as well.

    Like most game texts, it doesn't say anything about player-level communication -- but it does make a conscious effort to conflate the feelings of the players and the feelings of the friends. E.g. it addresses them both as 'you'.

    So in theory there are a lot of different ways of treating the un-explicitly-discussed possibilities of communication (Shakespeare and the friends talk about baseball; the friends talk to each other about their lives outside the play; the players kibbitz about whatever; etc.)

    I think that because the non-communication about the play is important, and by itself tends to create a sense of isolation between S. and his friends, that lots of players find it natural to just extend that absence of communication to most other parts of the game. But I do agree that this should always be limited to parts of the game -- i.e. the limits, insofar as they are presented as 'how to play the game', should govern only friend/Shakespeare-level interaction.

    If that spills over to how the players interact in an organic fashion, then cool -- that's something that I think has happened so often in the past that for some it is part of the game's play-culture. But I wouldn't recommend presenting it as 'this game doesn't allow table talk' -- better to be clear about what you can't talk about, and then (if you want) suggest that the group try to avoid lots of player-level kibbitzing and table-talk. That last step is not really part of the game, it's just part of how a particular group of people might want to play the game.

    --

    I will hopefully be adding something to the final version of the game that explicitly talks about in-character talk between Shakespeare's Friends (I want there to be more of it, generally), but I don't really feel the need to address how much general player table-talk there should or should not be -- I feel like that's really up to each individual group, their preferences, the specifics of the play they end up telling, etc.

    \n I think playing one of Shakespeare's friends as a character, especially when doing the initial line readings, and adding a bit of in-character dialogue between the friends really elevates the game -- provided it doesn't get in the way of actually playing the scenes.

    There definitely needs to more direct advice about how/what gets shared during the initial setup. The revised question sheets I brought to FabReal had slightly-clearer text, but so far all of the 'do I reveal or not?' stuff is filtered through what Shakespeare would know. I don't want players to tell Shakespeare stuff about his friends that he would not know -- which makes it difficult for them to share things with each other that they would know, but Shakespeare would not, such as the whole three-way-on-ecstasy type deal. Including advice about note-passing, etc. among friends is probably a good idea.

    But of course in general I am still struggling with the right balance between the various elements of the game -- in this case, between Shakespeare-and-his-daughter and Shakespeare's-friends-as-interesting-people-in-of-themselves. Most of us are very used to playing games where the character we are playing is the most important thing we have to bring to the game -- but this isn't like that. That urge is super-helpful to what the game is about, but as Joe alluded to it is not helpful because I want to fulfill it, but because frustrating it is itself part of what the game is about.
