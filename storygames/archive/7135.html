\n Some games I won't buy paper, but will buy PDFs. Partly because I'm a business traveler and can't always afford to pack even the smaller books to read, partly because I'm trying to reduce consumption of paper goods.
\n
\nI wanted to make a post because I know there are designers out there who read this -- and kind of wanted to hear if I was crazy on this one. I know a lot of people are interested in the artifact of their printed books but I'm at a point as a consumer where I'm making buy/no-buy decisions on a particular game based on PDF availability. And I thought that might be useful information to share, especially if there are other people with my sentiment.
\n
\nI know piracy is a concern; has anyone in the community explored encoding data of the authorized recipient of a PDF? There are some fairly incredible things you can do, such as encoding names and purchase information in the spacing between characters.

    \n It's interesting (for me, at least) how my buying habits changed following the changes in my gaming habits. Going from the "collector's" model (buying a lot of gaming books knowing that I could play less than 10% of them. only to read) to a Indie "playing" model (I can play everything I buy now) made me go, without even thinking about it, from a "I buy only printed books, pdfs are not as pretty" to a "I would like to buy only pdf, they are much more useful to play than a printed book"

    With a pdf I can print a copy for every player, I can write on my copy every rule change or errata, comments from forums, etc, without worrying about "ruining" it, I can print easily any play-aid without having to go to a copy store, and, last but not least, I spend less money and the author gets more money (win-win). And it's easier to include the changes and errata from following edition (the best would be a policy of free upgrades).

    Seeing that piracy is a issue only when you get to much higher sales (if it doesn't hurt Spirit of the Century...), and for almost unknown games like most of indie games it can even be a boost to sales, and people who get $2 or less from a retail sale at $20 can get $10 from a pdf sale without having to pay the printing money in advance, It's difficult to understand why anyone would not want to sell more pdfs than printed books...

    This is why we are internet BFFs that never talk. Awesome question.

    I am willing to pay more for PDFs. However (and this is an "if I had all the time in the world" thing), my idea if I ever wrote a game was to try and differentiate the paper and PDF products (if the paper product existed) by including content with the PDF that isn't in the paper book.     When I talk about encoding data into the PDFs, I'm not saying you should DRM it and lock it to a particular person. What I am saying is that when you sell me a PDF, it should have encoded in it, "This PDF was sold to Blake Deakin on the 26th of July, 2008." If you did so in a manner that's difficult to defeat (such as in the spacing of characters which I suggested... incidentally, this is impossible to discern to a human eye, a computer has to do it) you could recover an illegally-distributed PDF on the web and figure out who the jerk is. With a tightly-knit community like the indie RPG one, I think it would be a good deterrent.

    \n One thing I've been thinking about recently is the number of game books that get sold and then never get used. While some publishers may be happy to make a sale one way or the other, it is just creating more "filler" so to speak, on someone's bookshelf. This is not to say that play is the only use game books have, but if you're buying games to mine them for ideas, it definitely seems like PDF should work just as well or better. That's part of the reason I decided to, as with TSOY and SOTC, to make all my games available for free electronically and then release print editions on the side. Ultimately, I suspect this will result in less waste even if it also results in less "I guess I should probably check this game out" sales. People who buy print copies of the game are then likely to be people who actually plan to use them in play enough that an electronic version just wouldn't cut it, or they are invested enough in the game that they want a physical artifact. This may not work for all publishers, but it makes me much happier.

When I buy a printed book, I pay the printer, the distributor, the retailer, and the publisher. When I buy a pdf I don't pay the first three but pay double or more the publisher. In practice, we contract the sale directly without middlemen and share the savings. If a game publisher would say to me "I don't want only to get all the saving you offer me, but I want even more from you, you will have to pay something extra to be able to save me a lot of expenses", my first answer would be "sorry, if this is a problem for you forget about the whole thing, I will buy it retail, I take back my $15 and I will give you the usual $2.45 instead".     2) About marking the pdf with my name.

    If I buy a pdf from you, you can be assured that I will share with some people. Not as in "file-sharing" in some network, but more as "Hi! I will GM this game next Saturday, take this pdf and study it, you will need to know the rules". And for the greater part of indie game publisher, exposure and the simple chance to "get the game in the hand of more players" is rarer, more difficult to get, worth more for him, and a publisher in this situation that remove this asset because he fear that the unwashed masses who never even hear of his game (and never will) will decide to search for a pirated copy of his game instead of searching for a copy of D&D4, is (in my humble opinion, remember) simply shooting himself in the balls. Mmmm.... what a difficult choice...

    \n Just a note here -- the DMCA specifically makes it legal to remove "personally identifying information" from files, even if that information is used as part of a protection scheme. Hence, if you put someone's name into a PDF, they can legally remove it in the US. Even if they have to break copy protection in order to do so.

    From a legal standpoint, you're much safer putting in a serial number or other code that you can use to identify where a copy came from, but which cannot on its own be used to identify the buyer.

    Alternatively, you could do both -- it would be legal for the buyer to remove their name, but not the serial number.
