\n So I have a question for the more d20/OGL-friendly crowd here.

Say I was hacking d20 and really hacking it into little tiny pieces and then putting back together, not just polishing it up a bit. For example, say I had turned it into a point-build system (ala True20), but that every point used to create your character was tied to an experience that character had in the imaginary past before play started or (if gained through advancement) an experience that occurred in game. So you'd have traits like "+1 Strength, inherited from my mother, who toiled in the fields her entire life." And that might combines with a couple other traits to give you +3 Strength total, that kind of thing. Skills and powers the same way. And that's just the tip of this very hacky iceberg.

Where would I go to find other game designers to talk to about the implications of this? I need to find people who are way more familiar with d20/OGL stuff than I am, but are into batshit crazy hack ideas in the manner of a lot of indie roleplaying enthusiasts. Is there such a promised land of both milk AND honey?

    \n I like the idea, and I frequent this place, I'd love to give feedback. To prove it, here's an unformed thought you might consider:

    It sounds like what you've got is a specifically character creation hack, which could be segregated from other changes to the system and tacked onto any other d20 implementation so long as the end character sheet had at least the things the system requires it to have (attribute bonuses, skill bonuses, hit points, etc.) This I think might be fruitful, at least, I would be interested in it even for games where I was going to be running semi-vanilla D20.

    Other places include the smartypantses at ENWorld, and I've gotten good feedback from the d20 forum on rpg.net as well, if you are willing to shepherd your thread back onto the front page every so often with new material.

    \n Do it here, man. I pretend to be on hiatus from d20 but I can't resist a good thread.

    However...

    Everybody and their little dog too is bad at reading long blocks of text. Not just bad - TERRIBLE. So if you're going to do this and get feedback on the process, you've got to break your discussions down into tiny chunks, and let each of those chunks have its airtime before you put the next one up. That's torture, but that was the difference between no feedback for the "epic 6th level house rules" and an explosion of feedback for E6.
