\n@Ben_Robbins and I were talking in another thread, here, about how it was a mistake to focus on and compliment so called "good players," because it can give other players the wrong impression that being "good" (i.e. impressing others) is what the game is about and can also make them feel that they don't belong. This was a mistake I was unfortunately making in my club and Ben was helping me with it. Ben said that he gives a spiel to players before they start gameing. I was very impressed by the spiel; here it is:

"These games can be great, but they only work because of what each of us brings to the table. Your contribution is vital to making the game work...
...But likewise, if there are four people in the game, each of us are only a quarter of the group, so it's also vital that we listen to what everyone else brings to the game and work with their contributions. We're each a minority, so we may not get everything we want, but if we compromise and work together, we can make something together we all enjoy, something that may surprise us more than something we would have made by ourselves. If we don't do that, if we refuse to work together, these games can suck. You will have a terrible time."

On the Story Games Seattle FAQ, here, (Ben's SG gaming club), it also says:

"But I'm not very creative!...
...You might surprise yourself once you sit down and play. But either way it's not about impressing each other with amazing ideas, it's about playing well together and building on what what other people contribute."

So my question is: What is "playing well" in a collaborative Story Game?

I think Ben gave some really great answers above in regards to what it means to "play well." Is there anything you would like to add? Thanks

    \n I really like the idea of encouraging different "stances" in players so they can find a creative space they're more comfortable in. Ben does this nicely in the quote above, reframing "I'm trying to be creative to impress people" (a possible attitude a player might bring to the game) as "other people are creating content; my job is to support what they're trying to do".

    I've had a number of great conversations about this with a friend who is *very* conscious about this in his gaming. He likes to take motifs, themes, and aspects of other peoples' characters and use those to create material, whether he is GMing or not.

    A simple example is:

    A player creates a character who is a knight in shining armour - a real Hero, a person who is a paragon of Goodness.

    What do we with that character? Well, such a character is not complete without a counterpart - some clear evil to fight, something to stand in opposition to. So, in this case, my friend would likely move to create such an evil. Likely, this evil force would be framed in specific relation to the Hero's values. In the most obvious example, if the Hero were all about protecting the weak, the evil force might be a great enslaver of the helpless.

    (Of course, if the game were more nuanced this wouldn't have to be such an obvious counterpoint. The next-most-obvious counterpoint, for instance, would be a character who thinks of herself as good, but, in fact, goes so far in "protecting the weak" that she ends up doing evil. And so forth; with each level of inversion or remove, we lose clarity but gain a potential for greater nuance.)

    \n I think that looking at some of the concepts in improv theatre is really helpful for story gaming.

    For example, the idea of reincorporation, and building on what has come before (as Upstart discusses, just above me), is a powerful one.

    Another powerful one is the maxim to "Be Obvious". Many first-time story gamers, in my experience, try too hard to come up with something "brilliant" every time they open their mouth, and this can really drag a game down (not to mention causing them anxiety). Realizing that you're only responsible for a small part of the pie - leaving room in your contributions for others to answer and fill in - is a really key skill.

    I wrote about this a while back in this excellent earlier thread about the perfect "RPG curriculum".

    From my answer:

    "I believe that this may be the hardest thing about roleplaying games: our natural instinct is generally either to sit and listen (passively), or step in and take control of the entire process (like a railroading GM). For functional roleplaying/story gaming, a person needs to learn to occupy the middle space: to make contributions to play which always demand response from others - ideas which are incomplete, and require another to "fill in" the gaps. That's where the game is born."

    \n To answer the original question, without reading what has been said before.

    Be Bold
    It's usually a more engaging story if the character is risking things rather than playing safe. Show or tell your plans or secrets by, for example, hasty moves, talking to the other participants, or letting your character reveal things by taking stupid actions.

    Be Passionate
    Care about both your own character and the others, and their stories. Let that energy spread through the group.

    Be Giving
    Provide to the session with ideas and engagement. Share your ideas with the others.

    Be Embracing
    Give positive feedback to people, either verbally or by building on their ideas. That will make them want to contribute even more.

    Be Polite
    Even if you can do anything with anyone's character, ask first. Perhaps they got an idea of their own.

    \nI tend to think of PTA "fanmail" as a training tool for that: listening is important, and saying the "right" thing is only important inasmuch as other players are listening and like it. You can't fanmail yourself.
    \nI strongly advice against any PTA mechanic. a) It can make people do what others want to hear, instead of doing what they want to do themselves. b) Also, it favors certain player types. c) Further more, it makes people give petty mails to someone that hasn't gotten enough. d) Finally, the mechanical reward can in itself take away the fun of playing in the first place (overjustification effect).

    Designing through mechanical rewards are crappy design, in general, mostly because roleplaying game designers 1) doesn't actually know, psychologically, what a reward does, and 2) doesn't know better because mechanical rewards is a standard design solution in roleplaying games.

    ---

    I would otherwise support active listening in other ways, that forces the others to actually listen to what is happening because, if they don't, a part of the game is lost.\n
    • A structure where you can only say something by adding on what someone else has said first.
    • In Zombie Cinema, you can support other characters by giving them dice, making you want to listen to the story because you can be involved in how it goes.
    • In Shadows of the Trees, there are three game masters and one player, and each game master has their own agenda but needs to adopt their agenda to the other game masters. If non-active players can take a minor part of the game master responsibility in any game, then they can still contribute to the scene.
    • The detective in The Murder of Mr. Crow can call upon an assistant that helps the detective out. The detective chooses one of the non-active players to play the assistant, so they must listen on what's going on, otherwise they cannot contribute.
    • Archipelago has ritual phrases that others can use, and one of them is "describe more".
    • The non-active players in Black of Despair, White of Lust can throw in challenges for the active players, where the challenge is how the vampire's beast is trying to control the human side of the vampire.
    • Fiasco has the resolution die that can be given by non-active players.
    \nBy "non-active", I mean players that don't have characters that are in the scene.
