"silver and white"?\n
Four suburban teenagers encounter the mystery that will shape their lives. They explore, and each time they touch, we players exchange cards. For them a few days, for us a few hours. We make up a story together, our invented truths springing from the cards we hold; and they step into the unknown, pausing at every awkward touch, hopeful despite everything to come.
\nThe new draft has incorporated the feedback gathered from play at Go Play NorthWest, CozyCon, Geek Girl Con, the Self-Critical Hits podcast and other various instances of play and conversation. Huge thanks to those who gave that feedback.

    \n I've played it twice now, but the rules changed somewhat in between, making them a bit hard to compare. What we did in the first game would be impossible now, actually, according to the rules.

    The first time, we were poking around in the basement when I narrated the door upstairs creeping open and a bunch of people coming into the house. We locked the basement door but, in a few minutes, the folks who'd arrived in black SUVs were sawing through the lock with a police-style forced entry bolt-cutter. So we ended up all jumping into the machine and being transported to the white city, where everything moved in a strange blur and dark monkey-like shaped darted about the rooftops. That's when we discovered that going to the white city doesn't really work, because the player with that card is constantly narrating. But it was kinda surreal and Narnia-like.

    The last time we played, my character went out in the backyard and dug up some wires leading from the machine to somewhere else (another house?). We went next door and found that the neighbor's house contained a huge miniature model of the white city spread out everywhere, on counter-tops and tables. Then, when the other characters turned the machine on, it ended up causing one character's palms to start bleeding and ultimately killed him. So that game had much more of a dark psychological horror feel.

    It can go in a bunch of different directions, but, ultimately, the actual "plot" of the narrative matters a lot less than the interplay between the characters.

    \n We had an interesting discussion about this after the last game, so it may be that the rules will be clarified in this regard.

    I've done the touching thing both tactically -- to try to get a different card -- and non-tactically -- just when it made sense for characters to touch, and it worked fine both ways. For players who think tactically, it gives them an incentive to do something the game wants you to do, and I don't think it messes up the game too much. However, Jackson seemed to think (and maybe I'm misrepresenting him here, this is just my recollection) that you weren't really intended to actively describe stuff on your card -- like when I had the black SUVs show up -- but, rather, only answer the questioned posed to you about your subject by the other players. That makes it less of a "reward" to have a certain card -- because you can't actively narrative an interesting idea you have, only what people ask -- and I think it makes the tactical pursuit of other cards less likely. Still, I can see attempting to have characters touch when you feel like you're "done" or creatively exhausted with the cards you have and want something fresh, but that's a different thing and (I think) something that's more in keeping with Jackson's original intentions.

    I haven't played the game yet in the "only answer questions" mode, but I'm really excited about what that'll do to the game and I'm interested to try it out.

    \n Hmm, yes. I think the games I played had a SORT of "only answer questions" practice in effect. Basically, when you were messing with the machine, or the city, or whatever, people would be aware that player X was the only one who could make authoritative statements, so they'd ask her questions constantly. It essentially boils down to GM-like authority constantly vetted through the other players, which tapers off as you run out of questions to answer. Once the Machine-narrator establishes sufficient operating procedure, or the City-narrator carves out a sufficient space to play around in, you don';t need to rely on them except in special cases--trying something new, exploring a different area.

    We weren't really strict on that, though...I'm prtetty sure the owner of a narration card would supply information without an asked question from time to time.

    The real issue I've found in play is with the Narrator card, the holder of which answers questions about the characters' past and relationships. Both times I played, the second time especially, it was kind of hard to get this role revved up, because players simply didn't ask many relationship questions. The few that DO get asked mostly come at the beginning, to get a relationship sketch roughed out, then the role gets sorta dropped. I've held the Narrator card for large chunks of a game somewhat desperately wishing for the other players to ask me questions or otherwise give me a pretext for detailing relationships or past history. The role COULD be a robust means of exploring the personal side of the friendships throughout the game,, via flashbacks and piecemeal revelation...but it's hard to spotlight that in play.

    Posted By: ivanwhy did you decide to tie in the narrative-authority swap mechanics with the touching?
    \nI wanted the game to hinge around touching, for the players as well as for the characters. Contact is very important to me, and i believe it's important to most humans... thinking back to my teenaged years, when touch was a big social step and was never guaranteed, well, i wanted to put that in this game. Since the narrative-authority mechanics are the gamiest part of the game, game-gamers (part of my audience; in the venn diagram, most of the people who play silver and white and also post on these boards would count as game-gamers: they come to the table to push buttons and unscrew things and watch the mechanics work) would enjoy engaging them. Thus.\n
    Posted By: ivanWhat about the accepting/refusing the touch?
    \nThere are lots of games about doing a thing. I'm interested in games about responding to a thing (when the thing being done is done by another player). This pushes the players to examine their characters a bit, and it also makes statements about your character if you make a choice based on strictly mechanical reasons.

    For example, if i want to make sure i retain the Machine card, then i'll have to make sure i get to choose which type of cards we exchange, which means i have to knuckle under to whatever your character is doing, or else behave warmly to them.

    I don't think this is the best way to do either of these things, but it is a way. I am excited for the games that will do this better, in the future.

      \n So I did indeed play Silver and White at the fine Red Castle Games establishment Thursday Last. It was great fun. The new rules are tightened up and delightfully refined from those I played last.

      I played with Abi, Bryan and Michael (our very own Stupidgremlin!). I had not read the new rules prior to play, so it was a fascinating journey of discovery.

      I'll say this straight-up: the other players, through NO influence from me I SWEAR, immediately established that the machine transports you to the city. One player injected some doubt about that, like maybe it just atomizes you, but "the machine transports you" was the default assumption throughout the game, and it turned out to be true in the end. However, the game did NOT end up being about exploring the city. Only one character went there, and it was at the end, and we only got the barest idea of what happened to her there. So it felt like the hushed, terrifying mystery of it all was nicely preserved.

      I played Marlene, shy and straight-laced girl who cares about belonging and knowledge. She loves her little bro Pritchard but tries not to show it, and has a super-secret crush on Andi.

      Abi played Andi, a brash bad girl who cares about knowledge and herself. She's total best friends with jared, and oblivious to Marlene's crush. Indeed, she's kind of oblivious to Marlene in general.

      Bryan played Jared, a cocky thrill-seeker who cares about recognition and wealth. He's partners in crime with Andi and acts as a rival to Pritchard, pushing the sensitive younger boy to measure up to his standards of devil-may-care badassery.

      Michael played Pritchard, a timid and high-strung boy who's stuck hanging out with his sister's cruel friends, and cares about belonging and knowledge. He 's sensitive about death, can't stand Jared's constant teasing, and seems to want support from his sister that he seldom sees.

      In play: Andi and Jared got drunk on the teacher's booze, and were excited about trying to figure out the machine, just for the thrill of it. Marlene wanted to share their madcap adventure but kept saying stupid, cautious things that made them despise her. Pritchard was freaked out more than anyone about the presence of death, and wanted to put the teacher properly to rest. Marlene got fascinated by the unearthly photographs, and in finding out more about the teacher's sad, lonely life. She managed to pull Andi into this fascination, and they discovered together, but never quite connected the way Marlene hoped. They found the teacher's last will and testament in the unopened letter: to be placed in the machine, along with all the photographs, and sent away.

      Marlene wanted to fulfill his wishes, but couldn't bear to lose the photographs. She convinced Pritchard, in the name of honoring the dead, to help get the teacher into the machine. Andi thought that maybe the teacher came FROM the other world of the photos, and wanted to go there. While she and Marlene were alone in the basement, she convinced Marlene to operate the machine and send her. Pritchard saw the whole thing from the top of the stairs, and Marlene told him it was for Andi's good, and made him swear not to tell Jared. It was well past dawn, and they fled the house, the photos secretly tucked in Marlene's satchel.



      Their ultimate fates: Marlene became a celebrated surrealist photographer, living a bohemian lifestyle in Paris (envied by untold numbers, but understood by none). Pritchard became chef and a front-runner in a TV cooking competition, but committed suicide before the show concluded (die during my attempt at something great, and be celebrated posthumously). I honestly don't remember what happened to Jared, which is appropriate considering his Fate card (comfortable and forgotten). Andi appeared years later, in another small town, and began a a quiet life as an eccentric and reclusive photography teacher, building a strange machine in her basement (I will never get it back, though it will haunt me well).

      *****

      I love love love the new categories of cards: "thinking cards" and "feeling cards." Very simple, direct, instinctive even. And having a moment-to-moment prompt for what your character is feeling has much greater impact on play than only having a "fate" that you're somehow "playing toward." Bravo.

      I also really dig the way character relationships are assigned. Nice and simple, and a neat dealie where your choice impacts which Fate you're playing with. And the single sentence about deciding which items on your sheet your character keeps secret really excited us. We had great fun deciding which parts of ourselves we were hiding from our friends ("I love my brother? Yeah, I'm TOTALLY keeping THAT secret.")

      The change in the Narrator role from "describe relationships" to "give first-person, present tense voice-over" is brilliant. It still covers the fleshing out of relationships, but it's expanded to be broader than that, and particularly usefully, it gives you a concrete, easily understandable narrative device to do it with! It's a device that's fun to try out for even minor things, leading you to "practice" with it a lot more, whereas I found the old Narrator didn't get used very much beyond the first ten minutes of play.

      The rules for time passing are BRILLIANT. It's easy in this type of game (certainly in every time I've played S&W) to just "play straight through" a linear sequence of unbroken time, no scene breaks or editing. The rule that when someone decides to cross off a time period, the Narrator places the friends in the house (and in different locations!) is a nice way to gently force scene changes and (even more importantly) solo activity and private, possibly intimate conversations. We did, however, show a tendency to under-use the power at our disposal; often we'd end up only "cutting" a few minutes ahead, beginning the scene with "so here we are doing the thing we had just set out to do before the time shift." Maybe the mechanic could use some refinement or clarification that pushes more toward larger leaps and moving the story forward?

      Some things we struggled with in play:

      I found it awkward getting into the read-aloud portion of the book. I knew it was coming, but the first few pages didn't mention it. I read the flavor text of page 2 and page 4, then found the read-aloud instructions on page 5. Then I had to ignore the instruction on page 8 to go back and read page 4. I'd appreciate some clarification, and putting the read-aloud instructions as up-front as possible.

      Once we got into the read-aloud it went pretty smoothly, with a wee bit of confusion at the "don't read the italics" stuff, quickly smoothed over. I really like numbering off 1-2-3-4 and reading the social contract around the table. It felt very equalizing and bonding. Though I did notice that, as I happened to get the Narrator card, I did a lot of the talking in the next part. I understand the reasoning behind giving all the first person intro to the Narrator, but it felt like my voice was being given unfair weight, exacerbated by the fact this was "my" game I was bringing and facilitating in the first place.

      Some players were confused by the instructions on the Thinking cards. When asked to read the Thinking card text aloud, they sometimes only read the top section, thinking the bottom part was something unrelated. And the wording of the instructions threw off a player or two. Like, when it says:\n
      Ask questions in your character's voice or to clarify what they perceive...

      About what the photographs of the city of the city show, what it must be like there, and at what price we could get there.

      About how the machine works, how to operate it, and what it does.



      \nAt least one player couldn't make the connection between "Ask questions" in one line and "About" in the next two lines, requiring explanation from an experienced player (me) on what their duties actually were.

      Also, while as I noted above the Narrator is much improved, I still find there's a timidity factor to overcome in making use of the Thinking Cards fully. The Machine is probably the easiest: someone messes with the Machine, and the bloke with the Machine card's on duty. The photographs are similar, but not as much to explore about them (at least not as much OBVIOUS), so they tend to fade from play a bit. (I had Marlene go over to look at the photos deliberately because I noticed we hadn't really used the Photographs card.) The teacher is a bit easy to forget about because well, obviously, he's dead, and he's not a cool machine to fiddle with, and you have to really consciously strive to have your characters investigate his life.

      Which is all to say that I find myself wanting some way for the rules to nudge us more strongly to exercise our Thinking Card duties in general.

      We encountered some uncertainty regarding the lines of authority on describing our environment. Like, "create fiction freely, except..." seems clear, until you start wondering: "well, really, this is the Teacher's home, so EVERYTHING in the home is about the Teacher and must be answered by the holder of the Teacher card." After awhile, we kind of stopped making up environmental details freely at all, instead deferring to the Teacher cardholder for practically everything that wasn't the Machine or the Photographs. Not sure how to demark that productively; I mean, some stuff, like searching through the teacher's papers in his desk drawer, DO feel like they fall under the Teacher card, but I'm not keen to see people asking permission to describe EVERY little thing in the house.

      *****

      Anyway, all in all it was a marvelous game with new friends and old. Thanks, Jackson, and good luck on the new revision!*

      Peace,
      -Joel
