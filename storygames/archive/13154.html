\n I just read a little review of my game chef submission (which is really bad and sort of half finished*) that talked about how technical and hard to follow the game rules were, which is true, but it got me thinking about how much I do not care about fluff on any level, not as a GM or a player or a designer. I want premise and rules and sometimes advice... this is probably just a personal taste thing, but it seems like a great deal of really good game systems would be very difficult to present in an evocative way and that a great deal of confusion can occur when text tries to be evocative. I want rules that speak for themselves, through play.

I get the idea that I may be alone on this one.

*the game, not the review

So basically the discussion point is this: Do games and gamers hurt themselves (in as much as it makes actual play as opposed to interacting with the text less enjoyable) by wanting their game rules to also be good fiction and fun reading, and if so, why?

    \n If you want your rules to be purely technical it helps a lot if you include plenty of brief examples of play (which take care of the "evocative" part, while staying out of your way when you explain the rules), preferably in side boxes or somehow separated from the text.

    The danger of technical texts is that they tend to slide into either
    -jargon (using loads of game terms no-one has achieved fluency in yet, instead of explaining what goes on here, at the table, between these people or in the game's fiction, which anyone can imagine and keep track of. the rules will be harder to understand and follow)
    or
    -dry language without pazaz (if your game text is as boring to read as a washing machine operating manual, it won't excite people, it won't convey to them why it would be exciting to play. if your text has no charm, people will not be convinced the game has charm, because the text is the only thing they're going on)

    So, a definite no, to "are fun game texts bad?" Your definition of "fun" might vary.

    As to "good fiction", I think it depends purely on your tastes. RPG Manual Fiction is positively atrocious most of the time. In those few examples where it's actually good it can be as much as a nice read as a distraction. If you don't like fiction, you won't like it, if you enjoy fiction, that might be even the only reason to read the manual (I know people do that). Also depends on the exact definition of "fiction" in this context.

    \n The question isn't about whether or not people want to pay for fiction by game writers for a gamer audience, which is what I mean here when I say fluff... its about whether or not a gaming community that judges the quality of a game by how fun the rules are to read is doing itself a disservice. I mean, I'm thinking of some very complex old war games, like Squad Leader or Third Reich, in which the rules are both meticulous and meticulously presented. There is nothing fun about reading those rules (and I'm sure a lot of people would think there's not much fun in playing the games either) but no one expected the rule book to be fun, and, if they had tried to make them more enjoyable to read, I think it would have definitely hurt the game... it would have had to been simplified or modified so as to be enjoyable.

    Which isn't to say they didn't try very hard to make them understandable and readable. Squad Leader in particular had a very neat way of adding the rules in chunks with every new scenario.

    So, if we expect the rules to be fun, and don't engage in play unless reading them is enjoyable or evocative, do we, as a community, deny ourselves some good play? Are games popular because they're pretty or because they're good?

    And by fluff here I certainly don't mean engaging with fiction that emerges in play or setting or premise information that is an important part of "system" in the sense that it effects the play, or gauges or guides it. So I guess, its arguable, that any content presented by the designer is system in the biggest sense, but though I can't point to the line, I think there is one.

    \n If you're writing a game (as many games are) whose point is that I'll play this game that feels like X (or X+Y) things from other media, then chances are I have no time for your fluff, as the fluff is that other media.

    When you give me a brawny swords and sorcery game I don't want fluff, because I'll just go read Howard. Or when I get a Cthulhu game I don't want Lovecraft fanfic filling up the book -- I can go read the source material. (Chances are if I'm interested in your game, I already have.)

    Or less directly, say we have a game about adventurers who fight dragons and go into dungeons underground and so forth, I don't need fluff as I can always read Lord of the Rings and the Dying Earth. Or hell, at this point I don't even need to do that, chances are that just being part of modern North American geek culture I've picked up more crap about dragons and pointy eared elves who are lovely and wise and... blech. Anyway, I don't need fluff, I have it already. I just need big books full of detailed rules.

    For games where you just want me to play in a genre and we can all assume some combination of 1) the setting being generic and 2) the things that aren't coming out of the group and the interaction of the rules as we play, no fluff is needed.

    OTOH, for those very rare games where you actually want to give me something new to play, something that isn't "Like Tanith Lee filmed by Kurosawa" but is actually unique, having a flavor of its own, and with something specific to say then fluff may be called for.

    I'll be happiest if much of the setting, even in a visionary game, can be embedded into system somehow. Burning Wheel lifepaths for unique and alien worlds, for example, can tell you a lot about those worlds without having big sections of ready-text. But even then, without something in the way of context and tone, chances are they aren't going to give me everything I need to actually enjoy the game.

    And that extra context and tone in a game that is doing something unique, that's what fluff is good for.

    (That it needs to be good fluff, as others have pointed out, goes without saying, right?)

    \n@Tamara,
    First, a definition: I'll use the term "packaging", because it encapsulates the problem I envision - it's something you get along with the purchased product, but it's not the product itself. Metaphorically speaking, it can be discarded, leaving the substance of the overall text intact.
    I will add, based on previous comments, that "packaging" is material that neither a) informs the use of the mechanics or b) informs the immediate direction of play. I'll try to further define Clause B as I go.

    I'm taking a look at Vampire: the Masquerade, 3rd edition, for inspiration. Pleasantly, I'm surprised to see that a lot of the stuff at the very beginning of the book is actually fairly purposeful: there's stuff about the slightly-tedious Gothic-Punk motif they love so well, which tells us what kind of vampire stories we're going to be creating with these rules (in theory). Then there's stuff about different kinds of vampires - not the clans, but rather different age groups, and then a quick word on those who are politically unaffiliated, regardless of age. These are relevant options to consider for character creation.
    This material thus fits into B - it has little to no mechanical content (beyond a quick reference to the Generation occupied by certain vampiric age groups), but it does tell you important, basic things about how the age and Generation of a vampire inform where they fit socially. Thus, the age and Generation of p.c. vampires is reflected, indicating what players should expect as far as how their characters relate to vampire society.
    Interestingly, most of Chapter One falls in this category - 3 of the Six Traditions (the laws of vampire society) are pretty well grounded in activities with mechanical backing - i.e. p.c.'s can create more vampires, and three different Traditions touch on the interaction of vampires with their progeny. Since there are rules on killing, and killing vampires in particular, Tradition Six (Destruction) covers explicitly mechanical activities.
    Traditions One, Two, and Five (Masquerade, Domain, and Hospitality), though, relate to more abstract, hand-wavey subject matter: whether one's vampiric nature is revealed to humans, the bounds of one's own territory, and the bounds of others' territory. Still, these fall into category B - they give you some ideas about what will shape play: territory, both literal and societal/conceptual. Stay on your turf, and you'll be safe. Heck, I think that constitutes a theme! Sweet.

    The section "The Others", page 52, is akin to the brief section in Polaris about The South - there is very little mechanical connection or relevance, and it's a stretch to say this informs the bounds of play beyond a faint background theme. I'm ruling this section Packaging.

    More straight-up Packaging to follow, and then I'll call it quits: "Ancient Lore" (p54, clippings from the vampire-history text The Book of Nod) is Packaging, indeed - - you do NOT need to know this two-page spread even exists. Caine is faintly referenced in the text, but I am of the opinion that knowing that the biblical Cain (even if his name's spelled wrong) is the progenitor of vampires should be more than enough thematic input for anybody familiar with his story to use it effectively to capture mood and such. But that's being generous - the game isn't *about* Old Testament tales or religion or anything. Not inherently.

    Subsequent sections on Gehenna, the modern state of vampire society (really a recap and update on the official meta-plot; boo), and the Lexicon are also Packaging - they are far enough removed from what happens in play, or are really extrapolations from existing setting elements ("Modern Nights", the meta-plot update, hands down revelations about the fight between the sects) that any play group could arrive at naturally through actual play.
    For that matter, very little of the whole Sects and Clans thing escapes my definition: your clan informs who you're related to, and what kind of powers and outlook (on vampirism, etc.) you have, but you aren't mechanically supported to act one way or another about clanmates who aren't direct ancestors or parentage. Thus, how clans feel about one another, the sheer number of them, how they fall in the two sects... it's really just taking some archetypes, tossing them in two piles, and saying "You guys do it this way, too." Of course, the real, historical reasons for why clans are in their chosen sects probably has more to do with the order in which they were thought up and the order in which designers began to give support for players to actually portray Sabbat or independent-clan characters.       \n So as a consumer, I have a totally clear delineation in my head between "fluff" and "crunch." It has nothing to do with setting or rules.

      There's rules crunch: Stuff that directly and obviously impacts resolution.

      There's rules fluff: Little subsystems that sound totally rad but I never use. Equipment lists, that's seriously fluffy shit right there.

      There's setting crunch: Hardcoded situations right there in the world, like social injustices or pending revolutions or whatever. Clan/familial/governmental tensions. Rules of society against which to grind. Pictures of places you can go and people/creatures you might meet. Maps that matter.

      There's setting fluff: Utterly unimportant historic shit that doesn't actually provide any context to anything because, well, only the GM is gonna read it. Pictures of places you can't go and people you can't meet.

      There's procedural crunch: Everyone participates in the world burning session because that helps create common understanding and investment. You choose the color of your mouse's cape and that helps you visualize your hero. The MC writes love letters to his players after a long break because that helps everyone remember what's going on and lets you progress the situation.

      And there's procedural fluff, but IMO this is a highly contentious area about which many experimental-RPG fans are going to disagree. It's probably the most fertile area of design right now. Procedures I have personally found fluffy: The knife thing in MRG. Dance-for-the-GM XP schemes in oldskool D&D (was it good roleplaying? was it good enough? did you engage with my material the way I wanted you to?).

      Executive summary: If it provides a useful tool to me in any way, it's crunch. If it's non-useful or just vaguely evocative, it's fluff. Doesn't matter if it's a rule, setting material or a procedure.

      my fundamental argument is this: a game's identity is primarily defined by what happens during play and that the content included as the game's rules should be focused on being directly implemented during that play.

      \n
      \n

      Yes, except for that one word, "directly". There's no reason everything has to be direct. If it was completely direct, it would just give you procedural algorithms. But that would be painful to read, and you couldn't figure out what to do with it until the end, and you'd probably give up before you got there. So instead it's in sentences, but those sentences have tone to convey things like, "This is the part where you get what you want" or "This is where you invest in something" or "The consequences of this are severe" or whatever. And if that's written well, it explains not only the procedure, but how to use those procedures. And it probably does that through examples, some of which are extended into scraps of fiction. And some of those scraps of fiction are best expressed through pictures.

      \nFrom my point of view, the source material (like Coen brothers movies), the tone of writing, the illustrations, and many other things are not rules - but they do affect play. Role-playing games are not boardgames - they involve a whole lot of open-ended choices based on imagination, like what new characters to add, what plot twists to throw in, and so forth. The imaginative choices I make are guided by my inspirations for the game.

      Basically, I don't that such a 3-page hypothetical vampire game could exist that didn't depend on external shared knowledge about what vampires are, and what the vampire fiction is like that you're trying for. If you took someone who had only read the Twilight books, someone who had only read Anne Rice, and someone who hadn't read any vampire novels but had seen the Blade movies - and gave them a 3-page set of raw rules, I think their play would be a mess - which could be improved by the game providing a vision. A game needs flavor pieces to get people's imaginations on roughly the same page in mood and style.

      The flavor can be provided by what are technically mechanics - like My Life With Master's master creation mechanics - but flavor can also come from agreed source material (especially for licensed works like Mouse Guard or Buffy the Vampire Slayer); or in-game illustrations (Paranoia is excellent for its satirical vision via cartoons); or other fiction.
