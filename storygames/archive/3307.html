\n Here's the problem with the title: It's not very google-friendly. "Do" is a very broad search term. I love it as a title, but it's terrible for online marketing. So, I'm adding a sub-title. The full title is tentatively "Do: The Way of Ways," but I'm eager to hear other suggestions.

You guys came up with some awesome alternative titles for Mathematica, so I was hoping you'd offer similar magic for my little project. Here's the ever-evolving sales pitch about the game if you haven't seen it yet:\n
It's a Buddhist Dogs in the Vineyard set in a Wushu fantasy universe. People from many worlds send letters to a temple asking for help with their seemingly unresolvable conflicts. The temple sends young, flying monks on a pilgrimage with a handful of these letters, telling them not to return until all the conflicts have been resolved. The letters are a sort of menu for the player-group, letting them pick and choose what they'd like to play that session. Several people from S-G have already contributed letters to the project.

The title "Do" is a double-meaning. If you pronounce it as the English verb, it stands for the pilgrim's proactive intervention. (Kind of the opposite of the prime directive.) If you pronounce it as the Japanese noun ("Dough"), it means "the Way." (The temple's philosophy is called "the Way of Ways.")
\nIf you need any more details, I'll be glad to provide.

    "Heaven" is a rather Western/Christian overtones kind of word.
    \nIn Chinese, "tian" is heaven in the abstract sense, the will of the universe, fate, God, the sky, the cosmos, the opposite of the earth, the seat of authority.

    In contrast, "tian tang," the heavenly hall/palace, is a more concrete Christian-y or syncretic Chinese heaven, an actual place in the sky that you go when you die, ruled by the Lord of Heaven or the Lord on High or the Jade Emperor or what have you, filled with the celestial bureaucracy that mirrors the earthly one.

    So I think you're okay there.

    \n An excellent point, Fred. Let's say there's a spectrum for the tone of voice in which the game is written, a combination of its seriousness and poetry.

    At one end of the spectrum, you have games like Nobilis, where the tone is very much noble and classy. Very refined and beautiful, but perhaps not as approachable. Like a ballroom or a fancy wedding.

    At the other extreme, you have games like, I dunno, Kill Puppies for Satan. The tone is much more down-to-earth and casual. It's certainly approachable, but doesn't put on airs of elegance or grace. Like a diner.

    While Do does have the superficial elements of what would normally make an austere sort of game, I would still like to make it very approachable using lightweight, easy-to-learn mechanics and lots of fantastical situations. The letters I've received so far range from serious to humorous, but most are just plain fresh. A little girl swallowed by a giant whale, a living pen running out of ink, and a farmer's fields infested with talking insects. Crazy stuff.

    So that's the challenge. The unfettered zaniness within a thin shell of austerity. It's a Faberg\xc3\xa9 egg filled with candy. :P

    EDIT: Boy, that sounds waaaay more pretentious than I meant it to sound.

      \n I had some discussion with my co-developer Annie Rush, reining in the focus of the game a bit. I'm now leaning towards:

      MAIN TITLE
      Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple

      POSSIBLE CHAPTER TITLES
      Letters to Heaven
      Celestial Peacekeepers
      The Way of Ways

      I'm using "pilgrims" in lieu of "monks" because it's more gender-neutral and is more specific to the game's premise. You still have the word "flying" in the subtitle, but doesn't sound as zany as "flying pilgrims." "Flying Temple" presents a touch of the mystic and lets you know these pilgrims aren't religious zealots from England. "The Way of Ways" and "Letters to Heaven" may still find their way into standard descriptions of the game that aren't part of the title proper.
