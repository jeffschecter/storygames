    \n I'm sorry, you're right. This particular 'gamification' trend just tends to set me off. Developing systems to externalize your self-improvement is no doubt a useful process, but I feel like if the goal is to make it into a game then you need to include some opportunity for play, not just a reward system and some 'levels.' Temper is interesting as both self-help and game because part of what it is trying to do is demonstrate insight into how anger works; its rules have an intentional shape, it's not just 'every time you stop yourself from losing your temper, have a donut' or whatever.

    \n I agree there is much work to be done in this area, and I feel it's time for Rickard to remind us about intrinsic and extrinsic rewards. But there are also different types of "players" in the world, and if the concept of "game" is hybridized into things that typically wouldn't be considered games at all, I don't see how that's necessarily bad. Maybe not for you, or for me, but not bad.

    In the old days we dreamed of VR systems that would fictize the most menial of tasks; imagine being a sewer cleaner, but thanks to your VR helmet and powergloves you're playing a game like WOW, instead of seeing rats you're seeing tiny goblins, and instead of spraying away turds and coagulated fat, you're pointing your staff at magical lichen that must be harvested for her majesty.

    Am I joking?

    I may live long enough to say No!

    \n It's interesting how this contrasts with Duolingo, a language-learning site that uses game mechanics as a language to show progress. It doesn't set up a strict incentive model (save that you can earn some extra stuff, like a course on how to flirt, by doing particularly well), but it uses things like "strength bars" to track your mastery and continued knowledge of certain areas. You also earn XP and level up and stuff, but the major point of that is so that you can benchmark your progress.

    \n Paul, not sure if this counts as awesome-ifying or just diverging entirely, but my idea is to use all catalogued habits as barriers. Basically, "roll to do anything else".

    When we play fictional people, it's easy to optimize decisions in a way that we often don't in real life. Choosing to play someone dysfunctional is an option, but that can get old. So, here would be a system wherein you can try to optimize, but the system forces a certain amount of dysfunction on you, to strive against. Kinda like real life habits, maybe?

    \n There's an early phase of the hero's journey / monomyth, which goes something like "call to adventure, refusing the call, getting persuaded". In plenty of genres, the hero starts out as a regular person. Sci fi, magical realism, gritty fantasy -- a lot of it starts with engaging characters who aren't yet in the middle of danger and adventure. There are even some Batman stories about Bruce Wayne fumbling around and vaguely opposing crime in more traditional vigilante manner before he goes the Costume And Gadgets route.

    So I think it'd be fun to roleplay that phase. There'd be fictional stuff that needs addressing, and the conflict is to get off your ass and overcome your hurdles to the point where you'll address it. Once you put on your Batman costume, or step knowingly into Narnia, or accept captaincy of the starship, the game's over. But until then, I mean, gee, being a spoiled billionaire, or playing in the old house, or being first mate is what I'm used to, man...

    Manipulation games would be cool too, but that doesn't say "habit" to me.
