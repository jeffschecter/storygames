What would you like a historical setting book to be like?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2011 \n edited May 2011 in Story Games
\n
\n

\n
\n I really like history. I'd like to write some setting books about historical periods that interest me: Victorian times, Georgian times and so forth.

The problem is, historical setting books can be dull. You get a page on equipment, a page on transport and a page on I'm sorry I lost interest halfway through typing this sentence. At their worst, they read like Wikipedia articles.

They get better when the history is interesting. So not just, say, a chapter on Victorian transport, but a chapter on Victorian spiritualism. Or the Victorian love of sensation. Or Victorian homicides. Give me any of those things and ideas for games start sparking.

So how would you do a historical setting book? I'd like to write something that provokes ideas, that makes you think "I'd love to play that through in a game". (That, after all, is what history does to me, and I'd like to write something that shares my enthusiasm.)

Do think, particularly, about what kind of book you'd find interesting. For example, I sort of feel I should find a book of scenario ideas and situations interesting. But I wouldn't. I want to read a list of historical nuggets and invent my own ideas.

So what should a historical setting book be like?

The point is to learn about people who never got a dense biography in the first place.

    Posted By: JDCorleyThis is why the Forgotten Realms 3rd Edition guide is so terrific. There literally is not a single page that doesn't scream "HEY THIS IS A FUCKING PROBLEM THAT REQUIRES A MAGICAL WANDERING ROCKSTAR TO COME IN AND SOLVE".
    \nIt's also pretty much fucking useless for anything other than inspiration in prep. Just sayin'.

    For me personally, what I'd want to see:
    -Whatever you write keep it in small, digestible chunks. I'm not saying bite-size clips for ADD people, but it should be concise and light.
    -Bullet points! After a chapter, sum it up. "What have we learned?". A reminder and cheat sheet.
    -Random tables! It works for fantasy and all why not history. I'd love to see a "back streets of Georgian London wandering random encounter table".
    -Anecdotes, "Did you know?" flashes, common sayings of the era, stories. Anything that can help provide emotional impact, paint the atmosphere of the period. We all too often forget that history is not a dull collection of dates and "facts" (history and facts, ha ha, how droll). History is (hi)story. Tell us the story of the era (or stories rather), screw lists and dates (although a timeline can be useful...at the end of the book).
    -I think whatever facts you can tell us about hansom cabs of Victorian London (or whatever) you can tell in a couple of paragraphs. Anything more than that and you'll be writing a 500 page textbook about all aspects of a certain period. It's impossible to cover everything.
    -Equipment lists - no. Something that gives me a feel for the prices of the time and what a poor man can afford as opposed to a rich man - very yes. Value of money, social class.
    -Specific cultural differences. Why am I playing in the then instead of the now. What contemporary assumptions I have are false when I get in-character. What historical assumptions must I take on. A RP-guide.
    -System-agnostic game tools, aids, minigames. Let me roll some dice, pick some options and create something setting-appropriate.

    Posted By: GrahamA better example is the inch-by-inch description of cities I describe above. "Chelsea: The home of the Chelsea Flower Show". It's mostly dull, it's mostly fluff, but it often goes into location sourcebooks. I'm trying to find a more interesting way to do it.
    \nI think the reason such inch-by-inch location sourcebooks are often at odds with what people try to do is this:

    If you're playing strangers to the town, likely you're going to be following directions to where you need to go.

    If you're playing people who live in the town, you know where you're going.

    However, in terms of creating a living, breathing place, these sorts of books just flat can't be beaten. They are not really relevant for a story gaming approach, in which setting is connected to the situation of the characters, but (say) for sandbox games they're great. You don't have to work out who might find the body in the alley, the game material tells you. You don't have to make up some new character off the top of your head who works in the diner, the game material tells you. It's a big advantage for that kind of "nonfictional" kind of play. The next step in that direction is not to make it less detailed, but instead to leverage technology to make it more detailed. Imagine you had a Daggerdale supplement on a tablet or on a laptop. You punch in what time of day it is and the tablet tells you where everyone is and what they're doing when the goblins attack or the evil wizzard launches a fireball. It would make timing crucial and most of all, very interesting.

    But yeah, the Arkham Massachusetts/Kingsport, City In the Mists type of Call of Cthulhu sourcebooks are really not much use for a story oriented person except as a collection of scenario hooks.

    That's not a bad thing, scenario hooks are a good thing. Which leads me to this:\n
    Posted By: GrahamWould you actually use this? I know that I never use scenario seeds like this: I want to get creative and invent my own.
    \nI think the reason the Big Thick History Book is common is because the expectation is that we will make our own game with it. In other words, the designer/writer doesn't know whether we're going to be occult dabblers, secret agents for the Church holding the line against spiritualist madmen, or ghosts. So he has to spread his situation-material very thin across lots of setting facts.

    Whereas (for example) the old splatbook formulation started from a point of view - you are going to be this kind of character and here's a shitload of stuff about the kinds of things that kind of character does in this kind of setting.

    Some games have such a clear core story that there really is no point in going into great detail about situation in a supplement. Cthulhu Gaslight doesn't contain anything about how to play Call of Cthulhu. It's all setting, we know what we're going to be doing in Gaslight, we're going to investigate, and cultists will shoot at us, and we will blow up the monster with magic and dynamite or whatever.

    So that's why I propose the "point of view" setting material book. Sure, I might want to come up with things on my own - in that case, you probably won't sell your book to me, but in that case, the only kind of book that will give me what I want IS the big hefty broad-based thing.

    Where do people live? What can we expect to find an a typical house by social strata? Where do they hang out when they're not at home? What activities occupy their leisure time.

    A rough time line of key historical events. If the setting is 1832 what things that people associate with that general time have already happened? What things haven't happened yet? What are the key entries on Wikipedia I can go to to flesh out the details.
    What kind of stuff do people have...does thing X exist still/yet? Only for the wealthy/collectors of the obscure?
    How do people pay for stuff...by social status. Pockets full of coins? Credit that gets billed to a factor who takes care of such mundane things? Bills drawn on a bank?


    In brief...situate me properly in time and make sure I know what its like to live there so it isn't "Welcome to the 20th/21st century with funny clothes and quaint technology"

    A better example is the inch-by-inch description of cities I describe above. "Chelsea: The home of the Chelsea Flower Show". It's mostly dull, it's mostly fluff, but it often goes into location sourcebooks. I'm trying to find a more interesting way to do it.
    \nI liked the approach used in Ptolus where some of the chapters were written as if they were from a tourist's guide to the city. That strikes a nice balance between practical information and highlighting interesting and distinctive elements about the neighborhood. Plus, it challenges the writer to be engaging.

    While using "Chelsea: The home of the Chelsea Flower Show" is dull when describing the neighborhood, it is important to remember to include descriptions of festivals. All too often, our games take place outside any reference to religious or cultural festivals that would inform the lives of people in the setting. "Chelsea: The home of the Chelsea Flower Show" is deadly dull until the flower show is used as cover for an advance party of Triffids.
