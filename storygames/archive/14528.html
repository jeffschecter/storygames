\n Ok, folks. This is a cross-posting from the rpg site. Sounds strange, I know

Here's the deal:

I like octaNe. A lot. The only thing that irks me is the fact that a player can potentially end a scene whenever he rolls one single 5 or 6 on (usually) 3D6. Basically, it's a scene resolution approach.
I like to have good fights in my games, with a lot of flavor and detail. And I like to have tension in those fights, uncertainty that keeps us on the edge of our seats when we roll the bones.

Which is something that scene resolution just can't do. It's almost counterproductive in this way; at least this is my experience with it.

So how can I turn octaNe's scene resolution into something more blow-by-blow-like? I appreciate your ideas, fellas.

    \n Well, that's where Hazards come into it, right?
    If you're trying to draw it out more, without necessarily making your bad guys extra tough, how about giving baddies a "Plot Point Buffer"? It'd work like a temporary Hazard, so after a certain number of Player-scene-control results (4+ rolls) are sucked up into the great Buffer Black Hole, it'd be used up, and the baddie would be at the whims of the character as normal.
    So, for example, say you were fighting a gang of mutant bikers with a Buffer of 3. If you rolled 4 dice with a 2, 3, 5, and 6, since the 5 and 6 are the only rolls that give the Player control over the scene, they'd be removed, leaving the 3 as the final result (being the next highest), and the gang's Buffer would be reduced to 1 (since it removed two dice).
