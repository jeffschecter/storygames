    \n Awesome!

    Honestly, half the battle is just being willing to change as you go, which you've already got covered. If a rule suggestion comes up, and you don't have an immediately obvious reason to reject it, try it and see were it goes.

    The only other important thing is to make sure that everyone involved realizes what's going down. When playstorming with friends, they need to realize that you're not there to entertain them. Everyone's there to see where the game goes. They have to make their own fun. People looking for a regular play experience from a playstorm can get frustrated when you start shifting the rules around. So they need to understand that they're not going to get a regular play experience.

    Oh, and most importantly, you have to let us know how it all went down.

    \n We did a kindof Playstorm for Keith's new Dream Palace game a while back.

    The first two sessions were much more talking about the game then playing it. We'd start playing and hit a rule that didn't seem to work, so we'd toss around ideas for what the rule was trying to accomplish and why it was or wasn't accomplishing it. Then we'd play a bit more during which we'd start creating a list of things needing more definition.

    Then the next session we'd get a little farther refining some of the things that seemed to be working until we ran into the next problem area (or just unwritten yet area), and so on.

    The kind of things I've found playstorming good at is finding the details that get missed about procedure. If you come to your group with a complete game and you run it, and the players are actually "playing it" rather than "playstorming" it, and it works...there are probably a whole host of things you did or that your group as a whole just understands to do. Plus the willingness of players who are "playing" to just go-with-the-flow and not disrupt the game makes it even harder to spot holes. This leads to me getting cranky when the text is published without all of those things having been recorded in the text.

    When we Playstormed on the other hand, a whole host of critical decisions points were laid bare highlighting the oft repeated question "who gets to do that?". When you're writing text you can write ambiguous sentences like "Decide what scene to frame next." (which is an example of horrible BTW). When you're actually writing rules as you play the missing pieces becomes painfully obvious...WHO decides, and for what purpose. Since someone is actually about to DO that thing, you have to actually make a decision about how it gets done. Then you can write that down so that other groups will know how to do that thing.

    \n Eppy has much more experience at doing it than I so he can correct me if I'm wrong, but I don't think Playstorming will help much with math issues unless you've just got something completely backwards (like saying roll under when you meant roll over). Only the most obvious math issues will show up in only a few rolls. Other issues (of the sort that will eventually be figured out and optimized by players in a way that leads to play radically different from what you wanted) take either some long term campaign play (or lots of repeated play if your game is short form) or someone REALLY good at groking and breaking mechanics.

    What I've found Playstorming to be most useful for is:

    1) helping to bring focus on what the game is about...because the designer will need to be able to answer that question when the group starts brainstorming ideas (Seth and I bounced a TON of totally unsuitable mechanics ideas off of Keith before he was able to decide what he really wanted out of Dream Palace). The process of filtering out the suggestions that feel wrong from the ones that feel right will really help you understand your goals.

    2) specifying procedures. Yeah, its great that you recognize some of your text is vague. But once you start to playstorm that bit you'll be forced...just by the reality of having people sitting there actually doing things...to specify what the real person is really supposed to do right now. Chances are you'll wind up specifying it in a totally unfun way at first (cuz if you knew what way was fun you wouldn't have written it vaguely to begin with)...but eventually you'll hit on the procedure that rings true for the game. So yes, seeing what feel natural and what feels awkward or fiddly or WTF is a big part of it.
