Constructing the Grid

In Shock:, the players begin by constructing a Shock/Issue Grid (or Grid, for short). An Issue is a real-life concern of one of the players, while a Shock is a major way in which the imaginary world differs from ours. In the 1.1 rules, each player can propose one or two Issues, and then the group agrees on a single Shock. Then the group divides Ownership of the Issues and the Shock. If you Own something, it means that you have final say over the details of how that thing is expressed in the game world. Finally, you decide on a Shock/Issue intersection that is interesting to you, and that\xe2\u20ac\u2122s where you will make your character.

For our game, we said that we would deal with Issues that were important to us but weren\xe2\u20ac\u2122t hot-button issues. That way we could keep the intensity of the game down.

We lied to ourselves. Yeah, it happens.

So, our Issues were Littering, Public Education, Mood Suppressing Drugs, and Political Corruption.

Our Shock was \xe2\u20ac\u0153All Thoughts are Public\xe2\u20ac\x9d. This means that anyone can sense your thoughts, just like hearing or seeing you. As the game went on, we settled on various sense impressions for thoughts, which were a combination of sight and sound.

Then we spent some time making up details about our world. These are called Minutiae, and this is a great idea. With some quick brainstorming, we were able to have a reasonably realized world. I won\xe2\u20ac\u2122t bore you with our entire list of Minutiae, but here\xe2\u20ac\u2122s a sampling:

--Thought residue can be left around for others to encounter. This is called \xe2\u20ac\u0153littering\xe2\u20ac\x9d.
--Everyone takes a drug called Litox to control thought leakage and prevent littering.
--Thoughts are contagious.
--Redheads are trouble. Everyone knows it.
--Karma Police patrol the populace.
--Government is theocracy that combines Buddhist doctrine, Catholic hierarchy, and Islamic sharia law.
--\xe2\u20ac\u0153Dosing\xe2\u20ac\x9d on Litox is an act of prayer.

Finally, the group needs to define two sets of opposed values that will be used as Praxis scales. These are the ways to \xe2\u20ac\u0153get stuff done\xe2\u20ac\x9d in the game world. Our Praxis scales were Personal Happiness vs. Corporate Responsibility and Politics vs. Sex.

So, it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Equilibrium without gun kata, and everyone\xe2\u20ac\u2122s a telepath.

Nice, huh?

    \nPost-game thoughts

    It worked! Wow! After the first round of scenes, I had serious doubts about the story being satisfying, which I expressed to the other players. It felt like we were all over the place and that there was no way that we would actually be able to harmonize our divergent tales. I also expressed the opinion that the issues that we really wanted to address weren\xe2\u20ac\u2122t actually on the Grid. In particular, I pointed out the large number of Minutiae that had to do with procreation. (I didn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t list those off.)

    In retrospect, I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m not sure if these concerns were accurate or not. I am glad that I raised them, though. I think that we all worked a little harder to make sure that our stories knit together. And, to my surprise, everything wrapped up in a satisfying way. I was happy with the end of the story.

    We also noticed the different character arcs in relationship to the larger society. Martin started off outside the \xe2\u20ac\u0153system\xe2\u20ac\x9d and was personally conquered by the system, while still overthrowing it for others. In contrast, Kanjo started off locked into the \xe2\u20ac\u0153system\xe2\u20ac\x9d, and, while she couldn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t make any lasting social change, she was able to get free herself.

    Mirrim was the odd duck in all this. Her story was much more personal, largely due to the fact that her Antagonist was an individual and not a large social force. It felt like she illustrated the \xe2\u20ac\u0153common man\xe2\u20ac\x9d playing out her life against the backdrop of the larger social destruction being caused by the other characters.

    Crystal really liked the graphic design of the various sheets. By the way, Joshua, in case you don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t know this, the downloadable Antagonist sheet is apparently missing a Credit box. I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t remember when I downloaded this, so you may have already fixed this.

    I had read about the \xe2\u20ac\u0153large\xe2\u20ac\x9d scenes in Shock:, but I was surprised to experience them myself. 2-4 Scenes doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t feel like a lot, but there\xe2\u20ac\u2122s actually a lot of content that can be packed into those scenes, especially if you riff off the other players\xe2\u20ac\u2122 scenes. Crystal commented that she\xe2\u20ac\u2122d like the ability to play longer, so maybe we will try another game with the intent of stepping through several iterations of the game cycle, treating each Story Goal as a Chapter Goal or somesuch thing.

    I really enjoyed the orthogonal goals. That meant that each dice roll could potentially have four outcomes, without escalation or rerolls from Links. That structure enables the \xe2\u20ac\u0153large\xe2\u20ac\x9d scenes that I discussed earlier. I also enjoyed the need to allocate dice between \xe2\u20ac\u0153gaining my goal\xe2\u20ac\x9d and \xe2\u20ac\u0153interfering with his\xe2\u20ac\x9d. Very cool.

    Musing on Shock:

    After the game, I raised a question. Does Shock: front-load too much? As I looked at the story that we had developed, it seemed to me that the game was good for allowing us to make statements about the various Issues that we had placed on the Grid. However, I wondered if it would be as good for exploring questions about those Issues. Setup for Shock: feels a bit like building a maze, winding up some characters, and seeing where they come out.

    I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve found that the strongest post-game reflection that I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve had was the result of emergent issues rising from the game, not the result of front-loaded issues established prior to play. It doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t feel like Shock: can easily transcend its front-loaded issues to encourage the emergence of new issues.

    Or, to put the question another way, is gameplay in Shock: trapped by the Grid?

    I do have a counterpoint. My fellow players and I are\xe2\u20ac\xa6opinionated, let us say. So, we\xe2\u20ac\u2122re unlikely to have an Issue where we don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t already have an opinion. This could be the reason for my feeling of being able to express, not explore. If we were to consciously choose Issues where we had questions, perhaps we would have found ourselves doing more exploring.

    I\xe2\u20ac\u2122d be interested in hearing from other Shock: players about this issue.


    Conclusion

    I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t want the previous section to sound like I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m griping. We really enjoyed Shock:. It felt properly SF, like we were given the right tools to make this sort of story. The story felt like a Brave New World of our very own, a story which provoked thought about the Issues that were the underpinnings of the very setting. There weren\xe2\u20ac\u2122t any \xe2\u20ac\u0153I didn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t see that coming!\xe2\u20ac\x9d moments, but there were a lot of \xe2\u20ac\u0153Huh. Yeah, that makes total sense\xe2\u20ac\x9d moments. In the end, I think that I\xe2\u20ac\u2122d say that Shock: is a contemplative game, and that\xe2\u20ac\u2122s a rarity on the market these days.

    Thanks, Joshua!

    Hey, Seth!

    \n

    I'm sad to say that I don't have time to fully read your post right now. I'm in a Viennese brewhouse with a pint and a sausage on the way, so I'm just checking in.

    \n

    However, I want to address your questions, since I can, and put the rest off until I'm back in the States and suffering from the opposite jetlag.

    \n

    So:

    \n
    \n

    I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve found that the strongest post-game reflection that I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve had was the result of emergent issues rising from the game, not the result of front-loaded issues established prior to play.

    \n
    \n

    That's almost inevitable, and I consider it part of the Fruitful Void. We made the same observation early in playtesting, and I consider it a strength. Ben even went so far as to say that the issues you address are not the ones on the Grid. I think that had to do with particular games that we were playing at the time, figuring out how to use the tools. Suffice to say that what you put down informs deeply the moral landscape of your world, but does not define it.

    \n

    That's what you're exploring, really. Not just the Issues you list.

    \n
    \n

    Or, to put the question another way, is gameplay in Shock: trapped by the Grid?

    \n
    \n

    I could answer a couple of ways, but let's just say "No."

    \n

    The Grid, obviously, is a framework. You hang your world on it. Real issues that people really want to address can't be summed up by "Workers' Rights" or whatever. The realities are more subtle, but giving a concrete authority the Ownership of "Workers' Rights" means that you can figure out questions like, "Is it worth joining a union if it jeopardizes my family? Who am I fighting for anyway?" A good Antagonist player can tease those out (though I'm kind of a long way from understanding and canonizing the process, so I'd love to talk about it, but I'm not going to have any straight answers).

    \n

    Shock: is really a game about exploring the human condition writ large. Of course, you can't write down all your questions beforehand and expect them not to evolve as you compose an answer.

    \n
    \n

    I do have a counterpoint. My fellow players and I are\xe2\u20ac\xa6opinionated, let us say. So, we\xe2\u20ac\u2122re unlikely to have an Issue where we don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t already have an opinion. This could be the reason for my feeling of being able to express, not explore. If we were to consciously choose Issues where we had questions, perhaps we would have found ourselves doing more exploring.

    \n
    \n

    That's a valid way of dealing with it, of course! But the way I really suggest is, don't sweat it! You'll express your feelings about a particular question, and along the way, will raise others, less formally. The separation of Ownership and Protagonist helps the first to evolve in a meaningful direction, but really, you're sketching out a whole society where certain issues are paramount and others are ignored but no less significant.

    \n
    \n

    I had read about the \xe2\u20ac\u0153large\xe2\u20ac\x9d scenes in Shock:, but I was surprised to experience them myself. 2-4 Scenes doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t feel like a lot, but there\xe2\u20ac\u2122s actually a lot of content that can be packed into those scenes, especially if you riff off the other players\xe2\u20ac\u2122 scenes.

    \n
    \n

    Yep. More just seems to dilute the experience, not make it bigger.

    \n
    \n

    Crystal commented that she\xe2\u20ac\u2122d like the ability to play longer, so maybe we will try another game with the intent of stepping through several iterations of the game cycle, treating each Story Goal as a Chapter Goal or somesuch thing.

    \n
    \n

    See page 44, Ending a Story. That's more than kosher; it's the way I expect people to play from one session to the next.

    \n

    OK, those sausages (Weissw\xc3\xbcrst, if you must know, plus a pile of Spetzel) were spectacular and now I've drunk a lot of beer and have to go return it.
