    \n If the war is just an ongoing backdrop, then the war can have any old root cause. I would only really consider the question if the play was integral to the decisions that kick off the war, or to the decisions that could end it.

    If so, I'd want the war's root causes to be appropriate to the game's scale.

    For Reign or AD&D Birthright or some other org-scale game, you could have it be about larger things; resources, trade, unfinished national business, large-scale profiteering, social engineering, response to invasion, schism, whathaveyou.

    For smaller-scale games, it could be driven by more personal things: individual profiteering / salvage, vows, old family business, divorce, cuckolding, excommunication.

    \n Louis XIV said it best: Ultima Ratio Regum. War is the last argument of kings.

    Carl Von Clausewitz had similar sentiments: War is diplomacy by other means.

    Anything you might hope to achieve by diplomacy, you can also hope to achieve by military arms or the threat of military action. Also, a side note: war is not about killing people, though it does that plenty. It is about causing your enemy to acquiesce to your demands, whatever they may be (convert to Religion X, cede land, resources or sovereignty, stop doing troubling thing Y, eat more root vegetables, whatever.)

    Expansionist Fascism in Europe? Well, we can ask nicely, Mr. Chamberlain, or we can take Mr. Churchill's suggestion.
