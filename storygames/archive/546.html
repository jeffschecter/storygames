But here's the thing: I can't think of an example of play that relates to other kinds of very close, very real tragedies.

Part of that might be identifying conflicts that would have the same sort of difficulty-in-play to them without necessarily being the same emotional structure.

As a haphazard example of a situation, I'm going to throw down Enron and it's employees. Thousands of people worked for a Fortune 10 company for decades, trusting them to live up to their pension agreements. There is certainly at least one person at the verge of retirement, who has committed to a certain living arrangement, and certain relationships with a small, otherwise poor family, who's spent the last 3 years trying to put together something out of what they have left. That character strikes me as both analogous and very different from an "abused as a child" character, but ultimately there's a betrayal of trust that directly impacts their whole life.

What I'm trying to get at is this: does sexual abuse stand as a bugaboo in front of a huge territory of difficult to tell stories, which might be likewise rewarding to play out? Does anyone have hypothetical or actual examples of this kind of story, or thoughts on whether or not there's any validity to the characterization of sexual abuse in RPGS?

    In media, and this includes RPGs, there is a tendency to portray abuse victims as broken dolls and poor little destroyed things. This has to do with our culture's approach to rape.

    I'd say that our culture's concept of innocence has more to do with it, although I suppose it's debatable.

    There's a ladder of innocence, starting with babies, then little girls, followed by little boys, followed by women of any age, then elderly men, and finally the rest of the population. When writers wants to to frighten us or illicit our sympathy for a victim or show how heinous someone is, they pick someone at least as innocent as the most common member of their audience, judged on this scale. The stronger the reaction desired, the higher up the scale they move.

    This is why, in teen slasher movies, although teens of both sexes get offed, the tendency is to make the main character be a teen girl, because teen boys aren't seen to be as innocent as girls. If they want to up the ante, the horror writers will have the teen girl be a babysitter, so that a little kid can be threatened.

    The stronger the reaction desired, the higher up the scale they move.

    There's something profoundly and utterly wrong with that. Something completely cynical and mercinary, that strikes me as an affront to storytelling as an art and to simple human decency.

    And yet, there it is. It's related to the idea that, to be frightening, the victim should be untainted. If the monster kills a 40-year-old man, unless the writer has taken steps to paint a full portrait of that character and somehow establish his innocence, there's the chance some of the audience may make unwarranted assumptions and think the victim "deserved it". Many writers would rather play on our natural instincts to protect the vulnerable. Children are a sure thing for this purpose, and in our sexist society, women are also regarded as vulnerable and get used by writers in the same way.

    There's a similar thing that happens in Mystery stories (as opposed to crime stories or noir.) The ideal detective is one who is an amateur sleuth rather than a professional, or at least so G. K. Chesterton claimed, because the professionals are in a sense tainted. Thus, Holmes, the scholar who takes an interest in criminology, is superior to Scotland Yard.

    I wish I could remember where I first encountered the theory of ladders of innocence in horror movies. The idea is not original with me, just that term "ladder of innocence" is. I think I saw something on it in The Straight Dope, or maybe it was a minor point in that Chesterton essay I mentioned.

    I remember dealing with the intersection of real tragedy and fictional tragedy during the aftermath of the September 11 attacks. I played in a couple of World of Darkness LARP games in upstate New York. These games were committed to being contemporary and "real-time," so that current events in the game were as close as possible to current events in real life.

    The reponse of the players to the attacks was unanimous: in our game worlds, they did not happen. We couldn't address it or deal with it in fiction while we were still reeling in real life. It took months (if not a year or so) before anyone felt comfortable even mentioning it in a fictionalized context. Eventually, once the horror had faded, we gradually began to treat it as a past event in the game. But we never addressed it directly.
