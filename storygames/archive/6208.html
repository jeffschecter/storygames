\n So, I introduced In a Wicked Age to my friend Brendan the other day (Eric was there, too, but he'd played before).

Character creation was going smoothly, but Brendan wasn't jumping in immediately with a particular strength. There was wisdom in this, and it reshaped how I make my particular strengths, now. I told Brendan that he didn't have to come up with his particular strength until he felt it for his character, he agreed, and we moved on. I continued with my NPCs making and binding them to the characters and giving them 'typical' strengths (typical for me - powers and such).

Then Brendan rocked my world.

"Can my particular strength be Murder? And have it be unique?"

This radically changed the game. Reworking our fictional world so that Brendan's character was the only one that murdered, well, damn, it was intense and made the game for me.

The moral? Particular Strengths aren't just cool powers. They can define the game, when used with innovation.

    \n We had the seed with his Particular Strength to define the entire social climate.

    "No murders must mean something like a high level honor system where your social standing is more important that visceral revenge"

    It changed the "child who argues with philosophers" into a Advocate, from the Litigator Caste, where law is more powerful than royalty.

    It made the demon, whom the imbiber of poisons wished congress with, seek out this lone assassin, hoping the murdering property was hereditary and magical, and that her offspring would be more powerful than her or the humans around her.

    It made capital punishment a non-possibility (except for our fair executioner), so that the burglar that was to be caught stayed in a posh apartment in the castle.

    It made kidnapping the best form of political maneuvering for the rich and powerful.

    It was undiluted awesome.
