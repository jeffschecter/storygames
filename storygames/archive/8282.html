    \n Matthijs,

    a)
    only in the last few years have i been able to recognize that i played Ad&d "wrong", ie "not as intended"; i believe all the joy i got out of it was through "playing it wrong"; in quotes b/c playing it as i did fed me, so i must've been doing it right; doing what was right for me.

    & in recent conversations i've discussed different types of scenes present in our games; it seems that the games that i am playing are not very explicit about types of scenes- what i am driving at is that the way a game is actually played varies as much as the way two painters use the same color of paint, and this stems from what they sit down at the table (they, the plural) attempting to gain for themselves.

    i don't know if games need be made so needlepoint specific to fill needs though, i mean, we pick up a game that takes us somewhere and then go from there ourselves... this is not a statement that "design" should go in any particular direction, by the way. i mean, i have a wonderful ongoing half-totemic game of Don't Rest Your Head which is just wonderful. Fred took us into the front hall, we looked around and started walking through walls. after a bit we come back to the rules and enjoy them for a while, too; but the free-formy part (which has a whole bunch of unspoken agreements on how we players interact & so on) and the mechanical engine do very different things for me, and i'm sure the same is true for my co-player.

    so yes, i think we take games where we think they want us to go; this is not where the writer intended but where we wanted to go in picking up the book.


    b)
    i think that so much of this question is about space.
    in the world, we are crowded by technologies to the point that our human interactions are pushed out to the edges. therefore less community, less connection, less happiness.
    we have not yet hit the point in game design or the indie games renaissance where we are crowded; we simply have options.
    i read a magazine article a decade ago about how richard garfield wanted to have there be so many magic cards (for the collectible card game) that no one player could keep track of them all. perhaps that one game could be in danger of this, but for the most part, i feel very blessed to be present in this community & renaissance, and have what i see as a very navigable number of options in the way of games, and very clear directions in which to grow in our design.

    when walking to the food co-op the other night, i stopped under a series of bare trees and thought about how lucky i am to have these ideas for games that will help people, actually help people communicate and grow stronger together. it is only through the breadth of games available that i am able to study, and to understand. without all this, i would still be selecting spells from lists based on how much damage they did.

    but speaking about technology- we have gone too far, and we are totally fucked.

    c)
    i would agree that the "Amish" are the people who don't know that they are playing it wrong, and are loving it; though i don't think it's really applicable, nor useful.


    thanks for providing those quotes and some lovely thoughtfulness after dinner.
    (i had mashed potatoes with carrots and onions, the rice and beans aren't ready yet. oop, better go take those off, in fact.)

    \n Anyway, to drag the thread back to gaming:

    I take issue with the idea of there being a "right" or "wrong" way to use a game, just like there isn't a right or wrong way to read a book. What I write, game or story, I put what I created out into the world, and at that point it isn't mine anymore (except in the sense that I get credit for it, and money, if there's any to be had). It belongs to whoever picks it up. They have their own experiences with it, and that's great.

    As to the OP's question, I think it's important to note that firstly, happiness is overrated in American culture - Americans tend to desire "happiness," which is amorphous and elusive, while other cultures tend to work for "satisfaction" or "importance" or "the happiness of my loved ones," all more achievable goals - and secondly, we don't know how these researchers defined happiness in their study.

    So, my answers to your questions:

    Firstly, I think that the abundance of games out there is absolutely a good thing, which has expanded and deepened our gaming experience. The fact that I can play D&D when I want a linear, hack 'n slash experience of fantasy-heroism-flavored wargaming, dip into White Wolf's games when I'm interested in an equally linear but more narratively complex storytelling experience, mess around with Dogs in the Vineyard or Don't Rest Your Head when what I'm looking for is a linear but ideologically and thematically focused game, and then branch out to the edgier stuff, like In A Wicked Age, for nonlinear, non-character-centric, shared storytelling (or, hell, even Polaris, which I didn't like much) means that I have the right game for the right mood no matter what.

    Secondly, I think the equivalent of the Amish in the roleplaying community are the people who cut themselves off from the marketplace of game ideas. For good or ill, they have picked the play philosophy that works for them, and they experience nothing but that. Are they happier than those of us who continue to wrangle with what game we want to play tonight? Maybe they are. They certainly spend less money on books! It's important to note, though, that there is no difference in this regard from someone who solely plays D&D and someone who only plays Polaris or Burning Wheel. They're both Gamish.
