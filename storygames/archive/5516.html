    \n Found via the blog of comics writer Warren Ellis, an essay by an English Literature Ph. D. by the name of Alan Kirby on the death of post-modernism and the identification of what he calls "pseudo-modernism."

    If you are willing to take the time to read some hifalutin language, you'll find that identifiers for pseudo-modernism wrap nicely around RPGs, story games, and jeepform.

    Some bits to whet your appetite.

    "Postmodernism conceived of contemporary culture as a spectacle before which the individual sat powerless, and within which questions of the real were problematised. It therefore emphasised the television or the cinema screen. Its successor, which I will call pseudo-modernism, makes the individual\u2019s action the necessary condition of the cultural product."

    "Pseudo-modernism also encompasses contemporary news programmes, whose content increasingly consists of emails or text messages sent in commenting on the news items. The terminology of \u2018interactivity\u2019 is equally inappropriate here, since there is no exchange: instead, the viewer or listener enters \u2013 writes a segment of the programme \u2013 then departs, returning to a passive role."

    Something to think about.

    \n That's a very thought-provoking article. It's particularly interesting to me that he sees only the superficial and banal in architectures of creative collaboration. Twitter and Dancing with the Stars, and My Life with Master are the same. Yes, I think we're often superficial (sex and death) in our use of these architectures (witness the Dreamation Munchausen game), but he seems to suggest that this superficiality is fundamental. And I don't think it is. What he's seeing is broken architectures (America's Got Talent) and novice usage. The point of narrativist gaming, and architectures of creative collaboration is to have a conversation about the human endeavor. And collectively we're getting better at it. Our games are getting better. Online communities are blowing the whistle on corruption. And Twitter is trending up.

    Paul

    \n Paul,

    I also think its important, when dealing with that articles observations, to think about level of interaction. His focus on shallowness is because he focuses on shallow interactions (dial in a number and interact with pop star!). This is quite different than, say, the level of interactions in a MLWM game where you start actually looking at our broken relationships and the cycles of bullshit we put each other through. One is a shallow, phatic clicking in which we've reclaimed interaction with our media by clicking on it, the other is actually more like a return to a pre-post modern mode of communication in which we actually are the producers.

    Plus, the technology curve thing is vastly important. Using the whole global network to say "I'm on the bus" is a different beast than using some lumps of plastic and pencils to say "I believe that loyalty always trumps justice, even when it shouldn't, because we cling to much to what we know and the non-transitive bonds of blood and sex always outweigh those of idealism."

    \n Some folks at Midnight's Lair are speculating about the future of indie RPG publishing. An interesting example of how the community is perceived. I certainly agree that we are at (or are beginning to pass) a crucial transition point, but I'm not sure their predictions seem that likely, at least from my perspective.

    Our design process will become more secretive? I think that's already happened for most folks with a game in print, with few exceptions. Second time publishers definitely seem to be keeping their cards close to their chest, instead of designing in the open in front of God and everyone. I think this is a shame, actually, but I'm not sure much can be done about it. Will indie designers start writing supplements for mainstream games? I think this one is just off, honestly. I think you'll see more supplements from within the indie scene, like Dictionary of Mu and Journeying West. But it's hard to imagine folks writing for White Wolf and such (based on the experiences of Jared and others). Indie roleplaying already lives in a post-freelancing world, at least when it comes to writing. More group projects? Certainly. Slower publication with more time spent playtesting and polishing? Man, I certainly hope so.
