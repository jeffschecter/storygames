\n My supervillain snafu game positions the player characters such that they may never meet. They inhabit the same world by virtue of pursuing goals directed at the same set of NPC Guest Stars, with the Guest Stars acting as a small degree of glue to unify the fiction. The result is more like reading one issue apiece of Captain America, Iron Man, The Hulk and Thor rather than reading four issues of the Avengers. Mostly, I think this works out fine. But could another solution be better? After playing at Camp Nerdly, Keith had this to say (from this thread):\n
Posted By: BlazmoIntoWoweeThat was definitely fun. One thing I particularly enjoyed that I was wondering if you could codify is how elements of each of our stories popped up as background in the other villains' stories, like when King Orzomon was there when Captain Metropolis showed up to give Coldblood the Nobel Peace Prize. I think it would keep the game from being four unrelated stories.
It's definitely cool when that happens! I've run into a lot of downsides in trying to make it happen, though. I either give people instructions that are easily lost and forgotten among the rules, or I give rules that warp the game's incentives away from the character and story-telling stuff as it currently stands.

Hmm, though it might be okay for a GM to earn a token for bringing another villain into the scene when they're GMing...

Villains who get brought into each other's scenes get more opportunity to earn Fan Points, though, and I don't want a popularity spiral...

Perhaps you get a token only for bringing in the character of the player to your left. That'll be the character who's been offscreen longest at that point. It'd often be hard to do; certainly much harder than just bringing in whichever villain has already established a connection to the scene's Guest Star. So I'm not sure about that balance -- it might be just right, or it might not be enough to bother.

    \n Rewarding for reincorporation is cool. What about rewarding for "pre-incorporation?" Screenplay writers call it "telegraphing." You set something up to be reused later. Could you use the telegraph technique to foreshadow the inclusion of inter-story characters and elements? For example:\n
      \n
    1. Fun later: You mark a character/theme/location for pre-incorporation by spending a token. You can trigger its effect in another scene (narrative control, for example).
      2. \n
    2. Ask for more: You mark a character/theme/location for pre-incorporation and earn a token. You give that marked asset to another player to trigger its effect in another scene.
      3. \n
    3. Consequences: You mark a "conflict" for pre-incorporation. Whoever manifests the consequence of the conflict in another scene earns a token.

    \n Graham, I played a quick game of Polaris in which my group deliberately chose to tie the stories together. We chose a shared fate/destiny/whatever it's called, we shared some NPCs (my Full Moon is your New Moon, etc.), and we appeared in each other's scenes a bit. We did this because we thought it'd be fun, but I think the shared concerns of (a) the Mistake and (b) the coming of the end of Polaris made it seem natural. There also didn't seem to be any reason not to do this.

    In early versions of Within My Clutches, I attempted to give the villains an overlapping concern: Captain Universe, hero of heroes, who would eventually foil all their plans if no one stopped him/her/it. I eventually abandoned the concept because it felt tacked on. There just wasn't a natural way to inject Captain Universe into what the villains were already doing.

    One of the core features of WMC characters is that they're proactive, not reactive. Each scene is based on a goal, and players' imaginations consistently take them to weird new settings for new goals. Going from a cubicle in scene 1 to an asteroid in scene 2 has turned out to be a frequent part of the fun. So unity of fictional concerns isn't really on the table. Instead what I have is overlap -- the NPC whose wealth I want to acquire is the same NPC whose respect you want to win.

    Having said all that, posing the occasional challenge to the players at any given moment to integrate disparate threads might be fun, as per Contenders. Hmm...

    \n Sebastian, my experience with that sort of thing in Land of Nodd, Universalis, and Annalise is that tracking becomes an issue. Writing stuff down for later and then remembering what it is and noticing the right spot to use it has often wound up at odds with in-the-moment roleplay (as player, GM, or even audience).

    I do like the idea, and in an earlier draft I had each player tracking the Distractions their GMing had produced for each other player, with these Distractions to be played (brought into a scene) as chosen. And you got points for doing that. If the game was all about earning such points (most points wins, or something), it might have worked. But in a story-telling game where points are good but enjoying the fiction is better, it just wound up being a nuisance. "Oh, wait, was I supposed to play that thing then? What was it again?" Your idea of investing specifically in stuff that you decide you want to reincorporate would be an improvement, but there's still the issue of tracking and whether anyone would bother.

    NPC reincorporation is already hardwired, location diversity is part of the fun... but themes? That's interesting. I like the sound of that, but I'm having trouble imagining it. Can you throw a hypothetical at me? A moment of play, the marking of its theme, and then later, how it's invoked and brought into a new scene?

    \n Here's a virgin idea, totally provoked. For the purposes of bouncing.

    A deck of themes the players choose at the start of the session. Jamie wants to see Betrayal, so writes that down on an index card. Alison is interested in the anxiety of her character, so she writes Insecurity on another card. Other players add "Corruption," "Consumption," etc. (Things like that. Apocalypse World and My Life with Master have gorgeous little theme words scattered through the text. Perhaps the game would offer these words in context, or just provide a list, or leave it up to the crew).

    Fun later, part 1:
    It's Jamie's turn. Her hero is in trouble. A terrible creature threatens her home town. She's going to lose a friend so she decides to introduce one of the themes on the table... She spends a token and picks Corruption. She helps the GM narrate how the creature forces her to choose another victim instead. Jamie keeps the card in front of her with a token on top.

    Ask for more, part 1:
    It's Alison's turn. Her hero is about to deliver a rising speech at a revolutionary rally. Some people want the character to mess things up and she's having a hard time focusing on what's important. Particularly, she is struggling with her own self worth. As she steps out to deliver the speech, she decides to introduce one of the themes, Insecurity. She and the GM narrate how her anxiety gets the better of her. She receives a token and hands the card to Jamie. "Hey Jamie, I'd like you to be in charge of Insecurity for a while," she says.

    Fun later, part 2:
    It's still Alison's turn, but Jamie decides that she'd like to reintroduce the Corruption theme. She takes the token from the theme for herself and places the Corruption card back in the pile(?). "I have a cool idea... I'd like to tie this in with your anxiety." Jamie and Alison narrate how a familiar man approaches, voicing wise, cutting truths. She is momentarily turned from the cause, and abandons the rally without delivering the speech.

    Ask for more, part 2:
    In another player's turn, the villain is about to win. There are civilians involved and death traps. Jamie jumps in. "I'd like to use the Insecurity theme please. I'd like to suggest that the villain try to recruit the hero. He needs a friend." Jamie and the GM narrate.

    \n Remember Tomorrow.

    There are protagonists, and then there are cliques/gangs/corps/whatever. The game term is Factions.

    The only way to have your protagonist have a conflict with a Faction is if another player takes ownership of that Faction and throws it at you. It makes the players take interest, but not necessarily the player characters...

    Also, every time your protagonist makes a "deal" with a Faction, that deal necessarily puts you at odds with another protagonist or their interest.
    "Yes, we will get for you this plutonium shipment, for the regular fee... but the cops are sniffing around. It's harder for me these days. You will also get me the location of... / You will also help us to kidnap... / You will use your contacts to publicly denounce ... " Which will inter-mix, but might at times feel forced in your situation.

    Not that you should patch this on whole, but it might give some fuel.

    + Also, since we're talking card tricks earlier up in this thread...

    Have a few note cards with player character names written on one side. Put them face down on the table. Shuffle. When necessary, write on the blank side (the part that's facing up) write down a McGuffin, a thing that is needed for a player character's plot. Then have someone _else_ write down the obstacle.

    When someone has to go after that McGuffin, they have to get it from [player character] or through that character's aid. Maybe that's too forced, and I don't know how it might work in your economy, but it could be fun to play out at the table.

    \n I admit I was still a bit unclear as to what we were getting GMing tokens for and why being able to buy more cards was good. I also don't recall what we were getting villain tokens for, beyond a vague "entertaining the table." I certainly don't remember what we could spend the villain tokens on, because I only ever got a few, Captain Fahrenheit being the Charlie Brown of supervillains.

    Maybe instead of squeezing the "How to GM" rules into a corner of the sheet, you have a half-sheet or card that has them on it.     \n In While The World Ends the four players play four separate stories, each with it's own main character. The other players' characters appear as NPCs in their stories. And having them in a scene grants bonuses. The bonus is not dependent on the attitude between the characters. They could be enemies, but the bonus is still given. This is an incentive for the player to involve the other players, and on the story level it makes the at first seemingly unrelated stories tie together and intersect.

    So, basically what Polaris does when you put your character as a moon or mistake on another players char sheet, but with mechanical incentives.

    \nSebastian, ah, I get it. Think it up, bust it out, designate it for subsequent use, and then reintroduce it (or call for someone else to) when you want. Your examples with the call accompanied by a fictional suggestion seem fair -- put the burden for "how do we work that into this scene?" on the person who wants to invoke it. I think your examples also show a downside, that it's hard to do this gracefully. I imagine a whole lot of momentum and focus lost and derailed. Scenes in Within My Clutches are supposed to be relatively short and to the point. There's definitely room for showing us something about your character, but I think reconciling character vision with current objectives and a call for a moment that evokes a theme is too taxing. I'd rather do two well than three poorly.

    Harlequin, good call on Remember Tomorrow. I think the overlapping Guest Stars in Within My Clutches (your guy wants Captain Metropolis's love, while my guy wants her wealth) achieve about what Factions do in terms of bringing the world together a wee bit. When I played Remember Tomorrow, it didn't feel particularly unified to me. Maybe that's fine! Just wondering if I could/should do better.

    Making player characters go through each other to pursue their ends would be fun, but Within My Clutches requires that the spotlight character must be able to achieve their Goal. Thus, on my turn, if I go to you to borrow your laser cannon, me not getting it, and it mattering that I didn't get it is not an option. So now you must invent some reason why you want to give me this laser cannon (which maybe you hadn't planned on owning). This doesn't seem like the best use of scene time to me... though maybe it could be cool as scene framing:

    "What do you have with you when the scene begins, and which other character did you get it from?" Then you pick another character and brainstorm for a bit about what could have gone down. This refreshes that other character in the minds of the group and establishes some background that could be referenced later in play.

    That'd be nice if everyone could do it quickly. Racking brains on that one would be bad for play, methinks. Maybe an optional rule...

    Wilhelm, I don't find it hard to simply throw protagonists into each other's scenes. As you say, just provide a bonus. That's not really what I'm looking for here, though. In a given scene, the player of that scene's protagonist is the star and runs the show. This is their chance to show us their character and pursue that character's goals, and the GM's number one responsibility is to help with those. Inserting another player character into such a scene is risky! That player needs to know this is not their spotlight, and that they have the same task as the GM, to support the current star. I could simply ask people to keep that in mind, but if they're tired of waiting for their turn, or forget the rule, or just aren't used to switching gears that way, it's gonna blow up. I'd rather err on the side of keeping things simple.
