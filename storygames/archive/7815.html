\n I'm tired of the traditional "GM brings the content to the 4e game and the players explore it." So I'm giving up the Content Authority.
\n
\nHere's my experimental 4e Mountain Witch hack. I'd enjoy any suggestions on improvements or words of caution.
\n
\n4e: Tandun of the Underdark
\n
\nDeep in the underdark Tandun, an ancient red dragon, lurks.
\nEach player gives a reason why they want to go kill Tandun.
\n
\nEach session all players start with 2 White Tokens.
\nA player may spend a White Token by:
\n
\n1. Introducing an element relating to their character\u2019s reason to kill Tandun. All players gain 100XP.
\n2. Tie in an existing element to their character\u2019s reason to kill Tandun. All players gain 100XP.
\n3. Use as an Action Point in combat. All Players receive 0XP.
\n
\nEach session, a bowl of Red Tokens equal to the number of players is placed on the table.
\nA player may pull a Red Token from the bowl and give it to another player when they feel the other player:
\n1. Introduced an element they thought was really cool
\n2. Roleplayed in a really cool way.
\n3. Made a remarkable strategic choice in battle.
\n4. Rolled an amazing hit at just the right moment.
\n
\nA player may spend a Red Token by:
\n1. Adding +2d8 (take highest) to any dice roll. All Players receive 0XP.
\n2. Giving their character a secondary reason to be in the group and in the underdark. All players receive 200 XP.
\n3. Introducing an element related to the character\u2019s secondary reason for being in this group or exploring the Underdark. All players receive 200XP.
\n4. Introducing an element or information that creates a possible conflict with two or more character\u2019s reason to kill Tandun. All players gain 500XP. Subject to negotiation. If rejected by any player, then all players receive 100XP, and such factual elements no longer an option to be introduced.
\n
\nThe GM frames scenes and presents combat challenges to the players. GM should take elements introduced by players and run with them.

    \n Let me talk about the rules I'm using in the D&D mini-campaign I'm running. It has a lot of moving parts, and not everything is clicking, but there may be some stuff you can borrow and hack to your liking.

    First, the centerpiece:\n
    Quests

    Players announce quests by combining a purpose and a goal, where the purpose is the verb phrase describing what the characters need to do and the goal is the noun-phrase identifying the target or objective of the quest.

    The goal of a major quest is an element from the Uerlan map. The goal of a minor quest is anything else.

    Write the resulting phrase down on an index card in the format [Purpose] {Goal}. So for example:

    Major Quest: [Discover] {where the Lost God of Humanity is imprisoned}.
    Major Quest: [Save]\u2026 {the Children of the Sun} \u2026[from the yuan-ti].
    Minor Quest: [Convince]\u2026 {my family} \u2026[that I\u2019m worthy of their respect].

    There can be up to two major quests and N minor quests in play at any given time, where N is the number of players at the table.

    When a quest is declared, specify the level of the quest (from 1 to 10 on the heroic tier). Multiply the level of the quest by three for a major a quest and one for a minor quest. This is the sum of the levels of the encounters that must be completed for the quest to be fulfilled. So a Level 1 minor quest can be fulfilled with a single Level 1 encounter, while a Level 5 major quest needs 15 encounter levels dedicated to it. So after the Level 1 encounter, 14 encounter levels remain in the quest, which could be completed with a level 4 encounter, two level 3 encounters, and two level 2 encounters, for example, or any other combination of encounters totaling 14.

    To add uncertainty, you may optionally roll 1d4+1 to determine the level multiplier for any given quest instead of using a straight \u201c3\u201d.

    When a minor quest is fulfilled, players split xp equal to the standard xp value for a monster of the quest\u2019s level. When a major quest is fulfilled, each player receives the standard xp value for a monster of the quest\u2019s level. So, for fulfilling a 3rd level minor quest, five players split 150 xp, gaining 30 xp each. For fulfilling a 3rd level major quest, which would require 15 levels worth of encounters dedicated to it, five players each gain 150 xp each.


















    \nWhen I described this system to the players, one guy said, "Oh, so you're Inspectre-izing D&D." And I said yeah.

    Notice that's what nice about this is that invites players to provide rationalizations for why a particular encounter helps fulfill Quest A versus Quest B. To enable this game-mechanically, I've added SOTC-like "declarations" to the game. So, for example, in a fight against a kraken, one player declared that kraken horn was known for its medicinal value, in aid of another player's quest to find medicine for his sick father. Skill challenges, similarly, can be sick with player authoring.\n
    Declarations and Inquiries

    As a minor action, you can make a skill roll to make a declaration about some element of the encounter or the game-world more generally. Your declaration should specify the skill you\u2019re using and the fact you\u2019re stipulating. The DM decides if the declaration is plausible or interesting. If it\u2019s both, the DC of the check is easy; if it\u2019s one or the other, the DC is standard; if it\u2019s neither, the DC is hard.

    On a successful roll, the player\u2019s declaration is taken to be true; if it fails, the GM draws a card from the Deck of Many Things and uses it to determine the real truth.

    Alternately, once per encounter, you can take a free action to make an inquiry about how some element of the encounter or game-world relates to one of the quests the characters are pursuing. In this case, you merely specify the skill you\u2019re using to explore, investigate, or otherwise engage with something in the encounter. The DC is always standard. On a success, draw a card from the Deck of Many Things and read it as saying something about the connection between the thing on the map/in the encounter and the object or pursuit of the quest. On a failure, let the GM read the card.

    A successful declaration or inquiry may result in a new game-element being added to the Uerlan map, to a new fact being added to an existing game-element, or merely to a situational bonus being applied to certain actions during the current encounter.







    \nIf you're curious about the references to the "Deck of Many Things" above, let me explain. I have a deck of cards from the Game Mastery line that's been designed like the old Deck of Many Things from 1st and 2nd edition D&D. I use it as a tarot deck by assigning each card one of the gods from the 4th edition PHB and their associated domains. So you can draw a card and if it's "Balance" for example, that stands for Bahamut and his domains of justice, nobility, honor, and protection. Depending on the situation, that card can be interpreted for its significance to current circumstances and used to add something to the game.

    I recommend adopting some kind of "oracular" technique: it acts as a prompt to creativity, and serves as a little bit of a constraint on going too far, since others can always demand that you connect what you're saying to the card you drew.

    \n Rust, forgive me if I'm presumptuous, but it seems to me that you need to rethink your approach. This whole "theme" thing seems to be something your group is not interested in, and trying to artificially remove it from the game just to prove a point seems like it'll hurt the game more than help.

    As an alternative, have you researched the theory behind challenge-based fantasy adventure gaming? Have you tried it? The fourth edition D&D has not been written with this in mind, but it seems that you're not playing it by the book anyway, so it should be easy to go to this direction, instead. I'm asking because it seems that your group is looking for a) non-thematic roleplaying and b) the strengths that the tabletop form has over MMORPGs. It just so happens that such play does exist; it's not necessary to work with theme in the literary sense to make rpgs compete with computers.

    The tricky part of the matter, though, is that there really isn't very good sources for this style of roleplaying. <<a href="http://isabout.wordpress.com/2007/11/01/challenge-based-adventuring/">I wrote something about my own understanding last year, but it's not like I have all the answers lined up there. Still, perhaps something along those lines could work for you? Instead of struggling with theme, start to think of the game content as a strategic field shared by the players and held together by their common investment in the fictional situation. What you tell about your group and their disinterest in story creation sounds to me like they might be interested in something like this.

    \nI recently had a fairly similar experience.
    \n
    \nMy group of players has been gaming together for roughly 20 years. We have an every-other-Thursday game and a once-a-month-Saturday game. Mostly the same players in both groups but a bit of variation due to scheduling. Each group plays in a separate campaign. They have virtually no interest in any sort of player-motivated gaming. They seem to be fully content to follow whatever plot or setting that is laid out for them (within a fairly narrow, generic fantasy genre), and have not shown any desire to be pro-active.
    \n
    \nIt was driving me CRAZY. Here I was developing an interest in story-games such as Spirit of the Century, and My Life with Master, but I knew my players would not be on-board for that kind of game.
    \n
    \nAs a solution, I had the bright idea of trying a game of Ars Magica (which is all about pro-active, player-driven gaming, but is still fairly traditional is it's mechanics). It was a pretty much an abject failure. Whenever I prompted them for what their plans were, or what they wanted to do with their Magi, I was greeted with blank stares and uncomfortable silences. We played three sessions or so and then I killed it.
    \n
    \nI then realized that I wanted something different from my gaming than what they wanted, and there wasn't much I could do to reconcile it.
    \n
    \nSo I killed the Thursday group.
    \n
    \nI told them that I needed to take a break from GMing, and that I was going to no longer participate in the Thursday group but I would still GM the once-a-month-Saturday game (mainly for social reasons).
    \n
    \nI then grabbed the two players who were interested in trying something new, hooked up with some like-minded gamers, and have been having a blast playing My Life with Master, Dogs in the Vineyard, and other new-fangled hippy games.
    \n
    \nIt's one of the better choices I've made for my hobby time.
    \n
    \n-Pete

    \n You know, one thing I might suggest:

    Instead of trying to change the game that is ongoing (and I don't know if you're trying to do that), start a new game, with different expectations, at a different time, and see who shows up. Outline clearly what you're going to play, and that people can try it and leave after the first session if they don't like it.

    This could be good:

    * You'll still be running or playing in the regular game, so no one will feel like their gaming is being "threatened".

    * Those who are interested in something else might then be more receptive to it.

    * Those who are not interested probably won't show up, or will leave, allowing you to discover who's on the same page as you are.

    \n I've been in the same situation myself recently, toying with shared content authority and player-led stories with a group who are big into GM-plot, exploration, and immersion ... and it failed as badly for me as it is clearly doing for you. They just didn't want this sort of fun, and by trying to impose it I removed all the things both they and I liked about our regular games and stuffed it all up. Luckily I realized in time that I was doing the wrong thing and stopped, and the fun came back.

    Does that mean I had to just give up on the hippie gaming? Not at all. I found my players were quite happy to play that sort of game ... just so long as it wasn't part of our regular game. One-offs, playtests, 3-session mini arcs in between chapters of larger games, IAWA on a weekend, that was all fine (though complicated by scheduling) and went down well, with the main weekly campaign going on as a GM-led GURPS campaign.

    It sounds to me like your players know what they want from this bi-monthly game. They want a series of challenging but low-investment combat encounters and puzzles, lots of treasure, XP, and a GM-led plot that tells them why they are there, doing what they are doing with good strong fantasy tropes (save the city, kill the dragon, take it's stuff). They want a GM to challenge them, and provide enough story that it seems to hang together as much as any MMO does. My advice is, give them that. You seem good at the encounters, and to enjoy the combat, so give them that, it's what they are asking for. You don't need to worry much about theme and story (and pre-preparation effort) because they aren't asking for a lot of that, just enough so it makes sense why they are there. (This is of course why they won't engage with creating content, because it's way down their priority list).

    As Citizen Arcane says, the only other real option is to leave the group (or stop GM'ing) but my instinct says you can keep going *and* play hippie games like I have been doing ... i.e. have your cake and eat it too

    \n Thank you all for all the replies.
    \n
    \nI think a strong case as been made that bribery with XP or Action tokens will not get theme based, participatory play out of players who have no interest in it.
    \n
    \nMy original goal, of minimizing GM content authority so they can spend more time on the tactical fighty fun of 4e, is well served by this conclusion.
    \n
    \nHere's my next rules solution.
    \n
    \nIn Medias Res [4e] mod
    \n
    \nGoals of the mechanic: To maximize the Fighty combaty fun of 4e, with minimal delay caused by "rationalizing the situation" or "needing a GM to make a story to explain the situation." Very slight opportunity for players to explore theme or character, and it is optional.
    \n
    \nStep 1 (preparation)
    \nGM preps a scene for combat. It includes, map (to be drawn on battlemat), a monster challenge, environmental conditions, scripted events (i.e., events that happen upon certain contingencies). These scripted events include: environmental changes, monster strategy, etc.... They can be contingent or just happen at a certain time. (E.g., On round 3 the left wall collapses, if half the minions are dead the rest of them run away, etc...).
    \n
    \nGM comes up with very vague reasons why the Player Characters have encountered the scene including character's reasons why they want to fight.
    \n
    \nStep 2 ( In Medias Res)
    \nGM frames the scene. Characters are described in a situation moments before the fighting starts. GM announces reasons why the characters are here. GM should not offer complex, intricate backstory for the characters reasons to be here. It should be vague. (I.e., You found a map about a lost sword. You're a fighter, so assuming you like swords, you came here to kill and plunder for the sword). GM has the option to include critical information about the creature they are about to fight or the environmental conditions they face. ( E.g., You found a journal in a cave on the way here, which reports that the Ogres in this region fight with poisoned blades).
    \n
    \nStep 3 (Player's Opportunity to Contribute Backstory)
    \nPlayers then each have an opportunity to Block the GM's input.
    \nThey can change the backstory that explains why their character is here only. They cannot change their character's fundamental situation as presented by the GM. (Possibly the specific tactical position could be set as stake of a Skill Challenge check or Skill Check-- but I don't want to open the Stakes can of worms here). They cannot say, "My character would never go underground into a dungeon, he's claustrophobic!" They are not allowed to block any the scene's core elements. (i.e., Who, what and where). Slight modification might make sense. (E.g., My thief type guy would have hid in the shadows and been over on this side of the wall, is valid, with a successful associated skill check).
    \n
    \nPlayers should be rewarded with XP or action tokens for contributing Content which expands, clarifies or completely replaces the GM's backstory, only if the group really values this type of colour and theme based content.
    \n
    \nStep 4 (Fight!)
    \nRoll initiatives and get your game on.
    \n
    \nStep 5 (GM plans to plan)
    \nGM takes notes of any content contributed by players, and attempts to incorporate it in the next monster challenge.
