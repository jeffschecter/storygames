\n The basic example of play (at least how I write them) gives an example of one bit of instruction that was just explained. So you have a flow of main text punctuated by examples. I'm talking about the opposite of that, an example of play as the main flow of text, punctuated by some elaboration or page reference to explain what just happened in the main text.

One thing I like about a long-form example of play is that you can callback to previous events in the same running example. You can watch players start a plan of action and adjust those plans as the game continues.

What is something that you look for in a long example of play?

    \n My reply will speak more to typesetting techniques.

    I like it to be a dialog, primarily--that's what's happening at the table.
    When someone declares something that engages the mechanics, I like them below the declaration, on their own line, indented by as much as the dialog (excluding the dialog person's name).
    I also like for mechanics to be in a different font than narration--italics, or maybe sans serif (if the main body font(s) are serif, as they should be in print).
    Finally, page reference to rules engaged on mechanics line should not interfere with the flow of the text, if at all possible.
    Example:
    GM: So now what?
    P1: I hit it with my axe.
    \u2002\u2002\u2002\u2002P1 rolls a d20 for a result of 13, adds +3 for his Strength bonus, and states the total to the GM. (See Rolling To Hit, p.69.)
    \u2002\u2002\u2002\u2002I rolled a 16.
    \u2002\u2002\u2002\u2002The GM compares the 16 to the monster's Armor Class rating, which is a 10.
    GM: That's a solid hit on the grub. Let me check for hit location.
    \u2002\u2002\u2002\u2002The GM rolls d20 for a result of 12, consults the Hit Location Chart, and sees that it's a torso hit, which has no Damage Bonus.
    \u2002\u2002\u2002\u2002Square in its muscled guts; no bonus. Roll your damage with your sword.
    \u2002\u2002\u2002\u2002P1 rolls a d8 for a result of 4 and adds +1 for the sword's magical bonus. (See Weapon Statistics, p. 181.)
    P1: 5 Damage--the grub is slashed and some of its intestines spill out.
    ...
    HTH;
    David

    \n I find myself often being disappointed when examples of play are just invented out of the designer's head, because usually they are super lame and lack the life that a real game has when you actually have multiple players with different ideas. The example of play shouldn't come from some crazy idealized world where everything is perfect and wonderful. Instead, it's WAY better, I think, to base it on some actual transcripts of actual play of your game, so you're not promising things that your game hasn't actually delivered at the table. Sure, definitely you should edit and adjust the transcript show it demonstrates what you want, maybe even merging a bunch of different games together, so you have a super loaded example, but I really think making it real is super important.

    \n An actual-play example needs to be easy to read and engagingly written. It needs to read naturally or it'll feel fake and staged which isn't captivating. It should feel like eavesdropping on an exciting playtest. You're working against the drag of 30 years of bad examples of play here, though, so you have to do something that makes the example of play feel essential, not just clarifying. Otherwise I am liable to gloss over it out of habit (not by choice).

    I'm willing to explore outside of David's specific example of typesetting, but I agree, loudly, that the physical presentation of the text is vital to the quality of the example of play.

    I might also argue that a modern example of play should also be available as a podcast, no longer than 12 minutes, on the game's website. Said podcast should feature edited-in descriptions of what is happening with the dice and why, like the play-by-play of a poker game aimed at newcomers. I might argue that, I'm not sure yet.

    \n Brevity. Long examples are worth it only if they communicate something that a shorter one cannot.

    If you refer to both characters and players by name, and understanding the example requires being able to remember who plays whom, you will lose me.

    HQ2 is a great example of what NOT to do: examples are either too short or too long, the layout doesn't help any, names are easily confused, and they aren't discussed sensibly. I want to read text analysing the options present in the examples.

    \n Regarding player v. character names: Some games require it to be clear, unfortunately. My trick is pretty old hat: Use player and character names A__, B__, C__, etc, and match them to the PC+player. make the PC name obviously fictional and the player name common as rain. So,
    * Amy play Adharianna
    * Bob play Beofford
    * Cary play Corrialina
    * Don plays Durkon
    (Obviously, the PC names should be appropriate to the game genre, if any; I picked the above for their outlandish fantasy vibes.)

    Note the short versus long names, too, for additional distinction.
