\n Fantasy Flight's big box board game Android has made a bit of splash with Story Gamers. Let's talk about it.

This game has an unusually intense focus on the stories of the individual characters. Its concept reads like a Story Game pitch: a number of investigators try to sort out their complicated private lives and preserve their relationships whilst striving to solve the crime fo the (21st) century. Yet it's unabashedly boardgamey. Is this a board game calling out to be RPG-ified, or is it just Fantasy Flight's desperate call for help?

    \n "Call for help" is just my little joke. Well, it's mostly a joke. I'll elaborate.

    Using good board game elements to enrich story game RPG play is something I've been thinking about a lot lately. Android's pretty interesting in this regard, because it sets up a very story game situation using entirely board game elements. When I play Android, I've got a character with a strong person reason to solve the mystery. I've got some NPC contacts all of whom I want something from; all of whom want something from me. Many of my game play choices involve balancing these pressures against solving the mystery.

    And then Android has these bits that aren't Story Gamey at all. For example, there's this conspiracy map where we figure out who's tied to the Big Secret. Even though we map out the conspiracy, there's no attempt or desire to make a story of it. We don't try to explain WHY the mining league is tied to the conspiracy. We just know that I get 4 more victory points as a result.

    My opinion of Android is that these two faces of the same game are in conflict. This isn't because they're incompatible, but because of how they're executed in Android. Even though my character has (in theory) a fully realized story played out in their plot cards, just keeping up with that story while balancing the myriad other parts of the game creates a conflict in my experience of play. On top of that, everyone else has their own story, and part of what I do is supply adversity for their stories. But I do this, without having the time and ability to track their stories, or even my input into them, in any but the most peripheral way.

    My take is that Android is a really damn good board game, but that it also tries to be more than a board game and creates some incoherence as a result.

    Which leads me to wonder what a board game with similar goals but without that incoherence might look like. And I'm really hoping Eero will comment on that vis-a-vis Zombie Cinema.

    \n I find no incoherence in Android, Tony - or at least not creative agenda incoherence, let's say; I'm on record with my comments on the deep problems FFG style extreme color ("theme" as boardgamers call all this story fluff) games.

    The hole you pinpoint, the difference between the stuff your personal plot cards do and what the conspiracy puzzle does is exactly in the fact that the game is coherent in execution. Players are not trying to spin a story out of why the city hall wanted the miner dead or something like that - they don't care, they are focused on other things in the game. Compare this to some roleplaying game, which often give you exactly the same sort of creative constraints: in a rpg your detective might find out that the city hall is involved in the murder somehow, and on two different fronts to booth, and then you get to tell the other players why this is so. In the rpg this actually happens because the players are interested in what you say about it. In Android it doesn't, as the story doesn't affect the victory points.

    So why have so much story material in a boardgame? My current take is that it presents a secondary split-channel source of entertainment on top of the boardgame, essentially a semi-passive, lonely fun experience that gets semi-randomly assembled out of the cards and components the game is made of. Those players who care can get inspired by the color; often this is the majority of the players, as these games run rather slowly and you'll get bored easily if the color's doing nothing for you.

    Where this color-layer gets really ambitious is when the game manages to use the color elements to orient and structure the play experience. This is something that boardgamers tend to expect of all games, really - they'll accuse the game of having a "thin, pasted-on theme" if the game doesn't provide orientation in this manner. To take a really obvious example, consider Arkham Horror: that whole game is made of basically random draws where the only shot at meaning from player viewpoint is in how the random draws have been assorted into color-based structures. Thus, to pick a constant example from the game, the choice of where to set your ability slider at any given point is essentially meaningless because you can't know what you're going to draw as an event. Regardless, the player doesn't experience the choice as meaningless because the fiction is structuring the choice for him. Is my character more of a bookworm or a shooter type? Is he going to go to the library to pore over books, or to the bar to listen for hints? I'd call it false strategy, except that there's no strategy involved in these choices, just playing to the color. The point of the game is to set the story-generator spinning and see what sort of godless monster eats Arkham this time.

    Arkham Horror shows us the power of the color layer in boardgames at its extreme, but all popular gamer games use color to aid players in getting into the game, seeing strategic choices and making them while the player's understanding of the game grows. For example, Puerto Rico - the game is easier and more fun to learn because of the theme, and the theme aids the players in making choices even when they don't necessarily know what they're doing. It's very easy to remember where you need or don't need to put slaves, for example, thanks to the color. You also remember where they come from, where to put the extra ones, what happens if you don't have enough... the color elements orient and structure the play experience.

    There's a dark side to being theme-focused, too. I've played my share of the Descent: Journeys in the Dark boardgame and gotten largely burned out on it because it's ultimately quite boring and makes sacrifices in playability to maximize color. Setting up the dungeons and miniatures, for instance, isn't worthwhile enough for what the game offers. The same holds for titles such as War of the Ring, Memoir '44 and many other high-color games. In this regard I'm willing to accept that there is a sort of incoherency in these games: the attention to color sometimes detracts from playability for purely logistical reasons (there wasn't any room to make a real game of Arkham Horror after adding it chock-full of story, for instance), which means that the game gets boring after you've gotten used to it and start playing for real. These games tend to lack in staying power, in other words.

    --

    The above is a bit lengthy, but my message is clear: As I understand it, Android and other games like it are not to be interpreted as proto-rpgs like rpg hobbyists tend to do for any and all games that have a dash of story mixed in. It's just a game with the color element turned into an independent source of entertainment from its usual role as an orienteering tool.

    \n The history is simply that this distinction came to use in a situation where American designers were still making intricate simulation games when German (and European in general) designers were focusing on smarter rules and faster, more controlled play. The term was taken up (humorously) by hobbyists who first recognized the different design tendencies and preferred so called "German games" (which was the early nationality-based equivalent for "Euro game"). As for why such a distinction arose - it was likely because the Ameritrash games were and are designed specifically by gamers for gamers (they're essentially distant descendants of wargaming), while Eurogames have an origin in a rather tense marketplace that mixes mainstream market and gamer interests seamlessly.

    \n All this is to the best of my recollection.

    For the longest time, the term German Game was used to denote those games, generally coming out of Germany (though with some exceptions), as Eero described above: shorter play, less subject to the whims of fortune, less aggressively confrontational, more sedate theming, etc., etc., etc. Once someone realized that not all these games come from German designers, a quandary arose: if not "German Games", what then? The term "Eurogames" had been used (derisively, IIRC) by some in wargaming circles, and the name ended up sticking.

    At some point in the past (and please don't ask me to pinpoint it) BoardGameGeek's loudest voices tended to be those who espouse the Eurogame philosophy (and probably they still are). They tended to look down upon games originating in America. I always thought that was intended to signify the mass market games: Monopoly, Clue, Risk, Sorry, etc. But some folks preferred games with more "theme" , more swings of fate, more, um, adventurous theming, and more direct, in-your-face conflict, which tended to be produced by American companies (most notably, Fantasy Flight Games, but there are others). Well, these folks got pissed. A class battle arose, and those who preferred the latter style adopted the derisive term "Ameritrash", taking it as their own as a title of honor.

    \nWhat's really fun is when you get boundary challenging games like Memoir '44 where certain Ameritrashers decry because the random cards are just a game prop and don't actually simulate anything while other Ameritrashers (who are less rabid and more willing to be Euro) point out that the cards do effectively simulate a lot of Fog of War effects at a fairly abstract level.
    \n
    \nAnother key difference is that many (though certainly not all) Euro games can often be characterized as multiplayer solitaire where the interaction is more between the player and the game than the players and each other. The games are typically (though this is less universal than other features) less face-stabby, and when they are face-stabby tend to be so in an indirect way (like how in Puerto Rico you can chose a role for how it hoses another player, but you can't really hose that other player directly.
    \n

Ameri games tend to be much more confrontational and as such also much more prone to gamer diplomacy and negotiations around the table for how best to gang up on someone.

    \nEuro gamers will at times refer to Amerigamers as aggressive and hostile and Amerigamers will at time call Eurogamers passive-aggressive.
    \n
    \nThe difference in design philosophy can really be seen in the games themselves, where game-first (Euro designs) tend to have short punchy rules and play to completion in 2 hours or less. Theme-first (Ameri- designs) tend to have longer rules, lots more fiddly bits whose purpose is REALLY important to theme but often detracts from actual game play, and only recently have they started making them so they play in as little as 4 hours (6-8 to 10+++++++) being much more traditional for such designs.
