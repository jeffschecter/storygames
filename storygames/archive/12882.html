\n So Matt said:\n
So, here's how I see it.


There are a lot of good things that have happened over the last 10 years. As an ever-changing group of people, we've changed a lot about how people perceive games and game design. This has been a positive thing on the whole, and we're all very pleased with having done it. Yay us.

For example, we "democratized" design, which is to say we made design something that any schmo could do, and that also chipped away at the notion that companies and their employees should be giving us fun designs. And, that we should also elevate the status of those people for having done so.

However, there is ALWAYS a tradeoff.

Other things happened in doing so, too.

For one, we created an environment that cherished particular values, and either criticized or felt threated by others. I believe this results in a blinding effect, and probably also an environment where challenging certain things is a no-no.

For example, we cherish innovation. We also cherish the craftsmanship in individuals creating interesting looking publications.

We do not cherish, say, design that does not innovate in the interest of appealing to a wider audience of gamers ("trad" gamers, or whatever you wish to call it -- you get the point). We don't do this, despite the probability that doing so would mean individual publishers would have more sales.


When Keith says democratization is nonsense, I understand what he means. I don't think Jonathan has the same perspective. It's no crime; they're just coming at it from different angles.

I share Keith's perspective on a bunch of these topics above. It's something he and I share and talk about -- things like how limited in business scope we approach things in this community. We haven't talked about it with Jonathan ever, as far as I can recall.

I'm pretty sure I also understand what Jonathan means, and why it's good, not nonsense, that we've democratized design.

But, I think we have a situation here in which the community I have been a part of for 10 years is at a point where it's quite happy with the viability, direction, and commercial success of its endeavors. And, I'm wildly disatisfied with that same situation.

So, I read "nonsense" from that perspective. Democratization has been a fantastic thing. It's achieved good.

But, when that good is sort of abstractly compared to the good other things have done in gaming, the scope is painfully small.

This is not to say we accomplished zero. It is to say that our reach and resources have limited things far more than I ever would have guessed back in, say, 2006.

One can pretty easily argue that, say, Organized Play, did good for the hobby in terms of commercial viability, at least. In comparison to the indie scene, that amount of good is "bigger." So are several other things, games, companies, people, etc. And, thus, I wish we could have done more.

I'm beginning to believe, based on my experiences and observations, that our community is apathetic to this in general. That its values and mine diverged about 1-2(?) years ago. I'd love to find out I'm wrong about that, and our values and priorities overlap far more than I suspect currently.

This isn't a "fight" -- hippie games and I simply go in parallel directions or something. That's ok.

And, so, I think these kinds of things are hard to talk about without a pissing match because of the difference in those values and priorities. I'm VERY interested in talking about these priorities and how they differ from what I see in this community (i.e. hippe indie games, not just SG!). Another thread, I think.
\nAnd I look at this and say, yeah, we should (and will, I think) continue to push things. I don't see the lack of will or interest that Matt sees; instead, I see people who are quietly licking their wounds and easing their weariness from previous battles and efforts, preparing themselves for another big push (or, more likely, several big pushes in different directions).

Can we talk specifics, Matt? What are the next big pushes you would like to see? I'll think about the ones I want to see in the meantime.

    http://groups.google.com/group/open-game-design-project

    Indigo is me acting on this shift in values and priorities I describe above (the bit Jonathan quoted in the initial post here). I want to take what I (we) learned from the scene over the last 10 or so years and apply it in ways that are more appealing to more gamers.

    I want to do that with other designers who've faced similar frustrations as mine. We network together via Indigo. What we lose in specialization of designs we gain in marketing to an existing audiences and sales.

    Ok, second, I'd like to see a growth in designers and publishers eager to reach a wider net of gamers beyond the story gamer type crowd. I'm thinking something modest here, not a mass revolution beyond scales we've seen before. The metric I keep repeating is 2,000 sales in 2 years. I picked it as a reasonable number that would represent good success with some effort.

    Third (really a facet of #2 above), I'd like to see designers do less boutique game design and more product design (I don't know what other word to use). So, fewer hyper-specialized games, and more trad-indie hybrids (again, for lack of better terms) with top-notch design and wider appeal.

    In essence, I'd like to see indie games have wider appeal among gamers. I say this as opposed to non-gamers, for example, which we've talked about over and over again in 10 years and seen painfully slow, if any, real progress. Certainly no reliable marketing plans for publishers to succeed with.

    I should note that I'm not opposed to finding ways to reach non-gamers. I just am skeptical it can be done without some real marketing firepower, like WotC would have, presumably. So, I say abandon what we don't have resources to do and focus resources on more reliable success.

    One last point -- I think for this stuff to happen, the nature of designers and publishers and one-man-band operations will shift. Sure, that will always be part of this scene. But, if this happens at all, we'll see changes that probably stray from indie, where indie is defined as "creator owned."

    Again, that's a specific value this community shares, and I'm saying it'll be challenged. Not in a big "fuck you" kind of way, but in a natural, "help me" kind of way. Some will maintain creator ownership, others won't.

    Ok, Jonathan (and everyone), that's just a smattering of things. I'm sure there's lots more to discuss -- I feel like I haven't really hit on some of the things I've been thinking over, but this is good. I'm sure others have other ideas, too! Discuss away, and I can add anything as it develops.

    Posted By: Matt SnyderIn essence, I'd like to see indie games have wider appealamong gamers.
    \nThis is a really key point, I think. There are a lot of definitions of "gamer" but for me it's a person who likes using games as a way to have fun, test themselves, and connect with others.

    So, of course, there's a huge market for these people, and if you're going to reach them, you have to give them what they're looking for. Lots of us are focused on creating a good story, and that's awesome, but I fear a lot of people are going to be less interested if the game elements aren't strong enough. It would be interesting to discuss why more people are interested in games than in collaborative storytelling, as well as how to get people more interested in it, but that's sort of beside the point of this thread.

    The emphasis here is that to succeed with "gamers," we need to think about what kind of social experience we want a game to create more than what kind of story we want it to tell. Chess, Cranium, and Candyland all create different social experiences; all of them resonate with certain groups of people, and create an experience these people want to come back to. Our games will be very different from those, obviously, but we should never forget to pay as much attention to our games' social dynamics as those games' designers did. That's not to say that the story isn't important -- after all it's the story that defines the genre of the "story game" -- but it's important that a story fits inside the game, not a game inside the story.

Both innovation and refinement is necessary, and we've seen plenty of innovation among indie games (but not many that last or still seem innovative a few years later), and we've seen a fair bit of refinement going on with FUDGE, FATE and d20, which are incidentally other examples of the democratisation of design.

      EDIT: After posting, I realized it's not obvious how this addresses your question.       * He\u2019s as an existing gamer. He\u2019s around 30+ years old, and has been playing different RPGs for a while. He\u2019s not a hard core, D&D only person.
      * He works full time, and maybe has a family or active social life with a significant other. He\u2019s busy! He needs a game that fits those constraints in his life.
      * His game group is also busy, and they have similar needs!
      * He\u2019s either the frequent game master, or an early adopter of new games \u2014 the guy who has a ton of games on his shelf.
      * His group isn\u2019t as interested in early adoption and new games. He has to convince them to try new games, sometimes unsuccessfully. They may even voice reluctance for \u201cthose indie games.\u201d
      * He\u2019s interested in character driven play. But, he still wants enough \u201cfiddly bits\u201d to make play interesting, too.
