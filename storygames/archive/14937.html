    \n I'm on a 5pm Thursday panel at PAX Dev with Wolfgang Baur, Vincent Baker, John Harper, Sage LaTorra, Mike Mearls, Erik Mona, Elizabeth Shoemaker Sampat, and Jonathan Tweet. Our topic is "The Modern RPG: What Mainstream RPGs Can Learn from Indie, and Vice Versa."

    The book I'm currently editing isn't done yet, but I'll be up for running or playing any number of indie (or trad) games and will be specifically bringing a few things to try out: (1) a revamp of Transantigo, a surreal board game hybrid about bodhisattvas on the subway; and (2) a sketch of Fingers on the Firmament, a low-tech magical realism AW hack set in Hubble photographs; and (3) a demo of Super Farmhand, a Zelda-inspired action-adventure card game. Hit me up if you have any interest in any of these.

    I will also run Apocalypse World or any hack of it (like Dungeon World) on demand, because I want to share the love. My game here wrapped a couple weeks back and I'm getting itchy for more.

    Also, I love to just hang out and talk games, and am happy to do that too. Come introduce yourself if I don't know you already!

    \n So, the PAX Dev monster panel happened. Elizabeth couldn't make it to PAX at the last minute and Jonathan Tweet was absent, but we still had Mike Mearls, Eric Mona, Wolfgang Baur, Vincent, John Harper, Sage, and myself.

    I don't think anyone recorded it, but I didn't feel like there was that much being said that hasn't been said elsewhere.

    Before turning it over to Wolfgang, the tabletop games organizer framed the panel as very "mainstream vs. indie," which kind of got us off on a strange foot, since I think both sides were more excited about talking about what we liked about each other's stuff. Wolfgang tried to turn it around by having Vincent talk about what was awesome about mainstream games while Mike talked about what was awesome about indie games, but the panel often seemed to be focusing on differences rather than commonalities. A lot of publishing and business stuff got discussed, but basically nothing about design or the development process.

    It was a great opportunity and I was happy to meet Mike, Eric, and Wolfgang, but I ultimately came away a bit disappointed, but it was hard to know how it sounded to the audience. It felt like there were too many panelists to really have a good conversation and the discussion jumped all over the place. It also felt like we didn't do a good enough job of being excited and inspiring to the people who were there to learn about game development. But, it was the first year of doing this and there were not many previous connections and relationships between the indie and mainstream designers that we could draw on to help steer the conversation.

    Anyway, just my impressions. Maybe the other panelists came away feeling different.
