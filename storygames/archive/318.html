    Warren - you're not really getting immersion.

    Immersionists are not actors that don't want to "break character", for whom your suggestion would be excellent. Immersionists strive to "become" their characters, to change their perspective to theirs and to make all decisions from "within them" (this phrase is inaccurate).

    To force them to use a specific set of phrases means that comes the time to have conflict (something that immersionists don't seperate from free-play, because that would break immersion) they have to step out of their characters and pick from within that set of phrases. It's an improvement over other systems in that interruption of immersion is limited and thus easier to ignore, but it's still an interruption.

    To be honest, I'm not sure immersionists need a conflict system as elaborate as use-of-phrases would suggest.

    (Disclaimer - I am not the Grand Guru of Immersion, and feel ratehr uneasy making sweeping statements about it. I've only been practicing one type of it for ten years or so)

    "Part of the problem, I think, is that Immersion is a big, big word and everyone who talks about it is usually talking about a small, small part."

    That's 100% correct, Brand. There's a tendency to treat immersion like it's a single term, and it's not. It contains the state, the personal skills involved, the process of play, and a lot of other things. Unfortunately there isn't any comprehensive study/analysis of immersion in English (there is one in Hebrew, but it's in piecemeal, and I understand there's one in Swedish or Finnish or one of those) to point to. I think this is mainly because Americans are not immersionists (and yeah, that's a sucky term too. Another thread).

    Andy - both things you mention are interruptions. The immersive process (I say as if there's just one) is designed to handle some interruptions - players snap out of character, and then snap back in. It's the nature, manner and frequency of these interruptions that determines how much they affect the overall experience ("immersion", or at least one of them).

    In fact, what and how many interruptions they can "soak" is the main characteristic that seperates immersive processes from each other.

    General Statement - I don't think immersion can be explained to the point of being understood. It has to be demonstrated or experienced (preferably both). We can break processes down all day, but I don't think it'll get us any significant progress without all sides groking the concept.

    I suspect, Tony, that the real answer is "It does things you don't want to do." A friend of mine says that it's like explaining the varieties of nipple clamp to someone who isn't wanting to engage in BDSM. Here's my own take...

    There are times when I like to focus on the creation of the story as a human-scale process. I feel at my best in that regard with PBEM, where I have the time to ponder, we can talk explicitly about drafts and revisions, and so on. Trying to do it seriously in real time doesn't much appeal to me, but I can understand real-time play that is focused on narrative building and thematic engagement as a different way to the same general kind of satisfaction. (It's like preferring one kind of salsa over another, but agreeing that the right salsa makes even the best, freshest-baked tortilla chip a better snack.)

    But other times I'm after a very different experience. What I want is the sense of being there, feeling and thinking differently than I do in real life, experiencing a milieu not my own, and letting that life unfurl with as much surprise and mystery as my real life does. It's not that I'll never again want to be a craftsman approaching my work from the outside, focusing on the community of artists working together. It's just that in these times, it's the sense of being in the art that matters more to me.

    Now, not everybody ever feels that desire, and it's not (I don't think) a defect of character. It's just one of those things that some people have and some don't. Furthermore, of those who do, not everyone gets at it the same way, just as (I realized) I was getting a fair amount of the satisfaction of narrativist play as explained by its happy advocates as I do through quite different means of collaboration. Maybe you get that sense of "I'm there" through another medium - immersion happens for some people with film and TV, and for that matter with some musicians. The cliche of the foppish conductor or performer who loses all awareness of the world is anchored in reality. For that matter, my friend mentioned above says that at least some BDSM practitioners get the ecstatic trance via bondage.

    If you don't feel any particular yearning for that sensation experienced in the midst of real-time play with others, then probably immersion has little to offer you.

      Droog, I'll take up some of the slack. I've got a tag on my livejournal for immersion that might be interesting reading (or not). But here, in a nutshell. I have to preface with my own self-evaluation as "mild immersionist" and "I like it but I can also enjoy non-immersive play".

      Basically, for me, the kernel of immersion is the gamist/simulationist hybrid that's found in board wargaming. While it's not true of all wargames, there's a definite strain in wargame development toward attempting to represent the experiential impact of standing at a particular level of command and responsibility. E.g., while early wargames gave you control over all your pieces, and full view of the board, later wargames increasingly attempted to represent the difficulties of "fog of war" and "command and control" which shape and hinder decisionmaking. At some scales (especially where politics or diplomacy were a factor), there were also attempts to represent motivational issues--such as offering "victory points" for achieving national or personal objectives.

      Simulation in these games thus was not merely a matter of having events play out in an historically-plausible manner, but in reproducing the dynamics of the situation from the perspective of a specific person, or small group of people with a common goal. "Immersion" for the American player in a strategic representation of WWII would thus be supported by rules which created uncertainty in his mind regarding German development of the A-bomb, as his anxiety over that possibility would motivate him to finance the Manhattan project. "Immersion" would be harmed by a rule which put a card in the American's hand which gave him the option of eitherbuilding an army corps or causing the revolt of a Nazi satellite state--because while that might be an interesting decision from a game perspective, it's not a choice that Roosevelt or Marshall could have personally faced.

      This esthetic carries over to RPGs as an interest in placing oneself in the role of the character by inflicting on oneself an approximation of the constraints on knowledge and action that affect tthe character. It's easiest to do this in a gamist environment (i.e., where the point of the game is to win). But can also be found in more explorative contexts, in the pursuit of more open-ended goals and broader types of decisions. In the latter case, motivational factors may be modelled.

      A narrow example would be the wargame/RPG hybrid Men Against Fire, which takes as its premise the claim that only a minority of American front-line soldiers in WWII fired their weapons. So the game poses the question, if you've been ordered to attack an enemy pillbox, and you've got a few other GI's with you but you only know about your own willingness to pull the trigger, how does it feel trying to coordinate (or avoid) combat?

      In a more standard RPG context, the idea would be to find ways to make your interests or values, with regard to the character and its place in the imagined world, match those of the character. Then combine that with Actor-stance limitations on action and knowledge, with the ultimate goal of creating the impression of "first person" insight into the character and his environment.

      Huh.

      See, this is strange.

      I'm not a huge fan of immersive play right now, but I had a lot of fun with it for four years or so, barring some traditional frustrations. I feel like a lot of theoretical dialouge around that type of play is very mushy and not particularly useful for promoting enjoyable play, though, and I'd really like to improve it.

      I really don't understand the persecution thing. I mean, I'm also huge fan of Gamist play, which is basically way more persecuted in most role-playing circles (no, seriously, ask about mini-maxing sometime.) I don't understand why, say, people saying "I don't believe that immersion exists" or even "I think immersion is covering for social dysfunction" is particularly offensive.

      Let's take a hard look at what immersion is:

      Immersion is an induced trance state in which the player of the game identifies with a fictional character, to a variety of degrees. It is similar to meditation, guided visualization, or other intense mystical experiences.

      The visualization is guided by a game master, who is by and large and amateur, as opposed to the common guides, therapists and relgious leaders, who are well trained. The danger of psychological harm with intense immersive play is very real, and I think under-discussed in the critical writing about immersion, partially because of a fear that such criticism will rewaken BADD.

      Immersion, like all mystical experiences, can be used as a denial of real-world problems, most especially social problems. Socially problematic immersion (my key example being people in committed relationships hooking up with new people "in character") is the elephant in the room of immersionist play. I say this not as an outsider, pointing and mocking, but as an insider, self critiquing.

      Additionally, more at the artistic level, there is the huge problem of fragility. This is odd -- most induced mystical states are actually quite robust. I think that the core of this problem is that we don't have particularly good techniques for inducing or removing immersion, especially outside of the LARP context -- and as such it is very easily "broken."

      So, I think, before we can talk about conflict resolution, we need to talk about

      • Psychological safety
      • Social safety
      • Strengthening and deepening the trance

      I have some solutions for the third, but until I can nail down the first two, I honestly don't feel like talking about them.
