I see this said all the time -

\n
    \n
  • "Is this game still supported?" (meaning: Does the publisher still make new supplements periodically?)
    • \n
  • "I don't want to play a game without support." (Meaning: I want a steady flow of supplements OR I want access to a person, designated as the game's rep, who can answer questions about it.)
    • \n
  • "This game is supported poorly." (Meaning: This game has bad supplements OR this game doesn't have enough supplements OR no one answers my questions about this game.)
    • \n
\n

What is the big damn deal? Aren't we adults? Can't we figure stuff out ourselves? Why do we demand this constant flow of candy that so rarely finds its way to actual play? I mean, I for one like this kind of stuff a lot; I wouldn't really want to sink my teeth into a game and do serious modding anymore, without access to someone who is really system-savvy about it, but that hells doesn't have to be the author, and I like getting new stuff I might potentially use for a game, but I don't necessarily want to pay for it or care who made it. Some dude on the Internets may make really cool things that the publisher doesn't.

\n

So, like, what makes "Support" so...so...thing?

    \n I think the problem stems from the traditional RPG model which is partly to do with the way RPGs evolved and a lot to do with revenue. It has been the case for so long that a publisher would release book A and then back it up with monthly supplements. It worked, people bought the books and the publishers kept doing it.

    Now there are new models, like the ones a lot of people here use, the way a game is produced and printed has changed. There is no longer a need for gigantic print runs and then back up books to try and capitalise on the momentum from your initial release. However the majority of people who play RPGs still buy books from the big boys rather than the smaller publishers, and those companies still produce 'splat' books. I think the pereception is that if a new book isn't coming out the company must have lost interest in it.

    I think tackling the issue is a matter of making people aware of sites like this one, the Forge, Collective Endeavour where indie games are supported, just not in the traditional sense. Updating websites regularly with new information, actual plays, downloadable content may also help as it does in the computer games industry. Look at bioshock and the 'Cult of rapture' website. The amount of stuff they put up on there which has nothing to do with the playability of the game or sequels is staggering.

    \n You know, I'm kind of wary about this particular thread.

    One of the reasons that I am, really, is because of my difficulty in meaningfully separating what shreyas calls "community" and "support". I mean, take a look:\n
    1. A company or individual publishes additional material that is supposed to supplement a game: This is support.
    2. An active community forum that helps out with rules problems, game issues and posts actual play snippets: This is community.

(point 1 would be nice if I had a boatload of money, which I don't) but it's not the only, or even main, deciding factor.     \n I think the word support gets used as a stand-in for a host of related concepts.

    1. Like Tariq, I think the cornerstone of support is community. People want a community to reach out to for inspiration and sometimes validation. They want new ideas for approaching things, new ways to excite their groups. This can come from product or forum posts.

    I suspect this group can be divided into people looking into very specific, directed advice and suggestions for a game, and people who rarely or never get to play but want to talk about games and game concepts to maintain the sense of community and discourse.

    2. People want an assurance that they'll be able to find people to play the game with.

    Under this rationale, if support equals available product and available product equals popularity, then games with lots of support in the form of lots of product means I'll be able to find people to play with.

    Obviously there are a lot of assumptions bound up in that statement, but I think it's representative of the thought process. I suspect this aspect is more common with people who don't get to play often or don't have a group. Taken to an extreme, reading the continual stream of supplemental product can become a stand in for participating in play activity, allowing folks to participate in forums for the community.

    Bringing people together to play remains one of the biggest challenges in the hobby.

    I think gamer locators, maps and other social networking tools are one of the positive faces of this group.

    3. Sometimes, people really just want others to do the grunt work for them. Running games like D&D or Exalted can take a lot of prep and thought! Many people aren't up for switching to a game with less prep, but only have limited time to play. I suspect this is becoming increasingly true as the gaming population ages and gamers' real-life responsibilities increase.

    Absent any real numbers, my guess is that these folks have groups, but are struggling to maintain them in the face of other responsibilities and scheduling conflicts. They depend upon scenarios and other tools to lessen their prep burden and allocate more of their available time to actually gaming.

    \n Another feature of "support" -- at least in the 80's and early 90's, a lot of games would be released half-baked. Without the internet to distribute errata and additional material cheaply, sometimes game companies would put important chunks of the rules into supplements. Not as big a deal now.

    Posted By: shreyasWhat is the big damn deal? Aren't we adults? Can't we figure stuff out ourselves? Why do we demand this constant flow of candy thatso rarelyfinds its way to actual play? I mean, I for onelikethis kind of stuff a lot; I wouldn't really want to sink my teeth into a game and do serious modding anymore, without access to someone who is really system-savvy about it, but that hells doesn't have to be theauthor,and I like getting new stuff I might potentially use for a game, but I don't necessarily want to pay for it or care who made it.     \nAs others have said, it depends what you mean by "support".

    Support as in, company supports your favorite line

    I'm not into supplements and things like that. But it makes sense. If you are dearly in love with WoD, Artesia, Warhammer or anything like that... you probably want more stuff. Otherwise, you'd do like me and not bother with setting info.

    Support as in, company is active and responsive

    I think this allows many things. The first one is a central service point for official ressources (like the company releasing character sheets, errata, official rulings, new products, etc...) but more importantly, it's a ralllying point for fans, to build a community, exchange new ideas and the like. In term, the company can use this fan material, harvest it and use it in some way. Not to mention all the free publicity.

    The second one is that an active company probably has other products going. There's a synergy at work here. You might think Dungeons are lame, but you really like Star Wars roleplaying. Or you might not initially have been interested in Burning Wheel but now, you're drawn to it because you liked Burning Empire.

    The third point is peace of mind. I know that if I buy a GURPS product, chances are I will not be disappointed. Now, I was one of those who bought 4th edition and got a faulty binding. We were many. I dropped an email to SJG. I was given a friendly response and things were taken care of. I was sent a new copy pronto. There are few companies that could do this. SJG lost a ton of money in that debacle. This is valuable to me. It makes me trust them sight unseen. Same goes with the level of playtesting and reviewing that goes in their products.

    What do you get when all of these are put together?

    The central point releases lots of info on lots of new products. The central point has an active, functional message board. Lots of people ask questions, make suggestions. Community grows. More questions, more answers, more freebies. Fans write or program utilities. Company makes more products, draws more interest and is able to provide better customer service because they have more cash. And so on...


    Now, for a few examples, take Hero Games, Green Ronin and Guardians of Order.

    There was a time when GoO was the shit. But the website would go down and the release schedule became more and more hectic. And they would provide shitty customer service. The pricing of the books were also considerably off, despite any bullshit they might have said about the rate of exchange.

    Eventually, there was a clash between M&M, SAS and Champions. Green Ronin was thriving with a healthy schedule, high quality products and side products (pumping D&D support material). Hero was steadily rebuilding itself up after years of crappy service, thanks to a change of ownership.

    Despite cosmetic differences on the surface, SAS and M&M are pretty much the same thing (with the difference being that M&M is overly complicated). Yet M&M quickly crushed its rival thanks to being a much more active and responsive company.

    It pays to be an active and responsive company. Or in other words, a company that provides support. It's what fans want. For reasons that are sometimes just emotional but other times very practical.

    But I like the indie thinking. Products are more self-contained, so release schedules are not an issue. And despite that, many indie companies go the extra mile to provide stellar support compared to crappy publishers like Margaret Weis, Eden and the now defunct GoO.

    A stellar example of a small/middle-size company that understands the viral effects of being an active company is Pinnacle/Great White. I don't like the product (Savage Worlds) but man, do they know how to hype it and create those warm feelings inside their customer base. You feel good, you are supported and in turn, you go out and create more fans. The forum is always active and there is a healthy number of all sorts of cool downloads for your game.
