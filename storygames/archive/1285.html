So there's my totally anecdotal survey thread asking people who have bought one indie game about their purchases since.

So far on Story-Games, the response has been pretty plain. I am considering copy-pasting the same survey on RPGnet and RPGsite for comparison purposes.

However, I am starting to think this is the thing to talk about regarding indie games and indie games' success. It's not "indie games sales as compared to mainstream games in numbers of sales." It's the (apparent) fact that it's a one-way door for a lot of people. If the responses continue to go as they have gone so far (and if they are the same on other fora), it will strengthen my suspicion that indie games are not a small counter-entropic bubble in the vastly larger entropic system of mainstream gaming; indie gaming is (becoming) a different thing altogether.

Additionally, it simply doesn't matter if the rate of "conversion" is low as long as it's constant for any length of time. If the rate of people going through the one-way door is less than the people getting into mainstream gaming, indie gaming is a parasite on the mainstream industry, at least in terms of sales. If the rate through the one-way door is greater than the influx of new players to mainstream gaming, then indie gaming is a particularly deadly parasite over time. Unfortunately, I don't think we'll have those numbers to compare any time soon, so the long-term effect of the one-way door will remain pure speculation.

Judging by mainstream publishers' reactions to indie publishing -- from the harsh disdain to the attempts to subsume indie games -- I think they may be seeing the same thing. (Ignore that bit; talking out of my ass.)

I'd love to hear other's thoughts on the survey results as they develop. Should this survey get ported elsewhere? What patterns or significance do you see?

    I think there's multiple causes here:

    1. Games designed with the philosophy that System Matters, and that a group shouldn't have to guess how to toolbox a game into doing something. (much like boardgames or card games are playable "as is", without requiring design work on the part of the play group).

    2. Games designed for shorter play cycles- a single session of 2 hours up to a few months- quicker payoff, less commitment, more convenience.

    3. Games that are quicker to learn and pick up. (again, easier to get into)

    4. Game texts & a community that promotes functional social contract as the basis of fun play, as well as analysis and action, rather than "blame and try harder".

    5. A community based on play, not collection.

    6. Designers that listen to their customers, and also provide useful feedback and support.

    Basically, games that aren't as much of a commitment of time or work, pay off reliably, and a functional social culture based around fun, not geek social fallacies.

    At a local Con last weekend I met a guy who played two indie games -- InTERRORgation and Primetime Adventures. He had no idea such games existed. He had continued to buy games on and off for years, but not play them. My guess: he knew there was something like an RPG he would enjoy playing, but wasn't actually playing them.

    Suddenly, he wanted to play again. He was pumping me for information about websites and where to get all these games. They answered a NEED.

    Same for me.

    And when I told a friend of mine (a fellow writer) about PtA, he immediately (I mean immediatley) went on line and starting Googling the game and bounced in and out of Actual Play threads and more. He called me and emailed me several times that night. Before I'd gone to bed he'd notified me that he'd sent out invitations to more local TV and Screenwriters he knew set up a PtA game for this Saturday. I mean -- it was like he'd heard about this thing called crack and simply had to arrange a buy.

    Now this guy used to write RPG stuff. He loves board games. And he's been hankering to play RPGs. But he simply never got around to doing it. Now, suddenly, in a six hour period, he was making it happen.

    The night before the local Con, I introduced this guy and a couple of his casual friends to Sorcerer. Again, everyone had played in the past, but hadn't played for a while. I didn't say anything to them aboout the dice rolling part.

    I said, "So, you'd be playing a character that is so driven to get something done, that he or she summoned SOMETHING from a place not human -- that gives your character powers to reach that goal. But your creature -- a Demon, but not the demons from hell, but demon in the sense that it is a creature that is un-human -- has Needs and Wants. By fulfilling these needs and wants -- and they're often nasty -- you get your demon to help you. Each of you will create a Kicker for your character. A Kicker is the situation that begins just as we begin actual play. It's the beginning of your character's Story, a moment of choice, with an open ended set of possibilities, that launch your character into the tale. I won't have an adventure for you to "figure out." We honestly Author a tale together. When the situation created by the Kicker is resolved one way or another, the adventure comes to an end."

    Okay, a couple of things:

    Each time I spoke the Captailized words in the paragraph above (or explained the word, like Kicker), their eyes lit up.

    Normally in a mainstream game people's eyes light up at the description of the Color and Setting. Note that here their eyes were lighting up at the rules introduction: because Needs, Wants, Kickers, Demons are all Rules components. I was giving them the basics of how to play Sorcerer. But unlike a mainstream RPG, this introduction wasn't about metaphors of Radio Drama or thinking through Stats of a character sheet. And they wanted to PLAY. Badly. (It was late; we were about to start Settlers of Catan, but we'll be doing that game NEXT Saturday.)

    This difference, to me, is key to understanding the One Way Door image Joshua came up with. For some of us, including the people mentioned in the examples above, whether the games are Indie or not doesn't matter. What matters is that they are delivering on the promise of what we always wanted to get from RPGs but couldn't get the rules to deliver.

    Some people who are used to mainstream games hear a description of Primetime Adventures or Heroquest or MLwM, or even Sorcerer (with it's Kickers and Bangs and lack of a "story" ready to go) and say, "But those aren't roleplaying games." (There was once a guy on the Forge who kept insisiting that the game of Sorcerer had no GM -- because the function of the Sorcerer GM was so alien to him he couldn't square the job description with his expectations.)

    Whether PtA or Sorcerer are or are not RPGs is not particularly interesting to me. What I find interesting is that the difference is so strong tha people try to disown them.

    Which I think is fine. Because it's that DISTINCTION that makes me want to play them. The same distinction that makes me not particularly care about mainstream games -- why I stopped buying and playing them and why I have no plans to buy the "D&D is a metric" RPGs that most mainstream companies produce. (In this regard, the Pundit speaks to a large set of consumers and players. And I can only say, "Okay, whatevever, and I'll be playing the game that makes me happy over HERE, thanks.")

    Ok, so we have a survey on a site devoted primarily to indie rpgs which indicates that the people who care enough to post at that site prefer indie rpgs. Let's extrapolate out from that.

    Firstly, if each person who tried indie rpgs never went back to trad, but the rate of people taking up trad was greater, trad would still overwhelmingly dominate. If two people per day take up trad and one person per day leaves trad for indie, trad is not going to be replaced by indie. The OP recognises this, but since we don't know what the replacement rate is we can't really go on to make any meaningful statement about how trad and indie relate, assuming that is even that meaningful a split in the first place.

    Secondly, if you start from a low base expansion tends to be exponential, then slow down. In the 1990s several UK law firms experienced massive expansion becoming truly global players. If we extrapolate out, by about 100 years from now at that rate of expansion the entire human race will be UK lawyers, advising each other while the food runs out. Extrapolation leads to bizarre outcomes if taken too far.

    By mainstream publishers we've clarified we mean some freelancers whose income is directly threatened, so I don't think that tells us much.

    Lastly though, surely this is precisely what we would expect? Who is most likely to try out a new type of rpg? An indie game? Well, probably someone who is interested in playing something different to what they are playing now. Why would someone be looking to play something other than what they are playing now? Probably because they're not entirely content with what they're playing now.

    So, people who are not happy with their trad rpgs but who enjoy indie rpgs once they find indie rpgs tend then to play the games they like and not those they liked less. Um, yes.

    I'm not sure what conclusions you're seeking to draw here, but it looks like a bit of a statement of the obvious coupled with flawed extrapolations from catchup expansion figures tied to a deeply biased (in the sense of skewed, not prejudiced) sample source. If something starts with few people involved and expands percentage wise the expansion will look significant.

    If on day one 1 person likes my game, and one person more likes it each day for a month, at the end of that month I have had a 3000% expansion, I still only have 30 people who like my game. If I ask around on the historical gaming yahoogroup to see which people there only play historical rpgs, I will soon find that in fact a significant percentage of gamers surveyed only like historical rpgs.

    Ultimately, all this tells us is that people who like indie rpgs like indie rpgs, and I'm not sure that adds much to the sum of human knowledge.

    More broadly, I think most gamers either welcome the addition of indie games or are utterly indifferent to them. Such hostility as there is is not I'm afraid to the games, it's to individuals who promote them badly.

    In fact, I'd go further, given the support indie games get from the mainstream gaming industry, with people like Robin Laws, Mike Mearls and above all Ken Hite routinely plugging indie games, why aren't they getting more love and attention? Given the mainstream industry backing for the indie movement, why isn't it more popular?

    That's not a snide remark, answer that and I think they can be made more popular. Heavyweight industry guys back indie gaming, nobody seems to be listening, that I think is a much more interesting issue.

    Well, there's little value here in my going into stuff I've seen elsewhere. We've clarified that I misread Joshua and that's cool hopefully.

    So, I will add what indie games have done for me, which I think is linked to what they have done for many others here.

    They reminded me that gaming was about play. That rpgs were tools to be used for play and that an unplayed game makes the baby Jesus cry.

    I buy fewer rpgs now, those I buy are far more likely to get played, it's not yet where I want to be which is that I buy nothing I don't play, but habits take time to break.

    The thing is, when I started out in gaming, games were published mostly because someone had something they wanted out there. An rpg about Salem in 1692? Sure. A swashbuckling quasi-skirmish game based on Dumas? Why not? An rpg about post-apocalyptic gonzoid mutants going wild? Sounds good.

    Over time, I think a lot of that spirit got lost. The desire to make a game not because it would sell so much, as because it was a game you thought would be cool. Selling was often secondary, which may of course be why many of those guys went bust.

    There was a homebrew spirit, a DIY ethos, part of the hobby was making your own rules or kitbashing whatever you got.

    Now, this isn't quite the same. With the current diy thing you may well create your own game but generally you're not expected to homerule some other guys. But the essential spirit is the same. Games made not to sit on the shelf, but because some madwhack guy thought a game about time travelling inca car salesmen would rock and hey, maybe it will.

    Over the 1990s I think the hobby slightly lost its way, supplements, splatbooks, glossy art and pages of fiction. Stuff that didn't help actual play, and that to an extent started to get rated higher than stuff that does help actual play.

    The indie movement is all about the play, gloss is good where gloss serves play, but gloss is not of itself a goal.

    And that, that is most cool, hell it's even old school. And, for me at least, it reminded me that buying an rpg for the reading and admiration was a waste of my time when I could be buying rpgs and playing the hell out of them.

    So, that's my bullshit anecdote (to paraphrase JD), and although I do mostly still play trad games I think the impact on my gaming is similar to what many others are reporting, a renewed emphasis on buying for utility, buying for play and putting play back at the heart of the hobby.
