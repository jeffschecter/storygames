    What you're doing is definitely higher handling time than just a big die pool, though. If you want to reduce handling time, here's how I'd do it:

    If you have 10 dice, you roll 10 dice.

The game I'm developing this for right now is the v1.1 Kanthe game, but I plan on using this as a core mechanic in a couple other games, including the rewrite of IceRunner, so I'm going to leave out some Kanthe-specific stuff, like the mapping rules and reward rules as well as the details on interpreting Discovery results, because they don't impact Conflicts. I will mention, however, that Kanthe is a solitaire game, so one person is rolling two sets of dice for both Challenge (your character) and Opposition (the monster, a trap, a locked door, etc.) So, if you advance to 9th level and have three allies with a Strength of 5 each, plus a +3 sword, you're rolling 27 Challenge Dice vs.20-30 Opposition Dice in a Conflict. Simultaneously. I think you can see what I'm getting at.

    Here are the rules: characters have a Strength rating (which really means Power, not physical strength) and several weapons, tools, and advantages, each with their own Strength. Each tool or advantage applies to a specific type of conflict: physical, mental, social, stealth... the list is potentially endless. Defensive items and advantages provide Defensive Strength, blocking Actions; other items provide extra dice to roll in Conflicts.

    There are two reasons to roll dice: Conflict (who wins?) and Discovery (what's in the room? what's he waving around menacingly?) Roll both Challenge and Opposition for both rolls, because: (a) Discovery can turn into Conflict; and, (b) you interpret Challenge Dice dice one way in Discovery, Opposition Dice another way.

    For Discovery, you look for matches on the Challenge Dice. You look the value rolled up on some table to identify which monster, trap, or effect you're dealing with; the number of dice rolled with that value is the Strength of that monster/trap/effect. Two sets of matches means two critters, and so on. The Opposition Dice are used to modify the description of the critter. Either there's one descriptor per set of matches, or one descriptor per value; either way, you look up the values on a descriptor table. (Most of these tables you build yourself during setup, but I will have default tables.)

    For Conflict, every value rolled, whether there's a match or not, is an Action. If there is no match, it's a +1 action; otherwised, the Strength of the action is equal to the number of matches. Actions happen in numerical order, so every turn (roll of the dice) is split into ten segments, numbered 0 to 9.

    Actions can be combined, but can't be split. If I need a +3 action but roll a 0, 1 and 2, I can combine them for a series of actions that begin on segment 0 and end on segment 2. This matters because: what if my opponent kills me on segment 1?

    What you're trying to beat on any given segment is the Defensive Strength of the target for that type of action. I do a +3 hit with my sword and the creature has +4 armor? No effect. +2 armor? He takes one +3 physical wound. I lie to him about how his boss put me in charge, using a +2 action? He takes a +2 social wound. And so on. If the target gets more individual wounds than the target's Strength, that means death, or conversion into an ally, or whatever the typical stakes of that type of conflict are. Only the number of wounds counts for this, not the Strengths; you use a wound's Strength when trying to heal it.

    Situational Advantages add dice to the side they favor. Weapons, tools, and advantages are one kind of situational advantage, but you can also create favorable conditions to add dice to the conflict. You use actions for this, too. Wounds are situational advantages; if you are mentally wounded (confused, distracted) and I make a mental attack (trickery,) I get one extra die for every mental wound you have.

    Oh, and it's probably not relevant, but you can attack a defensive item or a situation instead of directly attack an opponent. Armor doesn't use its Defensive Strength when directly attacked, but might be defended by something else.

    One thing I constantly forget about probablity is that while intersection is analogous to multiplication, the following intuition that union is analogous to addition is wrong.

    In other words: The chance of a flipped coin coming up heads: 1/2.

    The chance of 2 coins both coming up heads (intersection): 1/4 -> 1/2 x 1/2

    The chance of N coins all coming up heads (intersection): 1/2^N^

    **but**

    The chance of at least one coin coming up heads (union) is **not** 1/2 + 1/2. It's easier to restate it as "not (both coins come up tails)", which is 1 - ( 1/2^2 ) = 3/4.

    The trick to remember is: if you're adding a numerator, it's probably wrong.

    As far as "the chance of a +X match of M dice" seems equivalent to "roll a 0 on X on M dice," which does have some weird crap to account for the fact that the dice can move around, as it were.
