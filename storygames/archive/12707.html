    I think Sandy Atunes' model is the key here: Build a dedicated fan base, essentially a sort of 'gestalt patron', who will pay for ANYTHING you do. If I were Vincent Baker, or Luke Crane, I'd be printing super-deluxe $100 signed and numbered editions of my games for the really hardcore fans. Make that content available before the broad release. Sell playtest positions and chances to game with you at Cons for real, serious money. I bet Luke could easily get $100 a head for a 4-6 hour Burning Wheel scenario he prepped exclusively for that event.

    If your work can be stolen, sell the one thing that can't be duplicated: YOU. I wasn't being hyperbolic when I invoked Neil Gaiman in the other thread. I really think he's right about the life of creatives coming full circle from mass distribution back around to personal contact; using the work to leverage peoples' desire to meet you, and not just to consume the work. The Penny Arcade guys have been masters of this as well, along with a few smart and savvy game creators: Monte Cooke, Margaret Weiss & Tracy Hickman, etc.

    -Jim C.

    \n Sell awesome tangible goods. This can be books (I would love to own a copy of Mouse Guard, even though I've never actually played the game), but it doesn't have to be. Ganakagok's tarot deck strikes me as a great example of this. Even just ordinary merch like t-shirts or dice bags would probably work; I would totally buy an Apocalypse World t-shirt. (One way to make something awesome is to make it exclusive. Jim's autographed books definitely fit this model.)

    Use Kickstarter or similar.

    Sell some other sort of exclusivity. This could be so many things. The Early Dark people have lots of great ideas, check out the packages for the various donation levels: Kickstarter Page

    Switch to some sort of subscription model. If I had written Fiasco, I would probably have reversed the format they currently use. They sell the main rules and give monthly playsets out for free. Why not do it the other way around? Alternatively, Halfjack over at Blue Collar Space has been talking about a different sort of subscription model here and here. (If you think ongoing subscriptions are no good, you can always do the Guild Wars thing and sell a one-time package that gets a subscription forever/for a certain amount of time)

    Use a shareware model. Offer the PDF for free, and have the first page be a request that the reader buy a hardcopy or send you some cash if they start playing regularly. (Some games, like Hell 4 Leather and Society of Dreamers, do this implicitly. A heck of a lot more do it accidentally, due to filesharing. Why not make it explicit?)

    I'm sure I'm missing a lot of ideas, but those seem like promising first starts.

    \n Another angle is institutionalized: set up artist vacancies in state and private foundations with wages and normal benefits, paid for by the usual public cultural funding. Or have those institutions, by the way of expert boards, evaluate individual artists and contract them for individual pieces of work. Collect money for this the same way it's collected now, only more of it. For example, because personal copying of culture products is legal in Finland, we already pay an extra tax on any digital storage mediums that can be used to store these copies, something like 1-5 percent of the price of the MP3 player or blank CD or whatever. Hike that cost up while you legalize piracy, then use the money to collectively support the creative scene. It's a socialist solution with socialist weaknesses, but it'll do its bit.

    I should note that some arts already work like the above, at least here in Finland. Ballet comes to mind: if you want to be a ballet dancer in Finland and get paid for it, it's essentially the national opera or bust for you. The opera is heavily supported with public money, so the artist in this case has to pretty much get accepted by a public institution to become an official, state-sanctioned ballet dancer if they want money for what they do. The system certainly draws its critics, but it's not an obvious catastrophe from day one or anything like that. Notably the ballet dancers do not seem to think that they're entitled to any more wide-spread financial opportunities from society.

    In fact, now that I think of it, I'll be properly institutionalized (ha) myself for the coming months as I work with an arts grant in place. It's not my full-time state, but the better sort of society artist can even now get a near-total coverage of his work by applying for long-term working grants, becoming fully supported by the public. The institutions already exist to pick up the slack, or part of it, when the publishing industry turns belly up. I won't for a moment believe that the society will want to maintain the same overall level of output caused by the lucrativeness of the culture industry today, but it'll pay for something.

    \n To compile a list of approaches, here's what we have so far:\n
      \n
    • Sell spin-off product that can't be digitalized
      • \n
    • Inspire small donations by on-going production and subtle pressure, like webcomics do
      • \n
    • Sell prestige associated with your fame and respect
      • \n
    • Get payments in advance and publish into digital distribution
      • \n
    • Sell subscriptions that compete with free distribution with the convenience of constant updates all in one place
      • \n
    • Get a patron, public or private, distributed or unitary
      • \n
    \nIs that all we got? It's a pretty good list already, though. All points have their weaknesses, no doubt, but it's a starting point.

    \n I think we can look to the music industry and see what they're doing, because they're 10 years ahead of the print industry on this. They seem to have been through the control-everything phase that print publishers are going through now.

    So a couple of examples of things I've done and why I did them.

    I wound up buying PDF copies of Polaris and In A Wicked Age after buying the print copies separately. I am pretty sure I could have found them on file sharing networks somewhere, but purely on a selfish, practical level, buying them meant I clicked here, typed a few things there, and they were on the way. The fact that the money went to designers I want to see more work from, well, that's pretty selfish too, when you think about it.

    For a Ribbon Drive playlist, I wanted an English-language version of Jacques Brel's "Amsterdam." I certainly have access to file-sharing networks, but I started with iTunes. Bang, less than 2 minutes later, I'm listening to a sample of David Bowie's cover and Ute Lemper's cover. 99 cents for each of them. This is why I use iTunes instead of file sharing networks.

    Back in 2002 or so when the Call of Cthulhu 20th Anniversary Edition came out, I already owned two copies of the rules. But I gladly shelled out extra for the 20th Anniversary Edition. Likewise with the Deluxe Edition of GURPS 4th - I paid $125 for those books, when I was volunteering as a MIB and could have gotten the standard edition those books for free for running two GURPS demo sessions at conventions. (Which I also did.)

Being "indie" has never been about NOT making a living off of art. It's about making art outside of established systems and industries, and possibly making money up to and including a living.

    Myself, If I never had to go back to work again and could feed and shelter myself and my family based only on doing ART, games or otherwise, I'd say yes in a heartbeat and never look back.\n
    Posted By: komradebobit seems like the main thing ages back at TF was exploring how to make and distribute games and possibly make a little side hobby money on them, not support the designer and their family on the income.
    \nMy understanding is that it was the Forge's assessment that very, very few people were able to make a living in the RPG scene (and even at that, overall companies tend to lose money and go out of business quite a lot). All the efforts of the Forge in promoting indie publishing has been toward making publishing for yourself more viable. That includes, but is not limited to, making money from it.

    Now, very few indie publishers are making a living from their games--perhaps not ANY; I'm not sure on that score. Vincent Baker of all people says his games are a very lucrative side business, but not his living. Maybe Luke Crane and the Evil Hat guys are making a living? I dunno.

    But I'll tell you what, *I* would sure as hell LIKE to.



    Without selling the hell out.



    Myself, I'm inclined to pursue that goal by raising the Personal Connection bar as high as I possibly can, through methods already mentioned here and any others I can think of. My version of un-digitizable product is experimenting with handmade book designs that speak of craft and artistry which have no electronic substitute. My version of leveraging fame and prestige is playing in person with as many people as possible. I like to tell people that a purchase of my booklet comes with a free game with me, at their earliest convenience.

    This all arises from my personal feelings ABOUT the game, and about games in general. When I released my ashcan of the Dreaming Crucible, I found that I was far more interested in A) playing with people, or HEARING about play after the fact, than I was in selling the book and never hearing a peep about it again. I realized that the play of the game was the real thing, and the book just a step toward that. In one sense, if I could, I would never sell a physical book, and pass the game on purely through play. I would spread an experience.

    However, I can't do that because A) I don't know how to leverage that into feeding my family, and I don't know how to convey the game such that people could play it without me, absent a book.

    Both of those things could possibly be achievable, however. I aim to keep pursuing both.

    In the meantime, my focus is on making the book itself a thing of beauty in its own right, which justifies its existence even if you DO learn to play without referring to it. And that art object is immune to piracy short of knocking me down and grabbing it out of my hands. And the experience, the personal connection we can share through the game...well, you can't pirate that at all.

    \n The article Better than Free has eight classes of things that people will pay for if the content is free. In summary, they are:
    \n
    \nImmediacy (get it now)
    \nPersonalisation (your name here)
    \nInterpretation (e.g. support)
    \nAuthenticity (pay for what's real)
    \nAccessibility (to new versions)
    \nEmbodiment (physical parts or experienceb)
    \nPartonage
    \nFindability
    \n
    \nIt might help prompt some ideas.

    \n Another point occurred to me.

    Make it possible for people to give you more money than you require.

    For instance, Joe's Kickstarter project for Perfect. There are recommended levels, but it's open-ended: if I give $X, I get Y, but there's no practical barrier to giving $X+10.

    Or the immediately aforementioned Misspent Youth PDFs. This gives me an easy way to spend more money on a game I like: sure, I have a print copy on the way, but a screen-friendly landscape PDF is just the thing for dull moments at work.

    And on a completely unrelated note, I think the real problem is relative obscurity, and I wish I knew how to fix that cheaply. I ran Apocalypse World for the current incarnation of my college gaming group yesterday (which failed to disintegrate, and in fact thrived, when I moved away 10 years ago). Three of the players as much as said "This game is awesome and I've never heard of it! Where did you find it?" (Maybe that's three sales. Here's hoping.) Or Ribbon Drive: a coworker who can see my shared iTunes library asked me why there was a folder of playlists labeled Ribbon Drive, and I told him and explained, and he lit right up and said, "That sounds amazing - can I play next time?"

    Posted By: avramI've been thinking for a while now that there are two kinds of books that people are going to want to own for most of the coming century: (1) electronic documents,
    \nI guess we'll see about this first one. If the devices to read electronic documents get a lot better, and more importantly, a LOT cheaper, this could happen. Maybe within the next ten years, even.

    I'm not sure that'll be a good thing, mind you. Yes, it'll save on paper and shipping costs and even on physical space, but building the ubiquitous, cheap, good e-readers and their batteries will have its own associated costs, too. And it'll torpedo the used book market, which will be sad because used bookstores are often great locally-owned independent businesses, and those are important to a strong local economy. Also, they'll need to come up with some solution for libraries, since I doubt the "please be honest and delete the book when you're past the due date" solution will still be acceptable to the publishers when people checking out an electronic document becomes more common than people checking out a printed text. And right now I'd only lose all of my books in the event of a major disaster (fire, flood, tornado, etc.) or a really weird burglary; my library will get a lot more vulnerable when it's electronic.

    So, lots of hurdles between today and the electronic-document future. It'll be interesting to see how it all shakes out, that's for sure. In the meantime, there are still plenty of reasons for companies to keep printing ordinary-quality books on paper, and plenty of people who'll buy them.
