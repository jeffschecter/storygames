So the guy known as RPGpundit has started his own Theory and Game Design forum on theRPGsite. I, for one, was really excited to see what stuff Pundit and the other people there would come up with and what kind of environment it would be. Pundit clearly considers himself to be against all of the theory stuff that has come out of the Forge, especially GNS, and supports a view of roleplaying that is so ultra-conservative that you might call it reactionary (something like "roleplaying is totally awesome as it is and doesn't need much changing").

He recently posted his Landmarks of Gaming Theory, which I will quote extensively below. I'm gonna gloss over his absolutism, tendency to define things negatively (i.e. "not A" instead of "B"), and the anti-Forge rhetoric, because I think that weakens his argument somewhat, but there's actually quite a bit of interesting stuff here:

A Landmark is a go/no-go test based on simple statements about the reality of RPGs as they apply to the mainstream of people playing them today!

...Here are my "Landmarks of Gaming Theory":

1. The vast majority of gamers are having fun gaming.

2. The vast majority of gamers are satisfied with the majority of their game as it is played.

3. D&D is the model of what most people define as an RPG, and therefore also the model for a successfully-designed RPG. It can be improved upon or changed... You don't have to say it is the "best" RPG, but you are obviously not in touch with reality if your theory claims that D&D is a "bad" game...

4. Given number 3 above, it is self-evident that games that have a broad spectrum of playstyles (as D&D does) are by definition successful games. ...Note that this doesn't mean that you must say narrow-focus games are "bad", or that narrow-focused games can't be considered appropriate...

5. Conflicts do arise in gaming groups; these conflicts are usually the product of social interaction between the players and not a problem with the rules themselves. The solution to these problems is not to "Narrow the rules", but to broaden the playstyle of a group to accomodate what the complaining players are missing...

6. ...The vast majority of players enjoy a game where the GM has power over the world and the players over their characters; and while a theory can suggest ways that GMs and Players can experiment with interactively creating the setting, it cannot suggest that the Players should have the power to tell the GM what to do (except for the "power" to walk away from a game).

7. Any gaming theory that tries to divide gamers into specific criteria of "types" must make it clear that this is only one kind of categorization, and not an absolutist and literal interpretation that is a universal truth; it is only one form of categorizing gamers.

8. Any theory that suggests, therefore, that its "types" are mutually exclusionary in gaming groups is in violation of the Landmarks. Individual people can end up being mutually exclusive to each other, unable to play in the same group, etc; but that is because of individual personal issues, not because of an issue of playstyle.

9. & 10. [cut]

    Okay, so 1-3 basically seem like an attempt to push for positivity and especially pro-D&D positivity in theory. Which is cool. Yay, positivity! Yay, D&D!

    4 is a move away from what has recently become the forte of indie games: being really focused on doing a few things well. I just hope that pushing in the other direction isn't going to result in more "generic" or "universal" systems that lack real direction in their mechanics. More things like Fate and The Pool and Active Exploits and Shadows would be cool. More things like Basic Role-Playing? I think I'll pass.

    5 is sorta a push to look for social and not system-based solutions to play problems. I wish he was a bit more specific about this idea of "broadening the playstyle" because that seems a little problematic. If the game can only include the stuff that everyone really wants, it can be successful? I'm not sure I quite see that, as yet.

    6 is an awkward one for me. It seems like a justification for the GM being able to overrule the players, unless that's somehow considered "being a bad GM" and therefore not considered part of the issue at all. I guess I don't see what's threatening about a little player empowerment, but obviously Pundit feels differently, and that's cool. Yay for being retro (though it seems like everyone's using some sort of resource-based narrative editing nowadays).

    7-8 are, I guess, his attempt to try to differentiate himself from Ron. In my experience, I don't think Ron's ever claimed that GNS was more than one classification scheme based on specific things he observed in play. So I guess I just see these two as an important basis of any theorizing, being the hippie relativist that I am.

    So I guess I look at this and go: "Huh, this isn't really gonna be much different from (ironically) The Forge or Story Games after all, except maybe for the different people being involved." Hopefully doing the same type of thing with a different crowd will give them interesting results. I'm hopeful that some really cool stuff might develop.

    Here's my take on his theories...

    1. Players enjoy the challenge, process, and/or potential for reward in the games they are playing, or they would stop playing them.

    2. Yes, otherwise they would stop playing it.

    3. The most popular games can not be defined as bad games, otherwise people would stop playing them.

    4. Players enjoy the challenge, process, and/or potential for reward in the games they are playing. Players might enjoy a narrowly focused game, like Chess. The might enjoy a game with a less than enjoyable process if the potential reward is great enough (eg. spending $ on the lottery). They may also enjoy games that are enjoyed on multiple levels, with different types of challenges and rewards.

    5. All players should understand the rules and goals of a game, and be happy with the game they are playing. Differing rules, goals, narrowness and broadness have varying appeals to different individuals and groups.

    6. The level of narrative control given to players and the GM, whether there is a GM at all, or even to what degree roleplaying is part of the game has no bearing on whether a game is "good" or not.

    At this point we're starting to talk less about an overall game theory, and more about how to make a particular style of game.

    I believe that limiting players to the control of their characters, and leaving narrative control to the GM is a superior method to give players a sense of immersion in their character and the game world, than sharing greater narrative control between all the players. If immersion is not an important goal of the game (perhaps it's wargaming strategy or collaborative storytelling) then the balance of narrative control could likely be different.

    7. Categorizing gamers into types is somewhat short-sighted as people can enjoy multiple types of games and styles of play, often during the same gaming session.

    8. Yes. People who understand the rules and goals of a game should be able to play it despite it not being their favourite type of game. A player may prefer storytelling games to strategy games, but you would not expect that they would make bad moves in a game of chess because "the Knight is in love with the Queen and wants to be nearer to her..." People are capable of playing a wide variety of games, but must be agreeable to doing so.

    9. Absolutely. Suggesting people don't understand when they are enjoying playing a game is somewhat absurd. The only potential for confusion is if the players of a multi-faceted game have different ideas about what the goal of the game is, and are in fact playing different games at the same table.

    10. Genre Emulation and specifically Immersion (meaning character-immersion) can be the most important elements in an RPG. Other games can offer superior challenges in other areas, but this is where a roleplaying game is unlike most other types of game.

    So, formally? Let's go ahead and deconstruct and all. And as always, promote no inter-board hate and the like. Heck, we can even frame it with, "What do the Landmarks bring to the table for Story Games/Story Gaming?"

    So, first off, I'm fucking ecstatic that someone is creating a new theory/design powerhouse. Folks whine and bitch about how the tenets of Forge design are seeped in Ron Edwards' essays (even if the designers haven't read them and don't believe them! ...but yeah, that's another story) about gamers being "mostly unhappy" and all that. So instead of continuing the whiny bitch train, folks have set up a camp, saying "Here we believe in something else. We have fundamentally differing opinions of how this stuff should work, and vow to make games seeped in our beliefs". It's almost as refreshing a design goal as Chad Underkoffler's move to create Dead Inside ("Instead of killing people and taking their stuff, I designed a game where you heal people and give them stuff"). So, for that idea alone, I give them mad props.

    The thing is, and I won't dwell on it (I'm not one of those "I swing absolutism to win arguments" types)... if the structure of the umbrella you work under is seeped in "The majority of gamers are having fun; the majority of gamers are satisfied with the majority of their game"; and the bit about D&D being the Model Game... Then... er... what is there to design? More feats for D&D? House rules? Games that look exactly like D&D but aren't in some way? (ie the "Imagine RPG") Again, I'm not following this path to absolutism (like, "Therefore this can only result in..." etc), but 1, 2 and 3 generally seem to lead to, "Well shit guys, we're happy with what we've got. So let's just make up better houserules for what we have" instead of making something new.

    Still, though, I'm really excited that there'd be another forum for gaming that acts as another powerhouse for game design. Sure, it might create "camps" or "schools" or "sides" in more shocking contrast/conflict with each other, much like academia does, but I'd love to see what the works of another camp with another fundamental viewpoint and membership can come up with, rather than "this one dude over here; that one dude over there".

    So having said that, I'll give a once-through on the Landmarks in a sec.

    OK, similar to what others have said, with some minor differences:

    1: Cool. This is a nice place to start. Most people who do anything find at least some kernel of pleasure in it, or with what it could be, otherwise, fuck, they'd stop, right?

    2: OK, cool. Getting into more of relative-land here, but that's cool, cause the foundation of a belief system has to have a few of these beliefs. It does give the assumption that most people settle on One Game Only (Which is true; D&D is King; but doesn't explain the majority of gamers on the Internet who play more than 1, more than 5, more than 10 games. Again, it's true, but it doesn't speak to a lot of us here, on RPGNet, heck or even half of EnWorld).

    3: OK, again, good starting place for a tower-of-belief. Personally, it's a Jenga tower ready to fall with a few simple metaphors:
    "ARMEGEDDON is the model of what most people define as a movie, and therefore also the model for a successfully designed and produced movie. It can be improved upon or changed, but any theory that suggests that ARMAGEDDON as a whole (in any of its versions or cuts) was a "bad" movie is by definition in violation of My Theory. You don't have to say it is the "best" movie, but you are obviously not in touch with reality if your theory claims that ARMEGEDDON is a "bad" game, and then try to invent some convoluted conspiracy theory as to why millions of people watched it anyways, more than any other movie that year."

    I liked the movie Armegeddon, personally (save for the "6 second scene syndrome"), but the above theory, rephrased with anything else (Replace RPG with "music" and D&D with "MC Fergie" or "Jessica Simpson").

    4: Huh, this is interesting, because I don't know any game "camp" that "speculates that games must be narrowly-focused to be "good" games" (even though he's clearly speaking of the Forge here). Most people who write narrow-focus games, or play them, play them because they're Fun, not because they are tied to this "narrow=good" post. In much the same way in computer theory, we don't recommend the same tool for every job: Sometimes the big tool has to be reconfigured. Most of the time, we construct or find a smaller tool that does just what we want it to. I can't for the life of me understand how the fact that those kinds of games exist infuriates folks so. However, as a Step in the Landmarks, I find it sort of a Red Herring. Up until this point, we had a solid framework to think about designing games, and all of a sudden we've got this shot on "Hey, don't tell us what we can and can't enjoy!". "...oh-kayeee, we won't then." (Note to self: Find the Forge post where someone suggested that roleplayers would all be happier if they played narrowly-focused games)

    5: OK, I see the start of getting back on track to a methodology/belief system here. Here, again, RPGpundit is thinking that most (or all) Forge games have narrow rules because without them the gaming table would be all chaos and rioting, and that these narrow rules prevent players from "going nuts" (ie rather than work on better table manners and interactions, these games codify this in the rules). Every once in a while a rule hints at this kind of thing, but in most cases I see narrow rules as a way to focus a theme, not to limit players from being dicks. There's no drowning and falling rules in My Life With Master because the game isn't about drowning and falling, not to prevent someone from arguing about drowning and falling. Same reason there's no "fighting and takeover" rules in Catan. So again, I like the first sentence of number 5, and the last part of the last sentence, but the rest is a red-herring mini-tirade reacting to a misperception in small-press games that simply isn't there.

    6: Pundit states elsewhere re Number Six:
    Number 6 is specifically a response to the Forge-mentality that GMs are the root of all evil and that players must be able to walk all over a GM or else the game won't be "fair" or "satisfying" to them.
    Awesome. Well shit, I'm reacting to the World of Darkness-mentality that I have to eat ice cream 5 times a week!

    ...Huh?

    Seriously, there are a few games where the GM is another player. It's because, for that game, the game itself works better if the GM is another player. For example, Monopoly works better if there is One Banker (preferably me, because I steal money like a dog at Monopoly). Settlers of Catan works faster if Everyone is their Own Banker (ie takes their resources themselves).

    It does add a sort of constrictive dogma to this belief system, which again can be refreshing (we all like constraints in our Game Chef contests, so why not adopt it for this theory as well): All Games Developed Under This Banner Must Have A GM. The rest of number six is all bollucks and red herring.

    7: Cool. I mean, it doesn't get us any closer to writing our game, but hey, no biggie.

    8: By this point, when I read "...is in violation of the Landmarks", I can't help but to hear that Asmovian female comfort-robot voice in my ear reciting the Laws of Robotics. But aside from that, sure, 8 builds on 7. This smells like a reaction again to a misperception, but whatever. (I am G! And you are S! Therefore we cannot play together!!).

    9 + 10: Again, not really moving towards anything, rather these are saying "stop picking on me!". That's cool, not really productive but again it's fine.

    So at the end of the day, only 1-3, part of 5, and 6 really work towards any basis of a belief system for creating new games.

    OK, so I don't agree with Pundit 100%, but I can see where he is coming from:
    1) Basically means, don't post here if you think gaming today is disfunctional and you have come to fix it. Personally, I think that there is a group of indie designers out there that beleive that some amount of players are just going through the motions trying to re-discover that thrill they felt the first time they played a good session of roleplaying. I think that that is unfortunate as it basically mandates certain design techiniques and precludes other out of hand.
    2) Basically means, don't design a game because you are unhappy and you want to fix it for yourself and others. Design a game if you want to make an improvement or fill the minority gap not filled by the successful games. Personally, I think that this is pretty accurate. I think that games like ditv shows that the minority can prove to be very profitable and effective. But they are never going to de-throne the games that are already successful. I think Pundits view is a little over stated, but not much. I don't think its really aimed at Forge-ites as much as new eager designers that think they will write the next D&D and then go buy their own island, but it is still a pretty valid point...
    3) I think he is sayting that a lot of communication can be improved if you take a step back and recognize that you can use D&D as a lingua franca to communicate with other players and designers. In some small way I agree. I think that the tendancey to try too hard to be different from D&D and the efforts made to re-invent the terminology is distracting and counter-productive to communicating your own theories and designs ideas. Don't misunderstand me. When the day is done, you have to make a game that is uniqely your own. But, don't throw out the baby with the bath water...
    4) I think he is saying, asking "What is your game about?" is not necesarily a step at improving someone elses game. Not every game has to have a central theme to be a good game. Basically, he wants to ascert that you can't lable a game as good or bad based on whether the game tackles issues or themes. I think that taken at face value, this is mostly true. Of course, I would say the opposite is true as well, You can't say a generic game is better than a narrowly defined game.
    5) I think this boils down to just because a play session goes bad, that doesn't mean that there is something wrong with the game being played at the time. And again, I would mostly agree. System does matter, but system may not cause happiness or unhappiness. I mean, System can make you pay attention to something you don't care about. But, system cannot make you get into a fight with your friend, can it?
    6) I think 6 boils down to two things: one, don't tear D&D (or other successful games) down to pump up your game and two, you can't tell if a game is good or bad just by asking if it has a GM. Personaly, I think the question, "does your game really need a GM" is overplayed. I get why it is asked, but it almost has too much currency. don't get me wrong, I think GM-less games can be a great source of fun and I have written one. But, i don't think knowing that a game has a GM is an indicator one way or the other if a game is good.
    7) This is obviously aimed at GNS specifically, but it is worded to apply to old school player types like Macho vs. Looney, etc. And I think the point of the rule is to prevent dogma wars. I think its a good idea, I mean how much time has been wasted trying to determine if a game is for X type of player or what X type of player likes, doesn't like can play with, what it actually means, etc.
    8) This is just re-inforcing 7, don't come in here and say All players are either G, N or S and never the twain shall meet. And I tend to agree. I think GNS has a place in RPG theory, but I think that the terms Gamism, Narrativism and Simulationism were poorly chosen and poorly defined with stilted language. Don't get me wrong, that doesn't make the theory invalid, it just makes it hard to communicate and use effectively.
    9) I think he is just trying to say, don't kid yourself, D&D players have fun. You can make another kind of fun, but don't try and say its the first kind of RPG fun ever created. Seems like he is just re-stating 1 & 2, but at least he's consistant. Again, I think there are players that wish A was better or wish B was better, but few players who are saying to themselves, "this is total crap, but I'll keep playing and see if it gets better"
    10) Is just saying that it is possible to create a consistant game world and play in character. Again, I can agree with that. I don't think this one is aimed at the forge-ites, and I don't think he means hardcore immersion like in LARPs or whatever, but I could be wrong.

    I can't say I would word it like that or that I agree 100%. But, I can see, and respect, where he is coming from.

    September 2006
    Things I think designers can learn from D&D:

    1) Having a mechanism where players are improving their in-game position over time is powerful. So powerful, that you can literally make a game where nothing else of interest is happening (Diablo). "Level Up!"
    2) "Level Up" provides a default goal for players. People who have no interest in engaging deeply with the creative aspects of the game can grab hold of this goal and have fun with the others. When creativity flags, a player can fall back on this as a spur to action. And players are still free to create and pursue other goals for themselves.
    3) Having a GM-created and controlled world lends a weight of verisimilitude to the proceedings that an obviously impromptu setting does not. Players can explore and feel that they are making "real" discoveries. Players improve their in-game position and feel they have created something "real."
    4) GM-privleged power distribution means that one person can fudge, provide on-the-fly content, and nudge the action toward areas of high awesome-potential without lifting the veil of verisimilitude.
    5) A GM-privleged responsibility distribution means that one talented person with an agenda of providing awesome potential for everyone can make a game a success for everyone. Some other models need everyone to step up or else the game falls flat.
    6) Particularly in its magic system, D&D provides an enormous complexity and breadth of pre-determined "handles" for the player. A player can explore this space and find something for his character to do in a given situation. He is left with the cool feeling that he invented a clever and unique solution. And because he found this solution among a pre-determined maze of possibilites, it feels more "real" than if he had been allowed to riff any solution at all.
