    \n I have an abiding interest in Gam + Nar (or Nar + Gam) hybrid or transitional play. I wish I had insight that wasn't splattered all over the other threads connected to or tangential to this discussion, but alas, I don't.
    \n
    \nI'm trying to decide, will games like IaWA, or the forthcoming BRS or HotB scratch that itch at all? And these types of discussions help me assess that, to some degree (recognizing that Actual Play accounts - useful ones that include some indication of what's going on among the players at the table - are equally good data points for such evaluations).
    \n
    \nSpecifically, I'm looking for a game that supports play that *feels* like I'm making tactical choices that have real teeth within the system of the game, but that these systems don't interfere overly much with how we, as players, build the SIS and/or address Premise.
    \n
    \nCapes, for me, is an object example of this. The concepts behind the game are mechanically very neat. But in two sessions of one shot play, those same neat concepts really, really got in the way of how we wanted to build the SIS. We were free to address premise to a satisfying extent, but the game was un-fun for us as a group.
    \n
    \nSo, I'm a believer that transitional play or hybrid play supporting games are already out there, I'm just looking for one that better fits my play style.
    \n
    \nSecondarily, I'm a Game Theory hobbyist junkie and inveterate game system hacker that loves this kind of design wonkery. Which explains a lot more why I don't have any friends than my evil thread-necromancer tendencies ;-).

    \n Blood Red Sands (BRS) looks like it attempts something similar to what I'm working on with Agora: that is, it is a pretty straightforwardly gamist design where players will be competing with each other throughout the game, and it throws off a cool and character-driven story as a byproduct. That these games kick off a neat story is a (very cool and intentionally designed) bonus.

Right now, having read half of the book, I'm prone to view this as a weakness in the design (and I wonder how often challenges actually come up in play) \u2014 but I haven't finished the book nor played yet, so my opinion should be considered heavily qualified.

    \n I agree. This touches on an earlier thread where Marshall contrasted Play to Win with Disco/Heavy Metal Samurai play.
    \n
    \nIn the latter, you play to win...but you do so within the voluntary (but just-as-important-as-mechanical-rules) confine that maintaining (or better...enhancing) the group aesthetic is one of the accepted contraints of play.
    \n
    \nIf you play IaWA without that constraint, I think you can have a rough time of it. But played "Heavy Metal Samurai" style you can totally play hard to win. Because the aesthetic is part of the win condition.
    \n
    \nThis is pretty much what Moreno is saying, but I think an important distinction is where the emphasis is put. I think this respect for the group aesthetic is the default standard for measuring Gamism and can be assumed to be inherent in the phrase "Step on up". By that I mean that the definitional phrase is not "Play to win at any cost". Step on up automatically implies social appreciation as the prize for winning. Playing to "win" where you are fighting for your character's goals irrespective of the group aesthetic is rather a more extreme hard core variant of Gamism...not the standard.
    \n
    \nEdited to add:
    \n
    \nTo tie this into earlier questions on Blood Red Sands, this is very much the design paradigm BRS is written within. The Challenge Mechanic, I view as a strength because it provides an ingame mechanism for testing and reinforcing this aesthetic. When gestures, sighs, and eyerolls fail one can resort to the rules rather than to charisma and social manipulation. Because the implementation has something of a Viking boasting flair to it (with people standing up to loom over the people their Challenging...and having to utter the phrase "as you will" when they fail and have to submit) it keeps players in the spirit of the fiction even as it resolves a meta level dispute.

    Posted By: JasonLeigh
    In both of these things, what I read is this "if your group has a good understanding of the fiction you want in the game, amping the Step On Up shouldn't be a problem". Sound about right?
    \nI think that the answer would be "how much" you would amp the Step on Up. And what you mean by step on up. I noticed that what passes for "step on up" for many story-gamers is a very, very cute, educated, timid and good-natured version of the real beast... ;-)

    (I think I remember this was the subject of a good thread some months ago, but I seem to be unable to find the link now...)

    In any case, I think that it could support Step on Up only as a very distant secondary priority. And I have a lot of doubts even about that. I don't think it endorse a Gamist creative Agenda (as it was written in the post I was responding to) at all.

    Reading again my last post I noticed that I didn't qualify well what I was talking about. A natural question would have been "but if you don't have a solid creative consensus, you can't play any narrativist game. What it's the difference in IAWA?".

    It's not a big difference, so it's not like I think that it's not a narrativist game or something like that. I think that it's an harder, more difficult game that it seems, though.

    Let's try to explain it using an example. Vincent will probably hate me now because I will use DitV, but really I couldn't find a better example of what I was talking about. In DitV you have both a veto system where any player can stop a disruptive raise, and a lot of bargaining between GM and players, or between players, first to set the right stakes, then to get the right (acceptable) raises and sees. The effect that I have seen playing it many times is that the system really HELP to reach that shared understanding of the fiction you need to get good results from the game. If you are willing to play by the rules, and you don't have some kind of strong personal issue with some other player, getting everybody on the same page is only a question of time. A very short time usually. So I have been able to play DitV even with not "perfect" group, or even with unknown people at conventions. Not that convention games has ever been too great, without the time needed to "attune" the group enough, but it got near enough to play good enough results.

    IAWA instead take that shared understanding as a given, before playing, and during the game not only doesn't really help you a lot to reach it, but TEST it. I was really surprised, playing it, about how much it re-opened some problems that I though were solved a lot of months ago in our game group. Not insurmountable problems. We talked about it and we solved them enough to continue playing IAWA. But it had to be done outside of the game session. And I could never play a IAWA session with people at a convention that didn't get, during the game, to a point where that shared understanding did break down.

    As I said, nothing too problematic for a lot of people here. But I think that IAWA is, from this point of view, a second-generation game. It's not anymore for people dissatisfied with their gaming, as it was the case for a lot of first-generation games at the forge (and was for DitV, that is written to a implied reader that know very well traditional rpgs and it's dissatisfied with them). It's for people who are very satisfied with the group consensus they have reached, so much that they, really, could play together without any written rules.
