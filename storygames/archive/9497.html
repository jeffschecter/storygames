\n I ran a playtest session of my game about vampires and victims, ... and cast no shadow at Camp Nerdly. Well, "ran" is maybe a bit strong of a word, since it's GMless -- "hosted", maybe?
\n
\nAnyway, players were Danielle Lewon, Paul Czege, Sandy Smith-Casey (my wife), and me. We started off with choosing setting. Sandy and I left it up to Danielle and Paul, who decided on an unspecified future time on a space station. Next was character creation; one of the unusual bits of ACNS is that everyone makes two characters.
\n
\nDanielle created Seth, a somewhat urbane vampire with the ability to walk through walls; and Sister Mary, an old nun. Paul made Tim, a child-vampire with power over rats and rat-like features; and The Bright Defender, a wannabe superhero. Sandy created Cavy, a bartender of not-determined sex; and Corrina, a cleaning woman. I made a doctor (whose name I forget -- he didn't wind up used in play); and Jerome, a high school student.
\n
\nWith the characters made, Danielle decided to start off Sister Mary, Paul chose The Bright Defender, and Sandy chose Corrina. I chose not to pick a character for the first chapter, instead taking the option to swap among different "minor" characters.
\n
\nNext step was establishing potential relationships. Sandy and I have had a lot of fun with this in the past, and from after comments, Paul and Danielle liked it as well. In ACNS, the players on each end of a relationship each pick what that relationship could be. We started off with relationships among the major characters for the first chapter (i.e., the ones that people had picked to play):
\n
\nThe Bright Defender (is protective of)/(loves) Corrina.
\nCorrina (is inspired by)/(is scared by) the Bright Defender.
\n
\nThe Bright Defender (respects)/(ignores) Sister Mary.
\nSister Mary (feels protected by)/(knows the secret identity of) the Bright Defender.
\n
\nCorinna (idolizes)/(thinks is smart) Sister Mary.
\nSister Mary (feels motherly to)/(is best friends with) Corrina.

    \nIs that all there is to setting creation? What do the rules say on the matter? I assume the rules specify (only implicitly, perhaps) that the setting needs to have vampires, at the least, right?

    The short version of the rules that I had with me doesn't say anything about setting creation. My current draft says:\n
    First, the players should agree on the setting of the game, and what optional rules they wish to use. The setting answers the questions "where", "when", and "who" for the "here and now" of the story, and possibly for flashbacks. They may also want to discuss what kinds of characters they want to play. No real details are needed at this point, just general ideas.
    \n... and then provides two examples. The need for vampires isn't stated, but is implicit.\n
    Posted By: Adam DrayCan a player create relationships between her own two characters? Is there an advantage or disadvantage to doing this?
    \nYou can only "own" one character at a time -- the "minor" characters who are not "owned" can change hands many times in the course of play. The rules do specify that, if possible, you have to create your initial two relationships to other "major" (i.e., owned) characters. Thus, in the situation in this playtest, with three major characters, each had to create a relationship to each of the other major characters.

    Later on, when its your turn to frame a scene, you could frame a scene with the character you're playing and the other character you created. However, that other character would be played by someone else for that scene.

    There can be advantages -- for one, you could "craft" a character to be vulnerable in a particular way, and the other character to take advantage of that vulnerability -- e.g., making one character a beautiful redhead, and giving the other a weakness for redheads. This could make it easy to cement a relationship, which you could later use to "call on" that character in conflicts, getting extra dice from them.

    This is intended to be part of the strategy of the game, particularly for victims, who are weak compared to vampires. My basis for including it is Lucy's three suitors in Dracula and, even more, Dracula's three "brides". Some of these characters seem to exist in the story purely to bolster other characters, allowing them to indirectly do things they can't do directly.\n
    Posted By: Adam DrayCan a player create two mortal or two vampire characters? Is there an advantage or disadvantage to doing this?
    \nYou can do either. Vampires are stronger than Victims, so a Vampire-heavy game is likely to be shorter -- the Victims really need to help each other out to "win".

    I should note, though, that the game doesn't have any "victory conditions". There are suggested "end conditions" -- e.g., when there are no vampires left in play, or when no vampire has an unsatisfied goal -- but since character ownership is fluid, these are more suggestions for "a good time to end the story" than anything else.

    \nSure. I'll take Seth's attempt to seduce The Bright Defender to "the dark side". I am making up the numbers, Keywords, and Weaknesses -- I may actually be right on some of them, but I wouldn't count on it.

    The scene started with Danielle, playing Seth as her major, framing it. She decided that she wanted The Bright Defender as the other character in the scene, and that the scene was taking place after dark, out in the open. Seth was waiting to spot The Bright Defender on patrol, and when he did, visibly accosted a young woman. (The young woman wasn't a created character, and thus, had no stats of her own -- she was simply a bit of narrative color allowing Seth to use his abilities.)

    Danielle had already established Seth's chapter goal as "dominating" The Bright Defender. To do this, she needed to reduce The Bright Defender's Will to zero, from its starting value of three. This establishes the "stakes" of the scene as Mental, meaning that the vampiric traits of Beast and Mastery can be affected, and the victim traits of Prowess and Will. Vampires' Darkness and victims' Innocence are off-limits without raising the stakes.

    Things start with open narration, each side saying what they're doing. Seth started a "join me" sort of speech, drawing on his Mastery. Danielle chose not to use all of Seth's Mastery, however, as each point of a vampiric trait used raises the corresponding Need, and bad things start to happen if Needs get too high. She also used Seth's Keywords of "Possession" -- by speaking through the girl, offering her to The Bright Defender -- and "Charming". She also used one of The Bright Defender's Weaknesses, "respects authority figures". Each point of Mastery used gave her a die to roll, as did each Keyword or opponent's Weakness. Using five points of Mastery (and thereby raising Mastery's Need, Desire, by five points) along with those two Keywords and one Weakness gave her eight dice.

    On the other side, The Bright Defender stepped forward and tried to pull the girl away from Seth, gaining dice from his "Prowess" of four. He also used his "protects the innocent" Keyword for another die, and his "does the right thing" Keyword for another, giving six dice. Looking at Seth's weaknesses, he didn't find one he wanted to use. He could have chosen to use Seth's Mastery Weakness of "Virgins", but chose not to have The Bright Defender be a virgin.

    They rolled, eight dice versus six -- even numbers are failures, odds are successes. Danielle rolled two more successes than Paul. Paul could use his successes to cancel Danielle's, but that would still leave her two which she could apply to his Will -- which would be enough to take it to zero.

    Now, at this point, Paul had two options. He could have stopped there and burned Vitality points to protect his Will, preventing Danielle from taking him to zero. With a Will of only two and only two Vitality, though, that would have left him with only one Will and one Vitality -- a bad position for future scenes. So instead, he chose to take the second option, and Raise Stakes.

    The Stakes were raised from Mental to Spiritual, meaning that Darkness and Innocence could now be affected. Each side kept the successes they already had, and a new round of narration began. Among the limits of raising stakes is that you can't use what you've already used in the scene again -- so Danielle used Seth's Darkness instead of Mastery, calling on supernatural abilities, and Paul used The Bright Defender's Will instead of Prowess. Seth didn't find any new Keywords or Weaknesses to use, but Paul decided that The Bright Defender was beginning to realize that Seth was something other than human, and called on a couple of Seth's Weaknesses -- I remember Seth having one that revealed he was a vampire, but I don't remember what it was.

    Dice were rolled again. This time, luck was on Paul's side. Danielle gained no new successes, but Paul gained four, giving him two more successes than Danielle had. He cancelled all of Danielle's successes, then used his two remaining ones to decrease Seth's Mastery. End of scene narration went to Paul, as the only one actually spending successes, and he narrated The Bright Defender managing to wrest the girl away from Seth and fly away, carrying her off to safety.

    \nYou realize you're begging me to target this, by mentioning a goth-looking kid, so I'm going to....

    It's about vampires, and how they need to feed on humans, but at the same time need human companionship to keep from becoming something totally other. It's about needing someone, needing them so badly that you'll literally die without them, and the kind of twisted relationships that arise out of that.

    (presuming he keeps listening...)

    That's one thing it can be about -- the "Twilight" end of things, if you will. On the flip side, it can be about vampires as monsters, like in 1800s horror stories and '60s vampire movies. Played that way, its about the victims, and how they have to forge real relationships with each other in order to defeat a seemingly overwhelming foe.

    Underlying both, it's about relationships -- healthy ones, where people help each other, and unhealthy ones, where one person feels the need to dominate and control another.

    You can play Stoker's little band of vampire hunters, trying to hold onto each other in the face of Dracula.

    You can play Buffy, fighting vampires with supernatural abilities of her own, but needing her friends to keep her from going crazy doing it.

    You can play the late '70s Dracula, trying to regain his lost love against the massive forces of his own cursed existence.

    You can play Hammer's Van Helsing, a man trying to fight overwhelming supernatural evil by making others believe in it too.

    You can play Angel, struggling with your curse as you try to do the right thing, and hoping for love as part of it.

    You can play Edward Cullen, torn between desire for the one you love and the overwhelming truth that you are the thing that puts her in danger.

    The only thing the game really requires is that vampires exist, and they need to feed on humans in certain ways. Everything else is up for grabs. You can make the kind of vampire you believe in, and either play it or play those struggling against it.

    Posted By: Adam DrayThanks for the scene explanation. There's a lot of cool stuff in there! I like how increasing the stakes brings different kinds of abilities to draw.     \nIn normal contests, you only escalate up the path: Physical -> Mental -> Spiritual. In an end-of-chapter conflict, the loser of the initial contest can't escalate normally, but can "escalate" down the chain by choosing to accept Consequences. It's probably easiest to explain this with an example:

    Let's say that a vampire named Arthur is trying to dominate a victim named Katie. In the course of the chapter, Arthur has brought Katie's Innocence down to zero. This triggers an End of Chapter conflict, where we decide whether Arthur really manages to dominate Katie, or she somehow escapes that fate (either way, though, the Chapter will end).

    The contest is rolled, and Katie's player loses. Since this is an end-of-chapter conflict, Katie can't escalate normally. What she can do is get another try by taking a Consequence; the Consequences available are (a) the consequences of losing a lower conflict and (b) "escaping out", which having the character escape but removing them from the pool of characters in the game.

    Thus, Katie's player can escalate to Physical for this, specifying whether Katie will either die instead of being dominated (the consequences of losing a Physical end of chapter, for a victim), or will "escape out" and exit the story.

    At least, that's the current terminology. I might do better to use different terms for the end-of-chapter loser thing....

One-on-one, vampires tend to always beat victims, since they have a lot more points to put into their traits. To have a real hope against a vampire, a victim needs to get help from another victim.     \n Yes, one player has a Victim and one has a Vampire. The scene could have been started by either player, in the normal course of going around starting scenes.

    To bring in a helper, you "call on" that character. To do this, the "called on" character has to have a relationship to your character. If the called on character is not currently being played, or the character's player agrees to enter the scene, then no dice roll is needed. If the character is being played and the player doesn't wish to enter the scene, then there is a dice roll, of Will vs. Will.

    Once in the scene, if the character called on has a player, that player rolls dice for the character and spends them. If the character does not have a player, the player who called on that character rolls the dice for that character and spends them.

    Edit: Blah, too much happening this morning...

    The limit of how many dice the "called on" character can contribute is the strength of their relationship to you. Thus, if the relationship is only at 3, they can only contribute a maximum of three dice.

    \n When contesting over a relationship, each side can put their successes into increasing the strength of one of the alternatives, to a limit of 5. A relationship alternative that reaches strength 5 is "cemented". If cementing that relationship was a chapter goal, that triggers an end of chapter. Regardless, a cemented relationship can't be touched until the next end of chapter (the remainder of the current chapter, if it didn't end the chapter, or all of the next chapter, if it did end the chapter).
