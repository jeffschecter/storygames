\n I noticed in both the texts of Beast Hunters and Hero's Banner that there is a clause warning that PCs can't accomplish their goal for the adventure with a single roll, that is, the stakes of a single roll can't simply be that they get the ancient sword or whatever. Now, of course this makes sense, you don't want anyone to just roll once and then win or lose. But, as a friend of mine put it when I brought this up with her earlier, doesn't this lead to a sort of Zeno's Paradox?

In contrast, in BW it often is possible to accomplish some Beliefs with just a single roll. That game provides so much push-back in so many ways that it's fine.

Thoughts? Is this just not a problem in actual play?

Matt

    \n This is easily solved by asking the player, "How?"

    If they can't give you a single course of action that their character can accomplish right here, in the scope of the immediate fiction that will result in achieving their goal then they can't roll for it.

    Example:

    Player: I want to over throw the king!
    GM: Great, what is your character doing to do that?
    Player: Well, first I'm going to form a secret mercenary band.
    GM: Sounds like a good first step. How do you do that?"
    Player: Well, I, uh, I guess, I need to find some people who aren't very loyal to the king.
    GM: Good, Good. How do you do that?
    Player: I guess, I go to the tavern and start listening for rumors of political malcontents.
    GM: Excelent. So there you are at the tavern nursing an ale and listening to the chatter. Want to roll Gossip for me?

    See?

    \n Jesse's example shows a common pattern that often works in games: The rule of three.

    When a task is one roll it is unsatisfying. When it is three steps it is strangely satisfying. If it is five steps it can begin to feel too complicated. So the rule of three.

    When games fall into a reductionist trap they are exploring the idea that total knowledge leads to total realism. It doesn't work but it is an oft trod path.

    \n Burning Wheel rarely makes you lose everything with a single roll! A lost roll may close off an avenue of pursuit towards a goal, but only until the situation changes significally.

    What this means is that if you fail to persuade the King to let you marry his daughter, you may still try to convince him later on. Perhaps a few years on, when you've been dubbed Knight and 'Defender of the Realm' and are seen as a Hero to the common folk (an quite a few nobles) of the Kingdom. Nobody said fulfilling your beliefs would be easy, but you rarely lose everything in a single roll, though you might effectively block an avenue to to your goal.


    The Fellowship: "We won't risk the mines, we'll cross the Misty Mountains and hope to avoid detection by these Eyes of Saruman, whatever they may be!"

    GM: "Ok, how will you accomplish this?"

    The Fellowship: "Aragorn will lead us with Orienteering, Legolas will help with Song of Pathways, Boromir will help with Tactics and Gandalf (NPC) will help too?"

    GM: "OK, but if you fail a Mighty Snow Storm\u2122 will block the Mountains and if you do not hurry, you'll be detected by the Eyes of Saruman\u2122!"

    \n Framing this issue as "winning in single roll" is misleading to begin with. Whether accomplishing something with a single roll is OK depends on pacing in the context of the narrative. Any single fictional task you might imagine would be perfectly acceptable as a single roll in all three of these games as long as taking the roll would be neither too insignificant to hold the group's interest nor too momentous so as to cause the depth of the narrative to suffer. Obviously you need some sort of pacing, as you don't want to get stuck fiddling with minor details, but you don't want to obviate your interesting set-up by skipping the meat of it on a pro forma dice roll.

    I mean, overthrowing a king on a single roll would be perfectly acceptable if the king were not a focus of play and the dramatic arc required it to be over with quickly and without hassle. Most flexible dramatic games give you pacing discretion for exactly this reason: we don't know in advance whether any single type of activity will be sufficiently important to make a big deal of. D&D tells us that a swordfight is always worthy of a few dozen dice rolls, but all these stakes-setting games with universal conflict resolution systems specifically leave these decisions to play.

    For this reason the game designer often has to make the choice: either he gives some simplistic rules of thumb to carry people over - and most importantly, cause them to pay attention to the issue - or he has to give real names to the issues of skill involved, and then teach players to make choices accordingly. "You can't achieve your most important goal in a single roll, even if you're constantly achieving smaller things on single rolls" is a reasonable rule of thumb about how stories need can't happen without pacing, while pacing a story would be the underlying skill that makes the simple rule of thumb redundant.

    (Obviously enough most games also include rules about pacing instead of just advice, it's one of the top three rules mechanics topics alongside resolution and framing. Most games don't have universal pacing rules, though, which requires the players to display some skill of their own.)

    I should also note that Jesse's practical "How?"-heuristic is an excellent pacing tool for games where players might otherwise be prone to disassociative narration. The reason it works is that it enforces fictional positioning: you certainly can get the ancient sword on a single roll, but only if you've already positioned your character in the fiction such that you can clearly describe your eventual success. And you'll only basically be able to do this if the preceding events in play have sufficiently dealt with everything between you and the sword. This works for many games, as positioning and pacing are so often intertwined.

    \nI encountered this recently with a homebrew I've been developing, and I'll tell you, the importance of positioning and pace are critical. Here was my mental dilemma: "What if the really little guy wants to slay the mighty dragon...would one roll work?"

    At first, I tried all sorts of crazy luck/extraordinary circumstance mechanics...all to no avail. Then I thought about "positioning" (which I find a very appropriate term for this - you'll see why).

    Compare positioning to the endgame in chess where you are on the path to victory. Every move you make can bring you a step closer to checkmate. Literally, however, you have to position your pieces correctly (or at least pretty well). Failure to do so could cost you an win.

    I realized that a "how do you do this" questioning system forces a player to think along these lines. By incorporating this simple clause, the player must act cleverly. It's very much like Socratic education - drawing the answers from within the player/student!

    (Ultimately, the little guy slays the dragon by hiding in its lair until it is asleep, quietly sneaking toward the precipice above his head, careful not to slip in the dragon's mighty drool, and drives his spear directly into the terrible beast's skull! I count at least three or four rolls there)
