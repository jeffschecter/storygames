    Design Observer has an article about instruction as an artform and controlling an interactive user-generated experience. Kind of a heady description, but it's pretty big portion of what we do as game designers and what we're doing a lot now that we have all these contests and challenges. The examples include artists ranging from the Beastie Boys to Yoko Ono.

    Check out this excerpt:


    The rock band Yeah Yeah Yeahs have also been experimenting with art directing their fans. The cover of their most recent album, Show Your Bones, is the result of a flag-making contest; another band-sponsored contest had fans voting on what outfit lead-singer Karen O should wear; in another, fans competed to play the bandmembers in a video for their song â€œCheated Hearts.â€ If Sol LeWitt left any doubt that there is an art to writing rules, consider these guidelines for the flag-making competition:

    1) Whatever size you want.
    2) Whatever colours you like.
    3) Relative to the Yeah Yeah Yeahs.
    4) Embodying the symbolic message that you have vibed off the band totally to the max.
    5) Recognisable as above all a flag that can be stuck on a pole and waved around.

    The language blends detached irony with reckless enthusiasm in a way that is instantly recognizable to a fan of the Yeah Yeah Yeahsâ€™ music. Through these contests fans learn to interpret the rules more accurately and the band the learns how to give effective direction.

    That article name checks Awesome! I Fuckin' Shot That!, which really brought the idea of instruction as art home for me. The best parts for me were the mosaic shots where you saw the same moment from the perspective of many, many Hi-8 cameras - unedited and visceral. The rest seemed lacking, mostly because I always imagined a better shot from another camera on the editing room floor, abandoned in the interest of a coherent "concert film" narrative.
