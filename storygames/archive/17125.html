\n Why did the nine hikers in the Dyatlov Pass mutilate themselves and each other before dying in the snow? What is the connection between Ixtab, Mayan goddess of suicide, and the US-backed overthrow of Salvador Allende, democratically-elected President of Guatemala? Why did CIA agents disseminate vast quantities of crack cocaine throughout Los Angeles? Why did Angels destroy the Triad region of North Carolina, butchering millions in a matter of hours?

Spite, a role-playing game of supernatural horror, has been available as a free PDF for some time.

As of today, it is also open source an open game. The Open Sores License allows you to revise/remix the text of Spite and distribute/sell your own RPG materials.

Follow the link for details and a link to the raw text file!

Please feel free to share/torrent/whatever.

Link to Open Sores document: http://www.rafaelchandler.com/blogger/?p=348

    \n Hi Rafael,

    Thanks for licensing Spite! I've made a page for it on my wiki, the FOSsil Bank. If there's anything you'd like me to add and change, I can do that.

    Unfortunately, the Open Sores License doesn't qualify as an open source licence: open source licences can't restrict how the licensed work can be used (for example, you've required that over half of the derived work be the contributor's own work, that they don't make board games, etc.)

    You could probably describe Spite as an 'open game', because open gaming is used a lot more loosely than open source is.

    Hope this helps.

    \n Open source doesn't restrict commercial use. In fact i remember a forum flame back in the day about someone selling Battle for Wesnoth (open source game) which ended with one of the creators stating clearly that they didn't violate the licence. Still, OS licences are designed for software specificaly.
    You could go with one of the Creative Commons licences as a basis for yours, just with added restrictions you mentioned, or forget about them at your choice. CC licences have many variations, including non-commercial or even no deriative works clauses.
