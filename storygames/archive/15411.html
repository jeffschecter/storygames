    \n- What made you want to do this, before you even tried?
    My high school gaming group consisted of 15+ people, not including teachers who would occasionally join in. Almost everyone took turns GMing. GMing was just something everyone did. We also shared the costs of all our gaming books.

    - What made youactuallyGM the first time? How old were you?
    I was 16 at a party and our Shadowrun GM didn't feel like running. He turned to me and said, "you are entertaining, you would probably do a good job, go for it."

    - Who did you GM for?
    I GMed for a group of 9-13 people with over 10 people watching at a loud party and I had little idea how the Shadowrun rules worked. After going almost all freeform, we stopped after an hour.

    - What do you wish you knew back then?
    Start with a group of 4 people or less. Probably not at a party. Don't run Shadowrun, go with something rules light. Read books on drama, project management, improv, psychology, and acting.

    - Why did you continue?
    I didn't for a while. But my gaming group was very supportive of new GMs so I had plenty of chances.

    Eventually GMing became one of my favorite activities and I've traveled all over the world playing with GMs people have said were the best of the best to learn their techniques. I've amassed quite a bit of GMIng lore. It's incredibly fun. I also build communities and love hosting events. GMing feels like hosting so it goes hand in hand with my interests. It's also a great way to make friends and I enjoy creating experiences people look forward to after a busy week of stress.

    \n- What made you want to do this, before you even tried?

    Well, when D&D first hit the Wargaming club I hung out at in '74, a bunch of games sprung up right away. But there was never enough GMs for the number of people who wanted to play. Also, the GM got to do stuff all of the time, whereas players had to wait their turn.

    - What made you actually GM the first time? How old were you?

    See above. I put together a map and simple situation/dungeon (The Shattered Towers -- the remains of the home base of a Wizards Guild that had been 'nuked' during the Mage Wars (even then I didn't believe in random dungeons)), and walked in one Saturday and offered to run. I was probably 16, but either 15 or 17 are possible.

    - Who did you GM for?

    A bunch of people at the club who knew me, and including, to my surprise, Glenn Blacow.

    - What do you wish you knew back then?

    Not to run with too many players at once. That random character creation was pretty much unfair.

    - Why did you continue?

    The bread and chicks of course, and the sweet, sweet power. Mu-ha-hah! Actually, because I enjoyed it a lot, and because the club really needed more GMs.

    \n The first two answers are more or less the same -- I started playing in high school (AD&D2e), the new guy in an established group. Over the course of a school year, we played four or five campaigns, some short-lived and others long-term, and different people DMed them. There was an expectation that you "owed it to the group" to run a game for everyone else, and the peer pressure worked on me. I also wanted to play a game that wasn't D&D, and that wasn't happening often enough, so I thought that running a non-D&D game of my own might help.

    That said, I ran my first game for other friends from school, because I wasn't comfortable jumping into DMing by running a game for that group. I wish that back then I had known, christ, ANYTHING that I knew now. I floundered around, had no idea how to play NPCs, had no idea how to organize anything, it was a mess. I guess the big thing I would want my younger self to know is that I should never run a game unless I really, really want to run that game, because it's only fun being the GM when I'm enthusiastic about doing it. GMing because it's my turn or no one else wants to or any other reason just sucks all the fun out of it for me.

    I continued initially because I thought, "Hey, I can't possibly do any worse next time," and because I don't like giving up on something after just one attempt. And once I learned a few things about how to run a game, it got easier and more fun, so it wasn't a hardship to keep going with it. I started paying attention to what good GMs were doing and asking questions, and trying out stuff on my own, and it started to fall into place.

    \n - What made you want to do this, before you even tried?
    The 3e monster manual. My first RPG book I'd seen, it just got me into the hobby. The DMG was my second book, and I knew I wanted to GM.

    - What made you actually GM the first time? How old were you?
    I introduced the hobby to a couple of close friends, and I was much more well-read in D&D than any of them- most of them had never read any of it, so I was considered GM by default. I was 14.

    - Who did you GM for?
    All close friends, 4 of them(usually 3 at any given time, scheduling etc.) One who knew nothing, one was an avid 40k wargamer, one had read the 3.5 PHB, and one had heard of D&D, but not much beyond that.

    - What do you wish you knew back then?
    Surprisingly, not that much. Maybe it's nostalgia, but I have extremely fond memories of the first few adventures I ran. I also recall doing it mostly through improvising, and doing a lot of the things that I've now learned were very good. It was later that I degraded a bit and fell into some railroading, so I guess I wish my past self knew to stay the course.

    - Why did you continue?
    Because we loved it! It was a very satisfying night for everyone, and they all wanted me to keep running it! It didn't last long though - not because of group breakage or me getting worse, but because others wanted to try. So I took a loooong break in the player seat. I'm usually the one to GM for newly introducing games still though, which I find very fun.

    Because I have the best fun, stories and friends ever as a result of gaming (and to some extent GMing).

    \n - What made you want to do this, before you even tried?
    I've always wanted to write a book, but I've not got the attention span necessary to sit down and hammer out the pages. GMing allows me to get the story seeds I've got in my head into action, so to speak. I've always created small stories, or the beginnings of stories. GMing seemed like the logical next step, with the added bonus of being surprised when the story takes a different turn than I originally expected.

    - What made you actually GM the first time? How old were you?
    I bought Call of Cthulhu. Someone had to GM, and I owned/read the book.

    - Who did you GM for?
    The same group I've always played with, friends from kindergarten and up. Roleplaying games create lifelong friendships!

    - What do you wish you knew back then?
    That railroading is ok sometimes, but mostly not. That all conflicts need a reason, and that they all need to have an outcome the players can get behind. And also that the dice/cards/whatever are there for a reason, and that they need to be used. And lastly, that the rules are just rules, not commandments.

    - Why did you continue?
    The laughs, the stories and the mountain of dead dragons. I'll never stop thinking up stories about things, and I really like the dynamic outlet GMing and roleplaying games offer in that respect. Also, I get to feel cool!

    \n - What made you want to do this, before you even tried?

    There was allure associated with being the center of attention, the authority and the creative driver.

    I was maybe ten. I played with my parents and my mom ran our first adventure and then my dad took the role of primary GM for years. By the time I was 16, I was a sought-after GM by my folks and at cons and stuff. More one-shot or short-game stuff than real campaigns.

    - Why did you continue?

    Someone has to. It's not my preference usually but sometimes you have to chip in.

    \nWhat made you want to do this, before you even tried?
    I read Call of Cthulhu and I wanted to play it, but no one else in our group seemed interested in GMing it. Also, I was in the punk scene, and an Eagle Scout veteran of hundreds of campfires, and was already super-enthusiastic about participatory media. Oh, and the Oulipo! I had just discovered them, too. I was charged up about the idea of game rules as potential literatures, and Cthulhu was already a well-worn potential literature in traditional, written form. It seemed like a good set of constraints for other forms of quasi-literary expression.

    What made you actually GM the first time? How old were you?
    I was 19 when that fateful Saturday rolled around, when I was involved in no standing game at the Gaming Club. I figured failing at being a GM was preferable to doing nothing, or to just playing Magic again (we did that every day, and Saturday was for RPGs!).

    Who did you GM for?
    And so it was that I GM'd for the subset of our D&D/OWOD Werewolf group that was still in Pittsburgh for the summer.

    What do you wish you knew back then?
    First and foremost: You don't need to have all 10 people in the same game! Second: There's no GM task you can't delegate to another player. I'm OK with having taken years to learn everything else, but those two would have ratcheted my stress level down a ton. I really burned myself out over those first few months of what became a very long campaign.

    Why did you continue?
    If I GM'd, we played just the games I wanted to play. If I didn't, we didn't. Just that simple. No one else was enthusiastic about running Cthulhu, Puppetland, etc., so they fell to me. And as I got better at getting out of everyone's way - as I got to step out of the spotlight more and more - I really started enjoying the GM role.
