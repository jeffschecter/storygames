    \n OK, just kidding.

    Here's what I did for the 10-Blade Samurai. In fact, here's what I always do:

    * When it is time to submit an event for the convention (GenCon, MACE, whatever), I reach down and pull out a cool, action-y description of a fictional scenario.
    * I do nothing for several weeks/months.
    * On the way to the session (on the plane ride over, at the hotel, at the food court 20 minutes before the session begins, etc) I begin to think about the scenario.
    * I take out a sheet of paper, and start writing NPC names on it. Then I write a few cool locations for potential exposition scenes, fights, etc.
    * Somewhere in this process I draft up some PCs. More often than not they are entirely different than the ones listed in the game description. I usually draft a few extras so that the players can pick whatever they want.
    * I come up with maybe 1-2 plot points. Leave them intentionally vague.
    * I spend the most time thinking up a good, explosive opening scene for each character. In TBZ anyway, the first scenes are called the "Zero Act", where we discover each character, find out what they're like, and the GM gives them a goal... kinda like a "bang", in a way, just given by the GM instead of the player.

    If the first scenes go well, then the rest of the session carries itself.

    This is pretty much how I do all my sessions, actually. Tenra I think takes the most work in that regard, but it only even then requires about 10-15 minutes of work (more when generating characters).

    -Andy

    \n Oh, and in the end:

    * I realized about 80% of the way through the session that there was, in fact, no "ten blade samurai". The main enemy was a samurai (actually a samurai-sorcerer), but I didn't give him 10 swords or anything. I was even intending to earlier, but forgot.

    * None of my sessions this year had monks or armour-hunters ("sword as long as her body"). But I did have other types, including Shinto agents, etc.

    The big lesson here is:

    * Make your game description sound cool.
    * Nobody attending your session will give two shits if the session they are playing matches the description in the catalog.
    * AS LONG AS the session is ultimately fun. If it was mediocre, they'd probably start to care.

    Note that I did list my other events three times in the exact same way IIRC (the same description 3 times, while the Ten Blade Samurai one was a second/different description). I did this to encourage new players, and discourage people (even if they were great players and we had an awesome time together!) from sitting in on each of my sessions, effectively crowding out newcomers (as game reg usually fills up and locks down months before the GenCon doors even open).

    A few years ago I followed the same procedure as I did above, but I had every single session use different descriptions (I was in a writin' mood at the time). And I had a few people actually sign up for several of the sessions, expecting something completely different. They were, in the end, since my games basically run off of player ideas and reactions in opening scenes. But it crowded out newcomer players who came to the con looking for a game but couldn't get in.

    -Andy
