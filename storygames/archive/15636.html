\n I've played Apocalypse World. I love it.

I've played Old School D&D. I love it.

I've heard the name "Dungeon World". I don't know what it is, except obviously that it's supposed to be Old School D&D, but using the Apocalypse World rules.

So tell me: what is Dungeon World, and why would I want to use it rather than, say, OD&D? Not a facetious question: I'm genuinely interested. In particular, I'm interested in finding out how Dungeon World 'does' logistics.

To me, there are two key attractions of the Old School D&D. The first is its emphasis on the sandbox and how story just emerges from what the players do, exploring the world in a haphazard fashion in pursuit of quixotic goals. I get how Dungeon World might do that, because it's sort of what Apocalypse World does.

The second attraction of Old School D&D is logistics, and the tough choices it forces on the players, like: should we try to take these 1,000 gold pieces back to town given that they weigh 100 lbs (or 1000 cn)? How many hirelings should we hire, if we're going to give them a 5% share each? Is it worth trying to take on these orcs, given the risks involved and the potential benefit of fighting them? How much oil do you have left in your lantern - is it enough to get back to the surface before it runs out? And so on. Does Dungeon World force the players to make these kinds of choices too? Is it set up for that sort of thing?

My questions aren't just limited to that - sell me on Dungeon World, please, because I want to like it.

    \n Reasons you might want to play Dungeon World (or any other D&D-themed AW hack, see a short list below) instead of old-school D&D:

    1. You can still use most of the old-school play style. AW tells you to make up the setting in play, as you go along, but nothing is really stopping you from using the moves and harm rules instead of the resolution systems in D&D. You just need to decide what xp system you're using.

    2. Characters matter more, and not just because of special moves. It's pretty hard to die in Dw and AW, partly because you can take those debilities, and partly because the GM is encouraged to kill NPCs easily. Maybe you will find it's more like starting at 3rd level, instead of 1st level. However, improvements allow players to shape their character's advancement more than in D&D, where if you don't have spells, the kinds of powers you have are often dependent on what magical items the GM puts in the dungeon.

    3. The rules cater to different specific pieces of fiction. If you make a move, you roll. If you do something else, the GM just decides what happens (although DW says a GM move is "roll on a random table"), which pushes players towards making those moves. That might clash with the "emergent story" or you might find it guides the game towards certain desired actions. Since you like AW already, if that's the kind of stuff you want to encourage players to do in D&D as well, then you'll probably like using those rules. Different hacks have different moves, though, so check each one.

    4. Play tends to move faster, especially combat. Having played B/X D&D and my World of Algol hack (which uses the basic AW rules) with the same (Red Box Vancouver) players, I definitely found the AW combats were over almost in a flash, and were much more dynamic, than the usual back-and-forth of D&D. Sometimes that back-and-forth can build up a lot of cool tension, but sometimes it just drags.

    5. As to the logistics of treasure, I found AW's abstract barter system actually freed me up to describe different sorts of treasures, which I could then assign a barter value to, instead of the ubiquitous, undifferentiated gold and silver coins all over the place. You can still have a coin horde worth x-barter that weighs several hundred pounds that the players need to figure out how to move, of course, but you won't get the same granularity that you get keeping track of every single coin and every single purchase.

    \n Mostly it removes a certain type of heroism from the characters, making them more human, gritty,and desperate:

    1. Greed is king, and all characters are thieves (or at least scavengers). You don't get xp for rescuing the princess, you don't get xp for slaying the dragon. If you can get in and get out with the dragon's gold, it can eat the princess and you can still get all the xp available. Some of us think this is a good thing, though! Only scoundrels and madmen go into dungeons! Some people use the term "murderhobo," and it is true that you can't make gp=xp characters very heroic. Even if they get xp for monetary rewards, they're still saving the princess in exchange for money. Monster-slayers can at least be portrayed as saving innocent people from horrible monsters, but in the end they are also murderhoboes, because they get xp for murdering stuff, and thieves don't have to be murderers.

    2. Encourages exploration over confrontation. You get crap xp for low-HD monsters, even if they are super-deadly, and treasure won't come looking for you. So you have to find ways to search every corner of the dungeon while avoiding the monsters you don't absolutely have to fight. Once you have a bunch of treasure, you want to get the hell out of the dungeon before you get ambushed and lose it all (along with lives). Or, you might be mentally doing the math and realizing you need just a little more treasure for everybody to level up, so you take a risk and check a few more rooms. Maybe you get lucky and there is treasure, maybe you get monsters and you die or you have to run like crazy. Because it really sucks fighting monsters, especially wandering monsters who have no treasure. If you get xp for monsters, you love to find monsters, because then you can kill them! Which is a bit weird, don't you think? Monsters should be scary bastards who allatime fuck you over.

    3. And yes, backstabbing. In some rules, surviving retainers get a share of xp, even if you don't pay them, which encourages you to let them die in the dungeon. In some other games, you only get xp for the treasure that you get to keep after the adventure. So it's possible to divide it up unfairly but so characters of different levels can level up, but it also means you probably argue over how the loot gets divided, and thieves might steal from other characters or conceal certain pieces of loot.

    So you can no doubt see how this is different from xp for monsters.

    Both of those systems differ from AW's xp-for-using-stats in that there's a lot more room for useless (in terms of advancement) action in D&D. As long as you use the moves in AW, you will advance, no matter what you are trying to do or where your character is in actually achieving goals in the fiction. Which is probably why AW encourages aggressive scene framing, so you only play out what is important. If you play a dungeon-crawl within the bounds of old-school play, but with AW advancement, you can end up with a situation where bumbling adventurers repeatedly fail to obtain treasure, escape from humiliating (but not deadly) traps, and spend most of their time running away from monsters can then exit the dungeon having gained numerous advances and stat increases.

    In D&D, that same activity nets no xp. The dungeon (a gp=xp dungeon) has a certain amount of treasure built into it from the start, and that's how much xp you can get out of it. How long it takes you to get it (and how many characters) is up to you.

    \n There are other aspects to gold as experience, too:

    One nice change I've seen done is to award XP for the *spending* of gold. So you go into a dungeon and collect treasure. You don't get XP: you get the XP when you go back to the town and spend the wealth to advance yourself in the world.

    This makes particular sense when the advancement system includes things like becoming a Lord, building your own Keep, or attracting followers and henchmen. This is why you go on adventures: you want to become rich, to move up--to advance--in the world.

    It makes a lot more sense in the context of an actual dungeon, too, since the player and the character have somewhat the same motivation and the same way of thinking:

    "I want to go into the dungeon to get the wealth no one else has been brave enough or smart enough to find. I also want to make sure I get out alive!"

    So it indirectly encourages problem solving and clever approaches to problems. If you see a chest of gold guarded by some incredibly beastly monster, XP for defeating monsters says: "I want to go into a fair fight with that monstrosity, so I can get experience!" Whereas XP for gold says: "Oh, man! That thing is scary. How can I find a way to get the treasure and get out without that thing hurting me too much? Can I trick it? Scare it away? Find some way to kill it that's hopefully less dangerous than just going toe-to-toe with it?"

    Which mode of thinking seems more "realistic", more likely for a treasure hunter? It certainly encourages more problem solving and lateral thinking, which can be fun. Combat is something to be *avoided*, an unfortunate consequence of insufficient planning...

    If your game is heroic fantasy, where the heroes want to kill the dragon to free the world from its menace, then, yeah, XP for treasure doesn't match as well, though.

    As another nice side-effect, the DM in an XP-for-treasure game has no need to make sure monsters are "balanced" for the party: it's your job to decide whether you can take it on or not, suckers! (Whereas if leveling up could only be accomplished by killing monsters, your DM is kind of a jerk if she doesn't provide you with monsters suitable to your level -- too hard and you'll die, too easy and you won't get enough XP.)

    \n It always staggers me that people don't get the xp for treasure thing.
    \n
    \nBasically:
    \n
    \nxp-for-monster means cleverly avoiding the monster is not incentivized.
    \n
    \nxp-for-good-deeds means it's hard to make a sandbox since the DM has to seed a doable good deed in each location (plus if you're really good you'd always do the most needful deed, so it's not really a choice)
    \n
    \nxp-for-individual-goal means the same problem as for-deeds plus it splits the party.
    \n
    \nxp-for-treasure is very smart--it is a simple way to incentivize exploration of any part of a setting. While different xp setups can be good for certain groups or one shots or campaigns, none is as universally applicable in sandbox mode as treasure.
    \n
    \nPlus it worked for the serial pulp writers--like Leiber. Fafhrd and the Mouser did not have to be "remotivated" for every adventure--unlike say Frodo who had to have a reason to leave the Shire. That may be why it was instituted by Gygax, then he realized it kept working and so kept it around.

Find some way to kill it that's hopefully less dangerous than just going toe-to-toe with it?"

    Which mode of thinking seems more "realistic", more likely for a treasure hunter? It certainly encourages more problem solving and lateral thinking, which can be fun. Combat is something to be *avoided*, an unfortunate consequence of insufficient planning...

    If your game is heroic fantasy, where the heroes want to kill the dragon to free the world from its menace, then, yeah, XP for treasure doesn't match as well, though.

    As another nice side-effect, the DM in an XP-for-treasure game has no need to make sure monsters are "balanced" for the party: it's your job to decide whether you can take it on or not, suckers! (Whereas if leveling up could only be accomplished by killing monsters, your DM is kind of a jerk if she doesn't provide you with monsters suitable to your level -- too hard and you'll die, too easy and you won't get enough XP.)

    Posted By: JohnstoneI don't know what Gary and Dave were trying to achieve with gp=xp. Tavis Allison and/or James Nostack fromThe Mule Abidesblog would probably be my go-to guys if I wanted to know something like that, since I don't usually frequent D&D forums, old-school or not. I do know that in some communities, like MIT gamers in the 70s, gp=xp was one of the first rules to get axed. Those guys used mostly goal- or quest-oriented xp with some for monsters and, like Marshall says, treasure being its own reward. It seems to be pretty universally loved amongst the OSR blogs.
    \nadamwb is correct; the proto-D&D texts show a lot of activity around gp for XP. The number of variant rules out there suggest that it was an active focus of design, not just one of the leftovers from "a bad rule now is better than a perfect one later". To quickly summarize:

    - In First Fantasy Campaign, Arneson says that the Blackmoor campaign started out with GP for recovering XP, but then very early on (i.e. in 1972 or 1973?) they decided it made more sense to give GP for spending XP. Many people in the OSR agree (my own play experience was much improved when we introduced this concept to the White Sandbox), although as far as I know no one actually uses the FFC "special interest" system as written, in which different classes get different amounts of XP for different activities; the concept is sound but the implementation is hard to follow.

    - Dave Megarry might know how Arneson was doing experience at the time he showed Gygax how to play Blackmoor and gave him the original set of notes that got D&D started. It seems likely that this set of notes was basically just charts, kind of like Arneson's DM screen, and that XP was a procedure that wasn't written down at that stage.

    - If anyone knows what was in the first draft that Gygax wrote up based on Arneson's notes and his own play experiences (that one session with Arneson and Megarry and maybe some others, and then Gygax doing it himself with his kids and locals), I don't know about it.

    - A manuscript that is likely to be Arneson's revisions of that draft exists in which only fighters get XP for GP. Magic-users get XP for casting spells, clerics get XP for good works. Whether this is Arneson correcting Gygax's misunderstandings of his original system, or providing updates to reflect the evolution of what he has decided works best in Blackmoor since then, is not clear. This manuscript includes lots of stuff that was not part of OD&D as published.

    - OD&D (1974) has the rule that you get XP for recovering GP from the dungeon. It is implied that this is supposed to be the main driver of XP from adventuring, but analyses of the monster and treasure charts suggest that this is not necessarily true if you follow random dungeon stocking procedures. The examples of awarding XP in the OD&D text are not only unclear but also do not match the text, further indicating that there was a lot of late-stage design work on XP going on in between these different manuscripts and playtest sessions.

    - Gygax's Supplement I (1975) calls the XP awards from combat in OD&D "ridiculous" and drastically cuts them down, making treasure more important. The vehemence of "ridiculous" seems out of place and is maybe part of an internal argument with Arneson, who we can imagine is by this point upset that OD&D was rushed to press without the revisions and polish he added to his manuscript & with a lot of design changes of which everyone getting XP for GP seems to be among the biggest.

    - Almost everyone everywhere decides that XP from GP is ridiculous, breaking one of the fundamental drivers of the game (especially if Greyhawk reduced-XP-from-combat awards are used, it will now take forever to level) and leading to all manner of problems that don't arise from the rules as written (although arguably a more clear presentation of the XP rules or designer's notes about why they matter could have helped).

    - "Orgies, Inc." in Dragon #10 (1977) presents a system for gaining XP by spending it on class-specific functions that is simpler than Arneson's in First Fantasy Campaign and seems to have been independently derived.

    - Moldvay's Basic (1981) explicitly says that the ratio of XP from gold to XP from combat should be 3:1, and does what he can within the legacy monster/treasure framework to devise dungeon stocking procedures that make it come out that way.

    - Lots of OSR exploration of GP for XP recovered and/or spent takes place.

    - Adventurer Conqueror King (2012) formalizes the ratio of 4:1 XP from treasure to combat that has been working for us in our campaigns, and designs new monster/treasure tables that allow the dungeon stocking procedure to achieve this result much more reliably than Moldvay (in which it works only at certain levels, and only if you kill dwarves and nobles on sight).

    \n Well, like Adam (skinnyghost) said above, he and Sage aren't trying to write Dungeon World to do the strategy game. However, my World of Algol hack uses the stats and moves right out of Apocalypse World and I've run old-school style dungeon crawl sessions with it quite successfully.

    Take a situation from the last Red Box Vancouver game: Graham is playing Holga, a magic-user who worships the Purple Worm God. Another PC, Ranndy Serpenthelm, has been transformed into an enchanted minotaur by a magic amulet (which makes him an NPC). While the minotaur is eating some dogs, Holga turns invisible and tries to stuff a potion of gaseous form into the minotaur's throat. So, what happens?

    If we had been using the Apocalypse World rules, I would have a total of two options, by my reckoning. The only move that could possibly apply here is acing under fire. So, I could say "roll+cool to act under fire, and the fire is that the minotaur is able to get violent with you, invisible or not." Then we would go to the move, it would return a verdict of success, fail, or success with consequences, and I (with Graham) would interpret what that looks like in the fiction. My other option would be to just decide what happens without rolling any dice.

    (I suppose I could technically write a new move on the spot, "when you stuff something into a minotaur's mouth..." but it's usually better to have your custom moves written before you start the session, I find.)

    Since we were playing B/X, I had more options. I could have used the rules on page B60, and said, "You have a 60% chance of succeeding," or "roll your Dex or less on a d20 to succeed." Either of these rules is exactly the same as acting under fire except there is no success with consequences result, just success and failure. I could also make up a new rule, or borrow a house rule from outside of B/X. Or I could also just decide what happens. In this case because the game also tells me to reward clever actions, I simply decided it succeeded, partly because Holga was invisible and can wait for the right moment, and partly because she still had to save vs. spells to avoid the amulet's curse herself if she was going to save Ranndy (a 30% chance, as it turns out, which Graham managed to succeed at -- and that is how the Purple Worm God saved it's cultists from the minotaur trap).

    Most situations I found worked like that, basically the same in either game. There is the case that on a miss with the AW rules, the MC makes a hard move, which can be to deplete the PCs' resources, but when the players didn't know how many charges they had in their laser guns to begin with, from their perspective there would be little difference if I had kept track of it secretly or whether I decided the gun ran out when they failed a roll. Logistics they DID know about, they had to deal with in the same manner -- as if they were these fictional people in this situation and had to decide what they were going to do with the stuff they had.

    I did notice a difference in combat, which goes faster using the AW rules. When you fight with swords in AW, you roll to seize by force, and probably somebody will be dead or seriously wounded after that one roll. Now in D&D, the process of going to the rules from the fiction -- i.e. rolling to hit -- is the same, just without the 7-9 mixed results. But when we get to damage, it can turn into simply trading numbers back and forth, until somebody reaches zero, and we neglect to describe much of the actual fiction. Or, we describe what happens but it's just colour. I notice that (Planet Algol) Blair likes to ham up the horrible effects of wounds more than I do, but neither of us are concerned with the actual in-fiction consequences of say, an infection because of a bite on the leg, unless the wound is very serious like a broken arm. You lose a few hit points, you keep going, you leave the dungeon, you heal up, you go back in. But, essentially, we don't always go back to the fiction in between rolls to hit and damage in D&D, partly because it's a longer process than in AW.

    The main difference there is that AW moves are all standardized, most involve rolling, and the actual tracking of inventories is deliberately abstracted. So you might not run out of ammo until the MC says you run out of ammo -- although Graham's character in World of Algol, a Polish guy from WWII, came to Planet Algol with only two clips of ammunition for his handgun. In that case it was something to be aware of (or would have been, if he hadn't lost the gun before his ammo ran out), but even then I don't like tracking individual bullets because it assumes absolutely perfect fire discipline on the part of the character.

    Additionally, just because the seven basic moves in AW lean towards certain types of behaviours, doesn't mean that D&D's rules don't. The top-down approach Vincent took in deciding what behaviours would be encouraged results in more-or-less the same situation as D&D's collection of rules for things that players often did in games. AW has rules for reading people, manipulating people, going aggro on them, and opening your brain to the psychic maelstrom. D&D has rules for fighting, finding secret doors, determining monster reactions and morale, and casting spells. Neither has a concrete rule for stuffing a potion down a minotaur's throat when it can't see you. Any player who has read the rules may still think "oh, I can do anything I can think of," but they will also be thinking about the actions described in the rules, and be encouraged to take those actions, knowing there are rules there to support them doing so.

    (tl; dr?) So that's a long-winded way of saying that, in my experience, the resolution part of the game is not what creates the divide between experiencing the fiction as a coherent story versus experiencing it as a coherent environment.

      I would say any game can be remembered or experienced as a coherent narrative fiction. Some games deal with that directly and explicitly ('Shock:' fr'ex) while others don't deal with it at all (most board games, fr'ex).

      \n I could also probably say why I liked using the AW rules for a Planet Algol dungeon crawl. These are some reasons you might want to mix these games, or reasons you might NOT want to if you don't like the same stuff I do:

      1. I like some of the moves. Occasionally, in B/X, a player will ask "can I tell if he's lying to me?" and I'll let him roll to read a person with his wisdom modifier. There's some D&D-specific ones that are missing, but I can probably write or adapt those without too much hassle.

      2. Combat is faster. See my post above. Also I like the lower hit points thing.

      3. I like how money is abstracted, actually. Standardized coins really doesn't do much for me, but in World of Algol, I could just describe a piece of treasure, like "it's a statue, looks valuable, maybe x-barter, maybe four hundred pounds." And that's that. Or I could describe a coin hoard with various different sizes of gold and silver coins and some gems and just ballpark how valuable the total is. Then I let the players decide how they are going to truck it back to civilization. It felt like they were finding unique treasures, instead of just picking up money off the ground (which I find logistically satisfying as a D&D player because I am greedy, but isn't exciting as a piece of fiction). Mostly, I want to get right into what the players are going to propose to solve their problem and not worry about exact numbers. If they're clever and creative, I feel like I should be lenient, if they do something stupid I should be strict.

      4. I like how PCs have special moves and improvements. The special moves make characters unique, add some flavour, but aren't overly complicated like feats and 4E powers (at least I find those things overly complicated). And you actually start with less individual special powers than a B/X thief! I also like how improvements let players define things about their characters. In D&D, your 8th level fighter's capabilities are mostly dependent on what magical items the DM puts in the dungeon for you to find. You get to decide what your 9th level castle looks like, but little else. The magic-use at least mght be able to pick new spells. In AW, you can use an improvement to get a gang, and hey, now you have a gang without having to role-play the whole recruitment process. And if you get them all killed, they don't magically come back just because you spent an improvement once -- you get the one gang and that's it.

      5. Finally, AW encourages certain DM behaviours that I want to do more of. In particular, it says to give the PCs the benefit of the doubt -- if they can do something, cool, don't try and make individual tasks harder to accomplish as a way of giving the players a challenge. Let them do stuff, then let them deal with the consequences of their actions. Complex, multi-stage goals can still be difficult, it's avoiding the consequences that is hard.

      So for example, what if the PCs run into Orcus on Planet Algol. One of the players says he wants to open his brain to the psychic maelstrom and read orcus' mind. I might ask how he does that, and maybe he says that he's been describing how his character interacts with the maelstrom as like telepathy with spirits. Orcus is an extra-planar being, so this player thinks it is reasonable he can read Orcus' mind.

      Some DMs might impose their own definition of the psychic maelstrom and say "no it doesn't work like that," (which is boring), or they might decide that no, Orcus has psychic shields and there's no way a mere mortal could get into his mind (also boring). But AW always reminds me that since the player thinks it is reasonable, why shouldn't I agree? So, I would say "sure, roll to open your mind, if you get a hit, I will tell you all of Orcus' secrets, either in vague detail or precise detail!" And then after the player has rolled I will say "so, Orcus is a death god from the deepest pits of hell and you are inside his mind. How, exactly, do you propose to stay sane?" Ha! Tricked you stupid player! You thought it would be so easy and you'd get off scott free, well... one out of two isn't bad, right?

      So, #5 isn't unique to AW by any means, and probably a lot of people who have never played it already play that way (although that's just the most prominent example, there's other stuff too). It's just nice to have a reminder there to not just run the game any old way that is comfortable, but to run it in a specific way, when that specific way contains elements I want to get better at.

      \nI think GM's say "no, that just doesn't work" for three different reasons, broadly speaking.

      1) The action is simply beyond the threshold of plausibility: a relatively normal human trying to leap a 100' chasm.

      2) The action would normally work, but there is a legitimate in-world reason why it doesn't in this case, although that reason is hidden from the players at the moment: the house is owned by a dude who's secretly a powerful wizard, so simply using knock to unlock the front door doesn't work.

      3) The GM is simply blocking / doesn't want to "lose control" of "the story": the players realize who the bad guy is "too early" and go to confront him, but nothing they do to try to find him works.

      I've had all three done to me, and I've done all three as well, though I'd like to believe that I've only rarely done the 3rd and not at all for quite a few years.

      But they all feel quite different, and it's not hard to tell which is which, in my experience. (For instance, in case #2, the GM will often give reasonably broad hints that something is up. "Well, that would normally work, but for some reason it's not.") Both S-Gers and OSR-ers agree that #3 is dumb, that the GM shouldn't become attached to his NPCs or emotionally invested in particular outcomes. In the case of "Orcus mind-reading" above, I think a plausible case could be made for the GM to simply say "no" on sincere reason #1 grounds. But, it's not, like, totally unthinkable that Orcus would let you read his mind in order to drive you insane. I could see an OSR GM, say Tavis, making the same call as Johnstone.

      In other words, while there are profound differences between, broadly speaking, S-G and OSR, I don't actually think this issue is one of them, most of the time. On the other hand, Zak isn't wrong\u2014I think GMs saying "no" is a huge funstopping fail for a lot of members of the S-G community, simply because we've encountered case #3 far too often, or have been in situations where the line between cases really wasn't clear.

      But reasonable and sincere "no"s on the part of the GM are well-supported by both the letter and general principles of the rules of all the major indie darlings that have a GM\u2014Dogs, BW/MG, AW.

      I also want to point out that DW requires prep and maps and all that stuff. Not quite as complete of prep as most versions of D&D, but prep nonetheless, and in a fairly traditional form.

We all love this roleplaying thing and many of us tinker to make it fit our personal needs when we encounter parts that don't work for us. Which isn't the same as saying a game is broken or bad. But by system they don't just mean rules but everything you do at the gaming table. This is partially why setting expectations and clear communication are so prized at the Forge. If I wanted to be more cynical, I might argue that by saying system matters, you also create opportunity to build multiple systems, and sell stuff. I don't think that is the case. I love the idea of playing focused game and I'm fortunate to have a very large pool of players to find the right people for the right game (I helped create a non-profit that reaches over a 1,000 local gamers partially for this purpose). But in practice, for most people I know, compromise is the reality and focused play is an ideal not always met. What I like about focused games is it makes buy in and setting expectations easier than playing something that is more ambiguous. But the idea that you should stop playing with your friends if they don't want the same thing as you seems a bit heavy handed.

      I don't think people here or in the OSR quite fall into distinct groups. There is incredible overlap.

      Posted By: NoofyWhat DW does for me is tap into the childhood joy of playing OSR at 10 years old, full of the 'story', that subsequent editions seemed to leech out of our play.
      \nNoofy, what you're talking about there is old-school play: how it was actually done back in the day. The R in OSR usually stands for Old-School Renaissance, with the implicit idea of re-discovering something that's lost.

      I bring this up not just to be pedantic - although fanatic Talmudic scholarship of the original texts and palimpsests is certainly part of the appeal of the OSR to me - but because it speaks to Zak's point. Although I started playing D&D in 1980, my own experiences back in the day weren't much like the way I play the same rules today. Back then I was very into the idea that as DM it was my duty and privilege to author a grand story for the players to experience, which predictably caused frustration for all involved. But the reason that Dragonlance et al. were big sellers back in the day was that lots of people wanted this!

      It's easier to talk about the OSR because (as cacophonously divergent as it often is) it is still a style of play people can choose from a range of alternatives, and that has lots of widely-heard advocates. Like Forge-style games, it shows some people how to do a particular thing they like and helps them identify exactly what it is that they like.

      My experience is that folks who are just doing what they've been doing with old-timey D&D all along don't often have much in common with the OSR; they weren't self-selecting into one of a range of alternatives, they descend from a chronological slice of trying to use D&D to do everything there was! And folks who've been doing the same thing all along are hard to find precisely because lots of old-school D&D players often jumped ship when another game came along specifically to do that thing; they became 3e players because their AD&D houserules had been all about minis and tactical grid options, or they went over to the World of Darkness because their D&D games had been all about metaplot and interpersonal drama.

      Noofy, it sounds like your back in the day games were like what the OSR celebrates, and guys like Finarvyn at the OD&D boards never had an "aha" moment, they'd been doing it all along. For me, though, there was a real process of revelation and guidance to figure out what I wanted to get out of D&D that had been lost from my style or never been there at all.

      Hmmm. Yes Matt, I agree that the Fronts and Danger model is a wonderfully structured means of generating focused prottagonism, but you can still play DW as very much emergent play, based on very little prep other than some daydreaming about fantasy stuff, and some group co-authoring using the moves as 'prompts' to the cause and effect of the conversation at the table and thus the resultant fiction.
      \nThe Fronts / Impending Doom / Grim Portents model is really just about "what would happen if?" and "what move should I make now?" and is sort of a way for the GM to codify and structure all the thinking people tend to do about games anyway. It might contain some tricks and traps and monster stuff but it might not, that might be on the nebulous end of prep, depending on the GM.

      Personally, I tend to be fairly "traditional" about my dungeon / campaign / whatever preparation with Dungeon World. Loads of maps (some empty rooms, some detailed exactly) and notes and junk because I tend to have a harder time coming up with stuff on the fly that I absolutely love right out the gate. Other GMs may play it differently. I think that DW allows either style of play without too much trouble.

      Also, to pitch in my two cents on the "people on SG have had bad D&D experiences" - we didn't write (at least, I didn't) Dungeon World to "fix" what we perceived as problems in Dungeons and Dragons Style play. DW is an homage to the stories we tell about those sessions we played. We've separated the game from the rules in an effort to, for people who want to play a certain way, allow the creation of the same stories that a B/X or AD&D or whatever game might create. I've been playing a ton of various editions of Dungeons and Dragons in parallel with DW - they scratch certain itches but I'd definitely say that most of my experiences in all the various forms of D&D or D&D-style rulesets have been positive.
