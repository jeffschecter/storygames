    I'm not sure about how to make an MMOG out of an existing indie game. However, I have a few thoughts about how to make MMOGs a little more like indie games. First, I would try to describe characters within the game by some standard other than their ability to kill things. Sure, you can tell stories about people that kill things, but I think the traditional tactical rpg approach has become a heavily reinforced box that MMOG designers have trouble thinking outside of. Second, I would change the way characters interact with their environment. Replace repetitive, unmeaningful interactions with the environment with player interaction and meaningful outcomes within the game world that result from player choices.

    For instance, players could play craftpersons organized in villages. They must trade resources and share information with each other to improve their ability in their chosen professions. Peridocally, villages are faced with some problem generated by the game engine (perhaps as a logical result of other events in game), and the players of a village must cooperate to overcome their problem. To keep things massively multiplayer, villagers could travel between villages to share ideas or compete with professionals from other villages, or the villages themselves could compete as entities either economically or for prestige in regards to different professions.

    Sorry, I've been excessively busy for a while. I was looking at this thread to be a jumping point for general ideas, so I didn't think it needed significant guidance, but there are a few ideas on this that I've been mucking with since my original post.

    First, just to clarify, I meant essentially "story-game" MMORPGs. But that brings up some different ways of viewing economies. I started with Dogs in the Vineyards, and took the idea of self-evolving towns with (flexible, albeit not totoally free) room for varying conflicts. Added on to this a use-evolving correlation of traits and relationships that determined whether they apply to the present conflict state - as a set of traits and relationships you have selected. I can just envision a handful of players getting together and playing through an automated town, and it being enjoyable.

    But where is the long term goal, the economy to manage the growth and changes? I don't think it ought to be a character economy, like in traditional MMORPGs, or a stuff economy. But what about a story economy? What if the story of your dog is the residual, available for all when your dog dies or retires, linking with other dog's stories, on-going or not. And what if the social prestige in those stories is the driving force of the economy. It's not a question of can you get the most powerful dog, but can you get the most powerful story of a dog's career - by some user inputs and much play of the game.

    From the rise of machinima and on the other side, fanfiction communities, this sort of thing ought to be viable. It may still be somewhat infeasible, but I think it merits some thought if only that. And it is interesting to see where a small scale game can scale if you free it from the typical requirements of MMORPGs. Perhaps that is a valid analytical question for RPGs in general - what is the core economy of this game if you had to extend it to 2,000 players?

    I wonder if there might be a viable path where you break out the roles of "game master" and "player" across the user base to cover all the peices of a story game that a computer program can't do well.

    For example: the server sets up a proto town with a number of characters, relationships, and lines of tension. Then a gamemaster refines the conflict situation and sets up the town for players. This is the "gamemaster" mode of play.

    Then some users in the "player" mode play the town as a story game. Moderation of conflicts is done by the players themselves within the limits of the situation. This is essential some form of computer-assisted story gaming.

    So to boil it down, the computer creates a proto situation. A player in gamemaster mode refines the situation and sets up specific conflicts for the players. Then the players play through the situation.

    I think the significant bottleneck here is in infrastructure. Coming up with the most sophisticated, leave-graphical-MUDs-in-your-dust game design doesn't really matter until you have the means to deliver that experience. Take a look at those network diagrams for Minions of Mirth. If you don't recognize it, those represent a pretty significant investment in time and capital for one person.

    Now, what are the odds that a person with the resources and wherewithal to get all the technical shit together is going to be the same person who has also developed talents for designing these new-fangled story game things we're so big on? As shown by Minions of Mirth, a person with the former capabilities can pull it off if he's working with a pretty traditional, widely-understood mode of gaming. But it seems to me that to accomplish something more, you're going to have to get people to work together without retaining any exclusive ownership of what they're creating, and now you're looking down the barrel of corporatization and all that entails.

    I mean, it all sounds cool and all, but I think it's all very "blue sky" until someone has addressed these less glamorous tasks. For this reason, I think the publishing model developed at the Forge, where a single creative individual can deliver his or her innovations without fronting a large amount of start-up capital, will be where the real game design innovation will continue to occur for some time to come.

    There's also the matter of how these hippie tabletop story games rely very heavily on modes of natural human communication which have been in development for thousands of years, whereas in creating a virtual social experience someone actually has to realize and implement avenues of communication, many of which are widely taken for granted. But that's yet another can of worms.

    Making something like NWN into a MMORPG would be a relatively trivial thing. But I agree that this is not fixing the problem. In point of fact, I play a weekly game of Hero Quest by chat, and, thus, Mr. Sugarbaker's proposed advantage for online play is already in place, without a MMORPG. For a MMORPG to really be advantageous over what we have in chat play now, or MUDs, etc, etc, are for the computer to act as GM, and/or for there to be some advantage to the massively multiplayer angle.

    I see absolutely no advantage to the massively multiplayer part of things for story games. How is playing DitV with thousands of people going to improve the play? Thousands of dogs running around? That doesn't even seem to be canonical to the game. Sure you can have multiple servers, and limit the number of dogs on each, but then you're starting to reduce the supposed advantage of massively multiplayer. Until you get to the point where you might as well just be playing by chat in terms of the number of players involved. Thats' all that's neccessary, provably, to make a good story.

    So massive is not an advantage, and automated GMing seems impractical. Or, rather, before a "Story Game MMORPG" becomes feasible, somebody is going to have to come up with a sort of game where the massive multiplayer feature actually is a plus, and where they have a model that can be run by the computer.

    And an in-game economy is not it. Oh, that'll make play less about power, sure. But it's a false dichotomy to say that all play that is not power play is about story. The "realistic economy" model will just get you Second Life, or the failed Sims game. Been there, done that, and it won't provide support for players creating thematic output. Neither does interactive fiction ala skotos. Just allowing people to create story doesn't promote story. You tend, in those games, to get massive incoherency. This is the PK vs "Storytellers" dilemma.

    To get a functionally "story game" MMORPG, you do have to have something like Mendel is proposing, where the system does reward you for making thematic decisions. And, no, no, no, this can't be to empower or enrich characters. Even if the rewarded behavior is story oriented, if the reward form is empowerment, some (most) players will default to power play. Leading to abominations of theme, as people try to justify decisons based on the output power level that it provides.

    Nope, both sides of the reward equation, what you get rewarded for and what you can use the reward for, have to be about making theme to accomplish the goal of the thread. How you do that in a medium that essentially reduces actual player social interaction (not character interaction, that's not the same - player interaction)? That's the challenge.

    Not impossible, I don't think, but very difficult to say the least.
