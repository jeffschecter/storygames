  • Clinton
    April 2006

    The things that struck me at the time, and have continued to:

    a) "Yes, and..." Man, I was blown away by this concept - that by doing improv together, we agree to accept each other's creative input and work from it. In role-playing games, there's often an elaborate system to vet input - either a GM/player control structure, a mechanical method (a la Donjon), or even a simple "bullshit rule." All of these, though, are meant to eliminate "bad creative input." We have mechanics of denial. In improv, we were taught this is not the way. We've agreed - we've got a social contract - to have a good time working off each other's stuff. We don't need denial mechanics. This is cool!

    b) "Agreement is more fun to watch than conflict." I don't agree necessarily with this statement. But it was said by people obviously better at making up stuff than me. What I did agree with was a later clarification: when people don't completely trust each other in the making-stuff-up process yet, agreement works! Conflict is too easily conflated with real personal conflict, and should be avoided until there's enough trust to have it.

    Both of these are heavily influencing my current design.

    Bloody hell, no offence, but that link has the dullest version of "Yes, and" I've ever seen.

    The idea behind "Yes, And..." is that you accept what the other person's said and build on it. Often, there's an instinct to say no:

    "I smash you to the ground!"
    "No you don't! I dodge the blow!"

    So the idea with "Yes, And..." is that it's more interesting if you say yes:

    "I smash you to the ground!"
    "Yes, and you put me in a coma!"

    This could be great for roleplayers for building setting...

    "Let's play a game in a huge city."
    "Yes, and let's have in the future."
    "Yes, and let's make it a dystopian future, with everyone observed by cameras."

    Or just for playing:

    "I try to appeal to the dragon's mercenary side."
    "Yes, and we explain that, if he leaves the town alone, they'll bring gold as a tribute."
    "Yes, and they'll leave human sacrifices to the dragon."
    "Yes, and they'll be the first born of every family."

    It's used in lots of roleplaying games already, of course, in different forms. But it's interesting stuff.

    Gosh, that was a long post.

    BTW, this is from here: http://www.humanpingpongball.com/improv_glossary.html

    [i]Blocking
    Not Accepting other players' offers, and actually destroying these offers. Not the same as Ignoring .

    Not a good thing.

    Canceling
    Canceling is destroying what has been established. Better is to use all elements that have been introduced - see Reincorporating .

    Denial
    We have Denial when players are not Accepting each other's Offer 's. Obviously not a good thing.

    Ignoring
    Ignoring others' offers. Not a nice thing to do. Note that this is even worse than Blocking .


    Wimping
    Wimping is accepting offers but refusing to do anything with them.

    Examples are

    * asking open Questions , thus leaving the action to be decided by the other player
    * Waffling , babbling without accomplishing any action. [/i]

    I'm surprised no one has brought up Keith Johnstone yet. His books on Impro are quite fascinating, and I think someone on the 20by20room wrote an entry about them a while ago.

    I read Impro for Storytellers. Actually, the whole book reads as if he improvised it. That's neat, in a way--you get a technique, then a story, then it reminds him of a game so he throws that in, back to an anecdote, then some advice...

    Overall, "Yes and" is, like timfire says, based on having an audience. The show must go on; don't argue about input, push it further. Notice that most Impro is comical, too, so it's fine to just go overboard.

    Sometimes he says he has actors redo a whole scene, though, or he rewinds the action back a few steps and says, "Instead, at this point, do this." So it's not all "Yes and..."

    There are some powerful techniques in that book. Frex, I love shelving. That's when you take an idea of the other player and put it aside for the moment. Later in the act/story, at an appropriate moment, you whip it back out. And suddenly things tie together and it seems like you planned it all along. Cool stuff.

    The blocks and dodges that Jack listed are the rules that Once Upon a Time should have included as things you can't do. OUaT is essential an improv game.

    Still, I think there's a place for vetting mechanics in RPGs. Because there's no audience, you have no sense of immediacy. "Yes, And" exists for live performance where you can't negotiate without boring the fuck out of the audience. When you're the audience, and you have hours and hours of future gaming at stake, you want to negotiate the fiction a bit.

    First Game

    Bob: So my dude Conan runs up to your dude Krull and lops off his head with his sword! I'm so cool!
    Karen: What the hell? Asshole. Fine. Yes, and Krull takes a dying shot at Conan and splits him in half. We're both dead.
    Bob: I guess we make up new characters?
    Karen: Why bother.
    Yeah, I know all the old adages about not designing for idiot players. There's more to it than designing for idiots though. You can have one player who just has a remarkably different vision than the other four players, and he's really not being an idiot because we've told him that the group has to accept his vision as equal to theirs. He might not be lopping off a PC's head, but he might be killing off an NPC that the rest of the group was going to use in a future plot.

    With no audience other than the actors themselves, why not negotiate? Not that I have firsthand knowledge, but I don't imagine screenwriters sitting around a conference table with "Yes, And" rules.

    Every participant should always strive for "Yes, And" and perhaps be encouraged and rewarded by the rules for doing so, but they should also be allowed to negotiate when they don't like something being introduced into the fiction.
