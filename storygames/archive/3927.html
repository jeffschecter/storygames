\n I was playing my COIL campaign on Saturday with a group of people who love story, but are used to using the D&D mechanics. We're 10-weeks into the very very very complex story.

I couldn't even begin to explain it all.

I have no idea how long it's going to go, but I have enough material for 30 additional weeks, easily.

Anyway.

There are three players. Two have made slightly evil, very selfish characters. Perfect for a city adventure with no plot, but lots of goals. The third player made a character that doesn't "mesh" perfectly, but for 10 weeks we made it work.

At the 10-week mark major things started to happen in the city. An inner secret society started to die off from mysterious circumstances, and the players found themselves in a precarious situation where their own power could be used to exploit the "enemy."

In the end, all of them failed to get themselves into positions of power, although narrow windows were open for this.

And an interesting revelation about roleplaying games struck me. And maybe someone can phrase this for me, that's also encountered the same phenomenon.

But when you're playing in something long-term, so long as a character doesn't die, success and failure are the same thing. The only difference is your emotional response to the events as they transpire.

Dogs, of course, has this built into the game.

I have more ideas, but I await your responses.

    \n Burning Wheel has a great spiel on this. In this game, Success and Failure are two directions to a game. No matter what outcome, the game should continue to move and be interesting. Making a failure a dead end is really boring, and creates a lot of frustration for the players involved.

    However, if failures cause interesting complications, then the players are still engaged, and things keep moving.

    I remember a specific circumstance in my Burning Wheel game, set in the new Blossoms are Falling setting book. A player wants to make a hunting skill roll to find some game. She fails. Saying "You find nothing" is boring and worthless, the act of rolling the dice is a very clear indication from the player that they want something to happen. So instead, I had a rather mean Fox gnawing on a trap line, trying to steal a rabbit from the snare. That fox snarls and tries to snap at her. Complication! The story keeps moving!

    It has led to failures being viewed with eagerness, rather than frustration. I even encourage players to suggest some fun complications to put their characters in!

    \n Here's an example of something I don't like in (any) RPG.

    Someone frames a scene, another player presents a single complication, and another player succeeds. The scene ends none of the players are really effected by the "speed bumb" of the scene. The inverse is also true. The player fails, and marks off six hit points on his character sheet and moves on.

    Obviously intelligent players can track their own successes and failures and don't need a system for it, but experience has taught me that most people want less paperwork. Not more.

    As Josh has stated, in FLFS, the game monitors failure. But that work is left to the GM who is still required to monitor failure and the long term implications of said failure... especially if he wants it to matter in the story. For example, a player tries to get a radio to work, but instead turns it into a transmitter, thus letting the bad guys know his location. But the bad guys probably shouldn't show up for about 5 scenes, right? In the meantime, the PCs continue to fail and succeed, here and there, each time requiring the GM to track yet another THREAD in the overall story.

    Which can add up to a lot of (rewarding) work.

    I don't have all the answers yet, but there should be a way to track all of this without taxing a single player and still making it fun and complicated for the players.

    \n Heya, James.

    Firstly, can you tell me if you've played any 'indie games' or 'story-games?' If so, what was your experience like?

    Secondly, I can tell you right now that you're approaching this scenario in a manifestly different way than most of the other folks in the thread; that's what's leading to the confusion. It's like you're on one side of the elephant, and you're all, "It has a trunk and big flappy ears" and we're on the other side, and we're all, "It's got big, thick legs, and a little tiny tail and oh my god what was that?" So our experiences differ.

    Specifically, and to get a little jargonny, you're approaching this by looking only at Task Resolution, and assuming that Conflict Resolution is the purview of GM Fiat (or GM Discretion). That is, you're assuming that players roll dice to see if they succeed at tasks, and the GM decides if the lumped-together successes and failures mean that the players get what they want or not. While that is a functional way of playing, it does have some drawbacks -- among them, the players expending a lot of effort on tasks that aren't going to get them anything, win or lose: like searching the cupboards when the GM has the clue hidden upstairs. This structure can (but doesn't have to) descend into a guessing game of "figure out the right solution" and then rolling dice to see if you could actually perform the solution... and often, the die roll becomes a formality and the real game is guessing the right solution. In such a situation, yeah, if you try the "wrong" solution, then you get a 'you get nothing' response, especially when you fail. Now, I don't want to give the impression that I'm demeaning that playstyle at all; it can be hella fun. It's all puzzly-figure-things-out sorta play.

    If you flip assumptions around, however, you get a very different play experience. Consider an explicit Conflict Resolution with outcomes controlled by the players and mediated by Task Resolution dice mechanics. That is, the players identify up front what they want and then they do a number of specific tasks that would forward that goal. The GM has NPCs and environmental effects that get in the way of those tasks. If the players' tasks overwhelm the GM's opposition, the players get what they want.     \n I disagree a little with several parts of this discussion. I play with a long-term group (some of us have been gaming together for 20 years and more), and we have very good communications. Some of the players are immersivist enough that a formal stakes-setting system is often not an option. We do experience occasional problems with intent, usually in situations where a conversation is being played out in real time. When I GM, I point out to the players that if they want social mechanics to help them achieve something, they have to tell me what it is they are trying to achieve, but still things do sometimes go wrong.

    On the other hand, it's often not fully acknowledged/appreciated by those who love stakes setting that it does come at a price, and will suit some games better than others. If you're OK with paying that price, there's a lot to be gained, but it *is* a trade-off.

    Finally, I would like to speak up in favour of fudging. In many game systems, there are effectively two parallel systems in operation. The first is that things are simply adjudicated, whether by the GM or (less commonly) by a majority of players, or whatever. Someone just pulls "what happens next" out of the air, either because it's obvious, or it's thematic, or it's somehow necessary. Then there is a second system, with dice/cards/etc., some mechanical rules, etc. Often this is used on occasions when system 1 seems too difficult or controversial to use.

    On this basis, "fudging" is simply the recognition, partway through using system 2 to resolve a situation, that the mechanics are not well matched to the situation, and are at risk of generating unsuitable results. To bail out and revert to system 1 is an entirely logical and reasonable solution, so long as everyone is on the same page about it. What's not a good idea is to pretend to do one thing while really doing another, unless people have indicated that they really want to be deceived in that way.

    It is of course possible to design systems where only one system is in use for the entire game, no matter what happens, and most posters to this forum will be familiar with them. Just like stake setting (which they often use) however, there is a trade-off to be made in using such a system. For example, the use of in-game factors (e.g. how heavy the rock is) rather than meta-game factors (e.g. what resources the player is willing to expend) to determine outcomes generally goes out the window in such a universal system. For some, that's a great bargain. For others, not so much.

    On the other hand, it's often not fully acknowledged/appreciated by those who love stakes setting that it does come at

    a price, and will suit some games better than others. If you're OK with paying that price, there's a lot to be gained,

    but it *is* a trade-off.

    Finally, I would like to speak up in favour of fudging. In many game systems, there are effectively two parallel

    systems in operation. The first is that things are simply adjudicated, whether by the GM or (less commonly) by a

    majority of players, or whatever. Someone just pulls "what happens next" out of the air, either because it's obvious,

    or it's thematic, or it's somehow necessary. Then there is a second system, with dice/cards/etc., some mechanical

    rules, etc. Often this is used on occasions when system 1 seems too difficult or controversial to use.

    On this basis, "fudging" is simply the recognition, partway through using system 2 to resolve a situation, that the

    mechanics are not well matched to the situation, and are at risk of generating unsuitable results. To bail out and

    revert to system 1 is an entirely logical and reasonable solution, so long as everyone is on the same page about it.

    What's not a good idea is to pretend to do one thing while really doing another, unless people have indicated that they

    really want to be deceived in that way.

    It is of course possible to design systems where only one system is in use for the entire game, no matter what happens,

    and most posters to this forum will be familiar with them. Just like stake setting (which they often use) however,

    there is a trade-off to be made in using such a system. For example, the use of in-game factors (e.g. how heavy the

    rock is) rather than meta-game factors (e.g. what resources the player is willing to expend) to determine outcomes

    generally goes out the window in such a universal system. For some, that's a great bargain. For others, not so

    much.

If there was a consensus that came out of that discussion at all, it was that while you can do this, it's not something the Polaris rules handle smoothly (with apologies to Ben). In fact, it may be a good thing that Polaris' rules aren't clear on this matter, just to discourage stories that divert from its focus.

You cannot criticize D&D for this - that's a different story than the one D&D intends to tell - but you can criticize D&D related texts that claim that the rules are to be used to tell epic tales of bravery, intrigue and the clash of nations, yet do nothing to modify the base rules to support such tales. Bzzt, wrong story.

    So, the games are different. They tell different stories. There is no system that can be used to tell any story proficiently - other than possibly PTA, and the jury is still out on that one.

    So what I think you are saying, Dave, is that if you're encountering a situation the rules cannot handle proficiently, there are two options:

    a) The game is broken, or
    b) You're trying to tell a different story than the one the game was written to support.

    Example. My wife and I were playing Spacemaster (Rolemaster space setting) with a really hardcore DM. My wife played a kickass gun-toting beefy Amazon type girl, 80+ hit points. I played a scrawny unibomber-inspired ship engineer, 9 hit points. We get into a firefight with some police officers, nothing we shouldn't have been able to handle. One of the police officers roll a critical. Out come the crit chart. d100 scores 69. Turns out 69 on the firearms crit chart means "Bullet penetrates your skull into your brain, instant death". The Amazon woman dies in her first combat, while the rest of us make it out without a scratch.

    Sucks? Yeah, it sucks. That's because the system, frankly, sucked. The system simulated life in space, with all the dreadful boredom that entailed, and we were trying to use it to tell a story of high action-packed adventure. If that roll had been fudged, we may still be bored to this day.

    Example. In our most recent All Flesh Must Be Eaten game, one of the players wanted to shoot a zombie 3 feet away in the head at point blank range. The AFMBE rules state that aiming for the head means a -4 modifier. Point Blank Range only changes that to a -3, although it increases the damage multiplier as well. She could have received bonuses to hit by declaring she was aiming and taken her shot at the end of the turn, but that would have given the zombie a chance to attack first. So she held up her hand in a faux handgun pose to some other player's head, saying "How hard would it really be for me to hit at this range?", expressly suggesting that the ZM make an exception because - as is stated in AFMBE - the rules are but a guideline and that rule is "broken".

    Disregarding the fact that AFMBE reinforces the IMHO broken model of "moving statues" combat, possibly making the player believe that her shot was being fired at a perfectly still zombie head only 3 feet away and that she was cool as a cucumber at the time, the main issue here is that the player is trying to apply real-world logic to a game that is about the living dead. AFMBE favours stories about people being badly hurt for no good reason, where you accidentally shoot your friend in the leg, where things explode when you shoot them. In such a game, it makes little sense to include a rule that ensures that you automatically score brains as long as you wait until your opponent is close enough.

    Another indication is that for that player to be truly happy, she should probably play GURPS (which, incidentally, is her favourite).

    Yes, a minimalistic or even freeform task-based resolution system with fudging whenever necessary will work great as a system for close-knit friends who want to tell the exact same type of story. Problem is, for every other situation it'll suck. And even close-knit friends might want to try out a more defined and focused system to experience a different type of story they may not have been able to easily come about without that guidance.
