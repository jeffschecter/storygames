\n So I've been playing Dungeon World with my weekly group for a few weeks now.

One of the interesting issues has been XP. We often get at least as much XP from failure as from end-of-session awards, and it is the reward that is most immediate and concrete. Personally, I'll thus tend to savor failures as advancement - I'll say "Yes!" loudly and/or high-five others when we fail. David doesn't like this, though. He finds it backwards to be rewarded for failure, and doesn't engage in this.

If we see XP as a Pavlovian reward, it does seem like the "best" session is one where the PCs fail a lot but still manage to muddle through enough to overcome a "notable" opponent. I don't mind this, but I can see David's point.

Have other posters seen people who didn't engage with XP-for-failure? Are there variants that people have tried?

    \n There's an XP variation that our local crew uses when we ran Living Dungeon World. The thinking here is that Dungeon World should be a game of heroic deeds and action so the XP system rewards that. It breaks types of actions down into two categories with each three sub-categories.

    Category A

    Attack, Stunt & Defend

    Category B

    Discover, Converse, Aid

    Then we use the Highlighting rules from Apocalypse World. The player with most significant bond chooses one to highlight. Then the GM chooses one from the OTHER category to highlight.

    When you do a thing in a highlighted sub-category you get XP.

    I can't remember who exactly was responsible for developing this. I think originally it was just the six sub-categories. I remember being physically present when it was decided to sort them into two broader categories and to highlight one from A and one from B.

    \nI agree with this. I would also add that it helps if you're only rolling the dice when it really matters. If the GM soft move was show signs of doom and avoiding the doom is the reason the PC is making the move, then failure means DOOM -- presumably it's something the PC would genuinely want to avoid. If the GM soft move was offer an opportunity with or without a cost, failure means the opportunity is lost, the cost is high, and the PC is probably in a very dicey situation. Don't just roll willy nilly because the characters are looking around or whatever. Roll because something is on the line.

    Also, if the PLAYER is happy about getting XP because that's what the PLAYER wants in the longrun, I don't see that as a problem so long as the character in the fiction is soundly beaten up as a result of failure. Even when my guy is crumpled at the bottom of a pit with a spike through his leg, I cheer a little when I get XP too. What's the big deal?

    (That said, I like quest based XP too -- mostly because it's easier to keep track.)

    \n I didn't mean to imply that his preference was invalid. I understand that giving XP for failing rolls has some consequences that people might not like. (Personally, the issue that troubles me sometimes is that XP for failure creates the incentive to use Player Moves that aren't really in a character's wheel house. It's a little weird that if I make slow-witted barbarian thug and I want him to be good at charging into a room and bashing stuff without blinking, the best way for me to unlock even greater bashing abilities is by using entirely unrelated moves like spout lore.)

    Ultimately though, XP is very abstract. It's so abstract that I don't worry about where it comes from. It's not really a reward. Neither the mechanical trigger, nor the thing you do in the fiction to get XP usually has anything to do with the actual effect of XP.

    So, in my head, the character's personal improvement isn't directly related to the moments in the game when I failed rolls; it's just that the mechanical representation of my character's personal improvement (in the form of XP) happens to equal the number of failed rolls I had. It's just a coincidence. Why? Because . . . I don't know -- whatever. We have to get an XP number somehow, and failure is as good as anything else. It's not like finding treasure is any more reasonable. What does finding treasure have to do with giving a cleric spell to a mage character? It's arbitrary no matter what.

    Regarding quest based XP. If a character has a clear mission, estimate whether the scope of the mission is such that it warrants leveling up or some progress toward leveling up; figure out how many points you think it's worth using that marker; and, award that XP when the quest is achieved. Or just give some set amount of XP at the end of every session.

    \n I can appreciate your line of thinking and comments, jhkim (and love this conversation you've started... very intriguing). At the table, I've found rewarding a highlighted stat to be cool in theory and less cool in practice. I've seen players push and push for rolls on one or two stats because they're going after that advance rather than legitimately trying to make things happen in response to the fiction. I've a) resented their grandstanding and b) resented the MC for giving in and letting them make a ton of rolls, typically on stats they're good at. End result? Less fun as one player hogs the spotlight.

    Meanwhile, rewarding failure has been lots more fun. Players take risks, go for it, and wow, that roll sucked ass. So the MC/GM gets to make a move, maybe a really hard move, on the player or even on another player (love that) as the story takes a tough turn _and_ the player gets an XP. The story gets more challenging and the character is that much closer to increasing agency in challenging circumstances. End result? More fun for everyone involved.

    I like the immediacy of it, too. I use colored poker chips for XP and it's just plain fun as the GM when the player blows a roll (groans around the table) to sit back with that "Oh boy, am I gonna mess with y'all" look on my face while I flip a poker chip to the player. It's a little like I'm paying him/her for the chance to lay down some fuckery and that just feels satisfying to me.

    David in your group has a different style, I can respect that, but IMO he's missing out on a great way of interacting. To each his own.. he'd probably find playing with me to be pretty frustrating, I'm sure.

    \nSo, I think the issue here is that the GMs might not be fully using the system. The rules should take care of the "spotlight hogging" and "fair distribution of rolls" that you've mentioned without any special GM effort.

    I've noticed that people tend to be excited and interested in Player Moves, but that they regard GM Moves as optional advice. I think that's a mistake. At least in my view, the Player Moves are only part of the equation, and the GM Moves are very important. I think fully implementing them may solve some of your problems.

    The flow of play, as I understand it, starts with a GM Soft Move like show signs of doom or whatever. Then the player does something to deal with the Soft Move. If there are dangerous consequences to ignoring the Soft Move and the player ignores those consequences (with plenty of explicit warning), the GM has a "golden opportunity" and may make a GM Hard Move. If the player tries to prevent the doom (or seek the good thing or whatever), the player will typically use a Player Move.

    If the player rolls a failure, the GM makes a GM Hard Move. If the Player is successful or partially successful, then the move dictates the result. Either way, the next thing that happens -- and this is the part your GMs might have been missing -- is that the GM makes A NEW GM Soft Move, and the process repeats.

    The result is that a character who is failing a lot shouldn't automatically be getting more "spot light" than the character who succeeds. It's the GM's JOB to make a new GM Soft Move in BOTH cases. The GM Moves, Agenda, and Principles keep the ball rolling no matter what happens. It is not the GM's job to monitor how many Player Moves are being made or to monitor the Player Moves at all. The GM doesn't "distribute" rolls. Player Moves are outside of the GM's province. The GM just makes GM Moves. Soft ones most of the time and Hard ones when the rules say so. That's all. \n

    \nIn our group, Spout Lore is the mechanical method by which characters gain information that the GM is not willing to just disclose automatically. It usually comes up when a character asks: "do I know [fill in the blank]?" If the GM decides that the answer to the question is either common knowledge or that it would be known to that character, the GM will just answer (or ask the character to narrate the answer). If it's something that would require technical or special knowledge, the GM will require a roll.     The intention there is not saving the move for later (we tried that, it didn't work well). The intention is that you make the move now, somewhere else in the fiction, and just jot down "the PCs are being watched by scouts in the trees, they can attack or report back to their dark master at any time." You've just put them in a spot, but they don't know it.

    Saving up moves won't break the game or anything, we just always found it less useful than making the move now, even if the players don't see it yet.
    I'm not exactly clear what the difference is between "Make a note of and reveal it when the time is right" and "saving up moves." The word "reveal" implies it's unknown to the players (and the PCs) at the moment the badness is generated by the missed roll. I've had great success with poker chips in this regard. Player blows a roll, I reach for the red stack with a smile on my face. They all know some future badness is coming but I don't feel compelled to state exactly what it is right then. Heck, it might not have anything to do with the failed roll itself. But the tension rises knowing I have a coupon to toss some extra badness into the story to enhance the drama of the moment. I usually save up a couple or three to make a final scene even more dramatic. It's a fun way of creating that rising tension in the adventure where initial failures don't mean as much as those when we're nearing the climax of the adventure. May not be your cup of tea but my group digs it.

      I'm not exactly clear what the difference is between "Make a note of and reveal it when the time is right" and "saving up moves."
      \nIt's the same as the difference between writing and sticking to your fronts and just winging it. In the case of "Make a not and reveal when the time is right," one way or another you've committed to the fiction ahead of time. The event is already set, and the players get to discover it in time. With "saving up moves", you make no move, nothing is set, and things are going get steamy later which is something you can do anyway. That said, if having the chips there is something that helps you create tension and your players love, that's fantastic and I hope you keep it up and don't let anyone say you're doing it wrong. I still think there is value in the difference between "this is what the rules say to do" and "this is my super awesome drift", though.
