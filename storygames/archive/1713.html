"Generic" Games, what do you like?

Andy
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games
Hey all, as I mentioned before my weekly group is going to pick up some kind of generic game (that handles SF) and play that for the better part of a year.

In my hunt through the Good Generics out there, I've found some pretty varied stuff: JAGS, d6, Action!/Fuzion (almost the same), Savage Worlds, GURPS Lite, EABA, and so on. The cookoff within my own group is quickly narrowing to Savage Worlds or GURPS Lite, with original JAGS and EABA "on deck".

Anyway, whichever we decide on playing, a few of us are buying (if they haven't already) Savage Worlds, and planning on running that in downtime and the like. It's a pretty slick game.

I remember hearing a lot of folks rave about it when it came out, and I took a look at it at the LGS. It came across as a pretty ho-hum everyman light gaming system, with a huge focus on miniatures and minis combat. So I didn't pay too much attention to it. Fast forward to about a month ago, when I gave it a second look at the F... uh, no L... GS. I treated it as it was meant to be treated: As a generic game where you pick and choose the components you want to use and ignore the rest, instead of feeling compelled from using all the rules all the time. This time around, I found it to be quite clever, well built, and a high potential for the kind of gaming that I like; fast and fun.

Here's the main things that I like about the game:

1) The stepped die system. So I get to use my d4s, d8s, and sometimes even d12s.

2) Most of the time heroes are rolling two dice at once (a d4/d6/d8 whatever, plus a 1d6 because That's How Heroes Roll).

3) MAJOR: As I went through the typical advantages and drawbacks area, I was pretty struck by the fact that they didn't include "Everything Under the Sun". You'll notice if you look through Generic games that a lot of them contain and repeat most of the same advantages and drawbacks. Most of them, especially drawbacks, tend to be really far out there or useless, and just get in the way of the say 3-6 drawbacks that people would actually use (you have to fish them out of the text, getting around the "No Limbs" "Cannot Speak or Move", etc ones. Even Advantages, like Feats in D&D, tend to boil down to two groups: The 5 useful ones, and the 20 ones that no one would ever take (they make no strategic or roleplaying sense).

However, there were FAR less of the "useless" advantages and drawbacks in Savage Worlds. Most of the ones I saw I could easily imagine using with very little "work". That really changed my opinion of the game overall; it wasn't just another generic game someone slapped together, but rather a lot of thought went into even the traditional "Every Game Has These Things" elements.

4) Combat/Miniatures. Normally I don't like either, because the rules tend to be either complicated or lame. And yet, I enjoyed Mordheim (Warhammer-style fantasy small-party skirmish system), and found Savage Worlds' mini-bits to be a great successor to a marriage of "roleplaying system" and "miniature skirmish system". Unlike, say, my bad experiences with Conan OGL, which was like a roleplaying game constrained into a tight, tight girdle of complicated miniatures movement and strategy. I'm actually interested in buing a bunch of cardboard minis and racking them up.

5) Does System Matter? Woah! So, Savage Worlds has some elements of social conflict and the like (which I always love), but part of me really wants to dig into this system more simply because it's Just Structured Enough... and Rules Light Enough (weird phrase, that) to, I dunno, hopefully encourage more roleplaying by the players, and more risk-taking by the GM in setting dice-based stakes. It really does what it aims to do, and stays the fuck out of the way for everything else.

The other thing is that two of the players in my group have stepped forward saying that they're going to give GMing a try for the first time ever. This system is really appealing to them simply because of how little you have to have "memorized" to initially craft that scenario. I'm probably going to have them use a copy of Savage Worlds with Andrew AKA Dr. Rotwang!'s Adventure Funnel stapled to it.

6) Character Creation. Two minutes. Seriously, when I ran a demo of it with my group, I was one character short. I said, "No Problem, cause it only takes two minutes to make a character anyway". Jeremie pulled out his watch and said "Go", jokingly. I laughed for a sec, then realized about 10 seconds in that he wasn't kidding. So I ran through the rules, and made up a character, gave him skills and Quirks and all, even equipment ("gun"), and all the while flipping frantically through the book two or three times. When I said "Done!", I was at one minute and fifty seconds or so.

In other words, there's no fear of character death (or at least not as much) like we had in some other games, where it takes like three hours to make a character from scratch if This One Dies.

Anyway, that's some of the cool things I found in the system. It's not appealing to all the members of the group (namely one, which is why we're looking at GURPS Lite and the like), but a lot of us are digging it.

Anyone else have a preference for some generic system or other? Why? What about it appeals to you?

-Andy

    Do things like PTA and Wushu count? The Solar System from TSOY? Generic Otherkind Dice?

    I've always liked the original, freely-released version of Fudge. You don't pick things from lists. You just write down the important traits, rank them, and you're off. If more stuff comes up in play, you write it down and rank it. You can use the core system to build complex mechanical things if you want, but it's pretty stripped down and lean at the core. That said, Fudge doesn't teach you how to do stuff you don't already know how to do. But if you know how to do something, Fudge lets you give it mechanical weight.

    That said, my favorite "generic" system is still TSOY, which has a lot of what I like about Fudge, but with additions that really help structure things. Sure, generic Fudge lets you improvize completely weird, bizarre things ("We're all the zeitgeists of various ages! Let's narrate scenes in which the Cold War fights Transhumanism and see who triumphs!"), TSOY is going work pretty damn well for any semi-traditional thing you might want to run.

    Last night at SG Boston, I ran Clint Kraus' hot new indie game Roanoke, which is based on Wushu but has a few interesting additions, a lot of cool flavor, and helpful GM guidelines. I like a lot of what Wushu pulls out of the players (it's low-key player empowerment for people who're comfortable with Exalted's stunting rules, but not much else), but the way it structures character development and plotting (i.e. not at all) is not as satisfying as TSOY. Makes me wonder what a TSOY/Wushu hybrid would look like, since both have been released under open licenses...

    Experience in Savage Worlds is awarded depending on overall party achievements per session. Looking over the rules though, there's no real definition of what constitutes "Achievements", so it's mostly going to be one of those unwritten things.

    However, from the rulebook:

    Experience Awards:

    1 The group accomplished very little or had a very short session.
    2 The group had more successes than failures.
    3 The group succeeded greatly, and their adventure had a significant impact on the overall story.

    So from this we see at least hints that the experience should be awarded for progressing the story rather than just killing stuff willy nilly. So killing a random group of orcs won't net any experience, but killing a very specific group of orcs may well do, as might convincing someone to change their mind on a specific issue.

    XP is awarded by whatever method your group usually uses. That is to say, in my group the GM arbitrates, but players lobby for each others XP based on what they enjoyed during the session.

  • Rich Stokes
    November 2006
    Bennies are SWs universal currency. They are spent by players for re-rolls, to avoid damage and in a couple of other ways. They're a pretty bog standard karma mechanic that I'm sure you'll have seen a dozen times before.

    Players start a session with a set number of bennies, usually 3, and can gain more by certain actions. The GM is supposed to give them out for whatever they feel is appropriate. So things like "good roleplaying", "being awesome" etc. But as above, each group will decide how the bennies are awarded since there is very little in the way of hard and fast rules for dishing them out. I tend to give them out whenever someone does something that the group agrees is awesome, whenever a player deliberately plays his character's personality in a way that makes life harder for himself and occasionally when players point out that I've forgotten a rule that would make life harder for the players.

    But that's just how I use them, because that's the behaviour that I want to encourage.

    Bennies work to even out the luck that could bugger up other games. D&D, for example needs to have combat's carefully balanced, because a too tough opponent can really bugger the players up. With Bennies and similar mechanics though, a GM who has perhaps made an enemy a little too tough has the "buffer" of bennies to fall back on. The players will spend them to even the fight out, to make a social encounter less difficult or to circumvent some other obstacle that the GM has placed in their way that they don't particularly want to engage with.

    But, since FATE allows scaling between what Fred describes as "Task" and "Scene" resolutions, I think it's far superior to any of the rest of the pure generic games mentioned.     I'm sure it's pretty easy to drift Savage Worlds to this point, too, but that's not FATE's only advantage. FATE allows for movement into the middle (or even right) columns of Fred's chart with it's resolution, especially where Aspects are concerned.     Unless you want to enforce the very traditional mode of play, I'd argue that FATE wins of Savage Worlds in every category. It even beats out HQ in that it's easier to create frameworks for character construction in any genre (though HQ beats it out in terms of things like granularity, IMO).

    TSOY is better, I think, for play in Near, than as a generic game. But, given a write-up of the genre/setting in question, it's as good as HQ or FATE.
