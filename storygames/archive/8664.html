\n Can I pick the collective brains of SG real quick?

Where did the idea of "experience points" first originate (the wikipedia article is very unhelpful)? I'm imagining that it's a pre-D&D thing, prevalent in certain kinds of campaign-based wargames, with units getting better over time. Perhaps it originated as pools of resource points that could be used to purchase or upgrade units and equipment, which eventually got applied to updating the actual abilities of individual pieces?

Anybody got real info, not just speculation? Speculation I have by the bucketful.

    \n I don't know, but I'd take a look at Chainmail, since that might shed some light on the question. I would look for you, but I'm scandalized to find I don't own a copy even though I thought I did.

    I will throw in one smidge of speculation, which is that if wargames had unit progression there would have to be a figure marking method, because in traditional war games all players have to be able to look at pike minis and recognize they are pikes (for example). So if you wanted "level 2 pike" or whatever there would have to be a physical way to show that, and I've never heard of anything like that.

    \n@Ben: ah, but not all wargaming was (is) miniatures. Not even the majority of it, I'd venture to say. "traditional" wargaming grognards play on hex maps with square cardstock chits with lots of small numbers on them... and they would allow upgraded units simply by swapping the chits for those with bigger numbers on them.

    Re: warhammer. If the poisoned arrows are not the standard for that unit (in which case you don't need to differentiate), then they probably are some kind of magic item/upgrade that is simply written down on the army list. And, mind you, Warhammer hardly qualifies at all as wargame, if you ask the grognards, let alone a traditional one

    \nBen, Robin, Renato: Yeah, marking units isn't that difficult in my mind, especially, as Renato says, when you'd traditionally just swap them out for other chits or figures. In fact, I'm imagining that XP was basically an innovation that allowed you to play again with the same figures and have it still be interesting, because things would have changed without you having to change your entire army around. Gorkamorka did this, speaking of GW, by changing the army list constantly, but I imagine it has predecessors. I might try to hit up OldGeezer and see if he knows what Dave's inspiration was.

    Matthijs: From poking around a bit, it looks like you're right about Dave being the first one to use both XP and experience levels in proto-RPGs. That may be good enough for my purposes, actually. Thanks!
