\n
\n Speaking as an anime and sci-fi fan, I likes my cat-people and my lizard people.

But they don't really act like cats or lizards do, if they were intelligent.

The Kzinti from the Known Space novels are - quite frankly - Scots or Japanese cosplaying as catboys. The Kilrathi from Wing Commander aren't much better.

I would much rather have a catlike, or doglike, or lizardlike... whatever... species that ACTED LIKE that animal, if that animal were sapient.

I did like the Traveller RPG's take on it, but I don't think it went far enough.

First - what fictional species would you recommend that do this well?

Second - how would YOU do that well?

    \n I dunno, I'm not sure I'd want to play a dog-like race whose main cultural foibles were being distracted by fast-moving objects, chasing sticks and licking their own genitals.

    Seriously though, I'm not a big fan of the old anthropomorphics in sci-fi games unless (and this is important) every race is anthropomorphic something and there's no attempt made to disguise them as 'original' aliens. If I were doing a sci-fi in this bent, and especially if these aliens took on the psychological profiles of the animals they resemble, I'd do it like 'Albedo' and make it a full-on Anthro RPG. No aliens, no humans, just anthropomorphic critters.

    But that's the thing about anthropomorphisation; the animals likely don't have the qualities we perceive in them, or even any concept of what those qualities mean. We project human values onto dumb animals, that's what anthropomorphisation means.

    Not being a fan of this type of alien, I can't really think of any successful examples. Unlike Graham, I found the Mass Effect aliens extremely stereotypical of the genre, but that may be because the game spent way too much time explaining itself when it should have been demonstrating by example. I always had a soft spot for Iron Claw and Jade Claw, but they're not sci-fi. Albedo I already mentioned, but I've never been able to get my hands on a copy to read it, so I can't say if it's good or not.

    -Ash

    \n Huh. I always thought the Kzinti were pretty well done, as a feline analog (note: they ain't "Earth tigers with language and tech"). Have you read all the Man-Kzin Wars books? Authors other than Niven do better with them (the Ringworld Kzin are more like you're saying).

    Other than that... I got nuthin'. It's a rare author that can actually accomplish what you seem to expect, Elliott. Most authors can't even write about *human* world views and behaviors other than those of their gender and socio-economic, ethnic, and educational background. To have to step beyond those mental bounds AND extrapolate how an animal (a) thinks now and thus (b) will think when sapient... well, let us know who pulls it off and we'll go read the next Nebula Award winner.

    There's also a practical element--one might call it "You Can't Write About Aliens"--in that most fiction tries to actually be of some utility (cathartic or otherwise) to humans, not to animals. So I'd argue that the pinnacle of success at what you seek... would mean little or nothing to you. It might be fascinating (much like some of the weirder LOLcat images that come to my RSS feed) but it won't resonate or provide utility to your human mind... unless it's so anthropomorphic that it succumbs to the "faults" you outline in your OP.

    That's my theory, anyway. It's kind of like Ashok says: a "real dogman" story would be all about fascination with anal scents and musk, and constant domination and submission exchanges (I mean CONSTANT, if we talk a large population--go to a dog park some time!). It would bore humans to tears, if it didn't disgust them.

    \n There's a couple of good examples of Cetaceans evolved to human-intellect level (or of them throwing off their millenia-long vow of silence...):

    David Brin's Uplift Universe books, Startide Rising, and Uplift War, both feature sentient nun-human Earth mammals as protagonists who have some interesting variation in how they act, think, and apporach problems. These are worth bonus points for the massive array of wickedly cool aliens of all type and description on display, especially the Jophur, creatures composed of stacked tori of living wax.

    The Image comic book series Elephantmen works the tried-and-true 'we evolved them to fight for us' model of animal sapience pretty entertainingly, as do S. Andrew Swann's Moreau hard-boiled cyberpunk novels.

    Phillip Jose Farmer, writing as Kurt Vonnegut's fictional SF author Kilgore Trout, wrote a gonzo SF masterpiece called Venus on the Half-Shell, where he postulated a far-future society of evolved cats that had the conversation-stopping detail of large buckets of water outside hotels and apartment buildings where the highly promiscuous and exceedingly fertile adult felines could easily and conveniently drown all those unwanted kittens that were the result of their sexual revolution.

    FInally, for something much more low-key and story game-like, I can't recommend Terry Bisson's Hugo and Nebula Award-winning short story Bears Discover Fire highly enough. The plot is hat bears learn how to use fire and start building campfires on highway medians in the Southern US. That's really all that happens, but the nuance and musings on community, sentience, communication, and what 'wilderness' means are sublime.

    As to gaming, I have real trouble playing something that's completely alien. I am much more enamored of the whole 'Uplift' conception; sentient EARTH animals are easiest for us to have a sense of their general disposition and drives and to extrapolate that to what sort of intelligence they'd likely have, and which of US they'd look to for examples of how to behave and start to deal with things like recorded memory and leisure time.

    It's the age-old 'other dilemma': we want our aliens to be cool and odd and different, but not so much that they'd have no interest in, or worse, capability of, interacting with us.

    -Jim C.
