Posted By: Double KingI upped this over at NerdNyc but thought it might find an audience here too. New communications nexus from our friends at Google. My initial response was that if this bad boy had a dice roller it might be the perfect virtual table top for story gaming...



from today's WSJ:

"Google Inc. previewed a new communications service that combines features of email, instant-messaging and document-sharing to facilitate multiperson conversations online.

The Google Wave service caps a years-long project to come up with a way to break down the barriers between different types of online communication services, said Lars Rasmussen, the Google engineer who led the development of the service along with his brother, Jens Rasmussen.

The service, which won't be open to the public for several months, will allow users to start a conversation, called a "wave," and to invite their contacts to join it. Any member of the wave can put photos, notes or other content into the group, which updates in near-real time.

People see an "in-box" with the most recent waves they have joined and whether others have added any new notes or content to them. They can click to scroll through the wave and see what content has been added to it.

...

Google, which announced the new service at its developer conference in San Francisco on Thursday, envisions Wave as not only a way to share messages with friends and colleagues, but also to share and access a variety of information online. For instance, a user could create a wave that is an article he or she wrote and then invite people to read and comment on it.

Mr. Rasmussen said it is too early to tell how consumers will use Wave, but he expects that the various applications and services Google hopes developers will build on top of it will be a big draw. Videogame developers could build a game that began as a wave, for example, and Google will allow other Web sites to embed waves into their Web sites.

He added that Google hasn't thought much about how the service could generate revenue. "One of the great luxuries of Google is that we get to not think about that yet," he said."
\nThis is neat. I can see this as the next 'wave' in virtual table top gaming. If someone had a tabletop they would be able to plug into Wave and get a whole lot of open source functionality.

    \n I've checked it out some and I think... well, I mean isn't it basically yet-another client, albeit served? I already can do all it claims, just not within a single browser instance... and I think I like the separation. When I'm emailing, I have *chosen* not to chat--there's a reason. When I post "an article" on my web site, I've done so for a reason that's different than if I email it to a massive list of contacts... or to a mailing listserv... or to a web forum.

    The best thing about their "wave" concept, as I see it, is collaborative authoring, which includes (most) gaming. And so, sure, i could see me firing up wave to, say, manage a game. But THEN AGAIN, I already build either Google Docs or a Group to run a game, or I put various play-aides on such a group. And, barring collision edits, a wiki or shared Google Doc does it all, too (minus instant-refreshing of changes).

    Anyhow... I'll play with it, I'm sure (just like I DLed and dumped Chrome, and will do so with Android, probably, when I get my netbook--install then dump to go back to Ubuntu). It's not a "killer app" as I see it; more of a merging of other apps to gain the "instant" benefits of chat while losing the channel control (mostly) of other existing mediums.

    No... I'd rather Google develop a social networking protocol that kills the 60-odd sandboxes that are Facebook and Twitter and Flicker and AOL and MSN and Yahoo 360 and Friendster and.... (it never ceases to amaze me how folks jumped right back onto that ad-driven model for contact management!)

    \n Cool, Nick, but not *quite* what I was thinking. It seems like the former just lets me make my own sandbox, while the latter lets me (and only me) cross-post to any sandbox to which I'm registered. Neither kills the sandbox, like a protocol (ex: HTTP) would.

    I'm thinking more like e-mail meets RSS meets XML: a way that I can put a file (or files) onto ANY site and, due to their extension (say, SOC) and their tagging (extended XML) they are displayed in ANY other site that calls or queries them, with embeds. RSS-style connectivity would make them auto-update. So if folks want to use (say) Friendster to keep abreast of my SOC data that I keep on davidartman.com, they add a widget and anytime I update one of my SOC files, it RSSes to their widget (or vice versa). If I want to track other friends, I'd just have their "RSS address" (ex: their Friendster page, if they decide to use an ad-supported service) in my list and I'd see their updates in some view (based, again, on where I embed their feed).

    These damned socnets are like if one was an AOL user and could only e-mail other AOL users--irritatingly backwards and exclusive, for the web.

    \nDEV TALK

    Sorry, I left out OpenID, OAuth and Social Graph...but the shared goal of the projects is to achieve a standard of interoperability, such that the social networking landscape would become primarily divide between social info syndicators (like Friendster, Twitter, Digg, Flickr, DoWeEverLikeX.com, etc.) and social info aggregators/viewers (like Plaxo Pulse, and the less-open-or-capable Facebook). That, at least, was the oft-repeated company line from Google, MySpace, and others at last year's Google I/O. Here's the broad view talk that I was lucky enough to see. You immediately reminded me of it, so I should have linked to it right away, rather than attempting laconicism. Again, sorry. I believe understand what you're talking about, and I also hope that's the direction things go.

    TALKING ABOUT WAVE NOW

    It sounds like a gadget could be written that allows a structured, editable character sheet to be inserted as a document node, that invitees (the other players) can comment on line-by-line, with IM-level immediacy (or more, according to the video). To me, one of the great limitation to remote play (pbm/pbp) seemed to be the impracticality of that kind of group-centric character creation, which is required in some games, and beneficial for almost all of them. I'd love to write something like that.

    But I've never played in such a game! Anyone out there who can tell us about limitations to remote play, specifically those that might relate to what Wave seems to be capable of?
