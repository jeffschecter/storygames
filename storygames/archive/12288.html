\n I made a list for our GM so he can check on what he's not doing right. Can you think of anything to add?

Typical GM failure points, in no particular order:
- not engaging the players
- not having enough for the characters to do
- having too much of their own agenda
- not mixing up the types of scenes
- not sharing the spotlight around
- not seeming to be in control of the story
- not knowing the rules
- not teaching the players the rules
- obvious railroading
- having super NPCs that the PC can't best or avoid
- having Mary Sue NPCs
- not differentiating between NPCs
- not having any leads to follow
- not having a story/plot/scenario that makes sense
- not preserving the mystery of the story/plot/scenario
- blocking too much
- not giving full rein to characters' successes
- not keeping to the tone of the piece
- going or allowing players to go gonzo
- not giving the players a clear sense of their characters' place in the world
- having many Deus ex machinas
- not making the game exciting enough
- not using the rules consistently
- changing the rules too much
- not discarded rules that don't work
- not making it fun
- not dealing with difficult players who are spoiling it for others

As long as the GM knows enough to keep the game going, that's all that's needed.

    \n- not keeping to the tone of the piece

    This implies that a piece has a single unique tone, and that deviations from that set tone are a bad thing. Pieces with only a single tone are, without exception, dull. Even what seems like the most inappropriate tone can help break up the piece, recharging batteries ready for a return to the norm. Hong Kong cinema, in particular, handles tone far better than most Hollywood films precisely because it's often far looser with it over the course of a film.

    - not preserving the mystery of the story/plot/scenario

    Why shouldn't players be party to secrets their characters are not? You can choose to keep the players in the dark if you want, but it is not the only way to play a mystery game. This rule would only be true if there was no separation of player from character.

      \n Right.

      Good version: "I'm going to GM. I accept the responsibility to do what I can to help the others participate and have meaningful input to play."

      Bad version: "The game's not fun! I'm going to cross my arms and complain, because that means this GM sucks." In other words, that personal responsibility is good; but the expectation to invest someone else with responsibility, the expectation among the group that fun is Bob's responsibility, is bad.

      I think where wires get crossed, though, is the traditional alignment of the GM role with the social organizer role and the rules teacher/facilitator role. While that's often the case in gaming circles, it needn't be.

      For example, if I'm running a one-shot for a bunch of strangers, and we're playing a game they're not familiar with, I know that I, as GM, should expect to be helping people learn the rules, making sure everyone gets a turn to speak, and trying to make the game experience as much fun as possible for them.

      But I've also seen situations where those roles are mixed up. Say Julie joins an existing roleplaying group. They like to play D&D at Bob's house. Bob invites everyone over for supper, including Terry, who is the resident rules expert, and who used to GM most games for the group. Julie is not very familiar with D&D, and it's her first time at Bob's house, but she has an idea for an adventure (or whatever) and would like to try her hand at GMing D&D.

      They get together and they play! I wouldn't be surprised if Bob was the one people look to in order to check on things like: "When should we get started? How long do we expect the game to run?" "How late is too late? Should we pick up the pace and try to wrap up once we hit 10:00, or 11:00, or what?" "When is a good time for a break?" I would NOT expect Julie to be responsible for handling those things--after all, she doesn't know how things are run in Bob's household, when people need to go to bed, etc.

      At the same time, Terry might be the one fielding the rules questions, and pointing out to Julie (or anyone else) where a certain rule might need to be applied or corrected in order for the game to run smoothly. Maybe the new GM isn't familiar with all the rules, and it's easier to let them handle the fiction and have Terry handle some of the mechanical issues that come up.

      Likewise, if Terry was used to GMing for the group, he might be looked to by the group in order to speak up when Kate, the quiet one of the group, hasn't had a chance to say/do anything. After all, Julie doesn't have the experience to know that Kate is shy about take her turn, or whatever. I wouldn't expect Julie to be responsible for that--she doesn't know Kate and doesn't know this group's dynamics, after all. (And I've seen this one happen a fair bit, when guest GMing for existing groups--a player might say, "Hang on, we haven't heard from Phil for a while. What's your character doing in the meantime, Phil?"

      But maybe the whole group knows that Kate, while quiet, tends to be the genius who pulls unrelated plotlines together or delivers great one-liners, or comes up with the best character concepts. That group wouldn't be expecting Julie to do that, if they know they can do it on their own--the GM giving them enough material to work with and then backing off might work. Whereas Julie trying to "create the fun" for the group, unaware of how Phil and Kate and Terry work together, might be less fun for the group than allowing a quiet moment for Kate to jump in with a funny in-character voice or whatever.

      My point is that thinking of "the fun" as the GM's responsibility, as well as many of these other roles, isn't necessarily wrong, but can be a slippery slope. I find it much more helpful to think of the group's fun: a team working to fulfill a shifting set of duties. And responsibilities are often spread out based on skills, experience, and social circumstances (maybe Jim's wife is the only one who knows how not to set off his painful childhood memories in a Dogs in the Vineyard game; it's then her responsibility to speak up if she needs to).

      A group like the semi-hypothetical one I've described can have a great time without expecting Julie to "give them one". It's kind of like orgasms. Yeah, there's another person involved, but really, you know what you need to do for yourself--they can't generally do it for you.

      My worry would be that Julie, expecting the "responsibility" of making the fun for the group (and all the long list up above) might actually squash some of those useful group dynamics, working against herself and against the group. Just like chasing after giving someone an orgasm in bed might totally ruin the experience if that's not what they're after at the moment.
