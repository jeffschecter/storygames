\n Hi to all and thanks for your time.

At the end of a VERY long road, we finally have our KickStarter page up for preview. That said, we're not going to run our campaign until coming late march this spring. One of the best advice I've read about KickStarter is this: "when you think you are ready, add three months". And so that is what we're going to do.

Our KickStarter page can be found here.

Some questions:
- Is the goal of the project clear enough?
- We have worked very hard to come with a limited, but coherent number of perks (reward level). What do you think?
- As you can see, we have chosen to be multi systems, as discussed previously here and I think we have managed to find people who would know what they are doing with each system.
- Please note the vote system we're trying to put in place. Do you think this would be engaging?
- Note the very long stretch goals we have and how we have decided to regroup them, and only show the first ones in each category.
- Any comments on our strategy to wait 8 to 10 weeks before launching and using our KS page as a promotional tool?
- We are hesitating to put the "explicit backer" level more around 4K or 5K. We're not sure what this is worth exactly ...
- We hesitate to include shipping cost in all rewards level, when applicable. Does it really change something?

Well, thanks again for your time.

    \n The previews look gorgeous and as a developer, I'm really looking forward to seeing what you guys are doing with the website.

    Some thoughts:

    Just scrolling through that page takes me over 15 seconds, and that's steady mouse wheel scrolling without reading the details. There is way too much going on there for someone like me who likes to just back the project and know what they're getting.

    The non-linear stretch goals are hard to understand and are frankly off-putting.     \n Random notes:

    Regarding the clarity and length of the page, does the Kickstarter page-building paradigm allow any sort of collapsibles? (A small Javascript script and the ability to define a div element in the html, basically.) It could fundamentally improve the approachability of the page if you could put all deep detail under some sort of links that can be opened up when the reader wants to see more detail about any given issue. Imagine if those vertical bars at the beginning of each section were collapsed when the page is first opened, and you could open each individually by clicking.

    Failing that, putting all ancillary details about e.g. the company or the details of the complex stretch goal system on your own site and just linking to it might be worthwhile in combating the page bloat. Not that long Kickstarter pages are bad (you need to give people enough time to decide to participate while reading the page, ideally), but the presentation still needs to be manageable.

    Also, a table of contents is a time-honored structural tool: introductory paragraph, ToC (with links to topics), and then everything else in logical linear order.

    The PDF materials that you link in the "About Existanc3" section don't need to have such large cover arts as to take the entire width of the page. It makes for choppy reading when you have a sentence like "Here's an introductory download:" and then you need to scroll down to skip a huge picture to find the next sentence, only to have that followed by another big image... I know you want to put your art front and center, but an introductory video (plenty of opportunity to showcase the art in one of those) and some restraint would serve better there. The ideal way to visually depict a download like that would probably be something like I'm demonstrating here: a small visual link directly related to the text paragraph that explains what it is.

    Technically the name of the rules system of The Shadow of Yesterday is Solar System (not my idea, Clinton named it in 2005 or so). It's like the name of the rules system for Vampire is the "Storyteller" system, two different names for slightly different, yet related, things. It's not much of a deal, and maybe the TSoY name is better known, but perhaps it would be better to use a phrasing like "the Solar System, as seen in The Shadow of Yesterday", if one wants to emphasize the TSoY connection. That way people who do know the game well won't get miffed about your perceived lack of knowledge about a game they like. Besides, these days there are some people out there (particularly in Europe) who know what "Solar System" is without knowing what TSoY is, as funky as that seems.

    Regarding the ordering of the various topics, you probably shouldn't have the "News and Updates" section right up top when you start the campaign; rather, have it start at the bottom and only move it to the top when the campaign moves into a phase where returning visitors to the page are more important than newcomers. The top of the page is the first thing they'll read, so it's better if you can provide something concise and concrete about the project there until you've gotten through the first rush, at least. Even better: put a link to the news and updates at the top, and put the section itself at the bottom.

    "About Existanc3" is a very good section to have early on in the project description, as it's the chapter where you explain what the product here is concretely. (In the lead you've established that it's a tabletop rpg campaign compatible with a bunch of rules sets; here's where you should make the detailed pitch.) However, it's named wrong, it should be "About the Project", and it should lead with as simple a description as possible: "Avalanche is a traditional high fantasy tabletop rpg campaign structured and presented in an entirely new manner..." and so on. Remember: corporate marketing speech that attempts to force immersion is not compelling in the Internet age, you don't need to drown your reader in a mass of specialty terminology you invented. You can explain about "Existanc3" later in the text, at this point it's most important for you to explain the underlying logic of what you're proposing.

    Speaking of which: you shouldn't end the aforementioned section without having actually explained the theoretical underpinnings of the project. I could be wrong, but I suspect that the two selling points of this project are the aesthetics (which you'll need to import with choice art and flavour text detail, much of it in the video) and the unique campaign structure. The latter you need to explain with a simple description and a couple of diagrams, I suspect: tell about what problem this new setup solves, and what kind of roleplaying it accomplishes, and you should get anybody interested in experimental grand fantasy campaigning on board. A sample is good, of course, but if you don't have a theoretical bird's eye explanation, many might not care enough to examine the sample.

    In the "Our Goal" section, swap the order of the list and the elaborating text; this way around it's more vague that the visual list at the end of the section is a listing of the project goals. Also, make that list look more like a list, now the elements sort of float in space. Maybe make the art a bit smaller and add list bullets of some sort, or make it a table with a thin border, or number the goals.

    In the "Rewards" section, indicate clearly which rewards go with which reward levels. E.g. you might put the graphic of each entry on the left side, the text in the middle, and a balloon of some sort with the minimum dollar amount on the right. Also, put the elaboration about what the voting perk means into the table, all logical-like. (If it feels difficult to modify your images with the reward descriptions, that might be an indication that it's a bad idea to have your text be in images in the first place; cut the text out, write it on the page, and align the images as floats.)

    Make a literal flow chart of your stretch goals; if it's a road-map of future development, make it look like it. Start it from the top of the image, put in a money gauge and a time gauge, and present visually how all these potential stretch goals inter-relate. Then, make the road-map clickable, or put in tooltip hints, so that the audience can interactively examine it and get details about the stretch goals that most interest them. Much more appealing than a long, long list.

    Hmm, I need to leave for a dinner date, but I think those are pretty much the biggest issues that come to me. As always, being clear and straightforward about the nature of the project and its biggest selling points is the biggest chokepoint for Kickstarter campaign quality: don't let the complex stretch goal game draw all the attention, you need to make the base promise compelling to get people far enough that they'll get hooked on the rewards carneval.

    Regarding the terminology you use about system compatibility - this was already said, but you should perhaps not rely on standard expressions like "compatible with" in explaining the relationship of the product to various rules systems. In actual fact the product is a systemless fantasy rpg campaign, which is an understandable and established product niche; it might gain compatibility with one or more rules systems via stretch goals, but it's too soon to say that it is compatible now, except in the way it's compatible with anything anywhere. Perhaps a way to express the relationship with your chosen rules-sets less likely to annoy pedantic customers would be to say that the campaign is "well suited to" these systems, or "developed with an eye towards compatibility with" (too long, I know).
