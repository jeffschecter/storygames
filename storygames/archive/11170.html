\n It seems to me that the Story Games community is having a small crisis these days. Not as in "OMG WE'RE FALLING APART", but more like "hey, we should talk about this stuff in a civilized manner and see what we can find out".

To me, it looks like we, as a community, agree on most things. We don't have very strong conflicting feelings about things like "is role-playing fun", because we all agree that role-playing is fun. Nor do we fight much about "can role-playing have artistic value", because we agree that sure, sometimes it can, if we want it to. We even all agree that System Matters.

However, when it comes to the role of system design vs. player skill, we've had a few recent clashes. Here are some viewpoints that I believe have been expressed - I'm pairing them up to show the potential for conflict:

- It doesn't matter what system you use; a good group will make it work, and a bad one will make it suck.
- A good system will help almost any group have fun; a bad system will be sucky for any group.

- Systems that leave everything in the players' hands and force the group to fudge and make up stuff as they go along are just bad design! What's the point of having a system that does nothing except cower in a corner and hope nobody will use it?
- Systems that don't trust the players' aesthetic skill and judgment are just bad design! What's the point of having a system that assumes players are idiots?

What I want to say here is that I think these viewpoints are equally valid. We, as a community, can contain them all. However, we need to recognize that - as in all such matters - there is no One True Way. Just as the Big Model helped us see (wow, I'm getting evangelical here) that role-playing has room for competitive play, AND immersionist play, AND all sorts of other play, and they're all valid - we need to recognize that there are also different philosophies of game design that are, in fact, equally valid.

What do you think?

And I do mean think.

    \n In sociologist Margaret Archer's terms, we may have a case here of "constraining contradiction." When there's a desire for social homogeneity--for a strong group identity, in other words--but low "ideational integration" (the possession of contradictory beliefs, in other words), the "situational logic" becomes one of correction: trying to resolve contradictory beliefs. Some contradictions are impossible to resolve, however.

    The problem of how to get people with different beliefs to communicate productively actually gets a lot of attention in the scholarly literature. Political scientists who study "deliberative democracy" spring immediately to mind. The thrust of that work suggests that establishing a space within which procedures for communicative rationality can foster public discourse and understanding. The question they are addressing has to do with how to preserve a pluralistic society.

    Leah Ceccarelli's book on successful and unsuccessful interdisciplinary appeals in science concludes that sometimes strategic ambiguity is called for when you're talking to two different knowledge-producing communities but want them to find common ground: let us agree on what we can agree on, and table those issues that don't require an immediate resolution for another day.

    I'm becoming increasingly convinced that communication style is an important first step. We should encourage communication techniques that open up conversations rather than close them down: charitable readings, "mirroring," non-defensive language, and so forth.

    \n The two sets of example statements seem to me very different things.

    - It doesn't matter what system you use; a good group will make it work, and a bad one will make it suck.
    - A good system will help almost any group have fun; a bad system will be sucky for any group.

    These just can't both be right. People can of course respectfully disagree, but at least one (and of course possibly both) must be wrong. They're not therefore necessarily equally valid (unless they're both wrong I suppose).

    People can be polite to each other, without disguising the reality that if they hold one of these views they necessarily hold the other to be an error.

    On the other hand:

    - Systems that leave everything in the players' hands and force the group to fudge and make up stuff as they go along are just bad design! What's the point of having a system that does nothing except cower in a corner and hope nobody will use it?
    - Systems that don't trust the players' aesthetic skill and judgment are just bad design! What's the point of having a system that assumes players are idiots?

    Both these statements are predicated on the assumption that one approach to system design holds good for everyone. But if we know anything, we know that's not true. It's entirely possible for you to prefer systems that allow for fiat and me to prefer more robust systems and for both of us to be right. Here, neither statement is valid as worded (statements of how systems ought to be), but both can be valid as statements of preference.

    All that said, if the point is that people listen a little more, and tell a little less, I'm right behind you. I no longer discuss immersionism in any forum, having been told on too many occasions either that it doesn't exist or if it does it's evidence of psychological instability (I don't think I've heard that here though, to be fair). The problem with terms, immersionism, Big Model, whatever, is they encourage fitting people within the terms rather than listening to what they say.

    Posted By: Bill_WhiteI'm becoming increasingly convinced that communication style is an important first step. We should encourage communication techniques that open up conversations rather than close them down: charitable readings, "mirroring," non-defensive language, and so forth.
    \nBill, this sounds like what old men say to young men, who ignore it because it doesn't sound as cool as Fighting For What You Believe At All Costs. But I believe what you say is true; it fits with my experience.

    At our Norwegian role-playing forum, we've got a strong focus on how people discuss things. Irony and sarcasm, for instance, are seen as something that breaks down productive conversation. Varying forms of rhetoric that fall under the term "hersketeknikker" - techniques of controlling others - are also quickly spotted and commented on. We've only got the one forum, and it's supposed to be for everyone, which means we HAVE to be tolerant to people who have different values and gaming interests. We've had big fights over things like "indie gamer snobbery" and "how stupid is D&D really", but we seem to manage to get through them.

    Max (Balbinus), you're wrong when you claim the system has no memory. A forum has at least three important memory structures:
    - Its rules, developed over time by the members as a collective, which influences the way all members post
    - The memories of its active participants, which influence the way they post, and provides context and example for others
    - The memories of its administrator(s), which influence how they moderate others' posts
    S-G isn't heavily moderated, and there are few rules, which means that the main responsibility for keeping it civil, productive and open-minded lies with the active participants. Each of us is a major influence on the culture of this forum. We are its memory. If we decide to change the way we post, it will affect the forum as a whole.

    \n Okay, following up on my original post here. Let's see what happens.

    Heres's what I'm trying to get at: Two things that seem to be mutually exclusive can be true at the same time. Especially if these two things are stated in absolute, extreme terms - like the statements I set up in my initial post:\n
    Posted By: Matthijs- It doesn't matter what system you use; a good group will make it work, and a bad one will make it suck.
    - A good system will help almost any group have fun; a bad system will be sucky for any group.

    - Systems that leave everything in the players' hands and force the group to fudge and make up stuff as they go along are just bad design! What's the point of having a system that does nothing except cower in a corner and hope nobody will use it?
    - Systems that don't trust the players' aesthetic skill and judgment are just bad design! What's the point of having a system that assumes players are idiots?
    \nLet me start with an example from the Real World of Science. There's been some debate over how we end up the way we do. Is it because of inherited traits? Or is it because of the environment? At times in this debate, people have been on the barricades promoting and defending these as two opposing views that can't be united.

    The accepted truth at the moment, as I understand it, is that it's ALL inheritance. And it's ALL environment. How many arms do you have? Two, of course! Determined by inheritance! Except if you lose them both in a car crash. Then you have zero - determined by environment. The attempt to claim it's either/or only mucks up the entire debate - you have to have both factors, both thoughts, in your head at once if you want to describe reality.

    My argument is that the statements I set up in the original post are false choices. By defending one of them at the cost of the other, we fail to see that the truth is somewhere in the middle. Here's one way of seeing how they fit together:

    The first one: A bad group will spoil just about any game session. A bad system will spoil just about any game session. A good system can make a mediocre group improve their game; a good group can make a mediocre system shine. They're both a major, but not exclusive, influence on the game session.
    The second one: A system that has no room for the players, is of course pointless, as is designing a system that isn't meant to be used. A system should support the players in achieving their aesthetic goals - which means system and group agendas must match for an optimal experience.

    I think what we're seeing right now is based on a lot of historical baggage, together with a clash of gaming cultures. For various historical reasons, we've made up our minds - in reaction to previous historical trends - that "dice are useless", or "freeform doesn't work", or "the GM is always a tyrant", or "using numbers ruins my immersion", or "system matters more than anything", or "the group matters more than anything". Principles like these stand in the way of learning and growing, both as a player and a game designer.

    I believe that if you want to be really good at what you do, you need to actively search for that which you don't understand yet. It will make you more humble, more nuanced, and less sexy and famous. Fuck that. I want to understand what we're doing on as many levels as I can.

    In terms of

      \nI have been reading those threads, and I find them as "clashy" as rustling newspapers in a smoking lounge at an old school gentleman's club.       So there's two sides to the conversation, right? The communicator and the receiver. The communication that works best, IMO, is when both sides take responsibility for their message. The communicator relays their message in a way that they hope the receiver will take to, and the receiver suspends their preconceived ideas and give the communicator some leeway in order to receive the message.
      Some of the biggest communication fuckups here usually happen when one of the two sides take no responsibility for their actions:
      "Hey, I'm just saying what I say. I am in no way responsible for how you take my message, or how it makes you feel."
      "This speaker is talking about me! Their words are a barely hidden message that is directly intended to rile me and belittle my opinion."

      When people chill the fuck out and act with empathy, communication happens. As Brand stated, a ton of "conversations"/"discussions" are little more than social posturing. Real game-changing magic can happen if you personally take responsibility for seeing past that bullshit and stop playing those shitty games.
