    \n I ran 6 sessions of Afraid several years back and they eventually led to me write Geiger Counter, because I was frustrated with what wasn't working.

    Honestly, I'd suggest avoiding Afraid and just running an Afraid-inspired game using the standard Dogs in the Vineyard mechanics. Y'know, make a "town" (a community) and keep having the sin escalate until there's a monster with d10 "demonic influence" that's preying on people. Use the standard Dogs rules, but swap out the coat for something else cool. Have the characters come to town Scooby Doo style or have them connected to one of the victims, Afraid-style. Maybe allow the characters to gradually learn "rituals" over the course of play as they gather more information about the monster, techniques or weaknesses that give them more dice to roll against the monster and its victims. Use Fallout as normal instead of Conditions, but you can definitely encourage players to take new traits like "d4 - claustrophobic" that's just asking them to be framed into specific kinds of conflicts or situations.

    Dogs is robust and works really well. Just go with what works and steal some of the good ideas from Afraid without trying to play a partially finished game that's known to have a bunch of problems.

    \n What problems did people have?
    \n
    \nWe played 4+ sessions and it went well overall.
    \n
    \nProblems we had:
    \n- it's super lethal, a virtual meat grinder (took a bit for players to get used to this but I was ok with it)
    \n- to win, many characters have to die (fixed with the "your character goes into hibernation" rule)
    \n- making new characters wasn't super fast
    \n- bonds and supernatural items were unclear (easily ignorable)
    \n- some monster creation questions were unclear (easily ignorable)
    \n- Dogs situations feel like they run themselves, Afraid situations were a bit more work (but less so than most traditional games)
    \n- "in trouble" condition didn't feel as meaningful as the other conditions since we were almost always in trouble anyway but we could have misunderstood how this was meant to be used
    \n
    \nWe loved:
    \n- base dogs system
    \n- conditions
    \n- keep 2 dice for giving
    \n- escalation = ignore an attack & cut between scenes
    \n- most of monster creation
    \n- monsters empowered by victimizing people
    \n- hurting monsters by cutting them from their victims
    \n- GM fallout revealing information about the monster and its victims

    \n I'm trying to remember from two years ago, but here goes:

    The biggest problem we had was there was no sense of making any meaningful progress on the game. By the time the characters had weakened the monster by freeing one victim from its control, the monster had successfully victimized at least one other person and had weakened the characters in other ways, so it felt like, overall, there would be steadily diminishing returns or, at best, take a really, really long time to get anywhere close to beating the monster.

    The other thing that was problematic was the limited number of conditions. We kept getting the same ones all the time, which just felt very artificial and didn't fit into the overall thrust of the story. If there were 10 conditions (hey, it's Geiger Counter!) or you could freely invent them (hey, it's Lady Blackbird!), I think that would go a long way towards mitigating it.

    And, yeah, there were other things about how the conflict mechanics worked that I'm less sure about, so I'll trust in John's assessment there.

    \n The biggest problem, for us, was the combination between scene framing, mechanics which forced characters to be alone and the lengthy Dogs-style resolution system.

    So you'd frame someone in their first scene. Nobody else could be involved in that scene, because they were Alone. And then you'd roll dice, which would take quite a while. By the time we'd done everyone's first scene, an hour had gone. It was quite exhausting.

    Graham
