Posted By: AndyAgain, our "not official, but pretty close to it" rules summary for these threads:

-----
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.
a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.
b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
-----
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").
Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.

    \n A while ago I had the idea of building some kind of RFID playing card game, where the cards face value was different to some other value only accessible when you scanned them.

    Well it appears somebody has beaten me to it and done this already , but as an addon to a kids active game station thing. It's called Swinxs, and appears at first to be a very complicated way of playing tag using RFID enabled armbands... However what's interesting is it has an developers kit, so you can build your own games and make the armbands trigger sounds, rules calls and logic based on whatever you desire. The possibilities of using this for storygaming or larp or jeep/freeform are still bouncing around in my brain, so I figured I'd share.

    \n Some few weeks ago, I happened across a mention of a Lulu.com contest relating to a new product line of theirs: photographic mini books. These are high-quality pocket-sized books that must be purchased in sets of 3 or 4, with a "break-even" price point of something like $30. Out of all mini-books created before a certain date, Lulu would choose 10 that represented the most creative use of this new format. Examples they gave were of wedding photo albums, resumes, travel guides, and the like. Of the 10 they choose, the public gets to vote on the top 3, which receive a cash prize for their creators.

    I entered the contest with four goals:

    1) Justify why you would need to buy three identical copies of a book at a time.
    2) Do something that no one else was going to do (a role-playing game), thus making it "creative."
    3) Do something that had wider appeal (i.e., not a fantasy RPG); namely, something with a horror theme.
    4) Do it at no cost; all images in the book would be Flickr CC By.

    My efforts paid off. I was selected as one of the final 10 and I am now eligible to win one of three cash prizes... if I get enough votes.

    Voting runs from August 4 through 17 and takes place here: http://www.lulu.com/contest/mini_photography_contest/

    My book is called "The House." Your votes would be appreciated.

    I also have an article about the creation of this game appearing on RPG.net next week Monday, which helps further explain my design aesthetic.

    \nA movie named Chocolate
    \n
    \nThis movie made me feel oogie on many, many levels. It's about a mentally limited girl (almost certainly autistic, probably also other issues) with a savant level ability for martial arts, who gets into trouble (as such girls will in movies) and kicks a lot of ass. And from the slick teaser with the pulsing backbeat you'd say "Oh my God, that is the most exploitative and terrible thing ever," but it's not ... the actual movie is tragic and heart-breaking and eerie and dignified ... and also features an unending string of spectacular fight sequences.
    \n
    \nBut, additionally ... the fight scenes are surreal, because the girl doesn't demonstrate any one fighting style. She's a mimic. Every scene she's doing an impression of a different movie martial artist, and Yanin Vismitananda is so good at mimicking the styles that it's hypnotic. If you want to see what it would be like if Michelle Yeoh, Jet Li, Jackie Chan and Bruce Lee (and some others I couldn't ID) all did a movie together by serially possessing the body of a confused young Thai girl ... well, this is that movie.

      \n Short article on usability for video game user interfaces, but I think is applicable to game texts, play aids, and even fundamental system rules (as pertain to handling, in particular):
      Upping Your Game's Usability

      Specifically:
      * Learnability (which also implies Consistency). One of my beefs with Shadowrun 1E was that every damned archetype used different fundamental mechanics.

      * Efficiency--at odds with Learnability, at times; particularly when efficiency is gained through arcane controls. Top Secret was a hobbyist joke for years because of its crazy Weapon A versus Weapon B charts. Hm... this could be one we don't share with PC games, unless you think in terms of "basic rules" with layers of nuanced "optional" or "advanced rules."

      * Simplicity--another component of Learnability, to me; and equally at odds with Efficiency in a PC UI, but probably NOT in an RPG's handling rules (our "UI").

      * Aesthetics--this is where folks talk about how they like rolling fists full of dice, or why minis players go bonkers for exacting paint scemes and hyper-realistic terrain. Obvious trade-off: setup (and maybe handling) time, cost, arguments of "distraction" from the "real game/story/role playing" because of all the fiddling with breakable walls, poseable figs, and effects templates or Marks.

      Anyhow... got me thinking, especially when you start to think about RPG-and-board-game hybrids, using cards, simulation with minis and maps, active PDFs (e.g. character sheets), and online resources for enabling game play. There's a SLIGHT convergence between TT RPGs (and LARPs) and computer aids or automation. Thus, GUI can be thought of as "game user interfaces."
