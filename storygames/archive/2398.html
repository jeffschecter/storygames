The significance of the dungeon

Ian Cooper
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
So I wanted to share this because it was pushing to get out of my head. I'm not sure how insightful it is.

When I started playing rpgs, in or around 1980, we were aware of two games: AD&D and Traveller. But we played AD&D and never played Traveller.

Now we tried rolling some Traveller characters, and the whole life path approach really sparked ideas for us as to who are characters were, but we could never figure out how to play Traveller.

By contrast AD&D was easy. You drew up a dungeon on graph paper, picked some denizens out of the monster manual to occupy the rooms, rolled some characters, and were good to go. The game came with a lot of helpers to play this way. Levels made it easy to work out what denizens were appropriate. Treasure tables let us figure out the rewards. There were plenty of rules to cover adjudicate the common things that could happen like falling into a pit.

AD&D was a game that told you how to play it. By contrast while Traveller had mechanics it did not have much advice on how to set up and play a game. So we played it less.

Published AD&D modules were great both as examples, and because they were usually better that what we came up with. Their dungeons were cohesive rather than random and progressing through them seemed to build toward a climax.

Games might differ in mechanics and setting but we tended to favour that dungeon style of play because it was easy. Even when we played RuneQuest with its rich setting of Glorantha, it was really just a backdrop to dungeon crawls.

It seemed that GDW had the same issue as because most of its early packaged modules were just dungeon crawls (The Kinunir, Research Station Gamma). It was a long time before we got anything that told us how to run a game that did not take place in the dugeon. The Keith brothers wrote scenarios that had chapters and scenes and later on we had Book 0 to tell us how to play. We also had ideas like 76 patrons where we had a crisis to throw at the players and improvised the session around it. Eventually Traveller ended up with the nugget system, and instead of a drawing out series of rooms, we sketched out a series of scenes. In time we figured out how to run a game.

Re-reading Sorceror recently (prior to running a game of Dictionary of Mu) I was struck by how much time Ron takes to tell you how to set up your Sorceror game. A narrativist equivalent of the advice AD&D gave us on how to create a dungeon. Ron tells us how to play a game of Sorceror.

By contrast when Hero Wars came out I think that some of its poor reception (beyond the editing) was due to fact that it did not have enough obvious advice on how to set up your Hero Wars game and play it. There were example adventures and talk about community threats and heroquests but no real advice on how to create a successful game. Interestingly I tend to use a similar format for HeroQuest games. Create a community and drive that community in crisis. I wonder how much more broadly popular HeroQuest might have been if it had included suggestions on how to run a game. Did it make Traveller's mistake.

I wonder if some of the success of indie games is that they tell us how to play the game. Dogs in the Vineyard shows us how to set up a town. My Life with Master, Burning Empires all tell us how to play the game.

Maybe there is some predjudice against role-playing having a 'game' element to it. Looking at some of the reaction to Burning Empires on rpg.net I do wonder that. Gamers seem to have priviledged the notion of 'open' games that do not prescribe a 'way to play', but 'closed' games that contain a formula for a play session do seem to have more success.

Surely the continued attraction of the dungeon (D20, Castles and Crusades) proves that it is easier to play games that come with a formula to run a successful game even if they are more limited in scope than an open-ended game that a lot of people find too confusing, and to time consuming to set up and use.

    I'd say that's a valid insight. The dungeon crawl is definitely a simple and compelling setup for an RPG, and translates to many other areas as well (think about how many dungeon-crawl computer games there are). By providing a "game formula", it is easier for beginners to put together a game.

    There is a downside, though. The 1st time you go into a dungeon, it's amazingly cool. The 20th time is a lot less interesting. The focus of the game also becomes it's limitation, especially when the story you want to tell goes outside what the rules of the game support.

    This is an issue I have with a number of indie games. Many of them focus on a very specific story structure. It is easy to get into them quickly, but after I've played them a few times I start to see the same elements appearing over and over again.

    There seems to be a trend in many of the indie-gamers I know to play a game a few times and move onto another game. They treat RPGs more like board games, where you play a one-shot or a brief campaign then do something else, occassionally returning to old favorites. This isn't necessarily an invalid way of playing, but it is different from what I've come to expect from the months-long campaigns of more traditional RPGs.

    Posted By: Ian CooperMaybe there is some predjudice against role-playing having a 'game' element to it. Looking at some of the reaction to Burning Empires on rpg.net I do wonder that.
    Chis Chinn once pointed out on his blog the idea that there are gamers who approach RPGs with an attitude of "Let's see what this RPG does"; and then there are gamers (the majority) who approach with an attitude of "I already know how to play and what I want; can this RPG do that?"

    Indie games seem to embrace the former attitude, and thus I can see them putting off gamers with the latter. My HERO group had exactly this reaction to BW/BE. DoW "got in the way of the roleplaying" and Infection "sounds too much like an RTS game." Basically, "This RPG isn't giving me exactly what I already expect the gaming experience to be."

    My attitude has shifted so much to the former that I don't even really understand most "Sell me on..." and "Can system X do [genre]?" threads anymore. They seem predicated on the ideas that a) the player has already decided what the game needs to "be," and b) that genre/setup has anything to do with whether the game uses, e.g., 3d6-roll-under or d20-roll-over in its mechanic.

    I mean, over on ENWorld, I see discussions of what d20 can "do" all the time. All I can think now is, "If you want the game focused on tactics, resource management, task resolution, and combat... yeah, d20 can do whatever genre/setting you want." It seems to me that the far more important question gamers should be asking is whether or not they want a game focused on tactics, resource management, task resolution, and combat. The genre trappings of the Color seem pretty irrelevant to me.

    One point that I haven't seen in this thread is that dungeons lend an serial structure to long-term play. Sure, you can play D&D for months or years, but that months-long play is divided into relatively discrete chunks called "dungeons" (or "adventures"). Maybe the wiley GM threads a recurring badguy or giant conspiracy into those dungeons, maybe you're tracking down and exterminating some spider-cult that's sprung up in a number of places. The important thing, though, is that at the end of every dungeon the players (including the GM) feel some measure of accomplishment -- they have done something, and they have finished something. Now they'll move on to another relatively discrete thing. For my money, this is what fuels the months-long games - there is a reward cycle at work every session or every couple of sessions that tells the players "you won today" and holds out the possibility that there's another dungeon just over the horizon that they can beat tomorrow.

    Yeah, good cite Max. Another game that nailed this down was Paranioa. Instead of the dungeon to inform the standard narrative, there's a plot cycle that you tend to go through with each mission in Paranioa (wake up, briefing, gear up, hit field, debrief, and all of he transit stages between each).

    So we can see the effective methods that have been generated over the years to inform the course of game-play: the wargame map method (AKA the "dungeon"), the channeled plot (nuggets, scenes and standardized plot), to relationship maps and bangs.

    What varies between these forms is what the player's participation involves. In the wargame map, it's deciding which way the units (characters) move, and what tactics happen. In the plot methods it's what you do at each juncture along the preset path - Paranioa does this well, because the play is not about choosing the plot, but choosing if/when your character decides to do treasonous things. In the relationship map method, the set framework is the circumstances and how they will react (the characters act much like the map from the first version), and the player gets to determine the plot.

    I think that Dogs just represents an alternate way to prepare a relationship map, or something like it (same with MLWM, and a number of other games). But are there other structures that are out there? Could there be?

    There's the InSpectres/UnSpeakable route, which is related to the channeled plot in that there's a basic structure resembling Paranoia, but what happens next is not predetermined. For those two games specifically, the plot is determined by whoever wins a skill roll. The end of the game is determined mechanically (earning all available franchise dice in InSpectres, having one character go permanently insane in UnSpeakable.) Call this one "unchanneled plot".

    I'm currently working on two RPGs, using the same resolution mechanics, but different plot control techniques. One is Depths of Kanthe, that Nethack-inspired game that evolved out of my entry for the 24 hour solo rpg challenge; it fits the wargame map method, except the map/dungeon is randomly created through play. The other is a not-quite-named '40 space patrol game that's more like the InSpectres model: generate an initial situation and try to resolve it, but there's no predetermined plot; instead, mechanics guide the creation of plot complications. The plot is finished when there are no more complications and the existing issues are more or less resolved to everyone's satisfaction. I'm expecting this "unchanneled plot" method to have a lot of potential.
