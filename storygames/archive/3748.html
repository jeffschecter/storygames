    \n Hmmm. Well, the thing about fantasy heartbreakers is that they all look only at D&D and nothing else for source material, add a house rule or two for how armor or weapons work, and add like 2-3 cool things in the mechanic and setting... but otherwise they're D&D (or sometimes Runequest) with the SN filed off.

    I see lots of Trends in Story Games. Stuff like:
    Scene Framing
    Dice Pools
    "I use my Ennui to roll an extra 2d6!"
    Endgame

    However, I'm not seeing people consistently take one game, like Dogs in the Vineyard, and make something exactly like it. I see tons of games influenced by Dogs (namely games where you get more dice by drawing upon character traits, flaws, emotions, and relationships), but nothing that looks like Dogs, But With 2 Different Gimmicks.

    So in terms of Heartbreaker, I doubt we'll ever see that. However, we might see some strong trending that's worth analyzing and perhaps even "getting past". Trends that the author internalizes and takes for granted.

    No, because lots of games do this:\n
    Posted By: lachekSwitch between conflict resolution and roleplay as needed as conflicts escalate or change
    \nThe key things in the formula that I poorly presented is that:
    1) scenes are recognized structural elements of the game -- roleplay happens 'in scenes' and not sort of whenever somebody does something
    2) there is one conflict per scene, and roleplay up to that conflict is unmoderated by game mechanics
    3) the fallout / stat changes are a direct, mechanical result of the resolution of the scene's single conflict.

    I like to refer to that structure as 'boxcars' because it turns play into a long string of structurally- and procedurally-identical scenes, each with different content inside them. It's easy to grasp, the formalization of scenes and conflicts does some good work in focusing play out of the default D&D splatter of "stuff happens," and it consequently gets picked up in lots of games.

    For my money, when that chassis is the bulk of the game, you've got a story game heartbreaker. Adding some fiddly bits on the sides, or some cleverly-named stats, is not enough. Your new ideas have to penetrate to the point where they shape play and make the game not a series of identical scenes, but something that has a shape and texture of its own. In Primetime Adventures, that thing is the combination of Screen Presence and Fan Mail. Those two mechanics, and the way that they interact, shapes play so that different scenes feel different.

    Of course, a lot of "story games" don't follow the boxcars format at all. Sorcerer doesn't; Dogs doesn't; The Shadow of Yesterday doesn't. These games don't have formal scenes and allow follw-up conflicts all over the place, and that structure works for those games. The thing that makes a story game heartbreaker is the assumption that Frame-Roleplay-Conflict-Fallout will work for your game without figuring out why.

      I know in my own work, as a writer of fiction, over the years I've learned to be a hard-ass on my own work in one specific area first and foremost: Clarity. Line by line, sentence by sentence, is it clear. And then, at the macro level, is it clear as a whole.

      And I don't think saying, "Well, there are emergent qualities in play," cuts it. If that's the case, tell me the emergent qualities. Be explicit. Be clear.       Posted By: vodkashokThe way I am reading it, in very simplistic terms is that if you are designing a game you must not:

      (a) blindly use the successful tools that have been used before, even if you like them
      (b) in your attempts to not use them, try not to be too wacky...

      To me, that leaves very little wiggle room to the aspirant designer.
      \nOthers have already posted better, more lucid responses to this. Listen to them first. Then there's my not-entirely-positive response: while a heartbreaker may not be a very good product, writing one can be an excellent learning experience. This is one of the reasons that I like all our game design challenges: it allows folks to write with few expectations. It allows them to try on the game designer hat, see what it's like, get a view from the inside, and 'complete' a game design. That sort of perspective is priceless when it comes to designing the next game "for real."

      Because more important than writing a game that lots of other people will play is writing a game that teaches you something.

      So don't worry; design your game, learn what you can, and then decide if it's a product. Then design the next one.
