I though it'd be neat to have a resource of magical-logic rules for when we need to rapidly portray someone being magic, along teh lines of Contagion and Similarity. So post a rule and an example.

Counterchanging
Similar situations tend to dissimilate.

The vizier sees that courtiers are moving to kill the sultan, so he takes an iron chess set and plays a game with him. One move before the vizier checkmates the sultan, someone interrupts the game and the vizier locks the chessboard away, the pieces undisturbed. As long as the chess sultan remains doomed, the flesh sultan is safe.

    Like, in the Coldfire Trilogy (fun books), magic, called the Fey, is a wispy substance, usually unseeable, that cracks up and pours over the surface of the seismically-active earth like a stew. No one can see it, save gifted "adepts" (who inherit the condition at birth). Fae erupts into the world "hot" with every often occuring earthquake, and if you use "hot fae", you can do powerful things, but it tends to explode your brain and kill you. Also, Fae is great at doing temporary magical-seeming things (remain hidden, influence people, enhance skills, etc) but there are no "fireball effects". The greatest mage in the world had to put all his effort and energy into making a permanent physical magic effect: A small knife that couldn't be scryed.

    In Dark Sun, magic comes from life, usually plant life (higher level magics can affect animal life). When power for magic is summoned, plants in the area whither and die. You can use magic in ways that causes plants/life not to suffer, but it's taking the Long Road, and the more ambitious mages will gain power more quickly than you.

    So, to that end:

    Godspears

    Across the land, miles apart from each other, are huge obelisk-like structures that appear stabbed into the earth. Ebon of color and covered in etched runes, people born in the land have a strange natural attunement to them. "The can cast magic". Magic, in this world, is all mental: Mostly it's the domain of what we would call subvocalization. The further one travels from the godspears, the weaker the power of the caster. True mages leaving the land of the spears completely can still work magic, but only with extreme effort and concentration.

    Mediation

    Magic functions best along boundaries of all sorts (social, physical, etc).

    Example: The necromancer wishes to call forth the soul of the dead king and interrogate it to discover how he died. He goes to the graveyard, the borderland between living and dead, in order to work his magic.

    Watery Way

    Magical energy resides solely in water. Wizards and sorcerors live on islands or near the sea. Large-scale magic always takes place during thunderstorms or blizzards. Magical items are always made of metal or stone, which are sunk to the bottom of lakes to steep before they are ensorcelled. Wizards are always thirsty, since their magical work costs a small volume of water in their bodies. Therefore they drink constantly, and can be identified by their behavior.

    Here are the classic ones. You may hate Bonewitz, but you gotta admit he was good at collecting this crap.

    I. THE LAW OF CAUSE AND EFFECT: The same actions under the same conditions produce the same results. A Law that is generally non demonstrable. Very few are skilled enough to replicate one of these conditions, much less all three of them simultaneously. Replicating one's actions exactly is difficult enough, but replicating the environment (which includes TIME) is another matter.

    Corollary - THE LAW OF RETURNS: "What one sends out, comes back." Or "What goes around, comes around." The Three-fold Rule is an example of the Law of Returns.

    II. THE LAW OF KNOWLEDGE: Greater understanding produces greater influence though greater affinity. Greater affinity (kinship) with any manifestation leads to a more powerful response when aid is requested, much is as a good friend is more likely to help you out than a stranger.

    Corollary - THE LAW OF SELF-KNOWLEDGE: Greater understanding of self produces greater influence though greater affinity.

    III. THE LAW OF NAMES: Having greater knowledge of a manifestation's true and complete name leads to greater affinity and influence.

    Corollary - THE LAW OF WORDS OF POWER: Having greater knowledge of words of power leads to greater affinity and influence.

    IV. THE LAW OF ASSOCIATION: Greater common characteristics between two manifestations combined with greater knowledge of the commonalties produces greater affinity and influence.

    Corollary 1 - THE LAW OF SIMILARITY (sympathetic magic): Effects resemble causes.

    Corollary 2 - THE LAW OF CONTAGION: Two manifestations which have been in contact remain in contact.

    V. THE LAW OF IDENTIFICATION: Complete knowledge equals absolute affinity equals identity.

    VI. THE LAW OF SYNTHESIS (or, the UNION of OPPOSITES): Synthesis of two opposing views of reality, or 2 realities, engenders a third which is more real that either original.
    Corollary - THE LAW OF POLARITY: Any manifestation is divisible into two antithetical sets of attributes, each of which contain the essence of the other.

    VII. THE LAW OF BALANCE: Greater balance is required for greater affinity and influence.

    VIII. THE LAW OF INFITITE MANIFESTATIONS: The number of manifestations within the Web is infinite. In other words, we'll never run out of manifestations to experience, or things to learn.

    IX. THE LAW OF FINITE SENSES: A manifestation's (ie, our) ability to perceive limits its ability to know. In other words, we can't see everything.

    X. THE LAW OF PRAGMATISM: If it works, it's right.

    XI. THE LAW OF INFINITE UNIVERSES: As there are infinite manifestations within the web possessed of infinite views of the universe, there are infinite universes.

    XII. THE LAW OF TRUE FALSEHOODS: Data exist which contradict your knowledge but which, never-the-less, are functional. Or, If it's a paradox, it's probably true.

    XIII. THE LAW OF PERSONIFICATION: All manifestations (including people) live and have personality.

    Corollary 1 - THE LAW OF INVOCATION: Any manifestation can conjure real entities from within.

    Corollary 2 - THE LAW OF EVOCATION: A manifestation can conjure real entities from without.

    The law of Equivalent Exchange

    In order to gain, something of equal value must be lost.

    Example: A necromancer wishes to reanimate an ally who has been slain. In order to bring back this life, the life of another will have to be lost -- a sacrifice, or suicide on the part of the necromancer if they're too altruistic for that shit.

    The Law of Emanations

    All emanations are functions of the events that cause them. All creations are thus under the control of their creators.

    Example: A wizard grows three large and terrifying homunculi in their vats. These beasts are under the wizard's absolute control.

    The Law of Opposition

    Mundanity is opposed to magic.

    1> Places where people do ordinary things (sleep, eat, work, play, etc) in ordinary ways are antithetical to magic. It's nearly impossible to do magic in a city, and damn hard in any settled area. On the other hand, places that have been ritually prepared for magic, and used for nothing else, are especially powerful places to work it.

    2> Doing completely ordinary things with magic (making food, for example) are nearly impossible to do with magic. Magical things always betray themselves with their alienness, as do ordinary things that have had spells cast over them. Having lunch with a wizard is always interesting.

    3> A person can involve himself with magic or with the mundane; straddling the boundary is difficult and unrewarding. This means that there are two parallel societies in the world, the mundane and the mystical.

    The Principle of Limitlessness

    Magic is not bound by principles of matter or energy.

    Magical power has no mass, or volume. Unless harnessed to do so, it sheds no light, no sound, and cannot be smelled or touched.

    Magic cannot be measured. It does not flow, cannot be used up, and does not need to pass through intervening space in order to move from one object to another.

    Principle of Instability

    Magic draws upon powerful tensions to create its effects, but these same tensions make the effects unstable and likely to degrade. Corollary: This can (not must) lead to dangerous side effects that run counter to, and may overturn, the original intent of the magic.

    Example: The emperor's magician lulls the visiting princess into a trance, convincing her that she wishes to bed the emperor. The next morning she is horrified and disgusted by her actions and accuses the emperor of having forced himself upon her. Her father declares war on the emperor.

    The Principle of Intention

    Magic does what you mean, not what you think you mean. In other words, magic takes into account your unconscious feelings and your long-term intentions and acts on those, not on the immediate effect that you're hoping for.

    (This implies that magic is controllable in proportion to how little you are personally invested in the result, which I like.)

    Example: Gerhard performs a spell to make some money, because he's broke and has to pay the rent. The spell doesn't bring him any cash, but his landlord has a heart attack and dies, leaving the estate in the hands of his wife who, in her grief, fails to collect rent this month.

    Principle of the Royal Flag
    In the magical world, things must be ritually ratified, or they do not exist. One does not necessarily have to use a traditional ritual for a thing, but to do so lends the effort power.

    If the mat was not straight, the Master would not sit. Being steeped in this form of magic, Confucius could sit for a meal, but if he did not straighten his mat, it was as though he ate nothing.

    The King of Fairies signed a treaty not to steal from my kingdom, but we did not fly his flag when he arrived, nor blow trumpets upon his departure, and so he is not bound by it.

    Magical effects are always excessive, never subtle. You don't use magic to start a campfire but to create a massive bonfire that burns those who even get close to it.     The Law of Echoes

    As sound carries in water, so the energy of magic carries in the aether. The greater the work of magic, the greater and farther-reaching the "sound," which can only be heard by those who have disturbed the aether themselves.

    These are a little different -- they're really principles of genre story construction. I've sometimes found it useful to interpret them inside a game setting as operational rules of magic, though no observable cause and effect is ever involved in their operation.

    The Crabgrass Principle

    Possessions of a magical nature (or, often, any unusual nature) do not come into one's possession by happenstance. If you have it, you will need it. (The name derives from the maxim, if the elves give you a magic ring of crabgrass, you will eventually have desperate need for crabgrass.)

    The Secret Power of Secrets

    A secret is powerful magic. Reality will alter itself to protect a sufficiently powerful secret.

    The Contrariness of Magical Restrictions

    The best-known, strictest, most thoroughly proven law of magic is the one that must be broken to achieve transcendent magical power. For instance, if magic works only for virgins, then only a mage who gives in to passion can fully master it.

    The Law of Caveat Emptor (an instance of the Law of Returns)

    You get what you pay for. Gains achieved through magic must be compensated by sacrifice, toil, and hardship. The main difference between white and black magic is whether the payment is made in advance or afterward. (Harry Potter could not have become a great wizard if the Dursleys hadn't mistreated him.)


    The example I used for the Contrariness principle reminds me: there are lots of laws in the literature and traditions of magic that correlate sex and magic. For obvious reasons such laws haven't been popular in role playing magic systems. For equally obvious reasons they offer a lot of potential there.

    The Law of Nookie

    Sexual innocence, and it's opposite, totally committed sexual passion, both run parallel to the currents of magic and facilitate its use. Sexual confusion or dissatisfaction of any kind runs perpendicular to those currents and will impair magic or block it completely.

    - Walt Freitag
