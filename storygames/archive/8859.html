    \n "Story game" is a very convoluted term. I think I'm just about the only designer who's used it on a game cover, now that I think of it. Thus I'm not 100% certain what you mean with the term, so I'm just going to go with a really wide assumption. If I had to start categorizing story games and looking for good examples, I'd start with GNS categories. Going like this, here's what I'd recommend for different types of story games.

    Narrativist games
    Dogs in the Vineyard is a fine example. My own favourites when recommending games blind are The Mountain Witch, Primetime Adventures, The Shadow of Yesterday and Dust Devils. There are a lot of others as well - my own Zombie Cinema is a very pure-bred example.

    Simulationist games
    Dead of Night, Spirit of the Century and Dread are my foremost picks, excellent games in their ways. Primitive, Houses of the Blooded and Wushu are challenging choices that have their fans. Maid might be interesting as well, but I haven't sampled it.

    Those are to my way of thinking pretty different categories, but others might see my two boxes as less important. Be that as it may, the above games are all fine representatives of different directions a "story game" might take.

    Posted By: JuddIs this the wrong place to bring up that I detest the term, Story Games?
    \nIt's not an entirely useless term. I like to use it when I discuss games that specifically, as a matter of system agenda, use the concept of story to structure gameplay. That is, you have your creative goals of addressing premise, creating Star Trek stories or whatever, and then separate from that you have this idea that you can achieve this goal by drawing on literary models and traditions. This is an useful distinction because adventure games, which are immensely important to roleplaying historically, do not share this assumption about stories having anything to do with game structure - you can get a more clear picture of the field by looking at it this way, and as a matter of design perhaps see when and where you should or shouldn't emulate narrative art forms of the past yourself. (This is also, incidentally, the sense in which I use the word in Zombie Cinema, which seeks to structure play via a movie metaphor.)

    As an example of what you can capture with "story game", consider the difference between HeroQuest and a Primetime Adventures series set in Glorantha. Even should the creative goals be similar, the former game is structured in the adventure mold, as an on-going yarn that might encompass stories while not being led by them; meanwhile PTA constantly leans on the structures of its television metaphor. I find it useful to discuss games like PTA, Zombie Cinema and Dead of Night (to mention a few games that lean on story structures strongly) as "story games" in this sense. The differences in structural assumptions are very large compared to adventure games.

    Of course, this has little if anything to do with how the term is used in everyday discourse. "Games that people at Story Games consider better than others" seems as good a definition of the practical usage as anything.
