\n I've been wondering for a while, are there any games out there that (from a mechanical point of view) have complete information?

A quick description of complete information, based on Wikipedia minus the economics language: A game in which knowledge about other players is available to all participants. Every player knows the payoffs and strategies available to other players, and everything about the current state of the game.

Chess is a good example: no unknown factors, no randomness. Are there any games that, at least mechanically, act like this?

How does a game with complete information effect your actions as a player or GM? How does it effect the end fictional product (the story) and the characters you create?

    \n Chess is a bad example, since in its ideal form (where the payoffs are +1/-1, 0/0, and -1/+1) chess is a game of perfect information, not just complete information. Complete information games can have fortune/randomness. Ideally, every rpg without hidden information is a game of complete information - every player is absolutely aware of all other players' objective functions. In practice this is not true at all, 'cause one day I might put high value on fictionally stabbing a fictional dragon and the next just want to cuddle with it, and sometimes it's hard to tell in advance which of these I value.

    To be clear: fortune/randomness is compatible with complete information, since all players can know "now he rolls a d10". Hidden information like your Dark Fate is not compatible with complete information, because all but one player can know "he has a random Dark Fate" but that specific Dark Fate holder also knows exactly which it is. Plenty of games theoretically have complete information... it takes an extremely self-knowledgeable, cohesive group to practically have complete information.

    My guess is you're asking a more specific question than "how does complete information affect player actions, the story, characters, etc." though. Or my hope, rather, 'cause I'm not sure I could actually apply whatever the answer to the broad question happens to be to anything!

    \n Universalis provides complete information in that everything validated by coin expenditures is known to all. Who the mysterious assassin really is may be a "plan" in someone's mind but it's not a fact until a coin is spent on it.

    So there is much unknown, but what is known is known to all equally.

    One can revise history in effect, (pay coins to have a villain turn out to be the hero's father) and in that sense players did not have "complete information" in that they didn't know everything, but they did in that "they knew everything knowable at that time".

    \n If randomness is allowed, then I think there are plenty of games that give you complete mechanical information. However, I think the inherent vagueness and freedom of the "shared imaginary space" means you can never have complete narrative information.

    I mean, let's say you and I are role-playing a couple in the middle of an argument, and we've stated that we're in a slightly wooded park. You say that your character starts yelling at me, so I say my character runs behind a tree and starts crying. Mechanically, that's meaningless, but maybe you didn't realize there was a tree close enough by for me to do that. Sure, it's just color, but color can have a big effect on the mood of the game. Mechanically, nothing's changed, but your character is going to look like a jerk if they keep yelling at me while I'm hiding behind a tree.

    That kind of stuff can have a big effect on your future choices.

    \n A couple things I think I learned...

    RPGs (including LARPs, etc.) which do push for some sort of "competition" in the vein of most non-RPG games (which are nowadays still the majority of RPGs, I believe) almost invariably rely on "secrets" and incomplete information (asimmetric information, actually) to provide a challenge.
    One possible reason for that is that designing an interesting challenge in a complete information environment is harder, and pulling it off requires skill. Not impossible, you know, just harder (last time I checked, Beast Hunters was an excellently gamist, challenge-based RPG which used complete information). I'd say that, being harder to pull off, it's also more fulfilling for all involved.

    RPGs where the goal is aesthetic, though, including "story now" oriented designs, most often work better with complete information. Complete information makes it easier for everyone involved to make aesthetic-grounded choices with insurance that those choices are to retain meaning in the grand picture.
    When obscured information is involved (example: transgressions in Spione), then the act of revealing such information (thus progressing the game to a complete information status!) is strongly emphasized as either a goal or a crucial stage of play: in a sense, employing obscured information in aesthetic-oriented roleplay is an advanced technique meant to improve the sense of meaningfulness emanating from the experience.

    Posted By: fnord3125Question, Jason:
    What if I'm playing a game and I decide my character has a secret. Suppose this secret never comes out and no one ever finds out about it, but that it still informs my play and I enjoy the game more because of the secret. Is this still a secret that should be eliminated or made open knowledge?
    \nIf revealing the secret to the other people at the table could have made things more fun and interesting for you, then yes, it was a secret that should've been made open knowledge. (If it's a boring secret that no one would have ever wanted to do anything with, I suppose it can stay secret and you won't be missing out on anything...but why waste your time on boring secrets?)

    And generally, I have to agree that when you're looking at the subset of "character secrets," sharing them with your fellow players at the outset is the way to go, especially if keeping that secret is a big deal for the character or the secret has to do with a central part of the character's concept. Having other people push, trample on, and shine a spotlight on things relating to your character's secret makes that secret more valuable in play: it drives action, it explains motivation, it lets other people appreciate your character and equips them to better participate in scenes with your character. The real joy of having characters with secrets is not successfully putting one over on everyone else at the table, but doing something with those secrets, and it's easier to do something if you can get a little collaboration from the rest of the group. Considering that everyone's sitting down so that they can collaborate, it seems only natural to spill the beans out of character so that everyone can enjoy finding out (or failing to find out) the secret in-character.


    But that, as Graham points out, is a very different beast from the "what's in the safe?" style of secret. I'm cool with the latter, in general...although I have to admit I do want SOMEONE to know what's in the safe when it appears, because it always seems unsatisfactory to me if it's obvious that the contents are only getting invented once the safe is opened. Your mileage might vary.

    \n If every player needs to know every other players' payoffs, then I think very few RPGs are going to be games of complete information, especially in practice. In something like DnD it's possibly not hard to know what other players' payoffs are, since they are all about killing monsters and not dying. But even here it's probably influenced by the difficulty. Some players just want to win and be heroes, some want to overcome very difficult tactical problems, and some are in between (I'm over simplifying to make my point). Where does a player lie on that spectrum? You need to nail it down to know his payoffs (note payoffs are going to be for players, not characters).

    Games like Burning Wheel that have Beliefs help us to know what the payoffs are, because we write down our desires for the game. This is assuming all the players know all the beliefs, including those of all the NPCs. But the GM is working on a scale larger than this. His job transcends any single character. And even if you have something like Beliefs, you only have 3 or so. Is that player's payoffs defined just by that? Surely what he wants out of the game is more complicated.

    Talking about what everyone wants I think is vital to a good game, because if you don't know the other players' payoffs (at least in a general sense, you don't need to know what the GM has planned specifically) then you end up playing different games.

    \n Perhaps even within the category of story secrets (as opposed to mechanical secrets, not where I had started but very interesting) there's a distinction between long term and short term secrets? I've found it extremely enjoyable to have a secret for, say, a session with a big reveal at the end. I have some plan that I'm going to pitch to the people of the town, but I don't want to tell everyone about it before I get to present it, or somesuch. It's very satisfying for me as a player or as a GM to have a little mystery that gets resolved promptly. Nothing central to the character, but important nonetheless. On the other hand, the big stuff ("I'm actually from another dimension") seems best shared from the get-go.

    Maybe it's just me, but I like a mixture of character secrets that are shared and character secrets that are player secrets as well. I just tend to like them on different time scales.
