\n There's a lot of love on this forum for the fanmail system in PTA. I've seen it brought up in all kinds of positive lights and I don't think I've ever seen a bad word about it here (this might be a case of selective perception). For me, this is surprising, since I'm coming from a game design culture where this sort of mechanic is considered Bad Design and the advice is something like "No. Seriously, don't do it. It'll ruin the game." (and THAT is a case of hyperbole. Exagerrations makes it fun, shuddup). So naturally, I have to ask you guys: why doesn't fanmail suck?

Notice that I'm assuming it doesn't suck and I need you to tell me why. This is not a claim that it does suck. I still haven't played PTA, but I've played some games with fanmailesque systems and I've seen all of the behaviors associated with this type of mechanic from the design culture I'm from If there's a shy or inhibited person in the group one of the following things happen:

* The player gets a lot less fanmail than the others, because he's not bringing as much awesome. The player notices this and is not having fun, since it lowers his self esteem.
* The player does get as much fanmail as the others because the threshold of awesome is lower for that player. He gets fanmail for things that others wouldn't get fanmail for. Everyone at the table knows this, and it lowers his self esteem since he doesn't feel he "deserves" it.

I've seen borth these behasviors in play and it's not fun. The fanmailesque systems I've tried (remember, I haven't played PTA) tend to call attention to the fact that the low-key player doesn't stand up and shout the way the others do and it makes him feel like he's not awesome. It establishes an atmosphere of competition instead of one of relaxation and fun.

So what does PTA do to prevent this? Or is it just that I've played with dysfunctional groups? Has this sort of thing ever happened at your table? If not, why? What prevents it from happening? How come fanmail isn't just rewarding the people who are already hogging the spotlight?

    \n First, I am compelled to say: play PTA. These other "fanmailesque" games might not actually get it right.

    But, beyond that, I wonder if the very construct of PTA allows this to happen when it would break in other games. Beyond the player-rewarded-only factor (which can engender mutualism in non-dickish players), there's the fact that the conceit supports this -- hey, someone just sent you fan mail for your awesome scene. It's less conceit-breaking than, say, a pulp action game where you reward each other for being awesome, or something like that.

    Furthermore, because of how PTA distributes spotlight -- every player has one turn that is inherently their spotlight and can regulate who is in it -- then you don't have "that one guy hogging spotlight" problems unless, frankly, everyone wants that guy in their scene. In which case, you're actually playing the game right still. In games where the spotlight is largely determined by the GM or by the loudest player, I can see where you would have the problems you're talking about.

    (Yes, one can use fanmail to buy into other scenes he wasn't invited into, but if constantly done and if done in an unwelcome way, such a player won't keep getting fanmail. You give fanmail for what you want to see, not what you don't.)

    In short, Fanmail doesn't exist in a vacuum.

    \n In PTA it is part of the game's ecosystem - If no fanmail is given the game brilliantly grinds to a halt, which is a little hint that it's time to do something else. This keeps it out of the "dance, monkey, dance!" category in an elegant way. It also means that for maximum fun, the fanmail must flow, and engaged players have to look for opportunities to give it away. If you are playing with people you like and trust, you find those opportunities in the play of all your friends. It helps that each piece of the exchange - giving, receiving, and using fanmail - is very satisfying.

    Worrying about whether you deserve the fanmail your friends are giving you is pretty counter-productive. Your friends love you and want to have fun.

    \n Simon, a few things:

    1. If you don't give out enough fanmail, like Jason says, the game grinds to a sputter. I've watched it happen multiple times, so it definitely sucks in that case.

    2. Sometimes fanmail economies (or any other "resource point for doing X" economies) become, um, tornados of "awesome escalation," where people try to competitively out-awesome each other in an ascending cacophony of over-the-top description. Unless this is specifically what you want (frex: Danger Patrol), this can totally suck, turning your orchestra into a monkey bashing the same note on a piano, over and over again. Even the better games that strive for awesome cacophony, like Danger Patrol, benefit from things like refresh scenes and other stuff that tones things back down a bit occasionally.

    3. Fanmail is totally a social contract level thing. As such, what earns a player fanmail varies massively from group to group and even session to session. For example, if Jason and I are throwing down in a TV show inspired by The Wire, I can give him fanmail for doing something really subtle with his characterization, something that's not "awesome" in the gonzo sense. Maybe he just pauses dramatically in the middle of this one line or something. This is something even shy players can do, right? Fanmail doesn't have to be about doing something punchy or dramatic that captures the spotlight for yourself.

    4. As other folks have mentioned, rotating scene framing and rotating spotlight helps spread the love around. Generally, I find that players earn the most fanmail during their spotlight episode which they then spend at the end of the episode to resolve their personal crisis. Also, I find that shyer players often enjoy how PTA allows them to play supporting roles, instead of making them feel bad because they're not stepping forward and contributing more.

    5. Ultimately, PTA (like any game) is only going to be as functional as your group of players is. If you have a shyer player, it's the responsibility of the group to come up with ways to include them while not being condescending or putting them uncomfortably on the spot. But I don't think the fanmail economy or any other part of PTA makes this any harder that it would be in other games, necessarily.

    \n 1) PTA is non competitive. The characters don't have a hp box, they can't be killed by anyone except their player, there is simply NO WAY to deprotagonize a character: the player will always have his/her scene. So, fan mail is not useful to survive or to "win", the most pronounced effect in game is that people with more fan mail play more and their ideas get used more often.

    2) PTA that is not used is wasted. When people understand this, they give fan mail every time they can, so it's not a matter of "should I give FM to someone?" as much of "who I will give fan mail to?"

    3) Fan mail is not given with "cool moves" or something like that, that can turn a game in a boring show of muscles. Fan mail is given for anything, anything at all, that you enjoy. So, the often stated (by people who never played it) "risk" of having people hog the attention to get fan mail, doesn't exist. If someone hog in a boring way, he will not get fan mail. it will get it only if he is truly funny. So, even the shy player will get a lot of fan mail if he play with the rest of the group instead of turtling. Fan mail work because it "attune" the group.

    \n Thanks, everyone. This thread has some good stuff in it, but I feel I need to clarify something. The issue here is not that someone would hog the spotlight to do stuff that's not appreciated around the table. It's the other way around. Some guy at the table is simply "better" at PTA than someone else. He comes up with awesome plot twists, he makes funny in-character jokes, he narrates things colorfully and vividly ... In that other thread about bad gaming habits, a lot of people confessed their spotlight-hoggingness, but I don't believe for a second that they (or we, as I do it myself at times) do anything but great stuff with the spotlight. The issue would be that someone is not as colorful, not as engaged, not as awesome as the others, where "awesome" means anything considered positive by the group. That's the issue and that's where the two things I mentioned in the original post can occurr.

    I also want to reiterate that the question is NOT "is fanmail broken"? but rather "how come it's not?". PTA clearly works (although a few people have said that it doesn't work well with shy people. I'm interested in hearing more about that), but why does it? If I want to design a fanmail system, what do I need to do to make sure it doesn't put focus on the players' differing abilities of bringing the awesome? Is it the fact that everybody gets a scene? Is it the spotlight episodes? Or what?

    Or we could phrase it like this: if there's a player at the table who's not coming up with the brilliant twists, who doesn't do the dramatic pauses, who can't come up with cool descriptions as well as the others, what happens? Does the player get as much fanmail as the others anyway? Does he get less, but it doesn't matter?

    \n Fan mail is not a good idea if your group's unspoken social rules include something along the lines of "everybody is a special snowflake, and roleplaying is not a performance sport, and everybody is absolutely equal in everything all the time ever". For groups that are comfortable with the fact that some people have more of the skills used in roleplaying Fan mail is not a problem, as the physical evidence of piling tokens does not contradict their values. For those who do not want to draw attention to the fact that Jimmy there at the end of the table is not the sharpest knife in the drawer and most of the time we don't even really understand what he's getting out of hanging with us, it can be very painful and embarrassing to play a game like Primetime Adventures where you're asked to constantly judge the contributions others make. It's no wonder if the conclusion in that situation is that the Fan mail system is not fun or it doesn't work or it's unfair because it overruns the players who don't know how to play it.

    I've seen both sorts of groups, so the phenomenon is real. I myself actively foster the realistic viewpoint, mostly because I personally consider the other some sort of geek atavism that can just go and die - it isn't doing anything good for the hobby to pretend that anything you do is OK and worthy of praise, you can't ever get better in what you do than the next guy (or yourself at this moment), it's a taboo to criticize another's contribution to the game and so on. It's not an interesting or constructive viewpoint on this whole thing we do.

    \n Simon,

    I still don't know what you think fanmail is, since I don't know the context you're using techniques like this, but I want to say one thing quickly:

    Fanmail in PtA is never about putting a player on the spot. Unlike some games where you have to come with the clever bit that justifies the use of a trait/benny/taking an action, PtA Fanmail is is produced when someone does something interesting. Someone says something that gets a smile or laugh or gasp or "gulp" from another player, and Fanmail is given.

    This is why I don't think there's as much "choking" on Fanmail as some people fear. The people who get Fanmail weren't trying to get Fanmail. They were just talking. And someone says, "Hey, I liked that. Have some Fanmail."

    Because of this I've never seen it having any of the "dance monkey" qualities one might fear it to have.

    So, that's one reason it doesn't suck: The whole process is spontaneous and never puts the focus on someone to be clever or creative "right now!"

    Second:

    Nor is it -- in any way -- a competition.

    Moreover, people, I've noticed, tend to send Fanmail on a kind of curve. If Jim doesn't say much, but every once in a while says something great, then he gets Fanmail. If Nate is always boisterous and adding details, he won't always get Fanmail. In fact, it's when Nate does something new and fresh for Nate that will often get him the Fanmail.

    So, here's another reason it doesn't suck: In the context of PtA making stuff up is the name of the game. (Not so much the manipulation of cleaver mechanics.) From character creation forward it trains the players in a session to look for details and bits of fiction and so on... Fanmail is only half-about saying something fun. The other half is about the other players getting used to listening for details that seem right and pleasing and saying, "Yes, Fanmail!"

    The mechanic, in this specific game, in the context of how character creation is set up and how conflict is resolved, is about building conceptual and fictional material the group finds pleasing. Once that gets cranked up, it has been my experience that even shy players jump on board. They start talking -- and other people are listening -- and Fanmail moves across the table.

    Also, what Christopher said is important: in PTA we reward relatively, it's a given that different people will get fanmail for different contributions. The guy who consistently produces good content will not get as much as his share of the content would indicate, because the other players come to expect that level of play from him and only reward him when he manages to hit a particularly high point. Meanwhile the slightly problematic young, inexperienced, shy, introverted, socially inept or otherwise handicapped guy will get fanmail for the least positive contribution simply because the players want to acknowledge contributions relative to what they expect, not relative to an universal metric. This is a very powerful feature, which basically means that everybody will get fan mail eventually - I've yet to play a full session with anyone who were so completely out of tune with the rest of the group that they'd never get a single point.

    \n I think it's easier, all else being equal, for someone to get fanmail for a quick, conspicuous act than it would be for someone to get it for quietly and consistently being interesting. Doesn't mean that the quiet player won't get any fanmail at all, it just means that most of the time it'll be easier to reward the conspicuous player.

    One of the great strengths about fanmail is that it can be handed out quickly without interrupting the flow of the game -- there's no negotiation for it, you just give the fanmail out. If you're doing it with coins or poker chips, it's easy to just toss 'em over. This puts, for good or ill, a feeling of immediacy to fanmail, a sense that you are giving it out for a specific act that rocks you back in your chair and makes you say "that was great!" And with a quick, conspicuous moment of coolness, that's a match made in heaven: you see the cool thing happen, you flip the fanmail token to the person responsible, and no one asks "what was that for?" because it's all so gloriously apparent. The moment and the mood coincide perfectly and awarding fanmail is easy.

    When someone is being awesome in a quieter way, you might have the mood but not the moment -- no abrupt, obvious crystallizing act that tells you "yes, now is the time to give that person some fanmail." You might go through several scenes before you realize that, hey, that character who isn't in the forefront of the scenes and isn't making a lot of noise is actually providing something really compelling that deserves fanmail, and, er, maybe it's time to give out some for it.


    Which is why really good fanmail-givers are the ones who pay attention to everyone, and think just as much about awarding fanmail for subtle but impressive things as they do for grandly obvious moments of coolness. Get a whole bunch of people thinking like that, and the advantage that quick-and-conspicuous has tends to melt away.

    edit:\n
    Posted By: GrahamIn my experience, that rewards active play, rather than more passive things such as listening and reacting.

When you can't see the reactions of the other characters, the scene is crippled.

      \n To quote the OP:\n
      * The player gets a lot less fanmail than the others, because he's not bringing as much awesome. The player notices this and is not having fun, since it lowers his self esteem.
      * The player does get as much fanmail as the others because the threshold of awesome is lower for that player. He gets fanmail for things that others wouldn't get fanmail for. Everyone at the table knows this, and it lowers his self esteem since he doesn't feel he "deserves" it.
      \nAnd so it is thus: If you don't see it as a problem, you also can't propose solutions to those who do, unless you manage to convince them that it's not an issue.

      \n Interesting thought that just came to mind: the dispersal of fan mail relative to the character's typical behaviour (such that the player who constantly impresses gets it less, and the player who surprises the others gets it more, than what one might expect them to objectively receive) is completely thematic. If I'm watching a Battlestar Galactica episode where Starbuck flips out and kills somebody, I might not submit fan mail because I expect it from that character. However, when Cally suddenly produces a gun and plugs one of the core protagonists, that's worthy of fan mail. So not only does fan mail work on a player-player level, but more importantly on an audience-character level. I think this is important because it defuses the "you're just pandering to the weak player" argument - no, I'm rewarding a surprisingly cool scene involving a character who's commonly not blowing my socks off.

      BSG fans will note I'm still plowing my way through Season Two. Pathetic, I know.

      Posted By: Moreno R.It's really simple.

      If you see as a problem, for your group and your play, that the active player get more fan mail, then... he will NOT get more fan mail!

      \n(I see that this has already spawned some giant argument, which I am not reading, so apologies if this was part of it.)

      This statement is, to me, similar to the claim that drug addicts should just stop doing drugs, if they think being addicted to drugs is a problem.

      Or, to be less hyperbolic: when I am in a room with a television I tend to find myself looking at it, even when I don't want to. It's because my brain is wired to respond to moving images. My behaviour is not entirely conscious, it is in part the result of other tendencies over which I have more or less control.

      What I read Graham as saying is that people who take "quick, conspicuous acts" are more noticeable in the same way a moving image is more noticeable than a static one. It has absolutely nothing to do with conscious preference and everything to do with how people in general react to social interactions in general.

      If I am flat out more likely to notice any quick, conspicuous act -- then I am more likely to consider it for conscious reward. The word 'subtle' means something, after all: it means less frickin' noticeable. Subtle acts will get rewarded less unless people consciously compensate for an unconscious effect by which their brain only selects a certain portion of events to react to. So yes, a group can consciously try and counteract the primacy of quick, conspicuous acts when it comes to rewarding Fan Mail -- but it is not a matter of simply choosing between two conscious preferences.
