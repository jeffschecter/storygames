\n This is something folks have been talking about for a while. But it's all "here's my oracle", and "what kinds of oracles can be combined", "what mix of elements does an oracle need" and stuff like that. That's all useful, mind you, but there's lots of information on that now. However:

Can we, as a collective group, put together a strong set of guidelines for what makes a good oracle element and what does not?

Kind of like how Dogs has Town Creation, can we come up with a methodology for Oracle Creation?

What are features of good oracle elements?
What are features or characteristics that make bad oracle elements?
What is a good, brief way to explain to someone how to come up with a good oracle element?

    \n Bret,

    I've been using a very similar method:

    Name a place, person or thing that seems cool or evocative in the setting/genre. Now tell us what's unique about it, a twist--why is THIS particular type of person, place, or thing featuring in the story? What makes it unique, unusual, and different from all other similar people/places/things?

    Some Oracle entries are also events, though. Can they follow the same logic?

    And is this logic sufficient, or are some other characteristics/qualities important?

    In order:

    \n
      \n
    1. People who need things from each other.
      2. \n
    2. Events that add pressure.
      3. \n
    3. Objects of desire.
      4. \n
    \n

    That's maybe 65% people, 30% events, 5% objects.

    \n

    "People" in this case is any entity that can need and act on its own behalf. Events are things like storms that keep everyone snowed in, attacks (which probably incorporate monster or a rival tribe, which are made of people), marriages (again, with the people) or other festivals. Objects are things that people want. If they act on their own, they're people for the sake of the argument.

    \n

    For Beowulf, Emily Care Boss and I took a couple of evenings and went through the Nibelungenlied, a book of Russian fairy tales, the Green Knight, and a bunch of other similar texts, looking for events that hit these criteria. I then assembled them by the phase of the hero's life I thought they best represented and put them in that particular Wyrd.

    \n

    Which brings up another element of the Oracles: you can have rules about which one you choose from. Beowulf has three, and you have to play three chapters in each before you can move to the next. You can't go back until the hero's died. Then you can go back as often as you like and different players can play the hero and the legend will rearrange itself in the subsequent retellings.

    \n

    We're playing a tweaked-the-fuck-out Anthology Engine game right now. We're coming up with Oracle elements by writing them down on a piece of paper every week, putting then into a bag, and picking from the bag. It's working out pretty neatly. I imagine we'll get to a point where we want to start with an empty bag again.

    \n I agree with Bret, pretty much. I'd add: an oracle element should suggest characters to play, and conflicts between those characters. They should be elements in motion in and of themselves, not just stuff sitting there going "look how genre appropriate I am!"

    I mean, you could take any one of the elements from the original oracles, and tell a story about that. I just drew one from god-kings of war at random, and happened to get my favorite: "A cask of honey wine, tribute to a fierce bandit-queen." Look at that. You've got the fierce bandit-queen. You've got her loyal bandits. And maybe some less-than-loyal bandits who wouldn't mind being in charge themselves. You've got the people forced to pay tribute (simple vintners and apiarists, obviously). You've got the sorry schlubs who have to actually take the cask, and bring it to the bandit-queen. You've got people who might try to steal and intercept it, for any number of reasons. And maybe someone is using the tribute as a ruse to try and get close to the bandit-queen so they can assassinate her? There's actually a whole wealth of stories that could revolve around that one simple cask of sweet, sweet honey-wine.

    \nYes. This.

    I've had a terrible time getting good oracle content from Abulafia. It's hit and miss; some are good, and some are bad (at least for one value of "bad"). The difference lies, not in any particular artistry or creativity about the entries as such, but in these explicit qualities that Joshua outlines.

    Let's take two Oracles as examples. Both are a blind 'pull" from the Abulafia generator, though one of them had to be re-drawn because of duplicate entries (a recurring problem, fine folks of Abu!)

    From the Shakespeare Oracle:

    A tribunal of senators, hearing a charge of witchcraft
    A picture, meant to signify devotion
    The funeral of a beloved daughter, struck dead with grief
    A clown and miscreant, seeing the lewd in all things


    See how pregnant with conflict these are? I got particularly lucky in that these entries cross-pollinate nicely (a daughter dead of grief? Perhaps after being charged with witchcraft? And someone devoted to her, perhaps secretly?), bue even aside from this, there's things people might want, and a tense situation where they might boil over, and factors which might further agitate. Cool. We could easily have a juicy and tense IaWA game out of this. Or write a cool short story, or whatever.

    Now, from the Modern Occult Oracle:

    Four of Clubs: A voodoo priestess is creating zombies from dead bodies left un-recovered from a terrible natural disaster.
    Four of Diamonds: Neighborhood cats all start taking their mutilated catches to the lawn of a single property, then the cats start disappearing.
    Two of Hearts: Every child that you encounter stares fixedly at you with obvious terror.
    Six of Spades: At Noon the sun is eclipsed...but not by the moon.

    OK, these all look really spooky and evocative. . .except there's nothing much happening in them. They're just a bunch of atmosphere that a GM might say directly to a player ("Every child that you encounter. . ."), but there's no context, no drive, no people wanting shit and doin' something about it.Even the one example with a "character" is light on motivation and desire. Now, combined with other, more potent entries, that could be a fine element. see again the "cross-pollination" concept. A single item can be vague and mysterious (creating zombies? Why?) as long as there are other factors for it to careen into and suggest answers.

    Without these qualities an "Oracle" is just a random Color generator.

    Peace,
    -joel

    PS. No offense to the creator of this or any other creator of the Abulafia fan Oracles. Most of the entries I'm talking about are indeed creative and evocative. They just don't give me what I need as an IaWA Oracle.

    \n I very much like Joshua's post. When I do Oracle entries, I always do something similar to that. I try to make sure that the entries are about things that are happening right now, people that have needs around those things, and that suggest characters both explicit and implicit.

    That said, I think there is something of an art to making oracles that isn't easily summed, because not every kind of oracle will work for every player.

    Look at tarot cards, for example. There are about a billion different decks of tarot cards available, and cards that aren't quite tarot cards but that are also used for divination. One of the reasons for this is different decks hit different folks in different ways. I cannot stand the Rider Waite tarot, for example. It does nothing for me, nothing at all, despite being the "standard." OTOH, give me the Shakespeare Dream Cards set, and I can get all divinatory all day long.

    Similarly, there is a sweet spot for me with some oracles that I know isn't sweet for other folks I play with. I hate any oracle that tells me rather than suggests to me. I want them to be oracles, damnit, to provoke me to create my own characters and issues and places -- not to foist them off on me. I don't want bits of story so much as I want tags that lead me into bits of story. (And in this my own oracles sometimes fail me. Alas*.) OTOH, some folks I play with like it when oracles give them chunkier, more tinker-toy like bits, whole chunks of story ready to be slotted into play.

    So really, one of the things about designing an oracle is that you've got to know your audience, and have some idea of what it is you're trying to provoke them towards. Do you want them telling stories about this specific place and people (like I did with the Tribe 8 oracle, as writing an oracle for an established setting is an odd thing)? Do you want them telling a specific kind of story? Do you want to give them chunks of coolness about which to build their stories, or do you want to give them guides towards them building their own chunks of story?


    *Though I'm in good company on that one. I've sometimes gotten draws of Vincent's original oracles that made me make vexed and annoyed sounds.

    When I design an Oracle, I use a worksheet laid out according to the following method (refined from the method I was using in the thread Roger mentioned):\n\n\n\n\n\n
    PeopleRelationshipsPlacesEvents/Objects
    Topic 1
    Topic 2
    Topic 3
    Topic 4
    \nwhere the Topics are things pertaining to the setting you're trying to evoke, and no box may be empty. If you are strictly trying for a 52-card Oracle, tie your Topics to your suits and make sure each topic/suit has precisely 13 in it. To follow Joshua's advice, I try to go heavy on the 'Relationships' (people who need each other), make sure my Places or Objects are tied to a person or a desire, and make sure my Events are pressure-builders. Stylistically, Bret's advice (and Vincent's examples) are excellent. I try to brainstorm past my usual 50-ish item goal, carrying the worksheet with me everywhere in my pocket and adding ideas as they come, and then whittle off less-potent entries later.

    \n My suggestion for an Oracle checklist for quality would be:
    \n
    \n* Is there future action implied or stated by the oracle?
    \n* Does it avoid being too specific to allow the reader to "twist" the meaning in a cool way?
    \n* Does it connect to a character or group?
    \n* Does it blend with other Oracles in the set well?
    \n* Does is create a sense of past, present, and future?
    \n* Is it open to creative interpretation by the reader?
    \n
    \nThe examples from the Shakespeare and Modern Fantasy lists above had some that referred to a single event either in the past or the present that seemed to stagnate beyond its resolution.
    \n
    \nThe duty of a good oracle is to get the reader(s) thinking of ways to put the piece into the whole in a way where the oracle both fits well but reinterprets the standing of the developing situation emerging from the prior Oracles. Oracles should be jigsaw pieces that, once drawn from the box and fitted, change the image being sought.

    \n My quick thoughts:

    Each oracle should have enough entries matching its theme to ensure it is reflected, without over-emphasizing it. Statistically, two items in a random lot that reflect theme will be sufficient.

    The entries are also good mix of (a) places; (b) individuals; (c) groups/organizations/communities; (d) events; (e) objects - sometimes alone and sometimes intermixed, generally bespeaking goals/interests/directions that can then be imagined to contrast/conflict etc.

    E.G. Unquiet Past is (very rough quick review) Place (12); Person (34); Group (12) ; Events (5); Object (3)

    Entries should often include loaded, emotional state reflecting terminology. Chris Chinn\u2019s ideas about good conflict.

    \n I'd say it's a content thing; all of my Oracles have the same "voice" I think.

    But it's not strictly "these are autumn things" - it's more of a sense I have. For example, when I wrote

    Soldiers, unwelcome, returning through liberated lands...

    To me, that's definitely an Autumn entry, but more because I picture it painted in browns and reds. I know historically wars were fought mainly in the summer, but that's secondary.
