    Kira,

    We may destroy your marriage in the next couple of episodes. We've sort of run into a bit of a problem that we're likely to talk about. We *think* the problem is more the way we're USING the game than the actual game itself but it boils down to this:

    The "mini-game" design components of Diaspora which lend heavily to the more "down to earth", "hard sci-fi" feel seriously run out of steam once the players run out of FATE points. There really aren't very many opportunities to re-gain FATE points once you're IN a mini-game and after you run out of FATE points they turn into a very tedious grind.

    Again, I think this has to do with a bit of an expectation clash on our part. Despite our best efforts to "ground" the game, I think most of the players, myself included, are still trying to play a Space! adventure game. We're also playing at the pace of something like Burning Empires. We jump from MAJOR mini-game conflict to MAJOR mini-game conflict and we're kind of burning the midnight FATE points so to speak. The stakes are RIDICULOUSLY high that no one wants to offer a concession and we burn through our whole FATE supply by Round 3. At which point not only is the mini-game no fun but we can't even continue playing past it because we need a refresh to keep things interesting.

    If anyone actually has their expectations set correctly it's Will. We probably ran in to our biggest "vision" clash last session (which you'll hear about in two weeks since we're always a week ahead). We got boarded by marines from the other T2 civilization. We locked them in the air-lock with our "token alien" character who comes from a vicious war-like culture.

    It turns out Will was expecting us to get taken handily by their superior military training.

    *I* on the other hand was expecting a total Aliens moment as our Rat'chkar warrior painted the walls of our air lock red with their mook blood.

    Jesse

    \n Jesse,

    Are you guys compelling each other to forfeit actions in the mini-games? Cause if you are out of Fate then the GM should be compelling you to not roll an attack or a defense, which will let the GM nail you with come consequences and give the player a Fate point to boot. If you are stuck for aspects to compel don't forget that tech is covered in aspects and that compelling those should also re-enforce the tech grounded hard sci-fi feel.

    Or at least that is my read of the game, I have yet to play Diaspora yet.

    Edit - Also to add don't forget Manuevers! Those are basically rolls to get a free taggable (and compellable) aspects so if you are low on Fate then place a temp aspect and free tag that next round instead of spending your own Fate. I forget if in Diaspora you can free compel a temporary aspect, but if you can then free compel those to force the NPC to not roll or give you a Fate point...

    - Colin

    \n Fate points are one of those places that needs serious tweaking by table -- play style seems to impact it hugely. We get good mileage out of five (except in platoon combat, but we tend to run out around the time I am bored with it, so the speed-up is actually welcome) but I know tables that need much more. An early rule that can be added back in if your table style is such that you run dry is simple: the first time you enter combat in a session, you get five new fightin' fate points.

    \n Well, FATE point expenditure doesn't seem to be at the heart of our disagreement about the system... more the aspects and how wishy washy they can sometimes be, and how I think it takes some practice to make some good, fun aspects that are applicable in good fun ways. We're working on it. We haven't played Diaspora yet, but in playing a bunch of Spirit of the Century recently, we're had no shortage of fate points over the course of one session (sometimes 2 combats, but usually no more than that). People burn through them and get them back pretty easily, and then they refresh in 1-2 sessions depending on how far we get in the story that night. I'd prefer a more strict FATE point economy like Diaspora seems to provide! That way, instead of bandying them about, players can think about their actions a bit more, potentially take more consequences and possibly not come out at the very top of every fight. That sounds solid to me.
