\n So last night at our weekly playtest, we had an interesting suggestion. After a not-entirely-satisfying game whose rules did not seem to really provoke much in the way of interesting roleplay, Judson suggested sitting down and attempting to roleplay without any book, any system, any character sheets, and so on. The idea being that this would identify the baseline, the experimental control, and that could be compared with a game session that used a rule structure in order to illuminate what the rules actually add to the experience.

I thought it was an interesting suggestion, and we'll see if we get around to doing it at LAGames. I'm wondering if anybody else has tried this?

    \n In my group's last get-together, we did something like this - we were trying to figure out what game to play, leaning toward starting a new Exalted game. Being somewhat iffy on it, we decided to sort of just "jump in" and see if we could get a good vibe; So, without bothering with the rules or character creation or anything, we set a scene in the world, and then took turns narrating action into it.

    It didn't end up being much like an actual play of such a game, however - at least not a traditional character-driven one - and we didn't do a very good job of making compelling story or scenario, either.

    We did decide to use the general situation from this exercise for the core of the plot for our actual game of Exalted (which we prepared, but may or may not actually play.)

    \n That's an interesting idea, and one that you'd want to take notes on, or maybe even have a non-playing person take notes.

    Because as you go along, you probably will either recognize the need for rules, or evolve those rules as you go along.

    Do you even have a gamemaster in such a game? That's a rule right there. Note it down.

    The observer could note when rules are called for -- or not -- and observe what goes on. How are conflicts resolved? Do conflicts even happen? If you've got a particularly polite group, then nobody will be "pushing" the non-system.

    \n There's a ton of research on this. The catch: only pre-school-age kids do it. Everybody else already "knows how" through acculturation. The "baseline", such as it is, seems to get established VERY early on in psychosocial development. In fact, role-play (in the literal sense of "playing with roles") seems to be a major method for young children in learning how to be social creatures. Taking on roles and imagining outcomes of actions without actually trying them are some of the root skills of human experience.

    So how far down the rabbit-hole do you want to go?

    If you want to know a baseline for the activity we think of as being "gaming", you probably start with a prompt and maybe a basic resolution mechanic and see how people elaborate on that foundation. But you're going to see a lot of general social skill and custom being used as the infrastructure. This is one of the reasons why some folks have a hard time with "System Does Matter" - their system is heavily internalized prior to any identification of that system with the specific activity of "roleplaying games".

    Posted By: KynnI guess it ultimately depends on what the rest of the "experiment" is about.

    In other words, what you hope to learn or show from the experience.
    \nMy hypothesis (which I think is what Josh seized on) is that there are a lot of craftsman games that don't actually bring more to the table that you'd get if you sat down with the idea of "hey guys! Let's tell a story together!" In the harshest form: the good reports I hear of some games are the result of the input of the table, and the rules text doesn't usefully contribute.

    So, the experiment is: sit down with 3-5 people with the idea of telling a story together. Examine the process and results.

    It might well be that the outcome is that we'd adapt techniques from games we know, but I suspect there'd be a lot of ad hoc technique cropping up to promote very different play.

    Probably a free-former would skew the results completely. (Contrariwise, I can't see a better way to learn freeform.)
