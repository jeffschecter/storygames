\n
\n Your new GM is very enthusiastic about "immersive roleplay" but he also likes having "social stats" in the game. He wants to experiment with a style of play your group may not have tried before.
\n
\nHis rule is - you don't speak FOR your character (that his, you don't say what he SAYS) unless you speak AS your character. You have to use direct speech. ALL THE TIME. None of the old "I'll ask the shopkeeper what he's got for sale". It has to be (manly voice) "My good fellow, please, show me your wares". Facial expressions and gestures optional, but you have to use the character's tone and accent as you conceive them to be. This is just for words, not actions.
\n
\nThe ref also does things like Bluff rolls or whatever, if he thinks they're likely to be necessary, BEFORE the dialogue. He keeps the exact result secret (even if he lets you roll your own dice when you're the one doing the Bluffing). He doesn't use the dice result to dictate the outcome of the conversation, but he uses it as a gentle steer for his roleplay, so it still depends on what you say and how you say it, but the GM might be frostier, or warmer, easier or harder to convince, depending on your dice-roll. His reasoning is he doesn't want to interrupt the in-character dialogue.
\n
\nLet me clarify some important points about the model I propose.
\n
\n1. NPCs' roleplay is adjusted to reflect the dice-rolls - the dice-rolls provide the GM with a gentle steer (NOT a straitjacket).
\n
\n2. PCs' roleplay is NOT adjusted to reflect the dice-rolls. The dice-check result is a secret which is known only to the GM. Excellent roleplay from the PLAYERS can make up for terrible dice-rolls and terrible roleplay from the PLAYERS can squash excellent results.
\n
\n3. You are not talking in character all the frickin time. That would be virtually impossible for a tabletop game. It's just that your character says nothing unless you say it AS him - or the ref narrates it.
\n
\nObviously, the ref can narrate a way around tedious conversations with your assent.
\n
\nComments please.

    \n I used to do something similar, but with the dice in the open.

    Roleplay through the conversation first, then roll the social skill (or whatever).

    Your roleplaying is the tactic that you tried.

    Your roll is how well you pulled it off.

    Just like combat:

    You say "I duck under his blow and take advantage of the opening!".

    Then you roll to see if that's what you did, or whether you failed to pull it off.

    Your method might be "more immersive" for some, since the die rolls are "invisible". (To me, personally, it wouldn't make a difference, though: in my head I'd be wondering if the GM is responding the way he is because of the dice or because the character leans in a certain direction anyway.)

    \n Why not just give bonuses based on performance?
    Make the system do some of the job, instead of having the GM do all of it.

    All it'd take is a set of conditions. For example:
    - The player performs the appropriate emotion accurately. With feeling, yeah. +1
    - The player's argument, if applicable, makes sense. +1
    - The player gets really into it, and you totally buy it. +2

    Okay, that's still a GM judgment call, but it at least has some external guidelines. Something along the lines of Sorcerer.

    Posted By: Paul T.Your method might be "more immersive" for some, since the die rolls are "invisible". (To me, personally, it wouldn't make a difference, though: in my head I'd be wondering if the GM is responding the way he is because of the dice or because the character leans in a certain direction anyway.)
    \nAs a player, I like seeing the dice first and then trying to fit my dialogue to the result -- pretty much the opposite of what the OP is proposing -- because I've played with this group so much that I can pitch almost anything convincingly to them. I like a good argument, I have 'em all the time, and after so many years together I know which people at the table will be swayed with logic (spurious or real), who'll prefer an emotional appeal, who'll laugh and go along with reverse psychology, and so on. If my only goal in the interaction is to "win" it, I can get there maybe 4 out of 5 tries without even touching the dice, and so most of the time I'm not in it for the win; I'm happier and more interested in letting the dice go first and following their lead.

    (This is also why systems that give bonuses for "convincing arguments" don't sit well with me; we have players who don't like debate as much as others, and they will consistently get screwed over while people like me are consistently getting the maximum bonus. The hell with that, that's not fun.)

    Posted By: omnifrayExcellent roleplay from the PLAYERS can make up for terrible dice-rolls and terrible roleplay from the PLAYERS can squash excellent results.
    This is something I would use with young players (10-15 years), who need to stretch their abilities. Young players need to develop the skills necessary to interpret characters in communicative ways.

    As for grown up players, I would prefer the game to support my roleplay whatever I do, as long as I really DO ROLEPLAY to my best ability. So I would like the dice to tell all players, including the GM, that my clumsy in-character speech is in fact the flamboyant oratory of a genius (my character). That is how I like to do it. So I would like the "excellent roleplay" to be inspired by the social dynamics of the game-group, rather than by a chance to roleplay my character out of bad dice-rolls.

    The dark shadow of measuring the individual roleplaying-feats like this, is that some players may fall through. The verbally bleak players may see their characters falter in the shadow of the characters with more eloquent players.

    Posted By: Zac in DavisWhy not just give bonuses based on performance?
    I do.

    But I make sure I reward the performer, not the performance. That way I reward the effort to contribute to the game, and not the quality of the contribution. Someway that makes all players perform on an increasingly higher level throughout a game-session. Players constantly roleplaying their characters inspires other players to roleplay too, and as interaction ensues the qualities of the roleplay soars.

    So: the qualities of roleplay goes up without me having to rate the quality of the individual contributions. ;-)
