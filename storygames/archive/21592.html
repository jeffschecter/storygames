    \n With regards to games giving bonuses built on roleplaying, I don\u2019t know of a game that does this to my satisfaction, and this is something I\u2019m addressing in a game I\u2019m designing called House of Spiders. HoS is GMless game about power, scheming, and courtley intrigue, (think Game of Thrones), which gives rewards based on roleplaying; I discuss how it does so in detail below.

    One thing that GMless games can often suffer from is story sprawl, people all have different ideas about the direction the story should take and don\u2019t build enough on other players\u2019 contributes to the story. HoS gives players an narrative reward (in terms of narrative authority) for building on another player\u2019s contribution to the story: this helps to keep the story threads related and relevant to one another, helps to keep a constant tone, and to produce a better story. The mechanic furthers the aims of the game\u2019s design in tangible and well thought out ways, as opposed to Inspiration which is somewhat shallow in design\u2014I\u2019ll discuss this in more detail below.

    In HoS, the reward for building on someone\u2019s contribution to the story is not as arbitrary as a bonus; instead, the reward is orthogonal to the core narrative resolution mechanic. Players are rewarded with an opportunity to change the narrative in a substantial way (ie more narrative authority) that simultaneously reinforces tension, optimal narrative arc and timing, and the overall design objectives of the game. An example of a narrative reward a PC could choose from\u2014because they previously built on another player\u2019s contribution to the story in a significant way\u2014could be the ability to narrate a scene in which an NPC, who is friendly to another player, betrays that player\u2014there are other similar narrative options a player could choose as a reward, which are related to that characters nature (ie personality, ideals, and the like) and their intent/aims within the story. These mechanics are designed and timed in a way that creates the ultimate amount of dramatic tension and PC buy-in.

    The mechanics reward players for a certain kind of role-playing because that roleplaying works towards producing the objectives of the game, they improve the story, they keep the story tightly waved and every player\u2019s contribution relevant to the others, they support the themes of the story in an emergent way, etc. In other words, they don\u2019t leave everything up to skill or an implicit or explicit social contract, which is one of the major reasons that GMless games fail, but instead work with other mechanics to create an emergent story that enacts the game\u2019s design goals, and prevents story failure.

    Inspiration in 5e, on the other hand, doesn\u2019t really work towards the design goals of the game in any substantive or deep sense; It just says, do some cool roleplaying and you\u2019ll get a bonus that doesn\u2019t really have a relevant or and coherent narrative affect, in the game design sense. Because it\u2019s superficial, and easy (in the non-elegant sense), it feels like an add-on rather than a deeply considered design decision. It\u2019s not bad, but it is lazy, sub-optimal solution.

    5e is an absolutely great game, and I like it that they are starting to incorporate dramatist play style options into D&D, and I\u2019d prefer to have a seemingly tacked-on mechanic like Inspiration to not having any dramatist options at all. It\u2019s definitely a step in the right direction, but I can definitely understand why people think that that specific aspect of its design can be improved. Although, it\u2019s not immediately evident to me what the that improvement would look like. I would have to think about it for a while, in the context of their overall game design goals.

    I'd start by looking at The Shadow of Yesterday, as well as all the games which borrow "Key" mechanics from it (e.g. Lady Blackbird). Same goes for Burning Wheel and Mouse Guard, for another "branch" of the tree which I feel "inspired" 5e's Inspiration.

    Lots of PbtA games use this kind of thing effectively, too. Consider, for starters, how "seduce/manipulate" works in Apocalypse World, and many/most of the moves in Monsterhearts.

    The way Relationship Dice work in Dogs in the Vineyard is also a really interesting contrast to Bonds in 5e. Similar in some respects and different in others. Notable is the common practice of assigning them *in play* as opposed to at character creation (something I've never seen done in D&D; and, although it's not strictly textually clear in Dogs either, it is definitely common practice).

    The Rules in Dog Eat Dog form an interesting example of this, too, where following or breaking them IS the whole point of each scene, and each offers interesting choices to the characters.

    \n Spellbound Kingdoms! In SK, PCs have 2-3 Passions, things they care about a whole lot, and if you have a high enough Passion, you can't be killed. ("Death cannot stop true love.") During play, if a Passion is relevant in a scene, you can invoke it once per scene for a massive bonus that will often guarantee at least momentary success. Whereas if no Passion is relevant in a scene, you can still spend the game's "Fate point" equivalents, but they're not nearly as powerful.

    It has some holes. It's not as clear as could be how you switch points around between Passions, and how you recover the other type of points could be more closely tied to the Passions, IMO.

    But the biggest way the Passions impact play is that they actually make social scenes more deadly than combat scenes: if the Villain can convince you that, e.g., your son has abandoned you and no longer cares about you, your Passion for your son might drop low enough that you could then be killed.

    \n My gold standard for this continues to be Tenra Bansho Zero; The Aiki/Kiai/Karma (They're separate, but I'm not sure folks care enough for me to explain in depth) system does everything I want from a system of this sort:

    * It's not dependent on the GM to be monitoring X players times Y special traits in order to award it. Everyone hands out Aiki, and while it should be rewarded to people who play their Fates, it's also explicitly used as a "That was awesome, I liked it" award.
    * It has a great deal of power in play. Inspiration has the potential to be a bummer, when you face the bugbear chief that burned down your village, charge into the fray, yell "For Hillsvale!" spend your inspiration, and roll a 4 and an 8. Kiai potentially has way more impact than your character's actual stats/skills. When you decide something matters, you get the Shonen Anime moment.
    * It has its own internal limiter; Karma accumulates as you spend Kiai, and too much karma is a problem, so even if you are rolling in Kiai, there are decisions on when to spend it (and how. It potentially has many uses)
    * It ties back around to your character's Fates; You reduce Karma by resolving your Fates, so your character is mechanically driven to change, rather than being a 5th level fighter whose burned village is still the most important thing in his life or whatever background you picked.

    It does have some issues though:
    * As a result of amount of power it adds to the game, the game is ill suited for long term play. (This was a conscious design decision.) So if that's what you want, you have to hack a bit - the usual method being to reset any long-term stat changes to your character between "episodes" or whatever.
    * It involves some bookkeeping. You need the occasional 10 minute intermission to sort things out.

    I probably forgot some stuff too. But after playing with this, I feel let down by "GM gives out magic points for Things" systems (including Fate to some extent.)

    \n Another game with uneven implementation is the Exalted "Stunt" system. there you get bonus dice for your dice pool if you describe your actions in a cool way. The advantage of this is it is a carrot approach, it is a one off that directly ties what the players do to the bonus. The problem is "cool" is poorly defined so some GMs shut this down accidentally by saying, that was an OK stunt, but it wasn't "cool" or the GM misinterprets the descriptions and applies additional rolls and difficulty to the roll.

    A better implementation is Wushu where everything the player says happens as described, the dice are rolled to see if the target has any will left to fight. You do get better bonuses for including better and more detail though.


    PTA Fanmail is a decent implementation. As long as the game doesn't devolve to using fanmail as a pity vote.

    \n That's a valid concern, and some games do fall into such traps.

    However, you're looking at it a little wrong:

    Having a Key on your character sheet is NOT a way to say, "this is who my character is".

    It IS a way to say, "this is how I want us to play this game with my character".

    In other words, neglecting to follow the Key is just as interesting. Doing other things is just as interesting. As play continues, you change and/or swap our Keys and such.

    It's about signaling to others, at root.

    The GM choosing to prep something based on your Key choices is more important to me than the mechanical reward the Key grants, for example, in terms of impact on the game. The rewards make it stick, they make it matter, though, and they make sure we don't forget.

    It's worth a try, to see if your fears are valid (they usually aren't, for people voicing such opinions, but you can only find out by trying).
