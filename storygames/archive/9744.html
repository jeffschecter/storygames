    \n The third game we played was kick-started by the song What Was I Thinkin? by Dierks Bentley, off a mix named Country Comfort (by Andy Henderson). Now, in our 4-person game, I was not alone in despising country. This was a first for me. I'd yet to play a single game of Ribbon Drive where I flat out disliked the music playing (parts of Ben Lehman's mix Marriage tried me).

    Dislike aside, by the end of that first song I had a really burning vision of what play would look like. I asked the others to share their thoughts first, and then introduced mine. This loud-mouthed guy and his girlfriend and his best friend rob their Texas hometown liquor store, take the store attendant hostage, and attempt to drive free to Mexico. We went with this set up.

    I don't remember what the second song was, but in the end we had:
    Tuck Allard, a loud-mouthed, party-seekin' guy who was quite content livin' and partyin' in his small town.
    His girlfriend (I think named Lizzie Monroe), a touch rowdy as well, but much more thoughtful (and smarter than she lets on).
    Langley, the best friend, who was getting over a semi-recent break-up and wanting out of the small town life.
    The 14-year-old kid who'd been working at the liquor store when we held it up, who suggested being taken hostage when the cops arrived.

    The first CD was quite mellow after that Dierks Bentley song. The road trip we created meandered a fair bit - we took dirt roads to avoid the police, and spent a lot of time thinkin' and dreamin'. Tuck proved to be insensitive to other people's concerns, often railroading the social space ("My truck. My rules."). Lizzie proved ready to mold to other people's needs - being Tuck's yes-woman, being Langley's sounding board. There was one brilliant flashback scene to when Samantha (Langley's old girlfriend) explains to him over dinner that she doesn't see him living up to his potential, and that she's been accepted to Texas State University.

    Langley met a new girl, when we stopped in Brooksville (or something). Lizzie matched them up and saw to it that they met at the bar. They were both a bit quiet and a bit mousy (at least, compared to their loud, go-get-'em friends), and formed this soft space that the trip had been previously lacking. Everything was going well, until Tuck showed up (he'd been missing for a few hours): bloody, without a truck, panting, the duffel bag holding considerably more money than before.

    Things suddenly kicked up a notch. The rest of the trip (fuelled by relationship squabbles between Lizzie and Tuck, fleeing just before the cops arrive and quiet moments at roadside diners between Langley and the new girl) had a different tone. We were now stress testing Langley's good fortune. In his new flame's car, we were fleeing the responsibility of facing up to two armed robberies, and tensions were mounting between the perfectly sane folk and the impulsive-and-dangerous Tuck (who had the futures "I'll show them how it can be" and "I'll never be bored and lonely"). The tension peaked with a chase scene. Several cop cars were on our tail, and we were totally busted. Then, Tuck queries the 14-year-old kid, "You play video games, right?" Cut to: the kid is shooting out the tires of the foremost cop car, which skids out and gets T'd by the second. Getaway!

    The next scene is in a restaurant (Tuck pulled out the big bucks to take them to the classy Olive Garden). Langley's new girl is in the washroom with Lizzie, sobbing. She wants to love Langley, but his friends are dangerous sociopaths. Is he, too? Meanwhile, cut to the boys: Langley wants this to work with the new girl, but he's endangering her and they clearly have no future. They both hit that brilliant realization at the same time, before meeting back up: this is what they've got. It's right here, and there's no reason not to embrace it.

    Langley and the girl hop a bus back to the home town.
    Tuck and Lizzie and their hostage-turned-killer keep heading towards Mexico, in a now-pretty-much-stolen car.

    Langley crossed off his second future, as his player (Darren) narrated Langley and his girlfriend arriving back in the home town, and knocking on his parents' door.
    Cut.

    Posted By: DevPI'm planning to bring a Fireflyesque mix
    \nInterested parties should note that the mixes for Radion-Accelerator Drive are a bit more constrained:

    Instead of having a free-for-all with the mix CDs, you have each person write a specific CD. Assign each player one of these mixes, going down the list. If you have more than five players, double up on Frontier Planet, Alliance Planet and In Space! (in that order).
    Opening Situation: This mix should be short, and starts with your theme song (either use the Firefly theme or choose a new one).
    The Job: The first song in this mix will detail a mission of some sort.
    Frontier Planet: When this CD plays, you\u2019re on a cowboy planet (or, at the very least, everything has an extremely wild west feel).
    Alliance Planet: When this CD plays, you\u2019re on a sterile high-tech planet (or, on an alliance ship or outpost)
    In Space!: When this CD plays, you\u2019re in space! (possibly stranded, or infiltrating a reaver ship!)

    Example. Joe, Andy and Lukas decide to play Radion-Accelerator Drive. Joe will make the Opening Situation CD. Andy will make the Job CD. Lukas will make the Frontier Planet CD.

    Example. Joe, Andy, Lukas, Lesley, Dawn and David decide to play Radion-Accelerator Drive. Joe will make the Opening Situation CD. Andy will make the Job CD. Lukas will make the first Frontier Planet CD. Lesley will make the Alliance Planet CD. Dawn will make the In Space! CD. David will make the second Frontier Planet CD.

    I'll be bringing a Frontier Planet CD. Someone shotty the others.\n
