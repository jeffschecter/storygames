Faery's Tale at DunDraCon '07

Firefly Games
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Directed Promotion
I ran five Faery's Tale demos at DunDraCon in San Ramon this past weekend, with from 2 to 8 people playing at a time. I had a good mix of kids & adults (not just parents) in most of the sessions. We played the Tournament of the Fey intro pack adventure, since it's already structured to gradually introduce the rules in a relaxed storyline - it's a faery festival, so the players can spend some time wandering around competing in contests & getting a feel for the game without a lot of pressure to rush off & save the world. :)

The last game was particularly wild, with 4-5 adults who were experienced story games players. They had a lot of fun with the plot twist rules, changing the adventure around quite a bit from the originaly storyline (which is great & all part of the game). Instead of fighting a dark faery at the climax to challenge his claim to marry a pixie princess, they tricked him into riding a pooka who'd changed form into a crow, and the pooka dumped him in a lake, where he got swallowed by a frog.

Anyway, if any folks here played in the games, I'm glad we got a chance to meet & I hope you had a good time!

    Posted By: Caesar_XI'm curious how the table dynamics were having parents and kids at the same table. My experience with gaming overall is that kids tend to take cues from grownups when they are around and that it would play differently with all kids. Did you want to talk a little more about that?
    Sure! Neat topic. Actually, I don't think I've run a game with all kids yet. As for mixed groups, it really depends on the kids & their ages. One of the boys who played with his parents definitely took a lot of cues from them, but it turned out to be very handy to have them there because he had a hard time with losing or things going wrong for his faery in the game, so they were able to deal with that much better than I could have. And it was very neat to watch his dad, in particular, using the game to teach him about being a good sport - having his faery go over to congratulate the winner when they lost a contest & so forth. And I was able to reinforce that in the game by giving their faeries Essence rewards (the Narrator rewards actions that make the game more fun with Essence, which is the magical life force of the faeries plus useful in lots of other ways). In the 8-player game, it was split between adults & kids. One father watched his two girls play, and the other kids were older & on their own (they'd come over from the kids room next door). So when the players disagreed about what to do, they were mostly able to resolve it through discussion, and sometimes they followed what the kids wanted to do, sometimes what the adults wanted. In fact, the two girls whose father was watching came up with some of the best ideas all on their own - one played a pooka who changed form into a beaver for the wood-carving contest (which came about because I mentioned a riddling contest, which another player misheard as a "whittling contest," and I said, that's a great idea for a contest, let's do that instead - and have an Essrence reward).

    I did notice the all-adult groups were more willing to go a little wilder - not so much in terms of bawdiness or grimness, but in changing the story - than the mixed groups. So I suspect the adults in mixed groups may be holding back a little to keep things on track for the younger players.
