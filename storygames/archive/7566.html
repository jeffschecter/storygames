\n Okay, I wanted to share this story.

I recently ran a Nobilis campaign for a couple new friends that went really well. So well, we're actually following it up with another Nobilis campaign. Now, I'd never gamed with either of these women before and it seemed like they were used to tyrannical GMs. Apparently he would come to the table with backstories written for them. I am on the complete opposite end of the spectrum. It actually took them awhile to get used to me, and early on there were a lot of interactions like this:

"So, what is my character's husband like?"
"Hm. Good question. Why don't you tell me what he's like and I'll just play that."
*her head explodes*

There was actually a lot of struggling. I never gave them clear answers. Problems were open-ended. Generally, I just reacted to what they were doing. In one of the first plots the landlord of their Chancel was dying. I had their Imperator come in and be like, "OK, this human is almost used up. Find another one." They then proceeded to investigate ways of making her immortal which I did not anticipate at all.

So anyway, that leads to this. Game over. Post-mortem. You guys have any feedback for me?

"Well, I had fun, but you led us around by the nose too much."

I actually laughed out loud. I then talked about what I did, how I prep, how everything I did was reacting to what they were doing. It was interesting. After I did that they were like, "Oooh. Everything seemed like such a natural progression it seemed like you came in with a story in mind."

I wondered how long they'd thought this. I mean, maybe I did lead them by the nose but I really don't think I did.

Anyway, it was interesting and kind of funny. If you've played with me (which an odd few people on here have) you might know that I suck big-time at prepped, linear stories. I can't do them very well even if I try. My most recent D&D game is an example of that.

    \n Back in the old days, when I couldn't quite verbalize how good games happened or why, it seemed like magic to all of us. Players would say things like, "How did you know we were going to do that? I don't understand how you planned all this out."

    I didn't plan it out.

    The first time I played BW with Luke Crane was several Gen Con's ago. We were playing The Gift (Elves vs. Dwarves!) and Jeff was gaming with us too and we were having a splendid time. But one player was sitting there, really upset and perturbed.

    When Luke went to the bathroom, obviously thrilled with where the game was going, the upset player spoke up, "This isn't what he had planned. We're messing up the game!"

    I tried to make him feel better but he seemed really edgy until he found a hand-hold for his character in the later scenes but his anguish really stayed with me. I make a point of saying something to players when I run con scenarios about not having any idea where this is all going. "I don't have an end-point in mind, all I did was write up a solid starting point and I'm going to go with your flow, here and push on you in interesting ways, not to get you to a certain set-up end-point but to keep things in motion."

    The reactions I get from that speech are always interesting, from nodding to verbal affirmations ("That is how a good DM should work!") to looks with a bit of fear in 'em.

    \n I've never played Nobilis, but I think this is a common misconception: "I just played a game that had an awesome story, therefore the GM must have wrote that awesome story in advance". I think this misconception is unfortunately the source of a lot of railroading when people take it a step further: "Therefore when I GM, I am going to write an awesome story in advance". This means that players who are future GMs can learn railroading not only from railroady GMs, but from non-railroady GMs too, if they misunderstand what their non-railroady GM is doing.

    For that reason, I think this approach is cool:\n
    Posted By: JuddI make a point of saying something to players when I run con scenarios about not having any idea where this is all going. Next time they go to run a game, maybe they will say "I had so much fun in that game where that GM was working with the players rather than planning in advance. Maybe I'll try that!"

    \n In many ways, this parallels the difference between the Manager and Radical modes of thinking. The Manager mode expects the story to proceed according to some known principles of 'how the story should go,' and thus expects you to have where it will go all figured out ahead of time. The Radical mode expects the story and story elements to be interpreted in a variety of ways anyhow, and thus expects that how the players interpret the situation will in large part determine where the story goes.

    \n As Bret suggested, this comes from that culture of play where the GM is a privileged position, both socially and with regards to the game procedures. In that culture, players are expected to defer to the GM in a hundred (little and big) ways. If a GM comes in and acts like he's not special and promotes equal status... well, those signals can come across in strange ways to people who are looking for ways to defer and lower their status relative to the GM.

    I've seen this happen several times. It's much, much worse when the GM is used to this culture and the players are not. "Why aren't you deferring to me?!"

      \n (In response to Robert--I see several posts have been made in the interim.)

      Well, I'm going by the books, the texts that are supposed to explain how the games are played, by the play experiences I've had, and by reading "actual play reports"; what I generally see for DitV, Sorcerer, and other games is the GM taking a very active role in pushing the action forward by means of "Bangs", or placing character issues into direct conflict with one another, escalating the tension, and so forth. It's easy to say, "Oh, that's just improv", but it's strongly motivated improv that works off a very wide leeway in use of the pre-existing facts of the scenario.

      By that I mean--sure, if you make use of the interstices of prep, you can improvise anything, but there's a point that strains credibility and undermines the legitimacy of the prep. If you don't define the cop's personality beforehand, then you can slot something in on the spur of the moment that'll hit character X's character issues perfectly. Say, character X has an aversion to violence but a dedication to standing up for the little guy, so you decide that the cop'll put the beat down on a hapless immigrant right in front of the character...and nobody else seems willing to do anything...so it's all up to him. Awesome! Conflict! Hard decisions! Well, some people might like that, but to me it feels heavy-handed, contrived, and shallow.

      Difference of quality or degree? Maybe you could work up to the conflict, lay the groundwork, give players a chance to get to know their characters and the NPCs. The DitV GMing section has something similar--the idea that you can give a player an easy decision in Town A, then push against that decision in Town B, keep going until a sort of crisis point is reached. In theory it still sounds contrived; in practice I haven't seen it either in person or in actual play reports. Every instance I've seen of people mindfully applying the Bang/Conflict/Escalation strategy, I've been turned off--except where the game is so radically outside the norm that there's no doubt it's an altogether different thing from what I think of as character-perspective roleplaying. (E.g., Polaris still looks like it could be fun.)

      So how does the GM make improvised decisions? Randomly. Unconsciously. Whimsically. Falling back on local tropes of the group and general tropes of genre. Extrapolating from the known to the unknown. Depending on the mood at the table, the GM might also choose to bridge from one piece of prep to another--a kind of "say yes". But not with an especially strong aim to push conflicts toward crisis and resolution.

      \nOkay, you're asking about three things: Purpose of the improv, GM manipulation, and OOC control. Suspended from the last is "what does OOC control have to do with being led by the nose?"

      Purpose of improv: The overriding purpose is to fill in blanks while maintaining an air of verisimilitude, rather than seeking to put characters on the spot. Now, you may say, "That's not how I play", or "that's a clumsy application of the principles". To which I shrug and point at dozens of examples supported by theories of Narrativism and the theoretical grouping of a subset, under Narrativism, of modes that eschew abstract verbalization or conscious address of "issues"--which implies that there's also a grouping, perhaps the default, where abstract verbalization, or conscious address of "issues" is indeed what's going on. Furthermore I think that any discussion of whether issues are addressed unconsciously is muddled by unconscious selection--"issues" come and go.

      But that's already an overly defensive response--necessitated only by the fact that the theory growing out of Story Now has become a totalizing structure in practice--serving both to exclude (exaggerating differences between ideal "agendas") and to coopt (negating real differences in preference). In actual practice I think I have a use for certain techniques such as "Flag Framing". Lately I've been working up a questionnaire in my head where one of the questions will be something like, "Are there any pressing issues or conflicts that will impact your character immediately?" Or "How directly or obliquely do you want initial scenarios to incorporate aspects of your character's background and relationships?" The idea is to treat character-creation as an element of collaborative world-building and scenario-creation.

      But that's a digression. The point is: there are games where the pushing of buttons is quite overt and quite direct, and clearly motivated by timing and pacing. If the GM has bullet-points for each character, as a reminder to build toward or pop up with a specific conflict ("try to hit 'sense of justice" vs. 'nonviolence')--bleah.

      That segues into "GM manipulation", particularly as it touches the idea of "escalation". Let's say we've got a character who has serious issues with his father--maybe the father beat him as a kid, or did something even more unspeakable. Or the father's a gangster and the son's a cop. So the scenario proceeds more or less naturally toward a conflict between father and son--maybe there's a family crisis, the son confronts the father, and it turns into a fight in a hotel room. Ergo, if the GM doesn't think things have been pushed far enough, well, there's a gun! On the nightstand! Nobody says there wasn't one, right? Bleah. It's transparent, melodramatic, railroady in a small way for the GM to add or subtract setting elements, or alter NPC* goals and motivations, specifically to ramp up the tension. (Another aside: I'd prefer, if the GM or anyone else thought having a gun would be plausible and interesting for any reason, to roll dice.       Finally, OOC control is a very simple concept. I don't know anything about PTA or Nobilis. I'm just talking about a player being able to declare things about the setting and have them be true, in ways that a character couldn't. You know, like in Dogs you can say you have a relationship with an NPC if you've got the dice. Or if winning a conflict gets you "narration rights" or "stakes" that extend into control over details of the setting or backstory--I win, so I get to narrate an elaborate scheme that my character planned and which now comes to fruition! (Or, "There's a horse under the window!" Or, "The mayor's a serial killer!")

      I don't have control over those things in real life. In small doses and certain contexts, I think those things can be fun in RPGs. But there's a vast range--and for that matter, I think this is addressed in the Forge thread I linked, so you can look there if my wording leaves you mystified.

      In my experience the definition of railroading is pretty much "being tracked down a single path by the GM in a way that I do not like."

      That "I do not like" and what makes a single path are important.
