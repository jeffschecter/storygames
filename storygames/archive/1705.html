The Purpose of this Scene is...

Josh Roby
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Play Advice
Okay, so I'm trying to work out some game-structuring and scene-framing mechanics, right, so I need to think of all the different things that scenes might do. I'm trying to atomize scene purposes as much as possible, with the patent understanding that most scenes do more than one thing at the same time.

Introduce a character
A character strides or stumbles or is thrown onto the stage. First impressions are made of the character.

Introduce an element of a character
This elaborates on the audience's understanding of the character thus far. This develops and complicates the character, and sometimes opens up new opportunities for the direction of the overall story.

Introduce an element of the setting
The scene either uses a new set (and here we are in the library!) or introduces new information about a set (you can find some books on that in the library). Alternately, it can present some fundamental mechanical function of the fictional world (every time a bell rings, an angel gets his wings!).

Relate a character to an element of the setting
Take a character and explain their relationship to the setting -- either "Hi, I'm the librarian here" or "I hang out in the library all the time" or "I donated half the books in the library" or whatever.

Relate an element of the setting to another element
Relatively rare, because it's hard not to do this with boring exposition. The usual workaround is to introduce a character who stands in for an element of the setting and then relate the character to another element of the setting (instead of expositing that the city trades with the tribes, introduce a tribesman who comes to the city to trade).

Introduce a conflict*
We are well-versed in this one. Oh no, something happens! Character + Desire + Obstacle!

Escalate a conflict
Increasing the thematic impact of a conflict. Stakes are raised, new methods are introduced, and the conflict's "footprint" expands.

Complicate a conflict
Taking an already established conflict and complicating it by adding more details that do not escalate the conflict, but provide more points of view and more motivations, often at cross-purposes to what has already been established.

Resolve a conflict
Unknot the character+desire+obstacle equation, often through reincorporation of elements that were introduced earlier in the game.

Denouement of conflict
This is sort of a clean-up function after a conflict or series of conflicts have been resolved, satisfying the audience's curiosity of what happens afterwards or what the resolution of the conflict means in the longer term.

* I'm actually not sure if the "conflict" items are on the same sort of level as those that come before this one, since you can, for instance, introduce a conflict by introducing a character to serve as the obstacle. The conflict-manipulation scene purposes seem to be more intents rather than actions.

What other things can be done in a scene?

    This reminds me a lot of Scott McCloud's stuff (Understanding/Reinventing/Making Comics), especially about the 6 different types of transitions between comic panels:

    1. Moment to Moment (a single action portrayed in a series of moments)
    2. Action to Action (a single subject in a series of actions)
    3. Subject to Subject (a series of changing subjects within a single scene)
    4. Scene to Scene (transitions across significant dimensions of time or space)
    5. Aspect to Aspect (transitions from one aspect of a place, idea, or mood to another)
    6. Nonsequiter (a series of seemingly nonsensical, unrelated images and/or words)

    These, I would argue, are good descriptions of transitions between individual "instances" of play, but they also recommend a few different purposes for scenes in general.

    Like, if we're playing out a scene and, all of a sudden, one player says "Somewhere across town, a man slips on a banana peel." That's a little scene based on a nonsequiter transition, but I don't see it described by any of the things mentioned yet. Sure, it's an extreme example, but it could be used with great effect in certain kinds of games (comedy, arthouse, Octane).

    Jason, I think Celebrate an Element is probably a good call, cause I think you're right in that it has a place that is not especially related to the progress of plot or story.

    On the complicate/escalate tack, almost all of that I'd call escalation. By complication, I mean taking a conflict that's been established as being about one thing and layering it so that it's about another thing, as well. Generally speaking, I'm not talking about stuff in the heat of the moment, but in the building up to the climax and resolution. Stuff like, in an episode/issue/adventure when the young upstart is trying to prove himself to the old veteran, there's a scene where the old veteran explains to some third character that he sees promise in the upstart but doesn't want him to fall into the same traps that the veteran did. So now the conflict isn't just "does the upstart prove himself" but "does the upstart prove himself and does the veteran prevent the same mistakes being repeated?" In the crudest terms, you go from two resolution options to four, and even then the resolution of the two issues will reflect on each other no matter what you do.

    The McCloud stuff is interesting; I'd like to see some more development along those lines in game structure. Ron has talked about crosses and weaves and whatnot, but it might be interesting to get down even further to discrete techniques.

    Try these definitions:

    * Introduce: self-explanatory, we see something we haven't seen before.
    * Escalate: the whatever-it-is gains more significance to the story, its story weight increases, we notice it more, losing it would matter more.
    * Complicate/correlate: the thing forms relationships with other things of the same or different types such that A affects B or vice versa or both.
    * Resolve: the thing goes through a process of transformation (which can include destruction or removal).

    I suspect that it's actually going to be fairly difficult to think of examples of escalation that don't work by complication/correlation. The conflict is more important because more things that we value depend on its outcome. The character is more important because we know him better and we like him now. The ring is more important because it belongs to the Dark Lord and he wants it back.
