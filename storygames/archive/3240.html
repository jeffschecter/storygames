    \n I remember the James Bond 007 chase rules being pretty fast-moving and fun. Successes would move you closer to or farther away from your opponent using range bands (i.e. close, medium, long, extreme, etc). And these same range bands were used for combat ranges. You could also try maneuvers like 'double-back' and 'quick-turn', and the GM could throw obstacles like "railroad crossing" in your way. But I would always go for the 'oil-slick' switch on the center console:)

    I bet it would work pretty well with spaceships.

    I also remember Burning Sands: Jihad (SF supplement for Burning Wheel) having some good chase rules.

    \n Ok, chases and dogfights are two different things, as distinguished by the goals of the participants. This is all well and good, but the difference led to me writing up some material for SotC (Which has chase rules that I like) to handle dogfights a little differently.

    The chase rules can be found, sans example, here and the dogfight rules can be found here.

    Beyond that, Spycraft is the game most commonly mentioned when talking about awesome chase rules. They've evolved upon themselves, and similar systems have appeared in Adventure! and 7th Sea, but the latest version of Spycraft is, by =reports, pretty much cutting edge.

    \nI've played in both SC 2.0 and SotC games that involved chases of one sort or another, and I can attest to both systems rocking mightily for that purpose. SC 2.0's chase rules (and all the dramatic conflict rules, really) are a good go-to if d20 is the game you're playing. Otherwise, the basic SotC chase and dogfight concepts are probably adaptable to other systems pretty easily. You're essentially pitting participants against each other via opposed rolls, with the loser of a given exchange taking some consequences, e.g., damage to their ship or person.

    Posted By: KynnI guess part of the question would be "what makes chases exciting anyway?"

    Can you describe what aspects of a chase scene you think are most important?
    \nAfter posting, I started to ponder about "what" I really wanted to be integral in the scene. I would say tops has to be tension, but second would probably be the ability of the players to make decisions that mattered and also somehow produced more tension.

    The antithesis would be the last horse/wagon chase I ran in Savage Worlds. And I'll freely admit that while I've played tons of SW, the chase rules have only come up a few times. But basically, the chase rules use 1 playing card per vehicle to represent both initiative and relative position. And the cards are re-dealt at the start of the next round.
    After about 3 rounds of random position changes without the players maneuvering seeming to affect their positioning, the play bogs down, players loose interest. Uniteresting.

    I just now read the SotC wiki rules on dogfights and I like them, and I'll track down a friends copy of Spycraft and see what it has to say on the subject.
