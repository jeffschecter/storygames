\n Ever been to wine tasting? They talk about things like "attack," "body" and "finish:" Three components of appreciation for a very complex and wordy analysis\u2014a fitting tool for game review?
\n
\nOn my blog I decided I'd like to talk about other people's games, partly to learn from them and partly to thank them. This is my ridiculously pompous method for scoring:
\n
\nAttack
\nIn a wine, that's the front flavours, the initial pop of sapidity as the wine spills over your tongue. So, for a game review, you'd be looking for a strong cover (impressive, stylish, cool), a strong interior (nice layout, sexy art) and strong writing (yummy words).
\n
\nBody
\nIn a wine, that's the texture of the liquid, the oiliness in your mouth. A full-bodied game will have innovation (lots of cleverness), inspiration (popping with colour) and excitement (I want to play now!).
\n
\nFinish
\nIn a wine, that's the balance of the flavour, how long it floats and soars in your mouth and throat, and how it changes, simplifies or swirls with complexity. A well-balanced game will have good clarity (digestible rules), good utility (reference, layout and cheat sheets) and good support (play advice and meta wisdom).
\n
\nIn the next few days, I'll be reviewing A Taste for Murder by Graham Walmsley with this method. Back soon!

    \nI forgot about play. Since the dissolution of my gaming group in spring, I can only read. So, for me, for now, game tasting was going to be a book review. However, there should be an additional factor for play.
    \n
    \nTo keep the metaphor, I'll add in a new heading, the "Nose," and shift the other roles around accordingly (highlighting the arbitrariness of the model):
    \n
    \nNose
    \nIn a wine, that's the warmth and complexity of the bouquet, the openness of the flavours and the mutability in their expression. So, for a game review, you'd be looking for a sharp cover (impressive, stylish, cool), a strong interior (nice layout, sexy art) and warm writing (yummy words).
    \n
    \nAttack
    \nIn a wine, that's the front flavours, the initial pop of sapidity as the wine spills over your tongue. A game with a strong attack will have lots of innovation (cleverness), inspiration (popping with colour) and excitement (I want to play now!)
    \n
    \nBody
    \nIn a wine, that's the texture of the liquid, the oiliness in your mouth. A full-bodied game will, during play, provide creative assistance, meaningful choices and a consistent experience.
    \n
    \nFinish
    \nIn a wine, that's the balance of the flavour, how long it floats and soars in your mouth and throat, and how it changes, simplifies or swirls with complexity. A well-balanced game will have good clarity (digestible rules), good utility (reference, layout and cheat sheets) and good support (play advice and meta wisdom).

    \n I see your point. The book, for you, is just an indicator of what's to come, like the smelling of the wine is just something you do before you drink. For me, drinking might be segregated into more equal components. The smelling is equal to the attack, and ever so slightly inferior to the body. The finish is what sells a wine for me, but it is only measurable in relation to the other components. I could drink a short nosed, thin textured, brash young red with a long, balanced finish, and yet still understand it's not an amazing wine. Or I could play a badly written, incomprehensible game text with terrible artwork, really enjoy the gaming experience, and yet still understand that it's not an amazing product. When I buy a game, I hope it will deliver a great gaming experience. Part of that comes from a great concept, but part of it comes from the colour, inspiration and innovation of the game text and design. I have to hit the tabletop enthused, and, unless I'm being taught in person, the game product has to do the enthusing.
    \n
    \nTangentially, breaking up the components of a game product is part of the review process. It allows me to compare one component of one game against another component of another game. These components will always be arbitrary. The trick is in finding a reviewer who has chosen to look at the components you are also interested in, and to find a reviewer who agrees with your taste. I've chosen to look at the game product\u2014the thing that arrives through the letterbox\u2014more than the game experience. For now.
    \n
    \nWhether or not you agree, of course, is moot. This is MY metaphor. Me me ME.
    \n
    \nI could have gone for "Game Boozing," for example, with a whole section on aftershave, late night boogie, and the odour of the puke on the side of the road.
