\n Something I've noticed is very common - there's no way for your fans and customers to contact you, or it's hard to reach you.

The PayPal link to buy your game on your site is broken, and there's no way to let you know.

They're reading your blog, but the comments don't work, so they can't give you feedback.

You've got a page for your rocking DitV variant where it says "let me know what you think"... but there's no e-mail address listed.

You say your e-mail address every week on your podcast... but it's not on your Web page.

You took down the forum/page for your ashcan because you're working on it, leaving no trace that it every existed. Is this project dead?

You've got a forum to discuss your game but there's no link on your home page and the only way to find it is for someone to send you the URL.

Your e-mail address is in your ashcan, but only the GM has a copy of the book, so the players can't find you.

So here's a little activity for you: check all the routes of access that people are likely to take to reach you. Make sure that there's a way for them to reach you no matter what entry point they're taking into your work.

    \n Ooooh, interesting excercise! ^_^

    I don't publish anything (yet), so it doesn't really matter, but I did look up the various ways to find stuff I've written and how they'd offer a way to get back to me. With virtually everything being in some forum or another, I looked up to see whether they all had up to date contact information and found that, surprise, surprise, in two of them the email-addresses were outdated. (They are still active but I don't check them very often.) Not really a problem as they aren't available to other users there anyway, but who knows when I suddenly forget what my password is...

    A positive surprise was that in every case, there were links to my forum and blog as well as other ways to reach me, like IMs or Private Messages. Also, both on my blog and at the forum there are ways to contact me, generally with email or, again, IMs.

    So, I think my online presence passed the test. Not that anybody ever contacts me, but it is good to know they they could. I guess. Although that means that nobody wants to contact me, which isn't all that nice a thought...

    \nNo, that's the downside of making your private space invadable. There's no way in *hell* I'd make my personal home number (or, damn, cell number!) available publicly; not in a million years (not that I'd necessarily be swamped, but I dream of future infamy!).

    Office or studio number? Sure, and I'd attach an answering machine. Public e-mail? Yep, but not my friends and family contact e-mail.

    But, hey... a web site is a POWERFUL mojo: through it, I can disseminate (and maintain) the only the contact methods that I want folks to use, I can direct most conversation to the forums, and I can easily communicate errata or upcoming projects to the fan base.

    Like we've said above; a web site is Step One, in this day and age. (Giving out your private phone number is Step Dumb.)
