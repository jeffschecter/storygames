\n I have long been working on my own wuxia SF project. I intend to use a reputation economy (after a fashion). However I need to balance this out so players don't end up earning too much or too little. The model is similar to what you see in games like Weapons of the GOds. Each character has a Virtue - Justice, Loyalty, Bravery, Tradition, or Kindness - with an associated score. When they abide by that virtue during play they earn rep equal to it's score. However I am concerned that certain virtues will be more playable than others - Justice for instance is likely to trigger more often due to being an action oriented milieu. Virtue scores will simply be increased with xp; the alternative would be for it to increase through virtuous behaviour but that conflicts with the earning of rep.

There must be a few readers familiar with those games/systems that know whether or not this might work and why not?
I never got the chance to run WotG. More than anything, this needs to be a simple system. Not an exercise in behavioural engineering number crunching or minmaxing.

Thanks.

    \n So rather a lot like WW dots. Roll enough successes on your pool, get what you want. Try for something beyond your means, you might get it and you might not but your pool will be reduced. Roll a botch, get punched. "Loose change" gets avoided there as each dot represents a significant amount of change.

    It sounds like you want to avoid even that much, because then you have to decide which favors are "4" and which are "5", where in EP you'd be deciding which are 40 and which are 50. Question! Are you planning on virtue/rep having any mechanical impact, so being kind improves your basket-weaving? Or is it exclusively channelled towards the gathering of favor?

    One simple hack might be: every character/faction has an associated Virtue. Merchant A embodies Kindness, merchant B embodies Bravery. If you gain merchant A's approval, you've just aided the cause of Kindness, and that benefits your rep and future interactions with other followers of Kindness. (Now I want to assign them each a kung fu style. Southern Crane finds your lack of faith disturbing!) The workaround is that it's not up to the player whether it was Kindness or Bravery to pull the thorn from the Martian's paw, it's down to whether merchant A is pleased - if they are, it goes to Kindness, full stop. The player has an immediate route towards improving a specific virtue; seek out NPC paragons of the virtue, do stuff for them, learn from their ways. The GM doesn't have to adjudicate anything, gets triangles between competing paragons, gets a spotlight on larger-than-life NPCs as quest givers and power players.

    Associated virtues is a good idea, however I think that operating in that way needs to be more subtle. Perhaps each vendor has an affinity to a given Virtue that rewards or benefits those looking to trade with it. But I think it might be better to have the goods and services on offer classified according to Virtue and have the hero use that virtue to acquire stuff. For example: if you want a new gun, you need Justice. If you want your starship repaired you need Tradition (for systems and tech), if you want medicine you need Loyalty.

    Perhaps the affinity works thus: if you want to buy a gun and you also want your Justice virtue a little higher then find a Justice merchant (obviously they are more likely to be arms traders than others, but not necessarily). He will then offer you a job or contract of some kind that, if fulfilled, allows you to earn something toward increasing your Justice score. Needless to say that task is affiliated in nature.

    I would be reluctant to have asystem where heroes can improve their virtues easily since it would be rather self defeating. Instead I would punish them for transgressing. Normally I wouldn't invoke penalising players as a tool of design, but here I think if the act is serious enough (after all they are meant to be heroes, it's not WoD) then they lose Virtue points. They don't get them for simply being heroes; if you're Virtue is Justice then pursuing it should be the norm. You ought not get rewards for carrying out the behaviour - except perhaps under extreme circumstances.

    \n In the past the Wulin ensured the stability of the kingdom.     \n I think White Wolf dots are going to be where it's at for you.     \n Hey, Ghostwhistler. I'm just going to apply a gentle touch here.
