The Golden Chain playtest

shreyas
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Actual Play

Thomas and Tom and I played the first few scenes of a game of The Golden Chain. Jonathan, who scooped me, watched and made some very insightful commentary.

It was pretty much awesome and ran exactly the way I expected it; we all created sort of exaggerated, poetic, Gilgamesh-like figures and festering monsters and yelled horrible things at each other and clashed in mythological conflicts. We kept them on a wiki.

It wasn't what I wanted, though, and I learned a lot very quickly from playing: the pacing was a bit off, the heroes reached the monsters too fast; the setup was glitteringly perfect; the rituals are in the wrong places; there isn't a sense of the world that is marred, only the monsters' pollution.

I'm mostly posting this to gloat because it was so good and where it broke, it was obvious and the solution was right there in reach.

(Also, I still need a new title for this game. Bah.)

    Shreyas, don't know if you've come across Booker's The Seven Basic Plots? One of his seven is "Defeating the Monster" and he makes the point that frequently there are three roles the monster takes (often in three encounters with the hero):

    * Predator (attacking and destroying)
    * Holdfast (defending its lair/hostages/treasure etc.)
    * Avenger (having been defeated, returning to take vengeance on the hero)

    I just toss it out there in case you want to use it.

    On a side note, I'm reading a few 1960s comics at the moment, and practically all of them have this plot.

    This is looking good, Shreyas. I can definitely imagine my own play of this game, which is a good sign.

    I like the overall structure, and how heroes cannot escape unscathed from their journeys. The writing style seems to facilitate reading aloud to the group to learn the game in the process of play. I think it's worth playing up this aspect of the text. I especially like the way reasons for game structures are explained in context: "Now we will do this, because it serves this purpose." Very nice.

    I know you probably don't want nitty-gritty critique at this point, but take this for what it's worth: Given the tone of the text, I think the game would flow very well if the "narrator" told the reader about the whole heroic journey at the beginning of the text, before Pillars are introduced. Something simple and easy, like, "Sarruk-whose-hammer-is-red must face three trials before he can purify the world and defeat the monster. In this game, we will create the three-part path of Sarruk-whose-hammer-is-red and discover the costs of his journey into and out of darkness."

    Any kind of general overview will help give some context to what follows. I know you know this, but I think I just needed to say it because the game has seized my mind so much. :-)
