\n One of the most appealing aspects of RPGs to me is the tension between the game element - which places demands on a player vaguely reminiscent of a boardgame - and the creation and consumption of a story.

A roleplaying game allows you to experience stories. It's common to consider your character as a kind of avatar, with which you inject yourself into the game world. That avatar is rarely a perfect transcription of your own personality. Some traits are emphasised, some softened and some even invented whole-cloth, because it might be fun to act like that for a while. It is after all one of the reasons people give when asked what it is that appeals to them about roleplaying. The character becomes a kind of second skin players put on, so they themselves can delve into the fictitious game world.

I think, it's just as legitimate to play an RPG without such close identification with the character or to not use close identification as the basis for the experience. In fact, such an approach is not any better or worse than the one which ties the character's personality and experiences close to that of the player. The two are perfectly compatible, actually. Most groups have dabbled in both at some point, anyway. Pretty much every decision made in the interest of the entire group or in regards to the logic of the events so far, is doing just that. It's switiching between "what would I do?" (sitting at a table chatting with friends) with "what would the character do?" (who might be in the middle of some questionable acts in pursuit of a greater goal of some kind).

From there it's no great leap (and by no means a necessary one either) to look at the characters and the events they are involved in as a story. Some enjoy this story as a nice but non-essential byproduct of playing the game. Others choose to divert their attention from handling the rules (in the sense of engaging the game aspect of the RPG) to creating an involving
story for everybody at the table. In most cases though groups move around in the area in-between the two, doing a
little bit of this and a little bit of that.

It's been my experience that every group that wants to pursue this story-focus in their gaming will eventually come into
conflict with some of rules in the gamebook. Using houserules is one way of adapting the results of certain rules to one's
personal preference. This way the group can finetune the game to be more cinematic/realistic/gritty/etc. But there are also
many who object so heavily to the results such rules lead to, that they attempt to use them as rarely as possible preferring to use other means to figure out what happens next. Most people opt to get involved in the nebulous but widely admired art of "just roleplaying it".

I have some difficulties with this, mostly because so much can be labelled as "just roleplaying it". It might be group consensus based on the engagement with the story. It might just as well be arbitrary decision-making masquerading as 'plausibility' or 'realism'. The latter is something that bothers me a little. There is an approach to "just roleplaying it" (often simply meaning that you directly affect the gameworld without employing some kind of abstracted mechanism) which I do find quite appealing. One which is tied to a very specific view of a game's rules and how they are to be invoked at the game
table.

In most cases rules are thought of as a means to determine whether the things that a player wants to happen in the game-world actually occur. You can think of rules as a sieve which is used to sift through the various things players suggest their characters do and only letting a select few of them actually happen in the game-world. The GM will usually determine when and how the rules will be invoked and is often responsible for translating the results into actions in the game-world. If you approach rules this way, "just roleplaying it" is a substitute for this sieve made of rules. You first determine what is to be "roleplayed", then characters will interact, which is then evaluated by one or more players at the table before being accepted as part of the game-world. "Roleplayed" actions are established in the game-world after successfully arguing for them. (I use quotation marks only to emphasise this specific meaning of roleplaying in my post.)

A different approach, which I consider far more compatible with a focus on the story side of an RPG, is one where the rules
are instead used as a palette players draw from to shape the story. "Just roleplaying it" is no longer a substitute or an alternative to using the rules, but instead the central activity around which everything revolves. Additionally you don't need to argue for or otherwise convince others of whatever is roleplayed this way. As long as you can count on the group sharing your appreciation of the story, you can roleplay anything you see fit. Rules here are instead used to introduce a random element into the proceedings and thus make the story more dynamic and unpredictable. Some ways of doing this is by swapping the role of Narrator around or putting constraints of theme or content on the roleplaying person.

Here it often becomes necessary for one or more players to be skilled in handling the story's momentum. Some games give you formal characteristics to help you recognize when the story needs a new impulse (cf. Primetime Adventures' scene conflicts) others leave it in the hands of the GM (e.g. InSpectres and stress rolls). When to invoke the rules or 'system' in such a case is often a very hazy issue. Since it has a lot to do with both picking up on the general mood at the table as well as having an eye for ...dramaturgy, I guess, it's not easy to help new or less experienced players hit their stride. Because it's not about codifying or laying down the definitive principles of good story-gaming, but about helping people find and develop their own style, which still has to be adaptable enough to work in different games.

Managing the momentum of a thrilling action show in PTA, does not work the same way as getting the most out of the tragedy in a Polaris game, the black humour in Shab-Al-Hiri Roach or maybe the rom-com giddyness in Breaking the Ice. I think there is a great range of helpful advice for story games waiting to be put in words. I just have no idea how.

Comments? Ideas? Threats?

    Whether it's called dramaturgy, techniques, or story-skills or whatever, there are these "gaps" in getting the game going that a game's rules can't handle.

    Sometimes the game is narrow, as the rules add in lots of details to carry the group through many of these decisions. Other times the gap is very wide. Primetime Adventures, for example, has nothing but story-techniques for minutes on end, and then boom! -- play cards.... and then you're thrown back on nothing but story-techniques for another minute or two. Note this doesn't mean that the players are just making stuff up out of any ol' thin air. The game's rules have provided a structure: the show's premise and the Issue of each character, for example, are there for the Players to use and focus on. By focusing on them, certain ideas will be generated that are still varied in their possibility, but will carry a certain creative weight so that they are "right in a way that wouldn't be possible if the Player narrating an outcome simply ignores them.

    \nI agree. As a side-project, I'm currently preparing a booked called, "Narrative Techniques for Games" which, in my dreams, discusses a host of "dramaturgy" techniques -- if you will -- to be used at the game table. It will be a collection of techniques collected from games and the internet, as well as techniques gathered from improv, dramaturgy, screenwriting, and the writing of fiction.     \nHi Georgios,

    I agree that people who make those arguments are out there.

    I believe I can write the techniques correctly to shift the person's focus from one set of things they are used to focusing on to a different set of things that will produce the story play.

    The trick is not to front-load an argument before play to convince them that it's going to work. That's an intellectualized debate about abstractions that you'll never win. The trick is to say, "OK, here's your Primetime Adventures character sheet. The first thing is we need to come up with a concept for a show..." and go from there. You focus on that concept for the show. After that is done yous say, "You need a concept for the characters." Then once that is done, you say, "Okay, every character needs an Issue..." and so on.

    Each of these is "soft" techniques when compared to "Roll a d20 and get under your strength." But they are ALL specific concrete techniques used in the creative act of making narrative in any form. (Coffee and lattes are optional.)

    By pointing the person's brain at one of these "Focus" points after another, you move forward. You're not trying to convince anyone it works, you're just doing it. In scene work, if things start to get muddled, the Producer or a player says, "Hey wait, what's the focus of the show? What's the character's Issue? Let's make sure to drive toward that, let's focus on that." That's the dramaturgy you mentioned above. And no one is getting confused along the way about whether it's going to work or not, because they're brain is occupied with focusing on the matter of the Show's Concept, the Character's Concept, the Character's Issue or whatever.

    Now, you might get someone at the table who wants to fight, I suppose. You might say, "Okay, first we come up with the show's concept."

    And someone might balk, slamming his hand on the table, standing fiercely, knocking his chair out from behind him and declare, "You mean you don't even know what you're RUNNING!?!"

    A couple of points about that:

    1) I would say, "This is how we play this game. You can sit down and join us in coming up with the concept, or you can leave. And I'd hate for you to leave. But this is how we play this game."

    2) My guess is, if you don't explain what's going to happen, and instead focus on what's happening right now moment by moment ("Now do this, now do this, now do this..."), they'll be caught up in making up the fun stuff before they even know that they should have been confused and contrary till after it's too late. So, don't give them the chance to gear up. Just start turning everyone's brain on one specific creative act after another.

    3) Short of that, you'll never CONVINCE someone who doesn't want to take the risk or want to do it. That's just the truth. If they signed up for the wrong game, they signed up for the wrong game.

    4) I've also really been lucky, I supposed. At Gateway last weekend I was playing all sorts of games that literally bear no resemblance to old school texts. No one squeaked. There was confusion, of course. Rules for PtA and Shock might throw people for a loop. But everyone arrived at the table to play the kinds of games that were delivered. There was a lot of self-selection going on. So, I'd love to hear more about the circumstances of the con, the promotion of the game, and the game itself.

    Thanks,

    \n Ah. Germany.

    That explains a lot from what I've read on several boards about what German gamers in general seem to think is meant by "story" in games -- and why you might have trouble. "Story games are all about not using rules, hand waving away any logic, the GM having a pre-built story that bores us, or all of us wandering around 'in character' until a story, supposedly, 'just happens'," is what I read all the time. I can see why that would be complicated!

    So, here's what I would recommend. Don't say anything about story-focused or story-telling or anything like that. The story will reveal itself in a PtA game or not. Your only job is to walk them through the steps of Show Creation, Character Creation and Scene Creation.

    I can only stress from my experience at Gateway: make sure to get the Issues nailed down well. Make the Issue that is something trips the character up from getting what they want, it makes them stumble. It's not a motivation. It's a behavior. In a game I ran last Sunday, one character's Issue was Pride, and the other the and the Issue Myopic [about Engineering].

    Then, when you get to scenes, drive toward the Issue. Throw a conflict at a character that puts the issue on the line. With Pride, I constantly had other figures of authority arrive and see if the character would blow his stack or stand down. With the Myopic character, he had a girlfriend, and I kept having her show up and test whether he'd focus on her or his work.

    Conflict to me in PtA is beginning to mean, "Putting the Issue on the Line."

    It sounds to me like you've had a chance to think all this through in a new way. I bet you have a great time. Please come back and tell me how it worked out.

    CK

    PS remember, people might be scratching their heads afterwards... because not every game is for every person. But at least they have done it and be able to react to what they've done, rather than reacting to the anticipation of what they THINK they'll be doing.
