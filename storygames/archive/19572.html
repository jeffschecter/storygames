\n I played Ben Robbins' new playtest game, Microscope Union, on Wednesday. Normally, when my group runs a Microscope, I'll write the play report in the style of someone going through fragmented historical documents, maybe even having translation issues, or otherwise merely telling what history is known, accepting the blank patches. I noticed that Union seems a little harder to write up, and we didn't fill out all the cards (we only had time for two lenses), but I figured I might share it anyway, because it's new.



An expedition to the peak of the tallest mountain, to slay the dragon who was the leader of a cult. Only one survived. His name was Max Pepsi.

He would not have been able to accomplish his heroic deed, had he not great resistance to the elements, such as the cold and low oxygen, a loose sense of ethics, that he might consume fallen expedition members, and great puzzle-solving skills, put to use when he tricked the dragon into causing their own demise.

But do heroes simply emerge like that? Where did he come from?

We know little of his parents. He claims his father was an apprentice chef called Diet Pepsi. He worked for a Lord or Baron or someone equally wealthy, but who has since lost his former land, money, and significance. It is too hard to verify with the limited documents on the subject. Max further claims his mother is none other than Princess Fanta, who was slated to marry said lord, but then disappeared from the records. Max claims it has to do with her secret affair with Diet, but we have no way of knowing.

On his Father, Diet Pepsi's side, we have Daniel Pepsi and San Pelegrina. Daniel was a half-dragon bodyguard; son to Midori Splice, who was famous in her time for stringing along human males with a... certain fetish, sleeping with them, and then disappearing. It may be worth mentioning at this point that dragons are about human-sized, and have similar intelligence. As a half-dragon, he was often outcast, but was very strong. He became a bodyguard more out of social necessity rather than choice. San, on the other hand, was the daughter of the royal cook. With a strong desire to follow in her father's footsteps, she disguised herself as a male in order to to become a cook herself. A kind lord who admired her resolve hired Daniel to protect her, and a bond slowly formed between Daniel and San. However, some time after the birth of Diet, San was discovered as female at a banquet by two guests: a foreign noble, whom she offended, and a dragon called Jack Daniel, who could smell her. She was subsequently executed, and Daniel went into depression and disappeared. With no family left, Diet was cared for by the head chef.

On Princess Fanta's side, we know that her own mother was Cherry Cola, born into royalty, and likely married to royalty, too. A product of her wealthy and powerful surroundings, it may come as a surprise to learn that Cherry's mother was part of a small tribe of natives to a distant part of the world. Even more surprising, that tribe was, in fact, part of the very cult to the dragon that Max would later slay! Her name was Jinx Lemon o'Bitter. It is clear through historical documents that she was kidnapped by the wealthy noble; Coke A'Cola. He mostly occupied his time in travelling, archaeology, and, while home, teaching. On one of his expeditions, he found an ancient tomb which contained a riddle that pointed him towards the tribe that Jinx was a part of. If Max got his puzzle-solving skills from anywhere, it was from A'Cola. After giving birth to Cherry, Jinx is strongly suspected of having killed her kidnapper, sacrificed to her own dragon cult. She maintained A'Cola's wealth and political power for herself, however, and Cherry's pampered future was secured.

We don't know who Cherry's partner was, beyond a brief mention that his mother was Ice Teresa, a woman who lived in the mountains selling ice. It is likely she came from a long line of mountain-dwelling folk who thrived in the cold and lower oxygen of the mountains. Again, Max would have her blood in his veins.

Unfortunately, if there's any further evidence of Max's ancestry, if he was even telling the truth, it's lost to the mists of time.

    \n It's been nearly a week now, and I don't have the cards any more, but I'll give it a shot.

    In the first pass, Diet Pepsi and Princess Fanta, direct parents of Max Pepsi, were created, but we never got to fill in their union or fate. We also filled in Coke A'Cola, the first Great-Grandparent, and Daniel Pepsi, the first Grandparent. Our first focus was one A'Cola's card. Immediately, we turned him into a kidnapper and an entitled rich arsehole. Interestingly, with four players, we were basically forced to have at least one scene, which covered how Cherry Cola learned about her Dad. I didn't include that in the above story, because we got confused about a vitally important detail to the question, and the scene fell apart.

    It might be worth pointing out that I've never introduced the conflict-resolution mechanics to my Microscope players, because we seem to have done fine without. I should have introduced it here, but I forgot they existed. My bad. <_<

    The first legacy introduced Ice Teresa, but we never came back to her. Then the next lens chose Daniel's card. That was great, because that card made the previously established apprenticeship of Diet's, which was light, have a much sadder history than we previously expected. We also finally worked out that dragons were human-sized, which allowed them to be involved in political affairs indoors, such as the banquet in which San was discovered. Finally, the legacy became Midori Splice, and we ended the game. That took three hours. Yeah, we're pretty slow.

    We did generally find it restrictive to be confined to one card and its progeny, and, on the other hand, choosing a card for the focus was daunting and pretty arbitrary. We just chose cards that already had stuff. Then we had the challenge of connecting them. We had parents who we never found out the connection of, Great-Grandparents that we suddenly had to connect to royalty, in all it was pretty challenging. The wonderful connection that just happened to work to make Diet's story much more sad was great, but that was a rare moment. Mostly, it was bending and twisting to make the connection make sense. In the rules, it says that those above in the tree don't have to be blood-related, but in practise we found that almost impossible to actually have happen.

    Interestingly, the limitations of trying to find links between parents, or between them and their child, put me in mind of how, in Microscope, you can't rely on any two periods or events being right next to each other for the whole game. This seems the exact opposite of that. Just an observation.

    At the end, one of the players said that it really did feel like a game in the alpha stages. Still, we had a lot of fun, and to be fair, some of the people I play with are the kinds who try to break games. I expect the next game we play will be even harder to make sense of, just because we have an idea of what's going on now, and can push it further. I guess, where, in Microscope, you have a lot of power to just add stuff until a connection makes sense, whenever you want, here you have limited space and opportunity, and you have to think of the connection <i>now.

    \n We were talking about this in the other playtest thread, but it's perfectly valid (and actually really useful) to discuss things like tone. You can just say "hey, I don't want to play a silly game" (or I don't want to play a serious game, or whatever).

    Even if everyone has different preferences, I think it's better to try to compromise and agree on one tone for the night than to have the game go in a bunch of different directions. You can trade off and play a different tone next time.

    Push can help, but that's not really what it's for (and it will only work during scenes, and only for some things). Agreeing on the tone of the game (silly/serious/etc) is a higher level discussion.
