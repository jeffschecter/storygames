\n War. What is it good for? Lots of stuff, particularly when it comes to roleplaying games. I'll venture every one of us has participated in some fictional war or another during the course of our fantasy- and SF-gaming careers, but I'm curious more about the historical wars that you've fought.

Even though wars interest me as an historian, given that they're such fertile ground for sudden socio-political change, I haven't done a lot of gaming set during these periods. I gather that WWII is about as popular as historical wars get in terms of roleplaying love, but I've never actually run or played during that war. WWI has always been just too damned depressing to game. I've often thought something set during the French and Indian War would be interesting, but I haven't pursued the idea with much verve. You'd also think the American Civil War would be a great background for a game, but I've never heard of anyone even trying such a thing.

In fact the only historical war I've gamed in was the Vietnam War, a gaming experience notable also for the diceless approach we took to much of the proceedings. We weren't doing proto-carry gaming, either, but something more akin to the movie, The Green Berets. John Wayne rules, Commies are bad, let's commence to the firefighting.

I suppose if you wanted to get very technical I also did some gaming in wartime Rhodesia using FGU's Merc system, but that was more like white-adventurer fantasy than anything resembling historicity. The Dogs of War and The Wild Geese done tabletop.

What have you done? What have you wanted to do? If you haven't gotten around to gaming in a particular war, why not? And what drew you to the war(s) you have played?

    \n Thanks to the recent (ish) release of the Duty & Honour and Beat to Quarters RPGs, I've been playing in the Peninsular War. It's an easy enough war setting to hook into, thanks to the Sharpe novels and telly series.

    I'm also running con game scenarios set in the Anglo-Zulu war of 1879, using the Duty & Honour system. That's also a war-setting that's got easy enough hooks to draw on thanks to the classic film "ZULU". I'm also South African and we got fed a lot of "The British Are Bastards" at school, so there's the draw of playing out the Anglo-Boer conflicts in later scenarios too.

    What's drawn me to those wars has been a flavourful story-game, easy hooks in terms of getting folks on the same page about a period of history, and an interest in the historical events of my mother country.

    I'd like to play games set during WWII. I think the only thing really stopping that is a) finding enough free time to do so; and b) when I do find free time to game, there's a richness of games in other settings to play too, so while WWII games are in the queue, they're well behind Kagematsu, Mouse Guard, Medical Hospital, WFRP 3rd, and Burning Empires... and that's just my list. My mates have their own list of games and settings to work through too

    Eventually I'll run miniatures game about the wars in the Middle East of the last 30 years but I've not quite got the figures collected yet. I'll probably disguise it by placing it on an alien planet but essentially it will be asymetrical war so I have lots of civilian figures.

    With straight out Matrix Games weve done lots of wargames and spy games. They get at the story behind the shooting which I like. Relationships are more important than firepower.

    \n I've always wanted to run a game focusing on the actions of a small unit of soldiers caught up in a larger war -- possibly WWII or Vietnam, but the Roman invasion of Britain also appeals to me. I've never been able to sell that idea to enough players to make it work.

    Even adding in 'kewl stuff' doesn't seem to attract anyone in either of my gaming groups. The Savage Worlds 'Weird War' setting look like fun to me, but not to my friends.

    Outside of historical truth, I've always wanted to run or play in a game inspired by VOTOMS and the early Gundam series, set amidst a quasi-hard-science mecha war -- same deal. Additional resistance to 'giant robots' from the mostly non-fans of anime in my groups may be a contributing factor, here.

    What wars have I RPGed in or with?

    As pointed out upthread, Sons of Liberty counts, being set during a war. I've played it and loved it. It's a blast every time, but, as also pointed out upthread, it's not exactly hard history.

    I've run a pulp game set during the wind-up to WWII, Indiana Jones style. High-spirited Nazi-bashing is always fun. Not hard history either.

    I'm involved in a Universalis game that is set (at least initially) during WWI. However, it doesn't address many of the issues or themes of that war, since the story we're telling is one of supernatural horror investigations. The war is just the grim backdrop for even grimmer events. If it weren't being played with Universalis, it would be easiest to do that game in Trail of Cthulhu...

    I'm detecting a theme in my RPG warfare: the people I game with don't seem to like playing soldiers. Maybe it's because being part of an army, with a command structure, feels too restrictive of their freedom to do as they please with their characters -- despite the fact that several of them are perfectly happy in games where some 'Mr. Johnson' gives them a mission at the start of every session.

    DainXB

    \n I have been working on a setting concept for bySwarm called Mist & Shadow that is set in an alternative history version of WW1. The themes of mist and shadow come into play via a conspiracy organization that is trying to destroy Europe in order to build a new world order from the ashes and that organization is being battled in a secret war with a more fantasy-styled organization that utilizes mystical and mutation-causing chemical weapons. The main thing that attracted me to WW1 was that it really represented the end of the old empires being run by nobility. This means that you can bring in some of the traditional elements of fantasy that come from feudal Europe but also mix a lot of different cultures in one setting. WW1 also interested me because I don't feel like it is a historical period that gets a lot of attention from a gaming context - WW2, Vietnam, the American Civil War, and the American Revolutionary War get a lot more attention.

    \nWhat I've Done: I'm currently playing a Burning Wheel campaign in the Viking Invasion period of Ireland. It may be the first historical roleplaying I've ever done, actually. It's kind of an edge case because it takes place at a time when the "war" is sort of over and the Vikings are all settling in and founding cities and become enculturated...but my young Irish fighter doesn't WANT it to be ove rand is doing his damnedest to set the whole works ablaze.

    What I've Wanted to Do: Play one of those cool focused historical war games like Steal Away Jordan or Carry.

    Why this War: I love Irish history and I like Norse history and I'm into Medieval reenactment and when we were kicking Burning campaign ideas around that one bubbled up and I went YES! I'm fascinated with the Irish underdog status, the passionate yet ironic Irish spirit, and in this portrait of ancient Ireland as this idyllic realm where hospitality flowed and everyone was a King and wars and love were great sport both. It's been interesting to see that idyll meet with (imagined) reality.

    \n I've done a lot of war/military themed roleplaying. WW2 is a huge interest for me, and I feel quite comfortable working with various themes, time periods, theaters, operations, etc. I also do not fear implementing a chain-of-command among the characters - I've gotten this to work many times - or having the characters just be grunts or other sorts of rankers and not commandos or special forces or some other type of unit operating outside of the box. Grunts on the line can tell powerful stories.

    What I've done

    WW2

    Godlike - Recently finished this campaign that took a Talent squad from the beaches of Normandy to the Liberation of Paris. Riffing heavily on Band of Brothers and nailing that vibe more than a few times. Perhaps of interest, we used a full minis table and Flames of War minis more than once (pics in post #72) but no minis rules - just pushed the minis around to show relative positions. I've done several Godlike oneshots (and I have another in the works).

    Weird Wars - While writing this Savage Worlds setting book, I did a lot of playtesting that involved various scenarios - a tank battle at Kursk, dogfighting in the Battle of Britain, PT boats in the Solomon Islands, and G.I.'s in the Ardennes.

    Grey Ranks - Only had playtest sessions with this. Really need to get a game going with the final version.

    WW1

    Call of Cthulhu - I ran No Man's Land years ago, but don't remember it so well. I'd like to try a focused game about the trenches (gloom).

    Vietnam

    Carry - This is another really good game I need to run more.

    Call of Cthulhu - Did a Vietnam oneshot with a bit of Delta Green crossover.

    Cold War

    Merc - An old game from FGU. Played this a lot in high school, running games involving various brushfire wars in Africa and Central America.

    Sci-Fi

    2300AD - I pulled out this old GDW sci-fi game and ran a French Foreign Legion campaign with it. The tone we set with this one was a preclude to what I did with the Godlike campaign above.

    What I'd like to do

    Having run a Godlike campaign set in Europe, I'd like to do one set in the Pacific, specifically the China-Burma-India theater, with Stillwell's troops out in the steaming jungle somewhere. That or island hopping U.S. Marines. WW2 has so many aspects to it and so many potential stories you can tell, and there's a tremendous amount of source material to draw on.

    I'd also really like to do a Das Boot in Space type of game (like A Passage At Arms) with 2300AD. Why? I think this situation is ripe for drama and since it seems like such a challenge to run, I want to give it a go. Also in the sci-fi vein, I'd love to do a Hammers Slammers game (but not with MGT)

    Very interested in Duty & Honor and Black Cadillacs.

    \n We played a game about the Gaza War roughly a year ago with Shell Shock. There's an AP thread on Story Games about our game. I think it is interesting to reflect why we did it. These reasons come to mind:\n
      \n
    • It was a current on-going conflict and we wanted to process the imagery and information fed to us through media.
      • \n
    • We were interested on ultramodern complex (sub)urban conflicts, what they are like to the participants as combatants and as human beings
      • \n
    • I was interested in trying out Shell Shock (and I'm glad I did)
      • \n
    • The Israeli soldiers seemed like a good identification point. On some level it seems that there are a lot in common with young (well youngish on the part of most of the players) men in Israel and in Finland.
      • \n
    • I think that we were interested in a setup where there were no easy decisions, and each decision would end up shaping the image of the characters as moral agents. We certainly got that out of the game.
      • \n
    \nThose are just some things I think interested us in the setup. How much our game represents the actual conflict is anybody's guess. As I said before I think the game portrayed our different perceptions and even conscious explorations around the theme of the real-world conflict more than the conflict itself.
