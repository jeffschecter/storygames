\n
\n I'm reading The Ungodly, which is a heart-warming tale of hubris and anthropophagy among the Donner party, and it got me thinking about how you could physically represent deterioration and collapse in play. So you're playing THE DONNER PARTY RPG, and you've got a sheet of paper that represents a family. It's got 16 squares. You get to fill it out and put whatever you like on it, one thing per square, or maybe there are rules about what goes on it, or a pre-determined list. Definitely people each get a square. Maybe concepts or situations or objects, too. And in play, you have to make sacrifices. Things have to be left behind, abandoned, or destroyed. And when you get to one of these junctures, you have to tear off a square. The rule is - two tears. You can only make two tears, so the corners (A) go first, then the edges (B), and finally the most precious stuff in the middle (C). If you are actually playing a family, your "character" survives as long as a single family member survives. Like so:

The appealing thing to me is that you are physically ruining an object in play, which ought to reflect the deterioration of the things that are important to you. Seemed like a neat idea - any precedents? Can you think of any fun uses?

    \n Ok, we tried this at lunch and it works fine. We used half-letter sized sheets and each rip was about an inch tops. Precision is not an issue at that scale. One variation we tried was a five by five grid, with the requirement that one item take up four spaces, one take up three, and two take up two. These are things that can be damaged or injured before being lost. That worked pretty well, and had the added benefit of a single, central space. Steve put "my soul" there.

    It takes about nine "turns" before a sheet is completely ruined and everything is destroyed on the 16-space grid. We randomly varied the number of rips per turn between one and four. Four hurts, bad. You are pretty much losing something on one of those.

    So the two principal questions are what causes a rip - what are conflict junctures - and what should the individual spaces represent. For my thought-game, the junctures are pre-defined events ending in rescue and relief, and the spaces would have to represent intangibles like hope, faith, and pride as well as actual living things. Originally I thought stuff like wagons and oxen and rifles would be included, but that's a resource-management rabbit hole.
