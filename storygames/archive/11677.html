    \n I haven't watched the link yet. I will soon and comment.

    But I wanted to riff on what Teataine's saying above.

    A common idea when introducing roleplaying to new players is to sell them on the idea that they can do anything. I agree overwhelming them with rules they don't need to know yet is a bad idea (see content). But the other extreme, "do anything" does not work in my experience.

    I've run around 8+ games in the last 2 weeks (not a common thing!), many with people completely new to tabletop roleplaying (from actors to garbage men to electricians to teachers to architects to former LARPers and war gamers), and before assuming what the best way to engage them is I will prequalify a bit and in every single case leading with constraints won every time. People preferred to play pre-generated characters. And preferred to play games with clearly defined situations and goals.

    \n Well, goal-setting theory (GST) has pretty consistently shown that self-selection of a goal does not really matter for the goal to be effective or for the individual to see value in the goal. What matters is that the goal is accepted and internalized. So, you can tell a player to pick a goal for her character and she picks something without really thinking and doesn't really understand or care. But, you can also say, "Hey, in this game your character really wants to steal the Crown Jewels from that guy's character." So long as she says, "Cool! I want to do that." That's all that matters.

    \n Good point, Brenton. Here's how I'd apply that to my experience:

    When handed a goal, I often accept it with a shrug. "Okay, this will allow play to proceed, sure."

    When picking from a few goals, I accept my chosen goal with more enthusiasm. "These will all allow play to proceed, but I like this one most."

    When creatively defining my own goal, I accept the goal with maximum enthusiasm. "Success will satisfy my unique creative vision for where the fiction should go!"

    As for the video, I just finished watching it. The first half doesn't have much substance beyond two points:
    1) faced with too many options, people often avoid making any decision at all
    2) faced with too many options, people often use the quickest/easiest basis for choosing, not the best basis for choosing

    But starting at 28:47 it gets into some more interesting choice psychology. I enjoyed it from that point on. (Also: 29:53 begins a discussion game designers might enjoy, about product capability vs usability.)

    \n I haven't actually taken the time to watch the video, I'll be honest, but I have read a fair amount of Schwartz's work in Judgment and Decision Making (JDM) research. He and other researchers have isolated some really cool effects of choice structures on people's eventual decisions.
    \n
    \n1) Like David points out, when there are too many options without a clear winner--when you have several (even just two) options that are very similar in perceived value--you tend to delay making a decision. This basically comes down to fear of making the wrong choice. You want to wait and gather more information, either about the present options (some new aspect that you hadn't considered before that shows you the "winner") or about a different, unforseen option (you hope something better will come along than any of the present options).
    \n
    \nIn gaming, imagine a player is not too familiar with the rules and is in an intense combat. The other players are impressing upon her how dangerous and exciting the combat is, so she feels like she needs to make the right choice of maneuver. But, all of the maneuvers look pretty similar to her. She doesn't know the game well enough to pick the obvious best choice, so she sits there anxious because she doesn't want to fail, waiting for another player to decide for her.
    \n
    \nTyler's exactly right, I think, when he evaluates most board games and point buy games. Most board games offer not only a small set of choices, they also make it clear what the advantages and disadvantages of each choice are. They usually avoid making choices appear to be equal. Lots of point buy games strive for "balance," wherein each possible expenditure of a point is equal to all other possibilities. While this is great for a robust, fair game, it is horrible for encouraging decisions amongst players. With so many choices that all appear to be equal to each other, it becomes very very hard to make any decision at all.
    \n
    \n2) Decades of research on JDM has shown that the idea of the "economic man"--the concept that people make the best possible decision most of the time--is very wrong. People deviate from this ideal in many ways. The first, David points out, is that people tend to pick the quickest or easiest choice. They pick the one that requires the least cost and gets them the rewards fastest.
    \n
    \nAnother example with our unfamiliar player. Given all of these options, she is confused, so the game master starts to explain each maneuver to her. She's still confused. There are a lot of details in each maneuver, and she doesn't really know what they mean. In this situation, let's say she's surrounded, so it might be the best choice for her to take a block action, to avoid being dropped. But the block maneuver is confusing, involving weird dice allocation and other complicated stuff she doesn't understand. So, instead, she takes a Strike action, which simply involves her rolling all of her attack dice against one opponent. Let's imagine this isn't a very good tactic. She's not doing it because she wants to fail or isn't trying to win; she just wants to make a decision and be done with it. She wants the reward of successfully completing her turn as fast as possible.
    \n
    \n3) A cool one. People tend to evaluate options sequentially, rather than simultaneously. They tend to examine most of the implications of one option before looking at the next at all. In doing so, another deviation from perfect choices appears--people tend to settle for the first "good enough" option. As soon as a choice appears that the person feels satisfactorily meets his goals, he'll take it, even if a different choice he hasn't evaluated yet would have been better.
    \n
    \nSo, same situation. As the game master is explaining the choices, he explains the simple strike maneuver. The play stops him right there and says she'll do that one. She doesn't want to hear the rest because that one seems good enough.
    \n
    \nThere are a lot more, but I don't want to bore anyone going on and on.
