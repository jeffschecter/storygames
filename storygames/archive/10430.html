\n So, tomorrow evening will mark the beginning of my House of the Blooded campaign. My players and I are all going to get together and run through character creation, as I explain the setting and rules to them. However, I have a few concerns...
\n
\nGiven that there is so much setting in there, what do you think the best way is to present this to players? How much information should I frontload them with? This seems like the sort of game that would run better with very informed players.
\n
\nI sent them the link to the preview section of the book, and I was thinking about making a cheat sheet with ven terms, laws, and customs on it. Are there any good resources like these online already?
\n
\nI was also thinking about introducing the different parts of the game in a modular way, similar to what I used to do with Burning Wheel. My idea was to have them make their characters tomorrow, but wait to do things like Devotions and Season stuff. I figured that, once everyone gets the core mechanics under their belts, and a feel for the setting, we can slowly inject more and more of the system each session. It seems like this would work pretty well for HotB.
\n
\nWhat do you all think? Any other advice or experiences with HotB that I should be aware of?

    Posted By: Charlie GilbGiven that there is so much setting in there, what do you think the best way is to present this to players? How much information should I frontload them with? This seems like the sort of game that would run better with very informed players.
    You may be surprised. Vast swaths of the "setting" material are basically worked examples of basic principles. The setting material in the book isn't so much "what is true" but examples of how you can make true things about the setting. I would make this clear to your players: the book isn't a checklist of things they can get wrong; it's a manual for doing things right their way.

    You could probably run just fine explaining just a handful of principles:
    1. The ven are good at everything... except restraint.
    2. Ven society has been back-stabby and power-grabby for centuries... and romantic love was just invented.
    3. The most important thing in the world is Style.

    Remember, wisdom/cunning/prowess risks allow the players to flesh out the world to a powerful extent. Follow that lead, rather than canon setting material from the book, and you'll be fine.

    I wouldn't start play without creating Domains \u2014 they're powerful context for nobles, and having that in the back of their minds will help them overcome dungeoncrawl adventurer thinking. Skipping devotions at first may work unless one of your players wants to make that his thing, so you may want to check first.
