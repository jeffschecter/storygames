\n So one of the things I'm doing for the text of Blowback itself is creating small protips for the margins. See, one of the things I like best about Burn Notice and some other spy-based media is that it explains to you how to do some crazy stuff. For example, wasp repellent makes an excellent alternative to pepper spray, because you don't need a concealed weapon permit to carry it and you can keep it around unobtrusively without people noticing. Or the problem with using pretty women as a diversion is that people want pretty women to stick around, which is why loud, annoying guys are a better bet. That kind of thing. So I want to include tips like this in order to help you, dear reader, more accurately portray spies (and also just to share cool stuff with you).

This is where you come in. What weird/crazy spy-related stuff would you like to know? I'll do the research, find out, and include it in the book. For more involved questions, if you come up with something really cool, I might put a brief appendix in the back of the book if it doesn't fit as a protip. Want to know how to bug a cell phone but too lazy to find out yourself? Ask me! Curious how to beat a lie detector? Etc.

Help me help you!

    \n Interesting synchronicity. I'm just now reading Spook Country, which has a decidedly modern take on how spying gets done. Some off the top of my head:
    * "Kidnapping" someone to help you spy, keeping the person "cooperative" by claiming to be a federal agent and/or getting the person addicted to a drug only you can easily provide.
    * Drops, couriers, mules - the care and feeding of sources.
    * Sterilization of a residence or hotel room to prevent DNA forensics discovery of your presence.
    * Transferring information discretely, e.g. on an iPod, bit-encoded into an image file (or actual image), botnetting someone's unsecured PC.
    * Impromptu weapons and munitions--zip guns, light bulb bombs (i.e. a LOT of The Anarchist's Cookbook).
    * Texting in non-Roman alphabets (e.g. some Russians text in a "dialect" that looks like L33tspeak, because the Cyrillic is composed of Roman letters and numerals and so-forth).
    * Corporate flavors of espionage (probably more common today than nationalistic agencies, on a per capita basis).
    * Local currency acquisition and laundering and transport cross-border.

    \n John: what you hit on is precisely the crux of the game. All of the spy stuff is super easy with the appropriate planning, because that's the stuff that spies spent their lives doing and because it's stupid to play a spy game where you start off sucking at being a spy. The interesting thing is trying to be a good lover/son/friend to the people around you. On the one hand, your resources are limited since you're blacklisted, so the people who care about you are vitally important. On the other hand, the closer you are to someone, the more of a liability they are. Here, I'll give you a sneak peek.

    There are three phases in every job: the Analysis phase, the Mission phase, and the Blowback phase. Analysis is planning and preparation, Mission is doing the job itself, and Blowback is.. well, the technical definition is "unexpected negative consequences of spying activity." It's the personal, post-Mission stuff where you're dealing with relationships and fallout from stress. Each of the four skills you have behave in different ways in each phase of the game. Here's an example of one of the skills.\n
    Pavement covers everything related to hitting the street; pavement artists know knowledge is not just power, it\u2019s the best kind of power.
    *During Analysis scenes, Pavement Artists prove to be great at reconnaissance. Reconnaissance is fact-finding, hacking, wiretapping, searching, making maps, knowing a guy.

    *During Mission scenes, Pavement Artists turn their knowledge into leverage. Leverage is blackmail, planting false information, manipulation of your opponent\u2019s resources, etc.

    *During Blowback scenes, Pavement Artists are undone by control. Control is a need to be in charge, to underestimate the abilities of others, to have to know everything.




    \nThere are also stress charts for Civilians and for Professionals: you stress relationships by getting people involved in the job, getting caught in lies, bailing on social obligations, asking for favors, etc. Civilians get stressed much more easily than spies. During Blowback scenes, one of the goals is to un-fuck your fucked-up relationships so that your relationships don't break down completely.

    \n Two things here: first, avoid any tradecraft that is astoundingly criminal, or speak in generalities. In the show, anything dangerous is McGuyvered (that it, they leave out key components to prevent copycats from hurting themselves).

    The best payoff things are the social engineering/hacking style Tradecraft, as that stuff plays well in the game. Things like the use of uniforms, clipboards, etc to blend in or look official, giving free drinks to people and collecting their cups to lift needed fingerprints, looking around the desk of people (under the keyboard) to find their password (also list the most common passwords used by people). These sorts of things give the players a sense of gaming the mark by actions and words that play well in a scene, as opposed to providing color for how/why your firearms/demolitions skill works.

    \n Go into each room of your house or apartment. Assume all doors and windows have been barred and locked from the other side.       \n What does it take to get on an airplane? What kind of documents do you need, and where do you get them?

      What's the fastest and least fake way to change your appearance? (I have heard that brunettes and lighter can blacken their hair with shoe polish, but what else can you do?)

      What's the easiest way to burn down someplace and not have it look like arson?

      How do you identify the easy mark in a place you're infiltrating?

      For the ideologically ultra-hard: you need to kill yourself and you've got no cyanide tab. What's the fastest, most reliable option here? (I understand that suicide is actually very difficult, and that lots of people mess it up. Maybe you don't want to put suicide advice in your book tho.)

      How do you retire as a spy? How do you leave the business? (see previous question, perhaps?)

      What's your backup plan if you think that your controller suspects you're compromised? How do you ensure your controller isn't getting set to burn you?

      Those final two questions are on my mind, because I just finished reading Between Silk and Cyanide, the war memoir of Leo Marks, a British codemaker. Marks helped manage the encryption of the communications between many Allied agents in occupied Europe and their controllers in London. Marks saw lots of traffic, and for much of the book he harbors an unprovable suspicion that a big Dutch network has been smoothly co-opted by the Gestapo.

      The British, apparently loathe to admit a failure of this magnitude, keep blythely dropping volunteers into the Netherlands. But maybe the British were just being clever. Even if these agents have been captured, they're still handy to have. They can feed information directly into enemy intelligence. Maybe London might burn their own agents, just to get that credible jack into the Gestapo. Maybe they were working a long con.

      Silk and Cyanide is a horrifying book, all the more because the horror is never front-and-center, but instead left to the reader's -- and Marks' -- imagination. Mr. Marks has a lot on his mind for a guy who hasn't gone mad.
