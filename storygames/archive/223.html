I've commented for years that one of the advantages of working on a big established game line is that it brings me into contact with people who genuiinely, truly, demonstrably are having fun in ways that don't seem at all possibly fun to me, but are for them. But of course that's not the only way to get the experience. I thought it might be a fun exercise to tally the dichotomies where it seems to you like nobody on one side could really mean it, but you have good reason to believe they do.

For me this is a big one:

Details invented as you go feel just as solid and real for game purposes as details prepared beforehand or gathered from an external source / Details invented as you go feel very different and substantially less real than ones prepared beforehand or etc etc.

I'm on the right-hand side of this one, and I find - on a deep emotional, personal level - the left-hand view as strange as the belief of some people I know that they have genuinely non-human souls, particularly if those souls originated in what I think of as fantasy races. I don't let this stop me from deal with it on an intellectual basis, because I can see that it exists, but it's something I just don't get way down.

And of course I know others feel the same way about my tilt. Keeps life interesting, it does, to know that I'm someone else's alien, too.

Let's see, other good ones....

Whatever the central conflicts of your game are, they are best served by mechanics that detail them and put them at the center of resolution. / Whatever the central conflicts of your game are, they are best served by being left to player and character choice, with the mechanics dedicated to resolving the incidentals around them.

Implicit in this one is a whole constellation of views about social relationships, the nature of conflict, etc etc. I'm less strongly tilted on this one, but have friends I deeply respect more thoroughly at each pole, and sometimes end up feeling like we're all Martians on this bus.

Anyone else got something you want to confess you simply don't get in that "yes, I understand this in my heart" way?

AFTERTHOUGHT: I'm not here looking to change anyone's mind. Nor do I really want anyone to try to change mine. Not that I'm not denying that people do have fun in ways on the far side of the gap. I'm just talking about what I (and maybe you, if you want to chime) in feel when it comes to preferences.

    Ben, the thing is, some people (I'm sometimes one of them) genuinely feel that the most important choices should not be constrained by randomization at the moment of crisis. They feel this for different reasons, not all of them overlapping much.     Okay, let's try this again now that I've had some sleep.

    (At least) four kinds of factors shape characters' options and therefore players' choices at any particular moment: the web of social, player-level preferences, style of decision-making, and so on; definitions established about the game world and its behavior before the moment (character traits and their levels, etc.); randomization, whether expressed through dice, coin-flipping, card-turning, or whatever; and player-level choice in the allocation of resources like plot points, whether spent simply, bid in challenges, or whatever.

    (This is NOT exhaustive or super-rigorous. I'm just trying to sketch out enough to illustrate the point of the moment.)

    I don't think I'm being unfair (seriously, I'm trying not to be) when I say that a driving concern of Forge-influenced design is finding the right mechanics to go with the key challenges of a new game - in practice, a matter of setting the prior definitions to point toward desired activity, and then some mix of randomization and player-level activity in those challenges. You've earned your currency, you spend it and consult fortune as is appropriate, outcomes follow, as expressed in the way that game sets up. This leaves out a lot, but I don't think there's anything misleading in it. I'll take correction, with the caveat that I'd want a better exposition not to be any longer than this, because my real point is actually elsewhere.

    The thing I wanted to point at is the fact that some people actually don't want any of that. They don't want to be steered toward some types of conflicts, nor to have to engage in player-level activity that deals with things outside their characters' perceptions (spending drama points, contesting for narrative control, etc.), nor to do any but the simplest randomization and resolution. The right system for them is driven only by the prior definition of characters' abilities and the social, player and GM judgment as to appropriate and interesting outcomes.

    I find that this is one of the strongest "you don't really mean that?!?" encounters, for folks who feel very strongly about one side or the otehr. Just recently I saw it set off some rather dull arguments on RPG Net between folks like my friends John Snead and Plume and a few folks who really weren't getting their point. It's a genuine "you're from another world" moment, when you're on one end and discover that someone is actually serious about it on the other end - informed, aware of the alterantives, and consciously choosing this other way.
