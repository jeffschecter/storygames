Story Gamers who think rocking out is undignified and, frankly, embarrassing to those around you

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2007 \n edited August 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n People who "rock out" over games are, honestly, being silly, and should sit down with a cup of tea and breathe.

I know people like this. They say things like "Best Friends is AWESOME" and "Contenders is THE BEST GAME EVER".

No. No, it's not. Best Friends is "quite a good game". If it fills you with awe, you need to get out more, and I don't mean to GenCon. Contenders is "fun". It is not the best game ever. Scrabble is the best game ever, and only because it makes you better at the Times Crossword, which actually serves a useful function.

Nobody talks like this about other games. "Oh man. Pass The Pigs was AWESOME. That ROCKED".

I keep a safe distance from people who "rock out" over games. This is for two reasons. Firstly, their enthusiasm is embarrassing, to the point where I assume they are probably mentally ill, perhaps brain damaged. Secondly, they are more likely to wear black T-shirts and to have a refreshingly liberal attitude to hygiene.

Graham

    \n Graham,

    I don't think most people who say "X is THE BEST GAME EVER" literally mean that. Saying "awesome" and "rock" are just common expressions in North America to express enthusiasm. It's not logical, nor is it meant to actually quantify something in any useful manner. It's utility value is quite low. It's meant to emote excitement and pure emotion. I know and work with hundreds of people, mostly non-gamers, and these are pretty common modes of communication. In Greece they have similar expressions. And many of my British friends and family say "brilliant" in place of "awesome."

    It's funny, you said:

    "Nobody talks like this about other games. "Oh man. Pass The Pigs was AWESOME. That ROCKED".

    But as I posted on Knife Fight, I just came from nerdnyc's boardgame night at ThinkCoffee and we were playing Pass the Pigs with strangers at the cafe. One was British, and I kid you not, literally said "this game is BRILLIANT! Bloody great!" This person seemed very intelligent, highly attractive, and well adjusted.

    Now if someone literally thought X game was the best game ever, I don't think it is a stretch to think they may be letting their emotions get the best of them. I can understand the sentiment.

    When you say:

    "I keep a safe distance from people who "rock out" over games. This is for two reasons. Firstly, their enthusiasm is embarrassing, to the point where I assume they are probably mentally ill, perhaps brain damaged. Secondly, they are more likely to wear black T-shirts and to have a refreshingly liberal attitude to hygiene."

    Are you being serious? Or is this hyperbole?

    Maybe we have different perceptions of rocking out. I will say, I am probably one of the worst offenders of what you are protesting in this thread. I say AWESOME and ROCK non stop. I pride myself on this. And if I seem bad online, I guarantee you, I am way worse in person. But I promise you that in most cases my enthusiasm is positive, infectious, energizing, and rarely embarrassing. I am certainly not mentally ill, brain damaged, and I tend to wear colorful clothing, pride myself on my hygiene, married to a very attractive and intelligent woman, have many friends, and am extremely successful at my profession (partially due to my enthusiasm). And I am happy to say many of my friends, gamers and non gamers, follow similar behavioral patterns.

    What is it exactly about this behavior that bothers you?

    Posted By: jenskotGraham,

    I don't think most people who say "X is THE BEST GAME EVER" literally mean that. Saying "awesome" and "rock" are just common expressions in North America to express enthusiasm. It's not logical, nor is it meant to actually quantify something in any useful manner. It's utility value is quite low. It's meant to emote excitement and pure emotion.
    \nIt's precisely the fact that it's useless in communication that bothers me. It is applied to such a wide range of things, and such a wide range of actual reactions/impressions [unless all the people saying it really are hyper-reactive, reacting to even the slightest positive experience as though it was the greatest thing ever in their lives], that it no longer tells me anything. Oh, and maybe around you "awesome" and "rock" are common expressions. I've never heard them once in real life--only on Story Games, RPGnet, and the Forge. Well, i've heard "awesome" used both ironically and in parody in film, but usually 80s/early-90s films.

    To answer your question (which i snipped), what bothers me is that at some point, everything can't be AWESOME!!!. Something has to be good, and something mediocre, and something bad, or it is meaningless. And while i'm sure many of the people saying these things also find things merely good, or even mediocre or bad, that is never said. Well, ok, the bad, or at least horrible, is occasionally identified.

    To use the old volume analogy: it's as if there are two volumes, 1, and 11. I do *not* want my game "turned up to 11" non-stop. That's not loud, that's a dull roar. In fact, i'd prefer it to be at '3' [or whatever the minimal 'this is satisfactory' rating would be] the majority of the time, so that when the '11' happens, it's really breath-taking. And have some lesser peaks along the way, too. An album, or concert, or dancehall would drive me away if it was all loud, all fast, all driving beats. Or, really, if it was monotonously anything. I want some slow, and some fast, and some loud, and some soft, and some ballads, and some driving dance beats, and so on. Carmina Burana wouldn't be such a powerful, moving, one-of-the-greatest-pieces-of-music-of-any-genre-of-all-time piece of music if it didn't run the gamut, from whisper-gentle flute and solo voice to full-orchestra-with-6-timpani-and-expanded-percussion-section-plus-3-full-choruses-plus-2-grand-pianos-plus-2-harps-plus-pipe-organ.

    Plus, for me, the undifferentiated, unfiltered enthusiasm seems like a reflection of the increasingly-ADHD nature of our society. It's flitting from one high to another, never sticking to one thing long enough to actually explore it, and having no patience for anything that takes time to get to the payoff. Which, in many cases, probably is not descriptive of the people saying it, or of their behavior, but i have no way of knowing, and it's sort of a button for me.

    Oh, and i don't think it's an American/British thing. I think it's an extrovert/introvert thing, as much as it can be tied to anything beyond the phenomenon itself. Some people simply don't show their enthusiasm on the outside--it doesn't mean they aren't having just as much of it. Plus, a classic definition of extrovert is one who derives energy from other people, and burns energy being alone, while an introvert is one who derives energy from being alone, and burns energy dealing with other people. So for an introvert, the more expressive other people are, the more taxing they are, even if there is no logical connection between their behavior and the introvert. \n
    Posted By: misubaIrrespective of all this I'd like us all to chant AWESOME over and over again in unison until it loses all meaning
    \nUm, that happened a couple years ago, at least in the RPG/story games world. I can no longer pay it any mind, because when someone refers to something as awesome, all i can be sure of is that they aren't describing a crappy experience. Which tells me almost nothing. And if not backed up with more-descriptive comments, leaves me wondering if there are actually any praiseworthy qualities to the thing being discussed, or if the cries of "awesome" are a cover for the lack of an actual positive experience, or lack of the ability to attribute the quality of the experience to the thing being experienced (as opposed to those doing the experiencing).

    "awesome" and "rock" have become essentially synonyms for "ok", as near as i can tell--but while still being synonyms for "this is really great" and "that's the coolest thing i've ever heard". So how do i know what you're saying?

      \n Mayhaps, no, mayhap, yes.

      Anyway, my real problem with the rant at the core of this thread is that it's a rant about some words having a lack of meaning, but in a way that seems to suggest that other words of approval have more meaning simply because they haven't been as extensively used. I don't agree with that in the slightest.

      In the end, we're all knocking around for various synonyms for "good". And what one person means by the concept of "good" when they express it, is never going to be on the same page as what another person thinks is meant when they hear it.

      So what if "awesome" is the current synonym du jour? Whether I'm calling a game good, awesome, exciting, scrumptious, fun, "banzai!", redolent with yumness, etc, is all simply saying "this has excited me and I think it might excite you".

      It's only by upgrading from words to paragraphs, and from online text to conversation and play that we can actually get at what's really meant here. In the end, the words are just pointers, and utterly devoid of true reasons to get one's panties in such a ridiculous twist.
