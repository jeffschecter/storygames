\n I was inspired by a topic that was part of the February things to watch to write about some stuff here.

I don't know why he's an opera singer.

The poster on the board that I was paging through said he loved when characters made mistakes. And not just "oh, I boned that roll, so I flubbed what I was trying to do", but situations where they know what they're going to do is going to get them into trouble and it's not in their best interests but they do it anyway. He gave an example of a knight slaying a magical maiden in the court of a king and getting into trouble thereby.

This segued into a related topic, the "incredibly detailed and drawn out planning session".

I've got some observations on these topics, because I think it's an area where my game group's play could be improved.

There is one player in the group that does all the plans. He's the one that's most charismatic and forceful and so his ideas are virtually always accepted. If someone comes up with another idea, he's always willing to listen and take their idea, but nothing goes forward unless he signs off on it. And it drives him crazy when he hears other players considering having their characters take actions that he considers mistakes, he energetically (usually in a positive and constructive way) corrects them.

There's another player who really doesn't pay a whole lot of attention to the game, she's mainly just there to hang out with others, and as a result she constantly makes all sorts of mistakes from the mundane to the sublime. I had difficulties with her until we figured out she doesn't like to have bad things happen to her character, so I stopped having her mistakes (or any actions) have any negative consequences and we've been cool.

What's interesting is that the Planner knows and sees this but continues to correct her, and to try to get her on board with the plans!

There's a lot of learned behavior here, and I'm getting a bit far afield from my eventual topic, so bear with me.

More soon.

    \n The planning session is an interesting thing to listen to. Because to some degree I don't think it's really about trying to avoid mistakes in the sense of getting into new and disadvantageous situations, it's more about managing risk. The big planning session typically happens when the decision has already been made - sometimes way back at the beginning of the game when we decided to play SWAT team members or vampires or whatever - to get into the risky situation, and the plan is there in order to either reduce negative consequences if they occur, or reduce the chances that they'll occur.

    Thus the Planner's characters, usually snarky, say things along the lines of 'oh great, look what you've gotten us into NOW, well, what are we going to do to get out of it? let's start with...'

    What's interesting to me is that after years of playing with this group, I have somewhat stopped even evaluating the plans made. I know they'll be tactically sound past the level of my focus. And I've said this. And the plans still get made!

    \n Way on the other side of this thing is my wife, whose characters most commonly (amongst this group) do the things that the poster in the other thread classified as mistakes. She likes playing "crazy" characters - the Malkavians in Vampire, the loopy psychic in Firefly, and so on. And when she is "on", playing her character to the hilt, she is never in any doubt about what her character will do next, consequences be damned. Her characters are, in those moments, both confident and unshakeable, and often very wrong. The Planner doesn't mind this at all, interestingly. I think he is able to rationalize that my wife's characters don't care about being wrong, whereas the casual player herself doesn't care about being wrong. As a result, he sees my wife's characters as another part of the game world that can (must?) be risk-managed, but can't do so with the casual player.

    Example from my recent Star Wars d20 game. The characters were fleeing a successful attack on an Imperial shipyard in two stolen gunboats. The chances that they'll escape without a fight are slim, there's literally open, laser-beam combat taking place in every 3D direction. But there's a slim chance they might be able to bluff. I say "The lead TIE Fighter hails you..." But my wife was so psyched and excited over the successful theft, previous combat, dashing swashbuckling and space adventure that she interrupted me and blurted out, very loudly and happily, "HI!" The group collapsed laughing. What I loved about it was that it was perfectly in-character, driven largely by out-of-character emotion, and it gained no advantage whatsoever. And suddenly there was no way this could be risk managed! They started rolling initiative! It was great!

    This led me to think back and there have been several instances where my wife's characters have done things that have escalated things for the group and it's never been a bad out-of-character experience, ever.

    \n I remember two interesting anecdotes regarding planning...

    The first one was this one time I was DMing... The players needed to deal with a Black Dragon in his swamp lair. They dedicated pretty much all the session to plan and prepare their big and success-warrant strategy. They reckoned every possible and perceived risk... And in the end, the big bad dragon surprised them from behind. They never really thought that they were just outside his "front door", and never during the preparation process did they worry about leaving someone to check the dragon wasn't spying on them... Of course, most of my players were 15 back then... Heh!

    The second one was one in which I was one of the PCs. We had this Mission:Impossible kind of quest. To retrieve a dark relic from a heavily guarded evil temple. The stakes were really high. We weren't only risking our lives (and possible our souls), but the safety of many people too. And since we got to bribe a double-crossing cleric and got some blue prints and stuff, we decided to make this big plan... We agreed to gather one day (without the DM, so he wouldn't have time to anticipate our plan), and spent literally hours making strategies and contingency plans.

    Our plan failed, of course. It seemed no one but me remembered that one of the Top Priorities was to not let any guard raise the alarm... It didn't matter in the end. We had already got far enough, and in the end we succeeded. Luck was on our side, and our plan was not all that bad. But I still believe the more important part was that we all had a great time, both during planning and during the adventure. Well, at least most of us.

    I did got a big bunch of extra experience both for staying in character and for being the de facto leader of the party and main proposer of the whole thing.

    Posted By: JDCorleyAs a result, he sees my wife's characters as another part of the game world that can (must?) be risk-managed, but can't do so with the casual player.
    And, this, right here, is the part of it that is sucky (or can be). The Planner is approaching the game as a problem to be solved - apply resources, circumvent risks, achieve objectives. The thing is, from that perspective, the other PCs are just resources and/or obstacles. They need to be managed or "walled off" in order for the Planner to get his happy. As long as everybody has a shared goal, though, it doesn't have to matter too much, but as soon as one of other PCs has an idea that isn't part of the plan and can't be walled off -- CONFLICT.

    This means that the other players aren't really allowed to have their own priorities and agendas unless those are entirely orthogonal to the Problem and don't intersect with it. Or, alternatively, that the planner never gets to make a plan that works.

    I've been in both spots - I've been the Planner in a group of scenery-chewers and casual players (although really my job was firewall between the GM and the other players - I had to do all the planning so that they could have their own plot threads without getting stomped by the adversarial GM) and I've watched as a table of Planners and casual players utterly shut down the one player at the table whose character had anything resembling a life goal other than Beat The Next Fight.

    It's great when you can juggle these priorities in play, but my experience is that that only really works when you've only got one alpha personality with a strong goal.

    Posted By: DannyK
    The Elaborate Plan is an interesting phenomenon. I don't think the real point is to gain advantage -- it has its own rewards. Why else would so many people have fond memories of Shadowrun, when the dominant mode of play is "Mr. Johnson gives you a job, you plan the job in elaborate detail, everything goes to hell"?
    \nAs a recovering Planner, I have to say that I like the "planning is all about risk management" insight...and from that perspective, the fond(?) memories I have of elaborate plans in Shadowrun look very different. We weren't making elaborately detailed plans because it would be fun when they went to hell...we made elaborately detailed plans in the hopes that this time maybe we'd GET IT RIGHT. Maybe this time we could finally cover all the bases, and we'd be the awesome superninjas who ghosted in, did the job, and ghosted out without a hitch. Maybe we could engineer a "Hey bad guys, fuck you" moment where the part that they thought would screw us would actually screw them, and then we'd be laughing at their expense for a change.

    Besides, the worst-case Shadowrun plan mishaps usually ended in TPKs, which really cuts the whole "mistakes are where the fun begins" concept off at the knees. Mistakes that stop the game entirely or sideline 3/4 of the players because they have to make new characters are where the fun ENDS. In an old-school killer-GM game, not having a plan (or having a bad plan) was the same as tearing up your character sheet...or an invitation to the (somewhat contrived and unsatisfying) deus ex machina rescue. Play in enough of those, and it gets really hard to turn off the part of your brain that's screaming "NO GOOD CAN COME OF THIS!", no matter how much your rational brain says "That's not how this particular game works."

    The Planning is fun in and of itself. It has nothing to do with characters making "big" mistakes of the interesting sort we're talking about. It is all about managing risk in an enterprise, not what the enterprise will be.

    The only way to ever separate these two things is to have characters with some emotional drive that overrides the "better sense" that triggers the planning session.     \n Hi, my name is David, and I'm a Planner. (Hi, David.)

    I have noticed in recent DitV play that I tend to be the Planner--have often been the Planner, in gaming--and I try not to be a bully about it. Now, though, I find that I don't really want to have the duty/onus all of the time, but by then it can be tough to get others engaged in making Plans. (Aside: A great thing about DitV is that all this player-against-player conflict over plans can Go To Dice!)

    This thread also reminds me of past play in Aberrant: Briefly, we were told it would be rollicking and rowdy, fights and chases, world-shaking events. After several sessions, it was clear that many other players (we had, like, eight or so) either didn't get the memo or disregarded it. The GM seemed to have forgotten (or was drifting to suit apparent preferences) and so a LOT of our play time would turn into Planning. Meanwhile, I'd built a combat monster (quite literally, if you know the Taint rules in Aberrant) and I didn't (a) feel like, in character, he'd be much of a Planner nor (b) want to spend an hour or more of each session on such (inevitably doomed-to-fail) Planning. And that's how my Catch Phrase was born, out of inspiration when asked (YET again) what I thought the group should do about the Problem of the Minute:

    "I am a weapon. Aim me."

    Don't know why I'm posting this, other than because I wanted to relate that cool line, and perhaps because it could be relevant to consider the AntiPlanner's agenda--be it personally chosen or implied in the game rules or setup/preamble. You've mentioned Shadowrun as a Plan-intensive game--based on its inspiration of caper fiction--and so maybe the root of the problem lies in the players' relative perceptions of what the game you are play is "about." For an extreme example, I'd cast a leery eye towards a Planner in Toon; and I would tend to kabosh a lot of Planning in stuff like D&D and CoC--exploration is risky, monsters more so, so get into marching order and ready your kit and GO, dammit!

    Hmmm... anyone else think that Instincts in Burning Empires are a form of Anti-Planning mechanic, in that they "cover the bases" for the most important elements of a character's competence or preparation?

    Also, there's something to be said for a point I made in a different thread, talking about granularity of detail and switching modes between general narrative and turn-by-turn, foot-by-foot declarations. In that thread, I offered a technique for making the switch: let the players position themselves "for free" when things get crunchy, under the assumption that this is a party of professional adventurers and, as such, they just "automatically" are in advantageous positions to support and protect each other. I used the parallel of a team of Special Forces guys entering a bar (in peacetime): they just automatically scan for blind spots and position themselves with back to walls or clear view of doors (or of each other's back). In the same manner, those GMs among you who tire of the 10' pole test and other machination at every door could just tell them, "Look, if something Bad is on the other side, I'll let you array yourselves however and presume you've prepped whatever you want to use, so let's wait until THEN to do all this, if it's even necessary." Of course, you needn't assure them that the door ITSELF is okie-dokie...

    Then again, your players might like this stuff--or might heavily embrace the resource and risk management subgame that is D&D--and they might think YOU'RE breaking the game for them, by allowing Instincts or presuming prep.

    OK, ramble off....

    \n Planning in my group is a function of the game and characters, really. If there's no reason to plan, nobody plans. Now when they *do* plan, the challenge is reconciling character portrayal with a coherent conclusion. We really, really like to talk in character (especially me, because, well, my current PC is extremely witty). When we start going around in circles we usually step out of character for a moment and soberly work it out and then step back in to chuck the plan into the IC environment.

    One thing that I've really enjoyed is doing practice runs and simulations. We did this once in our Mage game where the Technocracy PCs, after seriously screwing up a mission, had a chance to redo it in a virtual simulation while they grew a new body for someone who'd been cut in half by an Akashic. By contrast, the Trad group did several short exercises before infiltrating a Technocracy base.
