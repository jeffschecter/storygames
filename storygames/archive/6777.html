\n Motivated by discussions here and here.

What is the goal of the skill challenge? Does it accomplish that?

On the surface there are some immediately suggested problems with the skill challenge.

1. A complexity of 1 requires that my success to failure ratio be better than 4:2. At complexity level 2, it is only 6:3. The ratio of successes to failures has dropped; it seems easier. (If I am more than 65% likely to succeed at an individual skill check, this is true.)

2. At a given level, what is my chance of success for a given skill check? If it is around 50% does this make sense? What do I stand to lose or gain? Should I risk it?

3. How reliable should a skill challenge be? Would I ever undertake a skill challenge with a chance of success on a single trial around 50%? Probably not, unless I only needed a few successes. (If I had to flip a coin and get two heads before two tails, what do I know? I could flip: hh, tt, hth, thh, tht, htt; I'd have a 50% chance of flipping two heads before two tails. Now extend that, what if I had to get three heads before two tails, my chances drop below 1/3. Now get 4h before 2t, below 20%... I don't think I'd try it if I stood to loose much. Where'd my fun go?)

4. The skill challenge produces an s-curve probability distribution. This indicates some problematic characteristics, depending on certain properties of the curves.

Intuitively, you can see there are problems. Does the math bear out the intuition?

I've checked each complexity level with both an analytical model and a monte carlo simulation and both are in agreement. If the party has less than a 65% chance of success in a single skill check, then the chances are very low that they will succeed in a skill challenge (about 42%), with decreasing odds as the complexity increases. However, with chances greater than 65%, the skill challenges get easier as complexity increases. Neither of these things seems to make sense.

However, changing the model to make the party more likely to succeed at a skill challenge than any individual would have been at a single check, doesn't sound like a reasonable answer either, or does it?

Is it broken in play? What is the typical single skill check success rate? (I've based most of my evaluations on some quick calculations)

What would you do to accomplish the stated goal of skill challenges? What are you doing? Does it work? Is it fun?

(I'd be happy to discuss the analytical models with anyone who cares.)

    \n So I've been reading all this stuff about skill challenges, right, and the biggest issue I have isn't the difficulty, but that everyone seems to be acting as if play goes like this: freeform freeform skillcheck freeform BATTLEMAP freeform skillcheck freeform SKILLCHALLENGE freeform BATTLEMAP etc.

    And I'm like, "So skill challenges and combats are these big set-piece things that interrupt the normal flow of freeform play, but skill checks just fit into whatever's going on? What is this, 1985? There's got to be a better way of integrating all this together"

    If a skill challenge is just a series of skill checks, why does it matter whether you're current in a SKILLCHALLENGE or not? Can't you just be like "I want to learn more about the Shadowfell Keep at the library." And the GM can be like, "Make a history check. Okay, so you learned useful fact X." And another player can be like, "Great, I'm going to use useful fact X to pretend I already know all about the Keep and try to get more information out of the local lord." And the GM can say, "Sweet, roll Diplomacy with a +2 advantage." And then, hey, check it out, the players have now effectively creating a SKILLCHALLENGE by pursuing goals that are interesting to them and, when they reach a certain milestone in their investigation, the GM should probably give them some experience for this, either to the players directly involved in the investigation or to the entire group, depending on how your game rolls.

    Similarly, this allows you to have SKILLCHALLENGES occur over lengthy periods of time. Say the characters have a couple failures in their investigation. Maybe the lord get suspicious of their sneaky dealings and has them kicked out of town. However, when they get to the Keep itself, they continue poking around and uncover even more information. Since this continues their early investigations, the SKILLCHALLENGE is back on again! This also means failure does not cock-block you out of the experience or information you're trying to gather through investigation. It just closes off certain avenues of investigation and adds complications, forcing the players to try other methods.

    Basically, then, you've turned SKILLCHALLENGES into Keys (from The Shadow of Yesterday). Both are non-combat avenues of gaining experience by pursuing goals over time. And you've also reincorporated SKILLCHALLENGES back into the normal stream of play, instead of making them a whole different separate encounter, like combat. I have nefarious plans to reincorporate combat as well, but the only solution I have so far -- mapping everything -- isn't very realistic or desirable.

    \n The DMG suggests you can run skill challenges like Jonathan describes: segmented across combat encounters, scenes, etc., each check contributing towards the entire challenge which may or may not take place across a large portion of time. (Gandalf researching the One Ring comes to mind.)

    I've run a couple skill challenges now and they flow like Extended Contests in Heroquest, where each stage of success (or lack thereof) leads to helping realize the challenge (or causing it to crash and burn.)

    For anything with the name D&D on it, skill challenges are revolutionary. I really like them even though everyone seems to be running them differently. I think they're far from perfect (more structured pacing, like a "challenge ladder" a la Fate 2.0 would be really helpful).

    As for the issues with math, I'll won't bother to hit my head against the wall. Skill challenges seem to work just fine as written.

    \n Yeah, I was just chatting with Harper about how there's all this cool dirty hippie stuff in 4e, but it's definitely not underlined, in bold, with neon lights around it. In fact, it's hidden within a lot of other stuff while Wizards is walking past it, whistling, "Nothing to see here! Just D&D as usual!" In the case of Skill Challenges, they are clearly very cool once you actually start messing with all their hidden, unwritten implications, but the rules don't point out what makes them cool or how to enable them to be awesome at the table. In fact, they give you a lot of examples and advice that is really pretty lame. It's very much like, if this wasn't D&D, it would be the king of all fantasy heartbreakers, because it comes really close to being totally awesome, but then backs away from awesome really fast, like it's scared of it. My sense, increasingly, is that there was one person on the design team (and I guess we can take bets on who it was) who was really pushing this crazy stuff, but that it got heavily reined in, edited out, and watered down by people who didn't get it, were afraid the masses wouldn't dig it, or both.

    So I guess it's up to us to draw lines between all the cool bits, playing connect the dots and assembling something that does really rock.

    \n I think the "problem" of complex challenges being easier to succeed at than less complex challenges is somewhat overrated. As others have said, complexity is meant to equate not to difficulty, but to significance.

    In other words, when designing a skill challenge, you have two questions to ask:

    1. How difficult should it be? This will determine the DCs you set, etc.

    2. How big a deal should it be? If it's a big deal, go with a higher complexity. If it's not such a big deal, go with a low complexity (or even just a single skill check).

    Of course, I haven't played it yet, but I should get my chance within the next few weeks.

    Here it's the same - does the mechanic have any worth to begin with, that makes it worth adding stuff to it? Lets not rush in, adding vegetable and meat to what might just be a pot of water with rocks in it.
