    Well, I guess I'll just repost my comment from the other thread, for completeness' sake. Other than that, I once more happen to be in agreement with Brand.

    I have three kids (well, two stepkids I've been raising for the past six years and one toddler). The older two are 11 and 13. Their natural tendency in storytelling is to ramble and include every single detail. They don't know how to summarize. They don't even remotely have an idea of what a theme or premise is. When they tell you what went on in the latest Simpsons episode, they make sure to tell you not only every word of dialogue (and interrupt each other to say nuh-uh, that's not what he said), they also, in the middle of telling the story, reach back to tell you all the previous things that happened so you have the proper context. In short, they don't possess a natural ability to filter out the "core story."

    Now, you might say: Yes, but they're kids! True. But how are they going to get to the point where they can understand and relate the core of a story? They need to learn it. In fact, since we're homeschooling, Lisa and I need to teach them.

    That's one anecdotal piece of evidence. Now, the other pieces in my anecdotal chest come from being an aspiring author who has been to workshops and writing groups--and from my own learning process regarding stories. I can now, finally, distill a premise from a story, but I'm still not highly proficient in it. And most aspiring writers I've met are terrible at that and at most other aspects of writing a good story.

    Now, that's not a bad thing. In fact, I frequently have to argue with assholes who think that writing is somehow a "talent" and you either have it or you don't (like Darren did on the Forge, and in response to which I wrote him a private message). That's utter bullshit. Storytelling is a craft; in fact, it's a larger field with several sub-crafts. It can be learned by just about anyone, but it's always hard work. Even for people who seem "gifted", they need to practice and edit and spend a lot of time on making a good story.

    Now, maybe that's not the level of proficiency we're talking about here. But even the basics, when people are told and go "ahh yes, conflict and characters and resolution and change", are not that easy to translate into actively creating a story.

    That all said: while I disagree that most people have a natural talent for storytelling, I agree that so-called storytelling games have tended to muddy the waters, give people bad habits, and overall created what I like to non-confrontationally refer to as "preconceived or conditioned notions" about how (storytelling) play should go. Getting those out of people's brains is hard work. Empowering them to actually focus on the story instead of those built-up artifacts will take time and energy, but most of all, probably more of a cooperative rather than an incinerating approach.

    Thanks for listening :)

    - Christian

    I'm getting tired of the word "story" because it's quickly coming to the same place I keep words like "nice" and "like" -- communicatively useless.

    Can the common man of Western society recognize narrative structure (conflict, rising action, climax, denouement)? Meh, maybe. Not necessarily with those words but, given basic education, they can probably say "this guy was against this guy, and this happened and then this happened, and then the first guy did this-and-such to win, and then he got the girl."

    There are two leaps, however, to make. The first, harder-but-smaller leap is from being able to recognize these things happening in a story to recognizing that those are the essential parts of a story. The easier-but-bigger leap is then, when creating or retelling a story, to include these things in the telling.

    Your story-of-the-day, Brand, is usually lacking a climax or denouement -- although in my experience, the story-of-the-day is usually just bitching about how the person was abused (conflict and rising action).

    Note that I'm coming from a background without much respect for the common man -- I used to teach middle school.

    Just to toss this out there, to see how the act of observation changes that observed, here are some definitions from the Bedford Glossary of Critical and Literary Terms that I had lying about from my grad-school days:

    Story: A narrative of events ordered chronologically.

    Narrative: A story or a telling of a story, or an account of a situation or events.

    Plot: The arrangement and interrelation of events in a narrative work, chosen and designed to engage the readers attention and interest (or even to around suspense or anxiety) while also providing a framework for the exposition of the author's message, or theme, and for other elements such as characterization, symbol, and conflict.

    Plot is distingueshed from story, which referes to a narrative of events, due to an emphasis on establishing causality. Story is the raw material from which plot is constructed. Crafting a plot requires chosing not only which elements of the story in include, but also relating the evtns of a story to one another so that causality may be established conciningly. As E.M. Forester explained, to say "the kind died and the queen died" is to tell a story. Adding three words, "The king died and then the queen died of grief" transforms the story into a plot by including an emphasizing causality.

    So, are we all saying that people can tell stories, some people can tell good stories, but that many people have trouble constructing a plot?

    Two quick points about 'story-making' in rpgs:

    First: I think people are reasonably competant at making narrative--maybe not very good narrative, but there you go.

    However, rpgs are not about telling stories, they are about telling stories _collaboratively_, with _other people_. Everyday practice with this: not so much beyond brief sketch comedy. Transferability of other skills--patchy at best.

    Which brings us to:

    Two: Much of the coherent narrative creation that goes on around a gaming table isn't communicated. It's in people's heads--they are doing their darndest to make a coherent and enjoyable story out of the stuff that happens at the table, and that stuff includes both in-game SIS and out of game system mechanics. Tends to make a mess.

    The worse the game experience, the more the players do this editing of experience. Because they are desperate to make a good story, and they'll do what it takes. Which, of course, usually hurts the game as a whole.
