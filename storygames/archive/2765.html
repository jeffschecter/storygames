    \n Bill and Chris both have some important insights. First, even "GM-less" games need to cover the bases of GM-responsibility either through system or through distribution to the players. Chris points out that making a game GM-less "because you can" is often self-defeating. The experience of many genres is often enhanced by a centralized authority, coordinated opposition, and so on.
    [shameless plug alert]
    When I started on my current project, I explored how each of the traditional conventions applied to the experience I was creating. My game is partly about Exestentialism. One of the tennants of that philosophy is that life is without order or meaning (except that which you impose on it, generating Existential Angst, but that is a topic for another day.) GM==plot and structure, Existentialism =/= plot and structure, :. GM's gotta go. I would not have created an Epic Fantasy game without a GM, because the benefits of having one (coordinated opposition, structure, plot) are IMHO vital to that genre.
    In the process of creating my game, rough playtest draft though it is, what I think is an important insight did occur to me. One of the key responsibilities of a GM is to end the game. In a GM-less game, there should be an end-state. Without an endgame, the play continues until the players get tired of the game, so while the beginning of the game will likely be full of "HARDCORE!" the end will be mediocre, leaving the overall experience being average at best which is not-awesome. If the game ends in the "HARDCORE!" phase of play, the experience will remain "HARDCORE!" which is awesome.

    \n Hey Kynn! I agree with Andrew Morris... Universalis sounds to be right on the money for you (I can't think of any system that does the collaborative storytelling thing better)
    \n
    \nWith that being said, if you don't want conflict resolution... why don't you just run it in the way that 'real' collaborative storytelling is done. Essentially, you go around in a circle and each player adds a sentence or element that CANNOT contradict or 'reimagine' (at least in a cheap way) what has gone before.
    \n
    \nThis method is slightly more difficult if you want each person to still have a 'character' to play (in addition to general narrative and NPC characters)... if this is the case you might want to look at Prime Time Adventures. It has a GM and it has a card based 'conflict' resolution method (which are counter to your original point) but there is really no success or failure... just a question of who gets narrative control. Still, everyone is expected to use 'conflicts' to produce interesting stories.

    Karyn, I think you're making some unnecessary assumptions about what dice do and what conflict means.

    \n

    Dice and cards are a method of reducing predictability, but the options they give are not necessarily success and failure, if that's not what you want. Like, the way it works in Shock: is, I say what I want, you say what you want, and we roll different dice for the things we want and against the things our opponent wants. Maybe they both happen, maybe neither happens, maybe one of us gets what we want and the other doesn't, but it twists the story significantly every time the dice are rolled. Resolution of tasks is not really what's at stake; what the dice decide (as indeed they always do) is who gets to say what about the fiction.

    \n

    If you don't want unpredictability, I can imagine several other mechanics that might serve you: voting; spending coins on events; a map of the set and you can move counters from spot to spot and use the place, props, and characters that are in a particular space.

    \n

    Really, though, this is a perfect discussion for Forge First Thoughts. It sounds like there are particular features to this game that will make you happy, but you might need some folks to bounce ideas off to get those features defined well.

    \n

    Incidentally, Shock: started off diceless, with only human decisions and resources to push the story one way or another. The resource tokens became dice and the game became much better. It made it so that, instead of spending the maximum to get what you want every time (or not having the tokens to do it), you're instead able to take a risk. That adds tension.
