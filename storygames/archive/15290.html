    \n My own impression of international communication is that Nordic people tend to communicate reasonably similarly - I don't see anything strange in how eg. Norwegians communicate compared to us Finns. The Brits I've met must have been outliers, as Brits are often considered stuffy and formal, but to me they've seemed quite normal as well, with a fine sense of humor and humble kindness - not at all like they're depicted in costume dramas. Americans, in comparison, quite often come off as formal, self-important and high-strung; they're self-conscious about who they are and take easy offense at mere differences in opinion, and they're enmeshed in ideological systems that need to be accounted for. Central-Europeans are often what I'd consider reserved yet civilized, Francophones particularly, while Germans are pretty similar to Nordics, just a bit more formal.

    Of course, the above impressions are purely anecdotal, in case somebody thought I'd been making a study out of it. Still, I'm left wondering about how quickly or slowly this received wisdom changes in comparison to reality; it seems that many well-known national stereotypes are from the 19th century, and people do change from generation to generation. Brits, for example - my experiences have left me thinking that maybe the folks who grew up through the Thatcher era and punk movement and Monty Pythonesque deconstructions of their culture and whatnot have largely shed an idiom of reservedness earned by their forebears? Or maybe modern Americans have developed during urbanization out of their reputed rough-and-ready identity and into something more akin to Victorian Brits?

    For what it's worth, my most common encounter with cross-cultural communication issues is when somebody (often Anglo of some flavour, considering the demographics of the Internet) interprets me as offensive and overbearing when I merely intended to be analytically exact and neutral in tone. Seems that I'm personally prone to being offensive in this way, but it's also true that Finns interpret my writing/speaking style less often as if I were looking for a fight, rather assuming that I merely want a spirited debate. So it well might be the case that Nordics are rude, as Matthijs suspects up above, or at least that we're more thick-skinned so that rude people get more of a pass.

    It's probably no surprise that I find other Nordics writing here at Story Games to be mostly pleasant and polite, even when I disagree with them strenuously about matters of substance. They don't do the passive-aggressive shit Americans are known for, nor the snide irony thing, and they engage conversations seriously instead of trolling for reactions.

    \n Speaking as a New Jersey/northern Delaware native whose heritage is Central European and British, it would seem that the German-Irish side of my family is fairly direct, a little combative, and prone to asking blunt questions.
    The German/Welsh/Swiss side of my family, on the other hand, is prone to self-silencing, never saying anything negative unless backed into a corner, and uninterested in sharing strong feelings, especially if they are "unpleasant". We're kind of like WASPs but without the fancy pedigree or money.
    Two of my great-grandfathers were abusive alcoholics who terrorized their immediate families. Each of my grandmothers dealt with this in a very distinctive way - my German-Irish maternal grandmother became extremely emotionally (but, importantly, not physically) abusive of her own husband and children, while my Welsh paternal grandmother (who married my Swiss-German grandfather) did pretty much the opposite: she's a consummate, almost pathological people-pleaser who cries if you contradict her on anything with some emotional weight behind it.

    Dunno if that correlates to people's experiences at all. Certainly there are many, many other factors in play here, but the more-British-ish side of my family is rowdy, direct, and a little crude, while the more-Germanic side of my family is quiet, affable, and inexpressive.

    Posted By: Hans c-oI don't feel that Norwegians (and scandinavians & nordics) are abrupt and rude, but in many cases they have this kind of hilarious attitude where they think they've overcome all these problems that U.S. citizens have--racial tension, being "enmeshed in ideological systems", etc. Oh, you rascally Norse!
    \nThis is certainly true, I recognize it in myself and in others - the Nordic societal model has become a "thing" over the last generation, so much so that we can actually feel some sort of international pride about it. This is a change of pace eg. here in Finland - my parents' generation largely learned to think of themselves as a fringe culture where everything is more drab and backwards than the big and excellent things real civilized people down in Europe or over in America get. There's a lot of this syndrome still in Finnish public discourse, in fact; people will harp about how various choices will drive the country towards some sort of real becoming, as if we didn't have a perfectly admirable little society going. Things like having to get a Guggenheim museum to feel like a real boy (or a real European city, as the case may be) - ultimately shallow thinking, but the current middle-agers still seem to need this sort of thing. I imagine that in a decade or two we will fully join the Swedes and the Swiss and other such people in the permanently self-satisfied club, once the public discourse comes to be more dominated by my generation.

    (Sweden and Denmark, in comparison, are old countries with quite a bit of pedigree - I don't really know, but I imagine that they're much more comfortable about thinking of themselves as belonging to the narrow top of the global pyramid. Finland as a whole was still in many ways what would be termed a third-world country a century ago, so the change has been rapid.)

    Anyway, my point is that our younger, more international generation is sort of less humble in many ways. Of course there's pressure to conform to American models of narrative in many things, but often those don't fit because they're simply so alien. The two-party system, American racial tensions, the violence-based culture, survivalism myths, pro-life lobby, anti-gay politics, Bible as a credible societal guide, often enough the entire Republican party are just so out there that it's difficult to feel any responsibility for accomodating and applying them to our own lives here. This may easily come off as conceited, especially if you're yourself used to thinking of these things as universal facts, or perhaps facts that the rest of the world has to appreciate and accept due to how American owns the Internet etc. So I will totally own to this experience, I catch myself thinking close enough to what Hans describes now and then.

    I'm also personally somewhat prickly about American cultural hegemony, so sorry about that. It comes with the hobby in a way, as roleplaying tends to get a lot of people who've been overexposed to American cultural influences, and this starts to grow old over time for anybody interested in world culture and nuanced thinking in general. My elder brother, for example, has all but lost any real connection to life as we know it out in the real world, preferring to frame his thinking and life experiences according to what his American gurus tell him. As one might imagine, this doesn't make me personally too disposed to leaning back and just allowing everything to be framed in an American way by default - I get enough of that with my brother, and with the other Finnish gamers and various other geeks who think that Finland has a bicameral parliament.

    Still, to clarify, when we strip away the mirth and narratives about what we would like to be true, the bare bones of the matter is that people have different problems in different parts of the world, as the conditions are different. Nordic people often find it easy to take American social issues lightly simply because those problems don't apply to us. To my understanding this isn't because we're somehow smarter, but rather because we've never had to deal with the problems that come with building your country on stolen land and forced labor; that sort of thing has repercussions over long time-spans, and it will impact the national character. The truth is, we haven't overcome these problems - we simply haven't had to face them in the first place.

      \n I think generalizations can make sense when we keep in mind that, just because something is more common in some cultures, it doesn't apply to everyone (which is, of course, the difference between generalizations and stereotyping).

      One of my favorite non-fiction books is a German book called Bildung. In it, Dietrich Schwanitz has a section on cultural values to help people understand different cultures. These are simply ways of communicating within a culture that are more common to a particular culture than another, but again, this doesn't apply to everyone. Every cultural value (or generalization) usually works as "many people x, several people y, a few people anti-x."

      That all said, he thinks that in general, the British cultural way of communication is understatement, the Southern European way is theatricality (which he thinks is positive, full of joy and passionate expression), and the Nordic way is authenticity. I definitely find myself fitting in there. I have a strong desire to be "authentic," for whatever that means in the end, and I may have clashed with one or two Brits among you.

      This difference goes alongside the Catholic/Lutheran divide. Catholicism has a lot of "pomp and circumstance," with colorful attire of priests and involved rituals. Lutheranism, on the other hand, is all about the individual's direct and unadulterated relationship with faith and "don't give me any of that fancy ritual stuff, this iz serious bizniz." Not surprisingly, Southern Europe is largely Catholic, while the Nordic areas are Protestant. Whether the cultural values cause the adoption of certain religious ways or the other way around, who knows (probably a mix of both).

      In the end, we're all individuals, and without getting to know us, there's no way of telling if we're one of the many, several, or few.
