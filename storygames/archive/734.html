What are some of your favorite house-rules?

Andy
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
Hey all, I've been playing around with houseruling a system and it's gotten to the point that I think I broke it and need to wire together a new one to meet my needs.

Anyway, while thinking about it, I was wondering some games you've houseruled, and the favorite rules you added to it.

Here's a few of mine:

Dungeons and Dragons/Conan OGL: The Cool Rule. We were plain tired of getting into dramatic session battles, describing things all cool and all, only to get run down by a string of cold dice. For a Conan OGL game I played, we implemented a Cool Rule (ala Dread or Sorcerer). Simply put, if you describe the action with any sort of coolness, or make use of strategy, or do something plain cinematic, you get to roll 2d20 and pick the higher d20 for that action. It applied to all actions. The GM only got the 1d20, but I found that adding a few HP to enemies or making some difficulties just 1-2 points higher really balanced it out in the long run, and got people really into the flow.

Unknown Armies: Better Off Alone. This was for a horror/suspense campaign with a small amount of Hellraiser/Silent Hill thrown in. Basically, we found that all the characters liked to travel in one big pack from room to room. To get people to split up and do the "horror movie thing" (getting spooked while alone), I gave everyone a +10 to all rolls they made when they were truly alone. It worked out pretty well for the three sessions we used it for.

That's the major ones that worked well that I can recall. Anyone else?

-Andy

    I houseruled the use of Joss in Weapons of the Gods.

    Joss is a kind of active resource that allow players to add a die per point to their roll or substract a die from an opponents pool, depending on the type. According to the rules the points have to be spend before the roll.

    I didn't like that especially because you always have to ask, wheter anyone wants to spend bad Joss on your roll.

    Therefore I changed the rule, such that Joss points are no spend after the roll and allow to re-roll one die.

    That makes Joss a bit more powerful by the way, which I like.

    My house-ruled Nobilis runs like a race car.

    Unified resources (there's only 1 type of Miracle Point, called... er... "Miracle Points"), no difference between Lesser/Major Miracles of the same type (which leaves us with 7 distinct Miracle Levels), Spirit is "pumpable" (like the other Attributes) increasing your Auctoritas and your ability to shock-and-awe folks. I was considering allowing Social Miracles to be based on another attribute depending on whether you were using them on Chancel residents (Realm), based on your beauty or wit (Aspect), based on your status as a Noble or with spirits of your Estate (Domain), or based on the sheer force of your will and charisma (Spirit). Something like that probably would have happened.

    I second the greatness of that rule, JD.

    Eric started his Meta Points houserule for D&D 3 yesterday. You spend a point and hand it to the GM to author something significant, but leave its ramifications or full description up to the GM. The GM can spend the points to guarantee certain NPCs don't die or get a bonus on their roll. Those points, once spent by the GM, go into the group pool to redistribute by players to each other to reward cool gaming.

    Hey Mark, you totally left out the core important part of the MetaPoints thing; A player can spend a MetaPoint when their character dies to keep the character alive and suffer GM-authored consequences instead. Which means that, in the two combats we had in the first session, the players fought for the life of a young boy (and lost) and then fought to recapture a stolen wagonload of silk (and won). At no point were the players in any kind of risk of loosing their characters to a meaningless combat.

    And the points have a basic ecology similar to Fan Mail in PTA. When a player spends a point it goes to the GM. When the GM spends a point it goes to the middle of the table. A player may award a point from the middle of the table to another player fan-mail-style.

    That one session was pretty kewl with that rule. We'll see how it shakes out in the long run.
