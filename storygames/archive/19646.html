\n Hi everyone. I'm working on a game, simplest summary would be Grand Theft Auto RPG: an Apocalypse World hack set in a modern-era city, themed around crime, violence, and greed. I've had my first playtesting session with some success, but I've already encountered a problem I didn't expect: player motivation/alignment. I imagined that giving players crime-themed characters with powerful abilities and a big city to mess up would result in, well, a similar situation to Grand Theft Auto where things like "collateral damage" or "number of police officers killed" would be fun bonuses rather than tragic consequences. How many times have we seen a group of D&D murderhobos gleefully slaughter the town watch, right? I didn't think that players needed a impetus to break the rules.

Unfortunately, so far my players have been quite timid. Though I've deliberately steered the game away from the "vigilante" angle - I wanted to make that a separate game - they keep seeing themselves as fighting for justice and trying to avoid violence or breaking the law, when I as the GM am saying, "No, it's fine, your character is a hardened murderer, right? Just shoot the cop in the face. Stop worrying so much." I have some angles planned for the game economy, but I don't think that imaginary wealth is going to be enough of a "carrot" as I thought. Another idea I've had is adding a character alignment system, but how do I prevent every person in the group from picking "Neutral Good" or the equivalent?

This is something I'm interested beyond my crime RPG. I have plans for evil D&D campaigns as well. Of course, it's possible that this simply isn't the right group for such a game, and they're not interested in a game where they can't be heroic and selfless. But I'm still interested in your feedback on what I can do to make my players less afraid of the consequences of their actions. It's funny how this is the opposite situation many GMs find themselves in, where instead of following their nice prepared storyline, the players are just fucking around killing guards and robbing people. I'm actually looking for ways to motivate my players to act out, fuck stuff up, take what they want and don't let anybody stop you.

TLDR: How can I get my players to cut loose and enjoy their inner demons instead of being goody two-shoes?

    \n It sounds like your asking your players to be a sort of cartoonishly evil that rarely exists in real life. Combined with a realistic setting, you're going to have a hard time making that work.

    Real-life criminals don't go around smashing up cars and shooting cops in the face, because they'd quickly be in jail or dead. Real life criminals either do things with care or do things out of desperation. You're not going to have much luck making them less afraid of the consequences of their actions--but you might have more luck making them more afraid of the consequences of inaction...

    \n In my urban crime AW hack, The 'Hood, there's a two-pronged strategy to encourage players to embrace the dark side of their characters:

    1. Heat: Instead of marking experience when they roll with certain stats, they mark experience when they take heat, which can be gained as the price of a move or as the result of missing a move. Heat reflects the attention they're drawing from the authorities and from other criminals who are getting tired of them pissing on their patch: if it reaches 5, the character is out of the game, but there are moves to reduce it. The upshot is that the fastest way to advance in the game is to break the law and not worry about the immediate consequences, as long as you take care of things before it's too late.

    2. Income: Each playbook has an entry for how they make a living, based on what their particular criminal speciality is, so the drug-dealer type has to keep a minimum number of residents supplied with drugs and the getaway driver needs to have a fully functioning vehicle ready to go. If their means of supporting themselves is threatened, they end up going short, which means they can't depend on having the ready cash to buy anything, pay debts, etc. If they are short, they mark experience for restoring their livelihood, so again this encourages them to play in a way that pursues the character's criminal activities.

    TLDR: If you want a certain type of play, use rules that support, encourage or reward the players when they engage in that type of play.

    \n Heat at 5 has worked fine so far: there's plenty of time to lose heat here & there and if you're really up against the wall, you can always shift the blame to another PC instead of taking the heat yourself, but they get the experience as well.

    If 'burned at heat 5' seems too harsh, then it's easy to give PCs a buffer zone: when they're at 5, allow them to still act, as long as their moves are directed towards losing at least 1 heat as soon as possible, the same way the Feelgood's 'healing' move allows a character who is out to stay in the game as long as they get to the Feelgood in a timely fashion and don't get sidetracked by something else.

    \n Hold on though. There's a huge difference between playing GTA and playing a crime/heist movie.

    The sorts of things you're talking about don't happen in heist movies, for example, unless (until) things go horribly wrong. It may not be a matter of you wanting GTA and your parting playing D&D paladins. It very well may be a problem of you wanting GTA and your party playing Ocean's Eleven.

    If what you want is GTA, you have your work cut out for you. That saturation of over-the-top mayhem doesn't work in RPGs as well as it does in video games. It has to do with expectations of narrative. It's the same reason that my character will walk into someone's house and smash pots in Zelda, but would never dream of doing the thing in D&D, even though they're both games about going into dungeons to fight monsters, find cool treasure, and save the world.

    \n I'd say this:

    1. You can't (and shouldn't) ever "force" your players to do something they don't want to do. At the very worst, you can say, "Hey, I have a game in which the characters will act like this, and only like this. Do you still want to play it?" Make it explicit.

    2. Proper game design, as pointed out above, can work to create certain effects in the fiction. If you really want to have an "alignment" system (and I don't know if that's really the best thing for a GTA-type game), why not make a list of alignments like Greedy, Violent, Compulsive Liar, Compulsive Arsonist, and Serial Killer? That makes the direction of your game very clear, not unlike my point #1, above.

    3. Violence and crime, on its own, isn't very interesting or engaging. It sounds like you're giving your players more interesting things to interact with, which is an interesting thing. Violence and crime, on its own, is (almost) never a motivation in itself, unless you've got a cardboard 2-dimensional cartoon villain (and even that guy isn't interesting unless he has a hero to oppose or something good to fight against). Focus, instead, on interesting and desirable things, and then set up your game so that the only way (or the easiest way, or the most incentivized way) to get them is to commit crime.

    It could be as simple as asking your players things like:

    "What does your character want more than anything in the world?"
    "What's stopping them from getting it?"
    "If they had no moral compunctions/if nobody was watching, how could they break the law to get what they want?"
    "What would it take for you to [do awful things]?"

    Focus on the motivation and the rewards. The best villains all want something we can understand (like to save their little girl from harm, or revenge, or a desire to be accepted); it's the means they use to try to achieve that thing which we do not accept.

    But, no matter what, get the players on board! This shouldn't be a guessing game.
