\n Okay, we've had threads about movies that should be games and books that should be games, but I haven't seen anyone touch on a particular kind of inspiration I encounter all the time: music.

Inspirational music -- and by this I don't mean music played in the background of game sessions -- comes in two varieties for me: story music and emotive music. Story music has, obviously enough, a story built right into it. Emotive music either evokes pure emotion or brings images to mind that may or may not be directly related to the content of the song. Don't worry, I'm going to provide examples.\n
  • "Orgasmatron," by Mot\xf6rhead -- Sometimes a song says, "This is the story you have to bring to life." The first time I laid eyes on Metal \xd6pera, I said, "Hey, now I can do Orgasmatron!" I haven't yet, but that's no one's fault but mine.

  • "Spirit Horse of the Cherokee, by Manowar -- Manowar's music falls under the story and emotive columns. On the one hand, a song like this one brings out very specific story ideas, depending upon how one chooses to interpret the lyrics: I like the idea of getting the Indians' viewpoint of the many clashes between native peoples and the US government in the 19th century. There's good stuff there. Or one could go with a magical realist approach and do something involving an Indian campaign to retake ancestral lands. On the other hand with Manowar's music, there's plenty of stuff that just screams bloody murder, occasionally in a literal sense. I've never played a fantasy game of any stripe that's been able to capture the raw, driving bloodthirstiness of Manowar's best songs.

  • "Vrapogat," by Za Fr\xfbmi -- Albums by these two crazy Swedes can be hard to come by except via mail order, but they're worth seeking out. As with Manowar, these songs can be interpreted into games directly -- one musical cycle, of which "Vrapogat" is a part, tells a complete story -- or simply mined for inspiration. I based an orcs-versus-humans campaign on the powerful vibe I got from Za Fr\xfbmi, specifically "Vrapogat."

    \n I've been thinking about how to game the Boredoms by listening to the early albums(the one that would be really hard to game). I think it'd be incredibly difficult. It's the naked lunch of Rock. I think it'd be a game about trying to offend everyone and still rock. Most of all, it'll be a game of oneupsmanship. Take the Adventures of Baron Munchausen and turn it on its ear, and probably give it a lobotomy, too. Anger and destruction as a passionate creative force. So in the end: Naked Lunch - heroin + Baal by Bertolt Brecht + Baron Munchausen the game = Boredoms the game.

    This all refers to the pre-super roots albums. After the Super Roots series? Flex Mentallo's idealism and exploration of beauty without corruption. Beauty for the sake of being beautiful, perhaps as a way of making up for how hideous the past was. No regrets, however. Transformation of a larvae into insect.

    Sorry, this is all a bit of a run on, but this is what Boredoms evokes in me, and how I think about the music in non-musical terms.

      Posted By: Neko Ewen
      NIN's Year Zero is obvious, being a concept album and all.
      \nThe Warning from Year Zero was a massive influence on the game I will be releasing early next year.

      From the text of my game:

      Ahriman

      Ahriman punish the wicked and bring peaceful death to those who have been the long-suffering victims of corrupt authorities. Ahriman serves the god(s) as angels of divine wrath and retribution[...]

      Player narrators should use ahriman sparingly. Consider introducing them to a story when the insurrectionists fail in their responsibility to fight against the suffering caused by corrupt authorities. A player narrator should never use ahriman for the sake of giving the insurrectionists another foe to overcome.

      Psychology of an Ahriman

      Ahriman do not scheme or plot. They do not delight in causing pain. They do no ask for forgiveness or feel shame, and neither do they know what it means to feel guilty or shameful. When introducing ahriman into a story remember they only appear when people do not do enough to end the suffering other humans have caused. Ahriman tries to end such suffering as soon as possible. They do not seek power or dominion. As soon as they have purged their location of all suffering, they leave as though banished.

      Ahriman and Insurrectionists

      Player narrators should use Ahriman when insurrectionists neglect their calling to fight against corrupt authorities. An ahriman represents the darker side of a revolution; they symbolise the merciless side of the insurrectionists' nature or the bloodlust of a furious mob of oppressed citizens[...]
