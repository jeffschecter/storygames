\n Over in the playcraft thread, John lists a mess of techniques, attitudes and behaviors that can make games great.

If you were going to pick ten of these as exemplars, ten you'd really want to share with your friends who are new to tabletop roleplaying, what would they be? I'm thinking primarily about bigger concepts like, say, reincorporation, which would be on my list. "Listen to other players, and find ways to re-use and amplify their creative contributions later in the game." Stuff like that.

How about you - what belongs on the top ten list?

    \n I\u2019ll say it in my own words (in no particular ranking):

    - Be fair and show good judgment (this is a big one for me, really)
    - Catch the vibe of your fellow players and play along with it
    - Be inspired and inspiring
    - Communicate well, know when to say something and when to shut up
    - Be humorous but not silly
    - Be attentive and thorough about the fiction and its consistency (this is really several points from the original list crammed into one, sorry about that)
    - Think forward and mind where your actions might take the game
    - Talk eloquently and engagingly
    - Empathize with the fictional characters, not only with your own\t
    - Be willing to invest real effort into the game

    \n
    \n * Give each NPC, location and event a unique feel
    * Know when to say when, regarding scene/event length
    * Find ways to engage all players
    * Don't be afraid to get right to the good stuff; if you must employ a lot of build-up, make sure (a) it's incredibly engaging build-up and (b) the payoff is worth it
    * Understand each player's strengths and create situations that let him/her play to those strengths
    * Getting people to occasionally step out of their comfort zones is great; try to do it equally across all players
    * Create deadlines and inherent conflicts within your fiction
    * Try to keep characters in the same location regularly whenever it makes sense (for ease of keeping up with the story for the players plus greater potential character interaction); when characters MUST split up, make the separations a meaningful part of the story

    \n Jason: To listen to the players is to pay attention to what they verbally communicate.

    To see the players is about paying attention to what they are not communicating verbally.

    Partly is is about paying attention to non verbal signals, but it also about imagination, empathy and sympathy. Recognizing the other players need, without them having to speak it. Or figuring out what they might appreciated, even if they said anything about it. On an even more abstract level it it for me about seeing the other players as person and individuals and accepting and acknowledging them as they are.

    There is also the difference between being an passive watcher or active watcher in how you watch you fellow players. (Compare it to being an passive listener or active listener)

    \n A lot of mine are focused on advancing the fiction and keeping things moving.

    - Listen to others
    - Build on other people's ideas
    - Patiently help other players with rules
    - Share the spotlight
    - Shine the spotlight on others
    - Remember and reincorporate small details brought in by other players
    - Where the fiction goes awry, help pave over the holes and got us back on track
    - When another player needs space, give it to them
    - Look for fictional loose ends and tie them together
    - Show good pacing and help the game move along

    \n - Listened to others (as above, always #1, although engagement is close)
    - Maintaining engagement with the game (can be through calling for a break when needed, too)
    - Kibitzing but without overshadowing (help people out, but let them make their own choices too)
    - Authorship without worry (for me, I want players unafraid of sometimes using director stance)
    - Caring about internal consistency (repetition here later, but, so be it.)
    - Playing transparently and honestly (I'm sixteen hundred kinds of tired of "my character has a super-secret history the rest of the players can't know about!" But, perhaps this has come up extra often in games I have played in.)
    - Willing to have a character fail, while sometimes pushing for interesting failure (This may differ from advocating for one's character?)
    - Dedication to and influence on the game's tone (help create the tone of the game, but while keeping in sync with everyone else's goals)
    - Foster conflict, buy into it (That is, care about the conflict you're helping create on some level)
    - Look for fictional loose ends and tie them together (Fits into authorship but I like the wording!)
