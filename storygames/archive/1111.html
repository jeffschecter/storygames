These are all incredibly important - but what do they have to do with contextual action? Well, most (almost all) of the special actions that you can do with the Prince are controlled with the right-click of the mouse and/or the spacebar. The variables involved are purely environmental - the direction and speed of the Prince is compared to the environment at the time of the right-click and the correct special action is executed.

Figuring out how to start or where to go next is often tricky.

And so, context menus are by no means a static thing - contextual action can be dynamic and flow with the play of a game.

Ok, so what now? How does this apply to RPGs?

Consider for a minute that a large portion of a character sheet is devoted to combat statistics - but that these statistics often need to have extra calculation involved in order to achieve a result in the game. What is present on a character sheet is the beginning of a series of contexts that could be broken up and presented to the player only when necessary. Imagine (for D&D for example) a series of cards (color coded): one has â€™standardâ€™ combat information. Another has special maneuvers that the player can execute. If a character is wounded (or knocked prone or in some other special circumstance), simply flip the card and there are all of the playerâ€™s options and stats laid out for them.

On a more universal scale - why not a separate card for each attribute, with derived statistics covering the range of options that the character has? Going even larger - â€˜environment cardsâ€™ that provide global context for the action of the game - limiting action to social action unless certain conditions are met. Or pulling context for entire campaigns from situations the GM has created for the area the characters are in and deriving options which are presented as another card in the playerâ€™s arsenal.

By breaking up the information presented, and giving complete information on each context, you can enrich the player experience by offering more options, enrich it by offering new and different options built from context, streamline it by providing easy access to the numeric/arithmetic side of all these options, and channel it by restricting contexts.

    Awesome stuff. There is a huge amount that we can learn from video games.

    Take Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas. You start the game and walk up to a bike and get on. That's when the instructions for riding a bike appear. You get into a car for the first time and instructions for driving the car appear. Jump off a building with a parachute for the first time and instructions for the parachute appear. Go to the gym for the first time and instructions for the gym appear. Fly a plane for the first time, etc. etc. etc. There's probably a hundred things you can do in GTA:SA, and if you had to learn them all at one time it would be impossible. Yet for some reason this seems to not have been nailed down to people in games.

    How much better would D&D3 be/have been if there were pullout "combat cheat sheets" that said in clear big type "BULL RUSH" or "DISARMS"? How much better would any game be in an unfamiliar setting if you had characters fed-ex mission around a little while like every console RPG ever? They're not just having you fight rats in the tavern basement and carry a package to Biff the Blacksmith, they're showing you the combat system and introducing you to the town.
