    \n Hey Nathan,

    A cheat sheet is a good idea. I'll see about writing down the ways we handle it for this week's game.

    The way I understand BDTP (now, after some struggling), changing intention is all about the outcome, rather than about specific mechanics. A character can change tactics (which ability they use) without changing their overall intention. If my intention is to distract a guard, for example, I might rotate between Pray, Savoire Faire, and Acrobatics, all aimed toward the same end.

    Here's a longer example of changing intent:

    A PC starts a debate with an NPC about some obscure religious dogma in The Book. A regular Sway conflict results in the PC losing, so he brings down the pain. Both characters state their intent at the outset- the PC wants to assert his academic dominance, while the NPC just wants to embarrass the PC in public. They begin their argument, both taking (Reason?) Harm as they go.

    At any point, either opponent can choose to change intentions, simply by spending an entire round defending (using an innate ability, such as Resist, React, or Endure). So, for example, the PC, his blood running hot from arguing, might change his intention to throttling the old coot to death. He rolls his React to the NPCs Sway and either takes harm or avoids it. Then he's free to use whatever Ability is appropriate to bring about his intention- perhaps he uses Deception for a round and then Brawling. If he wins the BDTP, the poor academic is a goner.

    Keep in mind that characters can also change intentions freely whenever the dice are tied (perpendicular actions, no Harm to either side).

    \n In case you're used to thinking in terms of conflict stakes, you could say that a change of intention is used to change the stakes - previously my goal was to get you to marry me, now I change intentions and just want you to have sex with me. That kind of thing. It's used because sometimes characters change their minds when the situation changes, and because changing intentions might allow the opponent to give up. Sometimes it might even be that new opportunities appear in the midst of BDtP, allowing intentions that wouldn't have made any sense before.

    \n That sounds valid to me.

    Change in intention means how the story turns out, not how you're getting it done (that's why you might use different Abilities for the same intention). You might still be beating someone up, even using the same Ability, but the end result will have a different effect on the story.

    \n
    \n Deciding not to kill somebody has always been a cause for change of intention in my games, certainly. More generally, we tend to use intention changes pretty liberally - the house principle is that anybody can just ask another player to take the defensive action if they feel that the situation has shifted so dramatically that their character should take the moment to reorient - no need for the Story Guide to force the issue, taking one round in a defensive action rarely messes up anybody's play significantly. By taking the defensive action the player draws attention to his character's shifting priorities, which is just fun, not a punishment.
