\n So, every time someone wants to say (indie/story game/Forge-style/new-wave/focused-design) or (trad/old-skool/pre-Forge/etc), they end up wading into a potential definition war, semantic argument or history lecture. And, sometimes that's useful - some of those terms are pretty loaded, and problematizing their use might be a good thing.

But often, we end up miring good conversations by demanding a clarification or substitution of terminology.
And that's frustrating.

The problem, as I see it, is that we're lacking some useful terminology. Or rather, I'm lacking some useful terminology and you might be too.

The issue: Vampire and AD&D don't belong in the same category. Neither do The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach and Spirit of the Century.
But "our" definitions are typically binary and aim to be all-inclusive (to describe everything about a game, instead of a component).
So trad might get used to describe Vampire and AD&D, while indie might get used to describe Roach and Spirit.

Anyways, I'd be happier if I knew how to use terminology that ACTUALLY reflected the qualities of game that were being called into question.
"GMless" and "GMed" are great terms in that regard. Categorizing all games under those labels makes sense. It improves the ease of discussion.
I say that even while recognizing that there are games that don't fit that binary (some games with 3 different player roles, etc), and that games within one category or another are wildly different from one another (Ribbon Drive and the Shab-Al-Hiri Roach, for example).

Stepping outside of the practice of categorizing games altogether seems impossible for us. So, instead, I'd love to know a lexicon of categories that actually describe games and their playstyles meaningfully, that interact with one another, that compliment one another. I'd love to understand games as inhabiting a point in a 3-dimensional space, rather than on a singular line between "trad" and "progressive".

Are those conversations already going on?
Do those understandings of categorization already exist?
If not, am I right in wanting them to?

    \n I don't think that "trad" and "indie" are about games, so trying to label games as one or the other is generally going to be a mess. But really they are used, as far as I can tell, to refer to paradigms of social organization & assumptions about roleplaying in general. They are associated with particular games only in the sense that in some cases the games encouraged one set of organizing principles or assumptions over another -- a very real and useful association, mind you, but not one that is even remotely vulnerable to a criticism based on the unlikeness of Feng Shui and Vampire: The Masquerade. The use of the terms does not imply any relationship directly between any two games, it just implies an indirect relationship having to do with the formation of social principles.

    So I kind of feel like to some degree (unrelated to its general usefulness otherwise) this thread is an attempt to defend a straw man (from JD Corley, no less, there's some irony here somewhere.) I do think the idea of more refined terminology is a nice goal to pursue regardless, but it doesn't seem necessary in order to keep conversations on point -- that mostly just requires the people involved to want to keep them on point, instead of getting up in arms that some people use words differently than they do.

    I do kind of wish people would just get over the fact that terminology is imperfect -- it's not some personal affront, people, it's just how language works.

    \n You could do something of a scale.

    Like GM-Narrator-Director-Referee

    GM is literally master, their word is law, ultimate control
    Narrator is just a step down - the story and pot is in their control
    Director is starting to step on shared story-telling, the players are sort of in Actor-Writer roles, and the director pieces things together
    Referee is pretty much shared storytelling responsibility with a single adjudicator - the role is only really distinct in the case of disagreements

    If there's a GM you as a player can expect input in your own character's actions, but not control of them
    If there's a Narrator you as a player can expect control of your own character, but no input in the story
    If there's a Director you as a player can expect control of your character and input in the story
    If there's a Referee you as a player can expect control of your character and of the story

    That's a lot more useful than GMed and GMless, and usually the term used in the rulebook (unless it's GM) will reasonably reflect that scale.

    Another thing that could be looked at is tagging - have a set of unmarked characteristics, and use d20/D&D as the benchmark - something's only marked if it deviates from D&D, so a game could be described in a list of features that it has as different from D&D.

    diaspora has shared world & setting building, shared character generation, character advancement by change, narrative input by players - those are significant characteristics that stand out

    reign has hit locations, multi-axis task resolution, complete flexibility in random and designed character generation

    heroquest has paragraph and make as you play character generation, fuzzy encounter difficulty, unlisted character traits, conflict resolution

    No, seriously, the "how" is going to depend on what it is that you're describing, who you're describing it to, and why you're describing it in the first place. So there's not going to be one specific way that'll work every single time, and trying to come up with specific ways to cover every imaginable combination is likely to be a total waste of time, too.

    Mostly the art of good description is the art of good education. You have to be able to present the information so that someone else will understand it, which means you'll need to establish what they already know, correct any misconceptions they have (without calling them idiots, preferably), determine from their demeanor and their questions whether they're following along with you, and generally give them the information in whatever format makes the most sense to them. Maybe you can get there by talking, maybe you need to give them something written, maybe you need to show them a quick hands-on demonstration...and if you still get it wrong after all of that, you have to go back and try a different approach.

    Posted By: joepubThis was spurred by Rob Bohl's thread title, where he asked if Savage Worlds was "Forge-derived-y." He asked for people not to quibble with that word choice, and I wouldn't have anyways... but damn, if me and Rob Bohl knew how to express that thought better, that'd be cool.
    \nWell, I think that "Forge-derived-y" is a very fine and exact piece of terminology, myself. You just need to be aware of what you're describing: that term does not capture any game design solutions inclusively, it just asks whether the target game or idea is derived from Forge culture. (The answer in this case is a simple no, incidentally: there is no reason to suppose a link where there is no proof of such, not even any of the idiosyncratic Forge-like mechanical solutions.)

    It seems to me that the general problem in using various terminology is that people are relatively uninformed and lazy, not that there is a particular lack of terminology to use. To begin with, the whole notion of a robust universal terminology is a fool's errand; different discourses have different needs, terminology that would be highly relevant to one might be irrelevant to something else. The fact that "trad vs. indie" doesn't capture D&D 4th edition particularly well (it being a post-Forge mainstream game) does not prove that those terms are vague or somehow bad; it just means that they're not suited to the occasion.

    I tend to find the terms I need myself in rpg discussions from a couple of pretty powerful sources: if pure rpg analysis a la Big Model doesn't do it and the Forge-style historical analysis doesn't do it, then it's likely that I can borrow litcrit terminology or perhaps even boardgame theory terminology and find something useful. I don't remember that any discussion of mine would have been stymied for the lack of words.

    To demonstrate:\n
    Posted By: joepubThe issue: Vampire and AD&D don't belong in the same category. Neither do The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach and Spirit of the Century.
    \nVampire and AD&D have many similarities and differences. I might describe one of the most prominent differences as the difference between "adventure game" and "storytelling game"; AD&D presupposes a game with challenges and mediated interaction to enjoy semi-chaotic outcomes, while Vampire presupposes a dramatic exercise with literary creative goals, coordinated by a GM. Both use similar systematic tools derived from the same sources, such as being party-based, mission-based, GM-dominated, niche-oriented and character effectiveness -oriented, which justifies calling them both "traditional", to pick another piece of terminology. There are also lesser similarities and differences - we can talk of "simulationism" vs. "gamism" to hone in on the creative agenda of the exercise, we can look at literary genres of "D&D fantasy" vs. "neo-gothic horror", we can look into mechanics like "linear roll vs. target number" vs. "dice pool"... there are an arbitrary number of different ways for those games to belong in the same or different categories, and the terminology we choose depends on that. There is no God-model that'd capture the essential nature of individual games for all purposes, or even all purposes likely to be discussed on a web forum. Some people used to try to make GNS into such a God-model, but it just happens to be that saying that a game is "simulationistic" doesn't capture nearly everything that might be relevant about it. Pretending that a single label can exhaustively replace a conversation is naive.

    (Shab-al-Hiri Roach vs. Spirit of the Century, by the by, is a case of "GM-full" vs. "GM-less", as well as "narrativistic" vs. "simulationistic" and "campaign-friendly" vs. "one-shot" and "adventure drama" vs. "comedy of manners" - the similarities of the two include aspects such as "procedurally well-defined" and "liked by many of the same people".)

    \n I have no issue with using the terms 'indie' or 'trad' or 'forge-derived' to communicate some information that would otherwise require a ton of explicit illustration. I think, broadly speaking, that most of us up on the evolution of the hobby know what you mean.

    So my critiques here were not to utterly destroy the terms or to break them down to the point where they are rendered useless, but rather to point out that there are a lot of negative presumptions packed into the definition that are inaccurate and harmful to the well-being of our wonderful hobby.

      Posted By: joepubIn my understanding, games have multiple, overlapping types. Andy used the term "tags" and I like that - you can have ten tags attached to something, and they interact to tell a story about what the content will be. Tags don't replace content, tags provide guideposts about content. Tags are not exclusionary of other tags... most can exist in tandem.
      \nYeah, totaly! I love tags and this is exactly why. For instance, I used to sort pictures on my computer into folders, like Photos/People, Photos/Landscape, Drawings/People, Drawings/Landscape, Drawings/People/Fantasy, and so on. When I discovered I could tag them instead, I jumped ALL over that shit--now I can search for whatever parameters I want within my tags, and get a "folder" composed exactly of my needs of the moment--"medieval, female, mail, or "elf, horse, outdoors" and so forth. And it saves my brainspace from trying to decide what a particular thing "is", which I think is the real ppint for this discussion. Descriptive, not Prescriptive, yo.

      Of course we still run into the problem of definitions; like Joe mentioned he wasn't getting much from my Action/Drama distinction, and to be honest I wasn't exactly married to it either--I was fumbling for words to assign to some concepts that are distinct to me, but hard to define. So someone would probably have to ask me, "what do you mean by 'Drama'?" and I'd have to say something like, "oh, y'know, social conflicts based on tricky relationships and personal issues, as opposed to conflicts that are solved by punching stabbing or shooting."

      But that's OK maybe; the tags act as a start, not an end, to the discussion. There's no debate to be had; you're not defining PTA as a "Drama game," you're describing something about PTA, and if the simplest, shortest terms don't do it, you ask questions and elaborate, until you reach understanding. Which is how communication works, ideally, right?

      Peace,
      -Joel

      \n I am interesting in this notion (being an amateur linguist) and I think it's entirely possible to make a set of tags that describe a game, and I ALSO think it can be done with categories, so long as it's understood the categorical distinctions have a "slop" to them that a full set of tags doesn't.

      As a reinforcing point, consider electronic music and/or metal: they have, within them, umpty-odd scores of sub-genres, and if one like "drum and bass" or "speed metal" then one can pretty safely sample artists within those categories, with little disappointment. Compare that to, say, if "rock" was the only label on the shelves on which ALL that music rested. have fun reading spines and back covers for the next five hours!

      That said, now that tagging is "hot," maybe the way to go is to do the tag build up... and then look at sets of games that accrue the same tags and decide on a descriptive category for such games, while ever acknowledging that it's just the first "navigation level" through which one might drill to get to the fuller tag set, which is just ANOTHER navigation level before you're actually reading the back blurb or executive summary of the game (which is STILL just another layer of descriptive--you won't KNOW the game until you've played it, repeatedly).

      I find those who say, "categories can't describe a game, man!" to be laughable. Why do we call some practices "board games" and others "RPGs" and others "mini games" and others "card games" and others "LARPs?" Sure, there's TONS of overlap and edge cases... but the VAST majority of language users get a pretty clear picture of the activity when we use those terms. And yet we're to throw up our hands in despair when we try to add more refining terms to describe subsets of one of those activities, the RPG?

      Y'all is funny....
      (I'll table the argument that such objections also are threadcrapping.)

      Posted By: Adam DrayWhy are we trying to categorize indie-ness again?
      \nFuck, no one has said we should.
      My lack of clarity in the first post is shitty, perhaps, but I've endeavored to explain what I meant more clearly.

      People often talk about games in a "categorical sense".
      By that, I mean that they talk about games that share certain features or contexts.
      That's natural, and already happens all of the time.
      I'm not proposing we start doing that; I'm noting that we all already do that.

      What I dislike is when our "categorical sensibilities" are informed by binary, judgment-loaded, opaque terminology.
      What I dislike is when our models of categorization are rigid and mutually exclusive.
      I observe that trad & indie are words that get thrown about a lot.
      I don't like it when language is rigid and divisive and un-liberating, and feel that this "categorical sensibility" is just that.
      These are all 100% personal preferences, and if you enjoy the things I dislike, that's 100% fine and you can carry forth.

      What I like is when our "categorical sensibilities" are informed by multiple, overlapping, value-neutral and interactive terminology.
      What I like is when our models of categorization are fluid and modular.
      I like it when our language arms us with the capacity to approach new conversations, especially when it allows us to accept a diversity of opinions in those new conversations.
      These are, again, all 100% personal preferences.

      So, I was wanting to have a show & tell session, where anyone who shares my sensibilities talks about what has worked for them, in conversations.
      If you don't get the importance of that, then this isn't your thread.
      If you don't share my sensibilities, then this isn't your thread.

      Andy mentioned the word "tags". Tagging is a model of categorization that fits with what I'm talking about.
      Tagging is, more or less, what I'm talking about.

      Joel and Johnzo listed examples of specific language that does what I'm asking about.
      Daw65/Dave Wilson linked an example of a site that relies heavily on this sort of thing, to good effect.
      Mxyzplk described better than I have the usefulness of the stuff I'm asking about.

      So, like, people: I'm not suggesting we invent codes, that we re-define indie/trad, that we spark a revolution, that we start doing something new. I'm asking: to the people who enjoy fluid categorical language (as opposed to rigid categorical language, not as opposed to "no categories at all"), what stuff do you like the most?

      \n i find talking about the amount of specificity of action that occurs in a game 's fiction useful -

      like, is this a game wherein the characters are expected to stay in one (fictional) place and have conversations with one another for the whole story? is that what the story is? do the characters seek and capture small animals which they then kill? do the characters have to stay with one another? will i exclude myself from the game if i choose for my character to retire from the adventuring life?

      i want to be clear that this idea is mostly not "what will happen in the story", even if i'm describing that poorly. Mostly, this idea is "what it is possible for a character to do in the fiction, and still be playing this game."

      i like to know what to expect in the way of breadth of experience, and what is expected of me as a participant.

      and also, i like to know what i do at the table, as a player of the game. i guess this runs parallel with the concept of "crunch." Like, is this a game wherein i jack cars by rolling two dice from my available pool, comparing them to a set number, then pushing one of them physically toward another player, or do i jack cars by stating that i jack cars? (oops, slipping into binary mode)

      for the first part, i like to talk about "scope". what is the scope of the game, not in terms like "broad / narrow", but what, in a sentence, happens in a game?

      & for the second part, i'd use Tags like
      complicated dice mechanic (In a wicked age, danger patrol)
      simple dice mechanic (AW )
      bead mechanic (mist robed gate, dreaming crucible)
      collaborative description (ribbon drive, until we sink)
      opposed description or tug-of-war description (polaris)

      there's something that's useful to me.

      thanks for continuing with this thread, joe.
