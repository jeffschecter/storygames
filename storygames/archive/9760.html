This alpha draft needs testing! It got a good workout recently at Go Play NW 2009, but more is always better. If you have a chance to give it a read and especially if you play it, I'd love to hear your feedback.

    \n [I whispered this to John, and he said I should totally unwhisper it.]
    So, I'm reading this and I'm totally jazzed, except for one point. The bit where you pick your uniform colors leaves me pretty cold -- there's a prominent place on the character sheet for that, but unless I'm missing something, it's just "look at how shiny I am," and I feel like it's a boring choice. Like, one that doesn't inform character at all -- and I don't mean mechanically, I mean personality-wise as well. I think you have a fruitful place here to help inject some personality into the character beyond their archetypes, and to put some "lore" (for lack of a better word) into the Danger Patrol by giving meaning to each color.

    A totally off-the-top-of-my-head example:

    Red - Those who wear the crimson color have lost a friend in the line of service, wearing the color as a reminder.
    Blue - Those who wear the color of old Earth's sky are the dreamers of the Danger Patrol, fighting not just to keep the world safe but to make it grander.
    Yellow - Those who wear the color of the blazing sun have completed the Danger Patrol's most grueling training, and wear the color with honor.

    Something like that, or not. I dunno. But if you look at two Daredevil Flyboys, one wearing Red/Blue and another Yellow/Green, it would be cool to think "those aren't different uniforms. Those are different *people*."

    It would also make something interesting happen, where people with the same color didn't just have "we like the same color" in common.

    My last thought is that since you have a two-color scheme, each color could mean something else -- personality & background, or something like that. If it seems weird that a character would pick their own colors, maybe the Danger Patrol presents them with their colors upon joining or somesuch.

    In any case, I suppose it's something to say "Damnit, John, your game has inspired me, and it's just this one thing I feel could be turned up to 11" -- I never talk about games that don't make me want to play them anyway in spite of anything I might say negative against it.

    \n I just fell in love with Ryan Macklin's concept of meaningful uniform colors! Since I'm up at 3:30 in the morning with a 102 fever, I couldn't help finishing out the spectrum: Here are his originals and my additions: (Remember the 102 fever part, and read charitably. )

    Red - Those who wear the crimson color have lost a friend in the line of service, wearing the color as a reminder.

    Orange - The color of fire, and therefore vitality and energy. A Patrol member wearing orange is advertising his personal exuberance and joie-de-vivre in an already exuberant organization.

    Yellow - Those who wear the color of the blazing sun have completed the Danger Patrol's most grueling training, and wear the color with honor.

    Green - The color of the 'green hills of Earth'; a color that some have vowed to see come to Mars. Those who wear green are the terraformers, the world-shapers, the visionaries of a new home for mankind.

    Blue - Those who wear the color of old Earth's sky are the dreamers of the Danger Patrol, fighting not just to keep the world safe but to make it grander.

    Violet - In the tradition of the 'Purple Heart' once given to wounded soldiers, Danger Patrol members are given the right to wear Violet in honor of their own personal sacrifice or injury in the line of duty.

    White - Long a symbol of purity, white is worn by those members of the Danger Patrol who have dedicated themselves wholly to the cause of humanity, forsaking everything in their lives except their duty to the Patrol.

    Black - The color of night, and space. It is a relentless color, as uncompromising as vacuum. Patrolmen who wear black do so as a reminder that justice should be equally uncompromising.

    Nothing restrictive here; obviously. If someone wants to wear Brown/Gray for example, just have the player come up with what those colors mean to their character. The Danger Patrol is a young organization, they haven't had time to develop a huge body of tradition yet.

    Also, (feverish, remember) if you are a Robot or an Alien, colors might mean something different to you than they do to someone else... The potential for people to miss-read your personality or background because they don't know that to Neptunians, Purple is the color of mourning; or that Robots wear Silver instead of Red to honor fallen robot comrades is just good fun.

      \n I ran Danger Patrol last night after our regular game got cancelled. I thought it was fun; the resolution system was easy to apply, and I could see its potential in a longer game.

      It was less successful for the rest of the players. There were a few reasons for that (which I can get into if you want \u2026) but they boil down to:

      + me throwing the game together quickly;
      + the group not being 100% enthused by the pitch;
      + a sluggish first scene (because we were all learning the system); and
      + a feeling that there wasn\u2019t enough opportunity for in-character roleplaying.

      We got through the initial Crimson Ape attack scene and a round of interludes before one of the players said she wasn\u2019t inspired to play any further.

      But there was something deeper going on that we talked about afterwards, and I think some interesting stuff came out of that discussion that\u2019s worthy of consideration.

      The cliffhanger didn\u2019t really grip the players. It didn\u2019t have much significance to their characters, and they felt that there wasn\u2019t enough opportunity to roleplay with each other \u2013 that the action sequence was very much like a boardgame or Capes, where it\u2019s your turn, then the next person\u2019s turn, then the next person\u2019s turn.(*) So, they came up with some proposals(**):

      (*) Personally, I felt that once we\u2019d all gotten the hang of the system, the action sequences were going to take about 5 minutes (max) to get through, and there\u2019d be a nice rhythm to how things played out, but anyway, the proposals are interesting \u2026

      (**) Yep, \u2018proposals\u2019. I would feel bad about pointing out things that were problematic for our group, without offering possible alternatives.

      Establish relationships: Building a section into character creation where players establish the Danger Patrol characters\u2019 relationships with each other would give players something to roleplay off right from the start of the game. Maybe this is like SotC\u2019s \u2018appear in each other\u2019s novels\u2019 thing; maybe it\u2019s way simpler.

      Introduce or Incorporate flags: The starting cliffhanger could be based off things that are of interest to the players \u2013 which leads to the question of what those things of interest are. Or (perhaps) throw it on to the players \u2013 it\u2019s their responsibility to find something in the pre-created cliffhangers that\u2019s of personal significance to their characters?

      Act together: Perhaps, as an alternative to everyone individually declares their actions in turn, the group could collaboratively build up the action sequence? What I mean is that you could have everyone describing what they're going to do this round all together, all of the players building up their own dice pools as they add danger dice. There\u2019d be a whole bunch of riffing off of each other\u2019s Danger and once everybody\u2019s set \u2026 then perhaps you could all roll dice together (or in whatever order seems logical). Then it\u2019s the GM\u2019s turn to assess what\u2019s happened to the threat.

      --- --- ---

      Having played, I think I\u2019ll fine-tune my pitch and describe it as a pulp serial in the style of Raiders of the Lost Ark, emphasizing the way that Raiders alternates action with exposition/roleplaying. I\u2019ll also really hammer home the benefits of danger, encouraging people to take maximum Danger on their first rolls, so they can see how useful it is.(*) Basically I need to emphasise that Danger is helpful, it doesn\u2019t automatically have horrible consequences, and it doesn\u2019t \u2018take you out\u2019.

      (*) The first player to roll was nervous about taking Danger, because he didn\u2019t know if it would screw up his character or not. I was not helpful in my explanation.

      Hmm, but that lack of consequences for the Danger players introduce is something I find interesting, thinking back on it. Players introduce fictional danger into the game (to get dice), but there\u2019s no obligation to resolve those dangers in your narration. There\u2019s a bunch of stuff that strikes me about that:

      + In my one and a half readthroughs of the game before running it, I missed that the GM describes results of successful actions. So, I have a responsibility to reincorporate Danger
      + The group should probably enforce the resolving or riffing off of introduced Dangers
      + Maybe there\u2019s a system-level way of narrating the Dangers \u2013 like, for any dice that come up as giving you Danger, you have to describe what\u2019s gone wrong.

      Two other miscellaneous thoughts:

      1. It wasn\u2019t immediately clear to me whether your rocket-pack, gas mask, et al counts as Stuff that give you bonus dices. I assume not because there\u2019s no check mark next to them.

      2. I know you\u2019ve made some good alterations to the Danger track at the bottom of the character sheet; just as a data point, two of the players had trouble figuring out which box to mark off in the \u2018Stunned\u2019 section (because of the way it\u2019s layed out differently from the \u2018Bashed\u2019 section \u2013 a section we found quite intuitive)

I was really impressed at how much setting we generated so quickly - within those two sequences I had a couple of sessions worth of characters and material.

      I\u2019m also looking forward to running it again with what I\u2019ve learned (next time, I\u2019ll have all the components and characters sheets with me, ready to go).

      Posted By: Steve HickeyPlayers introduce fictional danger into the game (to get dice), but there\u2019s no obligation to resolve those dangers in your narration.
      \nThe main place where reincorporation of danger happens is when the GM makes threat moves. After pretty much every player roll, the GM will get to make a threat move, based on how many danger results the player got on their roll. The threat menu says stuff like "do a reduced hit" or "escalate the situation" etc. These moves are the perfect time to reincorporate the danger that the players just invented and use it as the fictional causes for the hits you inflict and the way you escalate the situation.

      For example, a player might add a dangerous detail like "I'm dodging around the speeding rocket cars." Then, when you have to make a threat move, you have the perfect opportunity. GM: "You punch the ape squarely in the nose. Just as you pull your fist back to hit it again, a robot-piloted delivery truck blindsides you..."

      The idea is, since the GM has to make a lot of these threat moves, the players provide details in their danger dice additions that give the GM material to work with.

      About flags, you said:\n
      Posted By: Steve HickeyOr (perhaps) throw it on to the players \u2013 it\u2019s their responsibility to find something in the pre-created cliffhangers that\u2019s of personal significance to their characters?
      \nYes, that's it exactly. That's what the "Previously On," bits are for, really. The players hear the vague opening sequence (which the GM has not created a backstory for!), then they use their Previously Ons to add dimension and backstory to that situation, on the fly. "You may be my brother, Beastmaster, but you're going down all the same..."

      As for acting together, the main way players interact during the action scenes is by giving each other danger dice. Saying a fun dangerous detail and handing the die over is pretty fun. Of course, if your players were afraid of taking danger (which really makes me chuckle, given the name of the game) maybe that dynamic didn't happen. Which is a real shame, since it's definitely the strongest part of the game, IMO.

      When we play there's also a lot of in-character banter in the classic pulp style. In our local groups, "action" never gets in the way of roleplaying, but I understand that every group is different.

      Thanks again for trying the game and for writing the AP! It's really helpful to hear the details when a game doesn't quite go the way you expect.

      \n Okay, so... I've been convinced by Alan and others that I don't need two kinds of tokens. No more separate Free Action chips and Power tokens. Now they're all just Power tokens. Spend one to activate a power. Spend two to take an extra action.

      The helping mechanic sucks. Here are two patches:

      - When you help, take an action and give your hits to someone else as d8 helping dice. Yawn.

      - After someone has rolled (and taken danger, and the GM has made her threat move), you can spend a Power token to help. Say what you do to help, and give the player your trait die.       \nThreat Moves, Danger 1: Escalating the Threat

      Leaving a danger die on the threat card is mechanically a bit meaningless. Since the player picks how much threat to take, it only matters if they want to go all the way to 6 dice instead of 5. If they want 4, they just pick up 3 and then accept the fourth from the threat.

      An alternative would be to put an actual point of danger on the threat. Next person to attack that threat automatically scores +1 danger. So whatever danger they would have gotten from their roll, they get one bracket higher (you rolled 3 danger, so you take a total of 4 and a full hit).

      An interesting side effect would be that once a threat escalated and had a danger sitting on it, the players _really_ should attack it before it escalates again: if there are 2 or more danger are sitting on the threat it's a real powder keg...

      This also makes splitting a danger 3 threat move into a 1 & 2 instead of spawning a new threat a much more balance choice.

      \n Free Role Abilities: 90% of them are LAME. Explorer is fun and flavorful by providing a bonus die when attacking, talking to, or interacting with bizarre traps, aliens, creatures, etc. Every other basic role ability is really "take a bonus die whenever you use this role, which is your primary role so it's already rocking a d12." Look at Daredevil, it gives you a bonus die whenever you do any of the things covered by Daredevil.

      A little rewording could make these really pop. Consider...

      Agent: [+] when facing sneaky or underhanded threats (good vs. ninjas, seductresses, and sneaky trap doors, not so much against falling rocket cars)
      Commando: [+] when facing multiple opponents with your incredible tactics. (great for troops of mooks, not so good when hacking a computer)
      Daredevil: [+] when fighting against (or in) an environmental hazard. (great on rooftops, or against a laser defense grid, not so much when in a cubicle)
      Detective: [+] when dealing with both extremes of the law. (great with cops and criminals, not so much with beasties or computers)
      Explorer: this one needs no improvement cause it already lets Explorers shine when dealing with cool Explorer stuff like ruins, traps, creatures, etc.
      Flyboy: [+] when facing vehicular threats (good against enemy planes and runaway rocket cars, not so good against individual people or organic creatures)
      Professor: this one is fine too cause it adds to any roll against tech or science related stuff
      Warrior: [+] when facing organic threats (an opposite to the Professor's tech based talent maybe?)

      Some are harder to work out then others, but you see what I'm going for. The [+] to any roll involving the Role would be dandy if the Role was automatically a d12. Since it's already high to begin with, [+] to things the Role deals with regularly would be fun instead.

      Detective - "Criminal kingpins are all the same. It's nothing but pride and greed with them. I'll take the boss man. I know his type..."
      Explorer - "Leave the Venusian Slime Trap to me!"
      Scientist - "I'll handle that security bot. Those RS-9s are so old fashioned!"

      Etc, etc, etc.

      \n Um, just a few thoughts, since I haven't playtested it yet and may not get to it for a while, but really enjoyed reading it:
      1) I love the Style/Role way of creating a character. Very flavorful.
      2) Even though there are only 8 roles listed, I found them somewhat confusing, especially the Commando/Warrior/Daredevil. As a geek, I also felt a little weird that there's a strict distinction between swords and guns -- in a lot of the source material, everybody fights with swords and fists, even though they have destructo-rays hanging from their belts. And the warrior gets a raygun, just like everybody else, and intimidation is a Warrior skill, not a Commando thing. It's not intuitive. Also, I noticed that both the Atomic and Robot styles get super-strength.
      3) The art on page 2 also jarred me -- I liked it, but it said Futurama to me, not Flash Gordon or Buck Rogers. Also, I kinda would like the character sheets to have a paper doll-like outline of the Danger Patrol Uniform, so you could color it in with your chosen colors and also draw your bulgy cyborg arms coming out of the sleeves.
      4) The checklist is neat. I wish more indie games had this.
      5) The setting is neato-keen, but I am left hungry for more! I was thinking it would be cool if there were a solar system chart somewhere in the game that you could fill out as you went, adding ruins and pirate bases, and maybe even a blank filled in here or there.
      6) I was somewhat unsatisfied with the two villainous factions. I like the Crimson Republic, and the hint that the CR dominates Jupiter's moons the same way the Soviet Union dominated its satellites, but their stated goal of mastering atomic energy and making a cyborg army didn't make a lot of sense to me, since there are apparently lots of Atomic cyborgs still around. The Stygians are cool, but I wish you'd leave room for them to either be twisted humans or have human cultists -- more room for repentent Stygian PC's.
      7) On page 9, Billy gives examples of psychological threat cards. Nice, but I'd like to see it used in an example of play, too.
      8) As always, I will speak for the lazy GM's of the world: it would be truly excellent to have half a page or so near the end with a whole buncha phrases taken from Actual Play reports, stuff like the Crimson Apes and the Anti-Robot League, things that the GM and players can grab at will and make their own. I'm a creative guy, but I like to have something to prime the pump.

      \n I gave the Alpha Playtest a whirl last night, and there is an action report elsewhere with comments. However I thought that this was the place for some more specific questions/comments. Some of them are in the report, but I'll redo them here.

      (i) If a move action is risky, the character has to roll dice. What are they rolling against? How are the results interpreted? Is the distance interpreted as a character-specific threat, perhaps, minor for most moves, major for something spectacular? One idea I had was that the roll just needs a single success for the character to make the move, but the danger rolled has all of the normal effects. So you might not roll too many dice, but could end up incurring a few issues on the way. However I would appreciate some examples of 'risky moves' to clarify how they work.

      (ii) Does using a power count as your actin for the turn. For instance in our game Grosnox the robot detective used his built-in weapons power to automatically inflict damage on a space-bat. We counted that as his action for the turn, so he did nothing else. Is that correct?

      (iii) I could really do with more, specific, example of how to run non-combat threats. How does following an enemy agent work, for example? Or investigating the identity of a mysterious professor with detective ability? How should those threats be defined, and how do they cause harm/damage to the characters dealing with them? The idea that these kind of activities are resolved in the same way as action is great, but I don't think that the rules as they currently stand explains them very well.

      (iv) I confess I'm also not sure how the interludes work, and would like to see some more examples.

      (v) The Daredevil has two blank slots for equipment. What goes in there?

      (vi) I like the idea of scrapping the gun effects, and just making the gun an item. I assume that you can use it as many times as you like within your narratives, but that you only count a bonus for it the once. The same for your Danger Patrol equipment.

      (vii) On the subject of the Danger Patrol equipment, we assumed that the goggles and gasmask are to allow you to operate In Space. In our game, combined with the rocketpack, it gave everyone the option to hurtle around the void, although the only character that did so was the robot.

      (viii) We were unsure as to how to handle a damaged vehicle. I just gave any action involving it an automatic extra danger dice, although treating it as injured (fail on a 1-4) makes more sense. Of course that begs teh question of what you do with an injured character piloting a damaged vehicle :-)

      (ix) I'd like to see the powers simplified a little, so that all powers of a particular type operate in the same way in terms of mechanisms, but with different special and narrative effects. So all defensive powers have the same mechanism, whether they are a force-field or demonic block; things like that It makes it easier for the GM to remember what players can do, and easier for the players to remember what they can do as well. Not sure how this might work in practice.

      Anyway, that's all I have for now. When we get chance to play again I'm sure that there will be more questions and comments. Many thanks for a great game; for all the difficulties we had it was good fun, even if my players had a disturbing tendency towards silliness.

      \n Just off the back of the game I ran the other night, and cogitating over various comments about the Danger/Power ratio, here are a couple of unrelated ideas:
      \n
      \nFirst, to incentivize higher levels of Danger, and to add scale to abilities, why not use a variable that is equal to the box your Danger meter currently occupies? So, at 'Bashed', an attack power would only do 1 hit, which would be enough to get you out of tight corner involving a countdown, but wouldn't be majorly powerful. It gets majorly powerful at the opposite end of the scale, where a character in the 'Vaporised' box would do 5 hits when using the same power! The ability to negate hits could instead become an ability to negate a threat move of equal or lesser level, e.g a character in the 'Injured' zone could prevent the GM from escalating the threat, doing a reduced hit or bringing in a new threat.
      \n
      \nAlso, why not use defeated threats as trophies? The player who does the last hit that defeats a threat gets to take it as a trophy; on any subsequent roll, they can include what they have learned or gained from overcoming that threat on one action to let them reroll some of their dice. A mook would let them re-roll only 1 of their dice, but the Arch-Villain could let them re-roll up to 4 dice on one action; it would all depend on the level of the threat when it was beaten. After being used, the trophy is spent and cannot be used again.
      \n
      \nOh, and I remodelled the absolute definition of 'KO'ed' and 'Vaporised' at the moments they happened, as there were only two players and removing one of them from the final scene would have sucked big time! When Dave KO'ed, I 'ringfenced' some of the threats on the table, so that he could not attack them due to extreme brainwashing; he was able to effect a couple of rescues, but Jef was on his own when it came to attacking the supermodels.
      \n
      \nThen, when Dave vaporised on his next action, I simply had him being abducted by one of the supermodels, which also removed that threat from the table, leaving Jef to do everything by himself! We were playing '3:16' the night before, so maybe the Strength and Weakness rules were lingering in the back of mind.
      \n
      \nThinking about it for longer today, I think I would probably generally rule that a KO'ed character is out for one scene, but a Vaporised character is out for the rest of the episode, rather than them being dead and needing a special narrative to re-enter the game. I even let Dave roll his skill as helping dice for Jef, so that he wasn't totally inactive while Jef finished the story.

      \n Just ran Danger Patrol again at Vegas' monthly mini-con. Five of the six players were brand new and loved every minute of it. I believe it's safe to say two RPGAers had their minds blown, or maybe vaporized.

      After the initial action scene I cut to Interludes, but after the Interludes I cut to commercial. I spent about 1min narrating a couple 50s style commercials, ripping off of Fallout pretty hard. Funny thing was, the players kept referencing the products I described during the game. In fact, so many out-of-game jokes referred to these fictional products that I handed out a Sponsor Die when Max "Danger" Armstrong pulled his X9 Turbo Pistol and narrated "a banner runs across the bottom of the screen saying 'Buy your own X9 Turbo Pistol at most fine retailers!!'"

      With the episodic format, "Last Time On", and Interlude scenes, I think it'd be neat to allow players to narrate a couple quick commercials and then offer a bonus die for incorporating a previously mentioned product. It added even more 50s flavor for our group and it's probably something I'll include with Danger Patrol from here on.
