\n In our grid-based Whitehack game I\u2019ve introduced the concept of defensive movement. In a normal attack, the defender gets to move straight or diagonally backwards and the attacker has to follow into the empty space. The defender can stand their ground, which means the attacker gets combat advantage of +2 to-hit and damage. The attacker can attack defensively to not follow up, which means -2 to-hit or damage or -1 on each.

This has neatly solved our previous problem of static fights. What happened before was that PCs and monsters lined up and traded blows until one side fell or the monsters failed their morale roll. What happens with defensive movement is that each new round presents a new situation since combatants have moved around.

Now my question! If I want to ditch the miniatures and grid and go theater of mind, but keep each new round a new situation, what can I do?

One option is to keep a soft version of defensive movement, which works if you fight next to an important piece of scenery. If you attack someone in a doorway, you step through the door as part of the attack and can be attacked by the two monsters waiting on each side behind the door. This doesn't work in the middle of the room, where the grid version would still create new situations.

It's not a requirement to faithfully recreate defensive movement in theater of mind. What I'm after is some way of changing the situation from round to round based on what everyone does to keep players from just repeating \u201cI attack\u201d. I\u2019ve thought about limiting what you can or can\u2019t do based on previous narration, but I haven't found a way to combine it with OSR combat resolution.

Any suggestions on how to avoid static exchanges of blows in theater of mind?

    \n I think you have the right idea: each "move" (turn) should, somehow, affect not just the hit points of the individuals, but also the tactical flow of the battle.

    It may help to visualize any given combat arena as having various tactical features. Even on a perfectly flat, level surface, you could say there is a ridge or a crack, an area covered in sand, or a puddle of blood, which threatens to be slippery.

    When you don't have a grid, then, instead, you can have each move/maneuver change the position of one or more characters relative to these features. You retreat through the door; you push your opponent into the blood slick on the stair; you're now within arm's reach of the statue; and so on.

    This can work mechanically, too, if successful or failed rolls generate modifiers moving forward: perhaps you can choose between striking your opponent extra hard and building or maintaining a sort of "momentum" advantage moving forward, or make other rolls to create bonuses and penalties.

    Also, though, don't forget the standard lessons of OSR-style play, and how to make it exciting. For instance, just keeping hit points low (i.e. low-level play) is enough to make combat exciting, since any one event has the potential of changing things very dramatically. So do things like Morale checks, unusual side effects from interrupted spell-casting, and so forth.

    The higher hit points are on each side, the harder you're going to have to work to keep things dynamic and interesting.

    I also recently read a neat article on differentiating weapon types in OSR-style games. Instead of modifying the amount of damage done, the house rules allowed various weapons different game rules. For example, if you have a sword, you have an advantage against your opponent whenever you win initiative, but a heavy weapon like a mace or hammer gives you a bonus against armoured targets. A small weapon (like a dagger) allows you to make double-damage attacks against an opponent if you've grappled with them - this makes a man with a knife dangerous in a very different way, should he decide to charge you!

    \n Put up narrative obstacles that the players need to describe their ways out of.

    Throw them out of buildings. Tell them to describe how they manage to get back the next turn. Fill the room with flames. Throw them on the ground. Choke them. Introduce environmental obstacles. Let them chase the enemy.

    But most importantly. Hold any descriptions of defensive maneuvers until it's the defender's turn to act. That makes the descriptions more into a flow, and give the defender time to think up a defense.

    \n I want new tactical situations to force the players and me to make new decisions. We can dress up "I hit" into multi-sentence descriptions, but there's still no new decision to be made unless the new fictional situation somehow limits or enables new things.

    Before the grid and the defensive movements, you could write a very simple AI to handle our fights. Line up and trade blows. With defensive movement, you could find yourself flanked as a result of attacking, and it didn't even matter if you succeeded or failed your attack roll. Or you could find yourself off by one square so you couldn't attack the monster you wanted next turn. The AI wouldn't be much deeper, but at least a bit more complex. As I said, I don't need to recreate exactly this, but I want something to happen from round to round.

    One limiting factor, in a sense, I haven't told you about is that I want to run pre-written modules as close to the text as possible. If there's a fire in the room, I can of course use it, or I can add a fire as a minor detail if plausible. But I won't be able to create a whole new set just for the fight.

    I'm not adverse to adding new fictional developments if they force new decision, perhaps by limiting what the PC can do next. But I'm not sure how to tie that into the standard attack roll without stepping on other parts of the system. If, after an attack roll that does 3 of an orc's 5 HP, I add that it drops its sword, I've now given the player a free disarm move. What precedent has been set? "If you lose more than half of your HP... ?"

    I could add status effects like "if the orc hits you with its club and you parry with your arm, your arm is broken either way" or "the orc's sword travels towards your eyes, and if the attack succeeds you're blind". That would change the situation for the next round, but it would be by adding something beyond HP to the attack. Whitehack has these status effects already, but they're tied to wanting to heal above 1 HP without finding a doctor.

    I've actually been sketching on a system for pressing, circling, engaging, etc, since my last post. I'll post it here, but I'm still interested in hearing about how other groups have handled this. I'm not even sure this is how I want to handle it, just that I don't want to go back to trading blows.

    SYSTEM SKETCH
    One side always have combat advantage (+2 to-hit and damage), and this advantage is transferred or kept depending on what you do this turn.

    A normal attack means that the advantage is lost.
    A defensive attack means that the advantage is kept. (-2 to-hit or damage or -1 to both, modified by combat advantage)
    An offensive attack means that the advantage is won. (-2 AC for +1 to hit, modified by combat advantage)

    If someone engages in melee and their side is now outnumbering the other, advantage is won.

    If someone disengages from melee and their side is now being outnumbered, advantage is lost.

    Each member that loses a morale roll counts as 0 for their side. If this means their side is now outnumbered, advantage is lost.

    Win advantage (pressing!)
    - Attack offensively
    - Outnumber as result of:
    -- Defeat on other side
    -- Engage
    -- Force morale loss

    Keep advantage (circling!)
    - Attack defensively

    Lose advantage (cashing in!)
    - Attack normally
    - Outnumbered as result of:
    -- Defeat on own side
    -- Disengage
    -- Lose morale

    RANGES
    Melee (the only range you can fight, usually)
    Near (can easily reach melee, and can atack with weapons with reach)
    Medium
    Far

    To move one range takes one move action. To use a second move action, roll CON with difficulty based on encumbrance.

    \n I do this exact thing in my D&D GMing: dynamic fights, fluid situations, no miniatures. (Well, we actually had a big, fat climatic combat two weeks back, and played it with minis for the heck of it. Interesting change of pace, but a story for another time.) I'll try to lay out some hints:

    First, length and structure of the combat round matters to me a lot. If it's too short, many combat events simply cannot occur round-by-round in good conscience, which encourages a static perspective on what happens during combat. My conceptual round length is "a single clash of arms, from the charge to separation, the length of intense action before the combatants are forced to catch their breath or look around to reorient themselves". I usually call it 60 seconds (post-round maneuvers included) when I need a number for somebody, but that's just a generic approximation; more complex and exhausting rounds are shorter due to the clash of arms being shorter, while calmer, slower types of combat mean longer rounds.

    So anyway, I'm firmly in the "1 minute" end of the D&D combat rules continuum in this regard, more like what AD&D envisions than the 5-second Basic round (which I consider a mistake for various reasons). There are many dynamic consequences. As a single combat round encloses entire maneuvers, the fight will, generally, be different from one round to the next. A group can break through enemy lines during a single round and perhaps even exploit the opening, for example.

    Second point: the skirmish combat environment, formed by multiple melees, is my primary canvas when it comes to varied combat terrain. The standard advice, seen in this thread as well, is to make the terrain in each individual combat unique and exciting - sort of like 4th edition D&D. I don't really pre-plan combat environments, the combats occur wherever somebody draws their weapons, so this isn't an option. Nevertheless, each combat is unique, and the situations shift from round to round, thanks to the variety of melee arrangements that may occur through-out.

    As a core tenet, consider this: during a combat round, every combatant engaged in active melee is actively threatening, and actively threatened by whoever they are fighting with; this is the very definition of engaging in melee, you're not actually doing it if you are not producing a moment-to-moment threat to your opposition. This fact means that whether you're using miniatures or not, the combat is not simply an arbitrary swirl of action; rather, it is a series of duels, chosen by the higher initiative count as they engage the opposition in whatever manner they may. This basic pattern of 1-vs-1 or 2-vs-1 or whatever the nature of the individual engagements has opportunities to shift between rounds, but it is no simple feat to merely abandon an active and bloodthirsty foe; there is a certain amount of stability to each duel, as long as it lasts, until either party is able to break away, or both decide to cease for the moment for whatever reason they may. It's not at all like a menu-based CRPG with the combatants neatly lined and trading arbitrary blows until one side's wiped out.

    Meanwhile, each individual bout as described moves around in the combat space, causing subtle interaction with the environment and other combatants. Perhaps one or both sides attempt to maintain an organized front so as to prevent the opponent from ganging up or sidelining anybody, which then has its implications for the dynamism of the individual bout; perhaps there are walls present, curtailing opportunities sharply. There are chokepoints, and any combatants crowding in on action of others will cause confusion.

    Third point: I play the psychological, social and cultural dimension of combat heavily, which makes individual combats unique even when the terrain is exactly the same. When the combatants have their own ways of doing things, their own reasons for fighting and their own attitudes towards pain and death, there are not only colorful descriptive opportunities, but lots of tactical nuances overlaid on top of the purely positional struggle. For example, consider how the particular combat deeds and their brutality affect the perception of the combatants on each side, causing them to be more or less amenable to a negotiated ending to the struggle. For another example, consider how very little attention a melee-engaged combatant, fighting for their life, can spare for other events occurring simultaneously in a dimly lit combat environment, and how the lack of information, the fog of war, will affect their decisions. All of these elements contribute to a rich tapestry that has little to do with the concrete terrain over which the combat is conducted.

    All of these matters are, I find, about as natural to handle verbally as they are with miniatures, given the right conceptual frame in which to speak about them. Specifically, I avoid speaking about precise positioning in static terms, because the constant footwork of the combatants changes spatial positioning constantly. Instead, we talk about who is ganging up on whom, who is retreating and who is advancing, in which general part of the room the action occurs, and how much attention each individual combatant can or will afford to the events occurring around them.

    Speaking of the details, I started to compile a list of standard concepts like "charging" and "opportunity attack" and "frontline" and "flanking attack" and so on, but ultimately all that goes into the details of the combat system heavily enough that I don't know how useful it'd be. I think it suffices to say that as long as the GM has the authority to decide on circumstances ("their attack pushes you against the wall, preventing a retreat through the doorway"), tactical play in verbal mode does not pose overwhelming difficulties. And if it is possible to discuss e.g. three different melee bouts occurring at once, each with their individual dynamics, then I don't think that it's very difficult to make every combat round unique: last round the enemy cleaved in between your lines, so what do you do now that half of you are cut off from the doorway altogether?

    \n That's an interesting angle here - all this is much more difficult if the GM is not the primary maintainer of the fictional space. This isn't unknown to me by any means, as I run a consensual D&D table (so there's no "it is so because I'm the GM" in my method), but for the most part the play culture seems to be happy with trusting in the GM's impartiality.

    As you said, laying things out in advance is a good basic principle to fall back on. I use that early and often, really, and when players call me on the probity of some decision that I haven't cleared with them at some point, I usually end up falling back on it. Thinking about things, I think that creative impetus is the key thing here: I need to want to do things a certain way and I need to be able to verbalize why I want that, if I want to establish a functional working relationship with the players. It looks something like this, perhaps:

    Me: "OK, you miss the orc with your strike, and as they press you hard, you find yourself back to the wall, in danger of being surrounded."
    Player: "Hey, why's that? I don't like my actions having negative consequences, and I don't see any connection between failing combat maneuvers and lost ground." (I'm caricaturing the position here a bit to make the point clearer.)
    Me: "Hmm, yeah, I see what's going on here - we haven't talked about how I like to do things, because you/I joined this campaign only recently. So I've this thing where I want to have all sorts of chaotic secondary events occur in combat, so as to spice things up and create a background for the players to improvise upon; I don't want combats in the campaign to become too static. Our traditional solution has been to read combat checks for additional information, degrees of success if you will; thus a bad miss on a combat check can become a negative side factor for your character. This is still reasonably objective, because we go from the formal fact of the dice roll to the creative details I provide, yet it's also simple and quick, and the possibilities are limited only by the imagination."
    Player: "I think it's quite unfair that I didn't know about this in advance. The idea you present is reasonable in itself, though, so if I were a saint I'd probably just let you get on with it, instead of trying to preserve my dignity by continuing to complain. After all, by opening my mouth I committed some of my social capital to this, so I can't drop the subject without making myself seem an idiot for taking it up in the first place."
    Me: "Yeah, it's a complex game and we're learning as we go, particularly as the game quite intentionally doesn't have a static rulebook. I definitely do not expect you to know or remember all the rules - I don't myself, and I've at least been here in like 90% of the game sessions where the game's been developed. So how about we forget about that side narration for now, and follow the rule later on? This way I get what I want - a principled and nuanced combat environment - and you get what you want, too, as we acknowledge that your complaint was valid, and got a good talk about the rules we use, too. Plus your character doesn't die pinned by the orcs right here and now; I don't care, I can always kill him later in the next room."
    Player: "Well OK, but I reserve the chance to think about this in length and come back to you with a better combat system."
    Me: "I would love it if you did that. Most of the players traditionally just let me worry about designing the processes, so I'm more than happy to have somebody commit enough to the campaign to walk through these things with me."

    Sorry for how lengthy that was, but I was trying to show a subtle interchange that might help in increasing the GM's authority over the long run: what I do is that I exchange my acknowledgement of legitimate player privileges (that I think are legitimate, I mean - I'm sure some people would abhor my thinking in that regard) for the players in turn respecting the procedures and privileges that I find crucial for performing the GM's job efficiently. Particularly, I will always exchange an immediate bribe for long-term acknowledgement of the rules, because of how very positive and constructive such an exchange is: the player gets to feel that they are being listened to, I'm corrected for my failing of logic or pedagogy (often it's the latter - I'm ruling correctly, but I forgot to tell the player the rule or fictional detail I'm basing myself on), and the actual rules we're following get an airing, so that everybody remembers them better from then on.

    (Do I always reward the squeaking wheel? Yes, so far I have done so, although I will tell the players if I think that they are complaining about nothing. Apparently players childish enough to complain constantly just to get their way do not exist, even my 10-year old nephew respects my authority once I explain the reasoning and promise to be more careful with my descriptions in the future.)

    Consequently, when the campaign goes through reformative periods, the reason for why I preserve the GM's privilege of being the primary conductor of fictional positioning is basically that I ask the other players for it nicely: hey guys, if it's OK with you, could we play so that I keep authoritative track of who's where and doing what? That's just the way I've found to be most effective for our shared agenda of having fluid, fair, detailed combat encounters. In exchange for you humouring me on this I shall continue being very flexible about the matters that you really care about, so it's really a negotiated exchange.

    \n I should also mention a few other details about my fiction-maintaining routine. One is that I always defer to reasonable player assumptions. This means that my personal picture of what's going on and who's where doesn't actually matter that much; it's much more important that we have some non-ambiguous facts than where those facts come from. Consequently, despite my primary responsibility as maintainer of the fiction, I tend to activate the players quite a bit, too. A basic way to do so is to ask questions about the fictive state. The most fundamental one is "Where are you again, now that this thing is going on", but you can ask whatever you need. The way the players answer, and whether they try for illegitimate advantage, will tell you a lot, and asking them to maintain the fiction with you will do a lot to move them away from the mindset of a child and into the mindset of a responsible adult. For the record, my experience is again that nobody intentionally tries to cheat when asked an earnest question; at most the players will make assumptions favourable to themselves on issues that have not been clearly delineated either way, but that's so minor most of the time that I don't really care - you want to be conveniently inside the room, then so be it, and next time I'll ask in advance if I really care.

    Another basic detail about my routine is that I repeat what the players tell me in my own words. This isn't quite as simplistic as it sounds, as I tend to add the mechanization of the declared action, and the expected consequences, on top of the communication. It looks like this:

    Player: "I'll try to break in the door."
    Me: "So you'll try to push the door open by sheer weight, throw yourself against it. Cool, roll a strength check and we'll see how it goes. DC is 20 for an instant success, 15 spends several minutes banging at it before it goes down."

    When this habit of communication gets entrentched, it helps out a lot in filling out communication gaps, which in turn makes it much easier to maintain complex fictional maneuvers in purely verbal play. There are several effects here, one of the neatest of which I think is the way the means-goals dichotomy gets filled: players are in the habit of declaring either their means or their goal, but not both, so if you repeat what they said back at them from the other perspective, you'll ensure that every resolution cycle only hits the table once both the means and the goals have been explicitly uttered. Like so:

    Player: "I'll try to open the door by force"
    Me: "So you'll try to batter it down, then, slam your bodyweight against it."

    Player: "I'll throw my weight at the door."
    Me: "So you're trying to open it by force."

    Another cool effect is that your restatement of the player action catches mutual misunderstandings - either one of you might be basing your understanding of the situation on flawed details, so the restatement ensures that you're close enough to correct on both sides to avoid having to argue about the results after the dice hit the table.

    Yet another advantage is that even if the player didn't invoke any particular rules, your restatement can do so, and the player has an opportunity to query for detail or cancel the action before they move on to execution. This is also a natural spot for you to explain the consequences of the action - what rolls are needed, how those rolls will be interpreted, whatever.

    I make "initiative" something that pays off fictionally, by giving that side some leeway about their fictional positioning. Sometimes this is more important than at other times: for instance, if the battle has two groups meeting on a bridge, the group which won initiative can decide whether they meet their enemy in the middle of the bridge, on one side, or the other.     * Grab the initiative, throwing yourself into the chaos of melee!

    or

    * Cede the initiative, choosing where to make your stand before the enemy strikes.

    I use that choice to really drive it home.

    This might still be too loose for you, of course!

    2. You could build "offers" into combat. Make them interesting enough, and you could get a lot of action happening.

    One idea is the "stunt".

    Option A: You say what you want to achieve (like a special type of attack, tripping the enemy, disarming them, etc). You make two attack rolls. If you succeed on both rolls, it happens! If you only succeed on one, you get what you wanted, but to lesser effect, and you're left in a difficult situation (your position changes). If you fail both, things go really badly for you, and something unexpected happens against you.

    Option B: You name what special thing you want to get (a battle maneuver), and the GM names something really bad which could go against you. If you succeed, you get the thing you wanted; if you fail, the GM gets the thing she wanted.

    -"I want to jump onto the dragon's back, and stab it through the weak spot in its armour!"
    -"Ok, but if you miss, it will pin you to the ground with one of its legs."

    Another idea is to simply build a "Devil's Bargain" into each roll that is made. This simply takes the form of the GM making an offer of a bad thing happening and a corresponding bonus. If the player agrees, they get to take advantage of the bonus.

    This could work with the bargain being based on the roll (e.g. on a failure) or always happening.

    On an attack: "You're lunging at the Orc? Ok, you can get +2 to damage by really leaning into it. But if you fail, your missed attack will carry you past the Orc, effectively switching places."

    On a defense: "The Orc is swinging its axe at you! Do you hold your ground? Or you can choose to give way, moving back to top of the staircase and that will give [you a +4 to defend/the Orc a -4 to hit]. Do you want to?"

    It would allow the heroes to face more dangerous enemies, but only in exchange for a more dynamic/less controlled field of battle.

    \n Mechanical formalism is a fine way to go if one desires a regulated system of play well known in advance. 4th edition D&D is all about achieving this, for example. The downside is that once you have pre-determined what matters mechanically and what doesn't, the incentive to care about the concrete events in the fiction disappears. Play will focus on the numbers and their simple, dignified interplay.

    But then again, on the other hand formalism does have big advantages to it in the old school context as well: this type of game requires that a massive amount of interpretation and arbitrary decision-making is funneled through the GM's desk, with the GM querying the fiction and making even-handed referee calls on everything from the weather to the positioning of furniture. Mechanical formalisms are absolutely core to abstracting out of certain types of arbitrary judgement tar pits that would otherwise become distractions or even show-stoppers. The idea that a character becomes non-capable of combat at 0 HP is an universal D&D example; instead of asking the GM to adjudicate whether a knife slipped under the rib distracts combat performance, and how much, the rules go mechanics first and merely ask us to fill in the fictional blank between "I attack him with a knife" and "he lost his last hit point and therefore became a combat casualty".

    Due to the above observations, my mechanical ideal for this sort of D&D is to design locally, and design clear concepts that abstract over the tar pits of judgement, without paving over interesting organic decision-making with the abstractions. This requires a certain adherence to a golden mean, as we have some powerfully abstract concepts (HP, XP, levels, HD, Ability scores...) around which much of the game revolves, yet we do not want these concepts to become the entirety of the game: a four-legged foe should still be more difficult to trip than a two-legged one, being prone should still matter more or less depending on what you're trying to do, and so on, for doing otherwise means that we might as well not talk about tripping at all.

    Incidentally, it occurs to me that for Jonas's purposes it might be useful to think of verbal tactical play in terms of "tags", sort of like FATE does it: individual fighters and bouts basically have traits that justify certain GM decisions, and that become the ever-sifting "terrain" of combat. This is basically what we all do while adjudicating verbal tactical situations, I think, except we don't usually write the status tags out explicitly. Nothing prevents one from doing so, though; I could see that a group struggling with the particulars of who gets to say what (and how much authority the GM has) could really benefit a lot from a procedure where fictional facts are written out before being referenced.

    To clarify what I mean, imagine if Jonas had that whiteboard at the beginning of combat, and he started the combat by querying who attacks where, so as to get an initial lay of the land.     Player: "Why am I fighting next to a bush, Jonas? Are you trying to fuck me over, Jonas?"
    Jonas: "Why no, I just rolled 1/3 for each melee to find out - I don't have a map or any particular idea of where the bushes are, I just know that there are some.     Now, what I'm trying to explain here is that what Jonas did here was not distributing bonuses or penalties to the combatants - instead, he's merely distributing environmental factors, and it still remains to be seen whether these factors come to matter at all; the shrub could be mere color, or the fighters might come to utilize it in some way that matters for the fight.

I could well see how a group with authority issues might benefit from this sort of technique.

If the major problem of not being able to have fictional positioning without miniatures (which isn't really fictional positioning at all) is solved first, then the rest is just fine-tuning.

    \n For me the notion of adding side-effects to attack rolls on a good roll has been just about perfect - characters get to "stunt" when their to-hit roll surpasses the target AC by more than 5, 10 or 15 points, and the stunts get to be bigger at each tier. This achieves much: combat becomes more dynamic as characters do more varied things than just hitting each other; higher level characters are actually more effective due to higher to-hit bonuses accruing more stunts; many traditional D&D combat maneuver ideas like "mighty blows" and "cleaves" and so on are simplified greatly, as are situational combat tricks like knocking an enemy into fire or webbing, as all are handed via a general stunting system; players are encouraged to pay attention to the fiction and use the details to their advantage, as the fictive context determines what sorts of stunts you can try in each situation; I get all that without complicating the game, as the stunts system recedes elegantly out of the way when and where we don't want to pay attention to the detail.

    Combat maneuvers in lieu of attacking are generally a bad idea, as instead of supplementing the hit point economy such additions to the system steal attention away. They are also difficult to balance, as either hitting to damage is still better than doing the combat trick, or it's not, in which case hit point damage falls to the wayside in favour of wrestling or whatever. (Worst thing: the more HP an enemy has, the more useful a non-HP combat effect is, which means that alternative attacks are difficult to balance over the entire level range.) For these reasons I greatly favour the notion that hit point damage is an abstract representation of various sorts of combat maneuvering, supplemented by the occasional specific highlight in the form of stunts or whatnot. You can't choose to not cause HP damage so as to be more effective in some other way in combat, as causing HP damage is already a representation of you being effective.

    An alternative approach to stunting in this sense could be to simply say that each HP of damage implies a certain amount of tactical gerrymandering occurring. You could have a simple table of effects as a guideline so that disarming a single-handed weapon may happen when you cause 3 HP of damage, tripping an unready opponent for 1 HP, and so on. This isn't buying an effect instead of HP damage, but on top of it, like a poisonous snake's poison comes on top of the HP damage. It's basically the same as my system that plays off the to-hit roll, but might work better for some mechanical contexts.

    But anyway, let's say that we're happy with the assignment of terrain, and adept at describing it to the players and keeping track of the various possibilities. How, exactly, does a D&D combat get from here to a varied and dynamic style that abhors "I hit" action declarations? My answer is clearly that all sorts of combat maneuvering should be applied on top of the routine of HP-exhausting attack work. This is exactly what almost all D&D rules frameworks already do regarding movement: they allow characters to both move and attack during the same turn. (If anything, many texts are much too liberal about allowing movement under melee conditions.) I think that the secret to dynamic combats is in accepting that not only movement, but also all sorts of other things occur during combat, all without interrupting the basic beat of attacks shaving off HPs.

    Why is it paramount to keep those attack routines moving: the HP attrition is the central way for a combat to end, so it forms the fundamental pacing device for combat. We want that average of 50% hits for 1d6 damage each to occur reliably each combat round so that combats drive inexorably towards their end. Holding onto this basic principle ensures that the D&D combat keeps doing the single thing it does best: low-level combats are quick and brutal affairs, while higher level combat scales into a lengthy, cinematic movie choreography. This part of the game requires no changes, the math is just fine as long as we don't introduce the notion that characters should spend most of their time on status effect attacks instead of causing HP attrition. 3rd edition D&D, as we know, breaks down in this specific way: because the game includes a massive number of attack-like status impositions (a fancy way of saying "save or die spells rule the roost") that are much more effective than HP damage could ever hope to be, the conflict system revolving around the hit point currency loses its meaning, and the game becomes mathematically ungovernable at higher levels.

    I suggested a few basic ideas for how maneuver play can be hooked into the basic to-hit or damage rolls, but let's look at it from a few more angles yet:
    * Missed rolls, "whiffs", do not do anything in D&D combat. This is a good thing because turns are short and the static beats are a chance for the opposition to seize initiative. There's no particular reason for secondary combat effects to trigger on misses as well as hits.
    * Every action causes potential changes in the combat environment, but not all changes are equal: it is generally agreed that a character can opt to go on the defensive or join a combat without a successful dice roll, while pushing, disarming or tripping an opponent, even if possible in the given rules treatment at all, is not usually a mere matter of declaration.

    What would the bare minimum treatment of combat maneuver rules look like? Assuming short rounds and a naive initiative scheme (basically round-robin, the way simplest D&D treatments work), I think that it's best if a player may (voluntarily) describe their attack in tactical terms, and if the attack roll succeeds by a specific margin, they get their hoped-for tactical result on top of HP damage. For example:
    Tripping: characterize your attack as attempting to over-run or literally trip your foe. If you hit AC+2, the opponent falls down, but your attack causes 1 less damage.
    Disarming: characterize your attack as a precise strike at your opponent's weapon, attempting to loosen their grip. If you hit AC+4, the opponent drops their weapon, but your attack causes 1 less damage.
    Forcing space: characterize your attack as driving your opponent back. If you hit AC+0, the opponent is forced back by the attack. Otherwise they stand their ground.
    Shield pin: characterize your attack as pinning an opponent with your shield (against a wall or the ground or whatnot). If you hit AC+2, the opponent is pinned, but your attack causes 1 less damage. (Being pinned is a minor penalty for both attack and defense, as long as it lasts.)
    Softening up: characterize your attack as a series of furious feints intended to drive a slower opponent to distraction and exhaustion. You cause minimum damage on a hit, but hitting AC-2 suffices.
    Defensive fighting: characterize your attack as taking opportunities against an aggressive opponent, while being careful and falling back under their assault. You lose ground and only cause half damage on a hit, but if you hit AC-2 you gain +4 to your own AC for the round against this opponent.

    Note that if you hit AC, but do not reach the elevated target number, you still got a normal successful attack in terms of HP damage, it just didn't achieve your intended tactical effect. Also note that it is simple to set target numbers here: just pick a number that reflects the fictional difficulty of the suggested maneuver, with perhaps a bit of an eye towards supposed game balance. I also included the idea that successful maneuvers might cause a bit less damage in some cases; you could run with that, or drop it and go with full damage all the time for even more streamlining. I intend this system to be used improvisationally, so while you might keep track of popular maneuvers and how they've been adjudicated in the past, it's thoroughly legit to make a ruling on the spot for a situation that hasn't come up before; players should have a subjective right to make up sensible combat maneuvers on the spot, rather than having to reference a rule book to pick from a narrow set of predetermined options.

    This set-up is simpler than my earlier suggestions because it relies on a traditional lumped action declaration: the whole action is described in advance of the roll. It is superior to traditional alternative attack maneuvers because it is thoroughly situationally optional, and it encourages tactical acumen without being particularly overpowering. It is still OK for low-performing players to just go "I hit it with the sharp thing" seven times in a row, but because it is strictly better to describe your attack in terms of tactical achievement, players are encouraged to pay attention to not only who they attack, but also how they attack.

    Once you have something like this at hand, every conventional detail of the battlefield becomes a potential source of a clever in-combat stunt: if there's a wall, you can limit your opponent's footwork to gain some situational bonuses; if there's dust, you can throw it in their eyes once they've tripped you; if there's furniture, you can move around it to control distance for defensive purposes. Because doing this costs you nothing in terms of "I hit it with the sharp thing", while potentially having lots of effective consequences (anything ranging from minor circumstance modifiers to enemies outright missing combat rounds or even dying due to circumstantial hijinks), players will be naturally trained into attentive, active combat choreographers.

    \n I myself favour a golden mean between describing in advance or after the roll. Specifically - and this is with our 1-minute combat rounds - it is normal in our campaign for the combatants to declare their intent (what you're doing in general terms) and means (what technical, tactical, environment factors play into it) in advance, while saving the outcome (how it plays out in dynamic interaction with enemy reaction) for after the roll.

    The above may seem like a no-brainer, but due to the way our system supports combat maneuvers as both a means and an outcome, there is actually a certain intricate logic to it that aids us greatly in supporting an intricate combat environment. Consider these examples:

    Example #1

    The party is engaging in combat in a dark cave against ghouls. The ghouls have cut off their retreat.

    GM: Who's next in the initiative order?
    Secretary: Player A, Sir.
    GM: Very well. Player A; what will you do?
    A: I'll bait the ghouls by dropping to my knees - actually just one knee, gripping to my sword on the ground - and begging for mercy. Just as they react, I'll spring forward, slash viciously and attempt to break in between them and on to freedom.
    GM: Very good. Make your attack roll and we'll see what happens. You'll take a -2 for the lightning conditions and -2 for the fanciness of the maneuver, but it does enable you to try to break through. Speaking of which, also make a supplementary tumble check against DC 23 - 20 base, +3 for the second ghoul - as I want to see whether you manage to sucker the ghouls so as to not draw opportunity attacks.
    A: I got 24 for the attack roll and 28 for the tumble check, Sir.
    GM: The attack hits, but no stunts. The tumble check indicates that your maneuver was a fine success: you surprise the ghouls, bash one in the face with the guard of your sword - roll damage, by the way - and run from them before they manage to close their ranks.
    A: Do I get stunts on the tumble check, Sir?
    GM: No, because it was not an Action, merely a supplementary check. It was basically the thing you bought for that -2 to the attack roll, the opportunity to get through the ghouls without being massacred by their claws.
    Secretary: Sir, why did you not have the ghouls make a reactive Wits save to see through the ruse?
    GM: It would have been a legit choice, but too much operational overhead for what amounts to a minor combat maneuver in a combat where I sense the group wishes to advance fluidly. The tumble check gives us sufficient sense for the imponderables of the situation to proceed.
    Secretary: Very good, Sir!

    Example #2

    Same situation, except the player just happens to formulate their action declaration a little bit differently...

    GM: Who's next in the initiative order?
    Secretary: Player A, Sir.
    GM: Very well. Player A; what will you do?
    A: I'll attack the ghouls viciously, Sir. I fight with a sword and shield.
    GM: Very good. Roll for attack, remember to take -2 for the dim lightning conditions.
    A: I got a 30!
    GM: The attack hits, two stunts if you want them.
    A: OK, let's see. I suprise them with my aggression, shouting in their face as I drive them back. For one stunt I get through them to the other side, for the other I draw their attention to me.
    GM: Both fine stunts, very well. The ghouls growl and the smartest one, Joker, accuses you of playing too rough. They're helpless to stop your swift movements, but most of them turn to pursue, intent on currying favour in Joker's eyes.
    B: My turn next, I'll be sure to use the distraction. Good work, A!

    \n There are two points that I tried to illustrate with those two examples:

    1) The action declaration system is fluid, it is not reliant on the players communicating according to a formal form. Everything in the way I construe D&D is built around enabling player communication, which is why the combat maneuver system is syntactically redundant: the same fictional ideas can be expressed and executed in different order and with different mechanical implementations according to need. The system is not reliant on the players knowing and "gaming" it; it is purposed from the start to also support players who focus on fictional tactics and let the GM referee them.

    2) The system encourages tactical gerrymandering by having multiple points that actively require the players to acknowledge and account for them: before the attack roll the players have the opportunity to describe their action in a way that garners situational bonuses or reduces penalties, or they can even construct special actions that the GM needs to make a ruling on on the spot, like in example #1. After the roll the dice themselves talk: if you rolled well, the stunt system actively encourages you to play the good roll for all it's worth, which means giving the combat choreography more meat to its bones.

    You can hopefully see how the system does not really engage the question of whether actions are described in advance or after the roll - either is fine! It achieves this freedom by having a solid baseline default action (roll to hit AC, deal damage on success), a rulings-making referee (who can interpret fictional events into mechanics in real time) and a consequence-system for the dice roll (the stunting) that encourages utilizing tactics for added benefits.

    Applying this kind of thinking to your situation, I would in your stead attempt to support both before-roll and after-roll descriptions. Just, you know, be natural about it: before the roll we don't know yet how it will go, so it is more natural for the players to talk in terms of goals and means. After the roll we have a formal basis to construe success or failure, so that's a good time to talk in definite terms about what does happen. Before the roll it is "I want to break through to the other side of the ghouls, and here's how I do it" while after the roll it is "Clearly this round of combat went my way, let's figure out what sorts of good things happened, exactly."

    Concretely, the rules conceit that encourages pre-roll descriptions in D&D is circumstance modifiers: if you consistently acknowledge what the players say, and not just in a "good boy!" way, but by integrating their ideas into the mechanical tapestry, then that encourages them to be more active about it. This is not about rewarding good description, note, but rather good tactics. Do you give a character a +2 to an attack roll when they have an opportunity to charge to melee from farther out, giving them dangerous forward-moving momentum and aggressive initiative? Should you?

    (Note, I specifically chose the idea of a "charge" as my example here because it has something that those Whitehack combat maneuvers do not: it is, in its traditional form, represented as an unambiguous mechanical bonus instead of a fiddly little mechanical trade-off. This is fundamentally a different thing - that charge bonus is saying that your fictional tactical choices can make you more powerful in combat, instead of saying that combat is a finely balanced mini-game where every choice has been carefully balanced to make it useful only after careful mechanical optimization.)

    Similarly, the best way to encourage post-roll description is, in my experience, the "stunt system" I've described: when the players roll well, let them add in some more tactical detail to the game. I don't often characterize some rule as outright excellent, but with this one I just don't see the downside: because the players don't roll well all the time, they don't get to trip their opponents on every attack, and because human psychology naturally desires to use what one deserves, even the most passive player will do their utmost best to turn that initial "I hit them" into a detailed action the one time they roll a natural '20'. The idea of using tactics to improve your position just flows better for the players most of the time when they have already "earned" the right to have good tactical consequences by having already rolled the die.

    For mechanical suggestions, how about you try these two little things on top of what you have right now:

    1) Whenever an attack roll hits AC+5 or better, let the player choose a "wildcard" tactical maneuver: this can override whatever they declared in advance, as long as the action still features the attack against the indicated opponent. You can justify the extraordinary privilege (in the context of your system, I mean) of getting to choose the action after the dice roll with the notion that sometimes lucky things just happen.

    2) Use circumstance modifiers on action declarations, and suggest them yourself to the players when situations appropriate for using them come up. Keep them small-ish for the most part, but strive for consistency, and favour things that represent real tactical advantages that characters can get from fictional positioning and massaging their action declarations in interesting and relevant ways. For starters, at least give a
    +1 per each additional combatant when multiple characters gang up on one;
    +4 when attacking from surprise;
    +2 on a charge;
    +2 for favourable armament interactions (armed vs unarmed for instance);
    +2 against a disarrayed or scared foe

    I loved the idea of an ever-changing scenario for fights, without static trading blows, and I was wondering how to maintain that without the need for a grid.

    Actually, there is so much focus in tactics, with a grid and the houserules described by Jonas Ferry, that is hard to imagine how to maintain such weight in tactics while dropping the map/grid.

    So I came up with some abstract zones: divide the playing characters and the enemies in rear, body and front, give each zone different prerogatives (for example you're hard to be attacked in rear, somehow protected in body, and exposed but aggressive in front), and regulate the movements between zones with simple rules (e.g. attacks in melee push you forward in the grid, a successful attack against you might push you back, etc.).

    The tactics remain (for example, an enemy in the rear ranks targeting characters with a ranged weapon or spells must be taken out from the Front of the PCs' zone, but getting to the front means getting exposed and having to resort to multiple attacks, round by round, to advance...), while the grid disappears in favor of an abstract representation of the battle field.

    \n Success! Or at least a partial success that we can keep improving on.

    In yesterday's session, the PCs were ambushed in a war-torn forest by a band of four goblins with bows and swords. It's been a while since we last had combat, so we were all a bit rusty on exactly which house rules were in play at the moment. I keep a master list of house rules, but we're always trying small and large things out.

    I introduced the rule that if you (PC or monster) roll AC+4 you get a bonus special action in addition to doing full damage. This went over very well, and was relevant in two exchanges: one where a goblin attacked a PC and one where a PC attacked a goblin. The goblin attacked a dwarf PC and in addition to doing damage tripped him, securing combat advantage for his side the next round. The second goblin in that fight stabbed the lying dwarf in the neck, almost killing him (successful save vs death roll). The PC who got a special attack killed the goblin anyway by doing high damage. I was about to suggest that he fling the body into the other goblin, but he was already describing a cleave maneuver (not part of the AC+4 rule, but a rule for the Strong class in Whitehack) and killed the other goblin anyway.

    The only complaint from the players, and I agree, was that using only the post-roll bonus actions remove any tactical decision, shifting it into pure luck. You can't trade damage for the advantage of tripping someone, you just have to get lucky. I didn't feel that shifting the AC+4 declaration to before the roll as well would make a big difference, as it's still down to just the roll of the dice. But we have the old rule of adding a guaranteed special maneuver for -1 damage on the attack, and using this together with AC+4 will give both a tactical choice and surprise bonuses from time to time.

    I picked AC+4 instead of +5 because all other modifiers are +2 or +4, so I like to avoid a special case. We'll see if these maneuvers will be too common, but I don't think so.

    I didn't use Eero's second suggestion of many small modifiers, instead keeping the house rule that one side always has combat advantage (+2 to hit and damage), and that this advantage can shift sides depending on what happens. You can't gain advantage and use it in the same round, but you can maneuver yourself fictionally to gain advantage this round and use it the next round. This combat advantage is huge and makes combat much more deadly. The PCs had 2 hit dice on average, bing level 2, and the two goblins hit the dwarf with 1d6+2 damage with their short swords for a total of 7+7 in a round. Hopefully this will make the players fight hard to gain combat advantage and I can try to take it back for the monsters, using the fiction to describe how and why it shifts.

    There are other parts of combat that can be improved. I was better at describing the environment, but it still turned into only color narration and the combat could still have taken place on an open plain for the effect the forest had. I didn't allow or limit attacks, or hand out modifiers or combat advantage, depending on trees in the way or underbrush affecting movement. I think I will try to tie the environment mainly into shifting combat advantage, and that using the environment as part of the description can shift advantage to your side. This combat the goblins kept the advantage the whole fight, mainly through two critical failures from the PCs, but after the first PC death in the "new" combat system, I'm sure that the players will fight hard for combat advantage.

    The post-roll bonus action was indeed a nice addition, and it effortlessly turned the exchange of blows into "now the dwarf is on the ground and the goblins are on top of him", which was nice. This is exactly the kind of shifts I'm looking for between rounds. With a pre-roll tactical choice, this looks like the correct path forward.

    \n Leading by example seems to be a good thing. I remember trying to come up with clever and inventive tactics for my "monsters" (within the limitations of their intelligence and organization, of course), which would really shift the battleground. But then it helps that I use a "monster generator" which determines relationships between my monsters, and tells me whether they are smart, capable of coordinating tactics, and so forth.

    For instance, I knew that my dungeon had a large, frightening beast, and a group of weak but intelligent humanoids that this beast hunted.     \n The "more developed form of combat" is definitely not to support high level play; if anything, it's the opposite, as the mainline of D&D rules focuses too much on unrealistically high level play, leaving low levels lacking in appropriate detail. Most of my complex combat rules (compared to Basic D&D, I mean - it's still more streamlined than most D&D editions) deal with basic combat situations that are the bread and butter of low-level play.

    As for how I embrace high level play, it is simply by accepting the mechanical premises of the game and running with them, seeing what they bring: more hit points evidently (1 hit die per level is the deal), slower level-ups (double the xp for each level), higher bonuses on rolls (e.g. +1 to hit per level for fighters) and more special abilities for everybody. It is a journey of discovery, and I try to engage it without forcing the game to follow some preconception of mine (such as an insistence that combats need to be quick and deadly because they were that a low levels).

    If you're asking me what high level play is like and how it differs from low level play, I'll have to say that I do not know, for I have never yet reached high level play. We've only been to mid-levels a few times. For what it's worth, mid-level play differs from low level play in that lethality goes way down and adventures begin more from player character positioning and less from generic hooks. Combats tend to be more deliberate and cinematic, less random and awful. You evaluate risks more and take less of them.

    My hypothesis is that "low level" and "high level" are sort of absolute ideals for the D&D conception of combat (and action adventuring in general), and the actual game occurs in a dynamic procession between these end-points of a continuum. The abstract ideal of low level combat is a single attack roll deciding everything, while the high-level ideal is an economy of hit points that makes the variability in attack roll outcomes statistically irrelevant, leaving behind mere resource management. One of those systems should be very usable for a gritty, nihilistic game of war; the other, a carefree, romantic swashbuckling wuxia game. Neither of these absolutes is practically relevant by itself to playing D&D, but in combination they form a campaign arc in which the relationship of the player character to the dangers around him slowly transforms. The interesting bit is that there is no formal transference at any point, it's all just in how the game mechanics interrelate to the world: at some point you'll just notice that hey, I'm no longer the adventurer who has to be afraid of a pit trap or a kobold's spear - I've outgrown these mundanities and become the Monkey King.

      \n Resounding success last session! We had a fight I really liked, and that I can hold up as an example to myself in the future.

      Before the session, I emailed two principles to the group, and at the start of the session I wrote them on the whiteboard:

      1. In combat = free maneuvers

      2. Setting = decide on the spot and write in chronicle

      The second principle has nothing to do with this discussion, and was prompted because I feel like the world in the module we use was barely defined, and that we hand wave a lot of stuff instead of making a firm decision. What I mean is that at the start of each combat round, before stating "I attack", we all look at the situation and the player or GM decides what the next obvious thing would be. The free maneuvers refers to the fact that you're free to do anything you like, and not just pick an option from a list of special maneuvers. I want to stress that Whitehack clearly says that the list of special options are codified examples and that you can do anything, so it has less to do with the game than with me and the group.

      \n Sounds like good dungeoneering to me. I suspect that the existence of a purposeful strategic overlay to the action (the purposeful sneaking plan, and a specific goal to the expedition) helps in building towards more meaningful tactical action as well. Nice to hear that you're cohering towards a toolset that works for your group!

      Regarding the drawbacks you're pondering: I use my own equivalents of both of your described stunt mechanics, which means that we do indeed need to distinguish between them before the roll. However, in practice and over the long term it's not really that the GM needs to ask the player every time which they want, because the players just get used to describing their actions in terms of the fiction and the rules: if the player gives you a maneuver, then obviously they want to prioritize that (and thus take the -1 to damage, as per your system), and if they give you an attack description (either "I attack" or something more verbose that boils down to the same thing), then equally obviously they want to do the opportunistic thing and only make a maneuver if the dice give it to them.

      In practice you'll want to keep asking the players for it (I prefer to formulate this as "do you want to prioritize damage or maneuver?", but you probably have your own phrasing) until one or two of them really get it and start using it purposefully to their advantage. At that point you can just stop asking and let the players naturally observe each other using maneuvers. It won't slow the game down anymore at that point.

      Note that the -1 to damage that you consider a tactical tradeoff here will probably shift in value as the tactical environment of the group matures: as the players learn to utilize tactical options more fully, they will come to view that -1 to damage as a negligible price to pay for all sorts of reasonable, fiction-based opportunities to lay status effects and sow confusion. I draw attention to this because I think that it's impossible to "balance" that trade-off in any meaningful way: the most you can hope for is what you now have, a nominal +1 to damage for a character who limits themselves to a a simple "I attack".

      In case my experience with a similar system is any indication, the real limit to using combat maneuvers is not the price paid for them (assuming that the price is generally to the low side), but rather the availability of tactical opportunity: some situations simply have more opportunity for various derring-do than others. I think that this is precisely as it should be, too: when players think up clever tactical maneuvers, it should be beneficial to use them, and when there aren't any available in the fictional situation, they shouldn't just be invented out of whole cloth. That organic variety is precisely the virtue I see in a task resolution based combat system like D&D's.

      From this perspective I think that worrying about maneuvers being used "all the time" or "not enough" is somewhat superficial: the real question is whether the rules support players in latching onto fictional details that enable clever and fun maneuvering. Once they do, the amount of maneuvering finds a natural, pleasing level that is not too much or too little by simply being whatever amount of maneuver is found appropriate by the group in a given fictional situation.

      \n Interesting thread! When my players resort to the "I attack" description I ask them back questions like "How do you attack? How do you hold your weapon and what are you aiming at?".       Then comes the roll, which will usually inform me of the rest. I take the PbtA approach here: a high roll will include something that happen by chance and opens an opportunity for that player, for the next or even for the whole group. A hit resolves itself well per usual.

      A miss is the perfect opportunity to depict the opponent doing something awesome. Why wait until you roll a crit as a GM or force the evil guy into finally doing something cool and evil, if you have golden chances like these? Does it make sense that a seasoned adventures could miss an attack? I feel like it's mocking the character and even the player choices by extension. Not that doing that it's part of the fun for a lot of people but there will be plenty of chances for that later, why ruin a good combat with moments of poor PC performances?

      And finally a fumble is a perfect opportunity to spell doom for all players. On a fumble I usually narrate how something new comes to threat the whole group. A new hazard changes the terrain disposition, sign of reinforcements appear on the vicinity, the boss makes an area attack, etc. That usually feels more fair than crippling one PC, destroying their weapons and other bad stuff. Not that this can't happen if the situation calls for it, but these aren't my default options anymore.

      There's also the monster tricks, resistances and immunities that the players don't know about yet. If those make the roll unnecessary I just jump to the description.

      \n I'd highly recommend to implement everything you can in a way that players (not PCs) could do things simultaneously. I mean, if as a player, on your turn, you have to engage with one or several mechanics that require you to take choices, you will probably stall the game a bit. Especially if you can't take most of those choices between turns, as whatever the opponents or other players will do has the chance to make you change your plans completely.

      On games with such granularity, dead time from turn to turn may end up making the players prone to chat, play with their cellphones or seek other distractions. Some groups are okay with this; though I prefer to keep things rolling, and if that means dropping some mechanics to do so I'll definitely go for it.

      But that's not a solution, that's just avoiding things "because they take too much time". If instead there was something you could do outside your turn (besides things like "opportunity attacks" or otherwise using a sort of reaction mechanic) then it would be easier to keep everyone's attention on the game. Just a thought.

      \n For us the things that keep the players focused on the combats even when it's not their turn are as follows:
      1) Tight chairmanning: I'm constantly pressuring, requiring the initiative caller for the next turn, demanding the player whose turn it is to decide what they'll do. The players mainly make fictional choices which I as the GM convert instantly into mechanics. No dead air time, something's happening constantly unless somebody explicitly calls for a time-out to think - which is an invitation for the others to debate their action choice with them. When I'm not doing this myself, I flag one of the other players to "run" the combat.
      2) Reaction mechanics: the way combat works in my homebrew, characters can pay initiative score to react to enemy action. Reactions are limited to natural instincts, prepared contingencies and special abilities usable as reactions, but that still leaves the players plenty to do off-turn. To play well, you have to theoretically be on hand 100% of the time to take that opportunity attack or shield block or backstep maneuver or other such minor stuff - if you're not playing attention, you're not getting those advantages even when they might be just the thing to save your bacon.
      3) Tactical tenseness: the way we run combats, they're f'ing serious business. You need to be on top of where your character is, what the enemy is doing and what your friends are doing, or you're just going to spout gibberish when it's your turn. The players can't afford to not pay attention, because that's how you end up declaring "I attack monster X", and the GM replies with "OK, but because they're through a doorway, and two other people have used the doorway this turn, you're taking a -4 to attack due to traffic congestion; furthermore, you obviously drop your lantern as you engage in melee, leaving the entire party in darkness." So good job champ, you just got everybody killed because you couldn't keep your head out of your phone/ass.

      Of course all that only works if the strategic-level context of the combat matters so that there's actually a reason to have a combat. Luckily the D&D system is so lethal and advantages of combat so contingent that a boring combat is quite rare - better to not have the combat at all if you're not invested in the outcome.
