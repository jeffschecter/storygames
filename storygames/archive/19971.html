    \n The contrasting idea to "people want easy games" is "people want clear rules". This has been a big theme in the history of RPG texts, the '74 D&D already addressed the conundrum by requesting the gamers to fill in the gaps themselves according to the needs of their own campaign. The immediate counter-reaction can be seen in many pre-'90s games that specifically compete with the sales argument of comprehensive, detailed rules that are presumed to reduce the amount of uncertainty and skill in the game. Rolemaster, for example, seems to me like it lives and breathes this ethos.

    Which is fundamentally true, or can one speak of fundamentals on a matter like this? Those two ideas are not technically speaking in complete opposition, as a game can be both simple and given to clear procedures, but there is still a hard core to the knot that cannot be split: a game that does not provide detail will necessarily require creative inspiration, and a game that provides the detail cannot be effortless.

    I went pretty far with the "people want easy games" doctrine in Zombie Cinema, including the fundamental admission that yes, making the game simple and detail-free also unavoidably makes it skill-based, non-fun for unimaginative people to play. It was a middling success by the measures I planned for the project, indicating that there is an audience and there probably would be more potential for that type of story game in the market for a bigger company to exploit; it's not a secret gold mine or anything, but all indications are that e.g. boardgamers, creative artists and other marginal rpg audiences are ready and receptive for that sort of casual/creative game. I understand that the experience from e.g. Fiasco is similar, although they might have charted the market potential more thoroughly, as their project was bigger.

    On the other hand, as Paul said, real commercial success has generally come to rpgs that are detailed in terms of crunch and setting, yet vague in terms of creative agenda. I think it's pretty credible that this reflects the genuine nature of the audience; there is a minority audience (of which pretty much everybody reading this forum is likely a member) that desires otherwise, but for many people the complex gamebook full of detail is an important imaginative tool and aesthetic experience in itself. Whether this is necessarily so due to human nature, or if the rpg hobby was transformed in this way by the early commercialization at the end of the '70s is a more complex issue, although I would personally lean towards nature rather than nurture here.

    Presumably the messiah product would include all the winning points so far: a compelling subject matter, a GM role for social construction of the gaming group, plentiful imaginative and rules detail in the rulebook to support and supply creativity, clean and elegant game design with intelligible creative goals, written in a lightweight and accessible manner. Not all of those can be perfectly accomplished together, but achieving near-perfection in all of them at once, coupled with an ambitious marketing plan, should provide an interesting case study on whether there is any dynamism to the rpg market, or if it's all just the same fuddies rebuying D&D over and over

    Frankly I'm stymied by how stymied you are. To me the whole issue sounds like: "Experimental theater lovers can't figure out why the audience's hunger for improv troupes and murder mystery games hasn't overtaken the appeal of movies and stageplays, nor obliterated the jobs of Writers and Directors."

    Perhaps "world domination" is the wrong goal for people who consider egalitarianism to be a core value in art?     The "IGT game" - like the writer/director/actor structure of stage and screen - understands the differences between people: their dedication levels, their talents and their tendencies. It empowers and rewards different types of people in different ways. It assumes, naturally, that greater responsibility will reside with those who desire it and make it their own. It augments and empowers that smaller set of people whose dedication to their artform is beyond that of the larger set of people, who enjoy playing.

    If we really want to increase the appeal of roleplaying as a pastime for "regular" people, it certainly wouldn't hurt to put our internecine squabbles behind us.

    (b) as you point out, there can be the same or similar issues of dissatisfaction / burnout in that role as with the traditional GM role

    You're also certainly right that you might be able to get a group of non-gamers to, e.g., play <i>Fiasco; they'll likely have a good time if they're the right sort of people, but they won't become gamers. I've also done some GMing with lapsed trad gamers; they loved the indie games I was running (BW and Lady Blackbird and a few others), but didn't become involved in the hobby again in any sort of ongoing way. BUT! I think those two things are *better outcomes* than having people try D&D or Vampire once and feel excluded and frustrated. At the very least, people who try (coherent) gaming once, or try it once as an adult, won't go away from the hobby embittered or thinking we're all basement-dwelling weirdos with terrible social skills. That's a win, in my book.

    Personally I'm convinced that there's no reason to try to make RPGs go mainstream, that they never will, and that this is ultimately a good thing. Interactive, demanding media will always be dwarfed by passive ones, for a huge host of reasons.

    I want to offer an additional hypothesis as to why it's actually harder to get people to stick with more egalitarian games: when you get a GM plus a bunch of players, the GM may burn out, but the players are basically happy in their roles: they can get their expected dopamine hit just by showing up every week. But when you get a bunch of GMs together, which is essentially what you need for many coherent games, everyone quickly wants to try their own thing. In other words, the very personality trait that allows someone to be, for example, an excellent co-player in Polaris, is the same thing (or closely related to the thing) that makes it hard for that person to play Burning Wheel (let alone D&D) for 50-100 sessions.

    Now, I do think there are some excellent games bridging the gap. Dungeon World, FATE, and 5E run by an indie GM, just to name a few examples, can both do a lot to gently teach people how to contribute a little bit more than just playing their characters, while still supporting both traditional aesthetic material ("adventure gaming") and GM/player distinctions. And there are some great apps and educational tools coming out that bridge the paper/digital divide, such as the "Choice Of" games and of course Storium.

    I am curious why you lean towards "nature rather than nurture", even while you recognize that it could be "the same fuddies rebuying D&D over and over". Is there anything in particular that causes you to lean in one direction over the other, or just a hunch?
    \nA hunch about human nature. If I had to choose between "humans in general are imaginative and independent" and "humans in general are pliant and conservative", I'd go for the latter more than the former. It follows that the nature of the rpg scene may best be characterized as something uniquely developed in the window between the invention of the form and the development of video gaming; a large number of people were inducted into the hobby at a time when it possessed actual competitive advantage versus alternate pastimes. (As opposed to today, when "imaginative and independent" are pretty much the only qualities rpgs compete in compared to video games.)

    So it could be the case that the actual hobbyists playing roleplaying games are running on a sort of a demographic deficit of imagination, caused by hobbyists in the 30+ age brackets having self-selected for the hobby before alternatives better suited to their personal nature became prominent through the '90s. This might explain the preferences of the rpg scene in part: while from an armchair it looks like you'd only want to play rpgs if you were especially imaginative and had deep creative needs (because other kinds of needs may be better served by e.g. boardgames or video games), in actual fact the people actually exercising the medium may simply be doing it out of habit, and due to accumulated experience overcoming personal leanings. Sort of like if you taught a child to play Chess at an early age, they might continue doing it despite it being out of character for their adult personality, simply because the game is so familiar and they're so good at it that the expertise enables them to enjoy it despite the natural handicap.

    (This, incidentally, would explain much about the popularity of the Game Master as a design solution: if the gamer population includes a large number of people who are not naturally inclined towards creativity and personal creative expression, then a role division that reflects these preferences might be quite optimal for the actual people in their social circles, making choices about their pastimes. Games that require everybody to be storytellers wouldn't flourish in a hobby where 85% of the people are naturally inclined to play World of Warcraft or such.)

    If something like this is going on, we should theoretically see a demographic shift in the rpg scene once the big pre-90s age groups leave the hobby with age over the next 20-40 years, leaving behind younger people who chose rpgs over modern digital entertainment, instead of coming into the hobby because it was the only game in town. Arguably we already saw one shift of that type in the '90s, when a large number of gamers opted for TCGs and video games. If these early quitters (as compared to us "rpg for life" types) came predominantly from non-GMing parts of the gamer population, then that would in part explain how the stature of the GM fell through the next decade; with the herds thinning, the individual alpha gamers banded together and discovered the possibilities of gaming in smaller, more dedicated groups. No more giant 12+ player games and "GM is king" when your group's only got 4 regulars and they all GM in turn.

    So in a nutshell I guess my argument goes something like this: gamers want the kinds of rpg books they want because they are the kind of people who would want those specific things from a rpg book. They want rpg books because that's their chosen hobby, due to the particulars of cultural history; if other kinds of people were predominant in the hobby, as they might be in the future, they might want/need different kinds of game texts. Thus, nature of the gamer stock might determine the nature of the rpg books. After all, if it were nurture, one would expect the clear "do it yourself" message of the original D&D to have persisted more than it did.

    \n The lesson I took from 4th edition was that D&D services an active and continuous player population that plays a specific game (which has a heritage reaching back to the '70s, even if it is no longer the same game). The heavy changes to emphasis that the 4th edition made disillusioned many people who wanted a game that could still be used to play in the way they liked. I've seen this personally among my friends, some of whom are serial D&D-heads (progressing along the game as new editions are published for the sake of novelty) - while I think they appreciated the 4th edition change of pace, it seems to me that they're happier with the 5th, which enables a more traditional playstyle.

    Applying this to your speculation, Paul, I would hazard that a Dungeon World with D&D's name would be similarly problematic for the publisher as 4th edition was; not insurmountable (I understand that 4th failed due to tactical errors in the marketplace more than strategic problems), but impossible to ignore. It seems to me that attaching a well-known brand name to a radically different game only makes sense for the people who were not happy with the old game, and imagine that this unhappiness is universal. Unless a change of direction can bring in a substantially larger new audience, it doesn't seem sensible to stop catering to the old.

    Of course, I also think that obsessing over the D&D brand name is intellectual weakness (unless you happen to own it, of course); if whatever your game is can't find its place in the market without attaching the D&D name to it, perhaps it's not such an amazing game after all. And even if you managed to undermine the D&D brand like that, what victory when the audience you coup apparently consists solely of trend-skimmers who'll switch to Dungeon World (or whatever) merely because of a brand name...

    Considering the substance of the speculation, I think that 4th edition is roughly as far away from the historical tradition of D&D (old and new school) as Dungeon World, so in that regard it'd be no more impossible a switcheroo as the prior experiment. Of course it should use the traditional dice mechanics and so on, but that's a minor issue.

    \n I think this idea that sales success and popular adoption has anything to do with rules coherence is wildly off base. It's not that the world has looked at indie RPGs from any particular point in history and said, "Nah, not my bag." Instead, most theoretical buyers/players simply never encounter an indie RPG, period.

    I seem to recall that Evil Hat has made most of their sales through retail. How many indie games get to a relevant number of stores?

    FATE, Burning Wheel, Apocalyspe World and a few others have managed to reach mostly the same old audience of roleplayers in unique ways that secured each a niche, and each of them is friendly to some audience out there in ways in which D&D is not. But this idea of finding people who don't roleplay and wouldn't like D&D, and wowing them with some indie design, is an extremely fringe proposition. (I mean, personally, I hold out hope of doing exactly that, because I can live off very, very little money. But it certainly isn't a game-changer for the hobby.)

    Fiasco might be a little different, I'm not sure. It might have some new audience members, but I get the same sense of a channel that doesn't leave room for piggybacking. I haven't heard of Fiasco acting as a gateway for prior non-gamers to get into Polaris or Zombie Cinema or Shock: or even other Bully Pulpit games.

    If you want to grab young teens with your storytelling system, you need to catch them in places they actually frequent, and show them how introducing your game to their friends will earn them those friends' love. I discovered roleplaying because D&D was in the comics store where I went in search of Spider-Man, and it enabled my friends and I to validate our power fantasies with numbers. Are any indie designers driven to produce something like that, which retailers will recognize as such?

    It seems more realistic to me to expand into the Party Games market by offering RPGs that are comedic, have roles for players who don't like to think much, and come in boxes with pretty game pieces. But even that is too expensive and complex a start-up proposition for most lone creatives. Take Shindig Machine: its rules make it a hit at parties, but a box of cards with no particular flavor or premise seems hard to market, and indeed, I've never seen it at a store.

    \n I would characterize myself as more of a traditional gamer, though I am open to new RPG experiences as well. Reading through the comments and the article, I will say you can catch more flies with honey than vinegar.

    For me the thing that deters me most from trying a new game is the tone of its fan base and enthusiasts. If someone brings me a new RPG and says I should try it because they love it and think I will too, I'll happily give it a shot. If someone brings a new RPG and tells me I should try it because it isn't like the rubbish I normally play, I am going to be a lot less inclined to listen because they started the conversation by insulting me. It is when people speak in terms of evolution of design, or frame styles of play in moral terms that I tend to tune out. There are many niches in the hobby and room for all of them. There is also always a mainstream that draws as needed from the different gaming scenes. It is unlikely the mainstream will ever be 100% indie just like it is unlikely the mainstream will be 100% traditional or old school. By its very nature the gaming mainstream needs to appeal to a broad demographic of players and GMs, so it moves ideas to the middle.

    If you look at a game like Numenera for example, it is winning over many folks on my side of the hobby (not all, but many). I think the reason is partly the incredible setting, but also has a lot to do with the fact that Monte Cook is an incredibly positive guy. He didn't compare Numenera to games that do things differently (and neither did anyone on his staff). He just talked enthusiastically about the game and its mechanics.     That most traditional games are amenable to a wide variety of styles, influenced by the GM's preferences and those of the group, than most Forgite (or Forge diaspora, whatever) games, isn't really in dispute, is it?

    One question that seems to be getting brushed aside here, but that I think is really fascinating and important to delve into, is whether indie or trad games require more rules buy-in from players. People have sort of implied things in both directions (for good reason, I think). My personal experience has been that most trad groups will function alright with around a third of the group not really grokking the rules, whereas with something indie, especially if it's GM-less, everyone really does have to engage with them (even if they're relatively simple, they may revolve around things of dramatic import like scene framing, so everyone has to understand what makes such things go).

    ***

    My own recent realizations regarding my play preferences certainly highlight what the article is talking about. As much as I love playing light games with memorable mechanics, that can deliver a meaningful experience in a few hours, I really crave the depth that can only come from campaigns. So I've really tried to focus on BW, 5E, and other games that can give that experience, relegating one-shots to cons or special occasions.

    \n Two separate thoughts:

      \n Some great discussion here, and lots of good points (particularly Dave and Matt, your experiences match mine). I'll throw in a few more points:

      1. Another aspect of our hobby that we sometimes take for granted is that it is dependent on skill level.

      The "indie" approach has been to try to make the games easier to play by developing more and more advanced tools.       2. Freeform online gaming clearly has a large following, and people commit to that kind of thing for huge periods of their lives. It works in part because it's a social activity (i.e. peer pressure, a sense of belonging, cycles of affirmation and feedback).

      All that said, I think the obvious halfway point here is what I briefly touched on when I started saying "from scratch" - a game in which you can/should play 2-3 SESSIONS, both to get better at playing it, and to get that sense of "unfinished business" where folks want to come back for more. The alternative is perhaps something like what Fiasco does, where it's the same GAME, but it has a ton of different "playsets" (I think this is the closest RPGs have gotten to the 'expansion set' model.) Though this has limitations too. Maybe a better model would be a ton of different "Powered by Apocalypse" games, where once you basically know how to play PbtA games, there's not too much new "mechanical" learning going on, even if the setting and the mood and flow of the game are completely different.

      And all of this is ignoring another elephant in the room - namely the previous (and very correct) assertion that "no game but D&D has ever 'conquered the world'." This, more than anything, is what leads me to believe that people in this hobby are, in fact, more creatures of habit than anything else. They find something entertaining, they play it one way, and they have trouble even IMAGINING doing it another way. I'm speaking from experience here, because this was me 5 years ago or so. I'd played D&D, WoD, Shadowrun, blahblahinsertTradGameHere. They were all, when you got down to it, basically the same. The trappings were different, but really, having played them all, I was left wondering "Why would I need another game system? I can do whatever with whatever system." It wasn't until I actually READ through (Not "heard about" or whatever, actually looked at, in depth, first hand) some honestly revolutionary games that my brain went "OH! This is completely different! Brilliant! Some games really ARE better at some things!" While I know I shouldn't project my experiences onto a bunch of other people, I think it's safe to say that relatively few people figuring out what is different or cool or clever about "Indie" games without actually playing or at LEAST reading one. And since the prevailing mindset is basically, "I like this game and it can do everything, so why do I need another game system?" I think it's also a safe bet to say that there might be quite a lot of people who would like this stuff if only there were some way to communicate it to them. But that is SUPER HARD.
