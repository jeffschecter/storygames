\nThe Point f this thread:

Have you ever had a moment of affirmation that you, my fellow game master, are, indeed, a good GM? Have you ever had one of your players praise you about the job and effort you're making? Say something that makes you know you are actually going in the right direction?

Or maybe quite the opposite: Some moment that made you realize you were doing it all wrong? That you needed to re-think the way you were doing things?


My Case:

In this thread (none of you guys paid any attention to T-T) I talked about how I've been trying to make the stories in games I ran more emotionally complex and compelling.

The affirmation of my tactics actually paying off came just a few days ago when the female player from that game said "You know, after that session, when I got into the car I was still feeling guilt about what happened in the game". And that felt just so great; to see that all the effort I'm making is actually bringing results! God, I just can't describe it!

And!

My personal moment of failure as a GM came long before I even started comming to these forums, let alone played anything that wasn't DnD.

After the first session of a campaign of Eberron I wanted to run I had my brother tell me something among the lines of "Man, you really need to make preparations for your game, this was boring as hell" Granted he was being an asshole... I came to the realization I just wasn't making the game entertaining for my players. This moment was good in itself because it was then that I decided to make some research into the RPG world and, a few years later, I find myself now with a better understanding of what i want out of a game, how to get it and when to leave my own desires for the good of the players.

And all this never sacrificing the enjoyment of the players nor my own.

Anyway! What about you?

    \nMy highlights as a 'good' GM: Running a campaign of Deliria, wherein the players succeeded in doing something horrible, then watching the look of mortified guilt that settled on to their faces. I was very worried I'd lose a few players that night due to the nature of the disaster. I didn't. They kept coming back for more.
    \n
    \nAs you all know, I run games through a meetup club I help organize. My games often fill up within 24 hours of being posted, have a waiting list generally attached to them, and when players sit down at my table, I often hear: "I've heard your an awesome GM. Everyone talks about how good your games are!"
    \n
    \nMy highlights as a 'bad' GM: Running players through a dungeon in D&D and having, I thought, a pretty straight forward puzzle solving area. It took two hours for them to figure out the name of the god was the element they were associated with. I didn't give clues or anything beside the obvious "This god's statue is in a room covered in ice..." and that the end of the puzzle demanded to be named. So the players felt tired and irritated after two hours of having me describe each statue and room in highest detail over and over again at their bequest. I should've helped the players out more, but I didn't.
    \n
    \nRunning a game of Dread and letting the players fight amongst themselves using the Jenga tower instead of focusing on the creatures after them. The game ended up without atmosphere, unless you count B-movie action. One of my players was insistent he steal the girl (another player) by killing her brother (another player) and then riding off with her in the sunset. She didn't want to. So it became a race on horseback away from each-other. *sighs*

    \nYou rule:

    I won the GM-mastership of Arcon three years in a row, and one year I got the full set of points (means ALL my players rated me a 10er on a scale from 1 to 10).

    I had my players cry in a session on a con, due to the scenario being so emotional, and our play of it being so great.

    I had some players laughing themselves to tears in a scenario on a con.

    I had my players cancel further play in other games on a con, due to them being so emotionally invested in the drama of my game to play any more that day (a dirty war-scenario). One of them had a place in a Call game that night, but told me the next day he had dropped it; "Any horror game would a bleak scare, after that session with you, so I chose to keep the feeling and went to bed without any more gaming ... but I slept badly" (he gave me a broad smile as he told about his bad sleep).

    I had a player that followed me from he was 9 until he was 19, declaring me his "second father".

    I had a mother of a player of 15 declaring me a "life saver" for her son (he was ill with cancer, and our weekly sessions was a huge driving force for him through much of his illness).

    I had a friend of mine in a campaign, declare that "this campaign blows my mind, being so close to my real life".

    You suck:

    Last week I had two 10 year old boys dropping out of the interaction continually, during a whole game-session. Nothing I did could convince them to pay attention ...

    ... goes to show that you are only as good as your current game. ;-)

    \n The Rock

    I realised GMing was something I enjoyed more than playing and knew I was doing it right back in the early 1990s when one of my players in my CP2020 game pointed a gun at two NPCs, they gave up immediately, scared witless at having this gun up close in their face. They talked, they begged, they told the PC what he asked and they fled...the player then says ...     Realising my players are fidgeting, reading books, talking to each other...I go off on a tangent and no one notices...I state "ok this game is over"...my players cheer!!!!
