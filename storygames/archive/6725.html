\n I'd say this is play advice, but I'm sort of road testing it here.

Okay, the idea is that if a game is ongoing, eventually the players will lose.

I'm noting this because of a recent AP where the GM was concerned the emotionally involved player would lose to some big boss - but then the GM says he didn't need to worry cause the player got past it. Which isn't true at all - if you keep playing, you keep playing the odds. Eventually the odds will turn against the players and they will lose. All perpetual games have a predictable end - the players losing.

That, or it's not possible to lose in the game. This may mean as a whole, the game loses as the tension players thought was there (the tension born from being able to lose) is completely lost. Note: Some players don't mind games where you can't lose - that's fine and good. But I'm going to ignore them in this thread, as that's kind of evidence the whole win/lose thing doesn't matter to them. Oh, and if the players retire their characters before the big lose then they can leave on a win - but then, that stops the game being perpetual.

It's not something that's immediately obvious, but when you look past any concern about whether they will make it past a boss, and realise there will be a boss after that, and after that, and so on, you know relief at passing any boss is kind of missplaced. It's just relief that the inevitable has been delayed.

There's a desire to "get the band back together" and finish it, but due to life changes (usually the ones that caused us to stop playing) that's all but impossible. So, in a way we all lost, every player, GM included. But we had fun on the journey, and really that's the important part. So, I'll take that minor loss over the win of every session leading up to the anti-climactic ending.

    Posted By: Callan S.if a game is ongoing, eventually the players will lose.
    \nYep. And WRT advice, the only advice is "be aware of it, and make less experienced players aware of it." The greatest thing Paranoia brought to our gaming group was becoming comfortable with character death and mission failure. Sure, you'd get your clone--My Guy, Mark II, formalized in the system--but even then, there were plenty of Code Seven missions (all seven clones of all players killed).

    The pragmatic "take away" point, here, is to be aware of the game risks and consequences of failure, and suck it up when you go down (eww, that came out wrong).

    As for the "end on a high note" points: I adopted another LARP's rule as a primary Toggle in my system, GLASS. It was Walt Freitag's Commit Rule: in brief, a character that was "down" (0 HP, torso hit, whatever) didn't automatically die; rather, IF the PLAYER "committed" then the character was instantly restored to full HP (whatever) BUT, the next time they went down inthat conflict (loosely defined--I am more rigorous in GLASS), the character died permanently. No resurrection, no GM fiat or divine intervention: gone. Walt's goal was to make death meaningful, and to do so he left the choice of when a player would take that risk in the player's own hands, NOT in the systems mechanics and NOT in another player's hands (PvP PKing). "Brilliant!" I thought, and immediate bult two different, Prime Toggles for GLASS; World Priority (Killing Blow or Slay = Dead) and Player Priority (Commit). Resurrection is ONLY a World Priority rule, and even then it has (at last count) Three Toggles (one of which is No Resurrection) and two Options (orthogonal to this thread--just stuff about "costs" of rebirth or whether there are chances of failing to Res).

    SO, in short, the Commit rule works to help players hit high notes, to go out dramatically, with a bang. Dread TFBOP has a similar mechanic with the Retirement rule: you get DOUBLE full Life and Fury, but you WILL die at the end of the case (if not sooner)--go out with a BIG bang.

    Um... rambling. The larger point I wanted to make is that a Player Priority "style" person in a World Priority game is going to feel the suck, when he's Killing Blowed. Thus, GLASS makes that very axis of play--call it NAR vs GAM, if it's convenient to imagine the distinction--a clear, obvious, principle statement about a game type. In other words, it communicates the risks and threat, from the player's perspective, on their agency in the fictional world. Unique and Special Butterfly? Or Just Another Grunt?
