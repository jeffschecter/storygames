\n As you might know, Dragon*Con have decided to dissociate themselves from the Backup Project.

Since you might not have heard of it, here's a description of the Backup Project. The idea is that you wear a ribbon, at conventions, to show you're available for support if anyone gets harassed.

Back up project

And here's the Dragon*Con announcement:

Dragon*Con

I think this matters. Campaigns about convention harassment have been growing in profile recently and this is a major test of them. By my reading, Dragon*Con have smacked it down heavily ("Quite frankly, scary to us...We have asked the individual to stop providing ribbons").

If you are going and you disagree with their position, you might consider dropping them a line. This is the sort of thing that can be changed, if enough people express an opinion. Their contact details, which I've taken straight from their website, are:

Email: dragoncon@dragoncon.org
Phone: 770-909-0115 (M-F 9:00 a.m. - 5:00 p.m. EST)

I'm also interested to hear your thoughts.

    \n I wouldn't call that FAQ clear, at all. There's one person's somewhat vague/bombastic post in the first answer, and then the question is begged in the third answer. OK, "groping without moaning" is one qualifier--granted.

    Without a clear definition of the behaviors which will trigger them to butt in/back up, this is a serious landmine. And without the right people involved and organizing it (e.g., Community Watch organizations are coordinated with and--to an extent--trained by the police), you're guaranteed to step on at least a few of those mines.

    On the one hand, you've got escalation of conflict by amateurs untrained in conflict management... or worse, amateurs untrained in conflict management but trained in martial arts... and with potentially lethal weaponry on hand! And before you say "WTF" to that, recall the number of "cosplayers" who show up as fully packing SWAT or Spec Ops dudes, except for the Airsoft gun (i.e., real combat knives, real combat boots). Now recall the number who carry perfectly functional swords and daggers (peace-binding be damned).

    On the other, you've got the risk of misreading a situation and being an ass. Yes, I'd rather err on this side, too--but how many miscues will an individual be willing to make before reactivating the SEP field? Or before the individual is the one talking to security/police, instead of suspected perps?

    And on the gripping hand, some of the verbiage of the FAQ suggests that these enforcers will gang up, as necessary--calling over friends to help. Awesome. Hope the perp doesn't have more, bigger friends with similar ethics as him. "Groping without moaning" becomes a gang fight amidst otherwise-innocent con goers.

    No matter how it's sliced and diced, it's vigilantism in venues where there are at least two recourses: calling venue security over or calling in the police/sheriff. Vigilantism by amateur volunteers who have a thing for heroes, no less....
    -----
    I would love to see trained law enforcement officers and even maybe social workers volunteering for such roles at cons, for sure! Coordinating with venue security and local law enforcement. Using known-good techniques for both recognition and containment of conflicts. Being professional and obviously present.

    (From reading the site more: this is something they're talking about for private con parties and such, surely? Does this actually happen in public spaces, with security a few paces away, and nothing is done to prevent it?!?)

    \nHey David,

    I don't think the Backup Ribbon project really asks its supporters to do conflict management, just conflict detection. This is a common strategy in responding to sexual harassment: be present, speak directly to anyone you feel might be being harassed, make sure everyone feels safe. If it turns out that no, this person isn't feeling safe, and no, this other person isn't leaving or backing down, your responsibility isn't to chase this person away - it's to get an authority or additional support. Nobody's asked to be a hero, just to be there. A lot of the time, that's all that is needed - a third wheel that disrupts a victimization scheme.

    It doesn't take making an ass out of yourself to intervene. "Hey, are you enjoying the show? Cool. Do you know this guy?" If the person's body language and response signal that they're totally comfortable and safe at present, then all you did was made some conversation. If the person's body language and response signal that they're uneasy, you can easily offer support in a non-intrusive way: "Do you want me to stick around for a bit?"

    Lots of people don't need an orientation on how to step in, just a sense of permission.
