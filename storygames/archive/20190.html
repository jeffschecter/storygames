\nThe Murder of Mr. Crow is an one hour long parlor game where you create a murder mystery together with your friends. I got to playtest it on two gaming conventions, and took the feedback and play experience with me so I could release an updated version of it.

It's free. It's on two pages. It can be played with non-roleplaying gamers. It requires a hat.

http://www.urverkspel.com/vara-spel/oevriga-projekt

Had three people now telling me that they liked it better than A Taste for Murder.

As always, if you see any misspellings or grammatical errors, please let me know.

    \n DIFFERENCES BETWEEN THE FIRST AND SECOND EDITION

    New layout
    The old layout was made to put emphases the playfulness, but were too messy.

    Replaced the die roll with drawing glass beads instead.
    It happened that too many avoiding rolls were made, which threw the game into a stall. I replaced it with a controlled randomness, which connected it to the rules of how the notes are handled at the end of the game.

    Clarified where the Suspects and extra information should be added

    Removed where Mr. Crow was murdered
    The information restricted more than it helped the inspiration.

    Added that all Suspects live in the same apartment building
    This was always assumed but never mentioned.

    Changed Cow to Horse
    Crow and Cow messed things up for us non-English speakers. Also, \u201ccow\u201d tells the gender of the Suspect where \u201chorse\u201d doesn\u2019t.

    Added that facts should have bullets and motives be underlined.
    Playtest revealed that it can be hard to keep track of what\u2019s what otherwise.

    Added the role of the Secretary.
    It eased up the explanation by adding that role.

    Removed how the Secretary wrote notes during the next questioning.
    The waiting for the previous Detective to write down the notes became a good break for others to come up with questions.

    \n This looks great, Rickard. I hope to get a chance to play this soon, and the second edition is much more playable.

    Some notes:

    * Under "The Suspects", it's not clear what "take two turns each" means. The placement of that sentence makes it look like it's about inventing Suspects. However, did you mean that each player takes two turns in this phase of the game, period, and then we move on?

    Maybe "take two turns each" should be the header for the entire section, if that's the case.

    * How important is the number of Suspects? I'd imagine number of Suspects is just a variable for the duration of the game - the more Suspects, the longer the game, right?

    * Is it possible for each player to portray a particular Suspect throughout the game, much like in an RPG? (This means that when you are the Detective, you only question other Suspects, but that shouldn't ruin anything.) Or does something go wrong if you try that?

    * Errors:

    "Before the questioning begins, takes all the
    remaining glass beads (starting at six) and let the
    person playing the Suspect to draw one without
    looking at them."

    This should be:

    Before the questioning begins, take all the
    remaining glass beads (starting at six) and let the
    person playing the Suspect draw one without
    looking [at them].

    ("At them" can be omitted without any loss of meaning.)

    * The Secretary role seems redundant. Can't you just say that in this phase the Detective makes notes about the Suspects?

    It could also be amusing to make the notes "in-character", with a little commentary.

    e.g. "Mr. Rhino was seen in the bathroom that night. (My first important clue, I feel!)"

    It seems like it would, again, simplify the text a little.

    * This part is confusing:

    "The previous Suspects keeps the drawn
    glass beads, but returns them to the new
    Detective when there are only two left."

    There is a small gramar issue which is confusing the sentence. If it is "The previous Suspects keep the beads...", that means each player keeps the beads they've drawn so far, then return them to the Detective.

    If it is "The previous Suspect keeps the beads", then it is one person who holds them all.

    I'm going to guess that it's the first, and recommend this rephrasing:

    "The Suspects keep the beads they've drawn so far. When there are only two left, all Suspects return their beads to the Detective."

    \n@Paul_T: Nice! I love when people find faults because then my game always turns out better. Thanks for the feedback. I really appreciate that. Made an updated version, and here are all the changes in the text.

    THE DETECTIVE
    Everyone takes turns to play the Detective, who
    has already figured out who the murderer is. All
    the Suspects have been gathered in the parlor
    and it\u2019s time for the big reveal: who murdered
    Mr. Crow? The fun in playing this game lies
    in seeing a scenario grow in front of us while
    contributing to it following these three steps:

    THE SUSPECTS
    Hand the pencil to the left.
    The pencil will go two times around the table.
    Create a total of five Suspects for a standard one-hour game.

    THE MOTIVES
    ... and let the person playing the Suspect draw one without
    looking. The color is then revealed to everyone.

    THE NOTEBOOK
    The person who played the Detective will
    make notes on the paper about what has
    just been revealed. To distinct one fact from
    another, put bullets in front of the \xb7 facts \xb7
    and underline the motives.
    /.../
    The glass beads are only returned to the
    Detective when there are two beads left.

    Why is it important to specify that the Detective already knows who the murderer is?
    \nThere are a bunch of vague reasons. Mostly because it follows the genre in how Sherlock Holmes (and others) does his revelation. Another reason is that I wanted to avoid acting out in character. That still happens, and it can be a nice flavor, but it's not a focal point for this game.

    The third, and most important, reason is that if the facts are already established, that will make the players playing the Detective more sure of themselves. If we were instead to play out the interrogation, the facts could be questioned. Now they are already "established" through that little sentence that the Detective already has done the groundwork. The facts are already proven, and all that is left is to reveal them.

    But all in all. It's probably not that important.

    But I can say that I created the structure of play first, by asking myself "How can I create a fish tank in a collaborative way?", and then found a genre suited for it. So the questions can be said to belong to the genre, but I came up with the structure of creating a scenario through questions and then picked the genre.
