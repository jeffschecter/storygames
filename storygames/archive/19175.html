\n Seeing the thread by Gravity asking for one-shots there\u2019s a load of one-shots out there. However I\u2019m not very experienced in the field, so I wonder what kinds of scenarios interest people? I\u2019ve tried some of John Harper\u2019s scenarios (Lady Blackbird and Ghost Lines) and I loved them. Especially the fact that they\u2019re so open that they indeed invite repeated play.

However, the project I\u2019m working on has landed at the other end of the spectrum. It has a narrow focus, a controlled chronology and the characters' secret backgrounds are integral to play, meaning once you\u2019ve played it it loses quite a bit in replay value. It also has intrigue playing built in, which I\u2019ve been told is not really a good idea for one-offs with strangers. Compared to Lady Blackbird it feels like making a list of everything that's good in it and ignoring that.

But my idea was born out of the feeling that I so far haven\u2019t found a scenario that captures Reservoir Dogs in a good way. But that may simply be me not having searched thoroughly enough.

So basically my question boils down to this: Is there already a one-shot out there that nicely captures the Reservoir Dogs feeling that you can\u2019t trust anybody?

    \n While not modern era, Witch does this pretty well. It's a one shot, the central Witch character is theoretically against all of the characters transporting her, but they also have reasons to change their allegiances and there are baked in reasons for them to dislike each other. It has good replay value, you tend to think 'I'd like to do that again, but be that character this time'.

    Probably unsurprisingly Love In the Time of Seid has the same feel.

    \n I'd second that Mountain Witch recommendation.

    But, while the asking questions/building collaboratively method of Lady Blackbird is integral to Lady Blackbird, it's not integral to awesome one shots. And while replayability is nice, honestly, there are so many games that it doesn't particularly matter (especially if a player can play, love it, and then GM it for others)

    There are problems that you can run into designing secrets heavy games, but you can run into problems with any game: if the game is fun, tight, and works, make it!

    \n To follow up more concretely: if having secrets and backstories help establish a charged, balancing on a knife edge situation where people have a lot of options of what to do, then that can be great, and often get off to a fast start and never let up.

    If the secrets and backstories are just about making sure that cool events A and B and C happen, then I might be a little wary? It could still be cool.
