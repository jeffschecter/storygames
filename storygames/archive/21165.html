    \n Different games handle this issue in somewhat different ways. A common terminology for talking about this issue is to talk about "freely formed traits" (as the players get to pick whatever traits they want) with "formalized mechanical weight" (as the traits have simple pre-defined mechanical weight) and "universal conflict resolution mechanics" (as there's just one set of rules for solving all sorts of situations, and traits are simply fed into that system to get a result). Heroquest is perhaps the most emblematic and clear example of a game like this, but there's a whole bunch of games out there that use this particular mechanical approach.

    A common element in these kinds of games is to accept that the players are genuinely responsible for what they put down on their character sheets. If you put down "my race car is red +100", then it's on you if being red gives a +100 to winning races. The only one who can prevent forest fires - and maintain sensibility in a free trait system - is you. This is true even if the game puts limits on how many traits you can have and how high their mechanical weights can be - the fact that the system lets you write your own traits means that it's impossible for anybody except the play group to police that those traits are "sensible".

    Another common element is that the game has a specific set of rules for trait scoping and applicability. For example, the game might say that "Strong as an ox" applies to a given situation better than "Long arms", and therefore the character using the former gets some sort of advantage over the latter. Or, another example, the game might say that you can only use traits that apply clearly and unequivocally to the situation. It all depends on how, precisely, the game applies traits to conflicts.

    But in truth, there's a whole bunch of rules detail that goes into this stuff. For example, The Shadow of Yesterday relies on rather arbitrary scoping, and gets away with it due to sharply diminishing mathematical returns: the second and third trait you add to the conflict have less and less impact on the outcome, and therefore players don't generally bother with it too much. Meanwhile, Dust Devils says that you can only apply one trait, the one best fit to the situation - again, it avoids most complications. Tales of Entropy basically just treats the trait thing as arbitrary narrative color for the fact that the player's gonna get roughly as many dice as he wants anyway, so we just need them to explain a bit about where those dice come from.

    So I'd say that there's a whole continuum of subtle techniques that games use to improve the play experience in this kind of free-trait formal conflict resolution system games, but which ones specifically are in use depends on the specific game. Learn the game you play well, and pay attention to how others get it to lift its weight.

    \n The other note about City of Mists is that even 10+ results aren't made equally. A 10+ fight roll where you rolled at +4 has a +4 effect (a serious injury), while a 10+ where you got lucky at +1 has only a +1 effect (a scratch).

    It strongly incentivizes players to tag spam, and makes rolling at low Power almost useless.

    The system seems dubious at best.

    Anyway, on a brighter note, in Lady Blackbird at least, there's a few subtle controls. Very few characters have tags which stack in large quantities together easily. (Naomi's Pit Fighter is one of the exceptions, and it represents how just awe-inspiringly badass she is). Always/Never/Now I think missed this, and it led to a lot more easy tag stacking.

    In general, as a GM, other than just being willing to acknowledge that occasionally you will push back a bit in LB, I think rewarding people for more precise actions with lower difficulties is your key lever. Fighting a big group of marines in the Hand of Sorrow is a 5 difficulty. Tricks or clever plans may have a lot lower difficulty. The more tags a person brings in, the broader the action, but also consider that the scope of the enemy's response grows broader.

    Similarly, each tag can imply fictional consequences: Kale can use Repair, Efficiency, Spare Parts, but now their cache of spare parts is gone. If they want to drag in Sabotage, well, what did they sabotage, etc, etc. If Kale is being Quiet, Sneaking, Hiding, and Perceptive, it's unlikely anybody else can follow along with that, and he's on his own.

    In the end, I think tag spamming isn't a big deal for LB, especially given that it's only a session or two. But I think part of that is the tag design is very precise and controlled.

    \n I can only speak to Lady Blackbird, not the other game being discussed.

    In that game, the number of tags representing any given feature is far more important, so balancing their descriptions is, to some extent, "on you".

    If you assign "big eyes" and "green skin" as Tags to a frog, and allow them to matter in a fight against a big warrior who has the tag "tremendous strength", you've set up an untenable situation. It's not resolution which is causing your problem: it's the types of Tags you've written and how picky you are about applying them.

    If someone is a fearsome warrior in Lady Blackbird, the way that should be represented is in the NUMBER of Tags they have. If you have things like "frog eyes" as Tags, then the warrior should have "Bulging Biceps", "Weightlifter", "Brutal", "Veteran", "Swordsman", and so forth...

    I think you need to decide how the game is going to serve you, as well. Is it kosher to add details to the fiction - like in a cartoon, as Sandra suggests, for visual effect - for extra dice? Or do we try to be true to the fiction, and pick a hardline approach where the Tags apply only in accordance to what we've described so far?

    Ultimately, I've found the Tags system to look great on paper but a little tedious in play, as well as susceptible to the problems you describe.

    Paul_T said:

    Ultimately, I've found the Tags system to look great on paper but a little tedious in play, as well as susceptible to the problems you describe.

    \nYes, that's my take on the specific mechanism as well - the idea is attractive, but it's somewhat difficult to put to work in a truly elegant fashion. It's one of those mechanics that's too universal for its own good. I suspect that the best results with it can be achieved in some sort of limited context (maybe the game forces you to select from a limited set of tags or something).

    I'd love to give some advice on City of Mists, because this sort of design is right up my alley, but I've no idea about that game in the specific. As a general observation, though: if the game just piles on freeform trait bonuses on top of a 2d6 roll, and there's no disadvantage or cost to doing so, there needs to be other, firm and real limitations to answer why a player wouldn't just whine their way into adding every trait their character's got. Usually for games that don't have anything particularly clever going for them in that department it's gonna be group standards: you write reasonable, useful traits, and you apply them consistently and clearly. No "stretches" (as Heroquest calls it when you apply a trait with weak justification).

    That sort of "naive" free-trait play can work well, but it truly needs to be economical: few traits, more important traits stronger than the less important ones, and no piling weakly associated traits into the conflicts. An individual conflict should look like "master swordsman" + "at home on a ship" vs "flight magic" + "master slinger" rather than a cavalcade of "but I mentioned my character's red backpack, so I should get the points for my fancy backbag trait" whining. And yeah, if the system assigns mechanical weight to the individual traits, the actually more important traits should be assigned higher numbers than the bullshit ones.

    And of course all that does nothing for the fact that most free-trait games have in-built attenuation effects, while a system that simply adds bonuses linearly on top of a bell curve dice roll actually causes further bonuses to be more effective. Practically all functional games with loosey-goosey dicing modifiers have attenuation: either it's a dice pool where adding even more dice has less proportionate impact, or the order the traits are added in matters to favour the early ones, or you pay incrementally more juice the more traits you add. It's a major issue for the overall game design, and should be considered very carefully.

    No idea if the specific game you're pondering about suffers from these sorts of issues. If it does, it's not that difficult to fix - there's many ways to do it, really, just depends on what one likes to do. For example, you could say that "stretch" traits are always only worth a +1 in total regardless of how many or how big they are, and only traits that are central to the situation (in the GM's judgement) are worth their full value. This would effectively mean that most characters in most situations get one trait at full value, +1 for having some bullshit traits they force into the situation - but they don't need to force more than one to get that full bonus, and there's no need to do lengthy beg-and-whine routines to collect every little bonus.

    \n Hi, the CoM Themebooks playtest doc has a section on Broad vs Specific tags, ways to talk about them, and how to resolve tags that are too broad. It looks good so far, though there's no telling how it will end up in the final draft.

    It describes "Specific" tags as being useful in one of three (or two of three in specific ways) types of scenes; Investigation, Action, or Dialog. "Broad" tags are useful in two of the three types of scenes, and "Too Broad" tags are tags that are basically useful all the time. A player is allowed to have one Broad tag, but the rest are supposed to be specific.

    Of course, it says the GM can approve whatever, but it also has some guides for either making tags more specific, or allowing broader tags with restricted use (the PC and GM would agree that the tag needs a Change the Game move to "activate" it).

    Again, it's still in playtest, so there may be more or less or different stuff when it's released.

    \n I'm arriving way too late to the discussion, but let me suggest adding a blackjack-like mechanic to your game to avoid the "tag-hoarding effect".

    In FU, for example, it can be as easy as modifying the default mechanic:\n
    Roll (1+#TAGS)d6, keep the highest and read the result from this list:

    1.- No, and...
    2.- No
    3.- No, but...
    4.- Yes, but...
    5.- Yes
    6.- Yes, and...
    \nby exchanging the 4 and 6 results:\n
    Roll (1+#TAGS)d6, keep the highest and read the result from this list:

    1.- No, and...
    2.- No
    3.- No, but...
    4.- Yes, and...
    5.- Yes
    6.- Yes, but...
    \nso the players will be interested in avoiding low numbers (1, 2 & 3) but would rather prefer a 4 or a 5 than a 6.

    I'm quite sure that this idea can be adapted to other systems as well (in Cthulhu Dark, getting a 6 while investigating triggers an Insight roll).
