    \n Cool, Matthijs. I'm glad you think I'm reading you right.

    There's a few things that I can think of that help lead to the kind of emotional engagement with other people that you're talking about. They're both things that are generally specific to a ritual environment, so I think you're right that ritual theory has some useful things to say here.

    1. Roleplaying often creates the illusion that everyone is having the same or similar emotional experience, that you're actually feeling the emotions of others and so on. I don't think this is actually the case, but rituals are really good (perhaps the best method we have) at perpetuating this feeling of sharedness. It's like, "Hey, I'm having these really strong feelings and, unlike in 'real life' where that's a private thing, everybody else I'm playing with (at least) knows what I'm feeling and (at best) maybe even shares in it."

    2. Roleplaying (like many other ritual forms) also forces players to deal with interpersonal tension, instead of avoiding it, dismissing it, or giving in to fight/flight responses like we monkeys normally do in 'real life.' The GM-player split is the traditional way this happens, with the GM stomping all over your aspirations (as a player, not just a character) or at least providing resistance, even if that resistance is ultimately temporary or limited. In larp and more collaborative tabletop traditions of play, the other players provide this tension in a more dis-unified way that I would argue is actually more effective at pushing a player's buttons and facilitating irresolvable tension. That kind of tension is truly maddening in real life but creates an incredibly furtive creative and emotional environment as us monkeys look for every possible way to release tension and resolve the situation. You know how you write better songs after a romantic breakup or if you have a massive crush on someone unattainable or have some pretty terrible things happen in your life? I think that's at least partially because your mind is struggling to resolve the uncomfortable tension, causing it to work in unusual ways and at a frantic pace.

    Combining 1 & 2 together is where the real magic is, though, right? I mean, if you're having this unusual, frantic, creative experience and feel that everyone else around you is having a similar experience and/or can sense what you are feeling, that's ridiculous awesome and I think that's the drunk-on-life glow that you're talking about, maybe. Too many endorphins or something.

    About formal/academical study of ritual in rpg:\n
    Posted By: Simon CSometing I looked at a lot in my anthropology thesis was the unconscious rituals that a lot of gaming groups use to re-enforce status, to signal in-game and out-of-game, to flag discomfort, and a lot of other things.

    Food was frequently a focus of ritual. Bringing snacks to the GM's house shows respect to the host, but it's also kind of a status signifier, saying "thanks for running the game". It re-enforces the social roles of the players. Conversely, the GM might serve food to the players, signifying a different kind of relationship. Whatever the ritual was, it was usually repeated every game. For example, in one group I observed, the players would always come to the GM's house first, and then everyone, including the GM, would go to the store for snacks. There was a very different relationship between the players than in another group, where the GM never bought snacks. The way food was shared, all in the middle of the table, or each to their own, was also significant. The type of food was usually ritually defined as well. It's weird if everyone's eating chocolate and chips, and one person brings apples. The type of food eaten might signal the tone of the game. For example, junk food often signals a relaxed, fun game, while more substantial food might signal a more serious tone.

    The way people talk often has ritual significance. For example, GMs I've played with often distinguish by their tone if they're doing "Dramatic Intro" - solid facts about the setting or scene, or just discussing ideas. Information for the whole party is often spoken more forcefully, while information for a single player is spoken more conversationally. Players who make a habit of speaking in character might distinguish between "important, character stuff" which is done in character, using accents and whatever, with less important stuff, which is communicated in a normal tone. Usually other players recognise these ritualistic uses of tone and pick up on the extra layers of meaning. For example, people are more reluctant to challenge GM narration if it's done in the declamatory style than if it's phrased more colloquially.

    Out of character jokes are often used to defuse uncomfortable scenes, or to bring the spotlight away from players who are demanding too much attention. In a lot of games I watched, out-of-character comments were used to discharge tension after important scenes. Two players got into an in-character argument, which involved a lot of raised voices and eye contact. Laughing afterwards and making jokes signaled the end of the conflict, but it also re-enforced the "in character/out of character" divide.

    Anyway, that's my (slightly over-informed) take on it.

    \n Ajax, I have just grown increasingly skeptical of neatly defining things, especially in roleplaying discussions. Defining ritual narrowly, as it looks like people want to do in this thread, would seem to cut off many important avenues of discussion. Indeed, even among scholars who study ritual, the definition is open for debate, as it should be, just as any definition of roleplaying is going to be found problematic. That's why I was trying to get at what Matthijs was really interested in discussing, instead of focusing on big picture issues. My preference, in regards to the big picture, is defining ritual as broadly as possible and then looking at specific areas or issues within it, because we shouldn't limit ourselves needlessly, but talking about things at the level of "real broad" leads to really vague statements that don't count for much.

    The big picture for me looks like this:
    1. any action that is performed over and over again becomes ritualized; many, many elements of roleplaying are repeated in this fashion
    2. social interactions, over time, also become ritualized; this is what creates the social contract, which doesn't need to be intentionally or explicitly described by the participants
    3. rituals do not have to signify anything other than what they are.

    I would go so far as to say that lighting the candle before beginning the first session of a Polaris game is only a ritual in so far as candle-lighting and the behaviors that accompany it (such as reading aloud from texts) are already ritualized for participants, from their previous experiences. If I instructed players, in a game text, to drop their dice on the floor, that would not be ritualized, probably, until the third or fourth time they did it (or maybe more), because dropping dice is a normal, un-ritualized thing that already happens in roleplaying fairly regularly. Now, if you recited some phrase when you dropped the dice ("Once again I cast the stones!"), then it becomes ritualized, not necessarily because it signifies anything, but because reciting phrases is already a ritualized behavior for most people, drawing on religious rituals and other ritual experiences.

    I don't think roleplayers are ritual-minded or seek ritual anymore than other people. Sure, in some cases, it may fulfill individuals' need for ritual that might otherwise be fulfilled in other ways: going to church, going bowling with your buddies, whatever. But modern life, while it has changed the places in which people commonly go to find ritual, is not as devoid of ritual practices as one might suspect. As I indicated above, anything that happens regularly becomes ritualized over time, especially if it is performed with the same people.

    Anyway, that's where I'm coming from. Matthijs is probably coming from somewhere different, as is everybody else. Do we really have to agree on all this before we can begin to have a discussion? I really hope not.

    \n I tend to think there are some blind spots in the conversation. Folks who look at some of the acts or events mentioned and say, "nope, no ritual there!" are missing something, from my perspective--discounting an act because it's commpnplace, or has a practical purpose, or whatever. Of course ritual has a practical purpose. that's why humans use it so persistently and consistently. There can be direct practical value in the act itself (such as a ritual meal--people need to eat), but even in the absence of that there are emotional, role-dictating and social-bonding needs that rituals address.

    For instance, changing a tire is TOTALLY a ritual in my upbringing, a male rite-of-passage that demonstrates one's competence in performing "manly" duties according to a societal norm. My Dad would v\\never have called it a "male rite-of-passage ritual" of course, and I didn't have that vocabulary to express it either, but when he said, "Son, let me show you how to change a tire," there was no doubt in my mind what was taking place. My dad made similar rituals out of chopping wood and playing competent basketball and football. (I failed each and every one of these manhood rituals, by the way.)

    It seems obvious that roleplaying is particularly pregnant with ritual. The procedures themselves are ritualistic, and using them to create a Shared Imagined Space is about as ritual an act as I can fathom. I say, from behind my rune-emblazoned screen, "an Orc burst through the doorway and attacks!" I toss my die, consult it, and declare, "he hits!" And here's the key part: everyone at the table totally buys that this "happens." Nobody's going, "OK, dude, whatever. I'm grabbing a beer." At least, if people are disengaged in that way, you don't have functional roleplaying. instead, you've got folks goin, "Oh! Another hit like that and I'm a goner!" or "I'lI circle around and attack him from behind!" "or "is he vulnerable to Frost spells?" That can only happen in a ritual space. And yes, all games create ritual spaces of some kind.

    I'm not too worried about this broad a definition of ritual rendering the term meaningless or useless. those who don't see or value the ritual in everyday things may not care, but given that I care, or Matthijs cares, what can we-who-care do with it? The way I see it, it's not identifying or creating some ultra-rare and elusive Ritual Moment that appears only When the Stars Are Right that's the point. It's rather, recognizing that ritual is all around, that we then focus on harnessing and directing that ritual force toward your particular ends, say, in a specific arrangement of game design. Which is what Matthijs is looking for, I think.

    (Also, just 'cause you don't see it doesn't mean it doesn't happen, or that it doesn't have it's effect. it just means that you don't notice--it's invisible, behind the scenes, and you're not able or interested in examining it. And that's fine! It still happens, and it works.)




    So--I'm not sure if I have any concrete suggestions for you, Matthijs. I think the idea of a specific act that signals, "enough chitchat, time for game" is appealing. But Graham's point, about every group developing their own instead of being prescribed one by a game text, resonates with me too.

    The thing is that while gamers are ritual-minded, they're also fiercely individualistic. They often bristle at an attempt to control or even advise their behavior--"Ain't no rulebook/GM/fancy website gonna tell ME how to play!" And that's just regarding actual game procedures; something that's perceived as "out of game" or "just fluff" or "play advice" gets even less respect. So for every group that wholeheartedly embraces the Polaris Candle or the Beast Hunters Salute, there'll be another who bridles at the very suggestion, and may even be turned off to the whole game.

    Perhaps stating firmly the need for A beginning ritual, to mark off the play time and space, would be good, while letting the group construct their own? You could provide some mood-appropriate suggestions, and let them take it from there. The important thing is establishing the importance of that play space, while letting each group work out their own comfortable and meaningful method for doing so.

    Animal rituals

    The formality, patterning, repetition and rhythms of religious rituals have direct parallels in animal ritualized display. Some examples of those displays: courtship rituals, including courtship songs and nest constructions of male bowerbirds; friendship greetings rituals of baboons. Those ritualized display are behavior whose sole and primary function is communication, as opposed to behaviors that are incidentally informative. They have evolved to communicate within a species and with other species. Animal rituals can be fixed, context-insensitive, or can incorporate many variants and individually learned patterns and may be very malleable and may be very context dependant.

    The signals used in innate rituals have, through evolution, emancipated from their original function from behaviors where communication was an incidental benefit, and been transferred to new contexts. The associated responses are still tied to the original function of the signal.

    Elaborate rituals are \u201ccostly\u201d: one need time, energy and work to perform them. There probably was and is a ritual arm-race inside and between species, leading to the complication of rituals, which means increased cost. Increased cost means that the authentic ritual is more difficult to falsify and works as a selection mechanism: only those fit enough can correctly perform the ritualized display.

    Rituals convey simultaneously behavioral intent and important information regarding the status and condition of the sender.

    Performing rituals elicits neurophysiological changes: it alerts and focuses attention, enhances memory and promotes associational learning. Theses changes improve the ability of the receiver to assess both the signal and the sender\u2019s \u201chonesty\u201d.

    Performing a ritual prime both sender and receiver for action and elicits neuroendocrine responses that influence social behavior. The type of action that results is dependant both upon the receiver\u2019s assessment of the sender and upon the encoded \u201caction releasers\u201d embedded within the ritual.

    Animal ritual functions to communicate social information and to coordinate social behaviors through the use of species\u2019 specific signals evolved to elicit neurophysiological responses.

    \nReligious ritual

    Like animal ritualized display, human religious ritual is demarcated from ordinary behaviors and is composed of the same encoded elements: formality, patterning, sequencing and repetition. They also incorporate signals that serve as \u201caction releasers\u201d, signals of dominance and submission. As in animal ritual, such signals serve to elicit innate or learned neurophysiological responses in participants.

    Religious ritual incorporates learned signals, for example masks, statues, other representations, icons,\u2026 They elicit attention and heighten emotion. Incorporation of evocative, grotesque and dissonant features intensifies such responses.

    However, the fundamental elements of human rituals are abstract symbols without any innate or inherent meaning. This means that the significance of abstract religious symbols must be created, both cognitively and emotionally. Animal ritual elicits behavior through encoded signals, religious ritual elicits behavior to encode signals. The creation of these symbols provides ritual tools for the shaping of social behaviors. Ritual participation generates belief.

    Religious ritual not only identifies that which is sacred, separates it from the profane, but also creates the sacred. (E.g. Holy water is transformed through ritual.) The critical distinction between the sacred and the profane being the emotional charging associated with sacred things (like \u201cawe\u201d, \u201cpurity\u201d, \u201cdanger\u201d). This distinction in emotional valence is created through participation in religious ritual.

    Emotional charge (valence), learning

    Emotions motivate behavior. They are evolved adaptations that weight decisions and influence actions. They are out of conscious control and difficult to fake, hence they are reliable communication signals, useful for rituals.

    Emotions are elicited by internal or external sensory stimuli. The first processing of stimuli appears to be based on a positive/approach vs. negative/withdrawal division.

    Positive stimuli activate the (dopaminergic) reward system. It creates positive motivation toward a goal. The repeated activation of this system reproduces the positive association from goal-intrinsic stimuli to associated stimuli, which were previously neutral. This is \u201cincentive learning\u201d.

    Negative stimuli are critical in the rapid appraisal of potential danger or threats and elicit a greater response than positive ones. Negative stimuli activate a cascade of neuroendocrine events that prepare the organism to rapidly respond to threats and danger. These responses are hard wired into the nervous system. Neutral stimuli may also acquire negative valence by association. This kind of conditioning is hard to reverse.

    Much emotional learning occurs on an unconscious level.

    Religious rituals share a neurophysiological substrate with animal ritualized displays, as they both incorporate elements that evoke extensive autonomic and neuroendocrine changes in participants. But also, the uniquely human elements of religious ritual contribute to these neurophysiological effects.

    Rhythm

    Human ritual shares with animal one basic structural components of formality, pattern, sequence, and repetition. Human religious ritual further amplifies and intensifies these elements through the incorporation of rhythmic drivers.

    Rhythmic drivers include music, chanting and dance. They have significant neurophysiological effects. They are a recurrent and important component of religious ritual worldwide.

    Rhythmic drivers synchronize internal rhythms to external auditory ones. Affected biological system include respiration, brain wave patterns, pulse rate, blood pressure, muscles, skin, \u2026 Rhythmic drivers have emotion inducing effects, with varying intensity and valence. Musically induced changes appear to be very close to \u201cnatural\u201d ones.

    Rhythmic drivers also instantiate the structure of ritual through their formality, repetition, sequence and pattern.

    \nRhythmic drivers in rituals

    Participation in religious ritual induce neurophysiological changes. These changes are a stimuli that kick off emotional processing in both positive and negative systems. Rhythmic drivers can intensify the neurophysiological effects of rituals and thus are a mean to create highly aroused and intense shared emotional states in participants.

    The emotion rhythmic driver most often evokes is happiness, which, in its most intense version, may reach ecstasy, and is not unlike that attained through the use of drugs. These positive emotions can then bind positive meanings to other stimuli. Negative emotions can also be used that way, using physical and mental ordeals, creating powerful and indelible neurophysiological substrate for the emotional conditioning of religious symbols. Here, rhythmic driver ease the learning.

    Religious rituals create social cohesion. Here we go golden

    The ritually evoked emotions are quickly and easily perceived by members of the same culture. The use of rhythmic drivers in ritual to evoke and amplify emotions provides a mechanism for the ritual synchronization of motivational and physical states among participants (\u201ceffervescence\u201d or \u201ccommunitas\u201d). Empathy is contingent upon such shared states. Rhythmic drivers provides the associational context for the creation of communally sacred through the conditioned association of such states with symbolic words, objects and places. Subsequent use of such symbols to evoke conditionally associated emotions provides a mechanism for eliciting reliable empathetic experiences among adherents across time and space. Through the creation of emotionally charged communal symbols, religious ritual constructs the neurophysiologival scaffolding for empathy, trust, and cooperative group enterprises. Ritual creates social solidarity. Religious ritual contributes to intergroup alliances.

    Group membership
    Religious symbols serve as signals of membership. The ability of religious symbols to evoke shared emotional responses in adherents outside the bounds of ritual provides a mechanism for motivating and coordinating individual behaviors.

    Ritual further functions to decrease individual stress through the stabilization of social organization and provides a mean of facilitating both group fission/fusion and the coordination of group activities.

    Symbols as ritual-generated portable rituals

    While animal ritual encodes signals as neurophysiological primes for behavior, religious ritual encodes symbols created through the ritual process itself.

    The use of ritual to create associations linking symbolic, social and affective systems provided social groups with a highly flexible tool for motivating individual behavior, forging inter-group alliances, and for discriminating between friends and enemies.

    The use of rhythmic ritual to invest artifacts with symbolic, emotionally-valenced meaning provide dispersed groups with a tangible and motivational symbol of the abstract social relationships codified through ritual process. The symbols of religious rituals a mean of engendering congruent motivational states across space and time. Religious symbols thus provide tools for creating cooperative coalitions across time.

    [EDIT] This is really long. I never write posts of this length, but this seems to be a more-or-less complete summing-up of where I am right now with respect to ritual, flow and group dynamics, and it's taken some years to get here... so I guess it needed to be this long. [/EDIT]

    I've been reading a little bit in a book recommended by Jonathan, called Ritual and Its Consequences. If you read the first 26 pages, which are viewable online, and the Lehrich and Ericsson articles that Jituomas referenced above, you know as much as I do about ritual theory, I guess.

    My position right now is that play itself is a ritual. When we participate in a role-playing session, we're performing a ritual. There's a specific point, often noticeable, when the leader/keeper of the ritual - often the GM, sometimes the host or someone else - says "the game starts now", and then we're in ritual time, so to speak. After that, conversation is structured according to certain rules, written and/or unwritten, that are different from everyday conversation*.

    And during this ritual, we create the shining ball - we can "go golden". This happens, in my experience/opinion, when we perform well, when the challenges we meet are at the appropriate level - when all the components of flow are present. This requires that the group has reached the performing stage of group development.

    This means, again in my opinion and experience, that in order to "go golden", a group must (from the description of a "performing" group) "function as a unit (...) without inappropriate conflict or the need for external supervision. Team members have become interdependent. (...) The team members are now competent, autonomous and able to handle the decision-making process without supervision. Dissent is expected and allowed as long as it is channeled through means acceptable to the team. Supervisors of the team during this phase are almost always participative." In other words, it's probably necessary for the GM to be a participant - not just an arbiter, referee or facilitator. This indicates that there are certain ritual structures that may be better if the group wants to "go golden" - specifically, those that allow for the participants' autonomy and the supervisor's participation.
