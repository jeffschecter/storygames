"I hope I convince the Elders that my judgment was sound."
"I hope I show my mentor that I've become a good Dog."
"I hope I teach a young Dog that the gun is the last resort."

I thought this was awesome and I'm definitely going to try it out in my game.

    \n Well, Vincent has said how the story of a Dogs game is the story of the band of Dogs themselves, and how they're changed through their experiences. The book mentions going back to Bridal Falls, so why shouldn't you play that out? It provides a unique opportunity to shine a light on the characters in an unusual way. And should things come to a conflict... well, we know how to resolve conflict in DitV, don't we? Say "yes", or roll the dice. If you stat up Bridal Falls as a proper town, you lose all of that.

    This is just another opportunity for Dogs to be Dogs. Not every moment of a Red Box D&D game takes place in a dungeon, right? You've got to restock your iron rations and door spikes. You interact with the Duke and the shopkeeper, and, sometimes, things get down to swordplay.

    Well, your Dog has to pick up a new mailbag, account to the elders and so forth. Maybe visit some family. Maybe help train that girl he sent here from Oak Barrel Branch. If your GM is doing his job, there'll be times when you reach for those dice and times when you have to face down the question of escalation in a very different way.

    \n Not much extra to add except these notes:

    I think the BEST opposition for a Dog is... the other Dogs with whom he or she travels. As a GM (never been one, but I can imagine), I'd try REALLY hard to get a wedge between the Dogs--this is, in part, the point of Relationships ("Hey, wait, man--that's my Aunt Margaret you're about to shoot int he face!").

    Also, don't forget Ceremony: I've always imagined that exorcism is the "doctrinaire" solution for possession and Sorcerers (never mind "minor" sinnin'), not summary execution. It's REAL hard to maintain a congregation if every transgression results in execution (that also puts a damper on recruitment, donchaknow).

    As for the Dog's Stewards: that's one way for the GM to "influence" the interpretation of The Faith. Normally (i.e. out on the range and in towns), the Dog players are the "authority" on what is just and right in The Faith; the GM is encouraged to let them do that, as part of the self-discovery of DitV play. But once we start talking about the Ancients and Prophets and the Dogs' Stewards, it become part of the GM's purview: NOW, the GM can get his points in (e.g. killing is a last resort, only used after prayer and exorcism and even incarceration and "retraining"; or "children can't fully sin, not being fully aware of right and wrong"; or "Dogs can't and shouldn't dictate to non-Faithful"--which makes a nice push-pull between "don't kill all our flock" and "you have no right to tell non-faithful what to do, let alone kill them if they oppose you").

    And never forget the Territorial Authority (or whatever they're called--I always get it wrong, it seems). They may or may not be Faithful (and, thus, under the Dogs' authority to some extent), but you can bet they've got guns (even more guns, possibly) and enough Traits to give the Dogs a tough run in a straight-up fight (being solders and often outnumbering the typical threesome of Dogs). What kind of campaign arcs could you have with the Dogs on the run from vengeful non-Faithful militias (never mind pitchfork-wielding mobs of citizens)?
