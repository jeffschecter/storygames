\n I'm working on a scenario toolkit for Dread based on the implied universe of Blade Runner (just the film).

Goals
Exploration of artificial emotions within a truncated lifespan (four years). Tower collapse often indicates that Replicant's expiration date.

Help Topics (Please Comment!)
* Would you, as a GM or player, want to see Blade Runners in this game as playable characters, or keep PCs restricted to Replicants? I'm leaning strongly toward Replicants only, with Blade Runners as antagonists... however, I know that some players will really want to play the hard-nosed corporate agents.

* What questions would you include in the questionnaires that would reflect the setting and themes?

    \n Yes, but "what questions?" is the question. I think you'd have to give players a choice of questions or you'll end up with some unsatisfactory answers.
    "A tortoise is on it's back, baking in the sun. You don't turn it over. Why?"

    Many players, if forced to take this question, will say "I wouldn't do that." But that's not a satisfactory answer to "Why did you do that?" They may then feel forced to answer "Because I'm cruel." Obvious answers are leading and unsatisfactory.

    I might choose this question and answer "Because it looks sickly and gross. I don't want to touch it" I've established an interesting fact about my character/Replicant. He's squeamish. Didn't see that coming.

    "Your sister comes to you in a panic. She admits she's killed her husband and wants you to shelter her. You refuse. Why?"
    -She's my sister-in-law. She's killed my brother.
    -She's my twin sister and probably wants me to take the fall for her. The selfish bitch.
    -I'm a BladeRunner. I won't shelter anyone; even my sister.
    -I don't want to be an accessory (lame/obvious and unhelpful to establishing Character).

    Edit: "Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?" If that's not a question then your game is null and void ; )

    \n Question: you say Attributes have "mechanical impact" but what is it? Other than getting
    less chips to start with, there doesn't seem to be any effect. Is it because a higher Att
    grants immunity from certain pulls (if you have an "A" in Physical, you don't have to pull;
    "B" or less, make two pulls)? I seem to remember that from the earlier draft. So, Chips will
    act as a "Luck" resource for less capable characters. They can be applied in any challenge
    but you only have a limited number.


    It's cool that you used the VK Test questions from the movie but will the answers actually
    be useful in play?

    Although less interesting as questions, something like the following would be more useful
    to "push" the characters; short answers, listed bullet-style on a sheet, that you can refer
    to during play.

    Basic Test
    What is your -
    name:
    occupation:
    greatest hope/dream:
    greatest fear/phobia:
    greatest hatred/enemy:
    greatest love/joy:
    fondest memory:

    Quick, simple and easy to reference during play.

    The actual VK Questions could still be used for "flavour" but also tie into another
    mechanic: Empathy. That's what the VK Test is actually testing to see if you are Human or
    Replicant. Replicants don't have the life-experience to react according to social norms.

    Rachel fails the Test when here reactions indicate that she feels more empathy for the raw
    oysters than the boiled dog.

    Imagine a third Attribute: Social. All characters start with a "C" rating. Anytime a player
    "takes the pull" for another player (puts themselves in harms way, when the situation is
    related to their answers in the Basic Test), they go up a Social level (or knock down the
    tower, showing that they are a Replicant). Attaining an "A" rating doesn't prove you are
    Human, but it shows that you must be at least a Nexus "7"; almost indistinguishable from human, possibly unlimited life span, certainly more moral than most humans (Roy saves his assassin's [Deckard's] life); More Human Than Human.

    Quick example: failed Colony over-run by spider-like aliens. A character is attacked by one. Another character with Fear: spiders wants to jump in to kill it or otherwise save the first character (take the pull for them). Because this character is testing his/her Empathy, the combat result is secondary to testing whether or not the character gains a Social level or revealing that they are a Replicant.

    So, you survive the game and want to know if you're Human or Replicant. As game moderator, you have already looked over the actual VK Test Answers (turtle, mother, etc.) and put a check or X beside each answer, according to whatever criteria you want (funny,original,well-worded, spelling and grammar, doesn't matter). Count up the X's. Each surviving player can then nullify one X for each Chip they have left. No X's left indicate that the character is human.

    For all it matters, you could just ask the player "How many answers do you think you got
    right?" and compare that to a random number. But the above method actually gives the players
    some say as they might save/spend chips towards the end of the game to skew the result
    towards the outcome that they want ("I wanna save these three Chips and prove I'm Human"; "I'd rather use them up and survive even if it means I'm a Replicant"; "I have Chips left over but I won't spend them because I want to be a Replicant/believe I am a Replicant (although if they got all checks on their answers, they are in fact Human).

    Scenario Titles/Themes: (from the movie)
    - They don't advertise for killers in the newspaper.
    -"Skin jobs", cops and "little people"
    -Have you ever retired a human by mistake?
    -Painful to live in fear, isn't it?
    -Wake up! Time to die!
    -I've done questionable things.
    -I've seen things you people wouldn't believe...
    -lost in time, like tears in rain...
    - The light that burns twice as bright burns half as long

    Just some fun thoughts. Good luck.

    Edit: I just asked myself "what if you survive the game with a Social of less than "A". It says nothing about whether you're Human or Replicant. Just that you're selfish/unwilling to help another character.

    At it's heart, BladeRunner asks "what does it mean to be human?"
    Answer: to be humane.

    Having a low Social Att means that your character is, in fact, Less Human Than Human; regardless of biology. Imagine realizing that just after a Replicant sacrificed herself to save you from a spider-alien!

    "Do Androids Dream Of Electric Sheep?"
    "No, they dream of Unicorns."

    \n True story time:

    Back in school (the grade you\u2019re in when you\u2019re 9 years old) we had an old, old book that had texts and then reading comprehension questions. This book wasn\u2019t used in education anymore. I was doing exercises from it because I was ahead. (I was after in some other classes, but that\u2019s how school works\u2026 when it\u2019s reading time, it\u2019s reading time.) One of the questions was, out of the blue, a \u201cmoral\u201d or almost Voight-Kampff-ish style question. No other question in the entire book was like this, they were all \u201cWhat color dress did the mom have in the story?\u201d The question was not related to the text.

    \u201cYou see a squirrel eating the eggs of a bluebird. What do you do?
    A) Chase it away.
    Look on gleefully.
    C) Smash the eggs.
    D) Walk away.\u201d





    Now, back then I was very, very squeamish and scared of gory, bloody and scary things. More than anyone else I knew. I also loved squirrels generally (I had a squirrel doll), but I also loved bluebirds and thought this was a cruel side of the squirrel that I was not familiar with. I also knew about the circle of life. Some animals eat other animals.

    I chose D) Walk away.
    I didn\u2019t think much of it until I graded myself (by checking the lettered answer cheat). I had made only one mistake in the chapter \u2014 this question.
    The correct answer was \u201cA) Chase it away.\u201d

    I asked my teacher and she said it was OK, that there was no right and wrong, and that that was a weird question. She agreed with my call to walk away since I told her it was scary to look at it.

    Am I human?

    \n @ 2097: "Am I human?"
    I can't say for certain based on one question, without staring into your pupils and inserting a
    rectal thermometer. But I am concerned about how specific your memory is in some areas and
    suspiciously vague in others. You were 9 but don't know what grade. You recall the question and
    multiple choices vividly but not the name of your teacher or the book in question. These have all
    the ear-marks of a dream or an implanted memory. My best advice would be to get your affairs in
    order and report for further testing ... or RUN!

    Aaron_S, you know this already but as a segue I'll point out the one glaring omission in your
    text: a scenario. The default Dread scenario, "Beneath A Metal Sky", could be re-purposed, as could
    others. Chad Wattler (www.morningskye.com) has the Dread inspired games "Silent Memories" and
    "Lucid"; both of which push the Dread rules in different ways.

    Speaking of which, I've been thinking that the rules should reflect the specific scenario. It need
    not always be about "revealing the Replicants". The Blade Runner universe seems ripe for all sorts
    of stories. To that end, I've been thinking about alternatives to "Jenga" resolution. Something that
    emulates the rise and fall of tension that Jenga produces but on a quicker, smaller scale. Thus, a
    "fall" need not represent player expulsion; perhaps just the need to reveal a memory, secret or
    regret. I've been toying with a scenario idea that focuses on the themes of isolation and loneliness
    that pervade the movie.

    Anyway, my first notion was using dice or cards as a tallying system. Instead of pulling, you
    would roll or draw and the amount would be added to a running total. When the cards/dice exceed a certain number (21, 31, 50, whatever), it would be a "fall/collapse". The "fall" number would be lowered (to represent rising tension) and the tally would start fresh. Special rules could apply when the deck is depleted (automatic fall; end-game; etc). A face card could be counted as "0" but automatically add 10 to the next draw (increasing the randomness of the numbers; theoretically, a few bad draws
    could bring an early fall). Matching a drawn card with the last card in the discard pile (by number
    or suit) could be an auto-success. Specific cards could have special rules attached to them; perhaps
    specifically to a character's sheet; or perhaps to compel another player (eg. the Queen of Hearts
    compels another player to reveal a secret, any Jack compels another player to take an unnecessary risk).

    I hope to provide a few bare-bone scenario ideas soon, with a list of character options and situations to be thrown at the players/characters. A bullet-point list of potential situations/problems would be perfect for open sandbox play without having the game flounder or stall.
