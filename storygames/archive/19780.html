    \n Thanks Andy!

    I've had Tags compared to Aspects a lot. I haven't had a lot of FATE experience and my word as creator doesn't mean much since I can run it how I intend and not how I wrote, but I still don't think that they're really all that similar in play. Depending on what you do with Aspects of course. The bits that always bugged me personally about Aspects was their dependence on careful and clever wording and their very numerical effect (+2 to roll). I tried to keep them very transitory and easy to use, plus trying to make their effect more impactful than just a little math boost (I also don't like just +1s in general in the system).
    Again, this is me espousing my own game. However, given hesitation around their potential similarity to aspects, if you DO play it I would love some feedback on whether they hit the same notes that make Aspects hard or if they somehow felt off.

    If you choose to use a different ruleset that's cool of course. And of other options I suggest Anima Prime, it's very capable of the spectacle of the show.

    \n Oh goodness yes! Anima Prime is a good pick as well. Wounds are also really easy to track: you have three wound boxes, and if you can't defend against an attack, mark one off! Otherwise, it's got this neat dynamic of building up dice pools throughout the combat to unleash them in a strike.

    There's also some neat optimization tricks that go on in the game, such as stacking conditions on an enemy (Conditions are laid out in the rulebook as things that have different mechanical effects; it's pretty neat).

    \n For sure, I just wanted to explain the distinction I draw between Tags/Aspects in case you do play it because it's something I've heard several times and want to explore how they're actually linked.
    You're correct about damage being modeled in phrases that more accurately reflect the new situation post-move. It makes changes to characters' situations more significant because it both allows them to define how they affect their foes and it allows me as MC to restrict what they're allowed to do narratively (making their struggles actually affect the sitch, rather than just being a numerical state).
    I totally understand the difficulty of breaking away from a stress-track. Pick a game that your players will understand enough to enjoy, that's what's improtant.
