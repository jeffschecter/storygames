    I've been thinking about this too, since I unsuccessfully tried to introduce my kids to roleplaying using D&D 3.5 (before my mind was blown open at the Forge). Here's what I most want from children's roleplaying games:

    They should allow the kid to use and hone their creativity, respect and promote their input, and allow them to learn in the process.

    I think there are two parts in this to consider: How to keep a kid's interest, and how to promote the creativity and learning.

    I've already thought about making a Beast Hunters Junior version, and probably will at least for us at home, with simplified rules, but keeping the focus on negotiation and on allowing the player to come up with their own solutions. The game is designed to be played competitvely between the players (who are trying to beat the scenario) and the "Overlord" (who is trying to beat the players). But it didn't take much to alter the intent to be more like a traditional RPG.

She loves putting the snap-together dungeon tiles into whatever configuration she wants and populating the rooms with monsters and treasure. I really think she's only a step away from being able to play (and ultimately run if she wants) a regular RPG game of some sort at the ripe old age of 5.

    A side benefit that I anticipated and was realized was the educational factor. Descent uses a dice mechanic that determines hits, damage and range all in a single roll of multiple d6's. She learned a LOT of math by virtue of counting up the pips on the dice, subtracting the armor of the creature being hit and noting the remainder as its remaining hit points. I think it is no coincidence that she just got her first report card ever and is performing above grade level in math.

    To say that this whole process has been a delight for me would be an understatement. I'm fully aware that RPG's may not be a hobby she chooses to pursue long term and that's fine. I'm not trying to force it on her. But I'm glad she's got the foundation laid if she does want to continue and regardless, she's learned a lot along the way.

    I'd say that starting small is really important, that you have to concentrate on an age and stage of learning is important. Brand says there's an old saying in education: every grade is a dog's age. I'd say that for this, younger is better. The preschool set is the place to start.

    I also think that there are cues that can be gotten by picturebooks and learning centre games for kids. Repetition and ritual are critical to this age range. Key phrases will get you more mileage here than they will in Polaris. Kids in this age learn be re-doing and re-stating. Most don't have the capacity yet to start from scratch, but they're all about amalgamating.

    Early in this band, they will more likely tell you a story they will already know and may swap only one element of the story to make it their own. They might not tell or adapt the full story depending on their attention, retention or forecasting abilities. They may retell the story of the Very Hungry Caterpillar using an ant, for example, but may stop after the Caterpillar has eaten all the junk food because that's only the part they remember, or that's the part they like, or they may go on, and realize too late that an ant doesn't turn into a butterfly, so just end the story. Or, realism and continuity, not often being high on the list of kid story priorities, might turn the ant into a butterfly anyway.

    I wonder if maybe you could base a game that draws on the child's own experience by using one or two or three of their favorite picture books as a to harvest elements with - a storytelling exercise in which elements are pulled from other stories in a sort of mosaic meets Mad Lib. Alternately, story cards, like a simplified Everway might be an interesting way to approach the construction of a game. With pictures of easily identified objects: princess, prince, dragon, castle, horse, in a fairy tale game, giving kids familiar options to move a story forward. Letter cards might be used to find elements that start with a letter that could be introduced in the story. Similarly, feltboards or magnets would also be an interesting way to go.

    The keys are to keep it simple, structured, with lots of room for exploration and the expectations of the adults low. "Correcting" the child in the creation of story at this age is a powerful action, and it's important to be aware of what message you're sending as you send it. Which, like, you already know already, but I'm thinking it through as I type.

    It's also important to remember that kids advance so differently from each other. I knew kids at seven years that couldn't read full sentences or tell a story with a simple beginning middle and end, and I was playing full campaigns of AD&D with a teenage group at Harbourfront. Maybe some way for the facilitating adult to do simple assessments of the child's ability and inclination would be an interesting addition.

    As far as playtesting is concerned, I'd say though that's down the road, you might want to check your local library, community center or YMCA. All would probably welcome someone (especially someone who used to be a teacher) setting up a program on Saturday afternoons. Or, if you wanted something smaller, then you could probably make like Emily and Meg and set up something with a local school.

    There's lots of games that aim to do just these things, but they're not called roleplaying games. Possibly because of stigma, possibly because they tend to be short things that go for 15-20 minutues max, because the target ages tend to have low tolerance limit for any activity, no matter how much fun it is.

    For example, I've been using a pack called Skills for Life with my brownie guides recently. This is a pack developed by Guides Australia to increase resilence among kids, and is full of activity sheets and short games and activites. Like roleplaying out little skits with a couple of peers to practice listening skills, or self-esteem, or how to deal with bullying. For example, we recently did a quick game on bullying, that involved the girls writing things that a bully can do on paper arrows, making a paper shield, and then throwing the former at each other while using their shields to deflect them, shouting out what they'd do to protect themselves against that sort of bullying and striking heroic poses.

    One question I have is why teach these things through a roleplaying game? It may be the best way, but I think it's a question that needs answering. Additionally, my experience has been that it's best to have little formal rules for such activities. Arguing over what dice gets rolled can easily distract from getting to the meat of things. And if you're aiming to teach listening/team work/empathy, I wonder if rules, particular if they govern exactly those things, might stop kids developing the practical skils themselves. For example, my brownie guides have a peer leader for every 5-6 of them. They elect these, and no only do they run the elections, but they decide the rules by which the election is held. They sometimes hate it and want me to tell them what voting method to pick, and I push them back into discussing it amongst themselves because they learn group decision making. They sometimes don't like it exactly because it's a hard skill that you only learn by doing.

In summary, this is a cool area, there's lots of resources out there doing similar things that aren't labelled roleplaying games, and I think it's important to consider how important it is that it's a roleplaying game in comparison to how important it is that it develops the target skills.

    Claire,

    Do they have to be roleplaying games? Maybe not, but availability of a wider variety of means to get these skills through to children and let them practice it, the more chance you have of getting through and getting it to stick. A kid who might not respond to your arrows and shield game might respond to an RPG version of the same. It could be a preference for a type of interaction, it could be a learning style, but the variety of tools means a wider chance of getting there. Josh isn't suggesting that RPG games should replace the games you're talking about, but that they should be available to compliment them.

    Some other points:

    A roleplaying game need not be about an issue (like say, bullying) to get the message through. Many pointed skillbuilding games and activities for kids take a didactic approach to the subject, like the example you mention: here kids, this is what bullying is, and this is how you can arm yourselves against it. This is a good thing. It provides a message of support from the facilitator and gives the child an official skill and an official sanction from an important role model to deal with the issue. An RPG can be about something that seems entirely unrelated, but it can give practice and instruction in dealing with the issue nonetheless, integrating the message with play, and letting it absorb on an entirely different level.

    In RPG's kids play someone else, and by playing someone else, they can get closer to an issue without having to get scared by it. Just like in bibliotheraputic books, identification with a character bears the hard burden for the child and provide the child with a whole different level of absorption, in an RPG, this goes one step further as the child interacts through the safety of the character, and thereby accumulates confidence with which to explore the issue.

    In RPG's kids play against a concrete opposition. Realism can be extremely important to making the issue seem emotionally real, and to building a concrete sense of emotional empowerment. In the arrows and shields game, there is no risk, nor any realism to the game. The only possible failure in coming out of the game is that my peers or role models will not think that I have done well. In an RPG, the kid has a bullying figure right there to deal with, and the very real possibility that the bully could win. The impact when the child does win is therefore more lasting and more intense, and the child has had to apply the skills in the simulation of a real environment. Being able to use the skills when there is real fear and real risk with a real bully will transfer more easily than if the child has learned the skills in an abstracted way.

    There's a time and place and a kid for both methods, and why would we limit the tools in the arsenal?

    There exisits somewhere a fantastic 'curriculum' that an old friend of mine wrote called Build a City. It worls kind of like this:

    Start out with a full-size sheet of plywood, or the largest poster-board you can find. If you have the space, use the wood.
    Get a bunch of wooden blocks, solid colors are ok, and sawed up scrap lumber is fine.

    Use Sharpies (best on unfinished wood), stickers, and paint to make the blocks into the buildings in your city. Discuss what kind of city this is, and how many people live here. Are there enough schools, stores, hospitals, fire stations, etc for your city? Is there a jail? How do people deal with law-breakers? What kinds of laws will your city have?

    Use sheets of paper, scissors, and whatever else you like to make trees, statues, play grounds, etc. What sort of holidays do your people celebrate? What sports do they play? What crops and animals grow in and around your city? How is land use determined? Duct tape makes a good road.

    Then, once you have your City, each session starts with What's Happening (decided by the adult/leader/GM):
    There's a fire that destroys a home; where will the family live and what happens to them?
    There's a parade; how will we reroute traffic?
    Two schools are in a close rivalry in sports - how do the communities get involved?
    etc, etc, etc

    This scales really well between two kids and a grown-up and a group of up to 50 kids of varuous ages - just scale What's Happening up if more people need some part to work on.

    Be prepared to talk alot, and for the kids to be in author-stance rather than being 'in character'. It's elaborate make-believe with very little rules and the only mechanic is 'come to consensus'. It can keep you busy for two hours a week all school-year.

    Hi Mike,

    Bibliotherapy is a process where kids read books that are about a subject matter that they are struggling with (E.g. divorce) . The book can be designed specifically for the purpose or could be just a suitable fiction book. Usually, the most successful bibliotherapy books are ones with a character that is in someway like the child, to facilitate identification with the protagonist. The child undergoes catharsis as a result of identification with the protagonist's plight, and then on their own or with a leader (a librarian, or psychologist) the child works towards insight to build coping mechanisms about the issue.
