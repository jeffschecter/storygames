    \n There was an example in that thread, regarding antivenin.

    It's like this:

    1. The players describe their intended course of action.
    2. My random knowledge database, which is filled with all sorts of shit because I am an incorrigible information sponge, may kick out some data that is relevant to what the players said.
    3. I check to see if I can incorporate said data into the game in a way that develops conflict.
    4. If I can, then I quickly explain the data to the players, tell them whether or not their characters understand it, and find a way to factor it into resolution.

    You know how, like, Stephen Hunter or Tom Clancy novels will give you asides about technical shit? And then the details mentioned in those asides become (later or immediately) relevant to the conflict, making them not just randomly amusing technical details, but an engrossing piece of the actual story? (Stephen Hunter's a lot better about doing this consistently than Tom Clancy, in my experience, but I don't think he's as widely read)
    It's like that. Me explaining the stuff to the players is like the aside you get from the narrator.

    Does that make sense?

    Anyway, from the original thread, and Marshall's example, I do this a lot too.

    I personally hate deep research in my RPGs. As GM, I don't want to feel that I have to read books I may or may not be interested just to get into a character and the like. BUT, I *do* expose myself to media: Wikipedia knowledge-blurbs, youtube vids on the subject, and so on. This results in... well, I wouldn't call it "developing conflict", but it factors into both narration (description) and resolution factors (rules bonuses for "a good/plausible idea" etc).

    The place where this happens the most is when I run or play in a cyberpunk-style game (We've been doing Shadowrun on and off for a few years now in one of my weekend groups). About 3 people in the group work with computers in real life: I manage large enterprise computer storage systems, so know all sorts of things about storage hardware, encryption/security, networking, protocols, hacks and the like. My friend Alan doesn't know jack about hardware, but he is a programmer by trade, usually application focused or theoretical (he is employed by the W3C). Beth is a C+ programmer, and also has a master's degree in Psychology. When we get to a scene involving hacking, *we go fucking nuts with description*. We add in little pieces of information (and don't get into arguments; it's just a game), little embelishments from our real world brought over into a futuristic setting that really *places* what we're trying to do in character, and gets some of that capital I-Immersion with the other players. When Alan describes some fictional hacking protocol, then Beth jumps in for a sec dropping little snippets about coding, then I drop some scene describing how some network security thingy was overwritten... well, it looks like those "jazz ensemble" metaphors Ron E drops in the Sorcerer book.

    \n However, note that this is us bringing in stuff from our real work lives into the game.

    To a degree, I try to do this about things that I don't know about in real life as well, but I strike a balance. Say for example, I want to play an Elizabethan Era character (which I will, next week, in our Castle Falkenstein game). I'm going to read a little Wikipedia about the era, for say 10-15 minutes total. I'll maybe look up a youtube clip on dress or manners from the era (I'm a visual learner: Picking up a book and digging in just doesn't work for me). I am not looking for things to memorize, I'm looking for little baubles of information that I can use to spice up the narration in things my character does. Some might enhance complications/conflicts ("my maid tied the knots in my bodice; I want to have a tryst; can I relace without her knowing?" etc), but a lot of it will just become fodder for bringing into scenes, or aiding in giving flavor to what is happening in the game.

    \n I do this all the time, because I really enjoy doing the research. When we played a PTA game of undercover cops in Cincinnati, I made up a cheat sheet listing TV and radio stations, neighborhoods, cool local businesses, street gangs, local and regional law enforcement, and peppered it with names. This sheet was on the table, but as Producer it fell to me to pull from it most often.

    None of this is sacrosanct, and as players introduce stuff it becomes part of the fabric of the game even if it is "wrong".

    \nNO, not necessarily, but\n
    is there a strong drive for accuracy in SIMulation of things in the setting, as contrasts with (say) the sort of narrative authority one has when instantiatingUniversalisFacts?
    \nYes to this, unless accuracy doesn't actually contribute to the game, in which case I don't worry about it.

    Bear with me here. This sort of accuracy does not define RtD, and it does not mean that RtD is occurring -- just as the sort of narrative authority you get in Uni does not define Story Now, and does not mean that Story Now is occurring (you get that same sort of full-tilt director stance content authority in Super Action Now!).

    Furthermore, "doesn't actually contribute to the game" has many different forms. It depends on the game. With any game, you have to look at it and pinpoint, what exactly is fun about this game? It's a big, usually complicated answer -- it's never just one thing, and you can never just point out a Creative Agenda and leave it at that. You gotta get specific. Anything that supports that something, that's what can "actually contribute to the game."

    So, sometimes I'll ignore accuracy in RtD play because it fucks over the thing we're emulating/appreciating. Sometimes I'll use it because it gets us deeper into it, lets us appreciate it more.
    Sometimes I'll ignore accuracy in Story Now play because it jumps conflict or is completely irrelevant to the conflict. Sometimes I'll use it because it develops and/or complicates a conflict. (This "conflict" in the lit-crit sense, not in the sense of "conflict resolution")
    Sometimes I'll ignore accuracy in Step on Up play because it obviates tactics, or makes gameable rules into nonsense. Sometimes I'll use it because it can be gamed itself -- as long as you have a language for it in your System, it's always fun to say, "This and this and this are true; do you have what it takes to take advantage of this rather than let it shut you down?"

    \n Man, I'm coming in late to this discussion.

    Anyway, Marshall, I'm wondering if you can give me any thoughts on ways you've managed to dole out appropriate knowledge, without info-dumping a ton of stuff on the players.

    From the threads, I'm getting the impresssion that you are acting as the overall keeper of knowledge. Have you tried any other approaches?

    Mind you, I'm asking for entirely selfish reasons. I like historical setting games, and have been wanting to do even just a one-shot or two set in WW2 France for ages. I wouldn't be as concerned with technical stuff, but there's a ton to know about political and cultural environment that I'm going to be assuming players are unlikely to know, and that stuff could make a huge difference in how the game plays out.

    Right now, the only particularly clever idea I've had (IMO) for this is "idea sheets". Basically, I write down in short form all kinds of stuff I think might be relevent to understanding on 3 x 5 index cards. Then, I take the cards and whatever images I can find online and create sheets with that info more or less randomly distributed on them and hand them around to the players. These sheets would be almost like collages, with 6-8 squares of info per side. There could be more sheets than players.

    The players can freely hand these sheets around. The info is actually supposed to be a bit disjointed and random to encourage cross-table comparison and conversation.

    Mind you, this is all hypothetical. I have no idea how it might work in practice. The closest I've seen to this was a mystery game I played as a kid in school.

    One other thing I've seen that might have an application is the "canon keeper" concept from Archipelago. In that game, each player has an area of the setting/game universe that they have say over. It isn't generally a literal geographic area, but more of a concept area, like religion, technology, magic, whatever. For that specific area, the player can veto or ask for better info or whatever, with some final say in it when it comes up in play. The overall affect on play is to split up responsibilty for keeping a consistent vision among all the players, rather than just one person.
