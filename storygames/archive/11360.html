    \n 3D art is an awkward thing to get just right, and there is a lot of prejudice against it in RPG circles. Don't know why, maybe people feel that computers are impinging enough upon RPG's traditional territory. Most likely though, it's simply because most 3D art used in books isn't very good. Someone gets their hands on a copy of Poser and a few free models, slams them inexpertly into stiff, unsuitable poses and, most commonly in my experience, doesn't know how to use the camera to make it look dramatic.

    Yes, I may be biased since I am a 3D artist of limited talent, but I can spot the common mistakes that others make and the big one is style. 3D models invariably end up looking like emotionless barbie dolls because a) human skin is hard to do, and b) so are realistic facial expressions, so most people leave them at default. I mean look at the example link kindly provided above. Not one of those images is even smiling! They look like shop window dummies. The generic models you get also tend to lack a lot of character. They're too perfectly human and too average.

    Another mistake I think is to concentrate so closely on the models that you forget about the atmosphere of the image. Depth-of-field effects can do wonders, as can volumetric lighting, proper use of shadows and so on. And then there's cel-shading filters that make the images look like cartoons, those can (under the right circumstances) be a boon too. You have to tailor the atmosphere of the image to match the product you're illustrating or it will look terrible.

    So yeah, I agree that the dislike of 3D art is mostly due to its association with crappy Poser images used by people on a budget who can't afford the time and effort it takes to individualise the scene.

    \n In the best traditions of hyperbole: 3D CG 'Art' is a crime against common taste and aestheticism at large!

    While is isn't strictly true, I shall endeavor to briefly expound upon the stance.
    In my view 3D CG isn't a form of expression or representation in itself, but a medium through which to do so. One might draw a comparison with, say, oil-painting or architecture; the oils and stones are not art, but the combination of them in such-and-such a manner - a finished product - could be . What defines a finished product (a thing, in exemplar cases, of great beauty) is the artist or designer's manipulation of these base materials into a unified, realised single element. Technological matter is transmogrified into an aesthetic whole.

    Material is made beautiful, or wondrous (and thus 'art'), by the concealment of its manipulation. If we see a painting or building but cannot fathom at the effort, design or practice of creation we are filled with awe and appreciate it as art. If we see 'poor' art, and can guess at it's design or believe ourselves-as-audience could do better (regardless of truth) then this enchantment is broken and we despise it.

    3D CG is thus worthy of being called art (or rather, will be called art) when it reaches the point wherein in can mask its nature. The video Johnstone cites is the example of this - we are delighted by the way in which the creation mimics reality, or convincingly misrepresents reality as a believable imaginative structure. The majority of 3D CG is like viewing a building covered in scaffolding; ugly and in need of further work.

    -Mike.

    \n Well, that opposite conclusion is just as obvious: "good 3D art is good". Of course it is, now just get some out there to fix the reputation of the form. At least one person in twenty right out of the street can draw nicer-looking illustrations than the 3D stuff out there (number based on observing high school art exhibitions over the years), so I guess I just don't understand the allure of the 3D in static imagery. An awful lot of trouble so you can still be a worse illustrator than a rough 2-3 high school students per class.

    My own guess at this point is that 3D won't really show its strength before people have had time to attach enough emotional baggage to it. Once that amount of baggage combines with a sufficient level of technical skill, we'll start seeing some traction for the form - first in pornography, I guess, considering how practical use is already present in video games. I suppose the art style can then continue on to become the manga for the next generation - a familiar set of visual conventions that become powerful in expression and transparent in application for all sorts of powerful art. From what I've seen we're just not there quite yet; but then I wouldn't know, considering how I dropped off the 3D culture a bit over a decade ago when I stopped following new video game releases - when and if the form becomes a vector for powerful art, I'll likely lack the context at first to make sense of it, so I'll just go "Wah, that stuff's so shitty, dude! My granddaughter draws better than that!"

    (Let me clarify the above by saying that I do recognize that art is already being done with 3D imagery as a medium. However, the various WoW comedy videos and such do not really make use of 3D visuals as the art itself, but only as a visual shorthand that carries the literary element. We've yet to see 3D art that actually looks good for its own sake, the sort that you could put in an art gallery on the strength of the visual alone.)

    \n So, the thing that occurs to me is that the pinnacle of 3D animation (IMHO) is Pixar, and if you look at anything 2D they do (notably their art books, and often the credits of their movies), it's at least as amazing as their 3D stuff. Kung Fu panda also had a great 2D animated opening sequence. Doing 3D stuff requires a specialized skill set, but I think doing good 3D art also requires a good foundation in the fundamentals of art. The principles of design, composition, color, etc. don't go away just because you're doing stuff in a computer.

    With those crappy clipart collections, we're seeing people using 3D as a shortcut to producing pictures, rather than a medium through which to use their actual artistic talents. And with RPGs there are both budget limitations (a 3D artist who can produce high-quality stuff would be getting big bucks at an animation studio or video game development house, and out of an RPG designer's reach) and the fact that RPG books typically have black and white interiors that don't do justice to 3D art anyway.

    \n The problem is that 3D programs are used as shortcuts by people who have zero artistic experience. Making quality art requires hours of experience and practice, no matter the medium, it is true for cave paintings, pencil drawing, oil paintings, digital paintings, 3D renders and sculpture. The problem with 3D rendered art is that people have not taken the time to learn the basics of making art: Seeing, composition, colour theory and such. But the most important thing lacking is personal style, by using the completely neutral ideal bodies you never tap into yourself and deliver nothing meaningful. It becomes an exercise in constructing pictures with purely technical skill and no artistic components.
    \nI have quite a few friends slaving away at a top animation school and trust me, they do more hand drawing in a month than you will likely do in a lifetime. I am positive that the producers of the soulless poser babes can barely manage to draw a stick figure.
    \nMaking good 3D pictures takes a lot of skill and hours of work, yes, but it does not necessarily result in art even though you get a finished work of perfect skill. Art is in the personal process of creation, not the end result.

    \n No one's mentioned the Uncanny Valley effect yet, which is the bane of all attempts at lifelike CGI depictions of humans. (But maybe everyone here knows about it, and everyone knows everyone knows, so they didn't bother to even name it...)

    Throughout Pixar's films they've gotten better and better at rendering people by learning just how cartoony to make them to avoid this. The baby in "Tin Toy" is pretty horrifying (especially the drool! My God, the drool!), the humans in "Toy Story" were disturbing, but in their latest few films they've gotten very good at depicting people who are plausible 3D cartoons.

    I don't know the tech well enough, but I suspect that doing cartoon-style CGI humans well is at least as hard as doing "realistic" ones, and less well-known; so off-the-shelf attempts to make Hottt Babez end up plunging into that valley. When someone figures how to imitate James V. West in stock CGI apps, we'll be getting somewhere.

    \n Random notions:\n
    1. Take someone with little artistic ability. Have them do two things: a) use Poser to build a 3d fantasy image and b) using pen and ink (or brush) draw the same fantasy image by hand. In every case, the former is going to look vastly better than than the latter.
    2. Given the two choices above, the average reader would still prefer that neither are actually used as "art" in an RPG book.
    3. CG fantasy art by talented artists is at least as good as hand made fantasy art by talented artists.
    4. The percentage of crappy traditional art in products similar to those mentioned in this thread (usually d20 supplements of some kind) is about the same as the percentage of crappy rendered art in those products.
    5. Un-talented traditional artists generally know that they suck. Un-talented 3d artists usually don't, because they evaluate the result as "this is so much better than it would look if I drew it by hand".
