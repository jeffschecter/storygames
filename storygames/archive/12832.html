\n My friend Shael and I had some time to kill during one of his visits so we played Mars Colony. It was interesting and at points not the game I expected. I think Shael made it that way though.

Kelly Perkins was a social engineer who came to Mars with a resume of six great cities predicted to fail whose infrastructure he redesigned and returned to functionality.

The problems the colony faced were a general lack of healthcare for the populace that was causing massive discontent, acts of terrorism that could be directly tied to a minority party that was inciting its base with violent rhetoric, and malfunctioning gravity normalizers that were causing the domes of the colony to fluctuate between Earth gravity and Mars gravity which caused major crises.

So, key scenes:

- Kelly Perkins hiring a hitman to kill Councilmember Stuart, who was one of the people to stand up against Kelly Perkins's universal healthcare plan. He was actually one of the key individuals indirectly fostering anti-government terrorism. After his death, terrorist attacts subsided and there were rumors that he was supporting them more directly.

- After initially passing a limp compromise bill that was never really intended to kick in, a gravity normalizer malfunction causes mass injuries in one of the domes revealing that healthcare is still a problem. The gravity normalizer malfunction, by the way, was caused by a new experimental wire created by a graduate physics student that was supposed to correct the problems in the old normalizers. The public blames Perkins.

- Kelly Perkins invites Councilmember Roberts to a private dinner, ambushes him wearing a ski mask, and pistol-whips him while shouting, "FIND A DOCTOR, ROBERTS. FIND A DOCTOR. WHAT? OH YOU CAN'T FIND ONE BECAUSE THERE AREN'T ANY." The next day, Roberts announces his support for an improved healthcare bill which pushes through.

This probably sounds more gonzo than it actually was. There were a lot of speeches, interviews, gladhanding, and failure due to bureaucratic roadblocks, political obstruction, lack of funding, and a news media eager to speculate on everything Perkins did. But all that stuff was fun to play but probably boring to read about.

Perkins actually helped things out. Mars was still a mess when he left it. Terrorist attacks restarted shortly after he left, two of the domes had been converted into agricultural centers after their gravity cores failed which led to greater self-sufficiency but overcrowding in the remaining sectors, and while everyone had free access to healthcare the colony lacked the infrastructure so there were long waits, rationing, and all those things people warn about.

Afterwards, Shael said, "I feel like this isn't a game of solving problems, it's a game of taking credit for the problems going away on their own." Perkins' policies (and violent felonies) did seem to correlate to an improvement in conditions on Mars, but it was a total crapshoot on whether they worked or not.

Shael was a lot of fun to play with, by the way. His angst when one of his plans failed was intense.

Afterwards on our way to eat sushi we talked about how Kelly Perkins can really enact some ridiculous plans and have them succeed based on die rolls. We want to to try Libertarian Kelly Perkins whose solution to everything is to cut taxes and reduce government spending, and we want to try Commando Kelly Perkins and have him dropped off on Mars with a machine gun and unlimited ammunition.

    \n The real mechanical value of Mars Colony is the other way around: bad rolls pressure the player to have Kelly do bad things. You're tempted to craft Deceptions to get the job done. If you start off gonzo, or totally amoral and unconcerned with consequences, that dynamic is kind of pointless.

    The design goal of the game is obviously to address real world issues, comfortably distanced through the science fiction of a city on Mars (like all good science fiction), and the serious quandry of an individual trying to make a difference. The rules try to get you in this mindset with the Fear cards, having the players use real political parties as models, and presenting serious health markers for the colony (healthcare, education, sanitation, etc.).

    Serious issues give the game grounding, so when Kelly _does_ lie to all her supporters, it has some meaning.

    \n Bret, thanks for posting your AP. It sounds like a great story (even with the gonzo "tangent" that made me go WTF). I think catharsis is a very common element in Mars Colony games. Shael's tendency to make Kelly violent is not all that surprising.

    I think the thrust of this thread seems to be about the extent to which the game allows you to use any sort of plan you want with similar mechanical effect. The short answer is yes, if you want to "pray to the banana," you can. The game doesn't judge you for that. I think Bret and Hans have it right, though. There is a certain trust placed in the players to make a coherent story.

    Bret, as you said (and this made me very happy), "The game makes no political judgments." In order to accomplish that as a design goal, I could in no way favor one tactic over another mechanically. And I think it works well. I've only ever played in one slightly silly game of MC -- but even then the silliness was restrained by both of us. If things got out of hand, we could always say, "No." That's the beauty of the two-player form. It's intimate and very easy to regulate the tone.

    Marc, let's leave the wind-up toy design thread aside for now. I'm not surprised that you're confused about that thread. I didn't really succeed in conveying what I really intended to convey there, and I fear it's only going to confuse things now. The take-home point is that my intention for Mars Colony was to leave things open-ended so that the players could map their own political frustrations onto the fiction. Mechanically, the game is supposed to illicit reactions to failure. Success is almost beside the point.

    Finally, I'm really excited to hear everyone talking about the different Kelly Perkins personalities they'd like to try out. I truly did intend MC to be re-playable. Thus far I've seen Kellys ranging from totalitarian all the way to hippie. I've played Kellys that favored everything from gentle persuasion to military action. It's all been great fun.

    \n When I compare Mars Colony with the playtest version I think it's really, really refined now. The rougher edges have been chopped off and the remaining parts have been streamlined. I love it.

    Every time I've played Mars Colony there has always been a point when a one has come up on a roll. And it always comes up at the "wrong" time. (The clever thing is that you roll enough for it to happen, and any time it does happen it's the wrong time by definition, because every f'king roll is critical.) And you're faced with failure. In your face. Nothing you can do about it. Well, except Lie your way through it and keep the score but open yoursefl up for more punches down the path. It's a big question about whether you do it or not. And while it's your "character's" choice, it's also your choice as a player too (even if you don't realise it).

    Tim's game puts you in a position of confronting your failure and it's subtly coloured by your own fears of government (and yourself, but, shhh don't tell anyone). In the notes Tim lays himself absolutely bare on the page. It's powerful stuff and something almost every games designer shies away from admitting they do in their games.

    I like to play it soberly because by playing it gonzo I'm kind of deluding myself. I mean I'm pretending this is how I'd really react to failure, and it's not really me doing this. (But it is. And that's frightening.)
