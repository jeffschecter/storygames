\n Okay, so in my longterm project, I'm coming up on some gamer-assumptions, and I want to air them out here and see what folks say.

In order to have a roleplaying game you need characters (ie, roles for players to play), you need adversity (something to provide challenge), and you need a situation (fictional details in which the characters act to confront adversity). Right now, I'm treating this statement as a given, and I'm not terribly interested in debating it in this thread.

To split hairs: by 'make characters' I mean 'assign player resources to elements of the fiction', and by 'create adversity' I mean 'assign counter-player resources to elements of the fiction,' and by 'create situation' I mean 'create fictional elements ripe for conflict.'

You can create these essential ingredients in any order (I think). You can create any or all of them before or during actual roleplay.

For instances:\n
  • In your assumed-standard dungeoncrawl out of a adventure supplement, the situation (dungeon) is created first, then gets adversity resources assigned, and then (without referencing either situation or adversity) the player characters are created.
    • \n
  • In a long-term homebrew campaign, the first situation and adversity may be created before the characters, but at some point the later situations and adversities are created with existing characters already made (and having been played through the first few situations).
    • \n
  • In a Wicked Age creates situation first, then makes player characters out of people in the situation, and sets the player characters against each other to provide adversity.
    • \n
  • In Full Light, Full Steam, you make PCs first, then situation, then stat up the situation to provide adversity.
    • \n
  • Using Palladium's Villains Unlimited, you might pick out a supervillain (adversity), figure out an evil plot for him to enact (situation), and then have the players create superheroes to fit.
    • \n
  • In Capes, you make PCs and Adversity first, then start roleplaying, then create the situation (although prep helps create good situations).
\nNow, I think all of these are certainly valid. I do suspect, however, that there are different advantages to be reaped by doing things in different orders. Like, in IAWA, you always get character deeply embedded in the situation. Or in FLFS, the situation is always based off of what the players have flagged as interesting in their PCs. There are a lot of variables, though, and a lot of different orders to do things in, and I'm positive I can't track them all myself. So I turn to you, Story-Gamesians: what have your experiences been with different setups, what have you enjoyed with them, what hangups have you discovered?

Also, I'm kind of shaky on adversity-first set-up: what's the advantage in that, ever?

And math-folk: how many different ways are there to order four elements (Sitch, PC, Adversity, Start Roleplaying)?

    \nStructuring your thoughts, picking the type of challenge you'll be answering, setting the tone of the game. For example, in Johnzo's Exodus II game, the group brainstormed the creation of the entire set, plot and characters as part of the first episode. Aside from agreeing to play "sci-fi writ large", I think the first thing we came up with was the central, overarching challenge: a partially built multi-generational colony ship, an Earth that just went through the Big Collapse, and the decision of whether to stay (and help Earth) or go (and fulfill the mission). This, of course, also created large chunks of setting, but this setting was built around the challenge.

    Other examples are games where we (my group) decide whether we want to play a story of heroic rescue, or gritty survival, or of suspenseful race against the clock. I submit that Wilderness of Mirrors is a game where the challenge is picked first. At least, when I ran it I stated what the mission objective was and the players' take on this then guided the character creation, then the setting/mission creation.

    \n Continuing to toy with ideas, here:

    Situations that are informed by already-made PCs can make use of player flags, resulting in a situation tailored to player interests.

    Situations that are informed by already-allocated Adversity can be of appropriate scale: I can remember running a lot of games where the resources I assigned to my NPCs were either way too much or way too little to pull off the situation those NPCs were supposed to be in.

    Situations that arise through play may feel more natural, or give players a greater sense of ownership since they made it together, through play.

    PCs that are informed by an already-created situation can be embedded in that situation, allowing you to play from within a conflicted situation, rather than coming in from outside.

    PCs that are informed by already-allocated Adversity can stack up appropriately, determining how hard the following play will be.

    PCs that are created through play will have more appropriate abilities (well, I just happen to be a mechanic...) and probably attach themselves to the situation pretty thoroughly.

    Adversity that is informed by an already-created situation can embed the adversity in the situation. This is so common we probably don't notice it.

    Adversity that is informed by already-made PCs can 'target' PC flags, either emphasizing their frailties (so Captain Super has a vulnerable ward named Jimmy Banks...) or intentionally giving them a chance to shine (since Captain Super has heat-vision, I'll make the Ice King, which will give Super lots of opportunities to melt things).

    Adversity created through play (proto-NPCs) will have abilities appropriate to the situation and can play off of PC roleplay as foils.

    \n I think he's saying spurious in terms of one ordering being automatically better than one or the other, or them really occuring in a set order. That is, often a player will, in fact, define their character by some central adversity. Creating that adversity in his mind, first. "Hmm, I want somebody who's fighting off an orc invasion. But, let's not make them a warrior. In fact that's his problem, he's weak, unable to fight. But he has this gift of sight that allows him to see when bad things are happening. But nobody listens to him."
    \n
    \nNote how in the example, the player starts with a central conflict from which to define his character, but then moves the specific conflict to something more personal to the character. It's no longer about the war, but about him not having a voice, though something important to say.
    \n
    \n
    \nBut, generally, yeah, what Ryan said. Note that Chris Chinn, who came up with Flags, typically does "Central Conflict" as his method of choice for game set up (See his work on developing the scenario "the Well of Souls" with Peter Nordstrand at the Forge). That is, players or the GM come up with a central conflict, and then the players create flag-bearing characters that are inspired by the central conflict. Note that often, at this point, the central conflict ceases to be central to the plot - often relegated to the backdrop. It serves at that point mostly as metaplot that exists solely to cause the stories of the players to intertwine. So you don't end up with five non-connected stories.
    \n
    \nPersonally I find it enabling to have some such thing to work off of in character creation. It's only stifling if the GM assumes that I have to create a character meant to address the central conflict directly.

    \nNote, too, that what Tony hit on above - R-maps created with conflict where you then select the character off the map - that's Alyria, created several years ago by Seth Ben-Ezra.

    \n Joe, when I refer to creating PCs and Adversity in this thread, I'm talking about assigning dice* to fictional stuff, not coming up with the fictional stuff in the first place. I don't think I'd even try to control when people come up with ideas for games; down that path lies madness. So what Mike described is not "creating a PC" as far as I'm concerned -- that's finding a character concept. "Creating a PC" is when you start writing down, "Foresight 3d6, Outcast 2d10, Family Seal 1d8" and so on: when you perform a specific implementation of the character in your head. So when I say the PC/Adversity distinction is spurious, I mean, assuming you have some idea of who they are already, does it really matter which gets statted up first?

    * and by 'assigning dice' I mean dice, points, cards, feat trees, whatever: statting up.

Particularly if stats are involved at all stages that force certain later choices.

    \n For reference, Full Light, Full Steam engineers situations by taking player flags, creating conflicts about them, figuring out what 'cogs' (characters, sets, props) are needed for that conflict, juxtaposing individual cogs into complex cogs (the bartender at the extorted bar is also the admiral's mysterious lover), then assigning them stats, then writing it all up in a (hopefully short) abstract. As far as assigning adversity, NPCs are built like PCs, sets have "situation effects," and props have functions.

    In a Wicked Age creates a situation with a randomized generator (kind of like They Fight Crime!) and then players pick characters from that situation and assign stats to them. IAWA assumes interplayer conflict, so no need for a GM/NPCs.

    Villains Unlimited is a sourcebook for Heroes Unlimited with lots of pregenned badguys and backstories.

    I really like collaborating creation of situation, too, second only to collaboratively creating the setting through play. The thing I'm finding is that it's a really simple matter to design for the most complex (multi-player collaboration) and then allow for modification to the simpler method (GM does it all). Which makes me happy, cause I design what I like.
