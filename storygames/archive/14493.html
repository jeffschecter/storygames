\n Post some fantasy treasure - it can be mundane, magical, insane, creepy, or just plain old valuable. It has to be fucking cool though.

Rotten Leather Pouch
An ancient leather pouch full of holes and abrasions. The end is tied with a thin silver string which feels real if one tests its strength with their teeth.

Inside are a handful of copper coins that are equally worn. The smooth non-beveled edges bear marks of a clipper on several coins. The face side of the coin bears a silhouette, although the head has been scratched out on all of the objects. The back of the coin is smooth save for several small bumps akin to brail. The bumps seem to be meticulously placed and no two coins are alike.

    \nCedar Wax Wings
    A pair of magnificent wings, individual feathers carved from fragrant cedar and covered in delicate, transparent wax. They are joined by an elaborate harness that can be adjusted to fit any human-sized being. They radiate magic.

    When trying to fly with the cedar wax wings, roll +DEX
    On 10+ you fly like a bird until you get tired.
    On 7-9 Choose two:

    * You don't scratch the magical wax
    * The wings don't take you where they want to go
    * You don't get tangled up and fall

    Fail, and it'll be worse than just falling out of the sky, for sure.

    \n The Staff of Rods is shaped from ebony and polished to a glassy shine. Its considerable heft and perfect length (somehow, perfect for everyone who wields it) grant a Masterwork bonus when it is used as a weapon. However, its more interesting power can be activated by depressing the single ruby stud, in a bezel of gold chased with silver vines, at its center. This causes it to shrink into the Rod of Wands, an iron rod with rings of gold and silver at each end, whose unnatural density and perfect balance (somehow, perfect for everyone who wields it) grant a Masterwork bonus when it is used as a weapon. However, its more interesting power can be activated by grasping the ends and breaking it on the knee. This causes it to reform into the Wand of Staves, a delicate-looking baton of ash that is always green and springy, tipped with a star of gold and silver, and which is somehow heavier than anything its size should be, bespeaking a terrible secret beyond men's understanding. Its mindbending mass and perfect springiness (somehow, perfect for everyone who wields it) grant a Masterwork bonus when it is used as a weapon. However, its more interesting power can be activated by speaking a word of power, which causes it to grow into the Staff of Rods. It pushes anything in its path with a strength as great as a mighty warrior, bending bars or lifting gates that would be at the edge of human potential.

    \nA Gorgon's Eye turns anything it looks at into stone at varying rates; it's "beam of effect" is pencil-thin.

    It works almost instantly on easily-held unliving items.

    On flesh, it create awful scars in (about a combat round), crippling scars after (a couple rounds) of lasting exposure, and kills after (three-odd rounds) of ongoing exposure. Tracking a target to do this is very hard - if you skip from their face to the armor, for example, you've likely turned that plate into stone, but you're not on them anymore. If a holder is struck while doing so, this always results in a wide 'sweep' of the effect; pick some targets and roll.

    It is always working, and cannot be turned off.

    \nJade Marbles

    A small number of jade marbles are found in an old pot helmet hidden beneath a loose flagstone. The first time you touch the marbles you are struck with a moment of subtle vertigo.

    When holding a jade marble in your closed palm, whispering the epithet of the Fanfir the Betrayer, and then dropping the marble from the height of your abdomen time freezes. The one who dropped the marble is free to act outside the boundaries of space and time until the marble hits the floor with a sharp clack.

    Breaking the bounds of reality and acting outside the theater of time comes at a cost. The offender is aged rapidly that night. The amount of time and decay which is applied to the offender is unpredictable.

    \nWoodweavers Thorn

    Small, wooden dagger, handle covered in papery bark, blade smooth as if polished. Comes in a dark green sheath made of leaf that does not wither.

    Will not break in the hands of a warrior. If used by someone who has no marked skill with a weapon, however, the dagger is exceedingly powerful. A nick or scratch on bare flesh turns that limb into the branch of a tree. If the dagger draws blood from an open wound, the person wounded suddenly transforms into a tree completely, their feet rooting into the earth, their arms neck and head sprouting leaves. Over their face appears a wooden mask. Break the mask and the spell is broken with it, their form shrugging off the bark that entombed them. Leave the mask intact and they remain in a sleeplike state until the spell is broken otherwise.

    \nCrown of Badassery
    This plain gold crown is made of thin filigree with a dark stone set in the front that rests over the wearer's third eye.
    An arcane spell caster wearing the crown has his daily spell point allotment increased by [(caster level)/2]*10%. The crown achieves this effect, when worn, by drawing in arcane energy from its surroundings. In doing so, it becomes a sort of spell magnet. Spells targeted within 3*(wearer's caster level) feet of the crown deviate from their target to affect the wearer of the crown, instead. Spells targeted within 6*(wearer's caster level) feet of the crown deviate to a point halfway between the wearer and the initial target. Spells targeted within 9*(wearer's caster level) feet of the crown deviate to a point one quarter of the way from the intended target to the wearer, in the direction of the wearer. Finally, spells targeted more than 9*(wearer's caster level) away from the crown are unaffected. Once the pattern is sussed out, a successful spellcraft check (DC at GM's discretion) may allow a caster to determine the proper location to target her spell such that it hits the desired location between the target point and the crown.

    Silly name, I know, but I was feeling uncreative at the time.

    \nMaxwell Silverhammer
    This relic is an intelligent silver-headed +1 lycanthrope bane warhammer. A cleric who channels positive energy can spend turning attempts to enhance Maxwell's enchantment. The first turn attempt spent adds the Holy descriptor; each turn attempt spent after that adds +1 to the enchantment, to a maximum of +4. The effect lasts for one minute per caster level.

    Maxwell bonds via Lesser Telepathic Bond with anyone who picks him up and retains possession of him for more than two minutes.The bond lasts until the bearer's death. Once bonded the bearer gains proficiency with warhammer, and Maxwell can see, hear, smell, etc. through his bearer. In addition, he knows their alignment. He will lie to any evil character to the point of leading them to death so he can be picked up by some other character. The owner can not leave Maxwell behind. Should they be separated by more than 50 feet, Maxwell automatically dimension doors into the bearer's personal effects, or hand, if no pack is carried.

    Maxwell is deathly afraid of undead, and will scream audibly and uncontrollably when confronted with them. Until he calms down, he can not be dropped, and the bearer must make a DC 25 grapple check to remove Maxwell from his hand. In addition, the bearer must make a successful Will save against Maxwell's Will save (base 11) or suffer a -5 penalty to hit.

    Maxwell's stats are Int 10, Wis 12, Cha 16. He is a cheerful, optimistic, and chatty fellow, despite his long centuries of life as a hammer. He will often cheer his bearer on in whatever endeavor is pursued, especially those of a more amorous nature.

    Maxwell was a rather ineffective street urchin turned rogue who broke into the wrong temple. Local priests were looking to make a powerful weapon to combat the threat of a local lycanthrope war. They convinced Maxwell that they wouldn't turn him in to the authorities if he would participate in their ritual. They also promised him immortality and several pounds of silver. Scared of death, and none too bright, Maxwell agreed and his soul was bound within the hammer.

    Abyssal Bloom
    This small carnation-like flower was found beneath the Withered Hand of Acherot in the Abyss. It's pale flowers are a tone of flesh with black and crimson veins running throughout the petals.

    When a petal of the Abyssal Bloom is placed in the mouth of the deceased, the person's spirit will be drawn back to their body for a brief period of time. Their flesh will appear as it did in life, all decay is momentarily gone.

    After your moment has passed, the person's spirit is forced from the body and the vessel appears as it did before the Abyssal Bloom was placed in their body. However, the person who's spirit you brought back from the underworld is now stuck on this world as a haunted spirit, unable to return to their final place of rest.

    I hope the moment was worth it.

    \nThe Spite of Iris

    In an overcrowded market you come across a vendor practically giving away a small ivory statue and can't help yourself.

    It's later discovered that the woman depicted in the carving is that of Iris, Ice Queen of the Wasteland.

    If the statue is buried 6 inches below ground and a prayer to the Ice Queen is audibly voiced it will take root. The ground around the statue will blacken and everything growing will die within hours. This will spread the following day to encompass several square yards. Months later, the people of the village over the hills will begin to panic and some will claim the end of the world is nigh.

    \nIron Stones

    This odd set of objects appears to be a collection of small iron spheres, perfectly round and of various sizes. Once warmed (vigorous rubbing between the hands will work, but holding in an open flame is faster), the user can mold and sculpt them like clay, while retaining all the other properties of iron (weight, ability to be sharpened, magnetic, etc). There is a command word for each set of these stones; when spoken, the stones immediately return to their spherical form. The stones cannot be merged together or pulled apart, though they could be molded in such a way as to fit into one another.

    (I think I'm detecting a theme in mine - I always liked the really "every-day" magical items from old D&D stuff - both because I can see how they'd be useful in general, and also because I think they imply some kind of interesting backstory that doesn't have to do with great deeds, ancient kingdoms, etc)

    \n A Rod of Fire and Frost messes with heat from one end to the other, in stark defiance of normal physical laws. That is, one end is always warm, and the other always cool. It is sharp on both ends, and about two feet long.

    If the warm end is exposed to something that should make it warmer, that doesn't happen. The cold end will get colder instead. Dropped into a fire, the cold end will often get cold enough to put the fire out. The reverse is true, too... Plunge the cold end into ice, and you'll get a beacon of heat (many of these rods are at the bottom of lakes due to poor handling).

    The heat or cold being radiated keeps on radiating for about an hour, even if the source is removed - making the Rod a handy, if odd, weapon. "Charge it up" with a campfire, and then go to work with your icy poker. Just... Wear gloves. The rod doesn't innately protect the user from it's own effects.

    \nA Slide Lens is a large, very shallow round bowl - about three feet across, but only about two inches "deep". The convex side pushes things away, to a distance of about a foot. The concave side pulls things towards it, if they're within about a foot. Both forces are quite strong.

    A slide lens can be piled with stuff on the 'inside', and if the load is well-balanced, will sit and hover. It can be moved easily, though not especially accurately, by sliding a foot under it - the foot gets some pressure, and the lens slides away.

    However, trying to get things on and off the lens is always a trial; if you put a hand over it and are inside it's range, it'll get pulled on. If you don't somehow keep the lens from turning, reaching over it will often result in it flipping up and sticking to your chest and face. Hopefully, there wasn't anything sharp or toxic in it....

    Slide lenses are most legitimately used by sticking three or more of them to the bottom of a load that is suspended or on it's side, and then putting the whole thing up to hover. Of course, if not secured, a crashing stop or other incident can lead to them 'squirting' off under force, skimming away over the landscape.

    \n A morph sword is a magical weapon crafted by an antique school of fencing masters who prided themselves on their mastery of every single variety of sword known to man. Once they had mastered the various native blades of their homeland\u2014which debating military historians have variously attested to be modern-day Germany, Italy, Nigeria, Syria, Spain, India, Mexico, Japan, Korea, the Philippines, China, Puerto Rico, and/or (oddly enough) the Bronx\u2014exponents of this school made a morbid game of traveling to exotic foreign lands, barging into local martial arts schools, inflicting violence on the grey and unsuspecting masters in residence, and returning home weighed down with sharpened souvenirs of their maraudery. A clique of popular students at the school in question, who dabbled in the fashionable alchemies of the time, began to forge/commission/steal (again, depends whom you ask) swords which, at a subtle mental or neural impulse from the swordsman, reshape themselves into any conceivable construction of hilt and blade, from shin gunt\u014d to macahuitl, with the fluidity of vinegar running down the slopes of a salad. The most elaborate examples of this bloodthirsty artifice, either through their own awesome construction or their wielders' smug sense of superiority, indistinguishably mimic named weapons of great fame: the Green Destiny, Durandal, Dh\u016b l-Fiq\u0101r, and their ilk.

    Depending on what game you happen to be enjoying at the time, a morph sword may have elaborately detailed tables of alphanumeric statistics spanning the full range of different swords (but only swords!) the sourcebooks lovingly detail, or it may be, y'know, wetwork omnisword quicksilver. Universally, the morph sword's functional benefit is to afford its wielder's player the obnoxious freedom to choose at each individual step of action resolution the properties of whichever sword of the morph sword's particular power level (mundane, superior, Artifact \u2022\u2022\u2022\u2022, whatever) its inevitably statistically overeducated player wishes. Position with a zweihander and wound with a chainsword? If you can find the stats, an it harm one, do as thou wilt.

    Collectors estimate individual morph swords' values over a wide and appropriately unpredictable range. Their originators cannot be reached for comment, as the entire school accepted a challenge from an obscure academy of Hawai'ian martial artists, who showed up at the m\xeal\xe9e with cheap wooden longspears and slaughtered the morph swordsmen to a man at range with little effort.

    \nTiara of Telepathy

    This light metal headband comes in a variety of shapes and sizes, though all have the small icon of a bird carved of some kind of flat blue stone in the center. All who wear one of these uncommon, but not unheard-of, artifacts gains the ability to send a brief thought (no more than a sentence or two) that can potentially be picked up by any other who wears one; a wearer is always aware of when they "receive" one of these thoughts, but can ignore them until they're ready to allow them into their mind at their leisure.

    Any wearer will send and receive to any other wearer within a small radius (say, that of a small town). Additionally, when one wearer meets another, one may decide to "follow" the other by concentrating in a certain way. You will always receive the thoughts of those you "follow" as long as you are on the same plane of existence; you may choose to break this connection at any time.

    There are stories of "dark" tiaras, which cannot be unfollowed and which may actually grant the wearer the ability to compel those who hear him to certain actions.

    \nChalice of the Heart
    A large, ornate chalice made of solid gold. Some shallow holes remain where precious gems were once set. The carvings depict some unknown ancient war between Good and Evil. On the side are two handles. To use the chalice, fill it with water, lift it with both hands and drink it all without stopping to breath.
    If the hands that hold it have shed no blood for at least a day, and the lips that drink it spoke no evil of anyone and anything for at least a day, the water will function as a potent healing potion.
    If the hands that hold it have shed blood today, and the lips that drink it spoke evil today, the water will function as a deadly poison.
    Otherwise, the water is harmless, though it might induce some harmless delusions if the drinker has capacity for magic.

    \nIcor Ore
    No one knows exactly what Icor is. Some theologists claim it is the fossilized blood of the Gods That Were. Some scholars theorize it is the legacy of a long forgotten powerful alchemist. Whatever its origin, its power is starting to become stuff of legend.

    In its "natural" state, Icor resembles an opaque, heavy and dark-red or brown colored mineral. When properly treated (with a mixture of some arcane chemicals and some willingly sacrificed blood) and heated to an almost unmanageable temperature, it becomes a metallic liquid similar to quicksilver (only red). Icor then remains in its liquid state up until it suffers a considerably swift temperature decrease (most of the time by magical means). After it has cooled down, it takes on characteristics of a red colored metal.

    This ore cannot be alloyed with any other substance (not even by magical means). In fact, the outcome of any such mixture will invariable become worthless slug.

    Its most common usage is in decorative and functional metal gear, but the most priced Icor artifacts are, unsurprisingly, weapons and sets of armor.

    Icor items have a very powerful latent magic. If the user participates in a long and painstaking ritual involving willingly sacrificing some of his own blood and a very long introspective meditation, the item permanently bonds with the user and its magical properties manifest. (The bond is retained even after death.)

    The first and most obvious manifestation is a change of shape. The item takes on physical characteristics of the user's unconscious self-image (famous artifacts include a stylized and frightening thorned bodice worn by a very brave and very defensive warrior-princess, a demonic looking set of plate-mail belonging to a sadistic general and a simple but beautiful crown worn by a very pious king). Unknown to most but the closest associates to a true user, the shape is not permanent, and changes along with the user's self-image and values. If the user matures, improves and learns from his mistakes, the item grows more beautiful, magnificent and mighty. On the other hand, if the user corrupts, looses himself and becomes dependent on anything or anyone, the item becomes twisted, dull and fragile.

    It is theorized that one observant person might keep track of the user's inner journey of self-discovery and personal growth. The more beautiful it looks and powerful it is, the better adjusted and mightier the user actually is. (It should be noted this effect is completely independent of the user's morality and ethic views.)

    A second and handy property of Icor items is the fact that they repair by themselves, in a process close to living beings healing. Given enough time, any Icor item will return to its original undamaged state.

    A third and less known manifestation of the magic in an Icor artifact is a lot subtler. With time, the item will loose its shine and become a dark red or brown color. If the item is spilled with any quantity of blood, the item will seem to absorb it and recover its initial shine and rich crimson color. (The bigger the quantity of blood it "drinks", the longer the time it retains its shine.)

    Any Icor item (even an unbounded one) radiates a very powerful but unidentifiable magical aura.
