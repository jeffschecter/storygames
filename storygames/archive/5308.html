\n I'm pretty new, both to the Forum and to system design, and I have question which may seem obvious to veterans. In our (I have a partner) system we have a stat called WPR that allows a character to have will-points (hero-points if you are more familiar with this term). The idea behind the will-points is for characters to spend them in order to perform a heroic action that they otherwise would not have been able to do. Unfortunately, during play testing, everyone has used it as a dump-stat. Is there a way that we can encourage players to make use of this avenue in the interest of not only game balance, but also for interesting play. We would like to avoid having specific bonuses like "spend a will-point and receive +5 to attack" but still have a system in place to help out the GM and not force a game that is otherwise a numbers game into a freeform mechanic.

    \n I tried this once with similar results. The reason for it is what rules do in the game - if the rest of the rules provide a baseline of agreement about how things work, the players will gravitate towards that because of what's under their control.

    The will points that you describe are freeform but more importantly there's no clear line between what they CAN do with them and what they CAN'T do with them. So players will put their resources into controllable elements.

    Does your game have character advancement? If it does you could tie advancement to the use of those points - if it were d20 you could halve XP rewards but grant 100 XP each time a PC uses an action point.

    \n Hey Don,

    Yes, what I meant by "enduring" is that STR or DEX (or whatever your other stats are) don't get spent away. They're a constant asset to the character's effectiveness. Whereas WPR is something that gets spent, and so the player might find himself in the position of not having it when he really needs it. If you were to redesign the game so the stats like STR and DEX (or whatever) were also spendable resources (like WPR), then the player would be deciding during chargen between allocating character effectiveness to the ordinary human actions scoped by DEX and STR, and the extraordinary actions scoped by WPR. And the same is true if you were to redesign so WPR was not a spendable resource, as you suggest, giving a +3 or whatever to actions beyond human. In neither case would WPR be a dump stat. Players would invest in it based on the concept they had for their character.

    Paul

    \n WPR is likely a dump stat because it's more advantageous to have high stats that are going to stay high (like Paul said), than to put points into a stat that you're going to wind up spending away.

    For example, if I had the choice (in D&D, mind) between a 14 and a 16 in a stat and 1 or 3 Hero Points, I'd take the 16 because that 16 is always going to be there for me. The Hero point might be gone in the first session, for all I know.

    One way to loosen up spending Hero points is to tie them to advancement. If you use your WPR points in combat, you get a price cut on spending your advancement currency on your swordsmanship, for example. If you use them in Diplomatic wrangling with the Princess, you get a price break on Charisma. The more points you spend, the more price breaks you get, and they stack. Of course, you'd want to be careful about advancing too quickly.

    \n
    \nSavage Worlds has a mechanic called Bennies, which are very mechanical. Not really what you're talking about in a lot of ways, but interesting and worth reading up on.     \n
    \nOther stuff:
    \n
    \nThe Buffy RPG uses something called Drama Points. It's a pretty popular game and while I'm not sure if there's a free preview I'm sure someone on the main forum of RPGNet (or this forum) would be happy to explain the basics of how Drama Points work. I seem to remember that they could be used for halving the damage you take from an attack (by saying "it's not as bad as it looks") and for finding useful items which might be around (like an axe when you need one, or a handy wooden pole when you need to steak a vampire)

    \nPrimetime Adventures has a great and simple currency mechanic called "Fanmail". Basically players award it to each other when they think someone has done something cool. it can be spent for a pretty straightforward but powerful mechanical bonus.

    \n OK, first humble suggestion, check out luck and destiny in my system
    \nhttp://www.lulu.com/items/volume_12/201000/201024/4/preview/Legends_of_Lanasia_RPG_Beta.pdf
    \n OK, second suggestion, check out SoY
    \n Third suggestion, talk to the players and ask them what the deal is
    \n 4th suggestion, OK, so ASPs (a$$ saving points) are tricky animals. two things you want to avoid (from the players' perspective anyways) is no objective definition as to what they do. For instance, if you say, "You spend your WP and the GM decides something awesome happens," well, that is no guarantee that it will be useful at all is it? A lot of GMs think its awesome when the PCs are addicted to crack and have to decide between money for food and money for crack... And if you say, "When something really matters to you, spend the WP, then roll and you will get X bonus," the problem with this idea is that most of the things that matter to the player, their PC is already good at. A guy who cares about how fighty his char is is going to make a char that has high fighty skills, stats, etc. So they aren't going to know before the roll if they need it or not and will want to save that point until they "really" need it...
    \n I had this same issue, and the solution I opted with was, allow the players to negotiate what the point does. And allow the character a chance to spend it after the roll and everything has gone to hell...
    \n You may not like that option, but I can tell you that my group really used that mechanic extensively and effectively for over a year.
    \n Whatever you decide, good luck man!

    \n Some possible mechanical effects for WP-like points:
    1. Re-roll one or more dice
    2. Bonus to die roll result
    3. Add dice to pool
    4. Guarantee one or more successes (for die pool systems)
    5. Activate special abilities
    6. Allow someone else to help with a roll

    Some possible non-mechanical effects:
    1. Gain right to narrate an outcome (see The Pool, InSpectres, OtherKind, and many others)
    2. Gain right to state a fact (see Donjon)
    3. Change something about somebody else's narration (see Code of Unaris, pg 101)

    Some ways to refresh points:
    1. Regular intervals, in-game (e.g. 1/day)
    2. Regular intervals, real-time (e.g. 1/session)
    3. Mission/objective completed
    4. Do something concerning character drive/desire
    5. Play out character flaw/disadvantage
    6. Participate in suitable activity (see Pool Refreshment in Shadow of Yesterday for some good examples)
    7. Granted by someone else (GM only, non-GM-player only, any player)

    \n Don,
    \n I've dealt with an issue like this in CP2020 (a gamist system if there was one). I wanted to make a psionics system, without writing descriptions of every possible psionic power out there. So I did this:
    \nPsionics:
    \n
    \n I have a simple system for Psionics and it works great! Psionics is Empathy-based. In order to have a Psionic Power you must forfeit you Special Ability (Psionics are pretty Special as far as I'm concerned). The effectiveness of your Psionics depends on the scope of the power:
    \n
    \nBroad Powers
    \n
    \n These are not real powerful, generally the player must make a second check after exerting their broad-based power. For instance the character with Telekinesis +5 would have to first make a skill check with there Telekinesis (EMP+5) and if that succeeded, would have to make a brawling roll with Ref and Brawling to "Punch" someone with their Telekinetic powers.
    \n
    \nExamples of Broad-based powers:
    \n
    \n Telekinesis
    \n
    \n Telepathy
    \n
    \n Clairvoyance (i.e., distant sight)
    \n
    \nAdvanced Powers
    \n
    \n These powers are limited in scope, but still can generally effect most situations. Many times the Advanced power will be a contested roll against whatever defense the target can muster. In this way his powers are not guaranteed to succeed every time. For example the character with Pyrokinesis (power over Fire) +5 throwing a fireball at a character could be contested by: Athletics, Martial Arts, Dodge & Escape, or perhaps another Psionic Power. Whatever the target chose to do.
    \n
    \nExamples:
    \n
    \n Pyrokinesis
    \n
    \n Empathy
    \n
    \n Clairvoyance (defined as seeing the future)
    \n
    \n Levitation
    \n
    \nSpecialized Powers
    \n
    \n These powers affect a very narrow scope, and consequently are more powerful than the other two categories. Generally, the Referee will decide what, if any, defenses a target can use in a given use of a power. Example, The character with Stun Bolt +5 bolts an oncoming Solo. The referee examines the different factors and decides that since the Solo is running towards the Psionic character his only defense is Psionic (tough break for the solo, huh?), so the Psionic rolls and adds EMP+5 and the Solo rolls and adds EMP-2 (he still has his Combat Sense)
    \n
    \nExamples:
    \n
    \n Fire Bolt
    \n
    \n Animal Telepathy
    \n
    \n Clairvoyance (defined as Seeing the future through the eyes of another)
    \n
    \n Flight
    \n
    \n These are, of course, only examples and the player\u2019s power should be limited only by their imagination. With the sliding scale I have built into the system even Munchkins who ask for Nuclear Blast +10 are shut down, because first they will have to make a Psionics skill check (hope they don't like Cyberware), then anyone that is targeted by them gets to use their best skill to dodge.
    \n
    \nAdvantages:
    \n
    \n Visibility not required: -1L
    \n
    \n Automatic: -1L
    \n
    \n Power does not require an action to activate
    \n
    \n Aptitude (+ to rolls): -1L
    \n
    \n The plus should be based on the power (maybe athletics for telekinesis)
    \n
    \nDisadvantage:
    \n
    \n Exhausting (Risk Required) +1L
    \n
    \n Character makes a stun save after each use
    \n
    \n Physically Draining: +1L
    \n
    \n Character takes damage points after each use
    \n
    \n Mentally Draining: +1L
    \n
    \n Character takes humanity loss after each use
    \n
    \n Material Required: +1L
    \n
    \n Power has some sort of component or reagent necessary to activate (maybe matches for pyrokinesis)
    \n
    \n Touch Required +1L
    \n
    \n Normally psionics work at range
    \n
    \n Sympathetic Requirement: +1L
    \n
    \n Character must have an article that belongs to the target
    \n
    \n Emotional Requirement: +1L
    \n
    \n Character must be feeling a certain emotion to activate this power (must be angry to use Fire Bolt, etc.)
    \n
    \n Spiritual Requirement: +1L
    \n
    \n Psionic power is tied to the character's religious beliefs and character must be adhering to the precepts of their religion in order to access their power.
    \n
    \n Uncontrolled (- to rolls): +1L
    \n
    \n Wild Talent (User cannot control when power activates): +1L
    \n
    \n Referee will control when power can be used (this does not confer the automatic advantage)
    \n
    \n Character Weakness: +1L - Character weaknesses have to be extreme reactions to everyday occurrences.
    \n
    \n Character is allergic to ultraviolet radiation or takes damage from silver or..?
    \n
    \nQ: What is -1L and +1L?
    \n
    \nA: Well, you can use advantages and disadvantages to tweak the system. For instance, if you wanted a broad power, but you wanted to make it harder to defend against, take a disadvantage and voila! You have a broad power that is harder to defend against (e.g., +1L - where L=Level of power). Or if you want to take a narrow scope power and make it even more powerful, take an advantage. This will make your narrow scope power easier to defend against, but the advantage you buy should more than compensate for it. Also, I just wanted to point out that contrary to most systems, there is not a psionics point and cost system. Although these systems are intended to bring balance, they do not fit most fiction that includes psionics (we have heard of more than one mage too tired to keep casting, but never a psychic - in fact most drama around psychic powers involve turning it off).
    \n
    \n To summarize, basically, players have to split the emphasis on applicability to different situations and impact on the game world. I think you can do the same thing with WPR. Let the player come up with the effect, and let the GM determine its limitations. for instance, you could have a wiener player say, "I want the dragon to see my face and have a heart attack" and the GM can respond with, "OK, but he gets a save throw, he needs 3 or higher on one d20." Allow the two to negotiate it, allow, in the rules, a step where the immature player says, "Jeez, alright, what if he dies if I get a crit" and the GM can say, OK, but it has to be a crit within 5 turns of when the WPR is spent" and in that way, its not an all or nothing venture. But the player and GM are free to explore their creativity and come up with a solution that brings on the awesome, but is not outside the realm of what all the players (including the GM) want.
    \n
    \n Sorry for the long post, hope that helps.
