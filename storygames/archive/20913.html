    \n Hope I'm not derailing, but your question led me down a metaphorical path...

    Suppose we compare the "Why are you GM" question to the question: "Why are you the frontman of the band?" Everybody in the band is a skilled musical talent, all of them may sing, and they all have to work together to make the wonderful happen. But the frontman has an additional role: somehow they embody the band's image as a whole; usually they're not just the lead singer but the lyricist as well, which means they also represent what the band's songs are "about". I've been in a number of bands, and I've never seen any argument over who the frontman was. When you have one, it's quite clear. I've also been in bands where everyone knew there was no frontman. That is also quite clear. The only disagreement that might arise is when you have a *bad* frontman who thinks they're *good*.

    Thoughts?

    \n Wouldn't it be nice if somebody made mass-media rpg portrayals that depicted skilled players who know what they're doing and are a credit to their art form? Or maybe somebody's doing that and I don't know - I don't follow much web media, when all's said and done. It just feels like every time I see something like this it's about people who are either first-timers or utterly mediocre players putting D&D through its most-tired paces. It's like if media consistently depicted the art form of dancing by filming high school promenade dances.

    (This is not helped by the geek media tendency of focusing on the foibles of roleplaying in rpg narratives - stories about roleplayers tend to be comedies about how difficult and frustrating it is, and how weird the people doing it are. If roleplaying was only as good as it's depicted to be in these works, I wouldn't even do it.)

    If there were more high-quality depictions of roleplaying out there in consumer formats, who knows, the hobby might even benefit by having inspiring examples. My understanding is that other art forms benefit from having public, emblematic master-pieces that people study and aspire towards in their own practice. Meanwhile, the best single example that a roleplayer can work towards is apparently the Knights of the Dinner Table (and I'm not even kidding, that's about the only story about rpgs that I remember off the top of my head with even moments of high quality play, in between the comedy).

    If roleplaying was only as good as it's depicted to be in these works, I wouldn't even do it.
    \nThis is the nub of the problem of online debates about roleplaying.

    Rhetoric so often precedes the reality of play experience that it truncates the possibilities of the experience; those playing are more often than not linguistically primed by a combination of (a) promotion and (b) negative reinforcement than by more-or-less neutral announcements from those playing the game simply to enjoy what they\u2019re doing.

    The pre-modern version of roleplaying posited the \u201cright\u201d way to play as a formalised ideal or substitute for something that might actually occur at the table. Brief commercial success meant that it was difficult to distinguish the image of roleplaying and what was actually happening. We\u2019re now in the more-or-less postmodern era of roleplay in which it is impossible to distinguish between its reality and its representation.*

    Any debate of this order very quickly becomes a bunch of people wilfully misunderstanding one another: terms are so cheapened by misuse and repetition that they have little practical use. It\u2019s at least as sweet as it is idiotic, however, and here I am joining in

    *With apologies to Jean Baudrillard for this considerable bowdlerisation of his little-known work D&D in My Tricolour Underpants (1974; rev 1977).

    \n Order of the Stick is pretty good, I thought about it myself while writing my above angle. However: it is not a story about roleplaying, but merely an epic fantasy story with a D&D comedy sideline. Stories like this do exist, ones that have a grounding in rpgs, but use that foundation as just a jumping-off point. Dragonlance novels already did this 30 years ago, and they're no worse than similar paperback stuff with no rpg connection. They never tell us about the people playing the game, what they do to succeed in the act of play, and so on. In fact, they're not stories about roleplaying, except with the most vague implication that "maybe you might produce something like this if you played a roleplaying game, but I'm not going to describe the actual process for you here, because this is just a story".

    I mean, let's compare Order of the Stick with a more recent type of gaming comic, exemplified by Friendship is Dragons; the latter is actually an explicit story about roleplaying (well, it's a postmodern satire remix of D&D and the My Little Pony cartoon), so we can judge it as such: there are players, a specific rules set (D&D 4th edition), and an on-going story about how these people play, scene by scene, procedures and all, why they play the way they do, and what they accomplish in artistic terms with their play. As one might expect from my complaining about this, the process of play the latter comic illustrates for us is, to put it kindly, mediocre; I haven't played that badly in 15 years (barring the occasional off-day, I suppose), and here a writer depicts this as a normalized picture of what roleplaying is like. Again, if roleplaying was that sort of clumsy mommy-may-I railroad storytelling, I doubt I'd engage with it. I actually know I wouldn't, from my own play history

    (I should note that I do get that Friendship is Dragons has a lot of stuff going on that makes for good reasons for why the comic depicts roleplaying the way it does - depicting roleplaying is something like fourth-important thing in that creative gestalt, I think. Still, whatever the reason, the outcome is that it joins a hundred other media depictions of roleplaying that I think many roleplayers will find at most mildly amusing, but not particularly ideal as models to aspire towards.)

    The more I think about this, the more I think that I might just have an unrealistic expectation of what kind of roleplaying people who write gaming stories engage in. It's not actually at all incredible that there's a large number of gamers out there for whom the OP video or Friendship is Dragons are pretty much realistic descriptions of how they play. From this viewpoint it might not be the case that they're willfully caricatyric; maybe it's just an effort at realism.

    \n The main takeaway I'm getting from this video is, "Don't spring a brand new activity on people without ever bothering to get buy-in." Which, of course, would have made the video way less funny if corrected.

    I suppose it functions as a decent catalog of what you need buy-in for, though...

    - Why we imagine fiction and talk/act to share that.
    - Why there's a GM and why their authorities are what they are.
    - Why your characters are adventuring, and why your character needs to want to do that, and why you need to invest in advocating for them to succeed at it. In other words, how to have fun pretend-adventuring, even if it's something you'd never do in real life.
    - Why the characters are defined by stats, and why we bother simulating strengths and weaknesses of characters and opponents.
    - Why you might not want to live-act out an intimidation check.
    - Why the rules don't always cover everything and you might need to invent a way to handle something.

    Not bad!
