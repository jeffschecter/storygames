    August 2006
    In terms of play,
    Setting is a tool to create situation. Period. Setting is only useful insomuch as it provides, guides, and inspires the players to create compelling and entertaining situations juxtaposing characters in conflicts. If it doesn't do this, it utterly fails the very reason for its existence in regards to play.

    In terms of reading sourcebooks outside of play,
    Setting is a rich and detailed situation (contrast already-a-situation with tool-to-create-situation) with colorful characters, multiple sides, and many potential resolutions. It suggests more than it explains and leads the reader to flights of fancy and imagination.

    There's a large portion of the hobby that really enjoys setting in the second sense and mistakes their flights of fancy in reading it with the experience of using the setting in the first sense. I don't so much take umbrage at the folks who like reading sourcebooks (I like a well-written sourcebook time to time), but the inability to distinguish between playing a game and imagining what it might like to be in the setting kind of drives me up a wall.

    So I like Tribe 8 as a setting since it's a simple simple matter to create situations with concrete, specific characters and interests that can appear in the ensuing game. Contrast that with, say, Exalted, which gives very little help in actually generating the situation that the players will involve themselves in. Or compare Dogs, even if you consider it without Town Creation, with something like Deadlands. You read the setting bits of Dogs and you know exactly what a game will be like; you read the setting bits of Deadlands and you're left going, "Wow, that's a neat world. Now I should think of something wholecloth that could conceivably fit in one corner of it."

    Joshua has definitely nailed it.

    There are many settings that I have dreamed about, but if that dream doesn't translate into real situation for actual play, it's just a solo mind exercise, or perhaps a bit of shared fantasy between the players, but creates no real play.

    Looking back over some of my fondest game memories, I can see how setting translated into situation:

    My favorite Cold Iron campaign used the map and bits of setting from Judges Guild's First Fantasy Campaign rendition of Dave Arneson's Blackmoor setting. The way this translated into situation was that the way I ran Cold Iron, travel from point A to point B was quite an adventure as I tossed various "random" encounters at the PCs. The encounters would be based on the terrain, and the various towns and other landmarks on the map were destinations for the PCs. Occaisionally, I would place a "dungeon" from some module and send the PCs there.

    A favorite Traveller campaign used a setting I cooked up myself. One of the most brilliant star systems was one that had been a former penal colony, now rebelled. I don't remember all of why I got PCs to go there, but there was always excitement when they got there.

    Tekumel flopped for me. I couldn't turn it into situation. There was all this staticness to it. And a layer of political intrigue, something that just doesn't play well for me (partly due to non-support from mechanics, partly due to that just isn't something I'm interested in). Beautiful detailed setting, crap for play.

    Glorantha has worked for me sometimes. The cults give players reasons to engage the world, and there's lots of cool stuff you can do with the intelligent "monsters" that aren't always opposition.

    The Diamond Throne setting for Arcana Unearthed/Evolved was a total flop. The one thing that grabbed me, the hint of potential conflict between the Mojh and the Giants, got blown to bits in my first campaign by the player who had a Mojh PC, who became a Mojh to protect his Giant master... Suddenly the setting had nothing to create situation for me. He further killed things when I started to get them to travel to someplace different, and he used teleport to negate travel. Now the setting didn't even provide a situation framework in the aspect of a map.

    But something I've also learned - setting has to be accessible to the players also. My recent RQ campaign flopped when (most of) the players were not at all interested in learning about the setting. Suddenly we were playing "kill things and take their treasure." That's cool, if the setting supports it.

    Encyclopedias are fun (sort of) to read, but crap all for play. If the details they present can't be turned into situation, they're just a load of fluff.

    Y'all are getting it.

    Special thanks, Brendan, for falling into my trap. I was specifically somebody would mention those settings. As it happens, I'm right now involved in a thread in which I'm "converting" Greyhawk for use with Hero Quest. Guess what? Suddenly I can see where the situation can evolve from the setting, and where the characters are going to have interesting things to do relative to the setting based on the prompts they find there.

    But this isn't because the setting is particularly well written or anything. Again, it's just like every other setting, and includes everything you need really. As people are now pointing out, what really makes things pop is player investment in the setting. To an extent, this means making up the salient and important parts as you go. That is, grabbing from the setting those elements that you need for fun, and expanding upon them.

    To this extent, I'd agree with what Sydney said. That there's a limit of data at which you have more than what you need to go forward. In fact, at a certain point, the data starts to get in the way of being able to mold the setting into what you need it to be for play. Note how everyone loves their homebrew, specifically because they made up what they needed as they needed it for play? Characters headed to place X? So you enumerated place X to make it interesting enough to play in.

    Anders points this out with Zen-like clarity.

    So, truth be told, I think that the "Awesemofie this Setting" threads are misguided except to the extent that they are about using a better system. Because, sans seeing the actual characters created, and knowing the actual players involved, and why they came to play? I don't think you can say anything about what will make a particular setting better or worse. The best setting is the one you take from what you need to produce actual play.

    Sure, some will be more inspirational to this person or that. But when the chips are down and we start play, as long as the participants are pulling what they think is interesting from the setting - making it up if they need it - then it's all good to go.

    In fact, I'll even say this. The only RPG settings that I don't want to play in, are those that thought that by putting in a kitchen sink approach that they'd be more fun to play. That is any setting that adds elements in, just so that it's MORE setting, under the notion that this'll make it more fun to play? Those are the worst.

    No, even Rifts I'd play - the central conciet is not kitchen sink, but multi-universe if you use the right system (yah, with the Palladium system it blows). I'm talking about a setting like FATAL or SenZar, where you're given the option to play a flying bear with cybernetic lazer eyes "because somebody might want to play that." Those settings tend to be completely devoid of the ability to actually produce actual play, because they totally skip the part where the setting is about anything at all other than "getting to play whatever you want to play." Like, OK, now I'm a flying bear with cybernetic lazer beam eyes, and I do....what?

    Traveller is the best example anyone has given. I loooooove the idea of 13,000 populated worlds in the Imperium, and that I know at least 7 things about each of them. Yet I never had a game run successfully for too long because, in the end, the game doesn't give you any idea of what you're supposed to do. OK, the PCs are part of a culture of interstellar travellers. OK, and so they...travel. And see stuff. OK. But why? The closest I ever got to making this work was to enforce the "Free Trader Beowulf" concept on the party, and say that they all had to stick to one starcraft. At least then all the mechanics for trading and such could sorta propel play. In fact at times play became nothing but an exercise in starship economics. Only years later do I realize that this is because the system is a "then you do whatever you want" system.

    Like all settings, the Traveller setting is infinitely large, and has an infinite number of things to do. Unlike a setting with a good system, however, it gives you no particular idea of what play should be like, and so play meanders and dies. Not even the modules for Traveller could save it, because they're largely scripts (even their "dungeon crawls" are linear), and don't give the players any place to make decisions.

    Yeah, Shreyas, best to originally design a setting by marrying it with system, in which case ommission will be an intentional part. But in terms of taking an existing setting, I think instead of trying to say "Ignore all this stuff over here, and this one thing here, and everything about Y over there" simply employing a system that does that focusing is much easier. Yeah, the temptation will always be there to go trolling through the canon, I suppose, but that'll always be there. Unless you actually edit and re-release the text. But even then, if the players are aware of the previously released canon, they'll be tempted. Once something's been published and it's out of the bag, it's really too late to stuff it back in. The best method is to have a system that doesn't encourage players to go off hunting down the canon in question, but to focus on the important parts.

    So, if we're looking at Awsemo-fying Planescape, I'm saying that saying "don't pay attention to X" is not nearly so effective as simply employing a system that won't promote players going off and paying attention to it. Or, rather, one that promotes them paying attention to the awesome parts.

  • TomasHVM
    September 2006
    Originality
    Substance
    Dynamics

    The explorative mode is essential to good gameplay. A setting of really good originality will draw you in, searching for the hidden treasures beneath the surface of the text and drawings.

    Substance is needed for the setting to come alive. That is: you have to reckognise and identify with something in it, and it must be presented in a clear way. A clearcut substance will grab you by its solidity, necessity and consequence.

    A roleplaying setting has to be dynamic. That is: you must be able to use it, to find the gold without having to dig and wash and swallow tons of gruel. Make the setting in easy to pick elements, ready for use, ready for inspiration and interaction. The setting must be ready to dance in the hands of the GM and the players.
