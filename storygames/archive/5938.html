\n Given the passing of EGG and the recent-ish interest in story-ized hacks of vanilla fantasy, I've decided that now would be the perfect time to finally attend to the old draft of Window as D&D sitting on my thumbdrive. For the most part, it will use the standard Window Competency Ladder (with the rungs being narrowed a bit, running from d20 down to d6), sport skill groups based on the Basic D&D character classes (yeah, and races as classes), utilize a simplified Window Frame point-buy option, and introduce Role Levels. Now. . .

I really like the PTA concept of characters re-arranging values, rather than growing exponentially more powerful. . . but a total excision of this concept move too far away from D&D for my liking. I'm thinking that each level, characters gain some feeble amount of CPs (per Window Frame) to build up character skills and such each level and are allowed only to upgrade skills in the currently chosen skill group or focused on Ability and, borrowing from PTA (and D&D) that they may shift that focus when they gain a level, as well (either choosing a new skill group or ability to focus on if they so choose).

Now, I'd also like to get some scene-based resolution ala Story Engine in there. Blow by blow resolution is great but The Window can handle broad conflict with just as much aplomb and there is no reason that it shouldn't. I'm not quite certain how I should reconcile this with the traditional hack and slay milieu of D&D but I'm certain that you'll have some ideas (so lay 'em on me).

Also, let's look at treasure. I almost think that players should be able to spend treasure like action points of some kind. I'm fuzzy on the specifics, but it would give treasure a new dynamic other than "money" (because that's boring, dammit).

Much to think about. I'll post more/respond to posts when I have time. Thanks in advance for any input that you may offer.

    \n The Window is based on using polyhedral dice. Clearly, this is the chosen game.

    If you want broader resolution for fighty-D&D, I'd suggest porting the combat model of combat stages (everyone moves at once, then spells start, then there's a melee brawl, then spells go off), from FtA and I think Tunnels & Trolls (citation needed).

    Another take on fighting could be: everyone involved rolls their relevant die, and the best result of the group is used to reflect their success/failure. If you're not doing fighting in that way, you can first attempt some other attack that will affect the stats/dice of others before the fight starts. (The Cleric holds back to do a heal spell; the bumps up someone's health back. The Mage is using magic missle; that does some damage to a foe before the fight starts proper. The Cleric is doing a Bless on the Warrior; that bumps up her die for the melee round. Or whatever.)

    For treasure, I feel like treating it as a stat could definitely work, letting it go up accordingly. As such it's a "hoard of gold!!" stat, and when you do a spending roll for truly remarkable things, it's understood it may go down a rung (or perhaps upon any successful use).

    Posted By: DevPSo, is melee combat a skill in the "Fighter" skill group? Would that possibly increase for someone without spending a level focusing on amping up their fighter-level skills?
    \nMore and more, part of me is looking at using only basic traits that map to things D&D characters actually do in play (e.g., Casting, Fighting, Thieving, Socializing), with skill groups serving as specialized areas of study (as that is what skills ostensibly represent in Window). Rolls made against the general traits will have a lower target number of 4, while those made against specilized skills will have the standard target of 6. So. . .

    A character may have a d8 Fighting rating and that's what he'll roll for all combat resolution checks, unless he has some specialized training the Fighter Package (which opens up possibilities like "Two-Weapon Fighting" or "Blindfighting", etc). If he has access to such specialties, he rolls the associated die type against a higher target number of 6. He'll still have a chance of failure, though it will only ever be as great as 1 in 6 as opposed to 3 in 6 (or 50/50).
