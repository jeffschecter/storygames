- Why do we have random generators (dice/coins/cards) in so many games?

- How random do they have to be to be satisfying (assuming you like randomness)?

- Would this resolution system work for you:

When conflict comes up, roll a dice. The person a number of seats to your left equal to the number rolled decides the outcome. (This could be fancied up with the ability to move an extra person, in either direction, or state constraints somehow.)

There's nothing inherently bad about the idea, it's just not for everyone. I don't mind not being in the target demographic for this.

    \n I tolerate randomness because it conserves time and mental effort. But I don't find it innately satisfying.

    Randomness in resolution feels icky to me right now. Suppose you are playing by GM fiat, as in the GM does whatever they feel like. Except, instead of letting a real player be the GM, you define an imaginary player be the GM. The random scores are just an expression of the imaginary GM's arbitrary decisions. I don't see how that improves things. No longer is at least one player is being optimally satisfied at any given time.

    The method you described is better because it minimizes how much is actually resolved by randomness. Ultimately, a real player chooses the resolution. However, rather than randomizing which player gets fiat, it would probably be more fun to take turns between rounds picking the turn order within rounds. So even though a game played as you described might be fun, I don't think the randomness itself is contributing anything.

    \n For me, games are all about some kind of magic that happens when people sit down to play. It's about an outcome that is more than the sum of the parts; unpredictable, yet tied to how I play, and satisfying. The magic comes in a lot of different ways, many of which make use of randomization.

    Randomization can add the element of unpredictability. Like in games with a critical hit or fumble system, there's always that chance of the very unexpected. It can mix up the possible outcomes so that I can't hold them all in my head: like drawing from the Oracle deck in IaWA and getting an exciting combination of elements. Or it can smooth things out statistically over time, like my chance of hitting monsters in D&D.

    So to give my answer to your questions: we have randomizers because they are an extremely useful tool. The degree of randomization is of no importance at all, except that it be an appropriate degree to the game you\u2019re playing. That mechanic would work just fine for some things and not for others.

    One side effect of randomization, is that it can create the feeling that the outcomes are "just random" and that how I play doesn\u2019t matter. That\u2019s perfectly appropriate in some games. The mechanic you mention has that element of randomness. I think it would be appropriate for something that's supposed to be outside my control, like an agent of chaos. Or it might suffice in a situation where you need an outcome decided, but how it\u2019s decided isn\u2019t that important.

    \n There's a good post by Ron Edwards on "The Ball". It's buried at the Forge somewhere.

    Basically, imagine you're throwing a ball back and forth with someone. Throw, bounce, catch. It wouldn't be fun if the ball came into your hands, in the same way, every time. There needs to be variation: either because the other guy throws it differently or because it bounces slightly randomly.

    So there needs to be some unpredictability. Often, that's down to randomness, like rolling a die. In a game like Polaris, it's down to unpredictability on the part of what the other player will say. That's good too.

    I quite like your resolution system but I'm wary of resolution systems that aren't grounded in a game.

    But we've got a devoted improv guy, too, and he just adores randomness in any form. And we've got a couple of players who, like me, wander back and forth across the line of liking or disliking randomness depending on our moods and the situation. As a result, we're fairly ambivalent about randomness as a group -- some of our game sessions have more, some have less. People who are randomness-averse tend to build characters that will let them avoid randomness in certain areas (high attack bonuses so that they only miss on a natural 1, for example), and it all works out more or less okay in the end.


    What I did comment on earlier was that my gaming group doesn't work with shared narration and narration-passing mechanics, and a game which shared or passed around the responsibility of deciding the outcome of tasks would also fall in the "this won't work for us" category.

    We've got a couple of people who do not want to GM a game, period, not even for a scene, not even for part of a scene -- it is not a fun activity for them. We've got a couple others who don't mind running a game or taking on tasks that are usually up to a GM every once in a while, but don't like a steady diet of it and/or don't want it dumped on their laps when they don't feel like doing it. And then we've got a couple of people who just love GMing, love doing all those tasks, and will be happy doing them whenever they can...and will yearn to do them when they can't, in some cases.

    If deciding the outcome of a task is left up to the people who love doing it, no one has a problem. If the people who like doing that stuff get to do it once in a while when the mood strikes them (or get to make all the decisions for one game out of every ten or so that we play), that works out pretty good, too. But if the job is handed to the people who do not want it, then it's a failure for everyone. All the energy and fun that we usually have in a gaming session vanishes, and no one wants that. We get four or five hours on Saturday to hang out and enjoy each others' company and have fun playing a game, and there are plenty of systems that let us do exactly that...so when we tried narration passing and other GMless/GMful systems and they flopped, it was no big loss. We shrugged, said "Okay, we won't do that again," and went on to have fun with systems that worked for us.

    \n To the first question in the OP, I think randomizers come from our assumptions about the universe: that it runs according to impersonal forces and physical laws, and that statistical probability can accurately model the interactions of those forces. I ran into this head-first with my game, The Fifth World, about feral humans living four hundred years after the end of civilization. While we accept this as a "true" understanding of how the universe works, and it works very well so long as you have a certain level of soil fertility, and Holocene-esque climate conditions allowing for widespread agriculture, the earth has rarely afforded such circumstances, and humans have rarely seen the world like this. In my case, with a world where those factors have stopped, people have adapted by becoming feral, including animists. This actually has little to do with religion; as writers like David Abram, Graham Harvey and Tim Ingold have explained at great length, it has more to do with extending the notion of "personhood" beyond just humans, to what Hallowell called "other-than-human persons." As you might imagine, this becomes quite advantageous if you have to hunt with a traditional bow, or even garden using the techniques of horticulture (see also, permaculture). Anyway, an early version of the game used dice, but I quickly saw this as immensely condescending. Here, I played a game trying to play feral animists who see the world defined in terms of relationship, with every event determined by the interaction of independent persons. And to determine those events, I rolled dice, effectively patting those characters on the head, thanking them for their quaint little view, and then proceeding with the adults in the room with what we all really know is going on. That really bothered me, so I spent a long time working out a mechanic that held true to an animist understanding of how the universe works, and wound up with something related to the Prisoner's Dilemma.

    Anyway, I apologize if that explanation looped off into Tangent-ville, but I think it illustrates my main point, that we use randomizers in games in order to model how we understand the universe to work. So, depending on the kind of game you want to design, you might want to pay careful attention to the kind of randomizer you use (if any), because it will say something about your game and your fiction, and if you don't pay attention, it might say something you don't want it to.

    \n If we want uncertainty, then we don't necessarily need randomness. Uncertainty can come from many different sources. With different people, as in the Prisoner's Dilemma, you get uncertainty from incomplete information that gets revealed through play. Everybody has a piece of the puzzle, but no one of us can see what it all amounts to until we actually play. In fact, it doesn't amount to anything until we play.

    Certainly, randomizers provide a well-tested, much-used means of introducing randomness. But I wouldn't say that they provide the best solution in every case. For my game, I don't think it fits. Yes, you can have points that give you the chance to "make up" for randomness. If you have enough points to cover even the worst roll, then you have a system where it becomes more a test of how badly you want it, and the game focuses on your ability to budget those points. That can make a very interesting game, and one I actually did experiment with for a while. But it didn't fit my game very well; it still had that same condescending attitude, since you still had randomness creating the baseline. It followed that up with a sentiment of striving and achievement able to overcome randomness, but my game dealt with people who saw the world as a matter of relationships. The Prisoner's Dilemma really reinforces the themes my game focuses on, and introduces uncertainty not from a die (which an animist would consider just a particularly fickle person), but from the way individuals relate to one another.

    I don't know if I'd say that a result coming from another player at the table has a greater value than a result coming from the dice, but I do think it helped my game specifically. I don't know if I'd feel comfortable pronouncing any hard-and-fast rules about the means we introduce uncertainty into games, except to say that each one comes with its benefits, its drawbacks, and its statements about the nature of the world, so you should take care to pick the one most consistent with what your game sets out to achieve.
