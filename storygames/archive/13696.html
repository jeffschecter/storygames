\n So, here's my conundrum. I love Apocalypse World and I'd like to hack it for a dark fantasy setting I have in mind. My only problem is how to do it in such a way that the players are able to retain their characters for the full-length of large campaigns (i.e. the kind of globe-spanning fare where your character starts out as a pig farmer and ends up eventually confronting the God-Wizard of the Nine Voids). AW seems basically structured so that characters have a more-or-less complete arc in 12-15 sessions, at which point they max out and (theoretically) get retired or that story ends. But is there a way to adjust the advancements so it would reflect an epic-scale campaign that would last a year or two?

Sure, upping the number of marked experience boxes needed to gain the next advancement is one option, and would emphasize the concept of scarcity in the game, but would there be any specific mechanical drawbacks to doing that? Also, there are built in limitations with that option. You can only up the amount of marked experience so much before it makes advances ridiculously unattainable.

Also, I was thinking of multiple unlockable tiers for advancements (instead of just the one), but they would would have to be written really carefully in order that the game wouldn't get structurally unbalanced\u2014a hard thing to do when you're dealing with characters who could end up with more than double the number of advancements of those approaching the end-game of straight-up AW. Is there a way to address that potential power-imbalance?

Thoughts? Thanks in advance for anyone's input on the matter.

    \n Thinking out loud.
    Well, changing types is definitely a way to do it. I mean, even the basic game does it. First you're a driver and ride around a lot, then you settle down and become a hardholder until it all burns down and you wander the desert and drink from a weird spring and start opening your brain...the thing here is, I think, that your story, your positioning needs to take a turn, you become a person with many lives, more or less on the same level which means characters usually retire sooner or later, but the game can always go on.

    The kind of zero-to-hero fantasy campaign you're aiming at has a different progression agenda. Instead of this flat landscape where lives change but never truly rise outside it (for then the game kind of ends), the fantasy arc has us pursuing the level-up treadmill of experience grinding and endless power-ups (which often seem pointless because the world levels up with us).

    So, where was I...oh, yes. Yeah, tiers. Maybe try it like this:

    Get a "party sheet" on which collective XP is marked. When everyone in the group has collectively gained [some arbitrary number] of XP or advances, move on to the next tier of the campaign. Each tier unlocks new playbooks, fronts, moves, locations, etc.
    Once the PCs are on tier 2, all the threats they had previously faced on tier 1 are beneath them. When you roll against someone or something from a lower tier, you cannot get a miss result. If you roll 6-, you get to pick a 7-9 result instead (7-9 is 10+, 10+ is 12+). Against tier 2 threats things work normally.
    If you ever meet something from a higher tier, you take -1 ongoing vs them or something.

    If you have three or four tiers, that's enough for 30-60 sessions. Considering the average weekly session cycle of play, that's a year of serious regular play at least.

    Btw, have you looked at Dungeon World? That has a longer XP bar and 10 levels (I think).

    \nCool. I think that's an excellent summation of what Apocalypse World is effective at doing, out of the box.

    So, it strikes me that your biggest barrier in creating the WORLD-SHAKING, SAURON-KILLING climax isn't the rate at which characters advance. It's the fact that in AW, there are no status quo's. Everything is shifting and versatile, tides of control that quickly ebb.

    What you want is some kind of mechanic/system to say, at a certain point, "Actually, there are status quos. Sauron rules Mordor, and is slowly eating up all neighboring lands. Actually, there are status quos. Gondor is an empty echo of its former glory, its royal lineage displaced."

    So, Fronts are all about shifting power dynamics and fundamental scarcities, right? You want to start with Fronts, and have the characters contending with those things. But at some point, maybe around session 12 or so, you want to introduce some other kind of thing - a Not-Front. And whatever you call these Not-Fronts (Stases, Quests, Wars, Truths, Empires, etc), they function differently. They aren't about fundamental scarcities, but are instead about forms of oppression. They're about fixed and unwavering power dynamics, and the pains people endure as a result of those things.

    Let's say you call them Empires.

    Mechanically, maybe Empires look like this: instead of Threats, they have Blocks (as in: power blocks; as in: you're blocked from entering; as in: building blocks). Each Block is a certain unwavering power dynamic, and is tied to a tool of oppression: fear, might, rhetoric, withholding, decimation, possession, etc. And the countdown clocks work differently, a bit: they function like hit points for the Block: that power dynamic is unwavering until about 9:00, and is only toppled at 12:00.

    In addition to custom move options, you can assign Defenses to Blocks. Maybe one or two to each Block, and Defenses are situations where the PCs are going to be at a disadvantage. One Empire might be Mordor, and one Block might be "The Black Iron Gates." That Block has two Defenses: "Impenetrably by the Armies of Men" and "Guarded By The Eye." Whenever the PCs attack/interact with a Block and its Defenses apply, the PCs are at a -1 disadvantage to all their rolls. If you're leading an army of men to siege the Black Iron Gates, you're doing so at a -2. Yikes!

    The more I type, the more exciting this idea is to me.

    You keep Fronts, but you also introduce Empires. They're different. They're about control, and stasis, and status quo.

    Each Empire consists of Blocks, which have Defenses in addition to their Custom Moves.
    Zing! World-shaking is now ready to commence, as there is a concrete world to shake up.

    \n There's something beyond Dungeon World. When you reach level 10, your Campaign Front may conclude, but there are bigger things awaiting. Ask Adam about it.

    For us, that's the shift point. Dungeon World is a game about becoming a force in the world. Taking on Dungeon Fronts to eventually make an impact in the Campaign Front. Then, in whatever's beyond, you are a force in the world(s).

    Tiers of advancement have worked well for us. I think it works better with a large number of available moves though.

    I also wouldn't worry too much about the pace of levelling slowing. There's time-honored design behind that. If it really bothers you, maybe something like this: each time you fill the advancement bar, you get a minor move and mark a level checkbox. When you mark a number of level checkboxes equal to the level you want to gain, you gain a level, get a major move, and erase your level checks.


    Our idea of Sauron is a little different from Joe's. Because Sauron's not really a status quo, right? He's a guy, with an agenda. He's a Power-mad Wizard, with an impulse to seek magical power (in DW terms). But he's also got this smaller plots going on, like sending orcs to Helmsdeep, and using Wormtongue to poison the mind of the king of Rohan. The way I look at that, is like there's this big, overriding, Front, and smaller Fronts that are kind of like the threats of that Front. Sauron's like a Front of Fronts.

    I think this is how a lot of classic big campaign adventures work. You're not the guys who finally decide to take on the giants in the Frozen Fields, the giants in the Frozen Fields are being driven out by some worse evil. The status quo isn't the thing being challenged.

    Sauron as the established badguy, who's got what he wants, is kind of a different game. I think there's less drama there, since he's not a threat to the players. He's already doing bad stuff, which means the PCs have to be the only source of action. Not as cool, in my mind.

    \n I just want to emphasize that Gregor's right and you can keep playing the same character in AW indefinitely as long as 1) you don't die, 2) you don't retire them to safety, and 3) you keep switching playbooks. With a little bit of fiddling, it would be easy to think of playbooks more like, uh, lifepaths in Burning Wheel or whatever-you-call-them in Warhammer Fantasy. Bits of knowledge and experience that you accumulate and -- as long as you keep learning and adventuring and don't just settle down somewhere -- you can keep going.

    Joe's also right that you'd have to change the way you frame Fronts and the campaign arc a bit, but not too much, really. Sauron can just be a Front, right? He's just a Front that starts out being narratively distant from the character's location and ability to deal with when the game begins. But after many, many sessions, far away, inaccessible, and invulnerable Fronts and Threats become close, intimate, and vulnerable. You see what I'm saying? There are ways to make this work without scaling the power level dramatically, slowing down XP, or getting up into +6 stats that totally break the mechanics of AW.

    Things don't have to become quantitatively better (or more dangerous, etc.) over the course of play, they can just become qualitatively different. And because you follow the fiction in AW, if somebody says "I go aggro on Sauron," then the MC goes "How do you do that?" and sometimes the answer is, "Uh, I guess I can't do that now." So maybe it just takes an entire campaign to arrange the fictional circumstances such that you can say "I go aggro on Sauron by tossing the One Ring into the fires of Mount Doom" and that immediately makes perfect, obvious sense to everyone as something you can do.

    \n Yup. Sauron's remote and distant but still a Front. He's a Campaign Front. He has all kinds of spells, servitors, and repercussions that the PCs can face directly. They're Dungeon Fronts.

    From the OP, I'm not sure how much of the typical fantasy RPG experience you want, but there's plenty of space for becoming quantitatively better without breaking the roll+X, 6-,7-9,10+ system. Adding extra options to moves that make you choose, allowing an extra choice, allowing a choice even on failure, increasing hold, changing the dynamics of who gets to make a certain choice (like spending Hold from persuade).

    J-Walt's right, you can rely on the fictional basis, and that works, especially for Lord of the Rings. But if you're shooting for the classic long campaign RPG play, quantitative improvement is a bit of a genre trope. In Lord of the Rings, Frodo can go aggro because he's fictionally positioned to do so, he hasn't improved as a character per se. In the long run campaign, the halfling thief can take down the evil overlord because he's learned the shadow secrets of the nether realm and had the ability to strike even unto the heart of darkness, so he's powerful enough to get past the elite guards the surround the overlord, and he's also dealt with many of the overlord's plots to drive back his influence. Neither's wrong and both are workable.

    \n Just to chime in:

    My thought was exactly the same as those above talking about the fiction. I didn't get the sense in Lord of the Rings that any character actually grew in power: they just grew in courage.

    The hobbits didn't topple Sauron because they got Killer Stats, but because they figured out a risky plan that just might work.

    \n Hey Chris.

    My thought is: start with the MC's agenda. In AW-Middle Earth, the MC's agenda is to:
    - Make Middle Earth seem grand, extensive, thorough and ancient;
    - Make the quest thrilling, perilous and inevitable;
    - Play to find out what?

    ("Play to find out what happens" is what you tell the eventual MC, who is by then fully supported by the architecture of your entire finished game design. "Play to find out what the PCs make of this hostile, violent world, where all they have is one another and maybe not even that, and something is terribly wrong," is how you think of it as the designer.)

    So, play to find out what? 3 possibilities off the top of my head, I'm sure there are more:

    Play to find out whether the PCs can defeat the dark lord and destroy the one ring. In this case, the open question - can they? Seriously, can they even do it? - is what drives your game design.

    Play to find out what the quest will cost them, and whether they'll bear it. In this case, design costs, and leave the choice of bearing them to the PCs. (This seems like the most self-grandiose and least fun option to me; a shallow reading of the source material.)

    Play to find out the character's hidden strengths. In this case, you have to design your game so that (a) the characters have strengths, that are (b) deep and hidden, yet (c) emerge seamlessly from the characters' selves, (d) in surprising ways. Like Sam's loyalty, right? (This is my fave of the 3. A fun design challenge!)

    Posted By: OrlyChris, have you played Vincent's other game, Dogs in the Vineyard? It makes a case that GM-defined good and evil isn't really that interesting in play. Moreover, your vision of "good and evil" might not be the players. I mean, there's a way to read Lord of the Rings as White Anglo-Saxon Men versus the Dark Skinned Others from the South and the East. In the setting, the industrialism of Sauron is set up against nostalgic pastoral feudalism.

    AW instructs you to leap forward with named human characters. I think if one is too quick to cast them as irredeemably evil then they are not long compelling nor human, and interaction with them losses any ethical punch.
    \nThe way I'm framing the game is that the Overlord is evil. Unquestioningly and irredeemably evil. The origin of the Overlord (to a certain extent) will be up to the PCs during the game set-up. He might be a dark god or he might be a man who has made the subjugation of the world his top priority, but either way, the evil of his (or hers or its) actions isn't up for dispute. When you get into the named NPCs, though, I want there to be a large amount of grey area, like the cast of the Song of Ice & Fire books. Nobody in those books is black and white and they're all doing what they think they need to be doing in order to get by in a harsh and brutal world.

    So, in that context, what I'm interested in exploring is the characters' experience with little evils and how they deal with them against a background of Cosmic Evil. Say the PCs are about to raid one of the Overlord's strongholds and they come across a battalion of his soldiers that are about to put a village to the sword. The PCs can't stop the executions from taking place without blowing their cover and jeopardizing the mission, but is it a risk that's worth it? For one PC it might be and for another it may not, but if they don't step in, will they slide closer to a Boromir-like state of moral ambiguity? Forcing the PCs to consider their decisions and consequences in a moral context is what's making this game interesting to me. That's what I'm shooting for.

    Incidentally, I do think the idea of providing an Alliance/Horde sandbox for the PCs to play in is a really cool idea, but that's not what I'm after.\n

    \nThis is great food for thought, Vincent. At first glance, this is how I see it:

    You're playing to find out how the PCs will eventually manage to defeat the Overlord.

    The Overlord is defeatable--that's ultimately the point of the game--so the fact that the PCs will eventually have the ability to take him down is kind of a gimme. Everything in Overlord will have a cost, but none of those costs will be up front, and everything will rely on the characters' strengths, but none of those strengths may be glamorous or immediately obvious.

      \n Hey, Chris. Maybe I'm entirely wrong, so don't take this to heart.

      I think there's a hidden, inherent disconnect between "defeating the dark lord is a gimme" and "how will you defeat him"? I can defeat the dark lord by gathering up an army of hobbit farmers and storming Barad Dur? Why? Because I'm the hero? Ok.
      In that light I would rephrase your answer ("You're playing to find out how the PCs will eventually manage to defeat the Overlord.") through the third of Vincent's options (I'm sure we can rule the first one out and the second is problematic right now).

      "You're playing to find out how the PCs will eventually manage to defeat the Overlord through the characters' (hidden) strengths."

      If defeating the Dark Lord is a given, why is that? Because the PCs are heroes. We're playing to find out how they find, develop and use their (hidden) powers to achieve that end, because we know they will. In that respect, Corruption should only be a colourful reminder of the ring's power, of the magnitude of the burden and of the heroism of our characters in overcoming it. I don't think your mechanics should let a character Corrupt themselves out of the game for example, for then the question doesn't become "how" but "will you?". You don't want to get to a moment in the game where the last standing PC fails his corruption roll and turns over to the dark side, and you as the MC then go "Uh...ha ha, ok, just kidding guys, you're not actually corrupted, Gandalf saves your ass, carry on." (Unless you want to make that into a feature of the game.)

      Similarly with death: the PCs cannot die, because then the quest fails. When Aragorn falls over the cliff in the movie, he just goes missing for a while. Same with Frodo when he gets stabbed (on several occasions) - when the troll spears him, it's to showcase his mithril chainshirt, when the Witch King stabs him it's to showcase the cool skills of the other characters and his own heroism in pressing on. When Gandalf falls into the darkness, he is reborn with 300% more badass.

      (again, I could be wrong)
