\n Hi,

I'm looking for some words of advice, and things to pay extra attention to when running Dust Devils.
How long do conflicts generally last?
How do ya go about writing for it?
How much prep is too much?
Should I plan some conflicts ahead of time or not?

    \n\nEero_Tuovinen \n\n\n

    \n
    \n Prepare for the game by building up a few NPCs with concrete, passionate motivations and history towards each other and their community/communities. Figure out how these NPCs will approach the PCs and entangle them into their plots. Invent other ways to entangle the various PCs and compromise their neutrality; your job is to entangle the player characters so that they feel obligated and motivated to resolve the irresolvable conflicts your scenario presents. The golden treasure of the deceased miner, the prostitute with the heart of gold, the golden religious truths of the PC preacher, the man with the golden gun (OK, got carried away with the theme there) are all pre-created for the purpose of engaging the characters with your scenario, which always has human interests at stake. Bonus points for customizing your entanglements (Bangs) with the individual characters in mind, although this is not necessary in Dust Devils; your basic drifting gunman will run quite fine as long as you give him a vulnerable woman to protect, a jerk sheriff to flip off and an evil cattle baron to confront. Just make sure all your NPCs are involved in interesting and morally grey ways so the scenario has exciting revelations that cast new light on the matters at suitable moments, and thus keeps the players chewing.

    Conflicts usually last 5-15 minutes, although a conflict might get delayed and span a much longer time when the players play a tight game and fold often. It's imperative to understand the idea that all conflicts can have very serious consequences, and a player who plays a hand, especially a strong hand, is taking artistic responsibility for the carnage that follows: there is no such thing as a non-damaging conflict in DD, and thus the folding rules are rather important; the smart player plays tight, bluffs and only lays down his cards when he has the winning hand and he's willing to shoulder the responsibility of fucking up the other guy beyond all recognition. The game is usually relatively short for characters that are not played like this. (Nothing wrong with that, of course.)

    Speaking of folding, learn to think of conflicts not in terms of resolution, but in terms of tension: Dust Devils runs much smoother if the Dealer is conversant with the idea that a conflict can be even more exciting if it ends inconclusively and the conflicting parties live to face each other anew another day over the same matter. I say this because most conflict-focused games do not have such a central mechanic for delaying conflict resolution, and consequently many GMs are instinctively too resolution-centric; embrace and enjoy the fact that you can throw a couple of characters face-first into a conflict situation and then have both back down because the moment (cards, rather) was not right.

    \n I've run (straight or hacked) Three Days in Hell three times and it's good fun.

    Eero's advice is great. I'd add only one thing:

    For a one-shot with new players my experiences have led me to encourage a Devil rating of only 1 for all players. One time I allowed players to bump it to 2 during the course of the session if they'd taken any Harm in the course of acting in accordance with their Devil and that seemed to work fine too.
