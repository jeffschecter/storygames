\n You are nothing if not extraordinary. For the likes of you, adventure awaits around every corner. The world is full to the brim with treasures both imagined and unimagined, deeds both great and sinister, foes both human and inhuman. You are an adventurer, and you will seize your destiny or die in the attempt.

Deeds and Doers is a version of D&D that fits in your wallet, takes less than half an hour of your time, and you can play on the way to work. It fits almost every rule I use to play D&D into lean, hungry package, waiting to be played.

Deeds and Doers includes a Player\u2019s Handcard, a Dungeon Master\u2019s Grimcarde, a character record card, a dicecard, and Adventure Module n1: Vengeance Upon Thieves, all illustrated by Tony Dowler.

It is available as a pay-what-you-want download on the TAO games site

If you have any questions about D&D, I'd love to hear them!

    \n A save roll is something that the DM can grant a player in a case when, outside of combat, they have almost certain risk of serious injury (or stress or trauma or death). For example: a trap, a spell, a dragon's breath, a poison potion. In this case, the DM can call for a saving roll: a roll without modifiers which on success saves the character's life.

    \n It's not a good game text for anybody not coming out of a current D&D practice, really; in that regard a very limited audience. As is the case with any D&D text, it'd need quite a bit more explanations to be intelligible without the background.

    You always roll a d6, '5' and '6' are successes. You add +1 to the roll for certain conditions listed in the text, and -1 for certain other conditions also listed in the text. The caps on the bonuses ensure that you'll end up rolling for a target number from 3 to 6. The dice are rolled in significant fictional situations, and each roll has to count - basically a stakes system, relying on innate understanding of how to break down fiction in D&D.

    The saving roll is exactly like the other rolls, except it's entirely luck-based (no modifiers), and passive on the part of the character in that the GM calls for it instead of the player (as the case might be for other rolls). It's really nothing special, just a way for D&D to express the idea that when you've maneuvered yourself into certain peril, there is still a chance to dice yourself out of it. (This is necessary in D&D due to the way limited player information causes blind spots in the fictional positioning, sometimes leading to characters dooming themselves - you understand this if you've played enough D&D to know why it uses saving throws in the first place.)

    \n The high/low attribute thing is a situational pass/fail filter: the DM determines in each situation what would be considered a low, high or unremarkable ability score. They may opt, depending on their D&D background, to use a traditional sort of default array (e.g. the Mentzer scheme, which considers scores 9-12 on 2d6 to be "average"), but specific situations could have different challenges.

    For an example of application, consider a pit to jump over. The DM might explain to the players that anybody with Strength >14 gets the bonus, while anybody <8 gets the penalty. Simple. Perhaps another, even wider pit, might require higher numbers, or a DM might forgo this sort of detail in all but the most exceptional situations.

    Another interesting thing one might do would be to give the character with the highest score in a situation the advantage, while others roll without (and perhaps the worst rolls with a penalty). For example, with the whole party running away from the tiger of the Indian parable, it might be fun to see who runs the slowest

    You could also easily do resisted situations with this sort of rule: decide that a given monster has a challenge rating of e.g. 11, and then give advantage to anybody who challenges it with an appropriate ability of higher level.

    And of course, there's no need to do a traditional 3d6 for abilities; I'd maybe just roll d6 for each, that'd fit the aesthetics of the game. Doesn't matter for the mechanics.

    Would like a little more guidance on when leveling up happens. I'm thinking after each dungeon?
    \nI'd make it that the GM grants a level after each adventure to successful characters, where "successful" is someone who accomplished significantly in accord with alignment and goals - achieved something that they would actually find desirable as a character, that is. I say this in a somewhat circuitous manner because lateral, secondary and even contradictory goals are often a feature of our play in this type of game, and sometimes players would like to get xp for releasing that demon lord which they totally, definitely intended to release all along. Goals would also get revised as play rolls along, naturally, but I would never consider merely getting out alive to be "success" in this sense. Get the big-ass gemstone, manage to join the exclusive assassin guild, save the princess you fell in love with, finish the actual mission; these are forms of success.

    \n I don't think that "beating a challenge" and "telling a story" conflict in very much. IMO, and in may others, most stories are about overcoming a problem.

    Rules, even ones as minimal as this, are there to (avoid / mediate /) lessen the endemic conflicts of "let's pretend" in presenting an encounter with a challenge. One could try to resolve conflicts by assigning (narrative) authority alone, but for many people that is not satisfactory. In the same way the lottery is more acceptable (for many people) than having a celebrity choosing a worthy winner of the pot.

    While De&Do is grounded in narrative authority there is still a factoid-("success roll")-producing check and balanace on that. To increase checks and balances, have the DM roll, too, or the DM may only injure on 1-2 on the roll. This is the crunchy variant of De&Do: let's have a new card for that: "the gameist crunchcard" ;-)
