    \n My experience is that collaborative storytelling can:

    - be harder than roleplaying
    - make people anxious, especially if put on the spot
    - bring out a lot of bad social behavior in people without moderation
    - put shy people into awkward positions

    To be very clear, I think "collaborative storytelling" and "storytelling" are vastly different.

    Stoytelling is easy. We tell stories all the time.

    But even amazing storytellers I've met, people who are professionals and do this for a living, have a really hard time with the collaborative part of collaborative storytelling. Working together to tell a story is its own unique challenge.

    D&D is quite brilliant. It's way easier to tell a story if you have a clear character, motivation, and situation. And the rules allow for collaboration in a functional, non-bullying way that is so amazing... people don't even realize they're actually telling a story collaboratively while they are doing it! Story doesn't really enter into it. Just do whatever makes sense to you, moment to moment, given the constraints you are given. Constraints foster creativity. But D&D hides constraints as features!

    Don't get me wrong, D&D (like everything) has problems. For example, note how some of these features can fall apart outside the brilliant structure of combat or dungeons. But this also highlights how the structure that does work is awesome.

    I should also be clear that these potential problems are mainly with freeform collaborative storytelling. Structured collaborative storytelling can avoid these problems, depending on the structure used. The problems I've seen with freeform collaborative storytelling are similar to: http://gamingaswomen.com/posts/2012/03/game-design-and-sexism-player-feedback-mechanics/. This is partly why I love GMing so much. I can circumvent these bad habits, make people comfortable, and encourage collaboration.

    I think the best way to explain roleplaying is skip explaining and just do it in a low-committal situation.

    "It would be easier to just show you. Do you have 5 minutes?"
    "What are some TV shows or movies you like? Did you see [X], [Y], or [Z]?"
    "Cool. Who's your favorite character?"
    "What is something that happened to [NAME], or something [NAME] did that you wish was different?"
    "Ok, imagine you are [NAME]. Just like [NAME], you are [X], [Y], [Z]" (name details to get the person emotionally engaged).
    Then describe the situation their character is in. Use the situation that the person said they wish turned out differently.
    "Ok [NAME], what do you do?"
    Then throw in a twist that didn't happen in that TV show or movie tied to their actions to make it clear their choices (and roleplaying) matter.
    Watch their eyes light up.

    For Game of Thrones fans, I have the person play Jamie Lanister and put them in the situation at the very end of Season 1, Episode 1. Ok Jamie, what do you do?

    \n Jamie, we've added a point to the Talk we give at Story Games Olympia:

    Say the obvious thing.

    When it is your turn to give creative input, don't worry about saying the perfect thing. Instead, say the thing that is most obvious to you. It will come quickly to you, so you don't have to panic during the freeze and it will seem amazing to us, because what is obvious to you will surprise us ('cuz your brain is different than ours).


    This point helps players relax a bit. Repeating it at the beginning of every meetup is a good reminder that we are here to have fun and that just participating is half the battle.

    \n For what it\u2019s worth, here is what I have learned the hard way.
    \n
    \n1) Don\u2019t be impatient. You need to learn the rules like with any other game. You will make mistakes. It will probably take more than one session to get it right.
    \n
    \n2) Don\u2019t be sheepish. Don\u2019t try to second-guess what the game or the designer wants you to do. Make the game your own. Try to strike a note that rings well with you and your fellow players. When the rules say A and your (collective) gut says B, go with B.
    \n
    \n3) Don\u2019t be lame. Many Hippie Games are very easy on the players, but that doesn\u2019t mean you should be easy on yourselves. Strive to be as creative, engaging and responsive as you can.
    \n
    \n4) Don\u2019t play with people who aren\u2019t genuinely interested. You cannot learn the game if you cannot allow yourself to express your own skepticism, because at the same time you need to sell the game to your annoyed fellow players.

    \n This thread is great. I especially agree with the following:

    1. "Our kinds of games?" Exactly. Who are "we" and what are "our games"? It's all roleplaying games. Don't get sidetracked with this "collaborative storytelling" stuff. Don't even use that terminology with people!

    2. "Gamers and non-gamers (or first-timers) are vastly different types of people to deal with." The former being a total PITA.

    3. "People already know how to do this." Just let them.

    I can't say I've done a lot of gaming with total newbies, but I've done some. My advice would be as follows.

    Keep it simple. See John's post above. Don't overwhelm people with rules or big concepts. Start with the 10,000 ft. view and then dive down to the 10 ft. view.

    10,000 ft.: "This is a game about adventurers in a fantasy world, sort of like medieval Europe, but with magic and dragons and stuff."
    10 ft.: "So, you're an Elf. You have a sword and a pouch with a magic potion that will turn you invisible for five minutes if you drink it. You're standing at the door to a big stone castle. What do you want to do?"

    10,000 ft.: "This is a game about Buffy the Vampire Slayer. It's just like the TV show, but we're making up our own episode."
    10 ft.: "So, you're Buffy, with your Slayer strength and fighting ability, and you have a book bag filled with stakes and holy water. You're walking through the graveyard at night, and you hear some strange noises coming from a crypt ahead of you. What do you want to do?"

    Under-promise and over-deliver. YES: "This is a game." NO: "We're going to engage in a collaborative storytelling blah blah..."

    Under-explain but over-assist. "What do you want to do?" [Response.] "Okay, here's how we play that out..."

    In general, start playing as quickly as possible. Don't go nuts explaining rules or concepts. Just start playing and explain as you go.

    \n OK, I do have some advice for Story Games:
    \n1) I think one of the best games to start on is InSpectres. It is light-hearted, the player doesn't always have to describe what happens and when they do, they are given license to be good, amazing, etc.
    \n2) These are games where table talk improves the game, not detracts from it. If you have an idea, blurt it out. Just don't get discouraged if it doesn't stick...
    \n3) There is a tendency to get writer's block or whatever. If it is just a matter of a person being shy, stop and give them a chance to think about it. If they are stumped, give them a suggestion
    \n4) When you are told in the rules to describe an outcome/whatever, pretend you are the GM for a moment.
    \n5) Have fun
    \n6) Try and make sure others have fun
    \n7) Use the first idea that comes to mind, if everyone is following these rules, someone will make it awesome!
    \n8) Don't get caught up on Story Games vs Traditional Games, etc. All of these divisions are immaterial to your experience at the table. Don't bother with terminology/story games sales pitches etc. They only appeal to a cetain crowd and the people at your table may not be part of that crowd. For instance, if I want to play Dogs in the Vineyard, I tell people "It is like an A-Team of wild west Paladins that have six-shooters instead of swords." Most people get it instantly and can tell you if that sounds interesting to them. As opposed to "A mormon wild west that never was" which has never sparked interest in anyone I know...
    \n9) Don't try and sell people on what is different about these games, sell them on how much less work it is for the same or more amount of fun.
    \nDave M

    \nThis is strong advice.

    I'd add:\n
      \n
    • Don't try to be original - many people self-censor out of a desire to be seen to be creative or original. Nothing's original.
      • \n
    • Don't try to be good. Be average. People who are trying hard are tense. People who are being average can be comfortable.
      • \n
    • "Failure" can be more interesting than success - if we wanted all the roleplayers to be like yourself and never do anything other than what you would do, then you wouldn;t play with them, right? SO a choice that I myself view as surprising or odd is the whole point of getting you to play.
      • \n
    • Create a safe space for doing it "wrong" - here's where your choice of game, and choice of setting within game, can be crucial. I like InSpectres because the premise is wacky and off-the wall, so almost nothing feels inappropriate for someone to advance.
      • \n
    • Accept their suggestions with enthusiasm - if this provision worries you because "what if they say the wrong kind of thing", you should probably play a different game.
      • \n
    • If they fail in way that really genuinely doesn't work, audibly and enthusiastically blame yourself - this helps convince them it's not their fault. Remind yourself, and them, that this is their first time, so no-one expects them to be good at it. Ask them to compare to their first time doing other activities that they're now good at (dancing, playing the piano, whatever) - hopefully you'll raise a smile, and a knowledge that early inept attempts don't preclude the idea of later good ones.
      • \n
    • It's okay to fail - if something's a mistake or problematic, we can walk it back. Retcons and rewrites should be an essential part of your roleplaying practice.
      • \n
    \nMost of this advice was shamelessly stolen from the best book about encouraging creativity ever: Impro for Storytellers.
