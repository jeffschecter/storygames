\n I need some help on a game design issue. I'm an author of fiction (sci-fi, fantasy, martial arts epics and superhero genres) and I'm interested in creating a system that I can plug-and-play my settings with. It will be marketed to fans of my fiction, not hard-core gamers, so it could serve as a gateway game for new players. Although, that's just an excuse for me to be able to play around in my own worlds, which I love.

The game, thus far, follows the theme of narration. It was inspired by Fate, Technoir, Lady Blackbird and, to a lesser degree, the Storyteller system. The concept is as follows.

The author (Gamemaster) lays out the narration and directs the plot. The story involves nouns (people, places and things), of which protagonists (player characters) are the most important. The author moves these nouns around until conflict erupts. Conflict happens when a noun tries to change the narrative by adding, revising or editing out adjectives on other nouns. Nouns use verbs (skills) to change the narrative by proposing which adjectives they want to add or modify.

The Law of Narrative Truth (LoNT) dictates what happens in the narrative and MUST be obeyed. To add/modify an adjective it must be believable to do so. Once it has happened, the narrative has been changed and the next acting player should consider this before they decide on their next course of action. The LoNT also states rules on the severity of adjectives that are allowed, which must match the length or success factor of the mechanics involved.

All nouns have two descriptors: adjectives (attributes that describe what they are) and adverbs (that describe how the noun can use verbs or how adjectives can be applied). Characters have a third descriptor: verbs, which are the skills. For instance, an elf might have "Agile" as an adjective and a "Low-Light" as an adverb on their (perception) verb, describing special ways they can use that verb - in low-light conditions in this case. Adverbs essentially add to the definition of the verb, which will dictate exact uses of the verb under normal conditions.

Obviously, that stuff is pretty similar to Technoir. Although, I would venture to say that it is the underlying element to just about any other game, Technoir just brings it to the fore instead of covering it up with confusing mechanics. I suspect quite a bit of the above will change with playtesting, but that's where I'm at with it.

Where I'm stuck is with the dice mechanics. I have some really good ideas for using a similar dice mechanic to MHR - that Effect Die has so many possibilities - and since I want to plug-and-play this system with many different settings, it could work. But Cortex+ has a pretty distinct rolling mechanic, and I don't know if I want to go so far as to take it wholesale. As an author, I'm constantly trying to make things unique in my fiction, so taking inspiration from other sources already has me feeling a bit guilty - outright borrowing of a mechanic like that just feels...wrong.

Another option I've been considering is a success mechanic, similar to Lady Blackbird, where the dice are rolled and the successes determine how severe the adjective can be, or how well you go about removing it. But this makes it difficult for the plug-and-play capabilities - I don't want massive amounts of dice being rolled in a supers game, for instance.

So, that's where I'm stuck. Any advice on choosing a mechanic would be awesome. Here are the goals for the mechanic in this game...

Simple - since this is targeted as a gateway game for fans of my fiction and settings, it shouldn't be of the complicated sort that hardcore gamers would enjoy.

Varying Degrees of Success - inflicting adjectives on a target can allow for some very devastating results from a single roll. By making the mechanic have degrees of success, I can determine how severe an adjective can be. This seems to conflict with the first goal a bit, which is why I like the MHR mechanic for its built in level of degree.

Evokes Traits - static modifiers to rolls make it easy to look at the numbers and forget where they come from in the narrative. By consulting the traits and determining which dice to roll from them, it evokes the character's attributes and solidifies their presence in the scene. I guess that's a long way of saying "reduce the meta."

Thanks for any help.

    \n You could use the Narrate a Trait to Pick a Die system used in Becoming Heroes. If you can narrate a trait, you add a d6 to your dice pool. Then the GM narrates and adds 1 die, then you narrate and add 1 die, until you run out of traits, roll the pools and compare them. Alternating the narration of traits (rather than declaring them all in one go) generates fiction beautifully and in dire straits it forces the characters to resolve the situation with some unusual traits.

    \n Hey, so lots to say here, but I don't know how to say it coherently.

    Advice on a mechanic
    This depends tremendously on the game you're building and the post you've written makes it sound like you're shooting for a "generic" RPG. There are no generic mechanics. The things you want your core mechanic to have are likewise things I would want for any core mechanic. That is, they don't help select from the vast and beautiful world of possible mechanics. Find the thing you're modeling and create a mechanic for that.

    Advice on terms
    I pretty much never want to inflict an adjective to anyone. Finding the right terms for what you're talking about will go a long way to find the right mechanical representations for those terms. If you have to explain what a term means (and not simply how it works) then it's probably time to find another name.

    I'm kind of terrible at these
    Just because I say these things are true isn't like, some claim to authority on my part. I suck at this. But I've found that getting better at these two points (choosing specific things to model and using the right language to describe those things) has made the stuff I make better. This is all intended in the spirit of "hey, there've been times I've screwed this up and it might be helpful to share that."

    \n I would stat up the environment only if it makes sense to challenge it. A wall to climb could be tall (+1d6) and slippery (+1d6) and treacherously brittle (+1d6).

    The wall could play Tall, then the character would play Mountaineer. Then the wall would play Slippery, and the character would play Careful and Patient to explain how he worked around the slippery parts. Then the wall would play Treacherously Brittle and the character would play Prepared For The Worst to declare he was holding on to a spike. Finally he would play a tie to his old teacher to explain how he was taught to climb. The character now rolls 4d6 against the 3d6 of the wall to see if he can climb it.

    \n After a good 24+ hours of diving into the Wushu-verse (and lots of coffee ), I've decided that it's the mechanic for me. That said, there are a few hold-overs from other systems that I'd like to mix in. For the most part, I'm finding it fairly easy to do so. My biggest problem is in a condition mechanic (the Adjective one from above, although I'm likely going to abandon that whole "Narrative" approach to trait naming).

    I like the Chi mechanic, a lot, but I'd also like to explore potentially using a condition mechanic. It's one of the things I wanted to use from the beginning of my design phase and, if possible, I'd like to stick with it.

    The idea is that achieving a success over an opponent (which would normally remove 1 or more Chi) the target gets a complication. Complications are rated 1-5, depending on how many successes were achieved - future rolls against the target can increase the Complication ranking or add another one. Complications do affect play, they aren't just narrative.

    That would do well in a one-on-one fight, but when you have a nemesis against two or more PCs, you run into the problem of which Complication to apply - and it can get real messy with a whole party against a Huge Bad (X-Men against Magneto, for instance). At the end of the round, when all the players's Yang successes are added up, which Complication gets added (there could be more than one, depending on the descriptors of the actions).

    I've already thought of splitting the phases into traditional rounds (one player acts, then turns over initiative to someone else), as that would make it easier to determine which Complication is applying at that moment. I don't like that for a number of reasons, the two most important being that reaction rolls will drastically increase the time we spend in the meta game, and the action is just so cool when done simultaneously. I only ever did it once before, with Marvel's old MURPG (the one with the stones). I loved it, but the mechanic sucked.

    I know there is a thing for setbacks in Wushu, but it's not quite what I'm looking for, although it's close. I'd like to remove Chi (or any other meta pacing mechanic) and replace it with a descriptor (or several) that evokes the actual injuries and conditions that are affecting the characters.

    Thanks guys. That suggestion alone (Wushu as a system) has already been a huge help.
