So, over in What's your preference?, Robert Bohl mentioned "history panic". This is an issue of particular interest to me, as I'm currently working on a Regency-era game. I'm a huge fan of Jason Morningstar's inclination to historical games, and pure historical fiction is always a goal of mine.

\n

All of which is to say: I'm not the person feeling "history panic". So can those who have a better grasp of it explain it to me? How does it manifest, how do you think it could be abated? I read Regency etiquette guides for fun (and aloud, much to the annoyance of my housemates), so I would be happy with a huge block of text explaining various important points, but I suspect that's not actually the best course of action.

    \n Various issues here. Broadly:

    First, is fidelity important to that group? Some games assume high or low fidelity, but any game can be played with varying degrees of truthiness.
    Second, the player - does he know enough about the setting to fake it convincingly?

    Low fidelity + high knowledge = irritating player (I hate this guy. He is often me)
    Low fidelity + low knowledge = fun for everyone (Who cares? Go for it!)
    High fidelity + low knowledge = uncomfortable player (History panic)
    High fidelity + high knowledge = fun for everyone (Wait, *you* set up Bellingham to shoot Spencer Perceval?")

    The only situation you need to worry about are games that, by choice or design, are going to be high fidelity. Those games(or social groups) need to have some way to support and encourage low knowledge players, who will be almost everybody. If games (or social groups) also help high-knowledge jack-holes loosen up and accept ahistorical but ultimately unimportant input, so much the better.

    \n Personally, I think there are a few things going on here.

    1. History feels like schoolwork. (I think this is one reason people avoid Steal Away Jordan. They like the idea, but it sounds like work.)

    2. People are interested in tiny little niches of history. For example, I like Regency, but I find medieval history rather dull. Your niche may not be my niche.

    3. History is culture-specific. When I played How We Came To Live Here, I found it difficult, because I know nothing about Native American history. I just had no reference point. Similarly, I think, I'm much more interested in 1930s England than many Americans.

    4. There's often an imperative to get history right. When we play Apocalypse World, we can invent anything, but when we play Grey Ranks, we want to know what Poland was actually like.

    5. History is often seen through modern, rational eyes. People in Regency England thought ghosts, demons and boggarts existed. But, because we see history through rational eyes, we eliminate all those things.

    \n On a visceral level, my history panic is twofold. It feels like you're about to have a History exam that you haven't studied for at all, and now my coolness in the group cannot be measured by my creativity and ability to riff on other players' ideas but also on details and unspoken contextual information awareness.

    I can play an orc because I can make up with orcs are like during play.*
    I can't play a coal miner in the 1920s because I feel bound to "get it right", often only on a personal level. Speech, mannerisms, education seem to become more important than the normal human issues that most everyone can roleplay like being a drunk or weeping over a dead wife. A "you ought to know this" feeling.

    It's mostly (edit: completely) irrational. Also, the more specific the historical setting, the worse it can be. A game set in a decade (say, the 50s) would make me feel less panicky than set in the summer of 1954. The 50s I can make into a stereotype or draw on tropes, but now in the summer of '54 I might want to know certain things.

    * - In games where there are racial strictures and "no you wouldn't do that" scenarios I get frustrated, too.

    \n I love history. Lots. One of the things that loving history has taught me is that there is always all kinds of shit I have no clue about, even in periods I'm decently conversational in.

    I think historical games generally might well benefit from being as super-specific as possible and also assume that the participants know almost nothing about the period in question, save maybe the broadest strokes.

    I also tend to think it's the kind of thing where building a single scenario with some well-built representative characters already interlinked and ready to go is the best approach.

    What players need to know about the history and how it ties into the situation and people at hand should be explainable in a single page of text.

    Other stff that gives texture and color to the period the thing takes place in? Make up some wall posters with words images and tidbits in a really chaotic, collage style and stick it to the walls around the gaming area and be done with it.

    And keep the mechanics simple or non-existent.

    \n Here's a post I made in an earlier discussion:

    There really is no difference between not knowing much about the Forgotten Realms and not knowing much about Poland in 1796.

    Why did I pick Poland in 1796? Because that's my next game project - a Vampire: the Requiem game set in Poland in 1796. That's right folks, put on your bike helmets to prevent brain damage, JDC's going to talk about his Vampire game!

    But first I'm going to tell you about a D&D game.

    It was set in the Scarred Lands, a really kickass setting concocted by White Wolf. I don't want to get too far on a tangent but the reason I like the Scarred Lands is that it really really needs heroes. It's not like a lot of dark fantasy games where things are just dismal, in the Scarred Lands things are dismal but if there was just a ragtag gang of hot-cha adventurers in implausible armor who could come in with quips and derring-do, things might just start looking up.

    Anyway, the game was set in a city that at the time had not been detailed by White Wolf. (Eventually it would be but I liked mine better and nobody else cared at that point.) I went into considerable detail in detailing the coinage, calendar, daily life, etc. of this fantasy city and gave the packet to the players.

    Then I got really annoyed because all of the questions I was getting were answered in the packet and gps were still just "gold pieces", not the actual name of the coin. It was, in my view, killing the atmosphere I was trying to create. I was having these NPCs say all sorts of awesome stuff and the players might as well have been playing Greyhawk or some other D&D setting. I got pretty pissed off, felt like my contribution was wasted, etc. That is the feeling the setting buff gets when people blunder over something they tried to establish, whether it is a fictional setting or a nonfictional one.

    Afterwards I realized this was all happening because I was a fucking moron.

    I had just given these packets to the players without explaining my expectations or goals. What, were they just supposed to read my mind and know what I wanted? I put my attacks of opportunity FAQ in the same packet, for god's sake. I didn't expect their characters to say "hurrr, I really stabbed that guy with an attack of opportunity".

    Fast forward to today.

    After getting psyched about Vampire: the Requiem, I do what I do with all games - ruin it by doing something with it the authors wouldn't ever give a shit about and would think I was stupid or maybe brilliant for doing. In this case, I wanted to run a historical game, but in a setting that most American games more or less shit on and ignore: Eastern Europe (hats off to e23 for their GURPS WWII supplements for Poland and Romania, though...). So I took Napoleonic Poland (just after the last partition) and I wrote up a one-page precis on life in Poland in that time.

    When I presented the page to the players I said, "I expect you to know everything on this page, and to use the terms defined on this page in conversation, and when I refer to something on this page I expect you'll be able to have your character converse intelligently about it."

    Then I said, "If there is some historical question that is not related to something on this page, I don't care if you know it or not, I will be happy to answer it if I can, and if you forget the answer, that's okay, I'll answer it again. If I don't know the answer, I'll establish an answer as true for our game - after all, we're already positting that vampires exist, why not a little historical wiggle room?"

    And: "If you surmise something that historically turns out not to be true or there is some historical reason that something you're working on wouldn't work, I promise to intervene and explain things. You promise not to get upset at this. This arrangement of page and JDC knowledge exists in lieu of you knowing anything about Poland in 1796."

    All three of those things work in concert. The page satisfies me that there is something about the setting that they'll know and they won't stumble over a prop while they're popping about the stage. The use of me as historical fact repository/decisionmaker eliminates endless attempts to remember something they saw on the Discovery Channel. And the intervention guarantees that the players won't concoct a plan involving something that won't be invented/developed/widespread for fifty years... ("We'll catch him, we'll take the train!" "Um...no." ) ...while at the same time cautioning them not to leap right to the edge of the envelope of the setting and invest in a plan that might not be feasible in the setting, hopefully dampening disappointments.

    Posted By: kobutsuSo perhaps that's worth exploring\u2014how to break fidelity in well-understood ways.
    I think that this is the most interesting element of Bloody Forks of the Ohio - I mean, other than the fact that it's one of the few games where Pittsburgh matters. There are a few characters who have sorcery-related tags and secrets, which you can make as literal as you like. To me, that's permission to ignore the historical facts where they're not dramatic and tell my own story, inspired by the bits that were good enough to remember. It carves out a space where History Panic Guy or Often Jason needs to back off and let me do my thing.

    Among my history-inspired games have been a couple of sessions of "Puppetland" drawn from events in recent Middle Eastern history, and a short campaign of Renaissance-era Vampire. Both games immediately give us that safe space between the facts as they were and our story, and both were very, very good. I'd have written "enjoyable" but actually the Puppetland games were terribly exhausting, even a little angry, because of the high level of engagement. Immensely fulfilling, though, and the only time I've felt like I was playing in (what I'd now call) "I Will Not Abandon You" mode. There, the historical inspiration worked for the groups to give us fluency and get us all into a richly-established fiction, if that's the right word, before the game began.

    On the other hand, my only attempt at 1920s-era Cthulhu ended in a kind of disaster because of an Often Jason moment. One of the players wanted to know if there were paper towels in the 1920s, and I didn't know. It threw me off for the rest of the session and I vowed never to return. The whole part about magically murdering people by speaking the name of a fictional god didn't bother me, but the paper towels did. Interestingly, I later noticed in the Keeper's Compendium that that VERY FACT was given as an example of the kind of detail you shouldn't get hung up over, but hung up is exactly what I got.

    Posted By: kobutsuHowever, what really strikes me as troublesome is trying to do that as a game-designer, not a game-runner. That is, how can you-as-the-designer give enough info that people who have only an interest in and not a knowledge of the period of your game will feel it's a useful resource to them? Er, awkward, but I hope clear enough. Particularly, I mean a useful resource for getting over history panic, not for teaching them history.
    \nWell, don't close all the doors, one way to get over history panic is to teach them history. I think some people are open to learning real stuff as a part of learning to play a game. That's one reason the "are there paper towels in 1920?" is an important question to some groups. They want to know!

    Now, there should be some procedure for what to do in the moment when questions like that come up and nobody knows the answer. For example, in my Poland game, there was a clear answer: Jason will give you the answer and it will be true for the game until we decide otherwise. So for example, if someone said "Hey, is there corn raised in Poland in 1796?", which is an interesting question because it was introduced to Europe by American explorers a hundred years previous, and it quickly became quite popular, but how quickly and how popular and where, I don't know off the top of my head. The wrong thing to do is think that the answer doesn't matter. The right thing to do is to say, "Well, let's say there is, and when we come back next time, if I don't have a definitive answer, we'll just go with that." If it turns out I'm wrong, then we can either replace corn with some other cereal crop or just go with the alternate history-ness of it all.

    I don't have any game design insights, and if I do ever have one, just let me know and I'll go somewhere quiet and shoot myself, but as far as a game runner goes, the main thing is to identify your goals and the goals of the group, and tailor the history (as you would with any other thing in the game) to those goals.\n

    \n It really depends on how you design the game too, right?

    \n I may be completely off base here\u2026

    For RPGs to work\u2026 they need to be a safe space! And to be a safe space you need to lower expectations.

    Like a brainstorm session, you don't tear down people's ideas, instead you encourage people to dig deep and share without hesitation for brainstorming to work. Otherwise people will pull back and refuse to participate.

    I'm not saying "anything is allowed" but if you expect everything in an RPG to be "correct", you're going to have a lot of problems. And it isn't just limited to historical accuracy. It applies to anything that may ruin your suspension of disbelief.

    For example, I play with a lot of actors. In RPGs, many of us "act". But our acting sucks. And that's ok, it's part of the fun. We don't require acting training to participate in RPGs. Yet I know a lot of non-gamers who are afraid to participate because they think they have to be good actors to roleplay.

    Now if every time our acting wasn't awesome my acting friends threw up their arms and declared "you're breaking my suspension of disbelief" then we couldn't play together. But they know better. Why would you expect someone else to match your level of skill without your talent or years of training? You wouldn't. You take pride in what you can do because you earned it. And expecting the same skill from a novice makes no sense. And seeing that an RPG isn't "acting class", you let it go.

    Have you noticed how a crappy movie can be unwatchable but fun to play as an RPG? RPGs lower the bar and let people who normally have no business being actors, writers, or tacticians have fun with it without expectations of being professional. And as long as people are patient and suspend their disbelief it can be tremendous fun.

    And here is the thing. Most of us are constantly suspending our disbelief. We're ignorant of 90% of the world because there is just too much to know and little time to experience it all. So when you do most things in an RPG, there is probably someone else at the table who knows much more than you do about whatever you are describing in the fiction. And if every time someone who knows more than you stops the game to educate you\u2026 we would spend more time in the "class room" than in having fun playing. This doesn't just pertain to history but for reasons I don't understand, a lot of history buffs have a problem letting go.

    \n A friend and I wrote a game (a teaching game for junior entrepreneurs) based on the career of Christopher Columbus. We're both educated historians, but we knew that the great majority of players wouldn't be.
    \n
    \nSo we took great care to tell the players that for the purposes of this game, history was something to be mined for cool stuff, not to be held accountable for. To get this across to the players, we included a couple of warm-up exercises that train the players to improvise stuff on the basis of bits and pieces of historical materials. Oh, and during the game, the players have a list of bits and pieces of historical stuff in front of them that they can plunder, in addition to whatever material they get from scene cards. The major point of the warm-up exercises as regards history panic is to establish as social contract between the players that it's really OK to go with it. So pockets were not yet invented in 1600? If you need your character to pull stuff out of a pocket, go ahead. So the players are negotiationg over ten million maravedis to outfit the expedition? In reality the relevant amount would have been 100.000, but is that important for the game? The important bit is that they're wrangling over money.
    \n
    \nI find that as an actual historian, I'm a lot less prudish about the details of these things than many laypeople. As long as people do their best to take the spirit of it and try to do something cool with it, I'm cool.
