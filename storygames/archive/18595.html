\n I'm super excited that so many foreign RPGs I've only heard scant, tantalizing details about are finally getting translated. I've really liked everything I've gotten my hands on so far, it's really interesting to see the design scene over there producing so many designs with SG-friendly elements.

Maid

I haven't really put this one through its paces, honestly; I played it once and it was a decently fun outing, but I don't do a lot of slapstick gaming. It was an interesting read though, and if nothing else I got my money's worth when I used the random events table to plot out a couple sessions of Nobilis.

Tenra Bansho Zero

Now this one, this is everything I could have hoped for, really. I've run Tenra a few times now, mostly one-shots, but the last couple weeks have been an ongoing game where the players ended up running a salvage town hauling technomagic junk out of the Soratsuzumi Sea. Last session had a completely awesome moment from a new player's Bright Lotus monk stoically enduring police brutality for the sake of the peasant workers and oppressed oni, and eventually being granted formal supervision over that ward of the city.

Running it as an ongoing game has been interesting. For one thing, I haven't seen that much Kiai spending yet. We are kinda new to the game (only about half of us have even read the book), and the somewhat slice-of-lifey nature of the campaign (see below) means there hasn't been a whole lot of die-rolling per scene, but I do wonder if they're stocking it up for XP purposes. I think the one-shotty nature of the default game might loosen people up from undue attachment to their characters and deferring "awesome now" for "awesome later".

I also have been a little loose about the recommended GM structure. The group has been doing a lot of one-shots and was hankering for an ongoing setting they could really invest in, which is a little in conflict with Tenra's default inclinations to watch everything burn in the fires of cathartic drama. So instead of putting together an NPC-centric outline and seeing how the players mess with it, I've basically spent the last few sessions just introducing significant supporting/opposing cast and seeing where things go. Even without much pre-planning, the Emotion Matrix usually provides an immediate exciting context and things go off like a bang (and gave me context for assigning compelling Destinies); the ex-shinobi chief of police took an immediate dislike to the head of a merchant family and made tossing him in jail his number one priority, and the martial-artist assistant governor's meeting with the city's resident Northern Shinto priest basically immediately turned into the Alita/Nova karmic frenemies thing from Battle Angel, which was pretty much the best possible scenario since Tenra got me rereading it anyway... and last session she ended up burning down the entire shrine with him in it, so I guess we can't escape our drama karma for long.

Anyway, we're putting it on hiatus for a bit to mess around with Fate Core, so I'm running one more session that will be run in a more properly Tenra fashion.

Golden Sky Stories

Just finished reading the Kickstarter pre-release. This will be challenging to run, I'd rather play first, but I'm likely to be the only one who's read it in my group for the foreseeable future. I did run the Free RPG Day quickstart, and it seemed to go well, but I still didn't feel like I had an adequate grasp of the intended mood. Perhaps in future I will judo my cynicism by inserting things that bug me RL into the setting and seeing what soothing tools of gentleness the players bring to bear.

Double Cross

I've just about finished reading this one. It's pretty cool! I've been interested in DX for a couple years, and the stealth release on Amazon was a fun surprise, though maybe if the translation project had been less closed the book would have been better copy edited. As is, it is absolutely riddled with typos, all through the prose and in the power mechanics, which is especially bad because the game very much encourages system mastery and synergistic power usage a la Exalted or D&D4 (and since there's no PDF version, I am lacking an easy way to print out and transcribe the power descriptions for easy reference).

All that said, there are a lot of cool and smart things in this book. I dig the seemingly Tenra-inspired fast-play design a lot; viral Encroachment feels like a sideways take on the Karma-Asura cycle, with Meikyo bonus built-in for everyone. The powersets are all cool (and I love that the default powers for every PC include "resurrect from death" and "clear out the non-powered bystanders"), and I especially love the game's cultural omnivorousness.

It's big beautiful mind-laser-shooting pulpiness the way I like it. I'm probably going to run it soon, after the errata settles down.

Ryuutama

Ran this a couple years back, super excited for the finished version and wide release! I really like the way it leverages classes and levels to make character creation super quick without locking character advancement into confining pre-defined niches or requiring system mastery to get the most out of it, and I dig the "dragon as anthropomorphic social contract and campaign diary" thing too.

Shinobigami

Confirmed for English release, but still way out on the horizon as far as I know. Looking forward to it though!

On the one hand so far I know of exactly ONE GM-less RPG from Japan ("Giant Allege," where you play lawyers who duke it out in giant robots to determine who's innocent), but OTOH stuff like scene framing and relationship rules are downright commonplace. There's a certain elegant simplicity to a lot of Japanese TRPGs (especially anything by Kawashima, the main guy behind Adventure Planning Service and games like Shinobigami) that I'm kinda hoping will start to influence Western RPGs. I know it's had a huge influence on me.\n

    I've scheduled GSS for this coming Sunday because after re-reading Maid, I realized I wasn't prepared to have my first GMing attempt be for something that can so easily get out of hand. (And deciding what information to prepare for the players was a bit overwhelming.)

    Prepping for GSS is almost soothing, really. I mean, of course I am agonizing over the layout of the group character sheet document and how to handle Dreams (settled on another Drawing document so players can move tokens from the middle and onto the character's name) - but coming up with a scenario was easy and thinking about how the different character types might solve the problem makes me smile. It also makes me laugh a little to imagine how some of the players might take to the game. I'm not sure how many of them have played anything like it - the ones who've signed up so far are mainly players/GMs for darker/more twisted games like Monsterhearts, Dramasystem, Apocalypse World, Fiasco, Don't Rest Your Head; etc.

    But reading over Maid again also makes me want to see where some of these same players take *that*. Especially if we go full Random and end up with these insane characters played by players who aren't afraid to fully invest in the spirit of a game and aren't likely to spend two and a half hours dusting lamps for fear of setting off a Stress Explosion.

    On the TBZ front, I've sort of started to brainstorm a scenario, but I feel like I need more intimate familiarity with the game before seriously working on it. I think of it as one of those huge novelty desserts with all sorts of things in it like brownies and nuts and a pint of ice cream and half a jar of cherries and probably two cans of whipped cream and a handful of pretty waffers. Boy is it exciting. Boy is it pretty. But good lawd, there's a lot of it and where to start when you don't have a group to help you...!?

    I'll try to report back later once I've done more than start prepping for these games. It's really wonderful to me to have an insight to how other people around the world game. Gamers are gamers everywhere, of course, but reading some of these books makes me think of how it's approached differently. Every once in awhile, I trip a bit over things that seem to clue into what might be common gaming problems in Japan that a Western (or at least American) gamer wouldn't think twice about. So I'd love to see games from other countries make it to English too.

    \n I want to run a game of Double Cross soonish, and thinking out loud about the book is the best way to me to internalize it enough to do that and make a cheat sheet for my players, so I figure I'll just do it in public and maybe someone else will find it interesting?

    For those who don't know (which is probably a lot of people; this came out very under the radar), Double Cross is a tabletop RPG from Japan that's been out for a few years and is supposed to be decently popular, a creation of FEAR, one of the big RPG publishers who also created the also-recently-translated Tenra Bansho Zero. TBZ and DX don't share any staff, but the former seems pretty influential on the latter.

    I first heard of Double Cross a few years back from some discussion on the always interesting but low-traffic J-RPG forums. The English release is only a few weeks old, and is actually of the third edition of the game. In fact, it's actually the deluxe version; the Japanese release is split up into essentially a basic book and an advanced guide, but ours includes both. Which does mean that it's kind of a lot to familiarize yourself with, which is why I'm doing this.

    The premise is basically dark superheroes, in that particularly Japanese way where they don't have secret identities per se, but kind of wage a shadow war that goes unnoticed by the normal world, and they usually share their powers with or were even given them by the forces they're fighting, which is often a keen point of angst. Kamen Rider, X/1999, Project ARMS, that sort of thing. An archeological dig 20 years ago unleashed "the Renegade Virus" into the world (and it is very much a comic-book "virus", in that it is semi-intelligent, can infect machines, and even create vampires from thin air, but we'll get to that later), and now an estimated 80% of the world's population are carriers, so basically anyone under traumatic stress can potentially have an origin and become an Overed. The UGN (Universal Guardian Network) are the setting's SHIELD/X-COM quasi-governmental secret taskforce to protect the world with virus-wielding agents (and a fairly easy hook to connect the PCs to one another), and the False Hearts are the Brotherhood of Evil Mutants who want to awaken the virus in everyone and have a big transhuman post-morality party. Oh, and both organizations may have been founded by the same guy, one Professor Caudwell, who was at one point presumed dead but then resurfaced working with FH. Oh, the melodrama!

    The book is not actually laid out this way, but I think it's a good idea to start with the basic rules engine.

    There are four stats, [Body] [Mind] [Sense] and [Social] (to use the book's notation), and then each has several skills that fall under it, like Melee and Dodge are governed by [Body], Ranged (weapons) under [Senses], and so on. Hit points are derived from your [Body] and [Mind], and initiative (a static value, no initiative rolls in this system) from your [Sense] and [Mind].

    To do something, you roll [stat]D10, choose the single highest die, then add skill to the value. However, dice explode, so rolling a 10 means you keep the 10, then reroll and add the new result as well. Dice can keep exploding, but multiple dice don't stack; if you roll more than one 10, you get to reroll all of them, but only count one of those 10s to your final result.

    Another detail is that "10" is just the default Critical Value, and there are ways to lower it, so if you make a roll with CV8, that means rolls of 8, 9, and 10 all explode (and still count as "10" in terms of adding up the numerical value).

    Also, if all dice come up 1, that's an automatic failure.

    This is a game about fighting and melodrama, so the rules mainly focus on whupping ass and keeping your mental composure and sort of leave everything else to roleplaying; like, there's no real encumbrance, and keeping track of money is basically governed by making [Social] Procure rolls to buy things off the equipment list, the Ranged skill includes all weapons that strike at a distance and you don't bother keeping track of ammo. There are an absolute smorgasbord of powers, though, most of which are special cases (though there are some recurring patterns, like powers that let you substitute one skill for another, such as using Perception in place of Dodge).

    Oh, why is it called Double Cross? It kind of makes sense in a poetic, Engrishy way. As the short comic at the book's start says, "There are people who struggle between human and superbeing. There are Double-Crossers-- Traitors to both sides of the world."

    Next time... man, there's a lot to get to. I guess I'll go over character creation and maybe get into the powers a bit.

    Let's see... if I want to talk about character creation, I have to address the powers at least briefly first, since they define so much about your character.

    So the viral powers are divided up more or less by theme between twelve Syndromes (as well as a smaller pool of generic powers anyone can buy, some of which are mandatory buys for all characters... including the sample characters, despite them not being printed on their character sheets), which have some pretty interesting names. I'll go more in depth on them later, but let's at least make their acquaintance:

    Angel Halo, "Manipulation of Light. Capable of laser attacks and illusions."
    Balor, "Controls gravity through the use of an 'Evil Eye'. Creates gravity wells to manipulate time and space."
    Black Dog, "Controls internally-produced electricity. On top of lightning attacks, cybernetic implants are also possible."
    Bram Stoker, "Uses one's blood as a weapon. Accelerated healing is also possible through manipulation of blood flow."
    Chimera, "Acquire the body of a beast. Gain superhuman strength and speed by restructuring the body."
    Exile, "Controls the elasticity of the body. Use nails as sharp weapons or relocate vital organs."
    Hanuman, "Enhances the nervous system and increases speed. Capable of generating sound waves."
    Morpheus, "Creates and mutates items. Can manipulate the residual dust from creation."
    Neumann, "Enhances mental capabilities. Faster thinking and better memory are this Syndrome's weapons."
    Orcus, "Creates a special 'domain' where the user can manipulate any event that happens in the created space,"
    Salamandra, "Allows one to manipulate heat. Fire or extreme cold can be used for attacks or enhancing physical capabilities."
    Solaris, "Grants the ability to create chemicals. Can control people through poison and drugs."

    OK, so the first step is narrowing down your power pool. The default character is assumed to be a Crossbreed, someone with access to two Syndromes. You can also choose to be a Tri-Breed, with access to three (at the cost of having a lower rating on all those powers compared to a Crossbreed, and without access to the top-tier powers of any of their Syndromes), or a Pure-Breed, with just one Syndrome, but a higher rating cap on all its powers, and access to the two special Pure-Breed-only powers each Syndrome has. Without having made a character or played yet, Tri- and Pure-Breeds definitely seem like advanced options for players who already have some familiarity with the system. And frankly, most of the Pure-Breed powers don't seem cool enough to justify losing an entire additional powerset, though one or two are pretty nifty.

    Your choice of Syndromes determine your starting stats; Chimera prioritizes [Body], Neumann [Mind], etc, but you also get a few discretionary points to throw around.

    Next you choose your Work; your day job from before you became one of the X-Men. There are some UGN occupations in here (including "raised by them away from human society" and "branch chief"), and a bunch of mundane ones, like Grade School Student, Doctor, Teacher, Hacker, Assassin (OK, maybe that last is a little exotic) and so on. That gives you another little [stat] bump and a handful of skills, but you again get some extra skill points to spread around. You also pick another Work to serve as your "Cover", which doesn't have any mechanical impact but adds some more color to your character, if you want it. "High School Student" twice is just some random kid who started shooting lightning out of his eyes on day, but "UGN Child" Work with "High School Student" as Cover is, like, if one of those kids from Akira went undercover 21 Jump Street style.

    This is also a good place to note that there are basically three main fighting styles; armed/unarmed via [Body] Melee, ranged via [Senses] Ranged, and "supernatural" via [Mind] Renegade Control. In general, powers that do things like generate bolts of lightning or attack the mind directly use RC instead of Ranged, but there is some overlap. It's worth keeping in mind as you build your character, at least.

    Then you get four powers from your Syndrome(s) and the generic pool, a couple more points to raise their levels (some powers cap out at level 1, though), and three powers every character starts with. Those freebies are worth going into, because two of them say a lot about the intended feel and playstyle of the game, in ways I think are smart and very cool.

    The first one is Resurrect. Yes, every single player character does not fear death; when you drop to 0 HP, you can immediately get back up, or wait until the end of the scene instead if you want to take the hint and stay down. There are some limits on it (you don't heal back to full, it raises your Encroachment Rate, and you can't use it when your Encroachment is too high... we will get to Encroachment later, but for now think of it more or less like sanity points or Tenra Bansho Zero's Karma), but I really like the thematic statement that characters are too cool and resilient to be taken down so easily, and the mechanical one that you don't need to worry about prioritizing defense from the get-go just to avoid getting one-shotted by accident. Which is extra good because it looks like characters can sling around some serious damage when they start stacking their powers together. Oh, and it's not the only "come back from 0 HP" power in the game, either, there are a few ways to get extra lives.

    The second gimme is Warding. I'll quote this one in full, especially since we should probably have a look at the game's power format for later.

    Warding
    Max Level: 1 Timing: Auto
    Skill: - Difficulty: Auto
    Target: Scene Range: View
    Encroach: 0 Restrict: -

    "This Power can be used at any time. All non-Overed characters in this Scene become Extras. All Overeds that are in the Scene will automatically sense that this power was used. The effects of this Power continue throughout the Scene."

    So basically, at any time, anyone with superpowers can flip the "it's GO TIME" switch and turn off all the bystanders (Extras basically have no game mechanics; they're not considered to have any HP, all actions against them succeed automatically). You know how the Power Rangers always seem to end up fighting in a deserted city or vacant stone quarry, or the magic zones people throw down in X? It's like that. It doesn't cost anything to use, it alerts everyone who's in the know, and it preserves the Masquerade (other parts of the book suggest non-Overeds pass out or flee when they get hit with this; it depends on your character's flavor, I guess). It's very meta, but it's again very useful for communicating the intended play style, and it even exists in-character so your character can worry about not letting Aunt May know your horrible secret without having to go through all kinds of contortions to actually prevent it. I like it.

    The last free power is interesting mechanically but not thematically; everyone gets Concentrate:(Syndrome), which lowers the Critical Value on all rolls involving the syndrome they picked when they bought the power. So Concentrate: Black Dog (level 2) means any rolls to use a Black Dog power have their dice explode on 8, 9, or 10. Or to be more precise, any Black Dog power rolls that also have Concentrate: Black Dog comboed in; the power/combat rules for Double Cross assume a fair bit of system mastery and willingness to build kooky combos that will seem very familiar to veterans of Exalted, though DX's combo rules are more forgiving by far. I think.

    Due to the tyranny of character limit, I couldn't post about the other character option, being a Renegade Being, which means "you are a sentient, nonhuman being created or uplifted by the Renegade virus that can pass for normal". This is expressed by picking Renegade Being as your Work, which also gets you another two powers, Humanity's Neighbor (which lets you fake being human) and an Origin (which you use when you want to take the mask off). Origin powers include things like Animal, Colony (like, you used to be a coral reef or a bunch of mold), Cyber (IT CAME FROM THE INTERNET), and even Legend (like, you are a unicorn or something the virus created from the memories of humanity's collective unconscious. One of the pregenerated characters is a Renegade Being who appears to be a vampire). There are a few other Renegade Being-only powers too, which is one of the things the "Restrict" entry in Warding's statblock referred to (or rather, didn't).

    All we have left for character creation is Personal Data, which is basically your character's backstory and some personality stuff. Everything in this section is "Roll or Choice", either roll percentile dice on a table, or just choose the one you like. It's worth noting that a few tables have options that can't be gotten randomly.

    The first three tables are for Origin, Experience, and Encounter, and each of them fills in a little of your backstory and establishes a supporting NPC for your character, referred to as a Lois (yes, like Lois Lane, though the book doesn't actually spell this out, unlike the Syndrome references). The Origin fleshes out your character's family (so Loises might be a parent or sibling), and Experience has a few tables dependent on your Work, with pretty variable results depending on if you pick Student, UGN, Adult, or Criminal (you might end up with a spouse or a monster hunting you). Origin and Experience just have suggestions for a Lois that you can flesh out, but the Encounter is specifically with one of the two dozen or so signature NPCs, to tie your character more directly into the setting.

    Once you have your three starting Loises, you go to the Emotion tables to add some context to the relationship (very Tenra Bansho). Every Lois has a positive and negative emotion associated with it, and you should decide which one is overt and which is covert. So, say, you might have a co-worker you outwardly respect but secretly or subconsciously despise-- or vice versa. It's worth noting that you don't have to have a positive relationship with your Lois-- your character just has to feel strongly enough about them to be invested in the relationship, to provide a tie to society.

    This ties into the last two Personal Data tables, which represent the ways in which your character is outside society and humanity, their Awakening (the circumstances that led to their viral powers erupting) and Impulse (the negative emotions or urges they have to keep in check or risk losing their humanity). Those two results determine your character's base Encroachment Rate.

    After that, pick up some equipment if you like, and that's the end of character creation! So let's go back and actually explain some of the things we had to gloss over.

    Lois, Titus, and Encroachment are all pretty closely tied together... Let's start with Encroachment.

    Your Encroachment Rate measures how strong the Renegade virus concentration is in your system. You start every session with Encroachment set to your Base rate (which will usually be in the 30s), and it can go as high as 300%. (MAX SYNCHRO, EGO BARRIER DISSOLUTION, etc)

    Encroachment rises when you use most powers (though permanent ones tend to just raise your Base Encroachment instead), by 1d10 whenever you enter a scene, and whenever the GM decides you should make an Impulse Check. When should the GM call for one? That's left pretty vague; any time a character receives "an intense mental shock" or "any time [the GM] feels it is appropriate," and the sample adventures in the back suggest the PCs should make one right before the climactic fight of the session. Impulse Checks are pretty harsh; succeed or fail, your Encroachment rises 2d10 points, and if you fail your character goes Berserk (which is less extreme than it sounds, but still not great). You can also just choose to raise it by any number of d10 at any time.

    Why do that? Well, having a high viral concentration means your super-powers are going into overdrive; you get bonus dice on every action based on how high your Encroachment is, and past a certain threshold the level of your powers increases too. You even get bonus XP at session's end based on how high you managed to jack up your Encroachment Rate.

    Wow! What possible catch could there be to filling every cell of your body with a mind-affecting mutagen? Well, if you end the session with Encroachment over 99%, you lose your character, as their higher brain functions can no longer resist their Impulse and they become... what the original book refers to as a Germ, but the English version re-transliterates as "Gjaum".

    (On the other hand, if you do lose your character, XP is explicitly assigned to players instead of characters, so you can dump it into your next character instead of just starting from scratch)

    So how do you avoid giving into the germ, if just playing the game is enough to raise Encroachment? That's where the Loises come in. Whether your character loves them or hates them, the emotional ties connecting you is an anchor you can focus on to avoid being swept away into viral madness. At session's end, you can Backtrack the virus' influence by rolling 1d10 per Lois, and reduce your Encroachment by the total. You can even make the roll more than once if your Encroachment is really in the red zone, but that penalizes your XP some.

    You always start and end a session with three Loises (after rolling for Backtrack), but during play you can have up to seven. The book suggests that players should form one with the PC next to them at the start of the session, and that the GM should also assign another to everyone at the start of every adventure/session as a plot hook. You can also pick them up during scenes if you take a shine to some NPC and the GM OK's it. The book suggests that more conceptual Loises are fine (like, faith in an organization, or dependence on drugs), but best saved for when you have some experience with the game.

    However, reducing Encroachment isn't the only thing you can do with a Lois. At any time, you can renounce your feelings for them and declare them Titus (as in Titus Andronicus, presumably, but as someone who's only seen the Julie Taymor movie it seems more like the PC is in the Titus role swearing revenge on these people... but anyway), burning your bridges with them and using that catharsis to fuel a short-term burst of strength. I won't list everything they're good for, but two big ones are reviving after losing all HP, and rolling an extra ten dice on anything.

    The GM is also allowed to suggest a Lois change to Titus if the relationship takes a sudden, drastic turn for the worse, like if the Lois dies or betrays the PC.

    I should also mention that Double Cross is fairly light on art. There's a brief intro comic (that's really more of a trailer for the game than an actual sequentially told story), a few full-page pieces to break up sections and introduce new concepts (each Syndrome gets one, as do the adventures), and there are headshots of the signature NPCs, but by and large this book is big ol' blocks of text. If I cared less about the spine of this book, I'd scan in the amazing diagram of where each section of character creation corresponds to on your character's body.

    So, combat isn't that complicated or different than most games you've probably played, though there is a fair amount of game-specific jargon that I'm going to skip over, since it's mainly for power timing and combo purposes (like, a character can be in either a Pre-Action or Post-Action state, or, as we usually know it, "hasn't acted this round" or "has acted this round").

    Combat is broken into rounds of roughly a minute apiece, and rounds are themselves divided into Setup (not much happens here, but it's a step some powers trigger during), Initiative (ditto, but this is also where you can decide to hold your action, but if you do you act after everyone else, no trying to interrupt someone else), the Main Process (where you actually act), and Cleanup (manage any ongoing effects and end the round).

    Characters act in descending order of their Initiative ratings (PCs win ties with NPCs, tied PCs can work it out between themselves), and on their turn can take a Minor action (move around, equip an item, use some powers) and a Major (make an attack, sprint, use most powers). You get one of each, no trading around.

    Combat movement is semi-freeform, and it reminds me a bit of the current edition of Warhammer Fantasy; pretty much every power or weapon has an effective range given in meters, but the important part is whether you're Engaged with someone or not. If you're moving around and bump into an enemy or an obstacle, you stop moving right there and can't break away without spending a Major Action (you don't get back attacked while moving away or anything, but you also can't move to a different Engagement in that same movement). You might need to make a Body roll if you're in tight quarters or actively blocked in, too. There are a number of powers that don't work on someone you're Engaged with, and to flee a fight entirely you need to spend a Major action to dash away in a direction where nothing will Engage you.

    Attacking somebody is a skill test like any other, the stat and skill depending on what you're using. The defender can choose to either make an opposed roll using Body/Dodge to avoid it (hmm, rules contradiction here; the general rules for opposed checks say the reacting character wins ties, but the combat chapter says a defender needs to beat the attacker's roll), or skip the roll and Guard instead, adding their Guard stat to any armor they're wearing to reduce the damage (Guard defaults to 0 without any equipment or powers, though). A successful attack rolls (1/10th the attack result)d10 damage +another 1d10 +any weapon/power bonuses, minus the target's armor. Engagement plays a role here too; someone in the same Engagement who hasn't acted yet can spend their entire turn to Cover the target and take the damage instead, calculating it as if they were Guarding.

    Speaking of damage, an interesting thing to point out here is that there is no first aid/healing kinda skill, and no way to naturally recover hit points. There are medkits you can buy, and a fair few powers let you heal yourself or others (and a few that let you ignore damage or self-resurrect), but it's unusual that all healing must be actively sought out.

    There are some other miscellaneous rules that cover fighting while flying, or from stealth (which are also affected by Engagement), running someone over with a car (pretty relevant given some of the Morpheus powers!) and a bunch of status ailments I won't bother getting into, they mostly disallow some kind of option (can't move, can't defend, etc) and are usually dispelled by using one of your actions to deal with it (either Minor or Major).

    I told you that story so I could tell you this one: on to the powers! They are kind of the meat of the game, since they cover over a hundred pages of the book, and come before they even explain how to make a skill check. I think I brought this up before, but I'll be frank; Double Cross assumes that you'll be willing to devote some serious time to gaining system mastery, since you can invoke theoretically any number of powers at once, as long as they all share the same timing (Setup phase, Initiative phase, Major Action, reacting to someone else's action, etc) and enhance the same thing (melee attack, ranged attack, use the same specific skill, etc). When combining powers, you use the fewest targets, shortest range, and highest difficulty of all the powers involved.

    There are also plenty of powers that specifically exist to boost other powers, like making them affect more targets, cover a wider area, and so on, and several that enhance actions (like, increase Guard or let you Cover at a distance or without using up your turn). However, a power with Target: Self can't be upgraded to affect more targets (but conversely, the user is a valid target for a power with Target: Single). Something that's easy to miss if you're just reading the power descriptions is that all the powers that increase your Guard stat apply if you Cover someone, not just if you're guarding yourself. On the other hand, powers that can't be Dodged or reacted to don't allow you to Guard either.

    Most if not all of the Syndromes have powers that add dice to attack rolls or damage to attacks, so I won't specifically call them out. They all also have a few powers that require Encroachments of 80%, 100% or 120% to use, and almost all Syndromes have a 120% Encroachment power that either reduces damage to 0 or revives you when you run out of HP, so you can really stack the extra lives, which is good because you can also stack damage pretty high, looks like. I won't be able to tell just how rocket-taggy the combat is until I actually run it, but I appreciate that they acknowledge that getting one-shotted is a potential issue.

    Each Syndrome also has some Simple powers, which cover non-combat-relevant but very useful powers which usually can't be used during combat-time... and I need to break up this post.

    \n So after entirely too much time off, I edited my posts here into a cheat sheet for my players and actually ran some Double Cross, specifically the intro adventure.

    First off, and very germane to recent threads, it is very railroady. I would actually call it downright theatrical-- Andy K likes to describe sessions of Tenra Bansho Zero as being like a stage play, but Double Cross' adventures really feel like it, it seems to go back and forth between treating the players as audience and actors. The introductory "scenes" are really more boxed text to provide context for the players, but it also suggests they're meant to be fully played out at the table, despite there being very clear boundaries for PC action (like, the first scene in the game has a set end point regardless of what the PC does, and the second specifies that a specific PC will pass out as soon as he tries to do literally anything).

    The basic adventure skeleton goes Opening Phase (intro scenes), Middle Phase, Climax Phase. It's the middle where the PCs get to do things, but even so, the book lays out a general outline of:\n

    -Player Characters will meet up with the other Player Characters.
    -The known threat will be investigated.
    -Any battles or introductions of new threats will happen.
    \nThe adventures progress via scenes that get "unlocked" when players pass certain information rolls-- like, the PCs discover some sort of information and so the GM runs a "cutscene" showing the villains advancing their agenda or bickering among themselves, or the villains notice the PCs following their trail and send minions to dog them or show up for a skirmish (it's also worth noting there are several NPC-only powers in the book, "Instant Retreat" among them-- but it also specifies that Instant Retreat is only usable once per session, so it's kind of an indication that it's poor form for the bad guy to constantly evade the PCs' attempts to get them). I also like that the game anticipates the "what happens if everyone fails their Gather Info roll?" issue-- the penalty for failing an Info roll is to end the scene, start a new one (with attendant increase in Encroachment) and let them roll again. So it's not an issue of "will the PCs defuse the bomb" but "how much stress will they be under while racing against the clock".

    Another interesting thing is that the GM gets XP they can spend toward their own character; kind of a nice reminder that GMs like being on the other side of the screen too. I guess the very entry-level campaign advice (and inherent one-shottiness) is meant to ease in players who might be a little gun-shy about running the whole shebang.

    Overall, the adventures are very thorough, maybe even disconcertingly so. It seems to assume that people picking up the book will be among the rawest of newbies, and lays out a step-by-step path and checklist for the session (complete with pregenerated characters and lots of handouts for the players). I can actually kind of appreciate this, given that DX is a fairly complex game that rewards long-term investment in system mastery, but it does feel odd to me after spending the last decade plus playing games that are less crunchy and more open to player agency. If nothing else it made for a good demo.

    On the other hand, the pregenerated characters do have some issues; two or three of them are, mechanically, not very interesting at all-- in a game intended to focus on cinematic action, two or three of the pregens are total support-buffers and healbots, and the game system doesn't really allow for them to even just drop the buffs on themselves and then go to town. Along those lines, none of the characters have any of the utility Simple powers, so I gave one to everyone for free just so they wouldn't feel so monomanically kitted out for combat, and giving everyone that leeway to do weird stuff without cracking heads really seemed to give them room to breathe.

    All that said, I'm used to most pregenerated characters being comically underpowered or mis-statted (based on years of playing White Wolf, though I don't recall D&D/Pathfinder being any better), so it was a nice change to only have a few of them being trap options. I also really really liked each character having their "ultimate combo" move pre-statted out so nobody had to count up how many dice they needed in mid-action (and really appreciated the adventure doing the same for the villains)... but on the other, you still need to vet those sheets a little because they don't list the crucial Resurrection power every PC has by default, or all the details of the individual powers (they do at least have page references, but rather than trade two books around between six people I just transcribed everyone's powers before the game), and they don't include the second page of character sheet that tracks important things like Encroachment Rate and Loises.

    Speaking of Loises, everyone ended up with five apiece (three from character creation, one with another PC, and then another one tying them into the adventure), which meant that I was only able to work in maybe only two or three of them. In a long-term game, or one with fewer players, it's probably a good tool for fleshing out the supporting cast, but in a one-shot with five players most of them were just wasted space (much like aspect bloat in some versions of FATE).

    The characters were also just as glass-hammery as I expected; pretty much every hit the PCs took or dished out was either a one-hit kill or close to it, so fights sort of boil down to the number of "extra lives" characters have being their real life bar. I'm not sure how I feel about this yet. Armor also sort of feels like a vestigial rule, since there are plenty of powers that straight-up ignore it, and of the two villains, one of them had no armor at all but bought up about twice as many HP as anyone else, which seemed like the more effective way to go.

    Overall, Double Cross seems to have some divided loyalties. The GM material suggests a light, almost breezy game you can drop in and out of or run with relatively light prep, but it's coupled with a front-loaded, very crunchy and system-mastery-intensive set of combat-centric powers that demands a lot of long-term investment of player attention and XP to get the most out of. I don't think just having a set of pregenerated characters at the front defuses this tension, but on the other hand the game might be borderline unplayable without them; it's been a very long time since I ran a game this crunchy, and even with them I had to do a fairly large amount of player-utility prep, a lot more than I've done in years. I'm also not looking forward to statting up more villains on my own, but to be fair it looks less daunting than doing it in a total points-buy effects-based system like, say, Wild Talents.

    Before running the game, I wasn't sure if I'd recommend it, but after doing it I'd have to give the thumbs-up; it's a little out of my crunch comfort zone (and it desperately, desperately needs better editing), but like Tenra it is an interesting glimpse into a different gaming culture and I like the things it does right enough to want to play more. It's definitely less polished than Tenra, but it won me over anyway. My copy of the Advanced Guide's in the mail!
