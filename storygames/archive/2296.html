[Con Etiquette] Leaving a Table

Blake T. Deakin
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
Inspired by a terrible D&D game I participated in at Dreamation and after having discussed the point with a few people at the 'Con, I was hoping to get people's stories about how they've managed to tastefully leave a table where they weren't having a positive play experience. Let's assume, for the sake of argument, that the game is unsalvageable, that you've either exhausted all potential avenues of making it more enjoyable and otherwise you just know you want to get out of it. I'll further qualify and say that I think I'm only talking about Con games here, for the most part with strangers.

Can this be done?

Is it more important to salvage your limited time at a 'Con or try not to hurt anyone's feelings?

What about disrupt a game for people who might otherwise be enjoying themselves?
  • TonyLB
    January 2007
    By the time I give up a game as unsalvageable, I'm usually having a psychosomatic sense of exhaustion. It draings me, having things suck that much.

    So, generally, I'll say "Y'know what guys, if I were feeling more up to this I would stay, but I'm just really spent. I gotta get some rest, or I forfeit the entire rest of the convention. Sorry, but you'll have to carry on without me."

    It has the benefit of being true (if slanted in what it omits) and I've yet to encounter anyone who is anything other than sympathetic. Then, when they see you bouncing off the walls later, you say "Yeah, I had some snacks and took it easy for a while, and I bounced right back," and they feel all good about having helped you enjoy the con!

    January 2007
    If the game is really that bad, then just stand up, get everyone's attention, and try something like, "Hey, folks, I am going to step out. I can see you guys are having fun but I'm not, so I don't want to drag you down. Later!" If they press for an explanation, I recommend soothing egos and punting a bit. "It's nothing personal. I'd be happy to talk about it in detail after your game is over, but I don't want to sidetrack you all more than I already have. Find me later and we'll chat."

    Do follow up with the GM or players, if they're friends of yours, and explain what happened. Be mature and expect maturity. Remember that not everyone enjoys the same things. Your lousy time is someone else's awesome.

    Cross-posted with Adam above:
    Is this one of those situations where we should find a polite white lie to avoid the truth of the situation?
    Absolutely.
    Why is "I'm not having fun" something that would hurt other people, especially if the other gamers seem to be having fun.
    OK, here's the Japanese response to that answer, as I've seen this kind of thing totally nuke social situations in Japan: Tone it down to get the result in US social communications:

    When you say "I'm not having fun", you've essentially issued an utterance. But not keeping that shit to yourself, you've basically put it on the other people to:
    1) Make it more fun for you, somehow.
    2) Reflect upon why it is not fun for you (ie 'become guilty')

    By keeping it to yourself, or issuing a white lie, you've extricated yourself from the situation without leaving points 1 or 2 in the air.

    In Japanese communications, saying "I'm not having fun" is nothing less than saying "Please make it more fun for me" and/or "You should feel guilty that I am not having fun". Again, Japan =/= American RPG Convention, but I do think the social nuance is still there.

    If you had a shitty time because the GM was a fuckhead, and one or more players were obnoxious boors, and you want to stick it to them, say that you're not having fun. If you want to get out of the situation without them thinking that they might have done something wrong, then say "I'm really tired guys", or "Huh, my phone just vibrated, I got an emergency, I gotta go and I'm really sorry".

    Basically, saying "I'm not having fun" is like serving a ball to start a conversation, then running away before that conversation can conclude. The ONLY good thing that can come from it is:

    (after you leave)
    PLAYER ONE: I dunno what that guy's problem is, I'm having fun.
    PLAYER TWO: Yeah, what an ass.
    "I'm not having fun" isn't at all the same as "what you're doing isn't fun," though I could see how others might construe it as such.
    It's not so much as "What you're doing isn't fun" (though there's a hint of that), again by issuing that utterance you're putting a burden on the listener: A burden to reflect on why it isn't fun for you, and to perhaps do something about it.

    Human beings have invented the white lie for a reason. It is a piece of grand social evolution. Deciding not to make use of it in social situations is like not making use of silverware to eat your food.
    Are the two things too easily confused and thus we need to take special care to avoid suggesting the wrong thing?
    I'm of the opinion that we should always take care to avoid hurting other people's feelings, or dampening their fun. Less suffering = better. But again, if I were in a game with a total dickweed GM or players, and others weren't having fun, I'd sure as shit loudly state that I wasn't having any fun, and perhaps make a group vote to call off the game. But in a situation where I feel that *I'm* the one with a problem ("This game isn't fun for me"), then I'll excuse myself in a way that doesn't put any burden on them. It's my problem, no need to burden them with it.

    -Andy

    I think part of the reason why the "white lie" mechanism works is because most everybody knows it for what it is- a polite exit excuse. They don't necessarily have to believe you, or even think about it any further- they officially don't have to worry about it. Politeness is all about forms, and this one is generally acceptable. Failing to use one of those accepted forms sends a challenge signal- it says that you expect them to respond in some way (to debate you, to fix it, to learn from your action, etc.).

    I think this is complicated further when you're playing a demo of a game with the creator. To stand up and say you're not having any fun is bound to make the creator (and all the players) feel uncomfortable, if not insulted. I've seen this happen at one of Luke's Burning Wheel games (understandably, it was toward the end of a very drawn out Duel of Wits), and it was a chilly moment for everybody.

    Here's the point. If I do this, and make the sort of instant friends that one can make at cons, I enable these players to be able to bow out of my game like this, "You know Mike, this just isn't my thing. Would it be cool if I left to try something else?"

    And then it's not some stranger dissing my game, but somebody I know telling me the honest truth.

    All I'm saying is to be social. It's that lack of socializing, treating RPG play at cons as if it were two chess opponent's meeting over a table, that makes any social interaction difficult at conventions. If you actually try to get to know the people with whom you're playing, spend ten minutes of your time on basic socializing, I garuntee you that your games will have a much higher rate of success.





    That's actually a very good idea, and totally fits in with all that Social Contract babble that most of us take at face value.

    * If you run a game, introduce yourself. Get some brief introductions out there, so everyone knows each other. Maybe do a non-lame icebreaker.
    * As a GM, tell people that if they're not enjoying themselves, that it's totally cool to leave in the middle, for whatever reason. But that you prefer feedback later at the con sometimes if that happens.
    * Then get the game rolling.

    I've had really successful games at pretty much every con I've been to for years now, and when we start I tend to (as GM) start off the adventure with a bang about 10 minutes into the session: The first 10 minutes are usually people finding the table, settling in, making conversation. Making it a little more structured would be a cool thing: 5 minutes for finding the table and chatty-chat, then 5 minutes for introduction, then an explosive game start.

    In any case, maybe doing some people-to-people introductions at the beginning of the game, followed by a quick "lines and veils" of the table itself ("It's cool if you need to break, just let me know. And if this game isn't what you were looking for, it's Totally Fine with me if you leave.") would work wonders. Promote behavior that is open, giving the players an out if they want it.

      First, I am an honesty fetishist, so I feel a powerful desire to be brutally honest where possible. However, I still don't tell people I'm not having fun when I leave. Worse, I usually don't leave. I suffer and try desperately to extract fun from the situation, sometimes to the point where I ruin other people's (bad, wrong) fun.

      To whit, I will merely invoke Hot Sauce and not delve into it except to say: I was a crappy player at this extraordinarily bad game. I mean, I think I did a good job roleplaying, and I think I entertained my fellow players, but if I was being careful and honest I would have recognized this as a railroady, illusionist game and that 5/7ths of the table were excited about it in this regard. Instead of just going along with this or quitting politely (or even rudely), I decided to engage in my-guy-ism and gave up the McGuffin to the opposition once given an in-character reason for it. I will partly defend my choices, because I really did have in-character reasons for what I was doing! But I probably should've been more mature and found a way out of the game.

      I can't stop myself, though, sometimes. I itch and burn to squeeze fun out of the situation, or not cut out before giving them the chance to improve.

      --

      I think that, rather than its being a weird cultural thing not to be honest when people suck, it's the weird geek culture that even makes us consider telling people that they're not entertaining you. I think the normal human way of doing things would be to just white-lie your way out. But the Aspergery, geeky, must-always-be-accurate thing we (I say "we" for a reason, mind) are afflicted with causes us to be compelled to be insulting without even realizing it.

      --

      Finally, I since I made the decision to only play indie games at cons, I don't have this problem anymore. I routinely play with people I don't know, and still get to have fun. I am not saying that indie games are a guaranteed panacea for this problem, but I am saying that they help. Or have in my experience.

      As a player: I guess I hijack too, though I like to think of it as being proactive. :-) I try very hard to make the game fun because I don't want to be a "consumer" player, one who wants the GM and the rest of the table to entertain her as if they were part of a movie. I feel -- and not just in games -- that if something is off-kilter, the first thing I should check is whether I can help improve things. This does in fact help in a LOT of games; I've seen GMs who were very grateful that one player would start dragging the others into being more active than reactive or passive.

      So if the game still sucks for me, it's usually because the GM is not interested in proactive players. On rare occasion, the GM is fine but the rest of the group is composed of cavemen. I usually tough it out to the bitter end, but if something is offensive enough to drive me to walk off, I politely but sternly tell people why. Note that merely dull is not enough to drive me off. It has to be rank ass-hat behaviour to do the trick, so I don't feel I need to give white lies.

      In addition, I often play alongside my husband; since one of us is usually the GM in our regular games, that is a big attraction of cons that they give us a chance to both be players together. As a result, we have more momentum when trying to rescue a game. But we also have much more impact if we decide to walk, which is why it's very rare (I can't recall us ever walking off together.)

      As GM: Give me the white lie, give me the truth, I don't care. But TELL me you're not coming back. Don't leave the rest of the group hanging, waiting for your return. Also, a related behaviour I really hate: people who register in multiple games at once and browse, deciding which one they'll stick with. It's disruptive, it prevents other players from getting the spot, and it's rude.
