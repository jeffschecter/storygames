    I have direct experience with this, in a variety of ways:

    * DragonCon, last year, several of the RPGs for the Friday (?) evening slot had trouble making numbers and, further, had to be allocated to tables in rooms that didn't yet have "enough" tables (there were plenty for the actual, full games that ended up being played). So they had everyone posse up in a room that was setup for speakers (chairs in rows, podium) and each GM with at least two or three players read his or her blurb and then others in games that didn't make could join in. As the game that I wanted to play didn't make, it was ideal for me... and for the GMs whose games didn't make, too (don't forget them!).

    * In every regular LARP I've played, there's enough influx of new players each session that there's a brief "staff intro" during opening ceremonies, where the organizers get up and introduce themselves and explain their functional role at the LARP. This way, new players know to whom (and where) to go with a particular issue, rather than playing goose chase bouncing from one ST to the next.

    * I have GMed for a couple of demo slots that never made, and as such it's a REALLY good idea to have a sort of "be five minutes early, start ten minutes late" time formally scheduled with each slot so that (a) folks can get to know each other a bit OOC, get their playing shit sorted out (dice, pencils, minis), and generally do the time-wasting faff outside of game time; and so that (b) GMs and players whose games don't make have time to realize it and quickly ask around and/or hop into games with open seats.

    As a result of one or two times being caught with nothing to run or play, I now have a "backup game" or two for EVERY slot of a con I attend, whether running or playing. Formalizing this sort-and-shuffle process with a scheduled block of time to "gather, pitch, and split up" is A Good Idea. I wish more mid-sized cons did it (obviously, the Majors can't).

    Potential drawbacks exist, though: if folks know this is how it works, you might not get ANY sign-ups, with people waiting to commit until the last possible minute, thinking something new and sweet might come along (males are VERY prone to this behavior).

    You also run into a Cult of Personality problem, with the inverse of the "pick teams" alienation: folks are pitching their games ahead of That Cool Dude Who's Running That New Hawtness, and getting no takers, and having to join the player pool after getting up on stage and pitching. Owch. At least when a sign-up sheet is blank, you can just shrug and say, "Oh, well"--you don't have to look out on the nonplussed masses and hear the crickets chirp for a moment before stepping off stage for the Real Act. Oh yeah, and that guy gets flooded, so you need some kind of lottery process.

    That could be an extreme case. But unhappy GMs make sullen alternate players. I hear.

    \n I prefer the more informal style, which we ended up using for Games On Demand at GenCon.

    Meet people as they come through the door. Shake their hand. Ask them what games they like and if anyone wants to run a game. Keep pitches short: "There's Inspectres. It's a mystery game that's a bit like Ghostbusters. The players end up making up the mystery". Matchmake people into games.

    If there's a larger crowd, use the SteveCon method, which is that Steve yells from the middle of the room "Good evening LONDON! I'm STEVE and this is my CON! Make some NOISE! YEAH!". After the wild applause and chanting of "Steve CON! Steve CON!" has died down, ask who wants to run which games, and matchmake as before.

    \n I think you're overestimating talking time, Jason. Here's how it works at Concrete Cow: similar to Jiffy Con, I think.

    First game is at 10.30am, so people drift in from 10 onwards and get themselves a cup of tea. If you're running a game, you put out a sign-up sheet. At 10.30, the game starts, and if your game only got one sign-up you sort it out: combine with another game, ask to join another group or similar.

    Then there's a lunch break. Same procedure. Put the sign-up sheet out, people sign up over lunch.

    Then there's a break for an evening meal. Same thing happens.

    So, because the signing up occurs in the natural breaks, there's no extra time added.

    \n Here's the thing about the concept of "pre-reg is the devil" as applied to a mid-size con with recurring attendees: pre-reg is going to happen regardless of whether there's an official process for it or not.

    For example, I knew Ryan Macklin was coming to GPNW. I knew he would be running Mythender at least once. If there had been a system such that games were given slots but players weren't allowed to sign up for them, I would have tweeted at Ryan on the side and made him promise me a seat; if all the games had to be pitched on-site for every slot, I would have made Ryan save me a seat before he even went up to pitch. I can do these things because Ryan is a friend, which is to say he is afraid of me (I am very strong). I haven't been to Jiffy Con, etc., but I would be very skeptical of claims that nothing of this kind happened there. Geeks like to prep.

    I'm pretty much right behind everything Johnzo says above. I took the approach of registering for five slots and leaving three open, and it worked out very, very well for me. I got to play with almost everyone I wanted to play with, and I had plenty of time and opportunity to socialize with both new people and friends whose schedules didn't coincide with mine.

    What about an approach where two or three slots per day are pre-reg-available--say, afternoon and evening--and the morning slot is all pickup and pitch-based? That might allow for the seat-saving that's going to happen anyway, but also encourage a little mixing and breakdown in the strata.

    (I should note that I'm proposing a model to be considered and critiqued here, not pushing for a new agenda at any particular con.)

    \n I witnessed part of the problem with sign-up sheets at Continuum last year (and to a certain extent at Dragonmeet).

    At both these cons, the sign up sheets are released at half day intervals (although at Continuum they didn't do this on the first day). If you can be at the front of the queue when the sheets are put up, you can sign up as much as you want, and not just for yourself.
