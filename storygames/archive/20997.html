    \n So... this is basically "tell me about your favorite indie GM'd RPGs (That I don't already own)"? I'm down with that.

    Golden Sky Stories was already mentioned, but mentioning it again as a truly unique item. It's a peaceful game about helping people with little problems.

    Tenra Bansho Zero is a crazy-anime-styled game where caring about worldly things gives you power to do great deeds, but can cost you your humanity. You accrue "Kiai" - power - through your "Fates" - worldly ties - but using it gives you Karma, which is bad. Too much Karma deprotagonizes you. You remove Karma by resolving your fates. It's a little bit crunchier than your sweet spot, but no worse than The One Ring, I think.

    Masks is a game I'm surprised to not already see on your list. It's PbtA with some interesting twists. The theme of the game is "young superheroes" and the main twist is that you don't have stats like "Fast" or "Strong" but instead have "labels" that describe how you view yourself - things like "Danger" or "Mundane". Honestly, I think this is one of the most interesting PbtA games to hit the shelves lately.

    InSpectres is an oldschool indie game. Setting is "Ghostbusters" with a smattering of "The Office". Very light rules, interesting division of narrative authority - GM narrates failures, players narrate successes. Has a built in pacing mechanism for when the mystery should be "solved". Some really good stuff here.

    Ryuutama is a game about traveling. It's relatively traditional, but it subverts a lot of the standard D&D-isms by making most abilities NOT about combat, and by making combat a mediocre source of XP compared to... making it to the next town through difficult terrain. A very interesting exercise in a different style of fantasy.


    Special mention for Shinobigami - a game about modern ninjas that's like 50% hardcore storygame and 50% crunchy, semi-boardgamelike battle mechanics. Special mention because you can't actually get it yet, since the translation isn't done. =(

    I've never heard of 'bliss stage' so I'm excited to check that out :-)
    \nBliss Stage is an early (but multiple times revised) design by Ben Lehman, sometimes described as "Neon Genesis Evangelion the RPG" - and, while that's not entirely accurate, it does include teenage protagonists given an excessive burden of responsibility, a shady adult authority figure, giant fighting... things and alien invaders, all in a peculiar immediate-post-apocalypse setting. Grim and tragic, but with a shimmer of hope.
    While maybe not as well-known as Polaris, it's another seminal Forge game, strongly influencing a lot of this community's current favorite games. It includes such (then quite innovative) mechanics as formalized scene-framing with a variety of scene types, multiple PCs per player, shared character pools, stats and HPs for relationships and literally using your feelings for other characters as the weapons you wield in combat. Conflict resolution is via (one of the best existing implementations of) Otherkind dice. In fact, having last played this game not very long ago, I feel most of its mechanics are still unsurpassed in quality of implementation (I mean, for each of them, no other games trying to do the same exact thing slightly differently have managed to do it better). I'm especially fond of its scene-judging process (how the mechanical consequences of a non-conflict scene are decided on), which is something I keep copying shamelessly in most in-progress designs of mine and I'm a bit surprised not a lot of other designers do.
    While there are a whole lot of reasons to describe it as the hippiest of storytelling games, and in fact most tasks and authorities are distributed more-or-less equally amongst players (including, for example, the task of describing the nightmare alien monsters in action, which is choral and cooperative), there is a separate GM role in Bliss Stage - which I understand is the main requisite to fit in your "RPG" category, isn't it? It's also designed for medium-length play, over multiple engagements (but not necessarily a lot of them).

    \nOh, well, I'll just point at some of my previous recommendations again, then.

    The Mountain Witch looks like it has a completely "traditional" GM role, but then each other player is "the GM of" their own character's secret backstory (which not even the main GM knows) and thus can sort of "take over as a GM" at any time, whenever it's necessary to do so for their backstory to make sense and fit with the current events. A seminal game.

    In In a Wicked Age, all have equal weight in establishing the starting situation, but then there's a "GM" who frames scenes (based on the established situation and a strict "precipitate conflict" agenda) and plays several named "NPCs" while each other player only plays one character. The other difference between NPCs and PCs being, PCs have a chance of being recurring characters (but still aren't warranted to). Who the GM is varies from session to session.

    In On Mighty Thews, when you take action and succeed (with a dice roll) you get to author parts of the current scene, backstory, NPC action or details that haven't yet been established, taking over what would otherwise be the GM's duties. It's a swords and sorcery game and its sorcery system works exclusively by retroactively establishing actionable facts this way (otherwise there's no "spellcasting" per se).

    In Anima Prime, the GM's distinct task is to play the opposition in conflict scenes. In non-conflict scenes, though, their contribution is equal to anybody else's. There's absolutely no pre-established "plot" or backstory: story is moved forward cooperatively - interspersed with conflict scenes (usually battles) which are instead decided with lots of dice-rolling.

    It's been a long time since I last played Ganakagok, but I remember the game is very structured, with formally defined scenes, acts and arcs. If the game had been designed a couple years later, I believe it wouldn't sport a GM role - but it does. The GM mostly provides opposition, a certain amount of direction and coordination as well, but is strictly limited by the mechanics in what they can do.

    Do you think you could tell me a little bit about DayTrippers mechanics/procedures? Are they crunchy? When it says they have OSR elements, in what ways? Maybe just a general overview? Any info is appreciated :-)Thanks :-)
    \nSure thing, Jeff...
    • The DayTrippers mechanics are very mildly crunchy; somewhere between PbtA and "Basic Role Playing".
    • The system uses d6s only.
    • The game produces episodic sessions which follow a TV-like narrative arc.
    • Skills are applied very flexibly and creative applications are encouraged.
    • Progressive Character Generation means you don't have to know everything about your character before you begin playing; it can develop as you go.
    • Action resolution is context-dependent and interpreted on the fly; possible outcomes are YES AND, YES, YES BUT, NO BUT, NO AND.
    • Players interpret positive outcomes and the GM interprets negative ones.
    • I consider DayTrippers to be "75% OSR" according to the definition laid down by Matt Finch, and I wrote an essay about that: http://asifproductions.com/daytrippers-75-osr
    That said, it's flexible enough to be played loosely or in full "Auteur GM" mode.
