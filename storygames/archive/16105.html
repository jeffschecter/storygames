    \n Terrain, environment, and maneuver are all within the realm of GM moves. I use giant maps and make more on the fly.

    So the Germans roll on the players' prepared defensive position and I have the opportunity to make a hard move for whatever reason, I look at the list and pick "when they're in good cover, move the enemy to a better firing position". So I do that, and announce that the Panthers are staying back out of bazooka range right here on the map in the treeline instead of rolling into the prepared ambush. What do you do?

    \n Another good technique is to print out a historic map on paper and start drawing on it.

    "The Japanese have invested Nissan Island, and their base is right here at the entrance to the lagoon. The Marine raiding party needs to get dropped off there. The most fuel-efficient approach to the island is from this direction. But the map shows some sandbars over here that you could run aground on.

    What's your plan?

    \n What Jason and Chris said.

    Also, use the engagement moves. They will save you so much time and effort. You know how people in mil-sim kinds of games (or Shadowrun) can spend infinite time planning things? Yeah. You can skip all that. Get a rough idea of the plan, cast that into an engagement move, and roll it. Then you know what their situation is when the action starts: advantageous, mixed, or "you're boned."

    So, for Jason's thing, they'd roll the defense/security engagement move. The panzers hang back in the treeline is a good way to say embattled while misdirecting.

    For Chris's thing, they'd roll escort, transport/travel or recon/infiltration, depending on the nature of the mission.

    \n I just draw. I prefer to keep the drawing really loose so it doesn't become a "battle map" if you know what I mean. This restraint can be hard for me sometimes, since I love making cool maps.

    A word about terrain: there are "terrain moves" on the GM sheet. Use those when it's your turn to make a move and terrain is a factor. A lot of them are good fodder for the travel/transport engagement move, but you can use them any time.

    In battle, terrain features are often cover. Encourage players to ask about available cover (for themselves or the enemy) when they assess. Terrain can also offer concealment. You really really want cover and concealment while you're moving under fire.
