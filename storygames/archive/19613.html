    \n I think the main issue is that it leaves very little space for later customization.

    You can say "My PC has a +2 to Go Aggro, but only +1 in Seize By Force". What do you do when you need a custom move for Hard?

    Having a Stats means that every move that falls into that domain, gets the same bonus. This can make the PC 'flat' and I understand your point in giving different bonuses to all, but there are implications - such as the one above.

    So at least you need to consider: a) how do you build custom moves? (i.e. always inherit the lowest bonus? inherit the one of the most similar move?) b) if you make custom moves, do you keep them tracked in the playbook? c) you need to rework the advancements to the stats to become advancements to the single moves (if you have more moves than stats, maybe you need to give away more points)

    \n I kinda think of Stats as a shorthand for what the character is good at, anyway. Moves, on the other hand, have very specific triggers. It gives the same info but in a way that doesn't constrain your choices by spelling out exactly what you have to do to roll well. So in my table experience, *world games work best when Moves are in the background, not at the forefront. That way they get triggered solely by fiction-based events with less mechanical worries.

    Like, if the players are looking at a big sheet of basic moves, they're often going to be trying to use their best ones instead of settling into the fiction and doing what comes naturally. My best sessions have been the ones where I didn't even give the Basic Moves out to the players and just let them go crazy and leave the dice rolls for me to call out.

    I can totally picture a player saying "I want to roll out of the way! Wait, I only have a +1 When I Dodge... hm... I guess I'll Stand And Fight with a +3, again..." I already see this, sometimes. Maybe your players are different, though!

    \n In the fall and winter, I ran a *world exploration-and-resource-management game with troupe play where characters had no stats; instead, each had 1-2 special moves in addition to the common move.

    (This was kinda necessary, as there were 16 viewpoint characters who got traded frequently between a rotating pool of 8 players, and players frequently controlled 2-4 characters each at a time, so each had to be very simple.)

    Every move described very precisely the things that would grant you a bonus. For instance:

    - "Move Into Position" is +1 if you're alone, +1 if the enemy doesn't know where you are.
    - The Mechanic's move is at +1 if you have plenty of time on your hands, +1 if you have the perfect parts.
    - The Hunter's move is at +1 on home turf, +1 if you expend ammo.
    - The Thaumaturg'es move is +1 for a blood sacrifice, +1 if you spend Vim.

    Here's the cheat sheets: https://docs.google.com/file/d/0B-0rYyd-SvqadjZIdFVzMzJTdkU/edit

    \n Another interesting approach to this would be to either a) write moves differently for different characters (this happens a little in Monsterhearts), or b) give characters certain abilities which affect how moves play out.

    Under option a), one character has a "hack & slash" move, where, on a 7-9, you deal damage and get hurt in return, and on a 10+ you damage your enemy. Another character has the "Demonic Battle Dance" move, which means they slaughter anyone they want (or maybe multiple targets) on a 10+, and get to choose options (which include the option of avoiding getting hit) on a 7-9.

    They don't have "different stats", but their effectiveness in combat will be very different - and feel very different to play. And it also makes it pretty easy for that second character to "improve" by learning the Demonic battle skills or whatever.

    Option b) might work in a game where a group of moves uses a similar design. For example, you could have abilities like, "When you use a move to influence those around you, hold +1 (1 more than usual)". Or, "You're a skilled warrior. In combat, on a 7-9, you may choose one additional option". If your game is designed carefully, this can work well. (It may make it harder to make custom moves, though.)

    I don't see anything wrong with "meta moves" like "Trained by the Bene Gesserit: when you take a moment to focus before taking action, take +1 forward", or Apocalypse World's Daredevil ("When you go heedlessly into danger, take +1armor"). These, combined with a list of basic moves, could make the entirety of your character.

    There's a X-Files/Call of Cthulhu hack called Black Stars Rise which handles this in a fun way: when your character gets "damaged", you flip over your moves (they come on index cards), and have to use the version on the back from now on (which is worse than the default version).

    It has stats, but the concept could definitely work without stats.

    Or how about FATE-style Aspects? By default, any move is 2d6, no adds. But if you invoke an Aspect, you add +1.

    Lots of options here. It's nice to see some of them explored (like in Jeph's hack, linked above!).

    \n There's one significant thing you're missing. In most of the good *World games, stats communicate something about the character besides just the basic moves they tie to.

    So a character who is hard is good at going aggro and seizing by force, but they're also hard people. That's a thing that defines them and the world they live in. If you go without stats, you lose that effect.

    Of course that effect has to be there in the first place. In DungeonWorld, for example, the stat's don't communicate nearly as much about the characters--they're communicating something about the heritage of the game instead. In tremulus, though, the stats don't really communicate anything at all, and that one of the reasons the character side of tremulus is so weak.

    \n I've been thinking a lot lately about alternatives to stats, especially as related to Dungeon World. Then I saw this thread. Now I think my ideas might not be as neat as I thought.

    My idea was...
    Characters don't have stats. Instead potential modifiers are represented by a three word phrase in the format of [adjective/species] [noun/class/profession] the/of [adjective/noun/verb]. You add +1 to a roll for each word in the phrase that applies to the task at hand.
    Whenever you roll a 10+ or a 6-, you may note the resulting action and gain 1 inspiration token. One of these tokens may be spent after a roll is made to increase the result by 1. At the end of a session choose one the moments you noted throughout the session. That moment becomes a Milestone. Once per Milestone per session you may "tap" it to take +1 to a roll prior to rolling for it. Milestones are "untapped" at the beginning of every session.
