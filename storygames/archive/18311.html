\n I was recently asked for advice on how to write a playbook for Apocalypse World and keep it mechanically consistent with the rules. I posted my advice on my blog but I was also asked to post it here since this is a comfortable place for people to converse publicly and communicate ideas. Transcribed with links intact:

I've written a few playbooks now, and I've looked through a lot of really promising builds for playbooks and some that are just atrocious. Having scoured the Barf Forth Apocalyptica forums and committed to memory many of the random details from Vincent about how he wrote the original 11 playbooks, I feel pretty confident in writing this step by step process for writing a playbook.

Step 1: Have an open-ended concept
This is key. Your concept is everything, it helps define what you're going to write about the playbook. But you can't cling to it like a bible. Your vision of the archetype can't be constrained and must be open to interpretation. The best playbooks have a concept which is clearly understood and open to enough interpretation that it doesn't force the character to behave a certain way. The archetype molds to the character, not the other way around.
Look at the core playbooks and you can see the archetypes clearly: the Brainer is a weird psychic, the Angel is a competent medic, the Driver is a car obsessive, etc.

Step 2: Balance isn't everything
Every playbook has a stat block that usually includes a "best" stat. In almost every instance, the stat blocks add up to a total of +3 (+2 +1 -1 +1 +0 = +3). In some instances a character starts with two stats at +2 and those break the mold, but those stat lines also have a consistent math of adding up to +2 (+2 +2 +0 -1 -1 = +2), the price you pay for starting with two +2s is one less beneficial point.
Also, every playbook should start with three moves, or things, that make it distinct. These don't need to be balanced in power with other playbooks, as long as the character simply has 3 things. Look at the core playbooks and they all have three things to start: the Gunlugger starts with a choice of 3 moves, the Driver has their car (1) the no shit driver move (2) and then a choice of a second move (3), and the Savvyhead has their workspace (1) and a choice of 2 more moves (2 and 3).
Again, there are some playbooks that break the mold, but these tend to be balanced within themselves:
the Battlebabe only chooses 2 moves to start, but they have a Cool +3 and that's their third thing
the Hardholder only has their hold and a gang, with moves that accompany each, however these are bigger than the holds and gangs other playbooks can get as improvements, plus the Hardholder explicitly never needs to spend money for food and lodging
the Operator gets gigs and moonlighting, plus one more move, but the Operator's gigs outnumber what other characters can get as improvements

Step 3: Scarcity is the name of the game
One of the key things when writing moves and improvements for your playbook is to remember the theme of scarcity. In other words, you can't get all of the options! Nothing is perfect in Apocalypse World and you're always left wanting a little more, this should apply to character improvement as well. If your playbook has 5 moves to choose from and you can choose 3 during character creation, then you should think about adding a 6th move, maybe even a 7th move, or limiting the "get a new playbook move" improvement so that there's one move you can't get.
Again, look at the examples with the core game:
the Skinner starts with 2 moves and can get 2 more through improvements, but there are 5 Skinner moves total
the Gunlugger starts with 3 moves and can get 2 more through improvements, but there are 7 Gunlugger moves total

Step 4: Peer review
Share your ideas with your gaming friends and ask them for criticism. Share your ideas in the forums and ask for criticism. There is no better resource for criticism than a gaming community, just remember to not let all criticism affect your work. When I first shared the Wolf some people noted the villainous nature of the archetype and said they didn't think it fit with the game, I ignored that criticism because FUCK YOU! Don't tell me how to play the game! Just because it wouldn't be fun for you or your group doesn't mean it won't be fun for me and mine. Those kinds of criticisms about the theme and tone of your work are useless. In a way, they are just an outsider reaction similar to that lack of trust one displays at the gaming table when somebody else is narrating the action.
But when somebody says "Hey, it looks like your math is wrong." or "Isn't this move just a weaker version of going aggro?" Those are valid. Listen to them.
Scratch that! Listen to everybody. Just keep your bullshit detector on a high frequency.
And don't wait until your playbook is finished to share it either. As soon as you have something to work with you should be talking about it and sharing it to see where the cracks are. Poke and prod at your work until you think it's solid.

Step 5: Play it!
Once you think your work is solid, you have got to play it. Get other people to play it if you can. Play it, play it, and play it some more. Because you will never have played it enough and somebody might still find something wrong with it.

That's all I've got. Good luck!

    \n So yeah, Patrick (nerdwerds) has the numbers bits right. Just to reiterate some of the details:
    - A second +2 stat gives you a 1 point penalty (or counts as +3, either way).
    - The Battlebabe's cool+3 is just a hidden +1 cool move.
    - One of the Operator's stat lines is wrong and adds up to only +2.
    - The Quarantine's strange roll-for-weird thing counts as weird-1 in the math.
    - And also the Driver's stats only add up to +2 instead of +3 because he gets a thing that boosts his stats. If you have a thing that boosts your stats you will be more inclined to use it if you have low stats. And it is a good thing for the Driver to be more inclined to use his car.

    How I break down the moves+stuff of the playbooks in order to judge them is this:
    After stats, each playbook gets 3 moves or move-like things and some colour.
    - Angel: 2 moves + angel kit.
    - Battlebabe gets 2 moves and a +1 cool (for +3 total). Custom weapons are just colour, they're not actually better, they're just custom.
    - Brainer gets 2 pieces of gear for a third move. This gear is not always as good as a third move, but if you consider how impactful the Brainer's moves are on the game, it is okay for this "third move" to be less good than the first two. Also the Brainer starts with a shitload of money.
    - Chopper's gang counts as half a move and I guess the bikes count for another half. Or maybe the gang counts for a full move, plus Pack alpha being a full move, even though you can get both for one improvement (but only if your playbook lets you, so...) but that would mean a holding counts for nothing.
    - Driver: car + no shit driver + 1 move, pus there's the stat penalty.
    - Gunlugger: 3 moves. All those weapons is just colour. Easy come, easy go.
    - Hardholder's gang and holding count for a half each. As nerdwerds notes, both of these are better than what you get from an advance where you get the moves and the people with it.
    - Hocus' followers don't count as a move, it's enough that Fortunes does. They are just not as good as a holding.
    - Operator's gigs count as a move even though Moonlighting also does. She still get more gigs than other characters that take this advance, plus a crew to work them, but she can still look a little drab at times, if that first moonlighting roll doesn't put her in the "main character" seat from the very beginning.
    - Savvyhead's workspace counts as 1.
    - And the Skinner's special gear counts as 1. If you check her moves, they are even more impactful, invasive, and spotlight-hogging than the Brainer's, so it is probably good she only gets 2. That gear is also stuff that nobody else gets at all (and even has a +1 stat option!)

    - Maestro D's establishment counts as 1.
    - Hoarder's hoard counts as 1.
    - But the Quarantine's stasis facility doesn't. It's colour. Probably because it can be abandoned or destroyed without remorse. It's not essential to his concept, more of an opportunity you are encouraged to explore.
    - Likewise, the Touchstone's token of hope is just colour, even though it has some mechanical benefits. Touchstone's a bit munchkin-y anyway though.
    - And the mask of the Faceless is also colour, but it's actually a weakness! Because all his moves are awesome fighty moves, this actually adds some depth and dimension to the character, which is good.
    - The Solace has two moves plus Threshold. Wolves is just colour.

    \n Okay, now let's talk about something that doesn't get discussed a lot. This is a giant long post but I promise it will be worth it.

    Notice something most of those playbooks above have in common? Almost all of them have a resource, a thing that isn't part of them but is part of their concept. These are not just D&D-style murderhobo classes that are all about what kind of special actions you can take and what you can do TO others. They are often about who you know and what you can do FOR others, or what you can get out of them when you're not killing them to take their treasure.

    Angel kit, workspace, car, holding, gang, followers, hoard, mask, establishment, even gigs. These things are all resources that the characters rely on, are responsible for, and must manage properly. Most people, when they write new playbooks, write murderhobo archetypes like the Battlebabe, the Gunlugger, and the Faceless. They are people with no real ties to the communities around them and no real baggage they have to tend to, just getting by on their raw ability. This is understandable, because that is what most character classes and concepts in most rpgs are and have always been. And it's fine for characters who wander the earth like Caine in Kung Fu but AW characters are best played where they live.

    There is another axis to this resource thing though, that seems to be less obvious, and that is what each playbook brings to the table and what it demands. For the most part, the 11 basic playbooks are those that add something to the setting. They contribute. By contrast, most of the limited edition playbooks are very selfish and instead make demands of the game.

    Take the following for example: Chopper, Hardholder, and Hocus. The simple act of picking one of these actively contributes to the game's setting. They introduce people and community that aren't monopolized by any one player. Anyone can live in the Hardholder's holding and interact with her people. Anyone can be in the Hocus's cult or interact with them. If you join the Chopper's gang, you even get a free bike! Plus, now you know what kind of people live in your Apocalypse World, or some of them.

    Now look at the Angel, Brainer, Driver, Savvyhead, and Skinner. Each of them has a resource that makes them, fundamentally, reliant providing services for other people. They need to actually interact with other characters because they don't have the capability to just take whatever they want, so their resources connect them to the setting and the communities in it. Angel heals, Savvyhead fixes, Driver drives people and things around, the Skinner has her art, and the Brainer reads minds for probably whoever has the most moolah. Each requires transactions of some kind.

    The Operator is kinda in-between these two types, because the MC has to ask her who the people involved in all these gigs are, which results in setting for everyone, not just the Operator. But she's not automatically in charge of them, she still has to buy, sell, and negotiate every new deal.

    And finally\u2014and on the other hand\u2014we have the murderhobos the Battlebabe and the Gunlugger. These playbooks, in and of themselves, do NOT actively contribute something to the setting (other than a sense of theme and genre). They give the player some cool shit to do, and make motions toward encouraging them to be social, but there's nothing there that demands non-sociopathic relationships with other people or gives the MC free stuff. They are, however, thematically appropriate for the post-apocalyptic genre and seriously, there's only two of them!

    Until you start adding the limited edition playbooks. Then you get a third murderhobo in the Faceless (who is basically a weirder version of the Gunlugger), plus many more made by not-Vincents.

    The other limited edition playbooks are resource-based, though, right? Surely they add something to the game? There is a problem though, because these are resources that monopolize the game's attention, which can be even worse than murderhobos!

    For example, if the Faceless can talk to his mask, this playbook is adding an NPC to the game that only one player can interact with. The Hoarder's hoard is even worse in this regard. An NPC that only one character can interact with takes up a lot of the MC's attention. If this comes with rules and stuff too, it can become really distracting. Anyway, I've criticized the Hoarder before, around the end of this AP thread. In that one instance, I found it really disruptive to MC for the Hoarder when the hoard wasn't central to the fiction and was just a thing for that one PC (and the other PCs had their own secret bases). Take a look at my Damned playbook for comparison. It uses almost the same rules but allows the player to manage them, so that all the MC needs to do is listen to what the player says about their stuff and then be an MC. She doesn't have to also manage that character's hold and private NPC.

    As for the others:

    The Maestro D' has a resource much like a holding, followers, or a gang. Except that, to interact with it, you have to go there. You have to go to the Maestro D' in order to get the stuff that this playbook contributes to the game. It doesn't come to you, even if she does.

    The Quarantine demands the game become about what caused the apocalypse. Every session, somebody (and it's likely the MC) has to make up some stuff about the apocalypse, so the MC had better be thinking about it! Plus there's that stasis chamber demanding the MC's attention too.

    The point here is that these are okay or even awesome to play IF the whole group wants them there. If everybody wants to know what is up with this guy's mask, if they want to know what he has in his secret stash of stuff, if they all want to go to her club, if they all want to know what caused the apocalypse, cool! Pick that playbook! But if these resources don't become a main, central point of the game, they become dead weight. If the other players don't care that much, it's going to fall flat and take up space. And if you have too many selfish resources, they're not going to get along very well. It takes a lot of work to get a Maestro D', a Quarantine, and a Hoarder to work together in the same game, because they all have their own lairs. Whereas you put a Hardholder, a Hocus, and an Operator together and your setting builds itself.

    Make sense?

    The Touchstone and the Solace are also demanding because they present particular visions of the post-apocalypse and then coerce the other players into going along with them. The Solace does a better job because it's mostly all rewards and no threats of ass-kicking, but she still demands the game be all about her deal.

    What the Solace has in common with the Chopper, Hardholder, and Hocus, though, is that it's not just about what cool things the playbook gives to the player who picks it. These playbooks are also all about what they give ALL the players when someone picks them.

    \n A little more on the Murderhobos:

    This aspect of contributing to the campaign as well as the one player's enjoyment can also apply to murderhobo playbooks and those with smaller resources to manage. As mentioned above, the Battlebabe and the Gunlugger are both thematically appropriate and reinforce the violent, lawless, and terrifying post-apocalyptic feel of the game, so they're not total dead weight. Likewise, the Skinner emphasizes how ugly and broken the world is by being the only really beautiful thing in it and the Brainer tells you right away that there's weird psychic shit beyond even the maelstrom.

    This is also something that can be ported over to AW hacks, and Dungeon World in particular, because the classes in DW are all mostly murderhobos. Pretty much all the new classes I've seen so far have been focused on giving the one player a box of shiny new toys, on the principle that players who do cool things make for a cool game. Which is not too bad as far as ideas go, but that weird sort-of disconnect you get when a game is basically everybody taking turns telling their own character's story, with maybe some inter-character references or interaction but not much, only works for short-term games. For long-term play, you want everybody in the game together, right? So, for example, my new classes for Adventures on Dungeon Planet are all about adding that science fantasy flavour to the campaign, in addition to giving players cool things, and the forthcoming Inverse World has its own specific campaign setting which the new classes are tailored for.

    So anyway, this is one of the harder parts of game designing playbooks and classes, partly because it's not immediately obvious from a lot of perspectives, and particularly not from an individual player's perspective. And that's why I am writing it all down here.

    There's also probably a bunch that could be said for synergy between playbooks, how certain playbooks interact with each other and how you design for that. That's also part of what a playbook can contribute to the game.

    \n Johnstone's analysis is very interesting, although I don't completely agree. In Apocalypse World, violence is a resource, and the gunlugger and the battlebabe provide violence for people who need it. Since the gunlugger has terrible hot and usually mediocre cool, they usually can't fend for themselves without some help, but they have to pay for that help, and they're easily handled. In a game with the gunlugger, part of the challenge is "controlling the gunlugger." I generally agree that the battlebabe is deliberately independent. The movie Yojimbo is a good example of what battlebabes do every day. (I would probably call the driver the second most independent playbook.)

    Here's something I would pose as a partly concurring, partly dissenting analysis:

    Some of the playbooks define places. The Hardholder and the Maestro'D especially, but also the Savvyhead, the Hoarder, and the Quarantine. If you have one place in the game, most of the action is probably going to take place at or near that place, and an important part of a character's definition will be their relationship to that place -- do they work for the hardholder or are they hunted by them? Do they have a VIP pass at the Maestro's place or do they have to sneak in? If you have multiple places, you're going to have tension between them, and more than likely extended conflict between them for the attention of the other PCs. If you have no places, the MC might have to contribute a little extra to define location, or the players might feel rootless.

    Some of the playbooks define groups of people. The Chopper, the Hocus, and the Operator especially, but also potentially the Angel and the Savvyhead, and the Maestro'D and Hardholder both come with groups to protect their places. If you have one group in the game, all the PCs and NPCs will be defined by their relationship to that group -- are they part of it, or do they work with it, or do they fear it, or do they fight it? If you have multiple groups, a big part of the game will almost certainly involve managing their relationships with each other, whether that means keeping them from fighting or making them fight. If you have no groups of people, again, the MC will have to figure out for themselves what the PCs community looks like, or they might feel isolated.

    When you're writing a playbook, strongly consider whether it's defining a place or a group of people, or neither, or both. Note that the Hardholder and Maestro'D both pay for getting both a place and a group -- the Hardholder gets very little besides the responsibility to manage them, and the Maestro'D's stuff is very small and vulnerable. At the same time, note that a character with neither a place or a group needs something to contribute to those places and groups, or they're going to have difficulty fitting into the conflicts that are probably inherent in the setup.

    Apocalypse World is about dependency. What is your playbook dependent on? Why are other people dependent on your playbook?

Of course, you have the option of choosing a movable establishment, so again I'm exaggerating a little for effect, but if you really compare the two, one has a greater range of options than the other, period. The second (the Maestro D') I actually think there's room to argue over.

Once you offer a playbook up to the masses, either you're saying "this works for my group, maybe you'll like it too, take a gamble" or you're gonna try making a playbook that appeals to, and works for, a range of players. As long as the playbook is solid and nobody hates it, it will work, probably nobody will even notice or think about it other than me.

    \n Oh! Sorry for the confusion. I should clarify that then:

    Murderhobo is a term for a type of D&D character (in one type of D&D) that only cares about gold, because gold = xp. Because the game consists of almost nothing but dungeons full of monsters and treasures, and doesn't care about who your character was before the dungeon or what they aspire to become after the dungeon, players find that sociopathic characters are often the most successful.

    Because they have no past and are adventuring in an unfamiliar land, staying at inns, and possibly wandering over large tracks of unexplored land, they are often quite literally hobos -- they have no homes.

    They are murderhobos -- hobos who are murderers -- partly because they kill people and take their stuff. And not just monsters either, but anybody with treasure who won't give them assistance. For example, once players realize that the Keep on the Borderlands has more treasure in it than the monster-infested caves, and that the people who run it are dicks, they often turn on the keep and raid it instead of the dungeons. These are just imaginary people, after all, who cares about them?

    But also because it is often very useful to hire NPC retainers to help you out in the dungeon, but they also take a share of the xp away from PCs. So it behooves you to hire retainers and get them killed before you exit the dungeon. Sometimes, if players are really desperate for xp, they will kill these retainers themselves. It's just an imaginary character, you can always hire a new one later, right?

    So a murderhobo character in general is one who has
    a) little or no real past, or nothing important in it or about it that relates to other characters;
    b) little or no desire to have meaningful relationships with NPCs; and
    c) uses violence to get what they want most, or even all, of the time.

    This mentality is usually strongest at level 1, when the players know nothing about the setting and have no attachment to it. They tend to find NPCs that entertain them later on as the backstory and setting is built through play. But because you get xp from finding treasure, not from doing good deeds or even defeating monsters, these characters tend to be scoundrels, not paladins.

    So when I see playbooks that don't come with mandatory relationships and are good at violence, I call these murderhobo playbooks. You can't play a Hardholder like a murderhobo because the Hardholder derives her power from her gang and her holding--from her relationships with NPCs. You can play the Chopper's gang like a murderhobo gang, but you can't play the Chopper like that because he has to socialize with his gang. You can play a Battlebabe, a Gunlugger, or a Faceless like a murderhobo very easily -- especially if you come from a D&D background. Nothing on those playbooks says explicitly "hey, you can't be a homeless dude who wanders around murdering people." The talking mask implies it, or the MC can say it through the mask.     But here's the thing: We're not playing AW or AD&D because we can't play rpgs without them. I don't need a set of rules to play a role-playing game. You don't either. We can just talk and make up rules or use ones we remember from games we played before, right? We play a specific game for a number of reasons, and one of those is for what that game adds to the conversation. So if "what the player brings" to the game is the solution to a game design problem, then why play a game at all? Why make any new playbooks at all? It is because you are building an experience for someone else -- someone (many someones) you don't know, if you are releasing it out into the internet. In terms of products, it is okay to say something is good enough and you don't know how to make it better and you don't want to work on it anymore. In terms of game design skills and knowledge, though, anyone who designs games and is done becoming a better game designer is dead or might as well be.

    So! With my analysis above I am talking about only what the playbook adds to the experience. And I've noticed that
    a) playbooks with a mandatory connection to the setting (Hardholder, etc) add more to the game than others; and
    b) playbooks that strongly imply connections and relationships (Angel, etc) add more than those that only weakly imply connections (Gunlugger, etc);

    AND that
    c) playbooks that add locations/people/relationships to the game that all the PCs can interact with and have something to do with (Hocus, etc) add more to the game than those that add things that are more exclusive to that particular playbook (Hoarder, etc) because
    d) exclusive stuff takes up more of the MC's attention and demands spotlight time on one player alone if other PCs can't interact

    BUT that
    e) feeding into the genre conventions of the game and setting is also a valuable asset for a playbook to have, even if it only weakly implies connections to other people.

    So anyway, I don't want to get too sidetracked with the murderhobo thing, because of course the game doesn't make you play a murderhobo when you're not supposed to, but it is important to distinguish between using violence to maintain and manage relationships, which is what hardholders and choppers do, and using violence to end relationships, which is what murderhobos do. One produces more opportunities for role-playing, the other reduces them.

      \n Okay, so here is an example of the importance of playbook design and some notes on incremental improvement of that design. Not everybody will have seen these problems in play, of course, but I have so I know they are real.

      A thing I notice that comes from the playbook format is when players pick up a playbook and assume that this it contains all the stuff they need to do to set up the game. Once they go through these steps, they switch to doing what the game asks of players, which is playing the character, and playing the character hard. This means, sometimes, that the same players who work really hard in, say, a GMless game, to build connection and conflicts between characters and really work together with the group, will barely talk with each other about which playbooks they want, will only have the relationships mandated by Hx and resources that come with the playbook, and will look to the MC to sort everything out in play.

      This leads to stuff like three PCs with secret lairs, as I mentioned above, but also to another game where I ended up almost playing a separate game entirely. Because we assumed the playbooks would take care of everything--and I did this, too, this is not a blame-post--and a few small mistakes that snowballed, the other PCs had little or no relationship to my character so when I went around offering them scenes with my guy, they all turned me down because they were playing their characters.

      So, this happened for a couple reasons. Our characters knew each other but weren't allies so we didn't help the MC weave us together at all where the playbooks didn't force us. One thing I noticed fell really flat, playing the Savvyhead, was the Hx questions on that playbook.

      In the Hx phase, you as the Savvyhead pick a character you think is strange and a character you think is a problem. so basically, what you get out of this phase, from this playbook, is some opinions. Sure, this is a starting point for the MC to ask questions, but there are a couple problems here.

      One is that I've got opinions about another character but I can't say things about them, because that's the other player's thing. I can't say "I think Sundown is a problem because she does this, that, and the other thing." I'm stuck riffing off another player's description of who their character is and what they look like, without being able to easily translate that into stuff that characters actually do. we can still hash it out, but these Hx questions don't actually make that process easier.

      Second, opinions don't frame scenes for shit. "I think Spider is the weird one" does not instantly translate into a scene. If the MC says "okay, let's have a scene that shows why you think Spider is weird" what happens? Yeah, we talk a bunch more before we get to the scene.

      Now, what does the Savvyhead do? He produces items, he fixes items, he collects information through items. The workspace implies that he produces items for other people, that he fixes items for other people, and maybe that he can be a detective as well. So, if we had Hx question options (even just options!) like "I fix things for ________ on a regular basis," and "I once built a machine for ________" then suddenly bam! we have actual relationships based on actions, we have potential scenes, we have the Savvyhead doing Savvyhead things. The MC can look at these and say "So hey, Cobra, Spector fixes things for you allatime, does he? Let's see that," and you have a scene right away. Why do you need to decide their opinions of each other? You can just ROLEPLAY YOUR CHARACTERS and find out! And seriously, I've seen several Savvyheads in play, and these fixing and building scenes are always outnumbered by the "mad scientist working alone in the shop on a thing for himself" kind of scenes.

      So, that is one small example of making incremental changes to playbooks, in order to make it more conductive to good play. This doesn't mean the Savvyhead is a crap playbook or anything. This is just one little thing. You tweak it, it becomes more efficient. You have to do this all over the place in order to build a good playbook, and there will probably always be room for improvement (depending on your audience, of course), but if you are ever overwhelmed by the amount of work making a full playbook is, go small and work on just one little thing like this.

      \n Okay, let's do the violence post, since that's a major component of AW.

      There's three points where fiction and mechanics meet when violence happens:

      1. When a PC can commit violence and thus when they can roll for a move.

      2. Rolling for the move and the probabilities of the different outcomes.

      3. Harm and armour.

      For the first part, every PC can punch or shoot somebody, or drive a car into somebody. But then there's stuff like gangs. You can use a gang to attack a lot more people at a time, or you can use a gang to attack people when you are not there. This is what I mean when I say a PC's ability to commit violence is expanded. With a gang you can attack somebody fifty feet away with a knife, if you get one of your gang to stab them. They also let you attack like five different people at a time without having to use explosives or full-auto. Psychic attacks can let you do this kind of thing, too. But really what expanded means is that you have a thing that allows you to commit violence where you wouldn't normally be able to.

      But then you have to roll it. The higher your bonus is, the more control you have over the outcome. On a 6 or less, the MC chooses what happens, and it likely goes bad for you. But on a 7+, you get a say in what happens. So something like a +2 bonus to a roll looks like it says pretty clearly that yes, your bad-assness has gone up, but actually it means your out-of-controlness has gone down.       It's harm and armour that really decide the impact of violence. Well, and the MC's judgment, but that also happens after this calculation. It's actually pretty easy to get more of both of these, just from the fiction, because you can probably get better weapons (unless you are already using the grenade launcher) and maybe armour too.       \n If you want something like Moonlighting, Fortunes, or Wealth, which are what I call "upkeep moves," it's actually pretty easy to get something rough figured out. The point of these moves is to create situation at the beginning of a game session, whether that's good or bad or just some kind of complication.

      Make a short list of the good situations that worldwide fame can get you: money, access, favours, travel plans, etc.
      Make a short list of the bad or troublesome situations that worldwide fame can get you: scandal, hounded by paparazzi, stalker, debts, contractual obligations, etc.

      Then roll:
      -- 10+, you get X amount of good things, either stuff that is decided beforehand (gigs can be one-off shows and events, if you model this after Moonlighting), or you could get nebulous social capital to spend during the session (if you model it on Wealth).
      -- On a 7-9, you get some of the good from 10+, but some complications, too.
      -- On a miss, you get only complications. Maybe the MC frames the initial scene around them? I've experienced how everything that happens in one session can stem from that initial Moonlighting roll.

      If you want a move for manipulating that fame during the session, might be trickier.

      \n I'm way late to join this thread, but I'd like to say a huge thanks to nerdwerds and Johnstone for some really intelligent discussion.

      I've always had mixed feelings about certain playbook combinations, as well as about most/many of the new playbooks (including Limited Edition and some of the fan playbooks), and this thread has really helped me to understand why.

      Here's another factor we haven't discussed yet:

      Some playbooks do not include any points of conflict or Situation. (e.g. the Battlebabe)

      Some playbooks imply possible points of conflict by handling a rare resource or situation (e.g. how the existence of an Angel implies that there will be some difficult situations with people near death to deal with--the Angel's resources and help are subject to scarcity, after all).

      Some playbooks actually generate Situation, by creating points of conflict. The most obvious examples are the Hardholder and the Hocus (and the Operator).

      When you have one of the latter in play, a lot of stuff happens just by itself. You pick the character creation options and then you roll the start-of-session moves, and--bam--there's a Situation and some stuff happening. For example, the Hardholder rolls a 7-9 and tells the MC that his holding is subject to retribution this session. Immediately, we have a Situation which must be resolved.

      These are really powerful tools. (Although I've never been a game where we've had several of these in play at once: maybe a game with a Hocus, Hardholder, AND Operator would get to be a little too much? I'm not sure.) Anyway, that's another side of playbook design to consider. I get the impression that the occurence of this kind of thing in the original 11 playbooks is pretty well-balanced.

      Real celebrity tends to work two ways, I've noticed. You can leverage it for money by doing celebrity appearances: you draw people physically or their attention towards a place, another person, or a product, and you get paid for it. This is how every no-talent celebrity makes a living.

      \n Okay, so I am happy to continue discussing any of the stuff already in this thread, but I want to move on to moves now. Moves are one of the main building-blocks of playbooks and are pretty important.

      In my opinion/experience, there are two key elements to consider when writing moves: the fiction and player behaviour.

      How a move affects the fiction gets mentioned a lot, and I think most people understand this one. What does a move bring to the fiction? How does it guide and shape the fiction? Moves are where the dice come in and change the trajectory of where the fiction is going, not only deciding which player's vision is the one that prevails, but also demanding certain concessions from each and every player, even the MC.

      The kind of advice I don't see that often though is that moves shape player behaviour, even though this is pretty basic game design. It's not just about the fiction \u2013 moves also change the way players behave, and moves should encourage behaviour that supports both the specific playbook they belong to, but also the game itself, and discourage dissenting behaviour.

      So, examples. Let's talk about +1s.

      The general rule is that +1s suck because they are boring, they don't add anything to the fiction, etc. In many cases, this is quite true, but not always.

      The worst kind of +1 is a move that after rolling the dice gives you a +1 with no connecting fiction and no fictional reason. So, something like this:

      Battle Frenzy
      When you go into battle, roll+hard. On a 10+, hold 3. On a 7-9, hold 1. Spend your hold, 1-for-1, to:
      * Inflict +1 harm when you inflict harm.
      * Take +1 to act under fire, go aggro, or seize by force.
      * Take -1 harm when you suffer harm.

      This move doesn't look especially bad or broken or anything, and you might think it's even a good way to play a barbarian berserker type character, but the thing is: it adds absolutely nothing to the fiction. The trigger is something you are doing anyways and not something you do specifically to receive the benefits of this move, and the results of this move don't add any fiction because they describe only mechanical results. Not only does it not add any fiction in and of itself, it also doesn't encourage the player to add anything extra to the fiction.

      Ideally, in a fiction-first game, you want to be in the fiction, trigger a move, roll for it, and then move back into the fiction before you get to another instance where you have to consult the rules and the dice. This is a case where one interaction with the rules directly affects a second interaction with the rules without putting anything on the table to get there. This isn't quite like a move that has a result which demands you act under fire, because in order to determine what the outcome of that roll means, you actually have to decide what acting under fire actually means in the fiction. And it's not really expressing what the battle frenzy is, either. If the move said, "when you frenzy, you inflict +1 harm," that would be a mechanical way of expressing what the battle rage is like. Instead, this move suggests you role-play some battle rage but really just gives you some limited abstract bonuses when you get into some violence. None of it really exists within the fiction.

      A move like read a situation (or discern realities), uses a +1 bonus to assist with reincorporation. Instead of getting a flat +1 bonus to another roll, you have to actually use the fiction that the +1 is attached to, to reincorporate something that was brought up earlier. Even if you act on it right away, you're still reincorporating something that someone else contributed, which connects both fiction and behaviour. It encourages players to use each other's contributions to the fiction. This is similar to how FATE operates, tagging certain elements of the fiction with a bonus in order to encourage reincorporation later (and there's a lot of room here for more AW/FATE hybrids, especially for people who don't like skills or Fudge dice).

      You can also use a +1 to encourage behaviours other than reincorporation. Take, for example, the Halfling Fighter racial move from Dungeon World. You get +1 to defy danger when you use your small size as an advantage. Since this is a game where you advocate for your character's success, usually when you do something dangerous you are looking to succeed at it, so you want that +1 to your roll. You look at your sheet and you can see right there how to get it: by talking about how your character is a halfling! It is a perfect confluence of encouraging behaviours that support the game and push it forward: playing your character as a protagonist, doing dangerous things, and being a fantasy hero.

      To bring it around full circle, it is also entirely possible to create rules that don't require you to add anything to the fiction but still influence player behaviour. But you don't need moves to do this \u2013 the stats do this already. You don't need to say your character is hot even if you have hot+2, even though most people assume that's what it means. But really all it means is you get a +2 bonus when a move says to roll+hot. Which means you will probably do a bunch of roll+hot stuff, because you want you character to succeed at things. But stats don't take up game time the way moves do. They just sit there on the character sheet until you have to use them for something else, they don't require you to roll for things that don't add to the fiction.

      So: Just because something involves +1 bonuses or whatever, doesn't mean it is bad. It's all about how it influences player behaviour and how it encourages players to contribute to the fiction, because if a +1 can do that effectively, it doesn't need to add anything all on its own.

      \n Okay, some more. This is a thing that really bugs me: "auto-parley" moves.

      You see it mostly in Dungeon World playbooks, with the parley move instead of in Apocalypse World with the manipulate move, where a special move says "you can always use X thing as leverage to parley." An Arresting Skinner is another version of this move, except without relying on a roll or anything. The basic premise here is that you will always be able to parley with NPCs. So, for example:

      Beat You Up!
      You can always use the threat of you beating someone up as leverage to parley with them.

      So basically, the effect that this has is: every single NPC in the game is afraid of you beating them up. Whether or not you actually choose to parley with all of them or not is beside the point\u2014at any time, you can choose to indicate that an NPC is afraid of being beaten up by you.

      Why is this a problem? Because you're giving the player a hammer and turning all the NPCs into nails.

      In a game like Apocalypse World, everyone is human. There's a limited range of setting and genre there, and even then the auto-parley move puts some serious constraints on the types of NPCs the GM can introduce. But in a game like Dungeon World, you're supposed to have the entire palette of the fantasy genre available to you. So now you've got deities, giant dragons, demons, emotionless ghosts, robots, and who knows what else all afraid of being beaten up by this PC, even when it is impossible for the PC to beat them up! It strains credulity, sure (why are these crazy powerful being afraid of being beaten up? you put them in the game for some impact, some gravitas, and then a single move just drains it away), but more importantly it creates one optimal path that is always open, which makes other options less likely to be considered. End result: it encourages a boring game with the same stuff happening over and over again. The GM can no longer introduce a variety of different NPCs, with varying motivations and desires. In the face of this PC, they all become the same.

      And I use the threat of being beaten up for comedic effect, but the issue is the same even if we go with something else, like:

      Meistersinger
      You can always use the power and beauty of your songs as leverage to parley.

      Okay, so now you can just sing your way out of any conflict with any NPC. Assuming you roll good anyway. But this still removes a sizeable opportunity for role-playing. The GM can't decide what the NPC is like, and there's no incentive for the PC to actually find out what they're like and what kind of leverage would be useful in manipulating them.

      This might be partially encouraged by the basic moves. In AW, you use read a sitch primarily in order to find out what course of action would be most beneficial (hence the +1 bonus for reincorporation), but you use read a person (and open your brain) in order to get information that allows you to make moves where you couldn't make moves before. In other words, you read a person to find out what you can use for leverage, and then you can manipulate them with it, instead of just taking a stab in the dark and coming up empty. This means more investment in the NPC, and more time (on the part of the players) getting to know them. Dungeon World combines these into discern realities, and the move that creates new opportunities to make moves is spout lore, which does a lot less to encourage interaction with NPCs. And the discern realities questions are of the read-a-sitch variety (or the locational variety, which is a bit odd because according to the trigger you can't use the move on a location per se) and not the read-a-person variety.

      AW has moves like An Arresting Skinner, of course, and playbooks like the Solace, that dictate the emotional reactions of other characters. But these is pretty rare, and also a Big Deal when it happens (and playbooks that do this by default are pretty disruptive, I would say selfish, just like I did at the start of this thread). The basic moves are a different story. You'll notice the last seize by force option is "impress, dismay, or frighten your enemy." So we know they are impacted somehow, but the MC still has leeway to actually role-play the NPC and decide what their specific, actual reaction is. If they don't feel fear, they can still be impressed. If they are completely dead inside, they can still be dismayed. This is definitely not in the auto-parley category, because it's still up to the GM's judgment\u2014the rules just narrow things down.

      Okay, so two peripheral issues. One: what if your GM never thinks your character has leverage to parley? An auto-parley move fixes that! Yes. So does getting a new GM who will be a fan of the characters. If you say your character is the greatest singer in the land and he only ever meets NPCs who hate music, somebody is being a jerk instead of a fan. Most NPCs should be interested in your character's singing. Then when one shows up who isn't, it's a Big Deal. Instead of all of them all the time, which either way becomes boring.

      And two is perhaps the counter-point that, well, the GM can just do that anyway. Except this is a terrible counter-point because technically no they can't, unless either they are breaking the rules or that line that says you can always use X to parley is just a suggestion. In which case why is it there in the first place then? If I want to re-skin the Ranger so I can play Afro-Galactus riding around on the Silver Surfer's borrowed board, I can do that my own damn self. I don't need a suggestion from somebody else to decide my Druid is actually a four-piece Japanese metal band. I need rules that actually make it interesting to play those characters. And throwing around those auto-parley moves, instead of saving them for something really crazy (like one move out of 11 playbooks crazy), makes play less interesting, not more.

      If the caveat to an auto-parley move is that it's the GM's prerogative to ignore it and disallow leverage when it makes sense then it's not a real rule, it's just a suggestion. Because it's already the GM's job to allow leverage when it makes sense and deny leverage when it doesn't make sense\u2014and to do so while being a fan of the character. Which makes it not a valid choice. Either you can trust the GM to be a fan when role-playing NPCs or you can't, and by phrasing it as a rule, you're kind of saying that you can't ("you" being the game designer). And if I have to break the rules in order to make something be interesting, then the rules aren't very good, are they? No. I think they can be better.

      \n Hey, speaking of shut down moves, Mr. Negativity (that's me!) has something to say about negative choices (that's moves that say "X doesn't happen") and how I feel like they are inefficient.

      Okay, so, back at the dawn of time, in the ancient, hoary days before Apocalypse World came out, we played Otherkind Dice, or rather we played John Harper's implementation of it: Ghost/Echo.

      And there was this one little annoying problem with it that goes along with the dice.

      So how the dice work is: a character has a goal, and then the GM states a danger. And maybe a second one, too, or two more. And actually, the whole table comes up with these additional dangers, not just the GM, if you want. And you take one die for the goal and one die for each danger and you roll them all and you assign one die to the goal and one die to each danger, to see what happens.

      And the thing is, if the player rolls really good, they achieve their goal and avoid all the dangers. Which means all the creative stuff you just spent the last few minutes discussing is just thrown away! Okay, sure, dangers can stay on the table sometimes (many times, even, but NOT ALWAYS), and sometimes you roll really bad and they all come true, but these rolls happen a lot, and you're usually avoiding something every time. So there's a lot of dangers that you have to come up with that get avoided, and that's the problem. Because when you keep dropping stuff out that you have to expend energy and creativity coming up with, it's not only draining but it takes the focus away from what is really important: what DOES happen next.

      (Ghost/Echo is still pretty damn fun, mind you. This is like the one bad thing about it. If you don't know Ghost/Echo do a search of Story Games for threads about it. Don't just follow the link to it.)

      And then came Apocalypse World and it was very, very similar. Except that now you could wait until after the roll to invent a hard choice! And so we went back to concentrating on what does happen next and not on what could have but didn't.

      And then people started writing moves for the game, and they wrote moves with lists of options to pick from, and they wrote options that said a thing doesn't happen. Okay, so, what happens when you pick a thing from a list and you say it doesn't happen? Well, for one thing, everybody's thinking about it, now. They can't stop thinking about it, which is a distraction when they're supposed to be deciding what DOES happen next. Second, there's uncertainty as to whether the other things that you didn't pick to not happen will happen or not. Does it automatically happen because you didn't pick it not to? It is a mystery! The real answer is no, of course: what happens is the GM's choice. But on a 7-9, they aren't really supposed to make a hard move, so it's like, what to do, what to do?

      So hopefully you can see why it is more advantageous to write positive options, which state that things DO happen? When you have to pick X options that DON'T happen -- instead of the move saying "pick 1, that happens, move on," which is good and keeps things rolling along at a steady pace -- you can end up stalled. You go into the moves in order to add uncertainty, yes, but also to guide you towards certain types of outcomes, and dictating the terms of what a player can describe after the roll is part of that. You want a move to be a machine\u2014you put a fictional action into one end of it, roll some dice, and it spits out the limits that you have to abide by when describing things. Inspiration likes limitations, but things you can't do are like the edges of a blank piece of paper. Your inspiration is better off starting with a drawing of anything on that piece of paper than worrying about its size and dimensions.

      Dictating elements that must be included propels the game forward because you use them as inspiration and build off of them. Negative options are constraints that you have to struggle to get away from. They aren't constraints you can riff off of. They just drain energy and creativity over the course of a game, and this is true even if you don't have to spend the energy to invent the things that don't happen yourself, just not as bad.

      tl;dr: Write moves that produce fiction, or that influence the players to create fiction, or that spur characters into action in the fiction. Stay away from moves that shut down options without also doing one of these things.
