What Good Groups and Systems Both Do

\n I've been reading these long threads about...well, you know which ones. I read them, and I translate them into language that makes sense to me. This is how I read them:

Some people say things like "This would never happen in a decent group, so let's not worry about it. It's purely hypothetical." Other people say things like "This would never happen with a decent system, and if we had one, then we wouldn't have to worry about it." And, of course, they're both right, at least part way.

But what is it that good groups and good systems do that prevents the sorts of breakdowns we've been discussing? It finally dawned on me:

They promote the free flow of information between interested parties.

Stakes setting? Yep.
Scene framing? Yep.
Effective social negotiation processes? Yep, same thing.

So I say, when you read a chunk of game text about how a group should function, ask yourself:

Does this text promote or hinder the free flow of information between group members? In what ways?
