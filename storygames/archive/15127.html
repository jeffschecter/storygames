\n In DW, players can't say "I Hack & Slash".

The only agenda the players have is to play their characters as if they were real people. All they have to do is say what their character does from their character's perspective. If what they describe triggers a Move, then the GM calls for that Move. Otherwise the players don't have to worry about the Move mechanics, just roleplaying.

But here is my experience:

If the list of Moves is in front of a player, they will often look at the list, pick a Move, and then describe actions to try to justify the use of that move. The mechanics lead the fiction, not the fiction leading the mechanics. The fiction becomes secondary and less "what would my character do here" and more "how do I convince the GM to let me roll this Stat instead of that Stat."

So I've run DW where the players don't have the Move sheet in front of them.

And...

...it was awesome!

That 1 change made the game feel a lot richer to all those involved. I'm not saying this will be the case with all players. I know people who would grow uncomfortable not having all the mechanics in front of them. One size doesn't fit all. But so far this has become my preferred way to play.

And what's great about Dungeon World and Apocalypse World, players don't have to spend a lot of time thinking how to avoid specific Moves because of fear of failure (relative to other RPGs). The average roll on 2d6 is a 7 so on average you will succeed. Where in D&D, the difference between a skill I'm good at and I'm not good at can range between +0 and +8 or more. You rarely want to roll skills you are bad at. And there is no reward for doing so. So a lot of people spend a lot of time retrofitting fiction to optimize for success and not simply doing what makes sense for your character. DW/AW's mechanics help alleviate this problem.

    \n After the first few games we moved away from the highlighted stat mechanic.

    I posted about it here:
    http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=15096\n
    - Highlighting stats tied to gaining XP works against the immersive feel of the rest of the game.
    \n
    Immersion is definitively subjective!

    But most of the people I've played Dungeon World with (over 16 so far, many more if you count Apocalypse World) felt the same way.

    Why?

    Dungeon World expects players to play their characters as if they were real people. There is little meta thinking in the game. I can't say I "Hack & Slash" a monster. I don't reference most moves / game mechanics directly. Instead I simply say what my character does in the fiction and the GM evaluates if my roleplaying triggers a game mechanic. This adds to our immersion (which is subjective to us). It helps us feels close to our characters and the fiction.

    But trying to figure out ways to use a Stat that someone else highlighted to gain XP so you can level up breaks that mind set for us (which is again subjective and may not be the case for many other people). The players in Dungeon World only have 1 agenda. And this mechanic seems to run counter to that agenda. At least that is how it felt in play for us.

    There were also other issues with tying XP to limited stat use. Certain stats may be used more frequently in a given session. If my charisma stat is highlighted, I may spend 10 minutes roleplaying and only roll charisma once. If my strength stat is highlighted, in that same 10 minutes, I may roll strength 3+ times. Combat has hit points which means rolling multiple times to achieve a single goal. There is also the issue that some classes make frequent use of a single stat, so if 1 players has their class stat highlighted and another player doesn't, the difference in XP gained was very apparent to us. Which meant the player not gaining XP started thinking of ways to get more XP. Which often meant not playing their character as if they were a real person, but more as a puppet or avatar.

    That all said, I don't want to make this sound like a bigger deal than it is. I really enjoy playing Dungeon World!

    \n Apocalypse World says you can play both ways:

    1. A player says, "I Go Aggo" and the GM responds with "how?" If the players says how, then they can use the Go Aggro move.

    OR:

    2. A player says, "I pull my gun on Bob who's unarmed and scream for him to get out of my way or..." and the GM responds, "sounds like you are Going Aggro?"


    I've enjoyed both ways depending what a player says when the GM asks them "how?"

    If "how" ends up being "I don't care about the fiction and just want XP for rolling my hard stat... how do I justify this... " then I don't enjoy #1. Yet I've played Dungeon World with the same players with no move sheet and "how" was never a problem (system matters). But I also have played with people who don't feel comfortable with #2.

    \nBut I think the great thing about role-playing games (and especially something like AW or DW) is that unlike, say, a boardgame, and certainly unlike a video game, the possibility space defined by the rule system is not comprehensive. The rules define only a small part of it. In Dungeon World, you can and should have a conversation between characters, wander from room to room, decide to avenge your brother's death, make your rogue do a little jig after a successful kill, or have your paladin heartily clap his fellow party-members on the back.

    These non-move actions are a key part of the universe of possiblity in DW, but the moves list can unintentionally make them secondary. I don't think playing without a moves list is sustainable across a whole campaign, but I do think it's an interesting and potentially very effective way of getting players into the spirit of the fiction.

    \n When teaching new players, sometimes the Move list confused things, sometimes it made everything clearer. Some players felt liberated by the Move list, other players stopped thinking about the fiction and focused mainly on the mechanics (which I like in other games but in DW, you lead with the fiction). It depended on who the new players were.

    \nI've also had success using a mixed technique. I'll sometimes give players a Move reference that only lists the fictional triggers of the default mechanical Moves\u2026
    \n
    \nIf you don't know what to do, pick one of the following and describe how your character does it:
    \n\u2022 Help someone you have a Bond with
    \n\u2022 Hinder someone you have a Bond with
    \n\u2022 Closely study a situation or person
    \n\u2022 Consult your accumulated knowledge about something
    \n\u2022 Stand in defense of a person, item, or location under attack
    \n\u2022 Try to get someone to do what you want (you must have something they need or want)
    \n\u2022 Take aim and shoot at an enemy at range
    \n\u2022 Attack an enemy close to you
    \n\u2022 Act despite an imminent threat
    \n\u2022 Make camp and rest
    \n\u2022 Throw a party (you must have just returned from a triumphant adventure)
    \n
    \nI've fine-tuned the order above based on actual play. Things that people don't think to do but can do frequently in a wide array of situations goes towards the top.
    \n
    \nUltimately what game you choose to play and how you choose to play it will depend on who you play with and what experience you want.

    \nThis way, it always pays to play to your Bonds, because even on a miss, it's possible to increase your Bond to +4 or higher (or lower it to -4 or lower), giving you an XP anyway. That's all I was really looking for. So now we always have a strong reason to look over our Bonds to see if they can reasonably be included in our action (and without the wonkiness of giving someone else XP).
