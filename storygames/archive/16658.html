\n All the theory and ideas I've been reading here and on other forums/games have finally taken my ideas for character design to this:

An RPG character can be reduced to:
-the options it gives the players to interact with the fiction.
-the character's reasons to interact with the setting in a particular way.

In that light, the stats of the character, skills, equipment, moves, special powers, feats, aspects, etc. are but options the player can mix to inflict a particular change/effect or avoid an specific change of the fiction.
Of course, these options would have different levels of impact on the setting depending on their nature and the circumstances in which they are applied.

I'm using this as a base for a game that, as a side effect, can be used to hack (at least some) other (trad) games. You could list all the options one game gives to a particular kind of character and determine the level of impact each option has on the setting. I'm pretty sure it could even help designers find a more proper balance between classes.

There's a couple of points about this theory I haven't touched yet but I can elaborate if you ask. But so far I've got a few questions regarding this:

-Do you know of any game/mechanic/philosophy/theory similar, older, better, already tried, tested and dropped?
-Does it sound totally off base for some reason?
-Is there something really important I'm missing?

    \n Well, this is pretty good dramatic thinking. It's how you put together a script, according to the how-to-put-together-a-script books I've been reading.
    \nUsing formula is a pretty good model for putting a game structure together. I love mixing genre formulas with game mechanics.
    \n
    \nI do not know of any game mechanics that have been tried-tested-and-dropped in this regard because so few game mechanics are dropped at all in this community. If there's a way to make it work, someone's probably made a game using it as a mechanic.
    \n
    \nThe only place you're off base is in thinking that you need to list all the different options as anything other than suggestions or guidelines - cards to put into a slot.
    \nUnless you want to make each suggestion a card with special powers and its own method of modifying the rules, thus creating a CCG story game, which is fine by me if you got the money to put art on the cards.
    \n
    \nAlso, worrying about balancing classes is kind of a moot point if you go down the road you seem to be suggesting, here.
    \n
    \nIf you focus on the formula of, say, a good action-adventure movie, then all the stats, the equipment, etc. become window-dressing or, at best, resources in the game's economy. The limitation of what a character can have no longer becomes class (their background) but what stage of the storyline they've reached (which you can log by making Motivation more dynamic.) Since a change to motivation is the simplest and easiest-to-recognize change to a character (and change to a character is a hallmark of a satisfying story) then what you want to do is examine how motivations change at different points in the act structure of the kind of story you want to emulate.
    \n
    \nLet's do action-adventure here as an example, again. Heroes in these things tend to go through three distinct motivational phases: Reluctance, Impotence and Awakening - though Impotence may be a bad word to use there.
    \n
    \nReluctant characters usually have more protections than other characters and a lot of unfilled slots, with a high potential for adding more cool stuff as they go along.
    \nImpotent characters have reached the point where they are facing impossible odds, have just been smacked down (brought to zero HP?) or otherwise seem unable to go any further. They tend to have very low defense and unpredictable offense. They are also usually in a position where they will have to expend a considerable amount of resources to turn things around.
    \nLastly, during awakening, characters have awesome attack skills and maybe moderate defense skills (this is the best time for a heroic character to die.)
    \n
    \nThat help?

    Anyway, let me add another layer of complexity to the theory, using inspiration from AW basic moves:


    An RPG character can be reduced to:

    -the character's reasons to interact with the setting in a particular way.
    -the options it gives the players to interact with the fiction. There are 7 types of options:

    ATTACK options. Anything that deals with harming or destroying fictional elements goes here.
    PROTECT options. Anything meant to protect the status quo of an element of the fiction goes here.
    MOVEMENT options. Anything dealing with changing the fictional location of the character and obtaining some sort of advantage from it goes here.
    DISCOVERY options. Anything that adds information to the fiction goes here.
    SOCIAL options. Anything that deals with the PC obtaining any advantage from social interaction goes here.
    AID / INTERFERE - here goes anything that witouth having a direct effect on any element in the fiction, imposes advantages or disadvantages to the rest of the options of the player and/or other characters.
    RESOURCE CREATING options. - finally, these deal with anything that give the PC new options under determined circunstances. Money to buy equipment (needs the vecinity of a store) skills for crafting (needs the presence of materials, time, tools, etc) xp used to learn new skills, etc.

    Of course, there are a lot of options that are a mix of two or more. Empathy as a skill is a SOCIAL option used for DISCOVERY of new information. When empathy is used to gain an ally, it becomes a resource creating option. Invisibility is a PROTECT option that can be used as an AID to gain advantage over an opponent. But somehow I still can't think of something that could be completely out from these types of options (hey let me know if you can see something I'm missing here)

    So, to know the full range of options an specific character has available, list them all under these categories, repeating the options that can be used in more than one category (like, say, the "Channel Energy" feature from Pathfinder's Cleric, which can both heal and harm undead) determine the level of impact it has on the setting on a 1-10 scale and then you'll have a more accurate way to determine if an specific character is balanced. Well, at least that's the idea. What do you think?

    \nUhh... I used the less generalized categories to allow some tactical choices for the player and assign one size of dice to each category. So, heartbraker-wise, you could have a better balance between a monk and a fighter, a fighter and a wizard, etc. without leaving one of them with less-powerful, less interesting or with less options. This could also make ALL skills usable in different situations, allowing to mix two different kind of actions.

    Say, you have a fighter with a d20 on ATTACK and a d4 in SOCIAL. In a battle he could mix the two stating that he taunts the enemy with whatever one-liner comes to the players head. In a -certain- social situation he could roll the two dice again to intimidate another character. It still doesn't make the bard and fighter the same (on all mixes that have SOCIAL options the bard with have advantage, while only in the specific case of ATTACK+SOCIAL the fighter will have some edge)

    About MOVEMENT, it could include options like Horse, Stealth, Teleporting, Acrobatics, (or a stealthy acrobatic teleporting horse if you want) etc. so I'm not sure POSITIONING describes enough what kind of options go here (but correct me if I'm wrong, my english isn`t too good)

    Now, PROTECT gave me some doubts. When it represents protecting others it's always an action, but when it represents a resource that only protects you, like an armor, is more of a passive resource, therefore it doesn't make much sense to roll for defense with it. Perhaps just in that case there wouldn't be a dice but an static number between 2 and 15 that would only decrease when the armor is damaged by an attack that beats it's protection. Oh, protect would also include magical healing, limited quick regeneration, etc. But in case you're mixing PROTECT and MOVEMENT you could state your character trusts his armor and runs trough a group of enemies. Then you just roll the MOVEMENT dice and keep the higher result (either the movement roll or the Armor stat) and compare to the enemy attack roll to determine the result. Perhaps it could still be included into the aid/interfere category so thanks a lot for your input!

    \n Yes, I took a few things from Dogs in the Vineyard, except that I simplified it first by using just one dice for each type of action, and added a bit of tactical complexity by allowing players two actions on their turn.

    So in this system you would be using 1d20 to shoot with your "big, excellent shotgun" while running across the room with your 1d12 "wolf steps". And if you have "learned how to shoot from the best" associated to your attack, roleplay it into your descrption and you could roll 2d20 instead of one. That makes 2d20 and 1d12. Take the highest and compare to the difficulty or the opponent highest dice. If you pass, you succeed at both. If you tie, you succeed at one, if you lose, you can't do neither.

    I'm also using stakes from DitV, but in a different way. It's not up to the active player to state what's at risk. Instead, any of the rest of the players can pay one token and call for a "Hunch", stating what's the worst or best that could happen, before the player's roll.

    In the previous example, When the player is about to roll, another player pays one token and says, still in character: "come on, don't trip, don't trip, you can make it..."
    If the roll fails, the player who called for a hunch earns one token, However the player who failed the roll can still pay one token to state that his character trips but somehow manages to roll behind a barrel...
