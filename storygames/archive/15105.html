\nWizards of the Coast rehires Monte Cook! Let speculations of D&D 5th Edition run wild (although my money is on a version of the game that spans all editions). \n
Starting next week, I'm turning this column over to acclaimed game designer Monte Cook. Monte should be familiar to D&D fans for his work on the Planescape campaign setting, Return to the Temple of Elemental Evil, Arcana Unearthed, the mammoth Ptolus city sourcebook, and, of course, Dungeons & Dragons 3rd Edition.

I've spent the past several months talking about D&D's past and how that relates to its future. It's now time to focus much more on the future of the game. Monte has an unmatched design pedigree in the RPG field, and for that reason we've brought him on board to work with R&D in making D&D the greatest RPG the world has seen. Over the next few weeks, Monte will use this column to share his thoughts about the game. As we look to chart D&D's future course, this column will continue to be a place where we share our ideas and listen to yours, and we hope you'll keep reading, discussing the contents, and sharing your feedback and thoughts with us and the larger D&D community. That's what makes Legends & Lore a conversation that we can all participate in.

      \n D&D is a brand. Being a brand means it must fit certain parameters, to be reproducible and marketable. So yeah, maybe we're all bored of the standard goblins and orcs, but that's basically part of the product identity. It's like McDonalds removing the cheeseburger from the menu. The creativity, sense of wonder and the fantastic is (and maybe always was) in the hands of the DIY/homebrew/OSR community.

      So, yes, when you say "roleplaying" most people will think "D&D" (and vice versa) in the way "fantasy" makes people think "Tolkien", and I don't see that changing. However, if you don't want orcs and goblins, you have to look elsewhere. I wouldn't say this is WotC's fault, no more than it's Tolkien's fault for being Tolkien. You want something else you're going to pick up Mieville, Harrison, Vance, Martin. Can't blame Tolkien for not being Howard. Can't blame D&D for not being Vornheim.

      I guess at the end of the day there isn't anything really stopping WotC from going out there and creating something new and different. But they've got established settings (FR, Eberron...), established parameters, product identity, brand name, overarching product aesthetic/art direction. They're comfortable with that.

      There's also the system bit. I think it was Rob Donoghue who made me aware of it. The way the system is set up, 4E supports the action genre, as opposed to older editions which supported the adventure genre. (The in-between editions aren't really sure which way they want to lean.) That and the overly encounter-based, linear-adventure design really drives against the "sense of place, situation, environment". But I think location-based adventures are making a comeback. 4E itself is being tweaked in a way that opens it up to a more "classic" way of playing.

      It is interesting how the modules, their art, their setup really influence the perception of the game. I think one of the reasons why we even have modern D&D in the shape that we have is because most modules published back in the day were really tournament modules and later metaplot modules. Unlike Caverns of Thracia or Keep on the Borderlands, which basically showed you how to set up and run a sandbox, open-ended, non-linear campaign, GMs were educated to run a breadcrumb-trail game with escalating encounters punctured by exposition pieces. Even if the people running the company tell you not to plan, to go along with the players, etc. the adventures they publish are the complete opposite. And those adventures have over the past 20 years bred a culture of play that is very much embedded in many of 4E and Pathfinder's design assumptions.

      Same for settings. For every high fantasy dragonlance plot, for every 1st level module with kobolds and goblins, for every re-hash of the central conceit, there was one Barrier Peaks spaceship less, one feverish hallucinatory dungeon less. The "moral clean-up" of 2nd edition that removed demons and devils also severed ties to the weird, the kind of fantastic fiction where (in Brad Murray's words) "there's not even an effort to find a distinction between fantasy and science fiction". It was a move to a specific, genre-ified, commercially-accepted fantasy. There's a reason why if you go to your local bookstore and look at the shelf marked "Fantasy" it's going to be full of books with swords, dragons, orcs and dwarves, even if there is not literary, historical or imaginary precedent for it (there are some good pieces on this by various people out there but I can't find them right now because I'm in a rush).

      \n The results of random tables still need to be interpreted. So like if the DM rolls up an arrow trap on the fly, he still has to decide how that actually works in the fiction before communicating that information to the players. If you have a string sense of your agenda and principles, it's easier to integrate random ideas into your presentation of a consistent setting without having to actually do the work beforehand. So you are discovering things along with the players.

      If you're playing strict tournament style, you have to invent everything beforehand and you can only invent details in play, the amount of actual "discovery" you do (not counting discovering what players do in the dungeon) is minimal unless the players consistently ask for highly detailed descriptions. The advantage to having modules is that you can have a different set of setting-based agendas and principles for each module (different modules, different writers). The disadvantage is what you find in, say, Carcosa: the setting assumptions that James is posting about on his blog are not made clear in Geoffrey's original document, so if the DM doesn't interpret the raw data correctly, it won't get used as it was intended.

      (To stay on-topic) Monte's passive perception by rank would be a concrete setting-based agenda/principle once certain types of traps are ranked. So, if only grandmaster trap detectors can find magical traps, that's a principle that says something concrete about the setting in a way that is much more easily understood by the part of the brain that understands narrative* than the more Gygaxian principle of say, magical traps are found 30% of the time by grandmaster trap detectors, 20% of the time by experts, and only 5% of the time by novices (or whatever). You would still need to interpret the result: why was this trap found / not found?

      (* Another example, perhaps somewhat tangental: In the Iliad, Achilles is the greatest warrior of the Greeks. He wins every fight, every contest, no questions, no rolls. Ajax is the second-greatest of the Greeks, he always comes in second, always at everything ever. Hector is the greatest warrior of the Trojans, and he is exactly equal to Ajax, neither of them can win against the other. This is invariable, set in stone, a principle of the setting that any game set in the Iliad would have to come to terms with. If one player is Achilles and the other is Hector and they fight, you don't roll dice to see who wins, because that's not the Iliad. You would roll dice to see how Achilles wins, and how Hector dies.)
