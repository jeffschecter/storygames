    \n Jim, I think that Dogs benefits greatly from the situation being: You are all given the same information, and you are responsible for interpreting and judging it.

    Dogs could keep things from each other... I had a Dog who was half Mountain Folk and desperate to conceal the fact.

    I just don't know that mandating or making more prominant secret-keeping would help the central and important dynamics at all. And... yes, you could introduce secrets as an interesting background, sideline, avenue, etc... but I don't see it benefiting dogs directly.

    Another way of putting it: I think secrets and hidden fates would work as well in Dogs as in other games about group dynamic and judgment... fine enough, but there's no immediately seen benefit to adding them.

    \n I'm in portland right now (visiting friends, etc.) and I just ran dogs for the proto-typical D&D group.

    It was, to say the least, interesting.

    None of them had secrets, but they did find themselves in situations where more depth of environment and less "rushing to resolution" would have helped. My opinion, is that D&D trains people to solve problems, quickly and without moral ambiguity. I think my town had 8 extremely complicated complications, and the PCs found the need to chase everything down and hit it with a hammer in that, "rush to initiative" sort of way that games like Shadowrun have trained people to play.

    It was exactly what you'd expect from gamers who are used to killing orcs with magical fire.

    They did however have a great time and two of the players would certainly be suited for playing again. But it's funny to me how hard it was for them get out of their chairs and author their own games without burning down the entire town.

    Sigh. I forget what my point was now. I'll post an AP soon on a new thread, since this went no-where.
