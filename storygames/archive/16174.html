\n I've been working on a GMfull game in the last years. One of the troubles I found with my group was that being most of them unexperienced GMs, there was a sort of creative block syndrome when it came to framing interesting scenes. On top of that I also wanted to play and I wanted the game to surprise me with interesting challenges. Oh, and I didn't wanted to prep, I'm still going for that lazy goal.

So I started making random tables to frame as much different situations I could came up with, but then it became cumbersome to roll a lot of dice and look up in the tables to create something. It took too much game time just to frame one part of the scene.

Then I stumbled upon the whimsy cards and hacked a game out of them that did quite good a part of what I was looking for, though players still had if not a complete block, at least they needed some extra time to frame the scenes, in the worst case.

So I ended up creating this:



Sorry, it's in spanish, but let me explain what you're seeing here.

This is a sample of a deck of 60 cards. On each one are 7 fields of information. Whenever you draw a card, you only choose one of those 7 options to frame the main element of the scene. This deck has been made with a fantasy setting in mind, so you would need to change some words to fit other settings better.

The information on each card is this:
-Top: things that could be find up the road (a place, monster, NPC or an object)
-Bottom: emotions and other mental features
-Sides: physical characteristics
-Diagonal: phrases from the whimsy cards
-spaces between: elements (for magic) and tactical advantages (position, initiative, terrain, speed, etc)

Basically it works like this:
1-Draw three cards. Advanced rules can use more or less, but for the example 3 would be enough.
2-Choose one of the 7 options in each card
3-Make sense out of your choice.

For example:

translates as a "bright illusory place" Which can be a blinding Temple of Ilusions, that turns into a never ending maze for the usual unexpected visitor. Or just a place with a bright illusion if you're not specially inspired that day. Which still can be kind of interesting, depending on how the player interpretates it (you can always do the witch mountain trick on them)

Also this:

can be translated as a "small elemental monster" which can be a magma jelly, a young phoenix, a salamander (hey, it can be a whole bunch of them running through your campfire in stampede) so you see, the possibilities are endless. And I still haven't used the whimsy cards part yet.

I would llike to dare you find what's terribly wrong here with this randomizer, what kind of menace it represents to RPGs in general, and of course, I would like to know how may before me came with a similar idea any why I haven't heard of them yet.

T\xc3\xa4hti doesn't count, I designed this before knowing of it and the feeling and philosophy of design is completely different. I also readed an article of a similar game based on a variant of tarot cards, but again, the feeling is different since the interpretation in both games mentioned is more open.

    I never thought about a Kickstarter for these... well, I better do it now. These cards come with a game where:

    -All players create the world together answering half a dozen questions and adding at least one element each (but you can add more if you want)
    -You start creating your character stating what do you want her story to be about. If you get enough plot points and don't sacrifice the most important things for you character liberally, you get what you wanted in the first place. But even if you don't, things can resolve in an interesting way.
    -Characters are built on a system loosely based on a mix between Fate, DitV and AW, with only 5 stats (hacked from the basic AW moves) that you fill with either common human abilities, skills, items, special powers, etc. etc. a la DitV. So the system can handle basically anything you want in a more or less balanced way.
    -Anything you put on your character sheet can become a part of a challenge, instead of a card. So you can have an scene where the challenge is that your main power fails or your sword breakes... in the middle of a fight.
    -3 card challenges are but the first level. It escalates usually one card at a time each turn. However other players can complicate the challenge by adding one card from their own hand.

    BTW Peter, in playtest I found it still takes players about 10 minutes for the less inspired/more tired ones to find a good/viable connection of 3 random words. You can also hold your last card until the player states her first reaction and rolls the dice, to add a complication to the challenge. On top of that, you can always rely on the group to help you complete the scene and bring it to life.

    \n I'm also thinking into adding a few more cards, specifically Rules Cards. These ones empower a player to apply a new specific rule, to his character or when framing an scene for other player(s). In the playtests I've seen how important is to keep the pace of the game changing from time to time.

    You can start by letting a player state where is her character and what is doing and then use the cards to add a conflict in that scene. Then you can keep adding challenges and have the players just react. But after a while you'll find is neccesary to shift the gears. You can stage an event that happens everywhere in the setting and have each PC face it alone. You can interrupt the game to let the players take the lead and frame an scene where they get one step closer to their goal, or where they get to state what's really going on and roll to see if their hypothesis is true.

    I was thinking from the beggining of several ways to allow players change the pace of the narration. I don't thing this would compensate the absence of a GM but could help less experienced GMs to introduce variations to keep the story interesting.

    \n I'm doing the translatons directly on the file where I'm designing the cards, so it's still coming slowly. I'm still translating the phrases in the middle of the cards, it's coming kind of slow yet. However my pals here had this couple crazy ideas for two settings:

    First one is loosely based on Pandorum with the twist that on their turn players PCs are the repairing drones of the ship. While they have a humanoid body, they are fitted with the proper tools for their specific work, which can be security, hardware fixing, software fixing, medic, etc. On other players turn, they play the part of one of the main AI of the ship: communications, engineering, navigation, life support, security, etc. They get to feed the active droid with data and give them tasks, which some times could be conflctive and it's left to the individual AIs of the drones to decide relevance of the tasks.

    A.I.s start with three cards to frame situations for the active droid. However randomly, one or more of these cards could be corrupted data or a virus, meaning the AI could become unresponsive at the worse moment or going nuts and following the directive overwritten by the virus. At the end of the turn of all droids, all cards are reshuffled, meaning AIs exchange data. 3 more cards of new data are then added to the shared database which is again split among all AIs. This leaves players to start getting paranoid about which AI is infected now, or if they are infected at all, since it could happen that the virus cards won't appear until later in the game.

    Droids will even get the chance to shut off one of the AIs on their turn, freezing the data flow in an attemp to stop the virus actions. But this won't happen without consequences...

    \n Many thanks Joseph! I'll be back working on the layout tomorrow. However it's not like I've been doing nothing, I've been adjusting the character sheet (which is also the GM screen) and designing and working on a horror version of the game.

    The first draft of the horror game used a similar deck to throw everyday problems to the players. Well, everyday problems with something really strange in them. Like an argument with your fiancee because she borrowed you her car and now the inside looks like a couple of huge racoons teared it apart.

    In the first part of the game, the players task is to find an explanation for these strange events. One that doesn't involve creatures from beyond our realm because, you know, even you would doubt of your sanity if you have to admit that. Of course, the more you fail to find good explanations, the more you believe in something and start giving shape and powers to it. And the more stress you accumulate, which in turn makes your reactions more and more extreme. Until the point where the thing is so real for you that it starts showing itself and attacking you and the things most dear to you, corrupting, destroying or even just making you push them away from you, which is what the second part of the game is about.

    Initial testing went quite good for the first part, but we have to work more on the mechanics and word lists for the second part of the game. Anyway I'll put it aside for a while and get the original game finished. More on this later!

    \n I'm working on some procedures to use this deck now. Our group is interested in playing Pathfinder, but nobody wants to GM full time, so I came up with this:

    1) We determine the place drawing 2 cards for descriptors and one for element. That could get us for example: empty, inaccesible, nature. So I went up for "The trail ends in a deep part of the jungle, where trees and bushes grow too thick for us to keep going. We may need to find some other way to cross this part of the jungle." - Notice that I'm not stating any inmediate danger yet, not because of the "empty" card, but to prepare the scene better.

    2) Then all players take a round stating "observations" This means they select one skill from their characters, roll fudge dice, and add one element to the scene according to the roll and skill selected. A "+" means their character spots something that could be useful, related with the skill they choosed. A "-" means they just found out one clue of something dangerous in the place, again, only related to the chosen skill. Still, since they spot it they can actually avoid it. And for each empty face they get to add1 to the CR of the next challenge, but what's the challenge gets defined only in the next step.

    3) Draw 3 more cards. The red letters on the first one indicate what's the danger here: a monster, an object (either there or brought by the PCs) an NPC or the place itself. The other two help to describe it. You can instead use the same descriptors you got on the first set of cards, to keep the challenge on the same theme with the place created.

    4) If the challenge is an NPC (or a group of them) one more card is drawn to set an emotion for these characters, if needed. Of course, there are more emotions than angry or hate among the deck, so the problem isn't always a fight; the group might find a tribe mourning one of their own so now the challenge may become a social one.

    5) Players can always add elements of their own background into the scene when they add their observations: their nemesis could be involved in this situation, one of their relatives could be in danger, etc, etc.

    Well, the "Object" card is still giving me some trouble. It could be a cursed altar, a magical weapon someone dropped or lost, a bridge about to collapse... but some of these could be related to challenges created with "the Place" card, so I'm wondering if I should delete that option from the deck or if I should be adding others. Remember: these are the only substantives on the deck, so all challenges are related to this. Is there any kind of challenge you would like to see this deck framing?

    \n Still toying with this. It almost turned it into a CCG with a complex mechanic, but then after a playtest I found that this wasn't the game I wanted to make, so I took a step back into a few previous stages of design. The current version of the game is again intended to be an RPG for newbies, either they are GMs or players. It must be easy to understand, require no prep but benefit from a heavy prep in the same way a trad RPG does.

    The GM deck allows the GM to randomly frame scenes with three key ingredients: a situation, a challenge and a mood/emotion. Two adjectives can be applied also randomly to further define the scene. Then a single 2d6 roll provides for the challenge level. Players roll against this number a single 1d6 and add +1 for each card they have that helps them overcome the conflict. They reduce the challenge levelby 1 each time they roll over it or lose a resource every time they don't.

    So players can try and push their luck to overcome the challenge and get a xp reward equal to the level of the challenge. They can cash it in the act for more cards and/or a single step towards their objective, which they get to create during the game. Players randomly get a card with a single word on it to define their objective, but they get to define and state it along the game. Whoever advances more steps to their objective is rewarded with levels, that allow him to make more complex moves, but also rises the minimum challenge level they face. Game progresses that way until players reach level 6, when they get to face the final challenge and fulfill their destiny or fall in the intent.

    There are a few more mechanics I'd love to use but I also need to simplify the game. Most of the rules have to be in the cards or it won't work the way I'm planning. I also expect that the way I'm building it will allow players to hack it and make cards of their own.

      \n Except for adjusting the colors in the back, the new version is done, so I'm moving onto the design of the character sheet and system fo the game. I'm going with an idea I'm not yet sure how will work on play. The core stats are 6 ways to solve problems: By instinct, with violence, Ignoring it, Paying attention, Calmly and With knowledge, which are always played in this initiative order. Each way of solving problems gets a bonus from 1 to 3 and the same number of characteristics linked to it.

      Notice that some ways of solving problems are opposite, it's on purpose: Players have to put the same number of bonuses as penalties for the opposite way of solving problems. So let's say your character mostly solves problems with violence, at a +3 she will probably find it easier to resort to violence to solve anything. Let's link it with the characteristics "strong", "agile" and "cunning" to make her a fearsome fighter; these characteristics now explain why she tends to draw a weapon whenever there's problems around. However this will also mean she has problems whenever she has to face problems Calmly. You'll have to put a -3 to Calmly and link it to 3 defects like "stubborn" "quick-tempered" and "happy trigger". Of course these will only give you trouble whenever your character needs to solve something calmly.

      So, more than describing what your character can do, this system is dedicated to help players describe how their character actually accomplishes or fails at something.

      There will also be skills and items, but these won't give exactly bonuses or penalties. Instead they allow the character to do something or not, and work as Reign's "master" and "expert" dice for the most trained skills.

      The other part of the character that gives out bonuses is her Humanity, a collection of "Fate"-like Aspects involving relationships, fame, objectives, memories, enemies and everything that represents a passion for the character, all reasons why she gets in trouble. Certain magical effects in the game may stole memories from the character, which will mean these Aspects may get erased in play. Others will be solved, reached, broken and be forgotten, while new ones can appear, be forged or cultivated.

      \n First playtest last night. I still can't believe it worked, and it worked in a totally different way than I thought. I was expecting the game to create an ordinary but familiar setting with a few interesting turns, like your usual medieval fantasy game. But suddenly the players ended up with very unique characters that wouldn't fit as adventurers in your usual game...

      Besilofeno (player actually loved this randomly created name and snatched it from another player) was a son of a soldier who decided he actually wanted to be an actor and tinkerer. Dog (player refused to use the random generator, which is fine since it means they have a more defined idea of the character they want) was a beggar, son of a beggar and descendant from a great warrior clan fallen in disgrace, who only kept his honor but not the fighting instinct of his ancestors. And Swatko was the son of an artisan with dreams of becoming a soldier of fortune.

      So I went with the idea that small characters fit better in a small story and created a relationship tree for a small town, added a corrupt religious cult as a front and kept adding NPCs and relationships among them and the players. And as a Mc guffin, the presence of a fat but nice bard who handed some magical tickets to the PCs, that were supposed to grant wishes.

      A lot more parts of the story strangely came from the excuses the PCs had for their equipment, most of which came from the cards. The beggar had a pig, some milk from the animal and a rusty scimitar. He came up with the idea that he saved a merc from death and he was given the weapon in gratitude. I inmediatly linked that to the corrupt cult by adding a giant merc with a horned helm and bad attitude, who had beaten Susan, a nomad caravan guard and left her for his sidekick to dispose. The beggar saw this and killed the merc's sidekick, saving Susan.

      Well, making it short, the good things were:
      - The Deck works! It kept the story flowing and added interesting and meaningful details; all I had to do was follow up, write things down and connect them later. Players noticed how easy their raw ideas were accepted, introduced in the story, completed with further detail and returned to kick them in the ass when least expected.
      - A relationship tree is the best tool to use with the deck, it's the easier and fastest way to keep track of everything and surprise the players with logical twists in the story. It also helps a lot to make the PCs more involved with the situation.
      -Players started separated and had one scene each, but the story helped to bring them together.
      -It's good to know exactly what kind of setting the game produces, now I can add things that fit better in it and finally have an idea of the art style the game should have.

      Things that need fixing:
      -Character creation took 30 minutes but it should take less time. I'm simplifying some stats and procedures, and including more of that info in the character sheet so players can do it on their own.
      -Character objectives need to be more urgent and clear. Perhaps I'll modify that list of the deck just for this.
      -Magic is random, though not exactly in the way I wanted. But that's something I need to fix in the setting, not in the cards.
      -The skills need to be more clearly related to the core stats, which are being reduced to 4. Virtues will probably dissapear, since they saw no use in the game. Defects worked better and players collected rewards as soon as they mentioned them.

      \n Still struggling with this. There are a few advances though:

      -Elements worked quite well, but it took too much space on the card, I'm reducing them to small icons in one corner
      -I want to introduce Themes as the main image of one side of the card, replacing elements. Mix anything with a theme and perhaps an emotion and you have a powerful clear story element. Previously things like adjectives, elements and events had suggested these, and many themes were left out of the deck. So why use three lists when themes could be all put in one, using images to give them a wider range of interpretations? After all, this is a lot of what makes Tarot cards so attractive.
      -After printing and using a bit the Tarokka Deck and seeing images from the Dragon Age Inquisition Tarot I'd also like to add Archetypes. That is, images of characters that suggest NPC ideas by themselves. At least archetypes allows me to drop the Class icons I was thinking of.
      -Using Items instead of Weapons, and making these into icons too. You can then roll a die to determine quality, ranging from cursed to magical This reduces two lists to a single one.
      - I've got a list of magic rules for freeform rituals that have made wonders for my campaign, I think I can cram it at the bottom of the card.
      -Will ditch random list of encounter types, hasn't been too useful.
      -Decided to add a proper "monster tactics" random table. Well, perhaps it can be used by NPCs or even PCs. I'm organizing it by strongest stat and even by weakest stat, though there are things that probably any type of monster can use. What I've got so far is this:

      STRENGTH
      - charges / runs over / tackles
      - throws opponents, obstacles, heavy objects, wrecks location
      - uses a heavy shield and/or armor. Shield bash.
      - grapples / chokes
      - big size, slam attack
      - uses heavy weapon, greater damage and/or armor damage
      - uses oversized weapon for greater reach and area attacks
      - can carry more equipment or another creature

      DEXTEROUS
      - precise attacks
      - ranged attacks
      - stealthy
      - can deflect/catch projectiles
      - flexible, can pass trough narrow spaces
      - acrobatic
      - boomerang attack
      - ricochet projectiles
      - spinning attack

      FAST
      - dash
      - multiple attacks or actions
      - great mobility
      - can dodge almost anything
      - can swim or fly
      - hit & run tactics
      - can appear anywhere
      - jumps through shadows, plants, etc.

      RESISTANT
      - big size, can soak tons of damage
      - immune to certain types of damage
      - resistance to certain type of damage
      - can regenerate / heal itself a limited amount of times
      - vampiric
      - natural armour
      - can ignore a certain amount of damage a limited amount of times

      INTELLIGENT
      - uses complex items or spells
      - uses illusions
      - uses the terrain or changes it to get advantage
      - may see through opponent's tactics
      - sends others to do the dirty work
      - won't show up until he feels safe/victorious
      - sets ambushes, traps and decoys
      - attacks when opponents are most vulnerable
      - separates their enemies, targets their resources
      - is prepared for its opponents
      - persistent and patient
      - cautious

      PERCEPTIVE
      - heightened sense(s)
      - darkvision, blindsense, tremorsense, echolocalization
      - true vision
      - telepath, sixth sense, can sense fear or anger
      - detects life, magic, traps, lies or an specific materal
      - can see/sense through obstacles or great distance, tracker

      CHARISMATIC
      - fast talker, confuses opponents
      - deceives, seduces, persuades or manipulates allies and foes against each other
      - manipulates masses of people
      - Hypnotic eyes, voice or pattern
      - earns their opponent's trust before backstabbing them
      - shapeshifts or disguises
      - deceives victims into thinking it's harmless

      WEAK
      - incorporeal
      - turns into smoke or liquid
      - pack tactics
      - uses cover / hides
      - is guarded by another creature(s)
      - buffs up itself for combat
      - relies on speed or brains instead
      - blinks or teleports away
      - supports other attackers
      - uses a single spell-like ability

      SLOW
      - remains static, uses camouflage
      - uses area and/or range attacks
      - lives in groups of several individuals
      - it's dangerous to stay in their territory
      - moves through / attacks from unsuspected places
      - paralyzes / attaches itself to victims
      - entangles victims
      - affects enviroment or uses attack to pull victims to itself
      - hard shell or carapace
      - telekinetic
      - multiplies itself if cut

      ANY

      - inflicts conditions
      - elemental damage with aura, area, breath, ray, touch, etc.
      - corrodes / destroys equipment
      - intimidating
      - aura of terror or insanity
      - natural weapons like fangs, claws, tail, horns, spikes, etc
      - projects some form of barrier
      - affects weather / visibility
      - suicidal
      - once opponent is weak enough it can steal their soul
      - turns victims into monsters
      - corrupts their surroundings
      - can call allies

      - no door or wall can stop it
      - harmful contact
      - parasitic

      - keeps victims alive for a while
      - invisible or inaudible
      - it's disgusting/revolting to fight against
      - resists or its invulnerable to conditions that make sense for it's type or shape
      - attacks the weakest first

      \n Ok Kenny, this is how it looks now thanks to you and to my boss at work, who caught me making these and decided to meddle suggesting a different color palette, instead of well firing me. It's not like there was anything else to do so it was better that I was practicing my design skills instead of doing nothing. Also, thanks a lot to Selene for suggesting the icons, it saved me many months of work and a good amount of space on each card.



      As usual, the cards above show the front, the cards below show the back. All images in the cards were picked from somewhere on the web, so obviously this version is just for personal use. After testing I'll see if I have enough time to make original pieces for all the cards (maaan that's gonna be an awful lot of work...) and translate it to english.

      So how the thing works now? color codes and arrows help a bit now to pair things up, like in this pic (sorry for the crappy quality of my cam)

      see where the corners of the cards touch at the center of the pic? Those are supposed to be the icons for fire on the top, a corridor turn to the left and an stairway up on the right, which make a great idea for a dungeon scene: the next room in the dungeon is a lit up corridor that turns to the left (or the right if that doesn't make sense on your map) and ends on a stairway up. You can read these things on a different way of course, after all this deck is just about suggesting ideas to the user.

      This next pic is also about places, though more of an outdoor thing:

      Here the corners can be easily connected by color to suggest at night, a place with fire as a predominant element and a thunderstorm for weather effect. My first idea was a volcano at night, under a thunderstorm. Dunno why didn't thought of this scenery before!

      Red sides are meant to be connected to create traps by suggesting a trap effect and a trigger, which makes up for something quite interesting to roleplay through instead of rolling dice to spot and another to deactivate. If you can deduct the nature of the trigger or the trap there are things you can do to avoid the trap without rolling the dice, or perhaps the players may never know about it nor activate it, since it's meant to activate whenever someone carrying metal objects cross this place and they are all druids.


      Here the trap is a rock cube powered by a piston that comes from the ceiling, crushing whoever dared to step into the area carrying a magnetic metal object. I'd say there would be giveaway signs like the metal pulling to the ground a bit or and hydraulic like sound right before it moves, to give the players a chance to roleplay through it.

      Also, there are items now! Draw one and roll a d6 to find out if the item is either cursed, barely usable, masterwork, or anything between +1 and +3


      It makes monsters too. The icon over the blue background says what type or shape has the creature (an extraplanar in this case) the text in the blue bubble describes the deal with this creature (this one is both perceptive -probably using echolocalization- and inmune to one type of damage.) You can pile up as many tricks as you like of course. The bigger ones on the bottom are adjectives which state that it looks colorful and reinforced, which probably makes it an extraplanar blind. Throw an element or a pic for theme in the mix to get an idea of which other abilities the creature could have, and random items for loot or equipment mantis shrimp from another dimension.


      Want NPCs? you've got them! Full with motivations, things they could be doing, an image for an archetype which you can mix with others, an emotion which can be used as a reaction to something the PCs say or do, and adjectives to add further characterization.


      And there's a list of conditions for high magic that make for great insta-quests whenever the players want to try dealing with unstable wish-like magical rituals and effects. I'm finally feeling like this is the closest definitive form of this deck, but only testing will tell.
