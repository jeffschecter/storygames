\n I'm writing a Lovecraftian investigation game. When you succeed, you roll dice to determine how well you succeed, like this:

When you succeed on a 1, choose two drawbacks to your success (or one, but make it really bad).
When you succeed on a 2, choose one drawback.
When you succeed on a 3 or 4, you simply succeed.
When you succeed on a 5, choose one advantage to your success.
When you succeed on a 6, choose two advantages (or one, but make it really good).

Hence, I need some suitably Lovecraftian advantages and drawbacks. Here are the advantages I've got so far:

- You discover what you need, but don\u2019t fully understand.
- You leave traces of your passing.
- You draw unwanted attention.
- You lose something you need.
- You suffer harm.
- You take too long.
- You earn dislike, distrust or fear.

And here are the advantages:

- You earn respect.
- You reassure someone.
- You reassure yourself.
- You discover the full history.
- You discover what people whisper about.
- You discover the thing they are afraid to tell.
- You gain something you need.

What other advantages and disadvantages might there be?

Bear in mind that I'm talking about Lovecraft, not pulp Cthulhu. So, for example, "A tentacle reaches out and grabs you" would be wrong, because that never happens in Lovecraft*. Keep it bleak and cosmically horrific, please.

* If you know differently, well done! Keep it to yourself, please.

    \n The first thing, for actual Lovecraftian style, that comes to mind is the degree to which the character is shaken along the way to the ultimate end of the story. To that end, simply:

    Disadvantage:

    - Your confidence diminishes

    Advantage:

    - Your confidence grows

    Specificity would make this stronger, of course (e.g. "Your hands shake," or "You quail" versus "You grow steadier," or "Your determination radiates"), but I'm thinking just of the internal doubts and fears that should creep in along the way, without resorting to something like Sanity loss. This creates a situation in which success may rattle the actor, which feels Lovecraftian to me.

    \n The game is Cthulhu Dark, if you're interested. I'm not particularly making a big thing of it yet, but there it is, just for information.

    Isn't it interesting that advantages are often the same as disadvantages? Lenny mentions how falling unconscious could be an advantage. Similarly, not fully understanding could be an advantage, while discovering the thing they are afraid to tell could be a disadvantage.

    Look, also, how many, but not all, of the advantages/disadvantages are conditions: exhausted, shivering, unbelievable, angry, confident.

    And I'm especially interested in advantages that give subtle prompts to the fiction (e.g. "You discover what people whisper about").

    Anyway, here are some more, taken from The Shadow Over Innsmouth. Advantages:

    You get a glimpse of blasphemous horror
    You perceive something alien.
    You get a warning
    The person becomes sympathetic / talkative / affable.

    Disadvantages:

    You discover only the barest details.
    The person becomes secretive / scared / furtive.
    You doom the person you are talking to.
    You draw unwelcome attention.

    \n How much deliberate investigation actually goes on in Lovecraft's stories? I mean, there's "The Dunwich Horror" and a couple of others, but they're kind of in the minority compared to stories where people's sordid family history catches up to them, or where people are terrorised from things out of time, or someone trucks with things beyond the ken of mortals and is punished for it.

    The "Call of Cthulhu" roleplaying game (and Trail as well) is a genre to itself, which emulates (or parodies) only a fraction of Lovecraft's work. There's a whole lot of other stories, more horrific stories, which are neglected. Graham, I think you've already written the basis for a great Lovecraftian game in "A Taste for Murder".

    \n Here's a little AW hack I did:
    \n
    \nChris's Pitch Perfect Cthulhu Game
    \n
    \nYour Character
    \n
    \nPick Your Name
    \nIt must be a first, middle and last name. None of these can contain less than seven letters.
    \n
    \nRelationships
    \nNow choose a family member, either two steps removed from yourself, like a second cousin, or else it's your father.
    \n
    \nThat's it. There's only one player.
    \n
    \nGameplay
    \n
    \nYou're gonna find weird shit in your family member's stuff.
    \n
    \nWhen you find weird shit in your family member's stuff, the GM will hand you a three page document about history.
    \nRead it. Are you done?
    \nNo, you're not. Here's another. Read it.
    \nAre you done? OK. Go to the library.
    \n
    \nWhen you go to the library, the GM will give you something else to read.
    \nRead it. Are you done? You know too much!
    \n
    \nWhen you know too much, roll d6 to stay sane.
    \nYou rolled it? You fail.
    \nNo, you're right, I didn't look at what you rolled.
    \nYou're crazy
    \n
    \nWhen you're crazy, you're crazy.
    \nAct that way.
    \n
    \nCthulhu eats you.
    \n
    \nGame Over.
