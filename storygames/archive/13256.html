    The main conflict I see in what you want is this:\n
    Posted By: SebastianI want a game in which we, collaboratively, build the specifications of our spaceship
    \n
    Posted By: SebastianAnd, at the same time, I want my choice to use Warp Factor 7 (as opposed to Warp Factor 4) to matter... A lot!
    \nYou can't have both these things together. One is either a celebration of some external fictional content (Memory Alpha ho!), the other is an affirmation of your own creation. In other words, if the game privileges what you and your friends create, then the game can't make warp factor matter more or differently than you do on your own, it can't give it more weight than you have decided to give it. Certainly the tactical challenges are gone out the window unless you have someone making problems outside the collaboration.

    Posted By: Todd L

    Universalis? I can -almost- imagine the players declaring things and bidding coins/ while
    maintaining the fiction that they're each at their respective consoles on the bridge.
    But... is that what you were thinking too, Jamie? Or were you just suggesting a generally-good
    gm-less game?
    \nBoth - I don't know why it would be any harder to imagine than the fiction that you're swinging a sword in a dungeon, and it's your best fallback if you insist on GM-less-ness.

    Here's a stab at a set of tenets / gimmicks:
    * one of the character-owning gimmicks from the Universalis website
    * all the players will own one officer on a starship (fact)
    * each officer owns a set of responsibilities on the starship - the ship doesn't move unless the pilot says it's ok, the engines don't do anything unusual unless the engineer says it's ok, the weapons don't fire unless the weapons officer says it's ok, you can't talk to other ships unless the communications officer says its ok, etc. (gimmick - I'm thinking these are much more binding than facts, that the officer has veto power over their area like in Archipelago)
    * the officers' choices will change the universe (fact)
    * all the officers must have at least one personal goal
    * we won't put silly stuff in the story (fact, social contract)

    And depending on how tactical you want it to be:
    for a 3:16 level of "tactics" Universalis is good to go. Tactics will be part of the narration. "We try to lose them in the nebula. Thanks to Khan's two-dimensional thinking, we're able to then dive down and come up on them from behind." (Roll conflict.) Or - "Our ship has 'Warp Drives x5' - we get the hell out of there, and I'm telling the Engineer to give it all she's got." (Buy extra dice for giving-it-all-she's-got. After the conflict resolves, even if you win, the adversary will probably add various "Overloaded", "Dilithium crystals blown", "Antimatter core unstable" sorts of traits to your poor ship...)

    if you want maps and "we're here, they're there" -
    * it costs 1 coin to draw a map. it costs 1 coin to add an entity to the map or change the position of an entity on the map.

    If you want a board game, then
    * to resolve tactical situations we switch to battlestations (gimmick) [But then you'd have to come up with rules for converting from Uni to Battlestations and back...]

    I think the biggest problem with using Universalis and everyone-plays-a-character might be that someone has to step up and antagonize hard. Here's an out-of-my-ass gimmick for that:
    * each session one officer is an "extra" - his player can't spend coins on his officer, but gets double the coins.
    Though at that point you might as well do a game that's already been built for space with a rotating GM, eh. I'm imagining the "Tholian Web" episode of Star Trek when it's the captain's player's turn to be the adversary.
