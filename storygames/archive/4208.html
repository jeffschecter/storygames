    \n Several years ago I read A Simple Plan. Good book. Good movie too, but the book is different. You know how people say they did this and that and afterwards they "felt dirty". That's how I felt after reading that book...but not in some cliched way, but really, really dirty. I loved the book, but at the same time, I felt less good about myself for having participated in it.
    \n
    \nAOE is a little like that. It seduces you with some real awesomeness. I mean, in one playtest, I got to play a newborn child trying to unmake his own existence amidst the chaos of Hiroshima mere moments after it was bombed. Another time I taught a man the secret language of the stars and then cradled him as he went slowly mad beneath the reeling night sky, after which he was my apprentice. I created that stuff...the characters, the settings, the mythology of it all. The game opens you up to that kind of stuff, and you gotta admit, it sounds pretty awesome. But once you're in there, oooh boy, because that stuff up above...that's the lighthearted stuff.
    \n
    \nOne of our ongoing players had to bow out after a few sessions. They didn't like that they could come up with the really awful stuff the game demands, or that they could do it over and over again, or that it seemed to get easier over time. It's a challenging game in many ways. There is, however, a secret reward for playing it...which I won't spoil here, but if it gets ironed out through these last bits of playtesting, the game is really going to sing.

    \n It's gonna be a bit before real sit-down AP shows up.

    Personally, I am going through it right now with a bright red pen, and have the following thoughts.

    1) The "set up time points in history" aspect sounds extremely cool, and I'm wondering how to pull them off.
    2) It is, without a doubt, the second most complicated game I currently own (the first being "Continuum". I even owned ARIA). The way the rules interact with each other, the way the setup works, the way things happen... the game is only like 100 pages, but every single page is filled with little rules you have to really know deep down to play a game successfully. Literally, I can see it being a thing where you're like

    You: "Oh, we played it, but it just didn't work right"
    Other: "Oh, it sounds like that you might not have been using that third rule on page 43. Was it not working for you?"
    You: "Which rule was that? (flip flip flip... read read read...) Aw, fuck".

    It is tight. There is no wasted space. And yet, this is probably going to be a game where a friend will have to read it through and run it for me (or perhaps like 5 friends each "MASTER" 1/5 of the book, then come together to play).

    It's that complicated. To me, anyway. Complication is enhanced by the fact that these aren't "side rules" like radiation and suffocation in high gravity, they're all core rules, and they all currently make sense. There's just a lot to always, Always have in your head.

    \n
    \nAs for how the players interact, that's built into the system. As occultists, other players are your natural rivals. And rivalry is a huge part of the game. Just the same, if you can dominate another player's character, you can force them into the role of your Underling...and Underlings are one of the most precious commodities in AOE (my strategy whenever is I play is to establish an Underling as quickly as I can). And sometimes these interactions won't be your choice at all...you get to pick (scene by scene) what types of characters you want to interact with (you might say, for instance, "I want a scene with a Rival."), but the GM populates the scene with characters of the specified type from the available pool (so, for instance, because all player characters start out as Rivals, any PC in your current time frame, or any PC at all with the ability to transcend time and/or space, could end up in your scene).
    \n
    \nOn a non-character level, there are shared creations within the game - notably the settings and some of the NPCs - and you'll have opportunities to mess with these too, creating further interactive overlap.
    \n
    \nRegarding magic, a lot of it is Color...constrained in broadly defined categories. But magical workings themselves are a specific kind of action too, with mechanical consequences and whatnot. You'll like it.

    \n Note: These are impressions from reading the ashcan (just finished doing so perhaps an hour ago), not playing.

    The point of the game is genius. There's no other way to say it - if Acts of Evil (either as it is currently or in any future iteration) delivers on its core promise, then it's quite possibly the most masterfully-designed game of all time. I don't want to spoil the big surprise for those who haven't read it, but Acts of Evil is built in hopes of achieving a very specific emergent property of play that I see as seriously mind-blowing.

    The big problem is that "if." The mechanics are, as Andy noted, very complicated. In some ways, this little 90-page book is more complex than the D&D3.5 Player's Handbook. Forgetting even one of the myriad rules and the ways they interact seems like it could bring the whole thing tumbling down. From reading the ashcan, it sounds like its been a complicated and difficult journey for Paul, too, trying to wrestle the mechanics into doing what he needs them to do.

    Basically, I think Acts of Evil is the perfect example of an ashcan, and what the Front is for. There's huge potential there, but it seems like it's not quite there yet. If a version of the game that achieves its design goals results from feedback from the ashcan, then that'll be a huge vindication for the Ashcan Front.

    \n Alright. I didn't know that it had been talked about in other threads already.

    The big surprise is that at some point towards the end of gameplay, one of the recurring NPCs should stand up in some significant way and get in the way of (or even halt) the ambitions of one or more of the occultists. And usually make a sacrifice in doing so. This is meant to signify that this NPC (or NPCs, if more than one of them manages it) was actually the protagonist all along, and the occultists were just the villains of the piece. Additionally, even if you do ascend to godhood, it turns out that what that really consists of is destroying and usurping an uber-predator microorganism from Earth's prehistory.

    It's a very interesting twist, and an equally interesting design goal for emergent gameplay. If it works, it's quite the accomplishment.

    Note, this shouldn't be surprising for anyone who's read the book. Paul comes out and says that Commination of the Weak is the ultimate dramatic purpose of the game, and explains what it's meant to signify, in the sidebar on page 45.

    \n
    \n Well, before you even do character creation, you do a sort of setting creation, where everyone gets a chance to bid for the right to create one of the game's initial setpieces. At least one player, maybe more but probably not all of them, will win this right, and then they get to propose a starting setting. Players who don't get to create a setting pick one for their characters to start out in.
    \n
    \nAnd this is all very relevant, because how you choose to bid has the potential to carryover to one of your character's starting "attributes", and winning definitely has a benefit, but it has disadvantages too...for instance, if your setting is so cool everyone wants to play in it, you'll find yourself up against a whole bunch of self-aggrandizing rivals right off the bat.
    \n
    \nCharacter creation emerges directly from the setting creation. You figure out what your starting setpiece is, and then you imagine the kind of character you'd most like to play in that setting. The nuts & bolts of chargen are pretty straightforward...you divvy up some points among your various aspects (knowing the game and what does what really helps here), give some broad description, and your ready to go. But there's a natural tension that emerges among the players - and I think the setting creation really winds this up - to make something cool, offbeat, and original.
    \n
    \nSometimes you hit paydirt right away, like with the baby in Hiroshima. Sometimes it takes a scene or two for your character to really hit its stride. My very first character was a s&m freak in post-Revolutionary France; not exactly original, I know, but within a scene or two he decided to shave his eyebrows, believing they were the betrayers of some inner conscience, and instead took to painting eyebrows onto his face (once, memorably, using his own excrement because it was all he had handy) to reveal to onlookers how he thought he should feel. That character was not easily forgotten after that, and the point is, that was the game in action...I was fetishizing my character to gain extra dice, but in the process, I was creating a really villainous villain and developing a myth worthy of a usurper god all at the same time. And fetishizing is the most basic and easy example to give, but literally every scene you have and every thing you do adds to your character's mythology.
    \n
    \nWhat other sites will you see along the way? Hard to say...anything's feasible. In the same game as my s&m freak, one of the other players got stranded on Mars (I think it was Mars, anyway) and was permanently rendered into a statue of unmelting ice. They were then forced to build a cult from the strange cat-like creatures who gathered at its base, drawn to the cooler temperatures like an oasis in a desert. It was a little more Edgar Rice Burroughs than Clive Barker, but it was weird and not without a certain creepiness.
    \n
    \nI think probably my favorite aspect of AOE is that it's deliberately structured to be non-linear. I'm not talking about having some scenes broken up by flashbacks, or anything like that. Literally, every scene you have can take place in a different time and location, unconnected to previous scenes in time and/or content, because there is no need for rigid continuity (although you can have it if you want). Time and time again I've heard people say things like "You know the cover of [insert name of favorite game]...I want a game that recreates that!". More than any other game I know, AOE fulfills that promise, because although each scene is building towards something big, it's also its own self-contained portrait...as beautiful and terrible and grand as you choose to make it.

    \n A nemesis is something you have a choice about including or not, on a player-by-player basis. Basically, when a Manaster (the thing that severs an occultist's link with their soul) is installed, sometimes they have visions of people who will frustrate their ambitions. So if you want to you can give a few details about the nemesis you had a vision of. Then, if during the game a character matching that description ends up frustrating your ambitions, you get a benefit. If one doesn't, you take a penalty.
