\n Many of you know me. If you don't know me, and that is okay, I am a professional artist. I've never worked for WotC, although I have worked for TSR waaay back in the day. Just wanted to get that disclaimer up.

I just wrote an article addressing WotC's announcement of a "5th" edition Dungeons and Dragons is in the works. My article, Romancing the d20, is aimed at what the art should, could be for upcoming unofficially named "5th" edition. It is on my Live Journal.

I don't pretend to be much of a writer, I am a paint and ink slinger after all. But I do have some passion and emotion about this one. While certainly not an idie or storygame, D&D has had an enormous impact on us, I hope you will take a look.

http://storn-cook.livejournal.com/47531.html

Thank You!!

    \n I completely agree with, 3rd and 4th edition art lost a \u201csense of place\u201d, in general (with exceptions). Strangely many of the artists in D&D 4E are great at depicting sense of place but their skills weren't utilized. D&D Essentials fixed a lot of this but relatively few people have Essentials.

    If I was in charge of D&D Next I would utilize an assortment of art styles from artists from every generation of D&D and match the art to the optional rules that best represent that generation. It doesn't even have to stop at optional rules, do this with spells. Have a spell that was introduced in 2E, use artists that were famous from 2E.

    It would be hard, but I would invite all these artists to this coming Gen Con, and have them work on a giant mural together during the show that blends all their styles. Include the final piece in the book as a symbol of what's to come!

    I would reach out to...

    Clyde Caldwell


    Larry Elmore


    Fred Fields


    Jeff Easley


    Brom


    Tony DiTerlizzi


    Wayne Reynolds


    And then I would reach out to fantastic artists from other RPG communities, like...

    Jennifer Rodgers


    Zak S


    Will this be expensive? Yes! Will the advantages outweigh the costs? Yes! Especially if these artists enjoy their work and are excited about D&D. I would even name magical items, spells, or feats after them. You also don't need to exclusively use these artists. Include 1-3 pieces from each, include artists with similar styles, and then include artists with completely new styles because this isn't just about the past but also the future!

    Posted By: Zak SWhile I appreciate much of this, I think the way Storn talked about the (short and relatively mild) gender-appeal part of his request is misguided--or at least it gives me the willies, as an artist.

    Just hire 50% female artists that are The Best Ones You Can Possibly Find whether they are flowery and unicorny or otherwise, and let them decide what style to use in their art.

    Telling them--or male artists--hey "We want to appeal to a female audience so draw more collars and cuffs than you ordinarily would" is not a formula for anyone to produce their best work and runs the risk of appearing (and being) condescending.
    \nI disagree in part. Its not formula. It is challenge.

    Illustrators should modify their style, within reason, to fit the needs of the product. That is our job. While I agree female artists should be hired in droves at WotC and the best artists should get the work... more cuffs, more frills is simply the subject matter being modified. Any decent artist should be able to do that. If I am doing a superhero book and you submit fantasy art, you are not getting the job. If I'm doing a superhero book and I want a semi-realistic style (ie, less spandex) and you give me Bruce Timm cartoony art, you ain't getting the job. But if you can take Bruce Timm and put folds in some clothes and give a bit more roundness to the face and come to a more semi-realistic melding with your style. Maybe you get the job.

    But just let artist do whatever they want? Those are called Fine Artists and they can bring their stuff to the gallery. Illustrators, for better or worse, are blue color artists. We serve the needs of the client. We hit deadlines and we try to please art directors and we try to support the text. We try to sell the product. And being flexible in approach of subject matter is a good thing. So, if a male artist puts a bit more frill and flowy dress, cool. If Jennifer Meyer wants to push her inner Frazetta... cool too.

    \n Another thing missing in lot of latter-day D&D art is a sense of whimsy. I'm not talking about just the "Papers & Paychecks" cartoons in the DMG or the PHB pic of the barbarian slipping on the banana. I'm also thinking about he wonderful Trampier piece in the PHB of what is essentially Gygax sitting on top of a giant d6 in the middle of a forest a reading an AD&D manual. The sort of "nod and a wink" that breaks the fourth wall, as it were, that lets the reader know that the book is speaking gamer-to-gamer.

    The 4e Player's Strategy Guide does this in spades, though one could possible argue that it feels a little forced, especially given that WotC felt that style was okay, for that one book alone.

    I don't know if I'd say the older art sensibilities had a "sense of place" -- I'd say "sense of narrative" instead.     \nJust a little counterpoint to this, which I am hearing all over the GamerWeb and really wonder about. The difference here is

    "Narrative" art - tells a story; says "this is the kind of story you can experience with this game". What if I want a different story than that? I sometimes think some of the call for this style of art is about people wanting to lay claim to a particular "core story" for D&D and cut off the stuff they see as "not D&D". I don't mind some of this, but honestly a lot of it just turns me right off.

    "Character shot" art - yes, some of it sucks, but it doesn't suck because it's character illos. A character shot says: "this is the kind of character you can be in this game." As far as I am concerned, lots and lots of this, with massive diversity of style, implied setting, race/sex/appearance in general, implied play style... this is great, ESPECIALLY when it is stripped of somebody else's idea of what sort of things that character should get up to.
