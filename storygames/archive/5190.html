\n I got a chance to go to the Lost Colony exhibit at the North Carolina Museum of History and it was great. John White's watercolors are the centerpiece and star of the show. Even though they were all damaged in 1865, they are still jaw-droppingly great - looking at them puts you only one remove from his eyes, seeing the people and animals of the New World for the first time. I spent a lot of time trying to puzzle out his cultural bias in the depictions of the native people he sketched. There are clues - White's animals are occasionally not quite right, for example (his bald eagle is particularly out of proportion) and his early watercolors of Inuit abducted during Frobisher's expedition show some unconscious bias - the Greenlander's paddle, otherwise quite accurate, has nicely turned stops, like an armoire. But it's clear he was trying to capture what he saw, and it is fascinating. The pieces that depict ceremony were a bit of a shock - I don't know much about the eastern woodland tradition, but he shows an ecstatic dance among the Secotan in a ring of wooden pillars all carved with men's faces, and a funereal building for dead chieftains, who were (apparently) carefully skinned and gutted, with their muscles and guts stored at their feet, and the skins re-covering the skeletons. This "temple" was guarded by an idol, a squatting coal black figure named Kiwasa. White painted a gorgeous watercolor of a dude with a mummified bird in his hair - a shaman who "trafficked with the devil". It was excellent, thought-provoking, and inspirational.

Also cool - the Dare stones, creepy fakes that supposedly tell the story of the lost colonists, post disaster. All grist for the Roanoke-players mill...
