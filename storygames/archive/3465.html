\n So I think this was asked once before but I was wondering if anyone thought there was a limit to the number of indie RPGs that could be released and remained a positive influence on the hobby - without market saturation, I suppose.

I have a solid array of indie games (maybe a score) and I have this laundry list in my head of "oh man, I wanna show so-and-so this game and that game." But I look at the list of games in the IPR catalog, the various design contests, and that list of "games I am not working on" and my brain spins. How many games can we sustain? Is the point of all this that we move from being consumers to producers? Am I to take all this in? What do I buy? Who should I support? Is there a line to be crossed that the hobby should be concerned about?

So as I think about this I think about the numbers.

Do you hope to sell you're game? If so, how many copies do you hope to sell? How many people do you expect to play it?

Those numbers have to then interact with the community of gamers that WOULD play it, no?

Quizically,
- Don

    How many games can we sustain? Is the point of all this that we move from being consumers to producers? Am I to take all this in? What do I buy? Who should I support?
    \nThe really simple answer is that you (we) should buy the games that you think look cool and that you want to play. We've moved past the point where anybody should feel like they have to buy one of everything to support the community, or whatever. None of us have to buy a game just because it's indie, or was developed at the Forge, or was written by someone who hangs out here. I don't own a copy of TSOY because it doesn't really interest me. Everyody says it's great, but I don't feel like I have to own it. And I don't feel bad about that. If someday I want to play a game that does what TSOY does then I may buy it.

    There are enough of us producing enough different games that it's unreasonable to expect a person to buy or keep up with all of them. And why would you? Chances are you arn't going to like them all anyway.So many of us are designing for niche audiences now. So many of us are producing games that are targete not at the traditional gaming market but at other audiences entirely. At this point keeping up is like trying to see every movie that will be released this year; it's impossible, and you likely won't want to sit through half of them anyway.

    "Do you hope to sell you're game? If so, how many copies do you hope to sell? How many people do you expect to play it?"

    Yes. When Matt and I released Panty Explosion we hoped to sell about 200 copies. That was all we wanted. Since last August we've sold about 1400. It seems likely we'll do the same with Classroom Deathmatch. To me that seems like a lot. I have no idea how many people are playing the game. My best guess is that maybe 1/4 to 1/3 of the people who bought it have played it. I get a lot of emails saying stuff like "I really like the game, but I can't get any of my friends to play it with me".

    Anyway, I think there are more and more games coming out like Panty Explosion, that arn't targeted at the gaming community at all and are instead looking for an audience in a different place. For PE it's anime and Japanese cinema fans that we're after. For Breaking the Ice and Shooting the Moon? I'm sure Emily had an audience in mind. Contenders? Best Friends? Cat? None of these seem to be targeted at the gaming market. Not really. It seems like that instead of trying to appeal to a limited and small group of people with preset tastes (gamers), a lot of designers are instead making games that will appeal to other audiences altogether. In that scenerio the potential market is much, much larger and much more free of competition then the gaming market. We also don't really have to worry about "oversaturating" the Story Game market, because there's no expectation that all of these games will appeal to the same auience.

    Although I will admit, if you like traditional "gamer genres" (fantasy, sci-fi, pulp, etc) there is an overwhelming amount of good stuff available now.


    Jake

    \n yeah, don't get me wrong, I so am enjoying what's out there but there was a time I could say "yeah, I own that and this is what my play experience was." If you look at Fred's list of games being displayed by IPR and run as demos the ratio is way lower than in previous years. Games on Demand is way cool but what's the chances of a GM being in house when a player says "I'd like to learn how to play Discordia or Alien Summit."

    So there are three reasons I buy games.
    1. To gain some insight on different design approaches. To be informed about what's out there when considering how to handle my own games. I plan on writing a new horror game based on Lovecraft later this year (based on conversations we've had here over CoC and GUMSHOE) and know that I will probably go in the direction of Heroes Banner. I would have been reinventing the wheel or maybe not thought about that direction without HB in my brainpan.

    2. To play. I like to be the guy that people come to and say "you've played this, right? How is it? What was you're experience?" I could do that with well over 300 traditional RPGs and about 20 indie RPGs. I could give folks an informed opinion. Roger Ebert watches the lion share of films out there even though he may not like them. Sure, I'm not getting paid to do it and thus you learn of my nuerosis but you get the idea.

    3. Because it's in print and it's purty or nostalgic. I recently bought Aces & Eights. God, I would never play that game nowadays - okay, maybe I would but not during con season - but it was in a leatherette cover and a hefty 250+ pages. I see Expedition to Undermountain. Gosh it's aweful. There are a few gems in there and for a trad D&D player there are some useful tidbits to run a campaign off. I bought it because I had the extra cash and I recall enjoying playing in the Undermountain as a 15 year old. Wise? No. But hey, it's what I do.

    I recently started finally shelving my game collection. 2/3 of it are shelved. That 54 cubic foot of gaming books. My buying slowed down dramatically since getting into the indie scene but once I could "collect" and play all these indie gems. No more.

    Again, I am not complaining nor do I hope to see it slow. I do agree that I wonder how many of these games are really good as Graham mentioned. How many of them measure up to Dogs or The Mountain Witch?

    Thanks,
