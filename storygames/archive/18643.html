\n I'd say design wise, the game is the most Story Game friendly edition of Star Wars I've ever seen. I love the way the dice allow for narrative control of battle. Allowing the GM to describe in game changes when advantage and disaster come up in a roll. Or how the Obligation mechanic allows for narrative back plotting of a character right out of the gate.

It's a bit more mechanical than say a Dungeon World game, but it has a lot of GM friendly short cuts to speed up rolls and to allow for the packing of the plot to rule over the dice themselves. (No seriously they have optional rules to turn whole scenes into 1 roll offs an done.) It doesn't help the book is damn pretty.

I thought I wouldn't like it with the funky dice, but, I dunno. It's growing on me.

How about your thoughts?

    Let's face it, it's doing more or less the same things that Apocalypse World does with 2d6+stat...
    \nEh, it's a really powerful difference at its core, I think.

    SW:
    * You can succeed SUPER WELL, but in the process set up lots of setbacks.
    (maneuver through an asteroid field with great success, not even a scratch; but your maneuvers are so spectacular, that word gets out. Now a lot of people know who you are, and are fans. Good luck on trying to sneak/anonymize yourself at the next scoundrel bar, Someone's gonna recognize you, and everyone there will be buying you drinks)

    * You can fail SUPREMELY, but in the process set up lots of advantages for the future.
    (Try to sneak through a blockade on some planet, but fail; get recognized by some stormtroopers, they surround you, capture you, and lock you up... with the legendary pirate captain you were trying to find this whole time anyway!")

    AW's range is about Perfect Success, Perfect Failure, or the Chance of Success in return for some hard setback right then and there ("you can maneuver through the asteroid field, and outrun your pursuers, but in return you get banged up and lose your communications system. Do you go for it?").
    Well, that could be GM-handwaived I guess, but that latter situation, the rule coded "you fail hardcore now, but have a great advantage coming up soon!" thing, is a cool aspect of the new SW game that really got under my skin in a good way. Not even my beloved SW d6 hard-coded that into the game, it would have been the whim of a GM making Failure Actually Success...

    =-=-=
    That said, I liked it. On the road to Warhammer without all the fiddly other boardgamey aspects.

    I was disappointed to see that the dice app for the game cost $5. But I guess that's an acceptable price in one of those situations where Just The GM has the game, the players all have dice apps...

    \n The Dice app is more useful than I thought. It's agnostic too and covers the dice used in the X-wing minis games, and has a full range of standard dice. I'm using it for all my rolling in games now. Fast and easy. I can pre-program dice sets to roll.

    Basicaly Warhammer took character traits and turned them into talent cards. (Very 4E D&D in a way.) In order to play you had the custom Warhammer dice, the talent cards, the character card, etc. It played like an ugly mashup of boardgame/RPG even though it was a full on RPG. Just ugh.

    Star Wars does away with the talent/pc cards and just has a nice clean character sheet and maybe a printed out (you can photo copy the talent spreads in the book, you are expressly given permission!) to make things easier. After that, the funky dice. Oh, btw. The funky dice have a table so you can convert normal dice to the funky effects. it has the ranges spelled out for the d6, d8 and d12s the system uses.

    So, just get a bunch of colored dice and you are set if you have the cheet sheet handy. The funky dice work. I love how combat is die pool building. Bad guys = their pool and you = your pool. Roll off. See results. Talents modify the character, and or how you can modify the results of that roll. The game/writers pretty much spell out higher raw attributes are GOOD early on in character gen and have that bolded in the character gen rules. But man, it's just so fast to make characters and there is a nice range of options.

    If they went back and did Warhammer 2nd Edition (5th? 6th?) and built it like SW:EoTE, people would be eating it up.

    \nNow that I'm home and have the book in front of me I'll clarify somewhat...

    Laminate Armor (Basic Stormtrooper armor) has a soak of 2 (the highest you can get without modifications in ANY armor), an encumbrance of 4 and costs 2,500 credits.

    Heavy Battle Armor (some special Stormtroopers would have this) gives 1 defense (1 Black d6 added to the attacker's dice pool) and 2 soak, and has an encumbrance of 6... it costs 5,000 and is restricted; i.e if seen with it your character better have a damned good reason to have it

    Modifying an armor suit to give it +1 soak and less encumbrance costs 5,000 credits and can only be done once. Making 3 soak the absolute maximum any player's armor can ever have.

    Blaster Rifles have 9 damage (Heavy Blaster pistols have 7, and normal blaster pistols have 6) an encumbrance of 4, and cost 900 credits.

    Characters start with 500 credits. Adding +5 to your obligation can get you +1000 credits (or 5 bonus starting XP) OR adding +10 to the obligation can get you +2500 credits (or +10 to starting XP)

    Basically what that boils down to is IF you want to be heavily armored, and have a rifle at character start you have to invest heavily Obligation wise. Certainly not a bad thing, but your past is more likely to come up and you are more likely to head into any given situation with some strain already marked off. (If your obligation gets hit when the GM rolls for it your strain maximum is marked down by 2. Strain is derived from your Willpower + your species value (10 for humans))


    Additionally Blaster Pistols and Blaster Rifles require 2 different skills Blasters(Light) and Blasters(heavy) respectively.

      What worries me though, is the report by some players that the "special dice" is kind of flawed as it can generate "narrative stress" because almost every roll results in lots of dis/advantages showing up and forcing the group to improvise new situations everytime.
      \nI don't know that it's *flawed*. I think it's the *intention* of the system. The outcomes map to the "Yes, and...", "Yes, but...", "No, but..." and "No, and" - similar to what you see in quite a number of story games. Anyone who's played Star Wars with a Lady Blackbird hack, or the AW engine, etc, should have no trouble. For players who are used to previous official Star Wars games, however (which were traditional games with straight-up success/failure and occasional criticals), it may be something of a change.

      I looked carefully at the dice probabilities a couple days ago. For an opposed roll between exactly balanced forces (i.e. equal types of dice on each side), there will be more success icons than failures, and more threat icons than advantages, resulting in success, but with a negative consequence. i.e. "Yes, but ...", or most 7-9 rolls in the AW-engine.

      The real killer of the dice is the price. Dice pools with 5-6 dice of at least one type appear to be fairly common. And a set of dice costs $15 (US) for two of each type, so you need three sets (or roll the dice three times for every check). So you have to spend 45 bucks just on the dice (and another $60 for the game rules). Admittedly, custom dice aren't cheap, and the book is about the right price for its size. So I don't think they're doing it solely to boost their profit margins.

      I think it's a really interesting system. It's got the crunch of traditional games - equipment, talents, etc. all have very specific rules about how they modify the dice rolls (e.g. this weapon gives you three boost dice to attack rolls). But the results of the roll are much more story-game like, and strongly feed back into the narrated fiction.

Mind you, it's a very flexiable list and is there as a guideline for new GMs to make calls. (Also nicely spelled out on the GM screen now that I can read it.)

      A lot of people panic but forget that success and failure die, oppertunity and threat dice counts cancel out. Only the Triumph and Dispair symbols on dice stick around after totals are calculated. That speeds up rolls a lot. I honestly think it's just a matter of 1 to 2 sessions of experience and the group will be making quick judgement calls on results.

      The Obligation mechanic is where most the Story Gaming takes place. You use it as a currency for favors and goods when you don't have cash. You also use it to add interesting character driven events in the middle of existing arcs.

      If the Duty mechanic from Age of Rebellion works like they say it will, I can see characters with both Obligations and Duty in a game and all the weird story fun that happens because of it.
