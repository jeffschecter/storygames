\n So you sit down to play In a Wicked Age, and maybe it's a pickup game so you don't know each other that well, and all the PC's choose best interests that are kind of independent, that don't really antagonize each other or point at each other. The game can end up being several solo games that are going on simultaneously, with a lot of audience time.

Or, in other games, you might see goals or beliefs that are sort of taoist or buddha-like in their not-striving. "I want to find peace" or "I just want a warm bed." Leaving the GM or other players to do all the work to get these people into conflict. (Great goals to have in real life, not so great for stories.)

I think goals and best-interests are possibly the single most important thing driving stories and story games ... it's a shame that it's so easy to cock them up.

So I've been thinking about getting much more structured about it. Cards or lists with fairly narrowly defined goals.

One could use them with a lot of games ... In a Wicked Age (for the best interests), Misery Bubblegum (for the dreams), Archipelago (for the destinies), Geiger Counter (for the goals) ...

Here's some examples of what some cards say - the last, italicized line is your best interest or destiny or whatever.

---
One of the other players has something you really want.
---
Answer now: Which player?
Answer in play:
What do they have?
Why do you want it?
How do you get it?
How do you secure it, so you know you'll never lose it?
---

---
One of the other players wrongs you.
---
Answer now: Which player?
Answer in play:
How do they wrong you?
Why?
How do you get back at them?
---

---
You want one of the other players.
---
Answer now: Which player?
Answer in play:
Why do you want them?
Why can't you have them?
How do you win them?
---

Oh, and I almost forgot to say, to play you deal a bunch out face up in the middle of everyone and then choose - you don't get assigned a single one.

So, I'm looking for feedback. Why is this a good or bad idea; what does it need; where does it go overboard; too constraining / not constraining enough; does it need to be tied more closely with setting, more specific ...

What's better, letting you decide which player your interest lies with or saying something more detailed like, "The player on your right wrongs you. How do you get back at them?"

And ... what games already do something like this, besides the Mountain Witch? [One difference between this and Mountain Witch is this would be in the open.]

    \n In my last Dogs in the Vineyard game, I...

    - put out a list of the town's NPCs including names, pictures, jobs, and 3 descriptive tags.

    - ask the players a series of questions... who are you related to? who has something you want? who took something from you? who do you love? who do you hate? who trained you? who wronged you? who did you lose your virginity to? who do you owe an apology to? who do you owe a debt to? who did you save from sin? who saved you from sin? who almost killed you? who did you almost kill? who knows your secret? who's secret do you know? and so on

    - everyone must answer at least 3 of these questions

    - no one can answer the same question

    - but 2 of your answers must be shared with other players (so 1 PC's mentor is another PC's lover)

    And that's it. The problems in the town naturally conflict these relationships and put pressure on players to act. There is no need to pre-play or pre-decide what your PC might do. It's all obvious in play and when it's not obvious, it's even more fun because the tension in deciding when no decision is right or wrong is glorious.

    I coupled this with brief solo scenes between PCs with their positive relationships that are negative relationships with other PC. The goal being to build empathy so when 1 PC goes gunning for that NPC, the other PC is invested in reacting.

    It worked perfectly. Better than when PC have had preset goals.

    "I want to find peace"

    "Great! Who is keeping you from being at peace? Why can't you be at peace?     \nSure, here's a goofy example. Say we have four players -- Alice, Bob, Carol, and Donald. They are playing Apollo, Bilbo, Conan, and Data. The GM has one NPC called Khan. The GM starts with Khan's best interest.

    GM: Khan wants to avenge the death of his wife. He's targeting Apollo because he thinks Apollo made Ceti Alpha go supernova.

    Donald: Data wants to recover his precious emotion chip back from...somebody? Does anyone want to be the holder of my emotion chip?

    Bilbo: I'll have it.



    Bob: Naturally, Bilbo wants to toss the emotion chip into Mount Doom.

    Carol: Conan wants to steal the sun from Apollo.

    GM: I'm creating an NPC called 'Emotion Chip.'



    Alice: Apollo needs to retire from being a sun god.

    GM: What's preventing him from retiring?

    Alice: It's more a sense of duty thing.

    GM: So that's his other best interest, then?

    Alice: Yup. Apollo wants to keep being the sun god.



    Note: Apollo's two inward-facing best interests don't feel like a big problem because he's already in the crosshairs of Khan and Conan. I doubt those two will let Apollo be the solitary emo god he wants to be. Also note that at this point the map is cleaved in twain, so the GM looks for an opportunity to join those two sub-maps:

    GM: Emotion chip wants to be implanted in Conan.



    And so on.

    \nThis thread on The Forge about Best Interests leads to this amazing tool Alan Barclay created, which I believe solves all problems in Jamie's first post -- if the players really commit to the tool.

    And Vincent has a listing of habits needed to play "In A Wicked Age..." on another Forge thread. I think if one does as Vincent suggests and uses the Best Interest tool Alan created, all the problems will go away all poof.

    Some of the words:

    creating driven characters

    From now on, every character you make, do one of these:

    - Make her best interests mutually incompatible. "It's in my best interests to seduce and marry the monk. It's in my best interests to drive the monk out of the region forever."

    - Give her a best interest incompatible with her driving goals. "It's in my best interests to fail to seduce and marry the monk, even though it's what I intend to do."

    - Give her a best interest totally dependent on other characters to make true or false. "It's in my best interests for the private negotiations between you, the new young king, and you, the elder of my cult, to go in the cult's favor."

    \n Hi Ben,

    I say all the following with full awareness of the risks of comparing media (and I consider a story created through RPG play just one more media). For some reason my brain can do it. Scene by scene we march forward in a film, just as scene by scene we march forward in an RPG session. Scene by scene in a movie we don't know what will happen next as we watch it, and scene by scene in RPG play we don't know what will happen next as the story is created.

    No big deal for brain to map them. I'm weird, I guess.

    Anyway, so Aliens...

    The scene your talking about, the one that got cut, is when Burke tells Ripley that while she was traveling through space for decades in deep sleep, her daughter aged, got cancer and died.

    And here's another thing about me: For me, there's what in the story, and there's what isn't in the story, and the two never meet. The fact that there's something, anything, on the cutting room floor is moot. There's no mention of Ripley's daughter in the whole movie. So it doesn't matter. What matters is what I see and hear in the movie.

    (This is why my brain is simply boggled by any interest in "Canon" or filling in backstory from a novel in what happened in a comic book or whatever. I simply don't get it.)

    All I know about Aliens is what I see and hear on the screen while the movie plays. That's it. Done. Anything that might or might not have been in one cut or another doesn't matter.

    In the movie Aliens what do I see? I see a woman tormented by nightmares. I am told she's lost her license and can't get her shit together and is working in a cargo dock. Burke shows up. He needs her help: the colony has gone silent. She tells him to fuck off. He taunts her with her current work situation, and then this:

    BURKE
    If you go.
    (pause)
    It's a second chance, kiddo. And
    it'll be the best thing in the
    world for you to face this fear
    and beat it. You gotta get back
    on the horse...

    RIPLEY
    (frosty)

    psych evaluation this month.

    Burke leans close, a let's-cut-the-crap intimacy.

    wake up every night, sheets
    soaking, the same nightmare

    please go. I'm sorry. Just
    go, would you.

    Burke nods to Gorman who rises with him. He slips a

    She sleeps. She has another nightmare, waking up in a horrible sweat. She calls Burke. Says yes. Boom. She's on her way.

    She doesn't know anyone on the colony. She's not connected to the colony. She has no reason to go but one, the one Burke offers: A second chance to face her fears, get over he nightmares and get a good night's sleep.


    ****

    So, some things:

    I noticed a while back that the "Goal" of a movie usually aren't introduced at first.

    A character usually has what I eventually termed a "Desire" floating around in his or her heart.... but it's not attached to a goal in any way.

    John McClain wants to re-connect with his wife in Die Hard.
    Sheriff Brody wants to keep the peace on Amity Island in Jaws.
    Indian Jones covets old things Raiders of the Lost Ark.
    Luke is an impatient farm boy who wants to get off the farm and have adventure in Star Wars.

    And then something new enters the picture, what I call an Opportunity or Threat, that can either make the heart's desire possible or will just crush it.

    Terrorists take over the building in Die Hard
    A mutilated body washes up on the beach in Amity.
    The government tells Jones that someone is close to finding the Ark.
    Luke gets a message from a princes needing help.

    At that moment the potential of what was a Desire is made concrete. (Just like Ripley's Desire wanting to get through the night without waking up, gasping, drenched in sweat has the potential to become concrete with the "second chance" Burke offers.)

    We don't even have a Goal yet. Not really. Everything is still tenuous, and the actual consequences and stakes involved in the story aren't quite revealed yet.

    John McClane doesn't have a plan or a goal when he hears gunshots and then sees men with guns. Luke doesn't go chase the princess by himself. And so on. But an opportunity is there.

    And then what we have is a series of Goals. McClane works to get a gun. Brody works to puzzle out what happened to the dead girl. Jones goes looking for his old mentor in Nepal. Luke chases after R2 when the droid runs away to Ben's old hut. And so on.

    And each Goal leads to the next goal. Goals shifting from one to the next as the circumstances drop out from under the protagonist or new information arrives or new threats arrive and so.

    And, in my experience, this is exactly how my best RPG play works. Exactly.

    ***

    I bring all this up because it can be incredibly valuable to look at GM prep and PC creation with this perspective for some game. Certainly the games I play and the way I play them.

    For example, there are no Goals In A Wicked Age... Or, rather, there are no Goals at first. There are Best Interests at first, but those aren't Goals. A Best Interest might lead a Character to form a Goal... and the Character might even attain that Goal... but he might not. He might think he has to kill someone in his Best Interest, and then discover he'd better off allying with this once-an-enemy against a pirate king. And now there is a new Goal.

    I brought up Ripley in Aliens in this thread, and now all this other stuff, because in the games Jamie seems to be talking about, and certainly the games I'm play (IAWA, Sorcerer, Dogs, Hero Wars, The Mountain Witch, and others...) there is a beginning of some kind that stirs the Player Character's heart -- Best Interests, Kickers, Traits, Dark Destinies and so on), but the goals are found in play. These qualities formed during character creation squeeze and propel play forward, but they don't tell us how things will end. More than that, they don't tell us what they ending will be about.

    Now, again, I kneel before the need to point out comparing media is problematic, but I point out that from the protagonists points of view that I have mentioned, not one of these protagonists has any clue as to where the hell their headed or what's going to happen or even what their final goals in the story are going to be.

    McClain has no idea he'll be confronting an armed man holding his wife hostage. Luke has no idea he'll be leading an attack on a battle station in space. Brody has no idea he'll be fighting a shark mano-a-mano in the open see. These are all new conflicts with new goals that only came about by pursuing previous conflicts with other goals, one after another, that started with a simple desire.

    I'm pretty sure that a lot of RPGs work exactly the same way in term of non-Goal character creation attached to some sort of open-end, non-concrete aspect to the Character's emotional life. Then, as the Game Master and other Players begin offering up actions and words at the table, the Player sees threats and opportunities to grab onto to make these things that were not concrete now concrete... and then these lead to others and so on.

    Anyway, that's how I see it.

    \n I think it all makes sense. And matches my experience gaming. Especially with people new to gaming.

    I've found what people write down before play, often quickly changes in play. From relationships, to motivations, to goals. Especially goals. I've seen plenty of people write down a Belief and then in a few scenes completely ignore, abandon, or break it. I've seen people resolve their Best Interests in IAWA in the first scene because in playing they realized they don't find them that interesting. Or the person they decided they hated before play is the person they want to team up with.

    We've been playstorming a few "make a movie as you play it" games and the way we've addressed it is by not making decisions till you have enough information to make informed decisions. Which often means delaying decisions traditionally made in character creation till the middle of play.

    So for example, you don't choose relationships till after the player characters have been introduced. You don't choose motivations till the source of antagonism has been fleshed out. And you don\u2019t choose major goals till mid-way through, till you have past the point of no return. There are mechanical ways for managing and revealing the various turning points that open these options as the game progresses but I don't want to derail this thread.

    When we play Dogs in the Vineyard now, I show the players the list of major NPCs in the town and let them create basic history with them in the form of relationships. But I encourage them to leave most of their Traits and Relationships blank. Rather, we determine and reveal them in play.

    I like Jamie's techniques in his first post. I like asking the players provocative questions that reveal history and create situation:

    - Which character wronged you?
    - How?
    - Why didn't you do anything about it? Or did you?
