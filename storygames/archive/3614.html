\n In preparation for Dexcon (this week!) I ran Shock a couple of days ago at the local game store. I was expecting some problems based on a few AP reports I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve seen, but I was pleasantly surprised. The game ran very smooth, with only a couple of pauses to parse the rules as written. It may be the case that forewarned is forearmed as those pauses were at points others have mentioned: escalating a conflict when a die roll ends up on a fulcrum, whose d4 roll to use of the players rolling, and what does it mean mechanically to re-roll a conflict (risking a link). And I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve just noticed that the rules indicate the player to your left makes your antagonist but the player to your right plays him. We didn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t do that (the player to the left created and played the antagonists). I suppose the division of labor could be intentional, and actually might be fun. Hmmm\xe2\u20ac\xa6I have no doubts that our solutions to these rules lacunae may have been different from other players and even the designer\xe2\u20ac\u2122s intent. So what, big deal.
The most difficult part of the game had nothing to do with interpreting the rules. What we found challenging was coming up with protagonist and antagonist intent in a conflict that did not contradict each other. Once or twice we actually rolled the dice, and were figuring out the results when we realized the intents were mutually exclusive. We quickly revised the intents and kept the rolls. More practice will make it easier. As well as being challenging, coming up with intents was great fun. It was almost like a logic puzzle.
Overall, the game as a blast to play. I can\xe2\u20ac\u2122t wait to play it again at Dexcon!
I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve used the last round of the game for my podcast Virtual Play
and the complete audio file at the Internet Archive
Mel
