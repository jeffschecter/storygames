\n Continuing my habit of posting provocative quotations about things tangentially related to gaming, I present SF writer M. John Harrison on worldbuilding.\n
Every moment of a science fiction story must represent the triumph of writing over worldbuilding.

Worldbuilding is dull. Worldbuilding literalises the urge to invent. Worldbuilding gives an unneccessary permission for acts of writing (indeed, for acts of reading). Worldbuilding numbs the reader\xe2\u20ac\u2122s ability to fulfil their part of the bargain, because it believes that it has to do everything around here if anything is going to get done.

Above all, worldbuilding is not technically neccessary. It is the great clomping foot of nerdism. It is the attempt to exhaustively survey a place that isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t there. A good writer would never try to do that, even with a place that is there. It isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t possible, & if it was the results wouldn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t be readable: they would constitute not a book but the biggest library ever built, a hallowed place of dedication & lifelong study. This gives us a clue to the psychological type of the worldbuilder & the worldbuilder\xe2\u20ac\u2122s victim, & makes us very afraid.
\nThoughts on how this statement relates to gaming?

--Paul

    \n Via Harrison again:

    "Tolkien is no longer an influence on the innovators of the genre he invented. If we want to see the back of the old fart, let's not give him deathbed support by having an opinion about him. The worst thing we can do is set ourselves up in opposition. Cultural opposites depend upon one another. They prop one another up. Something Tolkien failed to understand that when Barad Dur falls, Gondor falls too. Let's not make the same mistake. Let's not be one thing or the other, Tolkienista or Next Waver. Let's be something new."

    As someone who loves Tolkien and Harrison, I have to wonder just how much worldbuilding Harrison did for something like Viriconium. There's an interesting thing to know about Harrison. You can read one of his recent novels, like Light, and then read one of his short stories from the late 70s, and you find many of the same objects -- the same names for weapons, technologies, you name it. And even though the publishing of Viriconium and LIght are separated by decades they share a surprisingly large number of themes and motifs. As if he makes it up as he goes along and once he does, doesn't see the need to invent a new reality for his next fiction, it's all one -- his body of work.

    To bring it back to gaming, I'm pretty new to RPGs I admit, but what I'm wondering is if we'll see at some point a game developer, similar to Harrison, who puts out game after game that doesn't try to do something new every time, mechanically or thematically, but that expounds on the same mechanics and theme, maybe in subtly different ways, always circling around some big central issue unique to the developer and always striving for the heart of it.

    When you think about it this isn't much different from Tolkien's work after all.

    Posted By: georgeCultural opposites depend upon one another. They prop one another up. Something Tolkien failed to understand that when Barad Dur falls, Gondor falls too.
    \nActually, Tolkien did realise that, for what it's worth. At least on a literary level. he tried to go back for a sequel and quit, disgusted by the banality of what was left.

    I'm not sure exactly where I'd say Tolkien stands with regard to this worldbuilding problem, but it strikes me that he at least avoided this pitfall:\n
    Worldbuilding numbs the reader\xe2\u20ac\u2122s ability to fulfil their part of the bargain, because it believes that it has to do everything around here if anything is going to get done.
    \nTolkien may have spent his own time (years and years of it) privately worldbuilding, but when he set down to write a story, that's exactly what he did. He was able to use that hige library of Middle Earth in his head to flesh out the setting in little ways that tantalise the reader, giving little hints of a wider world beyond his heroes' adventures, but without ponderously belaboring all the details and mapping out every last nook and cranny. An element of the world is discovered as it is encountered, and fleshed out only as importance dictates. Many, many places, people and things go untouched or unexamined. In the end, it's all about the main characters, and their story. (I'd argue that the same applies to Frank Herbert.)

    I don't think the "Worldbuilding Problem" stems from Tolkien but from his imitators.

    Though I don't know quite how to relate all that back to roleplaying.

    \n All right, I'll seriously talk about the topic instead of being totally dismissive of the value judgment since some people seem to agree with this dunderhead.

    Let me take one particular version of the anti-worldbuilding canard. I'm not picking on you particularly, Mes, indeed you should feel proud that you gave the most succinct version of this oh so very wrong answer to the question of how much worldbuilding to do: "just as much as you need to build a compelling and coherent narrative."

    That is one style of story (and one style of game). There is another, there are many others.

    Let's not forget that Tolkein is not an aberration here, in fact he is barely a blip on the radar once you pass beyond the geek canon. There is Faulkner. There is O. Henry's "Cabbages and Kings". There is Dickens (good christ, a Dickens SHORT story kills this idea dead). There is Jan Morris' "Last Letters From Hav" and Garrison Keillor's Lake Wobegon series. Italo Calvino was already brought up, but why not Les Miserables and Moby Dick? Moby Dick has a whole god damn whaling textbook in it, and it's not by accident. What's all that stuff in "All The King's Men" about Cass Mastern? How come a whole chapter concerns a turtle crossing the road in "The Grapes of Wrath"? "Tristram Shandy" and P.G. Wodehouse worldbuild for laughs and as a joke on you. Terry Pratchett does it in footnotes and insert chapters, Doyle had Watson tease us with irrelevant Holmes cases that he never intended to detail. The Leatherstocking series worldbuilds to establish tone and as an independent support for the theme. "House of Leaves" does it to creep you out. All of these works contain extensive (and varying) segments, sections, chapters, volumes, not having anything remotely to do with anything anyone in the normal everyday world would call the narrative of the work.

    It's a modern conceit that "oh, the setting is really irrelevant once you get beyond the immediate situation of the narrative". And although following that conceit does create a certain kind of story, a kind I really enjoy, it is not the only kind in the world, nor, I'll wager, the most prevalent kind.

    What this means for gaming is that worldbuilding is fun and good even when it has no impact on any kind of "narrative" at all. It's just a different kind of fun than straight narrative.

    \nThe thing I notice is that I often fight with the pre-defined world I read about in a book.
    I've never been at odds with a player input piece of setting.

    Having half of the story elements come from a source and then the other half come from a completely unconnected source... is really weird.

    Con games work because the designer/GM shows up and says "Okay, this is the world, here are your characters".
    Something like Shock: works because you establish the world, then establish the characters.

    Having a designer/writer do half of that and expect you to slot yourself in and finish the other half... is weird.

      I just wanted to point out that Harrison's comments make a lot more sense if you don't look at them as litcrit -- "Stories that contain worldbuilding are shit!", I'm pretty sure he's not saying that -- but as advice to budding authors, who often seem to get so hung up on the worldbuilding aspect of fantasy fiction (an aspect which is admittedly a ton of fun for the author to work on) that the more essential competent of telling a compelling story becomes an afterthought. Readership of novels which only succeed in detailing a meticulously-detailed fantasy world will always be limited to a niche fanbase. A good story, however, has universal appeal. If you wish to be an author by trade and get paid for this stuff, you generally want to write a story that's interesting to more people than interesting to a few people. There's totally a market-oriented value judgment in there, moreso than a critically qualitative one.

      Like, I don't think anybody's actually claiming that stories include any sort of worldbuilding are bad. A great many good stories contain amazingly through worldbuilding.       \n It's very easy to cheer Harrison's comment or boo it, because it strikes a chord with your personal demons. But that's just it, whatever the comment means in relation to RPGs depends on you, and getting into arguments over it is kind of pointless at least until you point to a particular game or an experience that you apply it to. For example, I'll bet a lot of people read the comment as a manifesto against fat sourcebooks that require you to read 300 pages of background before you can play--or instead of playing. Others take it as a snide F-U to the idea that a reader (of a novel) or player (of a game) has a right to reject hokey, contrived continuity, and should instead be grateful to witness the self-indulgent emoting of the author/GM/fellow player.

      Personally my demons make me to react mostly in the second manner. I mean, I've spent a lot of time rolling my eyes at the bulk of Hollywood's product (film & TV) and for me part of attraction of RPGs is that they provide a platform for "interrogating" the fiction. This may be an end in itself; however it's also a way of ensuring that there's a robust foundation for everything else. Ultimately, when it comes to coherent worlds, the richer the better as long as it fits inside the participants' heads. The world (in a broad sense, including genre tropes and the like) is like a universe of signs; more signs, more connections, greater potential for building layers of meaning. (This is why viewing world building only in terms of constraint is mixed up--just as with language and culture, you don't create meaning out of nothing.)

      Take a look at the comment by Elf Sternberg: "The world is a character. It has wants, most of which involve stymieing the reader. It has a personality, it has impulses, and it has principles. You don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t need to get everything absolutely right; you simply need to not get anything wrong. Sometimes, yes, that means that in the writer\xe2\u20ac\u2122s notes for a story or a series, he makes up and writes down details about the biochemistry....Worldbuilding is the responsibility the writer has to create a context that hangs together from the beginning of the work to the end." I agree with all of this, and add that "world" can be empowering to the participants in a game just as (I'm sure many here will agree) concrete systems regulating player-character interaction can be more empowering than running a game based on pure social negotation. On the other hand I'd hypothesize that for RPGs, trying to nail down any element of the fiction in infinite detail at the beginning can be stifling. To begin with it's impossible, second if the language analogy holds any water, the expressive power of a fictional world also comes from its slippery adaptability.

      \n I take the quote as cautionary:

      "Here is a pitfall: watch out. Good worldbuilding does not make a bad story good."

      I know my teenage self could have really used that advice, both as a gamer and a writer. After reading and marveling at works like Lord of the Rings, it was easy to come away with the impression that a huge, well-realized world would magically produce exciting stories--whether as a game or as a novel.

      I would read that Harn (or whatever setting) had lists describing the manner of dress for every social class in every different city of the whole world... and my reaction was, "Wow! Cool! If I had that [or wrote it myself] my gaming will be non-stop awesome forever!"

      I'm not sure why I felt that way, but I've been much, much happier since I've squashed that impulse.

      And if you're trying to make a living doing something (like writing novels), that advice could save your life.

      \n I'm probably repeating something that somebody else already said, but let me say it anyway: in the context of a sci-fi / fantasy book, film or similar, then worldbuilding is dull if, and this is important, if it isn't worked neatly into the story as it plays out. It's inevitable that you're going to have to explain something to the reader during the course of the book, otherwise you're looking at a very bland and formulaic book. The trick is to explain it to them by example, demonstrate how it works rather than put in vast treatises about the different types of Hobbit (I'm looking at YOU, J.R.R. Tolkien, stop giggling at the back there!)

      That isn't very practical in an RPG book since most players and GMs will want to know fairly precisely how everything works, though it may be an effective device to use in a game where the players haven't read that book. The alternative is to have a genre-specific game instead and let the players and GM make up their own world. While this is quite valid and there's nothing wrong with it, as a regular Indie game critic I do get very sick of games that promise an amazing new experience but then don't give you any details to work with, expecting the player to do all the legwork. At the very least, such a game should include a solid set of guidelines (or preferably a customised mechanic, like Ganakagok's world map or Diaspora's Clusters) that aid and enable the players and GM in making these decisions.

      Put bluntly though, somebody will have to do some world-building unless your game is all about what the players do in their everyday lives already. It's just a question if the author wants to do it or leave it to the customer to decide.

      Ultimately if you do absolutely no world building your going to end up setting all your stories in your home town like Will Eisner.

      \n
      \n

      ... which has its place, of course. Bliss Stage, for instance, takes place in the very town where you're sitting right now.

      \n
      \n

      That isn't very practical in an RPG book since most players and GMs will want to know fairly precisely how everything works,

      \n
      \n

      ... or they need to know how to make stuff up that works.

      \n

      A few years back, Morrigan Press created the Terran Trade Authority RPG. The original 1970s TTA books were one of my first science fiction loves, but even as a kid, I could tell that the text was mostly gobbletygook \u2014 some obviously didn't match the pictures, for instance (an alien Pan Am starship?). They also told a history from 2000 to 2100 that included a space war between Terrans, Proximans, and Alphans, exploration of the space between and around them, commercial trading (obviously) and travel. Full of good stuff, and only very hazily explained. The descriptions, when they held together at all, made mysteries, not answers. When, during development, I asked one of the authors of the RPG how they were treating the material, he said, basically, "Well, you have to define everything to play it as an RPG, so we resolved all the mysteries."

      \n

      He was incorrect. Role playing games are creative endeavors. What you need is a system for knowing. Writing down all the answers is sort of the brute force approach. Principled ways to make stuff up a) puts the fun back in the hands of the players and b) makes a practically infinite font of goodies.

      \n

      Let's say we've got Enchivur, a fantasy world, and the protagonists are the only adventurers in the world (as is the case in Lord of the Rings). As the game designer, I've created a map, and it's got latitude and longitude. When you move somewhere on the map, you roll a 2d6, modded by the 12 latitudes and longitudes. That tells you what the people are like who live there. That's 1,728 possibilities, with neighboring people usually being similar to their neighbors. Much more manageable than a list of 1,728 cultures (most of which won't exist in play anyway), isn't it? Now, let's say that there are options so that, when you've already heard of a people in these parts, you can write down five things you've heard about these people, and prioritize them, with the highest priority most likely to be true, and lower priorities less likely to be true, but none of them are certain. And you get names not from the locals (though you can ask) but from the last people you visited, so all the people have names like "Snake Eaters", "Never-Get-Off-Their-Horses", and "Loose Women".

      \n

      What I've done as a designer is made constraints that make sense for this world, but given players fruitful directions to create stuff with them. The canon itself is unreliable \u2014\xa0maybe we even have to flesh out where rivers and coastlines really are. But what is reliable is that you have a way to figure out who's where.

      \n

      Canon is fun to write. But that doesn't mean it's the only, or even a good, way to systematize players' knowledge of the world of the fiction.

      \n

      A character, an Academic, is on a clandestine mission from wealthy Country A to cheap labor source Country B. Border guards in Country B thinks she's a spy plane and shoots her down. She survives, and winds up with limited contact with Academic technology. Within a few weeks, she starts genetically modifying the local crops. I ask, "Wait, you wouldn't have had your whole genetic engineering lab with you on your little ultralight airplane, which in any event, was shot down. You've probably got your standard toolkit, sensors, and weapons. How are you doing this?" So we wind up, the five of us, describing how she's genetic engineering the crops \u2014 the interfaces between the tools she's got and the local tools, how she's teaching people how to use them, and ultimately, how those techniques color the process.

      Because there was setting material, we knew what we had to make up on the spot.
