\n I heard Ben's passionate endorsement of basic D&D; on the Sons of Kryos podcast lately, and it motivated me to dig out my old red box and take another look.
\n
\nSo my question is: did gaming ninjas sneak in and rewrite this thing sometime in the last 10 years? It's great!
\n
\nThe beautiful simplicity of the adventure creation chapter makes me want to run it immediately! And so many great details, like the way there are fewer spells, but they each *do* more than in later incarnations of the game, and how the six stats handle practically everything an adventurer is called upon to do. The advice for players is largely golden, too, with a real focus on at-the-table fun.
\n
\nI had a couple of queries for Ben, or anyone else who's played this version recently:-
\n
\n1) Wizards get a tiny number of spells to cast. Won't they get bored before and after? Their options seem pretty limited.
\n
\n2) Rules Cyclopedia has rules for skills: Good? Bad? Unnecessary? How about the weaponmastery rules in the Master's set?
\n
\n3) There's not a lot in the way of combat manouvres, rules for stunts/cunning actions, or other options to affect your basic values in combat. Is this at all frustrating in play for the fighters?
\n
\nAny thoughts on the above would be appreciated, along with any fuzzy warm feelings towards Tom Moldvay and basic D&D.;

    \n The version I'm talking about is the Moldvay 1981 edition (easiest way to tell: look at the cover. Is it Erol Otus or Larry Elmore. Otus is the one), so concerns like skills systems and "the master set" don't figure into it at all. But let's answer your questions:

    1) That's the wizard player's problem, not the GMs. I've found that there is generally plenty for wizards to do, particularly when you realize that they essentially start with more money than any other class. If the wizard is bored that's the fault of the player: I suggest they play a fighter or dwarf.

    2) These are totally irrelevant, as mentioned above. I would urge people not to graft on additional rules for the first few times you play. I grafted on a skill rule early on and it was totally meaningless. Likewise, weapon specializations just prevent people from using the weapons that they find in the dungeon, which is no fun.

    3) There are four combat maneuvers: Talk, Fight, Run and Wait. All else is details. As a GM, part of your job is handling said details, which means making decisions on the fly about what the players decide to do. So, like, if that guy wants to pour holy water over him as protection from the ghouls? That's up to you to decide how it works. Don't get attached to one particular decisions: if something works well, players will try it over and over, and you might need to nerf it or change how it works. That's okay, it's part of the game.

    Posted By: IgnotusDoes the moldvay edition have particular virtues as opposed to other editions of BD&D (especially the one available on drivethrurpg)?
    \nIt's a little more elegant and easier to expand from. The goodness-per-page ratio is a little better. These aren't make-or-break virtues, IMO. So if you can't find Moldvay's without breaking the bank, you'll do fine with basic.

    Mind you, basic Red has virtues too. That edition seems to come into its own with the box sets that follow. "Expert" (Blue) and "Companion" (Green), in particular, are really functional and evocative, if quirky. The very last box set, Immortal, was fairly bizarre for me at the time but I'm starting to dig the concept, if not the execution.

    If you just want *one* box, go Moldvay, if possible. If you want a complete line, go for the five sets.

    Posted By: James_NostackCan someone who believes there's a significant difference between the 1981 Moldvay and the 1983 Mentzer version explain what the difference is?
    \nRules-wise, the Basic and Expert sets of these editions are identical. (Except in later printings after the Companion, Masters and Immortals sets came out, in which the to-hit and saving throw charts changed a bit, I think) The Mentzer edition was split into a players book and a DM's book, wheres the Moldvay/Cook editions were just single books. The approaches that the two editions take are different. The Mentzer Basic set starts off sort of spoonfeeding you rules info a little at a time, with a solo/gamebook style adventure to introduce them. Moldvay takes the approach of "Here's how you make your character. Here's how you explore. Here's how you fight. Here are the monsters you can meet and here are the treasures you can find. Here's how you DM and make your own adventures." It was much more of a straightforward rulebook. And the artwork was much more varied, which I liked far more than the Elmore work in the Mentzer books.

    \n Despite the fact that I've been roleplaying for almost 25 years, I have never read a D&D book before in my life. I've played games in the D&D family twice. The first was in 1990, when someone in the club I used to game at started an AD&D 2nd ed game. I didn't like the system and we went back to playing WFRP after two weeks. The other time was at CONception in either 2004 or 2005 (I don't remember which) when I ended up playing in an OGL/d20 Conan Game. It was a fun session, but from my POV that was despite the system, not because of it. Kenners can probably tell you more, he was running the game.

    So my past experience with D&D is limited because it's never really appealed. I started gaming with RuneQuest, Fighting Fantasy, Dragon Warriors and MERP. The UK never really had the D&D marketing that the US seemed to have and by the time I was gaming, Games Workshop were doing UK versions of a lot of stuff. D&D was pretty marginal in the circles I moved in as a kid. By the late 80s it was all about WFRP and then the early 90s bought me to Shadowrun and then Vampire etc. So I never did the D&D thing, never owned a Red Box and never calculated a THAC0.

    However, I did pick up a copy of Moldvay last week after Ben's evangelism on that podcast.

    Reading through it is... interesting. I'm approaching it the way I tend to approach indie/hippy games which is to say, with no pre-conceived ideas of how it should be played. The text does a wonderful job of conveying what the game is about: there's no "story" bullshit - this is a game about adventuring and getting treasure. The players are after the goodies in the dungeon and anything in their way is either going down, getting argued with or getting run away from. If you try to look at this as some kind of rules for simulating medieval adventures, it breaks very badly. The Alignment Languages make exactly fuck all sense for example. But on the other hand, I've been playing games like Legend Of Zelda for just as long as I've been playing RPGs and I can deal with the fact that sometimes in a fictional world when you cut long grass there's just money there to be had. I can deal with the rather bizarre economy in Hyrule, and I can deal with this sort of thing in D&D too, as long as it's in a "That's Just How It Is" context. In the same way, I can deal with the stupid "Forget a spell once you've cast it" and "'Veteran' Warriors often have few enough hitpoints to survive a single sword blow" rules.

    I can seriously see this being quite a bit of fun if approached in the right way. I intend to run it next Thursday, I'll probably run the adventure from the rulebook and we'll see how it goes.

    I have to say though, there's one thing that bothers me: The idea of the game seems to be to go up in levels. But from reading the book (and Ben/Rich/Vincent's experiences seem to back this up) is seems really bloody hard to actually survive long enough to get to second level! Ah well, looks good for a laugh.

    \n Well, I mentioned this thread to a couple of people and we decided to give old-skool Moldvay D&D a try. I've run two dungeons in the last couple of days and have the following observations:

    1. Ben is exactly right about the footprint of the game. Both adventures took around than 2-2.5 hours. Character creation is mostly randomized. Yes, you get to pick a class, but depending on your rolls, certain classes might not even be available to you.

    2. Letting the game be what it is works. The way I read the rules, dungeons are all you have in the basic book -- the wilderness rules are in the Expert book. So all adventures, by definiton, start in media res. You can fill in backstory as you go, but you're immediately in situation. The players have some immediate goals laid out in front of them (stay alive, try to complete the over-arching 'mission', get treasure (for the XP!), etc). And the reactions (Talk, Fight, Run or Wait) make their options crystal clear.

    3. The randomizer is interesting. It basically develops up set-pieces. There's a room. Does it have anything interesting in it? If it does, see if it's a monster, a trap, or something special. Now see if there's treasure in it. Treasure is appropriate to monster-type if there are any. Repeat. As the adventurers explore, they hit set-piece after set-piece (along with whatever you've put in the dungeon because you want it there), with some down-time (corridors, in which they might hit wandering monsters) in between.

    4. The game eats level one characters for lunch, then roars for more. I think we lost something like 4 or 5 characters in the course of an adventure. They started dropping like flies from the very first encounter. The first time it happened, I said the players could run back to town and come back a few weeks later (it actually worked well story-wise). They came back with more people. More people died. Like instanteously.

    5. The randomizer is really, really random. Like insanely random. See AP below.

    6. The combat is deadly, deadly boring. Which is odd, given the above. You'd think that since it's so kill-friendly, it would be interesting/nerve-wracking/exciting. But I didn't find it so. Combat is basically Rock-em Sock-em Robots. The DM and the players are rolling a d6 (initiative) then a d20 (to hit) over and over again until one of them gets a certain target number. One might have a slight advantage over the other, depending on AC (you have to roll at least 17, I have to roll at least 15), but that's about it. Hit points are so low at this level that the d6/d8 you roll for damage can sometimes seem superfluous. When you hit, apparently, you hit HARD.

    \n When I first brought up the Moldvay edition to my partner, I did it with the understanding that this was the game he, quite literally, grew up with. The Moldvay edition was the one he'd bought for his friend when he was a kid and they proceeded to play it obsessively. They eventually graduated to the Expert set, then on to Advanced D&D, etc, etc. So nostalgia was definitely present.

    For me it, was pretty different. Yes, D&D was my first role-playing game (I started with AD&D second edition), but I pretty much stopped in High School and never look back (I 'graduated' to White Wolf's games, if that's the right word, then got over my undergrad emo phase and put *those* long behind me), but I never felt any real nostalgia for the actual system. Settings? Absolutely. I'd still love to play the Planescape again someday -- just not using D&D.

    We tried out the Moldvay generator one-on-one and had fun. Quick, simple trip into a temple (the mission was 'Exploring the Unknown') that had long been abandonded by one of the character's sect. They eventually find the zombified ancestor of the other character and have to kill him. They end up with a Staff of Healing. Definitely fun, though I noted that concealed/secret doors are REALLY HARD TO FIND, even for Elves.

    So then we mentioned this to our friend Dan, who mentioned he'd listened to the podcast where Ben talked about old-skool D&D and we decided to try it again.

    I used the generator (in the meantime I'd coded-up a computer version of it) to roll up an adventure with about six rooms of encounters. We went with 'Destroying an Ancient Evil'. Four characters, two players: the Cleric and Elf from the first adventure (minus staff -- we said this was a flashback before that adventure) played by Neil, and a thief and a mage played by Dan. The first two were Lawful and Neutral, the second two were Chaotic (this became important). They're exploring a burial ground holy to the Cleric.

    The very first thing I roll up is a group of 3 Dwarves. Dwarves have Type-G treasure. The generator says they have treasure. I roll against the G treasure table and see that they have 6,000 platinum pieces. Which means they have 30,000 gold pieces. Which means these three characters (one of whom rolled 1 HP on his 1 HD) are worth 30,000 XP. To get to the next level from 1, you need about 1,200 - 1,500 depending on class, and then it usually doubles from then on. Which means these dwarves were worth 4-5 levels of XP.

    I did a double-take at that. That seems absolutely insane to me, but I went with it.

    So the encounter goes something like this:

    Thief is sent forward to scout (he succeeds on his roll), sees the dwarves ahead, arguing amongst themselves. One is really wounded (1 hp!) and is trying to get the others to leave. The other two look like they're hanging back. I roll morale to see what happens -- they decide to leave, right through the party who is still at the entrance.

    Thief runs back to warn the party. They're ready, the dwarves are surprised. Roll reaction -- uncertain, but holding their place. The cleric speaks Lawful alignment to them. It turns out there's a dwarven stronghold underneath the area they're exploring. These dwarves were travelling to get to it, but were waylaid and lost the rest of their party. The Cleric (undiplomatically) asks them if they're carrying anything they took from the burial grounds.

    The head dwarf places his hand on his warhammer and says that no, they didn't, and that they're leaving now.

    Which is when the Mage (who couldn't understand a word of what was being said) decided to rip the dwarf's head off with a Magic Missile (he rolled ridiculously high).

    Battle ensues, morale is rolled (and passed), and the dwarves fight to the death, killing the Mage and the Cleric in the process.

    The surviving Elf and Thief search the bodies and discover they're wealthy beyond their wildest dreams.

    We could, quitely literally, have ended it there. Why on Earth press on into an obviously insanely dangerous area (they lost two of their party on the first encounter) when you can go home and count your 15,000 GP?

    We role-played the Elf and Thief talking out what they would do. The Mage, it turns out, was the idiot cousin of the Thief (who's got tons of idiot cousins, as you'll see), and he has no problem burying him and walking off with his loot. The Elf is a little more sorrowful, but has no problem walking off with the money -- though he notes that he'll spend a share (precisely the amount he needs to reach level 2) on a small monument to the Cleric on the spot where she fell.

    I'll continue with their next trip into the dungeon.

    \n The Elf and Thief leave. They spend their gold. They level up to level 2 (it didn't seem right for them to be able to level up to a level beyond Basic before we even got to the second room of the adventure), and then come back with two other characters: a hugely buffed Fighter, another cousin of the Thief (he rolled *really* well -- 17 16 16 I think) and another member of the Cleric's sect. The Thief convinces his cousin to quit the army and come with him by telling him stories of weak Dwarves wandering about with tons of money. The Cleric is a dour, suspicious man who seems to be along to find out what really happened to the first Cleric.

    At this point, we realized the story was really about the Elf's descent from Neutral to Chaotic. The whole game became more and more about justifications for covering up what they did to the dwarves (they ended up not telling anyone about the platinum) and their greed for more stuff. At some point someone said it felt like a medieval version of Reservoir Dogs.

    The party heads further into the burial grounds and stumbles upon the hive of 3 Killer Bees. The Thief cowers behind the FIghter who bravely steps forward and slices at the creature. The creature stings him. I glance over the rules for Killer Bees. They die when they sting, but then the character they hit has to roll a Save against Poison. Oh, ok, and then they lose HP? No...I read further. They roll against Poison...or DIE.

    I blinked a couple of times at that one. Regardless of level, a character stung by a 1/2 HD creature can die if they don't get their saving throw. (The creature, with this insane power, is worth something like 8 XP).

    So hugely buffed armored Fighter dude died in the first 15 seconds of the second run through the dungeon.

    They kill the rest of them bees. They find the hive has magical honey (heals 1-4 hp) in it. They press on.

    They come upon the central area of the burial ground. The encounter I rolled was a talking statue. So I decide it's a statue of the Cleric's goddess. It starts chanting a litany of the Elf and Thief's sins (liars, thieves, desecrators, etc). The Cleric falls to his knees in wonder. The Thief decides to hit him in the back with a crossbow bolt before he realizes what the statue is saying and turns on him and the Elf. He misses.

    We kind of looked at each other at that point. I asked Neil for the Cleric's character sheet and we played out the battle, with the Cleric now a Monster according to the rules.

    They end up killing the Cleric. Dan looked up at me at that point and said "How much XP do you think he's worth?"

    They search the tombs to find they've been already looted. Oh -- so that's where the platinum came from. The characters look at each other, shrug, then stuff the body of the cleric in a tomb and set it on fire using a vial of oil. No evidence left.

    The randomizer says they hit an alarm that brings a monster. I roll on the Wandering Monster table and end up with 8 Acolytes. Perfect.

    The now shouting statue brings a group of suspicious clerics who are also here to find out what's going on in the burial grounds. An uncomfortable exchange ensues. Just as the matter seems to be headed to blows, they hear sounds to the south.

    What happened here was that I just decided to throw the remaining encounters together.

    They find a group of hurt dwarves (yup, I rolled dwarves again -- these didn't have treasure, though) surrounded by 11 wolves. Yes, eleven. That's what I rolled.

    Battle ensues. To make it interesting, I played the wolves, the players played everyone else.

    The wolves are obviously supernatural. They go after the Acolytes when they can, but avoid their leader, who seems to have a glowing holy symbol.

    Several Acolytes die, but in the end, the wolves are defeated.

    And the characters are again confronted with their sins.

    At which point they ran away. The End.

    We could have continued -- in fact Dan said he wanted to find out what happened to these guys and the Dwarven stronghold and the Ancient Evil to which the wolves were obviously connected but I decided was probably a couple levels further down. Fun was definitely had by all.

    I don't know if I'd do it again, though. Like I said, my biggest complaints are the difficulty and the fact that I really didn't like the combat system. The thing is - could you have a (in my view) fun, interesting combat system which still had the tiny footprint for character and dungeon creation this version of D&D provides?

Actually it's most nothing in the rooms, but there are two rooms of Goblins, one of Hobgoblins and a green slime (also a Crab Spider, but the players did not go that way).

    Character creation was a bit of a faff. Bearing in mind that there were 3 players and the game is clearly designed with parties of 5-8 characters in mind. It was decided to have two characters each, for a party of 6 characters. I've never created a D&D character before and neither had Claire. Dave had played Moldvay as a kid, but that's at least 20 years ago. Greg had played AD&D in the 80s, but nothing since. Creating 6 characters took quite some time. Stats were rolled, then rolled again (because Dave managed to get two "hopeless" characters in a row). Classes were chosen for these vestigial characters, but given that Claire's never played a game with character classes before and neither Dave nor Greg have an understanding of how the classes work in this version of the game it was fairly time consuming. They ended up with an obvious Elf and Dwarf, an obvious Thief and three characters who were firmly average. They ended up being a Halfling, a cleric and a Magic User for fairly arbitrary reasons. Next came the fairly fiddly "attribute Adjustment" thing and the noting of bonuses, special class abilities and what they meant. Then there was the whole "shopping for equipment" thing, which wasn't so bad because the list of equipment is mercifully short.

    In all, the character creation took quite a while. Probably an hour in total, although it seemed longer. Things would go a lot quicker if we ever do it again, because people now understand roughly what does what.

    The players ended up with one character they liked each and one which they had zero investment in. Restricting them to one character each might help with this, but I suspect that I'd just end up with players who like their characters feeling invested in them and those who didn't feeling excluded and disenfranchised, but there you are.

    Play was fun, but very random. The players seemed to use the "spare" characters as cannon fodder to clear the way for their "proper" characters. Dave had the Cleric (with, y'know, no spells at all) skipping merrily down a corridor with gaye abandon ahead of the party because the characters stats were rubbish and he expected her to trigger any traps and die. She had a very low Int score, which is why such a tactic seemed appropriate. there was some fun in character conversations on the topic. As it happens, she did die, but it was at the hands of some Goblins who surprised the players coming out of another room and just plain old bad luck which is very unforgiving in this game. The Thief (Shifty McSneak) proved very effective with her longbow despite having her leather armour destroyed in an encounter with the green slime. Grupp the Dwarf seemed to be an effective fighter and Leaf-Leaf the Elf was generally pretty useful to have around.

    I dunno, we had fun, but I think that it'll be more fun next time. Now that I've seen things in action I think I can make use of the random tables and stuff and let the game breathe a bit.

    \nI find them a bit dry myself, but I think they'd be even drier without some nod to the environment. For actual play, I'm more and more tempted to suggest to my crew that we steal ideas (feats, the IH stuff that was mentioned) from more modern incarnations of D&D and simply use them as modifiers rather than getting very in-depth.

    Minis use is a bit strange to me, coming from a background of largely dropping rpgs in favor of minis games for about a decade or so. Before really getting into minis games, my D&D combats were a bit like you described-roll until someone dies. After playing minis games, I tend to think about things like ganging up, flanks, cheaty tactics, high-ground, all kinds of stuff really ( and the way rpgs work actually let that stuff matter more than in most minis games).

    Personally, I'd throw in some easy modifiers as quick house-rules. Some are already suggested for cover, and a very sort of one-sentence discussion of other modifiers occurs in the combat section of the rules, so you aren't totally without justification. I'd be careful about mods more than +/- 3, however. I don't believe much in the way opf damage mods are discussed at all.

      \n A few thoughts on doors:
      \n
      \nFirst, by making it almost certain an open door will close, it eliminates a huge amount of book keeping on the part of the GM. Think about how hard it would be to keep track of which doors are open and which are closed in a large dungeon.
      \n
      \nAnother thought is that having doors be closed by default and tough to open or keep open, action is constrained to a small locality. By constraining the action at any given time to a small area makes the game easier to manage. It also helps deal with some of the problems of the close proximity of monsters in the dungeon where logic defies why they would be any where near each other.
      \n
      \nMaking it hard for the PCs to open doors also makes sure exploration takes time, which means more random encounters, and perhaps a more interesting resource management problem.
      \n
      \nIs the text describing doors automatically opening for the monsters still in there?
      \n
      \nIt is interesting to see various bits and pieces still in the rules when the game (as presented in the text) has drifted away from the "mega-dungeons" (a many level dungeon that is designed to be the setting for an entire campaign) that were the way the game started (at least Gygax's campaign) when those bits and pieces make the most sense for mega-dungeons (but then to some extent, I feel that can be said of the whole rules set).
