\n Um, are there any story-telling/narrative type RPGs that use some form of "story points" to move a plot or group forward through the story? I'm thinking about maybe each character having a set number of points that they can contribute to the story/narrative to have their characters do something. The larger the action/effect on the story, the more points it requires to do.
\n
\nIf so, and we assume a collaborative story effort, is there any reason to have a winner based on how many points they've added to the story? (Or should it be a more traditional we all win/lose sort of affair?)
\n
\nThoughts?

    \n Yes! The excellent Superhero RPG "With Great Power".

    Instead of "points" though, it's playing cards (five physical cards, though it could have just as easily been Stones or Coins or whatever). Each conflict you Lose, you can put a card on a track.

    At the beginning of the campaign, you can not possibly beat/defeat the antagonist: Rulewise it just can't happen. As you proceed through each of the five phases of the With Great Power track, the rules about how the story work change a little.

    Excellent game, check it out!

    \n This happens to some extent in The Shadow of Yesterday and games that are based on a similar form of play (like Apocalypse World):

    Characters collect "points" by contributing to the story by interacting with it in specific ways (basically, by pushing the drama along and/or heightening dramatic change to the characters themselves), which very quickly increase the scale at which they can contribute.

    It's fairly removed from what you're describing (neither has "story points" per se), but I believe the effect is derived from the same principle:

    Use currency to contribute to the fiction; gain more currency as a reward; use that gain in power to make greater contributions, and so on.

    \nFUBAR uses this concept in reverse.

    Basically the oracle/GM, has a pool of points to use for the generation of complications and complexity throughout the course of the story.

    The fewer of these points possessed by the oracle/GM at the end of the session, the closer the protagonists have come to achieving their goals.

    \n That mention of GM's budget reminds me of 3:16 and Agon (were you expecting me to say PtA?).
    In both of the those games, the GM has a number of 'points' to spend on scenes and challenges, and when they are gone, the players have achieved their goals (or utterly failed). You cant finish while those points remain (not strictly true, especially in Agon which is pretty loose, but that's the concept), so they make a good pacing mechanism.
