God-king


Hey all,

I am almost finished with the design for my game, God-king, so I thought it would be a good time to give it a public showing. The game is about a journey to a lost continent with Chinese and Japanese folklore stylings. Its focus is on collaborative storytelling and it provides mechanics to push the game in interesting directions. For example, the game relies on each of the players assuming an important role (the Scholar, Warrior, Mystic, Navigator, or Healer). Each role is integral to the journey so the group is penalized in various ways if some of the members are separated from them. This means they have to work together to succeed, but everyone also has an ulterior motive for joining the journey that will likely force them to go against the others at some point.

This release is geared specifically towards gathering playtest information which means it has been stripped of some content so that you can get right into the thick of it and start playing and then, hopefully, provide feedback after a quick session or two. If you provide me with a playtest report, I will gladly credit you and your group, should you wish it, in the final version of the game once I have completed it.

You can get the release at my site here. I would appreciate it very much if you could send me a playtest report either here or through my email at static.veil@gmail.com If you have any questions or comments about the game, feel free to ask away, as well.

Thank you,
-Daniel

    \n@Sam!: Thanks! I'm glad it looks good. I just threw it together yesterday so it's not as nice as it could be. The design of the final version will be much different from this one.

    The game definitely works well for one-and-done play. In fact, that's mostly what I had in mind as I designed it. The details as to what I feel is the best way to end the game are left out as I felt the playtest version should be more flexible, however, it is certainly more geared towards short-term gaming. You should be able to run the game over one session as the details of the "Journey" are left to be decided by the Narrator.

    I hope you do get the chance to give it a go. I am also open to hearing about what you think about it even from just the standpoint of reading the text.

    @Hans c-o: That's a very good observation and I admit I probably should have stuffed a larger portion of the Narrator content into the playtest release. As a Narrator, you should prepare an adventure beforehand - make up the plot points and the conflicts the players may have to deal with. The final version of the game will have a more fully-fleshed out guideline for the Journey, but it will definitely be meant as just that, a guideline. In God-king, the Narrator is encouraged to come up with his own interpretation of what the adventure involves. The material provided is more of a prompt than anything else - you should think of what kind of story it inspires you to tell and then go ahead and tell that story by presenting it to your players.

    I hope that helps a bit. If you would like further explanation, please let me know. I am very interested in hearing about what you have to say about the game. I will do my best to help you with anything you need to know about the game.

    \n Hey Daniel,

    Very cool alpha draft. Though, like Hans, I have a few questions about how it's intended to be used.

    I assume you've read and played The Mountain Witch, huh? The games feel really similar, though I honestly prefer the short-and-sweet text you have here to the lengthy explanations and overly detailed resolution (with more complex levels of success) of The Mountain Witch, which is a game that, in my mind, could easily be presented in 20 pages or so. Also, with The Mountain Witch being out of print (due to the decision of the author), there's definitely room for another game operating in roughly the same space.

    My biggest concern, starting out, is the super lengthy opening description of events on pages 10-11, which read like a series of cut-scenes rather than events that the players can actually play through. I'd much prefer either just a simple starting situation like "You land on the beach; this is what you see" or just cutting to meeting the princess and her presenting the group with some choices. This lengthy description reads like I (as the GM) am supposed to railroad the players through the early events of play and that their choices don't matter at all.

    On the other hand, your description of the various locations and inhabitants of the lost land are evocative enough and just the right length and level of detail. As GM, I can take those descriptions and run with them, rather feeling trapped like I do with the opening description. Any chance you can just describe the beach, the longhouse, and what not in the same terms, freeing up the players to actually play through the early events and make meaningful choices?

    Also, like Hans, I'd like to know more about what my job is as GM. How should I push the players so that the game ends with a giant bloodbath of recriminations like The Mountain Witch does? Without a central focus (like the witch), it's a bit more difficult, though it seems like things will come to a head around a few interactions, such as the players deciding to re-board the ships and return to their homeland. I'm a bit worried, though, that the betrayals might come earlier on and then the person doing the betraying won't actually die or achieve their ends, leaving them in a kind of in-between place, no longer welcome in the "party" but also unresolved both in their goals and in their existence, lingering around but with little real agency. I'd have to play it, of course, to know if this concern would actually be an issue.

    Finally, the only other question I have is about when to engage the resolution mechanics. Surely the example of "lifting a heavy rock" is a bit spurious unless lifting that rock is somehow important to the game. Otherwise, why not just lift it? Sure, GMs could just use a "common sense" approach, but it'd be nice to know a bit more about what the game is supposed to be about and, thus, when engaging the mechanics is appropriate and when it's a distraction from the core game experience.

    All in all, though, very cool and I look forward to seeing how it progresses and maybe giving it a shot at some point.

    \n Hey Daniel,

    I'm actually pretty stunned that you wrote most of this game without knowing about The Mountain Witch. I mean, I picked up your game and saw:

    -- group of very different characters only brought together by a shared mission
    -- secret goals that are in tension and sometimes mutually exclusive
    -- freeform traits that are mostly descriptive, not mechanical
    -- simple resolution that has degrees of success
    -- PC trust measured and used to improve or impede rolls
    -- GM guidelines that allow them to generate obstacles and situations based on specific locales on the journey
    -- game ends when the journey is over and PCs achieve their secret goals or not

    I mean, it's cool that you came up with this combination independently and some of them clearly follow from each other, but wow. That's nuts.

    Reading over your new GM guidelines, I'll admit to being a bit more confused than before. Previously, I was expecting that the GM set up situations, but it was up to the players how they would deal with them and the GM would basically follow the players' lead based on where they went and what they did (i.e. that it was intended to be run in a strongly "sandbox" style). But now, it seems like the GM is supposed to do much more planning, determining specific events and set-pieces before play begins. If that's true, I guess I'd like more information about how the GM is supposed to do this and how to present them to the players. I'm used to more GM-planning in games that have a "mission-based" structure (like Mouse Guard, Bliss Stage, etc.), because there are set tasks that the characters have to accomplish, but I didn't get that sense here, initially.

    To summarize, I guess, if I'm going to run this for some folks, I want both me and the players to be clear about when the players get to choose what to do and when certain things are set by just agreeing to play the game (like going into the dungeon in D&D or accepting the mission in Mouse Guard).

    \n@stefoid: Yes, sir. Though I will definitely be revising that section so it is more clear.

    @J. Walton: A lot of the content, I feel, is more reminiscent of indie RPGs in general than The Mountain Witch specifically. Most of my experience with RPGs has come from the ones that are made freely available as well as freeform RPGs that I used to play on play-by-post sites like RPoL in which all of these elements are present. My game design concepts have also come from videogames (the main place where I learned that backstabbing other players as a mechanic is somehow fun +_+) and card and boardgames. The latter two are more limited to what I've read about them, though. Just as with RPGs, I've only really ever been able to "afford" the free ones.

    For the Narrator section, I'm not sure how much of a snag I have hit upon. I feel that I may have underestimated how much detail is required to explain how the game is put into motion based on the problems you are having with it. I intended for the game to be relatively easy to plan and play, but it seems I may have made it appear more complicated than necessary. My idea was for Narrators to be able come up with some ideas, following the suggestions in the text but definitely allowing for them to customize their game, and then quickly put them into play with a minimum of preparation. This preparation would only require some notes of the general story the Narrator has in mind (following what the game lays out, this might be, "The adventurers must go to the Cold Palace retrieve the mask of the God-king for the Princess.") and some things the Narrator might like to see (a pair of ghostly children that ask for the group to give them their most valuable member; the group has to cross a rickety bridge - a chance to turn on the others?). Ideally, the Narrator would not require a high level of structure and is mostly meant to "go with the flow," if you will. The ideas, other than the players' main objective, would not be set in stone and the Narrator should adapt to the path the players take while still presenting interesting obstacles for the players (they may go West instead of East, but that doesn't mean you can't have them come across a hostile village or introduce a demon).

    I may have been too vague in my wording of the Narrator's section. I will review it and see how I can change it to better represent the game's ideas. I would very much like any more feedback you could provide in regards to this.
