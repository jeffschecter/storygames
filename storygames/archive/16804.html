\n There's a thing that happens sometimes in games, I call it doll housing. Have you experienced it? If so has it been a problem? I'll explain what I mean.

It happens sometimes when you start off a game where the players create their characters prior to the first session. They bring them with them and then share. Some folks write theirs up, some don't, and some people write a novel doing so. That last one is the doll house. It's like being asked to bring some toys to the sand box to play with the other kids, and dragging over your whole own doll house: furniture, dolls, tiny miniature dishes, pets, the works.

It's kind of an anti-social behavior, but one that really doesn't seem like it should be a problem. How awesome is it for the players to get so involved in the game that they make up all this great shit before you even start? But it can gum up the works terribly. Are the other players supposed to read the novel? Is the GM supposed to take it in and incorporate the material that's been created into the world they have created? Or get the other players on board? Why do you get to have us play in your doll house? Weren't we here to build sand castles together?

Of course, I'm describing this in terms of a traditional game. Where there is a GM, who shares a world, with players who aren't involved or empowered to make it along with them. Many, many story games nip this in the bud by making all the players create the world and their characters together. Going round the table, each person gets to have input that's fairly equally weighted. But it can still happen there. In fact, the egalitarian nature of games where you've got a more distributed authority among the players opens the door to people getting deeply inspired about something in the world. Then the other players, and perhaps a GM, have to make decisions about how to deal with it. And if that person spends so much time in their own dollhouse, they can get isolated and not help build the shared experience that the others are there for. The more collaborative and improv'ed the game is, the more everyone relies on having the creative support of all the players engaged. It can suck energy out of the shared play.

On the other side of the coin, some of the most deeply satisfying games I've been in have been open invitations to doll house. But then, everyone wanted to spend time in each other's houses. Bring on high tea with those tiny, tiny dishes. That was what the game was about: exploring the worlds that the others had framed, and fleshing them out by playing characters within them.

I'm curious to see if this is a common issue. And how it works out for other people.

    My first question is to what degree this behaviour is distinct from 'pre-playing' one's character. Is it a sub-set, or a branch off some earlier ur-tree? It sounds like dollhousing is more about coming to the game with a whole lot of your character's environment (and entourage) already built up in advance, as opposed to coming to the game with all the character's crucial story-decisions already made. That seems like an interesting distinction. Can a game help a player avoid one, while still encouraging (or failing to adequately discourage) the other?

    In any case you have described almost precisely the dominant mode of roleplay on online MU* (MUSH, MUX) environments, at least circa 1995-2002 -- which also happened to be my formative 'serious roleplaying' years. These mostly-freeform games (also mostly-WoD) consisted almost entirely of everybody building their most elaborate, multi-faceted, well-manicured dollhouse/diorama/backstoried characters and then taking turns showing them off to each other.

    Consequently, oh yes -- I do this all the time. So, so much. A lot less now, of course, but many of the same impulses remain strong even when playing games specifically designed to avoid this sort of behaviour.

    And one thing I will say is that while I agree there is some part of dollhousing that results from a genuine, personal enthusiasm about one's creation, I think it only gets to truly pathological levels when you add to that a distrust or even paranoia concerning what your fellow-players will do to/with your character, if you don't pre-emptively paint in every last detail.

    This was definitely my experience with online play, at least. So few of my fellow-players -- and there were hundreds of them on a typical MU* -- seemed to share even my general aesthetic, let alone the slightest investment in my particular character, and so I rapidly discovered that one of the best ways to get the sort of play I wanted was to create fully-realized 'sets' -- actual places, yes, but also NPCs and archetypal situations and bits of dialogue/interaction -- and then just wait until another player wandered into them. The more fully-formed I could make the starting situation, and the more avenues I had to demonstrate the sort of tone I was interested in, the better the odds that the other player would fall in line (or more generously, would have a chance of understanding what I was interested in.)

    For example, I would frequently create extremely detailed places of work for my characters, complete with like a full-network-sitcom list of NPC cast members, each of whom I would make as (or more) detailed as many of the other PCs -- backstories, personality, and most importantly a sort of generalized repertoire of interaction. I often played scenes where I was controlling my own PC as well as one or two NPCs -- not interacting with myself, but rather doubling up on the vectors by which I could interact with the other players, the better to demonstrate the sort of play I wanted. And, theoretically, the better to attract interested players through a wider variety of 'hooks' into my own character's story.

    In other words, crazy-ass control freak behaviour. That everybody aspired to, appreciated, and generally tried to imitate. Because the thing about playing with 100 fellow-players instead of 4 is that it actually isn't very reasonable to expect them to be all that invested in your character, or to have any sort of understanding of what sort of world you are imagining them living in. So if you have high expectations for continuity and consistency between all your character's scenes, the only person who is going to bring that to the table is you -- and sometimes you are going to have to bring it extremely aggressively to the table, or another player will unwittingly kick a hole in the wall of your dollhouse, because that's where they figured the door was supposed to be.

    \n I find this behavior really fascinating, creative, psychologically interesting. I tend to do this when I write fiction. I can spend days in a character's head before she lands on paper. Once there, characters have to adapt to the environment I threw them into.

    In games, I do this to some extent after the first or second session. I start thinking about a character deeply, about her background, about things that wouldn't come up in a game, but when I bring her back to the table, any of those things that I've imagined in her background that are incompatible with the other players' concept of the world, their characters, or my character, I drop so that my character can fit into the shared fiction.

    On reflection of my own dollhousing behavior and my observations of others doing it, I'd say it's potentially anti-social, but when it's dysfunctional it's generally just self-indulgent. Is this more potentially anti-social? I have seen this behavior work well when players have short sessions, or when dollhousers move their furniture around to accommodate others. Then again, I have played with someone who did this in an annoying way because the player had a difficult time merging their concept of their character with the pre-established world. Ultimately the character was anti-social (a typical mode for the player) and difficult for others to engage with. There were other non-game factors at play though. The dollhousing was more like the cherry on top.

    On the flip side, I think this is very common in larps, but it's the GM who dollhouses, and the players have to buy into their roles. I've seen this work well and fail miserably. I've also played table-top games with one GM who does this for every game she runs. I find that if there are qualities about my character that I just don't like I downplay them, and it's mostly not a big deal.

    So for example, there was this Vampire: tR game where the players were assigned pre-gen characters. The GM took us aside and told us how we were all embraced and what you clan was. We picked our names, and I got to pick my flaw, but that was about it. I ended up with a Nosferatu. I really, really didn't want to play a Nos. If I'm going to play V:tR, I do not want to be a disgusting vampire, so I gave my Nos dude a beautiful and romantic name. His flaw was that he was so handsome that he looked unreal and doll like, so perfectly charming, yet he triggered people's serial killer radar. So he was disgusting in a way I could play, but fit the image of the type of vampire I prefer to play. The GM didn't expect the beautiful serial killer flaw, I think it might have clashed with some of her plans, but we managed to merge our notions of this character.

    So to answer the questions (maybe rhetorical?), dollhousing can be a pretty neat and effective thing when you're master and commander of the characters and setting, as in creative writing. As long as the fiction you create in your dollhouse is mutable and you're willing to alter the character to fit better into the shared space, it's pretty cool to have and play with a well-developed character who has baggage and history that may never come up (like in real life relationships). It's dysfunctional when the doll house is inflexible.

    So my question is how do we as spectators and participants and community members help doll housing players let us in to do rearranging so that we can have fun, too?

    And is it a bad thing for a GM to do this? I know I hate getting character sheets longer than 2 pages when I larp, and I've been trying over the past couple larps I've written to create open ended characters with char sheets no longer than a page. The last game I did I had a couple of players who initially had a tough time with this, but at game, this creative license for the players was perfect for the game.

    \nInteresting observations! I really like that term, too, "Doll House".

    I'm thinking that there might be two subsets of this behavior, the "Open Doll House" and "Closed Doll House", for lack of better terms...

    In both cases, someone comes to Session One with a long backstory on their character. And the character is definitely "my character", with a lot of behaviors determined not by the story, but like most classic RPG by only the will of the player ("My character wouldn't do that", etc).

    In the Open Doll House, the player makes up a lot of backstory, but fully leaves open anything that pertains to adventuring or interacting with other PCs. In other words, the long backstory that ultimately might not really mean much -- at all -- in play, but is there to not only flesh out the character, but to help the player come to understand/immerse into her. The player puts "getting along with the other characters, progressing the story/adventure" above "my character's story", so leaves a lot of points of potential contention open (in other words, doesn't create backstory/behavior that would create conflict with the other PCs, or with the flow of the story/adventure.

    In the Closed Doll House the player does the same but puts "my character's story" above "getting along with the other characters, progressing the story/adventure". The long backstory still might not come out much in play, but in this case... sometimes the player brings things into the game through the character's actions that hints at the backstory or character secrets in a way that doesn't make sense to the other players or GM (unless they read the 10 pages of backstory). It may or may not shiv the scenario a little, as the PC turns left instead of right, or interacts poorly with key NPCs because of something they wrote in their backstory.

    In both cases, the other players really have no authorship into that character's backstory, it's all set up and ready to use (nothing to "make").

    At the very least, I've definitely seen Doll Housing, and within that these two kind of distinct styles of "doll housing" to boot.

    Interesting!

    \n Hi, My name is Michael...and I'm a Doll Houser.

    I should clarify this, by adding another identifying axis to the doll house matrix...Sharing and Possessive.

    On this axis, I'd be a Sharing Doll-houser and my wife would be a Possessive Doll-houser. Depending on the game, we might be open or closed...but mostly we start closed and gradually reveal what is necessary when the time is right. We can each write dozens of pages to get ourselves into the headspace of a character before the first session. I think this is a throwback to our decade of LARPing.

    As a sharing Doll-houser, I write an open history with dozens of loose plot hooks that can be thrown at other players to link the play group together. As a possessive doll-houser, my wife writes her character history to barricade herself from the outside and justify her actions in game even when a more social form of behaviour might be more conducive to play.

    I've played with all four...Sharing Open, Sharing Closed, Possessive Open and Possessive Closed.

    \n I've not encountered this much, certainly not in an overt way but there's also a sort of hidden dolls' house which occurs. This is when a player clearly has an agenda for their character, they will behave in a certain way or go after specific goals, however they never tell the other players exactly what these are. Sometimes it's even apparent that the player themself is not aware of their agenda, and they pursue it in an ill-defined way, being obstructive to other play because they know they want something and it's not what's happening now.

    There's also a half-way house between building a background in secret and doing it together. Night's Black Agents requires the characters to be ex-agents caught up in a vampire conspiracy. Characters have a drive, which is why they might be fighting the vampires (revenge, duty etc) and also a background on which service employed them and to what end (American assassin, Russian hacker, French dilletante, ...).
    At the start, the characters don't necessarily know each other but are on a joint mission which reveals the conspiracy. The group is required to handle this quite carefully, ensuring that the drives and agendas of the agents don't overshadow the conspiracy. There are some guidelines in the book on how this can be achieved, chief of which is to threaten everyone with something worse than they imagined and hence to develop a common, immediate and very threatening enemy but there are subtler ways.

Playing non-trad games people can lose sight of the normative boundaries (ie I say what my character does, you say about yours) and push past.

    Just to be clear, Emily \u2013 doll housing would then be analogous to "pre-play," only with a precocious sort of value ascribed to the outcomes of pre-play?

    "No, no \u2013 you couldn't be my character's brother. His name is Thamryn and he owns a barber shop across the bridge."
    Good question. How I define pre-play is saying what events will occur during play, similar to what Jason talked about up above.

    OH! I can pull out some of my favorite new terminology to get at this. Doll housing has the players creating restrictive constraints through affordances in the game world that they have created on their own. (These are concepts that Eirik Fatland discussed at Solmukohta.) Evan, in your example, the player who says "no my brother is a barber across the bridge" is constraining what someone else can add to the fiction. The affordance is their assertion about who & what the brother is already defined to be.

    So in an open Doll house you've got constraints with openings for other to build on them and ascribe meaning (a mandalorian, if you will). The Share-friendly Dollhouser will be open to offers and affordances made by others about the things they've created.You might even create the "hooks" that up-thread IceCreamEmperor talked about in the MUs. (Great examples by the way!)

    Possessive dollhousers would resist the affordances of others, like the example of Vulpinoid's wife:
    As a possessive doll-houser, my wife writes her character history to barricade herself from the outside and justify her actions in game even when a more social form of behaviour might be more conducive to play.
    And I can well imagine the real world game experiences that would inspire someone to take this approach! Doll housing is absolutely affective in motivation, but and defensiveness can be a motivating factor that leads to isolation and creative dysfunction.

    So back to the pre-play question. In pre-play, you create affordances not only about what has happened in the world, but about what will happen in the future. You've got your story mapped out. So you're creating constraints on the affordances of others based on what events you want to unfold: "no my character wouldn't do that", "no that's a stupid turn of events", etc.

    Doll housing gives you a basis upon which to do so. I think of that kind of affordance as "establishing". If I've established that my brother is a barber with a shop across the bridge, then everyone is blocked from making him be one of the pirates we fought, who tried to kill me. My affordance carries forward, and constrains what everyone else can say. This can be a currency for pre-play. "No, that evil wizard can't be my brother, he's just a simple barber!"

    \n@WPTunes: In larp theory, we actually call that part of the role-playing game's "first person audience" (credited to Markus Montola and Jaakko Stenros).

    It's when you withhold fiction about your character, you create a kind of innerplay that skips the stages of interaction and representation. The fact that Mel is totally addicted to Tic-Tacs may not come up in the spoken play, but it impacts the fiction by the pure virtue of the PC knowing it. (Whoa!)

The player knows the character is gambling their house or their kids' college fund or whatever, but they can neither mention this fact nor can they even drop the poker face they're keeping so that they maintain their advantage. Thus the first person audience makes self-sufficient fiction that nevertheless plays a part in the fiction (like if the character gets so stressed out over the game that they pull a gun or something).

    1.     3. But since many games (old school games in particular) leave the emotional/relational dimension completely unexamined, there's a clear void if you're feeling inspired by heroic fiction.       Are dollhouses related to oppressive social footprint?
      \nI would venture to say that they can be but aren't always.

      One way that they can be - You have a game with a lot of specific setting background stuff. You have a player who doesn't want to care about all that. They want to do creative setting construction themselves. So they write up a background for their character that doesn't neatly fit in with the rest of the setting stuff. It's just the stuff that they got inspired by and thought was cool.

      Then they bring that character with that background into game.

      Some level of friction usually occurs.

      I've seen this happen A LOT in medium sized fantasy larps. Especially ones that start out with vague backgrounds that aren't well fleshed out yet. And they're more desperate at the start to build up a player base than they are to implement a creative vision.

      I've seen 2 games do a really good job of reducing this problem substantially. They both did so by forcing every player, as part of their character creation process, to make a country/race choice that had both a cultural and character stat impact. (Yes the process was imperfect and represented a certain amount of cultural appropriation among the cultures that were amalgamated into fantasy analogues. But the result was 50 billion times better than the alternative, so I'll take it as a net positive.)

      Note - this is very much not the same as choosing a class/race in the vast vast vast majority of D&D campaigns where such things are poorly defined and don't carry significant cultural weight in the campaign world.

      A similar thing I have seen in a tabletop RPG was in 7th Sea, where players were forced to designate which pseudo-European country their character came from, thereby forcing them to fit in, at some level, with the game's setting. And it impacted the character mechanically. The way the larps worked was extremely similar to that.

      Another approach I've taken recently to avoid problematic dollhousing was this:
      We played in a game based on fairy tale stories. Every player was required to play a character from a known fairy tale story (though they would start with their story unfinished and the details of how it would play out would be determined in play). So everyone had an open dollhouse - if you weren't familiar with the particular story of the character chosen you could look it up and read it. And by explicit group agreement, the story your character came from was subject to change in play. It was tons of fun.

Players have the ability to create what they want, and then play in it! Characters become a reflection of the city. I mean, I suppose character creation in Spirit of the Century encourages/discourages it simultaneously too, because you're doing a lot of writing on your character sheet, but you have to do a round robin and share it with everyone else.

      In our recent Vampire: the Requiem game (another White Wolf example! I'm seeing a pattern here with "storytelling" games) Doll Housing happened SO MUCH. Individual character agendas were favored over group agendas, to reinforce a feel of genuine politicking. Emails happened in-between sessions that were "planning emails" so that each of us had complex plots none of the rest of us necessarily knew about. We would go around the table in session and have one on one scenes with the GM. I found it frustrating, because I wanted to get involved with the other players, but their stories and scenes were inaccessible. The only thing that tied us together as characters was a loosely contrived threat in the setting.

      I think it's really easy to head down that path in a game... focus on individual character's stories and loosing how to tie all the threads together. I think actually that's what allows doll housing to happen. So... turning that into something fun might be the trick if you want it to be a positive thing.
