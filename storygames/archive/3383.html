\n Here's an idea I'm testing out these days (one very successful playtest, so far). It's a resolution system.

Whenever a player's character - or an NPC - wants to do something difficult or dramatic, let another player draw a card and narrate how the situation resolves. This is important, so I'll repeat it: If you're trying to do something, someone else will draw the card and tell you how it works out.

The cards are:

Yes, but... You succeed, but something completely unrelated goes wrong, for someone you or someone you care about.
Yes, but... You succeed, but lose something valuable in the process.
Yes, and... You succeed, and achieve more than you expected.
Yes, and... You succeed, and that gives you the initiative for a new thrust or follow-up.
No, but... You fail, but another positive thing happens instead, unrelated to what you were aiming for.
Meanwhile, somewhere else... Cut the scene just before we get to know the result. Come back to the scene later.
The conflict escalates! Tension rises as the conflict, the problem or the stakes are raised a notch. Come back to the scene later.
You need help. You end up understanding you need the help of someone not currently in the scene to achieve this.
Locked! You're locked in the conflict or situation. You have to try a new approach.
Yes, but only if... You can get what you want - but only if you choose to make a certain sacrifice.

What I don't want from this thread:
- Reasons why the whole concept sucks, will never work, and/or isn't your cup of tea

What I want from this thread:
- Thoughts on other possible cards
- Thoughts on how to improve the wording of these cards
- Possible problems with specific cards

    \n The thing with these cards, I think, is that they work best when setting + character have been established fairly well. So established in-game facts will be used as narrative constraints.

    I'm seeing this as working best if everyone has a chance of providing input, and the narrator has final say over what happens. I'm not talking about debates taking several minutes - I'm thinking perhaps one or two ideas from other group members, a nod of approval or a wrinkled forehead, perhaps one or two more thoughts, and then the narrator says what the results are.

    Negative results should mostly be posed as creative challenges to the other player - not attempts to hose his/her character, although that may sometimes be called for, depending on the progress of the story so far.

    \n I like this idea a ton.

    Have you given any thoughts to some of the practical aspects of these cards?

    For example, in a LARP situation, each player will want a deck to carry with them, so the number of cards will probably need to be small and they'll need to be relatively sturdy ( probably laminated, not merely heavy card stock). You'll also probably want to stick with just a few cards per deck.

    In a TT game, you can use a common deck, but other issues crop up. Example: What is the spread of cards? Are any repeated? When is the deck shuffled (classically a joker is used as a reshuffle card in minis wargames that use cards something like this)? NOT shuffling everytime gives a bit of a different feel, for example, and can raise tension in a different way.

    Finally, you have the abillty to manipulate deck content. In addition to the basic outcome stuff you're talking about for the core deck, you could also add in stuff relevant to a given scenario, such as cards hat move time along or trigger events. At a more basic level, they could simply cause scene changes or move events through the sort of classic story progression. Some of that is already implied in cards that have been posited you could make it even more blatant.

    Good thread!

    Posted By: komradebobQuick question:
    Outside of these outcome cards, what's the rest of the game like?
    \nThe cards were designed as one of many experiments in my campaign set in Itras By, an as-yet-unpublished Norwegian RPG by Ole Peder Gi\xc3\xa6ver and Martin Bull Gudmundsen. The cards have been a success so far, and the authors are considering using them as the standard resolution system.

    Itras By is a surrealistic setting. It's a city somewhere in limbo, a place of dream, with a 1920s feel. The Machine God fights the Futurists, the spider queen Nindra sits atop the Moon Tower and controls everything. One of the richest men in the city has the head of a musk ox, is unhappily in love, and is laughed at by his peers.

    One of the main points of the system is the use of chance cards (not the same as the resolution cards in this thread). A player can draw a chance card at any time, though preferably not more than once per session. They typically introduce strangeness in the game, either in the game method or the fictional events. (One card requires you to re-play a scene; another makes everyone switch roles for a scene, so one of the players becomes the GM, the GM plays someone else's character etc; a third states that someone comes and gives the players a mission to accomplish).

    The resolution cards complement the chance cards nicely.

    \nItra\xc2\xb4s City: Chance cards \xe2\u20ac\u201c examples.

    Each session every player, including the GM, may pull one chance card from the deck. They are meant to increase potential player input to the story and support the game\xe2\u20ac\u2122s surreal motif. When a card is pulled from the deck, it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s read aloud. A card pulled must be used. The player who pulls the card interprets it, unless something else is specified.

    * Reverse. Turn the clock back a few seconds. What happened to you before you pulled the card did not occur. Instead, the exact opposite thing happens. It\xe2\u20ac\u2122s up to you to describe exactly what is reversed.

    * Chess with destiny. Swap roles with the game master. You describe the setting, he controls your character. The swap lasts for the duration of this scene.

    * Amor Victor: The power of love somehow affects the situation. Exactly how is for you to describe.

    * Fast forward! It\xe2\u20ac\u2122s been three hours since where you were when you pulled this card. Describe what condition you and the others find yourself in. You\xe2\u20ac\u2122re not allowed to describe what has happened in the meantime.

    * Flashback. Play a scene that has occurred in the past where your character is right now. The player who pulls the card sets the scene and distributes NPCs for the other players to control.

    * Haunted by your past! Something the character has done previously has consequences in the now. Tell the game master what you think this card means.

    * Nemesis! This card awakens your Nemesis. In some way, your arch enemy affects the action. Exactly how is up to you. You say you have no arch enemy? Well, now you do.

    * The character flaw. The last NPC you met, no matter who, has a dark secret, a weak spot or a negative trait. Your character doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t necessarily know what this is, but it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s up to you as the player to invent it.

    * Stare not into darkness\xe2\u20ac\xa6 Your character feels an irresistible urge to do something he will regret.

    * The wind changes. Temperature, smells and sounds mutate. It\xe2\u20ac\u2122s up to the player who pulled the card to describe the exact effect. If someone nearby is making a face right now, they will turn into a Grimace Face \xe2\u20ac\u201c see description elsewhere.

    * Double! The situation you\xe2\u20ac\u2122re currently in is doubled! If you are hurt, now you\xe2\u20ac\u2122re twice as hurt! If you\xe2\u20ac\u2122re searching for treasure, it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s become twice as valuable, and so on.

    * What\xe2\u20ac\u2122s in it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s pockets? Your character has something in his pocket that can prove to be of great assistance. What is it?

    * Masquerade! For the rest of this session, all players swap characters. This does not include the game master. Give your character sheet to the player at your right.

    * Slow motion. This scene is played in slow motion. All players must speak calmly, using slow, tempered movements. Since it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s slow-mo, you\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll notice a lot of details which would otherwise pass unseen. Any player may at any time interrupt the scene to describe a seemingly inconsequential detail. Afterwards, every player around the table must add another detail before the scene may continue.

    * In the mirror. Play the rest of this scene on the B-side of Itra\xc2\xb4s City, where everything is opposite. How are the characters on the A-side affected by this scene?

    * Falling dream. Your character falls. Why and how?

    * Mood change. The characters suddenly change mood. Explain what mood they are now in, and use this for the rest of the scene.

    * Bird\xc2\xb4s eye view. Play the scene as if seen from above.

    * Inner monologue. The next five minutes all characters are to think out loud. To show that one has an inner monologue, the players get up from their chair.

    * The thought bubble. A thought bubble appears over the head of an NPC. It may be real or metaphorical. Everyone may see what the person is thinking. You decide what that is.

    Most of the cards above have been extensively play tested. Mr. Haaken Lid has developed some new ones, which have not yet been put to the test. They are not included here. The chance cards above preceded the resolution cards in the game\xe2\u20ac\u2122s development. We will include Matthijs\xc2\xb4resolution cards as the \xe2\u20ac\u0153conflict mechanic\xe2\u20ac\x9d of the game. The chance cards are more or less \xe2\u20ac\u0153spice\xe2\u20ac\x9d, and not quite as integral to the game\xe2\u20ac\u2122s structure. Play tests have demonstrated that they are an effective way of grooming players into taking more responsibility for narrating/seizing more power in narration, and also for introducing a certain flexibility of thought and action fitting the setting. That\xe2\u20ac\u2122s my humble opinion, anyhow\xe2\u20ac\xa6 Some play testers have voiced the worry that Matthijs\xc2\xb4 resolution cards may make the chance cards redundant. I haven\xe2\u20ac\u2122t had an opportunity to test them yet, as I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve been in South America the past seven months, but I hope we can keep both. That\xe2\u20ac\u2122s the plan. Many thanks to Matthijs for the resolution cards (finally getting in place the one thing the game lacked sorely; a functioning, albeit original, resolution mechanic) and to both his and Haaken Lid\xe2\u20ac\u2122s groups for continuous play tests, reports, support and enthusiasm.

    I translated Haaken\xc2\xb4s cards today. Few of them have been rigourously tested, but I assume they\xc2\xb4ll work just fine. Many are very film-inspired, since his Itras-campaign is about a film studio:

    * Babel. The rest of the dialogue in the scene is played out in a
    nonsensical language which all the characters can understand, but none
    of the players.

    * Shift of status. High becomes low and low becomes high. All
    status-relations in the scene are turned upside down. It may happen
    suddenly or slowly, but the characters must have changed status before
    the scene is over.

    * Fast forward. This scene happens extra fast. Talk quickly, maybe
    with Mickey Mouse voices, and don\xc2\xb4t hesitate to use slapstick humour.

    * Black and white. This scene is in black and white. All colours
    disappear, and there is no longer room for nuance. Everything is
    either good or bad, virtuous or evil.

    * Silent movie. The rest of the action this scene is only to be
    described. No dialogue is permitted. If there is pen and paper
    available, the players can use text posters for dialogue, but the
    spoken word is forbidden.

    * Pantomime. The players get up from the table and mime the rest of
    the scene without saying a word. The scene is over when at least to
    players have sat down again.

    * Musical. The rest of the scene is a musical. The characters (and the
    players) must sing and dance.

    * Cross editing. You can cross cut between this scene and another. The
    other sceen can happen at the same time, or in another time and place.
    You decide who\xc2\xb4s in the other scene and when to cut back and forth.

    * The advisers. Two advisers appear on each shoulder of one of the
    persons present in the scene. Time stops while they try to convince
    the person of their (opposing) sides to some dilemma. Choose who will
    be given advice and which two players will control each advisor.

    * Dream. Someone wakes up from a dream. Who is it? Was the previous
    scene only a fantasy, or was it real?
