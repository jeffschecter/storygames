\n I was inspired by Epidiah Ravachol talking about his Micro Games, and I wrote about some design process thoughts. I'd love to hear some Story Games opinions! What do you think about this design process when you make games? Do you utilize these types of techniques? Do you find valuable correlations between visual design and game design? What are they?

Micro Games as a Game Design Experiment

"Learning how to reduce your design to it\u2019s essentials, and do it well, is one of the most challenging lessons for creators. I approach design primarily as a visual artist, so I reminisce about design classes meant to teach specific techniques. Form, repetition, pattern, color, proportion; these are every artist\u2019s arsenal. But where is the design class to teach specific game design techniques? Mechanics, narrative, player agency, tone. While we all know these are parts of a roleplaying game design, it\u2019s often hard to break a game down to it\u2019s most essential design parts. What those parts are, and how they function, varies from designer to designer."

    \n Oh, was it you who wrote that excellent article?

    I really enjoy designing micro games, in the small amount I've actually done it, because it forces me to break how roleplaying games work (or could work) into it's basic foundation. It makes me question or enforce some of the unspoken rules in roleplaying games. To bad that the Swedish keychain roleplaying game made by \xc5sa Roos isn't available in English, because I think it's a great example of what you're talking about.

    I myself have been theorising about how to engage players in roleplaying games this last year, and I've come up with the following parts.

    A player's attitude: exploration, competition, expression, sensation, destruction
    Parts of the game: meaning, group, setting, mechanics, fiction
    Components of game play: uncertainty, effort, choice, investment

    I would for example say that the Big Model is a combination of mainly exploration (of), meaning, mechanics and fiction.

    Recently on the interwebs, I\u2019ve been seeing some Micro Games surface from a few different designers.
    This is happening with non-RPGs too \u2014 there are a couple of tiny card games getting a lot of attention on BoardGameGeek. "Love Letter" and "Coup" consist entirely of tiny decks of cards and a few tokens. "Love Letter" uses a 16-card deck with only 8 unique cards, and "Coup" goes even further with only 5 unique cards but wrings more complexity out of them. The rules can be expressed on the cards themselves plus a little cheat-sheet. I've got "Love Letter" and it's lightweight but a lot of fun; I just ordered "Coup" yesterday.

    (Daniel Solis has also been experimenting with prototypes of a slightly similar 13-card game currently called "Suspense".)

    "Love Letter" and "Coup" get a lot of mileage out of secret roles, where you're trying to deduce what rank or abilities the other players secretly hold, by 'reading' their behavior. (The older card game "Citadels" does this too.) This might be an interesting idea for a more RPG/freeform game too \u2014 I don't think RPGs have done enough with hidden information, probably because it gets awkward to share knowledge with the GM but not other players. GM-less games might fare better with this.

    (On a tangent: part of what interests me about these games is that they seem to bring out people's DIY tendencies. With so few components it's easy to make your own, and to redesign the theme \u2014 kind of like the way everyone gets inspired to redo Lady Blackbird. There are literally about 50 homemade Love Letter rethemes already, ranging from Adventure Time to Game Of Thrones to the Sopranos. I'm working on a Coup retheme based on Jim Woodring's "Frank" comics, even before my official copy arrives in the mail\u2026)

    \n I think design process is the great unexplored topic in the design-interested corner of the RPG internet. We are overflowing with smart people saying smart things about what good RPG rules need to do, but I've seen very little discussion about how to create the rules that do that.

    Some people have mentioned using diagrams, though I've never seen one. Kira, as an artist/designer, have you ever tried diagramming out your rules ideas? Every time I try a user flow chart I just make a mess.

    As for the micro idea, it seems to me that "make a tiny game for a contest" usually produces stuff that's over-simple, over-focused, half-baked, incomplete, or all of the above. (I still enjoy this and find it valuable! It produces awesome concepts and techniques galore! But complete games? I haven't seen much of that, and I've never pulled it off myself.) On the other hand, starting with a full game and then reducing it down to its bare essentials -- that does sound cool. I know I've been removing D&D leftovers from my fantasy game for years! Actively trying to excise everything I could might have saved me some time and helped me identify the game's core.

    I'm still not sure how to simplify early in the process, though. Most of the rules bloat I produce is grasping for a solution I haven't yet found.

    \n Looks to me like the point of Beloved is to make the players choose between (a) spending time and effort taking steps toward a pre-conceived ideal and (b) giving up the pre-conceived ideal in favor of something else that is also good that they already have.

    In order to give the players this choice, the rules instruct them to come up with an ideal beloved, come up with obstacles that take increasing time and effort to overcome, then find someone one step closer to their beloved after each obstacle. The rules couldn't possibly do any less and still create the desired choice, and they don't do any more.

    (I'm not attempting to read Ben's mind re: "the point" -- that's just how it appears to me as a player.)

    Whether that means "conceive a simple point for your game, identify the minimum requirements for it, and create rules that fulfill those and nothing else" is a viable design approach, I don't know. To me it sounds like a piece of the puzzle, but far from the whole puzzle.

    Ben, I'd be curious to hear what your actual process was. I have no idea if it resembles my impressions at all.

    \n Micro rules are a great thing. Unexpected complex interactions in systems are what make so many games not work. It is amazing that multi hundred page RPGs work at all. On second thought they mainly don't. At least not as written.

    There was an old design principle in miniatures gaming in the 1970's. All the rules should be on the front and back of a 3x5 index card. The basics of most workable games can do this. I think this comes down to how much people can remember and apply. More and we start forgetting stuff and making up the game as we go along.

    Of course short rules aren't always ones that will capture people's attention. Saying that thing that lights a fire in people's brains, I don't know how to do that. It goes beyond lists of principles, but looking at the process is always useful. I see the game in my mind when I start, if I can't see it the project dies, but the process starts before that. Watching movies, TV, reading, pictures, music. Playing other games.

    \n Oh yes, paragraph 3. Every game I've ever successfully finished was just a process of writing up and refining a game that seemed to appear in my brain, more or less fully formed.

    The thing I'm currently excited about in game design is the idea that there are lots of things the people are naturally good at: narrative arcs, sharing spotlight, building consensus, collaboration - and writing rules that try to change the ways people do those things is hard and often counterproductive. There are also lots of things that people are quite bad at: Coming up with original and compelling content, negotiating conflicts of interest, doing things that are uncomfortable socially - writing rules that help people do those things is easy and productive.

    My sense is that small games tend to focus on the latter more than the former.

    \n To me, one of the best things about writing short games (whether 200 words or a few thousand) is the ability to be radically experimental, if you want, and still get people to play it. The level of commitment is so low for a game of that length that most people would be willing to give it a shot. And, like a couple people have said, you are forced to build the game out of behaviors that people mostly already know how to do. And, honestly, incorporating more of the normal behaviors of our lives into game design (rather than trying to teach people a completely different set of behaviors that are only useful in RPGs) is pretty radical and experimental in itself. "You already know how to do this; we're just arranging the pieces in a new way" is a pretty radical and amazing message, because it means that everyone can play and everyone can design (especially at a short length!).

    The "basics" are not the draw in many games, many people seem to love subsystems. I'm pretty much a minimalist myself, but "simple rules are the best" is just a taste thing, not an objective truth.
    \nIt does tend to be very context dependent, a bit like what you mentioned regarding Cthulhu Dark.

    IME, the more familiarity people have with a game and the more regularly they play it, the more they seem to enjoy some sort of complexity in it.

    What exact sort of complexity is pretty variable though. Complexity of fictional situation, whether initially or as it develops through linked return play can fulfill that urge as well.

    Certainly RPGs, drawing from the model set up first by D&D, having long-term linked return play as a core concept tend towards mechanical complexity and subsystems and set up an expectation among hobbyists that those things will exist.

    WRT those 1970s minis rules, context is important. Those 3"x 5" card rules would be cheatsheet charts, the game would likely be a presentation game in a convention style context ( if not actually at a convention), the person who cooked them up would likely also be the referee, and most people involved would already have background with similar games ( or be a newb invited by more experienced gamers).

    The game would be meant to be played in a single session generally and complexity would be something that was tied to the scenario set up and in player interactions during play itself.

    So kinda like CDark in some regards.
