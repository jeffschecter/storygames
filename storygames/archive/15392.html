\n Say what you will about the politics or economics of the Occupy mo(ve)ment, but not in this thread.

What games (be they story- or otherwise) are there that could teach us about economic justice, representational democracy, policy reform, nonviolent protest, and the like? What games are there that can be played in the commons, taught easily to others, shared, changed, grown, and improved to spread a story?

Do these games exist? How would one make them? What do or would they look like? Feel like?

    \n You could make a Dread game where you play Wall Street tycoons. The Jenga tower is the US economy, and every time you manipulate it for your own benefit, it becomes more likely to collapse. Of course, this is different from regular Dread, because your characters won't die when the tower collapses; they'll just receive government bailouts and huge bonuses.

    Perhaps you could play a game of Nomic, where each player starts with a random amount of money, that money shifts around during play and it acts as votes for changing the rules. Thus, the more money you have, the more influence you have over the rules, which means you can tweak them to make you earn even more money, giving you more power...

    I think illustrating economic injustices is quite viable through games, as a tool to educate people about what is wrong with the system.

    \n I'm working on a game now that could be about social justice topics. It's sort of a slice of life game.

    All the players run the same protagonist but each player takes on one of three world views (optimist, realist, pessimist). The game is about guiding the protagonist through a series of real life events. The players say what is happening.

    So for instance, the protagonist is faced with a protest march (one of the events I want to use because the game is going to be about 1969). One player starts off the turn by saying what is going on. The other players get to suggest additions to the action or challenge it to replace the action. If they add on, the first player can reject the addition which makes the player roll a reality check. If they pass it then the addition happen.s If they challenge, they offer up a different story, both players roll 2d6 and the high roller wins. Once everyone has had a chance to put in their two cents, play goes to the left with the player starting the next turn. Once everyone has had a chance to start a turn, the event is over. I see the game going through three events in a play session and take an hour or so to play.

    Because it is a slice of life it gives players a chance to imagine what could happen in that situation. I'll have the protagonist motivated by some powerful emotion (fear, hate, regret, etc.) so the game will not just show us the action but also get at the emotions.

    I've been using a variation of this game in psychotherapy sessions of late.

    Alternately you could play the real life LARP by joining your local Occupy group or toddle on down to Tahrir Square.

    Chris Engle
