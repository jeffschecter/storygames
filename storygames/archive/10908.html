    \n
    \n What kinds of activities do you, as a player, want to do in this game? Now, how does your character do those activities?

    And as a follow-up to the above, is there something that I, as a player, can do with my character to assist in what you want to do with your character? (i.e., do you need someone to argue with, do you need someone to feel protective of, do you need someone to carouse with...)

    Is there a problem with the character that you want to emphasize (a personality flaw, a source of stress, an unrequited passion, an unattainable goal, etc.)? What is that problem, how does the character deal with it currently, and do you have any thoughts on how the character will deal with it later on?


    Because learning details about the character is fantastic...but for maximum usefulness, go right for details about what the player wants to do with the character. And revisit the questions periodically as the game goes on (easy to do in pre- or post-game kibbutzing, or whenever you ask how the game is going for everyone).

    \n Why are you in prison for a crime you didn't commit?

    Why did you enlist in the Foreign Legion?

    Why are you certain you'll be dead in a year?

    What 3 personal items did you bring on the last rocket from Earth before it blew up? They all have to fit in a standard-issue footlocker.

    Who is the last person you saw the clown kill?

    When did you realize you were immortal?

    Sex, drugs, or rock and roll? You can only pick one. Explain why the other two don't even come close.

    Who plays you in the movie?

    What's on your iPod?

    What's the worst way to die?

    Why did the other aliens leave you behind on Earth?

    (Obviously, these aren't all for the same game. I really prefer to start with specific, more focused queries and let the general stuff come out in play. Answers that help me plot the campaign are much more useful than generalist stuff, and also help me establish the flavor and direction of the game with the questions themselves before chargen starts...)

    \n Here's some I've been thinking of using in a game that I've just begun working on set in the Post-Apocalyptic USA:

    What bogus political creed did you once subscribe to?

    What anthropomorphized deity did you once think it important to worship?

    What bullshit story did you once tell yourself to explain why you truly deserved your unearned prosperity?

    Which ethnic minorities did you once secretly despise?

    What mood-altering substance did you habitually ingest?

    Obviously not for every gaming group, but it makes for some interesting contrast between backstory and AP.

    \n Questions from Carolina Death Crawl. Everybody gets one:

    THE IDEALIST: What did you and the character to your left do during the battle for New Bern that scarred both of you forever?

    THE CRIMINAL: What did you do that makes the character to your left loathe you?

    THE IMPOSTOR: Who were you, and why are you hiding that from the character to your left?

    THE ARISTOCRAT: What debt haunts you, and how can the character to your left help you repay it?

    \n Don't Rest Your Head bases its chargen on five questions:
    What's been keeping you awake?
    What just happened to you?
    What's on the surface?
    What lies beneath?
    What's your path?

    On the whole, I'm a big proponent of every character coming with a built in answer to 'what's your goal', stated one way or another. Character goals are a powerful tool.

    \n\nWarriorMonk \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nOctober 2011 \n edited October 2011
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n 1st round of questions: You are a god.
    -Of what?
    -What do you ask of your followers?
    -What benefits dou your grant for those who follow you?
    -How do you punish those that dissapoint or defy you?

    2nd round: Now you are a king.
    -What does your kingdom looks like?
    -What's the main activity of your people?
    (you can roll dice for some numbers that indicate how big the country is, the population and how well are they doing)
    Now all players trace a map according to the size of the kingdom they just created.
    -From your neighbour kingdoms, who do you hate? Who do you fear? Who is your ally?

    3rd round: Now you're the leader of a faction, either a small army, institution, armed group, etc.
    -What is your goal?
    -How far will you go to achieve it?
    -What is stopping you from doing it?
    -What resources are at your dissposal? (name three)
    -Who or what is the main threat to your faction?

    4th round: Now you create a PC. Your character comes from the country you made in the second round. You can choose any of the deities created in the first round and if the GM doesn't want to have players going at each other throat, all players have to choose a single one of the factions they created. Then we go for the questions:
    - Justice, Glory, Power, Wealth, Revenge or Love? Choose one and describe the personal goal for your character. Choose another one for the thing your character would sacrifice to achieve your goal. Whatever goal you pursue, it applies to a single NPC you can name, but that the GM will then describe and control. The same applies for the thing (or person) you would sacrifice to achieve your goal.
    - What does your character fears most of this list: Shame, Pain, Loneliness, Hate, Lack of sel-control, Chaos?
    - What are your character talents and how are they used by the faction your characters belongs to?
    -How do you relate to the other PCs? write at least something positive about the character of the player to your right and one negative thing about the character to your left.

    \nMine too. When I ran Everway, one question I'd toss in was whether the character used locks.

    For a larp, I think we decided that there were four questions we needed to know the answer to as writers.

    Who am I?
    Why am I cool?
    What do I want? aka Why will I deal with other characters?
    What can I do? aka How can I get what I want and why will other characters deal with me?

    But, that's a very different focus.
