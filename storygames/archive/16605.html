    Above Decks and Below Decks are fine. As mentioned earlier, Aloft means you're up in the rigging.

    Abaft and abeam... okay so looking down at a ship from the top you have:

    Bow

    Port ---Beam--- Starboard

    Stern

    Walking from the bow to the stern is moving aft; walking from the stern to the bow is moving forward.

    Closer to the stern than the bow means you're abaft the beam, otherwise you're forward of the beam.


    There's also "Athwartships" which means you're lying down with head to starboard and feet to port (or vice versa.) On sailing ships, only corpses are transported this way. If you try to sleep this way, you'll fall out of bed.

    Nathan, for nautical locations, above and below are essential, the word 'decks' is often left adrift. (pun intended!) When aloft you often mention how high, which may be of import when characters plummet to their deaths on the deck. The join of each mast section is labelled a cap or 'top', a 'fighting top' if platformed on a man-o-war. The 'crowsnest' were usually on the main t'gallant masthead. So to locate yourself you would name the mast (fore, main, mizzen) then the height (course, top, t'gallant, sky, star). Not that you probably need that level of detail for a fiasco playset

    Oh, and the cathead is a very important location or simply the 'head' which are the arms to which anchors are lowered and raised at the stem (foreship), but also the place where you hang your arse over the side and defecate!

    Scuttlebutt is awesome too, that's the barrel on deck where the crappy communal drinking water is kept and crew congregate for a good bit of gossip. The binnacle is where the compass was housed and the the officers made navigational desicions. The bosun's locker where all the maintainence tools and equipment were kept.

    For items: a boathook, whaling harpoon, belaying pin, awl, heaving mallet, fid, holy stone,

    Steal Steal Steal from Poison'd Nathan. Vx has an excellent grasp of volotile nautical situations.

    \n What about the futtocks?!!
    I love the Futtock shrouds It was always the mark of a nimble sailor that traversed the futtocks (like climbing an overhang as you swing out from under the top) rather than going through the ignoble 'lubbers hole'.
    'Get yer buttocks over the futtocks, yer scurvy puppy!'

    They are the tensioned from the top to the lower shrounds and carry the weight of the mast above.

    The bowsprit too, is one of my favourite sailing words, the sprits'l being a wonderful sail hung off the sprits'l yard. As the ship dipped her bow into the ocean, the sail would fill with seawater, and thus needed large drainage holes sewn into each side to avoid strain on the yard and parrels.

    Oooh, which brings us to parrels! These (along with the trusses) allowed a yard to either swing away from its mast or slide up and down to 'stretch' the sail to full set or reefed as necessary based on the weather. Parrels look like those wooden beady covers that taxi drivers sling on their seats to assist circulation. They are quite wonderful and tactile things.
