    \n There are two ethical systems being discussed. One is the fictional ethical system of the characters. The second is the real world ethics of the players. It does no good to confuse them.

    However, do they overlap? I think that's the meaty question.

    Narrativist players might say that they do. That you're playing games to learn about how the real players feel about things by watching what messages they create through play. But I might play a really immoral character to make a moral point as a player, and that doesn't make me immoral.

    When you ask about players who ignore the fictional ethics of their characters' actions, I think it's important to determine if harm bleeds over from the fiction to the real world.

    Say we're playing D&D and we slaughter every last one of the damned goblins because they're worth 100 XP each. Maybe in the fiction, they haven't even done anything evil. We just lead our adventurers into the wilderness, find us some goblins, and corral them into railroad cars and send them to the goblin concentration camps for mass gassing. The paladin makes a few of the strong ones dig the graves.

    Wait, put that way, it sounds pretty ghastly, doesn't it? I mean, it's ghastly in the fiction, too, right? And what does it say about us as players?

    I submit that the answer is "It depends on how we act after we make our characters do those things." Are we disgusted by how we made our PCs act? Do we all understand that what our characters did is genocidal and evil? Did we play this session with an impressionable 10-year-old kid who now thinks it's okay and cool to round up folks and slaughter them because they're different?

    But that's not how it happens in D&D, is it? The in-game ethical system is different. It sets up Good and Evil as a simplified system of moral absolutes. Most often, those Evil creatures are doing terrible things to Good people. They won't stop. You have to kill them. And it's Okay to kill them in this world. It's expected. And you're fighting them\xa0mano a mano\xa0, not rounding them up in camps -- because that is an obvious reference to something in our shared, real world ethical system that we all know is evil. To make that analogy in the game would be pretty harsh. If we were the victims of Vikings, perhaps, then playing D&D the normal way might really seem evil to us.

    Really, no one plays D&D and then becomes a crazy racist genocidal motherfucker. Those people were that way before they started playing D&D and they brought their personal ethical systems with them into play.

    Posted By: Paul Bwhy is the question of ethical choices even relevant if they can be so easily discarded?
    \nBecause they are distinctly not-present in games which are otherwise similar.

    A not-RPG gives you a fixed value set for your persona, or poses only closed-ended questions about it in play.

    An RPG, through the act of play, poses open-ended questions about the value set of your character.

    The fact that such questions can be ignored doesn't make it a not-RPG, though I'd say that it makes your play not-roleplay.

    (And I'll note that you might be engaging in tactical play, or building play, or power-play, or venting-play... A lot of things. Lumping all those other things together as "Roll-Playing" is dismissive, and asinine. The point, to me, is that those other things are stuff you could get from another kind of game - not that you should.)

    \n Play is one of the most powerful tools to learn, and so you should expect engaging in play activities to change who you are. Thus you have a fundamental ethical and moral responsibility to choose who that future self will be - to make play purposeful and take advantage of fun, without destroying it.

    It is tempting to over-pack play with things like socialization, self-esteem re-building, creativity practice, ethical what-if experimentation (as discussed other places in this thread), and so on. But ultimately, by diluting the focus we can learn poorly at best, failing the over-riding ethical responsibility of play.

    Thus it is unethical for RPGs to always contend with the examination of ethics.
