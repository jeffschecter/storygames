\n Hey there! So far, Michtim only offers (kinda) physical Core Actions for the Attributes Joy, Love, Grief, Fear and Anger. But ever since I've dug into *W games, and reading helpful hints on the forum, I thought I really should create a better foundation for players to use. I'm not exactly sure if I want to follow the design language of the Moves (like it can't be rolled if it's not a Move), but the game probably would benefit from more non-violent/non-combat Actions.

The current Actions\u2026

Joy: Dash (Jump and Sprint), Detect
Love: Cure Others
Grief: Weep (=Curl into a ball to defend yourself)
Fear: Hide, Evade (= Dodge)
Anger: Attack

All very combat centric, which I thought would be required for an adventure game. On the other hand, I deliberately left Intelligence-like things out of the game so far. BUT, given *W's way of handling things, this might not be a problem. "Ask the MC questions" sort of things.

The Core Actions are greatly enhanced by Callings. Some Callings offer new Actions, others change the parameters of existing Actions. Every Calling benefits every Emotion though; so there are no preferred Emotions. Each Emotion should be valuable.

Some Social Actions are built into the Courtier and Bard Callings. But I'm willing to change a few things, so that there is room for new Core Actions.

Some possible Actions I'm looking for:

Joy: Find Opportunity (look for chances/options)
Love: Offer Help (=be friendly, make yourself valuable, like a non-sexy turn-someone-on)
Grief: Hear the Unspoken (~Gaze into Abyss, Sense Motive)
Fear: Make an Escape
Anger: Get Your Way (=be unfriendly, intimidating, forceful)

The core resolution mechanic is take a pool of Emotion d6. Set aside as many dice as you like to increase effect (hits). Roll. Try to beat 7. Players need to judge their chances and risk setting aside dice for hits. I think about a hold-based design pattern. You get 1 hold for every hit and pick options from a provided list.

So far I didn't have a way to include Hard Moves. If you failed a roll there weren't any strings attached. I think the game might really benefit from consequences, and a clear rule about "to do it, do it." I always thought that was obvious, but it probably requires explicit wording in the rules.

Your thoughts?

PS: To gain a better understanding of the core mechanics of my game, you can just head over to http://bit.ly/myFluffyRPG and fetch it for free. The game has currently a lot of issues with railroading and no clear thoughts about how to handle player agency\u2026\xa0so please don't be harsh. Honesty is welcome though.

    \n This is my house rule for when a player who wants to do something iffy that's not an actual Move:

    If there are no real consequences for failure, just let them do it. Maybe it takes some time, however.

    If there are only minor consequences for failure (hurt your neck, etc), let them make a custom move roll based on the appropriate Stat. Failure in this case will not bring about a Hard Move from me, just a Soft one ("You fall and pull a neck muscle for zero damage; if you fail that again you may be seriously hurt.") A roll of 7-9 would mean you did it, but you also hurt yourself for zero damage, drop something, etc.

    If there are real consequences (cripple yourself, alert your enemies, etc), it's played as a normal Move (failure means I make a Hard Move).

    \n Within reason, of course. No jumping to the moon (unless you are a Lovecraftian cat).

    Yeah these are basically Attribute Rolls, treated as (custom) Basic Moves. It's a throwback to my old D&D days too, but it's still often used and appreciated even in AW. We can't expect Vincent to write every possible action that can be performed by a physical body. The tendency of the rules is to just let these things happen, and in general I do that, but sometimes success or failure in these things can be pivotal to the emerging storyline. So in those cases I follow a different principle: Sometimes, disclaim decision-making.

    ... but this question has changed: When?

    In the old days it was a question of "Is there a chance the character might fail according to the laws of physics?" and if so, we'd roll it out (whether it was dramatically important or not).

    Today it's more like "Is there a chance that success or failure will affect the story in a dramatic way?"

    I can't answer your question about whether other people feel comfortable with "Attribute Rolls" or not. It probably depends on their previous experience. For me as a GM, I find them invaluable. That's why I argue for Stats in "DayTrippers", for instance. There are just too many possibilities not to give the GM a good fallback for.

    Of course, if you want to persuade players and GMs to do this more frequently in your game, you should explicitly state it.

    \n So far, I always thought that certain actions needed to be scripted. I abstained from scripting social and mental actions, because I wanted the Michtim characters to be like vehicles. Steered by the players social and mental capabilities. Deliberately leaving these aspects out of the game (where possible) was my try at creating a reasonable system that doesn't punish playing a character that you cannot portray.

    We had this issue a lot with social interaction in our games. Players that were good at social interaction got away without rolling; those with the stats were always forced to "please roleplay what you do" and often failed because their "social performance" wasn't deemed to be "good enough", although the dice might have said something else.

    The same with mental actions. Like my player character was a private investigator with high investigation + intelligence + wits. He is supposed to know where to look. But the GM always asked me *specifically* where my character looked. I tried to explain that I might not be a good PI, but my character is.

    This sort of discrepancy made me furious.

    \n Stealing stuff isn't the problem; Vincent has no issues with hacks as far as I've ever heard. A problem would be importing a subsystem that didn't really work for your world, simply because it existed in another *W system. The mechanics for interpersonal relations between characters (Hx in AW) are frequently modded, replaced or removed in *W hacks (Bonds, Strings, Trust/Betrayal, etc), and with good reason - because characters relate to each other differently in different gameworlds. Take a step back, forget about rules for a while, and just think about how you want Michtim PCs to behaviorally relate to each other and perhaps to NPCs they have formed strong relations with, both positive and negative. Then come back to the rules and think about how to model those behaviors in the simplest way possible.
