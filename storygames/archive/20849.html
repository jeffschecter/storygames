\n I've been struggling with a question in not just my designs, but overall design philosophy, and there are much smarter folk than I about inclusiveness in game design here, so could anyone enlighten me.

One of the things I've realized is that, as a white, straight, middle class cis-male, I have precious little insight into the experiences of more diverse groups of people. I am the "default", and that's an awkward place to be. I'd like to use my position of privilege to be an ally to anyone and everyone, but because I lack this insight, I have trouble doing so sometimes. I realize the best answer here is to reach out and get that insight from people who have it, but I'm also, frankly, a very poor storyteller. I can't hope to tell those stories in a way that do them justice. My skills, then, lie in providing a framework that includes people who have those stories to tell. I am not an authority, so I defer to those who are.

The question, then, is, is it "good enough" to simply not have defaults at all? To speak broadly without making any assumptions on the gender, sexuality, race, socio-economic status, etc. of my reader, a hypothetical GM or player, or a character? Or is that, in being the "easy way out", unintentionally exclusionary?

    \n Good point -- I was trying to draw this question broadly, but of course, there is no one answer that works in all cases. The game I'm working on now is called "Just Got Real"; if the title sounds familiar, yes, it's an implicit reference to Bad Boys II, but it's a genre-agnostic game for action stories. Rules-light, traditional GM/PC setup, with tight scene-framing and pacing rules.

    The "story so far" is created by the GM building a loose premise: for example, "A cult has stolen a nuclear submarine from the US Navy base in Alameda. They are attempting to extract concessions from the US using their new presence as a \u201cnuclear state\u201d as leverage.

    The GM then asks the players a series of leading questions to fill out the details:\n
    • Who are they?
    • What are their demands?
    • Have they performed terrorist deeds before? What was the outcome?
    • Who are the PCs? Why do they have jurisdiction to stop them?
    \nThe design goals I am trying to hit with the game are quick pacing and play, allowing all players to have their fair share of spotlight time by explicitly focusing scene cuts around each PC, and making all the players invested in the story by drafting their help in creating it.

    It's that last one that I'm drilling into here; since this game isn't about one setting in particular (instead about a genre in the large), I can't necessarily bake folks outside the white, cis-gender, heterosexual default into the setting, but I want to make sure my design is welcoming for everyone. At present, I'm trying to support that by not assuming defaults at all, and drawing up example settings and characters without drilling into gender, sexuality, and race at all, and leaving the door open for anyone to fill in what they want in their action story. But are there more or better steps I could take to fulfill that goal?

    \n Ah, okay, this is for a game!

    An action story game.

    I'd say the easiest options for inclusiveness in general are:

    - Make sure your rules give every participant an equal voice.
    - Write your instructional text without defaulting to male pronouns and the like.
    - If you have examples of characters, make those characters diverse.
    - If you have examples of situations, make those situations diverse, meaning not just the sorts of action stories traditionally aimed at hetero men.
    - Make sure your rules are supportive of such situations, rather than funneling everything into punching matches or whatnot.

    The reading and playing of the game will still take place in a cultural context that may not be as inclusive as you'd hope -- if you wish to fight that, I think you'd need to go a good deal further, but I can't claim any expertise on that front.

    Hoping someone with more experience will chime in!

    P.S. I said "easiest", but my first and last points could be challenging to work into your design if they aren't present already. Re: the first one, simply telling players they have the right to contribute is not the same as ensuring that they actually do in practice.

    \n As a white middle class middle aged male games author and referee I'm in the same boat as you, Jim. I keep discovering - to my horror - more and more ways that I've been exclusive over the years. Only recently at a games convention some parents asked me to run a game to introduce their young children to RPGs and I found I hadn't updated the pregens for the particular genre they chose to my current standards. (All my pregens these days come in a choice of gender - identical character sheets laminated back to back with different names and illustrations. With a range of racial and cultural types available across the spread.) One young girl didn't have a range of characters to choose from and had to pick the only available female. I made sure I updated all my convention pregens to my new standards as soon as I got home.

    BUT we haven't had to live it and - coming late to the table - shouldn't get on our soapbox and proclaim the evils of the world and announce that WE'RE going to solve things.

    The best thing to do is to keep quiet and to do no further harm. If you try TOO hard, it comes across as tokenism. "Bad Boys" is a specific case but, in general, if you wanted to truly simulate 1980/90s cop shows and films you'd be required to have a black Police Chief, in the same way that to simulate Sci Fi shows you'd need a black guy as the "warrior" figure. Because of the tokenism/positive discrimination/affirmative action - whatever you want to call it - prevalent when they were made.

    Don't try to "fix" things. Just make sure that the example characters in your rules are at least 50% female and all have generic names so that readers can picture them in their heads however they choose.

    If you're lucky enough to go to publication, make sure the ART presents a range of cultural and racial types, but don't make a big issue of it. The central figure on the cover of my most recent games is now always a strong black female character. It's got nothing to do with the game and I don't draw attention to it, it's just something I do.

    There's no way to hard code "inclusivity" into the rules and it gives the wrong message if you try to do it. However, there are things that you can do within your setting. For my SteamPunk game, I was well aware that some people can bring in Proto-Victorian colonial attitudes so I deliberately wrote my setting to make it clear that Prince Albert has brought forward social change and turned the British Empire into a Commonwealth of nations a century early and that people from India, the West Indies, Africa are all considered "British". Of course that means we need somebody else to be the protagonists and, I'm sorry, the Hun and the Yanks got to fill that slot. You can't please everyone.

    For YOUR game I'd say the trap lies in the fact that the bad guys in the films are almost always Identified by their racial groups. "The Cubans", "The Mafia", "The RUSSIAN Mafia", "Yardies", "Ku Klux Klan" etc. If you stick to this template, then make sure that for every negative image you include, you include at least as many positive images from that same group. Personally, I'd rip it up and invent a pan-global syndicate containing bad guys of every stripe. I know this is more GI Joe than Bad Boys but it's safer.

    But whatever you decide to do, just do it quietly behind the curtain. Don't bang on about it.

    Actually, this sounds like an interesting place to start.

    My - very personal, very biased - suggestion is: stop thinking of yourself as a "default" something. Stop thinking of yourself as a category, or even an overlap of categories.

    As a new writer, start by assuming you don't know your audience. Start from yourself, with sincerity. Think of your work as something peculiar to you as an individual, coming from your personality and personal history. Do not obfuscate your individual self in your work. Introduce yourself to your audience through your work - "categories", "groups" you think you "belong" to describe you inaccurately, while your work is an authentic part of yourself you're putting out for everybody to see.
    It's even possible your work is of no interest whatsoever to anybody else, but you know, maybe not, maybe other individual people will individually relate to it, each in their own individual way - an act of communication. But you can't start from them and create a work in their image, because you don't know them. You have to start from your self. Avoid the pitfall of believing you're starting from anything bigger or deeper than your self.
