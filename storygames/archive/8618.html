\n I ran (Antagonized?) my second game of Agon on Saturday (the first one being approximately a year ago, if not more) and it went quite well. The game definitely is much (much!) better with three heroes than with two.
I did not, however, manage to get any of my new boardgaming friends to join me, as I'd originally planned. They were all too busy with other things going on. I'm still fairly convinced that they'd probably like the game, though, and I'm planning on trying to get them to join us for another session later.
So here's my first question: what do you think is the best way to bring in a new player, in terms of mechanics? Granted, there's only been one quest so far, but that Glory can really rack up fast sometimes. So if there's new players and people that were in the first mission, should I make any changes? Should I toss the new people some free glory or advances? Should I make the returning players stat up fresh heroes instead of using their originals?

Beyond that, I have a few general wonderings. I ran The Temple of Hera, and overall it went great. I had no idea Menae would be such a tough obstacle just from looking at her stats, though! For powers like her Gaze and Hold, is it acceptable to use them both between every exchange?
In the end, they heroes decided they couldn't take her down in combat so they came up with a ploy to deal with her in another way. Two of the heroes lured her outside, angering her until she gave chase, and the third one slipped inside. I had the two leading her away engage her in a "non-combat battle" using athletics, and between exchanges the third make rolls to search for the horn. Does that seem appropriate and acceptable? I thought it worked pretty well, but after the fact I wondered if any of the special powers NPCs can have can be used in non-combat battles. Many of them would function mechanically, but I felt like it would be unfair to blast them with her Fear and Gaze during the chase since those powers were the main reason they'd decided to flee the initial battle and engage her in another way. On the other hand, special powers are the main thing some NPCs have going for them...

Finally, I was doing some speculating about NPCs and I realized that some of the special powers (like multi-attack) have no explicit limit to how many times they can be purchased. I pondered the idea of an NPC with a maxed out sword and athletics, a sword in each hand, a large name die, an extra d8 purchased for offense, Extra Damage and then 5 purchases of Multi-attack. As far as I can tell, he'd be technically legal (though using about the max strife with one hero have a d10 name die) and he wouldn't be good for anything but combat, but... that kind of build seems... well... pretty asshatty, I guess. That was more me thinking out loud than asking any kind of question, I suppose.

more thoughts may come later.
i really like agon. i kind of wish it was easier to create NPCs at the table, but beyond that it's got a real nice mix of interesting crunch and fast-playing, simple to grok rules. i'd really forgotten how cool it was. or maybe i didn't quite get to taste its coolness with only 2 heroes and a quest whipped up on the spur of the moment.

    \n Sounds like you did everything right, Brian. Running the non-combat battle like that is perfect. For NPC powers in non-combat situations... just use your judgment. Sometimes they'll be appropriate and sometimes they won't. Follow the advice on page 13 and don't worry about what "makes sense" -- just focus on making things tough for the PCs so they can seem heroic. If the NPC never uses some (or all) of her powers, then you can deduct those points from her Strife total and Glory reward. If it didn't help the NPC, it's not worth Glory after the fact.

    If you ever need to make up an NPC on the fly, just grab the dice and powers that you want in the moment. Maybe make a note of them as you go. Then, if the PCs defeat them, total up their dice and powers to figure out the Glory reward. If you go over the Strife limit, it's usually not a big deal. In fact, you can play really loose with the Strife budget overall and it won't hurt the game.

    \n Yep, two weaknesses for the Phoenix is the way to go. You might say it can normally only be harmed by ice and cold, and to stop its resurrection, you need to scatter its ashes at holy sites. Something like that.

    Oh, also, if you want to make Monsters more impressive, here's a more strict interpretation of the Divine Weapon rule:
    When you spend Divine Favor to empower your weapon, you still need to know HOW to empower the weapon to harm the monster. You call out to your god and ask them to bless your weapon in a particular way, in other words. "Oh, Poseidon, give my spear the icy cold of the depths of the sea!" etc.

    This makes those Lore contests more important.

    \nTwo ways to go with this one: give the new player nothing, which puts them clearly below the existing players, which is fair and logical. The new player may want to catch up, but really as the new guy they won't question being behind.

    Or be sneaky and do the exact opposite: encourage the new player to take the half-divine option (particularly if the existing characters haven't). What, the new guy starts with a d8 Name die and we still have a d6? Bogus! Instant rivalry.

    \n When I GM, a new player gets no extra stuff for her hero when starting out. They're at the bottom of the pack. This means they are the least glorious hero, of course -- but it also means they have the longest lifespan ahead of them. The new hero may complain about their lack of glory, but the old hands will complain even more about their lack of remaining Fate.
