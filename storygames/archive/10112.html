\n Brainstorming a bit here and looking for perspectives. In the game I am working on for my father, I am going to drive character choice through cards. In looking at the source material, there are character types:

The Singing Cowboy
The World's Greatest Cowboy
The Masked Cowboy
The Gentleman Cowboy
etc.

I could assign each type to a card and be done with that. Each type would have unique bonuses during the game.

BUT. I am envisioning expansions to this game, opening the genres to science fiction, superheroes, war, or other topics of serials.

So I can see breaking the above archetypes into two components. With each component having unique bonuses during the game.

Singing, World's Greatest, Masked, Gentleman, etc.

AND

Cowboy, with expansions adding Detective, Strongman, Astronaut, etc. later on.


So my quandry is this: Do I sacrifice simplicity now for potential expansion later? Or is there another solution I'm not seeing?


Brainstorm away!

    \n If you haven't taken a look at Danger Patrol yet, you should. It does this mechanic very nicely for a pulp science fiction setting, pairing traits like "Atomic" and "Two-Fisted" with classes such as "Professor" and "Robot." I'm not sure what your design goals are, but I think that structure would work well for expansion.

    As a side note, what got me into the thread at first was western character types. Just the other day I was reading this analysis of Stagecoach and was struck by the archetypal list of characters:

    a prostitute (or dance hall gal) forced to leave town
    a embezzling banker
    a Confederate gambler
    a whiskey salesman
    an alcoholic, disgraced frontier doctor
    a pregnant young bride, the wife of an Army officer en route to his post
    a stage driver
    a Marshal riding shotgun
    a rugged, escaped outlaw

    I guess my point is that a good story isn't all about the cowboys, so be sure not to neglect the other character types as you structure your cards.
