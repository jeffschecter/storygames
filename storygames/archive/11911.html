\n Last week we tried out Apocalypse world for the first time, we made characters (a Savyhead, Battlebabe and a Chopper) and built some basic color for our setting based on stuff that came out of making the characters. This part was awesome. We ended up with the settlement known as Now Playing, a fortified Drive-in Theater where the Savyhead Joshua would play old movies in exchange for bits of tech and scrap that were salvaged from the nearby ruins, Dice and his Chopper gang running a protection racket on the local hardholder and the Ruby the Battlebabe who just quit working for the hardholder to protect a child she found.

When we got to the actual play portion of the session it was already pretty late at this point and I think a good part of the frustration that would follow was due to some low blood sugar on my part and I should have followed my gut and ended the night there. I started the game out nice and slow a \u201cwhat happens when you wake up each morning question\u201d, I poked a bit and got some good color about the hardhold they live in and their various shelters, what they do for food and I Barfed Forth Apocalyptica all over their answers. Once this was established, I felt kinda adrift, like I didn't know where to go or what questions I should be asking of the players to generate the unbalanced situation or any of the stuff I would need to write fronts for later play. This led to some frustration and a lot of pauses on my part. We eventually stumbled on to a bit of conflict between the Ruby and the Dice's gang, and then ended the game. I think I was really missing something like a kicker or beliefs to zero my attention in on what the players wanted, which I know the 1st session is supposed to create as a result of play but I was really unsure of how to get there.

So as the MC what sort of questions should I be asking the players to generate situation? To those who have played it, what type of things have you asked?
Should my prep prior to the 1st session have included some ideas as to NPC's? Because once the game started my mind was a blank slate regarding NPC or should I be asking pointed questions about the type of people the players know and deal with to create my NPC with?

I have some ideas about stuff I want to try, but these have definitely come out of thinking and mulling over things for a week. But procedurally what sort of things should I have been doing in that first session.

In the end the only thing I think I really nailed was Barfing Forth Apocalyptica, man I did that at every chance and I loved it. I think this came nice and easy due to the pre-game prep of essentially day dreaming up apocalyptic imagery and to start forming an aesthetic so I had stuff to draw on already or at least vague ideas and wants.

I am still really excited about this game, and between the book, blog and posts here I think I have a solid understanding of how the game works for everything except that 1st session bit.

    \n When everyone looks at you to say something and you don't know what to say, make a GM move. That's what they're there for. You'll do the thing where you misdirect, etc., but first, just pick one that sounds good to you. Announce future badness and Put someone in a spot are favorites of mine.

    Now you've picked a move, but you have to wrap it up in fiction. So you have to daydream a bit. Put someone in a spot... maybe someone taps the Savvyhead on the shoulder while he's working and he turns around to see a shotgun barrel in his face. "Surprise, fucker! Hand over the stuff." Now this guy with the shotgun needs a name and a dirt-simple motivation and that's about it. He's Cricket, the asshole scrounge-thief. You don't have to know any more than that. Ask the character what they do, call for rolls, make follow-up moves as needed.

    If you do that for each PC you'll have a few grabby NPCs by the end and a growing sense of who the threats are. Try to stay in the present moment as much as possible and follow the fiction where it leads. Answer questions as they arise naturally. "How come this filthy scrounge-thief gets to just waltz in and out of our holding?" "Um.. because the hardholder is fucking him? That's the rumor. Supposedly it's hands-off when it comes to Cricket. What do you do?"

    Like the book says, look for the places where they're not in control. You do this so you can tie your GM moves in to the fiction. You're not looking at the PCs through crosshairs! They'll feel like you are, of course, but that's because Apocalypse World is all brutality and scarcity and danger when you make it seem real. You're actually looking at the NPCs through crosshairs and figuring out how you can bring them down.

    The secret voodoo of AW is this: Nothing brings down an NPC as fast or as well as sending them up against the PCs. Because, man, the PCs are fucking badasses. So, it might look like the GM is making the PC's lives harder by arranging all these threats against them, but really it's the other way around. Those threats are about to have their sorry little worlds permanently fucked by the PCs.

    Anyway, I'm rambling now. Does that help at all? I can maybe say more if you have questions.

    \n The book gives an example of having conflict in the first session, and it's roughly the same as "suddenly there's a shotgun in your face." Perhaps the key here is not to avoid conflicts but to keep them small and manageable.

    In our first session I asked the Angel player "so you're sitting in the outhouse and you see Kilo coming at you, fists balled up and blood in his eyes- what did you do to piss him off?" the result was s small one-on-one scuffle that was more like a setup for later trouble. Bringing out a whole gang armed for killing would be getting ahead of yourself.

    As for NPCs, I think of them as part of the apocalyptica- what kind of people make the background of the holding? Who is in charge of the resources the players need? Also look to the Chopper's gang, the savvy's crew, and other such followers for instant NPCs- who's the second in line for pack alpha? Who's working another angle against the gang's interests? Who's in love with the Skinner?

    \n Here's a thing:

    Try really really hard to never think or say the word "conflict" anywhere near Apocalypse World. I mean that both in the SG jargony way and also the plain English way.

    Action and consequences -- that's what you do. "Conflict" is too abstract, too movie and stage, too neat and tidy. So, don't set up conflicts between characters (even though that's fun in other games). Don't think about how this could be in conflict with that. Don't think about how you can drive the game towards conflict. None of that.

    Just have an NPC (who is not that complicated) take action toward getting something they want, or away from something they fear, or toward something they fear but still want. That NPC might be in a Front (by session two) and maybe one of that Front's countdowns ticks forward. Does anyone interfere? What happens then? And on like that, moves snowballing.

    It's pedantic of me, I know. But trust me. Kill the word conflict dead when you play this game.

    \n I'd be happy to throw down, Steve. I have a feeling I'm gonna be running it a lot at GenCon .

    @Colin: I may be reading too much into what you wrote, but "the first hard moves the GM makes" makes me wonder if you're doing the thing I did when I first ran AW. It was: I only ever looked at the GM move list when someone missed a roll. Then I was like, "Oh! Now I get to pick one of these and make a hard move."

    Instead, the GM moves are what you always say. Whenever you need to talk and you're not sure what you should say, you make a move. Cover it in fiction, misdirect, and barf apocalyptica on it (so it doesn't look like you're saying a move), of course. So the GM moves are constantly in play. I'd be hard pressed to think of anything I say during a game now that isn't one of those moves.

    Like I said, maybe you already know this. It's something I had to remind myself of a lot for my first few sessions.

    "The infirmary is an old burned-out bus that was stripped down and turned into a kind of metal shack. Used to be Dremmer's pad before he got marched out of the holding at gunpoint. Some big pieces of tin have been welded over the windows in places to keep the sun off you, but it leaks like a sieve and lets the hot wind off the burn flats blow right through -- shakes the whole thing and you don't want to be in there during a serious ash storm."

    That's, like, three GM moves right there (announce future badness, announce off-screen badness x2), setting me up for a hard move or two later. Namely, wrecking the shack in an ash-storm or having Dremmer come back looking to squat there (or both). Assuming no interference from the PCs, of course. If they know Apocalypse World, they know that when I say things I'm giving them the chance to react. "Oh, it's all leaky and shit? That sucks. I need to get some materials to patch it up better and secure it real good." "Cool. So you go talk to Roarke?"

As the MC the games procedures looked kinda like this from my read, though I did not realize the hole in my understanding till we got started:

    Step 1 - follow the characters around and take it easy, ask questions.
    Step 2 - ???
    Step 3 - Profit! You have an unbalanced situation to build your fronts from.

    I was having trouble forming the right questions and getting things started, and was wondering what questions I should be asking to get to the cool stuff and figure out that 2nd step. That second step was: Use the answers to make moves, and if I can't make a move then to just keep asking till I can or the players make a move. Everything locked together nicely after that little adjustment. And we had some amazing fiction that we played out and I have enough fodder to make 2-3 fronts for the next session.

    The thing is that you use the rules 100% of the time in AW, and remember rules are not just when to roll the dice and such.     Posted By: Kaare Bergand even better those rules are pretty much what every Indie or Trad GMs do all the time in games, only they have been focused and codified into the Agenda,
    \nI'd like to point out that this kinda-sorta true but not entirely. Why does the Apocalypse World thing work? It's not just "how everyone GMs anyway". I mean, almost, but not quite. There are several things working together all at once to produce satisfying, interesting, conflict-ridden fiction without an overt focus on specific Conflict like some other games have:

    1. Apocalypse World itself. By definition, we start in an unstable situation with the potential for violence and desperation (a large part of the "Agenda", thinks like "make Apocalypse World real" are about establishing this at the table).
    2. The advice for the GM to basically apply pressure and make bad stuff happen (the "moves").
    3. The various rules for rolling and moves and when they interact so as to produce strong consequences pretty much every time (whether positive to the PCs or negative).

    These three things work together; take any one out and they'll collapse.

    #1 enables #2, making it possible, potential, and available all the time. #3 helps to preserve that, taking actions and turning the outcomes into more #1. Like how you get those supplies you wanted, but, chances are you lost some other important thing or made new enemies doing it.

    So, if you take Apocalypse World rules but play in "happy present-day land", and you've got no Fronts ready, it's not going to work, right? Because there's little support for #2 from #1. Or, if you have #1, but the GM is waiting for the second session to get to #2, you also could fall flat. That's why the GM's got to be using the moves even in the first session. And if you're lacking #3, you can get all sorts of problems people have been having with D&D for years.

    So, Vincent's slick with this stuff.

    edit: Obviously, I'm agreeing with Kaare, not disagreeing, unlike what my first sentence seems to suggest!
