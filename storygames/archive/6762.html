\n Picked this out from a post in Andrew Doull's blog, Ascii Dreams:\n
From the Journal of Ysaneya:As you may know, natural language generation (NLG) is one of those programming concepts around which science fiction has woven circles of mysticism, along with greatly inflated expectations and misplaced hopes. Still, NLG is capable of a lot, if by 'a lot' we mean the NL expression of information according to carefully detailed rules and pre-written natural phrases.

For the purposes of story generation, this means that the computer program does not invent or "make up" the events that happen in the story -- the individual events are pre-written by the human designer. Nor does the program invent phrases to express these events -- those phrases are also pre-written by the designer. The program's job is simply to take these elements, the events and the phrases, and automatically combine them in a way that's presentable -- according to rules defined by none other than, yes, the designer.

So, as you would expect, automatic story generation does not make things easier for the human designer -- this you'll see firsthand if you decide to experiment with it. But once the system is in place, the program can automatically generate thousands of unique stories, by combining pre-defined elements in various ways.
\nThe site includes a link to download a copy of their prototype, a backstory generator of sorts. It looks interesting, but for some damn reason the app doesn't work on my PC. Screenshots and the docs look promising, though, so take a look.

What's primarily interesting is how they've structured the declarative language used to create the story generator. Instead of a purely static chronologically-based system, they've done it in a way that looks really interesting, but quite a bit to take in -- I've only skimmed the initial post explaining the algorithm, so I'm not exactly clear how it works just yet, and how it differs from previous random generators I've seen in S-G.

But, in any case, go take a look. It's not a S-G, but I'll bet there's plenty good ideas to shamelessly appropriate be inspired from.
