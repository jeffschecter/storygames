\n Hi!

I stumbled upon an interesting fenomenon in two boardgames. I think it is appropriate to ask this question in an rpg-forum, you'll see it for yourselves. (Excuse me for the wrong nomenclature, as I played the Hungarian versions of these games, so I have to translate back the specific names.)

First in Arkham Horror my character was travelling in an alien dimension (Yuggoth if I remember right) and had a whiskey. Then I drew a card which stated that everyone who has whiskey and don't succeed on a roll ends up in jail. I was wondering: how on Earth do the policemen get to Yuggoth to apprehend me? But I noticed that the card lacked the "in Arkham" condition, which is usual for cards like these. According to the game rules I should roll and see if I get back into the jail in Arkham from Yuggoth. I rolled for fun and succeeded the test easily, so it didn't become a problem.

Second yesterday (it was today, being after midnight) I was playing Battlestar Galactica, and one of the players (who were secretly a cylon, although we all knew that he was one) played a card which gave an action to another player on his turn - but this other player was a cylon, who already uncovered himself as a cylon and travelled around in the cylon locations. The card stated, that he can give that action to "any player", but the "coloring" of the card stated, that it's an executive order passed from a crewman to another crewman, and it would be really impossible for him to help the cylons organize their actions behind the enemy lines.

My problem is this: Much of the fun factor of these games originate from the theme and setting of the game, the color, the graphics and that the rules try to simulate/emulate/whateveryouwant the literal source which they are derived from. But being boardgames the players assume that they have to follow the rules verbatim and can't override them by pointing out irrealism or things like that. (I can't fly over the Battlestar Galactica, if the rules state that I have to fly around it.)

So my question is: How do you resolve these kind of dilemmas? How should it be resolved? Where is the point when I have to say that: the rules are worded inproperly and when should I say that this rule is more important than "realism" or "believability"? How much should we protect suspension of disbelief or how much should we assume that the verbatim rules are tested as balanced and more fun on the long run than changing them because of momentary dislikes? What's your opinion about this phenomenon?

    \n Largely, I think these are merely oversights either in the basic rules themselves, or errors in translation.
    I can't speak specifically for the Battlestar Galactica game. However, I am pretty familiar with Arkham Horror. The event that you refer to, where characters with the whiskey have to roll or go to jail, I believe specifically requires the character to be in the streets of Arkham, and it doesn't apply if they are in another dimension. It is possible that there was a translation error.

    I very rarely come across these conflicts between the story fiction and game rules, and in most cases, the creators release corrections or errata through their websites. More often, when I see these clashes, they are with the more mass-produced board games, rather than the more niche games.

    \n Boardgames like Monopoly are often held up as games you don't hack in play but Monopoly might be hacked more than most RPGs!

    That said, one thing I've noticed is these changes almost always take away options from the person wielding the card, monster or power in question. When the group could just as easily keep the mechanics the same and add a fun twist to the color to make it work. It ends up not only helping boost most people's sense of disbelief but also giving one side a mechanical advantage.

    Unless it is a blatant mistake, we try to add a twist to the color (which setting wise makes us even more immersed) or let the person gain a new advantage to make up for the lost one.
