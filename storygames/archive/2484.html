Gaining And Losing Players

Tim Boser
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Play Advice
Not sure if this has been brought up before, or if it even belongs in this category, but here goes anyway.

I recently ran an attempt at a Sorcerer game set in colonial America, specifically right around the time of the Salem witch trials, and it sort of failed. Partly because I didn't have a good firm grasp on Sorcerer (and quite possibly still don't), and partly because I was consistantly letting players join and leave at a whim, to the point where there were actually 11 player characters involved in said story. As I look back on it, I realized that the whole thing I created in my notes and in my head weren't cohesive at all to allowing people to just randomly add and remove their characters. I wanted to focus on just the few characters we had at the start but, since I'm a nice guy, wasn't willing to turn people away at the time. I have since learned my lesson, I think, and hopefully this won't happen again.

So I guess the first question I have is this; are there games out there that don't tend to work out, in any situation (extended or short play) when player characters are suddenly added or removed between sessions or during play?

My second question relates to the first: Have any of you had any situations come up where the addition or sudden loss of a player character has caused a storyline to cave in on itself? For this one, I think I should be specific and say that I'm not talking about situations about disruptive players or non-cohesive characters. I'm referring to the odd situation where the character in question doesn't seem like it will be disruptive by entering or leaving the story, but then as the story unfolds, it falls on its face.

Tim

    There are, in my experience, lots of games which don't tend to work out when player characters are suddenly added or removed between sessions or during play. So many that it still wouldn't be worth the time to list them even if you could separate instances of "this particular storyline/plot/whatever collapses if the characters involved are removed and new characters added" from the equation.

    I'd say that it's more important to think of the game you're actually running and determine whether that specific game (storyline, system, etc.) is set up to accomodate new PCs bopping in and out of the story at will. Some stories could easily support that with a minimum of handwaving, others won't ever manage it even if you poured all your effort into trying to make it work. Some systems can easily support it (fast and easy character generation, troupe mechanics, etc.), others can't. And honestly, some people can easily support it, while others simply can't: I could never, ever, EVER run a game with 11 players in it, and I sincerely doubt I could even play in one.


    Anyway, it sounds like you learned the most important lesson: saying "sorry, I'm afraid I can't fit any more players into this game" to people when you genuinely can't fit any more players in is really difficult, but it has to be done. Given what you've just experienced, you'll have better luck next time, I guarantee it!

    I would say that most games will not support players coming in and going out, unless you think ahead of time and plan for that contingency.

    Sorceror seems to me peculiarly ill-suited for this, given that it aims to provide conflicts that resonate at the player level, which must be hard if the players change week to week.

    The thing is, whether you go for conflict driven play, dynamic situations into which you throw the PCs, prestructured plots that the PCs move through or whatever none of these things will cope well if the main protagonists keep changing.

    Unless that is you plan from the outset to consider how the conflicts may alter, situation may change or plot may flex to cope with characters coming in and going out. And that will be specific to the particular game planned.

    But generally, I would have thought any game aiming at some kind of thematic significance to the actual players would be damaged by players changing week to week, whereas by contrast a game aimed at simulating the life of a tramp steamer in 1930s South-East Asia could probably cope fairly easily with characters coming in and going out.

    I haven't had any problems like this for some time, but I've found ways to structure around it. I no longer structure a game such that the loss of one or two characters would be the downfall of a game. Experience has taught me that you never know when a new job, romantic interest, or other event might cause a player to drop out of a game.

    Here are a few thoughts:

    If a character is gone temporarily:
    * Do a flashback session. Alternatively, play the bad guys or characters in the distant future. All of these things can continue to develop the current story and don't require a specific character to be present.
    * Throw out several side hooks, such as an old friend coming back from out of town.
    * Just don't include material that requires the presence of that character.
    * Run the character as an NPC.

    If a character is gone permanently:
    * Shift current, important plots to other PCs where possible.
    * Allow arcs specific to that character to fall away. If you hit the other characters with interesting arcs at the same time the players wont even notice.
    * Run the character as an NPC.
