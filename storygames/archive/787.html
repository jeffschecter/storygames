Just a random question here -
What games do you know that use props in actually determining mechanical success?

Like, physical objects aside from dice, dreidels, etc...

EXAMPLE OF WHAT I'M TALKING ABOUT: (example involves gun violence, to be warned)
Random game idea me and a friend came up with, called Desperation:
Each player has a capgun... before play, fire the gun 7 times... so that only one "cap" remains.
Spin the cap gun barrel, so you aren't sure how many times you will have to pull the trigger before hitting the cap. The cap gun is now set up for "Russian Roulette".

Gameplay works as such:
Narration goes around in turns.
A player can say, "I don't like that narration. I want to block it."
That player then points the cap gun at the narrator, and pulls the trigger.
If the cap goes off, then the narration is blocked, the scene considered void...
And that player's character will die, as narrated by the shooter.

For each time you point the gun at another player, you will then have to point the gun at your own narration. This means it will be shoot other, shoot self, shoot other...
If the cap goes off, then the narration is changed... into a scene where the narrator/shooter must narrate their character's own self-destruction.

[/END EXAMPLE]

So... in that, the props (fake guns) were being used to add to the tension and tone of the game.
The mechanic could have worked with pulling slips of paper out of a hat...
It could have been altered to work with dice...

But in the end the idea of shooting each other, and adding in elements of Russian Roulette... captured the exact mood we wanted:
-gritty, tense, violent, desperate, horrifying.

Any other games use props as a way to directly determine success?

    There's not a lot of games that require physical props, truthfully.

    But that doesn't stop us from thinking of some cool ways props could be used.

    I was thinking of a mod to the old d6 (Star Wars) game that could be tooled to any system, but it was specifically for roleplaying in MACROSS/Robotech. Basically all the players had to go out and buy:

    A toy airplane. Try to keep them all within the same general size.

    A toy robot. The robot needed a gun/rifle, and posable arms/legs.

    Basically, in dogfight scenes you wouldn't use tactics, you would pick up two planes and demonstrate how you're closing in, banking, barreling, etc, for a high bonus (like 2d, or in Fudge FATE a +1), with an option of getting double that if other agreed it was particularly cool.

    The robot was for the scenes ala Macross Plus, so you can demonstrate flying, "transforming" into robot mode to shoot, then transform into a plane again to continue flying (just swap the robot in for the plane).

    When I thought about the awesome action scenes of the anime, and then think about running it normal RPG style (on a 2D plane with little minis and rounds and stuff), it just totally didin't fit.

    There are Macross toys that transform:


    However, because I wanted it high action, the act of transforming it back and forth would be too long (in the anime they'll flip between modes within like 2 seconds, and it would take a minute or so to physically transform them back and forth. Plus, they're too expensive to buy two per person).

    Basically it's a game of "Action Direction" (ala movies).

    -Andy

    I like the cigarettes and the cap gun. It's interesting that the two main ways of using props are as randomisers and as timers.

    I'm working on a game called Tentacly Oozes Battle At The Edge Of Time which uses Plasticine. Each player has a Plasticine monster (a Tentacly Ooze).

    To attack, you mould one of your arms outwards, without touching the floor, until it hits the other monster. For the attack to succeed, the moulded arm must stay in place for five seconds, without falling. So that's a sort of Plasticine-based resolution system.

    The juggling is interesting too. I'm reminded of French LARPs where, to do something like pick a lock, you actually have to solve a puzzle (like one of those wire puzzles where you separate two twisted wires, or a sliding block puzzle or something).
