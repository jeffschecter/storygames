\n Hello everyone,

So I've been working on unWritten for several years now. It's now close to completion, for the 5th and last time. It's going into alpha or beta playtest, probably next week.
(Which is it? Alpha or beta? I need people to play it when I'm not there.)

Anyway, I need to figure out my elevator pitch. So, let me tell you a bit about it. Perhaps you can help.

It does for film & literature what PTA did for TV sitcoms.
It's GMless, the roll is divided up amongst several players.
Players frame scenes around their own protagonist.
It produces emergent play, in that the protagonists, the setting, and the story are created through play. At the beginning of the game you just have 3 setting details and your protagonists name and role (each player gets a protagonist).
It uses the Hero's Journey and the 5 act Structure/Play as a tool for character and scene development.

Those are the basics. I'm not sure how to bill this thing.

unWritten Continuum, my blog about the game.

    \n Thanks, those are awesome criticism.

    I have found that there are 3 types of people when it comes to the hero's journey: There are those who love it, those who hate it, and those who don't know what it is. I happen to be one who started in the love it camp, thinking it was this incredible thing, but now I've come to an understanding that it is really this vague interpretation of story in the guise of specific examples and expectations. Thus, from learning it one would think that it can only do Star Wars or similar types of stories. In fact, it allows for so much more because it's a bunch of really basic concepts. For example, you have one or more protagonists, there is conflict, there is something that is not what it seems to be, there is a cause to the conflict, etc. I just described 3 main components to the hero's journey: The Hero, The Refusal, The Shapeshifer, and The Shadow, etc. I wish I could have come up with some better examples, but it's late.

    That said, I think you will find that, despite the hero's journey, you will be able to produce stories that you both (Daniel and Antti) would appreciate.

    I, also, have my doubts about mentioning the hero's journey in the pitch (now more then before). Paul created a great line and I ran with that.

    "unWritten is a game that takes traditional story structures and embeds them into the mechanics. Through its play a story emerges for the players as it would for the audience of film and literature. In the end you will have produced a thematically engaging story set in your favorite genre."

    So, yes, it uses the hero's journey, but not as a strict framework.

    I am not familiar with New Wave or modernist novels. can you please refer some to me.

    Thanks Alex

    \nHi Alejandro. Modernist Novels:
    The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock by T.S. Eliot.
    T.S. Eliot's The Waste Land
    Virginia Woolf's Kew Gardens and Mrs Dalloway,
    James Joyce's Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man and Ulysses,
    Katherine Porter's Flowering Judas
    Jean Toomer's Cane,
    William Faulkner's The Sound and the Fury.
    Ernest Hemingway's The Old Man and the Sea, \n
    Posted By: alejandroWhat is it that Modernist Literature does that other "types" does not do?
    \nI got this from the wiki:

    Modernist literature is defined by its move away from Romanticism, venturing into subject matter that is traditionally mundane. Modernist literature often features a marked pessimism, a clear rejection of the optimism apparent in Victorian literature. In fact, "a common motif in Modernist fiction is that of an alienated individual--a dysfunctional individual trying in vain to make sense of a predominantly urban and fragmented society". However, many Modernist works like T.S. Eliot's The Waste Land are marked by the absence of a central, heroic figure; in rejecting the solipsism of Romantics like Shelley and Byron, these works reject the subject of Cartesian dualism and collapse narrative and narrator into a collection of disjointed fragments and overlapping voices. Modernist literature transcends the limitations of the Realist novel with its concern for larger factors such as social or historical change; Modernism as a literary movement is seen, in large part, as a reaction to the emergence of city life as a central force in society.

    The French New Wave is a film thing. Also from wiki:

    Although never a formally organized movement, the New Wave filmmakers were linked by their self-conscious rejection of classical cinematic form and their spirit of youthful iconoclasm. Many also engaged in their work with the social and political upheavals of the era, making their radical experiments with editing, visual style, and narrative part of a general break with the conservative paradigm.

    The movement has its roots in rebellion against the reliance on past forms (often adapted from traditional novellic structures), criticizing in particular the way these forms could force the audience to submit to a dictatorial plot-line. New Wave critics and directors studied the work of these and other classics. They did not reject them, but rather found a new outlet for the same creative energies. The low-budget approach helped film-makers get at the essential art form and find what, to them, was a much more comfortable and honest form of production. Charlie Chaplin, Alfred Hitchcock, Orson Welles, Howard Hawks, John Ford, and many other forward-thinking film directors were held up in admiration while standard Hollywood films bound by traditional narrative flow were strongly criticized.
