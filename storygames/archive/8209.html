\n
\n
\n
\nInspired by Ryan, I've been playing Final Fantasy III for the first time, an experience that I missed growing up, due to not having a Super Nintendo and all. I remember going to a friend's house and watching them play it, but quickly becoming bored by the repetitive combat and the lack of anything happening. Definitely one of those games that's much more fun to play than watch.

And, as I'm playing this, I'm like... huh, hit points in this game are weird because attacks never miss, they just do a variable amount of damage. However, the amount by which they vary (at least in the early game) isn't usually large enough to make gambling a key part of the strategy. You can't try to squeak by enemies by hoping that they "roll low" on damage, so it's best to be overprepared and totally outclass your opponents.

Instead, hit points (of the enemies) are a timer for how long the fight lasts. And the other hit points (your own) are a timer for how often you have to use your resources (potions, healing spells, etc.). So it's not really a tactical game, much. It's really a resource management game in the style of Nobilis or Marvel Universe or Mortal Coil.

And then I was like: missing totally sucks. Why do most roleplaying games do the wiff thing at all? Like, why don't attacks in D&D4 ALWAYS HIT and then you just roll damage. If that's too strong, then at least it seems like attacks should ALWAYS DO SOMETHING USEFUL even if that useful thing is not pushing the fight towards its conclusion by damaging the enemy.

So, like, Star Wars. Luke, Han, and Leia are trying to escape some stormtroopers, who are firing at them. The stormtroopers miss all over the place. But their fire does one useful thing, right? It pins the trio down to a location and creates a problem. They need to get to the Falcon, but they're stuck here with these stormtroopers. They can keep shooting troopers, but more keep coming. The good guys are shooting the troopers too, though. What useful thing does that do? It keeps th troopers a fair distance away, to keep the group from being overrun while they figure out a solution.

What doesn't happen, in any of the media I think D&D and most combat/adventure games are trying to emulate, is the characters trying something and having NOTHING HAPPEN. Why in the world would you want that? If you're gonna miss somebody, they should at least have to dive behind a table, changing the terrain a bit and maybe giving you an advantage. In all cases, SOMETHING SHOULD HAPPEN.

Am I going crazy here? Is there a good reason for designing static situations in games, where energy and resources (like combat turns) are spent on achieving nothing?

    \n I don't find "miss nothing" so annoying when "thus there is another chance to suffer harm/see your goal destroyed/other side's goal achieved".

    That said, I do like narrative games where "the fiction advances every round", IAWA some part of the challenge action does take place etc., Find Annalise - you're ver unlikely to end up with having a "nothing good and nothing bad" happens outcome and have to work very hard at it in fact.

    The key on those points is the move from simulationist to narrative basis of resolution I think. One can imagine simulating action where "he missed nothing happens" but narratively imagining a "scene" where "nothing happens that advances story or reveals character or somehow moves things" is more difficult.

    That said, yup, 4e D&D would be cool if "every action did something", "that melee attack missed you, as long as you say you moved back a square, or had to drop prone, or your sword or shield broke blocking it, or you used the chair and it was smashed". Of course the designers did create lots of powers with "miss effects" already so in that case the wiff-disliking player can focus more on moving to selecting those powers.

    \n Lots of things come to mind, some of which are more tangential than the others.

    1. When we played D&D, a miss rarely meant nothing happening. It might have been "You miss, so the monster gets a turn" or "You fail to pick the lock and the guard comes closer".

    2. When I played, a miss often led to colour narration: "The arrow flies past the ogre's head, enraging him still further". That narration didn't always stay as colour: "All right, if it's enraged, I'm going to get behind the pit and provoke it to charge at me."

    3. A miss can mean "think of more options". "No, the lock's stuck fast. Think of another way in". It's a sort of "Deal with it" statement. Yeah, that's how the fiction is now, what are you going to do about it?

    4. The more I play story games, the more I want rolls to be about the thing we're doing. You're trying to hack the computer (or, if you want to be conflict-resolutiony about it, you're trying to find out some information) and if the roll fails, I want that to fail. No "you succeed but narrate a disadvantage". No narrating flashbacks about your brother. You fail. Deal with it from there.

    5. I also think it's important failure exists as a contrast to success. Success would be dull if there wasn't sometimes a failure to complement it.

    Graham

    \n Hmm, in the version that only exists in my head, I think it depends on what you mean by "beating." As I imagine it, what happens is that -- as the count goes down -- the characters' actions negotiate with the other events occurring (the GM, basically) and, when the end count is reached, an outcome is arrived at. Outcomes could then include all sorts of things: slaying the monster, befriending the monster, finding out the monster wasn't actually a monster after all, even being eaten by the monster (what would in other games be achieved by "losing," in this game would still involve reaching the end of the count and attaining a resolution). "Does the fighter live or die", but see all those rounds of whiffing built *tension* and it was totally awesome. Sure, in a neat one roll conflict resolution system you could narrate about how the Cleric desperately climbs, slides back, climbs again, finally makes it up, and reaches the fighters side just as he's about to expire, but for me, without experiencing the moment by moment uncertainly myself, it just wouldn't have the same impact.

    \n That part is even less thought through than the rest. Here's a few unformed thoughts on various options.

    One would be an fiction-leading approach like In a Wicked Age, where the pacing mechanic would structure the challenge without providing clear guidelines for resolving anything. So, in your example, it would depend on the order in which all those things happened in the fiction, how the GM chose to play the monster, and other things of that sort. If I was calming it, then you intimidated it right before Graham poked it in the eye, it would probably run away, holding one claw over its bleeding socket. But, most likely, the monster would also be doing things in the meantime, things that might negotiate a different sort of outcome. Like, maybe it would use its Inflict Horror power to cause Graham's "but I'm really a pansy" trait to countdown to 0, making Graham wet himself instead of stabbing it in the eye. That hints at a slightly more complicated system, whereby one might target things besides a generic "reach the end of this encounter," such as outstanding plot elements, character development, and the like. So one could conceivably be working on multiple different counts at once, of which "the current encounter" might not even be the most interesting.

    There could also be more formal guidelines for negotiating outcomes. I can imagine a way of sorting out when players are cooperating at achieving a countdown and when they are working at different purposes, working on different counts. For example, all three of us might be trying to make different things happen and the one who reaches 0 first is the one who gets their result first. That doesn't necessarily prevent other characters from achieving their count as well, but it does help provide structure to the resolution if characters are being more competitive. If you scare the monster away before I can calm it, that probably means my plan is less likely to work now. Also, it becomes harder for Graham to stab it if it's running away. But, then again, the encounter if over faster if we're not working so directly at cross-purposes, which supports the majority or a consensus in a way I think it not uncommon or unusual.

    Does that make sense?

    \n I've recently designed an rpg which finally, for me, comfortably addresses the situation. We've had a solid round of playtests and the results are positively stunning.

    In a nutshell: You roll before you declare any action in the fiction, because you're not rolling to hit, you're rolling for priviledge in the fiction. Any action in the fiction which calls for priviledge is declared before rolling. If you roll low, you can still act in pretty much any way which isn't attacking the enemy. So, if you roll crap, then you can do whatever else you'd like, according to the priviledge you have, and there are certain actions which you can always do, and so while waiting for your turn (which doesn't take nearly as long), you have layered decision making: "If I roll X, I plan on A, if Y then B, else C."

    Rolling still matters, and being better at what you do accords you further narrative priviledge more consistently in those particular arenas.
