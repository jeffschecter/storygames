\n Happy 45th birthday to me.

So all these games are about sexy young people. Apocalypse World's gas mask fetishists, aerobicized multi-ethnic dungeon delvers in form-fitting armour, vampire Monsterhearts trouble teens, yadda yadda.

What about fantastic reflections on creeping age? It seems like the wizards of Ars Magica are my fantastical doubles.

    \n First edition ad&d had aging rules which we all ignored - mainly because of the stupidly long life spans of dwarves and elves which rendered aging rules for those races irrelevant. Half orcs suffered......

    I've never experienced aging a character through a campaign. I knew a guy once who on 1st January every year took out all his character sheets for all games and aged them all a year "like racehorses".

    I suppose it would only work if you "leapt" ten years every so often. Play a minicampaign to its conclusion. Play another game for a bit. Come back - "ten years have passed....."

    \n Inspired by Gemmel's Legend and Pratchett's Cohen the Barbarian, we once ran a BW campaign with only human stock that had to be at least 66 years old (7- 10 Lifepaths mostly), which limited their physical stat pool but bolstered their mental acuity and gave a veritable tome of skills and traits. The situation was one of aging heroes called out of 'retirement' one last time as a prophesied plague of dragons lay waste to all and sundry. It was epic and we played up to all the tropes of 'grumpy old farts with swords', as well as some timeless scenes where the character's wealth of life experience far outweighed their lack of strength or dexterity. Traits too - being numerous - were often invoked to cause trouble for the troupe and generate much Artha.

    \n The burnout factor is part of the reality. And all these discussions of the ficitonal fractalization of that reality are interesting too. But what about the love? I gotta watch all of these young people make the same mistakes I made, or their splendid idealism thrash about for something to latch on to and see them being offered a truly sucktastic range of choices. Trying to keep the young'uns from walking off the edge of the cliff without being Captain Condescending McMansplainer is a tightrope I find myself walking a lot these days. Gandalf seems to have no problem sending the unprepared on suicide missions and then berating them when they don't measure up to expectations. I can't cop to that archetype.
