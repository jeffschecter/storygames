    \n I think everyone approaches contests in different ways (or, well, doesn't approach them at all, which is cool, too). But it has to be a personal thing with meaning for you, because you won't win, and your game will be half-baked, and you might not get any useful criticism. So with that as a set of ridiculous benchmarks, just be clear about your own goals. I find the artificial constraints spur my creativity and, despite my expectations, I often get really useful feedback. Reading, playing, and commenting on other people's work is also very inspirational and rewarding. Half-baked stuff I do for contests often worms its way into more serious projects.

    Game Chef is a great way to get your feet wet.

    \nI agree.

    Also, the Endeavor forum at the Forge is a good place to look. Otherwise, Game Chef is annual (coming up in March/April or so), there's also the 24 Hour RPG challenge which you can conceivably engage in at any time. Also, there's a ton of challenges buried in these forums which are still valid (choose a competitor, and co-write games, etc), try the Advanced Search and search thread titles for "challenge" or "contest".

    I'm currently waffling on what to do about this year's Game Chef. Since we've had an awesome universe-like explosion of all sorts of ideas in all sorts of directions, I'm thinking that this year I want to try to roll things back to something traditional, perhaps even utilizing the theme constraints and rules constraints that were really successful some 2-3 years ago. I'm definitely going to take it further in a direction of "No Real Winner". That is, putting more into the feedback part of the contest (perhaps increasing participation level somehow), while completely killing the "To WIN!!!" aspect.
