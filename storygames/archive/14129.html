I can't stand it when people use "you" when they should be using "thou". "You" is plural. It's not rocket science. Those who do are obviously worried about offending speakers of modern English, when in fact, my actual English ancestors invented the language, so those people who can't legitimately claim that must attend to my authority.

\n

And as for which character you use? Man, it's like capitalization. You're revealing your illiteracy when you don't spell it "\xfeou". (And no, I can't abide the lazy use of "\xfe\u0367". Write the word out. We write from left to right in English, hippie!)

    \n I've sometimes wondered whether, sometimes, two English speakers can mean different things when they say "you" - and never know that they're talking about different things. For instance, when talking to a class, teacher A might say "you have to remember your homework" and think "singular you", addressing each student individually (in her mind); another might say the very same thing, but think "plural you", seeing/addressing the class as a group. Nobody would ever know the difference between what these two teachers were thinking.

    I find this fascinating. The idea that we can talk, believe that others see what we're seeing; yet a simple change in language structure can reveal that we're seeing very different things. Like going from two to three dimensions and seeing that what you thought was two crossing lines aren't actually touching at all.

    Historical digression: no. There were, at a certain stage in English, two pronouns, "you" and "\xfeou". Then, we stopped using the letter thorn (\xfe) and people had no clue what it was and figured it was a "y". This was a completely different change from the loss of \xfeou, which happened later. But it was the loss of \xfe that led to people writing the definite article "\xfee" as "ye", because they didn't know what that old letter was. End historical digression.

      \n From Bill Bryson's "The Mother Tongue", p61, telling about the different dialects under the Norman rule, up to Chaucer and, later, Shakespeare:

      "Although East Midlands was the preeminent dialect, not all East Midlands forms triumphed. Why this northern provincialism should gradually have taken command of a basic verb form is an enduring mystery. It may simply be that the -s form made for smoother spoken English.       Historical digression: no. There were, at a certain stage in English, two pronouns, "you" and "\xfeou". Two cases merged (accusative and something else, can't remember) and become 'you'.

      By the time of Early Modern English (which still had a case system, although diminished), we had the familiar paradigm. Thou and ye are singular and plural nominatives, thee and you are accusatives, etc. The plural was also used as the formal singular.

      \nYou and Thou are the same thing. Y = Th. Ye Olde whatever = The Old whatever.

      Historical digression: no. There were, at a certain stage in English, two pronouns, "you" and "\xfeou". Then, we stopped using the letter thorn (\xfe) and people had no clue what it was and figured it was a "y". This was a completely different change from the loss of \xfeou, which happened later. But it was the loss of \xfe that led to people writing the definite article "\xfee" as "ye", because they didn't know what that old letter was. End historical digression.

      \nWrong, it had nothing to do with not knowing what it was. It had to do with the printing press coming over to England, and not having a fount to support English writing, so Y was used. Eventually people started reading it as the present "y" sound instead of as a "th". You and thou are the same word, merely with a change in pronunciation over time.
