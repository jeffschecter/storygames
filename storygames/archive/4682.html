\n I watch the feed for PBeM2 on the off chance that I'll catch wind of something cool. Recently a game called Streets showed up.

The setting is stark and reality-based, centered squarely on the experience of homeless youth in San Francisco. A ton of story ideas pop up from this, but I had to wonder if maybe there were too many story options. At some point a GM has to put a frame around a concept, because with the ability to come up with anything, players will do just that and the end result will be something too unfocused to succeed.

Now I'm not getting down on Streets. As I wrote, I feel it's a great setting with genuine potential for meaningful stories. Beyond Streets, I've seen this happen with games/settings of all types, including many of which I've been a part. This seems to be one of those times when Jeep Truth #1 proves out: "Restrictions foster creativity."

    \n It seems like a lot of games are pretty broad. The grandaddy D&D is pushed as complete fantasy adventure. But a lot of them have the idea of an adventure made by a GM to give it focus. Others, like World of Darkness games, seem broad too, but usually have a lot of advice on how to give it focus as well.

    On the other hand, I also find play by post, or any online play, is extremely susceptible to evaporation of interest regardless of the game.

    So, I'm curious, was Streets' failing not giving any tools to the GM or players to focus a game (either advice or mechanics), forcing you to swim or sink on your own? Or do you think that the online play had a big roll in the lack of focus?
