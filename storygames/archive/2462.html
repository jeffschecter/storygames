Using wiki technology for (story)gaming

Radaghast
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Play Advice
I was writing a comment in tony dowler's topic about Actual Play Reports, and realised it might be just useful enough to make a new discussion out of it.

Not that long ago I discovered the fun and benefit of wikis in gaming, and have been advising other people to try it ever since. Well, there are basically four levels of wikis you can use for gaming.

The first one is your local wiki. You install it yourself on your website, tweak it with your own hands, let your players use it and bear with all the bugs that inevitably will be there. This is ideal for long-term projects that you don't even want to show to everyone just yet. Example.

The second one is pbwiki.com. You register there for free and have the engine installed and hosted for you. It's semi-protected in a sense that you let your players have the password, and if you post it on an open forum, well, there is a chance that someone's gonna see it (doesn't really happen in reality). This is ideal as a support for one single game (especially when campaigning, but I've never been a fan of one-session modules anyway), more than perfect for the play-by-post case. Example.

The third level is wikia.com (ex-WikiCities). This is a much more serious bunch of people who maintain the mediawiki engine for you and don't bother you if it's used too much (actually, they are quite happy if the Wikia gets popular). Before starting a new initiative, check for the existing ones first: great chance that you'll find something similar - even if that Wikia's dead and abandoned, you can adopt it and use for your own good. When creating a new Wikia, they make sure there're enough people interested, so you cannot open a Wikia for your game setting (yet you can work in the Wikia of the rpg system you use), but big ideas like "Wikia for Indie games" or "Wikia for actual play reports" usually are well-accepted. Example.

And the fourth level is, of course, the big Wikipedia. It's available in several languages, yet the English part is far greater than all the others together. Usually it is only possible to use it as a source of information (not on a pro level, I mind you), not as a place to put something in (well, you can probably write an article or two about some indies - not that anyone's gonna read'em there - but if you start writing about something too unpopular, you'll get banned, especially in English Wikipedia where everyone has a very strong opinion about everything). For example, if you're writing a setting for some tv series, you can check the name of "that guy from ep 27"; or if you're gaming in Wild West, see what year Jesse James was shot. Example.

    Level 3.5 is the one I tend to do; install Mediawiki on your own hosting, where you control all the configuration. Famous wikis around here:

    The Story Games Codex, which is an excellent source about story games
    Randomwiki, which has loads of good source material for indie gaming (new Keys for TSOY, list of towns for DitV, etc)
    Abulafia, which is a wiki of random generators aimed at roleplayers
    The Camp Nerdly Wiki, where all the organizing for that upcoming event is taking place
    If D&D's your game, DandDWiki has the whole SRD and lots of user-contributed content

    I [heart] wikis.
