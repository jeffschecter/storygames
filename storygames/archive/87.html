    Seriously, how can we expect not to? It's a behavior that is very common in a lot of fields, and the reasons for it run thick in the RPG community. Designers and writers are genuinely enthusiastic about their position, and it's easy for that to come off as overzealous. There are always going to be people who view designers as rock stars. Always always always. Similarly, there are always going to be designers who buy into their own legend. There's no way to get around it.

    It is exacerbated in corners of the community where the rough model of things is an academic one because well, anyone who's dealt with academia may have a sense that as far as big names and one true way-ism goes, RPGs end up looking pretty good by comparison. :)

    It's really easy to point fingers at the big names for hubris, or at those pointing fingers for attacking the person, but the trick of it is that either of these behaviors just help perpetuate the model. A lot of the churnfighting you see is of this type.

    So the question is "What do you do about it?" Ideally, it's good to say the emperor has no clothes, but practically speaking, the child that did that was stoned to death and the rest of the kingdom quickly got naked. Telling people to change, or chastising them for it is likely to get you nowhere. Just keeping it in mind is when you talk may be the best you can do.

    Choosing communities wisely helps too. One of the reasons I'm still enjoying it storygames is that while it's clear some people still follow the BN/1TW model, it feels much less institutionalized here, which fills me with hope. :)

    -Rob D.

    Gamer who says "I tried some other games, but they just weren't for me" > Gamer who refuses to play other games.

    Hnh. Spiky topic, but I think the more obvious considerations, such a fanboys quoting out of context, may be overshadowing some other problems.

    Here's the thing, if someone says "Lord Wazoo says XYZ!" then I think virtually everyone is well armed to deal with that, either by addressing XYZ, or by questioning the authority of Lord Wazoo. All well and good.

    The dangerous point becomes when our fanboy seeks to emulate Lord Wazoo, consciously or unconsciously. Lord Wazoo may argue in a certain fashion or may accept certain premises from which he builds his argument. When El Fanboy becomes enamored with Lord Wazoo, the concern is not that he will parrot what Lord Wazoo has to say but rather that he will accept the same premises that Lord Wazoo does, and go forth from those.

    Now, this may not always be bad. Presumably Lord Wazoo put a lot of thought into his premises, so they're hopefully not badly formed. But Adopting them rather than developing them allows from some strange mutations, and worse, when they are challenged, there is no basis except passion to defend them, and that always goes well. Now, this may seem like I'm attributing some failure on Fanboy's part here, but I'm really not - it is almost frighteningly easy to get drawn into accepting premises if you're happy with the end result.

    So this _can_ lead to tension when fanboy runs into someone who doesn't buy into the premise. Now, arguments are logical enough, but up until this point, Fanboy has presumably been talking the talk without needing to bring up the Sacred Word of Lord Wazoo because he's bought in, he doesn't need it, so this argument can go in a lot of ways, most of them bad in some way. Most often, it is frustrating for both parties because while there may have seemed an initial basis for meaningful communication, it doesn't hold up under stress, and that's a shame.

    Now, obviously, we _do_ have our share of jackass experts and drooling followers, same as any other crowd, but this is a problem that can go a little deeper than that.

    -Rob D.

    I think it's actually pretty clear that System Does Matter isn't a One True Wayism.

    1TW is a judgement call. An opinion. It's the belief that there is only one good, proper, or entertaining way to play role playing games, and that all other ways and forms are, in some way, inferior. The beliefs that real RPGs contain dice and a GM are forms of 1TW. As is the belief that the only real way to play an RPG is for the GM to provide story for the players and for the players to follow the story. Similarly, if one were to say that the only good way to play an RPG is GM-less, with shared narrarations, and completely diceless, that would be a 1TW also.

    On the other hand, System Does Matter is not an opinion. It is an observation. One that can be duplicated and demonstrated. When a group uses differing systems then differing results in play are inevitable.

    The fallacy of System Doesn't Matter comes from a long-standing tradition of overriding the game text in favor of the established system of the group. In these instances it's easy to believe that System Doesn't Matter, as it appears that no matter which game text is referanced, the resulting play is the same. What is being missed in these observations is the fact that the system of the new game text isn't actually being applied at all. Or, if it is being applied, it's only being applied in the instances where the text does not contradict the established system of the group. The system that the group has brought with them is dominant.

    You could use Capes! to run a Forgotten Realms game. However, to say that the rules of Capes! could produce the same play experience as D&D can only mean that you're ignoring the rules of one or both systems. In that case, you're probably using your own habitual system and not the systems in the texts. D&D, Capes, habits, or other, what system you use to play your RPGs does indeed matter.

    -Eric
