Like: Showing "scenes" that could happen from the game. People, action, something going on that can be attributed to the game, involving two or more characters.

Dislike: Still-life pics of characters and NPCs. Still-life pics of cars and ships and planets and stuff is doable if the subject matter requires some visual cues to "get it" (ala Blue Planet).

Very Much Dislike: Stock still-life pics of NPCs and Characters with their Gun or Sword. They're not doing anything with it (except for perhaps "aiming" or "practicing", as they're the only subject in the picture), but it's like, "Here's me. And here's my WEAPON. Which I CARRY WITH ME and AM CAPABLE OF USING. Because I am BADASS. With my WEAPON here."

-Andy

    Before JD comes on and hits me in the face and tells me for the 1000th time that I don't play art, I must say:

    I like art that gives a sense of the world and the situation, the color and flavor that makes it cool. If you're doing a book about a pseudo-ancient Sumatra, I'd better look at the pictures and go "OH! That's how they dress. Damn, look at the funky way they do their roofs! Hey, I wonder if I could do something with that funky step-temple...."

    Of course, this does mean that art in a modern-world setting game is generally less important to me than in a fantasy or historical world game whose vision I will only see with clarity through the art. I know what New York and a mobster look like, thanks. I may be less sure what a Rajput storming Akbar's fortress looks like -- so I'd like to see how he wraps his skirt, thanks.

    I want pictures of what's important to the setting, but what that is really varies. For Sci Fi it's alien races and spaceships, while for Amber its all about the character portraits because the setting really is the characters. By default, I'd rather see characters than scenery, unless the scenery is striking in a way that's hard to explain. I want it to work for it's 1000 words.

    I don't like character shots that don't show me anything interesting - specifically I'm thinking of the big-stack-of-headshots approach that Vampire and 7th Sea made use of. it was kind of interesting the first time the GM could hand over a photocopy and say "you're talking to _this_ guy" but it gets old after the 9th, especially since they're just faces.

    Plus, can't mentions bad art without remarking on the cover to Exalted's 'Savant and Sorcerer'.

    Judson:

    One of my players put together a giant archive of free headshots and photography from the web, plus scanned-in ads, etc. When I need characters for a modern game, I can just go to the folder of (say) middle-aged white women or young Asian men and pick a photo that conveys the character that I'm looking to convey, print it out, and put it on the table when I'm playing the character. (It doesn't work well for games that don't have a modern setting, because there's a lot less choice, but it's not impossible to use there either.)

    Admittedly, I don't really do this anymore; I abandoned the practice about three years ago for reasons that I'm sure made sense at the time, but that I can't really recall.

    At the time, though, it was great having 'casting' for all my NPCs. For a Vampire game I ran about five years ago, we had a website with photos and dossiers of every single NPC Kindred in the game. Players would actually start asking me things like, "Hey, did I see that guy at the party last week? Can I go talk to him now?" It let me create the feel of a much larger city than I could portray in any one encounter.

    I've got all the photo files (probably about 500-1000 different people) on a CD somewhere. If I can find it, do you want me to send you a copy?
