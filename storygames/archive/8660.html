\n We've been playing AD&D recently and it seems that many Story Gamers are dipping into the Old School well. I wonder why that is?

What's interesting for me is that I've been playing AD&D with two groups, one predominantly Story Gamers (in which I GM), the other decidedly unreconstructed (in which I run a character). Even though the approaches are different, both very good fun.

With the Old Schools it's all about killing the most monsters and getting the treasure but the group goes way back (20+ years) and the characters are so well established (20+ levels) that there exists a common history and understanding that in many ways is the same as that which Story Gamers seek to create. There's a lot of tension in the group dynamic that results from many years of adventuring together and ripping each other off. And whilst most of the characters are the cautious norm, one is so gloriously gung-ho that he drives the game forward at a speed close to that of Story Gaming.

We've got the same kinds of things going on in the Story Game group but without having to play for years to establish them. And the advantage of AD&D is that it is so gloriously vanilla and simple that there are no stumbling blocks in establishing characters because you really have to be dense, or wilfully disruptive, to misconstrue the background and purpose of play. So whilst XPs and levels are revelled in, for me, as a GM, it's a delight to be part of all the character play and the establishment of story.

That said, to a certain extent, I'm glad I'm not playing a character in the Story Game group because I find levels something of a bore but around 20th level they make little difference

    \n What's this "Old School"?

    No seriously: Is it playing an old game? Or simply using an old play style, generally understood to be the play style encouraged by the older games? What games or play styles qualify?

    There's some meat in that question, I think, but I'm only asking it because I recently started a 4E game, but for the sole purpose of experiencing old school dungeon crawls. Why am I not doing it in an early version of D&D? Mostly because I find the rule set dreadful, to be honest, leading to boring and repetitive play where the majority of the players have few or no interesting choices to make.

    So, if I qualify for "Old School" with that definition of play, what's my experience? I'm loving the game for what it is, and after the first and only session we've played so far, the players appear to as well. But are we really playing it "Old School"? Not particularly, no.

    I prepped a pile of encounters, some skill challenges, a dungeon, a city above the dungeon, and a motive for the PCs to be there. The players prepped a whole pile of PCs, maybe 15+ total, since we wanted to make sure that regardless of who showed on a given session they'd have all of the four PC roles covered.

    To help out with tying the PCs into the world, I posted some setting material - Dictionary of Mu style - on a wiki.

    Then, one of the players suggested that everyone write another entry, or add a major detail to an existing entry, for each character they had. Most of the players went nuts with this and greatly expanded the setting, forming a more coherent world - enough that I could translate it into an area map. Suddenly, we had a bunch of factions, cities, deities and ancient artifacts, and PCs tied into them.

    Play began, and the players - rather than triggering the neat little skill challenge I had prepped which would eventually lead them straight to the dungeon - decide to push on to the walled city and brave the night guards. They proceed to do a bunch of role-playing, helping rolls, and finally a big Diplomacy roll beating a DC of 25+. The guard - of course - lets them in, against his given orders.

    Off to the inn, and some more role-playing. I pull out my sheet of randomly generated NPCs and decide to make a few of them important, for some reason - why? A few moments later the wizard has been made a prophet of Sehanine and is granted the quest to find the lord of the city a mate - why? I don't know - I just took what the players gave me and latched on.

    And off we go to the castle, and now we're so far off the rails I have to stop the game and tell the players, "Fine, okay, you're going to the castle - but, I'd like to fade to black there, and then frame a scene where you've been taken to the dungeons to await the lord's arrival". I had an NPC in the castle that could give them something to do, and this would be a fine time to bring him in. Sure, says the players. So the NPC shows up, reprimands them for breaking into the city, and indicates the guard will be punished - the paladin makes a big deal out of this and swears he will show for the guard's trial a week hence (minor quest!). Now, the NPC tells them that no, the lord is unavailable, but - but, says the players, we must see him, we are on a holy quest, see, here's our divine artifact! More Diplomacy rolls, and the players are granted a brief audience with the lord, who I had no idea what to do with. I'm back in uncharted waters, and we're still not rolling any dice other than the occasional social skill.

    Well, the lord shows in his nightgown, obviously close to a nervous breakdown, and starts spilling all of his problems (goblins in the farmlands, kobolds kidnapping local women, disease, theft, and so on). Thankfully, the rogue latches on, here - "goblins, eh? how much per head?". The lord mentions a price and the players go off to find the goblins. Back on track! Finding the goblins is a skill challenge I've set up. The players start picking skills, rolling, playing scenes - I start dropping little clues here and there to reveal a larger narrative of what's been happening in the city prior to their arrival - and eventually, the players are attacked due to a failed Stealth skill roll.

    First encounter, after 3 of the 4 hours! The goblins have a surprise round and slaughter the wizard before she gets a chance to say hocus pocus. The paladin decides in that moment he is deeply in love with the wizard - whether due to Sehanine's influence or not - and charges into the horde of goblins to shelter her bloodied corpse. After a short, brutal, and vividly described fight scene, the remaining goblins run off. But the paladin calls out to Sehanine to restore her prophet and his soul mate to life. I can't miss an opportunity like that, so I give him a quest - one which should suitably challenge his own beliefs and establish Sehanine as a fickle divinity with a personality incomprehensible to mortals. Because, you know, I can't just give him a quest to recover some artifact or donate some gold or something, it has to be something that is transformative to his character.

    Moral of the story? I don't know, exactly. Maybe we're story games brain damaged? Maybe system doesn't matter? No, them's fightin' words. Here's what I really think: We were playing two different games. There was one really exciting miniature combat game with some vivid description thrown in, and one game of story crafting and character development. Oddly, we all agreed to go into it for the purpose of exploring old-school hack-n-slash, but in play we were all really happy to put that on the back burner anytime there was an opportunity for character interaction and development.

    Next, I think I should run Sword and Sorcerer.

    \n Awhile back I got to play Moldvay Red Box with Ben Lehman running, though I had a big disconnect because I was raised on the newer Mentzer Red Box.

    It wasn't until just a couple months back when I got to look at the Moldvay set that I saw a major difference. Whereas every edition of D&D says, "don't use the rules if you don't want to", only Moldvay really came at it specifically, "Say what you want to do, the GM makes up some rules and then you go", which is a very necessary thing to say, when you're trying to teach folks coming at it whose experience is from boardgames (if it isn't in the rules you can't do it).

    This crucial bit of info seems to have been handed down mostly via oral teaching, and the rest of us who didn't get it, simply, didn't understand how you were "supposed to" play the game.

    So basically, my experiences have been for old school - "Hey, here's a character" "dead" "Hey, here's another character" "dead", "Hey, one person survived the adventure- mad XP level up", etc.

    Now armed with this bit of crucial info, I expect the next time I run/play something old school I might be able to give a solid account. I'm a little bitter it took 18 years to find it.

    I'm currently playing Old School D&D (Mentzer), although I don't know if I really qualify as a Story Gamer or not.

    \n

    Anyway, I don't play old trad RPGs for nostalgia; I play because I have a lot of fun. And I do enjoy the logistics (even down to "how many torches to take", "how many nights to spend at a tavern healing vs how much it costs") and the attrition mechanic that wandering monsters are, even though I'm well aware of how boring that can be to other people. With most xp coming from gp, players drop the "let's kill everything that moves" pretty fast, and wandering monsters become a sort of ticking clock (since they barely give xp but waste valuable party resources) that I find quite satisfying.

    \n

    There's no pre-made character histories, since most level 1 PCs die quite fast: after the culling, personalities and even backgrounds are slowly developed during play for the lucky ones that survive. But that's not the point, it's just icing on the cake. Making a meaningful story is not the point -- it's winning. Which usually means "suriviving just one more day" :P

    \n

    There's no IC debating either. I don't want to get tangled in Forge-speak that I only know superficially, but for us it's totally about the game, about risk assessment, planning, making the right choices and being lucky. And, btw, I really do enjoy the fact that talented story-gamers have been working on this kind of stuff: I want more

    \n ccreitz - I don't want to get into a Big Model/GNS discussion because I don't get on with them and to a certain extent for the reason you touch on about the experience of play.

    But I'd agree with you that killing monsters getting XPs is a simple mechanism that drives everyone who buys into it in the same direction. That said, I scrupulously award everyone the same XPs, even if they miss a session, to make sure no one falls behind. For me, as a player, the problem with levels and XPs is that most everyone starts out as a klutz with a very limited range of options. I don't mind the klutz so much but I don't want limits on what people might try to do. AD&D to a certain extent gets round this problem by not providing rules for most things so I have informal systems for attempts to do anything else.

    The other thing is that even if you're not terribly bothered about killing monsters and getting XPs, you can do plenty of other things that don't get in the way of levelling up. And because I don't care who actually does kill the monster, even those who spend much of their time doing something else still get the benefit of progression. In effect, everyone is being rewarded for what they are doing (although personally, I don't think much of the reward). So for people who want story and are prepared to bring it to the table, they get story. And we do have story and character play and development.

    In the game where I'm a player, the place they've arrived at is pretty much the same but it's a very different route. The story has been laid down over years rather than minutes.

    \n We've been playing Old-Timey D&D; pretty regularly for the past year - any Story-Gamers in the NYC area are welcome any time.
    \n
    \nOne of the great advantages of early D&D; is that the expectations are extremely narrow and very clearly communicated. The system works quite well for many incidental details (wandering monsters, light sources, etc.). The game is challenging and fun, but the very informality of play means that it's okay for the DUNGEON MASTER to invent a new rule on the fly and everyone's okay with it. The system is just present enough to be useful, but otherwise barely exists: you're almost, but not quite, free-forming.
    \n
    \nThe severe downside of early D&D; is that it is highly non-collaborative. There is the Big Daddy DUNGEON MASTER who designs the setting, the situation, the other NPC's, the challenges, etc. As a player, you design your fungible doomed hero and hope for the best. This works, but the life-cycle of early D&D; play is presumed to be rather long - we've been playing for almost a year, and nobody has hit level 2 - and this means that there's a huge, and long-lasting, imbalance of creative forces.
    \n
    \nThe greatest achievement of the indie RPG crowd is figuring out a way to distribute creative authority around the table. It's a very good thing overall.
