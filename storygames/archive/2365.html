I love flowcharts right now. In a recent Norwegian game design competition, I designed the game "Zombieporno" ("Zombie Porn"). Each turn, a player chooses what type of scene he/she wants, and plays it out using the flowchart for that type of scene. It's the first time I actually use flowcharts in this way, and I was surprised at how easily people grasped the concept - I'd expected it to be a "programmer thing", but apparently not.

There are some great advantages to structuring and showing rules this way:

- Much harder to misinterpret rules when they're graphically displayed in a tight structure.
- No forgetting of all the "oh, and if you have 4 or more X, you need to remember Y"; just follow the lines from node to node and you can't go wrong.
- You actually see the flow of the game, so you can understand it more intuitively.

It works wonderfully. I haven't seen this approach used a lot. Trollbabe has a flowchart, IIRC; others? In my opinion, games like Shock: or MLWM could easily use flowcharts as a play aid, or to show the flow of the game.

See the Zombieporno flowcharts here. (They're in Norwegian, but you get the gist).

    Mechanical effect. In this case, "Add 2 points to success for every star present".


    Play procedure. In this case, "All stars choose their attitude: Cooperation or primadonna".


    Narration. In this case, "Describe a scene from the movie where you participate".


    Point of divergence - such as players choosing between options, dice showing different results, or scores hitting threshold levels. In this case, "Have all the stars narrated a scene? (yes/no)".


    End node. In this case, "The episode is finished."

    Chris, your Polaris flowchart baffles me. Here's a few things I would have changed:

    - Put the first "And so it was" somewhere more prominent - like at the top of the page. Put the last one at the bottom.
    - Wherever possible, place the other boxes so that each new choice moves you forward (downward), in one direction. That way, you illustrate the flow of the game through time.
    - Label your choices. I don't understand (from the flowchart) how to choose between the five arrows going out from "Free play".
    - You're not using any standardized node formats, as far as I can tell. You might want to put a note on the chart explaining the difference between a shaded box, an ellipse, a rectangle and a shaded oval. (Or you might, as I did, want to use/abuse computer standards).

    I do utilize flowcharts (and graphics, graphs, diagrams, animated versions of the above, and other visualizations) when I teach, since I don't like dumping a lot of words on a presentation. Nevertheless, I constantly remind students that the flowchart is my understanding of the concepts and encourage them to create their own rather than take mine as authoritative.

    In my mind, creating the flowchart is often more useful for learning than viewing a flowchart, as the abundance of Polaris flowcharts would seem to attest. That includes mine, incidentally, because creating one seems to be practically a rite of passage for an indie gamer.

    OK, flowcharts are nice and all, but the computational flowchart format is NOT ideal for human procedural documentation. In fact, they invented a whole flowchart format just for that: Swim Lane.


    The best thing about them, for teaching new players, is one sees quite readily how LITTLE one actually does, in any given resolution or situation. It also mitigates multi-way conflict explanations, so long as the process consistently scales beyond three-way (e.g. You, GM, Other, Another, Any Another); if it doesn't--if moving to six or more means even more process--then it can be tough to format because of all the lanes.

    Second best thing about them is you really don't have to fit into some kind of box or diamond or parallelogram or what-not. In the image above, the boxes can scale vertically as far as necessary, for pages and pages if necessary. Or they can just paraphrase then call a page reference to the full explanation. I've seen PAINFUL flow charts that look like the London Underground map, all because the designer insisted upon making every box the same, small size. You know you've fucked up when you have ten different "action" boxes ("computation," above) serially connected with no decision gateway (diamonds, above).

    Anyhow... just a tech writer and SOP author chiming in with a bit of a "check it out." Some games--those with VERY atomic stages and many decision gates--work better with a computational flowchart... which makes sense, as that format was basically built to handle very low-level, detailed stages in engineering (think machine shop to factory lines).
