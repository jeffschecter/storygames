\n Resuming and generalizing what started here we could approximate that GMs like to:

- Create / help creating a world
- Craft plots
- Play many characters.
- Set things on fire -err, i mean, movement. And then increase the energy of the game.
- Sit back and see the players set things on fire. Actually enjoy it.
- Have default spotlight and pass it along.
- Keep secrets and make big reveals out of them.
- Narrate stories with the help / creative input of the players
- Improvise
- Help players (sometimes just by allowing them) to bring the unexpected to the table.

Aside from the social things like

- Host & organize games.
- Help everyone have a good time.

that also players do in their own way.


On the same fashion we could generalize some of the things that GMs prefer:

- Less meaningless rolls and rules, more action.
- Have players invest in the story instead of destroying or just looting XP from it. (in story-driven games only)
- Sharing GM rights when the system makes it possible and doesn't affect the speed of the game.
- No NEED to prep to play, but being able to prep something that could make the game more interesting.
- And a large etc.

Now that I read just these things (I'm sure there's still more) I realize that most of the games I've read doesn't tell GMs that this is what you can expect from / do in a game. Most of this you can only
learn from experience and by reading enough gameplay examples/ listen from other groups anecdotes. Is this the reason why, while some parts of GMing are obviously fun, there are others that are considered a chore? Is this the reason why sometimes players feel like they have to make the sacrifice of being a GM for the sake of everybody's fun?

Has anybody read any book state these things as something that is actually enjoyable and not something like "As a GM It's your duty to..."? Was it helpful? Did it took some pressure out of you, made you want to GM that game more than others?

    Posted By: WarriorMonk
    - Less meaningless rolls and rules, more action.
    \nOne issue we might have is that a rule that is "meaningless" to one GM might be a critical part of gameplay in the eyes of another. This is probably more true in games where the GM's responsibilities are more nebulous.

    This tangentially leads to the biggest fear I've seen from players I've gamed with who don't want to GM: they're afraid of the increased cognitive load. I've had players tell me, when I suggest they try on the GM hat, that they're "not smart enough" to do it. Others have performance anxiety; a player can sometimes be "That Guy" and stick to the shadows, while the GM is necessarily involved in every scene.

    Even if GMing a game is easy, there's a psychological barrier that keeps people from wanting to try, and it's a distinct possibility that most of that is a stigma attached to the voluminous, all-encompassing responsibilities the GM has in some games, regardless of the game at hand.

    Posted By: WarriorMonkNow that I read just these things (I'm sure there's still more) I realize that most of the games I've read doesn't tell GMs that this is what you can expect from / do in a game. Most of this you can only learn from experience and by reading enough gameplay examples/ listen from other groups anecdotes. Is this the reason why, while some parts of GMing are obviously fun, there are others that are considered a chore? Is this the reason why sometimes players feel like they have to make the sacrifice of being a GM for the sake of everybody's fun?
    \nThat's not the primary reason why, but it's a part of it.

    The primary reason why is that most people do not want to work hard to have fun. It's why many prefer to play than GM. It's why more people play video games than table top RPGs (not GMing, just playing!). It's why more people watch TV than play hardcore video games. Casual video games have grown in large part because they're easy to learn and play. As we know, the rewards from active participation are more compelling than passive, so as the bar to entry (difficulty and effort to play) lowered more people are drawn in because even though it's more work than something like TV.

    I'm not saying it's laziness, but more work means more risk. Most often the excuse I hear is "I wouldn't be good at it." There's not really a guaranteed safe space to learn how to GM. If something goes wrong and the game isn't fun, then you may be facing down a table full of unhappy players. So, you either need not to care about that, or have confidence in your ability, or trust that the people who you're playing with are good enough friends that they'll help the learning process. I've met some very comfortable and confident players who simply do not want that responsibility. Some gamers I've talked to believe the GM is fully responsible for 50% (!!!) of the "fun" in a game. On top of that, the GM is in the spotlight for hours with little chance at a respite.

    I'm definitely more drawn to games that outline the roles a bit better. Though more than that, I'm drawn to games that take away power from the GM as the ultimate authority, with the power to change the game on a whim (which is actually explicitly power granted to the GM in some games). Of course, extremes of this are games like Fiasco and Penny for My Thoughts. And you might even say that everyone is a GM in those games. It's interesting that it seems to be easier to get games like that going.

    \n Paulo, I wish I had a better answer for you, but I'm not really sure. There are definitely games out there that are GM-less, or GM-lite, or share responsibilities in a way that make GMing not very much more difficult at all than being a normal player. But some people don't want to be GM's, and nothing in the world is going to change that for them. To be honest, that's okay.

    "Different strokes" and "tastes differ" are accurate, if pithy, ways to explain that. The best idea I can come up with -- and I offer this with no promises that it would actually work -- is simple exposure. Get players involved in as many different games, with as many different modes of thinking, and styles of GM, as possible; and if you run the game with the explicit intent of warming other people up to the idea, play with an open hand. Explain, if not while you're playing, then at least after you play, the thought processes and the decisions that you're making. If someone sees something they like, a game they'd like to run that suits their style, they'll be happy to do it.

    Posted By: WarriorMonkSo what could make GMing more tasteful for other people?
    \nThere are people who've tried GMing, and may have even been OK at it -- but just didn't like it. I have no actual data, but I think this group is a relatively small one.

    There are people who think they don't like GMing. Maybe they would and maybe they would not -- they've never tried, but their perception of GMing is such that they have zero interest in running a game. They know their tastes, and GMing doesn't smell like anything they would want to taste. I think this is the bigger group.

    I think the smells that GMing have are somewhat false.

    - Many game texts over-emphasize the responsibility and the amount of preparation.

    - Many game texts over-emphasize socially dominant aspects of GMing, and under-emphasize the facilitator aspects.

    - Many game texts give no direction how to give the GM feedback and validation. This results in many people seeing GMs as doing a good but relatively thankless job.

    - There is a boatload of mystique associated with GMing, and the interwebs are full of bad advice. Most of GMing guides are not worth the paper they're printed on. AW has the right of it: good trad GMing has a few relatively simple procedures at the heart of it, but instead of spelling out those procedures most everyone who isn't Vincent that writes about GMing seems to be intent on barfing forth the weirdest things.\n
    So, decreasing the cognitive load could do the trick? I don't think I could design a game for "That Guy" to GM. But what about other players? Can it be made so it doesn't feel like a sacrifice? What can be done about GMing so it doesn't feel like work, like a responsability? I think the mechanics for this are already working out there, but no design philosophy has focused on that yet. I'm wondering what the possibilities coud be if a game were to be designed thinking about how it could be more fun for any player to GM.
    \nI strongly believe clear procedural guidelines, less emphasis on the prep (and pre-made good-and-easy-to-run introductory content), and less waxing rhapsodic would help more people try GMing. Some of them would like it, others would not.

    Ie. I think the biggest bottleneck is the smell, not the actual taste.

It's like Apocalypse World says, the characters are fucking hot and they should be utilized in that way. "Make the characters' lives not boring" is another commandment from AW. It would have been well-taken by the GMs under which I played way back when.

    As a GM, I definitely see the wisdom in learning from the players what they want, because if what they want and what I provide does not mesh, it's best to pull the plug before things ever get started.

      \n Sam, good pint there. Though I feel it's not only good to know that the player wants to be a hero (or a villain insome cases) but it would be best to know what kind of hero and what kind of scene would be the best for every player. First I was kind afraid it could spoil the surprise at the end of the story, but whenever you play an RPG you can tell from the genre references what kind of ending it could have, and the real surprise comes in the way you reach that ending.

      I just adore the way good GMing is engraved in the very rules of games such as AW. Not every GM has all the good instincts to become an awesome entertainer, and some even go into GMing still thinking as players that reached a new epic god level. That never ends in a good way.

      Your english is fine, Alejandro, mine is a disaster. To relate your design to the discussion here, does GMing in your game feels like a chore? it bears any responsability on the player GMing over the fun on the table? If not, your design won't have any trouble at all. If it does, perhaps some players will love to be the GM and others not depending on their personality and talent for it.

      And that reminds me of one of the interesting things I've seen about rotating GMs: It can generate a sort of competition. In an early test of my design one player framed an scene where other PC got ridiculized. We all laugh, and when it was that player's turn to GM, of course he went for revenge, framing another ridiculous scene. Soon everybody' was at each other's throat and making short lived alliances and then betraying themselves. It was fun, though it wasn't what I intended. It doesn't happened in every game, in some we managed to actually make a collaborative story telling exercise. Some personal mood factors made the difference in each session, though if the game provides an interesting enough setting it has more chances to become an story instead of a vendetta.

      Jane, thanks for the good wishes. I know that a game that appeals to all is possible, though it can't certainly be a really good game. That's why I'm just trying to make a fun game for me and my group of players. The current version has many different uses, though. It's a way of learning how to frame scenes and improvise and it can also show the players that everybody can get their fun out of the game without stepping on each other's fun. By reading some GMfull games I also found that even with proper tools for framing scenes, not everybody in the table becomes a good GM instantly.

      Good GMing takes time to learn, can be learned out of neccessity but only flourishes when there's a true interest for it. It also benefits from the inherent talents and other interests of the individual (writing, acting, etc) as well as their interaction with the group. When it's present it's an asset that shouldn't be ingnored by the game design, however it's ignorance shouldn't stop a group of players from having fun with tabletops RPGs.

      Let's just say that in this design of mine, framing an scene is as easy as drawing three cards, choosing one word from each and organizing them into a phrase. With the proper list of words, combinations can be creative enough and the mechanic doesn't seem to be much of a burden. With some practice players learn to do it quick enough and even if they don't they can always use the help of the rest of the players to frame the scene. It's quite mechanical and it doesn't match the feeling of having an experienced GM frame an scene for you; nonetheless, it has the basic intention of it. Not that hardcore traditional players will love it, of course, but again, there's no way to make a game to please everybody :P
