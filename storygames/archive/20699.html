\n First of all! I love this forum and the mods have pointed out that we nerds sometimes start dropping a lot of Forge and post-Forge lingo without explaining. And they're absolutely right and I'm often guilty of this. We need to be clear so that anyone can participate.

I learned about the "teeth" of IIEE from Vincent Baker's blog, and about what IIEE even is from somewhere else I can't even remember, but I'm going to explain them in my own way here with my own diagrams, OK? So that anyone who is new, don't worry, this thread is going to be self-contained.

IIEE: how you're doing what you're doing in the game!

Sometimes players say things like "I want to use my climbing skill!" or they say "I want some of those cherry fruits" or they say "I want to go up that tree" or whatever. They usually jump to any one of these, you never know which and it's often weird.





This whole issue is what IIEE is! And IIEE at one point stood for "intent initiation execution and effect" but, the word now just means this whole thing, not just limited to those four categories. It's just what are you doing, really and what happens?

Now, what are then "teeth" in this context? Teeth are rules that help you suss out some of these things. Lack of teeth can just be you relying completely on your character sheet and never engaging any specifics of the situation: "Uh, I roll climbing" and "OK, I failed, I take 1d6 damage I guess, oh well". Or I guess it can also be the other way around? You forget that there even are rules and you just make up stuff in your own way.

And this is an important part: it's not superimportant to have "teeth"!
Either way of playing is just fine! This post is not about to try to convince you to put some teeth in your ruleset or that one ruleset does teeth better than any other.

Just in case your curious of why would anyone want these "teeth"? Well, some of us are currently [if "some of us currently" I mean me dredging up decade-old blogposts on Lumpley's blog] very into this thing, this part of the game play experence. We believe, rightly or wrongly, that it makes the shared imagined space come alive and things to have interesting and relatable consequences in this space.

Now, what are some examples of such "teeth"? The most well known is Apocalypse World. It needs to know how they're doing stuff because that helps you answer things when they go wrong and helps you be on the same page on the situation for future things they want to try. They want to know if it's rope you're climbing with because if you fail maybe the rope gets broken! They want to know if you're climbing slowly and carefully because maybe monsters come and eat you!

Possible (and, honestly, in AW probable) failure is one of the reasons why it's super important to know how and what exactly you are doing.

But now to the point of this post that's new and not just me parroting/rephrasing/trying-to-understand Vincent:

D&D 5e has its IIEE-teeth in a very different way. And I'm not here to argue that D&D is the best game or that this is the best way to do teeth. I mean I might in other threads or I might not, I might stab 5e completely in the back without a moment's notice. Who knows.
But here we are:

In 5e, you need to know exactly how they're doing something (or exactly what they're saying when they're talking to an NPC) because you have to know in order to even decide which rule to use.

In AW, you have to decide which move to use, if any, whether you go directly to a DM move or there's a chance for a player move. But 5e goes beyond that:

5e has attack rolls, saving throws, ability checks, encounter checks / time records, insp/traits, "just talk it out", and spells. These are all competing in the same space.

In the tree climbing example:

Sometimes you just want to go "OK, I can't see how that could fail in any interesting way, let's just say it takes 10 minutes to climb up there and get those , and we do checks to see if monsters come every 30 minutes so this 10 minutes count toward that".

Sometimes you want to go "Oh, wow, that looks dangerous, we have to make an ability check".

Sometimes you have to go "Oh, you really stepped in trouble now, make a saving throw!"

Sometimes you go "Wait, you said character is a town merchant, uncomfortable with nature. If you want to voluntarily fail on the tree, I'll give you insp."

Sometimes you want to skip over the whole thing "OK, you go out in the woods and gather up some various types of fruits and berries, we can hand wave the whole thing let's say the trip took two hours. That's four monster checks, OK with you guys?"

To even find out which of these it's time to use, you need to know more about the situation and the context and the time-scale you are in and things like that. Not so much about intent but a lot about execution and about the surrounding context.

So this is 5e's way, when a player just says "Oh I want to just do an arcana roll for this, can I?" or "Oh I want to just do an insight roll for this, can I?" or "Oh, I want to just do an athletics roll to get up there, can I?" you have very good reason to go "Hold on a bit" because maybe there are other ways to resolve the situation.

And the key phrase I use in these situations are: "What are you doing, exactly?" or "What are you saying, exactly?"

Don't roll unless you know for sure what the roll means (this goes out to you, YouTube DMs with your endless calls for tests).
And, this sounds time consuming now but you get in the groove quickly. If you do the same things over and over in the game it'll be faster -- everytime you pick fruit isn't going to be as cumbersome as the first time you picked fruit, etc.

It's about what's interesting in the situation before the attempted action and what's interesting in the situation after the attempted action.

And this should go without saying but GMs, let your player's characters just be awesome and succeed on basic hero things. This tree climbing examle is ridiculous, even many level zero teenagers can pick fruits. It's just an analogy, not something that would really happen. OK? Over-asking on rolls is the scourge!

    \n Good point about Tom & Jerry! How about Wile E. Coyote and the Roadrunner, then?

    As for focusing on intent, it's three things:

    1. First of all, discussing intent overtly (like transparency of method) helps people communicate. I've seen too many people describe stuff their character was doing and then have to "rewind" 20 minutes later: "Whoa, whoa! No, that's not what I was trying to do at all! I thought that wearing red would frighten the monster, I wasn't hitting on him!"

    Focusing on intent can cut through that kind of problem almost instantly. I've seen groups so vulnerable to this particular problem that half their play time goes towards "rewinding" misunderstood actions of this sort. Putting their intent out in the open makes it almost impossible to have this kind of misunderstanding.

    2. You experienced all kinds of un-fun things in play because GMs weren't playing in a principled manner, and, instead, pushed the game in specific directions, railroaded, or whatever other thing.

    Similarly, lots and lots of GMs (myself included) consciously or un-consciously did this with ability checks and similar rolls: since the roll handled the character's action and its success or failure, they used that as an excuse to resolve the intent themselves.

    The classic example is the burglar trying to break into the safe. They roll a success! Great, says the GM: you cracked that safe! But, look! The stuff you were looking for isn't there. (The task is successful, as the roll said, but the player's intent has been foiled.)

    The opposite happens, too. "You missed the bad guy... but the bullet went wide and hit a gas canister behind him. It exploded and now he's dead!"

    What these things did was remove the authority of the "gloracle" (as I believe you call it): even a success can actually be a failure, and even a failure can be a success. Effectively, when you play this way, the roll of the dice is purely cosmetic: did your character look competent when they did this thing, or not?

    As you know, most play methods which completely obviate the "gloracle" can lead to some pretty sketchy and some pretty awful play.

    Focusing on intent and making it overt and transparent means we can all use the "gloracle" once more (instead of just pretending to) and develop a solid and reliable method of play.

    3. When the consequences of any given character action are interpreted and mediated by the GM, basically all consequential actions end up being parsed by the GM. This means that, in the long-term, the entire scope and direction of the game depends on the GM's judgement. It can be very easy for GMs to fall into ruts, always send their games heading in the same direction, or frustrate player goals, and the players have no way to fix this.

    Playing a game which puts intent front-and-center effectively allows the players much more input into where this game is going and how it will develop. Playing a game system which empowers players to do this - set their goals via clear intent - and then backs that up with mechanical horsepower (i.e. a real chance to achieve those goals) is a very, very different experience. Because there are more active parties in play, things tend to move faster - the whole game becomes more dynamic and moves in less predictable fashion.

    This effect is really powerful!

    A stupid example, for illustration:

    A group of characters are stuck in a room and they don't know how to get out.

    In a game where your intent doesn't matter, they will keep trying some things, over and over, and quite possibly never make it out (as the GM gets more frustrated). More likely, they'll get out eventually, of course, but it will still take some time.

    In a game where you can state your intent overtly, they can simply say, "Ok, we want to get out of this room. What we're doing is setting up explosives against the East wall. OK? Let's roll for it." Once that roll is done, everyone knows for sure whether they can get out (a successful roll) or not (a failure). Either way, the game immediately moves on to something else, because this issue has been settled. They're out of the room, of they're in the room and we've determined that they cannot get out. Next scene!

    That's powerful!

    More importantly in the long term, it's not just a matter of pacing but also a matter of direction of play and subject matter. In a game where intent isn't stated, a GM can introduce a sorcerous witch as a "Big Bad", and then play her towards an awesome combat scene. The Paladin in the party dresses up nice and tries to compliment her and she gets pissed off, because she thinks he's mocking her (or whatever rationale, doesn't matter - the GM can think of a million things that make sense) and attacks! We get the cool fight!

    However, we get that cool fight *every time*, because that's what the GM likes and what the GM is expecting.

    In a game where intent is stated overtly, that player would have explained that he wants to woo the witch and take her out for dinner. None of us saw that coming! We usually just have cool fights. But now we have to deal with this intent: set a difficulty, make the rolls, whatever procedures we use. And suddenly, we have the Paladin and the Witch going on a date, or maybe even falling in love!

    And now everyone around the table is sitting there with their mouths open because *nothing like this has ever happened in their game before*. Turns out the Paladin's player has been trying stuff like this for years, but since we never asked what his intent was or gave it mechanical weight, it never affected the game. Now suddenly all these ideas are here in play, in front of us, and the scope of our gaming broadens dramatically.

    It's super late and I'm barely coherent, but hopefully that gives you *some* insight into what it was like for some gamers!

    As with anything else in gaming, other groups already considered intent without the game's rules enforcing it, and so such innovations seemed pretty undramatic to them - they were doing that stuff already, so they didn't see the big deal.

    \n On the subject of intent and stakes, I'll copy this over from our other conversation:

    The only downside to what you're describing is that you could potentially have stakes that aren't related to the player's intent.

    For instance, I say that I'm going to shoot my gun in the air. The GM says, "Ok, on a success, you scare the crowd and everyone goes silent. On a miss, they all go silent but also you're immediately spotted by the police."

    But what if the whole point of my action was to create a distraction, so my friend could break open the trunk at the back of the room without anyone hearing the noise?

    The GM has just totally denied me any chance of doing that: either way, my intent fails.

    Sometimes that kind of thing is appropriate in a game; in most games, however, it's way more fun and way more player-empowering to allow intent to succeed.

    I think the best compromise is something like the Otherkind dice/7-9 result option: the player gets their intent but ALSO something goes wrong. You get the important forward-moving action of the successful intent (yeay! you manage to draw enough attention that your friend can get the trunk open!) and also the GM's input in terms of the problems with your approach (e.g. you're immediately spotted by the police). The game now moves ahead while incorporating both players' input and gives everyone something to build on.

    For some groups, stating intent overtly/outright is really key to this.

    There's also the "player empowerment" thing going on. Why is this important? The players have cool ideas, and will contribute fun stuff to your game. If the GM or the game system is providing both the positive and the negative stakes of any given resolution, it's effectively like it's just the GM playing the game, with a randomizer to decide which of the GM's visions is dominant.

    This means less player input into the game, and fewer unexpected developments. Overall, the game is less "alive" and free, and it becomes more work to GM. (However, in a group that has trouble policing tone, more GM control could be a benefit - if your players *want* a more consistent game tone AND they cannot police themselves to do so, handing the control to one player who CAN could potentially benefit the group. That's relatively rare, in my opinion, although there is often a *short-term* adjustment period when people are "trying out the ropes" and they go a little bit too far with their newfound freedom.)

      \n Don't know if this is relevant anymore, but I just thought of a much simpler way to expres my point about Stakes and Intent, earlier. Assume some kind of basic game with a succeed/fail resolution process.

      1. When Intent is not (necessarily) taken into account:

      * On a success, the GM's idea happens. (Of what a success looks like/does.)
      * On a failure, the GM's idea happens.

      2. When Intent is taken account, or the player is allowed to simply state it:

      * On a success, the player's idea happens. (Of what this particular success looks like/does.)
      * On a failure, the GM's idea happens.

      The difference can be subtle, and disappear almost completely when the player and the GM are completely in agreement on what a particular roll is about. (I think this why you don't need overt Intent most of the time in a D&D adventure module - the purpose of rolls is generally quite clear.*)

      However! Over the long term, over many many repetitions... this difference is HUGE and makes itself felt with wide-spread ramifications throughout the game/campaign.
