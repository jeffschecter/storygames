\n I write for a webcomic called Neko Machi, and I'm looking to start making stuff to sell at small local cons. Thinking about possible stuff to make and sell, it occurs to me that a whole bunch of these things could also be used for RPG type stuff.

Buttons take a bit of an initial investment (a proper button maker costs at least $100, often $300 or more), but make it easy to make money back if you can actually sell them as an impulse buy type thing. I could totally see using buttons as some kind of game components, whether very small ones serving as pawns or miniatures, or larger ones containing game material themselves.

Indie comic events always have people selling mini-comics. You can make a quick 16 or 20 pages worth of comics, print up a little booklet that you can photocopy and staple, and sell it for a few dollars each. There have been a few RPGs to toy with this general kind of format, like XXXXtreme Street Luge, MicroLite 20, and more recently Weird West, and Annie Rush did a few such games (like House of Horiku) a few years back.

It's harder to imagine T-shirts tying into an RPG, but then it's *really* rare to see RPGs actually try that kind of thing. My rule for making T-shirts is that it should be possible for someone who's never heard of whatever you're making to want to buy the T-shirt. (And this is one of the few webcomic shirts I really want to get.)

Anyway. I'm not really going anywhere in particular with this (I have a headache right now), but I thought I'd throw this out there while it's on my mind.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarPosted By: Neko EwenOn the one hand I like the idea of even further breaking down the notion that an RPG has to be a giant hardback tome
    \nThat's fair. What isn't fair, but seems to be pretty universally true, is that people assign value based on value. So a shitty game you paid thirty dollars for has more subjective "worth" than an amazing PDF you paid nothing for.

    The comics guys selling artsy mini-books may have this beat.

    Too true. One week I was selling 54 page Modest Medusa books at Stumptowns so fast that I ran out half way through the 1st day. The next weekend I was arguing with gamers at Sakuracon that the 54 page Panty Explosion and the 24 page G x B were actually real, full length playable games. Of course, not everyone we met at that show had this bias. A lot of people, especially interested non-gamers and frustrated former gamers were really into our small, compact games. I've always argued that those are my target customers anyway.

    I've had great luck with Panty Explosion buttons and stickers, fair luck with shirts and novelty panties and no luck at all with miniatures (which we didn't really push that hard anyway). What I've learned is that people like to spend money, but they don't always want to spend money on a game, no matter how cool it looks. Handing them a button or a sticker (especially a sticker) helps them identify with your game. Nothing else may ever come of it, or they may buy your game next time they see you out of a sense of familiarity and loyalty. That really does work by the way. Ask any indie comic person that does a lot of comic shows. You always try to make sure that anyone who stops by your booth walks away with something free or cheap. At the next show they'll remember you, and they'll be way, way more likely to buy something from you because of that.
