    Tips on Feng Shui:

    Do not use a map. Do not keep track of exactly how many mooks there are. There should be exactly enough mooks to give everyone a shot or two at beating them down, or enough to threaten whatever goal the PCs have for the scene. If after a round where everyone got some awesome moves in you realise there's one or two mooks 'left over'? They run away or, if sensible, they were just never there in the first place.
    Despite what the game tells you, just having a fight probably isn't enough. It's more fun when the PCs are fighting in the middle of doing something else. Like, say, a wedding, or while on a double date, or grocery shopping, or buying a very expensive and very fragile vase for their mother-in-law.
    The system can be gamed, and it really only matters if it results in some players getting way more actions per round -- i.e. way more opportunities to do cool stuff. I've had some bad experiences with this. It not only creates an uneven sense of who is contributing to the game, but it puts the player who has twice as many actions in the position of having to come up with twice as much cool stuff to do.
    The actual kung fu paths are not very exciting, with the exception of the ones that let you jump around a lot. Mechanically they tend to only contribute to fights against named NPCs. Contrasting them with the very short list of gun feats is pretty telling in that regard. I haven't played the game enough to offer any solid advice regarding this issue, however.
    Similarly, fights against named NPCs really need to be about something other than the fight, because suddenly it's like you're playing D&D again. Everything has hit points. So many hit points. Another issue I've not really solved, personally, which is why the current kung fu game I'm planning is going to be run in Mortal Coil.

    I've been running a frightening amount of Feng Shui recently, and have a couple of tips for streamlining:

    Get some big piles of colored rocks/beads/whatever. All the bookkeeping goes much more smoothly if people are moving colored rocks around. This is especially true for combat timing. A bunch of stones for three-shot actions and a few of another color to make change for the little stuff is just easier to deal with.

    Divide all those hit point numbers by five, and round in the dramatic direction.

    Named characters who go down after being hit a couple of times are a useful in-between type of opponent -- they're a threat, but still a short fight.
