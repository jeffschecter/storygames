\n Suppose you have a game that has some pre-written secrets/endings built into it. (Perhaps it's in the vein of How to Host a Murder or something like that. That predetermined content is part of the appeal.) It's also likely this is a game that will be played in public situations a few times.

If the game is publicly available for purchase: is there a risk that errant players will spoil themselves by buying the game and reading its contents ahead of time? Do any games try to avoid this sort of thing, or do you just assume griefers/cheats will be filtered out of the game up front?

    \n This kind of thing happens ALL the time.

    I'm a fan of Deadlands and have been running it off and on for years, now. In a lot of their books they have a Player's section and a Marshal's (GM's) section, often with warnings that players should keep from reading the parts of the books intended only for GMs. I have a friend who is also into the setting and would often play in my games. I remember lending him one of the books once and specifically showing him where the Player's section stopped.

    When I got the book back from him, he said he really liked it and then proceeded to talk about things in the section he wasn't supposed to read. I asked about it and he shrugged and simply replied, "Oh, I just read the whole book."

    As I see it, it's pretty much impossible to avoid players finding out a game product's spoilers. If they want to know badly enough, they'll find out. I think it's probable that those games that have the highest degree of success at keeping their secrets have separate books for GMs.

    Now, that said, I've had players who can handle out of character knowledge before - there are plenty of players out there who can learn every single detail about a setting (maybe because they also GM it or would like to someday) and can play their characters as being completely clueless and enjoy it. But then there are also those players out there who can never resist spoilers and can't keep a secret to save their lives.

    From what I can tell, most game companies expect that sort of thing to be handled by GMs on a case-by-case basis, which is probably for the best, really.

    \n Paul: Probably the latter. Let me demonstrate the scenarios:

    1. Player A knows the secrets, and yells the answers to everyone. Everything is ruined.

    2. Player A knows the secrets, and secretly uses them to WIN. Everything is (secretly) ruined.

    3. Player A knows the secrets, and manifests a tremendous degree of will-power to play in a neutral and fair market. Everything is... different? But it depends on the player whether the end result is less fun for others.

    You can argue that #1 and #2 show bad faith; even so, I think #3 results in some suboptimal gameplay.

    \n This seems like you guys are creating non-human solutions to a human problem.

    Part one: Design secrets that are still interesting to explore even if everyone at the table already knows them. We did this in Love in the Time of Sei\xf0 - several characters have crazy secrets, and once you've played you will of course know them. It doesn't impact replayability at all - you just look forward to seeing them get busted out in the open.

    Part two: Ask players not to read secrets if they want to discover them in play. Put a fair warning in the text and trust them to decide for themselves.

    \n You know, now that I think about it, White Wolf did a sort of "here are the secrets, but not really" thing with their original World of Darkness. They were always very careful to present all of their books from particular characters' perspectives and say that they were only revealing those characters' SPECULATIONS on the truth, which could be entirely wrong. Heck, when they released their "end of the world" books they included several alternate "final truths" for GMs to use.

    Granted, that helped give GMs a lot of elbow room as they were given specific permission to ignore whatever bits they wanted to (though sadly this was of little help to OCD-riddled continuity freaks like myself). I suspect, though, that the real reason for it had to do with the fact that in those days they were on the product treadmill, having to release a ridiculous amount of books per year to make sure they stayed afloat. They had a lot of different writers, not all of whom kept up on what was in all of the books. I'm guessing they used the "this is one character's perspective and not necessarily the REAL truth" angle to write off the various continuity errors that arose. That's how Horus turned out to be both a faerie and a mummy at the same time and Rasputin ended up being a member of at least five different vampire clans. And a mage. And a werewolf.
