Let's say Alice is a known Forgite and explains what she has learned to Bob. She starts with GNS.

Alice: You see, different people enjoy different things in RPGs.
Bob nods. He probably met lots of gamers with different tastes.
Alice: There are three agendas in gaming. {Short description of GNS.}

Bob: Wait. What kind of sentence was this 'There are three agendas.' ?
A: It's part of the theory.
B: No, no. I mean, that's is a definition, isn't it?
A: Hmmm.

A: Well, probably.
B: Then you should say so. Otherwise people might think, you're stating a fact or make a conclusion from the things you've said before.

    The most common ways I have seen the theory badly explained probably fall into these three camps:

    1. Someone balks and suggests that their play preference is not captured or not captured adequately, the point is discussed and it becomes clear that they are unpersuaded, Alice assumes they do not understand and flogs the dead horse for a ton of posts rather than moving on. That alienates people, sometimes people understand but do not agree. It's fine to think they're wrong, but assumptions that they simply don't follow the argument do more to alienate than persuade. Sometimes it's better to let it rest and agree to disagree.

    2. Alice informs the person that clearly they do not understand their own play preferences. I've seen this a lot, and unsurprisingly it rarely improves communication.

    3. Alice refers the person to a wodge of background reading that would take days to wade through. If you're struggling to succinctly summarise, bail and let someone else have a go. Asking people to do homework rarely persuades. A succinct summary suffices, if the person wishes to know more that's the time to show them all the other resources out there. As an initial explanation though it's lackwitted to point someone to three essays and a hundred Forge threads, that is step two or three. Step one is a succinct summary.

    So basically, if you avoid treating people like idiots or like they're deluded when they disagree you will run into fewer problems. Most of the anti-GNS reactions comes down to one of those first two things, patronising communications. The third is more likely to make people lose interest than anything else.

    Oh, and 4, if someone comes in gunning hard for GNS it's best to politely say it's worked for you and disengage than to get into a fight, as the fight will polarise positions and you'll often find yourself inadvertently getting backed into 1 or 2.

    And that's about it. No magic, just don't talk down to people or oversell. I have seen a few other appalling errors, but those were individuals rather than a general problem.

    Another way I feel that people can sometimes poorly communicate their ideas about RPG theory is dogmatism. Most folk, when they design RPG rules, are thrilled if gamers lift the parts of the rules system they really enjoy and junk the parts they aren't very thrilled about. I have, however, occasionally noticed GNS advocates insisting that people have to accept the entirity of GNS theory, and that if people adopt the parts which they find useful and scrap the assumptions which don't work for them they are wrong and are misusing the theory.

    Taking Immersionism as an example, supposing Gary, a GNS advocate, was talking to Ingrid, who believes strongly that immersion exists:

    Gary: "...so the GNS model says that those three agendas are the ones which have been best identified through the examples of play we've collected on the Forge."

    Ingrid: "Hey, that's really interesting! I've noticed that when I'm playing my immersion can get wrecked if there are too many Narrativist or Gamist techniques at work, since I don't like to be reminded that I'm playing a game and I don't enjoy stepping back-and-forth between OOC Narrativist considerations and my character's IC mindset..."

    Gary: "Woah, hold up, immersionism doesn't exist."

    Ingrid: "What do you mean it doesn't exist? My whole group gets into it all the time!"

    Gary: "I'm sure that seems like the case, but there's no place for immersionism in GNS theory."

    Ingrid: "No place? But it's already helped me work out what parts of games hurt my immersion!"

    Gary: "No, it hasn't, and you're misapplying it if you try to apply it to immersionism."

    Ingrid: "Oh, well in that case maybe it isn't as interesting as I thought it was..."

    See, Ingrid was about to partially adopt aspects of GNS, but Gary's dogmatic insistence that you can't dilute it or cherry-pick the segments that work for you ruins everything.

    The main issue that always comes up in these discussions is one of dogma. Look, scientific theories--and RPG theory falls into the social science realm--are not a religion. Saying "I believe in GNS" is as misguided as saying "I believe in Diffusion of Innovations Theory". What you believe in is the utility of that theory in analyzing a topic at hand. That is, I'm not spreading the Dogma of GNS because it's the One True Faith (and I'm branded a heretic in that regard, anyway); I'm showing people how using the analytical framework of the Big Model can help me understand certain dynamics that occur when people get together to roleplay. Other dynamics I've found to be better understood using other theoretical frameworks.

    So any theoretical framework can only be "true" in the Pragmatic sense (see William James) to the degree that Truth means utility for understanding and predicting phenomena.

    Can we have a discussion about gaming without theory? Sure, if we're just going to look at specific events without establishing any patterns, categories, or conclusions. But those are mere anecdotes that won't help us understand or improve play and design.
