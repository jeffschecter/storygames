So in my Awesome-o-fy Lasershark history thread, a bunch of people chimed in with their favorite real historical events as gaming fodder. So I thought I start up a thread for that. Here are a few of my favorites:

In 1942, inventory Geoffry Pyke made a proposal to the admiralty to build an unsinkable aircraft carrier out of ice (actually a sawdust-ice composite called pykrete). The proposal was taken quite seriously by Winston Churchill and others.

During the cold war, Russia built a small number of Ekranoplans, huge steampunk-esque ground effect vehicles for staging amphibious assaults.

The Count de Saint Germaine, an adventurer, nobleman, and alchemist who claimed to be centuries old. No one knew where he came from, but he mingled in the noble courts of Europe pretending claim to a litany of disputed and obscure thrones and titles. Such luminaries as Aleister Crowley and the Theospists have claimed that he is/was an ascended master/eternal mystic master of one sort or another.

    I have no reference for this except my family's oral histories of early California.

    There were problems in the early days of the gold rush with gold ships being attacked by pirates as they travelled the long route around South America to bring california gold back to Boston and New York. As all the white sailors were struck with gold fever and became propectors, the ships would hire foreign sailors to fill out their crews and work as anti-boarding marines.

    At the same time, the Tokugawa Shogunate was in heavy decline, and many lower-class Samurai (little better than thugs, really) were exiled from the country. As opposed to taking up their exile in Korea or China, many of them caught rumors of the wealth available in California and got passage on Chinese boats across the pacific. Where, given their extensive hand-to-hand combat experience, they were promptly hired on the crews of gold ships.

    That's right: 150 years ago, ronin samurai fought with mexican pirates over gold bullion off the coast of California.

    After a widely reported career as an outlaw, Jesse James was murdered in his house by Robert Ford in 1882.

    He removed his guns to climb on a ladder and hang a picture. Brothers Robert "Bob" Ford and Charley Ford took the opportunity to kill James. Bob shot him in the back of the head. They were among James last trusted comrades.

    There is some indication they killed James with the approval of the Missouri governor, Thomas T. Crittenden. Robert Ford is considered by some to be a true coward (sometimes in romanticized song and movies), having killed Jesse James, whom some viewed as a Robin Hood type figure.

    Two years later, Charley Ford committed suicide. Ten years later, Robert Ford was murdered by a shotgun blast to the throat.

    Dust Devils, anyone? Sheesh!

    February 2007
    Generally, that it's weirder than any fantasy game I've yet encountered.

    Specifically, well, lots really.

    I ran a game set in 1770s Naples, a period in which Naples is rising to become possibly a world power (which obviously it didn't). It is a period in which fake alchemists con their way around Europe, an age of the con, and an age in which nobles run up vast debts at cards and settle them in vicious duels.

    Seriously, it's just plain cool.

    Japan during the reunification in the 16th Century, I ran a game in which Christianity had gained more of a hold than it actually did, with the PCs acting for one faction as another fuelled with Portugese muskets gained more and more power.

    Elizabethan London, ruffs, swordfights at the theatre, Spanish agents in the Southwark docs, what's not to love?

    My Crusades game took the characters across 13th Century Europe, they fought in the siege of Toulouse (and helped capture part of the city, so changing history in the process) and then fought in Spain against, well, mostly against the Spanish actually due to some unfortunate misunderstandings.

    History is the biggest ass setting there is, full of incident and extraordinary people, full of opportunities to achieve goals which resonate more at the human level. Characters grow rich, gain power, fall in love and marry, things which happen all too rarely in many more traditional genres but which speak to us as human beings of the things we ourselves hope and work for.

    I'm cool with people awesomeifying history, the awesome can always be made more so, but there is almost no period you can look at in which you will not find something awesomely cool and gameable happening.
