\n I recently picked up a copy of IAWA, and I'm hoping to play a session of it soon. I've been reading the threads here and elsewhere on the conflict mechanics, but I'm still a little stuck on how it would play out for social conflicts. It sounds like all attempts at convincing another player of anything against his or her will has to be encoded in physical acts (even if it's just a speech) which can be countered by other physical acts. If I win, I haven't necessarily convinced you. I can injure or exhaust you, with color text as to what the injury or exhaustion looked like. Alternatively, we can agree on some concession. Does the concession have to be a physical thing, or can the concession include being convinced (as opposed to behaving like you've been convinced)? It sounds like forcing a change to a character's inner life may not be acceptable, even as a concession.

    \n I played four sessions, and we wrestled with this issue. In the end, concessions can be anything, even non-physical, as long as they are immediate. "I am convinced" is not so good, because the character is free to change their mind at any time. Same goes for promises. But you can persuade the other person to take immediate action. We had social conflicts in front of crowds that ended with the concession of the losing character publicly declaring their support for the new king then and there. So, IME, concessions work best when they are actions right in that moment, not things in the future or changes in belief or attitude. They can also work for follow up scenes: "go and confront my rival" can work as immediate enough, because that can start off the next scene.

    \n This is how I do it which may or may not be the same as the way Vincent does it.

    1) There are no Persuasion mechanics in IAWA. That is not the same as there are no Social Conflict mechanics. So long as there is an honest attempt to convince someone of something on the merits of the discussion at hand, no dice. Period. You can't reason or argue with someone via the dice in IAWA.

    2) I use the concept of "The Weapon." As soon as someone uses a weapon we go to dice. There are tons and tons of social weapons. A social weapon comes into play when someone steps outside the discussion at hand to try and force you to do what they want. That's what the whole "enflames mens passions" example in the text is all about. Sorcery is being used to force someone's behavior. Bribery and threats are also weapons.

    3) When negotiating at the end of the conflict "I convince you" is a weak negotiation because they can just change their minds immediately. Instead it should be "I convince you and you do THIS about it." Where THIS is something they can do immediately as their very next scene or action. If it's a vague promise about a future event they can change their minds about that to but they can't get out of IMMEDIATE consequences that they agree to.

    Does that help?

    Jesse

    \n You don't go to dice when people are "just talking" in the same way you don't go to dice when people are "just shoving each other back and forth." It's not social vs. physical that is the distinction. It's action that matters.

    To go to dice, someone has to take concrete action that meets these criteria:
    1. It could conceivably result in injury or exhaustion.
    2. Someone else can and would interfere with the action.

    That's it. Physical, social, spiritual, whatever... doesn't matter as long as it fits the criteria.

    Another good shorthand is: you go to dice when someone stops caring about the other person's freedom of action. When you do something that the other person doesn't want you to do, but you carry on anyway. I think this helps clear up the false physical vs. social dichotomy. Imagine someone who wants to get beaten up out of guilt. So he goes and confesses his crime to someone violent, and that violent person holds the guilty person down and pounds their face in. No one interferes with the face-pounding, and no dice are rolled, even though it's physical action and harm. No fictional characters objected to it, so no dice.

    Now imagine the same guilty person who desperately wants forgiveness instead of a face pounding. So he goes to his wife, and, through concrete action, he forces her to forgive him. Nothing physical happens, but it's force and action nonetheless. I'm sure you've been in plenty of situations where someone forced you without punching you in the face. The wife doesn't want to forgive the crime, but, the guilty husband forces the issue to the breaking point, and the dice come out and decide things.

    At the end of an action sequence, the loser gets injured or exhausted, or you negotiate. The wife can hold fast and take exhaustion and refuse to forgive, of course. But these are not "conflicts" remember. It's not "roll and done" like a lot of indie games. You can always just go right into another action sequence if you don't get what you want (and you usually should). The guilty husband can continue to apply force (now going against the wife's reduced dice). How far will he go? Will she ever relent and just tell him what he wants to hear? And there's your non-physical social struggle, with dice, right there.

    \n Yup, that's it.

    Also, when I said, "the dice come out and decide things" of course what I meant was "the dice come out and decide who has the stick during negotiations." Just to be nit-pickingly clear.

    You can negotiate at any old time, as the course of regular free play.
    "Your guy really should forgive my guy."
    "No way!"
    "Aw, c'mon..."
    "Forget it."

    It's regular negotiation. It can stalemate.

    But after you go to the dice, one of you gets the stick to use during negotiation.
    "Your guy really should forgive my guy."
    "No way!"
    "Fine, you're exhausted then."
    "Crap, okay. I forgive!"

    Dice sequences aren't "conflicts" to see who "wins." They're struggles over the stick. If, in the end, the other party still won't give you what you want, you hit them with the stick and ask again (or you realize it's not worth it and stop asking).

    Physical vs nonphysical isn't the thing.

    Go read "Red Nails." Read the conversation Conan has with Valeria in the opening section. Conan's like, I totally want to fuck this woman and I totally want her to do what I say, but, well, fuck it, I'm not going to make her to.

    If you roll dice, you're no longer trying to convince someone to do something, you're trying to make them do it. Shahu Seen isn't trying to convince Mekha to go for Tajie, he's trying to MAKE him go for Tajie.

    So: if you want someone to do something, tell them your reasons. Maybe they'll do it, maybe they won't. But - while you're leaving it up to them, don't roll dice.

    Rolling dice means that you don't care anymore what they want to do, or whether you've convinced them, or anything like that. It means you're holding the gun to their head and telling them what to do (whatever the gun happens to be).

    Make sense?

    -Vincent
