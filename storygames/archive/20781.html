    \n I've always found four is the perfect sweet spot, but three works too; in either case, you want enough PCs to create complex, over-lapping relationships between them and the townsfolk, as this gives the Master plenty to threaten when giving out orders. As a rule-of-thumb, I usually give orders in the cycle PC #A is given one of PC #B's contacts as a target for their mission; PC#B gets one of PC #C's contacts, etc.

    Also with four, the players can draw on a larger pool of dice with which to help each other during the endgame when they attempt to take down the Master: this makes all the difference to insuring the game reaches a satisfactory conclusion, especially if you only have time for a single session of play.

    Finally, I just like four as a number for most storygames: there are more opportunities for shared scenes between PCs without a lot of repetitions, plus it allows for alliances & enmities to arise and change dynamically.

    \n Three players would be my pick - enough to paint the spectrum, so to speak, yet as few as possible so as to keep the length of the game manageable and the theming sharp. Four's good, too.

    Five is still playable. Six is too many. Two is barely playable (requires skillz); I haven't tried one player, but I suspect that it'd be too exhausting. The fewer players you have, the more of a NPC cast you need to build, on top of the PC contacts.
