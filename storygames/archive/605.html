When will you stop playing RPGs?

Andy
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
Interesting, question.

Before Jack Spencer Jr wiped clean his blog again, he stated that he was getting out of the hobby. Fine and dandy, as a lot of his play just wasn't satisfying. No need to dig further than that.

What struck me was that Clinton wrote a very cool comment to him (now probably erased) encouraging him to really get into another hobby that he could love: crafts, sports, whatever. Something solid to keep depression at a minimum and the like. He mentioned (whether he gave it a lot of thought or not) that he feels like he's got about 5 years left in the hobby before he may wander off to new fields. It was some real encouragement, and very cool of him to do so.

However, those comments were really interesting to me in terms of "feeling when the quitting time is approaching" and "finding something new to replace it". I was wondering if anyone else had given any thought to:

* A natural series of events that may lead them to leave this hobby.

* Other hobbies or activities that would take its place.

* What that shift in gears would entail. How would you change?

* And have you given it serious thought?

* Other related thoughts you had about quitting the hobby?

    * A natural series of events that may lead them to leave this hobby.

    It could happen, though I can't really see through the veil that far. However, I did actively look at other cultural-like hobbies, and the only one I could think of was music: I'd like to learn the piano someday...

    ...but socially? I can't think of any social activity that gets me more interested to be with people than gaming at this point. Video games don't cut it (not even online ones); music doesn't cut it, because most musicians in the area sort of get on the stage do their thing, and go home; crafts MAY do it, but I'm not too interested in crafts (unlike my wife, who is hardcore into pottery these days, going to several lessons at once). I'm not into sports that don't involve punching, so team games are right out (though I was contemplating starting a local dodgeball team, but that's more for my love of dodgeball than other people).

    Board games come close, and while I dig playing with friends, most of them I get bored with after 2 hours. Theatre is pretty solid on the social realm, but I'm a little wary of hardcore theatre types, as they tend to be just as fucked up as gamers.

    I may get burned out on having too many gaming groups, at which point I'd set up a bi-weekly "meet at the coffee house" thing. I may get burned out on talking about games, at which point I'd stop (RPGNet, which has been my daily bread and butter for like 7 years, only attracts me like once a day or once every two days, and no longer like 5-100 times a day).

    So at this point, I've given it lots of consideration, and while I can't make "cold, dead hands" statements, it's definitely not on my 8-year horizon.

    * Other hobbies or activities that would take its place.

    Oh, I kinda mentioned that above. There are other hobbies that I'd like to do that could totally cut into gaming time, at which I'd have to reconsider gaming: Getting more into Japanese study, and the aforementioned exercise and investing and possibly music (tho I had that tucked as an "aside" when I turn 40).

    But nothing else that's really social. Luckily, a lot of my local gaming friends also do social things, so I've been covered on that front (especially with local music and religious/spiritual events). I've been meaning to hang with Remi and Jason more, for their Play Readings and Karate Movie Nights and stuff, but again it's doing social things with gamers who are also friends, at which point gaming is just an inch away.

    * What that shift in gears would entail. How would you change?

    Hmmmm. I'd have to have a pretty fundamental shift. Like get into a hobby that I thought was non-social but turned out to be TOTALLY social. And at the same time get into another hobby that acts as a total creative outlet. The only thing that would change is the amount of praxis and creativity would be funneled elsewhere.

    I could just get like three promotions and be a total executive "all work and no play" type, but I do have a feeling that even if I'm making a quarter mil a year, I'd still be killing Orcs on Thursday nights (like Alan in my gaming group, who is almost there).

    * And have you given it serious thought?

    Yeppers. "Pry from my cold, dead hands" doesn't ring as strongly for me, but I've given it some serious thought and I'm still going to be a gamer for a long while.

    * Other related thoughts you had about quitting the hobby?

    No, that's about it, aside from the thoughts of what would take its place, and how well or not well that would all work out...

    The "cold dead hands" comment, in the context of what I was saying, at least, is that I don't see roleplaying as something I can get permanently bored with, or something that I can "use up all the fun" so that I can't possibly do it again. Like reading, watching plays or film, as Graham pointed out. You get bored with individual books or movies or plays, but who on earth says "I've read everything I will ever need to read. I will never read again!" At least, not without some kind of ideological motivation?

    Because I've "left" gaming and come back several times already, and because my gaming right now is pretty intermittent, I can't say "I'll never roleplay again". When the right game and players present themselves at the right time, I game, no matter whether a week has gone by since the last game, or a month, or years. My decision is based on my desires at the moment, not on a decision I made years before.

    I don't see gaming as an identity issue, either, but I have to take past experience as future predictor; even if I never game again for years and years, I still have to expect that maybe I will get a pick-up game going in the nursing home when I find out some of the other 80-year olds are feeling nostalgic, too.
