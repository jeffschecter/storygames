\n I buried this theory and its consequences in another thread, but thought it might be of more general interest. Its old territory, I think, but hey, it might be worth some thought.
\n
\nThere are levels of what is considered by players in any game to be "attackable" territory of their characters. By "attackable" I mean that another player, either by fiat or by mechanics, can force you to acknowledge something about your character. Typically, people would rank things in roughly this order, from least contentious to most:
\n
\n1) Character's passive behaviour (i.e. when the Ogre bashes me, do I get thrown backwards or stand my ground?)
\n2) Character's active, ancillary behaviour (i.e. when I bash the Ogre, do I do it in a flashy way, spinning around, or do I do the two hand over head whack thing?)
\n3) Character's active, meaningful behaviour (i.e. do I bash the Ogre or the orc?)
\n4) Character's speech (i.e. does my character say "Have at you, ogre!" or simply grit his teeth?)
\n5) Character's observable state (i.e. Does my character look afraid, or angry, at the ogre?)
\n6) Character's internal emotions (i.e. Is my character REALLY afraid of the ogre, or not?)
\n7) Character's internal beliefs (i.e. Does my character hate ogres?)
\n
\nThat's not a complete list, and the ranking may not be perfect for everyone (I especially find people vary greatly regarding where they would rank level 4) but it will serve. Think about the following sets of conflict goals I might have, from my opponent, the barmaid's, perspective, as attacks on different levels:
\n
\n* I kiss the barmaid (level 1)
\n* I kiss the barmaid, and she embraces me in the process (level 2)
\n* I get the barmaid to kiss me (level 3)
\n* I get the barmaid to tell me she loves me (level 4)
\n* I get the barmaid all flustered with my flirtation (level 5)
\n* I get the barmaid to fall in love with me (level 6)
\n* I get the barmaid to feel I am the only one in the world for her (level 7)
\n
\nEach player has a line on the chart above below which they will not accept any attacks, and also a line on the chart below which they will accept attacks, as long as there is some sufficient method in the game rules to defend themselves. It can vary somewhat by game: I accept fiat attacks down to level 4 or even lower on my character in Capes, but would never do so in D&D. It can vary by the exact nature of the "attack": I might have no problem with "the Barmaid gets me to fall in love with her" (level 6), but a big problem with "the Barmaid gets me to fall in love with the Evil Mastermind" (level 6). It varies also with WHO is making the attack: some people that I play with I implicitly trust to make attacks at all levels against me, because I KNOW they will make it awesome in the story, while others I don't.
\n
\nHere is the main point I am making.
\nIn any game...
\n but especially in a story game that allows for attacks at levels lower than 2 (which is almost a definition of story games)...
\n and even more especially in such a story game if you are playing with strangers...
\n and most especially if you are in a story game with strangers who have not played in a game that allows those sorts of attacks before...
\nit is vital that you have a discussion of the chart above or something similar and where people draw their lines in that moment. This is because if a player finds attacks below a certain level unacceptable, it WILL NOT MATTER that the mechanics of the game allow you to make attacks on those levels. You will just be pissing that person off. Having this discussion up front, and being very explicit about it, you can avoid a world of unpleasantness. You give players a chance to: agree to keeping attacks above a certain level; walking away from a game that doesn't really interest them; or explicitly agreeing to have their boundaries stretched by a new experience.
\n
\nI'm sure this seems like common sense to most of you, but I bring this up because I have experienced recently several instances of game play where disagreements or misunderstandings regarding the above have led to unpleasantness of a trivial to significant nature. I won't bother filling up space with examples, but can provide them if there is interest.

    \n I struggle with this question (if social skills work on NPC's, why not on PC's) vs the "player sovereignty over character" issue. I'm a keen fan of a player retaining the ability to explore the character they're creating. At the same time, I have a "mechanics" issue when some abilities "always miss vs PC's", which is the default preference of most of those I game with. I think this is indeed one of those early game contract discussions.

    My problem is when I find too many players never playing themselves as convinced of something against their best judgement, seduced when they think it's a bad idea, "letting the wrong word slip while kissing persuasive lips". Not all players are alike on this point ~ though the ones I encounter prepared to play their characters as sometimes foolish etc. tend to be ones who are the ones who have played newer systems. I myself am fine having it played that my character is fooled by something I know is untrue, as long as it was resolved by the system (if I wanted a hard to fool character I would build one just as I build a hard to hit character) or my choice (I recently chose to be fooled by another PC because it sets up a much more interesting situation if he at least gets his scheme off the ground behind by back). I'm fine with scenario's that result in changes to character since really to me that is the core "situation" to explore in a game (who is this character) and if there's no upredictability, no change, and nothing to improve, then it's pretty boring. Am I prejudiced against dwarves? Let's roll! (that was an actual stakes being settled in an early scene playing one character, I "lost" and was convinced I was wrong in my views of them).

    Even if it's not "submitted to the system", I still prefer to at least see players asking "well, how good was that social roll, hmm...." and then playing "what their character would do" against that good a social skill roll I would be satisfied. What I see instead is "how good they are is irrelevant, my guy's decision is unchanged". That seems to go with players who develop characters that themselves do not change over time in their beliefs etc.

    I suspect systems like Spirit of the Century's Aspects which allow players to "get a currency" for "giving into a flaw" convert over players into playing characters with a line drawn further down the chart.

    We had a conversation about this in the context of In a Wicked Age, the other night. The reason that trust between players is so important in a game like In a Wicked Age is that almost all the real consequences of what happens are wholly narrative. I stab your character in the throat. Now, for the rest of the session, or iuntil it makes sense otherwise, you are going to have to narrate your character as having been stabbed in the throat. Trust is important because I am trusting you to play your throat-stabbed character in a way that takes the whole throat stabbing into account. So I can make a challenge "your character will love my character", and it will work out fine, because that will last for as long as it MAKES SENSE that it should last, because you and I trust each other.

    Posted By: Hans

    Cydmab, it is interesting to me that you view this as strictly causal. I actually view it as something that can go either direction, as witnessed by my preferred mode, above, with people I don't know well and who don't know me well. In that mode, "Action" is actually what is decided, and then the player must work backwards to thought->emotion->motivation to fill in the blanks. Looking at myself, I find this is usually the way I play ANY character, not just NPC's as a GM. I always think "what should HAPPEN right now" and then back fill the motivations to make that thing occur.
    \nFor purposes of my point, the causal direction doesn't matter, it's whether there's a causal link at all and how "tight" it is. That is, if action implies thought implies emotion implies motivation, then someone who controls the actions of my character implicitly has a fair amount of control over thoughts (thoughts are limited to the subset of thoughts that would follow from that action); if the causal direction is thought -> action, then someone with control over action also has a fair amount of control over thoughts (thoughts are restricted to the subset of thoughts that would bring about that action)

    So what matters is how "Tight" the connections are; that is how many, say, thoughts I feel are reasonable for any given action. If I think the set is small (or equivalently, I can only come up with a small set on the fly to choose from) then my control over the thoughts of the character will be highly circumscribed given someone else's control over action. If the connections are "loose" or not there at all (any thought can be associated with any action... "I love you, therefore I must torture you; I must torture you, therefore I love you") then there's no problem.

    But then... there's may also be no ORDERING. I could control actions and motivation, someone else can control thoughts and emotions, and that would be about as reasonable as vice versa or any other combination. So in such a case the claim of this thread, that there is an ascending order of intrusion, might not apply.

    \n I'm a big [expletive referring to female genitalia deleted] when it comes to this. As I recently realized, I'm pretty keen on retaining full authority over as many features of my character as possible. That's not to say I don't enjoy playing games where the identity of PCs are downplayed in favour of their representation as a collection of skills, or games like Universalis or Dirty Secrets where you share control of a pool of characters, but in games where every player has control of a single character I tend to naturally drift into immersion (actor stance). When in that immersive state, it really bothers me when other players dictate facts about my character which do not correspond to my own vision.

    But yes, there is a big difference between\n
    You lost 13 hit points to the dark knight's swipe? OK, then "The dark knight slashes you across the throat, opening a large gash. You clutch at the wound but blood seeps between your fingers, soaking your tunic."
    \nand\n
    You lost 13 hit points to the dark knight's swipe? OK, then "The dark knight slashes you across the throat, opening a large gash. Wide-eyed, you clutch at the wound in panic, blood seeping between your fingers as you collapse to your knees in surprise."
    \nI can handle the first in a D&D-style game, but the second would bother me. I guess I'm somewhere between 4 and 5 there? I probably would have a bigger problem with someone speaking for my character than thinking (narrating silent monologue) for my character. And in an ideal world, I would rather be given the ability to interpret the situation myself.

    I would also be royally peeved if my character was narrated into a mechanically different state - for example, in the example above, if I were forced to take the next round to stand up from kneeling position to continue fighting even though the rules just said "you lost 13 hit points - your move".

    One of the design goals of 'Til Death is to transfer as much authority over PC narration into the hands of the player as is humanly possible, using a group voting system (in the current iteration, at least) to resolve any major transgressions of social contract. It does this (I think) by providing a set of hints in the process and outcome of skill checking to tell the group if the task succeeded, at what magnitude it succeeded (or didn't) and what character elements were involved. The hints can be interpreted however the player chooses, without the interpretation having any great effect on the mechanics of the game. In fact, this is my greatest revelation in the last little while - the less a narrative decision affects mechanics, the greater the level of authority players can be given over it without it negatively impacting the social dynamics of the group. Seems stupidly obvious, huh?

    That design goal was initially mostly included for purposes of combating trad "fear mechanics" which in my experience is blocking more than exciting, and frequently bleed into the realm of ridiculously trite, but it expanded to permeate the whole system. We'll see how it turns out in the end, I suppose. I guess we're all really designing for ourselves first, eh?

    \n Jonas, yup that was the thread I was speaking of and I think it is a good compromise.

    In terms of this forum, and story games being ones where there is a "gaming experience with a lesser focus on My Character and a greater focus on Our Story", I think in such games players should be willing to not be "method actors". If the story is about the femme fatale or con man who glibs their way through things, and the player characters are all "immune" the story's needs seem to fall through. Rather much like being in a shoot 'em up with characters that are bullet proof.

    If player characters are to be immune to persuasion, the stories should be ones in which it is not an issue. Shoot 'em ups or similar. No "persuasive dragons" with Diplomacy uber skills etc. (I'm about to run an encounter with a +29 Diplomacy dragon in D&D, using my heavily home brewed social rules that offer PC's the choice of go with the system's outcome for character actions short term, choose your 2nd thoughts later, or take an XP penalty).

    I think this all works only if players really have bought into the story, and the game has a means of letting them have influence over it just as they do over their characters in a traditional game. Asking players to care about and play to something they can't influence is asking too much.     \n Per a statement in one of Ron Edward's essays about "fortune in the middle" supporting narrativism, if it is "fortune in the middle" over "staked actions/outcomes not beliefs/attitudes" and "loser narrates loss", that gives a player a wide buffer zone around their character concept.     If the real point of seduction roll against PC was to isolate them, delay them, get them to reveal information, or set them up to have their spouse angry at them, a PC who loses can narrate how the seduction accomplished any or all of those but didn't actually seduce them, make them think that character was sexy (sorry, I just don't go that way), or result in them "doing the deed".     \n Having hopped from thread to thread related to social mechanics (social mechanics and RP, grimm/SOTC) I've come to the conclusion that a lot of the disagreements over social mechanics come from issues of player and GM trust. If you don't trust the other players (or the GM) with the fate of your character you will want to give as little control away as possible. Conversely if you (as a GM) don't trust the players to do the right things with their characters you will want more ways to make them do it.
    \n
    \nI think Story games require a lot more trust than traditional games. If you've encountered even the least of the creepy gamers discussed at RPG.net then you would know that there are plenty of gamers you would never go near with a story game, because you couldn't trust them with the fate of anything, even their own character.
    \n
    \nAs someone who GM's more than plays (in recent years anyway), I have often encountered the problem of players who can't RP a social situation without a mechanic, they just can't make decisions their character would make, rather than the ones they, the player, would want.
    \n
    \nSo for example (and its a real example) I was running a GURPS game with a character who was a gambling addict. His background was all about wasting his fortune away till he was living in the gutters, and even then anything he got he would throw away on the next toss of the dice. (Mechanically he had poor, and gambling adict as disadvantages, and quirks of never refusing a bet).
    \n
    \nYet when that character found himself in a glitzy gambling den with low, medium and high stakes tables the player insisted on playing low stakes only, and not betting more than the minimum, and backing out when he had lost a moderate amount, and going home instead of staying to gamble. And it happened more than once too.
    \n
    \nSo he had a character set up to be a certain way yet couldn't bear to actually do that. What the player wanted for his character was so much at odds with what the character should have wanted that leaving it up to the player to RP just didn't work. As a GM I needed the mechanics (will rolls and so forth) to enforce actions on the character because the player couldn't be trusted to do it ... just like the often mentioned kid saying "no you missed" in cops and robbers.
    \n
    \nSure that was bad RP. He shouldn't have had a character like that if he wasn't prepared to play out that sort of story. Its easy to put the blame on that player, but this sort of thing can happen to even the best player. Sometimes something happens to your character and you just don't like it, even though it is the right thing as far as the character is concerned. You cry when your much loved character dies, even if he died sacrificing himself to save his loved ones. You hate to give over the wonderful new shiny treasure you just got, even though your character took a vow of poverty. And so on.
    \n
    \nWe are not our characters, and we are only human. Sometimes we just don't want to do what our characters would want to do, for whatever reason. In combat we usually accept that there is a mechanic that takes over for us in such situations. I might prefer that my character doesn't get harmed at all when fighting the dragon but the system says "Sorry, nothing doing". But in social situations, without a mechanic, we can get stuck in that gap between character and player and just do the wrong thing.
    \n
