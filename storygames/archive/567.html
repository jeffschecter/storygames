Best Building and Trading RPGs?

jhkim
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
So D&D and other traditional RPGs developed out of wargames. Most of the mechanics were about combat and other conflict. However, in the last decade we've seen an explosion of boardgames which center on building and trading -- like Settlers of Catan, Ticket to Ride, and so forth.

So what RPGs have the most interesting building and trading mechanics? What games have this as a focus of play? There are some early games, like Champions and Ars Magica, which have interesting build mechanics that occupy some focus. But are there games which make this more the focus of play? Are there structural reasons why RPGs shouldn't focus on building and trading?

    Having played two campaigns of GURPS Riverworld, I can tell you that this is something that GURPS actually does support pretty well. Actual play basically boils down to finding the materials and the skills to put things together and then protecting your infrastructure. So McGuffin (materials), McGuffin (people with the skills or who can teach the skills), and Big Bad (raiders who want your infrastrucutre). No problem.

    Agora's trading comes out in the banking and support rules. At the end of a conflict, you get Resource dice. You can either bank those and grow them with interest or you can hand them over to other players. You can hand them over as payment for their help, as bribes for their later help, as taxes, whatever. Now, Resources aren't really that differentiated outside of die size, so there's no my-lumber-for-your-wool sorts of trades, but there is an interplayer economy to be used and exploited.

    First of all, I disagree with Vaxalon. There is no need for the building rules to be in-character (assuming that's what you meant by "ICly"); and more than that conflict rules have to be in-character actions (disproved by games like Polaris and others).

    For example, Champions has out-of-character creation, followed by in-character "action" phases and out-of-character "build" phases where you spend the points which you earned. However, there is only one currency and it has no trading (except perhaps that you can donate points to a group vehicle or base). Still, building Champions stuff can be quite fun.

    Ars Magica is indeed a better case. There are several currencies: the different types of pawns of Vis, plus books and a few other items. There is a clear turn structure of seasons. You can trade with other PCs for what you need, and have interactions over that. However, it can be a bit overcomplicated -- apparently even moreso in the later editions compare to the second edition which I played.

    As for computer moderation, I say feh. Power Grid doesn't need a computer, nor do many other great trading and building board games. Computer moderation inhibits good build and trading design, because it means that the players don't get to understand how the building really works.

    John (jhkim), I don't think you really understand my point.

    Ars Magica does, indeed, have some small measure of "building" rules... you need so many pawns of vis in order to accomplish permanent magical effects that you want. Trading (outside of PC to PC interactions) is relegated nearly entirely to GM fiat, however, which to my mind means that it's not part of Ars Magica, but simply part of certain particular implementations of Ars Magica.

    The economic life of the covenant, aside from vis, falls entirely in the purview of GM fiat.

    To me, this makes Ars Magica's building and trading component weak, in my opinion; certainly much weaker than Settlers of Catan's.

    You could say that Ars Magica makes the statement that the only thing that matters is magic, but I'd argue that point, given the importance that troupe play places on non-magical characters. If only magic mattered then the only PC's would be the wizards.

    In Settlers of Catan, there are rules for acquiring the resources to build EVERYTHING that matters. Admittedly, the game achieves this by narrowly defining what matters; a roleplaying game must be more flexible and so cannot be as complete, but if we're going to talk about a game that has a strong economic system, then there must be rules for acquiring the resources to build things that matter.

    Agora achieves this by leaving both the resources needed and the things that are built to be defined by the players. It's not the most satisfying implementation in my opinion, but it's a very good start.

    One of the things that's currently happening in Mike Holmes' thursday night IRC Heroquest game is that my character, Okhfels, wants to build a bridge linking a mystical island on a lake with the shore. How long will it take to build? How much stone does he need? How many people does he need? How much help will magic give? I have no idea. The whole question has to fall on GM fiat because the game gives no support to the question. Is that a bad thing? No, because Heroquest really isn't ABOUT that kind of activity... but for any game that DOES want to be about building and trading, there needs to be SOME way to answer those kinds of questions.

    Vaxalon wrote:

    An RPG is never going to be purely about trading and building in the sense that Settlers of Catan is. What I'm looking for instead is a game which uses trading and building mechanics as part of its goals.

    Consider as a parallel conflict games (like Chess) compared to RPGs like Dogs in the Vineyard or Rune which make use of conflict mechanics. I want an RPG which uses trading and building as effectively as Dogs uses conflict. Ars Magica isn't it, but I think it's worth noting how well it does work. Note that Dogs doesn't strictly define the opposition in the conflicts which PCs face, either. In Dogs, the GM can always beat the PCs by throwing sufficient numbers at them. By contrast, Rune is a more strict conflict game where the GM can't just beat the PCs.

    So getting back to trading and building -- I don't think that precisely defined supply is necessary for trading and building to be interesting. Within Ars Magica, yes, trading with the GM in some NPC encounter isn't interesting. But with the PCs forming a covenant which has limited resources, then trading among the PCs is interesting. Each mage will have their projects, and they need to trade off resources.

    So, we could have a trading/building RPG parallel to Rune which is close to a boardgame. However, I think it can also be interesting to have a trading/building RPG which is more a parallel to Dogs, using building mechanics but with looser constraints. I'm still in the process of reading Agora -- I'll see what I think when I'm done.
