\n I ran some Burning Wheel recently (The Sword a couple times) and noticed something interesting. There's a situation that comes up not-infrequently in which you've rolled some dice, gotten some successes but two fewer than you need, and have the option to spend a fairly limited resource to try to get your extra two. If you do, you'll pick up one extra die (for that one 6 you rolled), roll it, and if it's a 4-5-6 you get a success and additionally if it's a 6 you get to pick up another die, roll it, etc. So your chances of succeeding are merely 1/12: 1/6 to get the initial 6, then 1/2 to get your second success.

But subjectively, I remember this sort of roll succeeding far more often than one in twelve times. I have a theory why.

First you roll your new die. At this point you have very little hope. 1/12 is pretty small. On a 1-2-3-4-5, we move forward, you failed as expected, failure result, etc. But on a 6 suddenly everyone's much more invested! Despite all odds you now have a 50% chance of success at making this 8% roll! So half the time that everyone's super-invested, you succeed. Because of silly subjective memory, you'll probably remember this type of 1/12 roll succeeding closer to, let's say 40%ish of the time. And (for me at least) this compels me to try/suggest others try the 1/12 shot, intuitively feeling that it's kinda likely while knowing it's 1 in 12.

Can anyone think of other mechanics that have this slight of hand tension-focusing? Or ways to get it more often? I think I like it a lot.

    \n I love that mechanic. I love how the player stops and considers the odds and then shrugs and says, "fuck it." Every time!

    And I love how everyone pretends not to care about that first rerolled die. They all watch out of the corner of their eyes, pretending to be indifferent, but secretly hoping. And I adore how the table falls silent and everyone focuses on that tiny little cube when the 6 comes up. We all hold our collective breath for the reroll...and exhale only when we see the result: a triumphant shout or a cry of dismay.

    Interestingly, this effect only comes up with people who know or understand the game. It doesn't generate nearly the same tension with new players. During con/demo games, I find that I often have to explain, "Wow, well, you could get two more successes out of that one die, but it's a long shot." And I walk them through the steps, but the tension isn't quite there yet.

    \nThis is pretty much exactly what happens*.

    And it doesn't happen in 4e. I've played enough in the last year to state that confidently**. There are some rolls with lots of tension and attention, but no rolls that get the attention only when they're more likely to succeed than initally figured. Well, maybe that one where you can add a d6 to your d20 roll. It seems to have the same structure but I haven't noticed the selective tension... it certainly doesn't do the "you started with such a low chance of success!" thing. And in 4e low-chance rolls don't really come up enough to try a direct port, as far as I can tell. Whenever I'm making one it's 'cause I'm using an untrained skill at a hard DC, or trying to jump up something, or running before attacking... maybe this is 'cause a +/- 5 modifier is common enough that any common low-chance roll implies too-common impossible rolls. Anyway, here's a straight port:

    RULE: Whenever you roll untrained in a skill challenge, you have a +1 DC penalty, and gain the power "Discovered Potential: Spend a healing surge to roll a saving throw. If you succeed, gain a +2 bonus to your skill roll. If you rolled a 18+, you may use Discovered Potential again, immediately, without paying any costs."

    I have a suspicion this still doesn't work as well but I think it might be good enough to playtest to see if I can understand exactly why it doesn't work as well. (Maybe players can't see a d20 result as well as a d6 result, for instance?)
