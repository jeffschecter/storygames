\n

\n

    \n Not from a gamer perspective at all but from a behavioural economics one there is the dynamic of "sunk cost" and "decision reaffirmation". I see this favouring sticking with "traditional" where that means 'more expensive and complicated".

    I invested $40 and hours in rules and making character, if I don't play it was a waste of time and I made a silly choice.

    I invested $10 in pdf and an hour, less inherent psychological resistance to walking away, not such a waste of time, less critique of my own choice.

    These are both "false" behaviours in that neither reflects a utility/happiness maximizing path but for the psychology that drives them (e.g., sunk costs are already lost, it's a question of good time/money after bad not whether or not those costs are spent).

    All compounded if traditional book bought with two twenties and pdf online with credit card - people have different mental accounts for spending and tend to treat cash spending as "more real" than "credit card spending". Much to the glee of credit card industry.

    \n Here is what I've observed (all specific to our local community):
    \n
    \n- Many indie gamers play frequently.
    \n
    \n- Many traditional gamers we attract (because we offer short campaigns) play infrequently (but used to play a lot).
    \n
    \n- Many people new to RPGs seem (stress seem) to be coming in from Magic the Gathering, LARPs, Call of Cthulhu, or video games.
    \n
    \n- The games that stress advancement or change are better at retaining players.
    \n
    \n- Some traditional players are used to sticking it out, even if they aren\u2019t sure the experience is right for them.
    \n
    \n- Some indie gamers are quick to stop playing if it isn\u2019t working for them.
    \n
    \n- Many indie gamers are either adults or spend a lot of time doing lots of different things.
    \n
    \n- We lose players between seasons because people form their own groups. This is a great thing! I haven\u2019t seen any distinction here between traditional and indie. And this has never happened to my knowledge mid-season.
    \n
    \n- Many traditional games attract women, younger players, and non-whites here. Maybe because some traditional games are closer to video games? Are more known? Marketing? Next week is our last session of season 10. I\u2019m going to ask the organizer to put together a questionnaire and I will try to interview a few people personally.
    \n
    \n- I\u2019ve spoken to most of the dropouts. It\u2019s usually because of scheduling, work, or other unexpected social events. But many of the traditional gamers are also very busy but seem uncomfortable canceling events. We\u2019ve had 2 traditional gamer couples change the dates of their honeymoons based on our events. Where some indie gamers will cancel a game to see a new movie.
    \n
    \n- Many of the traditional games will have a character creation session before the official season starts.
    \n
    \n- Many traditional games with advancement will have more chatter online inbetween games. They also tend to be more team based games. That said, Burning Wheel definitely also has this going for it.
    \n
    \n- Many indie gamers we attract are from online who spend a lot of time on forums.
    \n
    \n- Many traditional gamers we attract are from word of mouth and don\u2019t seem to spend a lot of time on forums.
    \n
    \nTake the above with a grain of salt. These opinions are based on experience but I\u2019m sure I\u2019m missing many factors.

    \n This strikes me as yet another indication that there are many parallel activities all called "roleplaying" that are all actually quite different from each other.

    Are trad gamers "more committed"? I think their version of the activity is defined by long-term commitment. There's the play structure of the "campaign" already baked in, and there's a lot of cultural momentum there as well. Trad gamers who get into short-form story games are probably more committed to long-form play and a steady play schedule than players who were brought up on one-offs. Our Thu Crew is largely comprised of trad gamers, and our schedule is nearly unbreakable.

    I imagine it's a little like comparing the play styles of people who play board games like Cranium to the play styles of people who play board games like Advanced Civilization. They're both "playing board games" but wow, VERY different motives and expectations.

    One tangental thing I've found fascinating about 0e is the context of being made in an environment where a big chunk of the gamers were also minis gamers. from posts, again, by Old Geezer, there seems to be a pretty good indication that the levelling up process was at least partially geared towards making PCs into warlords who'd eventually engage in big batttles, presumably with the minis. That would be a big part of the point of the castle and army building rules in 0e and the expert rules.

    Apparently this was considered a fairly self-evident thing for early writers and players, but something a bit not obvious to me as a kid encountering the game and not being a minis gamer at the time, nor having access to minis gamers.
    \nI started playing D+D in 1976 at a general game club. The old minis players were the people who bought the first thousand copies of D+D and had played the hell out of it in 74-75. My brothers and I came in just as they were losing interest in it an returning to the other games they played (Historical Minis and board games). The initial idea may have been to run people up to warlord level but I didn't see that happen in actual play. So D+D didn't lend itself to wargame campaign play in the long run even if that was the idea Gary and Dave had. I was in that wave of teenagers that played in 77-78. Minis were always used by everyone I knew but not in a miniatures gamer way (no terrain - instead we drew maps). I gravitated toward miniatures more from the other games we played at the club than because of D+D. What I really appreciate from my early experiences is that we played all the games. There was no mono culture. It is that diversity that spurred more interest in new design than role playing itself.

    I'm with Komrade Bob on life circumstances dictating what I play. The thought of a rigidly regular game night just doesn't fit my lifestyle now. Which is why I've gravitated towards board games. They give a clean one shot replayable experience that doesn't take long and looks real pretty. Minis can give that but it takes a lot more work to achieve it.

    Maybe committment comes from having to work harder. I don't know... A lot of traditional gamers stop gaming when they find girl friends. Games versus sex... Games almost always lose this match up!

    \n The New York Red Box group that I'm part of has two old-school D&D; campaigns (mine is 1974 0E, the other is 1983 Basic) that run on a drop-in model inspired by Ben Robbins' West Marches. We're if not parasitic then at least rooted in the larger nerdNYC organizing effort - I learned about the group while reading the nerdNYC forums, and gained players from running OD&D; games at the nerdNYC gameday Recess - but also have our own outreach efforts (we play in a public space and recently made up business cards so that people drawn by the archaic books and funny dice didn't just get a scrap of paper on which we'd scrawled our URL).
    \n
    \nSome data about retention with a whoever-shows-up traditional game (although I'm a big proponent of the similarities between certain kinds of old-school and indie play, as whiteknife suggests) can be extracted from this experience point spreadsheet and its exegesis. It looks bimodal to me - we're about evenly distributed between people who get hooked and keep coming back, and those who try it out once and move on. The latter group is probably split between friends-of-gamers who are enticed to try out this roleplaying thing, and experienced, often indie, gamers who find it not to their tastes. (If I had to guess why, I'd say it's the big groups, often a dozen people, that emerge from the intersection of public-access and attempt-to-recreate-historical-conditions & which create their own disadvantages and constraints on play.)
