\n The other day, my son Isaac heard me mention Burning Empires. Immediately, he asked if he could play. Mind you, he knows nothing about this game except that Daddy is borrowing it from Mr. Mazza. After seeing the cover with the picture of the Kerrn, he's sold; he wants to play this game.

Now I'm feeling stuck. Because I don't think that Isaac is ready for a game like Burning Empires. He's eight, and I don't think that he has the necessary wherewithal for the game. But I think that he *could*. For a while, Crystal and I have agreed that we could totally see Isaac as a wargamer, and I think that the tactical crunchiness of Burning Empires (or Burning Wheel, for that matter) could be the the kind of thing that he would really get into.

So, here's my question. What games would be good for me to use to bring Isaac down this path? Should I just jump into Burning Wheel with him? Are there other games that would be an easier start while still having that tactical crunch which I think he will enjoy? Any thoughts?

I summon the power of Story Games. Assist me in my hour of need!

Seth Ben-Ezra
Great Wolf

    \n I concur with those who say Go For It. Just be sure to Take Your Time, also. Make World Burning a whole session, explaining about the backstory as each choice is made; also be sure to speak to what sorts of characters become (im)possible with each choice--this will Take Time. Ditto for character creation: you'll need that time, too! Then go straight into something simple: a Duel of Wits, maybe, or a one-on-one Firefight. Build progressively--make each session focus on one new technique or rule set while echoing last session's rule.

    BITs might be easy, might be tricky. Remember that they're Flags.

    Have you check the Burning FOO forums for other parents' actual play experiences?

    \n I don't think it's the rules complexity per se that's a problem with BE. I think that it's the investment that's a problem. That is, there's a lot of (admittedly fun) "work" to a BE campaign, or even a phase. He may not find doing all of that to be fun. Or he might for a while, and then his attention span would have him moving on to something else.
    \n
    \nThat last is what I predict would happen to my precocious seven-year-old who plays video games like Dawn of War. He can take that level of complexity, but only in short, high-impact doses.
    \n
    \nOn the other hand that might not be a problem either. If you don't mind that you might not finish the game.

    \n I've played with young kids several times at conventions and had a decent amount of fun to a good deal of fun. I would suggest trying games that have a good deal of narrative control with low handling time. My experience is the most difficult thing to convey is when they need to stop the narrative, so a game that requires a lot of stopping to roll dice can be frustrating for them I think. On the plus kids really seem to like being able to tell stories to / with adults.
