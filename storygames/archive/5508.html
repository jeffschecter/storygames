\n I'm starting this thread because I am struck by how online social RP, of which there is vast amounts, seems almost universally rules-less, while RPGs, even story ones, are almost universally defined *by* their rules. It seems such a strange contrast and I'd like to explore it. (Related, but different, threads touching on this include Play by Blog, A tabletop RPG is an activity for the medium of roleplaying and some parts of Indie Promotion Sucks appologies for any repetition).

A bit of background

I used to run a couple of forums where a lot of RP went on in a rule-lite or rules-less environment. The first was the website of the UK roleplaying club GEAS, which was used extensively by a number of groups in the mid to late 90s. The most notable of these were various factions of the UK LARP organization The Gathering, who did downtime RP and meetings on the forums. There were also plenty of non-affiliated gamers who used the forums for social RP. I think at one point we had around 30 different games going at once, some posting in excess of a 100 posts an hour (which made it more like IRC than a forum really).

At much the same time I ran a MUSH called The Halls of Marble, which was primarily a social RP venue. Unlike the GEAS forums the Halls had a rule system, with built in commands to support various skill tests and the like, but they were very seldom used. It interested me greatly at the time, in fact, how resistant people were to allowing such elements to "get in the way of their RP" in contrast to tabletop players, who are much more accepting. I played on a few other RP based MUSHes with rule systems and the attitude was much the same.

Slightly later I ran The Scorpion's Sting, which was an RP environment for the L5R world. The Sting was a little like a combination of a forum and a MUSH, with different boards representing different areas of the Inn of the same name (e.g. a board for the main hall, a board for the garden, a board for the rooms). That had a similar style of RP but less volume.

Currently I run a child-friendly virtual pet game, and it too has forum based (and private messaging based) RP going on, in much the same sort of way as those older boards did, except that since most of the players are children they tend to last for less time and have more fights (between players, rather than between characters) in them.

What links all these efforts is that they had a high volume of RP (involving 10s or 100s of people posting 24 hours a day), with basically no rules as we would normally understand them (even where rules were available). They were definitely roleplaying. People had characters, they spoke in character, they respected the environment, they went on adventures and fought monsters, but they did all that without stats, skills, tests, checks, traits, character sheets, or anything else usually associated with the process of RPGs, and without any reference to established characters from existing fiction to use as a hard guide of what could or could not be done.

There were, of course, rules, in the less traditional sense. Rules about play contracts, about what you could and couldn't do to someone else's character without their agreement, or a third party, or a detailed fight scene. Rules about what sort of themes were appropriate and so on, but they were almost all unspoken, and therefore hard to track or be sure about. Just like the classic cowboys and indians examples from many RPG books the RP on these fora was full of "Bang you're dead! - No I'm not!" scenes, and it didn't seem to matter at all! (Very occasionally people would get banned, or suspended or leave in a huff over misinterpretations of these implicit rules, but that was rare).

So why do our games need rules, and theirs don't? Or put another way, what sort of rules do they need that we could provide? Or what sort of rules do we have that they just don't need? Or are we playing two different sorts of things, and if so why?

    \n I've got some experience here. I was an active member of an online freeform community for a good long while. These conventions that are mentioned here were strictly enforced and explicit rules in the setting I played in. They weren't strictly enforced by a GM or any such thing (though moderators did exist to explain things to new players, answer setting questions, and to utilize their banning powers if things got out of hand) but by the community of players. The rules were basically:

    Each player had complete control over their character, and any additions to the setting that they introduced.
    Introductions to the setting that did not step on anyone else's toes, or violate established fiction were good to go.
    React realistically to the events around you, even if it means you get hit in a fight. (this was subtly enforced; "god moders" got identified and cold-shouldered pretty quickly)
    Don't be a dick. You can initiate scenes with people without necessarily asking first, but if you're unsure that it's welcome, ask first.
    Stick to the setting. It was open enough that many things were allowed, but certain genre conventions ended up being hard-coded because people abused the flexibility.

    These are, like most rules, all about apportioning credibility; who gets to contribute to the SIS, and if there's a conflict, how is it resolved.

    Anyway, I may have more to say later.

    So why do our games need rules, and theirs don't?
    \nPreference for that medium. "Our" games do not in fact need rules; it's not as if freeform play was invented with IRC and email. On the other hand, a text-based interface has several advantages. Text allows for firm reference and clear basis for third party arbitration. In many cases, the social dynamic is character first, player second instead of a more front and center awareness of the person outside of their character. The large scale of many games also promotes community affiliation outside of the intervention of any moderator, though moderation should promote opportunities to get together.\n

    Or put another way, what sort of rules do they need that we could provide?
    \nNone. Some systems and ideas could be imported for recreational value, but the fact is that we have more to learn from them than they have to learn from us. *They* are the cutting edge of roleplaying, not us. *They* are the grassroots future of the culture of gaming, not us. *They* are the people whose longwinded essays we should be reading, instead of calls to further subdivide an already minuscule hobby community.

    I would say that quietly listening to what's going on with fanfic and chat games has significantly improved my work or White Wolf, because these guys exploit setting with rigorous pragmatism. Compare to tabletop discussion about "fixing" X or Y and massive prep and brainstorming for games that will probably never happen. Adamantine Arrow for Mage has a local group construction guide that is a direct result of this influence. Both it and an upcomin book feature in-setting events that were written in reaction to how online, setting-focused games want robust story events. Some of those elements are *not* appropriate for tabletop play (such as the levelling of story importance for all PCs to give everybody a chance to interact with equally important things.\n
    Or what sort of rules do we have that they just don't need?
    \nRoleplaying is not an activity based on firmly defined procedural "needs." It's an art of preferences.\n

    Or are we playing two different sorts of things, and if so why?
    \nThey're different media. There are obvious commonalities and they fall in the general locus of roleplaying. If roleplaying is (as is commonly claimed) a fundamental human activity, it should be no surprise that it expresses itself across multiple media -- but each medium influences the activity.

    It's an order book for Mage. In Mage, orders have local organizations called caucuses. Since Awakened society is quite balkanized, two caucuses in different cities can have vastly different traditions.

    Online games need well-defined guidelines for these groups, because the luxury of a GM giving a briefing on local affairs isn't there. Players come with expectations about who they're joining up with, and GMs need to be able to exercise creativity within that structure, but in such a way that the end result runs itself. This is also handy for tabletop groups because these requirements can help them and won't hinder them, because they're entirely in-world situations with no hard rules.

    Thus book puts the huge set of options into a general structure based on caucus age, political stance and other factors. In an online game, Storytellers can lay these out explicitly in the text. In a tabletop game, these serve as excellent reference points to get the ST thinking, but they do not force any particular situation.

    \n One assumption I see here is that there are certain types of play that freeform cannot handle, which I think is a bad assumption. The types mentioned in one post above include dungeon delving, monster fighting and mysteries. All of these happened with some level of frequency in my old community. The trick is that the players can and are encouraged to play outside their PC. Often, one player would request another player to "play an NPC" for them. In those circumstances, the player playing the NPC had only to stick with the specific requests of the other player, and anything already established about that NPC. At least one of my regular characters was born of a throwaway NPC played for someone else, because the NPC ended up being engaging to me and others. This concept can be extended to monsters and dungeon delving.

    In one notable case in which I took part, three PCs were searching for an object of power in an abandoned tower. We would take turns introducing adversity which all three of our characters would overcome. There exists some potential for aggrandizement of your own PC, but when depth of character and play was more important than badassery (which was basically the rule when badassery was entirely based on your own desires and whims), the majority of effort was put forth into making the encounters interesting for your fellow players. So there, we had both dungeon delving and monster fighting. Additionally, much of the thrill of the unknown was also intact, because we didn't know what would be introduced next by each other, nor did we necessarily know how their introductions would inspire our own future introductions.

    Mystery is a little bit more difficult, but is still doable with the idea of "authority" players. In the community, there were many sub-divisions of setting, based on geographical region and organizations. Each major subsection had a "head", both within the fiction and without. Usually, the head player also played the head character, though there were a few exceptions to the rule. For example, I was the head of the main military force for a while before the community founder passed the General to me to play. But I'm digressing. Mystery can be introduced by these players. In the above example, we didn't exactly know what we'd find in the tower, nor did we know what it did. The setting-wide storyline coordinator told us what it was late into our play of the dungeon, and we played and wrote the story of how we acquired it.. Then the coordinator added the effects of finding it.. And the entire mystery came together at a massive play session that involved the historical bad guy of the setting being reborn through the unknowing efforts of characters of good intent or ill.

    Now, whether or not this can be considered a game, I don't know. We never called it a game, though we did refer to "playing" it. Whatever it was, it had rules, many of which I've seen reflected more or less explicitly in tabletop roleplaying games.

    I think my example demonstrates that freeform can include action/adventure play. The community I was part of was a tavern roleplay community, but it went out beyond the tavern pretty regularly. Usually this was in small storylines involving only a few people, but community "events" happen periodically which allow all players to participate in setting changing events. It was also not uncommon (somewhat unrealistic, but not uncommon) for battles to take place in the immediate environs of the tavern.

    conversely, I've played with groups that will spend sessions in a row without any action, doing things equivalent to talking to people in a tavern. For the most part I enjoy this sort of play as well, though I do confess to getting a little agitated if it continues for too many sessions; I do like my action, too. I may be atypical.
