\n So, I was thinking about how to track the development of communities in a|state. The current mechanics do nothing for this, that has been well documented. I was sitting in Midnight Espresso in Wellington, reading Grey Ranks. The grid caught my eye (as it does). And that started off a chain of thoughts...

The map of The City is a|state is, for those of you who might not be familiar with the game, an iconic image that appears as a visual cue throughout the book. It's a series of concentric circles, with eight radiating lines (these all represent the main canals which cut through The City). I'd been thinking about how to track changes in communities, but simple numbers appeared unsatisfying. Then it hit me: the map. Use the iconic image of the map as some form of visual tracking. Place different elements such as Community, Violence, Wealth and Poverty at the ends of the radial lines and use markers to show communities moving towards or away from these aspects.

I've really not got much further than that, but I just wanted to share that. It was a one of those "Aha! That would be great!" moments.

Cheers
Malcolm

edit: for those who have no idea of what I'm blithering on about, you can see what I mean via the wonders of the venerable a|stateLite download.

    Posted By: Malcolm CraigHmmm...I'm thinking that there are 4 axes, as there are 8 spokes running from the centre of the map. I was thinking that you would be able to maneuver 2 spots around the map, showing the influence in different areas. However, my knowledge of how things like this function in mathematical terms is sadly limited.
    \nHere, I'll demonstrate.

    Here is Jason's Map of Community traits:



    Violence/Peace (equal violence and peace at the origin point)
    Mauve Carpets/Linoleum (50% mauve carpets at the origin point)
    Rushing Percentage of high school football teams/Passing Percentage of high school football teams (50% of each at the origin point)
    Wealth/Poverty (Majority of population at poverty line at origin point)

    We put a community onto this map, Finland!


    Now we analyze Finland by checking out where on these four axes it lies.



    From this we can see (theoretically) that Finland has 60 percent of its population living over the poverty line, it is 30 percent more peaceful than violent, it has 60 percent linoleum floors and Finland High School Football (go fighting Finns!) rushes 60 percent of the time. Or whatever numbers are on those axes.

    Now let's say one thing changes. There's a massive run on linoleum supplies and mauve carpeting becomes vastly more widespread. That means Finland's dot on the map needs to move straight up, to reflect that mauve carpets now outnumber linoleum floors.



    But we have a problem. Because straight up also affects rushing/passing percentage and violence/peace! We now have a map that tells us that the high school football team is rushing 80 percent of the time and that we're only 5 percent more peaceful than violent. Eventually if we get enough mauve carpet, Finland will erupt in uncontrollable riots!

    There's a reason flat maps are called "two dimensional". They can only track two dimensions of independent variables!

    Now, if you wanted to have the diagonal axes be related somehow to the vertical/horizontal axes, that could get interesting, and could be used to make a political statement. What if I replaced the Rushing Percentage line with one that was Atheism/Religosity? The way I put that axis on the map would make a political statement about whether I see an increase in religosity as decreasing or increasing a community's wealth and violence level...

    And that could be hot.

    \n I like the idea that changing position on one axis naturally changes your position on others as well. I also like the idea of the physical world changing to reflect the changes on the axis, so that the City is constantly shifting as relationships change. That's cool to me.

    I think that possibly the chart Jason is using is a bit misleading though, because he's charting movements in two dimensions (x, y), wheras the map you're talking about works kinda differently, because it's circular, and movement is either around the pole, or towards or away from it. The kinds of movement Jason's talking about aren't possible on your map (if you want them to move through discrete units on the map).

    Movement around the map needs to be as natural a progression as movement along the axis. That will require some pretty carefully selected values for the axis, because adjacent ones will have to be thematically similar, or consequences of each other. Here's one such arrangement:

    Hope, Wealth, Greed, Violence, Despair, Poverty, Generosity, Peace

    These are arrayed around points in order, so that Hope is between Generosity and Wealth, and opposite Despair. So a communtiy in a high level of Poverty can move towards wealth either directly (by moving towards the pole) or it can do so by passing through despair, and violence and greed, or it can go the other route, Generosity, Peace, Hope.

    So I think that the distance from the pole could represent the power of the community, its size or influence, and then the position around the pole determines the dominant characteristic of that community.

    That's one way to do it, anyway.

    \nAnd bingo, we're back to the grid! Albeit circular. You could take the chart and have each community/figure/character represented by a counter. Success in conflicts/missions/whatever might move you one step one way, whereas failure another way. As you cross into the different coloured bands the makeup/demeanour/outlook/whatever of the community changes.

    You could tie events to crossing bands too, so as you switch from a low shift to high shift area, the next session/scene has to include a shift of some kind, and so on.

    EDIT to add: I love how Grey Ranks ties different situation elements to each square on the grid, and scenes and stories are drawn from these. You could do something similar, having it so that the communities themselves have a changing set of situation elements depending on where they are on the grid (homeless man asleep in a doorway, the sound of engines roaring deep below etc), and when action takes place in those communities, you bring these elements in somehow.
