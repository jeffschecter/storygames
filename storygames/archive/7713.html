    \n I'll confess to being up to the same thing as Ara...but it's not just an unholy mash-up of Traveller Done Right and Fantasy Heartbreaker. Having played a lot of pick-up Traveller over the summer, I've become enamored of the style of play that was employed: quick, collaborative setting generation enabled by having to rationalize random-table rolls, situation emerging from the interaction of character with setting, and mildly agonistic play with an eye toward the dramatic. Really fun stuff. Porting that over to a fantasy setting has the same sort of appeal as Red Box Hack and other story-oriented hack & slash games.

    The neat thing about my version of Wanderer is that I take seriously the notion that the life-paths of character generation ("terms" in Traveller) are "quests," framed in the idiom of the Campbellian hero-journey. So instead of enlistment, promotion, survival, and so forth, you have e.g., Initiation, Departure, Testing, and Return. Success or failure at each stage naturally has consequences for the rest of the quest.

    The other fun thing that I envision is rules for creating the "domain" or region you'll play in the same way you'd generate a Traveller sub-sector.

    It's a fun thought experiment, at any rate.

    \n We played fast and loose with the rules, though we generated worlds and characters pretty much by the book. The system we wound up adopting involved aggressive scene framing to figure out what the overarching challenge of the scene was, and then mapping out the sequence of successful die rolls needed to achieve that objective. So if you were trying to board a disabled merchant ship and take off some cargo before the space patrol showed up, that might involve 5 or 6 steps, the success or failure of which could influence your chances at the next step. Combat was handled abstractly; "taking a hit" was occasionally a consequence of failure (and we did have a character die as a result). The thing we figured out early was that you had to require narration/dialogue to accompany each player's roll. It became very engaging.

    So we retained "task resolution" by explicitly locating each task within a larger sequence of tasks whose completion determined the success or failure of the overall effort. You could say it was impressionistic task resolution, rather than realistic per se.

    You can hear excerpts from our Traveller play and us talking about it on my brother's actual play podcast Virtual Play on Episode 31: Origins Replay (the first 15 minutes or so) and Episode 33: Dexcon Recap, part 2 (starting about 2:20 in to about 16:00).

    Another question, based off of another thread around here: Let's say the Players failed to have their PCs board the merchant ship. What happened? Now, it sounds like that if they failed on steps 1 through 4 some new, interesting element would arrive that would then shift the focus to new resolution. Is that correct?
    \nSometimes. More often it's like early failure makes later success harder: the PCs are digging themselves in. So a failed roll in an early step usually means there's a penalty to one or more later steps. But an early roll could also be a branch point between the hard way and the easy way (which would "shift the focus to a new resolution," absolutely). Or it could mean the PCs just don't get what they want and have to live with that. \n
    And then the second question is, what happens if you made it all the way to step 5 or 6 -- and you still failed? The PCs, having done all that, are -- what? - stuck on the merchant ship with a lot of cargo? Or what?
    \nThis is where failure usually introduces complications: you get away, but you have to leave some of the stuff behind. Or the space patrol starts chasing you (and may catch you). Or somebody tumbles out an airlock (resulting in a penalty to the getting away clean roll if we have to go out and get him). Or what have you.

    So failure doesn't end the story, necessarily--it just ramps up the stakes until eventually we are talking life-and-death, which players sometimes lose. If you listen to excerpt from Virtual Play 33, you'll hear that that can still be fun.

    \n Now that I've been thinking about this game design stuff intently for a few years, my observation is that it doesn't always take much in the way of a mechanical change to make a huge effect in play. But that might just be me.


    As for creating PCs together:

    I just read today that the Mongoose's Traveller ties PC character creation incidents together (kind of like SotC does with their character creation process.)

    I'm already planning that for my Character creation session this Sunday.

    As I wrote to my players:

    Several assumptions exist for your characters:

    Your character has recently mustered out of a military service
    He is trained, experienced, and battle tested. He might be in his 30s, 40s, or 50s after a distinguished or
    undistinguished military career. Now that he has left the service, what will he do with his days? What will he
    see as a worthy activity? Given that your character is versed in combat, experienced in war, trained for battle \u2013
    what will he choose to do now that his life is his own?

    Your characters are bound together through military service
    Whatever ambitions or threats or unresolved business your character is focused on, you know you have each
    other\u2019s backs in a way no one else in the Imperium ever will. You know each other as brothers-and-sisters-inarms,
    having fought, survived and protected each other. The definition of HUMANITY for this game is
    FRIENDSHIP. The friendship between the Player Characters is what the game is about.


    While I won't be using the tables, I will be using four year terms to establish history and backstory. As the Players generate the details of their PCs, we'll all be sewing them all together.

    Posted By: Christopher KubasikBut it sounds like you guys have gone off the rails of Task Resolution (use this skill to see if you crack the code) which is how CT was built, and to Conflict Resolution ("You're PCs are trying to do such and such, and NPCs X, Y, and Z have a vested interest in stopping you...     \nKind of...each roll still represents an attempt to accomplish a specific task, but all the tasks are located in a sequence that defines the characters' plans, intentions, and goals ("Rendezvous with the derelict merchant ship"; "Open its cargo bay doors"; "Avoid tripping the anti-hijack program"; "Find the right cargo container"; "Get the container onto the launch"; "Return to our ship"). The task list is explicitly laid out, with specific skills enabling each task, and one player takes responsibility for making the die roll for each task. A failed roll either penalizes subsequent rolls ("It takes longer than you thought to open the doors! Your window of opportunity is closing! Minus 2 to all rolls from here on out!") or obviates the rest of the sequence, spinning the whole thing off onto a new tangent ("You can't get the doors open! You're going to have to find a different way in! What do you do?")

    So it's the entire sequence of rolls that resolves the conflict. It's not the case that any given roll is regarded as a clash of interests per se. Although I suppose that the level of detail is abstracted enough that each roll resolves a specific intention rather than a "task" as such. But I just wanted to clarify that we're not really assigning material agency to the adversity the characters experience. As Ara suggests, it's just a matter of articulating the consequences up front.

    And Mongoose Traveller is worth taking a look at for how it tweaks CT in character generation. It lets you change careers or just be a "drifter" rather than submit to the draft, includes life events and mishap tables, and gives groups of characters access to skill packages to ensure that a playing group has all the skills it needs to, say, run a starship. But I like your idea, Christopher, of explicitly stipulating that PCs have been bound together by their prior service. That's pretty cool.

    \n Hi Bill,

    Got it on your resolution system. I have more questions about it, but without playing it, and most likely sitting down and talking face to face, I'd be making far too many assumptions. So I'm going to bow out of that for now.


    Character creation: characters bound. Yes. I'll be posting the details of the game after character / situation creation tomorrow, but I'm really excited about this. I feel like I'm finally going to be getting the Traveller that I always wanted from the game. (It's actually heavily inspired by the movie, "The Man Who Would Be King" -- with two guys who leave the army of the British Empire after having served in India, and go off into the untamed wilds around Afghanistan to make their fortune.)

    I'll be using the rules for Sorcerer, and Sorcerer has a Humanity mechanic. Basically, on a scale of one through ten, you track a PCs Humanity. Each group defines Humanity for their story. Ours will be Friendship, so the idea of friendship will be front and center -- between the PCs and between the PCs and NPCs. If they do things that strengthen friendship, the make a role to see if their Humanity goes up. If they do things that betray friendship, the Humanity might go down. There's no penalty or bonus to high or low friendship; nor does it control behavior in any way like the Vampire Humanity rules. However, if the PC reaches 0 Humanity, he's no longer "human" enough to be an PC and the GM takes over.

    So, this puts this idea of friendship front and center in the game and the story. So the game will be about how these characters are there for each other, or not; and what it even means to be there for each other. Each will have their own agendas and personalities, but they're all decide to be there for each other -- because how can you not be for the guy you fought alongside? Even if he's getting really greedy? Or putting your life in danger for the woman he loves? Or is about to blow the whole operation because he just spotted the guy who killed his father? Or whatnot.

    Since my players are really good about mucking up situations for great story content, there will be questions of what to do with loot or women or ambitions -- that will only get us into the terrific trouble and torn loyalties. When you wrote: "...situation emerging from the interaction of character with setting, and mildly agonistic play with an eye toward the dramatic," I was nodding my head, thinking, "Exactly!"

    I am so looking forward to this.
