\n I was thinking on two things: Apocalypse World's suffer harm move and Thus Began the Adventures of Eowyn. The relationship between AW's voluntarily suffering harm through seize by force and Eowyn's player-driven risk management has lead me down twisting paths to a peculiar thought.

Instead of rolling the dice to accomplish something, what if players only rolled to avoid bad things happening? The expansion of the "saving throw" to encompass other areas of gameplay could provide an interesting mechanic. Save vs. poverty! Save vs. time loss! Save vs. angering the gods! That's a tad glib, but I take it you understand my meaning.

Short form: a player says he wants to do X, and the GM says, "Sure, but you're going to risk Y," where Y is a hard move. X automatically occurs, but Y is up in the air. Player then rolls to see how badly Y hurts him.

As a more concrete example, let us say that the Duke Boys wish to give the ol' one-two to Boss Hogg so maybe he'll ease up on them. Normally, this would play out as seize by force (depending on situation), and you'd roll +hard, then you'd roll +harm suffered. What if we cut out the middleman and said, "If you want to do that, you're going to risk suffering harm."

(This might lead to some negotiation between players and GM on how to mitigate the risk taken. For example, the player might know that Boss Hogg has a mighty big gun and that the player character is liable to suffer serious injury in the process, so he tells the GM he wants to relieve Boss Hogg of his gun, get the drop on him, etc.)

Beyond rolling the dice to determine the consequences (saving vs. the hard move), the success of the action is assured. Yes, you can punch Boss Hogg in the face, and yes, he'll lighten up for a time, but the harm roll is what really matters, so make your saving throw and see how much it stings.

    \n Hey, nice to hear some chatter about my game.

    What you're describing is an interesting nuance in this situation. We had some conversations about this some years ago.

    A few observations:

    1. The Otherkind approach, Eowyn's resolution, and AW's "moves" - all related concepts - are tricky in this sense.

    They *look* like mechanics which tell you if you succeed, but, REALLY, they're about whether you suffer the danger.

    So, the dynamic you're describing is already in there (with varying odds of actual failure, which is highest in AW and lowest in Otherkind dice). It's the most common outcome of all those mechanics!

    2. Taking the character'a success off the table - just assuming it's guaranteed - is definitely an option.

    I've seen people play AW diceless: just assume every roll is a 7-9. It can work!

    I thought about doing that in Eowyn. You'll notice that the phrasing of "challenge" allows this possibility - you can easily say, "defeating this guy is not in question. Clearly, you're determined enough to kill him. Rather, we're curious about: can you avoid his poison dagger?" I do that sometimes when I play.

    3. What it does do is change the nature of the conversation at the table in subtle ways.

    I find that I generally prefer interactions where the player must overtly indicate their goal or aim.

    Why? It makes for more player-driven games. Sure, most of the time it's the same whether we assume success or roll for it. But sometimes you get those moments where you didn't realize you were slightly off until the player is forced to verbalize their aims.

    Here's an illustration:

    "Ok, so your goal here is clearly to kill him. Let's roll!"

    Option A. This is overt and so, on a success, that's what happens.

    Option B. This is unstated. So we roll abd then we play out the enemy dying.

    Same, right?

    But! Once in a while it turns out that the player was attacking the opponent with his sword, but asking "what I said your goal" reveals more.

    Player: "Wait a minute! You know what? Sure, i'm probably gonna kill him in the process, but what I really want is to humiliate him in front of the Queen!"

    Under Option B, sometimes you play it out and the enemy is dead, and we never got to learn or figure out what the player really wanted. (And because we didn't have the conversation, sometimes the player didn't learn that either!)

    It's subtle, but, over time, the effect is noticeable.

    \n It's nothing magical: just as you describe.

    First, it forces you to have the conversation. Some people skip it, instead. If it's not required by the rules, skipping it becomes much easier, and you might find yourself doing it more often.

    For an extreme case, consider a hardened D&D fanatic trying to GM the game. He would almost certainly skip that step if it wasn't overtly indicated!

    Second, it indicates to the player how much power they have in the situation, and puts emphasis on it.

    For instance, if I have had lots of experiences with mediocre GMs, I might hold back or try to give a "safe" answer when the GM asks, "Ok, but what are you hoping to achieve?"

    If, on the other hand, an important die roll/choice depends on that, I'm more likely to sit back and give it some serious thought. ("This is going to cost me one of my die choices? I'd better make this good/take advantage of the opportunity.")

    Like I said, it's subtle, but I see it as having a small impact on the game.

    You're right that it's a minor difference, though.

    \nFunny. I agree, but for an entirely different reason. I've found clearly verbalizing your goals keeps consequences and risks consistent across GMs.

    For instance, say a player is trying to sneak into a fortress. They come up with a plan to climb a wall. The intended goal is to sneak into the fortress, and the method is by climbing a wall. So if the player fails, they might make a lot of noise as they're climbing. But if the player only says "I want to climb this wall", then the GM might think "oh, then if they fail, they fall off the wall". Now there's two different scenarios that can come from the same action, one of which (falling) is potentially more severe than the other (getting spotted). And when the same action can have two different consequences, the player has to analyze their GM. "If I roll low, will I fall off the wall, or will I get spotted? I think I can manage if I get spotted, but I don't think I can survive the fall. What will the GM do?" Not exactly a roleplaying mindset.

    But let's say the rules make the players verbalize their goals. Assuming the dice resolution mechanics are clear, the player knows exactly what they risk at all times, regardless of who the GM is. The GM and players are on the same wavelength; both know what the players are trying to accomplish, and so everyone knows the risks. Thus, no one needs to analyze each other's personalities, and so everyone can roleplay.

You'd need to find a way to get information across the table without tipping everyone off.

    \n That's a bit tricky, actually.

    First of all, having a chance of avoiding a complication is not that strong a motivator of tension. It's just not as ign interesting to see ifeel there's a problem while more exciting things [a]re happening.

    You have to carefully design the game so that the tension and incentives feel right - or people might not care that much about the roll at all.

    On a related topic, for fun/effecive play, we WANT those complications to happen as often as possible.

    Giving the players the means to avoid that could mean you're working against the fun of the game.

    But, if you set things up so that the complications are almost guaranteed to happen, then you risk making your game feel rather hopeless.

    "When you do stuff, there's a chance that there is a complication. The odds are about 90% against you, but you can improve them to about 85%."

    There's a danger that feels rather hopeless, from the player's perspective.
    "Why even bother? It's rigged, man."

    You have to consider these carefully. My "Eowyn" game, for instance, handles this well, I feel, but even there I might have overdone it by making "full success" too rare. I'll have to play a lot more to be sure, but it's a possibility.
