Wait, what?

It's a horror game: You're there drinking your tea, being a non-monster-hunter then suddenly FUCK! MONSTER! DEAL. NOW!!

While a proactive investigator is a possible way to roll, the clueless rube is part of the very set-up of the game. Because: horror. Most horror movies presuppose a nonprofessional nonmonster hunter who has to deal with the supernatural because otherwise they or the world are doomed otherwise. The Lovecraft source material very much included.

I don't understand how introducing nonmonster hunters ot monsters could foul up the game--thatisthe game.

...and how you do that without railroading:
http://www.dndwithpornstars.blogspot.com/2012/02/hunterhunted.html
\nWell, yes. There are solutions. The main difficulty with Cthulhu is that it's a very wide-open game. I think that anybody would agree that it's a wide-open tool-set, for the most part. Heck, take your average CoC text (any edition, they're near identical), and it's going to be 20% tools for creating all sorts of characters, 20% discussion about all the possible ways of running a horror game, 30% monster listings and the rest some ready-made adventure scenarios. That's not much in the way of focus. What gives the game its profile is the Lovecraftian color, but that's just color: there is nothing particular in the structure of the game to make it "Lovecraftian", nor does it really try to even define what that would mean aside from having player characters go crazy now and then. I have identified at least four self-consistent but mutually incompatible ways of playing "Call of Cthulhu", all with some degree of textual support.

I'm not saying that it's insurmountable to play the game in a fun way. I just personally happen to have had a long history of lukewarm sessions that have left me very aware of the game's weaknesses as a game text. Hit upon a functional and fun way to play, and you'll never have the same problems.

Interestingly, the game text is very much against your suggested solutions. Not that this makes those solutions wrong for CoC - it's very clear to me that it's a toolbox game, you do what works. But if you look at the actual text we were working with while learning the game, proactive GMing like "monsters jump out" or "the supernatural is going to get you whether you want it or not" were definitely, specifically not in the repertoire: old man Corbit in "The Haunted House" ("The Haunting", as this probably most well-known CoC scenario is also known by) is not going to jump out, and he's definitely not going to escalate beyond the types of tactics established in the module text. Or maybe he is, or should be, but reading the text you don't get this impression, the way it carefully delineates the things that can happen. Corbit also doesn't have any in-character motivation for going on a rampage, so I'd say that this quintessential CoC scenario is a very good example of how the game can go if you play ineffectively: a random bunch of characters sort of messing about with detective work, a monstrous backstory that never gets unearthed, and a monster climax that might happen or not, but probably won't happen with any sort of satisfactory pacing.

(For those wondering about the play experience the CoC game text teaches, I note that Chaosium is giving "The Haunting" away for free as part of an introductory pdf. Definitely nostalgic, I remember running this in my teens. The book bound in human flesh was something that I very much desired to show to the other players as a climax of sorts. I don't think us teenagers were at all equipped to unravel the actual system of clues and backstory, of course. Certainly when I GMed this for a fresh bunch two years ago we didn't fare any better - a dull session of mostly barking at the wrong trees, and the players never even thought to look in the cellar.)

And again, because for some reason our discussions resemble arguments more than discussions, so maybe I need to be extra-clear about my point: I'm not saying that the game is bad or doesn't work. I'm just saying that CoC's been a difficult game for me historically, and I think that there are recognizable reasons for that in the game's text. Nowadays, as I grow stronger and stronger in my roleplaying, I am positively brimming with ideas for what to do with the basic premise of CoC. One of these days I'll no doubt get it right (perhaps in multiple different ways), but I don't think I can attribute much more than the core inspiration to that game text I got in '92. An inspiring text, but not particularly effective for us in reaching a functional set of play procedures.

    \n I think part of what makes CoC "easy" is that the whole premise is RIGHT THERE. When I ran it back in the day, it always went wonderfully because I and my players all had an intuitive grasp of how the game is supposed to work. It's a very simple formula. The one time CoC ever failed me I was playing a written module exactly as written. The module just told you to do stuff, so I skipped all the intuitive horror bits that I'd been doing all along. The adventure fell flat. I've never read the Corbit scenario, but that may be where the problem comes in. It seems to tell you more about how to run the game, but in this case you end up with less because, thinking you've got a clear set of instructions, you stop doing the stuff you need to do.

    The thing with CoC though is that if you don't have the intuitions, it can just collapse. As an aside, some of the collapse states are pretty interesting games in themselves. I know CoC players who've had a blast hunting Shoggoths with rocket launcher or crashing aircraft carriers into Cthulu. Good clean fun.

    Graham Walmsley's "Stealing Cthulu" is a really great book to look at. The focus of the entire book is on transmitting the feeling of Lovecraftian investigative horror. Once you've read through it you'll be so brimming with Cthulu-esque awesomeness you won't be able to wait to play, and you'll know exactly what to do. Oh, the game also has a page of rules at the end. They are basically all you need to run CoC.

    Reading Graham's book made me understand what I was already doing intuitively when I ran Cthulu, so in that sense it was very useful. I'd probably use his game to run it again, just because it's so spectacularly rules light that I can pull it out any time and start running it. Old-school CoC is great, too.

    One other important bit that I never realized myself, but that Graham talks about very well, is the difference between PCs and Lovecraft protagonists. The protagonists in Lovecraft don't really investigate. For the most part they mystery is spoon fed to them until they hit the big horrible reveal at the end. PCs are not so accommodating. It's not a huge deal, but I think it throws people off sometimes when they're focusing on making the game look as much like a Lovecraft story as possible.

    \n Probability simulation task resolution mechanics + immersive character play + mysterious threats + investigation opportunities = "How am I going to solve this problem?"

    I'm there, in character, there's a problem that threatens me and/or the world, and look, the skills on my character sheet are a toolkit for tackling that.

    This is why, when people play Cthulhu wrong, I'm not inclined to lay the blame solely on them.

    Yeah, the flavor text in the book tells you to focus on atmosphere and horror and discovery. If this was a freeform game, and that was all you had to go on, then it'd be obvious that you make or break that with your own chops.

    But it's not a freeform game, so not everyone gets that you make or break it with your own chops at horror; and hey, look, here are some concrete gameplay options that are not about horror at all but about problem-solving*.

    I wish my first CoC GM had said the following to me:

    "Dave, your survival and progress are up to me. I'm aware of this and will take the responsibility for making it a fun arc. Your job is to get in character and experience what unfolds, reacting dramatically and letting everyone see how the horror is affecting you. When you roll dice, it's just to introduce a random element into the narrative, to color the shape it takes, so I don't have to simply decide how every little action turns out. Do not focus on solving the mystery and defeating the badguys; there is no guarantee that you will have a fair shot to do those things**. Think of success as an optional garnish on the real activity of discovering mind-blasting horrors and descending into madness. Final note: we want to sustain fictional plausibility, so don't do anything that'd obviously get you killed or remove you from the adventure, unless you really want to stop playing."

    Maybe that's a little extreme, and getting caught up in solving any given problem and succeeding at any given task is fine, but it's pretty clear to me that as an overall orientation to play, problem-solving makes for lousy Cthulhu.

    *Sanity loss is cool, but is not a gameplay option; as a player, you don't make any choices with a clear connection to losing Sanity, other than the choice to actually play (rather than hiding from everything interesting in the game)
    **as Tony said; Cthulhu GMing is about transmitting a feeling, not providing balanced obstacles

    \n Call of Cthulhu is not a difficult game.

    It is Moldvay era Basic D&D, set in the 1920s, with a mystery's network of clues replacing a physical dungeon's series of rooms. The GM is either allowed to use 1/2 to 2 HD monsters or 8+HD monsters, but if he uses 8+ HD monsters, he has to provide tips and tricks among the clue matrix to defeating those 8+ HD monsters by means other than physical confrontation.

    PCs have two kinds of hit points: Regular old hit points and mental hit points, which can have critical table type effects, but don't kill the character directly. You start out with a whole lot of the second kind, but the critical hits mostly invovle stuff like running away or otherwise being useless. Sometimes the clues that you discover to help you defeat the 8+ HD monsters whittle away at those second kind of hit points and make later criticals easier to suffer, so watch out! it's a trade off.

    There are only humans as PCs, but there is magic. Magic isn't the flashy kind, but it is the big and powerful kind, and mostly for PCs is related to defeating the 8+ HD monsters. PCs that can end up using magic usually lose a lot of those Mental Hit Points learning to use those more powerful spells. Those spells they do learn often take time to get cooking, so they'll need pals that are the equivalents of fighters and thieves in the 1920s to protect them while they go about their business.

    The other guys don't get much in the way of armor (watch out, you'll bekinda squishy) or magic swords or pretty pony warhorses, but they do get stuff like tommyguns, and nickleplated twin .45 automatics and fast cars and autogyros, so really, who cares? Mowing down a horde of gibbering 1/2 HD monsters is awesome.

    The PCs are heroes, but nobody around them much believe in monsters except them and the poor devils that encounter them, and usually those folks are the hooks for an adventure. The PCs don't get rich like in D&D ( sometimes they start pretty much rich!!!), but they do get to risk their necks doing terribly heroic stuff like saving a family, or town, or even the whole planet from those nasty big critters and their minions. Or rmaybe they die trying.

    There ya go. Done.

    Posted By: Zak SI mean, I guess you just assume the gamebook is gonna (or should(?)) give you way more guidance than I do.
    \nDefinitely seems that way. My own gaming has improved tremendously with the post-millenial game texts that have been written more with the multitude of subcultures in mind. I don't mind the loss of theoretical freedom, as I have plenty of freedom in choosing which game texts to use and how to use them. There are a thousand ways to be wrong for every way of being right, so I see a clear game text as a useful guide in the wilderness of all non-fun ways to play. Of course this isn't going to be that compelling for somebody who already has hit upon functional play by themselves or thanks to some formative texts or smart tutoring or whatever; I can totally see how a gamer like that would be less interested in the definitiveness of a text and more interested in whether it is inspirational or not.

    CoC is a good example here, because we can contrast it with later games with different relation between the game text and the game group. Games like Dread, Dead of Night and Dread: First Book of Pandemonium (no relation, believe it or not) traverse a part of the same ground CoC does, but do so with more concrete procedures and a more exact vision of what is going to happen at the gaming table. None of these games really embraces the entirety of what CoC purports to be, but I find that they're dependable at doing whatever subset of horror each attempts.

    In this regard I find the type of writing that e.g. Graham Walmsley does on Cthulhu to be extremely appropriate and compelling for our times: we need more interpretative and instructive writing on roleplaying, especially regarding the classical games that provide plenty of opportunity and depth, that have a valiant history and immense potential, but that are also difficult and ambivalent. I see much of OSR writing in this same light, and can only applaud it: write me your instruction manuals, I'll be perfectly capable of synthesizing them with the original texts and utilizing the resulting well-defined game to have fun with it.

    \n There's no one way to play Cthulhu, although I have my favourites.

    I've thought, that from a genealogical perspective, CoC sprang up from old-skool D&D. You face challenges and try to avoid violent death, but in CoC the challenges are intellectual. Thinking about CoC this way helps me understand the game in one way: make the game deadly and challenging, but logical and surmountable if you have luck and the wits. As the Keeper, you don't have to be a prisoner to the scenario \u2013 if a player comes up with a good solution that makes perfect sense, do allow it. Now, add memorable NPCs, evocative locations and all that story stuff.

    Being the Keeper and playing the game that way takes skill, which you develop. As a player, you become more resourceful, and as Keeper, you hone your skills in writing memorable scenarios and fair challenges for the players.

    I haven't ever played Cthulhu this way, but people have described it this way, and I get that feeling from Shadows of Yog-Sothoth (the first published campaign) and Masks of Nyarlathotep (a fine example of a sandbox-ish campaign that still has a plot).

    The published scenarios and campaigns make up a lot of the CoC culture on the net. The best ones might be more time-consuming than doing stuff yourself, but you make up for it by reading a 400-page tome on an Antarctic expedition in the 1930s, a very well-designed modern day conspiracy setting (designed by folks who know more stuff on that subject than me), or by getting to know what the Kerguelens are. The best CoC scenarios and books are based on a ton of research, and you can partake in some of its fruit by reading it. And you learn other ways to play and design scenarios, to boot, if you're not lucky enough to have another GM in your area.

    In the 1990s the Chaosium scenarios and campaigns took a turn for the pre-written, railroaded stories. That's one way to play the game. The Tatters of the King, a Hastur-themed campaign, published as late as 2006, is an example of that. It's been applauded, but I found it appalling: it's made for players to follow the story and play out the horror. There's insanely little freedom in the book, and several times the author instructs you to ask for rolls that matter squat. "Roll for Hide. Even if you succeed, they see you." And even if the GM would want to insert freedom into the campaign, it's made extremely difficult. The first part of the campaign is a huge, intertwined scenario, that has a gazillion moving parts and necessary clues, with no attempt to make it easy to digest or gather all the important stuff in one place.

    Surely it's possible in theory to handle handwaving such stuff \u2013 and that takes skill, again. I didn't have it when I ran the campaign and forever swore myself off of prewritten adventures.

    Then there's the school which looks like pre-written stories, but the authors make the assumption that you give players the ball \u2013 if they lose it, they just lose it and screw them. They don't see kill the monsters, they don't find out the truth behind stuff, they die ignorant like the specks of dirt they are on the cosmic scale. Dennis Detwiller, of Delta Green fame, talks of his stuff in this way, and a lot of my acquaintances play this way, too.

    Many players and Keepers don't much mind for the rules, and a session without any dice-rolling is considered a good one. They can handle the quirks and the faults, because they've houseruled them ages ago, or simply don't ignore them for the sake of a good fright night. GUMSHOE has gotten a quite a lot of flak from the more militant of these; they've houseruled the "I missed the clue-gathering roll" ages ago or simply ignore the result, and think that designing a new game just to circumvent this is lunacy, and from I gather from the tone, treachery.

    As I see it, Kenneth Hite formulated the most important rule and skill of playing Cthulhu: the Keeper's job is to scare the players, and the players job is to willingly get scared. Drop your guard and your cynicism. If as a player you ignore all the dangerous stuff, or throw inappropriate jokes, or can't stand the tension and the atmosphere, or if as a Keeper you're not intent on frightening the players, I don't want you in my game.

    (As an aside, I think the biggest problems in horror gaming arise from players who contribute nothing, resist the building atmosphere, and expect the Keeper to do such a marvelous job that even dicks get scared.)

    Posted By: David Berg If you have any tips for pulling that off besides "play with awesome people", I'd love to hear 'em.
    \n- Make sure everyone there is at least potentially awesome at Cthulhu. Dead weight is dead weight. I have lots of people in my regular D&D group who wouldn't be into Cthulhu. They know it, I know it, they do other stuff that day.

    - Make sure everyone realizes they are as responsible for the other players' fun, in their own way, as the Keeper or game designer are. You can reinforce this in micro-ways like:
    "What do you do?"
    "I don't know...hmmmm...."
    "Make sure it's something awesome..."

    - When someone does something cool, make sure you immediately say "Wow, that was cool" or otherwise back it up. You can have a scary Cthulhu game without total immersion all day long, use the "meta" level to Carrot good play practices. (Examples....

    - If a situation has turned out better (or worse)(or different) because of things the PCs did, point it out right then. "If only you;d left the key in the lock..." Remind them about their agency.

    - If someone is actually very scarable, when they get scared, go ahead and say "I love playing Cthulhu with you". Like, being actually scared is a good thing a player did at your table.

    - Emphasize the other players actions or inaction as factors in the scenario. If your detective is like "Stop freaking out, I'm trying to detect!" you're the Keeper, frame the freakout as a part of the scene. "Alright, if you can manage to get Adam to stop crying you might be able to hear well enough to hear the rats in the walls. Adam: you can see he's staring death-beams at you because he's trying to listen and you keep whimpering. What do you do?". Don't ignore what's there and hope it stops being a problem, use it. Make strengths out of weaknesses. Is someone not afraid? Say "Lisa is a rock in the face of the hideous abomination!" Is her character afraid but she's not? "Ok, Lisa, you failed your SAN check here, can anyone tell? Or is it some kind of silent thing has broken inside? How does the fear manifest itself?"

    - Related: the tensions in the real group can model tensions in the real game. That's ok. Show the players that: "Ok, Freddy is running around trying to find anything useful in the room while you two are just sort of staring dumbstruck in horror doing nothing, the awfulness of it all is kind of oozing up through your nervous system..." They either will do something about it or it becomes what the scenario is about.

    -Remember: It's actually really hard to derail supernatural horror. The players blocking or bickering or steadystating is perfect for the monster. They cease to be a moving target and it can come to them. And point out the cause-and-effect "If you had left the hotel room, then it wouldn't have noticed, but since you just stood there for five minutes, it begins to uncoil..." You don't kill them for behaving wrong--that's bad Keeping. But you prod them a little with their own decisions.

    -Be fair and never railroad. Point out when you are being fair. "Did the cops keep the car running? Ok, 50-50 chance..." If the players think you are being fair then they will indeed see that the consequences of their own actions are actually consequences of their own actions and take responsibility for stuff happening in the game. Many published Cthulhu scenarios are terrible at this--mostly because the "path not taken" path is one the game designer never had to GM so doesn't want to bother to write out. You don't have that luxury. If you railroad the players, they will stop thinking their actions matter and if they stop thinking their actions matter they will stop acting responsible for the fun at the table. Just because the foe is implacable does not mean the players' choices do not matter in terms of where, when, how, and with what resources the foe is confronted.

    -Remember your main aesthetic job is to make the players' curious. Direct any resource you can spare toward that. If they are curious, then their freedom is not threatening because they will use it to investigate what you or the adventure-writer dreamed up and then you are playing the game. (You can always scare them later. But curiosity and fear both require an unknown at the center. Mystery begets horror.)

    \n In my experience Call is a difficult game. I have played a fair number of times, and have observed that the game is very much dependent on how the leader handles it. The game falls flat with leaders that turn out nice game-play in other games, and that is an indication to me, that the game is harder to run than most classical games out there.

    I believe this has to do with the inclusion of two great challenges in role-playing:

    1 - The puzzle ... is hard to do well. You balance on a knifes edge, and if you fail to get the balance right your players are either left clueless and feeling stupid, or they are left with cheap victories and little suspense.

    2 - The horror: it is hard to do well. To make it hit home you have to exercise a lot of control over the game-play, but that must be done in a way that leaves the players the necessary space to invest in their characters too. It's both a balancing act and a psycho-social challenge. The psycho-social challenge is to have the players invest emotionally in the characters and the situation. Without that investment the game feels lame.

    The tools to do these things are not part of the game. They have to be invented by the leader. Leaving two great challenges like these to be met, simultaneously, by intuition and inventiveness, makes Call of Cthulhu a very hard game to run, in my view.

      \n I think there is a contradiction at the heart of the rules of Call of Cthulhu but it is a productive one. On the one hand, the mechanics suggest that you should have weapons, and use them. Cthulhu has hit points, for goodness' sake. On the other hand the text suggests that weapons are pointless and counterproductive. This contradiction has led to divergent play styles which in Trail are described as Pulp and Purist. Graham's Purist adventures and style of GMing are totally different to those at the more Pulp end. Lovecraft contains both, though it's his followers who tended more towards the Pulp end.

      I personally don't think that the text of Call of Cthulhu engenders what is now a considered a Call of Cthulhu game straight out of the box. It hasn't been updated as a result of how people are actually playing the game, and the CoC experience is transmitted orally. Like D&D and for historical reasons it's one of the most house ruled games in existence.       BTW, strictly out of curiosity because I've seen discussions of the resistance chart cause flame-fests online, is everyone clear on the basic idea of how that thing works?

      It's really just representing a basic formula that at its core says, hey, there's a 50/50 chance that something happens if two opposing scores are equal. The chance that the event occurs goes up or down depending on the difference between the active quality and the passive opposing quality.

      Specifically if your character is the owner of the acting quality, each point of difference above the value of the passive quality causes you to have a +5% chance. If your quality is orated lower, there's a -5% chance for each point of difference.

      The chart just lets you cross-reference the two numbers, rather than actually doing the math.

      \n Yes, it's a striking difference in thinking, and on a pretty fundamental thing - what a "game" means as a product and an object of critique. Actual play is easier, "game" in the sense of concrete activity: I don't think I've yet to see an Internet subculture that would insist on some completely contrary meaning for that. That generational spaceship game (Metamorphosis Alpha was the name?) is another simply delightful example of how far D&D can be twisted. Really interesting products from a way of thinking that insists on evolutionary thought more than analytical and experimental.

I admit that I'm sort of academically curious about whether he's ever encountered genuine creative agenda clash in roleplaying, but I also recognize that it's nearly entirely futile to armchair-analyze other people's experiences on this level. The best we've ever been able to do is to provide each other with theoretical models that everybody's able to apply to their own experiences, and then they either fit or not.

      I should note that when I try to have players with incompatible agendas build off each other, the results are usually either somewhat lame or can even approach bullying from individual player viewpoints. I've seen others describe similar experiences. There are just so many things that can go wrong. This is not an universal phenomenon, though, as I've sometimes managed to ride such agenda disagreements to keep a game functioning.

      A prominent example of success with Zak's method that occurs to me from when we were playing D&D three-ish years back with the local teenagers: a certain larper with a penchant for dramatic character roleplaying participated in the session with a fantastic character (she wanted to play a half-demon), and approached the entire exercise sort of as a dramatic platform for showing off her character's fictional background. (Obviously not quite on-message for the sort of hardcore challenge-focused OSR D&D I've been running lately, but she's one of those gamers who aren't very focused on picking up instruction on how to play a specific game - she just comes to play.) The way her play interacted with the D&D methodology (very hands-off about character actions, as we know, and realistic about consequences) ended up with her half-demon character positioned first as a sort of native guide to the rest of the party, and then as an antagonist trapmaster of the dungeon. I peg this as a genuine agenda conflict, as this one player was clearly not on-board any sort of challenge; she just na\xefvely expected that I'd keep her character out of trouble, kept establishing her own creative fantasy backstory, and clearly enjoyed outrageous events and showing off how cool her character was. I didn't stop these events even when she betrayed the rest of the party - mostly this was because I was uncertain about whether I should interfere just because of an agenda clash when the events themselves were kosher. As I continued running the game neutrally as a referee, the other players rerouted their own play and integrated the antics of this one player as part of the challenge their characters were facing.

      In hindsight that particular session of D&D was formally functional and the events were interesting in the sense that a lot happened and it had impact in the campaign and did much to generate fiction, but I do remember a certain sense of confusion from the group. I think that on average we felt that the events of the session were interesting and complex, but also the session was not satisfying in the way a well-executed and more purposeful dungeon delve could be. There was palpable social tension in the air during play, as the other players had to deal with the dawning realization that this one player's not on with the plan. (No doubt this was doubly complex for the teenagers playing - she was I think one player's girlfriend at the time, and used to be with another's brother; something like that, don't bite my head off if you're reading and know who I'm talking about.) I think that I incorporated the players' creative disagreement as perfectly as Zak could hope for, though, and I can sort of see that if a group were able to do this all the time then maybe play could be accomplished. As David mentioned, though, I would suspect that the overall sentiment of play would not be as satisfying, as the players wouldn't feel as safe with each other. Nowadays I'm no longer satisfied with merely fictionally eventful play, I also want to be able to look the other players in the eye and acknowledge what they're doing with appreciation.

      I could describe a few times when I've tried to do this stuff and it has not worked as well as above. The most common outcome is just that the session becomes pretty dull. This can go any which way: a narrativist session can get fucked up when a drama-blind player refuses to have his character give in in the face of immense resistance, for example, which can be dull for the other players if we end up spending most of the session dealing with this one crazy character whose player shows no commitment nor caring about his character's fate. (At the end the character always dies off in a meaningless way, accomplishing nothing; the player just makes a new one, ready to do it all over again.) The worst case of my trying to build on agenda conflict ended with a player walking out on the campaign: he did not appreciate it when he set his trust on me as the GM, and I ruthlessly killed off his beloved character because I was totally doing high-stakes gamism at the time.

      Interestingly, I've seen gamers who appreciate a really heavy immersion and a sort of denial of out-of-game issues use the specific technique of building off disagreement a lot: if somebody is tired and off in their game, for example, you don't say so, but rather just pretend that their character is similarly off. You sort of bring all the little problems and all into the game, and try to defuse/ignore them that way. You can recognize this mindset from how the GM will try to treat any dissatisfaction by asking you about what sort of content you'd rather have in the game - he thinks that you can fix players not seeing eye to eye about play with different types of orcs or whatever. I don't think that this is a very functional idea, despite the fact that I used to play this way myself for a decade. The level of play is simply so much better if you can speak directly to your co-players instead of doing this hypercorrect thing where everybody focuses on the fiction directly and never comments on its quality or how they perceive the proceedings of play.

      All in all, I'm willing to believe that there are different experiences, but my own attempts at salvaging play by playing creative agendas off each other have often felt like playing with fire. Even when it succeeds I'm just happy that it didn't blow up in my face. I'm sure that if you know your group well and you're committed to playing together and don't have any strong ideas about what roleplaying is like, then it's easier - the players will adapt better to each other, and perhaps the emotional stakes are not as high.

      \n When Zak says "scientific," I read him as saying "empirical" (i.e., based in some kind of real-world data) and I agree wholeheartedly. Using actual play audio to examine game play more closely is something that has great value for anyone who's interested in the table-level dynamics of play. I think Mel and I are groping toward some kind of critical program, but the act of reflecting on play by paying attention to interesting moments in the game (whether at the level of the fiction or of the social-level interaction) actually changes the experience of the game to a great degree. In my scholarly work, which uses actual play audio as empirical data, I've taken to quoting the Russian literary theorist Mikhail Bakhtin, who hints at the character of that change. Here's an excerpt from a thing I wrote for Jonathan Walton's Magic Missile anthology:\n
      Bakhtin notes that, to the players themselves, \u201cplaying . . . does not presuppose any spectator (situated outside their playing) for whom the whole of the event of a life imaged [i.e., represented] through play would actually be performed\u201d (p. 74). Play imagines rather than images, he says, such that \u201cthe boy who plays a robber chieftain experiences his own [imagined] life . . . from within himself. . . . The relationship of each of the players to the event of life they have decided to enact . . . is no more than the desire to take part in that event, to experience that life as one of its participants\u201d (p. 74). A transformation takes place once an audience is present, however. The spectator is able to appreciate the children\u2019s play as a whole rather than as the individuated experience that they perceive. \u201cIn doing so,\u201d Bakhtin goes on, the spectator \u201calters the event as it is initially given: the event becomes enriched with a new moment, new in principle\u2014an author/beholder, and, as a result, all other moments of the event are transformed as well, inasmuch as they become part of the new whole\u201d (p. 75). This new thing is \u201cno longer the event of playing, but the artistic event of drama in its embryonic form\u201d (p. 75).
      \nThe idea that AP transforms the ephemeral "text" of the game in play into a performance that can then be reflected upon is really interesting to me; I'm working on a paper about it now. What I think (or maybe just hope) is beginning to happen in the indie-games community is an extension of how the Forge used written AP reports to diagnose incoherent play to an "audio AP culture" where interesting moments of play are explored in reflection after the game. So--to tie this back to Cthulhu--Mel and I talk in an upcoming episode about the Trail of Cthulhu scenario I ran at Dreamation, Castle Bravo, which I thought was the best game I ever ran, period, but Mel found himself a little dissatisfied with his personal experience as a player even while enjoying the fiction that was created. Trying to grapple with that disconnect made for an interesting conversation, and I hope it will be up soon.

      \n To connect this back to the OP, Eero was saying that the text of CoC doesn't provide a lot of support for the "monsters jump out at you--deal!" school of adventure design. As I recall, Eero is right; you're told that you're supposed to design the game as an onion, with layers of mystery one on top of the other that you have to peel away in order to learn the deeper truths of the Mythos. The implication is that, by the time you're at the core, you may very well have left a trail of dead or insane investigators behind you, and maybe missed some clues or signs that would guide you to uncovering the optimal defenses against the Mythos. That's fine, but unless you plan on only playing one game ever (which admittedly many folks do in fact plan to do) there's a lot of benefit to being able to read a set of rules that presents procedures designed to create a specific experience without reading your own assumptions about game-running into the rules in a way that makes it impossible for you to create that experience. Briefer: sometimes we could all use a little help; that's what we're paying our game designers for.

      So Trail of Cthulhu, trying to solve a specific kind of problem that sometimes afflicts investigative games, imagines each adventure as a trail of clues that leads ultimately to a big reveal--which you may or may not be equipped to deal with, depending on how well you were able to manage your investigative spends and general skill use (i.e., manage your resources and be clever). The way I've learned to run Trail of Cthulhu is not to offer a breadcrumb trail that you follow but a firehose spray that you try not to drown in. So it's not a railroad; PC choices matter immensely in shaping what happens, and a player's ability to make sense of the welter of information he or she is receiving contributes to the success or failure of the investigation. In this case, while I think the players were just about there in figuring out what was going on and how to "solve" their problems, they ran out of time and the adventure ended in a climactic showdown that drove the PCs more or less satisfyingly around the bend. That was me flexing my GM muscles, of course--call it Force if you must--but the players' choices put them there. Nonetheless, the larger point is that I'm using the "textual support" offered by Trail of Cthulhu to design, structure, and run the adventure, adapting it in light of my own preferences and experiences.

      \nCamp Nerdly 2011, Bill GMing the Big Hoodoo ToC scenario for me (playing Tony Boucher), Don (Robert Heinlein), Seraphina (Phillip K. Dick), and Parke (Ginny Heinlein). Mostly what I did during this game was talk to NPCs in an effort to get info out of them, propose theories to the other PCs about what might be going on, and propose what we ought to do next. I quite like doing all of these things, and the game was overall a good time.

      However, I felt somewhat disoriented about how to play. What should I or could I do that would matter to the group? Here's a breakdown:

      What to do next:
      Don adopted the most forceful character persona, and got the final word on most of the "what should we do?" disagreements. I churned out the most proposals, and the group was happy to follow some of them. Seraphina and Parke each had one or two really good proposals that we went with, but otherwise seemed content to move forward in any direction. "What should we do next?" is a mixed bag in most games, with some players enjoying a drawn-out process and others hating it. In games where it's assumed that this is a weighty and consequential decision, where choosing wisely yields immense rewards, I've seen this turn into a focal point of play, for good ("Yes! Brilliant idea!") and bad ("None of these are good enough, we must keep thinking."). This was not at all the case in our Big Hoodoo game. When I came up with some exciting theory and clever plan of action, the group met it with the same, "Okay, sure," response as when I simply proposed the obvious next step.

      Theories:
      I don't recall a lot of interest, either from Bill or the other players, in carving out scenes for the characters to form theories. Mostly, the characters kept busy doing stuff, and Bill kept up the action by pushing to get to the next thing. Overall, a good technique in a late-night one-shot con session with a time cap; you don't want to lose the energy, slow progress, and not get to the end. However, the result was that my curiosity didn't get much chance to gestate. Doing the whole, "Hey, maybe this mystery is about this, what do y'all think?" phase makes me much more excited to pursue the mystery. Each new development can help confirm or refute theories. Without the chance to process each discovery, and fit it into the puzzle, the impression is more of trivia. "Okay, the dead guy liked rockets. Moving on..." I managed to work some theorizing into the flow of NPC interactions, and I think Seraphina enjoyed some of my idea-spouting, and Don and Parke might have occasionally raised an eyebrow in interest, but that was about it.

      Efforts and problem-solving:
      As opposed to theorizing and planning, the group seemed more intent on navigating the immediate situation in front of us. This generally meant talking to some NPCs who had help to give but reservations to overcome. I tried to roleplay in a sympathetic manner to elicit NPCs to confide, while everyone else simultaneously did whatever they thought would get the NPC to spill the beans. Whenever someone delivered a good bit of dialogue, and Bill coughed up the clues, that was a good moment that I felt everyone cared about. Nothing wrong with that. However, with 4 players all attempting one task, the odds of return on investment were pretty low. I think the less quick and assertive players in the group didn't get a whole lot of these types of achievements.

      "How to talk to this NPC" wasn't the only type of success-relevant decision. There was an occasional roll to know something or shoot a gun or whatnot. I don't remember anyone at the table looking over their character sheets for high scores and trying to steer the fiction into using those, though. They were a relative side note.

      As far as I could tell, the formula for character success was "chat with NPC, then choose where to go and what to do next", with the group's focus on the "chat with NPC" part.

      Portrayal:
      I don't think the players in our group were history buffs. I don't know anything about Boucher or Heinlein or Dick's lives. The character sheets gave us some basic areas of interest, and we all worked these into the roleplay reasonably well, but definitely as side notes. I would have happily followed the group into a "What does this mean to my guy?" style of play, but there was absolutely nothing going on at the table in that direction. Like most Lovecraft stories and Cthulhu scenarios, the majority of the adventure didn't have anything sanity-taxing in it. The emotions evoked were those present in any suspense story: "There's danger out there and maybe we should be nervous and hurry." The scenario gave us reasons to worry, and we acted on those like responsible problem-solvers: we took precautions, we didn't waste much time, and we tried to get to the bottom of things before it was too late. This may, in fact, account for the disinclination to spend too long on theories or plans.

      My point here is that "there's danger out there, let's be nervous and hurry" is not the most captivating thing to roleplay, or to see roleplayed. Portrayal of character emotions never became "the thing to do that matters".

      It seemed like this could have changed when some weird stuff happened to Dick, and Seraphina did a nice job with it, engaging the other characters to show concern. But that turned out to be an exception; it was quickly "back to the mission".

      Portrayal simply didn't seem to be a group priority; Bill handed me a sweet demonic monologue, which I belted out with gusto, and no one seemed particularly psyched.

      The GM:
      I would characterize Bill as a rather "hands on" GM. He doesn't micromanage people's character play, but he frames and cuts scenes and interactions where he feels they should be framed and cut, and directs the players' attention where he feels it belongs. Whether this was relevant for this group, I don't know, but I've seen the same approach lead to player passivity and "looking to the GM to model how to play" in other games. I did not feel that Bill modeled a specific way to play in this instance, meeting most types of player contribution with equal degrees of validation.

      Overall:
      The plot was excellent, the discoveries were cool, invoking a ghost made for a fantastic scene, and stopping the badguys at the end was satisfying even if I was fuzzy on how we earned it (was it luck? my brilliance? Parke's? Bill being nice? who knows!). I enjoyed being there and seeing what happened. But I never got a sense of how to contribute in a way that anyone else would value. Theories? No. Plans? Not really. Dialogue with NPCs? Yes, but there's only so much to go around. Roleplaying character stress? Probably not. Roleplaying character madness? Not applicable.

      Please don't take this long post as an assertion on my part that any specific thing should have happened. I raise it mainly for Bill's consideration as a GM and scenario designer. If he's 100% fine with what I've described and sees no need for improvement, cool.

      I also could be misremembering stuff that happened 10 months ago. The only thing I remember for sure is that I was surprised that I didn't get more appreciation for the stuff I did in play. I dunno, maybe my standards are too high, or maybe I just wanted the hot chick at the table to tell me I was awesome or something.

      \n The best games of Call of Cthulhu (and also Trail), in my long experience of the game, happen when players stop worrying about the system and really connect with what's going on in the story, SIS or whatever you like to call it. This is often in an immersive way but can also be in a story way (I don't think these things are exclusive). There is always a build up of tension which ultimately is released in an heroic success or failure. The outcome is not important but the management of the process is. Some of this is about managing the mystery and revealing it slowly, some of it is that Lovecraftian thing of finding out that you are not only fucked, but that you always, and inescapably, were. How you react to this in the game, as a character, is very important for its success. Railroad is not a useful term for describing this because to a certain extent, that was the whole point of playing the game in the first place. When the tentacly god beats down on you, when you find that your skin is made of alien DNA, that the world will end when the stars are right, when dreams are more sane than reality, how do you react?

      \n I've been enjoying playing Call of Cthulhu since about 1989. Whether this is indicative of anything objective.... That, I don't know.
      I've had good Keepers, and I don't know how much they tweaked or streamlined the rules.
      I've played at Origins, where the Call of Cthulhu fans, of whom I am definitely one, self select. We play together year after year, we know each other, we trust each other. This doesn't mean there are no subpar games, but my odds are good.

      I've run a lot of Call of Cthulhu adventures, but I've only run Call of Cthulhu itself twice. The rest of the time, I adapted the content of a solid published scenario to the setting and system I was using at the time. I've been inspired by Stealing Cthulhu to see if I can do this with Masks of Nyarlathotep for Kerberos Club, Fate Edition.

      I ran two published campaigns for my home group. Both went well, although I think I did a much better job on Tatters of the King (which I ran second) than on Beyond the Mountains of Madness. I did some tweaking in both cases, so while it's Call of Cthulhu, there were a couple of house rules, and where the campaigns had spots that didn't quite work (too much railroading, no obvious connection, whatever), I fixed it. What I did worked fine -- for my home group, people I know and trust, people who know and trust me.

      I love that the rules haven't changed and that I'm buying useful content. I would say that I've bathed in the Kool-Aid.

      I also have most of the Trail of Cthulhu products. The system doesn't quite work for me, but the content is golden.
