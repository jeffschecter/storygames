    \n I am definitely thinking about how to include a Monk in DW. I'd start just like you are, Orly, and think about "what does a monk do in D&D? What are they for?" Disarms, group attacks, trips - lots of fun fictional stuff.

    If we look at an AD&D Monk we see that he's armoured like a wizard (not at all) fights like a bit of a hybrid between the thief and the fighter (which is to say, small weapons, low damage) has kind of a weird hit-point allotment (2d4 at first level). Also, a handful of thief skills.

    I'd probably be disappointed by this, but us it as a springboard to look at the fictional "Monk" and what makes him cool. Something like that to reflect his inner peace with Death and so doesn't have it hanging over him the way other people do.

    Just spitballing random ideas, though. I love Monks - they're probably my favourite class to play after Paladins and Clerics.

    \n To me, the signature fact about the monk is that he is dangerous unarmed and unarmored. Most men, when they have no sword or shield, you can stop imagining them murdering you and enjoy a drink together. But there is nothing you can take away from a monk to make him anything but an existential threat. To be able to deal with people in a feudal society with no state monopoly on force, he needs to cultivate an aura of peace that overcomes the fact that he is always prepared to kill anyone he can reach. I'd want to see mechanics that stress how important it is to cultivate and demonstrate a peaceful heart, or perhaps instead mechanics that tend to force monks into hermitage.

    I love Gabor's (way's) idea that a monk might gain hold by not having killed for a day, a week, a month, a year, ever. It reminds me of the ascetics of Indian legends, practicing their rigorous disciplines for ages, gaining godlike power that they will lose the moment they unleash it.

    As the Monk walks through town, a pure and innocent smile on his face, birds fly behind him, and children come to him for a blessing and leave delighted. Everyone knows that his is the path of peace - the proof is practically overflowing from him. But equally, everyone knows that if he were to witness a terrible injustice, his wrath would in turn be more terrible than magefire, and the sun would shine less brightly on him on his next morning walk, the birds would not follow, and the children would make way.

    \n Monk unpacks into "kick-ass mysterious martial-arts-wielder" pretty universally, within the D&D space. Which is the fictional space we're trying to recreate, here. So, from a narrative / thematic / feel perspective, Monk means "D&D class Monk"

    That said, we're not replicating mechanics, so having some Monks do all kinds of other monk-ish stuff besides punching your face in is totally a cool direction to go. They're generally pretty wise, they understand riddles and philosophy, can engage in dialogue, write scrolls, understand the world in a spiritually-internalized way (I quite like this about Monks, they're spiritually-focused without being Deistic in the way a Cleric is) and do some neat things the other characters can't.

    They're a gaming archetype that, sure, may have originated as an homage to 70s kung-fu movies or whatever, still stands as relevant. I'd LOVE to see something that mechanically enforces that they come from "somewhere else". Since Monks are all humans, in my mind, maybe that's their race move. "Mountain Monastery" or "Seaside School" or something like that. Like the Paladin, too, I'd see their Alignment moves as limited - Lawful Neutral, Lawful Evil and Lawful Good.

      Anyway, let's get back to reality for a minute:
      The 1st edition monk was made in the era of Shaw Brothers kung fu movies. It was the Chop-Sockey kung fu shaolin-esque monk. Abilities like super powerful fist attacks that increase with speed and power as your level increases, to "falling indefinitely if you're near a wall", to poison immunity... This is all shit straight outta Five Deadly Venoms, folks. These aren't whirling dervishes, mead-brewing Swiss Christians, self-torturing Indian yogis, dutiful Talmud-debating jewish scholars, Sri Lankan pacifist Buddhist monasts... They're all kung fu using Shaolin-equse stereotypes. Oriental Adventures, all it did (and it was one of my favorite books at the time, too!) was offer you alternate martial arts paths to use instead of the default monk martial art: The abilities were all the same.
      (Ah, OE also did of course add all the problematic "China and Japan and SE Asia are all pretty much the same blobby thing! Go!)

      Let's not bullshit ourselves: If you said you wanted to play a Monk in 1982, then described yourself as Friar Tuck, then you'd have an ass-kicking Shaolin kung-fooey kickpuncher named "Friar Tuck". At 20th level you couldn't brew mead, but you could fall off a cliff and not take damage due to your kung fu, and punch demons like Kenshiro from Fist of the North Star. They weren't thinking "Hey, what about early Christian era friars! They're monks, too!" They were thinking: "Did you see 36th Chamber the other day on Sat AM Kung Fu theater on the USA channel? I want to be THAT."

Body-defying yogis, or keepers of a mysterious ancient power (which would work with everything from Talmud scholars to Zoroastrian astrologers to Skyrim "Greybeards"), with their own special laws that must be obeyed (or broken for story effect!).

      I think there's room for both, without combining them into one!

      EDIT: Or alternately, combine them into one! But do so in a way where the mysterious ancient power learned from the monastery does not by necessity have to be Shaolin-style kung fu. Leave it as an open damage attack, so that yogis could attack with aphorisms until you were defeated and become their disciple. Or like the Breath attacks of the Greybeards from Skyrim. And so on.
