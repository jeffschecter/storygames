\n So now that it's up for preorder, I assume some people have already eagerly devoured the PDF and done something with it.

What have you done????

My group weaved a tale of counterfeit drugs, pregnancy, and accidental murder. A seedy night shift doctor accidentally kills two people. A drug dealer dodges some good ole southern justice. And the reasonably straight daytime doctor and his cousin remind themselves that they aren't innocent either.

I was the seedy night shift doctor, and it turned out pretty bad for me. After poisoning the sheriff to cover for my secret counterfeit drug operation, I accidentally shot the day doctor's cousin while trying to hide the body. During the aftermath, I was trying to throw everything I had in a bag so I could flee and ended up accidentally poisoning myself and dying.

I don't remember many of the other details, but the best part was that the final aftermath point was the day shift doctor's cousin's corpse rotting under a railroad bridge, her favorite techno tune playing on a loop on her MP3 player. I'm pretty sure nobody got higher than a 3 or 4 for their aftermath totals.

All in all, the game works really, really well.

    \n There's a page in the book on authoring playsets. There are stories built into each one - the Boomtown set has a whole deep thread about Chinese immigrants, and I think part of the reason the sets work is that by absorbing the various choices during the setup, you get a feel for the time and place, even though you are only choosing a fraction of the elements as critical to the fiasco. So if you play Boomtown, you're reading over all this stuff about opium and the laundry and the brothel so forth, even if you don't choose those elements they are in the back of your mind as you play, and they may come out. If you need details, they are provided.

    Unlike IAWA, the playsets are just lists of stuff that means very little until combined with relationships. Some are wildly colorful and some are very mundane, and a balance of the two is nice for pairing with mundane or colorful relationships. It's a way to adjust the gonzometer and make a statement about the sort of tone you want. So a playset should have a good balance of crazy evocative stuff and really prosaic stuff. One of my favorite Locations is "medical records coding office" - so tedious, so boring, so invisible.

    \n Just had my first Fiasco session with two other friends.
    \n
    \nUnfortunately we ran out of time just after arriving at the tilt, but much fun was had up until that point.
    \n
    \nWe decided to play on Graham's Gangster London playset. There was Big Ron, the crime boss looking for revenge on the Crimewatch TV presenter who put him away by running the story. Then his underling with some useful contacts for their little scheme, and his former Alcoholic's Anonymous buddy Alex who happened to be the very put-upon production manager working on Crimewatch at the BBC who hates the presenter with a passion for overworking him and treating him like meat. By sheer coincidence, before the AA he did Ron a few favours and an uneasy friendship developed with... certain tensions (but they're not shagging, or anything... no...).
    \n
    \nThe plan was clear. Get some high grade weed, this time not for selling, but for the biggest poetic stitch-up ever devised. Dump the goods in TV-guy's Spanish villa, then arrange it so that it looks like the hapless sod is responsible for smuggling the goods.
    \n
    \nBig Ron and his underling manage to get poor Alex to both agree to help and give up useful information about the villa through a combination of drugs and drink that undoes most of the hard work that AA had achieved with him over the last few years, but the scheme was well under way. At the midway point, an ill advised attempt at bribery lands the criminal pair in a holding cell on a ferry, but that was where we had to stop.
    \n
    \nJust before we did, however, we rolled up the tilt, the most wonderful potentially disastrous element being 'Failure: Something precious is on fire.'
