\n Here's something that happens occasionally in online RPG discussions that makes me uncomfortable.

1. Someone buys, reads, and plays a game.
2. They experience some problems and post about it.
3. People say, "you're playing it wrong."

Everyone goes back and forth and usually discover:

1. The original person didn't read a rule; or
2. The rule to play correctly wasn't in the text; or
3. The rule was in the text but explained in an unclear, inaccessible, hard to find, easy to skip over, or hard to remember manner.

I find #3 to be the most common but that's highly anecdotal!

But the original person bringing up a potential problem is treated in a way that assumes the fault is automatically with them.

To be fair, the flipside is also true. If someone starts a conversation with, "I played this game and it's totally broken", that's also a problem (depending on your goals for being part of a community).

In my opinion, if you want to encourage open discussion free of bullying, that people assume little and try their best to reach mutual understanding before making harsh criticisms or absolute statements. Sometimes I make this mistake but I try my best to be aware of it and do better when I can.

In my threads asking people if they felt silenced because of gender or race, many of the responses indicated that they did feel silenced, but for some it wasn't because of their gender or race (although that was also a significant issue, especially gender) but because of threads where a person says they experienced a potential problem with a game and then are treated as if they are the problem.

    Here's really what i'm trying to get at:

    Roleplaying games and story games are basically Play Pretend at their core. They're a kind of play pretend with focus ( the games' themes, even in the most basic and broad sense). There are play traditions.

    Those things are the core of these activities, but rarely do those things get described well first and foremost. even with indie games of the type common around these parts, there is a real tendency to jump forward into describing the mechanics and structures ( and a big emphasis on those too) before ever hitting on the more basic stuff. And that more basic stuff is just really important. Much more important than the stuff we usually concentrate on.

    Damn this is hard to explain. I just think we should try more often to invert the emphasis between those two things, and everything would be a bit clearer.

    As one example of what might be something to make clearer in SGs/RPGs generally?

    One of the goals of play is to keep play continuing and to keep everyone involved as much as possible until some end state is reached. Or perhaps never stop, in the case of the very traditional on-going, open-ended campaign. Another goal is to encourage a core play group to return and play again, repeatedly ( again, moreso in traditional games with open-ended campaigns). Stuff like that gets implied and kinda discussed, but isn't put at the forefront of emphasis in text.

    \n I've been taking to making flow charts to describe rules systems around particular types of actions. For Lacuna, the flow-chart for the entire resolution system fits in about half a page. (This is great for convincing new players that the game is accessible to them, by the way.) The flow chart serves as a visual rule-book, with references to where the rules appear in the actual book.

    The idea for doing this originally came from running a game of Mage a couple summers ago in which the players were struggling with the magic rules. Looking around, I found a flow chart describing the casting sequence. If you click on that, you will find the underlying problem: it's simply way too complicated.

    I later made a similar flow chart for casting in Ars Magica, which was quite a bit simpler, filling a page.

    People tend to pick up information in different ways; flow charts make the sequence of events quite clear, and are an excellent tool while first learning the system. I think it would be awesome if role-playing books included flowcharts on the rules systems.

    \n Well...there does seem to be a big gap between The Rules As Written and the game's best practices, typically accumulated and propagated amongst an insular community of players.

    We want the RAW to be clear but not overexplain things, so there's room to breathe. I think Czege talks about this in his blogs/posts somewhere -- explain enough for clarity but not so much as to stifle play.

    So best practices pop into place eventually. In-the-know guys sort of...take ownership of those practices, even if they're really pretty organic and ultimately collaborative. Those practices are only available if you're in a forum, following threads with talmudic intensity, not pissing off the notables who are putting them out there.

    Someone new buys the book and has only the RAW to work from. The RAW almost certainly doesn't include a discussion of best practices until/unless there's a "player's guide" or "Adventure Burner" type product available. There is simply no way to quickly catch up to the best practices thinking of the community at large. They post a problem with their game, the RAW simply does not address the problem, and the big community -- or its notables -- step in.

    Maybe they're genuinely helpful (Graham sharing his favorite Lacuna tricks; anyone who had clever ideas on how to use the Psychic Maelstrom). Maybe they're fed up with answering the same questions every week (me, explaining for the millionth time why fictional events don't impact winning/losing in Burning Empires). Maybe they honestly don't know how to answer the question because, shocker, it's the first time someone's brought it up (Harper, holding out for eight pages because I found an unsolveable inconsistency in 3:16). But there's all this territorialism and chest thumping that comes with "the new player" interfacing with "the community" for the first time, and the new player doesn't mesh with the community. And how could they? There's no shared history, no shared knowledge.

    Can you write your game's text better? Probably. Honestly I think many/most indie-sized productions simply don't have the play time under their belts prior to publication to really know how they work out in the wild. AW was massively tested yet big swaths of the game continue to elude new players. BW, perhaps the most heavily tested game ever independently published, still needed the Adventure Burner and, soon, the Gold edition to nail it all down more usefully. I think it's just going to happen that way.

    * * * * *

    Second thought on this: Few things torque me off more than the "talk about your problems" bit JD mentioned. Oh man. I want to throw rocks through your windows when this shit starts.

    Not useful (example drawn from actual BW discussion):

    Newb: "It seems like there's really no upper cap on what you can specify your intent to be in a versus test. Why wouldn't I just ask to win the war with one roll?"

    Insider: "Does that seem fun to you?"

    Newb: "Not at all! That's why I'm asking."

    Insider: "Then why would you try? Don't you want to have fun?"

    Oh for fuck's sake are you kidding me? There is so much judgment and bullshit wrapped up in this: Why are you so preoccupied with winning? Why aren't you sensitive to your responsibilities to facilitate fun at the table?

    Useful:

    Newb" "It seems like there's really no upper cap on what you can specify your intent to be in a versus test. Why wouldn't I just ask to win the war with one roll?"

    Insider: "Because the GM can specify that an intent is inappropriate. Yeah I know it says "say yes or roll the dice" but in practice it isn't that cut and dried. GM still has a lot of discretionary power."

    Newb: "But couldn't the GM just deny any intent he didn't feel like allowing?"

    Insider: "Procedurally, yeah, but I'd hope everyone's on the same page as to why they're playing the game. I mean if you feel like you're getting reasonable intents shut down maybe you should talk to the GM about it."

    And then at least you've moved the conversation to talking between two people who are allegedly at least friendly acquaintances. No RTFM slams (didn't you read "task and intent on page X?"), no "you wouldn't have this problem if you didn't have all this baggage" nonsense.
