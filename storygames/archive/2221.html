The RPG Journal of Negative Results

Andy
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
The Background:

So one of my wife's close buddies is a bigwig in the biology/botany field (even got an article published in Science), and told me about how publication tends to work in evolutionary biology. Namely, you only publish in the two bigs (Nature and Science), not to mention other mags, until you perform experiments and analyses with provable results.

Unfortunately, a lot of time and money is put into experiments that end up failing (as in, everyone expected it to work, but it didn't, often because of unexplainable phenomena and new discoveries). However, since journals tend to not accept failed experiments, these experiments didn't get published, causing folks in the industry to repeat these mistakes, or worse not point out interesting unexplainable phenomena.

Now, though, there is a journal for these failed experiments, the "Journal of Negative Results" (another is http://www.jasnh.com/ ). It basically takes experiments that were set up to succeed and failed, or experiments that were meant to prove one thing but ended up proving something else (sometimes its opposite). In any case, it's a cool idea, a place to document and record failed experiments.

====

Having said that, what are your failed experiments, pieces that you gave up and most likely won't return to? (or perhaps early fragments of games that you totally changed since its first incarnation)? What did you learn from your endeavor?

-Andy

    So, this all started because I was going through some piles and piles of paperwork and printouts that accumulated over the past few years: Printouts, tax forms, work documents, etc when I stumbled upon the later incarnation of the game that I gave up on, that I ran for my gaming group probably back in, I dunno, 2002? 2003?

    Thanks to my office's "Scan then Send in PDF format to email" Xerox printer, I can share this Grand Mistake with all y'all:

    The PDF is over here. Not rules, just the character sheets.

    It was called "Kyuseisha", a post-apoc kung fu game, and it started off as a Sorcerer modification (and may eventually return to being so again). I used this system to run about 4 sessions, and then never really developed it further.

    The real surprising thing is, looking at the sheets, I seriously can't remember for the life of my how the damned thing worked. Not even the basic roll mechanic - I seem to recall it was dice-pool based, but I can't remember more than that.

    Here were some interesting points:

    1) A continuation of the theme brought up a while ago by Tony, "What is my character like today?" If you flip to the back of the docs, you can see these small sheets called "Modes". This was basically my stab at that a few years back. You coul dhave a number of modes up to your "Acuity" score, and what they did was basically formed templates that you lay over your character, to temporarily have them max out certain abilities and reduce others.

    For example, "The Professor" was a scientist and in charge of the Vault's underground water and hydroponic farm resources. One of his modes was "Sherlock", which boosted all of his notice and intelligence-based rolls for a period of time. The Professor also carried a makeshift halberd, and when that weapon came out he would shift over to Guan Yu mode, and things would go down like Dynasty Warriors (the PS2 game where you control a warring states era chinese general, hacking through armies of mooks). And when nothing else was going on, he just used the basic character sheet to determine his skill in other areas.

    What I learned from the above game:

    1) Mix in all the awesome shit you want (I cobbled together about 6 ideas that were floating around from indie and Japanese games, with no real sense of the goals). But if you don't have a clear idea of what the game is supposed to be about, then you'll have a hard time working on it.

    2) One thing I remember well is that there were psychic powers and the like. I started playtesting before writing down more than 3 or 4 (there were going to be around 20 or so), hoping that the players would just 'wing it' and come up with stuff on the fly. In a perfect world, that may have worked, but in the real world what I should have done was spent the extra few hours before the first session tossing at least the bare bones of all the powers into a document, and if they didn't work revise them from there. In other words, don't start playtesting if only half your system is anywhere near ready to go. Hammer out at least the framework of the whole thing, and revise from there.

    Anyone else?

    -Andy

    Oh god yes. So many failures.

    Valence, the sci-fi game I was working on before Sufficiently Advanced, is not a failed experiment, just an unhappily interrupted one. Stupid hard drive crash.

    Sapiens, the game of playing Neanderthals during the rise of Cro-Magnons, will never come back in its current format. It needs to be broken down and recreated, at least from a mechanics point of view. I learned from that one that you can't try making a traditional RPG where the environment is a major enemy. The lack of personality and reactivity makes it not worth spending time on.

    Starlight, a D&D world where the sun was taken away and the Drow invaded and won, is gone and not coming back. Midnight did it better, and I don't have time to care about writing setting info for yet another game.

    Olympus, where you played the children of the Greek gods, will likely never appear again because a: Scion will do it better, and b: Legends Walk already did it better. It was a good proto-game, though, and had a lot of good feel from just the attributes, the system, and Hubris levels.

    I briefly considered writing a game based on the Heresy card game (so...pretty...), but never did much with it. Lots of neat stuff, but just didn't grab my interest enough. I still have some cards from it, so maybe some day.

    I have many short, failed experiments that I don't remember well, which tells you that anything that doesn't captivate my interest is going nowhere, and I shouldn't bother trying to write it.

    My attempt to have a semi-profitable RPG design business is an ongoing, hilariously, pathetically failed experiment, and I've learned that it doesn't matter how well you can write games if you can't market them worth shit.

    January 2007
    Maw of Hell, later renamed something else.

    It was a card-based RPG, set in a world like WH40K's Catachan. For those not familiar, a thick jungle world which was teeming with deadly animals and constantly going thruogh this Vietnam-like war.

    The players all played squads of soldiers. The cool thing was this "helmet-cam" narration style, where you narrated through the eyes and mind and mouth of a single squad member in each deployment. So you could switch narrators between missions, whether or not you switch characters in your squad.

    I thought it was cool.

    The production cost for colour cards was phenomenal and mind-blowing, and I couldn't do it for that reason. I also realized that I didn't want to play a tactical gamist RPG. I also learned more about GNS, and realized that the narrativist and gamist goals I had would conflict really heavily.

    The cool thing was the factions: The Galactic Empire (name changed for comprehension) had broken apart. Not by regions, but by departments. The army/navy went rogue, the prison system (which was far-stretching and packed) turned into this giant penal-legion army of its own type, the enslaved farmers formed a Coalition of the Lower Classes...

    Because the armies weren't fighting over physical borders (they all inhabited the same physical space), they realized they had to fight their war elsewhere, otherwise they might exterminate themselves. Fighting quickly shifted to this basically uninhabited jungle planet.

    It was a cool idea, even if it didn't 100% make sense.

    The Morningstar Principle.

    I've got a couple. Agonistes was my first attempt at an RPG, and billed itself as "A free, diceless, character-centred, generic and universal roleplaying system". It had no randomizers at all and hence was heavily duct-taped together with GM fiat. I think the bolt-on magic system is probably salvagable.

    Rule of Three, on the other hand, has dice, but each die roll is against a number made up of three elements rated either 1 or 2. These can be skills, equipment, training, "drivers", relationships, background, attributes, insights or knowledge, as interrelated on a diagram. It may have seeds of goodness that I'll reuse in the future; again, the bolt-ons (a situation generator and some advice on plot shaping) are most likely to be recycled.

    One thing I learned from these was that ignoring the state of the art and building something completely innovative isn't necessarily the best way to go, especially for a beginner. I think Errantry, which is built on Clinton's Solar System with relatively minor but important modifications, is much more likely to work than anything else I've done.
