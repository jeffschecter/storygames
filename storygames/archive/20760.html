\n I'm determined to have a 'thing' in my game (be it attribute, skill, whatever) that every character has and that reflects the character's social status, standing, class and or fame. Using that 'thing' should palpably impact the course of the game like, say, using 'swordsmanship' as a 'thing' could impact the game if swords are drawn.

Can anyone suggest a game that uses such a mechanic? I've come across Traveller, and some mention of Victoriana . But I haven't been able to get a sense of what use is made of that concept in those games.

Is it as simple as affecting social interactions? What about also relying upon it to generate resources such as money and retainers or contacts? Surely there's a game out there that uses the element of social standing as a thing that can effect the course of play... Help?

    \n Our game GOLDEN HEROES (1984) was lauded for its "campaign ratings' system which seems to do what you want. Superhero game - three ratings - Public Status, Detective Points, Personal Status. Each rated on 5 areas at the end of each game. Eg. If you cracked jokes you got a high "expression" score which helped your public status total.

    It works really well. Iron Man 3, for example, is all about Tony Stark taking a kicking in his Personal Status score.

    You'd need to rummage about on eBay for a copy though.

    I refined it in "Squadron UK" converting it from "ratings" to "derived characteristics". Charisma, Perception, Luck. Behave like a hero, your Charisma goes up. Look for clues, your perception goes up etc.

    The basic rules PDF which contains the full system you want is only $2 on RPGNOW

    http://www.rpgnow.com/product/107240/Squadron-UK-Basic?term=Squadron+uk&test_epoch=0

    (I've just noticed I'm only charging $4 for the printed book! I've got to put that price up.)

    In my "Code" books (The Comics Code, The Code of the Spacelanes, The Code of Steam and Steel) pubic perception and personal morality are simply non-physical hit points. On minus "Status" you're a wanted criminal. On minus "morality" you're an actual criminal - the GM takes your sheet and the character becomes an NPC villain.) They're on RPGNOW as well but they're not as close a fit to what you want as the GH/SqUK systems so I won't include links.

    Alright, let's start from the top: What's your game about?
    \nYes, fair question.

    In brief, the game is about planetary romance. The main (alien) culture is nostalgic, honouring custom and tradition. Adventures deal with battle, privation, captivity and escape, but also moral debate and courtship. It is anti-modernity, in the sense of being a counter to the regulated, commercial, bureaucratic culture of modern Earth.

    In this alien culture, honour, renown, black-and-white morality are qualities to be striven for and cultivated. And if you have 'em, then you ought to be able to use 'em in the game mechanically to effect things. "I am honourable and noble!" would ideally be something that could improve your character's odds at achieving success in contests.

    The solution I have so far is a simple point system, along the lines of for acts of nobility, honour, or renown, your PC gets points, which he can then spend to increase his dice pool, reduce damage, improve a roll, etc. Maybe that's enough? Not sure. Which is why I was hoping to look at other systems that use that theme as a mechanic...

    \n So, based in part on suggestions above, I'm musing over something like this...

    A point system, in which you gain points for doing certain noble acts (i.e. acts that adhere to the game world's code of honour). You can then burn these points during play for appropriate advantages, such as increasing a roll, or catching a break on a bad roll, or calling upon a resource (e.g. money or manpower), as a reflection of your renown.

    But you also keep a record of the number of total points you've accumulated over time (kind of like XP in OSR). This then can be used maybe in a way like @Jangler and @Rickard suggest: it's a measure of your notoriety that can be used somehow to gauge NPC reactions to you.

    Nothing new under the sun, but the aim isn't to revolutionize anything. Just to create something that promotes players cultivating this sense of nobility amongst their PCs...

    Sound do-able?

But it would be excusable to yield to a Man of Honour who was your sworn enemy.

    \n Earning points for noble deeds which you can then spend to deal or avoid damage sounds purely metagame to me. Possibly quite fun on a player/game level, but I don't see any hint of fictional fame/status there.

    ICE's mention of BW Circles sounds closer, to me. Circles is a stat you roll to see if you can know/find the sort of person you need right now. It's not clear to me exactly what it means in the fiction, but "some sort of connectedness" seems obvious enough. I imagine you could work Renown the same way -- increase it through noble deeds, and then roll it to get the sort of useful things done that a Man Of Honour can get done by way of that rep. Maybe it's finding useful contacts or allies like in Burning Wheel, or maybe it's rallying a force for some venture, or maybe it's getting charity when you're broke or starving, or whatever works with your setting culture.

    I personally wouldn't muddy Renown and the process of earning it by incorporating spendable points, but that may just be my taste.

    If you rethink your "low crunch" aim, lemme know, because I have some ideas about tracking how a Noble action may or may not turn into Renown across various levels of remove...

    I'm determined to have a 'thing' in my game (be it attribute, skill, whatever) that every character has and that reflects the character's social status, standing, class and or fame.
    \nI really don't see how 'earn a point when you do a Correct Thing (according to our cultural list of Correct Things)' gets you very close to anything like social status, standing, class, and or fame. That's just so far from how social status and class work, particularly in a culture with an actual aristocracy.

    Class systems tend to reinforce themselves, they don't push 'correct actors' upwards through the ranks -- a 'noble action' for a peasant does not mean acting like nobility, it generally means being deferential to nobility. Someone who is already an aristocrat can certainly guarantee their position -- or adjust their position relative to other aristocrats, presumably... so I guess that system might be ok if the PCs are all of the same social class, and you are just measuring their progress against each other?

    Anyways, I dunno, I mostly re-quoted the OP as a gentle reminder of the original goals, which seem to aim to model something more complex than an earn-and-spend system for alignment-correct action.

    I still recommend reading up on BW's Circles, which seem well-suited to modelling this sort of thing across a wider variety of PCs/backgrounds/activities.

    \n A list of titles, honors, and markers of formal social status (permitted dress, entourage [bodyguards?], land, business interests, ships, commodities, etc.) bestowed on the characters by a person (or institution, or organization) of great status or influence?

    In other words, the wholly in-fiction option might be a good one. Utilize the "fruitful void"--leave prominent room for a list of these things on the character sheet, but don't directly mechanize them.

    (If players need encouragement, instead mechanize how titles can be leveraged to achieve goals, how businesses provide income, how land yields free resources, etc. Or, for a lighter approach, just say that having a relevant thing on the list provides automatic success.     (I'm not trying to be unhelpful--I know you're looking for a rules lite approach, and this may not suit your goals! I just wanted to leave it here for future consideration.)

    \n My basic thought is that not all Noble deeds proceed equally to become known.

    How many people are there on this world? Do they all know each other? Are they all very well informed about far-away events that don't impact them personally? Are they even very well informed about some small act of heroism a mile down the road?

    Does a Noble deed have to be witnessed by traveling bards and messengers, or influential people who employ them, to count as Noble? Or does risking your life to pull someone out of a quicksand pit, with no witnesses around, still qualify?

    If you want to purpose-build a setting and a scenario m.o. where everything anyone ever does is witnessed and recorded and disseminated, or if you want to work "newsworthy" into the definition of "noble", then you probably need worry no further, and the Noble Deed Equals Renown system will make in-fiction sense just fine. That actually sounds pretty interesting to me; I'd play that!

    If you're not building your world and scenarios and "noble deeds" definitions that way, though, I (as a player) might have some trouble making fictional sense of earning universal acclaim from private moments of virtue. If Renown is a big deal in this culture, then I'd want to explore that, including all the nuances of how Renown is born and grows and changes.

    \n So, here's how it works in Delve:

    The player characters start as nobodies. The first time they do something noteworthy, someone will ask, "Who are you?" They'll then try to come up with a catchy answer, something memorable, like a superhero team name, or a reference to where they come from, or their mission, or just something alliterative.

    Their main vocation is saving villages from trouble, and the first few times they do that, they get three types of recognition:
    - gratitude of villagers
    - gratitude and/or respect of landowner who controls village
    - respect of imperial govt. who told the characters there was trouble in the first place

    As the characters do this a few times, they start finding ways to milk this for further renown. They start looking for:
    - bards, storytellers, and other travelers to spread word of their deeds
    - villagers who are particularly taken with them, so they can deepen that connection, either by being friendly, or by showing off
    - recommendations or introductions to other landowners
    - authentication or vouching for their deeds by authorities
    - landmarks to erect, commemorating their deeds
    - disciples
    - audiences for their own storytelling
    - anyone influential they've particularly helped
    - romantic opportunities

    Eventually, they wind up with a lot of potential forms of renown to take into each new scenario. They may have:
    - letters signed by govt. officials
    - cool artifacts acquired during their adventures
    - practiced stories
    - a catchy name that's been circulated a bit and attached to stories by bards and the like
    - a temporary companion witness

    This tends to get any new people on board pretty quickly with the basic idea that the characters are capable and trustworthy and good news. Once you're introducing yourselves as "The Shadow Hunters, purifiers of Harkethal, slayers of Minecoil, wardens of Hesengard Keep" you tend to get a pretty good reaction, especially because the last of those is actually famous.

    When I think through a new NPC or group's reaction to the player characters, this is the kind of stuff I consider in their favor.

    Against them, I consider:
    - distance from where they did their deeds and made their name
    - remoteness from hubs of travel and circulation of tales
    - remoteness from the domains of vouching authorities
    - proximity to competitors -- maybe this locale has its own famed heroes!
    - customs, beliefs, or superstitions which clash with the characters' way of doing things
    - regional enmities -- maybe this baron hates the baron whose land you just saved!
    - corruption -- these NPCs might be evil and thus hate do-gooders!
    - xenophobia, terror, or anything that casts the characters or their deeds in a threatening light (they are pretty dangerous)

    That looks like a lot of factors, but it's really just "levels of remove" and a bunch of special cases.

    Anyway, If I were going to systematize this, I think I'd probably log deeds, and then next to each deed I'd put a rating for how well-shared it is, updating that rating after each time the players do something to share it (possibly after a die roll to see if it worked).

    Then, when the characters traveled to new places and met new NPCs, I'd make a roll for their threshold of awareness, using their distance (geographical, cultural, informational) as a penalty. A result of "5" means these people have only heard of the characters' deeds with ratings of 5 or higher.

    In Delve, noteworthy deeds are things like saving a village over the course of an entire session, not one character's momentary act of heroism. If I were charting momentary acts of heroism, I'd probably replace deeds with epithets, and have each Noble act feed into one of a handful of epithets. So instead of a full group with "purifiers of Harkethal 4; slayers of Minecoil 3; wardens of Hesengard Keep 6" you'd have an individual character with "loyal 2, selfless 4, brave 6, graceful 3" or something.

    If this basic way of looking at things seems useful to you, I'm happy to brainstorm other mechanical implementations.

Similarly, an aristocrat may have no Renown, as he's done jack: he just sits in his palace, eating the alien equivalent of opium... There could be a petty king on this planet, with zero Renown...

    The more nobly the peasant behaves, the more his Renown grows, and the more he can materialise his will in the game. That's the hope...
    \nOh, I thought you wanted to model social status and class, as well as fame. I suggested Circles because they do a good job of modeling status and social connection -- it allows for mobility/advancement but also stresses status' relatively non-transactional nature.

    The system you've described so far is completely transactional -- it's like a weird capitalist fantasy of social standing and class, where you earn social money and use it to buy social effects on a one-to-one basis. I feel like that approach is probably going to encourage your players to treat the system itself as a transaction, much in the way you discuss alignment systems being treated in other games.

    \n One way to look at this is to create "influential sources" in various areas of the game world. One could be a powerful ruler; another could be a newspaper or book publisher; a third could be a mob which gathers on Sundays under a monument.

    Each "source" has an influence over an area (and this could be tracked relative to other sources, if necessary - the King controls such-and-such an area, but doesn't care what the mob thinks... yet devotedly reads the Northern Wizards' Monthly).

    To achieve notoriety, you do not have to *do* things; you must convince a certain source of your qualities/virtues. To do THAT you *may*, of course, have to accomplish things (how else would you win their favour?), but that's not where this all hinges. It's, rather, about how much you have impressed a certain source.

    Those sources then are assumed to distribute their opinion of you to the "population" they can reach. (Perhaps you could randomize just how much, if that seems too deterministic - e.g. while traveling through the King's lands, any subject of the King has a 1 in 4 chance of knowing the King's opinion of you, or 1 in 6 if it's a distant province.)

    You create your reputation, then, by influencing various sources. (And, in the more involved version, the sources also influence each other - maybe someone who hates the King naturally hates anyone the King likes, for example.)
