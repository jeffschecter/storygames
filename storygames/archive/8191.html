\n For the purposes of this post I\u2019m talking about playing a game by using email, a forum, a wiki or something like that, not a chat room or skype or something along those lines.

These sorts of games have a lot of advantages and disadvantages when compared to tabletop gaming.

Advantages:

-The most obvious advantage is that with internet gaming you can get people from all over the world so it is much easier to get specialized groups than with tabletop game play. There barriers to entry are much lower since you can do it in your spare time instead of having to schedule face to face events.

-Because of the way the internet works you can communicate with as few or as many people as you want. This makes it possible to have massive games with dozens of people and makes cross talk much easier than in tabletop.

-Time constraints work out much differently so instead of having to have a lot of rapid back and forth chatter like in tabletop you can have things like people spending hours writing out massive posts that can be anything from describing the actions of their character in short story format or people writing out the entire order of battle of their nation\u2019s military in intricate detail. I think the ability to get into this kind of detail is a real strength of the online medium.

-Complex rules are often easier to manage since you often have a day or so to get back to the GM about what your character is doing instead of seconds or minutes.

Disadvantages:

-High turnover. There is much higher turnover wrt both GMs and players. Because of this making sure that no one person is vital and that GM authority is at least somewhat distributed is important. Because you often don\u2019t personally know the people you\u2019re playing with it is much easier to blow off a game (something I\u2019m as guilty of as anyone).

-Using the standard RPG party of adventurers format is often an exercise in frustration. Everything often has to move at the pace of the slowest player and trying to maintain an initiative order slows things down even more. A single combat can last weeks of real time. Often on the internet the more the party is split up the faster play moves.

-Rules that require a lot of back and forth between various players or GMs don\u2019t work well because they take too long. Some form of scripting (like Burning Wheel) or having everyone\u2019s turns be adjudicated at once (like in Diplomacy, not an RPG, but an awesome game nonetheless) helps with this.

A lot of RPGs that are played online are traditional tabletop games that are played online much the same way that they are played on a tabletop (except muuuuuuch slower, usually with a lot less combat and often with a lot of lengthy literary-style description of what\u2019s going on).

The main alternative (which I THINK is a lot more popular, but it\u2019s hard to measure these sorts of things on the internet) is freeform RPing based on internet forums or list-serves or games that have evolved from that form. Some examples I\u2019ve seen have been freeform geopolitical simulators, cooperative fiction writing, freeform Populus-style god games, etc. etc.

The basic idea is that each person controls one character and they can describe their character doing whatever they like as long as it doesn\u2019t conflict with what someone else says. If a group has a good Social Contract this can work surprisingly well and there\u2019s less \u201cwell my character just got elected King of the World!\u201d or \u201cI shot you\u201d \u201cWell the bullet bounced off!\u201d \u201cNo it didn\u2019t!\u201d than you\u2019d think. What often happens is that people go out of their way to avoid direct conflict with other Players, which works but can be a bit boring.

Often gaming groups of this sort go from being completely freeform to having a Black Box GM. In this system, each player says what they\u2019re trying to do and the GM says what the result is. Everything being up to the GM can lead to some problems however, the most notable being GM burnout which often kills of this kind of game. Another way to get around the problems of freeform is to write up a set of detailed rules. In the cases of this that I\u2019ve experienced the game went from being a very freeform anything goes Geopolitical RPing game (each Player was a ruler of a different country) to being more of a Play By Email Board Game (like Diplomacy, which again, is an awesome game but not an RPG). All of the long posts people liked writing (for example maintaining daily newspapers for their country) went from being the heart of the game to fluff that had nothing to do with what was actually going on in terms of the rules.

So how would you go about making a game that takes the best elements of tabletop gaming with some of the good elements of freeform internet gaming? The only criterion is that it has to be as internet-friendly as possible.

    \n Here\u2019s my very simplistic crack at it (the rules are based loosely on the PTA fan mail mechanic):

    1. Game play starts with Canon being defined. This could be something like \u201cthe world as it existed in 1,000\u201d or \u201cthe Harry Potter universe at the beginning of the first book\u201d or something more nebulous.

    2. People decide what sorts of characters are appropriate and each player makes a Character. Depending on the game, the Character could be a single normal human or an entire species of star-faring aliens or anything in between. Characters have no stats.

    3. Each player (except possibly new players or people who are short on time) would also be responsible for an element of Canon. How Canon is divided up depends on the game. For example a game set in the single city could have each Player be in charge of the Canon for a neighborhood. Canon could also be divided up thematically into things like The Natural World, Government, Military, Magic, etc. They are in charge of maintaining, coming up with details for and generally being the GM of that slice of Canon.

    4. As long as players don\u2019t come into Conflict with each other or try to change some element of Canon, players can post anything they want and no rules apply. For example if your Character is hanging out in a bar and talking to people things just go freeform, a Character tries to kill a Canonical NPC, then the rules kick in.

    5. Whichever Player started the Conflict then writes up a post that sets the scene and says what will happen (in general terms) if they win the scene. The person on the other side of the Conflict (another Character or a player in charge of the relevant slice of Canon) then writes another post that outlines the difficulties that the character that started the conflict faces and says what will happen (in general terms) if the character who started the Conflict loses the Conflict (so stakes are set). A Player can only be involved in one Conflict at a time.

    6. All players start with a certain number of Tokens. To initiate a conflict you have to spend one Token. Every time they read a write-up of a Conflict (see 5 above) Players can spend one (and only one) of their Tokens by voting for one side or another of the Conflict (you can\u2019t vote for yourself). After a certain amount of time has passed the side of the Conflict that gets the most votes collects all of the Tokens (including the one that the person who initiated the conflict spent) and writes up a post that narrates how the Conflict is resolved that is in line with what they wrote before when stakes were being set (see 5 above). If a person runs out of Tokens then their character can\u2019t do much, but they can still win Tokens through their roles as \u201cguy in charge of a certain element of Canon.\u201d So there is a strong incentive not to write up ridiculous thing that get people to vote against you since that will make you run out of Tokens eventually.

    7. So in order to get a big pile of Tokens, you need to start interesting conflicts that enough people care about to vote about and then win the conflict. To win a conflict what you need to do is convince the players in general that the resolution to the conflict that you envision makes more sense or is more awesome by whatever standards the players in general want to judge according to. One good way of convincing people that your vision makes sense is to base it on Canon or on the results of previous conflicts and to avoid shooting too high.

    Example:

    Player starts a conflict with \u201cMy Character becomes a Third Party candidate for governor of Illinois\u201d And they write up a fun post describing the course of their election campaign and maybe write up their stump speech.

    The Player in charge of the relevant bit of Canon (maybe political Canon girl, or the guy in charge of the Canon of the Midwest) then writes up a post about the difficulties that that Character faces and what their opponents are up to.

    The Player then has an incentive to not shoot too high while setting the Stakes. If they say \u201cI win 99% of the vote\u201d then people will vote them down with Tokens and they\u2019ll lose. But if they say something like \u201cI win 20% of the vote, and even though I\u2019m not elected I\u2019ve now got great name recognition and I\u2019ve got a big fundraising and volunteer rolodex\u201d they\u2019re much more likely to win the Conflict since people will think that\u2019s more reasonable. Then in later Conflicts they could bring up those things in order to convince people that there\u2019s a basis for them winning the Conflict (in future elections for example). Basically precedent is your friend.

    What this does is have very freeform rules that turn the player base in general into a black box that generates results, while encouraging people to be reasonable with the sort of things they write up.

    8. The rules can easily be extrapolated to three-way (or more) conflicts. If two Characters are allied they can work together on one side of a Conflict and beg for Tokens together.

    9. Stats could be added as a way to make Characters better at some Conflicts than others. Just add or subtract the relevant stat to the vote total.

    This is of course a very skeletal set of rules. Any additions or changes people can think up?

    \n In answering this question I always point to Phases of Tir na Nog'th (a farce, with knives), a wiki Amber game I played in a while back.

    Because it was based around characters who could retcon reality a bit, the wiki format fitted well. A lot of the time, we were in separate scenes or two people in one scene, and we could do a bit of back-and-forth (we had an email list where we did OOC chat and alerted each other when we'd posted). If there were more than two people in a scene, the rule was that you were allowed one update per day.

    There were about 4 Americans, a woman in the Netherlands and me in New Zealand. We had sufficient time zone overlap that it worked out.

    What finally killed it was that the GM got busy IRL. I think if authority had been split up more, as you suggest above - say, one person in charge of Chaos, another of Amber, a third of Tir na Nog'th, a fourth of the Greatmoon shadow - it might have lasted longer. We did a little of that towards the end but there was still a strong central GM and when he was no longer available the game ended.

    We did adopt a system of stats partway through, but they were more indicative than mechanical. It was like, "I have a 15 in Obsessed with Getting Benedict's Approval, so I think my character would do this," or "I have a 20 in Shaman, so my character should be able to do that."

    \n The great advantage of online games is that you can have lots of people involved, and you can play with lots of people you wouldn't normally get to.

    The great disadvantage of online games is that quite often, you discover that there are reasons why these people aren't playing RPGs face-to-face.

    I played in more "freeform" PBEM and play-by-post RPGs than I can easily count, over a period of several years. Here's some of the things that tend to go awry, which (IMHO) any good system for them will have to help the players deal with:

    1 - Pointless scenes and/or scenes that run on too long. These happen because of a lack of any scene framing mechanics that explicitly tell people when to end a scene (e.g., "when all conflicts are resolved" or that give explicit permission to "skip the dull stuff". Quite often in a game like this, the players (through their characters) will agree to do something -- e.g., leave the place they've met up and go investigate the spooky warehouse. However, instead of cutting off the scene and jumping to arriving at or already being in the warehouse, the game will ramble on, with people roleplaying going out to the car, starting it up, driving over there, etc.

    Alternately, people often roll on to the next thing in logical temporal order, regardless of whether it's interesting or not. I can't tell you how many Harry Potter games I've been in where a nighttime scene will be wrapped up, and someone immediately starts a breakfast scene -- not because there's a dramatic reason to, but simply because it's the next thing they can think of that should happen in temporal order.

    2 - Mary-Sue characters. For those unfamiliar with the term, a Mary-Sue is a character put into a story who has no faults and is uber-competent, often in many different fields. In a Buffy RPG my wife played in once, one of the players started off playing the group's Watcher -- who was then revealed to also be an ex-Marine, to have a PhD in Psychology, to be extremely rich (owning multiple homes and dozens of cars), to have a secret occult library.... well, you get the picture.

    Given unlimited character creation abilities, some people will take advantage, and a few will take extreme advantage.

    (A related problem is the premise-violating character. In almost every Harry Potter game, for example, there's someone who wants to play a character who *also* survived an attack by Voldemort.... or wants to play a character who's Harry's other cousin, who nobody knew about until now.)

    3 - Players who won't ever let their character lose a conflict. Without conflict resolution rules, some players will simply "refuse to lose", going on and on with "... but that doesn't work because ..." responses to anything that could have what they consider a negative effect on their character.

    4 - Over-posting and under-posting players. A player who only posts once every couple of weeks is one kind of problem. A player who posts every ten minutes is another -- and two or three of the latter can quickly overrun a game and prevent anyone else from having meaningful input, if allowed. Be aware as well that some of the latter type will try to work around a restriction on how often they're allowed to post by doing "joint posts" -- that is, carrying out their own exchange with another player via email, IM, or some other means outside the game, then posting the result of that as "their post for the day" or such.

    5 - Player cliques. There are people who will join a game in a group of two or three people -- and then some or all of that group will ignore everyone else in the game, and only post in scenes with their clique.

    A gamemaster can work around all of these problems, but adding in a gamemaster creates its own problems -- the GM can become a bottleneck. Over-controlling GMs seem to be a common problem in freeform RPGs as well, and GMs can be part of a clique. (IMHO, a big part of the problem is that the people who have the most free time to play in online RPGs, while having the least money to use their free time for other pursuits, are often high-school or college-aged... and so they often bring the social baggage of those age groups along.)

    So, the big things I see a system designed for this sort of play needing are:

    - Strong scene-framing rules, to prevent pointless scenes and scenes that last past their useful life.

    - Some sort of character-creation limits, to prevent Mary-Sues. (E.g., even something as simple as "pick three positive traits and one negative trait.")

    - Conflict-resolution rules, to prevent "refusing to lose".

    - Rules to limit over-posting, and keep under-posting from bogging down the game.

    - Rules to help break up cliques. This is the one I have no good ideas on how to do. Maybe some sort of rewards for posting with a variety of players?

    A way of dealing with High Turnover ( either distributed authority, or enpowering a figure who wont turnover)

    I think that these are important, but I'm not sure that Character Creation limits are in and of themselves important as long as there are good Conflict Resolution rules. For example "you can define your character however you like, but describing your character in Mary Sue terms won't help you win conflicts."

    Also I think something that is necessary is some way of tracking what is "real" in the setting, either through a Wiki or something like that. I think that a good way to do this is slicing GM responsibility so that a collection of different people are in charge of a different slice of reality each which some kind of coordination between them.

    And finally if you want the freeform forum Roleplay "feel" to predominate (it really is quite different than a table top "feel") you have to make the style and writing quality of people's posts matter more than juggling with numbers. For example if you have people narrate what they do and then translate that into crunch terms, you soon stop getting the sort of details narratives that make freeform roleplaying fun. What I'd like is for the narration to matter in crunch terms, at the very least through some kind of Stunt system (i.e. describe what you're doing in a cool way and get a bonus) or have your narrated posts basically BE the crunch (i.e. have you succeed or fail based on how awesome and well-written your posts are, not on what your character stats are).

    \nThis is true. It's worth remembering that most collaborative efforts on the internet end in failure, because starting a collaborative project is so ridiculously cheap and easy.
