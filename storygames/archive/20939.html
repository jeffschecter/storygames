\n In games which prioritize the #Narrative individual success/failure isn't meaningful, because both generate story, and if not it's an indictment of the system rather than my choices.

In games which prioritize the #Character individual success/failure is meaningful, as I'm making decisions based on my character's best interests which motivates me to seek success.

The problem is #Fate and #Fiasco prioritize the Narrative yet are built on the assumption that an individual's success/failure will be meaningful to the player. This requires making decisions based on what you want to see happen to a character rather than what you want to see them accomplish, which is a perspective I find extremely dissonant when playing just a single character.

Now I still have fun with both, but my engagement with the mechanics is all but arbitrary. At best I ignore them. At worst they're an unnecessary decision I have no qualitative basis to make, like deciding between red and blue dish washing liquid. However the latter has kept me from going shopping on more than one occasion.

    \n The use of success/failure mechanics in RPG design is often just a legacy. It's as much a prejudice we players tend to hold, though, as it is an artifact in design.

    In order to fully enjoy Fiasco, it helps if you stop equating white dice/black dice with character getting/not getting what they want, and you start looking at it as good things/bad things happening to the character: more of an overtone to the scene (are you familiar with Swords Without Master?) than the outcome of an action. The point is precisely in choosing (in the most vague terms) which of two opportunities of plot development to go for, not to evaluate a character's performance. As for the endings, they are what they are: consequence - though it's especially interesting, in dramatic terms, that Fiasco sometimes (but not often) allows for excessively tragic or undeservedly mild consequences to befall a character in the end. That's a form of dramatic irony that I quite appreciate, and I most appreciate that it's wholly out of my control and occasionally anticlimatic even.

    \nExactly, from time to time I have heard a small minority of people say that they have trouble running Fiasco, even people that I value the opinion of otherwise. One think to realize is that something that is good from the perspective of an ambitious person with poor judgment and impulse control, can seriously reek havoc in their lives, and the lives of others, and cause destruction in the larger context of the story. The suggestions, at the beginning of act one and act two are very important as well, they are as follows:
    Act one:
    "In the first act, we should meet our guys and see the dynamics of their Relationships in action. Everything you do should foreshadow things to come. If we meet an overbearing father, he\u2019s going to get a hell of a lot more overbearing later \u2212 and rebelled against. If we see a machete, it\u2019s going to hack some stuff to pieces later in the game. Use this time to set things up and begin to knock them down."
    Act two:
    "Act Two is where the wheels should start to come off. Things are going wrong. New problems have emerged. Fires \u2212 met- aphorical and maybe literal \u2212 will need to be fought. This is where you unscrew the jar of crazy and throw the lid at some- one."
    I think if you understand all this, it would be very hard for Fiasco to not accomplish what it is designed for. For anyone who has had trouble running the game, follow these principles and tried again and I'm sure the results will be terrific

    \nThis is a really good question, and the answer is defined by your local culture of play. Normally it is pretty obvious. Sometimes an outcome could be either, but at your table it is definitely positive and at my table it is definitely negative, based on our consensus about what is interesting and fun. There's no formal criteria, because I want you to make the choices that are perfect for you and your friends, and you are, collectively, really good at determining those.

    \nJeff, I see you keep upholding this dichotomy between what you call "RPGs" and "story games"... If that works for you, fine, but to me it looks like you're missing out a lot by conflating an excessive numbers of technical features of design and play which could be considered individually, and stuffing them into just two big boxes.
    For example, I know from other threads you conflate "there's one guy acting as the GM" with "RPG" and "there isn't (just) one such guy" with "story game"... But then, when you overlay "each other player plays just one character" and "make your decisions based on what you want your character to accomplish" on top of "one GM", on one hand, and "make your decisions based on what you want to see happen" on top of "GM-less/-full/-whatever", you make your boxes too narrow, too particularly shaped to accommodate many existing or possible games.
    There are more RPGs/story games on Earth, Horatio... ;P

    As for Anon's feeling of dissonance, my sympathy. I do believe part of a successful RPG design is providing grounds on which to make decisions, explicit or implied. Advocating for a character you especially empathize or identify with is a strong, important component of it, as well as a "default" design choice - thus, in games where that turns up not to be a factor, some equally strong alternative needs be provided. In my experience, that's quite a common failure state for both RPG design and play - and while I haven't usually experienced that playing Fiasco (I can't speak about Fate: to little experience playing it) I can well understand how that could happen... Dramatic irony and, possibly, narrative distance are involved in successful Fiasco play, but the moment that turns into detachment, the moment you stop caring about your own character - horrible person that they might be - you're left with a void no other components of the game can successfully fill. Then, you're just going through the motions of playing Fiasco, but it has become devoid of meaning.

    On the other hand, I do believe "stance" - as they used to call it on the Forge of old - is something fluid, which can and does change moment-to-moment during play. And, Jeff, most of my best moments in role-playing have involved some use of what was called "Author stance" - that is, roughly, staying with one character as both the point of view and main point of interest, but looking inward into them just as much as outward from them, advocating for them as a main character and/or human being in ways not limited to pushing for them to achieve their immediate goals, and caring about them maybe more than they would care about themselves.
