\nAge of Steel, my little sword & sorcery game, uses Keys (because - y'know - once you've used Keys there's no going back). By hitting them, the players earn resources they can spend to do cool stuff. The early playtest made it clear that the GM needed a resource, too, to challenge the players in important conflicts. So the GM gets access to Adversity (or Peril; can't decide) he can spend for extra dice on challenges.

Some Adversity is earned at the start of each scene, and some when the PCs do certain stuff (like use sorcery, because it's blasphemous wossnamery that man was not meant to wot of), but I can't help but feel I'm missing an opportunity here. One of the great things about Keys is that they encourage certain sorts of play from the players - could they do the same for the GM?

Is there any mileage in the GM having keys - maybe tying into the game or session's theme - that earn him a resource when he hits on them in play with NPCs or scene-setting? Something like:-

The Key of Recurring Villainy: gain 1 Peril when a villain engages a PC in a conflict, and then escapes to return in a later scene.

Or,

The Key of Blood-spattered Gore: gain 1 Peril for describing an injury or death in a way that makes the players go "Eeeew!"

Or might this mess up the balance of play? Could it lead the GM to spend more time on finding ways to bring his Keys in and put less focus on the PCs? Has anyone used anything like this in their games?

    \n I started using what we might call "keys" in my homebrews when 7th Sea was released.

    I was inspired to create what could be called "GM keys" when Atlas' Rune was released. They work pretty well for me, in a way generally similar to what Christopher Hearns and Nick explain above.

    More recently, I was inspired by EABA to have what could be called "Synergy Keys". To trigger them, the entire group of players needs to meet certain conditions, as determined by the GM. I'm not going to go into that in this thread as this may be a bit off-topic.

    The general goal of my post is just to say that what you guys refer to as "keys" is a powerful tool that you can indeed use for the GM, for individuals and for groups as well. It's an incredibly versatile concept.

    \nSure! The GM resource is called adversity. It's actually attached to specific PCs, so a hero might have 3 points of adversity in his pool. Adversity can only be used by the GM against the hero whose pool it was in. Spending adversity typically grants the GM extra dice on a roll against the PC. If one of the NPCs is opposing the PC, then Fortune basically increases one of the NPC's stats against that hero for the duration of the conflict. It can also be used to activate NPCs special abilities.

    Currently, a hero gets a point of adversity at the start of each scene, but heroes can do things that increase their adversity pool, too.\n

    There's an element of competition in the game - players compete to see who was the real hero of the tale, and I want both players and GM to have defined economies that regulate their accumulation of resources.

    Posted By: 1of3Chris, doesn't that miss the point of Keys? When my character has a Key, that tells the GM what I want him to provide. So if the GM had keys, shouldn't that tell the players what to provide?
    \nRIght - been thinking about this. I think Keys serve a few functions.

    1) They are (like you said) a message to the GM saying "This is what I want to see."
    2) They serve as a guide to players. A player with the Key of Compassion is more likely to behave in a compassionate way, even in situation that the GM hadn't established to test that Key. They're useful prompts in play. If I'm a player and I'm at a loose end regarding what to do next, I can take a look at my Keys and do something that brings me closer to hitting one.

    In Age of Steel, that first function is built right into adventure creation - an adventure isn't done until the GM has generated elements that tie into all the heroes' Keys. The second function is what ties into the resource economy - by acting in accordance with their Drives, the heroes earn Fortune which they can use to do cool stuff. The idea is: acting in accordance with your drives = increased fortune = increased ability to act.

    In the playtest this worked really well. By doing the things that made them who they are, the heroes earned resources that let them be even more heroic. People jived with this very quickly, even the ones who hadn't experienced indie-gaming before (This is why I love Keys - they're so intuitive. People get to grips with them right away. Thank you, Clinton!).

    What I'm wondering is: can the same benefits be achieved for the GM? Might it be productive to link the GM's resource system to specific behaviours and conditions in a similar way that Keys do for players, so that they provide a moment-by-moment guide to running the game? Something to go to if you're stumped or looking for inspiration, and reminders regarding what the game is about.

    \n This is pretty much exactly what I was setting out to do with the coolmap. My presentation of it is for a distributed-GM game, but it should work just as well for a single-GM game. Brief summary: You have a diagram of all the themes and entities in the setting and how they relate to one another. People initial the ones they're into. When these themes turn up in the game, you get one GM point; when they're the focus of conflict, you get 1 GM point per initial against them.
