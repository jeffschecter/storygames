    Charles,

    Do a search at the Forge for Copyright and Trademark. This kind of thing has been discussed a lot over the years.

    As I understand those discussions, the copyright is in the text, not the ideas. You can copy ideas from a game and, indeed, many games use mechanics that are clearly derived from other games. For example, you said you enjoyed The Shadow of Yesterday, and that game uses FUDGE dice.

    So, as I understand it, the short answer to this question...
    For example, in a game that i am working on, I am using a skill pyrimid concept that i first discovered in fate.

    Does this mean that i can never publish my game if i use this concept?
    ...is no. I do hope other people will correct me if I'm wrong.

    Honestly, I would contact the creators of Fate, and talk to them about it. I am doing a Shadow of Yesterday variant, and even though Clinton put it out under Creative Commons, I wanted to chat with him about what I was doing before I went forward.

    Heh. Well, quite honestly, I feel that outright theft of ideas is the heart and soul of RPG design.

    So in the specific example of Fate (and feel free to treat this as an official response), my thinking is pretty much this:

    If you take the text verbatim, then stick to the OGL.

    So long as you're just using the idea, but writing your own text, then you're free and clear. As courtesy, it would be awesome if you comment somewhere about where you got your inspirations, but that's not a necessity*.

    Ultimately, contacting the author is cool and useful because, hey, everyone likes to feel like they've been inspirational, and doubly so when they get soem credit for it. We're such a cottage industry that people are usually willing to talk about stuff and generally be cool about it. Also, frankly, being cool about it is so much less of a headache than trying to really track down the origins of any particular idea.

    -Rob D.

    * But man, considering the sheer amount of goodwill that giving credit engenders vs. the annoyance that it's absence generates, taking a sentence to give props is a total gimme.

    As others have said, asking the guy who inspired you is the simplest thing.

    Consulting lawyers is cool and all, but lawyers don't always agree and are expensive. Finding an IP lawyer who knows about roleplaying at any level beyond "that DnD thing" could take a while and without that you'll also be paying to explain what a roleplaying game is so that he can take a view on whether your treatment infringes anything.

    Also, you need a relevantly qualified lawyer. There are tons of people online who offer legal advice on the basis they did a JD or something. Avoid them. Any decent lawyer will caveat his advice as to his qualifications and so on, advice from a guy who isn't practicising or who practices another area of law is valueless. It has to be an IP lawyer qualified in the relevant jurisdiction.

    Trust me on this, I'm a lawyer (but not appropriately qualified to answer this question in anything other than a layman's capacity, I don't practice IP law).

    Edit: I'd also add that anyone who boasts about their lawschool grades is an amateur, if you've actually been practicing a few years and are doing ok you don't need to talk about what you did at school. Again, I have seen people do this on rpg fora and it's a clear sign they're not to be taken seriously.
