    Posted By: JDCorleyHow do you write a good Facts and Reassurances?

    In other words, okay, we've got kitchen sink dramatics, spy stuff, and Lovecraftian horror. What makes these good, what should I look for to do other good F&Rs?
    \nThere's some material on page 69 about doing this. The basic model I've followed so far is to use the F&R to establish:\n
      \n
    • Who the characters are (normal people, Investigators, government agents, etc.)
      • \n
    • The rough circumstances leading to their memory loss (physiological/psychological trauma, exposure to Unspeakable Horrors, something on your last mission)
      • \n
    • Time frame (modern day, 1920s, modern day)
      • \n
    • Supernatural/Weirdness/Action level (none, Unspeakable Horrors, Tom Clancy)
      • \n
    \nThe last one is about establishing what I call the "circle of plausibility." The range of things that can happen in your stories can grow during play, but starting with a rough idea of what you want to be possible and what you don't is a useful tool.

    Does that help?

    \n Those three scenarios are all asking essentially for the same thing: a specific event that was a turning point to the character. While the entire conceit of Penny is "remember your life," that's not what the third question asks.

    Original -> What event happened to cause you to lose your memory?
    Bourne -> What happened with the package?
    Lovecraft -> What do you know that can help us?
    (Roughly paraphrasing)

    Because those settings ask for something specific, they allow the previous two to be more general about filling in the setting. The memories prior to the last serve the role of context-building. So you want settings where you'd have something interesting around which it build context.

    To answer the question: a setting would work if there is a sense of a question to ask in that third memory, and something worth engaging to create the context in the first two.
