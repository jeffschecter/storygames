    The best two have already been mentioned ITT (M John Harrison and Gene Wolfe)

    \n

    Harrison's other Viriconium novellas are better and more science fantasy. Gene Wolfe's other Sun books are also post-apoc science fantasy, though not set on Earth.

    \n

    John Christopher's the Tripods is great children's post-apoc fantasy. This goes under both Books and Visual Media.

    \n

    I suppose we can acknowledge the existence of Neal Stephenson's Anathem but it's just a shoddy rip-off of Canticle for Liebowitz with a bad imitation of Wolfe/Mieville/Peake narrative voice.

    \n

    The Night Land by William Hope Hodgson is an essential precursor to the genre.

    \n The Greatwinter saga has elements of this. Sort of post-post-apocalyptic though (where culture and nation building has risen from the ashes) and the "super-science-as-magic" is more subtle and not so in your face (the Call, avians). It loosely fits your definition, though perhaps not if Gamma World is the standard. Fun reading, though.

    Another "kinda-sorta" post-apocalypse fantasy is the Second Chronicles of Thomas Covenant. That one uses magic (as opposed to super-science), though the society that fell wasn't particularly modern to begin with.
