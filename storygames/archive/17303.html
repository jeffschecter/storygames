\n The challenge is to write a full role-playing game. No more than 15 paragraphs in all, including title. No paragraph longer than a single line.

An exercise in effective game-text.

- theme is not important, as long as there are role-playing
- beauty is not important, as long as there are clarity
- the only interesting ambition is to post a full game

Good luck!

    \n An example:

    ANGST
    - a game for two
    - play "yourself"

    Take turns:
    - say what the other player should do
    - he stares at you, and must narrate what he does
    - but he may not do the thing you say he should do
    - you should talk to him about it; ask him why he hasn't done it yet
    - he may not answer that question; he must say something else
    - your dialogue is the end of his turn

    Then you switch sides.
    Do that as many times as you will.
    Fun is not the point. Angst is.

    \nDESTINY WARRIORS
    Every player rolls D6+1, they should not see their own roll but everyone else must.

    Narrate in scenes, like a movie.
    Every player speaks for their own Warrior.
    Roll D6 to see what the Enemy is up to this scene:
    6 - frontal assault
    5 - flanking maneuver
    4 - sustained barrage
    3 - covert (roll again when you find out what)
    2 - digging in
    1 - withdrawal

    Cede narration rights to players you think have a lower roll than you.
    Keep track of the number of scenes you have played.
    When the scene number equals a players roll their Warrior must die that scene, you narrate how.
    The game ends after scene 5.

    \nA Gun, A Dame, A Window

    A game for three players.
    One is the Sleuth.
    Another is the Danger.
    The last is the Mystery.

    To begin:
    The Danger writes one threat on an index card.
    The Mystery writes one question on another card.
    The Sleuth names and describes himself.

    Play:
    The Danger plays all NPC crooks, mooks, and dirty politicians.
    The Mystery plays all NPC rats, reporters, and coroners.
    Whenever the Sleuth digs around where he shouldn't, the Danger introduces a new threat or escalates an old one.
    Whenever the Sleuth calls on old contacts or reviews new evidence, the Mystery answers a question and optionally introduces a new one.
    Whenever the Danger or Mystery are done talking, ask: What do you do?

    \n Homeworld Project, a Game of Astral Space Opera (Ultra-Lite Version)

    To setup (for each player), describe three traits of your main character, called grains. These all start with 3 columns of details, leave room for 12.
    Then, in a circle, describe a reflection of your grain in your shared Astral Space. Give that reflection to a player, with 3 columns, too.
    Then, describe a grain for a ship or a supporting character related to your main character, and choose an existing reflection for it.

    Play in a series of chapters, made up of one-on-one bouts and involving anything in the action. Chapters take about an hour.

    Each entity in a chapter has a d12 and each chapter there are two numbers, the upper and lower, starting at 11 and 2.
    When in a bout, progress both dice: if it shows less than lower reduce by 1, more than upper raise by 1, else or 1 or 12, re-roll it.
    If a die progresses to 1, reduce the upper by 1. If a die progresses to 12, raise the lower by 1. When upper and lower meet, the chapter ends.
    If you spend a column to progress and the result is a 12, +1 column to that grain/reflection and everything tied to it, if a 1, -1 instead.

    Spend columns (write / re-use its detail) from grains/reflections: 1 to progress or introduce a person or thing of 1 importance per 2 columns spent.

    Bout: describe engaging another entity, both sides progress, higher die player describes inflicting or removing a consequence valued up to the difference.
    Total consequences on entity: importance x 5 = disabled, 5 more = doomed, 10 more = destroyed. With a grain your importance is 3.

    Start of a chapter: each player with a reflection introduces at least one entity from one of their reflections, to cause trouble.

    If spent to end the chapter: get a new grain, for a new or existing character/ship, new reflection gives 3, existing reflection gives its current columns.
    If a reflection has 12 columns, it and all grains tied to it have become a permanent fixture (can't be spent), and affected players can make a new grain.

    \n THE AMAZON'S MAGICAL UNICORN FRIENDS SPORT AT THE OTHER-WORLD TECHNOCRACY BROADCAST TOWER

    You are an Amazon with a magic unicorn that has miraculous powers, however you, your unicorn and your friends have been imprisoned deep below an alien planet surface within a technological death-sport arena under the watch of extra-terrestrial wizards, and see victory as the sole survivor of the televised arena as your only chance at million-to-one odds of escaping.

    In Contested Actions both reveal one of the three coloured dice and roll.

    Red ACTIVE You may re-roll one against Passive actions and act first.

    White MANOEUVRE You may re-roll one dice against Active actions and act before Passive and after Active.

    Blue PASSIVE You may re-roll one dice against Manoeuvre actions and act last.

    In case of a tie between action, the dice roll indicates priority.

    Assign 3+, 4+, and 5+ to the 3 attributes Active, Manoeuvre, and Passive.

    Assign 3+, 4+, and 5+ Body, Mind, and Spirit.

    BODY represents Size, Health, Strength, and Movement; pick any one of these and reduce the target number by one when using this trait.

    MIND represents Skills, Wits, Perception, and ESP; pick any one of these and reduce the target number by one when using this trait.

    SPIRIT represents Command, Composure, Sanity, and Telekinesis; pick any one of these and reduce the target number by one when using this trait.

    Roll the remaining two dice, and assign them to the two target numbers of the assigned action, and the relevant trait, if either one dice beats the target number it results in a mixed success, both dice beat the assigned targets it is a success, and neither do it represents a failure.

    Each player must write down or draw examples on separate cards of the thirteen impossible things to overcome within the tower, A TRAP, A MYTHIC CREATURE, A PUZZLE, A RIDDLE, A ROOM, AN ARTEFACT, A TECHNOLOGY, AN ALIEN, A ROMANCE, A TROPHY, A ROBOT, A WIZARD then assign a different one of the twelve traits to the cards, this represents the type of challenge which must be performed to win the prize card, the thirteen is marked wild and any skill may be used in that challenge.

    Each turn the stack of cards rotates clockwise, with the player drawing one randomly, you and a random player with the least amount of cards duel to see who is the winner of that card, and when any player gets five different cards they escape in a space-craft heading back to Earth, leaving the others to their demise.

    \n NEAR RETIREMENT!

    As a replicant-human near the end of your service you were to be retired, now having escaped with a few others from the work-prison and on the run on an alien planet, you are on a hell-bent vendetta to acquire bloody revenge with your robotic creators.

    Characters are given a pool of 50 ten-sided dice to divide among the categories of Body, Mind, and Spirit, the average person has 3 to 18 dice in any pool.

    The active player says what pool both they and their opponent are using, and they may be different.

    Resolution is derived from rolling the relevant pool and counting matches.

    The multiple of matching dice is the tens number, and the number shown on the matching dice as the ones.

    If you roll multiple sets of matching numbers each is counted discreetly and they indicate greater success or additional actions.

    Countering actions of other players is done by generating a higher score than the opponent.

    If there were multiple actions attempted at being countered they are resolved independently with the same number.

    If an action is successful mark that score to the losers pool, and for each ten points marked against a pool, reduce its size by 1 dice.

    Any dice pool of zero indicates Unconsciousness, Insanity, or Panic.

    You may move two dice from one pool to add one to any another pool, though not during actions.

    Death only comes from player choice or mistake, with any event being lethal for a human rarely affecting the replicant.

    Opening scene is the meeting with your robotic creator, scenes then progress backwards, with each player taking turns in narrating the events surrounding the other characaters, ending with the escape from the prison camp.

    The end is the final confrontation with your master and creator it is a personal experience and each player narrates a scene with the others around the table playing the masters of robotic creation.

    Snake_Eyes

    \n Oldie, but a goodie:
    -----
    FYAYAH! - Fuck You And Your Affirmation, Hippie!

    A too-real game of dysfunctional relationships
    -or-
    A warm-up drill for role playing characters to use for establishing and exploring group dynamics

    Play Start

    FYAYAH! is played with a beer bottle, onto which the rules are printed.

    * Someone spins the bottle so that it points at another person (i.e. not at himself or herself).
    The person at whom it points becomes Mr. Happy Sunshine (HS).
    * HS spins the bottle so that it points at another person.
    The person at whom it points becomes The Drunken Man In The Corner (DM).

    Play Cycle

    FYAYAH! is played as a series of turn cycles, which can continue indefinitely.

    1) HS says the best, most positive true thing(s) that he or she can say about DM.
    2) DM says the worst, most negative true thing(s) that he or she can say about HS.
    3) HS, heartbroken, becomes the next DM and spins the bottle so that it points at another person (i.e. not the current HS or DM).
    The person at whom it points becomes the next HS.
    4) Return to Step 1. Repeat until catharsis ensues or everyone realizes that no one\u2019s perfect.

    Credits

    FYAYAH! was originally conceived by Gilbert Kraag and Bolla Pinnsvin as two separate games. Its current, cynical combination and refinement is by David Carle Artman.

    \n You are one of the four competing powers for a privately owned city-state within the Honduras during the twenty-second century.

    Four players sit in a randomly determined seating order and play begins in a clockwise direction.

    Each player writes down an abstract goal for each of the other three players, so each player begins with three goals they must complete before the game is finished.

    Four players each start with four dominoes each, and twelve dominoes are left face down in an unused pool that is used as the exhausted pile.

    Each time you try to pass vote expend exactly two dominoes in the attempt to achieve one of your three goals.

    If you have the double blank domino you may try to pass a vote concerning a meta-game choice such as direction of play, or swapping two players seating order.

    Take the two dominoes from your hand, putting them face down in front of you together on your left to represent \u201cYea\u201d and on your right to represent \u201cNe\u201d.

    You may abstain from voting by casting up to four dominoes face up.

    Flip the tiles, if any of your dominoes has a blank face you may ante an additional tile for each blank, up to two additional dominoes

    Comparing them, add together the total of Yea versus Ne with the highest total determining the outcome of the vote cast, If it is tied the scene is decided by the highest tile.

    Then shuffle the revealed dominoes randomly into the exhausted pile face down, then replace used dominoes from the exhausted pile.

    Cheers,
    Snake_Eyes

    \nYou're all playing a group of individuals around a table.
    Discuss what kind of group it is: a family, a bunch of friends, co-workers, et c.
    Discuss what they are doing in the present: praying, recovering from a hang-over, arguing, et c.
    Decide which player takes what role in the group.
    Have one token beside the board, and take turns using the token.
    In the beginning of a turn, a player must place the token in either past, present or future.
    - You may only play out a scene during the present.
    - You may only establish facts during the past.
    - You may only show the consequence of the present and the past in the future.
    - If it will create drama, any role may know any established fact.

    How will the past change the present and what will the future be like?

    Example: a quiet family dinner. One player places the token in the past and declares: "the wife has been cheating".

    \n Just to point out:
    \u2013 Sometimes you want something to be efficient, meaning having lots of effect for little effort. When it comes to game txt I guess "effort" would be how long the text is, so an efficient game text would be one that is short yet conveys what it wants to.
    \u2013 Sometimes you want something to be effective, meaning it actually has the intended effect, or that it has a big effect (regardless of effort or energy spent). An effective game text would be a text that actually conveys the what you want it to, and I imagine the place to speak about effective game texts would be when you actually have something difficult to convey that might get lost \u2013 a new playstyle or something. You know, the problem where anyone reading your game gets what to roll and when, but it falls flat because no-one got that you were supposed to create problems for your own characters or something.

    A lengthy text will almost certainly be inefficient, but might still be effective.

    To me it seems like the thread start seems to be talking mainly about efficiency rather than effectiveness (also called efficacy) but I'm not sure. I know I had them mixed up a long time (not a native speaker).

    In a distant future, the orb is one of the key parts of the human existance.

    Discuss the following.\n
    • what is the orb's functions.

    A Betrayal Far Far Away

    \nIt's important that you narrate all the steps from when the turns starts.

    One plays Luke, a farm boy who wants to defeat Darth Vader.
    One plays Leia, the Alderaan princess who wants to save the home planet from destruction.
    One plays Yoda, the old jedi master who wants to restore the jedi order.

    Each player writes an aid - a faction, item or person - that the character to the left needs to fulfill it's goal.
    Place a six-sided die on each aid.
    Each player starts with one +1 token and two +2 tokens, and can only be given three tokens.

    In turns, beginning with Luke and ending with Yoda, give a token to another character and make a promise to that person.
    Roll the dice and place each die with the result unchanged back to where it was.

    In turns, beginning with Yoda and ending with Luke, give a token to another character, if possible, and tell that person how the character makes a sacrifice but wants a promise of something in return.
    During a turn, you may buy one of the dice if the points of your given tokens exceeds the result of the die.

    In turns, beginning with Yoda and ending with Luke, give a token to another character, if possible, and tell that person how you fulfill the promise.
    During a turn, you may buy one of the dice if the points of your given tokens exceeds the result of the die.

    The one(s) who has the highest total of dice reaches the goal with help from the aids, and the one(s) who has the lowest total dies.
