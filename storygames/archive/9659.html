\n Reading about the 24-Hour RPG concept got me wondering if anyone had developed a heuristic/outline/mantra/whatever to help people successfully accomplish something like this. I haven't done a 24-hour one yet, and my entry for Murderland took almost too long even for that contest. I think I came up with a good idea, but I had no real plan on how to approach the project. It was mostly just letting things simmer in the back of my mind until the deadline got close!

    \n Everyone has their own way of doing it, I think. Mine is something like this. Start thinking at midday the previous day. Wander around while the concept develops in my head. Start writing at about three o'clock. Have a draft finished by the evening. Take a break, edit the draft a little later. Go to sleep, wake up, go through the draft again in the morning and be done by midday.

    Others just write, writing up until the deadline. But I do suggest thinking time is important.

    \n Planning was fairly important to me , I started at 9pm and I researched, selected a topic, and brain webed for about 4 hours before going to bed for 8 hours. I started writing 12 hours in , and most of the time involved was still thinking. My game was only 3500words or so, but 28 pages.

    I spent some time on layout/font/images which were pleasant distractions but still kept me in the mindspace of the game. I had more more or less decided the night before (a little bit after 9) to stick to a simple roll under system.

    It didn't stay simple for so long ...

    either way, the best way to do it, I think is to avoid over planing and worrying. Also write down not up. I started with an outline of like 300 words, basically just section titles, and went from there and I think that saved me alot of pain, by preventing me from wasting time/words on bad sections and giving perspect as i want along.

    \n I do it just like designing any other game, except smaller in scope.

    For an example, take Knuckleheads, which I wrote in about a day in response to Tomas' request for a slapstick game.

    First thing I decided is what I wanted the game to be. I wrote it out very simply, like so (this is the actual thing I wrote out)


    a game in which the players collaboratively create, develop, and enjoy a humorous, fictional situation involving the following:

    1) Ordinary folks-type protagonists, usually down on their luck, with whom we can identify
    2) Protagonists engaged in an activity with an objective that we can relate to, usually work, courting, or enterprise
    3) Adversity derived principally from sheer bad luck, as though the universe were out to get the protagonists, that, combined with clumsiness and/or poor planning, leads to humorous accidents that threaten the above objective
    3a) Occasionally an antagonist, often an authority figure, providing additional adversity. He has a personal grudge against the protagonists, or at least seems to.
    4) Bickering and conflict between protagonists, due to the tensions resulting from above adversity
    5) Violence carried out in an absurd manner and/or with absurd results


    Then I started designing a system that would do that. The result was the thing I linked to above.

    Posted By: GrahamPersonally, I think that selecting the system from predefined prototypes is cheating. If you're designing a game, design the game. The best thing about indie games is that the systems are designed around the game, not vice versa.
    \nI haven't seen any truly new systems - just variants and evolutions of others. And you are always going to have a prototype system (or more than one) based on your experiences and personal taste (even if it's a negative prototype - what you don't want to do). So unless you expect the only people to do the contest are those who have never even heard of roleplaying games before - or unless you plan on giving yourself electro-shock therapy before you begin in order to get a nice blank slate to start with - then you're already following the steps I've talked about - you're just doing it badly.

    \nSounds right to me. Generally, anything I've designed, the actual design portion is a really short flurry of thoughts and fitting mechanics together and coming up with cool avenues for play to explore. Two hours or so, typically. Then the rest of the contest time is spent trying to take my initial notes and make them into something understandable by someone who isn't me. The difference between 24 hours and two weeks isn't in the time spent designing as it is in the time spent clarifying.
