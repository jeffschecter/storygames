After doing chargen and setting up some goblins and zombies as antagonists, I'm re-thinking my adventure setup. So far I have the following relationships:
  • Father - In league with goblin necromantic cult, placing personal power over family.
  • Mother - In league, hoping to get her son back.
  • Son (dead)
  • Daughter - Recently fled her family, trying to keep her son from being resurrected.
  • Goblin Leader - Community leader of cult, allied with human Father to push influence into the human community.
  • Goblin Brother - In love with daughter, not sure whether to abandon goblin community for the daughter's sake.
So there you have it. This is a pretty Dungeon-Crawly game, but I'd like to inject some story into things, even if it's solved through "Who do we kill, and who do we side with?"

The problem I'm having is how to present this to the players. The players are members of the Empire's elite Chimera Guard (government-sanctioned adventurers). They will be introduced to the daughter, who is in an infirmary recovering, and told to stop the Goblin Cult, especially their alliance with the Father. Is this a strong enough stepping-off point?

I'm also worried about good, gripping bangs. The party consists of: 2 human fighters, 1 gnome fighter (riding a war dog), 1 human cleric (Good and Healing domains). The goblin cult uses the Water and Death spell domains.

Any thoughts?

    As framed, I can understand how you would be afraid that players will take the info fromt he daughter and just kill from one end of the cult to the other: problem solved. Definately a motivation for adventure, but not one that's going to get them tied up in the complexities and characterizations. As such:

    * Who has fallen under the sway of the cult an needs to be rescued, not killed? And who else wants to make sure he/she is accidentally killed?
    * Where is the cult operating so that a group of adventurers can't just wipe them out dungeon-crawl style?
    * For that matter, why are they sending adventurers rather than soldiers? Is there missing intelligence? Is there a need for plausible deniability? Is this really abotu sendign a message to someone else, perhaps the patron of the cult? Or is this just an expression of a personal vendetta against the father?
    * Whose body does the cult have, stolen from a gravesite, animated as a shambling corpse and making an abomination of a beloved family member, put to rest? Which PCs family is having old grief's ripped open purely so this cult can have more muscle?
    * And while were at it, what about the rest of the bodies?
    * Who remembers the father when he was a good man? And what does he want power _for_? Power is never an end, it's a means, so what does he want to use it for, and is this somethign you'd want to stop?

    Some of these suggest direct player hooks, some of them hopefully just suggest things which might in turn suggest hooks, but they're the lens I would take to this setup.

    -Rob D.

    October 2006
    Ok, here's the rub - your questions paint a very complete picture of a situation, and they definitely deepen it, but I'm not seeing anything in the situation which is going to create a sense of buy in from the players, beyond the implicit contract of "it's a dungeon, so we'll go in". In short, how many of those answers are going to matter to the players?

    So that said, lemme throw some slightly more meta questions at you.

    1) Do you anticipate the players making any real choice (in the non-tactical sense) in the course of this? If so, what will it be? How will the outcome meaningfully change as a result?
    2) In 5 levels, when they've done a complete gear rotation, why is it going to matter that they did this?
    3) What would happen if the players didn't do this? Are you willing to let that happen?

    I think you've really set up an excellent situation, but I'm mostly not sure how your players are going to have any visibility into that. That may sound like an abstract concern, but it's really very concrete, since it means the players won't appreciate the work you put in, which will probably be frustrating for you, and as you take actions in support of your sense of the story, it may appear capricious and arbitrary to your players, which may frustrate them.

    Now, whether the route to visibility is IC or OOC is a point of genuine debate, and one that is really going to depend upon your taste, but the bottom line is that I'm really worried that the thought you've put into this will go to waste.

    -Rob D.

    ...and do the players know wha's going on? Sure, they could walk away and say "bah, who cares about goblins?!" but if you clue them in then the players can begin thinking about the dynamics their characters want to display. You usually can't assume a player is going to think about something like "how do I view death or the afterlife?" and in the pitch of battle is never a good time for those details to come out (it's just rushed and usually very self-serving-in-the-moment sort of decision). My suggestion? Tell them about the plot. Tell them some of the things you told us and what you hope the characters will find out and what's at stake? What does failure mean? If the players know that it will motivate them to care about what happens.
