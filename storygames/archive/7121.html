\n I fairly recently purchased quite a few RPGs in .pdf format. This has got me thinking about them and what they are good for and what they aren't so good for. And I hoped that some people out there on S-G, particularly people that have written and published RPGs might be able to shed some light on some of my ponderings.

First something that just struck me yesterday:
from a legal standpoint, am I even allowed to print a copy or copies of .pdf format books I buy? Obviously I'm aware that I'm not allowed to run one off and sell or give them away to all my friends, but am I even technically allowed to print a copy for my own use?

Secondly, the big them I'm wondering, is why do some authors choose not to release their books in .pdf format at all? I find having really big, long books (more than, i dunno 80 pages or so) in .pdf to be pretty unweildy and annoying, both to read on the computer and for printing. Smaller stuff isn't too bad though, and the price break for .pdf books, plus being able to have it INSTANTLY makes them fairly appealing. It would seem to my inexperienced mind that they'd be good for the author too, since once her time has been paid for (and, i suppose, the potential cost of a server to host the files, if necessary) a .pdf sale is pure profit. So why do some people not make use of that avenue? Is it concerns over people sharing/pirating/etc.? Or something else I haven't considered?

anyway, i've just been curious. if anyone can help enlighten me, that would be sweet.

    \n Speaking of the legal angle: here in Finland it is a well-established legal principle that copyright law alone does not prevent you from making copies of anything at all for your own personal use. Thus, making copies of music or books or movies or whatever and even giving them to your friends is legal as long as you do it personally and on a case-by-case basis (instead of serving them on an anonymous server, say).
    \n
    \nConsidering the above, I'm pretty certain that you can print pdf files for your own use with no trouble at all here in Finland. Can't speak for other countries, however.

    \n Sometimes I feel like I'm the only one who hates ordering physical objects online, and then waiting for them to be delivered. It seems like such a popular thing to do, and when I express my seething abhorrence of the process, most people just kinda shrug. Seriously, it's archaic, and it mucks with my psychology in a terrible way. It means day after day of excitement and enthusiasm followed by soul-crushing disappointment when I finally get home and realize that the book *still* hasn't arrived...and when it does, I'm usually bitter about the way it's treated me on the way there. The impulsive side of my nature is something I've been trying (unsuccessfully) to tame for a long time, and it really bites me in the rear, here.

    Consequently, legal, purchased PDFs comprise the lion's share of my RPG collection. All of my printed RPGs were bought at my local gaming stores, and so were in my hand the moment I paid for them. I own a couple games that I loved so much as PDFs that I bought additional hard copies.

    There are innumerable games that I would like to buy, but are only available in hardcopy through mail order. I will not buy them, and we both lose. I know it's irrational, but it's so much easier on my psyche, and it's my choice as a consumer. If you insist on forcing me to wait for a hardcopy, at least give (or sell) me a PDF to tide me over in the meantime (I'm looking at you, Reign and Universalis)!

    In summary: for me, PDF == purchase. Further, PDF also often means "impulse buy!"

    Posted By: fnord3125from a legal standpoint, am I even allowed to print a copy or copies of .pdf format books I buy?
    I didn't see someone directly address this question, but the answer is self-evident:
    If the PDF allows you to print itself, they don't mind. PDF format has a means of "locking out" the option to print.

    So, for instance, when I offer GLASS, I'll have a free, printable shotGLASS PDF which is like Wolfe's "stripped down" version of the full GLASS book, which is meant for players to quick reference and is not very "user friendly" for defining a game type or creating custom Templates and Packages.

    The full GLASS book--art, examples, design notes, GM advice, genres ready-to-run--will probably never be offered as a PDF; and if I do offer it in that format--I also like the "Free PDF Version with Book Purchase" deal; maybe only for the direct-sales hardback buyers?--it won't be printable.

    In fact, a good trick for anti-piracy of PDFs (though time consuming) is to make each one you sell have a unique password just to be opened, which can't be modified. Then, if you see it show up online, it will have to come with a note/post/whatever telling the password to open... and you've busted the perp; you'd have a clear paper trail to the original buyer who made it publicly available. Not much of a stick, as the damages would be hard to prove in court and harder to collect from some individual who may be in another country... but you could at least give folks pause, and have a way to let the public shame them if you "leaked" the original source of the piracy on message boards and so forth.

    Hope this helps;
    David

    Posted By: JDCorleyIt is the DMCA that provides the challenge for users of PDF, not the "normal" copyright law. The DMCA bans (among other things) distributing copyrighted material (PDF games) or pieces thereof (a character sheet) by means of using hardware (a printer) to circumvent the protection (explicitly including digital file type, attempting to prevent people from copying a proprietary format to an easily distributable format like mp3 to distribute), unless explicitly authorized.
    \nUmm... I don't believe the DMCA applies here. To quote from the DMCA:\n
    Sec. 1201. Circumvention of copyright protection systems

    `(a) VIOLATIONS REGARDING CIRCUMVENTION OF TECHNOLOGICAL MEASURES- (1)(A) No person shall circumvent a technological measure that effectively controls access to a work protected under this title.
    \nThen, later on:\n
    (3) As used in this subsection--
    `(A) to `circumvent a technological measure' means to descramble a scrambled work, to decrypt an encrypted work, or otherwise to avoid, bypass, remove, deactivate, or impair a technological measure, without the authority of the copyright owner; and
    `(B) a technological measure `effectively controls access to a work' if the measure, in the ordinary course of its operation, requires the application of information, or a process or a treatment, with the authority of the copyright owner, to gain access to the work.
    \nNow, typical PDFs are not encrypted, and do not bar printing. Thus, in printing one, you are not "circumventing a technological measure" as the DMCA defines it. Further, unless your PDF is password-protected, it does not "effectively control access to a work" as per part B, since no application of information or process or treatment is required to gain access to the work. (Another example of an effective control would be the need to "authorize" a computer to play a song downloaded from iTunes. Using a process to transform the file into an MP3 would violate the DMCA, because it disables that "effective control". For a file that has no such protections, however, such as an unencrypted, non-password-protected PDF, turning the file into another format doesn't disable any "effective control" and thus isn't a violation.)

    The DMCA doesn't cover just anything that's published electronically. The publisher has to make some effort to protect the work. Now, if the PDF doesn't allow printing without a specific password, and you used some process to print it without that password, that would violate the DMCA. But simply printing a PDF that has no protection isn't a violation, as the law is written.

    (Now, since laws can be extended through precedents established by court decisions, it's possible that the DMCA has been extended to cover such things. But the text of the DMCA does not make them illegal.)

    (Edited to change "standard PDFs are not encrypted" to "typical PDFs are not encrypted", which is closer to what I mean, before someone says, "but the standard allows for encryption, so encrypted PDFs are 'standard'....")

    \n Trying to make any blanket statement about what is/isn't legal is difficult, since laws vary from place to place. Laws and their interpretations are also subject to change over time. Thus, this is going to be peppered liberally with "in general" and similar phrases.

    In general, though, making copies of a work that you legitimately own a copy of, for your own personal use, is considered to be a legitimate use. In some cases, it may be considered legal even if you merely have access to the work, but don't own it (e.g., videotaping a program on TV to watch it later). Thus, if you print out a game you own the PDF of so you can read it away from the computer, that's generally considered a legitimate use. (I say "legitimate" rather than "legal" because it may be considered to violate the letter of the law, but it's established enough in common law that a judge or jury would be likely to let you off, if someone tried to prosecute.)

    So, the "very legalistic standpoint" for this depends on whether you're concerned about the letter of the law, or how the law is likely to actually be applied in practice. (And, of course, on the jurisdiction you're in!)

    Making copies and distributing them without permission, however, is not generally legal. There are specific exceptions codified in US law as "fair use", but they're fairly narrow, and they're subject to interpretation by a judge and jury. In other words, don't bet on "fair use" without a lawyer's advice on your specific case. Thus, if you print out a game you own the PDF of and hand out copies to your play group, you're most likely violating the law.

    Now, if the PDF says "you may not make copies of this", but it isn't locked from printing... well, that's a grey area right now. Traditionally, a restriction like that wouldn't be considered valid, as it wasn't presented to you before you bought the product, and you didn't sign anything agreeing to it. However, software makers have been pushing very hard to get "shrinkwrap licenses" accepted as legal, and that has had some impact on the legal environment. Back in the 1800s, book publishers tried to put all sorts of restrictions as "licenses" in their books -- for example, that you couldn't sell the book to someone else. Those were struck down back then, leading to the formation of such concepts as "the right of first sale" (i.e., that once you've bought the book, it's yours to do with as you please, including selling it, giving it away, etc.). However, things are happening somewhat differently with shrinkwrap licenses, for many reasons, and we're likely going to see some of that bleeding into the book end through electronic books.

    And that's the situation pretty much as it currently stands in the US. Remember, though, that no question is ever finally settled in the law -- laws can be changed and can be reinterpreted. In the end, remember what Lao Tzu wrote -- "Law is the empty husk of loyalty and good faith." Act with those, and you should never find a publisher taking you to court.
