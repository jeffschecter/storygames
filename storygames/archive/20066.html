\n In wuxia folklore the idea of the Jianghu (trans: 'rivers and lakes') is the outlaw world beyond the centres of civilisation, similar to Sherwood Forest in Robin Hood stories, though in a more generalised sense. These are places where the heroes live and have adventures, away from the authorities based in towns and cities.

In an SF milieu the technology behind space travel makes this concept rather difficult. How does such a place exist in the physical world (as is my intention, rather than some philosophical or even cyberpunk setting - like the matrix) when you can travel around the galaxy in starships? What is it that would stop the galactic authority (in this case the Empire) from flying into Jianghu space and claiming it for itself?

    \n There are several ways of bridging interstellar space. The oldest of all are of course sub-lightspeed (essentially stationary to modern eye) arcologies and cryosleep ships, but the story doesn't end there.

    The Empire exists wherever the dragon gates are built and maintained, but that cannot occur everywhere: it is expensive and requires certain conditions, such as stars of the correct type, and the correct feng shui (or local gravitic field, or the cosmic river flow). Dragon gates occur where important worlds are and where the cosmic river flows; what worlds are serviced by gates become great from the Emperor's touch.

    In the meantime the Jianghu is served by lesser forms of interstellar travel, all inferior to the dragon gates, and virtually unknown in imperial space except in the use of the soldiers and such men who by necessity have to step beyond the facile ways.

    An example of a Jianghu space ship engine might involve a great mirrorshade globe growing like a lotus flower around a space ship as it nears the local sun, the shade reflecting away radiation and slowly collecting neutrons in its funnel. The ship plummets into the sun over a period of days or even weeks, twirling to gather neutrons into its neutron trap as the occupants sweat and wait; they know that neutrons cannot be stored long, and thus they have to be collected from a major outflow provided by a star, quickly, to gather a sufficient amount; they know that it is too late to turn back, as the ship no longer has the power to escape the gravitic pull of the merciless inferno; if all goes well, the ship's neutron warp latches and the ship launches off through the star itself, crossing interstellar space in an instant and arriving near any other star within a certain radius dependent upon the neutron fuel and the size of the warp latch. The mirrorshade lotus engine is relatively cheap, it can go anywhere there is a star, and while it is true that using it shortens your lifespan due to the radiation exposure, it is always worth it for those willing to suffer the indignity and waiting involved in this method of travel.

    Perhaps there are many such methods of interstellar travel, different engine plans with their own advantages and disadvantages. People who desire the stars in the Jianghu spaces, who want to connect to the Empire or other Jianghu worlds, will be willing to use these as a matter of course.

    If you are worried about that in general, one thing I would advise based on my experience running and play testing my own wuxia system (just what I have learned from observing players who have minimal grounding in the genre) is make the way powers work somewhat explicit in the book (even if it is just in a sidebar). I discovered one of the reasons people get turned off to wuxia is they think stuff like leaping over roofs and flowing Chi into someone's body to heal them are arbitrary things chosen for stylistic or plot reasons alone. Once they realize there is a kind of physics to the genre (and of course your physics may vary for the setting you are making) it really helps them understand it a lot more and they accept what the characters can do as being real. That is at least my experience.

    \n This project is wuxia in a broad sense. But it's not about the mechanics or science of Chi. In the way Feng Shui abstracts all that stuff (as opposed to Legends of the Wulin which wholly uses the ancient systems of five element theory as actual mechanics) I intend to do something similar. Also the powers/abilities aren't simply applications of Chi in the way kungfu (wushu) moves are. It is there to represent how much of the cool stuff a hero can do, but not directly requiring it in the same way 'Shaolin Mighty Fist' works. This setting is intended to be its own thing and not China In Space. For that reason I keep the use of Chinese terminology to a minimum and make use of anglicised versions which, elsewhere, would be wholly ignorant. So, in Storm Riders, the English readers know the main protagonists as Wind and Cloud, not as their Chinese names (or even the Chinese words for wind and cloud). That is the sensibility here. So its wuxia in the sense of heroic chivalry outlaw warrior renegades, but not as much in the sense of a simulation of the mechanics of Chi energy or Taoist cosmology.

    That said, this thread has morphed into a discussion more of how travel works. Which is fine. This question throws up one of the difficulties I have working on this: that a given setting element (eg space travel and how it's done) needs to be expressed in keeping with this theme. This of coruse can be done any number of ways, as has been suggested. All of those ideas are entirely valid, but for me it needs to be something simple if not obvious. By obvious I mean in keeping with the source material in a way that is what you would expect it to be: for example, Jedi in SW are, as has been suggested, based on wuxia/eastern lore and instead of swords they use laser swords - lightsabres - because thats the obvious sci fi representation of a sword hero with mystical powers.

    If you see what I mean!

    \n Here is the latest iteration. Not sure about two forms of energy, but it does infer Yin Yang balance in a matter/antimatter kind of way.

    "Interstellar travel is made possible with a Tao Engine. This device uses hyper-dimensional equations to cross between normal and Tao Space. This is Wu Wei Mathematics, created by the Immortals as the language of creation. Tao Shamans study relatively primitive levels of Wu Wei Mathematics (compared to the Immortals) using Chi. They are always searching for evidence of higher level mathematics in secret places left by the Immortals as yet untouched.
    A Tao Engine balances two sources of energy: Yin and Yang Matter. The former is derived from the cold depths of space while the latter burns in the heart of stars. Ships are equipped with Taiji Sails, designed to collect these resources directly: Yang matter is absorbed in the orbit of a sun, while Yin matter is absorbed in Dark regions - unfortunately such regions have also been corrupted turning Yin matter into the Evil Soul, worshipped by Dark Celestials. This state is the product of conflict and war throughout the history of the 10,000 Stars.
    Crews in Jianghu Space commonly eschew shipboard AI systems (particularly those sourced from the Empire). Out here a Tao Shaman is a better substitute, able to calculate more intuitively than the limited set of Wu Wei formulae possessed by an August Intellect. However within Imperial Space most shipping is based around established routes run by machines. The Empire has its own version of a Tao Shaman to serve in the Imperial Dragon Wing: elite space fighters able to use Wu Wei calculations in battle. Unfortunately the experience is draining and even the most powerful pilot surviving battle expires before his time."

    (Dragon Wing is based on the Dragonwing character concept from the Great Ten DC comic by Grant Morrison. A team of communist china superheroes; Dragonwing is an alien retroengineered plane that merges with its pilot).
