\n In another thread, one Mr Accounting For Taste made the following observation:\n
But if that's not the case, then the people who aren't having fun need to find a way to say "cut it out" without being big downers. My suggestion would be to talk about it as a group some night before you actually play; just sit down and hash out what everyone thinks is an appropriate way to handle these traits, maybe house-rule in some resource management scheme if that'd help keep everyone in line. Having this discussion outside the game is important, I think, because you don't want this to feel like a personal attack on anyone, and if you try to negotiate it in the middle of a scene there's really no other way it can be taken.
\nThis is a common suggestion: that you talk problems out as a group. The issue we're talking about, by the way, is exploiting a rule: specifically, shoehorning freeform traits into every conflict.

Yet I can't actually imagine having a conversation like this. I'm not sure I'd want to. It sounds rather unpleasant. Certainly, I can't recall having had such a conversation.

Has anyone had a conversation like this? How did it go? If you have an issue like this (not rulebreaking, not blatantly antisocial, but something that bothers someone at the table), how would you handle it?

Graham

    \n I've had this discussion, and I've called bullshit at the table. The latter is easier than the former and both rely on trust, friendship and goodwill to avoid genuine acrimony.

    Here's something I like - Archipelago II encourages players to revise when things don't hit the right tone. The phrase is "Try a different way", and the more you use it the better the game gets. When "Try a different way" is a regular and established part of play and is just something you do, this sort of awkward social dance becomes obsolete. We're playing a lot of Archipelago and slowly training ourselves to incorporate the phrase - it still feels a little alien but it absolutely works.

    \n As to the specific example that Graham has quoted, I think it's a good idea to flag the issue during play. Don't make a big deal about it, just mention you are concerned about it, and you might want to talk about it later, unless maybe you're concerned for no reason.

    Then maybe the other players will be a little more observant of their own behaviour. Not that it's that bad to just sit down and play a game without thinking deeply about it etc etc, but if you just point something out without making a big issue of it, others might say "I totally didn't notice, but hey, now that you point that out, I think I agree."

    I've been at tables where concerns like this were blatantly ignored, and it's a bit annoying, so now when an issue comes up, I'm happy to discuss it.

    \n To add to what Johnstone said above and David's examples...

    When we feel odd about something, we ask for clarification and specify why we feel odd about it. Maybe we're misunderstanding or need more context or the person needs help fleshing out what they are trying to say and why. Inquire but give the benefit of doubt, listen charitably, and offer suggestions.

    Sometimes ideas are like babies. We don't know what they will fully look like till we give them a chance to grow. Feed the babies first :-)

    And also pick your battles! How will the conversation add or detract from the overall game?

    I find we often voice light objections with the results being tweaked rather than negated. We often go from "what?" to "oh... cool!"

    \nI've had that conversation, comfortably and uncomfortably. The way we've been doing it in our group recently is actually really cool, and I think it gets us closer to the place we want to be at as an RPG group. If a GM or players notice that a certain behavior is coming up in a game that's weird, somebody just initiates a brief chat about it before game or in emails in downtime.

    For example, in my Farscape game (I'm running it with SOTC) I've noticed that people aren't really taking advantage of their aspects, and this sometimes causes rules confusion. I've also had some players tell me that they don't feel like Aspects do what they want them to, like people are unhappy with it and how people aren't using them consistently. So I've written up to start my game this weekend with a run-down of the rules, a q&a in case people are confused, and specific examples of how aspects could be used and then the opportunity to change any aspects that people aren't happy with. Simple.

    Another thing we periodically do as GM's is send out emails saying "hey, do you still like my game, what else would you like to do with your character, what plots would you like to see, what special weapon would you like bestowed upon you" stuff that's kinda meta that you can't ask during the game. Its a way to keep the game focused on what everybody wants.

    Sometimes people will, over dinner break or down time, joke about things people do that bug them a little, and that lets off a bit of steam, is non-confrontational, and helps us all play together better in our prospective styles by learning what everyone prefers. We have the emo roleplayer, the indie (me!), the politics person, the systems person, the immersionist... y'know, every group has different dynamics.

    Its all about communication. I think the more you have those conversations, the smoother the game will run, and people will have more fun doing it cause they all get input. I guess this isn't 100% what you're saying, Graham, but it deals with techniques you could apply to what you're saying.
