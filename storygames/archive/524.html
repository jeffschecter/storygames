OK, so this is split off from "Playing Across Gender Lines". I'd like to separate it from the "Playing Across Gender Lines" because, well, you don't have to have any cross-gender play for this to be important.

kleenestar wrote:
Everyone: how do you find that creating characters with dysfunctional sexual natures or histories of sexual abuse relates to the sex/gender of the characters you create? How does it come up in play? Do you only see abused characters in play, or do people ever play sexual abusers? And how do you deal with the out-of-game issues that it might raise for players with histories of sexual abuse of their own?
An intriguing topic, and I think an important one.

Histories of sexual abuse are rare for my characters. I think the only clear case was male -- Inferno was born a street urchin in Victorian London, and abused before he became a vampire. It didn't really come out in play except in his rages. There are a few characters who were perhaps similar but not strictly abusive. My original vision for Idelle (an apprentice in an Ars Magica campaign) was that she had been having sex with her master in a clearly unequal relationship, but the GM wasn't comfortable with that so it was axed. Baraud (an Agrikan priest in HarnMaster) had had a relationship with an older woman who engaged in sado-masochistic behavior (which is quite different from abuse but can edge into it).

An interesting case is my current PC, Dot. So I decided during the first season that she had been a teenage mother and put her child for adoption a few years prior to the campaign. (Again, not abuse, but a traumatic experience.) However, I also decided not to tell the GM about it. Basically, I wanted this as a bit of backstory and motivation; and I was afraid that bringing it up would encourage melodramatic and/or cliched use of it. It eventually came out at the end of the season, and it was highlighted when she got pregnant during Season Two.

I think the sexes of the PCs are important, in particular how I react to stereotypes. I like to have characters who touch on stereotypes, but say interesting things about them.

I've had two characters who were borderline abusive -- both male. One was a self-hating assassin (Cain) and one was an abusive policeman (Grimmond). There wasn't any sexual abuse during play (though there were violent acts). I was disturbed by Cain, primarily because the other players didn't take his violence seriously. In particular, at one point he kidnapped a woman doctor to help with his sisters -- stalking her, then holding her at gunpoint while telling her they were "going out on a date". On the flip side, Grimmond was nastier than Cain, but the GM and other players also reacted to him as nasty. Mostly, it was clear that he would hit anyone including women that he was involved with, but that never came up in play.

I only know one case where I know history of sexual abuse of a fellow players -- that's my wife. However, I realize that people whom I play with may well have histories of sexual abuse that I don't know about. So I do tread carefully. Hence abuse has never directly come up in-game. But it's certainly an issue I've considered and indirectly looked at.

    Oddly I've felt more comfortable playing the abusive characters more than the abused. Both characters that I can think of were written to be at least heartless and at most actually evil, and what abuse occured was well off-screen.

Lucretta was blythely complicit in plans that IIRC never came to fruition. But something about the degree of premeditation, and the fact that my character was actively advancing a really despicable scheme -- by no means was she hiding behind "just following orders" -- made this the more disturbing situation to play out.

    Neither of these characters were written with "sexual abuser" in their premise. But when the character could go that way, I took them there. I feel like it was easier for me to play that out than to play the victim of abuse. Because it was easier to play that out in a useful, interesting, authentic way, than it would have been to play a victim.

    bq. So, you speak of taking the character there -- how much premeditation did you use? Was it your idea, or a collaborative thing from the group? How did the others respond to it? Support/oppose your story?

    _In Nomine_ was a large ensemble game, mixed angels and demons, and Christian was a very regular character. He's still one of my favorite characters, and I always get a little wistful for the game. His Demon Prince was devoted to human souls achieving their dark fates. So, as part of the concept for Christian, I sketched out a role for him where he arranged big and dangerous raves for young teens. During play it developed into casual sexual abuse as a further characterization. New players came into the game all the time, and part of the challenge of that game was continually re-characterizing for new audiences. An Christian was really fun to play - he was flambouyantly, twistedly evil, and there was a challenge to putting some flesh on that.

    Lucretta was an elf in _The Shadow of Yesterday,_ purely selfish, ignorant of love for another. She was the house mistress in service to the an Ammani lord whose twisted understanding of goblins had led him to arrange a mock wedding, and especially the consumation, between his nubile servant girl and a slave goblin. The goblin, Pendajo is one of the characters I've played alongside that I like best. Lucretta's tasks involved managing the virgin, and making sure she was ready. And in complete understanding of what was going to happen, and her role in it, she was going to do a good job for the pride of it. The exact details of the game situation weren't available when I made the character - but as they came up I rose to the situation and charged ahead as an accomplice to forcible bestiality.

    Feedback from the group - with Christian I think I always played the line closely -- I wanted a reaction but I didn't want to push people out of the game. And even the other demon players were a little uncomfortably. Lucretta was a denizen of an online game, and I don't really remember getting much feedback - except in character from Pendejo. There was a great great great exchange between a goblin and an elf about "what is love?"

    Playing that kind of character is like throwing up tar. You look at what came out of you and it looks horrible, but you feel good for having gotten it out of you. For me though, I can really get into the evil of the characters, but I don't think I could manage it if I was victimizing another PC, or even a well drawn GMC.

Although her master died during her apprenticeship and she'd escaped to the Guernicus, her model of how to have an apprentice was very Flambeau. So she basically sat down and said, "How can I torment this young boy for his own good, to put him through the pain that I went through and has made me the woman I am today?" Then she broke both his arms. Things went on from there . . . though admittedly, again, it was never sexual abuse, which I suppose made it easier for me to deal with.

    Yes, that was a little bit uncomfortable for me, but also an incredibly interesting place to go with the character. I'm able to recognize that what I'm identifying with about the character is not her abusiveness, but her feelings of suffering and her desire to do what's right, even if that's a little twisted because of her upbringing.

    It's also been really interesting to play her growing and changing; it took her about ten years to win her first apprentice's loyalty and trust, and she realized that maybe that was because of how badly she hurt him. She's trying to take a different tack with her second apprentice, which is not working so well yet.
