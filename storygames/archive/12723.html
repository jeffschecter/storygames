\n
\n
\n
\n\nJason_Morningstar \n\n\n
\n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So you go to this intense convention for four days, you play your heart out, you get a lot of love and make a lot of friends. Everything is, quite literally, awesome. You are high on community, on games, on everything.

By the end you are also exhausted and probably sick. You come home, you crash. A few days pass.

Then you check the Internet, and somebody is wrong.

So this happens every year - there's some stupid fracas, some hand-wringing self-assessment, some low-stakes flame war about some damn thing, the week following Gen Con. Every year. Sometimes after Dreamation, too. I'm guessing the savage physical, mental and emotional high-low are at least partially to blame.

This is not the thread to ignite or continue the post Gen Con flame war, or to politely seek directions toward its burning core, I just wanted to point out some very human possible root causes for this annual event and to urge restraint and compassion like usual.

    \n I think there's also something to be said about seeing the community as a whole in action and seeing things that you personally consider unhealthy and broken.

    For example, I once attended a screen writer's conference. The panelists were excellent about communicating the hard realities of making it in Hollywood to the audience of would be screen writers. They were genuinely invested in helping the people there succeed. And yet, it was an extremely painful experience for me because I literally felt all the hopes and dreams being smashed into little pieces all around me. I decided I never wanted to go back again. I wasn't even there because I wanted to be a screen writer myself. If I HAD been there for that reason I would have come back extremely angry and full of self-righteous fury about how things need to change.

    When I attend my local con I usually spend a about a week afterwards trying to brain storm ways to get more involved in my local gamer community. How to cultivate certain relationships and practices that I think would be more healthy and foster more community among the local hobbyists. That enthusiasm eventually dies down or I actually do come up with practical solutions that would be too hard for me to implement on my own.

    So given that, I can imagine what happens when one is exposed to what is a non-insignificant portion of the hobby as a whole. The tribal instinct can't help but want to shape it into something more suitable to one's own needs and drives.

    Jesse

    \n Oh hey, another reason for the post-Gen Con annual fracas:

    People who are used to being words on a screen have to interact for real, in meat-space, as human beings. So soft prejudices surface, awkward moments occur, we're all forced to cooperate/cohabitate/whatever on a level that isn't quite as safe as an Internet forum. We occasionally fail at this.

    \n I think part of what's going on here is that we're game designers, by and large, and we try to hack systems to make them better. The problem is that with a community this large, coming to a consensus of what "better" means, or how to get there is problematic.

    GenCon gives us a LOT of data about how we're doing in our efforts to sell games at cons. I think that people reviewing their experiences and trying to discuss how things can be better for the next year is logical and healthy. But yeah, it's too bad that this conversation has to happen online and not in meat-space. I imagine it might be a lot more civil in meat-space, since reading charitably online is always difficult.

    \n I'm not so sure if this is an actual phenomenon that is in any way linked specifically to GenCon. I just think there are conflicts that arise in some stochastic pattern (like I even know what that means) in our hobby. It's just like politics in the workplace. They usually have some basis in reality but often get warped and transmogrified so by then end the actual conflict is far removed from the original source of the problem. When we meet together, it causes some of these real problems to arise and then afterwards they get ironed out online.

    I prefer the general rule "don't get butthurt". If someobody says something you find offensive, take a moment and try and read it in the best possible light. Usually you will find what they are saying and doing is not directed at you and is not meant to hurt you.
