\n Yesterday I posted a question about hugely powerful characters and how they could be used in a mid- to long-form game. The question was inspired by my experience playing the Darksiders demo in which one of the Horsemen of the Apocalypse, War, is made into a playable character with all the general hyper-power fantasy that entails.

The game has a pretty involved story by all accounts, but the thing that interests me the most are the Angelic Host that are introduced early on. They are, like War himself, beefed up versions of Old Testament angels, complete with armor and weapons to match the milieu.

Playing angels is nothing new. Mayfair Games made an angelic supplement for D&D a long while back and there are In Nomine and Engel that I can think of off the top of my head. Heck didn't TSR do a quasi-angelic thing with D&D before WotC took them over?

The problem? I've played none of these games and know next to nothing about them except that there are angels involved. What I want to know is: do they support play involving badass angels versus badass demons in a world of badassery? I'd even be interested in something that would develop along the lines of the movie, Gabriel. At least there is much the action involved.

Which is not to say that I'm totally disinterested in the "human" side of the equation. A game without anything beyond hyper-violent ass-kicking has a limited lifespan, as I said in the other thread. But I'd like to see something that amps up the classic conflict between Light and Dark to near-Biblical proportions. Do those games do that, or am I better off looking elsewhere?

    \n In Nomine is more about politics than anything, though you could have bad-ass angels in it I guess. Engel I'm less familiar with, but I think it's even less open to bad-assery than Innom. There's also Nobilis - kinda - but that doesn't really have a workable combat system at all so is probably entirely unsuitable. Erm... Last Exodus? You'd struggle to find a copy these days though. Or there's Suzeraine, not really angels but close. Supernatural maybe? I've not played it because I hate the Margaret Weiss base system, so I don't know how much they'll let you get way with in the way of non-human PCs.

    To be honest, most of the games that include angels don't let you play one for the simple reason that they would be too overpowered. You might end up having to MacGuyver something out of a suitable system rather than finding a ready-made option. I'd suggest taking Exalted or BESM as a base system and changing the setting behind it.

    \n
    \n
      \n
    1. Ofanim (lit: wheels Chayos) animals. These give the connotation of wild animals: dears or lions, not cows They have four faces, 12 wings, and one leg.
      2. \n
    2. Serafim (from the verb meaning "to burn")
      3. \n
    3. Chashmalim (no translation; in modern Hebrew, the word "chashmal" was drafted to mean "electricity", but that's a 19th cent invention)
      4. \n
    4. Ishim (ish means man, but "men" is "anashim"; saying "ishim" would be like saying "mans" in English. ["Ishim" is more like "Beings".])
      5. \n
    5. Keruvim (transliterated "cherub" in English). These are described variously in the Jewish Bible, and are not like our mental image of a "cherub". One should avoid the English parallel. In Genesis,two keruvim hold swords of revolving fire, guarding the entrance to Eden so that man does not re-enter. In Exodus, the top of the ark is adorned with two keruvim that have childlike faces and two huge wings that make a canopy over the ark. In Ezekiel, the four faces of the chayos have are described as being those of a man, a lion, an eagle and a keruv. Two verses later, the list replaces keruv with a bull. So they're associated with bulls somehow.
      6. \n
    \n

    \n If you want to be badass, but be constantly forced to make tough decisions about how to use your badass powers, you might consider using Dogs in the Vineyard. The game is already set up to be about judgment and use of power. It's not crunchy in the "list of awesome skills" sort of way, but it's crunchy in play (that is, its dice mechanic has lots of back and forth and stuff).

    Angel-hacking it isn't so hard:

    Replace any mention of guns with divine power.
    Change one of "big" or "excellent" in the equipment rules to "holy" or "from God" or something.
    Change the Town creation rules a bit to create very similar situations in whatever setting you want. Basically, NPCs making bad choices.
    Continue to leave God out of it, at least as a GM. The players can bring God into it all they want.

    \n Engel has troubles. Not only is it a d20 system, it's a d20 system from very early on in the d20 boom, when many untested and generally bad ideas made their way into print from people who should have known better. It really is not a very good adaptation of the d20 system. Although the power concepts are really cool (all White Wolf games, even the translations, have this advantage) and the setting and character ideas are really quite stylish (and easily adaptable to be actual angels instead of what they are, ahem), it really isn't that great.

    The original tarot-card based system, as amazing as that sounds, was also translated and is still probably available as a free download somewhere, and really, it's not much better. It has the Everway style of "pull a card, now interpret it to see what happens, welp, good luck with that" sort of rules, which is not much in the way of guidance or game.

    I own pretty much all of Engel and lurve it, but it never found its feet as a game.

    Spite is the suggestion so far that is flat brilliant, I had never thought of Spite before, now I need to get it out and look at it because, wow, that might just be the home run in this thread. Kudos.

    I think Burning Wheel in general might be a good fit, I would keep away from the blow-by-blow combat system for everything except combat betwen divine beings in order to keep being badass, but the Beliefs thing really is a great idea for this. In fact, you might bump up Beliefs a bit to make chasing them even more powerful.
