\n At Metatopia, on a whim (and to hang out with John S/@jenskot more), I went to a panel on GM-less/collaborative role-playing.

At one point in the discussion, I kinda had a flashback to stuff I was into/looking at some 10 years ago, most of it created by a dude named Ian Millington, a PhD in Artificial Intelligence from the UK. He even made a bunch of small games focusing on collaboration. This is, like, even before The Forge was a thing!

So I dug some stuff up in the Wayback Machine, so that I can link it to friends and folks who were at the discussion. Maybe others will find it interesting, too!

* A discussion on "ERGO", a project that led to an imagination of the GM as a distributed role shared by all players. Contains magnificent pictures for demonstration of the ideas:
http://web.archive.org/web/20020209085841/http://www.agon.com/ergo/collaborate.html

* Ten Principles for Collaborative Role-Playing:
http://web.archive.org/web/20030816232255/http://www.collaborativeroleplay.org/information/tenPrinciples

* A mini-essay on "Avatarism", and how it can be disappointing. Dated, but it places some of his ideas:
http://web.archive.org/web/20021106195111/http://www.collaborativeroleplay.org/information/mythQueen

* Ian then went to develop four complete, playable games demonstrating these principles. Have you heard of them? The PDFs are still archived, and accessible!
http://web.archive.org/web/20030606153011/http://www.collaborativeroleplay.org/games/ian

* And look at this, the reason I had this at the tip of my tounge! 11 years ago (wow) I contributed an article to @GBSteve 's "Places to Go, People to Be", where I interviewed Ian about this Collaborative Roleplay thing:
http://ptgptb.org/0020/coop.html

Anyway, a blast from the past, whose echoes are still relevant today!

-Andy

    \n I think the article about avatarism was really interesting, from a certain point of play. When playing collaborative storytelling, I noticed that there are three types of characters: active, supportive, and token characters. Active are those who engage in conflicts, that stir things up. Supportive support the active character, and tokens are just there to be moved around.

    I thought token characters are kind of useless to play. Useless in a sense that they only bring either flavor or works as a filler for the active/supportive characters to interact with (which makes "fillers" pretty much nothing more than flavor). But if you play to put the story first, I think it's important to treat the personal characters as tokens in a very Stanislavskijesque way, where you take a step out of the character and instead treat it as a puppet. Immersion (in character) has no room for this very specific kind of play. That's my thought anyway.
