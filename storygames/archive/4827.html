What can you DO TO a relationship?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nNovember 2007 \n edited November 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So, assume that we're tracking relationships between two people as mechanical objects.
\n
\nWhat can you DO to them? What are the maneuvers that you can perform upon them?
\n
\nTo prime the pump: Clearly you can Test them. This involves going after the least-empowered member of the relationship, and offering them a chance to get something valuable (perhaps, for example, more investment in a different relationship) in exchange for removing themselves yet more from the tested relationship. The result of successive tests where the relationship is abandoned would be a relationship that degenerates into only one person being invested in it ... rejection!
\n
\nExample: Momo and Toji are in love, but Sai (oh, wicked, evil Sai! When will you ever learn?) sets up an elaborate deception meant to convince Momo that Toji is two-timing her. Will Momo back away from Toji, or will she have faith in him?
\n
\nWhat else? What do people do with these "relationship" things, anyway?

    \n You can Escalate a romantic relationship along both the physical and emotional track. You can de-escalate along these tracks too, but it's harder without resetting back to zero.

    You can Measure a relationship ("Let's talk about how this relationship is going") to bring each member's perception of the relationship closer to the real state of the relationship, and to reveal to each participant what the other participant's perception is.

    You can Milk a relationship for some benefit, which may impose a cost on the relationship, but which may also strengthen the relationship. (Calling your friend in the middle of the night to take you to the hospital might strengthen. Calling him to take you to get cigarettes for the fourth week in a row might diminish.) This might be more something you do WITH a relationship rather than something you do TO it, though.

    \n I think it might be useful to look at this in terms of what are the potential goals one would have when performing an operation on a relationship.

    A. Strengthening the relationship.
    B. Weakening the relationship.
    C. Personal profit

    A & B are mutually exclusive of course, but C can be done in conjunction with either of the other two. So we have five distinct types of goals: A, B, C, AC, and BC.

    A = Nurturing
    B = Neglecting
    C = Exploiting
    AC = Manipulating
    BC = Sabotaging

    Missing from this model is an operator for changing the nature of a relationship. I posit that a better way to model this would be to create a new relationship alongside the old one. So if Bob is friends with Carol, but wants something more, he doesn't perform an operation on the "friendship" relationship, he creates a new "lovers" relationship which he then nurtures.

    Of course, these relationships can affect one another. Does Bob ruin their friendship by trying to get Carol to sleep with him? This brings up what is at least to my mind a key point: operations on relationships are not necessarily unary. They can also be binary.

    What if Bob's player had a choice? He could try to do a simple Nurture on the "lovers" relationship for potential modest gain, or he could put the "friendship" relationship at risk...maybe for potentially higher gain.

    This model can then easily be brought to bear in other social situations. I want Bonnie to try to drive a wedge between Kim and Monique. I can do this by having Bonnie talk general trash about Kim and hope that works. Or I can have Bonnie make nice with Monique so that she can more easily plant her seeds of friendship destruction. This is the opposite of what I did with Bob and Carol, instead of weakening one relationship for the final goal of strengthening another, I am strengthening one relationship with the final goal of weakening another. I'm not entirely sure that these are different operations, but they might be.

    Anyway, I'm not sure if these observations are useful with respect to your game, Tony, or even if all of what I typed is even fully compatible with itself, but whatever it was, this stream of consciousness has petered out for me at least for the moment. I hope it dislodged something of use.

    Why does fairness matter? It sounds eminently plausible, but I'm missing some of the steps in the reasoning.
    \nHumans, like monkeys and other animals, will behave with irrational hostility in the face of a situation perceived to be unfair. You might call it the beginning of ethics, and it's quite deeply ingrained. It's probably a specific way to be mean or test a relationship rather than an independent class of action, but it's fundamental enough that it might merit a mention.

    Being unfair on a regular basis - or, in less flattering terms, cheating and stealing - is pretty much the best way to get ahead in life with regards to cost/benefit analysis, so it makes sense that you'd have to evolve some serious negative consequences for a group to remain stable. After all, altruism may or may not pay off later, but cheating pays off right now 100% of the time.
