\n When I was at Arcanacon in Australia, we played a multiform game run by John Dalton. The way we played it at Arcanacon, it was mostly freeform/larp - but there were tabletop elements. We had a map of the ship lying on a central table; the GMs sometimes rolled dice, or told us secrets; once or twice they made judgment calls on situations in the game.

Now, when hearing about multiform, I thought it sounded pretty awkward. But it ran pretty smooth, and I'm thinking the idea has some merit. When running Society of Dreamers at Knutepunkt, people started getting up and acting out scenes physically; we sat down between scenes to set up scenes, make cards etc, then got up again for the next scene. It was actually pretty cool, and gave us breathing room between some intense scenes. Also, it was a very clear ritual to mark "end of scene". It let us connect and plan when that was necessary, and perform when that was necessary. I would never have expected this to work, but it did, and I'm thinking more and more about using multiform in design.

Does anyone else have experiences with this? I know Vulpinoid does - we've talked a bit about it in the Australian Freeform thread (which is a great thread, search for it). But anyone else?

    \n Matthijs -- The "jeepforged" version of Ganakagok that I've run a couple of times works almost exactly like that. Players are divided into teams (or "spirit tribes") each with a particular scene-setting or scene-guiding function; once the scene-setting decisions have been made, some players take on character roles and enact the scene. Once it's finished, everyone goes back into scene-setting mode. What's neat is that it's GMless, but the GM functions are justified in the fiction by players taking on the roles of the spirits that shape the world. -- Bill

    \n I have played in many such games at cons.

    These days I avoid them, as I found they were usually "playerless GM:ing" where the GM/GMs had a fixed agenda and could not or would not diverge from it under any circumstances.

    But I have played in games that mixed traditional RP with boardgames and LARP (with costumes and everything), yes.

    I wonder if there is a cultural divide here that could be interesting to explore.

    The Fear the Boot-guys (US gamers) seem to be of the opinion that you LARP if you ever get out of your chair during the game.

    I have played in gruops (Swedish gamers) where getting out of the chair is mandatory and even physical contact is expected, when playing "tabletop RPGs".

    \n I got to play The Butter Forger in Denmark and it fell along those lines. No dice involved, but we did have a beautiful map to place ourselves with and some written materials. We started out the game with standard descriptions of the scene and setting by the GM, some shared description from the players, and then we moved into the main plot where we acted out the trial.

    With only 5 players, 2 played the lawyers and 3 played all the various witnesses (with the GM as the judge), so at various times we would sit on the couch watching (and interjecting) then jumping up to play a character as needed. I got the impression that this kind of play was pretty normal in Denmark, semi-live they called it. It's a great way to play. I also really enjoyed it in The jeepforged version of Ganakagok.

    When I've played with some folks from Europe (listen as I lump them all together) meta talk was frowned upon, as breaking the flow or immersion, but I've seen it work just fine to move from one type of interaction to another. I wonder if it is something to get used to to move from dice to talk to acting, but once you do, it's a great thing.

    \n When I was part of a roleplaying club as an undergraduate, we had a one-shot game which was presented as a straight-up tabletop RPG, but then quickly turned into a "multiform". There was a group of people with different agendas and relationships, and the situation was that we'd all arrived at the family's home to read the will of the recently deceased head of the family.

    All of gameplay was talking and drama, no combat or physical action, so we pretty quickly started walking around to have separate conversations with various characters, and it was a lot of fun. The GM would gather us back to the table for important moments when we were all together (like the reading of the will), but most of the time we kind of moved around and talked to each other in character. There was no effort made to map the physical space we were in to the fictional space the characters were in, but getting up from the table allowed us to have all our "in-character" conversations at the same time, instead of sitting through other people talking, and made for a really fun game.

    \nIn most parts of Australia, this description could easily fit between two modes of play.

    "Small Scale Freeform"

    or

    "Multiform"

    The dividing line is whether any types of game mechanisms are used.

    If the moments when the GM gathered the players involved any type of mechanism (rolling of dice, drawing of cards, comparison of traits, voting by players, etc.), it'd probably fall on the multiform side of the divide. This is especially true if the GM says things in an "out of character voice" (eg. "Now you see this", "Now you feel that", "Roll to see if you notice XXX").

    If the GM in this type of situation engages the characters through a role of their own, continuing the immersion of the scene but adding narrative elements to the story through this portrayal, it'd more likely fall on the "Small Scale Freeform" side of the divide.

    Like most terminology, the problem lies with the fact that different people use the same terms to mean different things.

    I've participated in "multiforms" that resolve conflict through "karma", "drama" and "fortune". I've participated in "multiforms" with one GM, 2 GMs, more...(I don't think the style of play can work GM-less, it seems to need the presence of a strong dominant figure to pull things back on track and keep the momentum flowing). Some multiforms use established game systems from major companies, others use ad hoc rules designed by a session designer to reflect a very specific set of circumstances.

    Some multiforms use the prefix "multi-" to represent a fusion of two or more game systems in the same session...

    "We're running a game set in an asylum...it will last three hours. For fifteen minutes at the beginning of each hour we'll use "A Penny for my Thoughts" to reveal something about our characters...for the remaining 45 minutes of each hour, we roam the room and compare traits with one another if a fight breaks out, or if any type of conflict between characters emerges."

    Some GMs use the term "multiform" to simply mean "Live roleplaying in which costuming is not necessary".

    I hope I haven't confused the issue too much.
