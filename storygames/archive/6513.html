I adore the Matrix. I know this isn't Knife Fight and not about delving into our darkest secrets or airing our dirty laundry but THERE, I said it. I love everything about it.

I get a thrill watching these films, reading the comics, playing the games. I can't get enough.

I am watching the second film and te temple scene - I get chills over Morpheus's St. Crispin's Day Speech, I get a rise out of the dancing sequence. I love the spiritual overtones and the hard-tech of Zion.

I want, no NEED, to play the Matrix game. And no traditional ruleset will do. I need a Matrix Story Game. Who's gonna help me?

So lets start with this:
What themes can we explore? What are the central principles? What is the Matrix? ... or more importantly what sort of stories need to be told?

I don't want to talk about mechanics yet. I'd like to explore those themes. No doubt the different movie have different things to say. i'm cool with that. Let's take these movies apart (in a constructive and loving way) and see what sorts of stories lie there.

Thanks,

    \n Heya, eruditus, I'd love to see how you'd set the game: In Neo's era, with no Neo (so that the PCs can All Be Neo instead)? Before those events? After? It's kind of the problem in media where "The World Ends, And Everyone Knows It", and I guess the question is how much is that part of the movie Canon?

    So, I can't help too much on the story aspect, but here's a die trick that may help you.

    I designed a d6-based Matrix game after I saw the movie, back when I was living in Japan. Played it a few times, lotsa fun. Here's how I handled People vs Agents. You have to know d6 Star Wars to get this though (I based my game on that one):

    Humans used all d6es for rolls, and could use Force Points and the like to double their dice (to simulate that "we can think outside of the box" effect).

    Agents stats looked very similar to the PCs or other characters: But they used d10s for all their rolls, all the time: They are the pinnacle of "human" power within the confines of the code.
    And, here's the kicker, they could not use Force Points or the like: The are powerful overall, but can not "get outside the box".

    So, gross advantage goes to the Agents, but there are those rare moments when a human can kick their asses, given enough Force Points saved up (I think I called them "Matrix Points" or something)

    \n Ok, so I'm a go for themes from the animatrix. Roger that control. I'm just going to flat out present the ones I see, and we can go from there. That said if YOU HAVE NOT SEEN THE ANIMATRIX, SPOILERS ARE COMING
    \n
    \nBeyond
    \n- Kids enjoy the surreal environment of a matrix malfunction
    \nThis one isn't too heavily themed beyond the awe and wonder of the kids as they play, and the oppressive force of the agents/system as they locate and repair the rendering error. Though actually, this is one of my favorite three. Not sure how you could capture this in a game sans lettings the PCs be regular people who are suspicious of the nature of their world.
    \n
    \nA Detective Story
    \n- Trinity tries to bring another person out of the matrix
    \nHere is a rather interesting twist on presentation, but basically the same kind of thing is going on that happens in the first movie. I really wonder if this has any themes to explore beyond the same kind of suspicion about the world around them theme presented in Beyond.
    \n
    \nKid's Story
    \n- Neo sends cryptic messages to a kid, opening his eyes to the matrix
    \nSo this really is just like A Detective Story in heart, with the main difference being the feel and the attitude of the person who is being targeted by Neo. Same theme with a lot more emo + rebellious spirit.
    \n
    \nMatriculated
    \n- A rogue group attempts to educate AI machines with positive human traits
    \nThis is also one of my favorite three, and has pretty interesting ideas. Here we are exploring the nature of the machine AI, something that is almost untouched upon in the whole franchise.
    \n
    \nProgram
    \n- Rogue crew member runs a loyalty test 'program' on a fellow shipmate
    \nI liked this one a lot, but it just missed my top three. Here we are using the virtual existence program in a very cunning manner, something more interesting than the rather dry approaches made in the movies.
    \n
    \nWorld Record
    \n- Top athlete with the ability to see the matrix learns a tough lesson
    \nYea, I can't help it, this one is my favorite even in the top three. It really speaks out to unlimited strength of the human spirit, esp. in resistance to oppression. This is one of the major themes in pretty much all the movies and much of the franchise. We also get to see how the matrix works on the inside, how people play down the freak occurrence so that it fits into their world view (he must have been on drugs, and so on).
    \n
    \nOk, I left out the other three. I pretty much hate Final Flight and don't think it should have been in the collection at all, and I would have to go back and watch The Second Renaissance with a notebook to accurately surmise themes there.
