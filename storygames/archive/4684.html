\nPumpkin Bomb tries to emulate The Night Gwen Stacy Died in Spider-Man -- as Joe points out, the end of the Silver Age of comics -- by crafting a game of heroes, villains, and innocents, where the players portraying heroes and villains tactically choose different kinds of scenes to play out (think My Life With Master meets Bliss Stage), all in the hopes of improving their chances (and traits) before a final climactic showdown in which the innocents are directly threatened by the villains and protected by the heroes.

Fanboy Praise

The mechanics are pretty dern solid. The six different types of Villain scenes (Corrupt, Villainy, Scheme, Stalk, Confront, Threaten) and the five different types of Hero scenes (Inspire, Fight Crime, Connect, Investigate, Confront) all seem really cool and fun to play out. Each one manipulates character traits or general setting traits (Justice, Disorder) in measurable ways depending on how the scene plays out. The game uses a straightforward playing card-based mechanic like some of Joe's other games (Contenders) that is easy to pick up. When the endgame is triggered (mechanically or when the players decide it's time for the climatic scene), the fate of the innocents is decided by a final conflict between heroes and villains with some interesting ideas for an epilogue about how society is affected in the end.

Questions

Would it really be that much fun to play the hero? The villain looks a lot more interesting and exciting. Maybe that's just me.

Does the game really need a GM (called a Facilitator in the text, possibly because they don't have that much to do aside from framing a scene every now and again)? Could you have this work just as well by having a third player who actually played the Innocent(s) and had scenes of their own? Even if the Innocent can't affect the overall narrative in the same way, they are still important and their choices do matter, at least as far as they affect the choices of the hero and villain. They wouldn't have Gimmicks (superpowers that allow you to draw new cards), but otherwise they could do some interesting stuff.

I'm less sure about how well the game would work with more than a single hero and villain. The Gwen Stacy story, after all, is basically about three people. Would it work the same if you had multiple villains targeting multiple, potentially unrelated innocents? I'm not sure. Maybe this is a three player game?

You mention, at multiple points in the text, that the innocents might be ravaged, molested, or defiled, presumably by the villain. I guess, when reading the Gwen Stacy story, this didn't really seem like a possibility to me. In the modern age of "Women in Refrigerators" comics, definitely, but at the end of the Silver Age? I'm not so sure.

How I Feel About It

I'd be really excited to see it in play with 5-6 people to see how it would work. Perhaps my concerns are unfounded. That said, I'd be really excited to play it with just 3 players, with the Facilitator taking a strong role in playing the innocent(s), because the game seems so solid.
