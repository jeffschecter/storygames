    \n I'm working on a game called "Adventures of the Space Patrol," which I call a "retro-cute sci-fi" RPG. I was inspired by a Japanese game called Yuuyake Koyake, where you play an animal with the power to take human form in a town in rural Japan, and you help people and make friends. AotSP is meant to be kind of like that, but with some B-movie sci-fi tropes thrown in. I'm using kind of a "junior" variant of Spirit of the Century, though I think I'm going to have to play with the conflict rules some. The violent approach will be possible, but not at all optimum. I'm making a quick scenario generator using playing cards, and violence won't really help if Time Agent Tina is accidentally preventing Timmy from getting his homework done.

    In general, I think that RPGs don't have to be violent at all, but there usually needs to be come kind of conflict or something. Yuuyake Koyake is totally nonviolent, but you do deal with people who have problems, so there are conflicts to resolve, and occasionally between PCs. I've also been working on a game called "Raspberry Heaven," based on slice-of-life anime and manga, which is not only non-violent but largely conflict-free. It's about as non-traditional as you get, a sort of storytelling exercise with playing cards involved rather than an RPG in a conventional sense.

    \n Hi Jeff,

    I think the question of violence and RPGs is more complicated than just RPGs. Look at the tales that last, from Beowulf, the Greek tragedies and so on. Violence matters to us, because violence is where human beings stake, ultimately, what we're really willing to put on the line. We use stories -- and games with conflict -- to negotiate with ourselves and with society as a whole where we think we're going to draw those lines.

    However, I think a lot of games (especially in the past) said, "Okay... so, we have a way of fighting, and a setting, and some other stuff. We're all set."

    Which is a way of saying that the ways the Players could interact with each other were pretty limited to fighting.

    Look at Primetime Adventures as a new and different kind of game in this regard. There might be fights. But the interactions between players is about who's going to control the narrative, not whether or not the .45 is going to pierce someone's chest. If the Players want the game to not revolve around violence, then they can make that happen! By getting control of the narrative.

    I would, in fact, suggest you look at Primetime Adventures for the game your searching for right now. No, it's not a game with specific SF color. But you and your fellow players will control the color to your hearts' desire. And, again, what the players do with each other at the table (wrangling the narrative) will let you all determine whatever you want to be the circumstances, conflict and resolution -- using whatever qualities of human behavior you want. [Edit: oops... I just noticed you didn't want PtA... Sorry about that.]

    Other games you might want to look at along these lines are The Pool and Universalis.

    Another angle would be to use rules where the resolution mechanics still go action by action like more traditional games, but are abstracted enough to plug in any actions you want. For example, HeroQuest (the rules, not the Glorantha setting). Since a character sheet in HeroQuest is just a compilation of characteristics that the Players (as a group and as individuals) care about and want play to be about, it might work out really well for you guys.

    Given that these games don't have settings, you might consider using the setting building rules from Universalis or Shock: to construct the game's environment, no matter what game you end up playing.

    CK

    Fnord,

    \n

    Xenon: is the second in my science fiction seriesUntil yesterday, it was an accruing set of notes. Now it's been played (or rather, played with), and at its core, it does what it's supposed to do. It needs to be much, much stronger before anyone starts seeing it out in the world. I'm not even sure what goes after the colon yet. "Alien Science Fiction"? "Exploratory Science Fiction"? Something like that, but less crap.

    \n

    Think of it as a science fiction National Geographic. The players are mostly specialists: a geologist, a biologist, an anthropologist, at the minimum. There can probably be a few more, non-specialists or specialists in non-obvious professions. In our game, we had a journalist. I can see there being maybe a hunter or an engineer along. It depends on what you're after, really.

    \n

    The deal is, the geologist draws maps, the biologist describes life forms, the anthropologist describes culture. You make scientific observations and take inferences \u2014 "Hey! I see some sort of large animal over there! It's got an exoskeleton! I infer from this that there is large life on this planet." Other people then might disagree. Say I don't want there to be large animals on the planet, only microbes. So I say, as the biologist, "No, the gravity's too high on this planet to support exoskeletal life that big." The other players vote with other observations, "As a physicist, I can tell you that gravity is inconsistent on this planet due to wildly varying densities of rock, " for instance. That support gives you dice, other earlier observations give you dice too, NPCs can be recruited to support you, and you roll. You take the biggest die on each side of the argument, and that player's right. The opposition then tears up the assertion they made, and the winning observation remains. Note that the inference doesn't get recorded. If an NPC is recruited into an argument and is wrong, they lose credit and become worth less in the next argument.

    \n

    The idea is, you create planets in a way that both allows everyone to contribute and surprises all the players, and in the process make something beautiful, textured, and interesting.

    \n

    That last part is important, because the output of the game is fully Shock: compatible. You can use it to create a planet, then use that planet in Shock: as Minuti\xe6 if you want to tell stories that use that rich environment.
