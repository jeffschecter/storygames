\n Seriously.

When folks are impressed with sales of a thousand copies of a frankly great book (insert your favourite game here) we have to ask ourselves how come our promotion sucks so very hard. The game I'm getting the most mileage out of right now, that's generating tons of enthusiasm from two tables of avid and veteran gamers, has a distribution that's awful. It's beautiful, professional, in the price-point, and available. And I don't buy that it's not flying off the shelves because it's not D&D.

It's not flying off the shelves because:

a) for the most part it's not ON the shelves
b) I don't hear about it on the radio
c) I don't hear about it on TV
d) I don't see a poster for it in my game store
e) I don't see it represented at my con
f) it's not for sale anywhere anyone is going to go by accident

All this given that I *know* there are 8 players of this game within a fairly small radius of this city and yet somehow only a couple thousand sold volumes at best. That's basically selling it to your friends and family and their friends and family. Selling to people at, say, Gencon, is great and all but you're not exactly tapping into a rich vein -- a handful of enthusiasts willing to travel for their hobby but who really are not the best way to reach everyone else in the game-playing community, let alone people who might be potential first-time gamers.

So how come there's no chatter outside the (extremely insular) internet+RPG scene? Is real promotion too expensive? Certainly given how few units are selling without it, it seems like a pretty good investment -- I mean if you had three minutes on a local chat show for a reasonably sized city, you should expect a few hundred sales just from people who have the money to wonder about it in more detail. Do we just not have the contacts to swing exposure the way a book publisher can for its author? Is it really that hard?

These are serious questions, by the way -- I'm not saying we've all tried and we just suck because we're morons. I have no idea what the cost is to really promote something and it may well be that the capital you need to risk to do it at all sets the bar too high. It just seems like it's the essential failing in the small run games community. The games are good, they look good, and they are not crazy expensive -- they just seem to be invisible.

    \n Hell, I have an honours degree in Marketing and I work as a Marketing Manager and the ever-shifting sands of the subject still bamboozle me!!
    \n
    \nSome things do strike me though:
    \n
    \n1. Cost is obviously an issue, especially when it comes to simple things like enjoying economies of scale with regard to printing and postage. Even the production of a reasonably priced and professionally printed A2 poster for a shop can be quite costly if done in small quantities. Fliers and other printed promotional material can also incur some heavy costs through distribution. For example, say I was to deliver a Duty & Honour A2 poster and 100 A6 post-card style fliers out to 20 shops? I'd be looking at print costs of around $140 (ish), around $160 postage and maybe a conservative $80 packaging. Thats $380 on a 'simple' mail drop. With NO guarantee that the store/club/bookshop will actually do anything more than throw the materials in the bin.
    \n
    \n2. I'd also be looking at the targetting of the materials - whatever materials - and whether it was accurate - how much of my materials are being wasted. For example in the UK I'm unaware of any suitable radio or TV outlets that would even consider taking an interview about an up and coming roleplaying games designer and his penchant for Napoleonic history. And even if they did - what chance that gamers or gaming-curious listeners or viewers would be listening. I think thats why people do tend towards the safe stuff - conventions, ads in other games, promotions through reviews, podcasts and yes, the slow drip-drip-drip awareness build from sites like this.
    \n
    \n3. Even with these methods we don't really account the costs. I'll be toddling up to Edinburgh in March to Conpulsion (cheap pop) to demo the ass out of D&H.; The costs of that con - entry, travel, food and accommodation - should really be accounted as marketing costs. Because you damned well know that when WotC send a dozen members of staff to a convention with a metric f*ckton of product and a massive stand every single cent of that is accounted for out of their marketing budgets. When you think about it, consider the costs in accommodation, travel and stalls for say .. Green Ronin... to attend GenCon US? And then compare that to the marketing collateral available to most game designers. Its scary when those scales hit in.
    \n
    \nI think we shouldn't be too dismayed however - there is some stuff that the small press creators can do that can be better than the bigger companies. We can excel at customer service and customer facing communications. We can build truly supportive communities for our games which enhance the products rather than parody them. We can come together and share skills and opportunities and contacts ( like The Collective Endeavour in the UK) as well as experiences. Hell, we could - if we got our act together - create something like a mutualistic printing agreement. Everyone lodges a poster in a central file space and agrees that they will print out say 10 posters and take them down to their local game stores and distribute them - on the understanding that their materials are also being distributed by other people.
    \n
    \nWe don't have the monetary resources of the larger companies or the massive corps that back them but we do have a smorgasbord of smarts and energy and almost no red tape. Thats got to count for something.

    \n I think it's worthwhile to make a distinction here. Marketing is not "advertising." It might include advertising. And, Harper's bang on. Even Wizards would be, well, really stupid to advertise in most mass media channels (especially TV and radio).

    There are lots of other ways to market products. Viral marketing is taking advantage of word-of-mouth. Direct marketing is contacting established customers with updates and calls to action, presumably via email (or even just snail mail, which can get pricey). Give aways and contests are marketing. So is sponsorship (of conventions, for example). And buying pay-per-click campaigns on Google. Etc.

    Harper's also bang on about laziness. I don't think that's really the culprit. Sure, that might be some of it. But, it's more about whether things are worth a publisher's time. So far, not much has panned out that I'm aware of. Again, a couple exceptions, but there's precious little conventional wisdom.

    For example, one of the problems is that there are very few niche media outlets, and those that exist aren't especially well-suited to indie games.

    \n Reading this thread is making me think seriously about the marketing strategy that I have for Solipsist ... and realizing that there isn't one, and that I probably don't have the time, or the money, to have one.

    This is probably true for many games designers. You write the game, playtest it with people you know (primarily), promote it to friends and on communities like this, hoping it will get noticed, make a website, go to a few cons, list it on IPR, and that is as much as you can do. I don't have the free time to go to a dozen cons with my game, or to try and sell direct to 100 stores, or to make flyers and posters (and I have no idea where I would send them anyway if I did), and I don't have the contacts (which seem all important) to guarantee reviews by people who count, or APs on the Forge, or any other other viral methods that form an alternative to traditional marketing.

    So, realising this, I am thinking about why, and I think my answer is like Filip Luszczyk's above, because it isn't the point. I want to publish my game, and I will be happy if people like it and buy it (make that overjoyed) but I regard the printing costs as an upfront loss. I am realisitic enough to realise I will probably never make them back and I'm okay with that, because the designing and publishing is an end to itself. Heck I can make as much money as I am ever likely to make on Solipsist by a few hours of web design, or by sitting back and letting account renewals on my web game roll in. I am not publishing this game for money, and because of that plowing in more money (and more importantly time) to advertising to affect my profit margin doesn't seem to make sense.

    Which probably makes me an unmotivated amateur, a sort of weekend-designer or something.

    \n I've tried lots of different marketing and advertising strategies for Pelgrane Press, with varying degrees of success.

    The first thing to do is try to ensure that you can measure the success of your campaign in financial terms, otherwise you won't know if the time and money you've put in is worthwhile. You need to take a scientific approach. For example, I run the webzine See Page XX. I add random banner rotations, and pass on an affliate or source code to the store I'm linking to. When I do print advertising (infequent!) I add a voucher code useable at the checkout. Even if you don't have access to this, you can set up a unique url, not linked from anywhere else which you can measure responses to using web stats. This works with any advertising which includes a url.

    Rpgnow.com provides banner impressions and front page text messages on rotation, and offers percentage clickthroughs for both, as well as letting you know which sales come from where. You can soon discover which swipe line, image or sell text gets the best response, and you can use that elsewhere.

    Your existing customer base, as long as you treat them respectfully, are also open to properly targetted marketing. Short pithy emails which offer something tangible in addition to the marketing message get a decent response for the time and effort.

    Fred Hicks is very good at another kind of marketing - that is, communicating with customers and potential customers online - first you've got to find them. See this post of his for some very useful advice. But, don't go around fighting people online.

    Send out review copies by all means, but read other reviews to see if they are likely to be well inclined towards your game; or misunderstand it. The most telling phrase to look out for is "I am confused."

    Then there is piggybacking. Many resellers offer cheap or even free marketing opportunites, including IPR, rpgnow.com and your fellow publishers. For example, for Page XX, I always offer banners in rotation in exchange for articles.

    For Trail of Cthulhu, I've tried something new. I've placed an ad in Game Buyer targeted at retailers, and a banner ad on Alliance Games retailer ordering site. I've run a long-term development blog with lots of really nice artwork and communication with potential advocates and customers. When it releases I'll be sponsoring yog-sothoth.com and putting banners around the place.

    \n Hmm, I'll come around to my real opinion on the matter. Personally, I'd love to see more indie RPGs selling. Ruthless capitalism aside, more indie RPGs selling = more market share = more like-minded people with which to game. I forewarn now that I'm a business traveler and in the likely event that someone doesn't like what I have to say, I'll probably have to be silent about it. :)
    \n
    \nAnyway, as a buyer I find the indie RPG scene, at least with regards to the few shops I've encountered, to be somewhat "brand schizophrenic." There are a lot of games that are awesome, some not so great, and we don't have the wealth of information available to us to make the determination as we do when buying, say, a vacuum. More importantly, there also isn't a cohesive vision as far as the breadth and scope of the products that are sold; if you look at a successful company (I'm going to use Apple because everyone likes talking about it), they design and sell products which fulfill certain functions and avoid overloading them with features or forcing short-term obsolescence their other lines because, unsurprisingly, it cuts into their volume and obviously their bottom line. In other words, there are games on IPR that provide an entertainment:$$$ ratio that is obviating the need for future purchases. Why buy a new game when your group is chomping at the bit to keep playing one you already picked up? Yeah, I don't know either.
    \n
    \nThis takes me to my second point: RPGs can, and do, work themselves out of a job. If you sell a game that has so much depth to it that people feel they haven't sufficiently consumed it in a short enough period of time, you will derail future sales, either for yourself or someone else.
    \n
    \nNow, I'd scarcely be a consumer advocate if I suggested that you should pare down your games and sell additional "expansions" to those games for the sake of increasing market share, but I'd be remiss if I said I didn't think that having people return to the altar (e.g. fork over money) occasionally doesn't contribute substantially to a positive selection bias. In other words, people will play and enjoy your games more because they've paid more to play them. Take it with a grain of salt; its like every other content model ever devised for the RPG industry (e.g. based on hunches or extrapolated from markets that share enough similarity to be comparable, and enough differences to make the same comparisons dangerous!).
    \n
    \nSo, ultimately, I'm not sure that the goal of moving more books is readily achievable with conventional marketing strategies (though in my world, design is marketing is engineering is design...). Instead, I think that if the goal is to move more books and get more people playing indie RPGs, more attention has to be given to the overall product life cycle (meaning, how that product is released) and the overall market in which these games are being sold. The ugly truth of that is finding ways to space out releases and aggressively retire content that reduces overall sales (solvable with post-purchase surveys). Anyone care to wear the black hood? I didn't think so. :-)
    \n
    \nEasy to say, tough to do! My two cents.

      \n Well, For the past six years I have been promoting HinterWelt starting with Tales of Gaea up to the last book Roma Ture20. Here is what I have done and what has worked and what has not worked.

      Forum Posts: Well, nominal success but not entirely great. This could be a function of my posting style or the way I have structured HinterWelt. It does help to get adopters and works a lot like passive advertising (i.e. you get your name infront of people enough times they remember you).

      Internet driven advertising: Works for the PDF side. It worked far better for Roma True20 than for our Iridium and OGL books. This is partly due to brand recognition.

      Print ads: We have advertised in Dragon, KODT, PS232 and a handfull of smaller mags. KODT gave us a great return while the others a much lesser return with Dragon being a bust. We do not advertise like this much anymore since the market has changed considerably and I just think it does not pay as well.

      Convention Support: this is different from Con attendance. Usually we send books, maps and posters and in return get an ad in thier program. This has gone well with a few even resulting in staff running demos.

      Convention Attendance: This can work. It requires some knowledge of the con you are going to and especially the theme. I have tried at Gen Con to run Roma, Neb, Tales, Shades and Squirrels. Roma has never had anyone attend and neither has Tales. Shades and Neb depends. Out of the 10 or so events about 7 were near or at capacity, while the others had enough to run. Squirrels have always been at capacity and turning folks away. This invariably results in sold games.

      Retailer presence: This trumps all others for us. Getting into stores is tough but once you are there it is easy to grow. Make sure you talk to the owners and get their permission to use them as references to other stores. I have found the three tier distro to work for me as an advertising tool, reaching stores I might not on my own. I work outside the usual channels also with about 60 stores as direct customers. This can assure me sales out the gate with new products.

      Direct mailings: I just can't bring myself to spam. Heck, I have had customers ask for such things but it always weirds me out. As a result, I have not had much luck when pursuing this approach.

      I am sure there are more but those are the big ones. Oh, and remember it is good to get listed with new print releases in things like Game Buyer and whatever the Game Catalog is being called today.

      \n Hi Brad,

      I have a different take. I think that in some areas, indie game publishers (and RPG publishers in general) are actually far ahead of the curve in marketing techniques. Because game publishers, on the whole, have far fewer resources at their disposal than larger companies (and I mean larger companies, not larger game companies), they have been forced to adopt new technologies and new techniques.

      When it comes to social marketing specifically -- using message boards, social networking sites, blogs, podcasts, videocasts, etc., to reach the public -- game companies are on the bleeding edge. Larger companies pay market researchers lots of money to tell them the best practices for using the techniques that we've been perfecting for the past few years.

      It is only in traditional marketing and advertising that RPG publishers fall behind. And the problems in that area are difficult to overcome.

      Only large companies with products that have extremely broad appeal can afford to blast their messages out to anyone and everyone, and even then it's inefficient.

      It's much more efficient to find and target influentials (people that other people look to for advice when it comes to purchases). Influentials are not of a type either. They can be broken down into classic influentials and new influentials (among other ways).

      Classic influentials like to be the first to discover a message and communicate it to their friends. They tend to have a narrow field of expertise, like White Wolf games or D&D or D20, etc., or even just a single game.

      New influentials like to be the first to discover a message and communicate it to everyone, for instance via blogs, podcasts or reviews. New influentials tend to have a more generalist approach, harvesting information about many different topics from many different sources and packaging or repackaging it. Because new influentials spread their message to friends and strangers alike, the network of those to whom they spread their messages tend to be much larger.

      There are also combination influentials, who play both roles, but they are exceedingly rare.

      Anyway, both classic influentials and new influentials play very important roles in spreading messages. New influentials are the best at creating brand awareness. They learn that a product exists and communicate it to their networks.

      Classic influentials, on the other hand, tend to be the ones who tip the scales when it comes to the purchase decision. Friends seeking advice tend to go to the classic influential to learn more about a product and get the thumbs up or thumbs down.

      The problem RPGs companies face is that there aren't enough data available to allow them to discover who the influentials are and how best to approach them. Who are the people that seek out/buy/play games? What makes them interested in a game? What features increase the chances they'll buy or play a game? How do they find out about games? What sorts of Web sites do they visit? What magazines do they read? What age are they? What's their annual income?

      We assume we know the answers to some of these questions based on received wisdom, anecdotes and gut instinct. But we don't really know anything. Our assumptions could be wrong or wildly off base.

      The closest thing we have to this sort of information is a study Wizards of the Coast undertook in the late 1990s as it geared up for the release of D&D 3.0. Other than that, we have very little information. And that information is necessary to developing metrics with which to measure the effectiveness of marketing and to ensure that resources are being used as efficiently as possible.

      If we were to attempt to do more marketing, developing this sort of data would be a necessary first step. I'd be interested in seeing an organization like GAMA create an annual study to gather this sort of information, if it's feasible.

      Recap
      ---------

      I think it's probably the case that we should admit that it is *possible* that there actually may be no solution to the question: How do I make it big selling my RPG? It's possible.       Factors of Prior Success
      ------------------------------------

      The first question to ask is: What made RPGs popular to begin with?

      Well, we start with, of course, D&D. It became pretty popular. Why? When I was a kid in high school D&D 1st Edition (the three magical parchment books) first came out. What made our group gravitate toward it?

      It was The New Thing. It was fun. It was imagination-enhancing. It was cheap. As a GM you could build out on the ideas yourself, and they presented themselves as extensible. It was something you could do with your friends. It gave you something to think about between games. It encouraged teamwork. It encouraged intelligence. It put you in a magical Other place. Note: D&D appealed to the Brainiacs (wargammers, chess players, and fantasyists). Note: Jocks did not play D&D. Note: about 25% of our group were girls, and none of them were what you would call "Hot" chicks, though many of them were actually very attractive. But not "Hot", if you get the distinction. The most successful people in our town were the D&D players. Most of them went on to become captains of industry, inventors, doctors and lawyers, and so on. Ok. So that was the demographic of the early RPGers, as far as the anecdotal example of my town was concerned. Success oriented Brainiacs played D&D. It was an interesting time, one aching for some sort of creative release. Remember we were post the Hyper-Creativity of Jimi Hendrix, the Doors, The Beatles, Quick Silver Messenger Service, The Doobie Brothers, and the Great Cultural Revolution was drifting further and further into the past. And D&D came along and filled a certain vaguely discernible void. But most of all, I'm going to say that we liked RPGs because they encouraged creativity and imagination unlike anything we'd ever run across before. Now you could be The Hero of Your Own Amazing Adventure Tales! And as a GM you could be The Mythopoetic Creator of Entire Fantasy Universes! It was Improv Theater wrapped around a Wargame nested inside of a Literary Epic. That's powerful stuff. No wonder we went gah gah for it.

      The Problem
      -------------------
      Now lets step back from our contemporary society for a minute and fly to 50,000 feet. Look down. What do you see? What I see is a dark cloud of intellectual smog pouring out of TV and the movies. It's a smog that says "It's cool to be stupid as hell. It's smart to be corrupt as hell. It's necessary to be sexy as hell". That message is casting its slouching dismal smog over the creative imaginative souls of the world, of which there are an outstanding few wandering around. There are a few would-be luminaries on the hilltops gleaming yet, but if we compare today's creative enterprises and their effects with those of a former time we might notice that their is an order of magnitude less ... enthusiasm. Our creative aspirations have become ... small. We used to want to save the world. Now... we want to make a cool RPG. We may not even care if anyone plays it. Don't be offended. We're all in the same boat here.

      So, lets ask another basic question: What makes RPGs fun? Imagination? Challenge? Social intercourse? Teamwork? Self Expression? The Spirit of Adventure?

      Now lets look around at the competition for RPGs (which I'm surprised no one has mentioned - even if you disagree, which many do, it should still probably be mentioned). WoW. EverQuest. Call of Asheron. Eve. All of these games and more have taken the place of what the P&P RPG used to be when I was growing up. They're the New Thing. Visual-Colorful. Easy to learn. Easy to play. Nix the following: Imagination-Enhancing. What these games are not, as we all know, is Creative for the Payers. You just run around and you can only do what the MMORPG programmers let you do, and pathetically enough in my opinion, a lot of that turns out to be Game-Churn.

      P&P RPGs? Do whatever your imagination inspires. They are open ended, creative enterprises with often unpredictable results and, when played in such a way, often produce amazing stories that we think mean something to us.

      \n Mike: Saying "if only RPGs weren't perceived as geeky they'd be widely popular" is missing the point so many ways from Sunday I don't know what to say.

      Geeky isn't the issue. The issues are:

      1) RPGs require social organization which is absolutely insane. Getting exactly the exact same group of people together regularly for 4-6 hour stretches? Jesus fuck. I have no idea how any of us get this done.

      2) RPGs require intense creative, intellectual, and logistical commitment. Most people, if they have that level of creative and intellectual drive, do things like pursue theatre, write books, etc. Things which might eventually get them acclaim, profit, or groupies. That aside, many people just don't have that level of creative energy.

      3) RPGs are presented in a stupidly hard to learn format.

      4) RPG texts are often totally impenetrable, even to a committed learner, without prior context.

      5) RPG players are generally ashamed, and thus make their hobby out to be something fetishistic and shameful.

      6) RPGs are still deep in the embrace of the wargame, which is its own pursuit, also awesome, but really doesn't necessarily need to be a part of ours (what if we could have the peanut butter without the jelly?)

      Any individual one of these problems is way more important than advertising, viral marketing campaigns, "getting into stores," or the perceived geekiness of RPGs.

      \n I have some things to say as a newcomer to indie RPGs (I've played RPGs before, but over 15 years ago).

      I've recently tried introducing folks to 'story games', for reasons relevant to VBWyrde's 'saving the world' post earlier in this thread. I see these games as an opportunity to teach story-telling and creativity skills, something I do as part of my living. As the blind leading the blind, I can personally speak to the hurdles in the way of connecting in-person rpgs to 'the mainstream'.\n
      The issues are:

      1) RPGs require social organization which is absolutely insane. Getting exactly the exact same group of people together regularly for 4-6 hour stretches? Jesus fuck. I have no idea how any of us get this done.
      \nWhen I read the opening sections of Polaris to a group of players, the several hours commitment of a half dozen plus sessions of play caused one player to express disbelief, even when I tried to explain that you could just play a single session. Others didn't react so badly, but it definitely remains an issue for some, at least until the games really justify the time commitment. Which leads to some of the more pressing issues...\n
      2) RPGs require intense creative, intellectual, and logistical commitment. Most people, if they have that level of creative and intellectual drive, do things like pursue theatre, write books, etc. Things which might eventually get them acclaim, profit, or groupies. That aside, many people just don't have that level of creative energy.
      \nTrue. So I've found that the game itself needs to 'get out of the way' as much as possible during the learning curve in order for folks to still have creative energy for the game itself. In trying to learn Polaris, most of the players had raging headaches at the end of a very short first session (that never saw a sequel). This frustrated me to no end, as I see a tremendous amount of gold in Polaris' structure, but learning it proved incredibly difficult for all of us, and eventually took the wind out of our sails. Even Primetime Adventures created this effect, though to a far lesser extent, and we managed additional sessions beyond the first.\n
      3) RPGs are presented in a stupidly hard to learn format.
      \nYES. Who cares if you put this games in every bookstore in the country? Nobody could play them without help, or extremely generous, faithful, and cooperative players, high on vicodin.\n
      4) RPG texts are often totally impenetrable, even to a committed learner, without prior context.
      \nAbsolutely.\n
      5) RPG players are generally ashamed, and thus make their hobby out to be something fetishistic and shameful.
      \nNot such a big deal for me, as what passes for 'RPG' on computers I personally find so juvenile and unsatsifying that I just call these games 'story games' or 'storytelling games' and expect folks to accept them as such. No offense to those WoW fans out there. \n
      6) RPGs are still deep in the embrace of the wargame, which is its own pursuit, also awesome, but really doesn't necessarily need to be a part of ours (what if we could have the peanut butter without the jelly?)
      \nSure, I've found enough story game designs such as Polaris, PTA, 1001 Nights, and DitV, that I don't worry about this. Learning to play even one of these games well suffices as a challenge for the present. I think the first session of Shab al-Hiri Roach and Polaris at one game night (I had about 8 people attend) drove away about half of the players out of sheer intimidation. So I've acted much more cautiously about the complexity of games I offer, sticking to PTA and 1001 Nights probably.

      So my question: When will designers start designing games so that a newbie can feasibly and ENJOYABLY learn to play them, without downloading dozens of cheat sheets and slumming around the internet for weeks?

      I mean these questions and comments in the most supportive way possible: I love the story games that the whole indie scene has made, I love their potential. I just wish they treated newbies more empathetically, and made learning them fun too.

      \n As others have said, RPGs sell the way they do because of the size of the market. When you grab a market estimate and divide it by your projected gross, interesting things happen. If you divide it by *somebody else's* projected gross, it gets even more interesting, even if you use rough numbers.

      So if you want to sell more than the standard number of copies for a success, you must:

      1) Create something that takes the existing market by storm.
      2) Create something that expands the market, or creates a new market.

      "Indie" games collectively do #1 -- and ironically, supplement treadmill dynamics seem to have helped quite a bit, given that the core community really treats them like one game line. Little indie games seem to do what WotC hopes the D20STL would: provide continuing focus on the brand without the leaders having to spend any money.

      They do not do #2. Designers talk about it, but it's still the same guys who hated their D&D or Vampire game or something. And of course, most of the marketing is focused on the existing community. The market is so small that this can be effective without much effort, but it can also backfire, because people get annoyed once the promo posts and fanspam reach a certain density. This should be a familiar sensation, because it's what happened to White Wolf.

      I think the primary problem at this point is the perception of a closed community. Roleplaying is more successful than it's ever been; tabletop RPGs, not so much. The difference is that other roleplaying modes are open communities by virtue of an online platform and either sheer size (WoW) or an invitation to play with no speedbumps, such as purchasing and learning a rules set (journal/forum based fan RP, which people don't talk about nearly enough).

      A game's design is not a particularly big deal. There is no magic system or method that will make new players play your game, and not another. I could tell you why this misperception exists, but I'm not permitted to do so. I've introduced and run a traditional RPG for total non-gamers in a 15 minute span, and the concept of protagonism was irrelevant. Sheer complexity is the only limit, and even that is only a matter of time. For Aeternal Legends, I worked with a non-gamer to get her to the point where she led the group in about half an hour. Mage? Same amount of time, though I didn't have to make characters then.

      When introductions work, it's because of:
      1) The sense that the GM/organizer really wants the players to have fun.
      2) A staggered introduction to concepts and systems.
      3) Immediately introducing a cooperative environment.
      4) Applying the cooperative dynamic to help a player through stalling points in decision-making.
      5) Applying game systems loosely and establishing this as a standard practice and not "training wheels."
      6) Not wasting time with people who don't really want to participate.

      I remember what I posted about the 15 minutes thing way back, it was critiqued on the basis that I was working with people who were naturally inclined to play along. I was amazed; why the fuck would I want to push something and people who weren't interested? But you do get people who want to force the idea of playing a certain way, and people who want to make a game conform to their expectations. As in all marketing, one of the tough decisions -- and one of the rewarding ones -- is identifying people who are not your target and not serving them.

      You get the odd jackass who thinks they're punishing you by telling you that they won't buy your stuff, but they're a waste of time in the first place. Accordingly, when someone like Gareth is the way he is, consider that, well, he knows that the people who are really pissed off with him would never spend a dime on his stuff in the first place. You will still catch these people, but not through direct outreach. They'll end up playing and buying because others -- the people you do want -- are doing it.

      So decide who you really want to reach, help them have fun and know more if they're receptive and really, don't bother with anyone else.
