\n For those just joining us, this is a spin off of Death #3 raised in this thread:\n
Posted By: Jonathan WaltonDEATH #3: Fuck antagonism, make a game that's not about conflict. This one is harder for me to think about. So much of our media revolves around conflict and there's an inherent tendency to want to simulate the conflict in rules. But, you know, maybe conflict isn't what your story is really about. Say you're running a buddy cop game where people get shot at and maybe even one of the cops dies. That's all cool, but... maybe the game is about the relationship that develops between these two cops, and all the mechanics are focused on that. Maybe the conflicts that happen in the game are just color, y'know?
\nI'm not sure if this was in the big list of cows, but I wanted to hone in on it, because I've seen mentions of Jeepform that it's been done and very well, to pick on Jason, like this:\n
Posted By: Jason MorningstarJust to echo Claudia and Christian, look to Jeepform and related traditions for Death #3. I played a Death #3 game at Viking Con for the first time. I am going to be playing lots more because Death #3 ishot sauce.
\nAnd I feel like calling bullshit. Claudia linked to this game, as an example and it's rife with conflict as far as I can tell, with only examples of play to go on. There's the conflict between players and the GM when the players try to make the story about a conflict instead of about love. In fact the game is about this when it asks "Where will they go wrong?" ("they" being the players)

But even on another level, if you play it perfect, there still must be conflict. Maybe, probably, it's "How to I focus and express love?" which can come from the player, or the character.

I don't want to pick on that one game, or even Jeepform (though the latter is what I'd like to hear more examples of). My gut reaction is "You're just saying there's no conflict because there's no villains or antagonist or guns, but there's still conflict, either internal or subtle, pulling and tugging on players or characters because that's what you need to tell a story (at least an interesting one)."

I want to be proven wrong and have my mind opened. Show some play examples. What really happens when there's no conflict?

    \n I am skeptical of any RPG that does not have conflict in it. I am reminded of the Robbinson novel with the can of beans "instead of" a character. The can of beans becomes a character. Stories need characters. Stories and games need conflict. It's an essential element.

    However, I can totally see a game that does not have rules that are about conflict. It is very possible to have a game that describes rules for how things happen in the fiction, and conflicts can emerge organically from that. This is hard. It requires very carefully-balanced rules which create conflict in the fruitful void. They tend to look and feel 'fiddly' and it takes a while to grasp what's going on, because the thing you care about is not directly manipulated by rules about what you care about. You have to kind of get a feel for how things work, and then you start seeing how you can make things work to affect the thing you care about.

    Additionally, I am very hopeful for games which discard the oft-repeated definition of 'conflict' as 'a conflict of interest between the players at the table as to how the fiction will proceed.' I find this definition to be utter bollocks. There are a thousand good reasons to build, manipulate, and resolve conflicts that aren't a dick-waving contest between you and your fellow players. This is a good venue, I hope, for more collaborative play, and I'm looking in this direction for some of the Next Big Things in design.

    Posted By: Alvin Frewer"How to I focus and express love?"
    \nIn the interest of talking about what conflict is, I don't think this is one. It's a question. Until there's more than one suggested answer on the table, there's no conflict.

    Maybe someone's internal state/psychology/drives/motivations will suggest to them two or more answers; then they have an internal conflict. Maybe the characters will suggest two or more answers. Then there's a character conflict. Maybe the setting will suggest two or more answers. Then the characters are in conflict with the setting. And so forth. Point being, one facet or piece of the conflict puzzle is a question with multiple answers where those answers are being actively considered. If I ask myself "How do I focus and express love?" and I have a solid answer which I'm confident in, there really isn't a conflict, even though there are multiple possibilities.

    \n Wow. This thread went somewhere else entirely. I'm not even sure where to begin.

    From the other thread:\n
    However, what I said is not that the story would have "no conflict," but that the conflicts are not what the story is ABOUT. They are not the key focus the way they are in most roleplaying games. You don't need to have a system for resolving them or anyone assigned to provide an antagonistic role because that's not really all that important. I don't think this is just throwing a sheet over conflict and pretending it's not there while we whistle past it. It could be because we're genuinely interested in other things, like exploring themes or character development, and don't really care how the conflict resolves. I think a movie like The Fountain is a great example. Or the first part of 2001: A Space Odyssey (before the conflict with Hal), with the monkeys and the business people in space. Is it really interesting to us which monkeys win the waterhole? Do we really care whether the businessman impresses his friends or is able to hide his real motivations for going to the moon? No, not really. Those are not interesting conflicts. But we are still interested in exploring what happens, so we focus on things other than conflict. Like, wow that monkey's really acting crazy. Or, sweet, let's have people, like, walk on the ceiling and stuff, because they're in SPACE!
    \nSo when people are insisting "Well, you have to have conflict, but what I really mean was no mechanics about conflict" I think you're watering down my point. Whether you have mechanics about them or not, you can still decide that play is about conflicts... or you can decide that play is about something else. Do I need to write up an example of play?

    Here's a scene (not a really good scene, but it's just an example):

    P1: Okay, so you're walking along the corridor of your spaceship, right? When you look out the windows and you see that aliens are attacking!
    P2: Oh, crap! What do they look like? They're the ones who abandoned that city we found, with really spikey glowing architecture?
    P1: Definitely the same ones, or at least these ships look similar. Kinda like spiney crystal manta rays made of light.
    P2: Oh, do they have tails like manta rays too, that can, like, knock spaceships about with a single swipe?
    P1: Oh yeah. That's totally great. They just took out one of your escort ships. Smack!
    P2: Sucks for us! Dude, it should totally crash into the side of our ship too! Send people flying everywhere!
    P1: Yeah, it totally causes the antigrav generator to cut out, now you're floating down the hallway!
    P2: Ha! Okay, well we probably have retractable emergency ladders on the wall, for just these kinds of situations.
    P1: Sure. And there's a pole that runs alongside them, which you can clip onto with the carabiner on your belt.
    P2: Cool, that'll keep me from floating away every time the ship shudders. So I guess I'll make my way towards the flight deck.
    etc.

    Both players seem like they're having a blast. And there's... no conflict in this scene. The aliens are attacking! Ships are getting knocked from the sky! Conflict is happening all around them! But the character in question is not really facing conflict. And the players aren't fighting over the narrative, they're building on each other. I think you can totally have entire games that are structured like this, either with a rules system that supports this kind of play, or just because the players have decided that's how they want to do things. They want to focus on, say, cool little bits of the setting or exciting description or performing kick-ass stunts or emulating a certain genre or creating a really cool story, rather than conflict.

    Note, I'm not saying this style of play is better or even enjoyable for everyone, I'm just saying that it exists and is enjoyable for many people. It's also not a style of play that is commonly designed for.

    Posted By: Alvin FrewerBut certain questions are starting points for conflict, precisely because they aren't being answered by a single person in their head.
    \nI'm just pointing out that the starting point and the conflict are not the same thing. The question may be intimately tied into and related to the conflict, but it isn't the conflict, at least not entirely. In order to construe the love question as a conflict, we need to also include that there are two people involved in the situation which raises the question, and that one of these people is going to take action on a feeling of love, which may or not be received well. All of that is part of the conflict.

    The question and the situation are both necessary but not sufficient conditions on their own. Together, maybe they're sufficient to constitute a conflict, but it's hard for me to rule out things I haven't thought of that are required. From that basis, I can imagine games that are about situation without questions. How one would do it, I don't know -- but if you could, there would be no conflict.

    \nWeirdly, the existence of a joke about the subject supports Jonathon Walton's assertion that this type of play is favored by some segment of players.

    I'd go further and say that alargesegment of players like this sort of playstyle.

    I think the commonly expressed "pure" form of this playstyle mostly exists in the minds of theoreticians. Actual fudging is one part of a complex process with aspects of interpersonal negotiation and on the spot hermeneutics. It is not really just the GM imposing his/her will no matter what comes up on the dice.

    Based on my intentional (i.e. I went and watched strangers and took notes) observations of traditional games, the general steps are as follows:

    1) Negotiation of the event and the system to be used. ("Is there a red button on the console?" "Can I use my button mashing skill?" "It's a small button, roll 15 or higher." "I pictured myself right next to it, though." "Okay, 12." "Okay then.")
    2) Resolution using the system of the agreed upon event. (*Button mashing roll is 11*)
    3) Interpretation of the results. ("Hm. You hit the button, but it only sticks bout halfway -- you'll need another turn to get that sucker in.")
    4) Integration into the flow of play. ("I whale on the button." "That's right -- you can't dodge the can of pasta!")

    Note that in #3, a roll of 12+ probably is a success and a roll of 5 might be an absolute failure, because the GM isn't inspired by those numbers in the same way as s/he is inspired by an 11. And in many games, all dice -- even "secret" rolls -- are in the open and are interpreted openly and collectively, because interpretation is often smoothed by collaboration. But it is almost *never* a case of the GM ruling a straightforward success regardless of what number shows up. Numbers have a lot of power to inspire the imagination.

    These are the things that happen in real traditional RPGs, as played by people I do not personally know.
