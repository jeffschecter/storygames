\n I was wondering if anyone knew of a program that could function as a deck of cards? I want to test out a design of mine that uses custom Cards as part of the mechanics.
\n
\nI would simply print them out but that is a long and expensive process and I don't want to waste those resources on something that isn't a sure thing.
\n
\nPlayers draw from the same two decks. Cards drawn are not revealed and instead form new mini-decks for each player (as opposed to forming a 'hand'). Later, these mini-decks are drawn from and revealed... some of the cards may form a 'hand' (very few), others go into a discard pile (most) and others still are laid face up before the player (very, very few).
\n
\nSo is there a program out there that can help me?

    \n I don't know any programs that can do that, but I do have a suggestion. When making my own cards, I generally get cue cards from the dollar store or a discount store. You can easily cut them in half to get smaller cards if you find their original size too bulky. They're fairly cheap and can easily be replaced if one is lost or destroyed in some coffee spilling pizza grease accident.
    \n
    \nI've done a few card sets for some of my games and I find having a dummy deck is easy before going to the length of designing them all. Using a simple pencil or pen to write on the card so the players know what they are is easiest for me, but I'm pretty low key on investment until I find it works. Then I'll spend money.

    \n For different card backs, I recommend getting different colored index cards. Most contemporary printers can print on index cards. You can print off all of your cards. If you print two cards per index card, then you can easily cut them 4 or 5 at a time using a razor blade or exacto knife.

    \n Vassal www.vassalengine.org has "deck of cards" modules. There's even one made for playing PTA (that obviously has a standard deck of cards).
    \n
    \nJust rename the module as .zip and open it, hacking it swapping the images on the cards is not hard. By the same token, editing a module in vassal (if you need to change the number of the cards in the deck, for example) is not that hard and does not involve programming.
