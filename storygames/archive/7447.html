    \n The point is that you can actually use it for anything. Heart of Darkness brings up other colonial games - against Zulus or Fuzzy Wuzzys or Afghans. If the enemy consists of hordes, then 3:16 works with almost no changes - just change the weapons and descriptions (though NPCs will have to fill in the slack to get a logical enough number of kills at range).

    As I mentioned above, rather than using a narrative punctuated with encounters that are played out to a conclusion, 3:16 uses a literary or cinematic approach, where there everything - including the battles - are really just scenes that provide opportunities for the protagonists to describe how they succeed or fail at something - and in the process fleshing out themselves, their enemies, and the world. As opposed to a normal rpg, where the scenes are what happens in between the battles, in 3:16 the battles are the scenes.

    I thought of Superheroes because of the difficulty that genre creates with most game systems. Characters are incredibly powerful - but at the same time, battles are almost never to the death - or even to the point of capturing the opponent. It's almost impossible to replicate that kind of encounter with a combat system. Hence, you need a descriptive system like 3:16 instead.

    Of course, a supervillain might be multiple threats in one.

    And while Agon looks like a very interesting RPG - it's much more traditional. 3:16 could be adapted to the same topic very nicely.

    \n Hmm, I feel like the gradually emergent "rotten to the very core" aspects of the premise are subverted when the problematic nature of what the troopers are doing is front-and-center from the get go. Seems like one of the major themes of the game is that, in the beginning, being a trooper and killing stuff is awesome, but if the players are sitting there wincing about killing Zulus or Sioux in the beginning -- as most folks will probably be doing in this day and age, unlike their contemporary counterparts -- it doesn't give much room for the moral "Fruitful Void" to grow over the course of play. The players don't share in the troopers' experience of waking up to the reality of their mission. This may be a problem for some players even with 3:16's standard "killing alien monsters" premise, as it was for Keith Sears over in this thread, if the horror of alien genocide is already too morally repugnant for them.

    I think, if you're going to preserve some of the core themes of the game, what makes it different from Agon or playing Inquisitor or a squad-based hack of 40K, you need to begin with a situation that seems perfectly legit or at least somewhat reasonable, instead of going right into icky my-trooper-is-a-sociopathic-mass-murderer territory. You can definitely end up there, no question, but starting somewhere more comfortable might be important, I suspect.

    For that reason, my first instinct for hacks -- though I have no plans of running one anytime soon -- involves supposedly righteous wars (for one or both sides), like the American Civil War or Operation Desert Storm. Vietnam might work, except the American consciousness already has a generally negative assessment of wars that we either lost or didn't outright win. Korea might work better, actually. So you start out giving the squad perfectly reasonable objectives: take out this sniper, take that hill, secure some ammo and supplies, take out a enemy outpost. And then, over time, the horror of what people do to each other in a war, even a war in which people initially believe they are there for perfectly good reasons, will emerge. You wouldn't even have to rub the players' faces in it. It would just happen naturally. And I think that would be much more satisfying, for me, than starting with a premise that was already really problematic.

    \n An Authority type big kaboom ultrakill supers thing might work.

    Assume a scaling to suit the milieu- a Threat Token here represents a gigaton robot or a giant brain made from the little brains of genetically engineered (pissed off) psychic lab rats. Whatever. 'Regular Trooper Shit' is replaced with hudrum "I'm cool" type powers that don't do much in a fight. FA covers punching through tanks. NFA covers teleporting to the moon. Rather than weapons and armor you get powers which mechanically do about the same. The ballance of FA and NFA defines you role in the team (Blaster, leader, brick, ninja, mascot), and that gives you power picks. Strength and Weakness work the same. Replace "Kills" with "Ratings", as your supers are also mediawhores, and more viewers mean more funding mean more kewl pwoerz.

    \n"Hmm, I feel like the gradually emergent "rotten to the very core" aspects of the premise are subverted when the problematic nature of what the troopers are doing is front-and-center from the get go."

    I find some 3:16 battle reports unpleasant to read because of that - but the only aspect of the system itself that helps create that feeling is that Threats can represent large numbers of creatures. The setting that has them minding their own businesses before the troops arrive contributes the most. Change the setting so a Threat only equals one individual - or so that you are already at war with them (anything from defending your colonial territories from indigenous peoples to the Bug Attacks of Star Ship Troopers) - and you ameliorate that.

    The theme of 3:16 may be darkly humorous, but the system is open to being used in a lot of ways.

    \n E. Larsen,

    You could rule that the aliens couldn't attack at longer ranges, but that would be a bit of a rules drift. Mechanically, range has no effect on the alien's ability to do damage. There are a few different ways to describe it narratively so that it works out, though.

    In the game I ran on Monday, the enemy had no ranged ability, but did have the ability Rapid Movement, which I parlayed narratively into how they would dart in from longer ranges, coming from all directions, to strike and then run back out to farther range. It wasn't a mechanical use of the ability, it was just a matter of how I used the ability to explain why range made no difference to them.

    Alternately, they may have means of transportation not suggested or explained by a special power that allows them to attack at close range but keep the troopers at farther range. Further, maybe they don't have projectile or ray attacks, but they can throw rocks, create directionalized earth tremors.. Or maybe they have smaller, expendible parasite-like creatures that they send in to do damage for them.
