\n
\n So imagine the following (toy) rule:
\n
\nAngie sets aside one of her dice as a "helping die" for Bob. This creates an Angie-Bob pool (starting at one die). Angie is the most recent contributor, and as such she can neither contribute nor take the pool. Bob is not the most recent contributor. As such, he may choose to take all the dice in the pool and add them to one of his rolls. OR, at some later point, he may choose to contribute another die to the pool.
\n
\nIf Bob contributes another die then he becomes the most recent contributor ... and now Angie is the one who has the opportunity to take all the dice in the pool and add them to one of her rolls.
\n
\nI can easily see social situations where neither side would want to take the dice from the pool: Angie wouldn't want to deprive Bob in order to benefit herself ... even if Bob was telling her in the most definite terms to do so.
\n
\nAssume, for the sake of argument, that Angie's roll cannot ever, directly or indirectly, come back to benefit Bob as well. It's for her, and her alone.
\n
\nThat'd be an interesting little dynamic. I wonder how it would turn out in play.

    There is a level where you can talk about sentimental effect and a level where it really is all about the cruel math. It seems to me that you want this tit for tat with multiple ways of using it. Good, multiple ways of doing it are about rational strategies. One of the know factors in games with tit for tat elements (like successive Prisoners Dilemma) is the increasing role of trust and cooperation through multiple iterations.

    Let's look at A and B (no C, here)
    A gives a die to B. B can spend the die or kick it back. The rational thing for B to do is to spend the die *unless* B can trust A to either kick the pool back or spend the higher amount on something that benefits B. The dice pool can *only* rationally escalate when one party trusts the other to act for mutual benefit. If this is the case, it isn't important who has the pool, only that it is maxed out as much as possible. If this isn't the case, there is no reason to even use the system. If there is a penalty for contributing this lowers the maximum to dice - some minimum competence, but the maximum is still desirable. If there is no trust, then any participation is even more undesireable.

    Now you can say, "But people aren't rational!" and you'd be right, but it's not necessarily a statement that you can hang a system on.

    \n Yeah, Tony's found the flaw in the math, which is that the value of helping others, if it can be +1, can be more than +1.
    \n
    \nA lot of this comes down to what the goal of the game is for the participant. If, for example, it's to show themselves tactically superior to the other players, then the -1 is actually probably higher than -1, and people won't use this. If, on the other hand, the goal of the game is to make value statements to each other, then the value of helping others could be quite high for the individual.
    \n
    \nThat's the problem here, Tony, you haven't given a context for the mechanic. Basically it'll totally depend on that context as to what the values are, and whether or not it'll work to produce the behaviors that you're looking for.
    \n
    \nAnd that's to say nothing of the range of players who may be playing. For instance, if the game is about value statements, some players may want to make a statement about selfishness, in which case the value for giving is very low.
    \n
    \nAs for the "pariah cookie" effect... it doesn't happen to some of us (and JD, how "Feeling" of you). I almost always eat the last piece of pizza. I see it siting there, I estimate if everyone has had their fill, and, if so, I ask if I can have it (I have, apparently, an infinite capacity to consume pizza). The "pariah" effect comes about because of a very specific reason... people believe that they will be resented for taking the last item. Worse, they believe that if they ask if they can have the last piece, that other people, not wanting to be resented themselves for being selfish, will tell you that it's OK for you to take that last piece, even if they don't mean it. In which case you're putting stress on them by making them lie to seem nice. So people don't ask, beacuse they don't want to put that stress on the other person, in case they do want it, forcing them either to contest you for the piece or to lie.
    \n
    \nI just think that the negative utility to those people is simply far less than the positive of my own satisfaction for eating that piece of pizza. After all... they might actually not want it. So I ask, assume people are being honest, and take the piece if they say so. And if they do want it, I don't fear working that out between us... maybe we can split it.
    \n
    \nCall this Mike's Pizza Version of Pascal's Gamble.
    \n
    \nIn the case of the dice pool, we can't split the die, but like Malcolm points out, we might trade off on iterative instances. I'll take the benefit this time, understanding that it's your turn next time. (Nash may have been crazy at times, but he was a genius, too, to have been on the Rand team that discovered the difference between single and iterative plays of games).
    \n
    \nIt's interesting that this phenomenon is sorta the reverse of the Tragedy of the Commons.
