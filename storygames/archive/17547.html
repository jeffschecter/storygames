\n Hi all,

Wednesday will be the first session of my storygame-style Vampire run.

Two players just finished a Burning Wheel game with me, so they know how I like to roll, but one player is new and I'm not so sure about his background. To get them all on the same page I've created a player handout with an Agenda and Principles for them.

(This is, of course, inspired by *World. It's also heavily derivative of Jonathan Walton's principles for Burning Wheel.)

Does this read right to you? Is there something else screaming out to be included?

    \n Vampire as I understand it (Thanks JDCorley!) and The Burning Wheel share a lot of commonalities. For one thing, both need a really strong GM vision at the start. The Adventure Burner goes into this a lot. After playing a bunch of BW, I can tell you the most successful game I ran was the one with the most coherent campaign concept at the start.

    That said, in actual play, I feel like what's good for playing BW isn't necessarily what's good for Vampire. But I can't speak a lot to what would be good with vampire do to lack of experience with it. For one thing, BW's rules are REALLY interconnected, REALLY deeply thought out, and do a lot to drive things. Vampire needs much more GM prodding from the look of it.

    \n One extra principle I thought about was "Keep to the city limits". The game is set in one city (Bristol, England), and I'll be prepping for that. Beyond the city, I won't have the NPCs, locations or event ideas I need, so I'd like them to agree not to leave.

    Conversely, they have the run of the city, and I'll roll with whatever they do there. I can give them this freedom, and still make the game work, because of the restriction.

    I'm not happy with those words, however. They're mundane and restricted, compared to the inspirational and open-ended tone of the other principles. Can any of you do better?

    \n The traditional vampire answer to "how do you keep them constrained within the city" is that outside the city, werewolves eat you. So, "nowhere to run." On that principle, you tell them what the stakes are in leaving the city and, if they go out, illustrate the point to reinforce the notion: introduce new threats and opportunities.
