\n So, on the design group Paul Tevis & I are a part of, Master Mines (yes, I'm going to keep plugging it, it seems to be working), I talked about how I wasn't sure if my game that currently has a GM should really be a GM-less game. So, I'm trying to better understand GM-less games. Paul suggested I start a thread here to get some input.\n
Posted By: Ryan MacklinSo I'm thinking about the GM-less mode and how it related to what I've been doing with the game using a GM. That means I've been mentally analyzing the GM-less games I have (Polaris, Contenders, The Shab-al-Hiri Roach, uh, off the top of my head) and will pour through them tonight and tomorrow, but the conclusion I'm drawing at this moment is that to make a GM-less game work, you have to be telling separate stories in parallel. In a GM-ed game, you can tell one story with several participants at the same time.
\n
Posted By: Paul TevisI've have confused thoughts about GM-less games that I can't quite articulate right now. Let's just say that my experience with both Polaris and The Roach has led me to conclude that you can have GM-less games that do something other than separate stories in parallel. You can tell one story with several participants at the same time, however, it will look slightly different than that story would look if you had a GM. My own experience with GM-less games is somewhat limited, and I think that other people\xe2\u20ac\u2122s perspective (especially, say, Jason Morningstar's) would be useful.
\nFirst of all, I've played Polaris once (though that was enough to count now as one of my favorite games) and The Shab-al-Hiri Roach once. I have Contenders in my library. (Yes, I need more in my library. This will be remedied at GenCon.) So, that's the experience I have and that experience tells me the following:

GM-less games inherently require more structure than a game with a GM. Now this is not at bad this at all, and I like games with structure as much as I like games that force me to be the structure. But this is there for the next point.

Games in general need someone to get the ball rolling in a given "scene." Often a GM's job is to get that ball rolling. In Full Light, Full Steam it's the players' job to get that ball rolling, but each time it's a specific player. We tend to call this "Scene Framing", and the GM-less games I've ready place the job of scene framing on the individuals in a "everyone takes their turn on this" sense.

Furthermore, as the authority is distributed, you're generally given a limited authority to frame scenes. In The Roach, you frame scenes around your character. You can bring other characters in, sure, but the scene is about your character and need not involve anyone else. In Polaris, you either frame scenes for yourself or for whom you're playing the adversarial role. In Contenders, it actually uses the term "Spotlight character" to say that when you're framing a scene, it's all about you (even if you bring others into the scene).

So, that leads me to believe that it's necessary for a GM-less game to involve heavy scene-framing and spotlight characters. To continue that idea, it feels like in order to have that feel organic in play, said players should have their own storylines that, at best, intertwine with the other characters. The farthest form this idea, I think, is The Roach, and I think that only works because it's stated to be a competitive RPG. In a collaborative, GM-less game, it seems like having the group constantly together would be an exercise in constantly stepping on one another's toes.

My Life With Master, which has a GM, feels like a GM-less game (to where I pulled it off the shelf and put it into my GM-less games research pile) because it's about individual stories that happen to perhaps cross paths more than a group-story.

So, here's what I'm asking: Tell me why I'm wrong. Blow my assumptions out of the water. (Alternatively, if by some fluke I know what I'm talking about, could you tell me why?) Help me better understand this topic I'm heavily interested in.

Thank you.

    \n First of all a couple of threads about distributing authority, that link to other discussions as well: here and here.

    You're distributing the same authority differently. That makes some things harder and others easier. Structure is obviously helpful to GMless games because that's a bit of authority usually relegated to the GM. Your comments about framing scenes around individuals strikes me as good advice in any game, but it is often codified in GMless ones (again, because of authority distribution issues). I'd be careful not to pigeon hole what is essentially a new way or organizing games along broad lines, because some clever dude is definitely going to shoot you down in the fullness of time. GMless games require heavy scene framing? I guess the current crop point in this direction, but that's because we're learning as we go.

    I can tell you from happy experience that a GMless game that enthusiastically focuses on the player characters as a functional unit can be successful and satisfying.

    Posted By: Ryan MacklinGM-less games inherently require more structure than a game with a GM.
    \nAs has been mentioned, this is not entirely accurate. GMed games have about as much structure -- it's just a lot of it is either implied or habitual on the part of the play group. But when writing a GMless game, your audience is almost certainly already enculturated in GMed games, and therefore you need to be more explicit about things.

    Additionally, though, GMed games require a more delicate and balanced structure than GMed games. There are counterexamples and well-designed, well-balanced GMed games, but at the end of the day, GMed games can rely on GM fiat. It's an option available to GMed games that is not available to GMless games. A lot of GMed games use GM-as-final-arbiter as a sort of last-ditch safety valve. GMless games can, at best, appeal to group consensus, and that's nowhere near as simple or clear as what one guy says.

    Alternately, you can rely on that group consensus and assume that your play groups are capable of productively settling their differences. I would submit that that is just offloading design work on your play group (just like telling the GM to 'create a story' and not giving him any actual tools with which to do that).

    And on that topic, you mentioned FLFS, which by default is GMed, but can be played GMless, and actually plays that way rather well. The dice mechanic becomes more explicitly narration-shuffling than stakes-resolution, and everybody picks NPCs to portray when they get framed into scenes. The work needed to convert to GMless is minimal, and I will probably be better elaborating what needs to be done when I get around to second edition.\n
    Posted By: Ryan MacklinSo, that leads me to believe that it's necessary for a GM-less game to involve heavy scene-framing and spotlight characters.
    \nHere, I'll be pedantic, now! Every game requires scene-framing; many GMed games just give that whole omnibus of responsibility to the GM. But somebody has to say where the characters are and what's happening, if only to kick things off.

    Spotlight characters... meh. Universalis has no GM and does not have spotlight characters. They're handy and all -- but they're generally handy, in GMless and GMed games. You could easily do a GMed game without spotlight characters.\n
    Posted By: Ryan Macklinto make a GM-less game work, you have to be telling separate stories in parallel.
    \nI think you're seeing that because, fundamentally, separate and parallel stories are easier for a group of people to create than a single story that everybody collaborates on.     Posted By: talysmanThe trick to GM-less games is, in reality, you're just distributing the GM tasks in some way, so the real thing you need to be looking at is how the tasks are distributed.
    \nNo, this isn't true. I mean, it will work as a way of building a game, but it's really only a small part of the design space.

    In a typical rpg, the GM is the guy who has veto power over everything that happens within the narrative, and so the "split the GM role" idea says that you can distribute the veto power, calling it "narrative authority". Basically, you're making a rule for your game that the other players have to accept what the guy with "narrative authority" says.

    But you don't have to do it that way. A different way of conceptualizing how things work is to use the principle of social proof. Ask yourself: how do you know that something -- call it X -- happened within the narrative? The answer is: you know that X happened when you see the other players act as if X happened. This is a case where your beliefs is set by what you think the beliefs of the other players is.

    This gives you a new way of thinking about how to structure play. You can say, "the purpose of these mechanics is to make it seductive to accept the contributions of the other players," or "the purpose of this other mechanic is to make it more obvious when a player accepts someone else's fictional invention." I think that this is a more useful mode of thinking when designing a "GM-less" game, because it lets you set aside the question of authority entirely.

    (Of course, I don't think this is the only other possible conceptualization -- it's just one that I have found very useful.)

Either way, you can distribute this in various ways: make the power of social proof fleeting, changing hands from moment to moment, based on either a rigid structure (rotate around the table) or a mechanical bonus (award dice or bonuses when you see something you like happen,) or whatever.

    \n

    \n

    In the post I linked to, I actually spent more time not on veto power or social proof, but on elements like setting, characters, situation, system, and color, because although affirmation and veto are certainly the most powerful of the traditional GM tasks, contribution and interpretation form the majority of thoses tasks. A traditional GMed game is notable mainly in that one person describes the setting, introduces supporting characters, creates the adversity, interprets the rules, resolves rules disputes, and fleshes out most of the other details, while the other players traditional contribute only a very tiny portion related to "their" characters. As games challenge this traditional arrangement in various ways, they either soften the GM's power by giving other players some small power to contribute in these areas, or they farm out one or more of these tasks to other people, or they decentralize the tasks completely or rotate the entire package of tasks among the players, using a variety of methods.

    \n

    To focus this more directly to what Ryan is looking for: whether or not a GM-less game is destined to turn into parallel stories probably depends specifically on how adversity is handled. For example, Rune rotates adversity and setting as a package, with each player taking turns designing situations for the group as a whole and basing the difficulty on the number of characters in the adventuring party. This forces players to keep their characters together, mainly because there are no provisions on what to do if the party splits. In Polaris, by contrast, the current player of adversity (Mistaken) does not scale the threat except through Drama, so there is no pressure to keep characters together (I forget: is there even a benefit of having other knights in the scene?)

    Posted By: talysmanA traditional GMed game is notable mainly in that one person describes the setting, introduces supporting characters, creates the adversity, interprets the rules, resolves rules disputes, and fleshes out most of the other details, while the other players traditional contribute only a very tiny portion related to "their" characters.
    \nthat I don't consider GM powers even in traditional games -- most notably interpreting the rules and resolving rules disputes, which are not powers I have much interest in signing away. When analyzing GM powers at this deep level, I think it's important to remember that at its heart a roleplaying game is just a social get-together, and groups are generally pretty good at nominating the people best suited to filling social lubricant roles such as "interprets the rules" (which is really "makes decisions everyone can agree on") and "resolves rules disputes" (which is really "resolves social conflicts and restores peace"). Trying to take all these roles away from the people who have earned them essentially by merit and pin them to the guy who's good at "creating the adversary" (a role which is unfortunately pretty unique to roleplaying games) isn't going to improve anything (at best, everybody will ignore it) and at worst create seriously dysfunctional interactions. In practice, I think you'll find that when these situations DO come up in most functioning trad game groups, the GM steps back and the person who happens to be aces at that particular lubricant takes over seamlessly, without anybody talking about it or even thinking about it very much -- if they did think about it, they'd either be uncomfortable about the weird power dynamic or the direct violation of the rules in the book in favor of the rules in everybody's heads.\n
    Posted By: Ryan MacklinYou might see how I'm not talking about it being GM-less yet.     \n It might help to not think of the game as "GMless." Like there was something there that you're taking away.

    There is no inherent need for a GM in a role-playing game. So just write your game, say who does what when\xe2\u20ac\xa0, and playtest it. If it isn't fun, or if you see people doing things that are not part of your who does what when, then clearly you need different rules.

    \xe2\u20ac\xa0 "Who does what when" probably needs some unpacking. This is simple
    \xe2\u20ac\xa2 Delineating each player's rights as to what they can say and do.
    \xe2\u20ac\xa2 Delineating any changes in this, and how they happen.
    \xe2\u20ac\xa2 Delineating what effect one player's words and actions have on another players rights to say and do things.
    \xe2\u20ac\xa2 Delineating any time that a player is required to say or do something.

    Basically, the rules.
