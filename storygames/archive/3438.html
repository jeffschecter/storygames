Patch 2 version 1
Whenever infected dice are rolled in a conflict there is a chance that one or more of the players will gain an infected die, indicating that their character has either become infected or the infection they already have has become worse.

Any player, including the GM, who rolls a 1 on an infected die in the last round of a conflict must choose one PC to gain an infected die. A player may choose either their own or someone else's PC, but they must choose a PC who was involved in the conflict. Naturally, the GM doesn't have a PC to give an infected die to, so they must choose someone else's.

Each player chooses a maximum of one PC to assign one die to, no matter how many 1s they may have rolled in the last round of the conflict.

    \n Hiya Darcy,

    Sorry it's taken me so long to get back to you on this. I plead both laziness and vacation. And laziness stemming from vacation. Lemmie see if I can answer all your questions to your satisfaction.

    Distribute

    The six cards is a fixed resource. No matter how many players you have, there will be exactly six of those NPC cards to draw from. There's been some concern from my playtesters over this. A few folks have expressed the desire to scale the number of cards with the number of players (meaning more NPC cards for more players). However, I'm pretty sure the fixed resource thing does exactly what I want it to do. Now I just need to figure out how to express that awesomeness to the folks who are jittery about it.

    Discuss

    You're right. This is the limit of pre-story character generation. I think it's been super-awesome in play so far. I don't think anyone who's playtested has disagreed yet.

    The lights go down

    The "we" is meant to refer to all of the players, including the GM. I'll be sure to make that more clear in future versions.

    The turn-order for framing scenes was one of those things where I intentionally left out any rules and then neglected to point out that the lack of a rule was intentional. It's simply inconsequential to the system who frames what scene. Therefore, any friendly system you naturally come up with is perfectly cool. Take turns. Flip coins. Draw for high card. Or let the loudest person frame next. It'll all work as well.

    What's my motivation?

    This is one of those sections that I thought was crystal clear, but has so far proven to be a big ol' stumbling block for my readers. You're right, it's essentially free-form right up until you have a conflict. And you have a conflict when one player's narration contradicts or threatens another player's narration. I expect the game to go (somewhat) like this:

    Player 1: My character does stuff!
    Player 2: My character prevents you from doing stuff!
    GM: Roll dice, you monkeys!

    I suspect examples in the text will make this all much more clear. There will be examples in the future.

    Can players request that the GM oppose another player? If so, how much of a contribution can they make to the GM's methodology?

    Now that's real interesting. It's a completely new question to me. In short, the answers are "Yes" and "As much as the GM is willing to accept." Does that answer your question, or should we go into more detail?

    I intended the stakes thing to be a function of narration.... which will almost always be regulated by conflict resolution. Let's say that your character is about to do That Thing. That Thing is what your character has been trying to do the entire film (find love, impress his father, drive a Ferrarri, etc). If, when you announce that your character is doing That Thing, no one opposes you, then it's yours. You win. Sail off into the sunset and play the happy music. But, since someone will almost certainly oppose you (like the GM, because that's kinda his job), winning That Thing will come down to winning the conflict.

    You: I finally got the keys to the Ferrarri, the key-code to the dealership, and killed off the Zombie Car Salesmen... I am so driving that car!
    GM: Well, that's when you father pipes up about how his father was killed by a Nazi driving a Ferrarri. He's gonna try to talk you out of it. Because Ferrarris are evil. Roll dice!

    \nI'm prepping to run the Infected this Wednesday (two days hence) -- which pretty much consists of re-reading the rules and getting into a really mean headspace. I've run into a snag that I thought we'd ironed out, but not really. Here goes:
    \n
    \nI'm going build a little glossary for the purposes of this post and subsequent posts in this thread.
    \n
    \nMook: any non-protagonist character whose name does not appear on one of the 6 cards
    \nMeat: any non-protagonist character whose name does appear on one of the 6 cards
    \nNPC: all characters who are mooks or meat
    \nTreason: an instance where the GM privilege summarized under "You Can't Trust the Infected" is used.
    \n
    \n
    \nProblem, Major
    \nGiven: at some point in time, a Meat will take Harm
    \nGiven: anyone can introduce an NPC into a scene
    \nGiven: once the GM activates the power of "You Can't Trust the Infected", the Meat turns Traitor for the remainder of the current conflict
    \n
    \nThen,
    \n
    \n1) Why wouldn't the GM always introduce Harmed Meat into every scene?
    \n2) Why would the GM ever wait until a potential re-roll to use the Treason powers?
    \n3) Why wouldn't the GM always use the Treason powers in every single conflict?
    \n
    \nI know damn well that there are perfectly valid "story" issues not to do that -- like overuse, building tension, etc. However, I'm looking for another "hardwired" reason to support those "soft" reasons. I just can't see it.
    \n
    \n
    \nProblem, Related
    \nObviously, when the GM uses a Meat without invoking Treason, they gain dice based on the "GM" column of the meat sheet. Is this also true when they "steal" a Meat by Treason? or does the GM gain the dice that the Player was going to gain?
    \n
    \n
    \nProblem, Simple yet Telling
    \nWhen two different players want to use the same Meat in a given conflict, who gets "dibs"?
    \nAre there special considerations if one of these players is the GM?

    As luck would have it, I'll have most of today (Tues) available to answer your questions. I'll start out with snappy and direct answers, and if those don't cut it before tomorrow's game, we should have time to iron things out.

    1 & 3) It's just the story-related answers, man. If the GM uses every single opportunity to bring in Infected or Monster "Meat", then that's awesome. I do not believe the mechanisms will break under that strain. It might strain the storyline, but it won't mess with the 'balance' of the game.

    2) Because sometimes a 'meat' character starts the conflict with no Harm, but has some after the first or second roll.

    Obviously, when the GM uses a Meat without invoking Treason, they gain dice based on the "GM" column of the meat sheet. Is this also true when they "steal" a Meat by Treason? or does the GM gain the dice that the Player was going to gain?

    The GM always uses the dice from the GM's column, no matter who introduced the character to the scene.

    When two different players want to use the same Meat in a given conflict, who gets "dibs"?
    Are there special considerations if one of these players is the GM?


    Early iterations of the text had rules for this. When those rules just never got used, or even discussed, I ended up leaving them out. This is one of those things that I'll probably end up covering with a blanket rule of some sort. Which will probably boil down to; "Resolve it however your group thinks is fair, and no, the GM gets no special consideration." It's been my experience that, during the course of either setting the scene or narrating out the scene, it becomes pretty clear who the NPC is helping or hurting. Which then usually leads to clarity in which player should have the NPC's card in front of them.
