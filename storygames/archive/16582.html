Posted By: Jason MorningstarNow that we're getting off on a tangent, let me clarify that what drives me nuts is this:

RANDOM PERSON: I'm looking for a finished, lightweight fantasy game.

RANDOM GAME DESIGNER: With major modification, my half-finished crunchy sci-fi game would be perfect for you! [link]

So, you know, celebrate your game and tell the world about it (predicated on play, I hope), but don't reflexively shout its name constantly regardless of context.
\nAnd later on:\n
Posted By: jenskotSometimes it isn't even the designer...

- Random Person: "I want a game to do Shadowrun but streamlined but still getting my gear porn fix on."

- Not Matt Wilson: "Prime Time Adventures is perfect for you!"

What?
\nPersonally, I've tried talking about my games and other people's games, not talking explicitly (describe, but never speak its name), and not talking at all about the games that interest me (mine and others). I suspect this has more to do about what and who is hip, but I'm willing to be wrong.

So what do you think is the right combination? When is a appropriate to bring up a game in a thread and when do you think it is inappropriate? When do you want to hear it, when will you ignore it, and when does it tick you off?

    Posted By: wyrmwoodWhen is a appropriate to bring up a game in a thread and when do you think it is inappropriate?
    \nIt's appropriate to name a game (any game, including if designed by you or your best friend) when you genuinely believe it's the answer to what other people are asking. It's just that easy.

    It's inappropriate to be names-dropping just for the sake of it, or to deliberately advertise something, when you actually know it's not really the best game the OP could hope for (for their needs). It is usually very, very easy to spot this sort of naive weaselry, which still doesn't make it any less of a nuisance.

    Thus, it's entirely about one's intentions, not a matter of netiquette.

    I think creativity requires a bit of the ridiculous. You're going to get people who go over old ground or throw out their own heartbreaker version of a particular genre. Saying "Hey, that's already been done" isn't particularly useful because designers will find out on their own and because of bias on the part of the designer (your game is the best game out there until you say otherwise.) I understand wanting to check other people's arrogance and maybe cutting down on the clutter, but that arrogance is part of what drives a creator onward.

    Some of the best story games, for instance, borrow pretty heavily from Improv Games and group bonding techniques and one might argue there's no need to reinvent the wheel there. I'm pretty pleased with what's come out of the community so far.

    Now, keep in mind that I'm only talking about advice like this when it comes to the creative process. When someone is clearly shopping around for a new system, I can't argue with touting your favorite system. In those areas it helps potential buyers see what's hot and what's not.

    But telling people that there's better out there when they're in the middle of trying to construct them mostly serves as an inhibitor. It's not real useful.
    (That's why when I do it out of panic or whatever base emotion I'm operating on that day, I try to preface it with my agenda so they'll know whether to ignore me or not.)
