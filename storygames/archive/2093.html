Disposable Pamphlets

shreyas
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games

Counterpart to Japanese Plastic Books.

So, a lot of boardgames come with their rules printed on like a couple of folded sheets of paper, often cheaply so, in black-and-white even when they are describing crucial colourcoding things that go on in the game and such.

The message these pamphlets communicate to me is, "You can easily learn these rules by heart and lose this pamphlet and everything will be okay." The physical record of the rules is an encumbrance/afterthought, rather than an object of reference and fetishistic interest.

Where are the disposable RPGs? It occurs to me that games like Polaris, 1001 Nights, and Mridangam would need only a trivial amount of work, mostly in the way of better-designed play aids (spiritual cousins of gameboards), to become disposable-rules games.

    Okay. Here's some play aids I've come across over the years for D&D. I have no idea how effective they were or how viable they ended up being from a financial standpoint for TSR.

    1.) GM screens. Key rules summarized on a prop that lots of DM's used anyway.
    2.) Spell cards. These were printed, index-sized cards that held information for each spell. Just hand them out to your players who have the spells.
    3.) Specialized Character Sheets. Specialized information for specific classes included right on the sheet.
    4.) Magic Item cards. See Spell cards above only these are for magical items.

    Gaerik,
    I think what Shreyas is talking about is more like putting an XP bar that the player fills in on the character sheet. But not just that: going on to remove that section from the book because it is now redundant.
    I think I'm getting what Shreyas is saying: with well-designed play aids, why do we need enormous volumes to contain the game text? To use the monopoly example, some game designers might create lists of property values, cross-referenced with rent values and relative locations and the accordant rules to go with them-- page count 150+. Parker Brothers instead included a board which has all of this information on it (graphically), and the rules occupy a single page.

    On a side note, I think the reason for the strong aversion of some roleplayers to play aids is two-fold. One is community and identification; to set "our" games apart from "those" games. This is usually couched in some mumbo-jumbo about how any play aid/board/cards constrains the immagination and leads to inferior play. The second reason is practical: any play aid has to be schlepped to the play-site, and if it's vital to the game (like the board in Monopoly) forgetting it means no play. Less stuff is less stuff to keep track of.
