\n Quick question as I get ready for my game Wednesday night:\n
    \n
  • The Players will be dealt Fates.
    • \n
  • If they want, they can trade them in for another Fate from the remaining Fates.
    • \n
  • I will then reveal the remaining cards -- implying which Fates are in play.
    • \n
  • The Players will subtly suggest their PC's fates in Acts One and Two -- and reveal them around the end of Act Two or there about.
    • \n
\nAll that is from the rules.

My only question: Does the GM know which Player has which Fate from the start, or is he guessing along with the other Players as to which Fate each Player has.

Tim? Other folks who've played? I'm not finding this explicit in the rules and just want to know.

Thanks!

    Thanks for the replies.

    The Trading away a Fate for another one in the unused Pile, as well as revealing the unused Fates after trading is done, is covered on pages 68-69 of the rule book. It's all under the "Default -- Randomly Assigned" section of the Fates.

    I'm sure the game works fine without either, though, especially if people are satisfied with their fates.

    \n Wow, I've been playing it wrong.

    As the GM, I gave out random fates and no one really expressed an interest in trading. But I knew about the fates for two reasons:

    1. The rules say that the GM should withhold new Acts until the players start to uncover their fates. But it's hard to judge that when you don't know who has what.
    2. Several players were confused about their fate and how they might uncover it during play. So I took they away from the table to give them a few suggestions. But I guess the GM wouldn't need to know the fate beforehand to help here.

    \n Trading? Sure but keep in mind that you then have to figure out who get to trade. Everyone that's going to trade should speak up now. Throw their cards into the new stack and get a random card. Sure there is a chance that someone MIGHT get the same card but that's okay. It's better than possibly knowing what someone elses fates are.

    I think the GM knowing is a mistake. I think 1/2 of the GMs job is setting up scenes that illicit the players into revealing their fates. That's it.

    "You enter a chamber that has a obsidian wall polished to a reflective surface. What do you see in it?"
    "You come to a body hanging from the branch of a high tree. Who is it?"
    "There is a sword plunged into the body. What does it look like?
    "What does the sword whisper to the other ronin?"

    This is what this game is made of, in my opinion. If the players are doing their job then everyone, including the GM should be able to guess what the fates are.

    So basically you withold acts until you, as the GM, have figured out (or think you've figured out) what they are.
