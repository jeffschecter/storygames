    \n Mark,

    It would be a mistake to think that you'll get professional results from your first attempt at layout, even if you are really reading up on it. I think the best you can hope for is that your layout won't completely suck. I know people with degrees in graphic design who have been working in the industry for several years, and their layouts still suck. I guess it depends on how professional you want your game to look. You should use a designer with a proportional amount of skill/experience.

    As far as newbie design mistakes:

    Using a typeface because it looks cool
    Not using a grid
    Not using enough white space
    Using fonts that come pre-installed on your computer
    Using free fonts designed by amateur type designers
    The text size is too large
    Poor kerning in display text
    Arbitrary sizes and spacing with both columns, margins, gutters, callouts, and images
    Text line length is too wide
    Centering headings

    ...just off the top of my head.

    \n Mark, you can absolutely lay out your own book, just don't expect it to look all glitzy and slick. You can totally manage a simple, straightforward, legible layout \u2014 and in fact, the way that first-timers miss the mark of "legible" is trying to be complex or clever. Really: the #1 thing is to make it easy to read your book. Stick to that and you'll be fine. Also, print out often: screen and page are very different!

    There are two things that go into layout: functionality and taste.

    \n

    It's easy to screw up both of them, particularly if you don't have one person giving each his full attention.

    \n

    So, if you're doing your visual direction and your layout grunt work yourself (and don't think these are easily separable), then you absolutely need someone, maybe several someones, to look over your shoulder and tell you what's what.

    \n Everyone is giving you good advice. Keeping it simple is the most important thing.

    My first real layout job was a 500 page book with illustrations on every page. It kinda sucked, but I learned a whole lot. If you can afford the time, do the layout, then put it aside and come back to it later. As later as possible, with fresh eyes. Even a few days of distance can be great.

    There is no shame in copying page construction from someone else. Find a book that has good layout, bust out your ruler, and measure. Line length, margins, type size, everything. Classic layouts are classic for a reason.

    Don't use a typeface that was designed in the last 10 years unless you are a huge type geek and know what's good. The world is flooded with shitty fonts now.

    Post stuff (on a blog maybe? I don't know if it's totally kosher here) and solicit feedback. We're generally helpful.

    \n Also, keep in mind: It's probably okay if your layout looks like crap if your game is good. Gary Gygax managed to share his ideas with the world and his layout was the worst. A number of fairly "classic" indie games have really strange layouts that I feel do injustice to the text, but I still play the games and have a great time. Embrace the "do it yourself" thing if that's what you want, if layout isn't something you care about.

    On the other hand, layout isn't just about "pretty." It's about clear presentation. Great layout can make your game easier to understand and play.

    Figure out what is important to you, the creator, and prioritize your time, energy, and money.

    \n There was a recent thread on RPGnet about editing and layout; specifically, whether those things being not so good would stop people buying stuff. General consensus was that we could all think of books that had problems, and we wished general quality standards were higher, but the RPG-buying public (online at least) don't actually care that much about these things so long as the product has good stuff they can use.

    Anyway. If you want to learn how to do layout you have to start doing it and try things out and gradually get better. If you want it to be good from the start, get someone in.


    Don't use Arial/Helvetica or Times New Roman as your body font. They're good fonts but people are overexposed to them (and Arial is really bland). Somebody said don't use free fonts that come pre-installed - actually some of those can be quite good, but you need to get an idea of which to trust. *In general* serif fonts are preferred for body text as being easier to read - you can have sans serif body text, but it needs to be picked more carefully. (I've published books in Gill Sans, which worked for them.) Fonts have different feels even if they look quite similar. Try setting up a document with a paragraph of text formatted in various different fonts, print it out and see what each block says to you.

    Don't pick a font that doesn't have the right embedding permissions and end up redoing your whole design partway through.

    Do use enough white space so that stuff doesn't look crammed together.

    Switch off end of line hyphenated word breaks. (And if possible hunt down the people who invented them.)

    Left justified/ragged right text is easier to follow than fully justified.

    Posted By: sovietWhat's the significance of a grid? I assume this is something that will become apparent to me when I get some layout software. What software should I get? Is Scribus viable compared to InDesign?

    Also: if I did have someone else do the layout for me, what would I actually end up with?     Other folks can answer what program is best, but if you are a student, buy a copy of Adobe Creative Suite, which includes InDesign. It's cheap for students, and then profoundly expensive for professionals. Get a copy while you can.

    What you get from a professional can vary widely, but in general, I would not settle for anything less than (a) the application files (ie, the InDesign files), even if you can't open them yourself, and (b) a press-ready PDF. That allows you to get the PDF printed and allows you to update the book when and if you have need. In InDesign especially, there's a menu option to create a nice little package of all the files and dropped-in images and everything.

    I might also advise getting the entire text in XML and have the InDesign file use the XML as source, so you could update the XML with a text editor at your leisure and then the InDesign would update automatically. But I'm a stickler for archival value, and for most indie game purposes XML isn't a valuable use of anybody's time.

    Posted By: sovietThe way I see it is that I'm making an object. An object that I expect people to pay money for. So I think it's my responsibility to make that object as wonderful as possible.
    \nI agree, for various and reasonable interpretations of "possible."

    You could spend 20 years and a million dollars making this object, and it might be the most wonderful object possible. People would cry when they used it, but you would have to sell it for $100,000 (and hope to sell ten) to recuperate your costs. You'd probably lose a million dollars and no one would buy it or play it and know how wonderful it is.

    You could spend a day and no money up front to make it, and it would probably be an ugly text document you printed out of Notepad and stapled in the upper, left corner and sold at cons. Every dollar you charged would be profit. You could sell it for 25 cents. At such a cheap price, maybe you'd sell hundreds or even thousands of them. Lots of people would enjoy your game.

    Somewhere between those two extremes is the right answer for you and your product. Every production choice you make will come with costs and benefits, which you must weigh.

    Remember, the first edition of Dungeons and Dragons looked like crap, and it sold thousands of copies and started a gaming revolution.

    Posted By: sovietWhat's the significance of a grid?
    One way to ensure an unprofessional layout is to NOT use a grid. Every semi-competent painting or building adheres to one, either consciously or not (if you guys want to argue this, let's take it somewhere else). There are tons of books about grid systems in design, and you owe it to yourself to read one or two of them. Note that these aren't just equally spaced squares, but proportionally spaced columns and baselines.
