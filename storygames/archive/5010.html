    I agree that that's crazy talk. That system requires some sort of group adjudication of when the compel is cool and when you're being a bully. There's a conflict of interest!
    \nTechnically, they both require that, whether it's that you're bullying another character to get fate points and/or do what you want, or that you're bullying another character to make them lose fate points and/or do what you want.

    I disagree that it represents a fundamental conflict of interest, however, because system is subordinate to social contract in all cases. If you don't trust the guy sitting next to you to use the system with the best of intentions, why are you playing an admittedly high-trust RPG with them?

    Think about it in terms of the example I gave: Rocket Red's planning something foul, and Mack Silver wants to run off and go stop it, but Sally and the Grey Ghost want to wait it out and plan a little first, let her make the first move. Mack appeals to Jet Black and puts forward two fate points during the scene. Assume we're using version 1 from your post, and the GM's an authority stopover.

    a.) By choosing to make this a compel instance instead of a social conflict, there's no going to dice here - it's all currency exchange to resolve the scene. Mack slides the two FPs forward, the GM gets no flak from Jet's player, and so calls it valid.

    b.) If Jet takes the compel, he gets two fate points and Mack loses two, meaning that in the scene that follows where they bust up Rocket Red's bunker, Jet has a little bit more awesome to bring to bear. Mack gets what he wanted, but has to be more careful in the later conflict, maybe providing a comedic opportunity for him to go, "Geez, maybe Sally was right about this running headlong into danger thing" if stuff starts to go bad for him.

    c.) If Jet doesn't take the compel, he loses two fate points and Mack gains two - Mack doesn't get what he wants, but when he runs off to Rocket Red's bunker by himself, he'll probably need those two fate points to help deal with her and all of her goons. Jet's played against type, but that means he's set up for a future scene where he takes a few compels himself to catch back up - he might go try and rescue the captured Mack Silver out of guilt, and then need Sally to come save him. He might get so restless that when Sally and the Ghost finally do decide to strike, he rushes ahead and gets in Over His Head, etc etc.

    For my money, there's nothing crazy about how that movement of currency affects the flow of play.

    Posted By: Paul BFate-powered declarations vs. skill-powered declarations. I fear that the phrase "declaration" is used two different ways.
    \nNot really, but I can definitely see where the tightening up needs to occur there.

    Let's look at page 83: "By contrast, knowledge skills will often allow declaration \u2013 in other words, using a knowledge skill successfully can allow a player to introduce entirely new facts into play, and then use those facts to his advantage. The new facts take the form of a temporary aspect."

    Really, what that last sentence should say, if I were going to nitpick it, is "The new facts can take the form of a temporary aspect." I'd put that up next to spending fate points on a declaration, too.

    The basic ability is being able to declare facts. You can spend a fate point to do that, or if you happen to have a relevant skill, you can roll the skill. Sometimes, this fact translates to something you can take mechanical advantage of in a situation, and hence should be represented by a temporary aspect that you get a free tag on.

    So, let's do some examples:

    You're breaking in to a facility. The GM says there are guards at the end of the hall. It's in the middle of the night, so you throw a fate point out and say, "They're asleep." The GM nods, and because you have to make a Stealth roll to get past them, the GM decides that they have "Asleep" as a temporary aspect you can tag for free. Note that for something like this, you'd need a fate point, because there aren't any skills you could roll to influence whether those characters are asleep or awake when you run across them.

    You're breaking into a facility. Your character has the aspect "Ex-Military Commando", and you throw out a fate point and say, "Hey, they probably modeled this after a military installation, so I'd probably know shortcuts to get around in there." The GM nods, and now the facility has "Shortcuts" as a temporary aspect that you might be able to tag later.

    You're breaking into a facility. The GM says that you come upon the powerplant for the facility, a weird-looking, compact reactor. You have Engineering, and you say, "Yeah, this looks like the same kind of tech I've seen on Doc Methuselah's weird gizmos. It has a weak point near the heat sinks where I can disrupt the flow of electricity and take out the lights and whatever else might be connected to it." The GM nods and has you roll your skill, and you succeed. Later, when you make your Engineering roll to dismantle it, you've got "Weak Point" that you can hit up for a free tag.

    You're breaking into a facility. The GM says you discover a warehouse full of crates, and tells you that the room has an aspect of "Cramped" or something like it. You say, "Hunh. I bet that there's some kind of central roster in here that catalogs all these - it'd be the only way they could keep track of it all," and you spend a fate point. The GM nods, and writes down "Roster" in his notes, so that when you're rummaging through drawers with Investigation later, you can tag that to find additional information.

    All of the above moves are rules-legal. Not all groups will be comfortable with allowing all of them for various reasons.

    Posted By: Paul BIs spending a Fate to make a "this is true" declaration fundamentally the same as "invoking for effect"?
    \nYes. Except, when you're doing it without an aspect, the GM should not treat it to have the same weightiness as it does *with* an aspect. When someone spends a FP to say "I have a flashlight", well, great, they have a flashlight. When they spend a FP to say "I have a flashlight because I'm a Boy Scout: Always Prepared", that moment gains more importance -- it's highlighting a core truth about the character. As the GM, there, I might then sit up and say, "Yeah, you really are. You've got the flashlight, and probably more. What *else* do you think you have because of this?" I'm giving the declaration a fatter footprint in the second case because an aspect is involved, and that is -- in fact -- exactly what invoking for effect *is*.\n
    Posted By: Paul BIf there's no difference between invoking for effect and spending a fate to make a declaration, does that mean you canalsotag an outside Aspect to make a declaration?
    \nYep. "Tag" in many cases simply means "Invoke an aspect that isn't intrinsically yours."\n
    Posted By: Paul BIs it safe to assume that Fate-powerered Declarations do not have the same mechanical effect as skill-powered Declarations?
    \nYep. I'm a little frustrated that this confusion made it into the rules, but it's entirely due to the rules being written by three guys instead of one, and each of us has a different level of (or lack of) Obsessive-Compuslive Disorder about precision in terminology. From the non-OCD perspective, both are cases where you're declaring something to be true, it's just represented in the world in a different way, so hey, call both of those "declaration". Me, I might be inclined to get very stressed and anal about that and say, no, the fate-powered stuff is "Declaring" (declare "this is true" as a binary operation), and the skill-powered stuff is "Asserting" (assert "this is true" is then something to be tested, so a skill gets rolled). On the other hand, these are really just two entry-points on the same continuum. Consider, a skill-powered Declaration can be done to introduce an aspect that reflects the declared reality. Then, the free tag that exists on that aspect can be used to Declare (via Invoke/Tag For Effect) that something else is true -- or to get a reroll, +2, etc. The fate based way is sort of a, "Hey, I want to mold the story with this truth"; the skill based way is "Hey, I want use this truth to set up an advantage for later use".

    Am I just being short-sighted here?
    \nReally, the issue is that for all the supposedly precise language, adjudicating declarations comes down to a lot of very nebulous feeling out of stuff that will vary from group to group. Fred and I aren't really saying different things, but we're revealing the differences in how we perceive that nebulous stuff.

    For example, the issue of "weightiness" (is that even a real noun?) comes up when you're talking about declarations made via invoking an aspect vs. just spending a fate point on one. If you have a relevant aspect, you should be able to get away with more stuff than you might get away with by just spending a fate point. So, in your group, if a player can spend a fate point to go, "The guards are asleep," he should be able to invoke "I Know a Guy" to say, "One of the guards actually works for a security company I own stock in, and I've seen him at company functions before. I go and say hi to him."

    But, this is all relative - in another group, declaring that the guards are asleep might not even be kosher in the first place. And for Fred, he considers invoking for effect powerful enough to want to add more weight to a declaration if he feels not enough is there, like, "Oh, not only do you know the guy, but he really likes you and is going to help you with the break-in attempt." I might not do that as often.

    Another issue has to do with the "locus of control" inherent to using different means of declaring. Anything to do with spending fate points is more about player authorship than character knowledge, whereas using a skill is explicitly the reverse. Like, in the above example, it'd be weird to me for the character to try and use a skill to say, "Someone I know happens to work in this building," - that's world editing stuff, stuff for fate points. Conversely, it'd be weird to blow a fate point on something like, "This device has a weak point I can exploit," if you're sitting there with an Engineering of Superb.

    As for the economy stuff, I think you're getting too hung up on wanting to put symmetry where none need exist. The invoke/compel relationship, for example, is asymmetrical on purpose, which is why compels never give you a -2 on a roll or force you to take the worst of two rolls. But let me show you something:

    Player invokes an aspect to declare that there's an aspect on a scene that he can use. He tags it for free, gets the +2 bonus, and has spent a total of one fate point. In terms of the economy, that's exactly the same as if the aspect was already on the scene, and he spent a fate point to tag it. In that sense, all the declaration did was justify that there was a relevant aspect available to invoke.

    That's pretty much the reason why I'm cool with it - in the end, that spent fate point does get around to modifying a skill somewhere down the line. It creates no ripples in the economy because there's no point break to take advantage of, and there's no extra charge for doing it either. It's exactly the same as just invoking an aspect - only the justification part changes.

    Also: I personally almost never charge a player for declaring anything that's pure color. If they're going into a dark cave, and someone slides over a fate point to say, "I have a flashlight," if I didn't have anything in mind regarding the darkness being a problem, I'm not going to charge for that. If I take that fate point, it's because I just came up with a reason for that flashlight to be damn important.

    Posted By: Alvin FrewerHowever, letting someone add aspects without taking an action for a manuever seems potentially game breaking to me.
    \nIt's not game breaking because there's no real mechanical advantage. Placing an aspect with an aspect is really about broadening the justification for an invocation.

    Like, look up the thread at the four examples I gave, and check out the warehouse one. He couldn't say, "Yeah, I tag the Cramped aspect when I'm using Investigation to find the secret files," because that makes no sense.     Could you technically call that a Leadership maneuver, because Leadership covers administration and bureaucracy? Eh, maybe, but that kind of feels counter-intuitive. It skirts that line between world-editing and character influence that makes it more appropriate for a fate point, to me. So that's why it's there.

    The situations in my mind are sort of like this:

    Scene has "On Fire!" on it.

    As the GM I can compel "On Fire!" to trap folks inside the burning house as the flames rise higher. Folks who agree to be trapped get a fate point.

    As a player, you could tag for effect as the villain tries to get away. "This place is On Fire! He can't get away." In *this* sense, the aspect's on his sheet, insofar as he's a viable target for compels of that aspect.

    As a player, you could tag (in the invoking sort of way) for a +2 or reroll: "I swing my fists at him, backing him up towards the flames. He starts to get singed. +2 on my roll!" This is just tagging a scene aspect; no fate point goes to the bad guy.
