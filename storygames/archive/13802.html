    \n You know, d20 really is a great system, and I say that without any sarcasm or moral judgment. It didn't get to be popular by accident, and the people who like it are not uneducated fools who don't know better.

    (The people who try to use d20 for everything, or who think that it's a great system for games that it frankly is not a good fit for, those people are fools. Horses for courses, as always. No game is perfect for everything and everyone, and no game ever will be.)

    Now, I don't really know anything about mjbauer's group beyond what he's said here, but one thing seems clear; the problem is not that they like d20 and want to keep playing games with it, the problem is that mjbauer doesn't like their d20 games and wants to play something else. And there aren't too many ways to proceed from that point.

    First and foremost, I think it'd be useful to know why the two diehards in the group really love d20. What specifically do they like about it? Is there something in particular about the experience that d20 provides that they enjoy, is it comfort/joy in mastering the system, is it the opportunity cost of learning a new (potentially unfun) system, is it something else? Just go ahead and assume that their reasons are 100% correct, logical, and unassailable: they like what they like and that's the end of the discussion. All you need to figure out is WHAT they like, without worrying about how to argue them out of liking it.

    Once you know that, you can do a better job of picking and pitching games that might appeal to them, and get them to meet you halfway. And if you can't find good compromise games, or you find that you don't enjoy the compromise games, either, then at least you know that you tried to make it work and can leave that group with a clear conscience and no hard feelings. If everyone enjoyed exactly the same things, the world would be a boring place, right?

    \n I agree with JD, but I'll go one further. When you say "now that I know what is out there (and what is possible) our bi-weekly game-night is sounding less and less appealing to me", it's sounding an awful lot to me like you are the one who went away to Paris for the summer, discovered Euro-Art-Hause cinema, and now want to share that (from your point of view) obviously superior film experience with your friends, who are still wanting to go out every second Tuesday and watch the latest H-Wood action flick with a tub of popcorn.

    This doesn't make either one of you right or wrong.

    But it could be a clear signal that your tastes and interests have diverged sufficiently from their tastes and interests that you might just be better off saying "You know, I like hanging with you guys, but I'm just not into the gaming any more, so I'll bow out of this bi-weekly thing for a while, and will take the initiative to set up movie-nights/pub-outings/boardgame-nights/pick-up-street-hockey-games/whatever out of band so we can still hang out", and then finding yourself another group of gamers to game with that are playing the kinds of games that you feel you'd like to try out.

    And my guess is that you might find that these guys you think you like hanging out with, you really only like the warm memory of the old gamer time context socializing, and that in actual fact, you have moved on and you don't really have much in common with them any more. Or, you might find that your friendships with them improve and deepen once the rut of an essentially not-in-common-and-unfun forced activity is off the table.

    I could be wrong, but the way you explain the problem makes it sound to me like the underlying situation might be that you're going through the motions because of memory. Life is too short. If you value these folks as friends, find some other way to express it, and move on. Or at least try a break and see how that makes you feel.

    But, at bare minimum, you should talk to your gaming group about this problem, about what you're seeking to get out of RPGs, what fun you get out of RPGs, about what you're willing to try playing and not. If you're the only one in the group that has any interest in playing a game that's not part of the well-worn-stable-of-games-we've-been-playing-for-ten-years then that should be a clear sign that you're coming to an incommensurable situation here and you might be better off moving on.

    These other folks have every much a right to say, "Nah, you know what? We call it Tuesday Night D&D for a reason, and we really want to keep it that way" as you do to say, "Hey, you know what? D20 is cool and all, but I'd really like to try other games."

    This is all part of negotiating the social contract at the gaming table, and sometimes (most times? all the time?) that contract needs explicit investigation and articulation to prevent people from growing resentment and un-fun.\n

    \n Hey everyone, thanks for the comments. I deliberately avoided asking for game suggestions, group help or sympathy because I am just generally interested in what people have to say. I really just needed to vent some frustration and I appreciate all the feedback.

    More of my d20 rant

    The thing that frustrates me about d20 (from what I've experienced in my group) is that the game my group thinks we are playing and the game they want to play isn't the game we are playing. They want to do awesome stuff, kill monsters and have crazy adventures. They want to be the heroes in an epic tale, but in reality we are participating in a bad comedy or even a comedic tragedy. The system doesn't lend itself to heroic triumph at crucial moments. It's possible, but as far as my experience goes, it rarely happens. The DM usually has to stretch the rules, get creative or show us lots of mercy to avoid killing our entire party. I'm tired of an NPC riding in at the last minute to save the party, it's ridiculous. Repeated failure just isn't fun. I just think that rather than kidding ourselves that this is what we want to play we could look for a system that creates the kind of stories we want to be in. Or, at very least the DM should be lowering the difficulty of the encounters we face.

    I guess I just feel like players should have a sense of empowerment. I love the idea of 'failing forward.'

    The realism in D&D comes in the form of bad odds. You can do cool stuff but the chances that it will work out is low. The cooler the thing you can do, the less likely you will actually do it. Yes, this might be "realistic" but it isn't fun. It's challenging, but not in an enjoyable way (at least, not enjoyable to me). I just think that a 'hero' should be able to be heroic (not just lucky). Heroes in movies succeed far more than they fail, and the success or failure is dependent on the situation. They don't fail at crucial times and when they do fail it usually escalates the tension and creates a new challenge that is even more interesting than the one before. But in our games we 'miss' or 'fail' and that's it. To me, this is the biggest deficiency with the d20 system. I know that the DM has the opportunity to change this, but since it's not explicitly built into the system it doesn't happen for us, and I'm assuming that I'm not the only one having this experience.

They are open to trying new things, I just don't see them adopting anything permanently, and that's fine. They are good guys, just really bad gamers (in my opinion). And, for now, the social aspect of it is enough to keep me coming back.

    \nTo the OP:

    This sounds like a good point, too. From your longer explanation it sounds to me a bit like part of the problem you're experiencing is in a not very skillful use of the game rules. Most D20 games (if not all) are rich systems that present lots of knobs and wheels to turn, and players (and GMs) can definitely turn those knobs and wheels in ways that make for exciting encounters and stories, and in ways that don't have such awesome effects.

    My experience with D20 (specifically the PFRPG and 4e incarnations of it) is not that it's fraught with failure and wasted attempts, nor rife with overwhelming encounters resulting in Deus Ex Machinae.

    But, building good, balanced encounters isn't easy. And learning how to use the D20 skill system in ways that makes failure interesting isn't exactly obvious either.

    You sound like you have a pretty good eye for the parts of the rules that aren't exactly producing successful results. I'd suggest two things: what can you do as a player to take action to prompt the solution of these issues at the table? (in the case of the scenario where it was bugging you that so much play was happening before player's characters really had any character-based interaction, what can you do at the table, as a player, to help address that issue?)     I come from a group where the GMing role gets shared around pretty evenly and regularly. I learned some of what I do and don't do in that role from watching what the other guys did and trying to build on, or change, the bits I observed form their performances.

    You can also try to "manage up" a bit -- try helping your GM get better by direct action at the table, or explanation between sessions, but that can also be a very tricky process. You can try to frame it in terms of your character: "Hey Bob; I think that in the next adventure my character might really like to do more of this kind of thing, and less of that kind of thing -- is that cool with you?"

Repeated failure just isn't fun. I just think that rather than kidding ourselves that this is what we want to play we could look for a system that creates the kind of stories we want to be in. Or, at very least the DM should be lowering the difficulty of the encounters we face.
    \nThis is entirely logical, D&D was never designed to be like that, that's just how it ended up getting marketed, because it was appealing to people who read fantasy novels.     I haven't played Exalted, but it might be a good pitch. If you describe an awesome stunt, you get extra dice. Extra dice means you have a greater chance of success.       \n Certainly creating something that wasn't there before is pretty damn impressive, and it takes someone unique to do it, but once an idea is there, it sometimes takes a quite different mind to make something new within the idea.

      Steve Jobs & Steve Wozniak came up with the personal computer idea, and brought it to market, but Jobs couldn't do anything with NeXT, and it took Jonathan Ive to come up with the iMac.

      You could also use a similar example with watches, they'd been around for over a hundred years being made in Switzerland, the UK and the US, and the Japanese came in and completely decimated them with Quartz watches. None of the guys who had made the Swiss powerhouse watches before could do anything about it, they could only keep making what they made, while Ernst Thomke came up with the Swatch and saved the Swiss watchmaking industry. He's done that a few other times in different industries as well, but it was always in existing but damaged ones.
