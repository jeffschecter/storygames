\n In thinking about the design contests I've seen, and those I've run, I see a pattern that seems exploitable: the "theme", purpose or reward offered by these contests seems not to matter as much as the notion of a public deadline and how it motivates action.

I think everyone can relate to how an impending deadline acts as a motivating force, but I think that the public aspect of it is the most important. A private deadline really isn't that much of a deadline, unless you have a whole lot of will. Having a deadline in the public eye appears to generate a lot more actual action.

I'm wondering if there are ways to harness this notion for personal game designs (that is, not as part of a design contest). Can the "public deadline" be implemented in a way meaningful enough to push designers into progress outside of a contest?

    \n I don't find either of those incentives particularly perverse.

    Assuming a person responds to both of those incentives, does the presence of the deadline make the production of a game more or less likely? I submit that it doesn't actually make a difference. The only real difference is that, with the deadline, the world gets the certainty that the game will be produced or abandoned more honestly and sooner than waiting for a delivery that will never come.

    In other words, there may be some fringe case of someone approaching the deadline and saying "screw it, to much work, abandon whole notion" and, thus, not producing a game that would otherwise get made eventually; however, I suspect that the type of person who would do that is the type of person who would have never finished and released the game anyway. In that case, the deadline is still positive, because it gets them to move on.

    More likely, someone missing a deadline says something like "darn missed deadline. sorry everyone. here is the new deadline".
