\n Specifically, I'm looking for potential imbalances and problematic areas in theory - nothing detailed enough for specifics yet.

Here's the basic mechanic, meant for a generic, non-setting specific system.

Players take turns describing actions in the narrative. They have a number of actions dependent on the dice pool for the scene (similar to Wushu). Every action involves a trait (rated d4-d12). After all of the dice are gathered, the players divide them into relevant actions based on their descriptions (Action, Reaction, Recovery, Support). Every die that rolls 5 or more (d4's must roll 4 twice) equals a success.

Example. Sorry for the embellishment, couldn't help myself.\n
Dramatis Personae
Rohan has a Battle skill at d8, Move at d6, Treatment d6 and a single Wound.

The Dragon Lord has a Battle skill of d6, Armor at d8 and Hellfire at d8.

The situation
The Dragon Lord is standing across a chasm from Rohan. Behind him is a castle where Rohan's goal lies (captured princess, magic weapon, whatever). Hellfire from the Dragon Lord is burning the forests on both sides of the chasm, raining ash into the chasm and the river below.

Description
Scene dice pool = 3
Rohan stabs his sword into the ground and rips a piece of his sleeve to tie around his wound, staunching it (Treatment d6 - Recovery). The Dragon Lord charges his hands with Hellfire and unleashes it across the chasm at Rohan (Hellfire d8 - Action). Rohan retrieves his sword, side steps and launches himself across the chasm in a mighty leap (Move d6 - Reaction). The Dragon Lord's eyes widen in surprise has his opponent leaps over the fire. He tries to turn aside, sidestepping the incoming blow (Battle d6 - Reaction). Rohan comes down, blade first, with the momentum from his leap behind his powerful swing (Battle d8 - Action). The Dragon Lord relies on his armor to take the brunt of the force, and it bites down into his should pads (Armor d8).

Resolution
Trait (Result)
Rohan: Action - Battle d8 (Fail), Reaction - Move d6 (Success), Recovery - Treatment d6 (Success)
Dragon Lord: Action - Hellfire d8 (Success), Reaction - Battle d6 and Armor d8 (2 Failures)

Result - Rohan heals a wound. Niether manages to hit their opponent (Rohan failed his attack, but made his Reaction).
\nI made all of that up as I wrote the post. Nothing felt off, which is cool. I might change armor and weapons to just be add-on dice that work beyond a scene dice pool. Not sure how that would balance with things like the Hellfire above, which works as a skill rather than a modifier (thus, not possessing a modifier and potentially unbalanced/gimped against weaponry).

What do you guys think?

    \n I have a number of questions and suggestions, I'll just go one by one. I suspect this might be harsh. There's no need to answer these questions here, they're just stuff to think about.

    1. I notice there's a huge drop in the chance of success between the d6 and d4 (like, from 33,3% to 6% or something). Is d4 supposed to be an "untrained" level? Rolling it twice seems a bit jarring, and might be confusing for new players.

    2. I was a bit confused about what the actual procedure was. Do we pick which traits we will be rolling before the scene starts? Do we roll them all together or as each individual action happens or at the end after all descriptions are in place? There are a few other questions...who goes first? Who sets the dice pool size? If this is a trait-picking game, what prevents me from going for my highest traits every time?

    3. I think it would be fair to say that your target audience is roughly the same as "the people who read your books and have (possibly) never played before". That seems to me like a pretty good target (it might not be! it's up to you). In this case, what are some qualities that your protagonists have? What do they care about? What are your books about (what kind of picture do they paint about the world/your world)? Ask yourself these kinds of questions and let them guide you as you're creating the mechanics. What makes this a better system to play your books in, compared to, say, FATE, PDQ#, Savage Worlds or Wushu?

    4. Writing examples of how play is supposed to go is a good exercise, but ultimately there's a difference between how you want/expect it to be and how it really plays. Get a rough draft together, grab a friend or stranger and test it out. See where it feels right or wrong. Look for where the system is acting against you and where it's helping you in achieving your ideal play experience (for this particular game).

    5. Finally, the fiction in the play example seems cool (mighty leaps! blazing dragonfire!) but I don't see anything that prompts us to such description. (to paraphrase a bit, one of the possible purposes of rules in a game is that they make us say interesting things - in your example that seems all on the players) The resolution feels more like we're rolling dice at each other and there's a pass/fail result (which relates to what - the character's action or their intent?). There's also no visible link between what Rohan is trying to do in the scene (get to the castle) and how what he's doing/rolling connects to that. Finally, what would happen if the hellfire attack had been successful? Would that deal damage? Impose a negative trait?

    Like Rafu said, this is all a bit in a vacuum.

    \n I'm kinda thinking in two different directions now, without being sure which would be more appropriate.

    1. Have you played any Burning Wheel at all? I have a feeling you would at least get along well with the philosophy behind it.

    2. (and on a slightly different tack)
    The way you described the dice pools and the two characters rolling against each other in the OP made me think it would be more appropriate as a conflict-resolution system than a task-resolution system. Then you write this here:\n
    In the first story, he learns that he's a good fighter when pitted against the average soldier, but not so good against someone who has trained all their life for fighting. Throughout the future stories, he will learn new techniques and become a better fighter.
    \nFrom this statement, it seems like you have a protagonist who, when he rolls against "the average soldier" does pretty well, i.e. he beats his opponent's rolls. But when he rolls against "someone who has trained all their life," his dice are not good enough, and he has to train and learn, and get more/better dice in order to overcome this challenge, which takes the form of a person.

    So in this case, it doesn't seem to me like you need players taking actions in turn, or rolling dice against a target number. It seems like what you want is to pit characters against each other, in fights, conflicts, tests of skill, whatever, and see who wins and who is defeated. In which case, they should be rolling against each other. And you can move from conflict to conflict around the table, instead of from action to action.

    Another thing you might consider is making it a requirement that, in order to overcome certain challenges, like that master swordsman, player characters must grow and learn and get better. This could range from simply taking some of the randomness of the dice out of the game, so a lucky d6 roll doesn't pwn the master's d10 roll, to characters actually being unable to win until they get an advancement related to the conflict they are trying to win.

    Also, you're talking about keeping everyone at the table involved, which is cool, BUT in rpgs this usually represents like a gang of dudes who team up to take on a threat or challenge together. Is this a staple of your fiction? If it isn't, there are still ways to set up scenes where a single protagonist faces a challenge that include contributions from a group of players.

    \n Cool beans. So, with your answers in mind, let's go back to the original example.

    Your description is certainly action-packed, but the dice kinda call bullshit on it. I mean, basically nothing happens. The Dragon Lord gets his hellfire off, but Rohan succeeds in his reaction, so... nothing? So the Dragon could have been rolling a d100 and it wouldn't mean squat, basically. What if he rolled 4 or less (50 per cent chance)? He doesn't get to use it? It fizzles? That doesn't sound very high adventure to me, he's supposed to be bad ass! But those dice results don't tell me that either of these characters are bad ass, and if I was playing one of them, I wouldn't feel very bad ass.

    Now, if you were just comparing die rolls, you might still come up with a situation where nothing happens, because if you're splitting offense and defense pools, both defense pools might overcome the offense pools. But in this case, I wouldn't be comparing my die rolls against an abstract measurement of success (or "bad-ass-itude"), I'm just comparing them to my opponent's rolls. So I can be as bad-ass as I want, and nothing tells me I can't, it just says that if I lose the conflict, my opponent gets to be even more bad-ass than me.

    But, hey, maybe that doesn't work for you. If you're still set on a target number, I'd suggest lowering it to 4, at least. With your system now, d4s are not even worth rolling. I've got an idea for a step-die system of my own sitting around, and what I decided to do was that every full 4 points on a single die gives you 1 "success" or point. So if you roll 4-7, that die gets you 1 success, 8-11, 2 successes, a 12, you get 3 successes. I was figuring that you'd always be rolling around 3-5 dice, so there's little chance of getting no successes, except if you're rolling all d4s. Then you compare number of successes between characters in conflict to see who wins the conflict. But you could make larger dice even more powerful by giving 1 success for every full 3 points, making players feel even more bad-ass, and encouraging them to strive for larger dice.
