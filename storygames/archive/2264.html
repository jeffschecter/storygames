    January 2007
    Jonathan, that's right. It was Andy's idea to get the games posted to the site, instead of posting them to The Forge or such. So that started when he took over administration in 2005.


    Anyhow, the strange subject of "compulsory publication" has come up again. This is somehow my fault, I figure, so I'll address it yet again. Nobody can make you publish any of your ideas. There's no point at which, during this contest that you're asked to sign over your rights. The authors retain all copywrite to the games that are submitted. The only "publishing" that they're asked to do is to have the game posted on the site so it can be judged.

    Which I'm assuming nobody has a problem with, right? It would be a very different contest if the stuff had to be kept secret somehow.

    Anyhow, so even if we said something stupid like "Ha, now you have to publish with us!" it wouldn't be true.

    Nor, actually, do I feel that anyone is actually making that claim. Just going step by step here.

    The next question is whether or not its right to have a publication with the words Iron Game Chef on it. Obviously we're not concerned that people publish, right? It's Ben's right to publish Polaris on his own. Yah?

    And nobody owns the rights to the words "Iron Game Chef" (I didn't make it up - if anybody does, it's Jared or Clinton). So we all agree that anyone can slap that on the front of a book that has...well whatever somebody wants to include in it.

    So the only real question here is whether such a thing is a good idea or not.

    I personally don't see the problem with it. Obviously not, or I wouldn't have attempted the first publication. Do I think that Sandy goes a bit far with his propaganda on the cover? Yeah, I probably would have put something else on the cover myself. But for Chrissakes, at least that one was for charity.

    The one I was doing, I was going to split the proceeds from with the designers and the publisher. Again, morally, I don't see how this is any different than publishing the games on one's own. Rather than cause some problem with the contest, I'd think it would benefit the contest.

    I'm willing to be convinced otherwise, if somebody can give me a good argument. But, to date, the best I've gotten is that some folks feel like in doing so that they're feeling pressured by the man to comply. Or something. That's not good enough by far.

    There can't be a mandatory product, and I think you can't tell people how to publish the outcome products if they choose to do so. So I'm not seeing any practical reason to rail against it.

    Mike

    • PaulB
      May 2008
      I've been thinking through the logistics of putting together a GC anthology myself. Printing, publishing, editorial, etc. is my day job so it's where my head goes first.

      I'm absolutely certain that there's a good-sized market for a print anthology of the "best of" a given GC year. That said, the rights issues with dealing with every GC participant is insane. Also, making an anthology compulsory is a deal-breaker for everyone who wants to take their game further on their own.

      Personal thoughts:

      * I think exposure and inclusion is adequate reward for a lot of the participants, especially if they look at the financial reality of vanity publishing, even via Lulu.
      * I wouldn't want to make the anthology inclusive or compulsory. Inclusive is too long, compulsory is evil.
      * IMO a strong anthology would include a) complete games and b) games collected with some editorial oversight. I've been watching the trends and picking out some recurring themes -- an anthology could be one good example that exemplifies each of, say, 10 '08 trends. I'd also LOVE to see some structured commentary from the designers whose works get included: what were you thinking? What were your goals? Were you trying to break specific new ground or just following your instinct? Same thing for essays or interviews with the GC judges.
      * Nobody's going to make any "real" money at an anthology. If you've run the numbers and see your standalone game doing better than break-even, then that's clearly where you need to go. Shoot, I'd be happy with a clean break-even on hard costs, with editorial time donated.
      * This sort of thing isn't going to happen by committee. Strong editorial oversight is probably the greatest value-add to an anthology. Otherwise there's no real benefit over just skimming through the collected PDFs at 1kt1km.
      * Branding: I get that it can't be "a Game Chef" publication. Nor can it be a true "best of." Really, the value proposition would need to be something along the lines of "Ten Great Games Cooked At Game Chef 2008".

      Anyway, just my $0.02. All all MHO.

      p.
