    I'm working on / waiting to start working on "We All Had Names", concerning a Jewish family before, during and after WWII. It's not going to be a fun game. However, I hope it will be a strong experience, provoking positive thought and possible personal change and growth.

    I believe that we should be able to do the same things that are being done in literature, film and comics. People will read Anne Frank's Diary, watch Schindler's List and read Maus. Not everyone will want to read or watch those works, and those who do so aren't in search of light entertainment. Serious games must communicate strongly that they're of a different kind than games meant specifically for entertainment.

    If one were to make a game about a contemporary conflict, I think a good approach would be to make a small series of games, illuminating the issue from different angles. The situation in Iraq - or anywhere - is far too complex to be understood or interpreted by just one game experience.

    Personally, I believe - as I think Kurt Vonnegut mentioned somewhere - that imagination, the ability to put yourself in someone else's head and heart, is the best inoculation against the potential to commit atrocities. This is, to me, the strongest possible incentive to make a role-playing game about a contemporary situation - or, even better, about a potential future area of conflict.

    I think a big problem with making any "story game" with a tight focus on "current events" is that the game will naturally reflect the writers own opinions about those events. It's right up there in the design process, starting with "what do the characters do?" and "what is the reward system based upon?"

    For history that is sufficiently "old" for a consensus to form (Nazis are bad) or people to get less upset about what happened, this is less of a problem. For events which are more current and more disputed (and therefore more interesting), people are going to get upset. And the author is more likely to want to make a statement, which gets in the way of allowing the players to make their own.

    (Note: making a game that makes such a statement isn't a bad thing. However, it's likely to provoke a backlash from somewhere.)

    On the other hand, I could completely see 9/11 as a PTA session - players only bring Issues to the table that they have authored themselves. It would still be heavy stuff, but if your group can handle it, then why not? It's a healthy thing.

    A while back Holistic put out "Afghanistan d20" as part of their "Real Life Roleplaying" line, so it's not like it hasn't been tried in RPG form before. The one review I found on RPGnet is very positive (a realistic, well-researched and informative look at the region and situation), but dismissive of controversy. Of course, browsing their website it seems Holistic also did a d20 game about the Rapture, so I'm guessing they don't care too much about controversy either.

    Video games have gone into some places where RPGs haven't (as far as I know), including obviously exploitative takes on the Gulf War and even the assassination of JFK (no joke; it was called JFK: Reloaded). The former, from the TV commercials, was obviously a lousy game, and the latter got a lot of media attention and the developers ultimately folded. For that matter, I really have to wonder how, for example, WWII veterans feel about the endless flow of WWII FPS games.

    There was also the Super Columbine Massacre RPG, a game in the style of a 16-bit CRPG where you play the role of the shooters. The creator -- who turned out to be a friend of one of the victims -- initially stood behind his creation as an attempt at using the medium as a way to explore the tragedy and the issues it evokes, but later said that video games were the wrong medium for that kind of thing.

    Story games/RPGs are probably a better medium than video games for addressing such topics, partly just because they've moved away from some of the older preconcieved notions of what constitutes valid subject matter, but how much better? Personally, this is exactly the kind of thing I play games to take my mind off of, and I'm not interested in anything that gets closer to modern military stuff than, say, Halo. I do think that in their present state story games have the maturity to tackle these kinds of issues, but whether they should is a much stickier question.
