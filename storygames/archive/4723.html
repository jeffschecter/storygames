    \nFirst, don't push your current group into a kind of game they aren't looking for, or try to "convert" them. You know that, but repetition on this point is important. Explaining *exactly* what you're yearning for might get you a couple of sessions, but it probably won't.

    Finally, if you've exhausted all advice, remember: there's always conventions.

    \n I think this is pretty much a basic-level divide in "where is the fun" among gamers. Some people play for wish-fulfillment - as you said, to be 80s action hero badasses. This is cool and fun and rocks my socks.

    However, it is pretty much completely incompatible with "story" in the sense of genuine tests of character. The action hero is always the same. They don't change, except perhaps to get cooler. The story of the action hero is how they are cool. The story of the protagonist is in the "agon" part of that word. They struggle. They might fail. They change or define their character through struggle.

    If your group is into being action heroes, chances are they aren't ever likely to want to play games where they don't get to automatically be cool and show off. They may be willing to play games where they chew scenery a bit in between action-heroing (your Woo-esque DRYH game probably falls into that zone).

    You can't sneak up on mode.

    \n I've found that gamers can be tempted to try something new so long as you make it clear that it isn't any sort of threat to their current source of fun.

    One thing you could try: schedule an "extra" game at a time that does not involve canceling or postponing one of your regular games. Make it clear that it's something different, not a substitute for the regular game. Make it clear that only those interested need show up--it's not a "group thing". Maybe involve some different people, like other gamers or non-gamer friends. Try a game that _can't_ be played the way you, as a group, play your regular game, like a pure storytelling game as opposed to a role-playing game.

    See what happens.

    (Of course, I've only done this once or twice, and it always went well. Anyone try this and have it go sour on them? They might have more useful advice than mine in that case.)

    \n Whatever imaginary battles Malcolm may be fighting, I don't think there's anything wrong with wanting something different than the other people you play with. It may limit your options, but that process of either adjusting your preferences or finding some new people to play with is how successful groups form (by definition: groups that can't do that aren't successful).

    For those who aren't fighting the hobgoblins, "gamers" refers to the stereotypical fannish, action-adventure, predominantly-male, poor-social-skills popular conception of what a gamer is. If I'd used the word without the scare quotes, I really would be misrepresenting what a gamer is. It's a selection problem - the majority of players and published games and RPG culture is focused on a particular narrow subset of "how to play, and why it's fun" - the adventure-gaming, fantastic, wish-fulfillment subset.

    If what you're interested in doing with the medium doesn't fall into that subset, you can either adapt (and become another "gamer" to a greater or lesser degree), drop out (this isn't for me, I'll go do something else), or (if you're fortunate) manage to collect a critical mass of others who also fall outside the dominant gaming culture (and become "those weirdo story-games people", or "those weirdo online-rp-ers", or "those weirdo boffer people"... aka "Not A RPG" in flamewars across the internet).

    There's no conspiracy, no philistinism, no failure of design or imagination. Just an accident of history and arguably a market failure that's resulted in a narrow gaming culture. Which - like geek cultures everywhere - is insanely defensive when anyone decides it's not for them.

    I don't think anyone but Malcolm (by indirectly attributing it to me) has brought any suggestion into this that Temple's fellow players are bad, "brain-damaged", lesser, uncool, "abusive" or anything of the sort. They just don't find the things Temple has the most fun with to be fun for them. He can either try to show them his fun and see if they like it (which it seems like he has done), or he can get his fun elsewhere, or he can try to have their kind of fun since that's what's on offer. No value judgments at all. Just a choice to make.

    I've got no axe to grind with the main line of gaming culture. I just think there's a lot of territory left fallow by it, and some of that territory is much more interesting to me than the current adventure-gaming-dominated style.
