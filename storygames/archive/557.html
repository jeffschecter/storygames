Closure and Fatal Goals

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

A "fatal goal," classically, is a goal that the character is pursuing, but which the author and audience don't want him to achieve (or don't want him to achieve at that stage in the story).

Buffy would (I suspect) become a sad, pathetic little show if the Scooby Gang succeeded in closing the Hellmouth and driving all supernatural evil from Sunnydale. That's a fatal goal.

That "I suspect" is important. One of the great insights of Story Now gaming is that many apparently fatal goals aren't. If your character's issue is being respected, and he pursues a goal of killing all his enemies and rising to the throne ... that is not a fatal goal. He can do all of that, and still lack for respect. And the things he'll do as a king in order to command respect are wonderful and horrifying. Still makes for a good story.

But once you dig down inside the skin, fatal goals seem to fly fast and furious. The classic: "This character reaches a state of contentment, where they need no longer strive for anything." That kills any story about that character. It can only be their climax or their epilogue. I, at least, can't tell an interesting story about a person who has nothing to strive for.

So in another thread, Graham looked at the question of "What do you do for a conflict of a young man trying to summon the nerve to confess his feelings to the girl he loves?" and he argued (reasonably enough) for closure: either the girl accepts his affections or she smacks him down in what seemed (to me) a pretty permanent fashion. The conflict is resolved and off the table.

But I have a strong suspicion that for some games (possibly for all games) closure on a PC-to-PC relationship is inherently a fatal goal: it is saying "This relationship is not going to change any more," which says (again, to me) that there is no more story between those two characters. You don't need to ever put them in the same scene again.

So are fatal goals and story-driving choices qualitatively different animals? Two ends of a unified spectrum? Two independent measures unrelated to each other? Or something else entirely?

    Back into center field:

    I would distinguish between player goals that bring closure, and character goals that do so.

    When it comes to character goals, I think just about any goal can be warped to make it a goal that works in a serial story. Every story ending can be the beginning of a new story, even if that story is in a very different genre or has a different tone. For example, if Buffy had closed the Hellmouth, one could begin a new story where she has to deal with the demons that she's therefore sealed on this side, and who want to get home. Not that that's necessarily the best story in the world, but it's an example of how taking the conclusion of the old story as the premise can suggest further problems. It sounds like Vaxalon's example of a "complete" relationship as a story-driver is much the same thing.

    When the player doesn't want to make stories with the character anymore, though, or isn't willing to follow through with any necessary changes in the nature or tone of the story that come out of the resolution of their goals - well, I'm not really sure that you can do much to keep the goals attractive. I think the goal of "The character achieves a state of contentment" is a way for the player to say, "I'm done with this story," even though there are certain ways for other group members to continue to challenge the character. The reason I think this is because this goal is not about something concrete (the character gets to date someone, or defeat their arch-rival, or whatever). The very nature of the goal says, "Whatever the character wanted, they got exactly that."

    Of course, if you're like me, you then proceed to show the character exactly why this is a monkey's paw situation. But I happen to be really mean and evil.

    On a slight tangent, I think this difference actually has something to do with the discussion in the thread about within-character conflicts and how those work. When you are dealing with how the character is going to feel, you're defining the end state that you are trying to get to, as opposed to the things that happen to (maybe or maybe not) get you there. I, at least, always have a much easier time dealing with the concrete than the abstractly emotional when it comes to making story, because people (both real people and the characters in the story) are much better at controlling actions than feelings. Feelings always come from actions, in the way that I think about stories; everything is always anchored in the concrete.

    For the record, I do plan open-ended games differently from the way I plan fixed-end (usually short-or-medium-term) ones. But I think that's another thread.

    --Jess

    So are fatal goals and story-driving choices qualitatively different animals? Two ends of a unified spectrum? Two independent measures unrelated to each other? Or something else entirely?

    I'd say they're different uses of the same thing, conflict. There are conflicts that, when resolved, lead to more conflicts or leave other conflicts open, and there are conflicts whose resolution clears the field of other conflicts. A good story uses both -- the first throughout most of the story, and the last at the end. I think there's a lot to be said for recognizing the difference and giving the players tools to keep their story going as long as they want it to, and then ending it when they want it to end.

    What an interesting thread. I think this thread isn't so much about stories ending as about stories ending prematurely.

    For example, imagine if we're playing a Lord of the Rings RPG, and the hobbits are hiding from the Nazgul, and suddenly we're faced with the dilemma: this conflict is about finding the ring, so what happens if they actually get the ring?. The story's over.

    Sometimes resolving a fatal goal can kill the story. But it can also re-energise the story: perhaps the story then becomes about the Fellowship trying to recapture the ring or defending itself against an almost invincible Sauron.

    Oh, and there's one point that I don't think has been made so far. In Tony's original post, he said:
    But I have a strong suspicion that for some games (possibly for all games) closure on a PC-to-PC relationship is inherently a fatal goal: it is saying "This relationship is not going to change any more," which says (again, to me) that there is no more story between those two characters. You don't need to ever put them in the same scene again
    I think it's important to notice that this is a PC-to-PC relationship.

    Let's imagine my goal is "Get Sally to be my girlfriend" . And I ask her on a date and she laughs in my face.

    If Sally is not a PC, this doesn't kill the story. Perhaps the experience makes me stronger and I forget about Sally and ask someone else on a date.

    But if Sally is a PC, the story is in real trouble. We need to keep both me and Sally in the story, but where do we go after that rejection? There's not many options: perhaps me and Sally become friends, but it's unlikely. If PCs are involved, I think, fatal goals are much more likely to kill a story.

    In my mind the idea of "fatal goals" is fatally flawed. I can't concieve of any plot resolution that doesn't leave room for further plot. I supose I can allow for exception if the universe is destroyed or all characters in the story are dead.

    But such extreme measures don't seem to be what we are talking about here.

    I'd prefer to talk about a "story pause". This is a place where the story could stop if it wanted to. Frodo gets to Rivendel. Buffy dies at the end of season 5. Joseph dies in Egypt. Sabriel accepts her role as Abhorsen. Mr. Collins accepts Elizabeth Bennet's rejection and marries Ms. Lucas.

    All of these would be dramatically different stories if they stopped early. Its clear (to me at least) that all of them could have been continued past their written end (consider all the apendixes of Lord of the Rings). A story pause is a place where the story could end with some degree of satisfaction. It feels like a natural stopping place, even if we want to pick the story back up again.

    Now I belive that there are prefered stopping places to a story, even amoung the pauses. Buffy might have been a better show had it ended in season 5 rather than season 7, for instance. Pride and Prejudice ends just about perfectally, to my mind.

    Maybe the prefered stopping place is what we are talking about when we talk about fatal goals?

    That is probably the case, but its also clear that there is no sharp division between a fatal goal and a non-fatal one, as the term seems to imply.

    One final thought before I go: The fatalness of a goal depends on the medium of the tale!

    Graham's example of a love story between two PCs features the fatal goal of rejection. I submit that this is only fatal in a RPG where there are two PCs who must be in a "party" of some sort. Its easy to think of a book or movie about the rejected high school romance that blosoms years later (though of course I can't).

    This would work in a movie or a book, but not in a RPG (because several character must continuously interact at the forefront of the story) or a TV show (because of the jaring effects of large amounts of time passing on the audience and the makeup artists).
