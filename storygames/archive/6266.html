\n One problem I have is that, when I'm GMing, players invent ideas that take them away from my adventure.

This happened recently, where I'd prepared an adventure in a mysterious village and, at the first sign of injury, the players decided to go to a hospital.

When you're GMing, which techniques do you use to crush / ignore / bypass inconvenient player ideas? Share your tips and tricks!

Graham

    \n
    \n Have a version of what they need there, as part of the plot.
    \n
    \nSo, in your example, the player is injured and they decide he needs hospitalisation. well, who should stumble around the corner but Dr. Higgins, the friendly neighbourhood retired surgeon and his lovely wife, Gladys. He's more than willing to deal with the wound.
    \n
    \nAnd then BANG! Hit them with something else that distracts them. Nine times out of ten they will forget their journey to the hospital and engage with the new situation and you've also got mileage by introducing a new NPC who can naturally turn out to be the killer/cultist/insane demigod/sunderland supporter (delete as applicable)

    \n Or another less immediate version is to have a conversation with the players about the game prior to the game itself and explain what the session is going to be about. Through this conversation get buy-in from the players regarding the session and approval of the setting and tone.
    \n
    \nThis is actually something that I have a bit of a chip on my shoulder about. In games which are not sandbox games, if a player has agreed to sit down and play the game and is enthusiastic about the game it really irks me when they try their damned hardest to avoid the basic concepts of the genre of the game or the tone of the game. YOU agreed you wanted to play this. YOU agreed you wanted to do this sort of adventure. YOU agreed that you wanted that sort of tone. WHY are you working against it now? Do you gain some sort of sick pleasure from trying to fuck up a game that you bought into?! GAH! (Read this doubly and with a lot more swearing when people try to 'break' a game because they can rather than playing within the spirit of the rules)
    \n
    \nGrab the playtest version of Hot War and see the bit about the pre-game game development and the B&W; photos etc. That level of consensus at the table might be a way to avoid some of these derailing diversions.

    I like to use body language - ominous silences at incorrect or inconvenient choices, heavy sighs, a certain look that radiates dissatisfaction and disappointment.
    \nThat's an excellent technique. I have used this effectively when combined with more traditional player-blocking methods:

    Player: "I go to the hospital."
    GM: [Sighs] "It's disappeared."
    Player: "What do you mean, disappeared?"
    GM: [Ominous silence] "It's just not there anymore."
    Player: "I call an ambulance."
    GM: [Slowly and clearly] "They've. Disappeared. Too."
    Player: "I ask someone where the hospital's gone."
    GM: "They tell you to ask in the village."

    It's very effective.

    \n Ruthless Savagery. Kill their character as many times as required with random overpowered NPCs who seem to appear from nowhere as soon as they try an inconvienient route. Breaking players is a tough task in some cases, but once their will to resist is destroyed they will follow your plot to the letter.
    \n
    \nPersonally I take my cue from a Storyteller who ran the Geovanni Chronicles for a group I was in a few years back. After walking down a street which wasnt on the way to the next piece of plot we were supposed to find, someone threw a dagger at us from an upstairs window. Since we were vampires and werent bothered by daggers, the next one did Aggravated damage and almost killed one of us. When we went in to investigate this odd occurance, we found a random cabal of Clan Baali vampires who chased us down the street in the direction of the plot. This was extremely useful to us and we soon learned the error of our ways.
    \n
    \nLater in the same chronicle, random voices would shout in the distance, explaining what to do next. It all became much easier after that.

    \nIt's both. I think we helplessly derailed it in a way more towards fun silliness than helpfulness.

    In truth, I do a mellow version of what Vodkashok suggests. I do one of the following:

    1) "OK, you go to the hospital, get healed up. Anything else you want to do before you come back?" (or play out a scene which is the equivalent of that sentence: Quick in, quick out, and back)

    or

    2) Move some "needed element to advance the plot" just happen at the Hospital instead, rather than wherever I planned on the characters going.

    or

    3) Rolling with the hospital scene: Introduce 1-2 new NPCs. See how the characters react. If they seem to dig what's going on, I'll move bits of my planned story over to the hospital, discard others, and keep on going. Basically, winging it, playing by ear, etc etc.

    -Andy

    \nYou're right, actually. In Britain, you don't get healed in hospitals: you just sit there waiting.

    So I could probably use the "Nothing happens" trick:

    GM: "OK, you go to the hospital. They tell you to wait."
    Player: "We wait."
    GM: "You wait. Nothing happens. It's very boring here."
    Player: "We wait some more."
    GM: "Nothing happens. There's nothing happening here at all. You notice the village out of the window, which is brightly-lit and exciting-looking."
    Player: "I'll keep waiting. Something must happen."
    GM: "OK. You continue to wait. You strongly get the impression that nothing interesting is going to happen here. The man next to you says 'You know, if you want to be seen quicker, there's a doctor in the village'."

    Almost always works.
