    \n IIRC, Earthdawn was tacked on the setting as the "fantasy past" of Shadowrun. I don't know if the connection is still in place.

    Fading Suns was born with a custom system (the Victory Points system) which was kinda bland and had several problems (with combat and armor, I think). Later it was converted to the d20 system like many other games. And like many games, in the d20 version it kinda sucked (CoC d20, anyone?), so it came back to the VP system (I think). The setting is widely considered ad "one of the few kitchen sink settings done well": it's a mishmash of fantasy, sf, wh40k, dune and several other sources... but yeah, done well.

    Redbrick has picked up the license and is publishing a couple of supplements for the old Fading Suns implementation, and will produce a new edition with a different system (I think).

    \nMalcolm kindly pointed me to this discussion because the original poster had some questions about RedBrick. I am the Managing Director of RedBrick and happy to answer anything you want to know about us.
    \n
    \nAs a lead-in, we currently license Earthdawn from FASA (Living Room Games' license lapsed some time ago, btw; there just hasn't been a formal announcement from them about it), and Fading Suns from Holistic Design Inc. Earthdawn uses its own Step System, designed by Greg Gordon of Torg and other fame. Fading Suns uses the Victory Point System, although there is a d20 rulebook and some dual-statted supplements available.
    \n
    \nWe keep a low profile as we are a small company, with a hard-working team. Because of the geographical nature of our business (we have developers in Germany, USA, UK, and New Zealand), we utilize the Internet for pretty much everything we do. Our web sites at http://www.earthdawn.com and http://www.fading-suns.com both run a full content management system, and we use project management methodologies to develop products. Many of us have full-time jobs and families; RedBrick fills in the cracks. *grin*
    \n
    \nWe partner with OneBookShelf for eBooks and Lulu for print books, the latter being a print-on-demand partner. We sell direct to customers through these sites. You won't find our books in retail stores or via distribution, and that is not likely to change any time soon. If you want to read a little more about the business and how we are going, I write up a State of the 'Brick report each year. You can read these at http://www.earthdawn.com/index.php?categoryid=22
    \n
    \nWe haven't had any formal offers from anyone regarding publishing new games, although we have had several casual enquiries. The original poster may be referring to our forthcoming Pro-Indie label, which is mentioned in the 2007 State of the 'Brick article. We haven't formally announced Pro-Indie yet as we are still "ironing out the kinks" on the web site, but it's not far away. Western City will be our first Pro-Indie release and we hope to publish more independent games in due course.
    \n
    \nHopefully that is enough to start with and I've not bored anyone!
    \n
    \nI look forward to answering your questions as they come up.

    \n Hi all,
    \n
    \nEarthdawn got bounced around a bit. FASA Corporation closed their (publishing) doors in 1999. WizKids picked up the properties and licensed Earthdawn to Living Room Games, who published a couple of First Edition books (an adventure and Barsaive at War), before releasing Earthdawn Second Edition and a number of other supplements. We started negotiations with WizKids in 2002, but the rights reverted to FASA and it wasn't until late 2003 that RedBrick picked up a second license. Our plans for the game line are somewhat more ambitious... *grin* The Earthdawn world is the Fourth World in FASA's cosmology, an age of magic earlier than Shadowrun's Sixth World (we are currently in the no/very low magic Fifth World). Equinox, our forthcoming in-house sci-fi game, is set in the Eighth World.
    \n
    \nFading Suns is very much a Swiss army knife science fiction roleplaying game. Dune is just one of the many influences. The VPS mechanics are probably most similar to those used by King Arthur Pendragon or HeroQuest. Because of the hiatus of the game line (about three years), it has taken some energy to bring the game back into play -- more so than Earthdawn -- but the two eBook adventure Shards, and the new Church Fiefs and Arcane Tech supplements have been well received. We released the Revised Second Edition Rulebook because the main rulebook was out of print. Work is underway on Fading Suns Third Edition, which will utilize the VPS system, but a revised (and hopefully better) incarnation. In the meantime, we are still working on new Shards and at least one more supplement for the Second Edition Fading Suns line.
    \n
    \nIn terms of the company itself, yes RedBrick stays low on the radar. As someone noted earlier, we prefer to just get on with the job. *smile* Our business model is based around managing the Intellectual Property interests of our licensors, and publishing the highest quality products we can produce using the newer on demand technologies available. We are a conservative company, and the model is intended to be one of steady, sustainable growth over the long-term. We assemble dedicated teams for each product line, and we work with team members who have proven to be "personalities" that we can work with. This is an important point -- everyone has an equal say in what happens to make a product work; we don't have the time or energy to waste on dealing with egos. Development is fluid and fun, and everyone gets involved as much as they want (most important for those of us with busy paying jobs and demanding families). However, we do work to a publication schedule. This is necessary to get books finished and into print. We have flaws, like other publishers. Sometimes we just get things wrong. However, we recognize these traits, accept them as important learnings, note them so we can try to avoid making the same mistakes again, and make corrections where appropriate (one of the advantages of on demand). We place great value on "constructive" feedback from fans. "Evolution, not revolution!" And we're happy to talk about what we do and how we do it -- if people ask. Otherwise, it's back to the wordprocessor and layout applications.. *grin*
    \n
    \nOh, and greetings Oliof! (Gee, there's a veritable "Who's Who" on this site!) Yes, I'll see if I can get Dammi to point J\xf6rg here. Western City is not far away from publication (and, bias aside, it rocks). J\xf6rg has done a great job on the game; the fact he's such a nice guy is extra gravy on the hash... *grin*

    \nHope this helps!

    \nThe Step System (which uses all the "funky" dice) is one of the things some people dislike about Earthdawn (some people like it, of course). One of the major contributors to complexity during play is players having to add/subtract step numbers then reference a table to see what dice to roll. We applied a number of tweaks to the rules in the Player's Compendium to minimize this. That involved adding to/subtracting from the results of rolls and, in many cases, modifying the target Difficulty Number. The net upshot is characters roll the dice that are listed on their sheet -- not as many table lookups, and faster game play. Of course, the original rules are still there -- just as an optional rule.
