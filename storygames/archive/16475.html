\n I want to talk through my own experience being called out as a perpetrator of cultural appropriation. From 2004 through 2009, I worked on developing and independently publishing my Game Chef game Ganakagok. Prompted by the key words \u201cice,\u201d \u201cisland,\u201d and \u201cdawn,\u201d I wrote a game where the PCs were members of a tribe of hunters who lived on a frozen island in a world of eternal night where the stars were gods. Each game began at the same point, with the PCs facing an epochal change in their lives: Dawn was coming. The characters\u2019 actions would drive the response of their tribe to the imminent sunrise, with lasting consequences for the fate of the world, the people, and themselves.

With this as the high concept of the game, it seemed \u201cnatural\u201d to rely on some Eskimo cultural trappings. These trappings served as a kind of shorthand for the setting and its tropes; what some larpers call the \u201cinteraction codes\u201d (i.e., suites of shared meanings and referents; see Eirik Fatland\u2019s 2006 Knutepunkt article) at work in a game. I instructed the GM to make sure that the players gave their characters names that were vaguely Inuit in sound\u2014\u201cprimitive and icy,\u201d I\u2019m ashamed to say, is a phrase I used. In my defense, my intent was to discourage cartoonish characters by reminding the GM of the need to take the setting seriously and enforce coherence of character and setting. But it\u2019s possible to read that language and understand it as \u201cexotification\u201d of Eskimo culture, and I regret it. But I\u2019m not embarassed about references to Eskimo material culture (kayaks, harpoons, parkas, and so forth) that appear in the game, since I think they do help create a sense of the interaction codes driving play.

The game also relies for much of its force on a special deck of playing cards that have oracular meanings associated with them; the Ace of Spades, for example, is renamed the Ancient of Tears and associated with the image of a (polar) bear and the motif \u201cto overcome or master.\u201d These meanings come up in play and get incorporated into the fiction in really nifty ways. But in trying to design the deck cheaply and quickly, we used clip art and public domain imagery, some of which were drawn from Haida, Tlingit, and other native American cultures of the Pacific Northwest. I regret not being more sensitive to this particular act of appropriation, not because the imagery mixes and matches from more than one native American culture (since it wasn\u2019t important to me that the people of Ganakagok be regarded as a direct analog of some real-world culture), but because those images were not properly attributed to their originary cultures. Taking the images out of context and appropriating them into the game as if they were manifestations of the (purely imaginary) Nitu culture was artistically wrong, amounting to a very real plagiarism of setting material.

Nonetheless, I wasn\u2019t attuned to these issues as issues until after I published the game and received some criticism along those lines, gently from a friend and more harshly by a fellow named Chris Chinn, who is a vocal on-line advocate for these issues. I thought about their comments and had a brief dialogue with Chris, which I discuss here. In it, I conclude that my obligations are to encourage the support of native American and particularly Inuit artists, and to make clear the provenance of any native American imagery used in the game\u2019s materials. Other than mailing a check, I am ashamed to say that I haven\u2019t done much to fulfill those obligations recently, but I\u2019m aware of them as outstanding debts that will need to be addressed sooner rather than later if I want to think about a second edition of Ganakagok. The important point is that I needn\u2019t have suffered those criticisms if only I\u2019d been more sensitive to my ethical obligations as an RPG designer\u2014someone whose business it is to take bits and pieces of human culture and recombine them into playable forms. We are all bricoleurs, like it or not.

In any event, the experience of being called out for cultural appropriation has made me more cautious with my current project, The New World, undertaken in response to my friend Simon Rogers\u2019 desire for a game about colonization that permits role-playing and is not a Civilization-like strategy game while at the same time incorporating some sense of long-term consequentiality. The New World is similar to Ganakagok in many ways, and my experience of being charged with cultural appropriation has shaped my design goals. The concept of the game is rich enough that it doesn\u2019t need to rely on a particular tendentious or problematic understanding of human history. So I\u2019ve tried to broaden the diversity of references I use to underpin the game\u2019s oracular motifs (which inspire character backgrounds and in-game events), enabling the free play of participants\u2019 historical imaginations, and the game makes no presumptions about the PCs\u2019 sympathies with what it calls Native, Newcomer, and Outsider cultures, allowing individual cultural allegiances to shift and cultures themselves to change as a result of their encounters which each other. I am waiting for playtest comments and recognize the need for some procedural refinements, but conceptually the game does what I want it to do.

Ultimately, I think talking about issues of cultural appropriation and intellectual property more generally is really worthwhile, particularly because of how role-playing games are fundamentally toys for playing with culture. Even if it can be painful to be called out on those grounds.

    I very much appreciate your sincere desire to do better. You should be expected to listen to criticism, and be open to hearing when you have made a mistake. You should be expected to be expected to be respectful of other people's feelings. Being respectful is the best way of gaining the respect of people.

    I have been in one previous discussion about culture and respect on this forum. The person who started that discussion never did listen to what I had to say. That person was not respectful. That person wanted validation for their "creativity", and was unable to listen to the fact that they were insensitive.

    I would like to say that I have respect for you for approaching this subject with humility, and with an effort to listen. While I have not read your game, and I do not really understand the critique you are addressing, I think that it is admirable that you can publicly admit your mistake.     Posted By: WordmanAlong these same lines, suppose a second edition of Ganakagok jettisons imagery drawn from Inuit, Haida, Tlingit and other cultures, and instead uses an artist-invented symbology and style without reference to other cultures (assume for a moment that doing this is even possible). Is that better or worse?
    \nBetter or worse in what way? I suspect people are talking about a lot of different things here.

    Here's how I see it:

    Fantasy lays a gauzy veil across its cultural sources. This is why euro-medieval-fantasy doesn't include very common terrible things about real European medieval times, say, virulent anti-Semitism. (Edit: Nobody freaks when that happens, though maybe we should.)

    Hell, sometimes extremely central features of the real culture are flat erased (try to find a church in Middle Earth - you will have to hike a long way in.)

    If you lay that veil across cultural sources that are already being erased in your audience's world - native cultures everywhere are a good example, then you're making the problem worse.

    Superficiality is not necessarily a flaw in creating a fantasy world! But it is, and especially damaging, when it is applied to things that the audience only has a superficial knowledge of anyway.

    So the audience and context also matters. A 1001 Nights game - ancient Muslim fantasy - has different impacts in Ohio in 2002 and in Cairo in 2010.

    (GMs normally have much more responsibility than designers in this area too. Just glance at GURPS if you don't believe me.)

    Instead of obscuring the cultural source, the fantastical elements (as well as any other elements) should enhance and develop understanding of the cultural source.

    This is hard and sometimes you will fuck it up, like all difficult things.

    (For something like 1001 Nights, you have the benefit of hundreds of years of different approaches to it, scholarship about it, etc., so there's a big wide road map about it, and a lot of the work is already done and widely developed. Contrast this to the culture Ganakagok drew from.)

    It's not an issue of culture somehow "belonging" to someone in the intellectual property sense, and you shouldn't "take" what is "theirs", culture doesn't really work like that. Everything is indeed up for grabs. But when you're dealing with marginalized cultures and histories that are being erased, you need to approach it very differently than you would approach a fantastical version of well-accepted history or famous mainstream figures. And your target audience really, really, REALLY matters.

    It's not an issue of culture somehow "belonging" to someone in the intellectual property sense, and you shouldn't "take" what is "theirs", culture doesn't really work like that.
    \nI think its very much like intellectual property. After all if it wasn't "property" there'd be nothing to appropriate, and it certainly isn't physical property. But just like there's a point at which the world as a whole is better off when the IP enters the public domain and can be used, reused, repurposed, reinvisioned, and reinvented without anyone being able to stop such by claiming of "ownership"; so cultural property should work the same way.

    There's a point at which members of society have to simply accept that a culture has entered the public domain and they no longer have any ownership claim over it.

    My current working notion for when that is, is the point at which no living person remains who had ever personally first hand met and spoken with an actual living member of that culture. By living member of the culture I do not mean someone who has learned about or recreated or attempted to preserve the culture, I mean when the culture was a living culture that the person was born and raised in it.

    To me, once a culture is no longer being actively practiced, and no one alive ever knew anyone who was alive when it was actively practiced then claims to having a stake in that culture are more wishful thinking than valid reality.

    Since that's kind of a hard claim to measure...if you figure person A was born and raised in a culture until the age of 10 when the culture was effectively destroyed and then went on to live to 100. And person B met person A when they were 10 and A was 90 and knew them and shared with them for the last 10 years of their life; and person B went on to live to 100...then doing the math backwards...180 years after the culture ceased to be an active living culture, it should be considered public domain, which here in 2012 would mean anything prior to 1832 should be considered fair game...placing Native North American Cultures near but not quite passed that point just yet...while American Slave Culture still has a good several decades to go...probably longer than many of us will be alive.

    Thus, while one might take umbrage for historical authenticity reasons over certain portrayals of Rome or ancient Egypt, it would be IMO a fair stretch for modern Italians or Egyptians to claim any cultural ownership save happening to live on the same piece of ground. Similarly umbrage over games like Victoriental can be seen because that period of pan-asian cultural history is still within the ownership window. By contrast while I'd sympathize with someone disclaiming the historic accuracy of a portrayal of Ming or Yuan China, I'd be inclined to tell them to take a flying leap if claims were made regarding cultural appropriate. Ming China now belongs to all of us...just like Rome and Egypt and Ancient Mesopotamia...and I'd say with respect to New Fire...Native Mesoamerican cultures which were all pretty much ravaged long before 1832.

    \n I agree with JDCorley here. The basic problem is that for many peoples, their own reality and culture is being drowned out by a much larger host of stereotyped views created by outsiders. So this isn't an issue of "intellectual property" in my mind, in that as long as the more authentic cultural sources are being propagated, then imitations don't matter much.

    I think that these issues may still apply even if you are using a historical culture like Ming China or Mongolia under Genghis Khan. The issue is that modern people's views are often strongly influenced by historical stereotypes. For example, a modern-day American's view of Mongolia is likely to be driven more by generally fictional portrayals of Genghis Khan than anything else. This is even true of China to a degree.

    Drawing from my own experience:

    I don't think Korea suffers from cultural appropriation per se, but it does have a history of cultural marginalization. When the Japanese invaded and occupied Korea (from 1909 to 1945), they had a very deliberate program to erase Korean culture. My father grew up going to mandatory Japanese schools in the late 1930s, where students were forced to only speak in Japanese, learn only Japanese culture, and were taught propaganda that Korea was an offshoot of Japan. While the modern relevance of this history is fading, it still annoys me to see the near omnipresence of Japanese cultural icons (ninja, samurai, etc.) while the deliberately erased Korean cultural icons remain largely unknown.

    \n So, I find Ralph's formulation of historical culture attractive, and he does raise a good point about history being a "foreign country." The culture of Egypt in the time of King Tut is just as alien to your average 20th century Egyptian as it is to any 20th century American, and it would also be fairly ridiculous, for example, to accuse George R. R. Martin of appropriating French or English culture because he writes fantasy fiction inspired by medieval chivalry.

    The problem I see is that it doesn't much account for historiography. Historical knowledge is not an abstract possession. It comes from somewhere, and it comes from someone. Some cultures preserve their own history, other cultures have been recovered in more recent times. Tutankhamun may be well more than 180 years gone, but our knowledge of him comes from a little over 180 years of "Egyptology" and archaeology in the Middle East.

    As an extended example, let's say you sit down with the Cantares Mexicanos, in preparation to create your Aztec rpg. It seems pretty cut-an-dry, no? Those Aztecs are all gone.

    And yet, there are modern speakers of Nahuatl. They don't make war to collect sacrifices for the old gods anymore, but they do continue to speak the same language as those people did. Granted, they will probably never read your game, but what would they think if they did?

    Or to expand on Zac's comment, there are also (non-Nahuatl-speaking) Mexicans who are proud of their indigenous heritage, who are trying to preserve that history, and who feel some ownership over it. What if they think you are disrespecting what they consider their heritage? Would that concern be legitimate, and if not, how would you convince anyone that it isn't?

    And what about the appropriation of other indigenous cultures? After all, you would probably be sitting down with J. Bierhorst's translation of the Cantares Mexicanos. Bierhorst portrayed these poems as ghost dances, by analogy with those of the Paiute Indians of the late 19th century, and has received quite a bit of criticism for this from other Aztec/Nahuatl specialists. Which raises the question: Is Bierhorst's interpretation legitimate, or does it constitute the appropriation of more recent indigenous culture, within that 180-year time frame?

    In any case, that's just one example, and a long way of saying that knowledge about historical cultures reaches us through a process, and that process may, in some cases, be heavily influenced by recent politics and may even include cultural appropriation of its own. Further, each case is different. The distinction between what I think of as "dead history" and "living history" (the difference between whether anybody is still alive who was alive at the time) can be a perfectly legitimate way to draw a line between what is and isn't a cultural possession\u2014in some cases.

    But every case is different. Ancient Egypt has reached us through a different set of scholars and cultural analogies and colonial infrastructures than Aztec history, both of which are different from those taken by, say, Imperial China or medieval England.

    If there's one thing I've learned from studying historiography, it's that studying historiography ruins everything and drains your enthusiasm like some kind of horrible vampire there are no universal metrics. Every piece of history has a different set of curators and a different relationship to people alive now, and that the distance between now and then isn't really a good indicator of what you can expect from those curators, or popular opinions. So, as much as I like the idea that the past, once it's been the past for a while, is something that belongs to all of us\u2014and while I agree that Ming China should belong to all of us, regardless of whether it does or not\u2014in practice I find it's just too complicated to get much use out of that position once actual research starts. At least, that's how it is for me.

    \nYou too easily chop culture up into chunks based on time. A culture is not so simple.

    I am Native. I have a culture that is actually very much far removed from what could be considered the norm, even in the US. My culture is alive and well. There are all of the aspects of traditional in my life, including every element that could be considered culture, from language, to music, to story, to dance, to art and artifice, to diet, to spirituality... My culture is not dead. Traditional can have various timelines. Something that lasts more than one generation has become a tradition, and many things do not take that long to become tradition. I do not need to go back 180 years to see traditional, and there are things that do not go back that far that are traditional. Your timeline has no real connection to living cultures.

    Is my culture identical to the culture of my pre-colombian ancestors? Obviously not. But culture is like a tree. It starts as a seed, grows to a seedling, then a sapling, then a full grown tree, eventually dies and becomes a snag, and then a fallen log. At each point along this timeline, there is no doubt that it is the same tree, despite not being identical. At every point along this timeline, there is value. Just like the tree, there may come a point when taking part of the tree will not kill it. But there is no point when taking something will not change it. And when a culture or tree has had so much taken from it, so much insult in it's lifetime, you can understand when the culture or tree says "No more. The rest is mine to share as I like, but you have no right to just take." While the culture lives, you have no right to take from it. And only those who live the culture have a right to say if it is alive.

    The other aspect of this conversation is that, for cultures outside of the norm, perceptions of the minority by the dominant culture are frequently inaccurate. The inaccuracies can be damaging. These inaccuracies often have far reaching historical roots, and far reaching contemporary consequences. I still meet people all the time who tell me how unfortunate that it is that Native people are so inclined to drinking, ignoring the fact that every culture with similar socioeconomics has similar rates of alcoholism, and that maybe the problem isn't race or culture, maybe it is poverty. When I have to think about how I will be seen when I drink a beer at a holiday party, just because of my ethnicity, then I feel the real effects of hurtful stereotyping. My culture is alive, but so are the inaccuracies held as truths by those outside my culture.

    When you take aspects from living cultures, then reuse them for your own ends, be careful that you know what you are talking about. Be careful that you are not recombining hurtful stereotypes, or even romanticized stereotypes in your appropriations. Be careful that you are not being disrespectful. The best way to avoid this is to seek out firsthand knowledge of the culture you are writing about, and be respectful of the feedback you get. When someone takes the time to tell you that it is hurtful, then accept it gracefully.

    And please do not dismiss an entire people and their experiences so easily.

      Expertise is probably the most important aspect of stakeholdership. Knowledge, practice, language, and belief are all areas where people do constant boundary work, making it perhaps a bit too simplistic to just say "anyone who cares" is a stakeholder, because just off the top of my head, there's a bunch of different, distinct categories that a potential game designer would have to deal with differently:

      1.
      The most important stakeholder group is the one with personal experience of the subject matter, i.e. people who speak the language, regularly engage in the practices, etc. So, if your game is inspired by Inuit cultural ideas and practices, as in Bill's case, this group is the Inuit peoples themselves. If you're writing a game about African park rangers, then it's African park rangers. They're not a distinct ethnic group or nationality, of course, but I assume they have their own culture and jargon and whatnot like any profession. If your subject is the pre-contact Aztecs or the pre-Hellenistic Egyptians, then there are no people in group 1. Which doesn't mean the next three categories are any smaller or less vocal.

      2.
      Then there are people with knowledge and experience but who, for one reason or another, can't identify 1:1 as part of that group. So, like anthropologists, linguists, and historians who specialize in Inuit culture but are not Inuit. Or non-Inuit in other fields who say, live and work in the arctic with Inuit people. If you're an outsider looking to write a game, this is probably the best group to make contact with first, because they are both knowledgeable and are experienced in communicating their knowledge to other people outside the subject culture. They also tend to write a lot of stuff, especially the academics, so you can just go read that stuff, too, and not pester anyone until you kinda sorta know what you're talking about, at least a little bit.

      3.
      Then there are people who self-identify with the subject matter and consider it emblematic of or essential to their culture, regardless of knowledge or practice. So, there are Mexicans who only speak Spanish but are still proud of their indigenous heritage and don't want to see it caricatured or exploited. There were Britons who felt that since they had started reading Xenophon when they were twelve years old that they had the right to dig up whatever they wanted and crate it back to England, and now there are Greeks who want the Elgin marbles back. But also, if Eero Tuovinen, Finnish game designer, goes and lives in Israel for ten years and writes a game based on his observations, he will at some point have to deal with American Jews who have never been to Israel but nevertheless feel they have a right to critique his game and its portrayal of Israel. However, if he really spent ten years in Israel, then he has already become part of group #2, right? So yes, you are of course a stakeholder in your own work insofarasmuch as you know what you are talking about. If, when you start, you know next to nothing about the subject you are interested in, then you are not really a stakeholder. Hell, if you don't know anything at the end, either, you might not even be part of group 3, you might still not be a stakeholder! If you know nothing about the subject of your game and can't self-identify with it, I would say that's a pretty clear case of exploitative cultural appropriation.

      4.
      There's also people who have a stake by association (for lack of a better term), mainly because of colonialism. Like, Bill's game is inspired by Inuit culture, but because he's an outsider, all indigenous peoples of the Americas are stakeholders. Even though Inuit, and say, Mayan, Navaho, and Coast Salish cultures are all completely different, white settler culture tends to treat them all the same. So, if Bill's game is some Orientalistic hack job, non-Inuit peoples can be fairly certain he would treat their cultures in the same way. If Bill writes his game in a more informed manner, and it promotes respect for and understanding of the Inuit, then other indigenous peoples can point to it and say "we would like our dealings with the dominant culture to be more like this more often."

      And these are just general types of groups who might feel they have a stake in your game. There might be more that I'm forgetting, and certainly there will be other perspectives on this. I'm just one dude.

You probably don't want to consult them about your living-in-Israel game. Group 4 is probably the easiest group to impress? If you show respect for groups 1-3, that's kinda what group 4 is looking out for, so you don't have to do anything extra, really.

      \n Well, there's still the issue of how you actually come to know about a historical culture. As I said before, there's no such thing as objective historical knowledge.

      Like, let's say you want to make a game based on Ancient Greek literature (epic, tragedy, comedy, let's say). There are no issues of appropriation whatsoever. For centuries, the people who have preserved these works have promoted them and done all they can to spread them. Meanwhile, they are part of European history, and Europeans have done the same thing with their history. They preserve it, they promote it, they want everybody to know about it, and they keep all this stuff from the past around, as distinct pieces of history compartmentalized from everyday, living culture. It's actually really, really easy to pronounce this stuff public domain, and all the aspects that encourage this are most pronounced with regard to Ancient Greek literature.

      On the other hand, there's the archaeological knowledge, which has been recovered as part of Western Europe's physical and economic colonialism. There's a lot of material culture whose ownership is contested because British people and other Europeans dug it up and took it without asking anybody and then used it to promote their own political ideas. There's a lot that isn't contested, too, but that's still something to keep in mind. Re-framing it so that these works belong to a dead culture and thus are "up for grabs" is a tactic that's been used against Greeks and Egyptians before.

      In the Americas, there's no history per se, since there's no written documents. Knowledge of historical cultures comes from either archaeology or oral history. The archaeology, if anything, is vastly more problematic than Classical archaeology or Egyptology in terms of colonialism and the violence done to living people. It's also entirely material culture that is recovered by these methods, so there's certain limitations put on what there is that you can even appropriate.

      Oral history on the other hand is, by definition, part of a living culture. So any access to the past you can gain from it comes to you as part of a living culture, so any issues of copyright or public domain you could possibly think of applying are completely nullified.

      Now, in certain parts of the Americas, we have a long history of European settlement and thus written records. And some of those records were made in order to preserve indigenous art and culture. So, like, I've written lyrics for songs that were inspired by Aztec poetry (which I can only read in English translation). I don't write songs about the Aztecs, but I've borrowed some imagery and some metaphors, because I like them a lot. And these are ideas that have exited the culture of living people long ago. This is sort of a separate issue and not exactly an argument against your idea, but it's a complication to be aware of.

      \n The distinction between "living" "dead" and "dying" cultures that's being attempted here reminds me of that scene from Monty Python, where the ostensible corpse feels obliged to point out that he's not dead yet.       And it matters, too, that these processes of cultural evolution, transformation, and competition take place in a social and political realm where who gets to be a cultural stakeholder is "contested" (i.e., fought over).       \nI think I've established my position to the extent it has value to do so. I'll add this comment only because Eero has specifically requested my train of thought.

      I don't consider self-identification in a culture to have any validity. Having ownership rights in a culture is not something IMO one can just choose to have because they feel like it, any more than a person can self-identify as the owner of a tree, or a car, or a bag of chips. Ownership rights have rules, you either are or aren't an owner, and there are specific ways to become an owner. And just like you can't simply wake up one day and decide you own my car, I can't simply wake up one day and decide I have an ownership stake in 15th century Finnish Culture.

      To me a culture exists in a specific time and a specific geography and is closely tied to the families (or family proxies) who lived in that time and that place and passed down the elements of that culture. If you lived in that time and in that place you have an ownership interest in that culture. If you didn't, you don't, and self identifying that you do IMO doesn't make it so.

      I also don't accept that culture is a long extended thing that exists continuously throughout time (see my earlier evolution example). I grew up in North East, Pennsylvania; USA in the 70s and 80s. That's my culture. My culture was built on the culture of North East, Pennsylvania; USA from the 50s and 60s...but that's not my culture. That culture was the culture of the 50s and 60s...I wasn't alive then, it doesn't belong to me...it belongs to my parents. Because of the role my parents had in my life their culture was essential to mine, and so one can argue I have a stake in it...and a lesser stake in my grandparents' culture...and a lesser stake yet in my great grandmother's culture (but I only ever knew 1 of my great parents and none of my great great grandparents...so no...IMO I don't have a stake in those.

      The idea that this is somehow all part of the same culture...that somehow I have ownership rights on the whole history of North East, Pennsylvania going back to its founding in 1794 I think is Ludicrous with a capital L. The demographics were not the same, the industry was not the same, how people spent their time was not the same, how they worshiped was not the same...that's not even touching on technology and politics. Sure we have plaques commemorating old historical events, and old maps in the court house and old dates on old buildings and streets named after old families (and some of those family names can still be found in the phone book). But no...that's not the same culture. Maybe my culture is descended from that culture...but its not the same culture, and trying to claim it is would be ridiculous.

      So that's my train of thought. You own what you actually own...you don't have a right to claim ownership to something that existed and was gone before you were ever born, or your parents were born, or your grandparents were born. If you don't even know anyone who'd been born in that culture...it's no longer your culture. And once its no longer your culture...you can self identify all you want and make all kinds of grandiose claims...but to me...such claims hold pretty much no weight...and nor should they. I can't even comprehend the idea that some have expressed above that cultures don't die...of course they die. And at the pace of the modern world they die faster than ever. American culture has changed so dramatically in just the last 12 years that I'd argue its very damn near a completely different culture today than it was in just the year 2000. Maybe that's a bit of hyperbole...but it damn sure is a completely different culture today than it was in 1984 when I was 13. In 1984 we were worried about the Soviets and Cold War and terrorism. Terrorism was something that happened in Europe and making a phone call away from home involved feeding dimes into a phone booth with a rotary dial.

Whatever culture a person is living in today, and however dedicated they are to preserving yesterday, reenacting yesterday, remembering yesterday...its not yesterday.       \nI get the impression -- and please, correct me if I'm wrong -- that you yourself do not feel particularly strongly about or self-identify deeply with any specific culture beyond your own, immediate, personal life experience. That is, you think of yourself as a modern individual, and that while your beliefs and customs were influenced and shaped by your childhood, the real importance of those beliefs and customs are that they are the ones you personally have right now, today, in your actual life. You don't feel any need to define yourself by or shape your identity around something that happened before you were born.

      That's kind of how I feel about my own cultural background -- I'm aware of my family history going back a few generations, but I don't have any allegiance to or affinity for the countries my ancestors came from, or feel like I need to observe the same holidays as they did, or cook the same food, or learn my ancestors' languages or even identify myself to other people as a descendant of the half-dozen European countries I could claim heritage from, or whatever. That sort of thing has no real appeal to me, so I don't make it part of my own sense of identity. And that's pretty easy for me to do, since all my connections to that family history are indirect and somewhere around a century ago, and so as far as I'm concerned, it's got nothing at all to do with my life or who I am. My culture looks like the mainstream, modern American culture, and I fit into it just fine. I don't feel like anyone cares where I came from or where my parents' parents came from, and so I am free from having to care about that, too: no one is going to look at me and think, "Oh, he's German/Irish/Bulgarian/whatever, how exotic," no one is going to ask me what race I am or what nationality I am, no one is going to give me a weird look when they ask where my family came from and I say, "Michigan."

      One of the interesting things about this thread to me is getting to see the other side of that coin, and get an inkling of why someone who is NOT like me would have excellent, compelling reasons to care about where their family came from, and why they would want to feel connected to it, and especially why they would want to protect and preserve it. I feel like I can get away with not giving a shit about my great-grandparents, because everything about the culture I'm immersed in makes it easy for me to feel that way...but that's so clearly not true for everyone, and it shouldn't be: people preserving, observing, honoring, and defending their cultural identities is important. Also admirable, and enriching, and -- here is where I think you and I differ -- not something that has anything at all to do with what year it is. If you're invested in your family history, you are invested in it; it doesn't have to be 1832 and you don't have to live like it is for you to value and feel connected to the way your 19th-century relatives lived. And if someone from outside your culture dismisses not only your connection to that heritage, but actually tells you that you have no right to claim that connection or to complain if they do some damage to it through misrepresentation or outright disrespect? Even I, the mainstreamiest member of the American mainstream, can disagree with that argument.

      \nThis has a high potential to be wrong. I will not say with absolute certainty that it is, but there is a really good chance that it is wrong. If this idea has no room for the reality that my eagle feathers mean the same thing to me as they did to my great grandfather who passed them down to me, if this idea has no room for the reality that my sweats and my vision quest have the same meaning to me as it did to my father, and his father, and the unnamed great-great-great grandfather in my ancestry, if this idea has no room for the fact that the stories I tell my children mean the same thing to me as they did to my ancestors when they told them to each other in Mississippi prior to the Trail of Tears, if this idea has no room for the fact that the reverence I feel for the corn I grow or the deer I shoot or the fish I catch as my ancestors felt in Indian Territory, then it is totally wrong.

      This idea seems to assume that "culture" has to always include every aspect of life of every person alive at a certain time. This is not true. There are things that are maintained, not for history's sake, but for the intrinsic value in them. They are maintained because that is how people do things. My ancestors danced because that is what they did, AND for the sake of their ancestors. I dance for my ancestors, and this can be likened to some sort of historical preservation, but this is not why I do it. I do it because that is what I do, what my family does, as part of my culture, my living culture. This is not historical preservation for preservations' sake. Preservation is a byproduct of the fact that this is something that we do. I do it for the spiritual connection I get from knowing that I come from this direct line, this thing that is bigger than just me, this thing that transcends time. This thing makes me immortal in a sense, as I am but a part of a much larger whole. It is the very fact that you try to separate a person from their history that is the huge disconnect for me. You may not feel the connection to your past that I do, but denying that there is no connection, no present and important element of lineage and the past in anyone, and that there is no need to respect this connection will leave me unable to agree with you. It is also the reason that the Yakima are offended that a museum holds the bones of a man dug up on Yakima land. The Yakima are not the only ones offended. Many people will be offended when historical context is ignored. To disrespect someones history is disrespectful of all who value their own history. This is the morass and mire that you will encounter should this rule ever come into play.

      Any model that cannot account for the fact that some people do the same things that their ancestors did more than 180+ years ago is not accounting for reality, and any model that ignores the importance that many people hold for their own history and ancestry is not accounting for reality.

      And again, even if this model did work the way it is intended, I have yet to hear of an example where it could be applied. So, what is the point? At this point it just seems like a model without possible practical application that is an insult to people who are not disconnected from their past.
