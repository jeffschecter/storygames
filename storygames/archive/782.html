A while back I picked up the I am 8-bit book from the art show of the same name. The artists took the imagery of old-school video games, especially from the NES, and reimagined them in more detailed art. This approach really highlights how utterly surreal those video games are. Super Mario Bros. is a serious acid trip when you think about it, what with the mushrooms and floating blocks and everything, yet in the context of an 8-bit video game most people don't question it at all. (Where else could you get killed by being squished between a wall and the edge of the screen because you're in an auto-scrolling level?)

So, I'm wondering what kinds of abstractions RPGs have that don't make sense in other contexts. Other kinds of tabletop games can get away with a lot more abstraction -- like how Brawl is basically about matching colors really quickly -- since they don't have the same need for narrative, but I think there's still some out there. D&D is probably the obvious, what with the AC/HP/to-hit system and character levels. I haven't played all that much D&D, but when I did this kind of stuff tended to either stay relatively invisible to the story, or be purposely shoved into it (like the game I played where the DM required PCs to go get special training to gain new levels).

I got a Japanese RPG anthology called "TRPG Super Session: Daikyouen," which has a sentai parody game called Eiyuu Sentai Seigiranger. ("Heroic Fighting Team JusticeRanger") Sentai shows always have mook characters, and in Seigiranger they're they're called "Dicemen." They're humanoid figures in black spandex with big six-sided dice for faces, which show how many hit points they have left. So, instead of being used for action resolution (you use playing cards for that), they're a sort of metagame miniatures thing, which I find really cool and, at least for this particular game, very fitting.
