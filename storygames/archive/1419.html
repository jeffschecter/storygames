I just put up an AP report from last night's TSOY session, the first in a mini-campaign deal I'm running. I'm super happy about it.

It's set during the Klondike gold rush and set in Skagway, Alaska, a time and place I'm very familiar with. I was surprised at the depth of detail I was dredging up during the game, and found myself really enjoying a mental return visit to the region I haven't been around in six years. That, combined with my love and knowledge of that time in history, seemed to bring the game to life for me. It helped that I'm in a great group that is always game to go along with whatever scheme I come up with, but the fact that this particular game is rooted in something I know a ton about seems important and awesome.

Have you participated in games in which you were essentially a subject matter expert? Did it help or hinder play?

    I think we might break it down into the sorts of expertise being used. There is the know-that expertise, which really can get kind of boring except as used for splashes of color (there is a reason we don't have Encyclopedia Britannica: the RPG), then there is the know-how which can be wicked cool when it adds opportunities to the game but frustrating when multiple know-hows conflict (breaks SIS)--often times a 'real world' know how and a 'genre' know how.

    I have a friend who has been doing martial arts seriously for some time as well as having worked as a bouncer. Add that to having close family and personal ties to the realities of military life and violence, and you get a guy who has an amazing sense of what combat looks like. There is color, yes, in the way the wounds get described, but also 'hooks' in the description that let us who are playing think of interesting ways to further the combat.

    I think this luring you in with opportunity is what people are getting at when they talk about the goodness of authenticity--it shows you some place you can go in the game, even if that someplace is hemmed in a little with limitations from that knowledge. It is when your knowledge lets you 'see' the opportunities and dynamic possibilities of a thing rather than just as a series of facts.

    I'm not sure it's just bad if expertise provides limits--limits are the tools you use to figure out what you can do as much as things that indicate what you can't do. It seems like the real problem lies in the conflict of what people feel they 'know.'

    So, did it add pressure? Yes, but I think in that mostly good way, that let us think of resources in a real serious way. When people start failing endurance checks and have to lose something from their pack...well, it was at least in my head that these could be pretty important choices. It also encouraged us to *do* and engage with the setting. Then, when we did engage, other knowledge came in handy to provide us with interesting engagements, which started to dovetail with the story elements (like keys). For me, at least, the knowledge gave the setting some depth, made it feel like it wasn't just window-dressing.

    I suspect the conflict between Zeke and the Indians was also partially motivated by that sense of pressure from the setting, in addition to pressure from the character concept. This is a dangerous place, we *need* help.

    It probably constrained some narration, but it didn't feel like contraint, more like opportunity--sure we can't go stomping up the trail without thinking about it, but that means we *get* to feel out the town a little more. If the town hadn't been interesting in its own right, yeah, I suspect it would have felt a little frustrating, like going through the motions because we had to do it or else.
