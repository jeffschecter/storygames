\n I've played a few of these, but never designed one. Well, not counting miniature/skirmish-type games, which I've designed a few of, and those have no GMs.

But I'm not sure about designing an RPG or story game with no GM, and I'd love some input from people that have done it.

What were some of the big challenges? How do you write the rules when you know that there's no one person responsible for managing the flow of the game? I mean, sure, I can read a few GMless game texts (and I have done so), but I'm still wondering about the design process.

Any huge differences in the way that you approach the design of mechanics/resolution, the writing of text, the playtesting process?

    \n Just lost a longer post, but here's my bullets, based on where I'd selfishly like to see GMless design go.

    1. GMless design is in its infancy; don't necessarily draw on other GMless games: do something new (maybe don't do rotating scene framing, for example).
    2. Draw on the premise of your game for inspiration. Don't start with making GMless rules; start with what the game is about.
    3. Consider not having all the player roles be "equivalent" or balanced. Asymmetry is a cool new area for exploration.
    4. Maybe start by developing a fun GMless style of play with one of your local groups, and then trying to formalize that into play procedures.

    \n Ah, GMless games! I used to want nothing more than to make these. The game that I got furthest on, besides Dog Eat Dog, was called Facing the Beast. GMlessness was only one of its like seventeen exciting new design approaches. It had huge problems and needs redesign, but one thing I decided on was that GMlessness needs to grow organically from the game. I also think the key thing to think about is what you actually mean by GM. Here are some of the things that GMs occasionally do in games:

    * Set scenes
    * Provide opposition
    * Manage the plot
    * Keep the party "on track"
    * Keep the players on task
    * Produce extensive description
    * Require narration from players
    * Judge the quality of narration
    * Wield the power of life and death
    * Decide intraparty conflicts
    * Decide intra-social-group conflicts in real life
    * Provide and maintain the game space
    * Resolve questions not decided by the rules
    * Resolve questions arising from differing interpretations of the rules
    * Resolve questions explicitly mandated to the GM by the rules

    This is obviously a lot of different game and social roles all stuck together, and I'm sure there are more. Which of these does your game need the most? Which of these are easiest to peel off and give to generic players, and which are difficult to do that with? Lots of indie games have already divested some of these, even with a GM ("judge the quality of narration" or "set scenes," for example). "Provide opposition," by contrast, is probably the biggest problem most GMless games have to grapple with. Figure out what the key elements are, and what your game needs to function.

    \n Also, I disagree with Evan on consulting existing games for a simple reason: basically all the existing GMless games -- bar a few -- kinda start with a GM-ed game and then work out a way to remove the GM (typically by splitting those responsibilities up among the players). The future, at least in my mind, is starting from a premise in which a GM doesn't even make sense, assuming GMlessness from the get-go instead of trying to add it afterwards. The best example of this, of the top of my head, is Jackson Tegu's Silver & White.

    \n I've been working into GMless designs myself for some time. Story-oriented games are easier to work this way, if you just define some procedures to limit players input and participation in order to allow every player have the same amount of spotlight. However things complicate when you go Tactical (not Gamist, boardgames are gamist and those rule) because you need to find a way to offer players a method to create interesting challenges. Of course, seems best option here is to go somewhat gamist instead of tactical, but that would limit the types of tactical challenges the players would meet.

    You will also need to add a mechanic able to deal with player's more subtle choices (because in the best case scenario, players will come up with a solution that gives them an automatic win without dealing with the challenge directly.) Of course that would depend of what kind of challenges the players could meet.

    After a few attempts I've finished with a GMless game that still doesn't feel the same as a traditional one, which is part of the goal that I'm aiming to. I'm still looking for a particular strong mechanic to deal with players motivations, including their relationships with NPCs.

    \n To my mind, design starts with imagining a conversation around a table. For a conversation, you need people to know when it's their turn to speak.

    GMd games work really well because it's very clear whose turn it is to speak. Conversation tends to flow back and forth from player to GM and back, shifting players as the GM shifts attention. The only exception is conversations between characters, which follow the patterns of conversations between people, so everyone is familiar with that.

    To design a GMless game you need to imagine how the conversation will flow around the table. How will people know when it is their turn to speak? You can use a formal turn structure like a board game, but I've found that that can result in more stilted play than you get from a GM.

    It's not impossible though! People have free-flowing, multi-person conversations all the time. Think of a good multi-person conversation you've had, and what made that work. Chances are the same things will work in your game.

    \n "GM-less" games have a much larger design space than GMed games, which can be overwhelming.

    I've used at least three different approaches to designing games without a GM figure:\n
      \n
    1. Take a GMed design (preferably with a clear designed GM role) and excise the GM role. You can use automation, distributed decision making (voting, and the like), or taking turns to fulfil the original GM responsibilities.
      2. \n
    2. Design explicitly to not have a GM, this tends to work best when examining how board, card, gambling, and improv games all work to produce tension, uncertainty, and meaningful decisions - and then transforming the uncertainty and decisions into a narrative.
      3. \n
    3. Just design your game, focusing on what it needs and being honest with yourself about your assumptions, including but not limited to there being a GM role. This approach can generate the most innovative games, but has two caveats: it may not actually create a GM-less game (although if you are really being honest about your assumptions this is less likely than you'd think) and it may create a game which is too out there (making folks refuse to believe that they actually do what they do).
      4. \n
    \nI prefer method 3, but I've used all of the above and there is a tremendous amount to be discovered from each starting point.

    - Mendel

    \n What about rules as a list of instructions? Some of the designs I've been seeing recently teach the rules only when there's need for them. So you can give players a list of instructions / answering specific questions that help them build the game and the characters. For example, in the last design I was trying, I found out it's enough to point players that this is a fantasy setting and then ask them what threatens the setting (just one event, monster or NPC), then ask them for one hope (again a creature/other NPC/object/event or place) against that threat. Then a few questions to define their characters and a couple NPCs each and then proceed to play.

    I'm still working in this set of instructions for my game but it's perfectly possible. Actually a lot of players tend to look for these kind of instructions first in every game they read: checklists fo making characters, checklists for the GM on how to make adventures, etc. Boardgames work with specific instructions as well, so I'm wondering why RPGs don't usually present the rules in this fashion.
