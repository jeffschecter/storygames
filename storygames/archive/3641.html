\n Hi. I'm new around here. I've been lurking for a bit, but one comment I read here intrigued me enough to register. It was this one by eruditus.
\n
\n
c) I can't say much about the "coolness" of D&D.; All snarkiness aside I think the fun came out of imagination and the fact that we were 12 (or in my case 8). I recently played a D&D; dungeon crawl and to be honest once I put myself into the mental framework of "okay, I'm 15 again" it really was a lot of fun. But it was a social fun. A little puzzle solving. A little role-playing. A little number crunching. A lot of soda, chips and side comments about relations between giants and hobbits. 15 again.
\n
\n
\nThat seems like exactly what I'd consider a fun & wildly successful RPG session. Social fun. A little puzzle solving. A little role-playing. A little number crunching. Snacks & side comments. That's what I'm going for. Even if I'm 38 & not 15. I don't have to try to summon up my teenage mindset to enjoy that.
\n
\nNow, there are lots of things about how I played the game when I was a teenager that don't appeal to me anymore. Too much rules lawyering. Too much emphasis on detailed simulation. Too much complaining about what's wrong with the rules & trying to fix them rather than really trying to understand why the author didn't think they were broken.
\n
\nI'm sure I can come up with more, but all the things that eruditus listed are things I still want. Maybe even more now then when I was a teenager.
\n
\nThat leaves me wondering what it is eruditus wants from an RPG when he isn't taking on a teenage mindset.
\n
\nBut I want to read everyone else's thoughts as well.

    \n I began playing the D&D; basic set when I was about ten years old (1982). It was the first I'd ever heard of RPGs. And, to be honest, I find it to be a defining contributor to who I am today. Back then my friends and I played for the sheer joy of being someone else in a world that wasn't reality. We rarely concerned ourselves with the mechanics of the game, using them mostly only during combat; everything else was fairly freeform. We learned to play without dice, since bullies tended to take those away (as did teachers).
    \n
    \nIt was fun, escapist gaming. The fond memories of those days is what brought me to Story Games, looking for more narrative control, less number-crunching, and a chance to feel like a kid again.
