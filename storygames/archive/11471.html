\n It's useful, I think, to look back at the classic games and analyse the themes at the heart of them.

Snakes and Ladders, for example, is clearly a Buddhist game. Its "fruitful void", if you'll excuse me stretching the expression just a little, is that it teaches you to throw yourself to the winds of fate. What is this game about? It is about fate. What do the players/characters do? They roll, until fate determines whether they win or lose.

Within this, the snakes are not evil, but simply arbitrary. You randomly land on a snake. You randomly land on a ladder. I slide down a snake, but you climb a ladder, at the whim of the dice.

There is something incredibly powerful about such themes in gaming. Perhaps, indeed, themes like this are at the heart of all roleplaying games. You roll the dice and your fate depends on fortune alone.

Graham

    But I do remember one thing VERY clearly about that campaign, and it's the most vivid memory of those entire 3 sessions:

    When I rolled my fighter character, I had an 18 strength (we used a die assignment method from one of the AD&D supplements which pretty much assured you of one "18" if you wanted it) and I had to roll percentile dice to determine what my "percentile strength" was.

    I rolled a 97. I rolled it, my best friend was watching. I hollared and started writing down this great score on my sheet.

    But the DM didn't see it. He didn't watch me roll it. He called BS, saying "Look, I trust you and all, but for this and hit points I need to *see* the roll. That's the way we play." And to my credit (I was 16 at the time) I thoughtfully nodded my head, agreeing "thems are the rules". 'Sides, it was only a mini-campaign, so I didn't really care too strongly. So I rolled again, in front of the DM.

    I rolled a 00. One hundred. The single highest score, which has its own special damage and attack bonus categories. We all cheered, even the DM.

    Again, I remember little about that campaign, but man I still remember making that die roll.

    Sure, but you're "competing" in a lottery, too. Caillois's distinction is that in agon, the outcome is determined by the skill of the participants, while in alea the outcome is the product of chance: you're subjecting yourself to a turn of the Wheel of Fortune. I suppose you're right that some people might kid themselves that their victory or defeat in the game is a reflection of their own virtues. Children are particularly susceptible to this error in thinking.

    Of course, Caillois does admit the possibility of mixed types: there are many games in which skill combines with luck to determine the outcome, such as poker and backgammon. He implies that they are structured something like this:

    Agon <-------> Alea
    | \\ / |
    | \\ / |
    | X |
    | / \\ |
    | / \\ |
    Mimicry <-------> Ilinx

    where agon is fundamentally contrary to ilinx because agon is all about control (using the resources at hand to secure victory in a contest) and ilinx is all about dislocation (spin in a circle and you get dizzy! Wheee!). Similarly, alea is fundamentally contrary to mimicry because alea is uses the element of play to inform "real-world" consequences (roll the dice! what do they say?) while mimicry uses elements of the real world to inform the "magic circle" of play (I'm the cop! You're the robber! That means I'm supposed to catch you and you go to jail!)

    Caillois also distinguishes between paidaia and ludus as modes of play, where the paidaic is "playful" in the purest sense, and the ludic is rule-bound, centered on the game as game.
