    \n First of all, "fun" is a loaded term with many meanings.

    I can't speak for Jared, who I suspect is being a little hyperbolic with all the shouting at and destroying of people, but I'm just saying that "fun" is not something you should try to evoke mechanically, because you can't. The pleasure people derive from your rules in play is a function of their elegance and clarity coupled with these people's own ability to enjoy their friend's company and collaborate in a functional way.

    If you want your game to be funny, that's a viable design goal. You can put procedures in place that will push game play toward humorous situations, just like you can write a game designed to model an abusive relationship, but there's nothing you can write down that will automatically make it fun, by any given definition of fun.

    So if you want your game to be fun, know what you want and write it really well and make sure it is absolutely bullet-proof. Good players will take care of the rest.

    \n Hey Hans,

    Raph Koster's book, A Theory of Fun for Game Design, is instructive. Koster delivers, in fairly plain language, a vision for the design of games that illuminate the human condition.

    But my big take-away from A Theory of Fun for Game Design wasn't so much the theory as it was Koster's process of carefully defining "fun" for himself. Seeing the work he does of splitting various enjoyments (like schadenfreude) off from "fun" as tangential to his own focus is what I think is most worthwhile about the book. What he does gives focus to his sense of purpose as a game designer.

    \n I think Nathan's got it. Game design is about transmitting guidelines that structure the behavior of players in interesting ways. Whether it's fun or not in practice, the designer only has so much control over. Honestly, you only have so much control over whether people realize your vision either. I honestly think we need to have more of a "the designer is dead" backlash in roleplaying design, but that's a different issue (basically, the designer doesn't fucking matter once you put a text in people's hands and they're ready to play). But ideally, what you're trying to do is communicate ideas about play that get people to adjust their practices to be more-or-less similar to the structures that you've put into place at your own table when running the same game (assuming you've done playtesting and are trying to replicate the best experiences, otherwise it's just an abstract exercise based on imagined structures or an exploratory thing based on throwing some guidelines down and seeing what happens).

    Actually, check that. Design is always a mixture of trying to 1) replicate certain structures / types of experiences and 2) setting up structures that will generate unknown kinds of experiences. So it's a mixture of A) emulation and experimentation.

    \n What I'm thinking is the main issue with the usage of fun here is that it's used as shorthand.
    \n
    \n"My game is supposed to be fun." is a simpler, and much less informative statement than "My game has you doing these things, thinking about those things, and making this type of decision... which should be fun."
    \n
    \nI think it's obvious for the majority of games that you should have fun/find enjoyment, fulfillment or satisfaction while playing them. But to leave it at the word fun doesn't give enough information about any specific activity that we might call gaming as to whether or not you and your friends might find it fun. It also suggests that maybe, just maybe, I as a game designer haven't thought about why the game should be fun, or who the game should be fun for.
    \n
    \nOn a very personal level*, if you're not having fun, by your own definition of it, you shouldn't be playing the game. If you don't think the game you're writing will be fun, by your own definition of it, then you shouldn't be writing the game. But try to give more detail, not by writing an essay on what fun means, but by explaining the sorts of things your game does that are "fun", thus allowing people to make more informed decisions.
    \n
    \n*said personal level doesn't acknowledge the validity of calling something a game if it's not fun by any definition. This doesn't mean that the activity isn't fulfilling and worthwhile in some other fashion, but I don't think the activity has any claim to being called a game at that point.
