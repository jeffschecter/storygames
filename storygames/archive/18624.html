    \n P.S. Here's a draft agenda for our follow-up panel on writing player-side moves (GM stuff might be later). What else do you want to know? Anything missing?

    Overview
    - exception-based design
    - mediating/complicating the conversation
    - many moves start from & return to the fiction... but not all do

    Which Things Should/Shouldn't Be Moves?

    Parts of a Move (Vx calls this the "Moves' Architecture")
    - trigger (when/if X... [also: "from now on, right now & done"])
    - player input (say/do/name Y... [Vx: "call for specific details"])
    - mechanical bits (roll+/spend/sub/XP/etc.)
    - fictional outcomes (then...Z [choose/hold/ask/choice])
    (Vx treats the last three as the same thing, the "then" in your "if...then...")

    General Types of Moves
    - GM-player: workspace, jingle, reputation
    - player-player: seduce/manipulate, oftener right
    - more one-sided: arresting skinner, visions of death
    - love letters

    Writing Triggers, Questions, Outcomes
    - language used in these: clear, concise, tone, evocative, constraining?

    The Stuff Between Triggers & Outcomes
    - what does this need to do?
    - 10+/7-9/miss: when to use it, when not to
    - when to have choices of outcomes (& how many)?
    - hold & spending it
    - nothing in between: sometimes things just happen
    - other possibilities

    One thing I found myself wondering, which maybe fits into the Player moves panel (but maybe not), is why do most AW hacks preserve the "MC never rolls" feature? I've always understood it to be non-fundamental to the game, just something that late in the process Vincent realised he could do with the mechanics as they stood, and thought it a cool thing to implement.

    \n That's a great question! I'm not sure if it fits the player-side panel or if we should talk about it at a future GM-side one. Hmm...

    Short answer: I don't think it's necessarily fundamental, but it's an interesting design constraint and allows the GM to focus more on what their job is, rather than being distracted by other kinds of mechanics. That said, a lot of hacks have the GM rolling dice on tables to generate content, though not as part of resolution as much (sometimes I roll the Die of Fate in World of Dungeons, though).
