    \n Burning Boston. Samuel Maverick (John Harper) is trying to bind the ghost of Leonard Digges,a recurring enemy, to within 7 paces of a tree. He's angling for a challenging Sorcery test, so he declares this spell will bind him to the spot for all time. This being a challenging test, his chances of success are kind of low, so as he gets ready to roll the dice we start throwing out ideas of what kind of twist I can inflict if he fails (a failed roll never means nothing). There's some talk about obvious missfires, having the spell temporarily bind Sam or something. Then Paul suggests the best twist I've ever heard: Instead of binding Digges ghost to within seven paces of this tree, he would bind the ghost to within seven paces of himself.

    I've never wanted a PC to fail a roll more.

    But he succeeded, and typical Burning Boston hijinks ensued, with demon possessions, a crisis of faith, an impending assault on the island. Just the usual.

    This all took place on Noddle Island, now the location of Logan Airport, which half makes me want to run a game of InSpectres with a ghost haunting one of the runways, never more then seven paces away...

    \n WFRP 3e, 8 sessions in.

    After burning down the Imperial town of Rottfurt due to Chaos incursion, the PCs found an orphan child in Marienburg who many professed to be Sigmar reborn. The child crusade pushed to Altdorf where the boy was kidnapped and went missing. The PCs are currently tracking a cell of the same cult that they routed from Rottfurt who they think to be linked to the boy's disappearance.

    OK, so you're caught up. PCs beat the hell out of a captured cultists and ascertain that a "shipment" was sent from the Altdorf docks to the town of Bogenhafen in the Southern Reikland. They believe it to be Chaos contraband (like, maybe Warpstone) and upon arriving in Bogenhafen discover the cultists arrived with a coffin which was turned over to the cult of Morr (Priests associated with death/burial) and buried in the Bogenhafen garden of Morr (cemetary). The PCs are lead by a Witch Hunter so easily gain access to dig the SOB up and what do they find? No, not Warpstone - Karl Franz, Emperor of the Old World.

    So more awesome a session later. One of the PCs is a Dwarf who has a Belief concerning his swearing allegiance to a prominent Imperial Baron. The Baron arrives in the chaotic rioting battleground of Altdorf for Karl Franz's funeral where he meets with the Dwarf PC. After some time revelling, he praises the Dwarf for all the good work he has done concerning rooting out cultists and promises him more land and wealth in the coming future. Then he hands the Dwarf a leaflet of letters and explains that he has discovered the Countess Emmanuelle of Nuln (one of the Elector Counts of the Empire) was responsible for the assassination of Karl Franz. They do not have hard evidence yet, so he wants to the Dwarf to plant the forged correspondence during their investigation and have the other PCs discover the letters which will implicate the Countess in the cult they are chasing as well as the disappearance of the boy believed to be Sigmar's heir.

    The player broke out of character to call me a dick (in a good way) for putting him up against the wall.

    \n Last session of Broken Rainbow (post-Ragnar\xf6k rollergirl valkyries on hoverbikes) just happened.

    We started out the session with a bunch of mediocre scene framing about bullshit nobody cared about. It was definitely one of those anime episodes where you're like "What the fuck, writers/animators?"

    But then in the end, despite all that, we really pulled it together. The four valkyries stabbed Odin in the chest with a spear carved from the World Tree and the spear, in turn, blossomed into a giant tree that brought Valhalla to the ground and began destroying and remaking the world.

    Even experienced players totally fuck things up sometimes, even in a big performance situation like the climactic last session of a campaign, but on a good day we can pull ourselves out of it and that's what happened here. Big thanks to Lesley, Lukas, David, and John from helping me not ruin the apocalypse. It was great playing with you folks.

    \n Monday night, and we're playing Vampire: the Masquerade using a slightly hacked version of the Hot War rules. It's our first "proper" session, having done the game creation session last week.

    The game is set in London, in 1985.

    Maria (Gangrel, kinda of an outsider who tries to keep waifs and stray safe) has just finished quizzing Julia (Nosferatu, sociopathic low-rent madame) about some dead prostitutes and is leaving Julia's brothel. On her way out, Maria spots Daisy, one of Julia's girls who she's trying to "save". In fact, saving her from Julia is Maria's (very powerful) Personal Agenda.

    She decides that she's going to take Daisy away from Julia, right here and now.

    It's Maria's Influence vs Julia's. Maria has both 4 points of Personal agenda and 2 points of positive relationship with daisy.

    Maria wins by 2, despite Julia also having relationship dice in the conflict.and Julia's negative "Inhuman" trait comes up as her highest die.

    Daisy leaves with Maria, and Julia's negative trait causes her to beat a customer to death in a fit of rage after hearing that Daisy has left with Maria.

    The episode ends with a silent scene as Maria arrives at the hospital A&E with the abused girl, and hands her over to Dr Keith. He's an NPC we established during the game creation session. He knows about the Kindred, is vaguely in love with Maria and wants to help the downtrodden.

    (At the start of this scene and lasting throughout, as if on queue, Public Image Ltd's The Order of Death comes up on the 1985 appropriate playlist. Which was fucking perfect!)

    Dr Keith looks back up, about to speak, but Maria is nowhere to be seen.

    \n A week ago. Mountain Witch, Act II.

    Me: "When you're a guest in someone's house, you put your sword to your right when you sit down because it's hard to draw from there. On your left, it's easy."
    Ben: "Ah, got it. The guy sees you do this, and hesitates for a moment. He then turns and..."
    Me: "I kill him."
    Everyone at the table: "DUDE!"
    Me: "What? I gave him three whole seconds. You can't ask for much more than that."

    \n We played Awesome Adventures in a JRPG/Console Fantasy, Urban setting (think Shadowrun meets Final Fantasy meets Burn Notice). The PCs were trying to free the Garment District of a Protection Racket Gang and solve a series of burglaries.

    AA played fast and fun and we had a great time.

    The PCs ended up getting the merch back to it rightful owners, helping out the kid burglar in over his head, and the Skull-cracker of the group actually spared and worked to re-honor the gang boss who long ago traded his honor for revenge.

    One player fussed a bit because his min-maxed character (so he thought) ran out of Fate Points because he refused to take any aspects that could be compelled to his disadvantage and would not allow any attack of his to do no consequences to the opponent.

    I really got into the swing of using maneuvers and concessions by hostile NPCs to tune the story pacing. When the action was even remotely looking like it was going stale, I was quick to concede for the bad guys (all save the big Final Confrontation with it very surprising ending). My concessions always led to some complication to the group's plans, but the team whipped enemy butt and cleaned up the town in the end.

    My favorite example of a maneuver was when some summoned monkey-mooks aided their beastmaster boss by placing the aspect "Poo-flinging suppressive fire" on the rooftop setting of a chase scene, forcing the freerunner PC to have to divert around the area. Players used them to place taggable aspects like "caught unawares" and "has seen his own death" on targets.

    I didn't miss stunts in FATE at all.

    \n Sorry, this ain't bite-sized, but it was kinda special and I want to have it down for posterity.

    --

    The Crimson Blades, the Greyhawk 4E game that I am privileged to DM, is going on hiatus after 32 sessions and 1.5 years.

    We went into the break with a very special three-part family-focused episode. Lots of stuff happened.

    Our fighter's friend-with-benefits (an infernal grad student from Dis) asked him to choose between an intensified thing with her and the Paragon path he's all hot for. He chose the Paragon path. Romance is dead!

    Our tiefling warlord's family is causing all kinds of trouble. Warlord Dad is poised to take the family mercenary company and sack Bahamut's millenium temple -- the Bahamutiest place in the whole Middle World. It's gonna be a hoedown, as there's 1000 years of platinum and a bunch of religious fervor involved. If you can hold a sword, you're gonna be there.

    Our PC warlord is uncertain where he stands on all this. Warlord Dad's an asshole, but the Church is hella-corrupt. It's complex.

    Meanwhile, Warlord Mom is put out that Warlord Dad knocked up their daughter-in-law (who was widowed, but still....). So Warlord Mom is conspiring with an Asmodeus-worshiping refugee army to unleash a Rain of Colorless Fire WMD upon the battle, killing all those capable warriors and leaving the battered old Empire ripe for rebirth...as Hell On Earth.

    Little does she know that the Crimson Blades have a city-busting WMD of their own, a so-called Writ of Wrath. The Writ was issued to our deva avenger -- our platinum Terminator, our Vineyard pitbull -- by Bahamut himself. Our avenger is never uncertain about anything, which is why I wanted him to have the power to kill an entire city.

    The avenger plots with some NPCs to smuggle the Writ of Wrath into Warlord Family's compound. There he will invoke the Writ's inexorable platinum radiance, melting Mom's WMDs and Warlord Dad's mercenary company, and also Mom and Dad, and a whole bunch of sympathetic cousins and an unborn bastard niece. Maybe the Blades too. The Writ has no instruction manual.

    Warlord Son, the PC, seems strangely sanguine about this tidy solution, and as I'd entered the evening with the intention of provoking him into murdering his extended family and their comrades, I was happy.

    The Crimson Blades, though, do nothing the easy way. See, it's actually the fighter's fault that Warlord Mom has a Rain of Colorless Fire on hand. It's a long story, but suffice to say that his special naked devil friend got some benefit from their relationship as well. The fighter's conscience is all screwed up, and he's prepared to sacrifice himself if he can prevent the 1000 people in Warlord Family from being de-rezzed by grey light.

    The Rain of Colorless Fire WMDs are actually adult children, hyper-powerful sorcerers bred to be absurdly good at doing just one spell, and with their development arrested to keep them morally simple. After a couple improbable rolls, the fighter bluffs them them out of their safe chamber. And, unmindful of the ~5000XP of potential badguy on the battlematt, he starts the killing.

    Of twelve WMDs (the numbers are for redundancy, not potency), eleven die in his first attack. But the last one nipped away, and it took the Blades the entire rest of the night to kill it. They slew 5000XP of opposition without one single rest. The PCs rolled eleven death saves. At one point, only a barred door separated the reeling avenger from his final prey. (Pro tip--if you've got an inexorable avenger who's tweaked out with a low STR, put a DC20 barred door in his way and watch him wail!)

    In the end, none of the PCs died, though I had most of them knocked down at one point or another. They did lose a treasured goblin companion NPC, who had a bad run of death save luck. They left nothing in the locker room and just barely squeaked out the final kill, And afterwards, they were too worn out to do anything about the sacking of the Bahamut Vatican, which proceeded on schedule.

    We'll explore the results of this when we pick up again sometime later this year. But I need a rest first.
