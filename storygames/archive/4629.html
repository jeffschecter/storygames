\n I was reading Chad Underkoffler's LiveJournal entries on preparing for the Beta playtesting of "Swashbucklers of the Seven Skies" (http://chadu.livejournal.com/560765.html) and started asking him about how he picks people for playtesting. I also heard a similar discussion on Master Plan Episode 17 (http://masterplan.libsyn.com/index.php?post_id=265412). After asking Chad several questions, we both agreed this would be a good place to hear from all of you how you go about finding playtesters and what kinds of things you are looking for when you enter that Beta phase.
\n
\nThis is just an area of curiosity for me, so thanks ahead of time for responding.

    \n I have learnt quite a bit from choosing* people for whom it was obviously the wrong project, or who came with certain preconceptions on what a game should look like.

    I have learnt what shouldn't be in the game, and who the game isn't for. This is also very important.

    * I chose the people, and then found out the match wasn't right, not that I chose them knowing it'd be wrong.

    \n I mostly design games I think will be fun to play with my local friends, so who the hell else am I going to beta test with?

    But I usually throw out a PDF for anyone who wants to to try the unfinished version out, which they sometimes do, albeit not in great numbers. The new version of Thrash may turn out to be an exception since (1) it's for a genre that's totally neglected in RPGs, and (2) the old version had a decent following. I haven't had really tried actually soliciting people to play...

    \n If you want to beta test my game you are my best friend forever. Even if you end up not doing it right. Especially if you end up not doing it right!

    Playtesting at any stage is usually a crap proposition, so I'm not too particular - if somebody is interested and enthusiastic, they're in. Outside of this small, weird, lovely group of people, I can rely on people I know and trust to help out, and this group includes other designers for whom the quid is definitely pro quo.

    \n Jason's more or less on target - I would add that playtesting well is not just a crap proposition, but it is hard work. You have to take notes, you have to read and re-read, you have to lead discussions, you have to formulate thoughts and report back. I've only ever done it when I'm enthused about the idea of the work.

    \n I got to interview board game designer Jason Mathews this past weekend. He and another guy created the game Twilight Struggle, which has been out for a couple of years and is really highly regarded (it simulates the Cold War between the U.S. and the then-Soviet Union). He talked a little bit about playtesting, but said when he goes to cons to play with people, they still find errors or do things he hadn't anticipated when developing the system. That has to be both thrilling and infuriating, and I would assume applies to any kind of game design, whether board game, RPG, or other.
