...which is a fancy way of saying that I'm thinking about bonuses and penalties.

The core mechanic is a die roll that must exceed the current scene number (1 through 9) for a success. Your die size scales up from an initial d4 to d10 or, rarely, d12. Due to the gradual increase in die sizes, the odds are stacked against success on straight rolls pretty badly. If you just phone it in you will probably fail, as well as slip your character toward suicidal depression. Good times.

But there are a bunch of things players can do to increase their odds - injecting situation, reincorporating, including other characters in their narration, sacrificing important and non-renewable resources (their own and those of other players), and so forth. Some of these just add color and excitement and some burn stuff you care about forever. Right now I'm trying to figure out the best way to apply these benefits.

There are die increments (in Scene three you roll a 3, bump it to a 4), which are powerful in early scenes and weaker in later scenes, but a sure thing. These are also, in terms of play dynamics and at-table fun, sort of boring.

There are re-rolls, which are chancy but powerful in later scenes (In Scene eight you roll a 2 on your d10, then re-roll to get a 7, which you can work with)

There are substitutions, which change your d4 into a d6 or whatever before a roll, which are always useful in a general way. Right now the "big sacrifice" (of which there is one per player to burn) gives you a d12 to swap with your die, or a sure-thing +/-3 increment.

I'm sure there are other fun dice tricks, and I'm open to suggestions. What I want to figure out is how to offer minor and major benefits, based on the degree of initiative and/or pain a player invokes. Playtesting will tell, but if there are obvious statistical tell-tales, I'd love to get schooled on them. The escalating difficulty makes it a little beyond my mathematical ken.

    Jason,

    I think things become interesting in the later scenes. Take scene 8, for example, where you have to roll a 9 or higher to succeed (assuming that you actually need to exceed the scene number, not get the scene number or higher).

    That d12 is only going to give you a 1 in 4 chance of success (it rolls 1 - 12, with equal probability, and you need 9 - 12 to succeed). Modifiers increase that chance gradually (a +1 gives you a 1 in 3 chance, a +2 gives you 5 in 12, and a +3 gives you a 50/50 chance). A reroll increases those chances significantly: a single reroll, with no modifier, gives you slightly less than 50/50 odds; another reroll on top of that gives you slightly more than 50/50 odds.

    Let's say you had a d8 instead. Basically, you're screwed. You can't succeed, even with rerolls. But now the modifiers make a huge difference: with a +1, you've got a 1 in 8; with +2, 1 in 4; with +3, 3 in 8.

    So...as a quick overview:

    1. A die substitution won't help as much as you think. Even that d12 only gives you a 1 in 4 chance in later scenes.
    2. Modifiers help quite significantly: a +3 modifier really raises your chances.
    3. In later scenes, unless you've got modifiers, you actually can't succeed with some dice.
    4. Rerolls really help.

    And dice tricks you might consider:

    1. The Capes trick of rerolling, but only accepting rerolls which exceed the original roll. Actually, perhaps that makes no difference with your game (if the roll fails in the first place, there's no point in keeping it). Forget I mentioned it.
    2. The Breaking The Ice trick of narrating something bad for a reroll.
    3. Narrating to increment the die (so a 8 becomes 9).
    4. Rolling two dice and taking the highest.
    5. Rerolling and adding the reroll to your roll.
    6. Narrating something to swap for a higher die after you've rolled.

    Anyway, hope some of that helps. If I've got any of the probability wrong, people are welcome to correct me.

    Graham

  • Graham
    October 2006 edited October 2006
    Jason, an exploding d6 works like this: roll a d6. If you get a six, roll again and add the new roll to the previous one. Continue rerolling and adding every time you get a six.

    Judson is also suggesting we consider an increased "range of explosion": roll a d6. If you get a five or a six, roll again and add the new roll to the previous one. Continue rerolling every time you get a five or a six.

    Interesting probability facts:

    1. Exploding dice don't actually increase the probability of getting high rolls much because, in practice, you're still fairly unlikely to roll a six and, if you do, you've still got to roll something useful after you roll that six.
    2. But they make things more interesting.
    3. And they mean that you can, theoretically, pass any threshold with any die. In your game, you could still succeed in scene 9 with a single d4: it's just very, very unlikely. (Oh, actually, now I've calculated it, it's a 1 in 16 chance, so not astronomical).
    4. Smaller exploding dice are more interesting than larger exploding dice: a d4 is quite likely to explode (because you roll a 4 a quarter of the time) but a d10 will hardly ever explode (you roll a ten one in ten times).

    Graham
