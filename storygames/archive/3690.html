    \n Hmmm... I think I can agree with your first two points with a little modification.
    \n
    \nFirst, I'd say that a majority of gamers enjoy a fortune-based resolution mechanic. I'm not sure why that is, but I suspect there are many reasons. I, for one, like collecting strangely-shaped, colorful shapes that are slightly mysterious (music of the spheres, anyone?) and I also enjoy the slight bit of suspense they provide when I physically roll them. I could provide similar reasons for enjoying cards, especially if they help provide color to my game. I'm not sure my this is a problem.
    \n
    \nSecond, I'd say that risk-aversion extends to the majority of all people. They inherently fear the unknown and when someone comes along and attempts to turn their established construct on it's head, they react with fear and loathing. Recently I attempted to explain Conflict Resolution to a very intelligent and entertaining friend who GMs for me. His reaction was very negative. He argued that it would result in a single dice roll determining the outcome of the game. Even after I clarified that he was conflating scale with resolution, he attempted to argue that using Conflict Resolution would be a threat to the fun of roleplaying. Ironically, he doesn't realize is that I've been using Conflict Resolution for some time now in my game and he's enjoying it immensely. He's simply reacting to a perceived threat.
    \n
    \nTaking this point of view, I come to a different conclusion. Most people are myopic, including the fairly intelligent ones. I would go so far as to suggest that most of us here have been myopic at some point or another. It's usually induced by a lack of information and a lack of understanding that we lack that information. The "average gamer" simply lacks some information.
    \n
    \nAnd I wouldn't say that most people lack the skills necessary to evaluate and build criticisms of what they don't like, they lack the courage, or even the will, to try new things. Rather than appear afraid or unwilling to try a new experience, they revert to the "that's stupid" defense, which, of course, has little to back it. The "average gamer" lacks the will to try new games.
    \n
    \nAgain, if you stereotype "most people," you can generalize that the majority do form opinions quickly. And they do it with whatever information they have at hand, which is usually personal experience or popular hearsay. THEREFORE, if you want them to change their minds, you MUST provide them with new information that is comprehensible, personal, and unpretentious. Part of the problem I had with my friend is that I presented Conflict Resolution as better than Task Resolution. That was an error both in theory and in presentation.
    \n
    \nIt is our challenge to explain our new knowledge and information to the "average gamer" the best we can without making them feel threatened, stupid or unfashionable.

      "I hate trailers..."

      So, let me guess. This is how you go to a movie:

      Without having seen any trailers or flipped through a paper to look at ads, you get in your car.

      You drive to the mall, fighting through traffic and driving around to finally end up in a parking space.

      You get out of the car, walk up to the teen-age ticket-seller behind the glass and say, "Give me a ticket for whatever's playing next. I don't care what it is. That's how much I'm against risk-aversion. That's how much I don't stay in my comfort zone. That's how much nobody ever'll be able to call me myopic."

      Because unless that is how you see movies, get over it.

      Assume it's your job to let potential players know what they're going to get in exchange for giving away 14 hours of their day. You better offer up enough to make the trade worth it. You don't have to give away the whole game. You have to give up enough details so I can make an informed decision -- if only based on the weightless compass of taste -- if I'm going to play your game.

      That's not a problem with the average gamer. You want people to show up, that's what you do. You don't get snooty about it. You don't blame people for being risk averse. (Unless, of course, the above description is how you go to movies. Because if that's how you do it, everything you've written at the start of this thread snaps into a whole new light. But other than that -- get over it.)
