    \n Thanks for the feedback - I hope there is more coming. Silence is hard to interpret.

    Robb - I've got a proof copy coming to me from the printer. They are printed on 2.5x3.5 inch cards. I expect the colors will tone down considerable but I won't know till I see one.

    Bob - I'm not home printing these puppies. I'm done with that. At best I can make something that looks like it was made in a garage because they are. Card printing is now cheap enough that I can go to an outside printer for short runs - which is what I can sell.

    I'm saddened that the reaction is meh. I like them - of course - since they express the style of art I like.

    I'm heavily influenced by 1960's pop art - Andy Worhol, Peter Max, and the use of color in abstract art. I like bold loud direct messages that has clash and abstraction. Realism isn't a prime focus so when I do the skin tones I'm thinking as much about the abstractness of it as I am about creating depth. The game is set in a plastic Las Vegas like world where the contrasts of lights and colors are garish and people are flat. In those terms it looks like I've succeeded in communicating that - unfortunately it isn't appealing to anyone to get them to play the game.

    I'll be taking the proof copy out to do more play tests - to see how the art works for people. It may be that I just am not going to be able to speak to this audience (if the silence means people are being polite and not saying what they don't like). Perhaps I should switch to kids games. This is as edgy as I get.

    Please to others - I really do want your feedback. I can't improve my work in a vacuum.

    \n I, too, really like the cover. I think it succeeds whereas the cards don't quite...although I love Big Red Wolf's pose from the above photo.

    Now, Wharhol in my opinon, is really responding to Lichtenstien. And Lichtenstien was using 50s and 60s comics to do his pop art thang. But here is the rub on what Lichtenstien was doing, he was standing on the shoulders of some real craftsmen. Here is what Lichtenstien and Warhol had going for them, their outlines? There was real variation to them... a thick and thin in the same stroke. Warhol "cheated", he was using photographs, which had pretty good contrast to them prior to slapping on the outrageous tones of color. A solid foundation, in other words. \n
    like one of those Hammer horror films where they dropped dark shadows and replaced it with full color
    But it doesn't matter what color the shadows are, if the value of the shadows and the placement of the shadows are right. Which is precisely why Warhol's photos work and why maybe your cards are not quite working. Your foundation isnot quite there.

    For example: Your outlines are pretty uniformly 1 thickness. Like on "I'm going to kill you"... the arm/hand pointing towards the viewer would benefit from having a thicker outline than the lines behind it. The arm could use a thicker outline and the bottom of the form (where shadows are more likely to be) than the top part of the arm. You can still retain the playful "simplicity" (not simple at all) of the art, but make them more alive by varying your line weight...and it will give you more structure and foundation to have the insane color schemes to hang on.

    Best book on inking I've come across is The Art of Comic Booking inking by Gary Martin (Dark horse) (link here: http://www.amazon.com/Art-Comic-Book-Inking-2nd/dp/1593074050). Even if you aren't trying to emulate comics... you are trying to create your forms with so few lines, every line really matters.
