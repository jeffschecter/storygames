\n The announcement for the Wii U got me thinking about the use of asymmetric play in tabletop RPGs...
\n
\nThe closest I've ever done to this is in a Dead of Night scenario, where one of the pre-generated PCs is actually a baddy who is working with the Big Bad to bring about the death of one or more of the other PCs, but the baddy character was stil working within the same ruleset as the other characters and didn't have any special advantages to draw upon during play.
\n
\nSo, has anyone ever experienced truly asymmetric play? If so, how was it achieved and did it work for you? If not, would you like to see asymmetic play? What sort of games would it work well in? Is there a potential for an RPG designed to incorporate asymmetric play? What would that game be about and how would it work?
\n
\nFor example, imagine a party tasked with safely transporting the Chosen One across dangerous terrain. Most of the party act as normal, engaging in conflicts when they arise and rolling dice to check how effective they are, but the Chosen One only operates by bestowing gifts or curses upon those whom they wish to affect, they never get to act directly against any foe. Therefore, the other players have to persuade the Chosen One that they are most worthy of his gifts, at which point normal intra-party conflicts may enter play.
\n
\nOh, and suggesting "GMs represent asymmetric play" is weak sauce. Imagine the GM as the embodiment of the game system: they are the hardware that the game 'runs on', not a player in this case, ok?

    \n It's a tricky design challenge, because you're really talking about different kinds of fun for various players. In your example, the Chosen One has no direct agency. This is strictly awesome but only for the right player, when it is done right. I've got some projects in the works that change the way a player interacts (for example, once your character dies, you transition to a collective GM-like role).

    Breaking up character monogamy will also point in this direction. If I control my guys thoughts but you control his actions and Sally controls his emotions, that's some weird, radical asymmetry in our ability to interact with the world.

    Injecting an adversarial element into the composition of a team probably doesn't meet the standard of asymmetry. I do this all the time, and it is just an exercise in goal and situation definition.

    \n Well, if GMing doesn't count, what about Polaris? In any given scene, the Heart, the Mistaken and the Moons all have their own abilities, goals and resources they can draw on. The rules for how you can act as a Moon are totally different than those for the Heart.

    Also: The Rock of Tahamaat, The Court of the Empress, lots of other games.


    \n
    Posted By: Jason MorningstarIt's a tricky design challenge, because you're really talking about different kinds of fun for various players.
    \nRotating roles is useful here, to make sure everyone gets to try each role and thus plays at least some of the time the role that they really enjoy. Otherwise, you might accidentally get stuck in a role you don't like when you might enjoy a different role a lot more.


    You could perhaps have a game where the roles bounce around for the first part of the game, but naturally settle onto the players who most enjoy that particular role.

    \n Okay, so it's the classic GM-player model, but I'm a fan of Steal Away Jordan's asymmetry. The GM arbitrarily decide how effective your character is (as well as your name), but then you get to conspire with the other players using information about you the GM doesn't have.

    The game is all about how to sneakily maneuver around absolute power in order to get what your characters think they want.

    \n Coincidentally, we playtested a thing for the first time last night, a sort of Star Trekky GM-less thing where the post you have at the bridge determines your narrative authority - Captain gets to set your initial mission via the captain's log, then the science officer "detects" (invents) enemy ships & space anomalies while the communications officer "contacts" (invents) alien NPCs. And everyone who is not on an away team acts as the GMs for those who are.

    Maybe doesn't count, because you can switch posts, but whoever starts as SO & CO are pretty much going to set the vector for the whole game.

    It was hilarious fun. Which wasn't exactly what I was going for, but I'll take it.

    And I thought I stole the idea of differing levels of narrative authority from mere whispers I've heard of Jason's Medical Hospital, so I'm surprised he hasn't mentioned it here...
