\n EDIT: This thread was created before the DMG was out and that book ended up bringing up these issues in a much more direct way than I expected. Some things here look "Yeah, duh, it's right there in the DMG" and some look "What, no, the DMG contradicts that".


OK, so I loved Fate. When it first came out, I was playing Fudge and I was like \u201cUh, Fate, what is this? I don\u2019t get it\u201d. When SotC came out a few years later, I first didn\u2019t give it a closer look but as the hype grew, I gave it a try GM:ing it and I did like it. I bought Diaspora and it made us happy for a few sessions. Fate Core fixed everything that hadn\u2019t really worked with Diaspora. Now Dungeons & Dragons 5e is here and it has a few features that look a bit like Fate. Does it improve on it? Yes, I think so.

Character Aspects
As Fate evolved, it evolved from a list of ten generic aspects into five specific aspects \u201cHigh Concept\u201d, \u201cTrouble\u201d, etc.
5e\u2019s character traits are pretty much straight out of latest Fate. It sorta downplays them, which makes sense \u2014 they can come into play when players are familiar with the basic gameplay of d20 + mod >= AC/DC. And they also don\u2019t have to come into play at all \u2014 the game flows and works very well without them being used.

I\u2019ve mentioned earlier how I\u2019ve had many players being confused with the difference between skills and aspects in Fate. That seems like way less of an issue here.

And I guess it downplays them in order to look more like \u201cGood old D&D\u201d and less like \u201cFatey weird newstyle D&D\u201d. That\u2019s not something that\u2019s a big deal for us who were already on board with Fate and weirdness.

Getting inspiration vs Being compelled
I\u2019ve mentioned earlier an experience with Fate similar to this scenario:
\u201cYour character has the aspect: \u2018Always looking for a fix\u2019, now here you are in a new city \u2014 don\u2019t you want to go look for a new dealer?\u201d
and the player would go \u201cNo, I\u2019ll buy off the compel\u201d. And that\u2019s usually what happened \u2014 they bought off every compel. They used Fate points, but relied very heavily on the refresh at the beginning of every session.

With 5e, there\u2019s no need to buy off because you aren\u2019t explicitly compelled in the same way. Following the characteristics into trouble is something the players do or don\u2019t do themselves.

Fate Core\u2019s compels also have a weird situation going where they\u2019re sometimes phrased like this:\n
You have ____ aspect in ____ situation, so it makes sense that you\u2019d decide to ____. This goes wrong when ____ happens.
\nThe frustrating part about that, to me, is that you get or pay the Fate point when you make the decision in the first sentence. If it will go or would\u2019ve gone wrong in the second sentence. I don\u2019t mind the author stance but I can\u2019t see the future and it\u2019s especially hard to communicate the future. The post hoc nature of 5e\u2019s inspiration enables me to give the inspiration after it has gone wrong.

Of course, a lot of the time it\u2019s\n
You have ____ characteristic in ____ situation, so it makes sense that you\u2019d decide to ____. But if you didn\u2019t have the characteristic, not doing that would\u2019ve made much more sense because it\u2019s a dangerous or sub-optimal decision, it\u2019s only because of the characteristic that you do it.
\nThose are situations that would easily result in inspiration in 5e or a player-initatied compel Fate point awarded in Fate.
What\u2019s called \u201cretroactive compels\u201d (and allowed) in Fate can kind of be the norm in 5e. In both games, you get awarded for being true to your character when it conflicts with your inner \u201ccompetitive game player\u201d.

But the two major types of compels in Fate Core are events and decisions.
Decisions are the weird, almost dependent on precognition compels suggested in the quoted template above.
Events are more like \u201cbad things happen to you because of your aspect\u201d. When \u201cbad things happen\u201d is something that\u2019s a little strange in D&D, historically, compared to PbtA and Fate. The DM had to be a fair judge and not just be all \u201crocks fall everyone dies\u201d whereas in Fate the voice of narration is more distributed \u2014 this could just as well be something the player initiated: \u201cI have the aspect \u2611 Allergic to Falling Rocks so it makes sense that unfortunately, some falling rocks would hinder me in this situation.\u201d

In our latest 5e game, one player had the bond \u201cI will do anything to prove myself superior to my hated rival\u201d. The related \u201cevent\u201d was that this rival appeared and was up to shenanigans. That, in itself, didn\u2019t lead to inspiration. It was only the players own subsequent actions that lead to inspiration.

It\u2019s the Economy, My Friend
It was always hard to get a good sweet spot between \u201cHaving too many Fate points and every roll becomes like +8\u201d and \u201cHaving no Fate points and not being able to interact meaningfully with most of the game mechanics\u201d. The economy was fragile. I sometimes would ponder solutions to this problem but nothing struck me as good.
When I saw 5e\u2019s solution of having a cap at just one, my jaw dropped. After all the ideas I pondered, I had never been close to this genius idea. Felt like a Gordian knot being cut in front of my eyes.
No economy not only means no distracting meta-game, it also means that you never leave the sweet spot.

Scene Elements
Of course, part of what makes this binary non-economy work is that a lot fewer of the mechanical interactions are fueled by the resource in question.
In Fate, you can spend fate points to invoke scene aspects to get advantage. In 5e, you just get advantage straight up, if it makes sense in the fiction.
It\u2019s also a question of templating or wording.

In Fate, a scene would be described as: \u201cYou enter the room. There are some \u2611 Huge Unlabeled Crates here, and there are some nasty \u2611 Chains Hanging From the Ceiling.\u201d
You can interact with these elements mechanically (by invoking them as aspects), which is great because it gives a feeling of tangibility to the scene elements.
But really there\u2019s nothing special about them. The same room description could easily be followed up with the exchange \u201cWhat about the floor, is it slippery?\u201d \u201cOh, the \u2611 Floor Is Slippery, for sure!\u201d The game is clear that the labeled, aspected game elements aren\u2019t the be-all, end-all of what\u2019s in a scene.

This feature of the game affords a few really appealing game examples, like \u201cI try light their jacket on fire\u201d \u201cOK, that\u2019s Create an Advantage into \u2610 Jacket is Burning, which you have a free invocation on.\u201d, examples that seems to work well and model the world well, in a sort of Lego piece fashion. But as appealing and smooth-flowing it often it, it also seems that a lot of the time the system is getting more in the way, making it hard to phrase exactly what\u2019s going on.

In 5e, the description would be more like \u201cYou enter the room. There are some huge unlabeled crates here, and there are some nasty chains hanging from the ceiling.\u201d You work within the fiction and when it makes narrative sense, you do get mechanical advantage in a consistent and easy way. It\u2019s decoupled from specific scene elements and it\u2019s decoupled from a character-specific economy.
With Fate, if you\u2019re good at following The Golden Rule and The Silver Rule (Fate Core, p185), you are already working fiction first and then translating that into mechanical elements as needed. The problem I had with this was that I would either be bad at following these metallic rules and let the game mechanics get in the way of diegetic, narrative sense, or I would follow them too well, and forget about the game mechanics and drift off into mere narration (often to the detriment of the experience as a whole).

    \nUsing Inspiration
    This is something that\u2019s still a bit unclear. I\u2019ve seen in places that, when you use inspiration, it should be when you\u2019re doing something related to the characteristics, just like you invoke character aspects in Fate. That\u2019s not in the PHB, though, and my experience so far has been that it\u2019s good to just let it be used whenever. Now that the game doesn\u2019t have a fragile economy, it\u2019s good to just let it flow freely.

    In Fate, you alse need to invoke your aspects in order to channel the fate points into bonuses on the rolls. This is something that I\u2019ve seen cause many beginners to tangle up what\u2019s \u201cskills\u201d and what\u2019s \u201caspects\u201d. It\u2019s also yet another coupling between the economy and your character\u2019s traits.

    It\u2019s also cool to be able to give inspirations between players. The example that someone posted here was \u201cParty rogue makes a bad impression at the court, gets insp, gives it to the party bard that uses it to clean up the mess\u201d. That\u2019s a cool little interaction right there, but I have to be careful so I don\u2019t fall into the \u201cJacket is Burning\u201d-trap of a few situations very well modelled that gives a false impression of a generalizable rule. Instead, I\u2019ll just say that if inspiration is supposed to be a reward, especially a reward for things that put the whole party in trouble, it make sense that the reward benefits the whole party.

    Player\u2019s can also reward each other for any reason they wish, such as a cool speech or interesting idea, but this is one of the few spaces where the non-stackable nature of Inspiration gets in the way. If you play to your characteristics to get inspiration as soon as you lose it, you won\u2019t have any space left to be rewarded by other players.

    One oft-houseruled difference between 5e and Fate is that you need to use inspiration before you roll. I can respect changing that but I\u2019m going to try playing the 5e way for a while. I think the rolls get devalued and the tension deflated if you can just change it after the fact. But that\u2019s just me.

    Spending the Fate points
    To summarize or rephrase the ideas in this post...
    Looking at Fate Core, you can spend fate points to

    \u2022 Invoke one of your own aspects
    \u2022 Invoke another aspect
    These are gone in 5e. If there are elements in the fiction that gives you advantage, you just... get advantage. No points needed.
    In Fate Core, this is the most \u201creward-like\u201d use of Fate Points and the use that\u2019s most similar to 5e\u2019s inspiration. But it\u2019s coupled \u2014 your aspects give you the points and your aspects are used to use the points.
    If you want to use that reward in 5e, you just use your inspiration. Typically when you don\u2019t have in-fiction advantage, because it doesn\u2019t stack.

    \u2022 Power a stunt
    Many of the player characters have their own weird little currencies in 5e (so we\u2019re getting back to the problems of fragile economies \u2014 oh, well, you can\u2019t win \u2018em all). In Basic, examples include the \u201cslots\u201d that the wizard and the cleric gets, or the \u201cshort rest\u201d that powers the fighter\u2019s Second Wind and Action Surge abilities as well as the wizard\u2019s Arcane Recovery meta-ability.
    In Fate, this is yet another use of Fate Points, creating a simpler game but a more highly coupled system.

    \u2022 Refuse a compel
    As I\u2019ve said, this one led to problems in my group. It\u2019s free in 5e which I think increases player agency. I\u2019m happy about this change.

    \u2022 Compel another character
    This one was always something I could understand, but not truly grok. A player compelling an NPC that \u201cthey can\u2019t make their move due to the \u2611 Suppressive Fire\u201d helps the Lego piece modelling somewhat, by being the flipside of the bonuses that invoking aspects give, but it\u2019s weird. Can the NPC refuse the compel? \u201cWe\u2019re brave, we cross the fire\u201d (and they pay a fate point). This isn\u2019t in 5e.

    \u2022 Declare a story detail
    Here\u2019s another weird one. If you\u2019re telling the story together, a system where a player can spend a meta-game resource in order to affect the story is like a drop in the ocean. And if you\u2019re using a traditional actor stance, this is like a big wrench in the gears. Which can be just what the doctor ordered, sometimes, but there\u2019s still a lot of restrictions. You have to convince the GM that it make sense and they have the right to veto.
    So in any game without this rule: You can suggest a detail and the GM can accept or reject your idea.
    In Fate: You can suggest a detail and the GM can accept or reject your idea. If the GM accepts, you lose a fate point.

    Rules for players doing what has been traditionally relegated to the GM is something we haven\u2019t seen yet in 5e. It\u2019s not part of the Basic core but it\u2019s been promised in an old Legends & Lore article to appear in the DMG as an optional module. I\u2019m really looking forward to seeing what they come up with.
    Being pessimistic, it\u2019s probably just another ocean-drop/wrench mechanic like Fate\u2019s \u201cdeclare a detail\u201d or it\u2019s been dropped altogether from the DMG in the space and time crunch as the release grows nearer.
    But we\u2019ll see, it might be good.

    Overall System Design
    I\u2019ve mentioned coupling and decoupling a few times in this post. They\u2019re interesting ideas from programming that\u2019s basically this:

    A highly coupled system has the parts really depend tightly on each other. If one part breaks, the others will too, and it\u2019s harder to replace parts. A change in one will cause a ripple effect in the whole system.

    So one way to look at it is:
    \u201c5e is a redundant, complex, almost bloated system whereas Fate Core is simple, doesn\u2019t repeat itself, and reuses solutions.\u201d
    Another way to look at it is:
    \u201cFate Core is highly coupled and is a fragile system where many of its parts are dependant on the same economy to flow smoothly. 5e is decoupled and robust.\u201d

    Writing this post reminds me that the creators of 5e are all programmers (and so is, if I remember correctly, many of the folks behind Fate Core, right?). I love 5e.

    It also makes me think that maybe the connection between Fate and 5e is weaker than I thought. There are so many differences.

    \n What was the point of the post?
    Simply to look in detail at what Fate does, break it down and see how D&D does the same thing.
    The overlap of the two games is small but interesting, and relevant to things like how the fiction interfaces to the mechanics.
    Fate doesn't have wandering monster tables or experience levels and D&D doesn\u2019t have the fractal. I get that they\u2019re different games. One wants to emulate narratives from other media, the other wants to enable exploring dangerous locations.

    But. I\u2019m happy with how the post turned out. I think it\u2019s interesting to see how both games enable mechanical advantages due to manipulation of scene elements. How both games reward character flaws once the game has started (unlike games where you get points at character generation time for adding flaws). How the economy of Fate Points, thought central to the game, could successfully be replaced with a simple have-or-haven\u2019t Inspiration box. It\u2019s interesting systems design to me and I was glad to get a chance to look at it closer. I want to commend the creators of Fate Core and D&D 5e for interesting design in this space.

    \n I think a lot of people who have played Fate and similar games are trying to read Inspiration and BIFTs as being akin to things they know already from those games, such as aspects/compels.

    It's my opinion that while anyone is free to do that, they are actually reading that into the system instead of discovering it there.

    In the scope of D&D history, Inspiration is pretty clearly rooted in various mechanics for "rewarding roleplay," which has especially been used for enforcing the DM's views of what it means to "play your alignment" and/or "play your class." The DM was empowered to reward or punish characters for breaking their concept -- as defined by the DM using those simple tools (alignment and class, e.g.).

    Rewards and punishment usually consisted of experience points; violating your chosen alignment could see you stripped of a level even if you weren't a paladin, and 2e added explicit rules for rewards based on your class functions. (A rogue, for example, gains extra XP by stealing things of value -- so if you chose to play your rogue as a scout or investigator, you might miss out on bonus XP.)

    Like other games of its era, earlier (but not OD&D games) sometimes adopted rewards for just "roleplaying well," where this was something rarely defined but seemingly having to do with both a combination of your character's personality and how the DM, as the (presumably impartial) judge, felt you roleplayed. Thus in addition to running the opposition, building the world, and in some cases setting up the story plot, the DM also took on the job of RP critic.

    Inspiration follows this pattern, at least as presented in the PHB, of making a tangible reward given by the DM for "roleplaying your character well." The DMG may offer additional insight, but for now the rule is simply that if the DM thinks your roleplaying is acceptable, you can be rewarded with Inspiration (if you don't already have it). There is no actual requirement that your roleplaying reflects any of your BIFTs -- bonds, ideals, faults, or (personality) traits -- although those are mentioned vaguely in passing during the discussion of inspiration.

    BITFs are not things that can be compelled one way or another; they are simply guides to roleplaying the character's personality. Apart from pregen characters or players irrationally forced by their DM to roll randomly, BIFTs are all self-chosen by the player and reflect how she is intending to play her character.

    Thus, Inspiration awarded simply for playing out one's BIFTs is essentially a reward for doing what you said you were going to do. There is no requirement for Inspiration that you actually make things worse for yourself in order to play out a BIFT; you are not being rewarded for taking a temporary setback, but merely for following what it says on your character sheet.

    "I am faithful to my friends and would never desert them" could be a bond, one of the BIFT types. You would choose this if, presumably, that is the way you want to play your character. And because that's how you want to play your character, you could get rewarded with Inspiration for playing that out, if you have a DM who chooses to reward for BIFTs. You can't ever be forced to be faithful to your friends, and the game never really proposes a coercive DM who, Fate-style, offers you a bribe of Inspiration if you decide to stay instead of desert.

    Of course, as noted before, there is only a tenuous connection between Inspiration and BIFTs in the RAW. Many DMs may not even know what the BIFTs are at their table and there is no suggestion that the DM should track them. In a party of 5 players, that comes to 25 BIFTs total, all of which are one or two sentences, adding a burden to each RP interaction of not only running NPCs but also discerning whether a player is playing out one of her BIFTs or not -- unless she already has Inspiration, in which case the point is moot because she can't earn it twice.

    5e offers no mechanic or ritual phrasing by which the player can communicate to the DM and the other players that she is engaging with her BIFTs in order to gain Inspiration. Lacking such a cue, this leaves the player either hoping she was unsubtle enough that her RP was identified as an attempt to earn Inspiration, or she can speak up after the fact to point out that her just-concluded roleplay interaction was based on the hope of gaining a reward. Either scenario is fraught with problems and can make it less appealing to players and DMs alike to play with Inspiration as a clear-cut mechanical reward for roleplaying BIFTs.

    This, then, leaves BIFTs as a reward given simply for interesting RP, where interesting means "the DM chose to give it to me." Unlike a fan mail mechanic, all Inspiration originates with the DM; players can semi-freely trade it among themselves but they are not creating more Inspiration, just passing it around the table. In theory the players can only exchange inspiration when impressed with someone else's game play, but it's unclear how this is adjucated. If the DM's approval or consent is needed then it raises the question of why the DM isn't simply rewarding the RP herself with new, instead of traded, Inspiration. And yet if the DM's tacit approval is not required, then it seems players can freely trade Inspiration without concern as to whether it was legitimately "earned," e.g. for tactical reasons instead. "I'll go out and be kind to that peasant, then give you my Inspiration so you can get advantage on the Deception check to bluff the guards."

    My point of this long post (which seems longer because I am writing it on my phone) is not to say that Inspiration is a bad mechanic, or a good one. But rather to examine how it actually works without burdening it with assumptions that it is a Fate-style storygame-esque mechanic, when really it's a DM-originated reward for the always vaguely defined concept of "good roleplaying." If BIFTs are included in a table's implementation of Inspiration, that reward is, in essence, a reward for playing your character as you said you were going to play your character. Which is actually a really weak mechanic for encouraging depth in roleplay -- but for a game like D&D, it may be about the best that you could expect based on the history and legacy of the game.

    \n My thoughts on Inspiration in my game are twofold. I haven't figured out which I am going to use. Maybe a combination of both.

    First, Inspiration is given on the honor system and not a reward from the DM. There is a bowl of Inspiration tokens (glass beads or poker chips) in the middle of the table. If you, as a player, play out one of your BIFTs and you think you did a good job -- and you don't already have Inspiration -- then you can just reach out and take a token. You are the one who chose or wrote your BIFTs; ergo you know what they mean and can judge if you are playing it out, not the DM.

    Second idea, Inspiration is only given when one of your BIFTs or other roleplaying causes you difficulty on a roll. This is represented by you choosing to gain disadvantage on a roll, which you do not already have disadvantage on. Once you do that, you gain Inspiration.

    The first is more fluffy and means lots of advantage probably, and also relies on players being able to self-judge and not start arguments with each other. The second is purely mechanical and forces players to look for ways to gain disadvantage with the goal of gaining advantage in the future -- it is closer to the idea of a Fate compel in that way. It is also a lot harsher in that fewer Inspiration points are likely to be gained.

    Both ideas are designed to move Inspiration away from being a DM-generated reward system and put the control over when Inspiration is earned squarely in the players' lap. Which is the way I would prefer to have it, because I honestly hate any subsystem that requires me as the DM to subjectively (but ostensibly objectively) judge whether my players are "doing a good job roleplaying" or not. Which is what the default BIFTs/inspiration mechanic appears to do.

If you get a Banana Point for good roleplaying, and the only way to cast spells is to spend a Banana Point, people are going to rail because the metagame resource doesn't seem connected to the act the character is actually performing, but as soon as it's not "cast a spell" but "receive a karmic benefit" or something, a lot of people will nod and say "Sure, no problem." and if Banana Points are instead awarded for, I dunno, kindness and mercy, rather than "good roleplaying" you have a feedback loop that encourages a type of gameplay and that jars relatively few people, even though it's still a 'dissociated' metagame resource.

    Hm. That was quite a tangent. Anyway, Inspiration is a poorly selected name, because the effect granted doesn't map cleanly to the name.

      \n I want to get back to this thread now that I know the lingo a little bit better.

      First of all, in my OP I kinda speak... warmly about Fate? But I've never seriously considered going back to it after 5e.


      Second, what's the similarity between 5e and Fate? I would've gone much harder in on this similarity in hindsight, re-reading my old posts I was hedging & falling back instead of standing my ground.

      Obv they both have a consistently adjudicated mechanical reward for using fictional positioning in the SIS to get an advantage. This, at the point of 5e:s release, was an unusual design feature and it's brilliantly realized in 5e. This, more than anything, was Fat's legendary "killer app" and 5e does it better.
      Fate "I get on top of the \u2610Toppled Heavy Bookcase (andinvoke the tag I got earlier), so I can reroll my roll".
      5e "I get on top of the toppled heavy bookcase to get advantage so I can get the highest of two rolls."



      And they both have a meta game resource for when your freeform descriptions ("Aspects"/"Traits") get you into trouble (or however you want to phrase it).

      The two key features of Fate, and they're right there in 5e's core (well, adv/disadv is). The third big feature of Fate is roll+adds vs TN, which, well, 5e also has.

      \u2022 To advantageously fictionally position yourself in 5e doesn't cost meta resources. I mean, you're doing something cool (actualizing the SIS) and you have to pay for it in Fate? 5e making it always free makes so much more sense.

      5e fixes issues I hadn't even realized were fixable in Fate, I had noticed how the gears creaked and went rusty sometimes, but I thought it was "worth it".

      \nThis thread and the ones related may give you some cool ideas on how to handle social encounters. I use partially an internalized version of some things here and social skill rolls only to resume things when discussions go for too long and the game seems about to stall.

      Edit:

      Also, I believe a lot of how a mechanic makes sense or not comes from how you interpret the results. It was a huge game-changer for me when I read some PbtA games and stopped describing player's low rolls as boring "You miss", and instead describe it like "This was a sure shot, but then the orc turns around as if he sensed your killing intent, moving his shoulder just enough to turn your attack into a glancing blow over his crude pauldron". Before that I had to wait until I rolled high to describe the NPCs and monsters like a real menace, relying too much on my good luck to create threatening battle scenes.

      Social rolls can also be thought and interpreted in a more complex way. Instead of "you failed your intimidation roll so miserably that even though you're a 6' tall brute orc you come out as a furious fluffy puppy" it could be turned into "You tower menacingly over the puny human. You can smell his fear, but he doesn't yield and now you're either suspecting he's got backup, maybe some magic available or just skill. He might be bluffing or underestimating you, the thing is that there's so many things that could be going on that it makes you doubt intimidating him further could be a good idea. At least until you find out some more things about this guy and this place"

      It's like using the dice roll more like an oracle to guide the development of the scene than as a way to adjudicate developments that make no sense or are just boring. Does it make sense for the innkeeper to suddenly trust the PCs so much that he decides to not charge a dime for their stay? What does he get in exchange? If pcs or npc's arguments make no sense on our table, no roll is called. If it makes perfect sense and everyone gets what they want or realize it's better to give up, no roll is needed. If there's a slight chance the situation may develop an interesting twist, or if the person being convinced is in doubt, that's when the roll is called.

      So whenever a player straight calls for a social roll, I ask them: "how do you do it, what do you say to the person?" and if their arguments are solid, I just narrate the consequences. If the stance of the person being intimidated or persuaded changes but he tries to hide it, I call for an Insight roll. He may try to do something of signal another person, that's a perception roll. If the pc is trying to deceive someone, I ask the player for confirmation "Are you trying to deceive him?" and then we either proceed to the roll or go back. Then the NPC reacts to the new information accordingly to his personality, principles, situation etc.

      You can make even less rolls if you compare the PC and NPC stats instead of calling for rolls.
