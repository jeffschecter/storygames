\n Okay, so I'm watching Planet Terror last weekend when I get to this scene where the aging prom queen turned junkie nightshift nurse locks her kid in the car with a loaded revolver and tells him to shoot anybody who comes to that car that isn't her -- and not to point the gun at himself because it's dangerous. You know what is going to happen before it does but part of you thinks "Naw, he (Rodriquez) wouldn't go there. . " and then *BLAMMO*

That's right. The kid blows his face clean off, man.

I got to thinking about the screenplay and how Rodriquez must have approached it. What I walked away with was this -- If an opportunity for excess presented itself, the man isolated it, processed it through ten buckets of corn syrup, dusted it with gunpowder, and kicked it out the other side drenched in excess that would make John Waters squirm in his latex assless chaps. Why don't more games work this way?

I'm envisioning a system that makes use of "Money Shots" -- or scenes that work kind of like hot spots in video games. For example, when Nightshift Nurse locks her kid in the car and tells him not to point the gun at himself, a player might tag this as the Trigger for a "Money Shot" and initiate an event (the aforementioned "BLAMMO" sequence). Another good example might be that scene in Deep Blue Sea where Russel Franklin (Samuel L. Jackson) gives that moving speech and then *WHAM* a giant shark jumps out of the pool behind him and swallows him whole.

So, basically, what I'd be looking at are Triggers -- either deliberate or unintentional events that set the stage for a subsequent scene of excess (the Money Shot). What I'm hung up on is how to activate these Triggers in play. I'm tempted to say "Spend some points to make it happen!" but I don't want to go that route at all. As I see it, these Money Shots are things that players should get rewards for because they enrich the game, not something that they should be forced to pay for in order to implement.

Obviously, I have a pretty clear idea of what I want. What I need is a better idea of how to get there. Your suggestions and help will be appreciated. Thanks much!

    \n I've been thinking about this a bit more and have decided that this really needs to be a complete game. Over my morning coffee break, I decided that in true vomit bag cinema fashion, the game needs a different kind of game mechanic for resolving action. Specifically, when a player wants to do something big, they need to bet their life. If you've seen Planet Terror (or Hell of the Living Dead, I Spit On Your Grave, etc) you know that characters aren't afforded the level of plot immunity that they are in more. . . uhm. . . subdued cinema.

    So, I'm thinking that you roll X to determine Y, with each character wagering points of Plot Immunity against actions. Doing so grants them more dice to roll, if the action fails, Plot Immunity spent is lost. If a character's Plot Immunity is reduced to zero, they bite it -- this will be a Trigger (as previously discussed). Now, in marked departure from most indie game, I'm going to say screw all of that "You get to narrate your own character's death!" stuff. In Vomit Bag Theatre (has a nice ring to it, don't you think?) other players get to decide your fate (and they gain more of their own Plot Immunity points for it).

    Obviously, I'm still a bit sketchy on how all of this comes together. But it is coming together.

    \n James, I think there's one critical weakness in this idea:

    You shouldn't get to wait until your Plot Immunity drops to 0 to be affected. Plot Immunity should act as a "soak" mechanic or shielding device.
    When it operates at full capacity, your loved ones are safe, your appendages remain attached, your gun has ammo, and you aren't a zompire.
    When you temporarily reduce your soak/shielding, you can be hit with those glancing shots. You can take wounds, drop the radio, lose track of the team.
    When it's been reduced over time, or is damaged, you are vulnerable and your loved ones can be killed off, you can be stranded, prolongued plot damage can happen.
    When it's gone, you're completely open to all threats.


    The idea is that wagering plot immunity in a scene opens you up to temporary plot damage. Having a reduced plot immunity (because it was wagered and lost, or for some other reason) means that people can give you prolongued or permanent plot damage.

    But in both cases, you've still got some soak. So if your plot immunity is still at 6, then they can't kill off Sally, but they can kidnap her. But when it drops to 4, she can be killed. Or... maybe your opposition waits until it hits 2. Because when it's at two... that's right... she can become possessed! OMFGz!
