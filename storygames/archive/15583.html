\n How do you schedule games?

How do you send out reminders?

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We use http://doodle.com to schedule games.

We use emails to send reminders but I've found more and more friends prefer Facebook or text messages rather than email.

This is meant for carpooling... but why not RPGs?
http://lifehacker.com/5873184/damn-simple-carpooling-organizes-your-carpools-via-automatic-group-text-messages\n
Gathering everyone up for the morning carpool can seem impossible. Damn Simple Carpooling sets up scheduled SMS messages for your entire group so you can keep track of each person.

The app is very simple: just type in your phone number and your name, and then anyone involved in your car pool can join your group by sending a text message to the "group number" Damn Simple Carpooling spits out. You can set a time and place to meet, and they'll get reminders every day, asking them whether they'll be there. If they reply with "no", you'll get a notification. They can even message the entire group if they're going to be late.

Damn Simple Carpooling is a free service that works wherever SMS messages do. Hit the link to see more about how it works.
\nMore here: http://www.damnsimplecarpooling.com/

Maybe we could use this for GMs at conventions! (assuming they agree, have a mobile device, and a flexible texting plan).

    \n Do you mean, like, "when are we going to play this week" scheduling, or "who wants to play Polaris weekly in January" scheduling?

    I'm in the process of doing the latter, and it usually takes care of the former. I sent out an email a couple months in advance (to get people to tentatively plan for it) to 8 or so people; around double the needed amount for the game. I sent out another email in late December, and got just enough replies to set up the game. We discussed place, time, and days of the week that work. In my experience doing it this way makes the schedule pretty easy to stick to, as people have voiced their general weekly scheduling needs beforehand. And, of course, it's easier to stick to a weekly schedule for a month than for six months.

    If people need to say "I can't play this week," usually it's just an email to the whole group.

    \n I really like When Is Good for figuring out meeting times. Although honestly, I've had less than a 50% success rate with it. Either it turns out that there's no time when everyone can meet, or, more commonly, one or more people just refuses to use the scheduler.

    In my experience, When Is Good is very handy for scheduling one-shot games and events. If you're interested in scheduling a regular game (weekly, bi-weekly, whatever), then you're better off just talking to everyone in the group. A regular game is more of a commitment than a one-shot, and there are more factors at play in people's lives than "I happen to be free that day".

    Edited to Add: The campaign wiki Epic Words has a built-in calendar and game reminder e-mails built in, which is a nice feature. I think you get those features without having to subscribe. http://www.obsidianportal.com/ might have something similar, but I'm not as familiar with that site.

    \n I use Facebook events for my OD&D open table: Create the event. Invite the 25 people involved in the campaign (plus 10-15 more who have expressed interest but haven't managed to make a session yet). First 7-8 people to respond get seats the table for that session.

    For my "regular" campaign, we use e-mail: As GM, I put together a list of dates and times that work for me during the coming month. I e-mail that out to my 5 players and tell them to delete the dates/times that don't work for them. It's usually a simple matter to then pick 2-4 dates that work for everybody.

    \n Our group has a fairly regular weekend game, and we're all good about knowing what our schedules will be, so we just work out when next week's game will be at the end of the night (typically this is, "Is Saturday at 5 still good for everyone next week?"). Then there's usually an e-mail sent around by the GM/whoever is hosting the game towards the end of the week as a reminder, and we also use e-mail to handle unexpected changes ("I'm getting dragged into work on Saturday, so can we do Sunday instead?") or potluck arrangements or whatever. It's been a long time since anyone had to bail out on a session without giving everyone else several days' notice first.

    We also talk about when we'll play at the very beginning, right along with what we'll be playing and who will be playing and character creation and all that stuff, so we can all be on the same page about what days we want to play on. We also talk about how to handle absences -- if someone's going to be out of town one weekend, will we play without them or wait until they get back? What if two people are missing? Addressing those questions up front sets everyone's expectations properly.
