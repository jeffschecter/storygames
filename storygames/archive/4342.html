    Posted By: Consonant DudePosted By: woodelfHuh? Esoterrorists isn't new ground? Who/what did it first?
    \nAre you speaking specifically of the (bland) investigation procedures? I'll assume so since the rest of game is fairly mediocre. I believe Sorensen's Inspectres addresses investigations in a more interesting way thanEsoand it predates it by many, many years.

    I was doing this years ago and far from being the first. I lifted and tweaked the concept from Clinton Nixon'sDonjon(facts) and White Wolf's Adventure! (Inspiration) as well as years of actual play, fudging and exchanging tips with dozens of GMs who have tackled with clues/railroading issues. I've used variations in Story Engine, CORPS, GURPS for five years or so.

    OK, pulled out Esoterrorists and InSpectres for a quick re-read, just to make sure it wasn't my faulty memory. Donjon and InSpectres are both the same as each other, and very different from Esoterrorists: in both of those, successful uses of skills result in player authorial control. So, the players get to create the clues. Inspiration in Adventure! is much along the same lines: it gives expanded authorial control to non-GM players. The innovation i'm seeing in Esoterrorists is two-fold: 1) players don't even make skill checks where cluefinding is concerned; it just happens. 2) players don't have to step outside of their character role to move the investigation forward.

    Personally, i prefer, say, Donjon's structure, where everyone is an author of the story. However, i've dealt with a lot of other RPers who, when they're not the GM, don't want any authorial control beyond their own character. Hell, i'm pulling teeth right now trying to get my current group to engage in such radical activities as deciding for themselves whether or not the character cursed to always have the wrong people fall for her happens to have at this very moment a love note from such a person on her person, rather than me doing all the deciding. [After a lengthy post-game analysis, i realized that when they suggested that, and i responded "you could have such a thing" meaning "if that's what you want, then say so, and it is so", they seem to have heard "maybe--but it'll depend on some sort of determination that the GM or the rules have yet to make".]

    So, Esoterrorists has figured out a way to handle investigation scenarios that (1) doesn't risk coming to a grinding hault and (2) works within a traditional RPG structure, where only the GM has authorial input on the rest of the world beyond the PCs. Now, maybe, this, too, is prefigured in other works that i'm just not familiar with. But it's definitely not the same thing you see in InSpectres, Adventure!, or Donjon.

    \n So, I'm reading the Silmarillion again, not having read it for two decades or more. I remember a lot of it, but it's a hard read, and I'm finding some themes that I think I may have missed the first time through.
    \n
    \nOne of the themes is "some things can only be done once." When Ungoliant comes and destroys the light of the two trees in Valmar, at the urging of Melkor, and all light but the stars leaves the world, Yavanna comes to the site of the disaster, and says precisely this, "some things can only be done once." Can she just make the trees again? No. And this is why Feanor will not give up the Silmarills, he says that it would kill him to do so, because he put all of himself into that one project.
    \n
    \nHow often have you said of your favorite rock band, "You know, I still prefer the early stuff, it's their best work." ?
    \n
    \nNow, that's just a theme. One can hope that lightning will strike twice. And then you always have the Assimovs of the world, people who can churn out goodness over and over and over (Sorensen?). I think that to the extent that somebody makes something spectacular, it greatly reduces the chances of them repeating or exceeding that spectacular event. Seems pretty obvious on the face of it in some ways.
    \n
    \nThat's not to say the Reign isn't a good game... it is. It's just not as spectacularly out there as, say, Unknown Armies was for it's time. That's not to say that Law's new edition of Hero Quest won't be a huge improvement... I'm pretty certain that it will be. It's just not going to be the landmark that, say, Feng Shui was. Etc, etc.
    \n
    \nAnd that's not to say that the next big thing won't come from Stolze, or Laws, or Tweet, or Tynes, et al. It might. But I think that it's just as likely to come from sombody who was like those guys were before they made their big contributions. Why not somebody else?
    \n
    \nI think expecting the same people to be ground-breaking over and over is just not realistic. They've got a shot at it, but so do lots of other people. And these things are so relative.
    \n
    \nThe brilliantness of these folks is not gone. It's just being built on by others. Far from expecting them to continue to brighten our RPG world, we owe them a debt of gratitude for what they've already done.
    \n
    \nOr that's just how I see it.
    \n
    \nYou want something new? Something brilliant? OK, make it.
    \n
    \nNot so easy? Well then understand that it's not easy for anybody. Age aside, Shreyas... when can we expect to see the world-shaking Torchbearer from the young designer? Should be easy for you, right?
    \n
    \nMike
