    I'd have to go with Per Fischer's advice to me. Go first. Push for something amazing, huge or difficult - killing your turncoat of a lieutenant, framing your wife for a crime, confronting your daughter over a slight and then drawing your sword. Make the player opposite work *hard* to introduce conflict.

    I've only played a little Polaris, but didn't find it got silly. There was plenty of humor, but nowt silly. It helps that scenes are chosen very deliberately. You request a scene, play till it sings, then snap the scene shut (and chat about how well it worked).

    Very good luck.

    My experience with Polaris silliness

    In my recent four-session play, things got silly from time to time. But, I had done a poor job of getting the colour across (the other three players just read the "Moments frozen in time" and thought they were abstract).
    Plus, two were long-time players and they had difficulties understanding how the key phrases worked (actually, the greater the experience (in trad games), the more difficulties, it seems). The only "newbie" managed the thing quite well, even catching me off balance with clever use of key phrases (even though I had already played).

    All these problems can be overcome by some good communication (or better still: have everybody read the book ), setting an example and just playing a session to oil the gears.


    Scene framing

    One thing I will keep in mind (and the understanding of which did enhance our game experience) is that scene framing is very important.
    Fuck dialogues and lengthy introductions.
    Follow Ben's advice in the book and get straight to the point: who is insulting who, with whom is the protagonist's wife screwing, what demon is being slain, what catastrophe is it that is falling on the character, what ignominious accusations are being made, etc.
    And when the climax is done, end the scene. (We often dragged on a bit in the beginning, but it's of no use and it can actually be a source of silliness when the players run out of ideas.)

    During the scene, always, always remember to include consequences in your statements. There's no meat in trying to tune down what the other said (except with the use of the You ask far too much key phrase, which is actually a bit different to just tuning down).
    You don't like the consequences that the Mistaken proposes? Either take the risk with It shall not come to pass or hit back with great consequences of your own.
    In-character dialogue which has no consequence should be banned. (A lot of roleplayers like to "act-out" their characters just for the sake of it. Don't do it in Polaris. It slowed down the game for us and led to some absurd scenes as we were flailing around trying to find something to do.)


    Authority

    Finally, one should realize that Polaris isn't all that "shared authority" over the course of a given scene. The Moons have hardly any authority (in conflict), and the Heart has only control over his Protagonist (barring the use of conflict phrases). The Mistaken has a lot of it: he controls all rivals, demons and the environment and has nothing to loose.
    And while the key phrases look symmetric in theory, in play the cost to use them is, in the long run at least, greater for the Heart. The Mistaken can always steer things towards an experience roll, which will either reduce the protagonist's Zeal or refresh the themes (allowing renewed use of those powerful key phrases again, bringing the Experience cycle in motion again).

    That's were the Mistaken has the upper hand and should, IMO, take advantage (and responsibility) to steer the aesthetics of the game to the group's interests. Of course the story is about the Protagonist, but it's about the Protagonist getting hammered at by lots of things, mainly under the Mistaken's control. If the Mistaken throws silly stuff at the Protagonist, chances are that he will either disconnect from the game and grow passive or address the menace with even more silliness.
    If the Protagonist acts silly, the Mistaken can always claim he is possessed by demons and steer things back a bit.
    That said, the Mistaken hardly has full, classical GM control. But still he has probably got most of it (remember that he has final say over Experience rolls, the game's overarching mechanic.)

    One major caveat about this point is scene framing, which lets the framer set the scene with whatever he likes in it. And both the Heart and the Mistaken can do that (so the Mistaken's monopoly of environment, rivals and demons gets mixed into the lot). This goes back to my point about scene framing and basically takes the same idea as before: the scene should be framed aggressively so that it gains impact and pushes for conflict rapidly, letting the authority play flourish to the game's advantage.


    This is all a bit sketchy and perhaps I've missed a few issues. Hopefully Ben or other more experienced Polaris players can add their comments to the lot.

    Cristoph has good advice.

    Here's my extension on Larry / Per. If it's the first scene of the game and I'm the Mistaken, and I don't know what else to do, I set it up so that their son or daughter's one true love is a demon. If it's the first scene and I'm the Heart and don't know what else to do, I kill the senate and declare martial law.

    Polaris can turn silly or not. There are three things to keep in mind:
    1) The more invested everyone is in the color, the less likely this are to get silly.
    2) It's okay to get silly, because that in no way stops you from being tragic. To look at famous tragedies: Romeo and Juliet is laugh-out loud funny at times. Even Macbeth has a few yucks.
    3) The most important thing is the get authority right and play up the moons. This is the stumbling block I see new players hit a lot.
