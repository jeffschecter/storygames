\n I have hit a dichotomy in the last couple days. Matt Wilson talking about his SWN campaign was anticipating character kills (which apparently didn't happen) and Jamey's OtherQuest playtest draft specifically states, "You must bring the PCs as close as you can to defeat and doom... without killing them. ". Over on Grognardia, James quotes an old Dragon article about fudging rolls that would kill the party too early.

I think that the OSR has brought back a sense that character death is a feature rather than a bug and that got me thinking. What is the up side of character death? and What is the benefit of keeping them around?

    \n Seems like there are two pretty different good things that come out of character death.

    In a game where character death is common, the characters who do not die long enough to build up substantial cruft of past, story, reputation, wealth, etc. -- those characters really mean something. They're the ultimate bad-ass, even when it's just a product of anthropic luck. But it's a fun thing to play through.

    In a game where character death is uncommon and relatively easy to control, but still on the table, it's an opportunity for the players to say something about the world or the game or whatever. They can underline that this thing, right here, is important.

    \n Let's not forget the value of death in a game like Paranoia! Humor, sure; but also a way to play a variation on your character (i.e., the next clone is subtly... different). Much like having a stable of PCs for OSR play--set 'em up, knock 'em down, new flavor every couple of sessions!

    I second Christopher's second point, too--so much so that I baked it into the GLASS rules for Player Priority games: a PC can't die unless its player Commits. He or she has said something significant about the conflict, now that death is on the table (and has also drawn every murder-hobo in the site!).

    Finally, I think playing AS dead is a largely untapped play space. I never played Wraith; but it would be cool if more games supported PCs converting into some form of afterlife entity that can still participate WITH the rest of the PCs, not orthogonally, in parallel, or at the system level. An extension of this notion of "death, played" would include games in which death is the price of entry to play (e.g., D&D-Descent-Into-Hell-type thing).

    \n I've had more character deaths in the current Call of Cthulhu campaign that I'm playing than I've had in a while, and I have to say I like it. We've had six deaths over little more than a dozen sessions. I'm the only player who hasn't had a character killed yet. Maybe I would speak differently if and when I do have a character die, but I don't think so. In particular, I like how there isn't imposed dramatic meaning on the deaths, which contrasts with the sense of drama of many games.

    Character death is not under player control (although it is certainly influenced by player choices), and death is generally neither glorious nor melodramatic. This Monday, we almost lost two characters, when three of us went into a cavern in Scotland. The retired soldier at the end left behind one body but risked himself to save another who was knocked unconscious.

    Meaning is created by how the other characters react to the death later (sometimes much later), not via a death scene. We are growing hardened to death, though. It's not something I'd want to do for every game, but I think it is working reasonably well for us in this campaign.

    \n My understanding of D&D is that having characters die by the droves is necessary as a loss condition. If we did not have character death in our campaign, we would not have a real bottom-level loss condition, and thus the game would become a mere hamster wheel of patience. This feel has been remarked upon many times during the campaign: even the somewhat laborious bits of play have enhanced significance because the game is nothing like an experiental hamster wheel where you're just there to experience a ride that ends the same no matter what you do. In D&D the contrast between success and failure is stark and irreversible.

    Specifically, the "character death" here is the loss of fictional positioning and experience points. If we hypothetically played in a way that allowed the "same" characters to reappear with zero experience and without their contacts, wealth and responsibilities, then that would satisfy the structural requirements a D&D campaign has in this regard. Even taking away the experience points, the dominant score-keeping device, might be sufficient to retain a clear loss condition.

    One way of phrasing this is that without character death we don't have a clear end to the story of "how high can this guy rise before he burns in the sun".

    \n I think a lot of players work hard to empathize with their characters, and those characters -- were they alive -- would not want to die, so the players don't want them to die. (Carefully not using the I-word here, so you shouldn't either. Ha.)

    There's something about Stance bopping around in my brain, too, but I'm not sure how valid it is.

    At the very least, you lose fictional positioning. In some games, that is hard-gained position. No one starts out being the Conquerer of the Nine Kingdoms; you had to "earn" it through play. Probably you've also lost a lot of in-game currency: your hit points went from 50 to 5, you no longer have a +2 long sword, your gold pieces are reduced from 25000 to 80, and your level dropped from 8 to 1. If it took two years to accomplish all those things (fictional position or game currency), that's a serious investment. Starting over feels like a real loss. You have two choices: either let the players start at the "cool place" at 8th level or whatever, in which case being 8th level is no longer an achievement, or make players start over to earn their way back to the achievements, in which case death is painful.

    Of course, if you care only about the journey, and not about any kind of achievement I'm talking about here, then you're playing a certain kind of game. People who care about those achievements are playing a different kind of game. Wisdom gleaned from one type of play must carefully be applied to the other kind of play, if at all.

    \n They serve two equally valuable, but completely different functions.

    Character death at the hands of dice and system function, OS (Old School) style, reinforces survival as one of the major challenges. It got a bad rap from me for a long time because of games like those White Wolf put out, with heavy focus on story and character development, and a combat system that was enjoyable and detailed, but also surprisingly lethal with a good roll of the dice.

    Outside of old school games, I love it in Unknown Armies and Godlike/Wild Talents (ORE). In UA, it works because the combat system makes it clear that gettig into potentially lethal fights is BAD NEWS. In fact it starts with some great text on how bad of an idea trying to murder someone else who is aware of your intentions and does not want it to happen. The players don't know their hit points, which just reinforces that. In ORE, the tone is that, despite super powers, death still can come easy. A lucky roll, or the right superpower, can perform one hit kills reliably. WT offers some rules to make it less lethal for more traditional super hero stories (which I sometimes employ). It works for the game. In both of these games, death is rarely meaningful. It's messy and stupid. Though it still can be meaningful. I've seen more than once players in Godlike games put their characters at risk to save another. Being a hero usually gets you killed, so when they survive (or save the person and die in the process) it really makes it memorable.

    Keep characters around if you're trying to emulate fiction, and build stories. That doesn't mean character death can't happen, it just shouldn't happen randomly. Involved players should have a say. It can suck when people disagree on a character death being important for the story. Sometimes people get too attached to characters. Other times people get too attached to the idea that Death needs to happen for a dramatic and memorable story moment.

    This is my favored mode. I want players to get invested in their characters, understand them, and grow them. But if there's a player who wants death to strike, there's usually room to make that happen for them without putting all characters at risk. I find it best when the system forces big changes, and death is implied as an option, but not required. Like In A Wicked Age, forcing players out of play (not a big deal for a 2 hour game). Though also good when it's not on the table (like Smallville). You get really powerful moments when a player chooses to have her character die. It's the culimation of a moment. And there's nothing stopping it from being ultimately stupid and meaningless, but it's up to the players to determine that. It gives power to the group to set the tone.

    I've heard arguments that fiction operates the first way, but that's never true in good fiction. The characters who die do so for reasons, even when those reasons are to show the cold and meaningless and randomness of death. The characters who die that way were still in the story to die for that purpose, ironically. One popular novel series about iron thrones has a famous death that seems to be random and pointless, but it's the culmination of that character's story, everything leading up to that point makes sense, and it shows that the world is not fit for characters like that, and that this world is brutal and kills its heroes and villains, as long as someone else has something to gain from it.

    Posted By: jhkimThe important question to me is - why do I sometimes want randomness (and unexpected results in general) for other fictional events, but not randomness for character death? (I'm not sure I do, so really I'm speaking hypothetically.)
    \nThe problem isn't the random component. The problem is that there is a single specific, incredibly massive outcome from the random component.

    Could my character randomly fall in or out of love? Randomly get have a child? Randomly discover his father presumed dead is alive? Randomly get dishonored? Almost certainly, no to all of those. Death is unique for some reason for being the only prescribed outcome for getting into a conflict.

    All those other things can happen, but there needs to be some significant scene setting and interaction going on, in character or out, to get us there. Unlike combat systems, which are usually interesting and fun systems that I want to engage in, there's no equally compelling system for figuring out if my character falls in love or becomes dishonored in most games where random death is possible.

    For some reason, it's recognized by many rules that fiction driven by player interaction should be a major influence in the outcomes of every single type of conflict as long as it is not physical. Once swords or guns are drawn, death is on the table, regardless of how meaningless it would seem to the fiction being created. And worse, it's often the only victory condition.

    I have no problem with randomness. But what I want it to do is guide the fiction, not determine it. Is this action I am about to take going to benefit me, or will it cost me? That's a great question to bring to random resolution. Maybe the cost is my character's life. But maybe it's my character's honor, or the life of a person my character is trying to protect, or something else.

    Unless a game is very specifically about life and death struggles (such as early D&D and Godlike), forcing that outcome isn't just a problem with the fiction, it's completely unimaginative for the myriad of bad outcomes that might be possible in a fight that could fuel our fiction even more than a character death could in a given moment.

    \n How I learned to stop worrying and love the death.

    In our WeD&D game, we've had 11 deaths in 13 sessions (not including nameless NPCs). We have six players and all players except one have lost at least one (if not multiple) characters.
    I inwardly cringe every time I roll an attack. I try to keep a brave face, but I dread the look of crushed disappointment on the face of the damned. I think sometimes I might hide behind the screen and mutter. "He hits\u2026and you're killed."

    The players, for their collective part, get sad/mad when their individual characters are slain. But I have to hand it to them, they have a good attitude. After the sting of death subsides they say, "It's cool. It's not your fault. It's just the game." And then they make new characters.

    And, to their eternal credit, they are against changing the rules: "This is the game we signed up to play!"

    That, to me, is the key. They derive some value/enjoyment/utility from this game. The character death is part of the game. And, conversely, they beamed with surprise and pride when they leveled up through dint of luck, wits and hard work.

    -Luke

    \n When there's so many deaths, either the game kills randomly (too randomly, I mean), or the players are not good at staying alive (and the game encourages them to improve). As Luke says, "through dint of luck, wits and hard work."

    I think the amount of luck required is a dial one can tune for a group's preferences. Let's say it's 99% luck and 1% wits and hard work. There's no achievement there. Even games of chance generally let you decide when to roll the dice. If it's 1% luck and 99% wits and hard work, then I'm not sure if it's as exciting. Also, I suspect "luck" fills in system here. The game designers can't enumerate all the ways to adjudicate if you fall in a pit or not, but they can give you some rules for determining the likelihood.

    That is, it's hard to write rules for GM fiat but easier to write rules for random chance. We accept those abstractions, and they don't even have to be accurate. We accept a game's statistical probabilities as the physics of the fiction. Of _course_ I fell in the pit: I rolled a 1.

    \n Because of the store, I run a lot of games for people who only play one game. Like, they have 1 character that they've put a lot of work into and really enjoy playing.

    In general, if I'm setting up a long-term game for people who are not lifestyle gamers who play LOTS of games like we mostly are, I tell the players that they can play their PCs for as long as they like, and I'm not here to arbitrarily tell them they can't play those characters any more. I'll create serious and dire complications, make their lives crazy, maybe even impose the 'condition' of 'death' on their PCs, but if they want to keep playing those characters, then 'death' will be surmountable (ESPECIALLY if we're playing a supers game; it's essentially in-genre to come back from the dea there.... I'm not going to tell someone who only gets to game 8 hours a month with me as their GM that I'm going to decide when they have to throw away their character sheet. A mindful GM ought to be able to keep the story interesting and the players engaged and tense while still doling out some limited narrative immunity in the same way any skilful creator of serial fiction can.

    It surely comes down the the common-sense rules lots of people have invoked here: be very clear about expectations and the range of possible outcomes when you sit down to play. Not everyone is a jaded, con-going grognard. Some (I'd say the majority of casual) players just want to be part of a fun ongoing narrative, and I've had 20+ years of totally awesome gaming without ever telling someone who wasn't ready to give up their avatar that the dice said different, so go in the other room and rewrite your life. People get unexpectedly fired and divorced and lose friends to car accidents and shit in real life.
