\n I've noticed that a number of designers are producing quickstart versions of their rules, which is a very good thing IMO, as games based on simplified rules have a much better chance of attracting time-strapped con-goers, and for various other reasons. However, I saw the other day that a designer I know (sorry, can't remember who offhand, and which game) is writing quickstart rules BEFORE going on to develop them for longer term play. Now at first blush this struck me as a bit counter-intuitive: wouldn't it make more sense to design the standard rules first, and then boil them down to a quickstart ruleset later? But I then I thought about it a bit and decided that it might be a good way to proceed, but I'm not completely persuaded.

So, what are folks' thoughts on the idea of designing quickstart rules first, and gong on to expand them later? what are the advantages and disadvantages of both approaches?

    \nPrecisely so. It's foolishness to build out the entire ruleset before you start playtesting. The more rules you've built out initially, the more changes you have to make to your game when playtesting finds problems with some of your rules. You need to start with your core, and make sure that's stable, before you work in peripheral rules and options. It's an iterative process.

    Round 1: make sure the core is solid
    Round 2: using the core as a foundation, test various subsystems
    Round 3: within the established context of the core and subsystems, test different character and gameplay options to make sure they're at least roughly balanced mechanically

    \n (Disclaimer: the following won't make a whit of sense unless you're familiar with the definition of "system" and "rules mechanics" as they used to be used at the Forge. Basically, "system" is the structure of the game that enables the players to manipulate the fictional space, while rules mechanics are merely a variety of tools that players use to structure and facilitate this process. Having a GM is part of the "system", while rolling dice pools is part of "mechanics".)

    Creating simple rules first only makes sense if you're working in a design space with a well-defined systematic framework underlying your work. Effectively, you already do have a complete game, it just happens to be one that can be mechanized in lighter of weightier ways depending on specific needs.

    I cannot recommend trying to make a "light" version of your game before designing the "real game" as an universal practice, simply because thinking about design in this way is counter-productive to putting down the actual system of your game. Game design often occurs in the context of an implicit system (e.g. the traditional "GM narrates an adventure, players decide what their characters do"), and in those cases it is entirely feasible to work bottom-up, relying on the pre-existing, often somewhat implicit systemic framework. However, if your game happens to require a new, weird system, then you'll find it essentially impossible to develop that bottom-up - the system is such a thorough center of a game that you cannot create some sort of a light version first and then go on to expand on it; that makes sense for rules mechanics, but not for the system of play itself.

    For example, if you're doing a D&D variant of some sort, then it is entirely feasible to start simple and work your way up to your preferred density of mechanical complexity; the pre-existence of the actual system of play with the GM role, adventuring party and dungeoneering procedures makes it possible for you to play fast and loose with the mechanical details. However, if you were doing something like say My Life with Master, it would be impossible to create a lightweight mechanization of the game before you'd figured out the actual system of play. As it happens, the game text of MLwM actually is pretty much the minimal mechanical treatment on its concept, so you could presumably work up from there to add more rules now that the game has a well-conceived and functional system, plus the "quickstart" rules mechanics

    \n I think you're not actually disagreeing with us, Eero.

    In both cases you need to define the "minimum viable text" or, to put it another way, "the simplest possible rules" for your game - no frills, no extras, no potentially optional processes, etc, first.     The idea that it is somehow possible to write a game WITHOUT starting with the simplest possible implementation sounds like a recipe for disaster to me.

    \n Yeah, it's also important to keep in mind that you absolutely should be modularizing your rules in some way, because otherwise you can't effectively mechanically test it or tweak it. (I'm a big fan of Vincent Baker's philosophy of concentric game design for this reason.)

    For example, I might have a core die mechanic that I really need to make sure is working correctly, so I write that up first and test it out, to make sure it's taking inputs and putting outputs out. Then I write up the modules where it interfaces with the narrative in different ways, and test those out. As I go back to the game, I first make corrections to the fundamental pieces of it, then start to smooth out the seams between the parts.

    It's rather like software development, in that way. If you try to tackle the whole thing from the ground up all at once, it's a nightmare.

    \n Personally I think trying to design a quick-start first is usually a bad idea.

    Developing good quick-start / sampler versions of a game is much harder than developing the full game. You are aiming for a much tighter experience and an easy learning curve ramp which also supports your full game. In order to do that, you need to have a clear idea of the load-bearing parts of your system and fictional elements too.

    This really has nothing to do with the notion of a minimum playable game. Certainly you should arrive at that quickly and playtest often. Just don't confuse a minimum playable subset as a quick-start. They serve entirely different purposes.

    I'm not sure why you couldn't test all the components of your game in isolation. Each piece of a game has a particular functionality. You should be able to test it in some way to ensure that it has that functionality.
    \nMostly this is because tabletop games are built out of simple components that interrelate in complex ways. An experienced designer generally does not need to run e.g. dicing exercises that we hear people doing sometimes, because they can predict what the given arrangement will do. What they will need to test for, though, is unexpected emergent behaviour caused by the specific interaction of all the components that go into their design. Of course not all of the components are equally likely to cause significant knock-on effects, but many do.

    (If the medium the designer was working with had complex components and simple interactions, like e.g. software design, then the situation would be the exact reverse: you would write up expected behavior specs for the various components, and how they'll ideally interrelate, and then test each module thoroughly in isolation to make sure it works as intended.)

    To illustrate this, an example: we would not generally expect a designer of a D&D-like game to have to "test" their d20+stat rolls to ensure that they have the expected functionality - they are probably pretty justified in expecting that the dice will provide a flat random distribution, moved up or down in the natural number space by their simple linear modifier. However, we would expect them to test how the dice handling fits in with their combat system's multitude of other components, with possible effects ranging from dice interpretation (do the dice provide for the right kind of random input at right times) to handling time (is this dice mechanic too hard to use for its intended purpose).

    Of course one can speculate about games that are so deep and complex that they have an ordinary game's worth of emergent complexity in mere subsystems. I guess that it would be meaningful to test Ars Magica combat rules in isolation from the rest of the game (although, to be fair, that's also an example of the vice of detached design - the game does not need that combat system to be itself in the first place). I would consider these cases to be more of an exception than a rule in tabletop game design.
