I think that the term 'magic' is a big tent word--it gathers together all manner of disparate ideas beneath its category. I would even suggest that this is where its strength lies, in the productive ambiguity it leaves open. Here are some questions I hope will help explore that tent (please answer one or more):

1. What games have magic mechanics that feel 'magical' to you? Why?

2. What games have magic mechanics that don't feel 'magical' to you? Why?

3. What do those answers that tell us about what you think of as magical?

4. Does presence or absence of that 'magical-ness' hamper you enjoyment of the game?

    "Magic," for me, works by association rather than causality. It's not a matter of A affects B to accomplish C -- that's science, and the "real world" -- it's a matter of A is like B and therefore C. The bridge is made of wood, the stick I hold in my hand is wood, I break the stick and the bridge collapses. That's magic.

    My poster children for this kind of thinking is Tribe 8 Synthesis and Mage: the Ascension (2nd ed). In Synthesis, you have two words called Eminences and you can tap into the flow of the River of Dream to bring the supernatural power of that realm into the one you live in, and do so in accordance with those two Eminences. So if I have the word Truth, I can make you tell the truth, but I can also move firelight to reveal hidden things. In play, this piles just boatloads of significance on your Eminences, and you build up a good sense of an associative network of how the entire world relates to your Eminences rather than it being a big uncaring Newtonian pinball machine. Mage does this, too, just not as well.

    For counter examples, anything with a spell list ranks pretty highly up there, if only because a spell list presents a list of options which all have discrete requirements and effects. This format frames magic in the cause-and-effect framework of science and the real world. It builds up no meaning or significance, it's just technology by other means. Now, that can be fun, certainly -- building an address out of different abilities can be an enjoyable tactical exercise -- but it's the sideline, insignificant in terms of the thematic content of the game. When playing like this, it's your own character's cleverness that's important, not their relationship with Truth or Vengeance. It's just a different kind of game.

Imagine for a moment a world in which such things exist (he says with a straight face), but in which they only worked when 1) the priesthood authority was personally in a state of purity and 2) only when God wanted it to.

    So you can pray and bless someone, and there is a logical process by which they will or will not be healed. If you are pure, and God thinks it is good for them to be healed, they will be healed.     Now, given a world in which the mind of God is not knowable, and the state of someone's ritual purity is always open to question, you're going to have a fuck of a time proving or disproving that blessings work scientifically. They are repeatable, but never independently repeatable. Because the background process is not a mechanistic or naturalistic process, but depends on intangible and untestable variables, it isn't going to meet that whole "scientific empiricism" thing. It works on a different set of principles than those that say that water freezes at 0 degrees Celsius (assuming the correct amount of purity and no movement in the water). You can't know ahead of time how it is going to work, or test for why it failed (or possibly even test for why it worked).

      Hi all,

      I agree with Fred, of course, that HQ magic is magical feeling. In fact, I'd say that this is the biggest reason I play HQ.

      But Fred is wrong about Clarke's Law, and in his error we find the key to magic. The person doing magic doesn't see it at all like it's technology. To the person doing magic, he feels that it's supernatural. That it breaks the rules of the physical universe. Because it comes from beyond.

      And that's it, gents. To make magic feel magical, you have to feel it's source every time you use it - or, rather, the character's belief in it's source. So, the earlier comment that somebody made about HQ magic not feeling magical at first can sorta be true. Rather, for things like "common magic" this is actually appropriate. Because this magic is so, well, common, that the characters who use it probably don't think much about the source. Any more than the average person does when they cast the little spell to cast out cold demons when they say, "God Bless You" when you sneeze.

      But if you take a second to think of where that blessing comes from in the Christian religion, from Christ telling us how to cast out demons, you can see the source of the magic. God, in this case. Some little saint's Outsmart Pig Spell might fall into this category in Glorantha.

      For specialized magic, you have a "Worship X" sort of ability rating, and a "Know Myths of X" ability rating. These determine your character's connection to that otherworldly entity that's delivering you the magic. One doesn't "do" magic in most cases, really, one opens up a conduit to the source of the magic, and it happens. If one is doing magic, then one is magical oneself, and that's special too (called Natural Magic in HQ).

      I just wrote about this in the FATE forum. Making something magical is not about making it mysterious in it's functioning. It's about making it mysterious in terms of knowing that it comes from some otherworld, and knowing how "impossible" that is. How does some diety allow you to channel some feat replicating some act they performed in myth? Nobody knows how it works, that's the mystery. But it does, and that's the cool part.

      That's what D&D lacks - the coolest spells in D&D are those with the names Bigby, Mordenkainen, or Tensor in them. Because you get at least a taste of where they came from. GURPS pre-requisites give a feeling that there's some background process that one is learning, so that's it's one advantage, though otherwise its pretty dry.

      Actually I think that mages in GURPS are required to have relevant knowledge skills. Same in Hero System, actually. Why don't these work like in HQ? Because in HQ you can figure out a contest about losing faith, or about magical backlash, or about other things that result from the context of using or abusing the magic in HQ (no rules for that in GURPS - though I've seen some offered). Defy your god and use a feat to do something that he does not like? Oh, big time contest to see if he decides to strip the character's abilities for a while to teach him a lesson.

      With Animism in HQ, this is all painfully up front. You don't "do" magic at all. Spirits do magic. Practitioners of animist traditions just know one thing: how to ask the spirits to come along for the ride and help out. Each spirit is it's own little relationship that you have to work out.

      Wizardry is, perhaps, the most fascinating. At first I thought that it was somewhat like the others, but it has a subtle difference. The monotheistic god leaves us on our own to decide how to use the magic of the essence of the world. Yeah, that means you can use it for evil deeds if you want - this is referred to as sorcery. When you die, he'll look at your magic use, and sort you to solace or hell at that point. But life is, in fact, a test to see how people will use things like the magic he's made available to them.

      I have a character who is a vizier sort who just found three books of sorcery (and two about demonology) in a HQ game I'm playing. Oh...my...lord am I psyched about the question of what comes next. I've already decided that he's going to read them, "Because it's important to know what the enemy is capable of." Can you sense the massive bangs I'm creating for my character? I intentionally created him nearly devoid of useful magic...he's going to be tempted, tempted, tempted. Hmmm, maybe if I use these spells with good intent, then they're not sorcery, right?

      See the deceptive road to the dark side, rising ahead of him?

      HQ also makes magic feel magical, because the political conflicts are really all about belief systems. So each use of magic becomes almost a political statement, a religio-political statement. That provides massive context to the magic, making one reflect on the otherworldly sources that the character believes in, and the mysteries that he has to deal with when using magic. That is, the conflicts of the mundane world are really all about what magic is "right" and such, and who's beliefs are right. Magical practitioners even train themselves to see the sort of magic that they believe in, blinding themselves to that of others.

      From another perspective, perhaps the other forms of magic are bogus. Who's to say what's real and what's magical chicanery? The fact that in HQ, that the characters do not have 100% faith makes them seem like us in our quests to find magic in the real world. Be that modern day communion, or the ancient rituals of fire performed on the slopes of Vesuvius. That's the real mystery, what's the truth? Magic isn't an answer, it's people trying to prove the supernatural nature of the universe.

      Another part of the context is that the magic ability must make sense to the source of the magic. Soooo many RPGs get this wrong, with priests all selecting spells from the same list, for example, to say nothing of magic schools all having the same spells, etc. In HQ, all of the feats of a diety are specific to myths of the diety (even if they're not all spelled out, looking at them you can almost imagine what the myths are from the names of the feats).

      This means that there is a bewildering variety of magic in this model. Nothing breaks down the magical feel more than realizing that a particular spell exists because it's useful for adventurers traversing a dungeon. Fireball isn't actually a bad ability this way, if it's obtained as a feat from a fire diety, or from a school of fire magic. And this is why Outsmart Pig Charms and such exist. "Real" magic doesn't exist just to help adventurers, it exists based on it's sources. So "homey" magic like a charm that helps farmers wrangle their pigs feels very real. It makes the source apparent in it's use, and that, again, is the context that makes it feel magical.


      And this is all before doing rituals or going on heroquests to the otherworlds themselves, which, as pointed out, is just more magical feeling than anything else I've ever played.

      So it's magical context with regard to character belief. You have to feel your character's belief in his magic for the magic to feel magical. Note that there were a group of people in Glorantha that did, at one point, try to prove that there were no gods, and that magic was just a force of the natural world like any other to be manipulated - the godlearners. These arrogant men stopped the sun in the sky, rearranged the reality of the gods, and did other amazing things. But then the gods made a comback and kicked their asses.

      Or did they? Only by playing can you find out. Mystery, mystery, mystery surrounding beliefs in things supernatural. Magical.

      - A relationship to the source of the Magic (Worship Orlanth, Know Discord)
      - An understanding of the Stories by which we understand the source (Orlanth Myths, Principia Discordia)
      - Relationships to the people and social structures that are involved in getting to know the magic (Know Priest, Member of Temple, alt.religion.eris)
      - The magic itself as it comes from the source, matching the source (Storm Affinity, Cause Chaos)
      - Virtues that are good to have to make the magic work (Bold for Orlanth, Freethinking for Discordianism).
