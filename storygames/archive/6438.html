\n It's fun to compete in RPGs.
\n
\nIt's fun to create interesting stories in RPGs.
\n
\nIn practice, however, when you try to do both things simultaneously, play can range anywhere from uncomfortable to completely dysfunctional. There are those who say that they don't have this problem. For those of you who believe you've already found El Dorado, I can only say that the methods by which you play will not find it for me. I've never seen any approach to play that makes for a comfortable combination of these priorities. I won't argue that you're in denial, or that you're actually playing one way or another, but I'll just note that if you think you have successful play of this sort, it's very unlikely that you'll be able to help me.
\n
\nNot impossible, however. In fact I'm looking for a new way to get to El Dorado, the place where competition and stories are both generated, and if you know the as yet undiscovered northern approach, go ahead and let me know. We've already tried East, West, and South, and we're doubting that El Dorado exists at all now (hence the name). But I for one am going to try the North approach, and see what that gets me. If you know the way, or can see that I've strayed from the path, let me know.
\n
\nI'm pretty firmly of the belief that decisions can't be made that prioritize more than one value at a time. The basic premise of Creative Agenda. If you don't buy that, you probably won't be of any help here... but, again, if you're the genius who has figured out a different perspective on this subject, fine, make your case. I may shunt you off to another thread, however, and I'll be quite skeptical. Just to warn.
\n
\n
\nNow, before we get into the details of possible solutions (or even getting started on the path to the north approach), why is this important to me? Well, as I say, since both forms of play are fun, I'd like a game that incorporates both forms. But moreover... have you ever had a player play a RPG and afterwards explain to you that it seemed problematic to them? They couldn't explain why, but they simply seem uncomfortable with RPG play?
\n
\nI can't blame folks for not being able to explain the phenomenon. Because it's only after years and years of theorizing about RPGs that I think that I've put my finger on it. Basically they see it as a game... after all, you've said it was one, an rpG. But when they played, the playing ground was more like life... there are no hard and fast rules for how things get done. You can have your character do anything, and at some point some judgment step comes in that's all-important.
\n
\nThis is key to understand. In RPGs the question of even whether or not to roll has some aesthetic standard behind it. Usually the rules will give some very vague guideline like "Only roll if it's important." State of the art descriptions are actually similarly vague, if having a Zen feel to them; things like, "Say yes, or roll the dice."
\n
\nEven outside of resolution, there is a collaboration going on between players to figure out how the action is proceeding. "Freeform" play that has no mechanical resolution is all to do with each player trying to figure out if their contribution to the fiction is suitable at every move. Players don't just narrate "anything," there are standards, and ones that are often very hard to negotiate through, because they usually remain implied, and not explicit. Worse, you can't really be explicit about them because, in the end, there are infinite numbers of "moves" one can make, and each one could have a rule written about it to say whether or not it's Kosher.
\n
\nInstead we rely on player judgement to decide on how a particular maneuver goes. Do we simply allow it to become fact? Do we challenge it by forcing the player to go through a "Resolution System" in order to get the narration he wants to be accepted fact in the play space? If we do use a resolution system, how "difficult" is the task, or how "dramatic" is it? That is, the resolution system itself often has some variable in it that's adjusted by a player (often the GM) so that we can adjust how likely the outcomes are. Again, this requires that some participant in the game make a judgment as to something like liklihood, or dramatic importance, etc.

    \n The point being that in no other human endeavor called a "Game" does human judgment have so important an impact on the outcome as in a RPG. Usually in games we do all we can to ensure that the rules can be dealt with in as objective a fashion as possible. In RPGs this simply isn't usually possible.
    \n
    \nYes, in fact, we can create "RPGs" where all options are mechanically laid out, and there is as little judgement involved as, say, in calling balls and strikes in baseball. But what's interesting is that when people play these games, the narration that occurs often feels "tacked on." Like one was playing Monopoly and narrating the purchases of the properties. Why would you bother?
    \n
    \nIn fact it would seem that it's a unique and valued property of RPGs that we adhere to some aesthetic standard as the means by which we determine success. Often this standard is, by the way, "realism." That is, we attempt to make the game world seem as though it were a "real" place, and the aesthetic that's reinforced, intends to reinforce this illusory feeling that the setting is a real place. In other games, the aesthetic is more dramatic. But in games where there is little aesthetic, many RPG players report that the RPG becomes "just a game" to them.
    \n
    \nI don't want to create a definition of RPG here, but to the extent that we want this unique part of RPGs in RPGs - and I do - any move to create a single set of hard definitions of all moves possible in play works against this property. What I (and many others) want from an RPG is that the narration matters in terms of outcome, and in specifics. That we don't group all of one sort of action into one sort of resolution, rendering the nature of the narration unimportant.
    \n
    \nSo this human aesthetic judgement step seems intrinsic to the sort of RPGs we want. And, yet, it's also what makes us uncomfortable about RPGs at times. Well it does to many who play them for the first time, I've seen it first-hand. That includes people who have played D&D as a minuiatures game in all practicality, and see RPGs that are far removed on the opposite spectrum. They find them suspicious.
    \n
    \nKnow what? I do, too. For all my talk about "wanting failure" for characters, there's a part of me that says that approaching the drama, without still allowing for the player to be competing in full, is asking the player to simply dissociate from the character to some extent. Which, of course, renders the drama inert to some extent. Care, but not enough to always win. It's simply not quite right. Yeah, we can play that way functionally, but it always feel like we're denying some part of ourselves. Does to me, anyhow.
    \n
    \nIf we had both going on, I think we'd have a game where people could feel comfortable that they were competing in their best interests, and yet also were coming up with satisfaction in creating story at the same time. Tautological, I know. I want both because I want both, beacause both would be good. But I honestly think that it would solve a lot of problems.
    \n
    \nI regularly hear the clamor of people in the "indie" crowd saying, "we need to create games that open up the market to the mainstrea." I'm dubious that this is really a mandate. But I'd also note that it's already been done. It's called World of Warcraft, and it's got ten-million players. One game that dwarfs any other RPG in terms of player-base. And, at ten million, it's rivalling any other mainstream activity.
    \n
    \nNow, again, some people will say that WoW is not a RPG by their definition. But to me, it's precisely that WoW is identifiable as a "Game" activity, and that other RPGs are not, that makes it viable to the mainstream. Any RPG that doesn't market itself as a game, and doesnt' play as a game, will never reach mainstream attention. Any more than improv theatre will (note how comedy sports actually make a game of this, and how the mainstream suddenly pays more attention).
    \n
    \nSo I'd posit that, in fact, coming up with a functional way to combine the dramatic/aesthetic/simulative elements of an RPG with making it an actual "game" that people can deal with as a game (meaning having some objective playing field), is actually key to making an awesome RPG. It is the single most important nut that needs to be cracked.
    \n
    \n(If you disagree, then this discussion may not be for you.)
    \n
    \nThe question is, can it be cracked?

    \n The only two reasonable theories that I've ever heard for getting both sorts of play are as follows:
    \n
    \n - "Shifting" agendas during play. That is you play to address challenge one moment, and you play to address theme the next. A game that is meant to cause shift (rather than it happening haphazardly), is said to be "Transitional." Fang Langford's game Scattershot is an attempt at a transitional game where the transition occurs based on mechanical changes in the rules decided upon by the players. The game promotes one sort of decision one moment, and then we change the rules and it supports another sort of decision the next.
    \n
    \n - The other is far more problematic and theoretical, and I've dubbed it "Universal Play" where the system somehow manages to promote decisions that are always both tactically correct, and thematically appropriate at the same time. This seems impossible, however, when you consider that most players will, at some point, decide that the best way to create a theme is to have their character do somthing "stupid" (meaning tactically unsound.) I'm willing to hear arguments on why this is not so, however, and my approach below is to some extent informed by this idea.
    \n
    \n
    \nAnother design that's interesting in this regard is The Riddle of Steel. The game has a massive combat sub-system, and a very influential "Spiritual Attribute" sub-system that interact with each other. Ron says that the thematic content overwhelms the tactical content, and by some readings he might be right in some ways. But interestingly that's never made me feel any less like I was competing when using the combat system in TROS. Sure, with enough dice from SAs, I can dominate the combat... but interestingly if I pay no attention to what's going on, I still might be beaten. Both drama and tactical play lead into character success to some extent.
    \n
    \nFurther TROS does some other interesting things in that the tactically sound decision tends to be the dramatic one. That is, if my character's SA is "Loves X" and X is in trouble, it may well make a ton of tactical sense for him to dive in right there, to get those extra dice.
    \n
    \nIn fact, one could argue that TROS goes back to being gamey this way, in that the player doesn't have an option as to what the best solution is in a given circumstance. But the game does something else interesting in that it allows one to change their SAs in ways that tend to give the player back their ability to make dramatic decisions about what's important to their characters.
    \n
    \nIt does so, it seems to me however, by having dramatic decisions made one moment, and then tactical ones made the next. Overall Ron might be right as to what it tends to promote in play. And it's far from perfect. But it's always interested me as a model. Basically it's making the player think of these different priorities at different times in play. So it seems somewhat transitional, while also having the decisions be somewhat "universal" (both tactically sound and dramatically sound).
    \n
    \nSo it's always occured to me that you could "partition off" parts of play such that we could comfortably be making one sort of decision at one point in play, and be making another sort of decision at another. Note that Ron's contention is that there are "instances of play" that form the observable units in which we can discern a creative agenda. And generally it's supposed that these are fairly long. If not, then the data might get confused in going back and forth, and you might get incoherence.
    \n
    \nMy supposition is that we can create transition very rapidly by making different sub-systems, each that promote one thing or the other. Such that when you're using one system, you're obviously playing tactically, and when using the other, you're obviously playing thematically. This is actually almost trivial to accomplish. The real trick is doing so in a way that links the two systems together so that the effects of one flow into the effects of the other, without the one agenda also leaking into the other. Because linking them is how you get the actions to each seem more "universal."
    \n
    \nBut can this be done? Without leakage that will create a slippery slope that likely ends up in the whole thing being gamism?

    \n The general notion I've got is to have scenes be segregated into either dramatic or tactical. Or to, again, have subsystems that are engagable by players, each of which is suitable for only one agenda.
    \n
    \nA basic problem at this point is that I generally want to allow player freedom in terms of which agenda they're engaging. But the problem is that I have a feeling that this will cause many players to just use one or the other. This is also why I generally think that the one system should flow into the other, linking them, so that one can't get away with only playing one system or the other. This is, however, where the "leakage" may occur as well.
    \n
    \nLet's say we have system A in which I get rewards for kicking ass tactically, but they're used in system B to produce drama. System B, on the other hand, produces rewards for drama which can be used to give greater chances of success in system A. The problem is that players may start to ignore the real drama, and simply mechanically orchestrate the dramatic scenes in order to churn them out so that they get the bonuses for system A again.
    \n
    \nThe way around this is, it seems to me, is to have the rewards in system B be awarded by players for dramatically appropriate stuff. Like Fan Mail from PTA, if you will.
    \n
    \nI'll stop at this point and let folks jump in. Does this seem to make sense where I'm going? Any obvious problems? Any better solutions?

    \n Mike,

    I'm curious do you draw the same distinction between Adversity and Competition the way I do? To me Adversity is a component of the fiction and I like it to be real and threatening and driven, not fake and willing to take a dive for "the story." Competition is something that happens between players. I am actually trying to demonstrate that I play this game better than you do.

    I really, really, really dislike people who make decisions "for the story." I think it's actually antithetical to Narrativism believe it or not. I think that such decisions are actually a form of Simulationism where the pre-play aesthetic standard for the "sim" is what the story *should* look like. Instead I much prefer it when people play with unrelenting emotional commitment to their character's passions and only "quit" when something in the fiction (sometimes coupled with the state of the mechanics) genuinely undercuts that commitment.

    But playing that way is still about prioritizing the players emotions and feelings about his character his fiction. Even if he's playing the mechanics HARD to express them, and not about actually beating *ME* the other player.

    So if we're talking about my passion driving unrelenting Adversity that you're genuinely opposed to and you're genuinely fighting against then absolutely that's compatible with story creation. Sorcerer, The Riddle of Steel, In A Wicked Age... are all BASED on this. It HURTS those games to play weakly and without commitment.

    \n That's a really well explained statement of what's frustrating about a number of recent Gamist games. It's that "compete, but don't compete too hard" kind of thing.

    I think this has to do with the nature of creative agenda.

    For me, I think, your "play tactically for thematic advantage, play thematically for tactical advantage" game would not feel right, and no amount of "policing" of input is going to make it better. If you're trying to add thematic content to the game for a tactical advantage, then you're still making your decisions based on tactics. If there's some "challenge" mechanic, or if there's fan mail, or whatever, that's simply another hurdle to jump to get your tactical goal. You're thinking "how do I push the other players' buttons so they give me fan mail?" or "How do I get away with this without being challenged" rather than "what do I think about the issues this game is raising, and what do I want to say about that?" or "What would my character do here?"

    One of the many things that creative agenda is said to encompass is the choices players make at the table, and their reasons for them. I think if you're making thematic choices for tactical purposes, then you're playing Gamist.

    Perhaps a way to think about this would be like a fighting fantasy novel. Some of them have pretty interesting stories, and it's not impossible to imagine a fighting fantasy novel that adressed a premise and was a "capital S" Story, in the sense Ron uses the word. But as long as one of the endings is marked "you win" and the others are marked "you lose", the player is still playing Gamist, and not addressing premise. You can look at the fiction that was created (or discovered) through play, and think "wow, that was great", but it's not Narr play if the player made the decision they did because they were trying to get to the "you win" ending. It's only if all endings are valued equally that the player's choices have thematic meaning - when the players decide which ending has value for them. That's a thematic statement, and it's the basis of Narr play, as I understand it.

    So I think that Fan mail can't work in a competitive game. You're asking players to make decisions that are counter to their interests - to work in two mutually exclusive modes. They're competing with each other, but they're also being asked to judge their opponent's contributions, and reward them. It can only work (in my opinion) if those rewards go towards an entirely different goal, that's not tied to the competition at all. (I'm a bit of a Fan Mail hater in general though, so take this with a pinch of salt).

    I think the best play experience I've had with a game that employs some elements of competition is The Mountain Witch. You've got these characters with competing goals, and the players are motivated to get their characters what they want, but the players also have other goals - reveal their dark secrets, ensure the success of the group as a whole, and so on. These goals all conflict with each other, but in a kind of productive way, where whatever you choose, you're making a thematic statement, and advancing the game. The competition in the game isn't very strong. There's no "winner", but there's definitely a frission of competition between the players that makes the game spark. That's the closest I've come to "El Dorado".

    \n A couple of things:

    In order to articulate where I'm coming from, I need to say what I think of Sim (and by extension Nar).
    1. I think that ultimately there is nothing "good" in Sim that is incompatible with Nar play. This puts me at odds with how a lot of people view the model but it's key to my understanding of how this kind of play works. I think that a lot of what people call Nar play is at odds with The Right to Dream because of the way people approach the games--and that's what I'll talk about.

    2. In order to have an exploration heavy Nar game you need to do the majority of the work "up front." That is: before play starts. The key concept revolves around making a "fit character" to explore the premise and then playing from an immersed, actor-stance position using task resolution.

    3. In order to make a "fit character" you have to know several things:
    (a) what the premise will be--in order to know this, you have to know what the action in the game will revolve around more than a standard game might reveal--not "everything"--but enough to know what the likely conflicts will be and how you, as a player, might want to react to them.
    (b) You need to fit in with everyone else: everyone should talk about their characters before play starts and possibly have a lot of interaction between them--including front-loaded conflicts.

    4. The GM has to do a good job of making the game stable in the face of immersive play. The situation must explicitly involve premise, the characters directed at it, and the ability for them to act meaningfully on it. The play of the game must then be structured to support this.

    This is a best-practices approach: we know that the players will simply be playing their characters in an immersed state so the game needs to survive a lack of actor-stance. This means:

    (a) attention to pacing--the GM will pay special attention to pacing because bad pacing will not only kill literary merrit but will bore players and pry them out of actor-stance / immersion. In order for the group to be "living the dream" they will need to be properly paced. The situation should be designed with elements that will help pace things such as NPCs who are close to and trusted by the PCs that can generate "pacing events."

    (b) The game is hard to stall / disengage from. The situation should be one that will grip the PCs and the Players. The GM cannot count on the PCs engaging "because that's where the game is." The situation should be impossible to simply ignore.

    (c) The action should allow for character exposition. There can be lower intensity scenes that simply put the characters in a position and let them be who they are--these can't be too long or you are moving away from both tight literary structure and, likely, address of premise--but not all character development must be in the face of hard, premise-related, adversity. A few of these scenes, especially in the early parts of play, will help the players establish character in an immersive fashion.

    (d) The game should not be brittle. There are a number of flaws that can kill a game like this--such as having the villain appear in early scenes to taunt the PCs. In these cases, acting in actor stance, the PCs try to kill the antagonist (in this typical hypothetical) and the GM must deal with both structural frustrations (you don't kill the villain in Act 1) and mechanics issues (how do you engineer an escape?). It is, for example, best-practices to send a flunky of the villain to taunt the PCs and let them kill him if they can. Some thinking like this will help a lot.

    (e) Run the game from a what-would-happen perspective: the GM will be controlling pacing and such but most decisions will need to stem from a what-would-happen place rather than "what is cool" or "what makes the best story." The PCs need aid in immersing and, it is my thinking, this helps. This allows for some anti-climax but if the situation is well developed and the players understand what is going on, it should be a low-rate-of-failure.

    I submit that this model of play reliably produces what was called Vanilla Narrativist play that contains everything positive from The Right To Dream (Sim).

    \nThe important bit is where competition is relative to Exploration (as per the Big Model).
    \n

I've got a game called Super Action Now! that works that way. And when you do it this way, it's not Gamist, it's Sim because Exploration is the end-all, be-all. (Or, you can take that Exploration, with competition as the engine, and use it as an arena for addressing Premise, and you've got Narrativist play; from what I can tell, this is the way that IAWA works--although I haven't read or played it yet, so I might be mistaken)

    \n When a choice appears to be either one of two modes of play, we say that it's "conguent" with both. But what's important is that, in fact, the choice was made based on one or the other priority primarily. It might be that it was tactically the best move, and it also happens to make an interesting thematic statement, so I chose it for the first reason and the latter is a nifty side-effect. It might be that it was made because it was thematically kickass, and just happens to also be a good move. We don't know.
    \n
    \nThe big model says that Creative Agenda is not composed of individual decisions, in any case. But the overall agenda of play over an "instance" which is probably something more like a session. In point of fact, I can defect from the agenda, detectably, or undetectably, in making individual decisions, and still really not be working against the agenda. What's important to the agenda, and it being clear is what you find in terms of a "reward cycle." This may involve reward mechanics, but perhaps even more important is the social reinforcement a player gets for their acts. A player may get loads of EXP, but if other players are playing narrativism, they may call that player a powergamer, and punish him socially for his acts.
    \n
    \nIn fact game reward systems only inform and reinforce behavior, they can't make you enjoy it. They can, however, by making the agenda coherent, make it *enjoyable.* Rather, incoherence tends for many people to cause a lack of enjoyment.
    \n
    \nMe, for one.
    \n
    \nSorry for the refresher, but if we're going to use the terms, we need to be on the same sheet of music.
    \n
    \nThe point is that, at some point in play, there will tend to be friction between varying agendas. Sure, the game can try to keep the decisions such that play is always "congruent" and so you don't see the differences, thus preventing the appearance of incoherence. But this has only been done in theory to my knowledge, and even the theoretical models do it by limiting thematic choices to an extent that seems to actually be detrimental.
    \n
    \nThat is, you basically have to eliminate the option to try to make a poor tactical decision in order to make a thematic statement. You can't have the character do something tactically "stupid" to prove how valuable something is to them. Which seems to be a powerful tool.
    \n
    \nThat said, this is sorta precisely what I've been proposing, isn't it? All thematic decisions being zero sum.
    \n
    \nOften the "solution" to this in other games I've seen is for bad tactical decisions to be rewarded with some player reward. These come in various types:
    \n
    \nUse it Now Reward: Sorcerer Bonus Dice, for instance. "That's cool that you leapt into the firey pit, you get extra dice!" The problem with this method is that it means that we're not longer at all judging the tactical acumen of the player, but have gone totally over to narrativism. We're rewarding the cool, not penalizing the tactically unsound.
    \n
    \nDefered Reward: The character is at a lower chance of success for this action, but the player has a resource to call on in a later contest. Very "karma," this method. If you give now, you get later. In a Wicked Age has a great example of this in that if you choose in a contest now to use lower dice than your optimal ones (such that they're lower than your opponents'), then you get on the "Owe List" and are able to scratch your name off it later to get a bonus die. This is the ultimate example of bleed, however. That is, did I make the decision in order to be thematically interesting now? Or did I make because I know that having the potential of losing now is not as important as having the greater chance of winning later? This might be a case of "Accidental Story" where we get cool stuff as an outcome, but only because the system causes this, and not really because of player creativity, which is instead really about player challenge. Given the mechanics of IAWA, I played it Gamism style, and only tried to get on the owe list because I saw the mechanical advantage in the long run (interestingly my opponent's thwarted me in this, and I had trouble getting on the list).

    \nThis is why I want to reward players for winning in gamism with something that gives them power in the thematic realm. Because the reverse seems to automatically have the bleed problem. Which, again, begs the question as to whether or not any full cycle can avoid the bleed problem. I think it can be avoided for a very subtle reason. Paying people to fail, as a way to get them to make thematic statements doesn't work, I don't think. I've always had this problem with FATE, for instance. Instead I want to pay them for making thematically interesting moves, even if they happen to be congruent with tactically sound moves.
    \n

    \nAnother theory of how to make such a game system work is to have two systems operating independently of each other. One rewards for good tactics, the other for good theme, and the points are used to fuel entirely different systems. You can't use the rewards from the tactical system in the thematic, and (maybe more important, if you believe in the power of gamism bleed) you can't use the rewards from good thematic decisions to enhance your chances of tactical success.
    \n
    \nBut that has two problems. First, what is this other thematic system? What do you use the rewards for? Second, if, in fact tactical play tends to corrupt thematic play, and both priorities are available constantly, won't things slide to gamism? If not, then don't you have a strong case of potential incoherence in play? Perhaps not if the player simply makes it clear which reward he's trying to obtain?
    \n
    \nCall this the fast-transition method: before any conflict, the player states which sort he wants it to be, tactical or thematic. If he chooses tactical we use the tactical system and give tactical rewards. If he chooses thematic, we use a thematic system and give thematic rewards.
    \n
    \nAgain, key to this would be that the rewards for using the thematic system could never benefit the player when using the tactical system. Begging the question as to what such rewards would be (in form and function).
    \n
    \n
    \nThis is why I've sorta settled on a cycle as the viable method. In the end, the rewards do end up being tactical. But in the interim, we award them only for thematic (or other) sorts of activity.
    \n
    \nThe temptation to use the "bad tactics reward" is strong. Because it can be created objectively. In IAWA, you use your less powerful abilities, and if they're less than your opponent, you've obviously made a suitably bad tactical decision. No judgment involved. But the problem being that it's just hidden gamism in this case, a queen sacrifice to win three turns later.
    \n
    \nSo am I right in demarking this dichotomy? Is it different to reward tactically unsound play, as opposed to rewarding thematically good play, in terms of the actual agenda that's created? If, in the end, the resulting reward is more or less the same in function?

      \nOn another related subject, going with Josh's notion of complexity of goals and such, I'm thinking that players can try to angle for multiple goals. And that these can change during resolution. Again, this varies from stake-setting prior to the contest in several ways. Most notably, the player doesn't have any direct understanding of what they stand to lose, other than that it'll be appropriate to the contest at hand in some way. Even if unexpected. (I'm hoping, in fact, that the negative results of conflict end up being very surprising, in fun ways).
      \n
      \nMy current idea for resolution, then, revolves around using something not totally dissimilar to the rules for games like Puerto Rico (or the space version, even better). Basically players can use action during resolution to orient their character's actions towards achieving some goal. But they do so by building "factories" that head in that direction, and then put fuel in, to make them produce, and then these products are then traded in for the thing sought.
      \n
      \nTactics, then, involve blocking player's abilities to advance a particular goal by taking the resources that they need to either start up production towards he goal, or the initiative to convert, etc. Thus, working with the idea above, "manuevers" might be the equivalent of "factories" that take certain sorts of "effort" and convert them into certain sorts of "advantages" (products), which are then traded in for victory. Other actions might be the acts to do the conversion.
      \n
      \nThus a "feint" sets up an opponent so that you can get an advantage on him that you can then convert to damage on the opponent. Wheras the actual stab maneuver is the conversion of the position advantage to the damage.
      \n
      \nWhat's fun about this sort of thing, of course, is that it allows for the multi-variate outcomes that we want. A player may get some of what he wants out of a conflict. Or none of it, or all of it. And his opponent may get none, some or all as well, of what he's seeking to get. And you'd also get intricate detail of how the maneuvers were working against each other, as opposed to just "it worked for 5," or "it failed." Instead you get, "My counter made his feint not only fail to be productive in changing the situation to his advantage, but actually set me up to have an opportunity to lauch my own maneuver."

      Thanks for indulging my questions. Some more thoughts, hopefully not tangential:

      I'm sensing basically two, and possibly three, kinds of behaviors that can be rewarded, with numerous ways those rewards can be delivered.
