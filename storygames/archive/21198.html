\n BTW, by Story Game, I mean in the sense that Ben Robbins defines it, more info here if your interested.

In my GMless game "Story" there is the ability to have a discussion if one player believes another player has:

1. Potentially compromises the verisimilitude of the story.
2. Potential introduces anincoherent idea into the story internally inconsistent or illogical.
3. Potentially excessively violates the tone that the group agreed the story would have during the set up stage of the game, without the groups consent.
4. (I'm not sure I should include this one; I'm debating its usefulness and whether it can even be applied correctly or in a meaningful way. With that said, here it is):
Potentially makes the story threads difficult to weave back together into a coherent and satisfying ending.

The phrase I have been thinking of using when these issues arise is: "Should we go in another direction?" or "Let's discuss that," or "Let's have a discussion," or "Can we have a discussion?" or "Can we discuss that?" or simply using the ritual phrase, "Discussion." Do you think one of these phrases works best and is most polite and least likely to cause defensiveness or upset the player narrating the scene? Or do you have a suggestion for phrase you think that would work better?

I wan't to emphasize that while a phrase is used when a potential issue comes up, it doesn't mean that the issue is automatically decided by the player raising it. It means the group will have a conversation about the potential issue and decide whether it has merit and compromise to find the best solution moving forward.

It is also important to stress that you wouldn't use this phrase to address creative differences or something you just disliked in the story. Raising a potential objection would have to strictly fit the criteria, and after a group discussion, be found to actually be problematic to the story.

I know that some of you don't think interceding this way in a story game is the role of other players. However, we all must design own games and address problems we see in the GMless games we play as we see best. While individuals creative freedom is the most fundamental part of a story game, the story as a whole, and the satisfaction of other players regarding the story as a whole, is an important issue. The story as a whole effects all the players, and their creative contributions, and if one incoherent idea sends the story off the rails for some of the players, I think it is worth discussing, coming to a consensus and going in a different direction. This is a recurring issue in these type of games and it needs to be addressed. Anyway, I've said enough about that.

Any responses would be super appreciated! Thanks!

    \n I like "Try a different way", from Archipelago, if you want to give one player the "power" to ask that of another player.

    In Muse/Musette, my friend Jon instituted the "Call for a Time Out", which has been really effective. The name isn't significant, but the process is good:

    Unlike "try a different way", which prompts one player to change their input, this, instead, just signals for people to drop out of the game flow and to chat about it. We can ask clarifying questions, dig deeper, and chat about how and why we see things going in various directions. ("Why is Jane in this scene? I thought she was at the hospital?")

    95% of the time, in my experience, when something seem jarring, it's more of a communication issue than a content issue.

    In other words, they can be cleared up with a quick, "Hey, what was that all about? I don't get it." -"Oh, did that not come across? What I meant was THIS..." -"Ah! Now I see!"

    Ask a few questions, get the player to explain their thinking behind the seemingly jarring addition to the story...

    And then back to the game.

    \n In my experience the most "polite" way to go is to ignore the contributions that don't work for you, and let the other player off the hook - just go your own merry way, downplaying whatever it is that you would rather ignore. This can even be a good idea in certain rare-ish circumstances.

    The "best" way in my experience is to stop and explain why this or that isn't working for you, and why it needs to change. It's better because it improves the long-term skill and cooperative habit of everybody included, instead of trying to paper over the cracks with a quick fix. At first players may find it emotionally difficult to be put on the spot by either challenging or defending creative contributions, but it's better to undergo this mild amount of stress now and get used to it, rather than try to avoid the fact that sometimes players need to veto each other.

    Also, I agree with Paul about the nature of most creative difficulties: nine times out of ten it proves unnecessary to challenge a stupid contribution, as it proves out that it's not so much that the contribution is stupid, but rather that it was explained poorly. For this reason my single most favourite way to "veto" is by asking clarifying questions and restating the contribution in my own words:

    "Do I understand correctly that your character had been lurking there for the entire morning, so as to jump out right now?"

    "So you're saying that the prince can't marry because your character is also in love with him, but they can't quite say it out loud, right?"

    Are you sure that you're choosing a wise direction here - perhaps we shouldn't go there, considering that we have children at the table?

    Asking clarifying questions about player contributions will most of the time either explain the contribution to you in a way that makes you accept it, or the other player, when trying to answer your questions, will realize that there's a problem, and amend their own contribution. Either way, the Socratic method has saved you from having to directly challenge something that did not, after all, require a direct challenge. You can always start actually complaining when you're certain that there is a problem, and the other player is doubling down on the error, so that there's a genuine disagreement in existence.

    \n My take is that all of those "let's talk about it" just feel weird, if it's something you *know* you don't want. And, mixing the metaphors with "let's Storyboard this" makes it even weirder.

    \nIt depends on the game, but I personally would probably not put this stuff in any more central role than just a piece of advice in most games. This is because - again, for most games - I don't feel like this is a major element: most of the time you do not, as a rule, bicker with other players about veto-worthy things. The games where you do (perhaps because they're actively adversial on a storytelling level) are a different and specialized breed.

    Ritual phrases and other similar high-maintenance rules mechanics are an inappropriate tool for marginal concerns. For marginal special case situations you want to use a mechanical solution that does not require players to have freshly memorized and practiced maneuvers. The classical solution of "have a good GM who recognizes a creative difficulty and knows how to address one" is an example of a solution that passes this test: it's not incredible for a GM to cultivate this skill, and it's credible that they would be able to act successfully to address this problem.

    Of course, a game with no GM doesn't have that authority position to help in chairmanning the creative discussion, plus the players are much more often in creative interaction with each other in a game where everybody plays "towards" everybody else, rather than everybody playing towards the GM. (Hub model vs. web model of interaction there.) This means that the traditional solution becomes "you don't have a GM, but hopefully you individually as a player know how to resolve creative differences - or surely you have at least one player in there who can act as a referee between you two idiots?"

    But then, surely the game designer can do something about this - merely giving advice like "be an experienced gamer with highly developed skills, or lacking that, at least read a bunch of advice I wrote to simulate it" is a little bit weak-sauce, admittedly.

    Thinking about this, a ritual phrase could be part of an appropriate solution. I recommend having one ritual phrase for "meta-game control" purposes, though - I do not think that it is realistic game design to have multiple phrases for various types of meta-level concern, at that point you're just loading mind-space for no good reason. (Unless it's the point of the game to learn a lot of special beautiful activity, of course - like tea ceremony, for instance.) So you don't want to have a "I gotta go to the bathroom" metagame ritual phrase and a "I gotta talk about your use of rape as a narrative theme" metagame ritual phrase separately. It's enough for the players to remember one simple thing, which might be phrased strongly in this way:

    It's OK to want to stop and talk about what we're doing, because understanding each other is important. When I want to stop, reconsider and talk in awareness about what we're trying to accomplish here, I go [X].

    One phrase could have merit in this regard, simply because it might help the players shape out an attitude of open consideration and criticism. Then again, it might also cause such communication to be categorized away, made into something inappropriate for the game - this is my personal basic concern with techniques like the X card and ritual phrases that limit player communication, because using those seems to strongly imply that you're not allowed to "disrupt play" by talking about those things without activating that special game mechanic. In that regard it's better to not hide the basic attitude of clear communication behind a ritual phrasing.

    If one underwrites the idea that good communication is an ubiquitous attitude rather than a special maneuver, then perhaps one has to rule against a ritual phrase that activates a special "critical mode" where the players are allowed to air grievances.

    But then, what should one use, rules-wise? I don't have an off-the-shelf solution for a GMless game - for GMed games we know that a clearly written training manual that takes powerful stances suffices to train a GM who can perform in the appropriate way. But what can you do for a GMless game, aside from training whoever happens to read the game text to act as a quasi-GM?

    Perhaps a "credo", a text that is read at the beginning of play? I could see that working as a tool of openly establishing creative routines. Make it punchy and tell the players to read it at the beginning of every session, and I could see it do more good than the introduction of a ritual phrase that fences off and spotlights a particular type of behavior. Tell the group that they should revise the credo if they find any faults in it, and it's not even annoyingly authoritarian as an idea.

    Alternatively or in addition, how about a reverse ritual phrase? A "reverse ritual phrase" is a ritual phrase that you use to respond to an initiative, instead of making one. So in this case, instead of having the player who has a problem memorize a ritual phrase that legitimizes their complaint (as if they didn't have a legitimate right to complain otherwise), how about instead having the complaint-receiver have a mandated response? Something like "Thank you for bringing this up, I appreciate your courage in talking about it, and it is good to the game and appropriate play content for us to spend a moment talking about it."

    Reversing the ritual phrase requirement has a couple of advantages to my eye:
    * It's policeable: the other players can remind the target of the critique about how they should respond before continuing on.
    * It's didactic: the ritual phrase itself reminds the player about the appropriate attitude, instead of merely being a formalized polite phrase.
    * It puts pressure where it belongs: instead of requiring the player with a possibly difficult social initiative to remember and perform the appropriate ritual to avoid backlash, it forces the player who might otherwise answer harshly (or otherwise in a bad way) to reply with a formula of acknowledgement.\n

    If your game has a premise, metaphor, or central conceit, you can use that to reinforce the intended aim of the phrase/move/priority. What are you trying to achieve, as a group, and how might the intended metaphor or conceit benefit from that?

    \n Jeff,

    There is no obvious way (at least in my limited imagination) to use a framing conceit in a totally generic story game. You're back to ritual phrases and that kind of thing.

    However, I don't think it would be impossible to introduce a conceit without limiting the genre and tone of the game.

    These aren't great examples; just off the top of my head:

    Imaginary Audience

    When you play the game, you create a story for a group of fans/audience. Maybe they're populations - "the fans", "the critics", that kind of thing - or maybe they're specific individuals - Lucy, Dan, Rafael, Mika. Maybe they're Greek muses or Gods.

    When you set up the game, you define what they care about together. "The critics are really concerned with consistency of tone." "Rafael loves stories that are funny, and can't deal with serious material." "The fans are obsessed with plot continuity, and they demand that everything be realistic." "Aphrodite won't be appeased unless someone falls in love." "The panel will never approve our script unless it has gender parity."

    Or whatever criteria you want for that particular game and that particular story.

    It makes it easier for people to say to each other, "Hey, that's a cool idea, but the critics/Aphrodite/the panel aren't going to like it. Can we fix that?"

    "This is supposed to be a romantic comedy, right? If it stays this grim, it will be reclassified by the censors as an R-rated horror film. Can we turn that around in the next scene?"

    Mechanical Contingencies

    If you have any kind of resources or tokens or pools, you can assign them to negotiated "pillars" for your particular story. Maybe "Dark and desperate" is one pillar, and "moral ambiguity" is another. If there are mechanics in your game which could be associated with these (even very loosely, or even if the mechanics aren't terribly important), that forces people to think about these "pillars" on a regular basis.

    (If it's really a generic game, you'd set these up as a group when you start playing.)

    Terrible example:

    At the end of each scene, the group collects one token for each pillar which was supported/respected in that scene.

    As a group you spend the tokens for stuff like:

    * Reveal the secret behind an important mystery
    * Trigger the endgame
    * Assign players roles for the epilogue

    Or whatever other stupid thing which you look forward to completing. If it was Fiasco, you'd trigger the Tilt at 20 tokens and then the Aftermath at 40 tokens; that kind of thing.

    This forces everyone to be thinking about these "pillars" all the time, or at least most of the time, and makes them feel like they're working together to support them.

    Again, these are horrible ideas, because I haven't thought them through, but maybe they will inspire you with something better!

      \nYeah, a playset - or more generally, a scenario - is definitely a way to go in getting players to the same page creatively. There's this whole range of generic, punchy story games that succeed admirably by leading with a solid scenario that everybody relates to when making their first moves.

      Universalis, Microscope and similar games are what you get when you don't have a solid starting point: extremely exploratory play to begin with, and much of the skill is in actually getting the game to cohere towards a singular vision. There's usually a discussion stage at the start, but the set-up is never truly finished by that.

      Consider how Tales of Entropy does this: it's a very generic story game, but every specific time it's played the players choose a scenario with its own color and stylistic constraints. They do this by voting between the scenarios: every player gets to mark down either a plus or a minus next to each scenario's name, and the scenario that doesn't have any minuses but has the most pluses gets chosen. This is fundamentally the same as the Uni-style "content discussion" phase, except instead of trying to phrase their preferences the players vote on pre-packaged ideas.

      And when the scenario gets chosen, everybody sits down to listen as one of the players reads up some pretty prose about the premise. That's not only an opportunity for the scenario-author to provide some intellectually interesting starter ideas, but also a shared aesthetic homebase in the context of which the players formulate their own ideas. It's generally a given that a group starting from a kernel of this sort will be much more coordinated from the beginning than one that starts with a truly empty board.

      \n I favor experiential over set-up procedures for tone-establishing. Don't just talk about it and write it down; carve out some play time to walk through it. Play out some scenes with no stakes (e.g. "get to know this character") and apply your tone guidelines/mechanics to those.

      \n Try another way, has already been mentioned. That one work great, when I played it (as well as "Describe more in detail").

      You can also use "Yes, but" to limit what someone has just said. If you want to make it more structured, create two roles. The head and the but. The head is on your left side, and the but on the right. You follow the head by building on what that person says, by saying "Yes, and", and you restrict the but by saying "Yes, but" and then makes up a complication or states something that limits what that participant just said.

      A third option would be to set up a framework for what's not allowed, where the game has already state a few points, like "abandon the setting". The film-noir game Nerver av St\xe5l (Nerves of Steel) does this by setting up a framework for what wasn't allowed due to censorship during the 1930s era. You were allowed to curse, or show people having out of marriage sex. This created workarounds ("darn it" - or having two people putting on the last clothes in a bedroom).

      Whenever someone oversteps this framework - anyone is allowed to stop the session and point it out. If this is part of the rules, then people will be more accepting of this happening.
