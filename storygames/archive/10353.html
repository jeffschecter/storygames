\n In the 1000 sales thread, Jeremy asked:

Posted By: Gregor Hutton[...] and DesignMatters has a strict "no demos" booth policy at GenCon (I like to mischievously call it an "AntiRob" policy).

That seems like an odd policy. What's the thought process behind that if you don't mind me asking?

Kevin and I observed, over our couple of years at the Forge Booth, that demo's didn't really sell our games. Or, to be more precise, the number of games sold on a given day didn't really seem to sync up with demos given. Also, while actually conducting demo's is fun, both of us find the process of getting people to sit down to demo painful and awkward.

Design Matters is about creating an atmosphere that attracts customers that we think will enjoy our games. A demo table is chaotic and messy, and without having to (a) ensure everyone is able to potentially demo every game and (b) have space somewhere in the booth to store demo kits and materials, we avoid a lot of headaches.

So that's our reasoning, and it works for us! I'm not saying "don't demo at Gen Con", by any means. But it's like a game design, y'know - who's your audience, how will demos or lack thereof change their experience, it is in pursuit of your goals, etc.

Oh, I'm also not saying that actual play doesn't sell games. It absolutely does (see Harper for 3:16, Adam Dray for Annalise, etc). But, in the vast majority of cases, 10-minute convention demos are not actual play.

So....there's that. I'm happy to discuss!

    \n I agree with Nathan P, at least for our games.

    At GenCon we run 40 minute demos for 5-6 people. Getting people to sign up is the biggest headache, and we get some no-shows. In a change to our original strategy of concentrating entirely on getting new customers, we also encouraged people who have bought the books but haven't played yet, or those familiar with the system who want to try actual play with an experienced GM. It really gets them primed to play with their group. You could maybe give a flavour of a GUMSHOE game in 15 minutes, but I'm really not sure. Our ideal would be to run a master-class style game for multiple GMs.

    That said, demo space in the hall is fantastically expensive, and we'll make more effort to get games in the program book.

    This year, though, it was no longer a matter of introducing GUMSHOE, though we were still pitching settings. For Mutant City Blues we had no demos, but with one exception, every person who saw the Quade Diagram bought MCB.

    \n I was really confused by the whole demo thing the year It's Complicated came out. I didn't think a ten-minute demo could accurately reflect the fun of the way that the engine builds weird, three-dimensional characters throughout the course of play, which I think is one of the major selling points of the game. PlayCollective was the anti-Design-Matters in that no, you had to have a demo, no matter what. Tony LB, who is the demo KING, helped me design a cute little demo of IC that, while it didn't follow the rules exactly, definitely helped carry the spirit of the game to potential buyers. (That was another point of confusion for me: the demoed rules don't have to be the same? Won't that confuse the players when they sat down to play the game for real?) I had a lot of people buy the game when I wasn't at the booth and then come back for demos. That also really confused me-- wait, demoing is a sales tool, and you want me to run a ten-minute example of play that isn't even exactly what you bought? Why? I was happy to, but there was always this niggling feeling in the back of my head that selling my game to people who had already bought it was not the best use of my energy.

    I asked people about that, too. "Oh, some people just want to play the new games with the designers."
    "If they said something, I'd run it after the hall closed," I replied.
    "Oh, well, people tend to have full plates after hours, they might not want to play it then," was the response I got.

    That's when I got it. Oh. Demos are a really, really shitty replacement for actual play. Some people like that can say they played a game without actually having to play it. Even if what they played for ten minutes had completely different procedures.

    I don't know. I really enjoyed having the full ten minutes to goof off with new friends and sell them on my game, but the physical demoing of the game itself is something I really regret. I think it's probably one of the reasons IC showed up so much in that "What games have you bought and keep meaning to play but don't" thread that happened a while ago.

    I should have found the people who actually wanted to play my game and actually run it for them, and just glad-handed everyone else.

    (Note to people I demoed for: I am glad I hung out with you! I do not begrudge you your demo requests! I just wish I'd known a more effective way to spend that time with you.)

    \n I suppose you need to know where your game delivers its WOW. If you can't get there in a demo, you may actually be sabotaging sales rather than generating them.

    Reign, for example, can deliver WOW in 15 minutes: random character in 5 that is awesome and a miniature-free combat chase scene in ten that is fun and rich and exciting. I'd demo this game in a heartbeat.

    Diaspora, however, I wouldn't even run a one-shot. It's a multi-session game before the WOW. I might run a single-session mini-game though -- say a space combat chase scene or a boarding action gunfight. I think you could get to WOW. Running a cluster creation and character gen session can probably also get there. But none of these are hitting the moment before minute 40, so I wouldn't demo it.

    If your game has a structure that delivers its awesome through completion, a demo sounds risky.

    \n I've worked The Forge booth once (in '06, which was definitely its hayday) and Design Matters once (this year).

    The Forge booth was really fun, but also really chaotic and tiring. In order to have adequate staffing to be running demo's plus telling people about games plus actually ringing in customers, you needed lots of booth people. This created the "game designer wall": just across from the booth hovered like 20 designers at any given time. Some talking, some hawking, but all of them gathered in this big storm cloud.

    When I showed up to The Forge booth, I knew I had to demo my own game, I knew I should learn how to demo others' games, and I knew it would be a bit action-packed.


    Design Matters staffs two people at a given time. We don't run demos or try to rope people into the booth. If you're browsing already, we'll ask how you're doing. If you've got a game in hand, we'll see if you want to talk about it. No hard selling, no pressure. Coupling this with the coffee table & chairs, the menu and the simple design... I think we really created a kind space where people felt at ease.

    Different people took that "no hard selling" motto differently. Gregor, for example, would conversationally share his favourite things about a given game, when someone was clearly interested in it. Eppy would often explain something procedural or structural that he thought demonstrated play well. Nathan would do nothing more than say hello, up until the point when they actually asked him specific questions.

    I liked both experiences. Design Matters left me energized and feeling good, left me feeling like every sale was the right sale.

    \n Think of it this way. If you've got 15 minutes to explain your game, are you better off talking about it or demoing it? The answer will vary from game to game. For Mortal Coil, I imagine, talking would be more effective, for Zombie Cinema, demoing. It takes very little time to explain the Mortal Coil world creation method, but a great deal of time to demo it - particularly as a lot of it is brainstorming. That said, there is something special about a demo with the game's designer which is more effective than just a chat.

    Looking realistically at our customers at GenCon, though, I'd divide them up into five categories:

    1. People who knew what they wanted already, having seen the game online, and are frequently existing customers.
    2. People who pick the books up, read the blurb and buy.
    3. People we talk about the game to at the stand. It particularly helps if this is the designer.
    4. People who are demo'ed to, generally after a pitch .
    5. People who buy as a result of their friends pitching them the game at the stand. This one is particularly gratifying.

    For a new game, 1 and 5 are non-existent. This year, I think pitch sales outweighed demo sales by a large margin. But, demos aren't just to sell the game; they are to teach the game and spread stories of fun actual play. Playing Ganakagok, for example, has made me an advocate for it, and a willing player, wherease otherwise I don't know if we'd have got round to it.

    A really on-the-ball company would mark each sale according to category.

    Another thread might be, how would you demo this apparently difficult to demo game?

    \n So here's ANOTHER wrinkle - the short (10-15 minute booth demo) has a really specific historical generation. My understanding of the whole thing is that the folks from the Forge looked around Gen Con in, what, 2003-2004ish, and saw that the booths that really attracted attention were the board and card game booths where they were providing said short demos. Ron et. al. said "well, why can't you do a 10 minute RPG demo?". So they did. And it was new and exciting! People were, like, holy crap, I can get a demo of an RPG in 10 minutes? With the designer? WHOA! And it was good.

    Fast forward to, oh, 2007 (which was the second year of my Forge booth attendance, and by then it was the last year of the combined Forge/IPR booth). Everyone going to the booth is required to have a short demo prepared. People for whom the Forge booth is a destination are expecting to received demos of the new games, or old games that they're thinking about getting. In my experience, there were many, many cases where potential customers were expecting to receive a demo simply as part of the booth experience - the demo was one component of their purchasing pattern, but it wasn't the blow-your-socks-off holy crap kind of thing it was when it was new. That is, the "demos-will-sell-your-game" model didn't seem to be as strong as it once had. ALSO, people were coming to the booth having already purchased the game, and wanted to demo so they could learn more about it, or say they played with the designer, or whatever.

    So here's ANOTHER wrinkle - the short (10-15 minute booth demo) has a really specific historical generation. My understanding of the whole thing is that the folks from the Forge looked around Gen Con in, what, 2003-2004ish, and saw that the booths that really attracted attention were the board and card game booths where they were providing said short demos. Ron et. al. said "well, why can't you do a 10 minute RPG demo?". So they did. And it was new and exciting! People were, like, holy crap, I can get a demo of an RPG in 10 minutes? With the designer? WHOA! And it was good.
    \n
    \n
    \nActually, I don't think that's quite the correct chain of events. The short board and card game demos were pointed at after the fact as a confirmation of the idea not as the catalyst for the idea.
    \n
    \nThe catalyst for the idea was much simpler.
    \n
    \nIn the early days demos sold. They sold because The Forge had no Rep. It had yet to become a GenCon Destination Booth. The designers had no Rep. No one came to GenCon with a plan to go to the Forge Booth and buy one of everything. The game were completely outside the norm. So the original demos served several purposes, among them: 1) Proof that this actually a real game you can play. 2) Providing a relaxed atmosphere where folks could take a load off for a while as long as they agreed to sit through a 30-45 minute sales pitch for a game they otherwise didn't know existed. 3) ensuring there was a crowd of people at the booth engaged in an activity distinctly different from what was going on in other booths and thus creating a level of interest to attract the curious. The crowds of people sitting around tables playing games was like having a carnival barker shouting out "come one come all and see something you've never seen before".
    \n
    \nThe "problem" arose when the 30-45 minute demos began creeping to 60-90minute demos, and in a couple of cases...pretty much complete games were being played. That monopolized table space, reduced traffic volume, and led to a lower sales per unit of effort rate. That's what spawned the desire to require demos to be kept to a time limit.
    \n
    \nThe 15 minute time limit was one of those brainstorm ideas that became a meme that became enshrined as booth policy. I believe it came about at the same time as promoting the Elevator Pitch and occupied that same brainspace of short, punchy, to the point, attention grabbing and then move on.
