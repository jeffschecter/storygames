\n I'm 5 sessions into DMing a 5e campaign and while things are still far from going south, I feel there's still space for improvement.

But there's a few catches:
-I'm trying to do prep with less effort and still end up with interesting stuff
-I prefer to impro as much as I can and do zero railroading
-I can't spend more than half a minute searching and rolling on random tables or looking up for stuff

What I've so far in my bag of tricks are:
-My trusty old Resourceful deck
-a Tarokka deck
-another deck for making traps (I'm sloooowly trying to bring all these into a single deck, but it's gonna take time)
-Prep based on consequences of whatever the players did on previous sessions, though some of this stuff can't be brought up on the next session and must rest until it's logical for it to pop up.
-We're using taverns as an excuse for players to brainstorm and choose their next quests. I give them a couple of random words their characters overheard and each player comes up with a rumor. Then they choose one and start making questions about it. I impro most of the answers, if there isn't already something in my prep I can use.
-I'm using a few hexcrawler guidelines so players spend part of the session travelling to the place of the quest. We often end the session when they have just arrived, which gives me time to prep the actual place.

So far I can impro places, NPCs and dungeons on the spot quite nicely, but monsters and encounters pose an entirely different problem. It isn't enough for me to pull a few monster cards with stats and calculate the encounter. I've got to come up with a story for the encounter: a reason these things are here and will threat the party in a way that makes them react (I'm not handling XP at all in this campaign) From that I infer what traces the party finds in their way to the encounter that could either pick their curiosity, give them a hint of how hard the encounter could be or even what kind of stuff they should prepare in advance.

Then I've got to design the encounter: play with the variables as visibility, terrain, hazards, obstacles, things that can be used against the monsters or things that could make the encounter worse, time constraints and monster equipment if applicable. Then comes treasure, I've got to include there something interesting but balanced.

And along all this process I've got to hit a few player flags. Maybe the treasure includes a weapon that gives the fighter an interesting new tactic, or it's a new spell for the cleric. Maybe there's something that brings up something in the rogue's background. Maybe the place of the encounter is important for the cleric. At least I made all the backstory from things that the players created themselves, either on the setting brainstorming session or on their character sheet, so I'm hitting flags left and right most of the time.

I'm leaving the encounter calculations and monster lists to a couple of online random encounter generators, and then prepping the rest myself. It's not as fast as I'd like but it yields good enough results. I started using Evernote to help me with this but it isn't as good as I expected, with the 60mb upload limit and the fact that on the table I'm using a tablet and not a laptop to visualize the files. However, the model to organize the info stuck with me and I'm trying using it on googledocs next session.

So, any tricks to handle encounter prep? Anything else you have found to be quite useful when doing prep?

    \n WarriorMonk,

    It sounds like you're giving yourself very little to work (many limitations), and then saddling yourself with incredibly steep expectations.

    For instance, why is it necessary to make "something interesting but balanced"? Why is it necessary to hit player flags with treasure? Why do you have to have backstories for monsters in place? Etc.

    Each of these is entirely optional. In fact, my favourite games of D&D didn't involve any of those three things, leaving alone your other various requirements.

    For example, many D&D campaigns simply feature a fictional explanation for the existence of monsters. ("Twenty years ago, a magical cataclysm opened rifts around the world. Before they closed, thousands of monstrous and alien beings came through them. Now, those who remain wander the landscape or hide in abandoned caves. All are hungry and fierce.")

    You could also move a lot of the onus for this stuff onto the players. Hitting flags with treasure? Have each player come up with a fun item for another character to find. ("Hey, Jim, send me an e-mail: what do you think Alice's character would love to find in a dungeon sometime?") Then, in secret, roll a die for each one, and on a bad roll modify it somehow - add a complication, a curse, or change its parameters. ("Yes, you find a magical item that allows you to teleport. However, it requires human sacrifice to operate/it's so big you would need some kind of vehicle or many, many beasts to move it.")

    Sounds like fun to me!

    \n Money, fame, leveling up and getting better stuff, of course. They came up with a couple interesting features in their backgrounds that Im exploiting and since they made the world, whatever that targets their specie's culture is also game.

    I've never prepped anything considering distances, though we use a grid that now I've covered with transparent film to draw on it with markers. I always improvise whatever location features are needed, and lay bottle caps to signal bushes, trees, columns or whatever that could count as cover. This alone often ends up making the location quite interesting in tactical terms, at no extra prep cost.

    My prep for an encounter now looks like this:

    -On the way: you see a tenuous red intermittent aura from far away, as you get close it looks like a mass of dancing red lights, accompanied by a low humming sound.
    -Four columns of dirt/mud stand surrounding the lights, amidst a few trees and bushes -it's a colony of giant fire beetles.
    -In the middle, old and half buried, lie the remains of a humanoid creature.
    -There seems to be someone sitting with their back against one of the columns, though you can't see much from where you are.

    Then comes the giant beetle stats, a pic I found on the web and a similar amount of lines explaining things about the two corpses and their belongings, where the rest of the interesting stuff is. And that's it. The bushes and trees around the colony present the tactical opportunity, whict I'll randomly position when the encounter starts, so no further prep there.

    \n With the whole distances and planning thing, just grab an old module and for your own purposes, make little ticks on the GM map based on Turn Movement/exploration rate for different categories to get an idea how it all comes together OSR dungeoncrawl style.

    If you really want to get in depth, draw little circles for light at each mark to roughly represent a torch or something in the middle of the group.

    Then, to get really, really in depth, do the same thing again, assuming they are sending out lightly armored pickets/scouts at each stop.

    ...And then, getting even deeperer, roll some encounter distances for random blips of wandering monsters from those pickets/scouts.

    Once you've done all of that, go to the combat round sequence and see how that all interacts with ranged attacks, initiative, light sources, infravision, and tactical (round) movement rates.

    You don't need to spring any of that on your players. Just do it for yourself. Heck, you can practice just with assuming the center of the party is at a T-intersection, and each of the corridors stretches out to an unlimited distance in a straight line.

    It's enlightening for understanding how some of those old games were different, even Basic D&D, if played RAW accounting for that stuff.

    (Here's a hint: Pickets should really practice running unless they're very confident in their Hide in Shadows roll)

    \n Well I can always try to test whatever I make before playing it. I think now that the general idea that works best for me isn't to pre-conceive an scenary for the players to solve in a specific way, but, instead go with this procedure:

    -Choose a theme to define and color the whole encounter
    -Link two or three obstacles to a clear goal.
    -Then make use of whatever happens or gets rolled on the table to complicate the situation or make it easier, either right away or on future sessions
