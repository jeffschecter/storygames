    \n I tried the outline for years, because that is what the books told me I should do. It felt like work, so I ended up not enjoying it and losing interest. I have SO many unfinished novels as a result of attempting to use the structures and formulas I read about in all those writing books.

    What worked for me was just writing without any preconceived notion of how things were supposed to turn out. When I did that, I was a writing monster.

    I can't tell you guys how much reading and playing story games (Primetime Adventures and Sorcerer, especially) have helped me with my writing and confidence as a writer.

    \n\nAccountingForTaste \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nOctober 2009 \n edited October 2009
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Yeah, the whole point of NaNoWriMo is to write. Not to write well, not to write something worth reading, but just to spend a month on a schedule putting 50,000 words down. If you ever find yourself hitting backspace or delete, you're doing it wrong. If you ever find yourself thinking "I don't know where to go from here, nothing seems to make sense," you're doing it wrong. Just go with whatever you've got, don't worry about how much it sucks (trust me, it's not alone in sucking), and you'll hit 50,000 with no problem.

    More importantly, you'll start to learn to not be afraid of writing crap. You can edit crap and make it better (when it's not NaNoWriMo); you can't edit a blank page at all.

    Totally invigorating and re-focusing? Yeah!
