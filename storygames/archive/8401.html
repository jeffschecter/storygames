\n I had a conversation with Kamil on Somalian Children* before Xmas, and found it to touch upon some of my own late designs, in particular Raven.

After posting RAVEN - claw and beak here, experiencing the usual comments, I started thinking about game-texts as an "independent" way of expressing something. "Independent" in the meaning: not dependent on actually being played, but written to be read as a possible game. As such the game text may be considered a kind of literature which is harboring some extra connotations.

I find that idea intriguing.
__________________________________

* Somalian Children
-the rules:\n
Posted By: trzy worki cementuGame is divided in turns in which one of the players creates the somalian kid and resolves his/her future.
Player needs to choose name, create thing the kid loves and hope the kid wants to achieve.
Now it's to roll 1d10 and checking with table below:
1 - 3 kid is forced to join children's army and is raped/drugged into submission
4 - 6 kid is forced to prostitute for food and catches HIV
7 - 9 kid dies (choose the way - hunger, bullet, etc.)
10 - roll again and check the the table. If player rolls triple 10 child's story catches interest of foreign journalist or international movie star and is free to fullfil its love and dream.

Roleplay the scene with other players. The acting player gets a role of a child while other players play npcs. If you roll some 10s but didn't reach the triple 10 result incorporate glimmer of hope and survival (which fades away and leaves kid to its own sad fate) into the scene.

In a next turn another player creates another somalian child.

In short: yes, it's a joke.
____________________________________
Please note:
- this is not to start another discussion on Raven or Somalian as such, but to discuss the idea of writing game-texts as a purely literary genre. After posting Raven I had an idea for a book solely consisting of "games" that are meant to touch the readers as a text (in any way you may perceive), but not necessarily played by them. For my own part this kind of writing is interesting in regards to political, social and psychological themes.

    \n Yeah, we have a name for this phenomenon - they've been called "concept games", usually with at least a bit of derision. The idea is that your text communicates something different simply by presenting the challenge of playing the game as a perspective for the reader to consider, even when in reality the author is delivering his actual message in the act of reading, not the playing (which usually does not come). It's a theoretically valid literary genre, although one with a horribly narrow audience. Like writing limericks about particle physics. And I've yet to see anything written in this form that'd be both respectful of the audience and not better expressed as an essay.

    \n For what it's worth, I find Somalian Children a pretty positive example of a concept game. It's not too long, and it has a clear message about something else than narrow-minded rpg theory (the favourite topic of concept game writing) that can be appreciated without commitment to some rpg discussion. The literary strength here comes from the simple fact that the game can be interpreted as a concretization of the odds of living in Somaliland - getting to be happy is like rolling three tens in a row. Punchy and simple.

    Of course, it's still an over-complex vignette presented in an odd context. I'd rework this sort of thing into a simple newspaper opinion column - the key message could be condenced into one sentence, something like "The great majority of children in Somaliland will meet an ugly fate, I find it a quite awful place." Formulated like this the piece would be understandable to others aside from roleplayers. A tricky thing, to try to write a concept game that actually is worth the trouble.

    \n Interesting. I sort of backed into something similar with a game I was working on a while back called Nameless/Faceless: Rememberance Day.

    I didn't actually set out for it to be a joke or for it to be meaningful exactly. It was more meant as an excersise to teach players not used to using miniatures in their games to treat them as inspriational art for character and story development.

    Anyway, the game itself was pretty simple. Using some WW1 plastic army men, the player advanced 3 man "squads" across a board representing No Mans Land, with the goal being to get a certain number in to the enemy trenches by the end of the game. As players moved their squads into the various spaces, they'd flip a card with text on it. The text would call for the player to provide some kind of perosnal info about one of the miniatures in the squad, and then would give some kind of instructions to follow once that info had been provided ( generally representing some kind of battlefield event).

    The personal info tended to be something about the character's pre-war life or family or connection or childhood event.

    Anyway, given the subject matter, the various characters had about the same chances of survival as the Somali Kids, and the goal line of enemy trenches was mostly an "off-switch" for ending play.

    I did try an initial form of the game with Caesar_X, and it turned out fairly poorly, so I went back and created a one-side only version of the game, which also had players basing the names of the minis on people they know or had known.

    Now I kinda want to pull it back out and take a second look at it. The expiriment wasn't intended to be a solely teaching game; I'd also thought about making a few rough copies and mailing them to people who wanted to try it out ( about a half dozen sets, since that matched the amount of extra WW1 toy soldiers I had lying around).

    \n It should also be noted that the most meaningful concept games have been the ones that have dealt with roleplaying as their message, simply because that way you have a 100% overlap between the people literate in your form and your target audience. Perhaps that's why the typical concept game concerns itself with roleplaying, specifically.

    Also, it seems to me that there is no appreciable difference between a "concept game" and a "game I don't want to play", except for the intentions of the author. Something like RaHoWa (or any other love-to-hate darling of the Internet rpg culture, really) is clearly only consumed as a concept game by the overwhelming majority of the audience, regardless of how the designer planned his game to be enjoyed. This also hints at the negative impression we tend to have of concept games - if you're going to make a game that is not intended for play, then you're probably making a game I'm not interested in playing, and why would I care of your shitty game? It's always annoying and even painful to read some shit that's not playable, and this is ten-fold so when the text hints at or even outright states how the writer is not really serious about making a game, but is just jerking your strings in a manipulative attempt to make a point.

    Difficult form, all told. Might be an interesting challenge, and certainly that Somalian Children game is annoying me less than the usual wankery. Could be still improved by removing that joke explanation, proper captioning and whatnot. I'd also rename the game into something that challenges the reader and frames the concept nature more clearly, like "Want to know what life's like in Somaliland?"

    \n Well, I'm just musing on games that I and others have categorized as concept games before. If a game is playable and clearly makes you want to play it, then it's not really a concept game anymore - it might be the sort of dysfunctional game that they wrote during the '90s, full of interesting fiction and crappy mechanics, but it's still a game that transmits to the reader a clear and genuine impetus for play. So limiting myself to just games where I'm genuinely left to suspect whether the intent of the designer was to create a playable game at all (that is, a concept game), those were my thoughts on them. It wouldn't cross my mind to categorize a game that I personally felt playable and interesting to play as a concept game.

    However, I do agree that this notion of defining concept games based on whether they're playable or whether I want to play them is incomplete. "Playable" is a traitorous concept here, as something like Somalian Children is clearly able to be played - what makes it a concept game is that the reader can easily imagine how the game'd go and thus has no need to play it, just like you said. But then, what of somebody who finds the concept interesting and can't quite figure out the whole game without playing it? The whole notion of games-to-be-read is highly subjective, as what is obviously not playable or interesting to one might well be for somebody else. In this way it'd be natural to only call games where the designer intended the game to be merely read "concept games".

    Thinking further on the idea of games intended for mere reading, I have to say that the concept games are not the full truth here, or at least the way the term is used normally is somewhat misleading. For instance, I myself have written at times quite many games that were intended only for reading, but I don't think of these as "concept games". For example, let's see if I can find it... ah, yes: Werewolf: BUTF- was something I wrote in passing a couple of years ago to roughly sketch out how I'd go about hacking Polaris into a WoD game. This sort of thing happens relatively lot in this culture - or at least, I tend to do this sort of spontaneous sketching. And the thing is, these "games" are most of the time not intended to be seriously played, at least not in the exact form they're offered - they're just sketches to demonstrate something to somebody else. So are these the concept games I have a distaste for? At least there is the difference that a sketch like that tries to be playable (in the wide sense of being meaningful to play, not the narrow sense we discussed above), even if it's not intended to be played.

    So thinking of games that have been written to be read rather than played, we have several categories that are pretty different:\n
      \n
    • Concept games: games written to prove a point. Usually not playable, or at least not enticing.
      • \n
    • Supplement treadmill games: written as prose for collectors of games. Playable, but not the main purpose.
      • \n
    • Sketch games: written for other designers as part of design dialogue. Borderline-playable, but again not the main purpose. Interesting how I have a strong gut feeling that these are somehow different from the concept game category - I get offended by the concept game's unplayability for some reason; perhaps it's important to me that the sketch game is still pointed firmly towards play, even if it's not written to be immediately utilized for such.
      • \n
    • Awful games: games like FATAL or such might well slide into the category of written-to-be-read if the designer is at all aware of the unlikeliness of the game being played. I cite Sorensen's The Farm here - he must have been aware that the game would be enjoyed primarily as reading material when he wrote it. The game is fully functional and sort of enticing to play, but most probably wouldn't.
      • \n
    \nSo yes, there are quite many reasons for writing a game for reading, after all, now that I think of it. Concept games are not the whole truth.

    Any game may be read as a literary text, so it has inherent in it some literary quality. What I am proposing (and to some extent: doing) is writing and reading your games as literature, and look for the special literary qualities of this genre. A obvious way further from this viewpoint, is to experiment with games as solely works of literature, confining your intention with them to that of how they reads.

    I consider the possibility for actual gameplay as an important feature in such a text.

    Posted By: TomasHVMRobert: your game sounds interesting to me. How far does it go in feeding the players with realistic elements pertaining to WWI soldiers; their trench-traumas, comradeships and family-backgrounds? Did you research this theme while designing the game?
    \nI didn't do specific research for it. rather it was an impression based on both fiction and non-fiction I'd read. there exists a style of "war" book that tends to follow soldiers of a given unit through some campaign or battle. Band of Brothers was a recent example, but there are plenty covering different periods. My own choice for WW1 was related to the materials I had on hand, plus a general impression of WW1 as very much a "nameless, faceless" war, especially as relates to the big positional assaults that are so archetypical.

    having said that, I geared the cards to stuff I thought was either in-period or nearly universal sorts of experieneces ( which was important to the training experiment). Certainly, Britain's Buddy Brigades from the First World War were an inspiration for the mechanics. Briefly, there was a period when the government there had encouraged organizations like school graduation classes and football clubs to enlist as a group, promising them that they'd all serve together.

    Which they did. I think you can guess at some of the results, especially at places like the Somme assaults.

    OK, one thing RPG manuals tend to have is a structure which is influenced by the procedural nature of play: when do you do what and why and what's next? Other than technical manuals (in all their forms, from "How To" books to IT manuals), no other "genre" of writing does that. What does that buy us? I'd say it brings a sort of formalism and pacing: aside from authorial voice and varied diction, they are going to give a sort of "march" feel to the work. Maybe even meditative, as the pace is felt and matched by the reader.

    They also tend to present information in referential manners, be they summaries of procedures, or just your typical charts and graphs of laundry lists of shit. This referential format strips out every nuance, dictional curlicue, and "voice" to present the bare facts. In that way, they can be like the "HALT!" shouted by a drill sergeant, to continue to (ab)use my marching metaphor--the cadence breaks as we rattle off a list of terms or numbers or both, like presenting arms. Compare that to, say, those statistics list one reads that convey a message, e.g. (stats not real, but close):
    * Billions spent on heart disease research in 2007: 45
    * Number of American death from heart disease in 2007: 500,000
    * Billions spent fighting terrorism in 2007: 300
    * Number of American deaths from terrorism in 2007: 16

    The point is made crystal clear (above: our government spending priorities are FUCKED), but with nary a jot of expression or style. Editorialized by the timing and choice of what is listed, not by the tone or mood conveyed in the writing of the list.

    Baseline writing and what's left unsaid
    So I'm going to write a "LitRPG" (I need a shorthand). I can approach it like Somalian Children, with an essentially neutral tone--no rants, reads like a tech manual--and let its very starkness carry my meaning. Here's a chart, roll your d10s, consult the chart to see your fate. BUT, if you know anything about math, you see that you've got a 1:1000 chance of surviving--that's not said in the text, that's left for you to realize. And the realization of the unsaid carries the message and theme and impact. Now you can re-read and the whole tone is changed; the cynicism just drips off the page. HOW? The text hasn't changed. The tone is still there, sill consistent and neutral. But now, having "got it," you can imagine the author staring balefully at you, accusingly, his voice so flat he sounds like the dead. Becasue isn't that the REAL point: what have YOU done to help these poor children? Isn't that the takeaway message, the unsaid?

    Projecting attitude
    That neutral tone, however, needn't be the whole bag... in fact, more and more "RPG texts" are conveying a strong authorial diction and style, moving away from (and even mocking) the neutral tone of a tech manual. So our LitRPG can take that tack, and present a seeming "game," but with an editorialized voice that shows it's clearly not meant to be played and, rather, is meant to carry a message or cause a change of thought. I'll bring up HoL, here, as an easy and obvious example of this (IMO). Yeah, sure, the game is somewhat playable, with a lot of rule repair and addition (or a freeform-loving play group), but it's REALLY suppose to be a screed. It's a punk zine disguised as a game which (it seems) takes the piss out of all the "structure" of gaming--could they be one of the first "system doesn't matter" writers? Are they trying to say, "look, just have fun and fuck the details," or are they actually MOCKING those gamers or that gaming culture which get so buried in stat and crunch that they get twenty minutes of WOW for every four hours of play? (Sound familiar? HoL authors as first Forrgites?!?) Or am I bringing my own experiences into the mix; am *I* the one projecting meaning and attitude onto the book? For the record, I'd say no in this case: I read HoL when it came out, WAY before exposure to all this theory, and I still saw it as taking the piss out of many contemporary RPG systems. But another LitRPG could well work with ambiguity, leaving each reader to project onto it their own interpretation and intent, just as much poetry does.

    Atonality
    So above we have the two poles of a tonal continuum: writing between the lines and bitch-slapping with editorializing. But there's a third path, an orthogonal axis: one can use shifts in tone in a LitRPG to really hammer a point. If I have you lulled into the meditative march of neutral tone procedural writing and then, WHAM, start off on a screed about how this fucking chart is WORTHLESS if you don't have a heart to care about the children, you fucking DICK!

    Well, you sort of snap to attention, no? Where did all THAT come from, what did I just miss? Is this guy schitzo? Etcetera. You, as a reader, have to engage different mental gears to address this shift in tone... and then engage still more when I drop back into a staid and steady, neutral tone again. Done poorly, this atonality will seem like Tourette's Syndrome (just as bad atonal music sounds like folks in different rooms, tuning up or adjusting their synthesizers). Done properly, it can underscore the moments of consistency AND convey a message, via contrasting tone, with the moments of insanity (just as the completion of an atonal music progression can make all the disjointed notes attain a sort of "metaharmony").

    Structure
    This one is the big one, because for all the talk of tone, it's the order of presentation which carries at least half the weight. In a typical RPG, we often see a color piece, to establish the mood of play for the game, followed quickly by a series of definitional sections, so that one isn't totally lost as to what to do when the procedural stuff starts using the game jargon. Suppose that was tossed out the door? Suppose an RPG was written like A Clockwork Orange, with immediate total immersion in a nearly thoroughly different language? What is said, by that? One has to read it twice, just to get the sense--or jump to the glossary in the back of some editions, to try to get a baseline. A LitRPG can do the same thing, by eschewing the standard structure of a typical RPG.

    But what is said by FOLLOWING the typical RPG structure: intro, define terms, establish character, present procedures of play, flesh out setting (again, fiction or reference material or monster lists or whatever). That goes back to tone and diction: is it homage or satire? Or is the fact that it's hard to answer that question part of the exploration of the LitRPG?

    Or, rather than eliminating common structure or following it to convey additional meaning, what about a disjointed structure? Cart before the horse stuff--all the procedures of play presented before you even know if you are a character or in author stance or what; absolutely no information about setting in the presentation of what is clearly NOT a generic system? Can a LtRPG carry surprises, nestled in the sequence of presentation, just like a novel can use flashback to clarify what was, prior to the flash, a very ambiguous or downright confusing scene? What is meant when such structure conventions are violated? A whole branch of "LitRPG Theory" can grow out of just the considerations of structure and how it informs the piece, just has been done with conventional (and, moreso, experimental) literature.

    \n One more note on atonality, in conventional RPG (meaning non-LitRPGs):

    We game designers use atonality all the time, but it's to reinforce STRUCTURE, not theme or intent.

    There is the cold and clear, neutral tone of a process or rule statement, highlighting its importance or canonical nature.
    Then you get the more authorial and looser sort of writing which is, like, in sidebars or advice chunks or those little "talking head' icons folks use to say, "Hey, now I'm just talking to you, to let you know what's going on under the hood here."
    And, yeah verily, there be in-fiction tones that put thee into a mind to portray the shining heroes and scurrilous villains in a way which is meet.

    See there? Three tones, each with a functional role in the text, but none of which is intended to layer on nuance of the overall book's INTENT... because it's only real "intent" is to teach you to play a game the way the author envisioned it. EVEN IF proper gameplay enables the underlying intent of a game to educate or inspire (think Grey Ranks, here).

    But using atonality in a LitRPG would (should? could?) drive at the message, at the theme, at the takeaway of reading the text itself, without ever engaging in whatever "rules" or "procedures" are presented as carriers for that message.
