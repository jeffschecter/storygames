\n Hey again. I just grabbed Fiasco as my very first RPG (yep I've never played any RPG, let alone a 'Story Game'). Well it looks fantastic, lots of creativity, very little number crunching, which was exactly what I was looking for. I'm planning to give it a shot this weekend (hopefully) with a bunch of folks who are also new to RPG's. So far, I'm pretty clear on how the game works, but I'm not exactly sure how to handle the scenes. I've listened to a couple of actual play podcasts and it seems like some folks actually act the thing out as if it were a play, while others sort of take turns narrating as if it were a story. I'm hesitant to suggest improv to new gamers that might be a little self-conscious about it, so I'm leaning toward a more narrative style that allows us to take turns telling the story. But the book itself implies (in its play example) that the game will be played in a more improv-theater manner. I also feel that--and let me say this just might be the writer in me talking--that a narrative approach would lend itself to a more stable and focused story, especially for a group of first-timers. I have these nightmare visions of pointless and wandering 'in-character' dialogue and dead-ends. Who knows though, I haven't played it yet. I'm just trying to get an idea of how to best approach the game-play with totally new players.
\n
\nSo, given the usual caveats about the nature of any given group and the like, how do you all play? Either/or? Both? Does one style or the other obtain a better story? How about other games? Is there something about Fiasco that is more suitable for improv-theater style play as opposed to more traditional fair? Are the so-called Story-Games as a whole better for one style of play or another?

    \n Using "improv" may be a bit of a misnomer. I think you're really talking about voice, right? First person vs. third person? Me vs. my guy?

    MIKE: (As his character Clemont) OK, I go into the veterinary clinic and rifle through the drug cabinet. "Where's the ketamine?"

    or

    MIKE: OK, Clemont goes into the veterinary clinic and rifles through the drug cabinet. He yells "Where's the ketamine?"

    Right? Both are perfectly natural and, as Jason says, people tend to switch organically. Neither require or even benefit from getting up and acting stuff out, which is what I'd consider touching on improv.

    A game that exclusively uses third person is going to have a writer's room vibe to it. A game that uses first person is going to have a more performative vibe to it. Generally some mix of these two is what every group naturally falls into without thinking about it.

    \n I don't think I've ever played a session of any RPG, even D&D, where we managed to get through the whole thing without at least one or two lines of in-character dialog.
    It's just an easier shorthand, for one thing:
    "Surrender, villains," is easier to say than "My character tells them to surrender."
    You don't have to put on a funny or mannered voice or a bad accent ... though I often do ... and I don't really think of it as improv theater, I think of it as reading a bedtime story to my kid - the stuff in quotes I try to put on a different voice for, so she knows who is talking.

    I've played a fair amount of Universalis, and the rules there suggest a more third person expository approach - but I always end up speaking dialog.

    That said, I often surf around between an expository third person and an immersive first person ... one sentence I might say, "Joe (my character) heads out to the bar to canvas witnesses", and the next I might say, "'What the fuck, Carl, I know you know something!' I slam him against the wall!" Sometimes I want to Say Something, sometimes I want to Be There. (From http://www.lumpley.com/hardcore.html)

    As for your nightmares, pointless and wandering dialog does happen, but it doesn't usually ruin the game for me, and there are things you can do to mitigate it. http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=13762&page=1#Item_17

    \n In talky scenes, it's more natural to speak in character, since you're mainly just describing what your character says. In more action-y scenes, describing from a third person style is more natural. If you're in a fight or chase or the like, it seems a bit weird to constantly be speaking in character describing everything that happens. Some Fiasco games wind up with a lot of talking and personal, emotional betrayals. Some games wind up a frantic, madcap chase across the city as you desperately fight for your life. Some combine the two.

    But as pointed out, every group will naturally fall into a style that is comfortable for the group itself.

    \n OK, sorry, I misunderstood. This is something everybody intuitively knows how to do, so I wouldn't spend a lot of time thinking about it in advance. I don't think people need any encouragement one way or the other - you and your friends will find your own tempo and preferences, and chances are the urge to drop into character will become overwhelming at some point - that's pretty fun. If you start to play and for some reason it isn't coming naturally, you can stop and talk about it. My guess is that it'll go swimmingly without too much prompting. I think it is super cool that you're giving it a try!

    I'd think more about facilitation as something you can do to make the game better - keep an eye on pacing (help bring those talky, going nowhere scenes to a gentle conclusion), the flow of dice (remind everybody their scenes are sharply limited and to use them directly and wisely, ideally pointed straight at Needs), and being a good listener so that when you get to Establish for others (sometimes people choose to Resolve a lot when they are new to the game) you'll be ready with some juicy scenes to throw out.

    \n Yes, game flow and pacing will be more important than the details of "how do I say something?"

    That said, if people seem confused, most gamers tend to go with something like:

    * If you're describing something in the fiction, or your character is taking action, just describe it as a narrator would. (e.g. "Bob leans forward and grabs the gun!")
    * If your character is saying something, say it yourself, like an actor would. ("Oh, hello! Where do you think you're going?")

    You can add body language cues, if you like, like leaning forward, making a grimace, rubbing your hands together, etc, but most people remain sitting when they play.

    In play, you'll switch between the two, often even in the same sentence. Say you're playing a character named Jeff. You might say something like:

    "My, it's getting cold in here!" Jeff shivers a little as he is speaking, and his lips are blue. "Did anyone bring some matches?"

    Many people will use a slightly different "character voice" to make it clear that the first part was character dialogue and the phrase in the middle was not. It's much easier than having to specify "So, then my character says..." every time Jeff speaks a line in the story.

    Hope that helps! Let us know how the game goes.

    I think studying the "replay" in the text will be really helpful.

    \n In Fiasco-- there's the initial setting-of each scene, which defaults-toward third person; especially when setting a scene for another player.

    When I have a really juicy idea, I tend to stay in third person, speak quickly, & narrate all the characters around like pawns, gloating aloud at what a brilliant idea I've had to steer the characters up shit creek. "So... it's the next morning. Jimmy Olsen wakes up, and wonders where his pants are!"

    If I can think of a way to say the same thing in first person, I go for it. Because doing that seems to get a big laugh out of the other players.
    "My turn? OK. ahem. [looking upward with forearm across my eyes] Fucking sunlight. What time is it? Dawn? And... Where Are My Pants?!?" But often my acting skillz don't extend beyond one liners, so I switch back to storytelling mode.

    If any player, in third person, makes suggestions to make my idea even better-- I try to find a way to incorporate it into my idea. But I don't hesitate to hash-it-out player-to-player (rather than character-to-character), if I think there's a good reason Not to do it their way. This is different from theatre Improv, where they teach actors to say "Yes, and" or "Yes, but" but not "No-- that's not what happens."

    On the other hand, if I'm setting a scene, and somebody jumps in-- and starts adding to it in-character, in first person; then I'm more likely to defer to whatever they're doing, even if it isn't what I had in mind. Because-- I see 'acting' as a delicate thing, and if somebody is brave enough to give it a try, I want to do all I can to help them not-crash.


    For me, the really-thorny part of acting-mode is prolonged dialogue. Often, I'll intentionally not-even-try to act out dialogue. If I know it's beyond my limits. If I'm playing a girl out on a date, I'll tend toward, "Betty says this, and then she does that", to spare everyone the awful spectacle of me trying to impersonate a teenage seductress. I do this, even if the other player is in-character. They act their dialogue in character, I reply with "She does this..." or even "I do that" but not actual dialogue, beyond a few phrases.


    One time when I do make a point of going into character-- is when I can't think of a good idea for where the scene should go. It can be uncomfortable to fish-around in-character, haltingly thinking up dialogue on the fly. But sometimes you gotta just inhabit a scene to see if something gets said-- which would make a good 'point' for the scene.

    \n I usually do a little of both. For me, interaction between characters is best improved (both players in character) while interaction between character and environment is best narrated (I.e. Describing an explosion, the creaking of an old stairway, the smell of the greasy burger joint).
    \n
    \nNewer players may feel more comfortable narrating (take a look at Prime Time Adventures for this type of gameplay), as though they are writing a TV show or movie. One thing that I find helps in getting people to interact more is when a person says "I do this", ask them to show you how they are doing it. Are they excited, hurried, dragging their feet? What about the tone of their voice? Their body language? Once players get the hang of expressing their character, it quickly becomes addictive :)
