\n My first thought for an monster hunting game was Monster of the Week, but it doesn't really capture the flavor of that era, the gritty late 1800's, electricity, telegraphs, traveling through the air rather than sea.

Things were gaslight and still had an air of spiritualism, where as I felt as it was, Monster of the Week was very modern.

I also thought of Hunter: The Vigil, from Whitewolf, and again, the same kind of feeling that it was very modern and not exactly there in terms of setting and evocation. Call of Cthulhu or Trail of Cthulhu are closer to what I'm aiming for, but their more cosmic horror rather than action horror.

Any of you kind folks have suggestions on good role-playing games (or hacks of games) with a narrative focus that can capture action horror in that era?

    \n I ran a fate-based version of the old "Ravenloft: Masque of Red Death" setting that went quite well. I used the "Strands of Fate" rules, which I felt were a bit clunky, but I think you could do a great job with Spirit of the Century, Fate Core, or Dresden Files.

    It was a very pulpy superhero-esque version of the setting, though: A mysterious and insane Russian assassin, a shape-shifting witch, and the son of Mycroft Holmes versus Frankenstein's monster, the Phantom of the Opera, and the Empress Dowager of China. I take it you're looking for something a bit more down to earth?

    \n Sometimes the best approach is to just go with whatever mechanics you and your players like, and use a collection of sourcebooks for mashing the world together. The CoC sourcebooks for instance, still contain a lot of useful world stuff, even if you don't use the rules or adventures.

    A broadly-applicable "generic" system like FATE or FUDGE is often a good choice for such an approach, because it gives you the flexibility to adapt the rules to the sourcebooks you choose.

    \n Savage Worlds setting - Rippers is basically that. Victorian era monster hunters society (started by Van Helsing) is fighting a cabal of creatures that bump in the night. You cultivate your own lodge, harvest parts of the fallen creatures, use them to your advantage and film\xf3w the rules of the XIX century society.

    Worth checking out if you are not opposed to traditional RPGs.
