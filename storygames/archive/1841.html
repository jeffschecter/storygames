I Got My Mind on My Game and My Game on My Mind

Christian_Griffen
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Forum Discussion
In the thread on where this forum is going, Jason Morningstar wrote:

Here's something I notice and dislike - people shilling their own stuff. Not in Directed Promotion threads, where it is fine, but in threads where people ask for advice. "The answer to your query is, in all cases, my game."

That's something I've been thinking about recently, and I'd actually like to talk about it. Here's the deal.

Several of us just finished designing a game. It's a process where you think about it all day long, rewrite it every day, and basically immerse yourself in it. That makes it take over a certain part of your brain. So when I see a discussion, more often than not, my mind automatically turns to how that relates to my design. Not because I want to market it by talking about it all the time, but because that's just the thing that's been on my mind all this time.

I'm aware that I did this with one of John Kim's threads recently, not purposely to talk about my game, but just because I felt I could share some insight based on the design I'd just done. But afterward I did feel weird, like it could be perceived to be "shilling" after all, and that's not what I want.

So my questions then are: Is this always annoying, or does it actually help people when you have that as a concrete example that the designer really knows well? Is there a difference between saying "This is how that relates to my game" and "You should buy my game because it does exactly that?" Anything else that relates to this topic you'd like to get off your chest?

Thanks,

- Christian

    Enh. I admit, once I accepted that I'd be taking people's money for a game (suckers!) I became much more ok with saying "Y'know what's awesome? My game." I like to presume that anyone who has written a game did so to do things they thought were cool, and if those things come up in conversation, I would far rather a game designer speak up and say "Yeah, we thought about that when designing our game, and we did X, and it's worked out for us in Y fashion" than have the designer (who hopefully has useful things to say) remain silent for fear of appearing to be a shill.

    That said, I also expect a game designer to be keenly aware of what their game _doesn't_ do well. You can do a lot of different things with a lot of different games, but if you can't step up and say "No, you don't want my game for that" (and ideally "But this other game might work") then yes, you're shilling. If you don't know what your game can't do, it's probably worth figuring out, because if you really and truly feel your game is the best choice every time an issue comes up, then you really, really owe yourself a long, hard look.

    -Rob D.

    PS - That said, I usually see the worst of this behavior from fans rather than the designers themselves.

    Nobody's saying you shouldn't talk about your own games. What Jason's talking about (and, correct me if I'm wrong) is when people bring up their games at inappropriate times. If you mention your game when it's really and truly pertinent to the conversation, AND you have something to say about the topic, you won't come off as a shill. At least, you won't to me. That's because you're mentioning your game in service to the conversation at hand. It happens a lot in design or theory threads.

    Another thing to consider is overall community participation. If 9/10ths of your posts include the phrase "my game" then you likely come off as a shill. Whereas if you frequently have things to say that are interesting and on-topic that don't involve your game, people will know there's more to you than selling your game, and will be more inclined to take posts that are about it in the correct spirit.

    Then again, this may be just the way I perceive things. I imagine other people are different...

    So, in my case, I'm primarily a consumer, and that means that I see other people talking about other peoples' games a lot more often than I see Shreyas talking about Shreyas's games or anyone else's.

    I really like to see designers talk about their own games, or at the very least playtesters, because I really don't trust other folks to understand the game well and be able to explicate it in its full flexibility and depth. That's the designer's special knowledge, and I suspect that not a few of us know about cool things that they can do with their games and didn't put into their books explicitly.

    That stuff is great. I hunger for it.

    But there's a limit... if you the designer are the only person at all talking about your game, maybe that's not so good. If you find yourself never talking about anybody else's games, maybe that's not so good.

    And, of course, you can get real jewels out of the things that naive players, or very design-minded ones, derive from games that aren't of their own design, and this is stuff that's really important to talk about.

    All that it takes is a bit of sensitivity and attention, carefully applied.
