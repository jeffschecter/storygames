\n During Open Gaming Circle at Gamestorm this year, we would give a little spiel at the beginning. The spiel varied. What are the important points that we should hit and what shouldn't we hit? I've been looking to hone this spiel and specialize it for Conventions. Let's build a specialized spirel for Gamestorm, Indie Hurricane, Open Gaming Circle. Here are some things for consideration, give us reasons why they should or shouldn't be in there (and what else can we add).

Elements:

Gaming with Strangers (Hurray for you, this is courageous).
Introduce yourselves when you get to the table.
Ask Clarifying questions.
Take as much time as you want when it is your turn.
Say the Obvious thing.
The (New) Veil (mid-game-non-sorcerer kind, the "you're not a jerk" clause
Rule Zero (don't be a dick).
You don't have to have any experience to play.
Pitches should briefly tell us how many people can play, and what players will do.

    Posted By: J. WaltonI feel like it's better to encourage the people pitching games to be clear about what the game is like. That way, nobody's put in the position of aspiring to speak for all the games and GMs that are participating. For example, there were times when the way Open Gaming was presented as a whole was misleading in terms of describing the game that I was preparing to run. If people want to present their own games in whatever way they like, that's cool. But if I'm running Dungeon World, having it described as a collaborative storytelling exercise doesn't really help and may in fact alienate the kinds of players I want to engage with.

    \n I generally rule out all technique discussion. It's too much to absorb and it's meaningless before you play. It's far better to have people bring that up during play when it would help. To wit, I'd cut:\n
    Ask Clarifying questions.
    Take as much time as you want when it is your turn.
    Say the Obvious thing.
    \nLikewise I really don't think you need to tell people not to be a dick. I'd like to presume adults are adults (even when they're kids) and be shocked (shocked!) when people act like jerks at the table.

    But add: we are playing together. The game will be great or terrible based on how all of us contribute and participate. We're it.

    \n My practice is similar to Sage. Generally, if I know or have a good sense of everyone at the table, or if we're playing a relatively light and fun game, talking about "veils" isn't necessary. I can either trust the players to speak up or I'll just go ahead and -- as game organizer -- veto anything that might be problematic ("Yeah, can we not go there? I would appreciate that. How about something else?") or ask questions before proceeding ("Is everyone's cool with the ratman eating the dude's face?" which happened in our DW game).

    For more intense games, talking about "veils" in a general way, in front of a huge crowd of people, isn't really sufficient to create safe space at the individual tables, once people break up into groups and start talking and playing. In some ways, I worry that it creates this illusion of safe space that may or may not actually be there in practice. It's really up to the organizers of individual games to engage with their players and create the kind of social environment that's going to facilitate a positive play experience (or a negative one that at least doesn't abuse people's trust).

    Honestly, I'd rather work to develop a culture around "how to safely run games about difficult material" -- based around shared expectations and individual responsibiluty -- and say something about that beforehand, during pitches. Like, during pitching, say: "This game deals with some difficult material, and we'll talk before play begins about how we're going to handle that. But if you're really uncomfortable with X content, maybe consider playing something else." More often, though, I find that -- when I run games like that -- I only tell the people I'm interested in playing that game with and it isn't pitched in open gaming at all, but seats are filled by word of mouth and negotiation. Would I pitch Poison'd in open gaming? I'm not sure I would. But that's just me.

    \n Ben championed (via Story Games Seattle) a newer, friendlier approach to talking about Veils. While Ron's earlier formulation felt very, "Bad shit could happen, here's how you solve that problem," the way I've heard Ben talk about it is much more, "Whenever you want something to stay off the table, here's a simple tool that you get to use in that moment." I think couched in really positive terms, that little discussion of Veils can turn into an empowerment thing rather than a defensive thing.

    Lots of people have expressed a fear in starting story games, due to the worry that "it could get really weird or uncomfortable." The Veil is potentially a way of reassuring people that they are empowered participants, and can control their environment as needed.

    I've seen lots of people benefit from this newer expression of "The Veil."

    \n Shoot, this turned into a long post. I started writing it to respond to Sage, but now I\u2019m glad that others have also chipped in. Because I think this is great stuff.

    This is fascinating to me, because my default would be that the Veil is especially necessary for cons. If a stranger at your table brings up something at the con table that another stranger at your table thinks is triggering or too much or whatever, what do you do? Do you try to deal with it then somehow or gloss over it and move on? (edit: JWalt talks about this above). I know that this doesn\u2019t happen often, even at Cons. Usually people are just delightedly motivated to game and there is no trouble.
    In fact, I had been doing the veil thing before games for a long time, and I had never seen it used, so I was starting to think that maybe it was unnecessary. But then two things happened:

    1. Knowing that the safety brake is there changes how you drive (for the better, I'd argue).

    2. I saw it get used at the Emerald City Comic Con we (Sage and I) volunteered at.

    So, to help me get across the first point, I want to mention the points I always cover when I talk about the Veil. In my mind, this is very close to Ben\u2019s version of the Veil. (Also fans of Sorcerer might notice that this is inspired by that, but different).

    Playing with strangers is the best, but since we don't all know each other, we have this one rule that trumps all the other rules in the games today. It's sort of a safety brake. If something comes up in the fiction that is triggering or makes you uncomfortable, or dealing with that today will just ruin your fun, you can just say, \u201cI Veil that\u201d. What this means is that we will remove it from the fiction or narrate around it. For example, maybe a clown scorpion killed your best friend. When I say, \u201cThen there are, like, 15 clown scorpions all over you.\u201d You can say, \u201cI Veil clown scorpions.\u201d And here is the thing, you don\u2019t have to justify why you don\u2019t want something included. In fact, we don\u2019t really want to know what your deal is with clown scorpions. Sometimes, if you are one of those people who are good at social cues, you might notice that someone looks uncomfortable with how the fiction is going, but they haven\u2019t drawn the Veil yet. You can Veil for other people. Just point out the thing in the fiction that looks like it might be the culprit and say, \u201cHey, maybe we should Veil this.\u201d If you get the slightest head nod or agreement, go ahead and Veil that shit. And of course, you can Veil your own stuff if it starts to squick you out. The final thing to know about the Veil is that if you introduce something in the fiction and someone else Veils it, you aren\u2019t a jerk for having brought it up. You didn\u2019t know that I was afraid of clown scorpions, and we are all just riffing stuff off the top of our heads. We will not assume that you are a clown scorpion fetishist. Now, if you bring up something that was already Veiled, your jerk status will be reviewed. Just remember to Veil easy.

    Okay, long speech. Anyway, I believe that these points about the Veil make people feel more comfortable than less. Also it protects me when I want to introduce nutso stuff. I can worry less about how others might react if I know that we have already talked about removing that kind of thing. Finally, the combination of the "you don't have to justify and the "jerk clause" as I like to call them is invaluable to me. It means in the unlikely event that something ugly comes up, we can deal with it and continue playing with minimal bad feelings.

    The second point I made above touches on this. Another facilitator and I sat down to demo Shock for "one or two hours". I gave a short Spiel, which included the Veil. The other facilitator ended up drawing the Veil over an element that the new player introduced during character creation. This brand new Story Gamer, who had never heard of this kind of game before seeing the sign on the table, shrugged their shoulders and switched that element out. Then we kept playing.

    Wrapping it up here: I gave the Veil speech a lot of times, but it only got used once. Was it making more people uncomfortable than comfortable?

    \n Regarding veils, here is what I do and it has worked almost every single time (and I run games for 500-1,000+ people a year):


    - I usually wait till after we introduce ourselves.

    - I grab an index card and draw an X on it.

    - "I'd like your help. Your help to make this game fun for everyone. If anything in the game makes anyone uncomfortable, just simply lift this card up. Don't explain why. When the card is lifted, we simply edit out any uncomfortable content and move forward. I know it sounds funny but it's helped us play amazing games together and more often than not I'm the one who ends up using the X card to protect myself from you all!"

    Note: Each of these words is chosen precisely. But I will change these words depending on the game.



    Why do I do this?

    - I've repeatedly had someone say they don't want Y in a game and then they are the ones who bring Y into the game!
    - I've repeatedly had people say they were ok with something when clearly they weren't.
    - Some people who are uncomfortable are already on edge and sometimes won't clearly communicate. Lifting a card is easier than saying something. You don't even have to look anyone in the eye, interrupt the person talking, or talk over someone to do it.



    If we are playing a game where it's important to pre-decide what isn't in a game, I'll have everyone secretly write what they don't want on an index card and hand it to me.

    If I know a game is going to feature sensitive content, I will let everyone know asap and give them an easy way to opt out of the game if needed.

    Next time I GM I plan to try giving each player an X card so they don't have to reach towards the middle of the table to grab the card (depending on the table or their arm length, they may not be able to reach it).


    Usually I use a lot of other techniques in addition to the above to create safe spaces. Like tokens to track spotlight and giving the person an option to pass but earn additional token to do even more stuff later when they feel more comfortable. Or making it clear that you don't have to speak in character or act at all. Just saying what your pawn is doing as if it was a video game works fine. And I have lots of ways to use body language, voice, and seating arrangements to increase comfort as needed. It's also great to make it clear how they can get help, that no experience is needed, that we are all here to help each other.

    \n I find the pitch is as important as the X-Card itself. Here is a breakdown of why I chose these specific words...



    "I'd like your help."

    A lot of times we don't get what we want in life because we don't ask for it. This also makes it clear that it's not all on me.


    "Your help to make this game fun for everyone."

    State the goal, set expectations. Make it clear it's not about individuals but the group.


    "If anything in the game makes anyone uncomfortable..."

    I switch from saying "you" to "anyone" and "we" below to reinforce it's about the group's needs. I say "anything" so it's not limited or specific because I don't want to prejudge what people find uncomfortable because it's subjective and I'm not here to judge.


    "...just simply lift this card up."

    It's so easy! I will then actually lift the card to make it clear physically how easy it is.


    "Don't explain why."

    Explaining is bad because it's extra effort, a higher barrier to accomplish your goal, and it can feel like being put on trial. Plus explanations means more time not playing.


    "When the card is lifted, we simply edit out any uncomfortable content..."

    Repeat what they need to do "lift the card" and emphasize how it is simple and fast.


    "...and move forward."

    Nothing to see here. The focus shifts back to having fun, not judgement.


    "I know it sounds funny but it will help us play amazing games together..."

    It does sound funny to most people so I prefer to acknowledge what most people are thinking than let it go unsaid. Saying it out loud takes away its stigma and says "I understand" + "but trust me".


    "...and usually I'm the one who ends up using the X card to protect myself from you all!"

    It's not just about them, it's also about me. We're in this together. The rules apply to everyone. And it takes the edge off a potential serious topic by ending with humor.

    I will then usually X-Card myself early on in the game. Say I describe a gory fight, I might say out loud, "whoa John, relax there" and lift the X-Card on myself to show everyone it's no big deal and I'd rather make the game comfortable for everyone and put my own ego aside.
