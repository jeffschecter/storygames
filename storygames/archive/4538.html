    \n I think you have to ask yourself what you are after in long-term play. There are a lot of different things that can be emphasized and it may affect your choice:

    * Do the players want to play characters that change, or gain new capabilities, or both, or neither?
    * Are they familiar with the system or will they grow in their skill with it? (Do they value a system that rewards more skillful play as they learn?)
    * Do the players value a detailed setting that long-term play illuminates different portions of?
    * Do the players value consistency and continuity, bringing back a limited number of elements in different combinations and situations?

    There's probably a lot more reasons to enjoy long-term play, but the design choices you make for your campaign will vary considerably depending on what part of it you like.

    Posted By: StornClinton, not to be a pain in the ass, but "is it"? Is TSOY really tied into the idea that gods aren't around? Because, maybe I'm dense, but I don't see that.
    \nHeya, I wanted Clinton to back up his point because he's much better at it (knowing his game more) than me. But now that he's put the ball into play, I'll dribble for a bit. This is also a reply to Mike H above.

    So yeah, what Clinton said, but as a player who used a homebrew world for a year (think Fantasy Chinese Stargate), here's the things that need to happen to use TSOY and have a good fit:

    "No monsters. No Gods. Just people." I think that was the tagline. Clinton emphasizes it again with the mention of Gods and the wide skill range above. But in any case, here's why:

    * Monsters. Monsters are there to fight and overcome. You can't like, all of a sudden, stop killing a monster in the eye and then start negotiating with it or becoming friends with it. This MUST be a possibility in TSOY, because that's the way the rules work. You can literally cook a meal for a monster to charm it while it tries to attack you.

    For that reason, settings with "monsters", that is creatures whose only intention is "I kill/eat the character" and nothing else, just does not work right in the rules. It's a total shoehorn, and from experience it makes the game unfun: It makes it slow because every missed kill-roll means that you're engaged in another one-way Bringing Down the Pain with only two possible outcomes: It dies or you die. Most classic settings don't support a team of 3 famous adventuring negotiators who charm bugbears and settle territory disputes for drow and xorn. This has to be a possibility, because it's a possibility (and encouraged) in the rules.

    * Wide range of abilities, or in other words, "unbeatable foes". Like "gods". Anything, ANYTHING that appears in a TSOY game has the possibilit of being overcome by one person. Yes, it is quite literally possible for a child with a fruit knife to kill a Terrasque or elder Red Dragon. Or punch Elminster in the cock and make him cry like a baby in front of the Black Hand at Zhentil Keep. In the default setting in TSOY, there aren't "Red Dragons", "Gods", "Elminsters" etc. However, if you use the TSOY system and introduce an opposing force, the rules make it possible to overcome at any skill level (of course easier at higher skill levels, but not impossible at low skill levels).

    Counter that to, say, even other games like the 1ed Conspiracy of Shadows. It is possible for a group of dudes to not be able to overcome an evil witch, because that witch is statted thus that the highest roll cannot penetrate her defenses (many games are like this, mind). It is expected that the charcaters train/gain xp first before taking them on. In TSOY, it *must be the case* that a 1st level barmaid has a possibility, however slight, of killing a heavily plated Death Knight of Krynn in one hit from a rock. If you say such a thing can not happen, then the world is probably not suited for TSOY.

    Further, on the sly I learned that the best adventures for TSOY happen in places where there is always contact with other NPCs: LIke city or community environments, not wilderness environments. And even better, if the game is set within a localized area and not just "travel around and do adventures", in order to make reoccuring NPCs meaningful (so that you can take Key of Love and stuff with them).

    So again, to summarize:

    * No "Monsters"
    * No "Gods" or "Powerful people that can't be punked, however unlikely, by children carrying sticks". In D&D, it is impossible for a 1st level hero to overthrow Zhentil Keep, stab Elminster to death, or go after a terrasque with a spoon. In TSOY, it is possible (though of course not "common")
    * Possibility of escalating a conflict from a swordfight into a tea ceremony or negotiation.
    Then, helpful:
    * Set in places filled with NPCs
    * Generally set in a localized area, not wandering all over the place from new town to new town like Final Fantasy.

    Make sense?
    -Andy

    EDIT: On Gods: Jason runs a TSOY game one-shot where you are Aztecs who are on a mission to kill three Gods (or secretly save them, etc). By the nature of the game, the Gods are killable as they have stats just like PCs (albeit powerful, but imminently killable. I know cause we did). If that's cool in your Gods and Mortals game, then it works. But if you want heroic Gods who bellow orders from the clouds, who cannot be overthrown, talked down to, etc, then it won't work in TSOY.
