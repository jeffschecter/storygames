\n Some guy at Google who deserves a sloppy kiss has arranged for all US copyright renewals between 1923 and 1963 to be digitized.

Why is this great news? Because it makes determining rights status for works in that time period feasible. The way copyright worked in that window meant that without action by the rights-holder, things would slip into the public domain. Many did. This is why Night of the Living Dead is distributed by 23 different companies.

These records have always been public, but very difficult to access and use. The last time I searched for rights status on some moldy book I was interested in using material from, I had to rely on dodgy PDFs of photocopies provided by Project Gutenberg. It was time-consuming and error-prone. Next time, I can run queries off the data in the XML file, which makes me really happy. I have not yet looked at the data but this is a positive step in so many ways.

    \n What from that period doesn't interest me? People wrote good books about all kinds of stuff from 1923-1963 and some of them forgot or chose not to renew their copyright protection. I can wander over to our research library, find a stack of books from the thirties about whatever I'm interested in (clowns! Torquemada! metal lathes! Dorthea Dix!), run simple queries to determine their status, and use stuff from them if they turn out to be in the public domain.

    \n I'd take care using this data - double-checking is always a good idea, and nobody is making any claims about accuracy or completeness. Most publishers have a clearance office that can confirm who, if anybody, owns what. I was surprised how efficient this process was when I had to use it a while back with Random House UK. Maybe I was just lucky. I inquired about the status of a book published in 1923 by a company they had bought out long ago, and they were like "We've got nothing to do with it, but this guy owns the rights. Here's his email address."
