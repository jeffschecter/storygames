\n I've been toying with some ideas for a leaner, faster hack of DoN 2nd ed. which would allow for players to take on the role of multiple characters easily and last night I got the opportunity to try out a few things.

First, we decided to have a 'Jeeves & Wooster vs. Zombies' setting, as a reaction to Cockneys vs. Zombies, so came up with a list of potential characters: from that list, each of the three players then chose three to be the PCs they would directly control.

For each PC, I asked the players to provide them with a name, two stats, a specialty and a bad habit: I cut down the four stat pairs in DoN to a single pairing of Proactive/Reactive, with the former covering attacking, searching, chasing and persuading people to do things, while the latter applies to defending, hiding, escaping and persuading people to stop doing something.

As a consequence of that change, I also had to change how specialities worked, so I asked the players to create one speciality for each PC: when using the speciality, they got to roll 3 dice instead of 2 and pick any 2 of them to be their result. Bad habits worked as normal and each player was given 5 survival points at the start, to split between their three characters anyway they wished.

The big change I made to GMing was something that lifted it's inspiration from AW and its hacks: I decided that I wasn't going to roll dice! This meant that I didn't have to stat the monsters up fully, just pick a couple of abilities off the list and they were ready to go. When the monsters did anything, I had the players roll their Reactive stat to see if they could prevent it.

This seat-of-my-pants hack burned through plot even faster than I expected, reaching a climax in little more than an hour, when 'Spiffy' was persuaded by his manservant that he would have to marry the Zombie bride that had gatecrashed the garden party with her horde, in order to put the undead to rest. Given that at least one of the players had never seen DoN before, it was an impressively fast game and made a perfect little time filler; for a longer timeslot, I think I'd just raise the number of Survival points the monsters started with and maybe make the players pick flaws for their characters, which would work as anti-specialities, allowing the GM to roll an extra d10 and replace one of the player's results with it if they chose.

    You might consider removing the characters from player ownership, like in Remember Tomorrow: put all the characters in the middle, and players can pull out the ones they want to play at the moment.
    \nI like that idea, though it does complicate the survival point rules somewhat, especially risk checks and bad habits; perhaps when you fail a risk check, you have to pay a survival point to save the character? And when the character you're playing uses a bad habit, it must immediately cause another character, held by another player, to be placed in danger? Or, more interestingly, a bad habit immediately places that character in danger, so you as a player might gain a survival point but the character you were using could die as a result!

    Food for thought... I might have to try a bit of this again at Concrete Cow, to mop any floating gamers in the afternoon; we could guarantee players a game on the spot, no matter what time of day they turned up!
