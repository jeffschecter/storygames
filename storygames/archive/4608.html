    \n I read both of those, and posted about Shared Fantasy here. I posted some distilled thoughts on both here.

    Shared Fantasy is a fairly in-depth snapshot of what the hobby was like (in a particular area) in the very early 80s, done by a sociologist who has done several studies of subcultures. It is, frankly, not a very flattering picture in many ways, but an interesting one nonetheless.

    Mackay's book analyzes RPGs from a highly academic performing arts perspective. I found some interesting and unique ideas in there, but there were a few places where the writing literally got so jargon-tastic that there was at least one chapter I found unintelligible.

    \n The best book I've read is, unfortunately, only available in Finnish. It's the not-so-aptly-named Roleplaying Manifesto by Juhana Pettersson (who wrote something about it back in the days in his rpg.net column). I don't know how good it is from objective viewpoint, as it pretty much describes the foundations of why I play and the methods I play with. Subjectively speaking, it's the best book about roleplaying (games included) I've read, because it's inspiring.
    \n
    \nIt's not actually a manifesto - compared to some other manifestoes, there's not a lot of preaching in it. I'd imagine it would rub some people who are used to play in other styles the wrong way, since it doesen't really care about anything else than the subjective experiences of the writer. There's a history lesson (by now, ancient history of roleplaying) and a setting included, but the meat is in the how to play and run roleplaying scenarios -stuff. One can hope it'll be translated someday.
