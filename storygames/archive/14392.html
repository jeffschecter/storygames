\n Inspired by this other thread, I want to ask if it's even possible to make something like the Dimensional Portals used in the Portal computer game work for a story-based roleplaying game. Portal, after all, is a physics-based first person puzzle game. It has a nifty story attached to it, yes, but the story isn't what the game's mainly about. It's about that awesome feeling of flying/falling through the air and figuring out awesome puzzles and getting the timing right and all that stuff which a story game can't replicate.

Still, as you can see in this fan movie, it's still something that can be incorporated into a story (a movie in this case), even if not very centrally -- that movie is really more about the cake than the portals, but the portals are in it at least.

So if Valve contacted you and said, "Make us a Portal tabletop RPG!" What would you say to them? If you'd say, "okay!" then where would you start in trying to make it work?

    The first thing that gels in the brain pan is Portal: Live Fire. A tactial RPG/board game hybrid with way too much papercraft used to build a modular 3D proving grounds. The game would have a simple turn based vector movement system for when players go airborne. There might even be flight stands if it turns out that serious hang time is possible. The opponents would be androids that have pre-programed behaviors (such as patrolling) as part of the puzzle building level design. The game could be played solo, co-op, or in versus mode and would probably be GM-less as long as you run the canned scenarios.

    In my mind's eye, the shooting system would use templates and overlays. Templates would be grid pictures of targets at point blank/short/medium/long range from various angles with overlays for walls and portals that would provide cover. [A bit like the Bounty Hunter: Shoot out at the Saloon picture books when played in advanced mode.]

    Why a shooty game for Portal? Well, you can't duplicate the detailed physics except at a coarse level, but you still want to work with portals. So, besides the puzzles you provide the simulated complexity of a combat system.

    I mentioned GM-less, but I could also imagine a player taking on the role of GLaDiUS, the live fire version of GLaDOS. They would handle all the androids, moving platforms, and other environmental items. [Include chart of triggered and random things to say.]

    \n Thanks guys, that's a bunch of awesome answers. I like the ideas of using boardgamey mechanics and creating some sort of puzzle game with it. The opposite idea of taking the basic relationships at hand and making a more narrative game out of it (as with Paranoia) is also really interesting.

    The idea that really resonates with me most is right here though:\n
    Posted By: doho123I would probably center an RPG (if I was forced to keep it in that genre) as sort of an industrial espionage in the not too distant future that would be similiar to the world of the tv series Caprica.With the various factions being Apeture Science, The Black Mesa group, household robots with some amount of questionable sentience, and an inter- dimensional spruces of aliens that "walk amongst us" or something.

    Like most "games interpreted from another game type," I feel that just doing portal-type puzzles as a board game would be kind of silly; if I wanted to play portal puzzles, I just play the real game. Obviously ,you can still have all sorts of neat portalish puzzles when performing various industrial secrets capers using all of the wacky tech-guns that the Valve games provide.

    \nI'm thinking Leverage, the RPG, or something like it, where the ability to open portals is a player ability of some sort that helps drive the narrative forward. I'm really inspired by the idea of portals as a narrative mechanic instead of a puzzle mechanic. I'd see it together with other kinds of weird science fiction also -- not just portals, as you find in the game -- and I wonder what other sorts of magic-like science fiction tricks might fit well with it.

    Limiting portals as a narrative device here would be just as important as limiting them in the game environment. Just as there are some surfaces that portals don't work on in the game, there would have to be situations they don't work in the fiction as well. They'd have to have a different logic to them, though, not just "surface A works but surface B doesn't." Say, for instance that portals could only work on flat surfaces, or if the fiery stuff around the edges of the portals really was a fire, so that portals could only work on flammable surfaces. Either of these would make portals extremely useful to characters in some situations but useless in others, and might help preserve niche protection for other characters with other powers.
