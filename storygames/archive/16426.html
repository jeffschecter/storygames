\n I just back from NordNordOst gaming con, and been playing a lot of great games for the last 4 days, the vast majority (85%) where Story Now/indie games.

Something struck me, the body as a tool in almost all of roleplaying games is left out on a rules level. The systems of the games manipulates the verbal telling of the game, but almost never the player bodies.
Which is a shame, because the body is a great tool to create powerful gaming experiences. Our bodies and senses have a lot of power to affect what we are feeling.

I feel like experimenting with that, yet stay within the physical restrictions of tabletop roleplaying places. Like that most people probably play in a quite small kitchen or living room when people can't run around. So I will assume people are going to stay in their seats, and not move across the room.

I wanna have psychical aspects and experiences as part of the roleplay on a system level. Not just the \u201cI use my body for playing my characters body language\u201d but also in symbolic ways.

The of physical sort of things I come up with and think could be explored, without braking the assumption that people will stay in their seats is:

Eye contact
I think a lot could be done with this. Holding someone gaze creates a lot of tension, that could be used in awesome ways. One simple way to implement it that characters need to speak the truth when they have eye contact. Either as a part of the fiction (The characters are mages, and you cant lie to a mage while looking her in the eyes) or just a part of the rules system OOC.

Touching hands
Touch is a great tool for communication, and out hands are generally not \u201dprivate\u201d body parts. It part of the body we generally let other people, even strangers touch in everyday life. Yet they are sensitive and very communicative.
Generally, at most gaming tables, groups can place themself in such a ways that two players can reach across the table to touch hands while sitting down. Perhaps placing their hand on the table as a signal when it times to reach out and this technique to take effect.
One way this could implement it could be for players to touch the other players hand in a way that expresses how the character really feels. (Describing how someone acts really warm towards someone outwardly, but bu grasping the other players hand not at all gently show that the character actually is really angry at the other player. The angry, and hard grasp giving you physical experience of that anger.)

Standing up
Standing up as marker for the player slipping into monologue mode has been done a bunch of times, and is a great example. (Erm. Technically you don't stay in you seat, but you stay in your place and it can be done at most gaming tables, by most people.)

Whispering
This is actually vocal, but using \u201dstage whispers\u201d to communicate something on another level of the game then what is said in a normal voice during a scene.
Could for example be used for giving a character in play emotional impulses.

Gestures
Gestures to indicate taking out of character, cutting the scene etc, is used my many groups but could perhaps taken even further.

Any one else got ideas like this and wanna comment?

    \n A potentially useful gesture signal that is sometimes used in larp - the OK sign.

    You can hold up an OK sign when you're doing some really intense acting and you want the other players to know that, as a player, you're fine. That way, you don't need to stop the flow for the "hey, are you just acting or are you really mad right now?" conversation that can really kill a charged emotional moment.

    It also works great if your acting out something like a coughing fit or something.

    I like to place the OK sign over my heart with my right hand. That puts it in a place that is easy to see (unless you're doubled over with fake coughing), but holding it up against the body makes it feel less intrusive.

    But yeah, a lot of the work in this area has been done by larp groups rather than tabletop groups. But there is some definite potential there for using it in tabletop.

    I particularly like the idea of using eye contact as a significant and meaningful thing. I think I read about a game that used eye contact rules to enforce caste positioning, which was a major theme being explored in a particular game.

    And I seem to remember about a game that was meant to be played by a small group of people holding hands in the dark, but I don't remember the details.

    \n Until We Sink is so elegant. It's such a beautiful mechanic, the getting up and leaving.

    At Solmukohta I was trapped in a cramped bathroom penned in by zombies, and I was supposed to be a guy who wanted to get away at any cost. So naturally I started freaking out and trying to "escape", having a giant panic attack and rushing the door, which would have killed us all had I opened it, and my fellow survivors had to hold me back. And I realized I was doing an awesome job of simulating a claustrophobe freaking out and had to say "Metagame: I'm totally fine", which was not nearly as cool as holding up an OK sign.

    \n In Jackson Tegu's Silver and White big changes happen in the game when the characters touch in any way. Different than the players, but very evocative in play.

    Also forgot about several roleplaying poems I wrote:

    At the end of The Believers, the players all close their eyes at the end and hold their palms out over the table. Palm up if they go with the aliens, and palm down if they stay. Then everyone opens their eyes.

    Eye contact also plays a part in this game.

    In Seance, players ask questions of the deceased and wait for an answer with everyone's hands held underneath the table. Someone secretly knocks once for "yes" or twice for "no".

    In a long running game I've been playing in we try and keep as much of the game in character as possible. Most of the time we use gestures like passing something across the table or shaking hands when our characters do the same. Stage whispers get used a fair bit. Sticking ones hand up to show they want to say something out of character with the more fingers up the more urgent it is.

    \n

    Every so often we all get up and stand in our relative positions and act out a scene to help the story along, although it gets a little awkward at times with the discrepancies between us the players and us the characters. One of the characters is a six foot five brick out house of a man played by someone considerably shorter and of a much less severe build which makes the action of hiding behind him kind of funny.
