    \n I'm not sure how much of an 'indie' minis scene exists, in the way we here might think of it. You've got a few major manufacturers, who make the minis and the rules that they support, then you've got a pile of smaller rules publishers and mini manufacturers. Sometimes those overlap, sometimes not. But there's often not much conceptual difference between the big and small writers. The big guys - Warhammer FB/40K, Warmachine/Hordes, Flames of War - tend towards simplicity and tournament-balance armies. The little guys tends towards 'use what you have/like' and some rules innovation. But in my experience, the difference between those is significantly less than the difference between, say, Champions and Apocalypse World.

    Here's some links to free rules you might want to check out:

    Infinity.

    Two Hour Wargames. Check their 'FREE!' section for 'Chain Reaction 3.0.

    Judge Dredd Miniatures Rules.

    To understand how those are different (or not, in some aspects) from the 'big guys', I encourage you to find/borrow/whatever a copy of Warhammer 40K, Warmachine or Flames of War, recent editions, and peruse those as well.

    \n There are some great gus, very active, who you might want to contact who can tell you a lot about what they did to publish in the indie minis game scene ( although i doubt they think of themselves as Indie anything). A number of those sites I mentioned, or their yahoo groups (including the 2Hour wargames guys that Noah D mentioned) are great places to make online contact.

    One thing I have noticed about indie minis games compared to indie SGs/RPGs: Indie rpgs, especially those associated with/talked about on sites like this have really developed a cultural attitude that is very good about describing not just the mechanics of the games, but also one of explaining very well the intent of how the game is meant to be played.

    Many times, indie minis games just give you the mechanics part, and are a bit short on the How is this Meant to be Played part. You need to do a bit of questioning to get to that second half, but it can be terribly enlightening when you do. It's almost as if the indie minis scene is still a bit stuck in the mindset it was in when D&D 0e was released. If you were ib the know with folks close to the designes you had one impression. The further you were removed from that, the more you might actually drift the intent a great deal without realizing it.

    \n Ah, gotcha!

    That type of more rpg-lite with minis is something that does ted to be found more with stuff that's more skirmishy, and especially with lower figure count games. Even the Games Workshop games that are more gang warfafre level seem to be more like that.

    Strange Aeons is another one that seems to go in that direction. The " Chaos in Carpathia/...Cairo/ etc" series from Blue Moon also. Don't know those ones personally, but have been reading threads on them elsewhere.

    They are the sorts of games I was thinking of though when I was talking about folks writing a bit incoherently where getting the message of "How I intend for you to play, if ya wanna play like I do" comes in.

    Howard Whitehouse's games, for example, are ones where you're vastly better off reading the advice type stuff that you are reading the rules mechanics. From asking questions of Howard and other folks on his Astounding Tales! yahoo group, his games are super, double-plus RPGish. (I'm thinking of his Astounding Tales! pulp rules in particular, but also his Victorian era Sudan War game)

    You wouldn't necessarily get that from the rulesbook, though. In fact, I got more info on how he plays from AP reports and questons at the yahoo group, that I think anyone could gleen from going over the purchased rulebook, at least for core important concepts of how to run a game.

    I think sometimes that the behaviors/playstyles in minis games that are more story game or rpg like aren't seen as often by folks because you kinda have to get out to conventions to see them, and you really need to look around for the people putting on certain kinds of small company or homebrew rules based games.

    There's still a lot of "legacy rules" think in the actual written dcuments for those games. IT's a little bit like watching folks in the RPG community trying to pound a sqaure peg design into a round hole for ages and ages, while not realizing there's a community of other folks who've already kinda taken up the banner of designing mechanics towards your core concept/style of play.

    Taking up that banner may well mean abandoning certain much-loved legacy mechanics.
