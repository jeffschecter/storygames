    \n Want to learn how to design a city? Attend Designing Cities, a free online course taught for ten weeks starting October 7, taught by professors at the University of Pennsylvania. Browsers welcome, but encouraged to do the three assignments. No pre-requisite background required (i.e., you don't have to have a degree in city planning, architecture, civil engineering, etc.).

    Want to learn how to program games? Attend General Game Playing, a free online course taught for 8 weeks starting September 30, and taught by professors at Stanford University. Requires understanding of symbolic logic and the ability to understand snippets of programs, but you don't have to be a programmer (but it helps).

    Also find at Coursera.org courses on game theory, futurism (search for TechniCity), gamification, history, and fantasy and science fiction.

    \nRunning a rules-light RPG for 12+ non-gamers

    I was asked to run a role-playing game at a friend's bachelor party a few months ago. I had a few constraints: there would be about 12 people at the session, hardly any of them had role-played before, and I had a few days to figure out what and how to run.

    I decided InSpectres would be the best bet. Fairly light rules, a comedy bent, lots of scope for players to decide where the game went.

    The session was a success. It was remarkable how quickly people took to it. There were only five players at any stage, and I played it prettily ruthlessly so that characters died often and were replaced by other people at the party. I also let non-players use the confessional booth whenever they wanted.

    But it was still a little too rules-heavy for non-gamers. In particular, the Stress rules (where bad results mean you reduce your skill levels) felt like too much pointless book keeping to a bunch of people who just wanted to hang out and drink beer.

    So I rewrote the Stress rules slightly. Here's the result: Four Realities and a Wedding.

    It draws a lot on our personal gaming history, and on the amazing Fiasco playset Alpha Complex by Dan Curtis Johnson. InSpectres by Jared Sorenson is available here.

    \n [Four Realities and a Wedding 1] Not sure if this is the place to give game feedback, but my problem with InSpectres has always been that unless you hit people with tons of Stress straight away and get their stats down, people roll sixes all the time and never fail anything. Removing that aspect of Stress without lowering the stats seems to me like it would make people only roll amazing successes all the time, which gets old. Failing in InSpectres is hella fun, after all, but with a skill on 2, it's already 1 in 36 to get that awesome '1' result on your roll.

    \n [Four Realities and a Wedding 2]

    Thanks for that, Simon. It's a good point.

    One thing to note is that I call for Stress tests for lots of other stuff aside from failed rolls. At one point in a very long running game of InSpectres, I could legitimately call for 4 and 5 dice Stress tests just because the office phone was ringing with another client.

    That said, I can see what you're saying about this hack reducing the frequency of catastrophic failures. It's reminding me that complexity of rolling skills was also almost (but not quite) too much for the bachelor party/non-gamers audience. If I run it again, I'll have a think about simplifying it in a way that addresses your concern.

    (Although, if anyone else has an idea, let me know!)
