    Me and a friend were discussing using something simple like rock-paper-scissors as the core mechanic.
    LARP's with firearms are very, very hard to run, systemwise - LARP's with stealth and firearms mixed (as I suppose this one would be) are even more so. Systems like RPS tend to very bad at it, since they cancel out the stealth element and block the firefight with long resolution. Long-range firearms fire pretty much cancels the use of RPS as well - and a lot of other systems as well. Negotiation/bidding -systems are clunky to use when you're shooting from cover, a vast distance away from someone.
    We also had this cool idea that people have HP, and that HP is measured in marbles.
    When you get shot (for say, seven marbles or something), you might fall to the ground wounded. You (or someone else) buries the marbles in the mud...
    The medic comes along. How healing works is he rummages around in the mud, and the marbles he retrieves you get to add back to your health. Maybe not all seven can be retrieved.
    This sounds very cool; another possibility might be that the marbles are hidden in players clothing; to cure a X point wound, you'd have to find so many marbles. That would ensure that the medic is actually working with the players body, not digging earth.
    3.) Suggest a way to run it freeform (never done this, don't know how it'd work in a confrontational setting)
    I'll try to tackle this one.

    I'd use pre-determined results for fighting. Maybe dealing several playing cards for the players - when in firefight, player checks his "fate-cards" - if it's red, shot misses, if it's black, it's a hit. The number on the card could be used for calculating the severity of the hit, or that could be left for player decision. Players can pretty much handle these things by themselves, judging the severity of the hit by factors like ranger, if it's stray fire, etc. The cards could also be used as a kind of meta-level resource to add suspense to the characters; a player getting a black card could have a bad feeling about this particular mission, sense of doom, and act according to it.

    As for the scenario, I'd focus on the physical enviroment. LARP is a very physical medium and in action-oriented wargame that should be the focus. You won't probably have much discussion and little social aspect; being in jungle, doing special forces -kind of stuff is the central point. So, if possible, I'd use a lot of time for transition between the "hotspots" and guide the players to do the stuff they've seen in war movies and tv-series. One man to the point, slow going, watching out for mines (excellent opportunity for propping, here), etc.

    For the hot spots, I'd drop the use of resolution mechanics, in mentioned way. Again, concentrating on the physical aspects; getting close to enemy unseen (and noting the NPC players that they really shouldn't be too alert), killing silently, and so on. One way of using this could be that if the encounter at the first guard post ends up in firefight, the guards at the actual base are more alert because they hear the shots. If the characters manage to do everything silently, the guards in base are more relaxed.

    Something like that.

    I've been thinking about the fate-card system for a while, because it would streamline things even more and would empower the players to make decisions. If the players would only receive a card that would tell them if their characters are having bad or good luck, they could decide when and how they get hit based on the situtation where the firefight occurs. Thus, if they get a bad luck card and later stumble into the enemy standing up, in the open, they could decide that they get hit really badly. With good luck card, they could decide that they only get a flesh wound. If, on the other hand, they have a bad luck card and are well covered, they could decide that they get a flesh wound from stray fire; with good luck card, no hit. This would remove the element of needing to dictate whom you are shooting; if the groups are further away from each other, camouflaged, taking cover, et cetera, it could hard to tell whom you are shooting. Also, the shouting could be replaced with firecrackers or some safe devices that go bang.

    I haven't tested this out, just played with the idea, though. Obviously, this kind of system does not take into account character skill when it comes to shooting. In messy jungle firefight this could probably be taken into account by giving the NPC's worse cards and maybe some kind of rule for aimed shots. Here one could use shouts like "Bob, aimed shot to head" or maybe beamers or some other marking things to notify that someone has been targeted and hit.
