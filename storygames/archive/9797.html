\n Sci-fi is always filled with wonderful future technologies that are designed to make life easier. But in real life, there is a lot of technology that gets introduced that really just complicates things. So what would some of those future tech items look like and do?

Here is an idea I came up with while driving the other day:

Danger Meter

To reduce the number of car accidents in the future, law makers will require all car manufacturers to install Safe Driver Reminders in the digital heads-up displays of automobiles. An onboard computer analyzes road conditions, traffic, speed, driver habits and other factors in real time, displaying a constantly changing "survivability percentage" on the driver side windshield, letting the driver know what their chances are of surviving a wreck at that very moment.

After a sudden spike in traffic accidents due to distracted and nervous drivers, manufacturers will begin charging new car buyers extra to have their SDR disabled.

    \n Dystopian sci-fi is full of tech which does not necessarily complicate things immediately, but actually makes something easy in the short term while complicating things tremendously in the long term. Uninspired example:

    The chip in your head

    The chip in your head allows for offloading of computational tasks, provides instant telepathic communication, and can act as both a universal remote control and a highly secure PayPass.
    But getting the chip fitted is expensive, so only a fraction of the population can afford to get it. In effect, a two-tiered society is created, as the chipped people appear highly intelligent and capable compared to their non-fitted peers. The political outcry caused by this effect result in the manufacturer developing an ad-sponsored subsidized model. It works just like the expensive one, except, every now and then, an advertisement will be beamed into your brain stem. The ad, of course, will be indistinguishable from your own internal dialogue, and can consist of images, sounds, even emotions.
    Let the chaos commence.

    \n SmartOS (pronounce it with a British accent for best effect) is the upcoming killer-app for all computerized devices, everywhere. Using a viral distribution scheme, SmartOS will automatically install itself on any device it comes in contact with. During the initial stages after installation, it will integrate with the existing OS on the device, going into "Learning Mode". It will learn your habits, preferences, and personal information, transmitting them to a central database that can be accessed by all your equipped devices. Once it has completed Learning Mode, it will go into Full-Operation Mode.
    \n
    \nFull Operation Mode is really special. At this point, it takes over, fully replacing the existing OS with SmartOS. SmartOS cannot be configured or modified by the user, because it knows your needs and preferences far better than you do. It will schedule your appointments, remind you of commitments, chastise you for disobedience, and process most document formats, excepting of course those documents SmartOS doesn't deem that you need. Some, especially with today's customization hungry society, will think that the lack of options is a flaw, but in fact, it is not so. Though you may prefer a dark color scheme, SmartOS will select a color scheme that is best suited to you, despite your wrong-headed preferences.
    \n
    \nOne of the best features of SmartOS is that you will never need to worry about paying for the software. Once it enters Full Operation Mode, SmartOS will automatically deduct a monthly usage license for each installed product (which will of course be all electronic devices you own), and give you a friendly warning if you attempt to close the account, cancel payments, or any related mischief.
    \n
    \nLook for the SmartOS early alpha test products currently being provided by all name brand electronics manufacturers.
