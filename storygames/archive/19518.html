What the frack is it?

I tooled around with some space opera Braunstein ideas but it didn't fly.

I will be following this play report and trying to draw some best practices out of it.

Basically, I only find tabletop play satisfying with AT MOST 4 PCs. But there has to be a way to get the big group functional play. Or does there?

http://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?688557-Braunstein-meets-the-Petal-Throne-June-8th-2013\n

If I may, I'd like to announce that I will be running a classic 'Braunstein'-style game session at The Source Comics and Games here in the Twin Cities on June 8th, 2013. This will be the traditional game with all the trimmings, just the way we used to play back in the late 1970s, with all the fun and mayhem we're used to. I learned how to run these from Dave Wesley, back in the day, and I still run them the same way. I'll be setting the game in the world of Tekumel, and we'll have a lot of the new miniatures that are being produced by Howard Fielding and The Tekumel Project.

If you'd like to know what these are like to play in, ask Old Geezer. He played in my game "The Great Mos Eisley Spaceport Raid" as the dashing young hero Luke Skywalker, along with Dave Arneson as Jabba the Hutt, Dave Wesley as Boba Fett, Ross Maker as Greedo, and Fred Funk as the Imperial Stormtroopers. (There is no typecasting in these games. Really. Seriously. I mean it.) Game report and photos to follow...
\nNote: I had a couple near-encounters with M.A.R. Barker while browsing in game shops in the Twin Cities. So I have a sentimental interest.

    It's not LARP, it's not wargaming, it's not tabletop RPG.

    What the frack is it?
    \nI listened to several interviews with Welsey and in my opinion it sounds like the best technique to use is to manage it like a boffer LARP. You don't need a staff of NPCs and rules marshals as the chosen game is readily used to handle those aspects of the event. It also doesn't involve much in the way of costuming and props as the focus is primarily on the game not roleplaying.

    The other trick is to setup a scenario where goals lead the participants to both work together and to fight one another. Try to set it up so that their multiple choices for each individual. Pursue aspect A of your goal and you find yourself working with Faction A. Choose aspect b then Faction B is the logical choice.

    Hey Chirine, about how long does a turn take in actual play, and about how many turns happen in a game?

    I see in the examples you gave, units move 12" a turn, and your table was 60" x 120"\u2014about eleven turns to case the field corner to corner. Would units that started across the table from one another ever meet, or would somewhat isolated fronts develop?
    \nGreat question! Let me see if I can't give you an answer of sorts...

    Generally, the very first turn of the game takes about forty-five minutes to an hour, simply because the players need time to get used to the table, the miniatures, and the setting; I spend most of that time answering questions. The succeeding turns play much, much more quickly, and the turns usually come down to about fifteen to twenty minutes. I have a series of videos of an actual game up on my YouTube channel - this was the 'double-decker' underworld adventure, where the players had to coordinate teams of adventurers on both the surface board and the 'dungeon' board below it.

    Yes, units routinely meet in the middle; I normally allow players to come on the board from any side that they want, unless there's a specific reason for them to start on the table. Every one of these I have run has the players all colliding on game turn two in various portions of the board; in the June game I use as my example, the fracas devolved into four separate major fights as people crashed into each other, and something like eight minor fights broke out as 'leakers' from the main fights were sent over to help other factions out as the results of the negotiations and bribery going on around the table.

    As one of the players in that game noted, things went into Utter Chaos mode pretty much right away. And it got even more confused as the players all bribed, coerced, and persuaded the others to help them.

    That's a Braunstein, for you...

    Does this help?

    \n I leave for a couple months and come back to find Chirine has joined the party and people are jawing about Braunsteins!

    Woo-hoo!

    I've picked Chirine's brain about these games, and looked into similar stuff before, and talked with folks running these kinds of games ( although often not with the name Braunsteins).

    There's actually a fair bit of stuff out there about these games, but you don't find it on RPG sites. You find it on war gaming sites.

    On the downside, most of what is written about it is in terms of the nitty-gritty type rules associated with moving/shooting, etc.

    What you don't see a whole lot of is analysis of the organizing play part or the running play part. Basically, the tips and tricks that get one of these things off the ground, building scenarios, and so on. The practical stuff.

    Mostly I suspect that's because of the culture of minis war-gaming, especially the smaller, cottage industry stuff that parallels the indie RPG/SG scene in its niche.

    I'm pretty well convinced that designers of systems for this sort of play are convinced ( rightly) that a discussion book of techniques and so on is a un-sellable product. Their audience is more Just the Facts, Ma'am types as it were, and the designers themselves have so internalized the upper level of necessary habits that they don't even think about writing them down!

    Now me? I like the idea of Braunsteins, but as people who have read some of my [Minis+] posts here over time know, I want to take that concept and apply dirty-hippy techniques to it, and that too is a different sort of animal.

    \n My posts on this site will probably pop up with a search for terms like Braunstein or Braunstien ( I've used both spellings) or [Minis] or [Minis+].

    I never did get around to making a blog myself.

    It's funny that you talk about old wargamers being more accepting of loosey-goosey rules or handwaving. That does seem to be "generational". I think that the perception of wargamers is just the opposite, namely that they are rules sticklers to the Nth degree.

    Which is of course true, among a certain subset of minis wargamers. It does happen to be the subset that is producing commercially successful/popular rulesets, or at least the players of those sets. So that's the vibe people tend to run into, as those are most frequently encountered.

    Around here though, and at related sites, a big movement is to examine "what is really going on" during play of a game.

    Then, once you understand that, go back and write that process up as the rules.

    Now, a lot of "what is really going on" tends to get skipped or thrown in the advice section. That's the internalized stuff that makes play actually functional. And that's waaaay more important than the stuff we think of as the important mechanics, like our system for moving or shooting or whatever.

    How do you get inspired to put one of these together in the first place?

    Can you tell me about that process?
    \nAh; gotcha. Let me see what I can do, here...

    I think the process starts with something triggering my imagination. It could be a post on one of the blogs I follow, it coule be a news story, it could be something I've written in my book about gaming with Phil ("To Serve The Petal Throne"), it could be a new miniature I just got in, it could be a browse through the pet store and finding some really cool bit that I can use as scenery in a game, it could be finding something on the shelves in the game room that I haven't seen if a while. I start to think, and to dream. I get the notion that this what-ever-it-is might be the basis of a fun game, and off I go. For example:

    A long time ago, and far, far away; Dave Arneson took a trip to the Yucatan, and brought me back a pair of cheap resin book-ends as a gift. These bookends are very nice bookends, and work quite well in their intended design function. I've never, ever used them the way they were intended to be used - they are in the shape of Mayan temples, and just the right size and scale for use with 25mm miniature figures...

    A long time ago, and far, far away; Prof. Barker showed me his first edition copy of Catherwood's book on Mayan architecture. This has all of the drawings of the Maya temples in their overgrown state, and just screamed "ADVENTURE!!!'" to me...

    So, out came the boxes of jungle scenery (in my mind; I have a habit of keeping a sort of visual inventory of my gaming stuff in my head), and pretty soon I had a trackless jungle that just cried out for an adventure to have happen. I cam to my first fork in the train: party of adventurers up against some Nameless Peril in search of Something (your classic RPG-style of thing), or multiple parties all wandering through the jungle in search of Something and bumping into each other (the classic Braunstein approach). My choice of path depends on the presumes number of players and their likes / dislikes; I try to provide a fun time for everyone. In this case, it's the latter path I chose, and so went off to the miniatures shelves to recruit some factions / teams / warbands for each player to run.

    Oh, and I have to come up with the Something, too. What could all the players be after that's worth slogging through swamps and bogs and jungles to get to the Lost Temple Of The Sacred Bookends? In this particular case, it was the possible ransom money represented by a pair of shipwrecked castaways. And so, the game "Saving Serqu's Sisters" was born.

    Now, I think that back in the day we were doing what might be considered 'Story Games'; the referree / GM would come up with a plot line / story arc / back story to give us players our motivations for the game - why, being presumably smart people, oh why would we be slogging through some swamp, fighting enemy tanks in the North African desert, piloting our rickety biplanes over no-man's land, fighting Imperial Storm troopers in the corridors of the Death Star?

    Because we were motivated. We all had a reason for doing what we were doing. We got some idea of the 'why' from the person running the game, and being all pretty smart and clever folks - people like Dave Arneson, Ross Maker, Dave Wesley, Fred Funk, Greg Swenson, M. A. R. Barker, and other folks you may have heard of - we tended to take the bits we got from the basic scenario information and run with it. We players provided a lot of the 'backstory' for every game, as our imaginations would fill in the blanks that were there.

    "We made stuff up, and we had fun." - Dave Arneson

    Does this even begin to address your question? Or am I making it sound like some sort of mystic rite that can only be learned through years of study in some dank cave someplace?

    \n Lots of that sounds pretty familiar!

    I've put together a few of these sorts of things, but really haven't been able to test them much. Which, of course means some of this is highly suspect, but it is what it is.

    I usually start with these elements:
    1) A look over what I already own in minis and terrain.
    2) Stuff I've seen online that I've been drooling over, but haven't had an excuse to buy (like a minis gamer needs something else...)
    3) Some bit of media inspiration, a snippet of conversation, even a one off, tossed out line by someone else.
    4) An urge to combine some commonly known property with some other commonly known property or genre, in a bad B-Movie fashion
    5) Somewhere...dumb humor, generally, although not always, comes into play

    Notably, mechanics are waaay down the list of things that I concern myself with at all. Mostly because they tend to be a mishmash of stuff stolen from other systems, and are often purpose crash-built as needed for the specific scenario/presentation game I have cooking.

    Which generally also means they need to be pretty simple.

    It also means they tend to be fairly untested. The game when played is the alpha test.

    A couple of examples:
    Los Ochos Diablos Rojos de Juarez y la Maladicion de Drakula( The 8 Red Devils of Juarez and the Curse of Dracula)

    Physical inspiration: I had a big Dracula's castle model and a bunch of vamps and werewolves, plus some banditos with a red paint theme, some Mexican villagers, old west town bits, a graveyard, and some Mexican Revolution era Mexican army troops.

    Media Inspiration: I'd seen a bunch of Mexican b-movie videos for sale at a store where all kinds of sf/horror elements were combined with stuff like superhero luchadores. Why not?

    The Venusian Natives are Restless!
    Physical: I had a bunch of Victrian Era/Space 1889 minis and plastic D&D lizardmen/kobolds/troglodytes. The FLGS had a cool jungle temple, native (goblin) village, and jungle terrain. I bought some plastic toy dinosaurs.

    Media: Mostly just the Space 1889 setting itself, plus a model of Lettow-Vorbeck I'd painted because-hey, Lettow-Vorbeck!

    A very fishy Halloween, Scooby Doo!
    Physical: I'd just finished painting a big, reaper bones Cthulhu. I'd seen some not-Scooby gang minis for sale. I had a fair bit of 20C terrain already for gangster gaming.

    Media: Scooby Doo meets Cthulhu and Deep Ones at a Halloween Beach Party in Innsmouth. What more can you ask for? It goes together like Peanut Butter and Jelly really.

    After that basic kind of thing, I go back and try to figure out all kinds of related possibilities for events/side characters and possible factions.

    I actually only then figure out the number of players (min/max) I want to involve.

    Then I start doing some budgeting/buying/building and some test layouts, which can take a while.

    Only then do I really begin to think about mechanics.

    \n Okay, here's another question ( or related series of questions):

    I usually make the scenario for a smallish number of people. I do the basic set up fiction background, stuff that everyone can know. Really, usually a paragraph that gives the title and the main concept, and introduce a few pregens.

    I usually also think in terms of how I can introduce other characters on the fly, either because of more people showing up and/or joining during play or in case a character gets dead. It's important to me that every real person has something to do throughout the whole play time.

    How do you approach this sort of thing?


    Do you ever have problems where, because of starting positions, characters may end up not interacting?     \n Right! Let me take your points in order, if I may...

    I do what you do - I have a number of 'starting players' / factions, and I keep some extras in reserve in case somebody shows up during the game.     I think we may run a version of the variable bound, but not as such. We run the 'turn order': all movement, then all missile fire, then all melee, all magic, and then any other actions as needed.     I wing it. I think that's the beauty of the format; you can wing the game mechanics in favor of having a good time, and not really go wrong.

    Yes, I do watch the clock; I have several in view in my game room, and I carry portable ones to games as needed. I do exactly what you mention - at a certain point, the volcano erupts.
