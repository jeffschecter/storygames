SHORTLIST FOR 2006 DIANA JONES AWARD ANNOUNCED
An RPG, a board-game, a TCG, an ARG, a contest and a charity vie for hobby-gaming's most exclusive trophy

London - The committee of the Diana Jones Award has released the shortlist for its 2006 award. This year the shortlist contains six nominees that in the opinion of the committee exemplify the best that hobby-gaming can offer. In alphabetical order, they are:

- The Game Chef game-design competition
- Irish games convention charity auctions
- Magic the Gathering, 9th edition (Wizards of the Coast)
- Perplex City (Mind Candy Ltd)
- Spycraft 2.0 by Patrick Kapera, et al (Alderac Entertainment Group)
- Twilight Struggle by Ananda Gupta and Jason Matthews (GMT Games)

The winner of the 2006 Award will be announced on Wednesday 9th August, at the annual Diana Jones Award and Freelancer Party in Indianapolis, the unofficial start of the Gen Con Indy convention.

For more: http://www.dianajonesaward.org/

I especially like this part, from the "reasons why":

THE GAME CHEF CONTEST
The Game Chef Contest was invented at the Forge website by Mike Holmes in 2002, based on an earlier contest at Gaming Outpost run by Clinton R. Nixon and Jared A. Sorensen, and is now an annual event, run independently at its own website (http://game-chef.com/) by Andy Kitkowski. Each year the competition has spawned a number of powerful, widely diverse RPGs and semi-RPGs and is now one of the best break-in points for new game designers and small press publishers. One of its strongest features is its development of a community of review and interest in one another's projects. Game Chef now serves, effectively, as a grass-roots equivalent of the Origins Awards. The results not only reward the past year, but generate effort and participation toward the upcoming year.

  • Mike_Holmes
    May 2006 edited May 2006
    Clinton, when Andy asked me to do a blurb about where Iron Game Chef began, I frankly couldn't remember precisely how it had gotten started. I rememberd participating in a couple of events there, especially one run by Sean Wipfli that I won mostly because I think my game was the only entry (or one of very few). Anyhow, I looked back, and, sure enough, there was a post about how you, Clinton, and Jared had come up with this idea together, from which all the other IGC action that followed flowed from.

    It's your idea - yours and Jared's. From all the evidence I could find. In fact, I believe that this pre-dates the Iron Game Designer competitions held at conventions, too. So, credit, where credit is due.

    Meaning mostly to Andy, however. That is, while we all had parts in coming up with the format, it has to be said that today's very successful IGC events are due more to the product of Mt. Kitowski's efforts, than to anyone else's.

    Well...actually what's interesting is how Andy has turned some of the responsibility back on the designers. In large part, the accolades for IGC should go to the participants who are what really make it happen. Especially today with the peer review rule. I think it's not coincidental that IGC was not recognized until the peer review system was instituted. I think it's everyone working together that makes it a great event.


    Unsurprisingly I've put a lot of thought into trying to be able to state what the value of IGC is all about. John Kim has a reasonable gripe when he notes that in some ways all IGC means is a ton of new entries into his encyclopedia each year - that they don't individually average much in the way of actual play or publishing that comes from them. Heck, the way the judging works, one of the criteria is how complete the game is, realizing that in one week it's pretty much impossible to create a game that's got everything you need to play.

    Simply, Iron Game Chef puts out half-baked games. Lots of them. So is that doing any good?

    But it does do a lot of good. Even if few of these games are touched up later, some few are, and they tend to be pretty good. But that's not even the most important benefit that we get out of IGC as I see it. We get two things.

    One, we get people experience in designing, and in the role of designers in amongst a community of designers. That is, the games are open to scrutiny by others in the process, and that means that we can all see how open designing can work. Did anyone steal Mountain Witch before Tim got it to press? Because it was presented in the open? No. I think that IGC has helped promote an openness in design that's important because everyone benefits. Designs made by people in this community go under rigorous examination by many before being passed on even to playtesting, for instance.

    Not just IGC designs, but any made by people of this community. This is actually an effect of The Forge, truth be told, and IGC just helps to reinforce this, I think. The idea that, to create a new and innovative game, you have to do so in the context of all of the other RPGs that exist. Seems obvious on the face of it, but somehow RPG design has always been the the purview of sheltered individuals in the majority, previous to The Forge.

    The people who get propelled into design from IGC almost always become avid critics and aids in constructing other people's RPGs. Even if they never publish, they often become valuable members of the community -if they weren't already. For those who are already avid members, I think it just reinforces this sort of participation.

    The second benefit is that new ideas are constantly coming out of IGC games. Maybe not entire games always, but you see bits of IGC games showing up in other RPGs. Real innovation occurs as a result of the pressure cooker that IGC provides. While it's not always functional as presented, the ideas become grist for the mill of other designers to use in thier designs. And I think we're seeing tangible benefits coming from that in many designs.

    It's like a skunk works that produces lots of devices that don't get used neccessarily in the context for which they were designed, but get used by somebody else down the road.

    That's what I see as the benefits of the IGC. Just thought this might be an appropriate time to put that out there.

    Thanks,
    Mike
