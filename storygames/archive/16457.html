Play examples: real or fake?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nRafael \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2012 \n edited May 2012 in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In the past, I've gone through the trouble of recording actual play sessions, and then typing them up, and then inserting this text into my RPGs as examples of play. But let's face it, it's a lot of work. And my gaming group... well, they're decent people, but man, they love to talk about the Avengers. And Mass Effect 3. And Game of Thrones. It's not something I notice at the table, but when I'm transcribing, it's just a ton of work to cut all that stuff out.

So, what do you do? Do you record, then transcribe/edit? Or are your play examples faked, in order to provide an ideal play session that serves as a picture-perfect tutorial for your reader?

Personally, I like the idea of a faked play example, and I'm thinking about doing it, because it just seems so much more logical than my current approach. But I'm curious to hear what others have to say, as I've never seen any discussion of this (though if you can point me to a relevant thread, I shall read it, of course).

    \n Depends on the use. If it's an illustration of a point on the rules, I'll fake it. If it's a purportedly true story illustrating some deeper point, I'll do my best to adapt a real experience. I won't bother with recording, I'll just narrate from memory. Often enough make some minor changes to strip down unnecessary complications or personal details that'd obscure the point.

    I'd say that it would be a good practice to not use people's names in an artificial hypothetical visualization of play. Call them A and B and C, or call them "me" and "you" like people have done lately. Giving names feels too misleading to me, it implies that you're discussing a real case even when you're not. I guess it's just about acceptable in the common style where the same example runs through the entire game text, as there it's so obvious that it's written to order.

    And of course, if the point is to examine a genuine exchange in an article, say, then it's obviously not kosher to invent your material. But for most purposes in a game text, why not.

    \n I've always assumed that play examples are to some degree invented in order to illustrate a particular concept. Of course, I've also been assuming that the person writing the examples has played the game as well as watched other people play it, so this:
    Posted By: Jason Morningstarretelling of actual events, often adjusted a little to demonstrate some concept or another. So they start as actual play that really happened and get turned into something different.
    ...seems to be pretty much exactly what I expect from a play example. Play the game, figure out what example the random person reading your book (ie, me) would benefit from knowing, and write that up. Use as much or as little of what the actual people playing the game did or said as you want; as long as the example is easy to follow and useful, you've done right by the reader. I don't need to know their real names or anything about them, I don't need to believe that they are real, living, breathing human beings...I just need to be able to figure out whatever it is the example is supposed to help me figure out.

    \n I'm dealing with this question right now. I want to take the biographer's approach and basically create a composite of things that I have seen more or less happen in a real game, with the minimum of fakery. The problem is that it's not enough to just have an example -- you need the example to indirectly answer as many questions as possible that people might ask you about the rules. (That aren't, for one reason or another, worth writing an extra paragraph in the rules for.) For example, my example of play for Dog Eat Dog needs to address:

    * What's a reasonable scope for Traits?
    * How do you go from a Trait to a character concept?
    * What if none of the Traits call out to me for a concept?
    * What does "richest" mean, anyway?
    * I haven't played Settlers of Catan. How does this "one order, then reverse order" thing work?

    Some of these questions I don't want to nail down because it might be too restrictive (or in at least one case because I just want people to have to think about it). Some of them would be immensely difficult to expand upon except by providing an example. But they're not all likely to come up in an actual session, so to some degree I have to fake it. But hopefully not too much.

    \n I tend to synthesize a number of play experiences together, creating a synthetic frankenstein example.

    Part of the reason is that each isolated play experience tends to have some subpar moments, or moments that are underscored by group convention but might be mystifying for an outsider. Another reason is that, for lots of types of games, it's uncommon for a single session of play to hit on each important mechanical bit/procedure/question.

    In short, I don't think telling someone what actually happened is the best way to tell someone what actually happens.

    There's a great quote from a Canadian author, whose name I can't recall right now: "If you want to tell the truth, you've got to write fiction." Semi-applicable here.

    \n I like it when the example is a "real story" of play, even if details like names are changed (and I really like Artman's method of both short alphabetical names and alliterative characters).

    By "real story", I mean that it's based on an actual occurance of play but is edited to demonstrate a point.

    And by the way - that's what a story is. Something happens, and then a narrative construction is developed in order to encapsulate that event into a story. The story of what happened is not what happened - it's how you tell what happened, which is a different thing.

    And usually I can tell when a real story is being used because the players in the story will say a couple of quirky "truth is stranger than fiction" things during the narrative - presumably the interesting things that stood out in the writer's mind and made them remember that instance.

    If those kinds of things aren't there, the play story usually ends up reading like it's an awkward 4th grade school play about some historical (or pseudo-historical) event. "We are the pilgrims. We came to America on a ship called the Mayflower. We are the Native Americans. We bring you corn to share. We are thankful. Every year we will celebrate this day and call it Thanksgiving. Yay!"

    \n I think it depends as well on what the play example is trying to illustrate. If It's just about clarifying the execution of a particular rules process, a confected example is probably a cleaner and more focused way of going about things. If it's about demonstrating a wider issue like how the game plays, real life examples carry more depth and feel more solid on the page. A game consisting entirely of focus-grouped made up play examples (cf D&D3e) feels less accessible.

    I always liked HeroQuest 1's play examples. They're colourful enough to convince you of the merits of Glorantha but also contain enough wacky tangents and OOC discussions to puncture the idea of it being this really po-faced, pretentious, highly detailed and anthropologically correct MYTHIC SETTING THAT YOU MUST GET RIGHT. I think a lot of the subsequent books shat on this idea, but it was nice while it lasted.
