    I'd add "Complication: Political pressure." In a big way.

    You probably also need a -style mechanic for creating crimes and webs of witnesses, informants, suspects, etc. -- generating those on the fly can lead to really ugly consistency problems. You don't need a GM to do this: You could deal out cards face-down, or something.

    Pedantic note: There's a critical distinction between the police procedural (your model) and the "mystery" genre overall, vs. the hardboiled detective story (e.g. LA Confidential, The Big Sleep). In the former, which lends itself to Simulationist play, the assumption is that the world is basically just, and the criminals are a problematic element to be rooted out, putting the focus on the tactics and costs of finding out Who Did The Bad Thing. In the latter, which lends itself to Narrativist play, the point is that crime and criminals which the investigator (and the criminals) discover initially turn out to be part of afar larger web that challenges the notion that society is just and people are decent, so the focus is on the difference between "immoral" and "illegal."

    I don't know about "Scotland Yard" but what I have in mind is something like "Clue".

    Here's an idea I had.

    Each player has one suspect.

    Each suspect can have "Means," "Motive," and "Opportunity" cards played on him. "Clue" plays can put these cards on a character, move them from one suspect to another, or remove them from a character. When a suspect has all three on him, he can be moved into "Police custody".

    Each player also controls one investigator.

    If your investigator is the one that puts together the case and gets a conviction, that's one way to win.

    If your suspect is the one that's caught, that's another way to win. The game has one or two winners.
