\n So I used my new favorite tool to populate a train in Deadlands. One of the guys came up as suspicious drinker, sitting sullenly at the bar looking over his shoulder incessantly. I had no idea who he was or what he was doing there. One of our PCs works for the Agency, so when someone acts suspicious it's a nice, flashing arrow for her. She asked if she had seen this guy before. "Yeah, he looks familiar, but you can't place him." She sidled up to him to find out more and ended up freaking him out. He turned tail and ran, she gave chase and er... interrogated. Turns out this sad sack used to work for the Agency and was given a special project for them. In attempting to carry it out, he burned down nearly a sixth of Denver after releasing a malevolent spirit from a crystal. All this from a random NPC. For me, connecting the dots is a way more fun way to GM than the play-through-my-unfinished-novel mode. I must add more to my diet!

    \n Last Kerberos Fate session had the PCs courting, being courted, or watching courtships as Queen Victoria married.

    Lady Alice Beauchamps complained about the debauched, scandalous, yet honorable Lord George Mace's habit of calling her Lady Cassandra (as she tends to have visions of dangers to come).

    Alice: He still persists in getting my name wrong. It's most irritating!

    Sophronia, the Clockwork Faerie: You could tell him,

    Alice: I think he knows.

    Sophronia: I could spell it out in fiery letters above his head.

    Alice declined the offer.

    Lord Gregory Sage, Heir of Pennsington (who is actually Lord Sage's servant, taking his master's place so that the master can live happily with his wife in Africa) is courting Sophronia, the 9 inch clockwork faerie.

    Player: You realize you're courting a nine year old, right?

    Sophronia: Yes, but I was born with my current level of maturity

    Gregory: While it is near the bottom, it is on my list of things to worry about.

    Player: That she's nine or that she acts her age?

    Gregory: That's nearer the top of the list.

    Player: Not that you're the Earl of Pennsington?

    Gregory: That's the top of my list.

    Gregory's player asked Sophronia's player to see her character sheet for a moment.

    Sophronia's player: I don't think it's absolutely fair to have access to a lady's character sheet.

    Lady Alice disguised herself, taking on the identity of Lord Alec Campion, as she often does. (Why, yes, we are fans of Ellen Kushner's works.) In this identity, she paid Lord Mace a visit, mentioning that Lady Alice's cousin (and head of the family) didn't want Lady Alice going off on adventures before she was safely married, and generally intimating that she might perhaps like Lord Mace to be her husband. Lord Mace said that Lady Alice, if she did have someone in particular in mind, should make sure that her messenger would make her intent clear and unmistakeable. And that is why Lord Campion kissed Lord Mace in Lord Mace's box at the opera. You see, there is a good reason for everything.

    But, there were other things than romance on people's minds. Victor, for instance, decided he no longer needed the maid that Abraham Lincoln said he should hire (long story, and Young Lincoln is no longer a PC), so he turned to Gregory to see that she could find another situation and not go back to being a prostitute. Gregory told Sophronia that the maid, Lydia, should clean the house Sophronia is living in with her creator / father, who is working to give sentience to a mechanical giant (Talos -- Sophronia has decided that Talos is her cousin) that a colleague of his with anarchist ties created. The idea was that the maid could get caught up in schemes of the anarchist leader, Croyle, and be pumped regularly for information by Gregory. Sophronia wasn't sure about this plan.

    Sophronia: Our rehabilitation skills need much to be desired.

    Gregory: Croyle has not yet killed anyone that I know of.

    Sophronia (indignant): He took Talos from me!

    Gregory: He's not totally disagreeable, mreely mostly disagreeable.

    Sophronia: He's a cousinnapper!

    Gregory: Fair enough!

    Sophronia: I do not think most maids want to blow things up!

    Player: It's a much quicker way of getting things clean, however.

    Gregory: Ah, I see where I am miscommunicating.

    He explained that he was quite sure that she wouldn't be doing anything with bombs; more likely, she'd be distributing pamphlets.

    Sophronia: I will be very upset if I find out that you have convinced Victor's maid to get involved in blowing things up! It's very dangerous!

    \n We played our second session of End of the Empire (Fantasy Flight's new Star Wars RPG) last night, with five in-person players and a virtual player as a laptop/skype combo. It was fantastic. We did a sort of linked-test interrogation where each player was taken aside and grilled by Imperial Customs which led to the ship being scanned and some cargo being confiscated.

    The next scene was a blaster-filled, chaotic escape from the Imperial Interdictor in true Star Wars fashion.

    The game is giving me all kinds of interesting thoughts about cultural touchstones. I mean, I can say "you're going to Nar Shadda" and everyone in the group (because we're all Star Wars nerds) knows exactly what that means. Everyone has this rich background to draw on and so can get excited at a name instead of me, as GM, having to say "which means..." and explain what Nar Shadda is about. It's the closest I've ever been, I think, to that sense of the characters and players sharing an equal knowledge base about the universe the game is set in.

    \n Organized a game of Sign in Stranger at SGSeattle last night. It was really fun, though it was just an introductory session to show off the game. Our Peace-Corps-in-space members were doing something vaguely like cave diving (gaff dakking) for the slime-armed Clodhoppers who spoke the language Kizzizzizzaz. We had the hardest time finding food!     \n With Halloween coming and us usually enjoying playing teenagers one of my groups started a three session game of Monsterhearts. The first two are over now. The first was mostly setup and character creation also learning the game and all that. I as the MC did not enjoy it as much as I hoped for that session. I felt rather limited by the Apocalypse World system and the Moves system at my disposal and also had to get into the rules. It almost seemed like a chore to me and one of the few games I'd much rather be a player in than a GM.
    Still over the week I thought about it and planned with the little town and the characters we set up and came up with an idea and a big bad for the Chosen we have in the group. Gained some confidence and motivatione back and was ready to try it again.
    Todays game really was fun. The bickering and interactions between the characters and npcs really was what we looked for in the system and some perfect comedic timing really made it a joy to play. Also the plot ideas I had worked out well within the stuff the players were doing and all the choices mattered. I am warming up to the Hard Moves too, one of them did bring in a whole new element to the game and now one of the characters has to get rid of drugs. I liked the conditions as well. With stuff like "Drugmule" and "In Love" causing various troubles.
    Three player characters seems a little low though. I'd rather play it with four or five I suppose.

    \n Grammar and romance seemed to be the topics of interest in Kerberos Fate.

    Victor: "On" I think is the preposition you were looking for, Alice.

    Lady Alice: With what does Victor agree?


    Sophronia, the 9-inch Clockwork Fairy to Gregory, the shapeshifter: How do you expect me to argue with a duck? Turn into something reasonable!

    He turned into a platypus.


    Gregory to Victor; No! Nonono! One of these days, I will finish a sentence in front of you!


    Victor (about one of Alice's suggestions): I suggested that ten minutes ago.

    Gregory: I couldn't hear you -- you were too loud.


    Alice (talking about psychic mediums they know after a young nobleman has asked for her to use her psychic talents on his project, rather than on her more urgent one): Do we have to count the one in Africa? (nb: He is British and rather a putz. I suspect Lady Alice would be perfectly happy to count a competent African medium, if she happened to know one.)

    Victor: If we want to make it plural. But there is Dominic Creahador. Who hates all nobles.

    Player (obviously Out of Character): We should introduce him to Dominic Creahador. They can have a buddy movie.


    Gregory (as he's trying to court Sophronia): I'm afraid I'm badly botching this.

    Sophronia's player: Sophronia kisses you.


    It only remained for Gregory to be browbeaten into telling his mother the news about his ladylove.

    Gregory: There is one who outshines the rest.

    Gregory's Mother: And does this solar paragon have a name?

    Gregory: If it were anyone other than you, I'd say to sit down.

    Gregory's Mother (dramatizing for effect): Oh! She has a name. I must sit down at once!


    Gregory's Mother (on learning the details): Does the anatomy work?

    Gregory: We're still figuring that out. (realizes how that sounds)


    Now, Gregory did not think at all kindly of Lord Winston Beauchamp, Alice's cousin. Nevertheless, he found himself in an odd position when Winston filled Gregory's mother in on just what Gregory's important Save the World (from the Martians) mission was.

    Gregory (as Winston leads his mother into a dance so that they can talk): Thank you very much, Lord Beauchamp. (beat) That is not a sentence I expected to utter tonight. Or any time this century. (NB: The year is 1840.)


    Gregory, aka the Pennsington Heir (sort of) to Sophronia: How would you like to be the new Lady Pennsington?

    Sophronia (flustered): Can we work on there still being a British Government first? (on account of the Martian problem, naturally)


    I'd originally thought the PCs would sail from Britain this session, but realized that Josh was correct and there was far too much to do in London. I figure they've got a 50-50 shot of getting away next session, though Lady Alice will have to explain to her cousin that she plans to marry the Odious Lord Mace, who kissed Alec Campion at the opera, Alec Campion being, unknown to Winston, Lady Alice in disguise (not that Lord Mace hasn't kissed rather a lot of men, as well as rather a lot of women, not to mention doing rather a lot more than kissing....)

    \nApocalypse World:
    After the generation ship's society collapsed, the only knowledge of past society comes from my PC's religious teachings based off a couple old superhero comics.

    The session began with a PC Quarantine being integrated into our little hardhold. No one was certain what to make of this guy, or if he was a friend or foe. Signs pointed toward "foe" as he worked for the US Military (which is always incompetent or evil in superhero stories.) Guns were drawn and items were looted and no one could agree on what to do with him. My guy kept asking him questions, trying to understand the past and who he was and why Spiderman had organic webshooters sometimes but mechanical ones at other times.

    Then suddenly it hit me. "You're a soldier. You fight to defend America. You were frozen in ice and then thawed out to help in a time of great need. You're Captain America."

    From that point on, I was on the Quarantine's side. And many NPCs just started calling him "Cap".

    \n [Monsterhearts 1]

    There was SOOO much more to this. Alex, the Angel in question, had previously been given a mission by Alimon, messenger of God, to exonerate Benedict, the Ghost, from the murder of Samuel, a monster hunter (and son of IFPD Sergeant Yusef). Alex had been in love with Samuel and blamed the Hollow, Clara, for his death (even though Clara and benedict knew that it had really been the werewolf, Jennifer, who'd mauled the boy to death at Benedict's house on the night of the full moon (whoops!).

    Alex tried getting some drugs off of Mike, the skeevy drug dealing kid at Ichor Falls High School, which took some doing, in order to put them in Jennifer's body in order to make sure Benedict didn't take the rap for Sam's death, but Benedict spilled the beans to some kids at school about the real cause of death, with Alex present, making Alex's meeting at school the next morning with Mike now useless.

    Not to be deterred, Alex was also jealous of Clara because the Lord loved Clara more than He loved Alex, so Alex Gazed Into the Abyss one too many times (this time trying to figure out how to frame Clara for Samuel's death), and...SATAN answered.     \n A truly gonzo game of Hot War this past Sunday.

    Taylor S. Compton (Nigel), drug-crazed journalist for Rolling Stone magazine, makes his way to a small town called Innsmouth in search of a scoop -- something about a new religious movement rising from the ashes of the counterculture. Compton manages to remain sober for about seven minutes, then he's off. In a scene that we later realize is reminiscent of real events, he takes a crowbar to the face from an angry ex-biker. It was my first time playing with Nigel, and holy fucking Belial, that guy is guano-loco cool.

    His photographer, Judith (Gaz), doesn't particularly care for Compton, but she grits her teeth and tries to get the job done, even when it turns out that the locals are a bunch of free-love wackos who think that the ocean is our mother. Things get weird when some people try to get Judith into the water. "You belong there," they say. She doesn't want any part of this, and tries to escape these fish-loving weirdos.

    Moses Silver (not the name on his birth certificate, natch) is my character, a former member of the Weather Underground who's haunted by memories of the violent acts he committed in the name of the people. Moses is now the leader of a cult called The Waters of Life, but he's a total fraud. When he's not sleeping with female members of the group, he's getting his marching orders from his muse/mistress/priestess, Annabelle.

    Annabelle Waite (Elina) is the true source of power in the cult, and is Moses' spiritual advisor and commander. She turns out to have a profound connection to the ocean, and she's hell-bent on getting Judith into the water, where she belongs. Over time, we realize that Annabelle is most definitely not human, and she is utterly without compassion. The worse things get, the more serene she becomes. The things in the water are hers, and she is theirs.

    Compton managed to escape, bent cigarette holder clenched in his teeth, eyes wide with horror (or amphetamines). He had the story of a lifetime, but no one would ever believe him.

    Judith was dragged into the water, where she saw them: phosphorecent tentacles rising from the sea, and above those, innumerable translucent egg sacs floating in the air, like glowing skate egg cases, tentacled fetuses writhing within. Freaked out, and lucky to be alive, she escaped the carnage, even as another woman was torn to pieces by the things in the water. She managed to get out of town without ever discovering her true nature.

    Moses got pregnant. Half-mad with terror, and consumed with guilt for the terrorist attacks he committed during his time with the Weather Underground, Moses pretty much lost his grip. He tried to prove to Judith (who, at the time, was being held hostage by the cult) that it was safe to go into the sea. "Nothing will happen," he said. Annabelle tried to stop him, because Moses is just a human, unlike Judith -- for him, it was actually not very safe at all. But he entered the sea, and bad things happened. He came out of the water with something inside him. Short version: Moses gave birth in a scene like something out of a PETA video. Bad stuff! Good times!

    Annabelle, the cult's true leader, put Moses' mangled body back into the water and told him that he would heal. "They will care for you," she said. "And you will bear me many more children."

      \n Tenra Bansho gets more awesome and enjoyable every time I run it. Even the pregen characters are pretty great. Tonight's highlights:

      The PCs are enmeshed in the plot of the province's regent Shingen, ravaged by illness and plotting to have his soul captured after death and placed inside the undying iron body of a kongohki to continue ruling from beyond the grave. His heretical karma draws in:

      Mi-Hal the Oni weapons master, last free Oni in the province, searching for his son and fellow villagers, abducted to be slain and their hearts harvested to power the lord's new body.

      The oni were kidnapped by the ninja clan of Seikichi the shinobi, but after an attack of conscience renounced his clan and became a fugitive, searching to free the victims his clan wronged.

      Seikichi's clan was hired by Haruko, armour rider pilot of the mecha Star Chalice and eldest child of the regent. A somewhat peripheral NPC in our first session, this time she was upgraded to PC status by a player who missed last week, and during the Zero Act established her as less malicious than loyal to her father's will above all other concerns.

      Haruko's uncle Okinosuke is torn between his family loyalty and his natural compassion and doctor's oaths to help the helpless. He has also been hiding the fact that he has become an annelidist, symbiotically bound to various weaponized insects.

      Before his illness, Lord Shingen was arranged to be wed to Kurako, a noble's daughter from a neighboring province. Unknown to all, including herself, Kurako is an artificial being, a kugutsu carved from wood and magicked to appear living. She is devoted to her desperately ill fiance, and dangerously naive.

      After a quick round of introductory scenes during the Zero Act, things go into high gear almost immediately, as Mi-Hal escapes "protective custody" (brought on by his wild accusations that the lord's own daughter was responsible for his people's disappearance) and is met at the gates by Haruko in Star Chalice, here to eliminate her accuser. The Oni stands fast as the mecha-scale sword whistles toward him, declaring an aiuchi; he declines defense in exchange for a devastating stroke of his own and spends 28 Kiai to roll 36 dice in his counterattack (which leaves him at 107 Karma, almost totally consumed by rage and sorrow). Both fighters are grievously harmed but still standing.

      Mi-Hal decides the province's rot begins at the head and reenters the palace, telekinetically flinging away guards and sliding open doors as Star Chalice follows outside, reaching in through windows and smashing holes in the wall. The commotion attracts the rest of the group and they converge in the lord's chamber where he lies in his sickbed, too weak to move. Panicking, Haruko readies her Dragon Howler Cannon, but Mi-Hal's player spends Kiai to interrupt, reaching out with his mind to use her own father as a human shield. Haruko fires anyway, but her uncle the annelidist leaps forward to take the blow himself. A pitched battle ensues over the lord's helpless body, full of interrupted actions, ninja shadow duplicates, and mighty blows desperately blocked at great cost to the defenders. At the end, Shingen lies dead in his gardens, floating in a koi pond staining red with his blood, Haruko paralyzed by annelid venom and watching impotently from her shattered armour with one good eye as her traitorous uncle and stepmother lead the oni villagers from the dungeons. Vengeance blooms in the new regent's heart.

      \n Dog Eat Dog: The very first scene has Old Mos, the local elder, taking two men to the sacred site where only he and a few others are allowed to go. Here, he finds construction workers from the occupation forces, setting up fences for everyone's security. "But... can't we go there anymore?" One engineer puts his arm around Mos' shoulder. "Sure you can. Just come back in office hours, and one of our team will probably guide you around. Wear a safety helmet."

      The last scene has Old Mos, now completely assimilated, working as a tourist guide in a carefully restored and hygienic version of a sacred hut. Here, he wears a Disneyfied version of "traditional" ceremonial garb, which he never wore before. Afterwards, he goes home to his flat in one of those new, ugly apartment buildings.

      ---

      Our game played out quickly - an hour and a half, I think. It was interesting and fun, tragic and stark. It was not heavy on immersion (for us), but the stories were compelling and rang very, very true. We did not attempt any historical or cultural correctness.

      \n My Daughter, the Queen of France: A few factors made this into a bit of a farce when we played it. A few beers, some physical acting out, and the constellation of players (these are the players that made me create the "Do it differently" rule for Archipelago). We had a lot of fun, laughing at silly stuff in the fiction - and on a meta layer: Two of the actors were rivals, and in one scene they played each other. One spent most of the scene talking about how wonderful the other character (i. e., the actor himself) was, while the other portrayed the character (i. e., the other actor) as a constipated idiot with his foot literally up his ass. In the next scene, they both played themselves... so we had a player play a character playing itself.

      I'd like to give this game another shot, but am afraid I might have to hack it a bit and work on player expectations to make it work for this particular group.
