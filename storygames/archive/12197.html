\n With all the theory talk floating around these days, I might as well ask about something I've wondered...

One of the notions that warms my heart about Vulpinoid's Vector Theory is the idea that it explicitly starts with the idea that since other theories have their roots in anthropology, his would start with a different basis (optics). Along those same lines, I'm wondering if anyone has done something similar using economics as a starting point.

Perhaps a better explanation of why I am asking will help... Any time a theory or person tries to tackle notions approaching "everyone plays for different reasons", "what do people get out of playing RPGs" and so on, what's really being talked about is utility. Talking usefully about utility is damned hard, but economists have been doing it since the 1800's. It seems to me, therefore, there might be a useful toolbox in economic theory (including an obsession with games) for talking about game design.

Unfortunately, my training lies in routing electrons about, not economics. Anyone else want to take a stab at it? Or point me to someone who already has?

    \n Lester,

    Economics is my natural "lens" to look at things. I think most non-economists think economics has to do with money. You're taking it a step in the right direction by examining utility. A step further in the right direction is David Friedman's definition of economics:

    "Economics is that way of understanding behavior that starts from the assumption that people have objectives and tend to choose the correct way to achieve them."

    And then a further refinement on top of that would come from all the latest research and theorizing about patterns of human irrationality and evopsych and cultural influences and other neat stuff.

    So, yes, I think economics is particularly well-suited to understanding games and why people play them and how to shape artifacts that will be more useful to people.
    But then on the other hand I look at games like Farmville and I always think of Skinner's pigeons.

    \n As you suggest, mathematical game theory (versus the game design theory we are usually talking about when we say "game theory") is economic in nature; it presumes outcomes or "payoffs" (measured in units of utility, naturally) associated with particular combinations of strategies chosen by players. But the thing about utility is that it's exogenous rather than endogenous. In other words, utility comes from outside the model rather than inside; there's no economic reason per se why a player might prefer one outcome to another. So the payoff structure in any given game must be taken as given, either as an assumption of the modeler or an empirical fact of the game. So looking to economics for an explanation of why people assign different values to particular outcomes strikes me as a tough row to hoe.

    On the other hand, what game theory does give you is a much stronger sense of how to construct cycles of play that do what you want them to do. Tony Lower-Basch's Capes is a good example of this; I seem to recall him discussing the development of that game in game-theoretic terms at one point.

    \n I am going to interject and say that I solidly disagree with the Friedman summation of economics, above.
    Given the Smithian and Malthusian influences on economics, which force the assumption of natural scarcity, many people forget that economics is fundamentally about distribution. Mainstream American and Anglo economic theories (and not just these schools of thought) operate from the premise that scarcity is a fixed, unchanging fact of societies, even of life itself; operating from that basis, the field of economics focuses primarily on taking advantage of existing scarcity conditions rather than seeking to break through these barriers to make life better for humanity.
    In that sense, economics is very, very different from nearly every other scientific field. The medical profession, for example, seeks to change the conditions in which humans live their lives - - certainly not on its own, but with the collusion of waste management, education, and other sectors, both public and private.

    I back up my distribution claim with the fact that economics is more properly known, at least historically, as "political economy" - the study of the manner in which political and commercial systems interact (forgive the glib definition). And personally, I think this is a fair assessment, on the basis that all transactions are exchanges between larger systems (families, businesses, neighborhoods, states, nations...), and that corporate, military, and governmental systems must have distribution systems to function, even on the smallest scale of operation.

    Wordman, I'm really intrigued about the notion of using econ as a lens for analysis and design, but as-yet I don't see how one could go about it, short of simply teaching gamers *about* economics. Keep up the thinkin', though!
