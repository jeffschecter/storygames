\n How long has it been since we had a huge fight about Simulationism? Let's not do that here. Let's have an intelligent discussion.

So I have some theories about GNS Sim and people's perception of it. There's this thing called Sim and it's pretty well described and circumscribed but a large majority of gamers don't understand it very well. They don't get it because either they don't understand what folks (primarily Ron Edwards) mean when they say Sim or because they do get it but have very little in their gaming experience that matches up with the Sim definition (yet they clearly don't fit into Narrativist or Gamist play).

My thesis is that a large majority of gamers aren't playing Sim, Nar, or Gam because their essential gaming experience is passive and reactive, and not actively creative.

Now, before you tar and feather me and ride me out of town on a rail and do all kinds of other things that haven't been done since 1850, let me explain.

First of all, I'm not saying they aren't having fun. I'm not even saying they're having wrongbadfun. I believe they're having a good time and good on them.

Second, I'm not casting any kind of value judgment on "passive and reactive." When you go to a movie, you are passive but not very reactive. Passive is a good thing when you go to a movie, because it keeps the other patrons from shouting at you to shut up and be quiet. You're there to be entertained. Hopefully, your movie entertains you. RPGs are participatory, so there's a requirement for the players to react. Being reactive is a requirement of all role-playing. If you are not reacting, you might as well not play with other people.

Third, I'm not casting an kind of value judgment on "not actively creative." Again with the movie analogy: People go to a movie to be entertained, not to write stories. I think a lot of gamers come to the table with that in mind. I think a lot of people in the story games community think that's lame, but we should reconsider. I wrote about this a while ago in a blog post about Illusionism, inspired by the Theory from the Closet interview with Mike Mearls (#44). Mike thinks that some gamers game to be entertained, and they want the GM to be the entertainer, and that's okay. I think it's okay, too, even if it's not always what I want.

People who want to be entertained, I think, want the movie experience. They want to have a good time, follow the GM's story, and give just enough input to feel like maybe they're in the movie. Without input, the movie is about someone else. With too much input, it's not a movie anymore, and it feels too much like being the director or screenplay writer, and that kills the magic.

So is that a Sim Creative Agenda? A CA is a means of filtering role-play input such that you get other people to be excited about the stuff that excites you. It's a set of priorities for play. What is the filter mechanism or priority for the movie-goer-style gamer? Certainly they have a set of criteria (probably unconscious) for deciding when to talk, what to say, and so on. But what? The personal experience they're seeking is a combination of social connection and "being part of the story." I don't think that really says anything about how they figure out what they say during the game, or how they get other people excited about the stuff that excites them. In a way, it's a very internal way to play.

A lot of GM advice gives a nod to this. Consider the "player type" advice in Robin's Laws. As the traditional wisdom goes, there are different kinds of players and the GM should figure out what they want and give them opportunities to do that, so no one gets bored. The GM is the entertainer and the players are there to be entertained.

Some story games attack the "no one gets bored" problem from a different direction. Find a group of players who all like to do this narrow(er) activity. Make the players responsible for making it very clear what they want. I'll bet a lot of these games were written by frustrated GMs.

Wanting to be entertained isn't a Creative Agenda. It's a social mode. It's my guess that some of those people are actually playing a Sim game, as defined by the "Right to Dream" or "constructive denial" models that Ron and others have described. The heavy-immersion, I-want-to-be-in-this-world folks are probably Sim players. Others might be doing a Vanilla Narrativism thing (addressing premise in light ways without game tools to support it). A bunch of players--especially those playing D&D 3E and 4E--are doing a Gamist thing at least some of the time. And probably a large portion of them are mixing different CAs, wanting different things at different times, prioritizing things in different ways at different times, and not always achieving their creative goals.

    \n Okay, then, before there are too many discussions for me to throw this one in:

    Simulationism is hard to pin down because the "how" is defined locally.

    It's easy to talk about what is explored. High Concept Sim pitches are grabby. "Order of the Golden Dawn in the Gulag!" But then we get to how we're going to explore that, and all of a sudden there's division.

    There's the "how" of focus:

    "You want to focus on the moral dilemmas of the characters? You must be playing Narr."

    "You want to focus on how they might escape? Sounds like a Gamist challenge."

    "You want to focus on 'what does it look like to do ritual magic in an icy prison'? Okay, now you're Sim."

    There's the "how" of angle-of-attack:

    "You want to emulate a movie you saw about the same concept? That's not a real creative agenda."

    "You want to parody the 'out there high-concept pitch' by mining this situation for humor? That's not a real creative agenda."

    "You want to simulate what would really happen if you put these people in this situation? Oh, okay, that sounds like Simulationism."

    It seems somehow easier to say, "Okay, I think my premise is cool and yours is lame, but it's clear that we're both playing to Address Premise," and harder to say, "I'm emulating horror films and you're simulating Romans with muskets, but it's clear that we're both running with our Right to Dream." It really doesn't sound any more meaningful than, "We're both roleplaying."

    \n Trey, I only know of really long explanations and really short ones. Long ones here and here. Short one:

    We could say that Exploration is prioritized as Simulationism precisely when:
    a) the constraints on player contributions to the SIS are dictated by the players' reference to a package of shared input-material, and
    b) these constraints are the primary criteria in determining player contributions to the SIS, and
    c) the process of dictating constraints is experienced as if the package itself was doing the dictating rather than the players, which is only possible if
    d) the players deny that they themselves created, and continue to create, the shared package.

    \n I've been beating the "creator vs fan" drum since at least 2003, and it never gets any easier. A quick scatter of responses...

    Passive vs Active as continuum (re: Corley)

    I think the key difference is intentionality. Yeah, players will generate new content spontaneously during play in response to prompts. Is that what they mean to do, where they get their kicks, or is it just a necessary part of the process of play? Obviously, if the players never contribute anything at all, then you don't have a game - you have a story being told. On the other hand, I have seen, again and again, players reacting to even that level of need for input with stress. They don't LIKE having to be creative. They just want to find the right answer to the problem and relieve the tension.

    So I think in order to have creative play that rises to the level of a possible creative agenda, you have to have players seek out the opportunity to create content, rather than simply do it in response to tension in a "what's the right answer" mode.

    Where does "play" happen? (re: Bob & David)

    That strikes me as a Technical Agenda thing: some people prefer that their creative work and their enjoying the fiction that results from it be pretty starkly segregated, but they still want to do the creative work. Out-of-band procedures are key to a lot of successful vanilla/naturalistic Sim, it seems to me.

    Definitional Nonsense (re: Trey)

    I think the odds of Adam getting his useful discussion go down immensely if we get into definitional wrangles in thread. Sim, for these purposes, is play whose reward comes from actively creating a product that matches a group aesthetic standard - "gets X right" where X is a genre, a setting, a narrative structure, any reasonably recognizable set of aesthetic flags.

    \nOkay, Sim is short for Simulationism, a term used in GNS Theory and, more broadly, The Big Model. Sim is a Creative Agenda (a term that I defined in my original post). Ron Edwards describes Sim in detail in an article called "Simulationism: The Right to Dream."

    Specifically, Sim is the Creative Agenda that prioritizes constructive denial as a means of Getting Things Right. The Getting Things Right part trips up some people, because they haven't realized how many Things there are.

    Different people care about Getting different Things Right. Some care about the setting. They might want to run the perfect Silmarillion (Tolkien) setting, or maybe their own slant on the Silmarillion setting with new gods they invented. Some care about the characters. Maybe they want to play a different historical person who travel through time together, and the player values portraying Benjamin Franklin as accurately as possible. Perhaps they're focused on situation, like what it's like to be a soldier in Vietnam in 1969; in this case, they might handwave characters and setting but really drill into how the events happen. Some people care about the game system and Getting it Right -- not to "win" but to explore the nooks and crannies of what the rules do -- and playing it Wrong is bad. You can focus on getting "color" right, too, but I'm having trouble explaining that.

    This "constructive denial" thing is just a way of thinking about Sim play. Players are exercising their right to say "NO, that is wrong." Ron Edwards calls this The Right to Dream. He chose those words very carefully. The "Dream" is the imagined fiction that the players share. It's actually less important than the other big word. The word "Right" is very important and it has several meanings. Players believe they have a right to this Dream and they insist that the Dream be right. When you have a right to something, and when something has to be right, you protect it. Sim players continually filter what they say by making sure that it faithfully executes the Dream. They are made happy when the other players go, "Yeah, man, that's right. That's it exactly." That is, they want validation that they are Right.

    Remember, Creative Agenda is about priority, not about technique. So Sim players prioritize the Right to Dream over the other things (like Gamism's "Step on Up" or Narrativism's "Story Now"). That doesn't mean they don't care about those things.

    Here's an example. I was running 1st Edition AD&D and was very Sim at the time. Matt was very Gamist. He prioritized winning and showing off his mad playah skillz. His thief character encounters a bear. He considers the rules he's memorized and decides he can take the bear. I consider the situation, imagine a 350-pound bear charging his scrawny rogue, and basically see disaster. I warn him. We start arguing. Does this mean he doesn't care about in-game reality? Not at all. But where it gets in the way of his Gamist play, it gets a back seat. Does this mean I don't care about the rules? Not at all. But where the rules conflict with my Simulationist view of the setting and how things work, the rules take a back seat (or, in this case, I find some DM-fiat rule that lets me run things differently).

    Also, I think a lot of players read somewhere (or had it drummed into them by GMs who had read somewhere) that metagaming is BAD. "Your character says what you say!"     \n So, here's an observation about Sim: it's hard to generate a reliable flow of mutually-appreciated contributions if your aesthetic standards are undefined.

    For example, I think a lot of groups who got into roleplaying via D&D and went in a Sim direction with it wanted (and still want!) some vague combo of "fantasy" tropes. But that can be an awful jumble -- one player prioritizes a LotR setting, another prioritizes mythological hero story arcs, another really wants to play X-Men (misfit tac-team) with "Orcs, Elves and Dragons" color.

    I've come up with a system of covering the bases for myself, as a designer, but it's kind of a crappy tool for getting the players inspired -- it probably needs to filter down into play examples or faux-fiction or something. Anyway, here's the brief version:

    Fictional Conceit: About what the gameworld contains, in terms of beings, objects, and structures (e.g. societies). Also, how it will appear to player characters.

    Functional Conceit: About how the gameworld works. The core of this is how player character actions affect the gameworld, and how the gameworld affects the player characters. Also, any more general gameworld "hows" and "whys" that explain that interaction.

    Protagonists: The behavior patterns the players will explore directly (by playing PCs of these types) or indirectly (by playing PCs who are noteworthy for how they differ from these types). A sort of character measuring stick.

    Taboos: If you do these with your character, you're playing the game wrong.

    Conventions: What kinds of things tend to happen in play? What are some activities that represent play well?

    Motifs: Color beyond the central conceit, that gives play its distinctive personality.

    References: Narrative fiction or non-fiction that helps players fill in any blanks and find inspiration. Movies, TV series, comic books, novels, etc.

    I've run into a problem in my game, in which the Protagonists description doesn't map to something familiar, and none of the References are quite right (I won't sidetrack this thread by going into the specifics, unless Adam asks). Otherwise, though, I've found this to be helpful, and at least a couple designers have found Functional Conceits and Taboos to be eye-openers.

    \n I need to get Joe McDonald to write a summation for me, 'cause I suck at trying to sum up my games in a compelling fashion.
    \n
    \nReCoil is about Agents, who are actually spirits of the deceased who have joined the fight against a faceless force which would destroy reality. Not just physical reality; all of it. One of the defining concepts of the game is that reality is consensual, formed by belief. The minions of the enemy, called Naughtwraiths enter the physical world, where belief is strongest and most stable, and try to undermine and weaken it by destroying the foundation of belief upon which it is all founded. The Agents also enter the physical world, called the Coil (as in the mortal coil) to try to stop this from happening. There's one fairly serious wrinkle: Being dead, they don't have bodies. To be in the Coil at all, they must join with a Host, a mortal person. To complicate things, their enemies do, too. Being from outside it, Agents and Naughtwraiths have an unusual level of control over what is and isn't possible. This translates as the ability to do harm to reality, purposely or otherwise, as well.
    \n
    \nThe play of the game is *supposed* to feel something like Quantum Leap and the Matrix rolled up together. You have to discover the Naughtwraiths' plot by discovering the situation you're in, and at times, there should be some crazy martial arts and guns action.
    \n
    \nLacuna is, IMO, a spiritual sibling. Whispering Vault is also, but it's a much older sibling that was put up for adoption a long time ago, and ReCoil never knew.
