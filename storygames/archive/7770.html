    \n Ryan,
    \n Its not a matter of caring. People want to game or they wouldn't say it to you. The few that are "just saying it" don't even show up for one session. Its just that people have a lot of demands on their time. Make it easy for them to show.
    \n There are certain things you need to do to keep a group together for a long time.
    \n1) Weekly is the minimum frequency (bi-weekly, etc. doesn't work) you can set and still get consistent attendance.
    \n2) You have to ask them why they missed and share their excuse with the group. Don't be a dick about it, but be completely open. I do this every time. I usually don't comment on the quality of the excuse and if it seems like they feel bad about it I'll make a joke (like, "Dinner with your mother is more important than gaming with me" etc. to lighten the mood).
    \n3) Especially in the beginning, you have to be a nag, the day of the game, send an e-mail reminding them to come, call or text message them if they don't respond.
    \n4) Be consistent, treat everyone the same, since you are turning into an event coordinator, be fair and consistent, don't nag one person and let the other off the hook, etc.
    \n5) Schedule (unofficially, so people don't feel pressured) time for people to make small talk, etc. This will get people bonding and make them less likely to bail for weak, weird reasons.
    \n6) Be flexible, let people know you would rather have them late than not at all. But start the game on time and just let them hop in when they show up.
    \n I've kept a regular weekly game running since 96, with only a few weeks a year where there was no attendance (darn family and holidays).
    \n I hope this helps.
    \nDave M

    \n So here's some of my experiences:

    If you have the time and the opportunity, run two games, each with a dedicated day of the week. One of my current groups is all Microsoft employees, so everyone's using Outlook and we send out 'meeting' invites so our sessions end up on everyone's calendars. We meet every other week, sometimes two weeks in a row when things hit a climax, and it works.

    \n

    \n Make the event important, a date with enough time and ceremony that people will not want to miss it for other things.

    I used to be part of a 7 person GURPS campaign that ran for 2-3 years, same time every week. What made it work? Well we started early evening and played late, so we had a good 5+ hours devoted to it, which meant it could handle both chat *and* focused gaming. We ate together, we had a dedicated room without distractions, and there was a lot of ceremony to settling in to play (putting out a map, sitting in favourite seats, breaking out the miniatures etc.

    Now a lot of that is just general advice to encouraging focus (see the other threads on this at the moment), but I also think it increased commitment and helped the game last so long, because playing was a real event, not something casual. When we got there to play we knew play was going to happen, there was never a chance that it wouldn't, and that made the whole thing more solid. People really didn't miss that game very often, they put it down as a rock-solid part of their schedules and kept to it. Heck a player even moved to the other end of the country and still turned up fairly often. It took the GM moving from the UK to Canada to actually kill it.

    So that's my advice, make the weekly session a certainty, the gaming inevitable, and people will take it more seriously.

    \n Try being more formal about it. Make it a specific, named club. We did that in Milton Keynes and thus the MK RPG club was born. We started with about five or six people who all agreed to meet up every Tuesday evening. That was about five years ago. We now get 15-20 regular players (from a town with a population of around 250,000) with normally four or five parallel games. Create a website for the club, set up a mailing list, and advertise it everywhere you can. People will come.

    We also took the view that the benefit of a club was being able to game with different systems and players. We have a fairly fixed timetable of 8-week mini-campaigns interleaved with 3 weeks of one- or two-session games. People propose games they're willing to run in the long blocks and everyone votes for the games they'd like to play the best. Sometimes games span more than one long block, but not normally.

    If you want to know more, let me know.

    \n Some people have brought up group belonging, and that's a big part. Try to get the group together as often as possible for anything, gaming related or not. The most sucessful group I've ever been in was a group that were honestly my best friends. There wasn't much incentive to miss a session, because even if the game feel apart (it occasionally did) or it was a one shot, I knew I'd enjoy the company.

    Granted, not every gaming group is going to be best buds. But consider trying to kick off a game with a character creation party. Get everybody together for whatever refreshments make sense (I'd suggest grilling something and your booze of choice, but that's just me) and let them just hang out for a while, then eventually steer the night towards discussing the upcoming game. Hopefully characters come out of it, but at the least everyone should have an idea of the game. There's also less pressure since it's not a 'gaming session' it's a party.
