  • Zak Arntson
    August 2006
    I played a bunch of Halo & Halo 2 last Saturday night. Near the end, we set up Capture the Flag rules a canyon map where everyone had TONS of health, rockets and grenades only. We ignored the flags, and treated the game as a big physics sandbox, what with the two Warthog vehicles you could flip into the air with a well placed rocket. There were three emergent games from this sandbox mode:
    • Hogball, where one Hog is placed in the center of the field, and two teams try to push it to touch the opposing teams' base for a point, using their rockets & grenades
    • Hog Chicken, two people play, driving the Hogs. You drive to either end of a road, then head straight at each other. If your opponent is thrown from the vehicle, you get a point.
    • Hog Melee, where the goal is to stay driving a Hog for as long as possible. Two Hogs + Four players = Lots of rockety explosions
    Tying this into RPG design, I was wondering how effective a sandbox design method would be, particularly for a gamist design goal. You start with a set of rules (either loose or complex), then just have people fool around with the system, creating their own goals on the fly. Eventually you may come up with some fun things, some not, and winnow the rules down (or expand them) to handle a particular concrete play experience.

    Though I could be talking out my other end, too.
