\n Paul Riddle and John Harper just released an Alpha playtest kit of their military-themed story game, The Regiment

I have some experience with it as I've played/ran it several times, both using the off-the-shelf rules and with some mods to play in WW2 South Pacific.

This game really allows you to play action-oriented military conflicts without all of the "fiddly bits". While also keeping the character development and interaction that we love.

Have you downloaded it? Read it? Planning to run it? What excited you about it?

Let's hear from you!

    \n I've downloaded and browsed it. Based on this and my pre-alpha glimpses I think I could run it, but might prefer to play.

    Paul Riddle introduced us to a really cool "scenario" for the default world of The Regiment at GPNW last year. I am wondering whether anything like that will be forthcoming during the alpha phase. Some world details and terrain maps, etc. If nothing else, I'm guessing Jason will be along to put something amazing together that the rest of us can crib off.

    One of several things about it that excites me is the prospect of mechanics to Assess a situation and then narrate and Make a Battle Plan with concrete mechanical effects without it taking a ton of time. There are individual Soldier moves that support this same dynamic. Then you see where the dice take you. With some similar games the GM might create a really a static and opaque target and the players spend a lot of time scouting out the best approach, then settle on one and all that planning goes out the window as they pass the dog kennel or whatever. In my one play, this felt more fluid, and the PC/GM authority ratio just seemed right. Kind of like, uh, Apocalypse World. Apocalypse World wasn't necessarily built for team-based operations play but it looks as though it was a phenomenal skeleton for this kind of game. Bravo (Zulu) to Paul and John for recognizing this and starting to build it.

    I'm in a couple ongoings at the moment but if there are any Stumptown folks who are similarly enthused and would like to run a game or three, please radio me asap. Tango Yankee.

    \n I obviously haven't played The Regiment yet but a lot of movies/TV shows tend to alternate 5/7ths front line/mission and action with 2/7 base camp drama, local love interest, shenanigans, court martial, funeral, etc. I wonder if there is room for either a Mouse-Guardesque player turn or a Lady Blackbirdesque refresh scene. Something to break up the missions and form part of the long story arc.

    Also, at the end of sessions, players should all take 5 minutes and write a letter home, then read it as an aside (session epilogue) for the group, and finally give it to the GM. At the beginning of the next session, the GM should show up with one or more letters from home. Better, maybe you get an XP for writing a letter home and get letters from home when you gain an advance (fictional and mechanical reward). Letters would tell the GM what the player/character liked or didn't like by what they focused on. Letters to players would be things to play off of, share or withhold from the others (Sam's brother died in the pacific, should I tell him? Dear John... It's a girl, she's so beautiful. Jimmy got an A in math, he's growing up so fast), and be some lonely fun prep for the GM (use a typewriter font and print out a sheet of period stamps; remember to redact liberally).

    Assuming characters will die along the way, consider a game end condition when there is only one original character left or when the last origional character dies. The story becomes their memoir/bio.

    \n Dogtags. They're an iconic part of the modern warfare genre. When people die, their comrades or officers retrieve their tags. What about a move that plays on that?

    ..., roll+tags collected.

    Maybe you use them to leverage your superiors for reinforcements. Maybe when a player has to make a new character you spend them to give an XP boost to the new character so that, as the game progresses, players don't feel their new character is quite so far behind the veterans. Just a thought.

    Additionally, trophies. A lot of war movies have a scene or two about claiming items from enemy soldiers. A flag, a pistol, an officer's stripes, etc. What is appropriate to take and what isn't? What's it worth in smokes? Who's looking for what specific thing for their little brother? It might make a neat element.

    \n Anybody who likes this kind of thing should really read With the Old Breed, by E.B. Sledge. Probably the best American book about infantry combat ever. Sledge served in WWII on Peleliu and in the invasion of Okinawa, and he is very eloquent and evocative about what it's like. He has a nice bit in there, if I recall correctly, about some REMFs coming to the front and getting themselves shot to pieces trying to grab trophies -- and the trophies weren't even that great, just the little banzai flags that the Japanese soldiers carried with them. Not like an officer's sword.

    T.R. Fehrenbach's This Kind of War is also quite magnificent, about the Korean War this time.

    \n Orlando, I'll do my keyboard-commando best to answer your questions. Wall of text follows...

    First, modern body armor is an amazing development. It most certainly helps you not die...at least in its direct application. Modern BA can be anything from a simple vest of light kevlar that carries a 'strike plate' - the solid plate that covers the center vitals - to a full-on getup of heavy kevlar fabrics in multiple layers that goes from a high collar down to a crotch flap, with epaulet-like upper-arm (deltoid) covers to keep the bullets from going in the arm-holes. Mostly. A modern Kevlar helmet is night-and-day from the old steel pot helmets. It will stop a rifle bullet...pretty much all of the time. This stuff would be a scifi dream for anyone before about 1975. The fabric parts stops pistol rounds and fragments, the plates (front, back & sides) stop assault rifle rounds.

    All that said, though, it is heavy & hot. When you get shot, it still hurts - energy is transferred to a larger surface area. Serious bruising, broken ribs, other possibilities. Did I mention heavy & hot? The lastest and bestest stuff used by the US Army, the Improved Outer Tactical Vest, with every option available, weighs in at about 30-35 pounds. SpecOps types tend to use a different model that has less protected area, but weighs about half as much. Doesn't sound like a lot, until you count in everything else a soldier tends to carry. 60 lbs isn't uncommon.

    Modern body armor is a tradeoff, like all armor. Protection vs. speed & agility. If the enemy is faster, they can have an advantage. The armor will protect you from being shot, but it won't protect you from better tactics.

    Drones: depends on what you want them to do. Drones do 2 jobs, at the moment. Take pictures and deliver ordnance. A recon drone allows you to look ahead, around, all over the place, and depending on the size, can stay up for a few minutes to days. Little ones are easily backpackable and are operated like a video game. Big ones are the size of aircraft, and are flown like one from a central control station, which can be half-way around the world. The camera tech is getting better all the time, and so is the software to support it. You can still hide from them, it's just harder to do...sometimes. If you want to do something about the pictures you're getting, you can hang a few missiles off the wings. They can follow a laser spot down to the target, and take care of most vehicles short of an MBT.

    WW2 vs. modern day: The more things change...well, they do sometimes. And sometimes, they don't. Infantry tactics, while having evolved over the decades, would be recognizable to a WW2 Joe, Tommy or Ivan. The guns would be somewhat familiar but a significant departure. Light-round assault rifles are the norm rather than heavy-round battle rifles. LMGs and Designated Marksman weapons (sniper-ish rifles, but the user stays with the unit and gives direct support, rather than staying back and observing/spotting) make up a good deal of the killing firepower of a group. As a consequence of the assault rifle being a hugely successful compromise between the SMG and the battle rifle, everyone does both long-range and close-in fighting.

    The guns themselves don't make that much of a difference. If you swapped out the weapons between the US and insurgents in Iraq or Afghanistan, the results would be about the same. Training and aggression make far more of a difference than what gun you use.

    There are a few things that do make a serious difference. Night vision devices can be decisive in the complete confusion of a night combat. A modern tank, handed to untrained insurgents, would be effectively useless. Communications gear, when it works (it does - most of the time!) is also significant. Then again, insurgents often have cell phones, observers, lots of innovative tricks. Sometimes it helps, sometimes it doesn't.
