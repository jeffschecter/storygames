    \n I find early drafts of games very interesting. They show you what the designer feels is important or lacking from other games. In this case, you included a table on aging, which makes me think you feel it is important and left out of other games.

    As a hypothetical player, what does the aging table add to my experience? (this is a rhetorical question, btw)

    My answer:
    Perhaps, it is a game about time control, where every player has the ability to speed up time, slow down time, or even time travel. But each attempt whittles away at the person's life. Time traveling advances the character and the plot, but brings upon the person's death. [Inquiry: do the players visibily age, or maybe they are slowly overcome by time corruption, meaning their real limit isn't really known and they don't *seem* older, but once their time has come, they just fall down dead, or perhaps they wink out of existence or turn to dust?]

    \n Interesting. I recently read a little bit of Traveller, and having that quickstart guide on hand helped with this. It mixes some of that sandbox character creation with some story-gaming hacks here and there. Here's a character I rolled up:

    Gabril Bhorzo
    Age: 36
    Characteristics: 65B862 (i.e. very high endurance, very low social standing)

    Careers:
    - privateer, where he gained Strength
    - privateer, where he was promoted but decided to leave. Left with a good amount of gold and two ships (?). He presumably sold one. (I quit the privateer path here because I was thinking: "man I don't want to end up an Old Pirate")
    - merchant, where he learned Blade combat. Business is tough! He got a horse, but probably sold it.
    - traveller: injured after 2 years. -1 End
    - privateer: returning to friends after his adventures, gained Strength again

    Skills: seafaring, gambling, survival, trade: carpenter, persuade, combat: blade, navigation

    I totally see a character peeking out from this.

    There are a lot of parts that are unclear in the rules, though. (And here again, I borrowed some assumptions from Traveller.) I'll whisper you a few comments.

    \n Feedback: (unwhisper'd upon request)

    - As I described, I got two ships when rolling for benefits as a privateer. Yay for ships? But maybe that's a little rich for an adventurer to get. (AFAIK traveller characters begin the game by mortgaging for a ship, basically? I don't think I get to have a ship. Though I'm glad to have it.)

    - The rules are vague about what it means to be injured on a test; went ahead and gave myself a -1 to the attribute of my choice, and cut that career short by 2 years. This made the aging rules weird, as my fourth term ended when I was 32. (So, I just took a single aging roll for my five career turns. But, I gained no training or benefits from that traveller career.)

    - I was totally caught by surprise that "Trade" (the merchant skill) meant an actual trade skill, rather than some merchant thing.

    - For the fourth skill training table, you say they're open if "insight is higher then 8", but I think they're clearly not.

    - For the Privateer Advancement table, there is still a reference to Edu instead of Ins.

    - Some of the stats/requirements on the Weapons table don't seem to have rules attached.

    - Do skill ranks and characteristic modifiers work the same was as normal Traveller?

    - I was surprised to see the Sword require Soc 11. Seems high?

    - Damage: I'm uncertain about the damage system. If you happen to hit a character's low and squishiest attribute, you can knock them out in one blow. (Or, it will be a grindfest as you keep hitting different attributes without pushing any to 0.) I'm not sure if these balance out.

    More generally though: there do seem like several rules-pieces missing, and both an edit and a layout-refresh on the tables could make things clearer.

    Did you have a chance at playtesting any of this? What did you find?

    Thanks for catching that. I am not fond of the privateer class so it's been moved into a different book. I replaced it with a tradesman path instead.

    - The rules are vague about what it means to be injured on a test; went ahead and gave myself a -1 to the attribute of my choice, and cut that career short by 2 years. This made the aging rules weird, as my fourth term ended when I was 32. (So, I just took a single aging roll for my five career turns.     The rules are vague in the LBB as well. I left it that way. But that might change so a player can have several options for an injury.


    - I was totally caught by surprise that "Trade" (the merchant skill) meant an actual trade skill, rather than some merchant thing.
    opps. Trade is for a trades skill and Broker is for the Merchant. I fixed that.

    - For the fourth skill training table, you say they're open if "insight is higher then 8", but I think they're clearly not.
    I am not sure what you mean here. If your character has an insight of 8+ then you have the option of rolling on the advanced table. I am checking to make sure I stated that.

    - For the Privateer Advancement table, there is still a reference to Edu instead of Ins.
    Heh, thanks fixed that and a bunch of other spelling errors.

    - Some of the stats/requirements on the Weapons table don't seem to have rules attached.
    Hmmm could you point out an example

    - Do skill ranks and characteristic modifiers work the same was as normal Traveller?
    Yes, I clarified that in the new draft going up later today.

    - I was surprised to see the Sword require Soc 11. Seems high?
    Yes, but swords are largely status symbols so only those of the highest social ranks can acquire one.

    - Damage: I'm uncertain about the damage system. If you happen to hit a character's low and squishiest attribute, you can knock them out in one blow. (Or, it will be a grindfest as you keep hitting different attributes without pushing any to 0.) I'm not sure if these balance out.
    This is the First Blood rule from CT. I like it but I think the combat section is going to be overhauled so this may be tossed out.

    More generally though: there do seem like several rules-pieces missing, and both an edit and a layout-refresh on the tables could make things clearer.
    Yes, working on that too.

    Did you have a chance at playtesting any of this? What did you find?
    I've ran several but I need to find some folks to run it without me. I tend to fill in the blank space. This is why I really appreciate your feedback.

    I rolled up a small grid, and here's what I got: (and I hope it's interesting to y'all lurkers)


    ocean [ ][ ][ ] plains
    ocean [City][City][ ] plains
    ocean [ ][Ruin][ ] plains


    Coastal City:
    X Strongholds
    5 (2-sq-km) Size
    5 Guilds/Merchants
    C Culture
    5 (1k) Population
    6 (Captive Government) Government
    1 Law Level
    A Trade
    9 Technology

    Inland City:
    X Strongholds
    4 (1-sq-km) Size
    11 Guilds/Merchants
    B Culture
    7 (8k) Population

    10 Law Level
    C Trade
    5 Technology

    The two cities are clearly old rivals. The Coastal city has more culture and openness and is advanced in some ways. The inland city is bustling and busy, with heavy commerce and a heavy-handed dictator.
