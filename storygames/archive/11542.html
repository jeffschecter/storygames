    \n The one time I played this was impromptu at a con. I was talking to someone who expressed some doubt about playing RPGs at conventions because she was worried character creation would take too long and she would mess up (she had played a lot of Shadowrun). I was like, nah, the RPGs I tend to play don't usually have that problem, character creation takes ten to thirty minutes. She still seemed unsure, so Nick Marshall whipped out Ghost/Echo, and I said I'd run it even though I hadn't read it. So I read the text on the way to the men's room and back, and then I ran this 30-60 minute game, and the doubtful girl just played her Shadowrun character and everyone else improvised new characters during the process of play, and it was actually my favorite game at that con.

    \n Just for fun, this is how I started off my PbF game of G/E last year...

    Grip skidded around the corner on slick cobblestones before slamming his back against the cold brickwall. His breath came in
    heaving gulps and his heart hammered in chest. Back around the corner he could hear the wraith running in full pursuit mode.

    Sucking in one more lungfull of air, Grip waited from the beast to come. The pony sized 'dog' bounded around the corner and started
    to pass Grip. The beast glowed like it was made out of light filaments powered by rage. He stabbed his arms out, his fingers hooked
    like claws and pushed into the ghost world.

    [//Channel the Ghost Field, GOAL: Kill the wraith, DANGER: Paranormal backlash. ROLLED: 4/6]

    Grip felt a jolt like sticking his fingers in a light socket, but he kept tearing at the wraith as its momentum carried it past him.
    There was a yelp, the dog tumbled, skidded, and got back to its feet. The right side of its face was a ruin of sparks and aetheral
    filaments. Only one baleful red eye glared back at Grip. It growled and gathered to leap at the mechanic.

    \n Having finally had a chance to play Ghost/Echo with players who read the rules, here's my opinion of it:

    Great stuff!
    The game lends itself to rising action very well.
    We interpreted the rules rather broadly. Where the rules told us that a danger remained, we passed the card with the danger written on it to the next player. This created an interesting dynamic where players began calculating dangers based on things they'd like to see happen to one another as well as being an element they were willing to risk. It also meant a sharper increase in the dice than the other method we tried (we ran two sessions in a row) where we let dangers stay in front of individual players. (That one was interesting because some players built up a huge "trail" of dangers that were constantly partially-coming true.)

    All of this suggested an idea for a great hack to me, which is a mark of good design (that moment of clarity where you see how something works and want to build on it.)
    Anyway, it more than makes up for my earlier disastrous experience.

    I might have to ammend the motto to "System does matter, at least a little, but it helps if you read the rules."
