\nDrift ever since its time as Technobabble has gone through numerous interative design phases. One of the more interesting things to arise from that has been the mission maps, which are essentially duals of relationship maps:

In a relationship map you have nodes which are people, things, and so on. And then you draw lines between them (vertices in the technical sense) indicating the relationships between the nodes. The Drift mission maps turn that on its head: the nodes are relationships (really commonalities - Cultural truths or Technological insights) and the lines between are the people and situations involved in the mission.

The advantages of that are that these truths and insights are also precisely the mechanical features you need to know about any NPC or situation. This started out as a rapid way to link characters and situations together and produce interesting mission situations based on a handful of truths and insights (which are typically based on the planet the mission occurs on). And it does this quite well.

But now I've been thinking about how to do more with it. Specifically to use the mission map as a dynamic chart of the mission progress. As any play (including the GM) succeeds on actions with the situations and people, they can (in addition to forcing more direct outcomes) add some number of tokens to one of the two nodes linked by the person or situation or remove tokens from those nodes (up to 3 on any node).

Once you have your three tokens on both nodes you can resolve that person or situation, essentially as a free narration. And that locks that person or situation in, it cannot be directly affected by the tokens again, unless one of his, her, or its nodes changes - which can only happen if one of the related people or situations is affected first.

There are two concerns about this:

1) I'm not sure how to end missions without it being a let down, especially a final tug-of-war between the GM and the players over a few nodes. I could see some free currency stemming from resolutions, which can turn a few sequential victories into a rout (to be specific - free impetus).

2) If the mission doesn't conclude with a total victory, what happens to the situations or persons trapped between the two sides? I can see this being unresolved or open. Or that things are worst when trapped this way. There are some reasons to leave room for re-occuring characters, but some of the tropes of Drift makes that difficult (30 year or so transit times before you return to the planet).

- Mendel S.

    \n Worlds in Drift are described by a list of seven truths, which represent the different cultural ideas and tendencies of the planet's inhabitants (well it could be a moon or a station, or what not). So for example, when you were recruited from Epsilon Gamma Phi Gamma it had the following truths:

    Aspiration: Knowledge
    Aspiration: Peace
    The Great Wheel
    Sin: Self-Denial
    Virtue: Instinct
    Virtue: Reason
    Virtue: Strength

    with a sharp dichotemy between the night cycle and the day cycle of the station (a Dr. Jekyl / Mr. Hyde sort of dichotemy). After a circuit when you return to that world again, it will change one of its truths, lets say making the place more free-wheeling at night, and less winner-takes-all:

    Aspiration: Chaos
    Aspiration: Knowledge
    Aspiration: Peace
    The Great Wheel
    Sin: Self-Denial
    Virtue: Instinct
    Virtue: Reason

    At least that's how things work right now. I was considering linking the changes to these truths with the outcomes of the mission, success or failure. But I really can't link that at the same time as linking unresolved people and situations with those changes. Frankly, I'm warming up to the later idea though.

    I'm also thinking of adding a shorter list of insights (technological aspects) to the planet. If so, the change could arise using the following technique:

    Each person or situation in the mission map uniquely links two truths or insights, if those nodes are won by opposite sides, leaving that link unresolved, then one of those two must go. The players decide what goes, but the GM decides what replaces it.

    This works because the list of truths and insights for a world is also the list from which the GM draws in the first place to build the mission map. And so, those unresolved links will have a direct effect on the tenor of future missions on that world. And the more you leave unresolved, the more quickly your homeworld becomes utterly alien to you (that process is where the name Drift actually comes from).

    - Mendel S.
