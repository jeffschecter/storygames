\n I pre-ordered Mouse Guard the other day and was very excited to run the game using the free pdf I got from the order. Mind you, I've been waiting months to play this game and was stoked to bring it to the table. The players Karen and Chris P. played in a BW mini-campaign with me earlier this year, so we were all familiar with the game this was based on. And even though they hadn't read the comics, it's not too hard to get excited about "fricken mice with swords!!"

But unfortunately we ran smack into a wall of suck and it didn't end so well. Let me go into more detail.

First, we really enjoyed the character generation. The whole process feels more organic than the BW lifepath system. Instead, it's assumed that all the characters are on the same basic lifepath goal, which is to become a Mouse Guard. So the GM asks a series of questions and as the players answer them, their characters slowly take shape. As we were finishing up the character generation, we realized that we were coming up with some interesting answers to these questions that were simply turning into mechanical bits on the character sheet. So Karen and Chris P. went back to add some notes to the back of their character sheet to fill in some backstory on their characters. As the GM, I knew this would be useful for me in the future when I wanted to bring in aspects of their histories.

The last part of character generation was creating a belief, goal, instinct and gear. But to create a goal you need to have a mission in mind. So we needed to step back from character generation for awhile as I looked at the rules for mission generation and came up with one.

We talked about the seasons, and based on the rules it seems pretty natural that we would start in the Spring. THIS WAS A MISTAKE. None of us realized that the Nature rating of 6 for the Spring would mean frustration down the line for the characters. And there were no admonishments in the book about starting off in an easier season, especially if you are only playing with two characters.

So I wrote up a quick mission based on the following elements:

-Weather: flood
-Wilderness: river
-Animals: Otter, protecting her young
-Mice: Ivy, a young mouse caught in the current

I led off with Weather and Wilderness as per the rules, and kept Animals and Mice in reserve for complications.

So Gwendolyn met with the two Guard Mice and told them how the thawing snow had created a river cutting off the Rustleaf and Frostic from the rest of the Mouse Territories. Nearby Port Sumac had enough trouble of its own to be supplying both towns over water. So the Guard Mice were to travel to Port Sumac and find a way to reconnect the land bridge to Rustleaf. Even though Gurney was a Guard Mouse and Sloan was a Patrol Guard (and usually patrols were led by a Patrol Leader), I noted that the spring thaw was creating such chaos that Gwendolyn had no choice but to send a less experienced mouse to led the patrol. And then they finished up their Beliefs, Goals, Instincts and Gear and they were off!

Karen made Sloan, an over-ambitious brown-noser who was the youngest Patrol Guard at 21. Belief: Refusing help will prove my self-sufficiency. Goal: I will demonstrate my leadership abilities to Gurney. Instinct: I accept all challenges and assignments. Traits: Tough, Natural Bearings.

Chris P. made Gurney, a bookish young mouse who was more of a sneak than a fighter. Belief: Even small Guard Mice can solve big problems. Goal: I will not rely on Sloan to get me out of trouble. Instinct: I never attack a problem head-on. Traits: Inquisitive, Calm.

As this was the "GM's turn", it was my job to describe the problem and how the characters were going to need to solve it. This felt a little too heavy-handed to me, so I simply told them that the winter had covered up most of the tracks to towns outside of the inner circle, so they could either make a Pathfinding roll to take the longer land route to Port Sumac, or a Boatcrafter roll to get to Port Sumac by water.

They decided the land route would be safer, and Karen rolled exactly the two successes they needed to get to Port Sumac with no complications.

    And here's where the game fell apart for us. I described the river that had been created by the snow melting in the hills northwest of the area and was cutting right between the two towns. I wanted to see how they were going to fix this problem, and it seems like more than simply a single skill test. So we decided to use the full conflict rules. So those of you who have played BW/BE before, the conflicts are dealt with using a simplified version of Fight/Firefight/Duel-of-Wits. Both sides have a goal for the conflict, both sides roll for their beginning disposition, and they they script three actions at a time which are unveiled one by one, with mechanical rolls happening for each action.

    The Mice Goal was "Build a bridge"
    The GM Goal was "Flood Port Sumac"

    Mine didn't seem to harsh given what the characters were trying to accomplish was difficult. We decided that the conflict type was "Other". So they were rolled dice for starting disposition.

    Karen rolled her Boatcrafter skill of 2, getting one success to add to her Nature of 4, giving them a disposition of 5.
    I rolled the Nature value for the Spring season of 6, getting three successes to add to the Nature of 6, giving me a disposition of 9. Oops.

    Now both sides scripted their first three actions. They knew things were going to be tough, so they scripted Defend-Defend-Attack. As I have done in other BW conflicts when I don't want to overwhelm the players, I thought I was taking it easy and scripted Attack, Defend, Maneuver. By the end of the first set of actions, the Mice disposition was down to 1 and I was still at 9, not having lost any. The Mice were basically screwed because they were rolling 2, 3, maybe 4 dice per action, and I was always rolling the Spring Nature of 6.

    In the second set of actions, Karen did her point of Persona to tap her Nature and add 4 dice to her roll, but by then it was too late. They lost the conflict and only knocked two of my dice down. So I only had to give a minor concession to them. We discussed it and decided that due to their efforts the river changed course and started flooding Port Sumac, but that the Mice helped to get the townsmice to higher ground and no one was seriously harmed.

    But I could see the player frustration while the conflict was going on. They totally felt like the deck was stacked against them when I was rolling all sixes and their relevant skills for the conflict were twos. The character creation talked about "well-rounded Guard Mice" and they didn't see any necessity in making specialized characters until this point. And they hated getting trapped into the three action choices they had made without being able to change them when the circumstances changed.

    So by the end of the conflict, the players weren't really in the mood to continue the mission and neither was I. We've persevered through worse, so we will probably play again, with several important changes:

    1. We will play in the Summer season, where the GM gets a lower Nature of 4. Hopefully they will be able to increase some skills before Fall hits, but I think that's unlikely given how few missions there are in each season and how many chances you actually get to attempt rolls on each mission.
    2. I will let the characters redistribute some of their skill points to bulk up important skills. This will make them less well-rounded, but I can't see a way around this.
    3. We are scrapping the "pick three actions at once" and just picking our actions in a conflict one at a time, secretly. I usually don't like to drift core rules in Luke's games, but this one is a no-brainer for us and I think will make our conflicts more satisfying and less frustrating    .

    The rules of MG definitely seem to be geared towards having more than two characters and/or having more experienced characters. Rolling three and four dice when the GM rolls six or seven is just not fun. We ran into this in BW and it was frustrating then as well.

    In the past when I have posted notes from a less-than-stellar AP session, people on this board tend to walk away and not comment on it. Which I think it weak sauce. So I hope that people will jump in here to discuss. What are your thoughts? Good or bad, let's hear them!

    \n The common factor I always seem to see when first-time Burning Wheel games go wrong is that people never follow the basic training advice- in the case of BW it's the recommendation that you take time with the basic conflict rules before doing all the nitty gritty rules, and for MG I was wondering how many gamers would skip over the advice that you use the pregen characters and missions for the first few times playing.

    BW games require a level of understanding on the part of the players about how the system works- you cannot jump in without an understanding of the system and expect to use the rules to make a good story (anymore than Capes, Grey Ranks, Polaris, etc.)

    I look at BW games especially as being something where the first 1-3 sessions are system training as well as play:

    1) What are the odds of success/failure? (1/2 your dice will probably succeed. Seems obvious, but based on reading lots of reports, apparently it isn't.)

    2) How do you get more dice? (What are your options? Help, Teamwork, Wises, here. Fate, Persona, Traits.)

    3) How do you play off your BITs to get rewards, and shape the story? How do you shape your BITs as you play to continue that?

    Though this helps the players figure what they're doing, it also helps you, the GM figure out what kind of things they can and can't handle.

    \n You played correctly.

    Here are some pointers:

    1) Teamwork is vital. That's one of the fundamentals of this game. Teamwork gets you +1D per character.

    2) Seasons are HARSH. You fought the spring weather and you expected to win? That's not what's going on in this game. It's possible to overcome, but you must be diligent and lucky. Even two guardmice can be bested by the weather. That said, Seasons conflicts are rare.

    3) The I Am Wise rules are easy to use and often provide bonus dice

    4) Traits are another source of bonus dice.

    5) Nature is incredibly useful and must be tapped in order to rise above your mouseness and actually beat the environment.

    6) Failure isn't failure in Mouse Guard. I know it stings to play a game and lose the first time, but in MG all that means is that a new, unexpected situation crops up.

    \n Hey Chris,

    From the outside, this looks like an absolutely great Mouse Guard story. Two inexperienced mice try to stop a raging flood by themselves and they fail (of course), but they manage to save lives against all odds! What a wonderful opening sequence. I can picture the panel with the guard mice standing on a hilltop with the wet survivors, looking down on the rooftops of Port Sumac poking up through the water.

    I'm sorry to hear it was a frustrating experience. From my armchair, I'm seeing a big disconnect between the player's expectations and the reality of the game and system. To me, that conflict played out perfectly and gave you great material to work with. But if the players expected to go up against the toughest opposition in the game and win on their first try, without good tactics and no teamwork... well... like Luke said, that's not what's going on in this game.

    I don't see anything in the book that suggests that newly-made guard mice are superheroes who stop raging floods without a lot of help. The message I'm getting from the book is the exact opposite. Mice are small and vulnerable against the big, cruel world. When they succeed, it's due to perseverance, teamwork, using their mousy Natures, and luck.

    Having said that, the mice could have done better in the conflict if the players had used all their resources. With Nature, teamwork dice, wises, and traits, the mice could have been rolling more dice than the Season. It's still far from a sure thing, but with some luck they might have pulled it out (leaving them a bit resource poor, but hey... they would have stopped a flood).

    The mice take some conditions and/or they face a setback, the situation changes (the Twist), and they move on to the next obstacle. That's the general shape of play, with "failure" as a main feature. Having the deck stacked against the patrol is good and normal play.

    \n I dunno if I want to touch the brain damage thing... but there is something that could be going on with precedent here.

    Like, there are a lot of games out there that are basically mission completion games. The point of those games is to use your resources well in order to successfully complete a mission. In those games failing the mission is failing. It isn't game destroying, but it is a failure. You had a chance to step on up, and you didn't step, as it were.

    Mouse Guard looks a lot like a mission completion games. Mouse Guard feels a lot like a mission completion game. But I don't think Mouse Guard actually is a mission completion game. Mouse Guard is a game where you tell a story about mice who go on a mission. That's a close thing, but its also a sharp and important divide.

    If you're playing MG as a mission completion game, then failing to stop the river is frustrating and annoying, and feels like a player failure because (in some ways at least) it is a player failure. If, otoh, you're playing MG as a story game about mice on a mission, then you get the exact response both Ralph and John gave -- "Yay, the small mice lost against the big cruel world, but at least we saved the other mice!"

    It's a paradigm thing, as much as anything.

      \n I think most people in the thread meant "failure" not in the sense that the characters didn't get something out of the conflict, but in the sense that the original poster's group said "let's go build a bridge!" and then "oh shit, we didn't build a bridge". That's failure in the normal everyday walking around talking sense of the word "failure." It may be a good or a bad thing for a game or a story or a story game but let's not pretend it isn't what it clearly is. Come on now. There's failure in this game. The original poster experienced it.

      I don't know where all the sidewise in this thread came from, I kinda thought all the suggestions were good, especially the observation that the mission identified by the group and introduced in the fiction is not in fact the point of the game. That's a huge distinction that can easily be missed, especially since most other games that are organized by mission (Spycraft, MURPG, etc.) explicitly give you rewards for beating the mission and sometimes even punish you for not beating it, and their missions may or may not be linked - by contrast, from what I'm reading here, in MG, they are required to be linked. Are they required to be a response to the outcome of the previous mission or is the link permitted to be more tenuous? (Am I getting the right impression from the thread?)

      The little heroic miceys standing on the roofs of their flooded homes, that's such a great image, I want to dive right into the next mission right away! And that is a big emotional difference from "well Mr. Bond, goldfinger ripped off fort knox because you and your team flubbed it, oh well, your next mission is to fight Dr. No". One is up, the other is down. You should feel bad if you let Goldfinger rip off Fort Knox! You lost! The end! But if it's not the end, if it's a loss in a string of challenges, that's different - like in With Great Power, where you have to lose 5 times before you can defeat the villain's plans. 5 times - you can win Over 9,000 times but your victories will never ever put the villain's dastardly plan for world domination in jeopardy until you lose 5 times. It makes the losses more sweet, even though they're painful. "Arrgh, the bank was robbed and my girlfriend betty jo is now betty bee the stinging insect-girl of the destructulons!"

      That's not to say that previous mission-based games don't have advantages. Self-containment makes it easy on everyone to keep characters on the same page, introduce new characters and get rid of old ones, etc.

      Anyhow, stop fooling around, there was good stuff here.

      *arms crossed defiantly*
