\n I've been spending most of my videogame hours playing Riviera: The Promised Land, a GBA title I got for a song via the classifieds board at work. Nearly to the finish of it, really. And it's infected my brain.

The "Mood" and "Trust" mechanic, by which you cultivate the affections of your four female party members to determine which (if any) becomes your lifelong friend in the game's ending, reminded me a great deal of some story games. Most notably, The Mountain Witch and Bliss Stage.

So I'm thinking: action-adventure harem anime story game??

It'd be a disturbingly easy kitbash, if you wanted to do it that way. Mash together the abovementioned Trust mechanics (each scene, you can improve or damage the girls' Moods, translating into a shift in Affection at the end of the chapter), stir it up with some Primetime Adventures style Screen Presence and/or Issues (every girl has a Problem that will come to a head at some point in the story--will our hero prove himself capable, or bungle it?), top it off with "Confessionals" from Inspectres (so we get the gals' inner thoughts) and a My Life with Master or Contenders style epilogue constrained by the different Affection ratings--could be excellent fun! So long as you had players willing to run with the different roles and play them to the hilt...

Of course, building something from the ground up would be better, but I've never designed a game ever. Unless you count a weird fantasy-cyberpunk thing called "Legacy 2000" I did in grade school. Plus I haven't watched anime in ages unless you count Avatar, so I'd need some inspirational material. Hmm.

    \n I've taken a couple whacks at this genre (and failed to get anywhere good). The big obstacle I've faced is the structure - one protagonist, many supporting cast. The way I think I'd want to do it is to have the players control the supporting cast (the harem, in this case) and have the protagonist be a rules-construct/GM-character sort of like the Master in My Life With Master. The problem is that the supporting cast in these kind of stories doesn't really have much agency - and when they do, the story model tends to blow up.

    \n The model in this sort of story is that the supporting cast are essentially challenges/puzzles that the main character has to decipher in order to get his power-ups and Save The World. (Or get the True Ending, in the harem model). You burn through the side stories en route to the resolution. If you want the supporting cast to remain interesting, protagonized, and worthwhile to play throughout the whole story arc, you probably need to get rid of that. When you do that, you're left with a drawn-out struggle for the protagonist's attention between the supporting cast.

    See Nathan Russell's Space Rat for one take on how to make that into a fun game. 1km1kt link seems to be down right now, though.
