    Jeff_Slater said:

    What would you say are the advantages to posting and commenting on Ron\u2019s site rather than, say, story-Games.com? Do you know what his selling points are, Tod?

    \nI don't want to put words in Ron's mouth, but I see it as a "branding" thing. After all, he is Ron Edwards, his name itself is a draw, and he doesn't post this kind of material in other places. If you (or your games) want to get his attention, AdeptPlay is probably the best place to do it.

    The core of the site is his new Seminar series and Game Consulting (which the consulter may opt to make public or keep private). But as we developed the site, it grew to include "Actual Play" and the ability for non-Rons to post their own game stuff, in a place where it could be discussed by Ron and his crowd (both Patreon supporters and others), as well as the general RPGing public.

    TL/DR: Now that he's set up in his new digs in Sweden, Ron has started a games consulting business, and Adept Play is the internet face of it.

    \n I think that with any creative process there is a continuum between "let's set all the parameters upfront (or have one person in charge of setting them), and enforce that constantly" and "let's all contribute different things until we eventually meet somewhere in the middle, and find out where that is together".

    For our particular hobby/interests, the former is a strong auteur GM or carefully designed scenario with pregenerated characters and style guides (perhaps to the point of "no, your character wouldn't do that"!), whereas the latter is improv theatre, where the mantra of "yes, and" rules supreme - you've got to roll with *whatever* someone else throws at you.

    \n I don't think that he has a magic formula per se. He's just developing his game design, same as all of us. Plus, I've only skimmed the thread so far, so I'm not even sure if I understand the question [grin].

    For me, the big thing I've learned about design-relevant-to-GMless is the visceral aesthetic communication that he emphasizes in his post-Trollbabe works. The GMlessness is not central to this, really - you look at Trollbabe, it's precisely as concerned about this as say Doctor Xaos is. The way I understand it (and apply it) is that if you don't grok the trollbabe herself as an appealing protagonist figure, then we need to talk some more about the aesthetic antecedents of this game, because she's the key to the whole thing. We get to the same page aesthetically by sharing a mutual appreciation for her.

    So I guess that's one sort of formula or technique that is easy to generalize, and has been applied in many games: you take some aesthetic content, text or pictures or whatever, and you lay that down for the gaming group. They are either enthusiastic or not, depending on whether they can relate emotionally to these keystone elements. You need to get everybody on board before continuing further into actual play, either by changing the keystones (e.g. try a different Sorcerer campaign premise, if there's a player who doesn't get your antropomorphic animals idea) or by "opening them" for the other players by further explanation (maybe show them a movie that uses the kind of aesthetics you want).

    I did a very intricate form of this technique myself for our on-going wuxia princess play thing this winter. It involved picking out illustrations for all the NPCs and places in the game so as to get the key aesthetic tenets (ultrahigh fantasy, east/west mixture, romantic rather than realist, etc.) communicated concretely, via example.

    \n My take on this is simply that there isn't any fail-proof way of ensuring that, especially on a GM-less game, everyone will be on the same page aesthetically, thematically, tonally, whatever. No design or mechanics will solve that problem. People have different tastes, cultural references, etc. The more freedom there is for everyone at the table to contribute to the salad, the higher the chance that sometimes stuff will come up from one or more players that someone else at the table will dislike to a certain degree. It's the reason why making movies or books by committee usually is a bad idea.

    So imho Ron is right in that quote above, that creative freedom comes at a cost of higher chance for creative dissonance among the creators, and we just have to accept this. The "workshoping way" helps, using pictures, music, film, other cultural refs help, but none will be perfect. Sometimes you'll have a group that has a very clear unified vision, at other times you won't, or anything in between those two extremes.

    Personally, this is one of the reasons why I tend to dislike GM-less games. If they are one-shots, or just a couple of sessions, like, say, Fiasco, Zombie Cinema, The Roach, etc, then this is usually not much of a problem, because there isn't an investment in any real prep nor is there a significant time commitment for play, so you don't loose much if for some reason you weren't fully satisfied with the overall experience. For long term play, though, you may have a problem. Also, some people have an higher threshold for this kind of divergent creative input, while others (I confess I'm one of them) will be sensitive to these creative departures.

    \n@David_Berg that looks like reduction ad absurdum to me.

    Concretely speaking, if we're playing Sorcerer and all of a sudden you're jumping rooftops, my options as a GM are:\n
    • to just nod and move on - I don't think it's a conflict;
      • \n
    • to realize you're doing that to escape pursuit, get the drop on an enemy or whatever - thus proceed to resolving that conflict;
      • \n
    • as above, but I award you bonus dice because I think jumping rooftops is cool (or surprising, or the best idea you could have);
      • \n
    • to declare jumping rooftops to be a dangerous conflict of its own and have you roll your Stamina vs an arbitrary number of dice chosen by me, based on how crazy I believe rooftop-jumping to be - let's say if you win, your victories translate to bonus dice on whatever follow-up action, but should you lose, you'll suffer wounds from the fall based on my victories on the edged weapons/small guns table;
      • \n
    • say "that's crazy", but since you insist on doing it anyway, call it a conflict, as above.
    \nI wrote "I think" and "I believe" and "I choose" a lot in the above bullet points, but it's been made clear (Annotated Edition and whatnot) that the Sorcerer GM is supposed to "channel" the table - i.e. I have to make those calls based on what I think everybody is thinking, getting my cues from you, the players (which is somewhat hard and actually my biggest issue with Sorcerer, btw).     \n I tend to operate as a strong but supportive GM, eliciting input from Players and using it to construct and describe the shared imagined space. There is no perfect solution to the problem of conflicting visions arising from two or more Players, because that always has a chance of happening (and I am leaving aside cases in which some rule of reality has been miscommunicated or misunderstood), but I find that the best way to handle it when it does happen is similar to the methods of attaining consensus that I learned while I was an OCCUPY facilitator. It requires breaking the proposal (action, whatever) down into its constituent elements and determining exactly which of those elements are causing the conflict to arise. Rarely is the entire proposal objectionable outright.

    Basically, the GM suggests modifications to the action or description in question which remove or soften only those specific parts of it that cause conflict, while retaining all the rest of it. In group facilitator parlance this is called "modifying the proposal to remove blocks." Once the blocks are removed (i.e. those elements of the proposal that caused an issue with someone - perhaps even myself), consensus may again be assumed and the proposal flies.

    At the gaming table we have one more tool at our disposal that activist groups don't have: the dice. If we're unsure of which way to go, it is often possible to ask those magical little polyhedral solids.

      \n Listening to Ron running Sorcerer, I notice he does a lot of tentative floating when things get stuck, like thinking for a few seconds and then saying something like "Oh! You know what might be cool?" He tends to be speaking to a particular person when he says stuff like this.
