  • Ben_Lehman
    November 2006
    Oh, well, in that case ...

    My list of games that a designer has to have:

    Breaking the Ice
    Shooting the Moon
    These are because Emily Boss has the best grasp of mechanical role-playing game design of anyone.

    Under the Bed.
    Primetime Adventures.
    These games are my go-to for what *not* to write rules for.

    My Life With Master.
    Along with being a good game, it's a fundamental primer on pacing.

    Riddle of Steel.
    It's not clear from the text, but the combat, advancement, and plotting mechanics of this game are tied together tightly in a way that's often been imitated (and sometimes by very good games) but never repeated.

    Trollbabe
    It's incredibly clean, simple, and clear. More than once, simply reading the appropriate section of this game has clarified a design issue for me.

    There's nothing here from this year aside from Shooting the Moon because I haven't really had time to digest the design lessons of this year's games yet. Ask me again in a year.

    --Ben

    What Dictionary is is an interesting artifact of book design (it makes some choices I do not agree with but it's clearly intentional and very crafted) and an interesting approach to presentation of setting - it's clear from how it's laid out that it is only telling you interesting things, and furthermore it is not telling you all of them.

    I think it's pretty neat for the way it starkly acknowledges, and then proceeds to take advantage of, the obvious fact that you are not going to literally use everything in the text of a setting, so Judd's obviously worked hard to cram useful subtext into it too, primarily in the form of a really strong, pervasive mood. I think that once I get through with reading the book, I'd be confident in my ability to create Mu-ish stuff off the top of my head, and that's what settings are really all about.

    I.e., I think it's really cool for that, and you don't even need to play it with the rules it provides to get good stuff out of it (which is awesome to me because Sorc does not particularly interest me). It is basically the ideal supplement.

    November 2006
    Scarlet Wake is another implementation of the "Player-GMs split" (seen in Great Ork Gods and almost in Polaris, though Ben [Lehman] decided to lean away from that), where players take turns playing their PC while the other players scheme in the corner and invent crazy shit to throw at them. It's kinda Great Ork Gods-meets-Rune-meets-Wushu, if that makes any sense. Brilliant piece of work that will hopefully someday get a commercial release or at least a polishing. Get someone to email you a copy of the playtest version.

    Continuum is utterly brilliant but very difficult to run as written because of the record-keeping involved. Notably, it inspired Nathan Paoletta's Timestream, which is a "Continuum heartbreaker" so to speak. The idea is that you play characters who can time travel at will but have strict limits on how far back or forward you can go, so, at least in the early stages of the game, you're generally bouncing around within the same few weeks or months. The main plots revolve around resolving the paradoxes creating by time traveling anarchists (the true heroes of the setting, really) through the neat Time Combat system which works sorta like battles of wits in the Burning Wheel (as far as I understand BW). Players declare certain types of actions like "I'm gonna travel to a certain point in time and spread rumors that I was conducting research in place X at time Y, so the other guy will show up then looking for me and I'll cream him." And you roll dice to see if that succeeds and sorta go from there. It hurts your brain, but in a totally good way that gives you a look at hitherbefore unimagined possibilities. Roleplaying in 4 dimensions. It's also hugely about player empowerment, kinda by default, because it's nigh-impossible to plan a scenario in advance.

    EDIT: ...interesting thread. As I read the above selection, I have a hard time seperating "What I recommend for a game designer who wants a look at indie games", and "What I like" posts.

    But if I had to recommend 5 indie games, they would most likely be:

    Sorcerer (what condensed, CONDENSED rules look like, and a new focus for a way to go about the roleplaying activity. Plus lots of quirky, interesting rules)
    Prime Time Adventures (clever, focused and simple rules, but more for the effect that it has on play; Simple Game Make Awesome Funtime)
    Dead Inside (what a "traditional campaign setting" should look like/be written)
    Spirit of the Century (where The Bar is in modern game design, not just "production" so much as "completeness")
    and a toss up between Shooting the Moon and Dogs in the Vineyard. Since most folks have Dogs, I'd say Shooting the Moon (games with a laser focus and new ways of coming to the table).

    That would be my top five, for various design purposes. Of course, I'd want to add about 15 games to this for various reasons, but the 'top five' are above.
