\n Okay, so I'm looking for creative ways to kill time at work, on my lunch break, over the next several days. And you folks seem to be pretty good with fun tests of mettle and such. Starting tomorrow, what I'd like you to do is post a single word or concept that you find cool (or k3wl, if you prefer). The following day, I'll post a quick-ish setting for a free-press RPG built around that single concept or word.

The caveat is that how I build upon that concept or word is entirely up to me.

So, for example, if you say "Handlebar Moustache" I might come up with a setting in which a bigger moustache entitles you to more civil rights (and in which failure to grow a moustache brands you forever a member of the lower class). Or, if you say "Dr. Seuss Meets the Mythos", I might come up with an absurd world of bright colors, floogelated architecture, and creatures of marvelously, marvellous, madness that reside within a children's book.

You get the idea. Post away, please

    Honor Amongst Thieves: A Game of Dandy Lions

    In the city of New Alexandretta, across the Cerulean Sea, on the Eastern shore of the Unatmed Lands, amidst the vermin-infested chess parlors, thick with the fog of khurt smoke, babble of money lenders, and stockpiles of absurd trinkets intended to remind wayward travellers of their home far away. . .

    New Alexandretta

    The city of New Alexandretta is the only port of entry to the Untamed Lands, that faraway realm of wonder seperated from the Empire by seemingly endless leagues of deep, blue, sea. Powerful as the Empire is, its reach does not extend here, despite claims to the contrary. Here, in the lawless city of new beginnings, real power belongs to the entrepreneur -- the man with the boldness of character and courage required to seize it.

    Here, in the city built upon the ruins of an ancient metropolis, true power is wielded by brutal street gangs -- the 'dandy lions' of this brave, new, world. These men and women are "lions" because they live by a brutal code. They're "dandy" because they understand that social refinement need not be relinquished merely because one relies upon violance to ensure compliance with their will. A rapier thrust to the throat may be delivered in a purely polite fashion.

    The Neighborhoods

    The city is divided into a near endless number of burroughs or neighborhoods, each under the control of a different criminal gang who is responsible, not only for ensuring that things in their respective domain run according to their will -- but also affording their protection to the people who live therein.

    The Gangs of New Alexandretta

    Each gang in the city of New Alexandretta is created like a knightly order per the rules of Paladin. Each has their own code, though it (obviously) won't conform to the ideals of Imperial Law. The gangs make their own laws in their own land, and they hold each other (as well as their subordinates) to them. For example, the Clock Watchers of Chembly Whaite always observe the Imperial tradition of taking Tea at 1200 marks (a measurement of time) and they never suffer a fool to live (they believe that life is a reward for those blessed with the quick wit to survive and that it is their sacred duty to ensure that those individuals lacking wit not be burdened with such a horrible existence).

    The Gang Members

    As individuals, gang members may have their own codes -- things that they will or won't do but that aren't necessarily doctrine observed by the respective gangs to which they belong. These codes are generated just as any other in Paladin, merely denoted as being personal, as opposed to group goals.

    Death and Chess

    The one rule ostensibly observed by all gangs of New Alexandretta in the interest of facilitating negotiations of a civilized nature in an otherwise civilized environment is that of Parley Over Chess. The long-standing tradition of Parley Over Chess is best described as a kind of mobile neutral ground upon which disputes can be settled.

    While sitting over a chessboard (and playing chess), two parties with a dispute agree to suspend any and all acts of violence toward one another for the duration of the game in an effort to reach a civil resolution to what would otherwise be a very uncivil issue. During this time, formal rules of conduct are adhered to by both parties, each exhibiting perfect manners (indeed, seeing who can be more polite in such circumstances is a game in and of itself).

    As soon a game of chess ends, however, all niceties are cast aside and, if no resolution has been reached, swift and immediate violence may be employed to resolve the otherwise unresolveable issue in question. No holds barred. Employign such force or brutality prior to the official end of a game is a violation of Parley Over Chess and is, itself, punished by immediate and swift execution that mirrors the brutality displayed by the lout who violated said rules.

    Dying as a result of losing your cool during Parley Over Chess is referred to, appropriately enough, as a 'death playing chess'. For example, "Oh, old Morley? He absconded from this mortal coil as a result of death whilst playing chess. Bless his ill-tempered soul."

    Rules Crap

    The only real rules bits worth mentioning here are the manner in which this setting takes the rules of Paladin and spins them.

    First:

    You don't play virtuous heroes here. You may have virtues (as does every man) but at the end of the day, you're a right bastard who uses the ultraviolence to enforce his will.

    Second:

    Scope. This setting introduces new scopes of play. The default scope is Parlour Play, which is to say that play focuses on gang codes. This is also the default scope for Paladin, albeit with Order codes replacing those of 'house rules' for street gangs. The NEW scope is Personal Play, which is to say that play focuses on personal moral codes.

    During Personal Play, you largely ignore the codes of Parlour Play, and vice-vera because that's not what Xl play is about. The exception is when a character's personal code clearly conflicts with the code of his gang, in which case you momentarily Split the Character, essentially pitting the character against himself with a contested action, each 'self' resolving an action with the appropriate attribute.

    In the case of such resolution, the version of the character who wins out substitutes his code for that of the other. Permanently. For example, Fernando Regalius Bastardo the Third has a personal code of "Never harm children" and a gang code of "Punish those who abuse our generosity" -- and he finds a young, starving, street urchin pilfering a loaf of bread that is the rightful property of a merchant protected by Bastardo's crew, the Fenagalen Avenue Boys.

    In this instance, Bastardo the FAB gang member, squares off against Bastardo the man. If Bastardo the gang member wins,. "Punish those who abuse our generosity" replaces "Never harm children" in Bastardo the man's personal code. If Bastardo the man wins, "Never harm children" replaces "Punish those who abuse our generosity" in Bastardo the gang member's code. Remaining human and keeping your credibility as a brutal, powerful, albeit well-mannered, lord of the street is a tight rope to walk.

    And that, ultimately, is what being a Dandy Lion is about -- walking the line.
