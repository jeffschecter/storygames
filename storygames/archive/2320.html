I could do with some help on this.

Last night, I played GRS with my girlfriend. If you like, have a look at the latest rules draft: it's not essential, though.

So the set-up is this: there's these guys, the GRS, and they're sent into a nightclub to convert a straight. They get through the club by charm, seduction and pure luck: not by violence.

The GRS have different skills, from useful ("Banging and yelling at technical devices until they work") to useless ("Baking a nice Victoria sponge"). Each has his own seduction style (e.g. "Targets lonely straights and bonds over their shared rejection"). Each also have issues: Baggage (e.g. "Last boyfriend dumped him, saying he was too old and boring"), a Neurosis (e.g. "If in an untidy room, must make some effort to tidy it up") and other quirks.

So the Squad make their way through the nightclub. They get past the security systems and seduce the guards. And these conflicts, especially the seductions, are the best part of the game. They're funny.

But the game seems like just a series of conflicts. Now, they're fun, and that's a big start: they remind me of the conflicts in Best Friends.

And, although our game was fun, this worries me. It needs some narrative structure, I think: something to make sense of the conflicts. I think the answer may lie in the Baggage, but I'm not sure. In fact, I'm stuck.

Has anyone got any suggestions? How can I make it more than a series of conflicts? What should a game like this be about, really? It's something to do with relationships, I think. Aaaargh. I'm missing something bloody obvious here and I can't put my finger on it.

Graham
