Vow of Poverty

wyrmwood
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
As a GM in most games you have (roughly speaking) three types of authority:

Propaganda - the social expectations of your role, including the "GM is God" rhetoric, but also includes more benign aspects, such as coordinating or hosting the game.

Ownership - the ownership of aspects of the fiction, in many ways this is analogous to the player's main source of authority, as well.

Ubiquity - the facilitation role of the GM in the system, this includes both arbitration and instigation.

It's clear that keeping all three of these forms of authority poses a significant risk of abuse.

Propaganda is the most obvious form, hinting of unmitigated exercises of power. Ownership tends to be a common source of problems, as it leads to possessiveness - GM's character, GM's setting, GM's story, or even just GM's theme. Ubiquity seems to be something that gets clouded over by the ownership and propaganda, but ultimately it is the the thing the GM does that is unique to the role.

A Vow of Poverty is to try to give up the first two forms of authority: propaganda and ownership. Focus on your role as arbitrator and instigator. Let the rhetoric of power lie, and treat your possessions (characters, settings, and stories) as communal property - at most given over to your safe-keeping.

As GMs, its easy to ignore the importance of our facilitating role, to focus on what we have or what is said about us. But ubiquity is an authority that is qualitatively different, one of synergy and buy-in. If you give up everything else, you might just find something you never knew was there.

- Mendel S.

    DON'T BE A JERK.

If you're not a jerk, then you don't make insane comments about wearing the viking hat and how rocks fall and everyone dies. If you're not a jerk, people will be willing to accept you as an arbitrator and instigator without a lot of fuss.     Mike is pretty much right.

    I think the main disconnect here is that authority and responsibility are not the same thing. While responsibilities need to be met, the authority under which they are met is not as inflexible. This also makes the naming for Propoganda clearer - translating "I'm the GM" or "This is my house" or "I bought the pizza" into game authority borrows from the social awareness of the roles.

    The key thing is that most authority is unnecessary, and in my personal experience, ubiquity is largely enough to meet your responsibilities. The problem is that the GM role is rich with authority, making it easy to be heavy handed or rely on one approach to excess. Ubiquity is underutilized precisely because it is more subtle and often requires more care in its use.

    The purpose of a Vow of Poverty is to learn how to be a GM differently, to expand your set of tools and skills. It's not saying that propoganda and ownership are irrelevant or abusive by nature, but to reveal that other means exist, and to explore their use.

    The more options you have the easier it is not to "be a jerk". If you wanted to be a jerk, then not much prevents that. If you didn't you likely do not have the tools to know what you were doing or know another way to do it. The purpose of a vow of poverty is to ease that problem, among others.

    - Mendel S.
