The GM Section: Any life left?

Larry
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games
Just so we're on the same page, in this thread I'm talking about how traditionally, RPGs have had the main player section, and then had a block of additional content for the GM's eyes only, expounded things like advice on how to hose the players, monster stats, suggested metaplot crap. You know the drill. Then these newfangled indie designs come along, wisely realize that keeping players in the dark about this stuff just encourages all sorts of bad behaviors, and assume that the whole game text is to be consumed by all participants.

So anyway, there's this game design I'm thinking up. There's a big horror/exploration of the unknown element to it. By force of habit, my thoughts started conceiving of material that would go into a "player section" and sooper-secret setting advice that would go into the "GM section." But wait a minute, that's so not vogue! I was sort of thinking that the prohibition against player reading would be explicitly tongue-in-cheek, but that might end up endorsing an illusionist style of play where players feign ignorance of having read the material.

So my question is, does the notion of a "secret GM section" have any milage left in it? What is it really good for? Or is it a sacred cow that deserves to stay dead? More importantly, what might an "enlightened" GM section look like?

    It's useful to delineate for your audience what parts of the text are crucial for different player roles. If a player at the table only needs to know X to play the game, and and the one player who takes the role of the GM needs to know X and Y, letting the reader know that is valuable. The reason is, many players (e.g. many of my players) don't enjoy reading rules or text as much as I do. If there is a subset of knowledge that is applicable to them they only want to have to read that. I wouldn't make my players read the Town creation rules in DITV to play. They might find it informs their play and makes the game more interesting / cohesive. But it's not crucial for their play so they can't be bothered. It's a simple but unfortunate fact in my opinion. And one that a good gaming text can recognize usefully in its structure (especially when the full text startes getting over the length that can be read in the course of a few hours).

    Essentially, this delineation lowers the barrier for entry of new player. That's a good thing.

    I think sections that contain "advice for the GM" can be invaluable. FLFS has a great one (see my upcoming review). However, such advice is not "secret GM info."

    A game based on horror or exploration (or other elements of the unknown) will either need to preload secret info into the GM, or give really solid advice on how to create their own secrets. You could also give a half-thousand "secrets" in the style of Unknown Armies and let GMs decide which ones to use on their own.

    What I'm saying is that a secret GM section can be done, and makes sense for some kinds of games, but does not *need* to be done if you don't mind people creating their own answers to your setting's big questions.

    December 2006 edited December 2006
    If you're using a traditional GM/player split, i.e. GM makes decisions about the setting, players make decisions about the characters, then the GM advice chapter should show the GM how to make those decisions.

    Players rarely need this type of coaching, because their decisions are usually quite easy to make. Many simply do "what I would do in that situation". Some put together a kind of psychology for their character, a framework so to speak, and base their decisions on "what the character would do in that situation". Only the really passionate go beyond that and try to meticulously fit their character's personality and actions into the game's setting.

    GMs on the other hand, sometimes have a more difficult task. I know many GMs who "make the setting their own", that is they do what ever they want or what they think the players enjoy whenever they make a decision. I don't think that those GMs ever read the GM-only section, or care much for it. But there are also GMs who, like their player-counterparts, want to have some kind of framework (a number of axioms that tells them how the setting works), they want to use when making decisions. One of the most prominent axioms is "realism", where the GM tries to base his decisions on what is most realistic, plausible or likely in this setting. Others prefer "coolness" or "humour". But these are most often GM-specific, as opposed to game-specific.

    A good GM section would help the GM understand what kind of axioms the setting is build on, or at the very least advice on how to get the "style" of the game right. How to make "unique fantasy game" feel like "unique fantasy game", instead of Bob's Homebrew #23 or "same old fantasy game but with silly rules". In my experience it is rarely helpful to spell such things out. Instead adventures (introductory or otherwise) and fluff texts help the GM get a handle on a game's/setting's style.

    One thing that I've always found incredibly helpful, were hearing different GMs talk about what made the setting unique. I've always wondered why GM sections never had more than one take on how to run the game printed .

    Larry, I think there is still a lot of mileage left in the Secret GM Section, it's just that we've realized that the Secret GM Section is not applicable to all games. Books like Dogs or FLFS (with its sidebar "This is not the GM Section") don't require sections describing secrets, since whatever secrets are used in play are created for the individual play experience. If you look at, say, Thirty, the whole game is hinged on a powerful, informed GM and protagonized, uninformed players. The set up of the game requires a Secret GM Section.

    So I think the question you need to ask is not, "Are GM Sections out of date?" so much as "Would my design benefit from a secret GM section?"
