Death Spiral + Escape Clause: What's it do?

TonyLB
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
So, suppose you win a roll, and that gives you an extra die that will help you to win future rolls (which will, in turn, give you extra dice to be yet more likely to win future rolls)? That's a positive feedback loop, often called a "death spiral." On its own, this will tend toward extremes.

But now, suppose that you get to choose, on each roll, whether to stake those extra dice. If you stake them and win then you keep them (and, as always, get another extra). But if you stake them and lose, they all go away. You only get to keep them as long as you keep winning.

If that's strapped to an economy system that lets people (particularly your opposition, but also you) manage other resources ... that's no longer a simple death spiral, is it? What strategies and responses do you think such rules would evoke?

  • Mike_Holmes
    February 2007
    JD has a point. In terms of colloquialisms, a Death Spiral is a feedback loop where failure reinforces the chances of overall failure. The lesser known colloquialism for the effect where winning reinforces winning is the Steamroller Effect.

    Possibly the best know example of the Steamroller Effect are in "StratCon-like" games (such as Warlords), where capture of resource points allows you to create more troops, and thus capture more strategic points, etc, etc.

    Note that it's easy to assume that these two effects are simply the converse of each other. But that's not always true. That is, in the example of the games that I cite above, losing a city doesn't penalize the player in terms of what the rest of his production is like. In fact, the Collapsing Ballon Effect may mean that he's proportionally better off than he was with the other strategic point in hand. So, while the Steamroller Effect is in play for the player taking the strategic point, the Death Spiral is not in effect for the person taking the hit.

    If it were, then these games would likely be unplayably bad.


    BTW, the term for the point at which success or failure becomes assured, or nearly assured, is the Point of No Return. In a "strat-con" type game, often the game will end when one player captures a simple majority of the strategic points, because, barring very incompetent play, that's a Point of No Return. Playing beyond that point is largely pointless.

    Mike

    Well, I think this is broadly how Ars Magica combat works.

    Combat involves opposed rolls, if I win the roll then I have an amount by which I beat the other guy. I can now choose either to apply that immediately as damage from which armour will be subtracted, or to carry it over.

    If I carry it over, it gets added to my next opposed roll next round. If I win that, I have a new total excess which again I may choose to apply in damage or may carry over again.

    I can do this indefinitely, I can keep carrying over as long as I keep winning the opposed contests, but the moment I lose one I lose all my carryover.

    The idea is to allow a guy with a dagger to take down a guy in platemail, if the dagger guy is way skilled. He keeps round after round accumulating his total until finally he has so much that he uses it and wastes plate mail guy, otherwise his dagger just wouldn't do enough damage. In game, it represents tiring out platemail guy and gaining positional advantage until he is effectively helpless.

    How does it work in actual play? Quite well in my experience, players judged the benefit of further accrual against the risk of losing accrued benefit, if they figured they were better sometimes they would accrue so as to hopefully take their opponent down in one fell strike. If it was more evenly balanced, they'd try and wear him down round by round. It introduced a simple, but quite satisfying and evocative, tactical element to play and made combats more interesting than otherwise might have been the case.
