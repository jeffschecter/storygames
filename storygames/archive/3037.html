Posted By: Adam DrayI ran a 007 game once using the classic Victory Games ruleset. It was like 20 years ago so please excuse me for lack of details. I had two players, my brother Jason and my friend Mark, who made up agents and they went on their merry way trying to do agenty things. I think they failed some critical roll and the villain got away and managed to place a bomb on a train and blew up the person they were protecting. Something like that. Anyway, we thought the game sucked. It didn't give us the kind of experience that we wanted.

For us, we wanted to feel like James-fucking-Bond. We wanted to succeed most of the time -- or have clear alternatives when we failed. We wanted to come out of gunfights without a smudge on our clean, pressed suits. Most importantly, we wanted more tools to say, "No, fuck that, this dumb-ass thing doesn't happen." Instead, we were slave to the dice, which said *whiff* "you miss." Sucktastic.

Posted By: Adam DrayI wish I could remember. What do the rules suggest? If they recommended starting low and working up to Double-0, that's what we did. If they say, "Hey, just pick the level at which you want to play," then I have no clue what we did.

Still, I have to ask, why does a Double-0 game have rookie agent PCs in it? Why support that kind of play? Do people buy that game while thinking, "Man, I don't want to play James Bond. I want to play one of those mooks who gets wasted by the villain in the first scene"?

I'll grant that I was a dumb teenager who hadn't much clue about how to run a Bond game. I probably should have jumped right into Double-O's (if I didn't) since, as you say, the game seems to handle them very well.
\nIt's a good question. The game doesn't present Rookie as the default option. There are three levels: Rookie, Agent, and Double-0 -- and it presents sample PCs for each level and supports all three levels in the character creation and the rest of the book. However, a number of the modules are supposedly balanced for one Double-0 PC, two Agent PCs, or three Rookie PCs. I had to beef up the opposition in the module I was using to face three Double-0's.

So the game deserves some blame for *not* having Double-0 as the default, and the other ranks as optional. However, I think that also some players suffered from assumptions from other games. i.e. They came from D&D, and to them it felt like cheating to start at higher than first level even though they wanted to be James Bond and the rules allowed it. I started with JB007 after Champions which probably helped.

The module issue is a little tricky. Balancing for one Double-0 agent is good in the sense of emulating the genre. After all, that's what the movies have. There should have been better guidelines for beefing up the opposition like I did, though I can see that it's easier on module writers to just write one module and handle more players by lower rank.

    \nThis is an excellent point, and one that's always struck me whenever my group starts a new game and we're told to make characters at whatever the minimum power level is in that system. I keep pushing something like using pregenerated low-power characters in a one-shot "introduction" game to get people acquainted with the rules and the setting and the possibilities, and then ditching the pregens and going with more high-powered "Big and Cool" characters...but the additional work involved tends to keep that from happening.

    \n Something about this reminds me of the advice Vincent always gives for people playing Dogs for the first time. Rookie Dogs GMs often want to create Towns with small amounts of trouble going on. Vincent usually tells them, no, dude, crank it all the way up to murder. Don't be subtle.

    I think for Bond, you don't want subtle for rookies, either. In this case, subtle means sliding away from the genre emulation and creating characters who can't do things easily. I'd recommend Double-0's for beginners and tell players that the rookies and agents are for NPCs and for more advanced players who want to try something different.

    If the trouble with doing that is complex chargen, make chargen simpler. John's right that Bond needs templates or something to make chargen faster.

    If the trouble is too many options during play, just have a rookie player option that limits the number of options while still making the characters strong. For instance, instead of getting 20 skills with high to very high values, you get 5 skills with very high values and every other skill defaults to high or something. The complexity dial here is how many skills are not left to a default value. The difficulty dial here is the default skill value.

It's got a good character sheet with necessary charts, including an example one filled out. It's got a solo adventure.

    \n Over in "How best to support Sim", Mike Holmes had some comments on JB007 that I thought were appropriate to this thread...\n
    Posted By: Mike HolmesFirst, while I think that Genre Sim is relatively rare, as I then say, I think that games like FATE do it well. So it does exist as a newer phenomenon in greater quantity. Put another way, if you like Genre Sim, I think that right not is a good time for you. Systems to support it strongly are appearing.

    Also I have been called on that statement, in terms of whether I can back it up, and I should say that this is just my own observation. The point I was trying to make is not that people do not play genre sim (much less should not), but that as a proportion of sim play I think it historically has taken a back seat to the prevalence of more immersion-driven sim. That is, where something like James Bond does not seem to support Genre Sim, that's because it intends to support Immersion Sim.

    The border between Genre Sim and Immersion Sim is like where somebody says, "I know that James Bond always gets the bad guys, but without a chance to fail, it doesn't feel like I'm there and in the action." That's stilted, sure, but hopefully you get the idea. That's not to say that Genre Sim can't have a chance to fail. It means that in each of these something different is being cherished at the expense of something else. In Genre, we do more to promote the genre, at the expense of the feeling of "being there," whereas in Immersion sim we sacrifice the story looking like a James Bond story for the feeling that we're "being" James Bond.
    \nIt doesn't seem to me that Gerard Klug's JB007 RPG is immersive to me. In my experience, the players are all interested in the ongoing plot, without a lot of delving into character depths. For us, the characters were iconic -- i.e. Sebastian Hyde, 003, had his signature drink of Bombay Sapphire and tonic, and has a wife and daughter. Also, many of the mechanics are abstract rather than representational -- like Hero Point spending and the Ease Factor bidding mechanics.

    I would say that JB007 does a lot to support the genre -- it just doesn't do it by guiding play to reproduce the plot formulas of the movies. Instead, it concentrates on the tropes of the movies, like having thrilling car chases and step-by-step seductions. Because of how it is structured as a game, the overall plot might not go the same way as a typical movie does, but the game play captures the feel of ongoing action fairly well.

    Obviously, opinions may differ. But I'd like to hear about the varying experiences with this. \n
    Posted By: Mike HolmesNow, does the whiff factor support immersion sim? Tough question. It's going to come down to whether the people playing think that the level that the factor is at is "realistic." I'm not saying that James Bond is, in fact, well designed to deliver what it intends. Merely that this seems to me to be the goal. It man, in fact, fall short of even that goal.

    And is that possibly in part because of the rules also being from an era that assumes some gamism in play (meaning that the system promotes multiple modes, and thus incoherence)? Probably. James Bond, you have to understand, was pretty remarkable for it's time in how it made much of any attempt to model genre. Top Secret, actually, had started down this road with the inclusion of Fame and Fortune Points. That's a very rough beginning. But it's a tiny move from "Gamism Spy Dungeons" to something a tad more like the genre. James Bond was a huge leap forward from that. Tremendous. Just including rules for car chases, for instance, meant that they would occur in spy play (whereas before, as I say, the play was all about traipsing across quadrille rendered maps with rooms with contents descriptions). Rather, some people were doing this stuff despite these systems to be sure. But the systems were trying hard to keep up. Top Secret at least allowed you to have your character behave heroically while he still had points. As opposed to those dungeon crawling cowards who run as soon as they're out of magic missiles.
    \nHaving run a campaign fairly recently, I think JB007 is still competitive with most games as far as modelling its genre. i.e. It kicks the ass of the Lord of the Rings RPG (admittedly a low goal). I consider it competitive with recent genre games like Buffy the Vampire Slayer, Dead of Night and Spirit of the Century.

    As far as whiff factor, it seems to me to work fairly well for the genre. Rookies (i.e. Mary Goodnight) will tend to have around 50% chance of success in their good skills for standard tasks; Agents (i.e. Felix Leiter) will have around 100%; and Double-0's (i.e. Bond, Anya Amasova) will have around 150%.
