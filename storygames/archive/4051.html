\n We played a game of Shock 1.1 last night at our Go Play PDX gathering. The world we came up with ended up with a few parallels to The Matrix and Equilibrium, but with plenty of aspects of our own that infused it with all sorts of possibilities. Overall, we probably spent half of the session with setup and the other half with playing our protagonists. Since we had 5 of those, we didn't get to play many scenes for each, but surprisingly we managed to resolve one story goal and get close to a couple of others.

I have a bunch of things to say about the game, both about Shock and our playtime in particular, but I need to let that stew for a while before I write that out. In the meantime, let it be known that 1.1 was very clear in most aspects, and the rules led to a good session that we all seemed eager to continue next time.

I wrote up one scene (and a half) in a fictionalized version on my livejournal, along with my protagonist data. Fictionalized just means I added a lot of details and wrote it like a fiction scene; the conflicts, minutiae and events were the ones that came out in the game.

    \nYeah, it's funny, it wasn't until I was trying to describe the game for someone else that I realized, "Y'know, this sounds like we're cribbing from the Matrix. . ." Believe it or not, the thought never entered my mind during setup or play. Hell, our setup session started with the idea of Galactus as a shock, and evolved from there. Our thought process was something along the lines of, "What if there's an imminent destruction of society/the world coming, which cannot be stopped?" From there we went to "OK, so the world's technology is slowly failing, and people have to cope with that in the face of total dependence on that tech." And then it was just a short hop to "Total dependence? OK, so how about everyone's wired into the virtual world, increasingly leaving their bodies behind, and only savages and outcasts spend their lives in harsh reality?" Mix in a little theocracy (the Church that preaches abandonment of Sinful Flesh and excommunicates by jacking you out, for whom Christian was an Enforcer), and we were good!

    Jake, ditto on the Blade Runner and Ghost in the Shell (I haven't seen Avalon), plus I wasx drawing a bit on the Void Captain's Tale for my portrayal of a decadent and conflicted Cyberistocrat.

    Also, I too found the text to be quite clear, aside from a couple of fiddly questions that Joshua cleared up for me over on the Forge. 'Course, I haven't seen the original, so I have nothing to compare it to.

    Christian, on the Antagonist-changing, I would probably (as Joshua alludes to) wait until after your Story-goal conflict resolves, and take (if appropriate from the results, which it probably will be) the new Antag then. It still makes sense to have the outcasts as your Antag during the final conflict, just like it made sense leading up to it. After all, they're the ones causing the inner conflict between your conscience and the Church's directives! When you've good and made an enemy of the Church, then's a fine time procedurally change it over.

    Naturally, there's probably a good bit of personal preference in the above, especially as I'm playing your Antagonist.

    All that aside, I thought I'd relate one of my favorite bits from the game, a dialogue exchange between Jake's protag Nix, who believes the virtual world is all that exists, and Annie's protag Annie (chosen for her own ease), a programming tech who's currently jacked out:

    Nix: [s/he changes avatars on impulse, and her current icon is a 2-D videogame image of two cowboys riding a snake] Hello, I was wondering if you could give me some info on this game map I was just playing, it says you helped design it.
    Annie: Sure, what did you want to know?
    Nix: Well, I--hey, you're not showing an avatar. You having some technical difficulty?
    Annie: No, I'm just logged out of the system right now.
    Nix: Logged out of the--but where are y--well, whatever, can you tell me [specific info about the map]?
    Annie: No problem, I've got that info right here.
    Nix: OK, great, stay there, I'll be right over to get it.
    Annie: Come here? To my location? But how will you get here?
    Nix: [Distracted, boggled at the question] Huh? I'll just take the snake. . .listen, never mind, just go ahead and mail it to me.

    Probably the best "two characters are completely misunderstanding each other's frame of reference and the players both know why" conversation I've ever seen in an RPG.

    \n Haha... see, you've got the Ideal, which is the virtual world. Run by the church. It's holy. And then there's the physical world, which is unholy and filthy. That includes any kind of real physical contact.

    Some people get kicked out of the Ideal--i.e., they get Excommunicated. If you've lived all your life in a virtual world where you can be anyone and anything, dealing with the limitations of the physical world can drive you mad. Who are you? What's right and wrong? Which emotions are real? What are you supposed to do?

    So there are some crazies out there. Cannibals that live in the sewers. They couldn't handle the transition.

    One of our protagonists, Joel's to be precise, recently left the Ideal and is being chased by the authorities now. He's naked, starving, and afraid. And he can't change it instantaneously! That's horrifying. So he comes across these strange-looking teens that are roasting something over a campfire, and they invite him to eat. And now we've got a conflict: their intent is to make him eat human flesh. His intent is to find hope. They both win: our protagonist realizes too late what he's eating, and in return the teens tell him about Lazarus, another protagonist who's gathering outcasts to lead them to the promised land.

    See, it all makes sense

    \n Fair enough, Joel

    To add to the circumstances: if you've read the previous account, you know that Anastasius was off his drugs because of his promise to the street urchin. He's been feeling guilty all along that he beat that kid up. He found out in the meantime, because of his previous scene, that many of the outcasts were excommunicated through no fault of their own, and that the Church is expelling them for some other reason. And so it really did all lead up to this, because if he had been on drugs and less unsure about the Church, he probably would have followed orders.

    In system terms, the conflict was this:

    Anastasius wanted to save the kids. The outcasts (my Antagonist group) wanted me to join their ranks.

    We figured out that, if both fail, I'm going to blame Lazarus for the whole deal and become a total rogue agent against everyone. But as the dice fall, they succeed in their intent and I fail. That's why we end up busting out together (and commandeer the troop transport gunship that had played a pivotal role in capturing Lazarus just before).

    Now, Annie's minutiae die could have changed that... but after some discussion, she goes, "I'm sorry, but it just makes for a better tragic story if they die." And so it was.

It seems to me that we ended up with a lot of conflicts where one outcome was preferred, either unanimously or by a great majority, around the table. Like the killing of kids in Christian's scene. So there wasn't really a lot of competition over outcomes--the Antags of course had to direct their dice maximally against their opponents, but the protags don't need to do that, and I even remember using my D4 in favor of Annie's Antag goal of "Make Dox eat human flesh" because I just couldn't imagine the scene without it.

    Now, I'm perfectly happy with the conflict outcomes and resulting story, but a bit disappointed that there wasn't more opportunity to engage the mechanics more, e.g. by risking links to reroll a failure. Rather, we proceeded with a fair degree of consensus on when it was best for Protags to succeed or fail, so that A) when success was preferred, there wasn't any need to risk Links what with everyone pulling for/halfheartedly opposing you, and when failure was preferred, there was no desire to risk Links and ruin a perfectly good tragedy. Which is all the more frustrating when I realize that I did have a great opportunity in the first session to risk a link to contest an outcome (Dox Proteum's ejection from the virtual; he was still trying to change the system from within), but was too green in the system to remember I could do that. So I arrived the second time all charged and ready to risk left and right, and no opportunity emerged. :P

    I dunno how big an issue this is; after all, I certainly had loads of fun with the game. It just detracted from the emotional charge of conflicts somewhat. I've also noticed this in the one game of PTA I've played, which I know Shock: owes a lot to, mechanically. Perhaps this sort of mechanical design leads to such things? Particularly the level of resolution granularity? Like, in a more action-to-action scene, your opposition can push, push push at every step of the way and still allow you the opportunity to overcome the adversity and achieve your desired outcome. It's easier as antagonist to keep fighting even if you prefer the protagonist to win, since you can really hamper them at one round (or what have you) without destroying their ability to rebound and triumph. But when it's all down to one roll for the scene, there's not much leeway if you want to lose. You either do, or you don't, right NOW.

    Does that make sense? Another factor I noticed was that a lot of our resolutions had a "Yes, but. . ." quality. "You discover the hope of an Exodus. . .but you're tricked into eating people." "You escape from the Church and ally with the resistance. . .but a bunch a kids get gunned down." And so forth. Which at least tells you where our group preference lies, but I think the above musing steps behind that and points to the systemic factors that caused that preference to be enacted with less than fierce competition.

    I dunno, Joshua or anyone else, what do you think?
