    \n Hey folks: nobody has said that just playing a different game without talking will change the players' habits.

    "Just talking" is not always adequate to changing habits. Even if you get everybody on board and enthusiastic for "being more descriptive of stuff," that does not mean that the players will then leap into describing everything in appropriate levels of detail or making those details interesting to the other players. In fact, I would suggest that most of the time the "big talk" is most effectively followed by reliable exercises that guide the players in practicing the new skill or habit. Talking is going to get everybody on the same page of "this is what we're going to do." All the talking in the world will not get everybody on the same page of "this is how we're going to do it." For that, you need to actually do something. This thread is for suggesting appropriate things to do.

    \n I totally agree with JoshBR that the way to address these (perceived) problems is to tackle them head-on by talking about them, and with JD that simply switching games won't solve them. It seems to me the best approach is a combination of talking and demonstrating, but I think we need to be careful about "curing" the unwilling.

    It would be pure agony for my wife, for example, if I decided that her method of playing RPGs was "sick" and needed me to change it "for her own good." I'm not questioning the discussion of techniques, but rather the way in which we judgmentally declare certain things to be "problems" which need "curing" in a group which seems to have settled into its own style of play. I've heard of a bunch of groups get turned off to story games and the like just because someone pushed a little too forcefully the idea that Something Is The Disease, And I'm The Cure.

    \n Our group does this all the time. We see a peice of our game that could be better, and we find a game to help us work on it. Here are some games that I think help work on a specific aspect of play:

    Contributing to the other player's scenes:
    Contenders
    Everyone plays NPCs for the other player's scenes.

    Trying to pack too many stakes into one action:
    Spirit of the Century
    This game rewards breaking things down a bit. This action I maneuver to place an aspect so that next action I fan tap that aspect to do what I want.

    Making characters that give the awesome to one another:
    DitV, because everyone gets entangled in each others traits
    TSOY, because it rewards character who re-inforce each others keys

    Thinking about the big campaign picture
    Burning Sands: Jyhad or Burning Empires
    Because what's at stake in the big picture is part of the rules

    \n Mark, "Sneaking Up" in my essay, is only changing a few select rules in an attempt to cause a change in play-style.
    \n
    \nI'm not against talking about these things in general terms, but I am, in fact, against implying to the other players that how they're playing currently is "wrong." It wasn't I who used the term cures.
    \n
    \nIt's like we've been going out and eating hamburgers. And people are suggesting that, in order to get people to try italian, they're suggesting ketchup on the hamburgers (as a prelude to red sauce). I'm suggesting that it's OK to suggest going to the Italian restaraunt to try it out. Nobody is bad or wrong here, we're just trying something else that I think and hope we might all like. Since I like Italian, and don't know anyone who doesn't like Italian.
    \n
    \nNow, if I come at folks and say, "You know the reason we eat hamburgers is because we've all been enculturated by the hetrogenous dictatorship of American culture" that might just put some folks off. If all the talk amounts to is, "I like Italian, wanna give it a try?" then I'm all for it. But the only thing that's going to really discover whether or not they like the Italian food is if they try the Italian food.
    \n
    \nI learned this from my mother who always said, "How do you know you won't like it, if you haven't even tried it?"
