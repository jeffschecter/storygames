\n I had this idea, being inspired by playing Inuma and In A Wicked Age and some recent threads around here.

OK, you've got blank index cards, one per player. Each has four edges. Each edge corresponds to a category of thing - character, location, motivation, or situation. Everybody writes down one of the four, whatever they are interested in, in the appropriate edge of their card. Then rotate! Fill in an edge of the next card. Rotate until all the edges are filled in.

Now there's some system for assigning order, but it resolves and somebody slaps down an index card they like - any card. The person on their left adds a card, but here's the deal - it has to match up situation-location or character-motivation. Then the next person adds another card to finish the set, and you've got one of each, like this:


So in this example, we've got a case of mistaken identity, the cafeteria, nurse Bontus, and revenge! Sounds good to me.

Possible ideas:
Each time an element is used, you give it a hash mark. When every element has a hash mark, maybe the session is over.
Hash marks add mojo somehow when elements are reincorporated.
"Character" edge is for the person you want to play, and the player of the character built into the scene frames it.

Index cards are cool because they are visual, have space for notes, and are easily portable from session to session. Being asymmetrical means that matching elements up is easy, but you still have lots of interesting choices. Going second lets you define the nature of the scene very well. In creation, you can focus on what's important to you - situation, characters, whatever.

I'd value your thoughts.

    \n Pattern game:

    A collaborative warm-up or foundational exercise in performance. All the players arrange themselves in a semicircle facing the audience. They solicit a word, and then begin free associating. Each word is explicitly influenced only by the previous word, so no general theme necessarily emerges, although an emotional tone often does. The goal is to return, after an elliptical excursion, to the original word. So, as a simple example for the word "clay":

    Clay ... pottery ... wine ... drunk ... homeless ... cigar ... relaxation ... tennis ... clay!

    As in scene work, one of the goals is to trust your partners - even if you don't understand the jump from on word to another, you must believe it is the best possible jump and work from there. If you didn't know that tennis is sometimes played on clay courts, that leap might seem incongruous, for example. In practice, it is common for players to make a word arc that returns to the original phrase and departs in a different direction three times. This free association leads to an wide array of thoughts, feelings, and images that will be used as inspiration for scenes.

    \n Here's me re-stating the basic idea:

    HOW TO START YOUR SESSION

    You and your friends need to put together the intriguing bits that will come together in various ways to form scenes. This involves writing out some information on index cards. Give everybody one - 3x5\xe2\u20ac\x9d blank cards are perfect.

    Write down a character\xe2\u20ac\u2122s name along left (short) edge of an index card. A name can also be a description or title, like \xe2\u20ac\u0153the homeless guy\xe2\u20ac\x9d or \xe2\u20ac\u0153the head resident\xe2\u20ac\x9d. You can add a personal name later if it becomes necessary. Pass the card to the left.

    Write down something the character wants along the right (short) edge of the index card you\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve been handed. A character can want an object (\xe2\u20ac\u0153Dr. Connoly\xe2\u20ac\u2122s secret file\xe2\u20ac\x9d), an abstract concept (Revenge!), or an emotion (Joy). If you pick another character as your character\xe2\u20ac\u2122s want, that character will be treated like an object in scenes where they are involved as a want \xe2\u20ac\u201c it will be about who controls or influences them rather than what their own goals and motivations are. Pass the card to the left.

    Write down, along the top (long) edge of the index card you\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve been handed, an interesting location where the character would be likely to be found. These should be locations in the hospital or places where hospital staff would be likely to hang out. They can be large (\xe2\u20ac\u0153The children\xe2\u20ac\u2122s wing\xe2\u20ac\x9d or \xe2\u20ac\u0153The Wagon Wheel Saloon\xe2\u20ac\x9d) or small (\xe2\u20ac\u0153A forgotten storage locker\xe2\u20ac\x9d). Pass the card to the left.

    Finally, you\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve been handed a fourth index card with a character, a location, and something the character wants. The final edge, the bottom, is reserved for a situation the character will need to confront. Situation should be something engaging, not too specific, and end in an exclamation point. Think melodrama, and call on the three elements already in place to inspire you. Good examples might be \xe2\u20ac\u0153Mistaken identity!\xe2\u20ac\x9d, \xe2\u20ac\u0153She must never find out!\xe2\u20ac\x9d or \xe2\u20ac\u0153Vicious dog on the loose!\xe2\u20ac\x9d

    When you have finished this, you will have as many cards as you have players, and each has a character, a location, a motivation, and a situation.

    LOOSEY-GOOSEY METHOD

    Once you are comfortable with the regular method, feel free to try it less scripted \xe2\u20ac\u201c simply add one element to each card rather than adding specific element types in sequence. That way, if you really want Dr. Mulligan to show up, you can add her as a character (or a want!) to multiple cards. You might like this better, or you might not. Either way is fine.

    HOW TO MAKE A SCENE

    [How to determine who initiates a scene is TBD]

    If you are the person initiating a scene, look over all the cards and pick one that contains stuff you like. Put this card in the middle of the table.

    If you are sitting to the immediate left of the person initiating a scene, you get to add the second card. You can take any remaining card and match it up to the one already in play. You do this by placing it above, below, or to one side of the first card, matching either a situation to a location or a character to a want. You can\xe2\u20ac\u2122t turn cards sideways to match a character to a location or a situation to a want.

    If you are sitting immediately to the left of the person who just laid down a second card, you get to finish the scene elements. If there is already a character/want match, you get to match situation and location anywhere you want, on either of the two cards already in play. If there is already a situation/location match, you get to do the opposite \xe2\u20ac\u201c decide on a character/want match.

    Once you have three cards in an \xe2\u20ac\u0153L\xe2\u20ac\x9d shape, you have the four elements that make up the scene. Make a note of these by putting a hash next to them on each card.

    The character with a want attached is central. The scene should revolve around them. The character opposite the scene\xe2\u20ac\u2122s want is peripheral but should be in the scene. The player of the third character (the one who isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t central and isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t on the card with the scene\xe2\u20ac\u2122s want) gets to frame the scene. Go play!

    EXAMPLE

    Me and three friends get together. We whip up some cards and end up with:

    Card one: Dr. Perriera, the hospital cafeteria, love, and way out of her depth!

    Card two: Dr. Lattimore, the emergency room, Alice Perriera (as a want) and he\xe2\u20ac\u2122s bleeding out!

    Card three: Powell (a medical student), Wagon Wheel Saloon, the exam key, and an old enemy comes calling!

    Card four: Mike Fisher, RN, Exam room one, compassion, and one chaste kiss!

    Mike Fisher is my guy, so I put down the card with him on it.

    Steve looks at the possibilities and adds Powell\xe2\u20ac\u2122s card, attaching \xe2\u20ac\u0153The exam key\xe2\u20ac\x9d to Fisher. He authored Powell and wants to push his story.

    Mike has to connect situation to location. He\xe2\u20ac\u2122s got a bunch of choices, but really likes \xe2\u20ac\u0153an old enemy comes calling\xe2\u20ac\x9d. In the cafeteria or in the emergency room? Emergency room! That element is on a Card with Joel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s character, Dr. Lattimore.



    The scene revolves around Mike Fisher. Powell, played by Steve, will be in it for sure. And the scene is framed by Joel, who plays mean old Dr. Lattimore. ER Nurse Mike Fisher is going to deal with the key to an exam (presumably one of Powell\xe2\u20ac\u2122s), and an old enemy is going to show up, probably in the E.R. Cool! I give each of the four elements a hash mark, and we\xe2\u20ac\u2122re off to the races.

    \n Thanks guys, this idea is on fire in me.

    Ijust tried it out with seven cards and here's how it works:

    First of all, the thing opposite the character's name? It's the thing they cannot have, ever. Powell is a medical student? His is sleep. Lattimore's hot for Perriera? His is "Perriera's underpants". Perriera wants Lattimore's job? Hers is "Lattimore's job". And so on.

    You've got three cards that define a scene:

    A is who the scene focuses on, and it is the first card laid down (Fisher in the example).
    B is A's interestfor the scene (Powell's card in the example)
    C is situation and location (Lattimore's card in the example)

    The guys on cards A and B haveto be in the scene. Anybody else can be in the scene, but A is the star and B must be, because the scene is about the thing he wants but cannot have.

    The next scene will focus on the guy on either B or C, which becomes the new A. It's like a camera drifting from actor to actor in an uninterrupted take.

    EDIT: One more thing - the "thing you cannot have, ever" ends up working really well as a point of interest, sometimes in weird ways. How do you reconcile Mean old Dr. Lattimore and "sleep"? Well, since Powell must be in the scene, there's the obvious rousting of the student, but other more subtle possibilities present themselves.

    \n Here's my thinking on how I'm going to integrate this idea into Medical Hospital:
    * * *
    Coins are currency. More matches, either heads or tails, is better. This is the universal rule.

    Tails indicate negative outcomes, evil, and disaster. Heads indicate positive outcomes, goodness, and triumph. This factors into narration color, mostly. You can win a conflict using tails, but the outcome must be negative overall. You can lose a conflict using heads, but the outcome must be positive overall.

    A card is \xe2\u20ac\u0153owned\xe2\u20ac\x9d by the player of the character written on the card.

    1. Set up the three cards as discussed. Players bid coins for positioning; high bidder chooses who will lay cards and in what order, or can elect to choose a tactile surgical procedure instead. (If a procedure is chosen, stop reading this checklist)

    2. Place one coin on each card, plus one for each hash mark. Hash marks indicate card elements were used in previous scenes.

    3. The owner of the Character card plays their protagonist in the spotlight for the scene. This player takes all the coins on the Character card.

    4. The owner of the Location/Event card \xe2\u20ac\u201c the one that has neither Character or Issue \xe2\u20ac\u201c frames a scene that includes Location and a secondary character. This player doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t get any coins! The Location/Event card coins go into a general pool.

    5. The owner of the Issue card is responsible for explicitly tying the Event and Issue into the scene. This player takes all the coins on the Issue card.

    6. You now have a scene framed. There is a protagonist, a secondary character (who may be an antagonist), a Location and an Event all pointed at an Issue. Play the scene out.

    7. Is there a conflict? Resolve it. The player of the protagonist puts forward coins from their own pile, up to the number originally on the Character card. Other players can contribute coins for adversity from their own piles, or from the general pool. Both sides flip their coins and look for matches. The side with the most matches wins and narrates the outcome. Ties go to the protagonist. Used coins are discarded.

    8. Is there a chance for transformation? One player may spend and flip coins to attempt to change a card element. Protagonist, Issue, and finally Event/Location player may elect to do this, in that order.

    Three matches = re-write the Location on any card in the scene.
    Four matches = re-write the Event on any card in the scene.
    Five matches = re-write the Issue on any card in the scene.
    Six or more matches = re-write the Character on any card in the scene.

    Depending on how the coins fall, you may have two choices.

    9. Add hash marks to the four elements used in the scene. The cards used will gradually become more potent as they are reincorporated. Repeat!

    * * *
    This is rough, so I'd welcome comments.
