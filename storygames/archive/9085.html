\n (Indeed, I keep returning to the subject of running D&Dish campaigns while allowing for the chance of not-every-player showing up every-single-time without breaking the fiction.)

My thought is: rather than individual, deep, treelike dungeons that can last for multiple sessions, there is a larger landscape of thin dungeons that can be entered. I'm referring to these as "ruins"; the adventuring party is covering the terrain of a Fallen Civilization, and has to track its resources along the way. They'll set up camp outside the Darkfell Manor, and the adventurers who are ready will go in, and spend a session delving that "dungeon". Maybe at a different time they'll camp outside the Shadowfell DarkWell, where they must rappel down several hundred feet to enter the dungeon, and this is known to be a committed two-session dungeon, but not much more than that.

Any push to make things more episodic will certainly weaken the strategic / resource-management focus somewhat. I feel like this could still be dungeon-y, though, and still make some fictional sense.

Here's what the fictional explanation could look like:

One thousand years ago, the the Empire of Grey Leaves fell to its own inevitable corruption. Long ago, the powerful of the republic built their estates, prisons, and fortresses on top of sources of powerful mana flows, often using their magic to carve deep caves into the rock below. However, the overreliance on Mana corrupted the hearts of the leaders, and they fell into wicked heresy and vise.

It is now a barbaric age, with few points of light amidst the great ruins. In walled cities, the people fence themselves off from the corrupted magic of the old world. However, some adventurers must enter the ruins - to find some ancient treasure, to defeat encroaching monstrosities, and to eventually reclaim the territory of the fallen empire.

    \n Here's an alternative that might also prove interesting. You mention that one goal is "to eventually reclaim the territory of the fallen empire". What if, after each week's episode, the areas of dungeon that have been cleared out and declared safe get resettled? In effect, a steadily growing town would follow the heroes as they plunge deeper as wandering merchants set up their tents and eventually take possession of the dungeon's various rooms. Not only do you have the advantage of episodic play, but the world will genuinely shape itself around the players' actions.

    \n There was a supplement for EarthDawn (I am blanking on the name right now) that did something like this. A series of ruins that brought adventurers from all over.     \n You could also do this, if you were so inclined:

    Say you've got group ABCDE and you're having a lot of fun in a dungeon. Next time you meet, though, it's CDFGH and you all decide that there's just no way you can continue in the dungeon you're in and maintain the same feel or get rid of the awful feeling of non-continuity.

    So, one of the players, C or D, says something like this:

    "So, we're eating our rations while people are trying to relax for a moment. I point to the sword we just found and I begin to tell them the story of how I and some other fools were in Lochmere Keep ..." or "So, we're eating our rations while people are trying to relax for a moment. I shudder at the memory of our downed fighter and I begin to tell them the story of these berks who went off to slay the Trollich of Iron Pass ..."

    Then, as the gaming groups keep on getting moved along, you keep on having this continuous flow of things. It'll be fun trying to remember all of the connections, you know? And you could reuse characters, even with different players.

    \nI LOVE this. Love it. I've been experimenting with flashbacks in RPGs recently, and one of the things I've had rattling around in my head (which I'll actually start a new thread about) is the idea of the "Canterbury Tales" game, wherein the characters are all on an adventure together, and then they all take turns telling stories/having flashbacks to their own personal story that somehow influences the events of the current story.

    I also like the idea of the "modular dungeon", wherein you're not building towards any -specific- endpoint, so having players not make it to a session is not a big deal, because the core of the group can assemble a different "team of specialists" for the next bit of delve.

    \n I had a chaotic demi-god named Count Gravity in my campaign. He was a decent but inscrutable immortal fellow who liked to teleport people around for his own obscure reasons. If a player couldn't make it to a session and/or another player showed up unexpectedly -- why, their respective characters would just pop in to/out of existence in the near vicincy of the party.

    Game-y, yes, but it sure made my life a lot easier.

    \n In a long-running D&D campaign I played in, if we didn't make the quorum, the GM would run us through scenarios in which the "Dream Fish" would gather us together, lucidly dreaming or in a parallel universe or something like that, for surreal action one-shots. I remember well the Tower of Undead and Bureaucracy, in which our party's two priests \u2014 one of a goddess whose followers (among other things) went around squashing the undead, and the other (my guy) of a god who expected his priests to go out and just directly help people \u2014 were brought improbably face-to-face with their nemeses, all at once. Many a skeleton filing clerk's skull was crushed by my Staff of Wands that night!
