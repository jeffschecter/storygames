\n The player's kit for The Wildlings is ready for your perusal and feedback.



This game was inspired by discussion about "D&D For Kids" after WotC posted their Heroes of Hesiod introductory D&D module. It's also heavily influenced by Penny Arcade's "Lookouts"</> comics.

The game is meant to be run by an experienced gamer for players who are new to RPGs (kids or otherwise). The GM's kit isn't finished yet, but will contain the full rules, the adventure module, and techniques for running the game.

Take a look and let me know what you think. There's enough here to play it if you're so inclined, too.

Then it simply becomes an extension to the narrative options of color described when taking action: the pet can be seen as a part of the character, giving him some more possibilities ("I send my sparrow high to spy on the path" is not functionally much different from saying "I scout ahead and gather reconnaissance info for the path").

    \n I think somewhere in the 12-14 range is good for the age. Thirteen seems significant since it's the first "teen" year, fourteen is good because you're not a "new" teenager anymore. Older than fourteen strays into "grown-up" territory for younger kids, I believe, so fourteen's a good age for players who are, say, 7-9: they can imagine being that age, it's relatable, rather than being in the distant realm of adulthood. (I am not a childhood development specialist, obviously, so grains of salt are recommended.)

    Adverbs: Not sure about "calmly"; given the reasons to roll the dice, when do you think a player might choose that outcome? "Bravely" seems like a better adverb; bravely/boldly, kindly, cunningly/cleverly are a few possibilities.

    Pets: Not having any idea what you already have in mind for Marks, I'd think that one possible benefit of earning a Mark would be getting some kind of pet. I think you're right that if you put pet under Weapons, you'll end up with a bunch of pets and no weapons.

    \n Since I wanted to wait until we had multiple players to use the 'Into the Ruins' hook, and because I wanted to see if this was even something Elias would be interested in, we only played a short little episode. It was the middle of winter, and the village was going hungry. The adults had left for several hunting expeditions, only to return empty-handed. In the dead of night, Dov decides to sneak away from the village and make his way to the forest, in hopes of bringing back something to share with his people. The subsequent adventure mostly involved a lot of tracking animals through the snow (Wolf rolls), battling a hungry bear (again, a Wolf roll - Elias's Warrior Die), lifting a boulder in order to frighten a cave troll (a Boar roll), knocking the troll down with the boulder (a Wolf roll), wrestling with the troll after it knocks Dov to the ground (a Boar roll), and finally slaying it with his sword (a Wolf roll). Dov returns to the village with troll meat for everyone, where he's welcomed with cheers by the villagers and relieved embraces by his parents.

    I wrote down the name, weapon, and armor Elias chose on the character sheet, but those were all things he remembered easily, and were more for my benefit. He hasn't drawn anything yet, but I imagine we might fold that in on later plays.       \n Once a month, a local gaming group named GASP puts on a Games Day in Pittsburgh. These events have collectible card games, miniatures gaming, board gaming, and some RPG's. In the RPG's, the RPGA takes up maybe half of the slots, and the rest usually goes to other ways to play D&D. In the minority of RPG's that don't use one edition of D&D or another, you have games like Barbarians of Lemuria, Labyrinth Lord and Shadowrun. So, this is the environment.

      I ran the Wildlings in the first slot, starting at 11 AM. I had two players that I knew would come: my brother, who shares many of my interests, with less fanaticism; and a guy from our D&D group who is usually the most crunchy, tactical guy at the table. A fellow wandered past and asked about the game, so I gave the pitch. He wasn't familiar with the Lookouts or How to Train Your Dragon, but upon hearing that it was kid-friendly, he sent his two sons, aged between 10 and 14.

      I didn't have the Guide booklet, so I might have done things improperly. I tend to lean on whatever adverb they didn't pick as the direction I'd go to move the story forward. I might have relied too heavily on "carefully"; I think I made one of the kids paranoid about that. He made sure to do everything carefully after a while.

      I didn't know what to do with the woodsman and the fire bats at first, so I let them just try to track the monster. My first time using the "carefully" they neglected came in then, as I had the monster find them and attack them. They fought it off, but lost time and had to make camp. Around the fire, they talked about how they knew Rylka. Most of them had some kind of blood tie, but my brother played a big, Boar-ish character (named Bor), who harbored a secret crush on her.

      In the morning, I had them fall into a booby-trap set by a hunter. This alerted them to the existence of the woodsman, whom they then sought out, and found his cabin on fire, and the fire crows attacking. They rescued the woodsman and fought off the fire crows. The woodsman revealed himself as the brother of Yuri Red Hand, and thus the uncle of Rylka and the two characters played by the two kids at the table. Apparently, he considered it negligent for the parents to go raiding, abandoning their children at home. He got into an argument with Yuri about this, and so, he had to leave the people and go live in the Old Forest. He worried about his niece, though, and helped the wildlings in their quest.

      When they descended into the ruins, they started to get cut off from one another, first two of the PC's in one group, and the two others with the woodsman. Then, the woodsman got cut off from the other two PC's. One group found a secret passage, and found the monster's lair, where they heard Rylka singing it a lullaby. As the monster slept, Rylka explained how sad she became in the village with her parents across the sea. Every night, she would cry. The monster, yearning for the sound of voices speaking, would creep down towards the village each night just to listen to people talk. He heard Rylka crying when no one else did, and tried to comfort her. Finally, he could not take her pain anymore. He stole her away one night, and, knowing that a human child would need human food, stole the food as well.

      Just at that moment, Bor kicked in the door and saw the sleeping monster. The monster sprang up to protect Rylka, Bor charged to save Rylka and impress her by slaying the monster, and a moment later, the PC's had slain the beast. Rylka fell on his corpse, crying, and calling the wildlings monsters.

      They got her out of the woods, and then two of them went back in to find the woodsman. They found deeper tunnels in the ruins, which the monster had blocked off and warned Rylka not to go. They eventually led down into a dragon's lair, where he slept on a huge hoard of gold. An enormous battle ensued, as first the other two PC's, and finally the woodsman, all found their way. In a great battle, they slew the dragon. Rylka threw a rope ladder down for them, and one by one they climbed up. As Bor climbed up, though, she threw the rope ladder down, and ran off. The PC's helped get Bor back up, and then chased Rylka, who had run off into the woods. Having pressed through the night, they'd now made it to morning.

      They tracked Rylka through the woods, until they found her with a stick she'd sharpened to a good point. Bor moved forward to grab her, so she stabbed him in the shoulder. Seeing the harm she'd done, Rylka began sobbing again, and begging forgiveness. The wildlings offered to vouch for the woodsman's honor if he wanted to return to the people, but he declined, saying he felt content with his life in the woods. They took Rylka home, where the people acclaimed the wildlings as heroes. Rylka never told anyone that the monster had not abducted her at all, and no one ever knew of the tragedy that unfolded in the ruins that night. Bor gave up on his hope of winning Rylka's heart, and contented himself with the treasure they'd taken from the dragon, and the glory he won from the people.

      Since, again, I didn't have the Guide's book, I kind of made up marks. When Bor grabbed an unconscious wildling from the fires in the woodsman's burning cabin, and the hot metal rings of the chain mail burned into his hand, I gave him the Mark of the Boar's Rings, which gave him one more adverb whenever he got at least one adverb from a Boar roll. Another character caught fire three separate times, so I gave him the Mark of the Burning Snake, which gave him +2 to Snake rolls.

      Fun was had all around. The crunchy player said he had fun, but he probably wouldn't want to play it as a regular campaign. The two kids apparently liked it, because they came back for the next slot, when I ran a playtest of my own game, the Fifth World.

      And I imagine a few hints on how to present that information to the players ("The construction of the tree-house is thin, made of thin branches, probably only used by younger kids...") and some hints on how to adjudicate adverb choice ("'forcefully' breaks the branches holding the chest in the false bottom of the treehouse...").       \n I'm trying to come up with a number of hooks for less fighty missions for Wildlings. I want to balance some of the more violent battling with semi-educational/moralistic scenarios for my own children. Here are some that I've thought of based on a list of Boy Scout merit badges. I think of the problems as a series of challenges for the Wildlings culminating in their earning some rite of passage (either a mark or badge or just pride that they helped out). Can anyone think of some others I can use?

      Badge: Hunter/Provider
      Problem: Feed your tribe during a harsh winter (or some crop disaster). What interesting ways do players think of to provide enough food? Hunt? Gather? Trade with other towns?

      Badge: Camper
      Problem: The Wildlings are sent to send a message or take something to another village, but are stuck in the woods overnight. How do they set up camp and feed themselves?

      Badge: Builder
      Problem: A flash flood washed out the only bridge to the village. The village relies on cargo brought via carts over the bridge. How can they rebuild the bridge in time for the next caravan in a week.

      Badge: Climber
      Problem: As training, Wildlings have to go through a ropes course built by their fathers. It is frought with peril.

      Badge: Caver
      Problem: A miner is stuck after a cave-in. The Wildlings must go navigate the caves, and bring the miner out (or at least bring help to the miner).

      Badge: Boater
      Problem: The village ships barrels of their famous mead downriver to a larger city on the river. The boatkeep is ill and isn't showing signs of recovery. The wildlings must drive the boat downstream and deliver the mead.

      Badge: Merchant
      Problem: See "Boater" badge above. When they reach the larger city, the mead buyers don't want to deal with the kids and when convinced want to buy at a much cheaper price than expected. The Wildlings must bargain with the buyers to sell the mead for a decent price.

      Badge: Runner
      Problem: A fire has gone out of control in the woods and while it does not threaten their own village across the river, it could reach another village. The fire is spreading fast and the Wildlings must run all out to get to the next village and warn them. It will be exhausting and the wildlings must prove their endurance.

      Badge: Peacemaker
      Problem: The elders in your village and another have long had a feud based on a misunderstanding many years ago. When the Wildlings are tasked with escorting a stoneworker to the other village, can they ease the tensions between the two sides?

      Badge: Healer
      Problem: While playing deep in the wood, they hear the faint sound of someone crying for help. A young woman was attacked by wolves in the wood and is bleeding out quickly. The Wildlings must stop the bleeding, patch up the woman and get help (or bring her to help).

      Badge: Starwatcher
      Problem: An elder teaches them about stars and following the Warrior Constellation to navigate in the dark. Days later, the Wildlings are lost at night and must find their way using the technique that the elder taught them.

      Badge: Husbandry
      Problem: Each child has to take care of a troublesome pet for a week. The pets are wild, like to escape and cause other problems. At the end of the period, perhaps they Wildlings are given a choice to keep the pet (now somewhat trained).

      Badge: Diver
      Problem: Jakob the jeweler dropped a small chest of valuable gems in the river. The Wildlings can dive deep into the river and retrieve the gems for the Jeweler.

      Other thoughts: Weatherwatcher, Tracking, Water Conservation (during drought), Geology (identify precious rocks), Botany (identify important herbs and plants)

For a moment, Savra was tempted to leave him there - after all, she had vowed to get her revenge - but after some deliberation, she and Dov used successful Boar rolls to pull Felnir from the icy water. At this point, the kids decided to return to the village for the night. When they awoke in the morning, however, they discovered that the troll meat Dov had brought back back on a previous adventure, meat meant to feed the village for weeks, had been stolen.

      The three Wildlings rolled their Wolf dice and followed a set of tracks from the village to the forest, tracks made by clawed feet with a tail dragging between them. The tracks ended at the base of the tree, and Maria rolled her Owl die to see what was up in the tree. She chose Thoroughly as one of her outcomes, and spotted a lizardman hiding in the branches. The lizardman had seen them, but didn't yet realize that they had seen him. Conferring quickly, the three Wildlings decided to make a run for it - Dov successfully rolled his Stag die, but Savra and Felnir roll a 3 and a 1, and the lizardman leaps down from the tree, landing right between them. Felnir attacks with his daggers (Maria chose Snake as his Warrior Die), but rolls another 3, and when he lunges, the lizardman twists around and seizes him from behind. Anyway, I'm looking forward to playing again soon!

      \nThe new choices have not been playtested, at least by me or the people I play with. They were words I jotted down the last couple of times playing the original Wildlings, when people were looking over the existing list, and saying "well, the closest to what I want to do is this word...but not quite."
      It's all about context, I think. For example, I could "carefully" climb a tree, but not "precisely" climb a tree. I could shoot an arrow at a monster's eye "precisely," possibly even "carefully," but not "thoroughly." I could probably search a room for secrets either "carefully" (trying not to disturb anything or set off traps), "precisely" (finding exactly what I set out to find), or "thoroughly" (trying to find every possible secret in the room).

      But of course, you don't have to use the new words if you don't want to. Again, they were things that sort of popped up in-play, and this was even narrowed down from my original list.

      \nThanks for these; printing some out tomorrow. Had an idea for how the GM can mess with the players:

      While the players roll for feats, the GM rolls to see what twists will challenge the player's story. The GM rolls a die, depending on difficulty, with 4-6 creating 1 twist, 7-9 creating 2 twists and 10 or higher creating 3 twists. Each twist grows in how specific it is, so while 1 twist might be a simple "rock", a 2nd twist could be a "white rabbit" with a 3rd twist being a "wolf tooth necklace". When the player tells of their feats, they must work the twists into their story in a convincing manner or risk creating a miss.

      So basically the idea is to let the quality of the players' stories determine success / failure in addition to the auto-miss when rolling 3 or lower, but giving the GM some power to make them be creative.

      Enemies might have some common twists, such as:
      - Bear Tribe Raiders: spear, anime hide, bear tooth necklace
      - Cavern Drake: rock, scales, poison
      - Foreign Invaders: sword, ale, cloak

This lead to a snake roll by Thordis to sneak into the hunter's hut and 'borrow' his bow and arrows, both quickly and quietly, Isvel to open her heart and woo old gramma Grelka for sweets and treats from the bakery (Stag roll) and Arnedra to fail in her Owl roll to seek a map from the chieftan's roundhouse, unbeknownst to her she took a map of the nearby bear Tribe instead!

      Thus they wandered off into the night into the dark forest following the (wrong) map. Thordis made a three adverb success to track their way back toward the ruins, after Isvel's pet chipmunk, 'Squee' scurried up the tallest tree and espied their true location. Arnedra tried to convince the others that it was at least a 9 day trip and they should be careful about where they camp in the snowy forest each night, the trio managed to traverse the dark forest in a few days, avoiding the miniature goblins and tracking the tree smashed path of the monster to its lair at the ruins. We whipped out "Stones of Habrek's Hill' from Dyson's New Mapbook as the location, and zoomed in the action.

      Poor Arnedra unsuccessfully scouted the ruined stone circle, looking for a secret ring of glowing mushrooms that would herald the entrance to the underground lair. A band of Goblin Guards mobbed her with the intent of capturing her for their 'master'. She stood firm, waving her oaken staff about with true grit and fearless strength to drive them off (failed Boar roll). A list of Dungeon World Principles in your head isn't such a bad accompaniment either

      \nSo, probably one thing I'll need to clarify when I get around to the next revision is that when a Wildling declares their action, the Feat involved is what they want to happen. "I want to hit the troll forcefully" or "I want to scout the camp quickly".

      If the Guide states a threat, it'll be something like "Because of xx, if you don't do this yy, then zz might happen!"

      As for your ravine example, when the Wildlings see the bridge, and decide to cross it, you may say that because of the fog, they would need to roll...perhaps...the Owl Spirit, to keep their senses sharp. (note, the Guide determines which Spirit is appropriate, as per third paragraph of the How to Play section). Perhaps only the Wildling with the greatest Owl Spirit would actually roll, guiding the other. She would then need to state which Feat she wants to use to describe her action, perhaps, "I want to cross the bridge quickly so we can get to the other side before nightfall."

      As for the fog, is it an Enemy? Is it something that will actively try and stop the Wildlings? If so, then yeah, start giving it some Traits and think of why it does what it does and what its place is in your story. Then decide if any of its Traits would actually count as a threat against the Wildlings in that situation.

      If the fog isn't an enemy, but instead some environmental effect, whether mundane or magical, it's just a threat. Think of the possible consequence for wandering into the fog, and what might prevent it from being threatening.

      For example, if it is just plain, mundane fog, but it conceals a couple of broken boards in the bridge, then you could say "Because the fog might conceal broken boards, if you don't cross the bridge carefully, you might fall through to the river below!"

      If it were a magical fog, that perhaps makes people see strange apparitions within its mists, maybe you could say "Because of the fog's strange, spooky, swirling mists, if you don't cross the bridge bravely, you may lose something as you shake with fear!"

      If the Wildling's roll results in two Feats, then yeah, she can choose to describe her action both quickly and whatever was needed for avoiding the threat (either carefully or bravely). Three Feats? Boom! She gets to add something else to the mix!

      If she only got one Feat, though, she'd have to choose: does she try to avoid the Threat? (which may or may not actually result in harm or terrible consequences, it could instead be more of an inconvenience or force them to make a new roll for a new situation...such as trying to grab on to something instead of falling). Or does she focus instead on her original description?

      Also, @Deliverator, as for Marks, my solution at the moment (though I need to do more thinking and testing, but right now too many projects on my hands) is:

      -When Wildings act with Defiance or Nobility (no matter the situation), they can roll twice (taking the lowest result) and earn a mark.       For example, if you were trying to earn a mark by leaping across a narrow ravine to land and hook a rope to a tree on the other side all in one go (certainly defiant of danger), and failed, not only would you risk maybe a sprained ankle or falling and getting hurt, but maybe a fellow Wildling gets their foot tangled and fall down too!
