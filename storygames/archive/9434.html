\n When I'm GMing Trail of Cthulhu, I can get away with telling players things like this:

Player: I make a chalk mark on the wall.
GM*: OK. After a second, your eyes are drawn back to it. You find you've drawn a strange symbol.

Similarly, I can do this:

[Character is sitting at a table in a pub]

GM: The whisky glass you've been given is roughly cut and sharp at the bottom. It scratches the table.
Player: I take it back to the bar.
GM: Sure, so you look down at the glass. You see your hand is moving, and you're drawing out a strange symbol, scratching it out on the table, bigger and bigger until it occupies the whole table.

And I think I could get away with this:

GM: When you wake up, you're on a train. The countryside is foreign, dark and strange.
Player: I'm where? I'm terrified. I shout for the guard.
GM: But that's the thing. You're not terrified. You're really comfortable here. Deep in your blood it feels like, wherever you are, you belong here.

So, under various circumstances, I can dictate characters' actions and mental states. I can even overrule what the player wants the character to do or feel. But there must be limits to this, right? What are the limits?

Graham

P.S. Of course, I mean "Keeper" here, but you can use similar techniques in other games, so I'll talk about GMs.

    \n I think that what makes it ok is that the players know that when you say dictate these things, it's the crazy talking. The crazy inside their characters, that is. Similarly, I don't think anyone would complain if you described them as having a really warm reaction to someone while they were under a charm spell in D&D. In both cases the character does not have full control of his feelings and reactions for reasons that make sense within the fiction. If there was no in-fiction reason for your control over their characters' inner life, they would be upset.

    One exception to this is mind control - typically when the GM mind controls your character and proceeds to determine your actions, it sucks. But it sucks for a different reason. Your character has been turned into an npc who exists to provide opposition for other players while you have nothing to do. When you're going crazy, the point is still to creep you out, or to amuse you with the experience of insanity, or whatever. So I think one limit is that taking control of a character's inner life has to be for purposes that are at some level geared towards that character's player.

    \n Shouldn't there be game-mechanical follow-up to overriding player control (or, if you like, character ego) of actions and emotions?

    Player: I make a chalk mark on the wall.
    Keeper: OK. After a second, your eyes are drawn back to it. You find you've drawn a strange symbol.

    Also, I think it's the case that the limits vary according to the degree to which "Keeper override" can be redeemed by the end of the game as a kind of information: if I feel comfortable on this train, if I feel like I belong here, that must mean something in the context of the investigation; it's a clue that I can pursue -- perhaps to some soul-shattering anagnorisis, but a clue nonetheless. To the extent that it's not redeemable, it may be less satisfying. So I guess there may be degrees of "getting away with it": it won't break the game instantly, in Trail of Cthulhu.

    \n Another bit: especially in the first two, you as the Keeper are handing the player new information. The symbol is most likely a clue or some other indication of whatever happenings are going on. You've given the player something to chew on and enjoy, and presumably they will shortly thereafter get to act on the new information you've given them.

    As to the third \u2014 I think you're right that it's one more step and you "might" get away with it, and I think the clincher is what happens next. In other words, by doing that, you are making an implicit promise that something interesting is going to happen next, and that player gets to experience it. If, on the other hand, the train just steamed through the night and ended up stopping at the Lincolnshire platform, with nothing interesting in Lincolnshire, I suspect the player would be perturbed. Or to go in-theme about it, if the train just kept going, forever steaming after the dawnlit horizon and never quite catching it, the player would be annoyed that he's trapped on that strange train. In both these cases, you the Keeper have failed to live up to the implied promise of something interesting happening next.

    \nI think the potential feeling of disjunction is what makes these sorts of narration work. Sometimes, as Paul says, it's merely "context for the scene," but in Graham's case the players accept the narration because it allows for a break between objective perception ("you see you've drawn"; "you feel comfortable") and the character/player's logical reactions. Compare: "you decided to drawn a strange symbol" and "you feel comfortable, therefore you don't do anything." As it stands, there is a clear sense convey by the transgression of ordinary boundaries of narration that something strange is going on here. The player could, for instance, get up and try to get off the train while still feeling comfortable--in doing so, it would emphasize the difference between logical action and emotional feeling. So long as there is the opportunity to reflect on the difference between this situation and normality, these sorts of episodes aren't necessarily deprotagonizing, because it draws attention to the situation and gives the player a chance to address it.

    \n I sometimes like to do this by framing the elements as questions:

    Player: I make a chalk mark on the wall.
    GM: OK. But this is weird... after a second, your eyes are drawn back to it, aren't they? Like, in a strange compulsion way. I don't think that's just some random mark on the wall. It's something else...
    Player: Maybe it's a weird symbol like the one I dreamed about!
    GM: Yes!

    GM: When you wake up, you're on a train. The countryside is foreign, dark and strange.
    Player: I'm where? I'm terrified. I shout for the guard.
    GM: But that's the thing. I think maybe you're not terrified. Maybe this feels right to you now? Like this is where you're supposed to be.
    Player: Hmmm. Yeah, maybe so. But on another level, I'm freaking out, you know? Like a scream caught in the back of my throat.
    GM: Excellent.

    The "maybe" and "I think" question style is very low pressure. A bit like the fishing technique, in a way. Other times, I like to be more blunt about it (like Graham's first examples) but this softer questioning mode works well too. Whenever I think of horror gaming, I picture Vincent running KPFS:

    Vincent: "You don't... like... put the gerbil in the microwave... do you?" (mock horrified face)
    Player: "Yes. Yes I do. Mwa ha ha ha."

Maybe my character don't even want to talk about it, maybe he refuses to acknowledged he did it, whatever... By saying "you did this" you close the door to any other interpretation.     I'll use another example, in a Deadlands game one of the character was taken over by his manitou ("the thing that will make you do bad things" if you don't know what I'm talking about).

    GM : "you wake up in a house, you find the corpses of five people, two adults and three kids"
    Player : "Uuh, ok, how many bullets are left in my colt ?"
    GM : "One"

    The player decided that his character didn't feel like he did it and decided he was framed. This would have been harder for him to pull out if I just said : "you've just killed five people and feel bad about it".

    As far as I'm concerned, in a Cthulhu game, yes, I know that my character will become insane but suspecting that you're becoming insane and flatly knowing it are two different things (once again, that's my opinion, YMMV).

    In the Call of Cthulhu scenario "The Truth shall set you free" (from Unseen Masters) if you tell, at any moment, what the character is feeling or what he does you just ruin the whole plot which is "you're going insane and you don't even know it".

    So, under various circumstances, I can dictate characters' actions and mental states. I can even overrule what the player wants the character to do or feel. But there must be limits to this, right? What are the limits?

    The only limit I see is the control you have should leave some room for the player to play with like John Harper did (or in my above example for Deadlands). Ignorance is an important tool in any horror story, giving facts that don't leave room to interpretation may be counterproductive in a horror game. But once again YMMV.

    Posted By: John HarperOther times, I like to be more blunt about it (like Graham's first examples) but this softer questioning mode works well too. Whenever I think of horror gaming, I picture Vincent running KPFS:

    Vincent: "You don't... like...put the gerbil in the microwave... do you?" (mock horrified face)
    Player: "Yes. Yes I do. Mwa ha ha ha."
    \nThis is important. Horror roleplaying requires numerous moments of:

    GM: "The eyepiece of the telescope slowly swings towards you. What do you do?"
    Player thinks, DON'T LOOK IN THE TELESCOPE!
    Player: "I look in the telescope!"

    And some players need help or a (friendly) push to get there. Because the first instinct is self-preservation, and it's precisely that instinct that you have to overcome from time to time to get a satisfying horror experience out of it -- rather than an adventurous monster-stomping romp.
