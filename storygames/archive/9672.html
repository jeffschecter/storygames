    \n Creative Agenda doesn't map to individual people anywhere outside internet identity politics. We can say that somebody enjoys a given CA, but the people for whom there is a singular identification for one and only CA are a very marginal minority, and often a socially awkward one at that. Most people can take a given activity on its own terms when those are explained honestly, and thus enjoy a wide range of CA. Even those people you often meet that refuse to play anything but D&D and actively sabotage play if anybody tries anything new - I wouldn't characterize most of them as "having" a CA, but rather I'd just say that they refuse to blur their rpg cultural politics by accepting that there are different ways to play than their own. Trick them into playing some other CA either within D&D or in a game they don't recognize as a roleplaying game, and it often works just fine.

    This being said, the simplest method I use in recognizing how people approach different CA is to play different games with them, making notes of how they do with them. This usually gives me data on what people don't like, and even then only if the person stands out from the rest of the crew. For example, typical CA information for me comes in the form of having a really successful, clean session in a certain CA with one player who gets totally sidelined by the play because he doesn't understand what we're doing. This might indicate some other communication trouble, but it might also mean that the person outright doesn't understand the CA we demonstrated, and is unable or unwilling to participate in it.

    In other words: insofar as a person can be said to have a CA, you might say that their CA is the one they perform in best.

    Another viewpoint would be to ask the people themselves. Play different CA games with them and ask which they liked best. I get lots of data this way as well. The players don't actually need to understand CA or GNS here, as I was in the game with them and can thus say what the CA of the given game was. If somebody consistently ranks the narrativist games above gamist ones, for example, then that's a pretty clear sign that the player has a preference. Most people have this sort of slight preferences, I've found. They are only semi-permanent and tend to shift through time, as opportunities in different CA come up. For example, D&D 4th edition has caused a global increase in gamist priorities, as people are excited by the new gamist possibilities the game has to offer.

    \n At a particular time is easier, but only in the sense that I can only perceive the CA the group is expressing in their interaction. CA is only perceived in how people interact, so an individual's CA preference can really only be deducted by asking them or observing how they don't fit in with a group. If they're fitting in what the group is doing, then you can just say that they are playing along with the group's CA, but what does that say about "their own" CA?

    When we play and the CA is working, I have a constant feeling that I am communicating in a pertinent and active manner with the other player(s). My part in play is not just a rote participation, but it pushes an envelope actively, and the other players can see that and nod, smile and otherwise react to what I'm doing. In other words: a functional group CA is recognized as coherent communication of the purpose of play. When we're not coherent, I feel like I'm talking to a wall: my cues get missed and the other player gives input I can't work with. And even if I can work with it, I know myself that I'm just twisting their intent around to make it fit my own desires in the game.

    As you can see from above, recognizing the CA when play is on-going is pretty easy if you're one of the players, because you can check what your own internal agenda is and then look at your social contact with the others and judge whether they're getting enjoyment out of the same thing you are. Through the last years I've had increasingly less trouble with recognizing the CA because I've become a more methodical and purposeful roleplayer; I don't usually go into a game without having a clear systemic model for what the game is supposed to do and how. Understanding this model in the abstract makes it trivial to recognize the GNS mode as well.

    If I'm external to the play and just observing it from outside, then I need to empathize with the players and basically try to understand their focus from outside the communication loop, just by observing the signs. This is still possible, especially if you trust that you understand the general thrust of what the players are doing. It's not that difficult to notice whether somebody is having fun and contributing in a relevant way in a 4th edition D&D session, for example.

    (In any case note that I'm not discussing a single moment of play here: I'm observing the processes players engage in during the game, not individual choices, so there is always plenty of history that comes before a given moment and makes the reactions, interaction and initiatives the players take understandable. I don't have to observe an entire reward cycle if I already have a good guess as to what is going on, note; for example, in all CoC play I've seen there have basically been three different CAs, so I just need to observe enough to distinguish between those to have a pretty good guess as to what is going in the game.)

    Graham said:Do, alternatively, tell me how to identify what creative agenda someone is using at a particular time, if that's easier.
    \nAs defined, CA is a large-scale, group-level phenomenon. So, your question is out of scope. You don't have moments where people "are Gamist" or "use Gamism". You have a pattern of everyone at the table encouraging and elevating challenge and risk above other aesthetic elements in the game, and that is Gamist.

    At a particular time, what you have is someone either a.) having a positive, supporting reaction to someone else showing guts and acumen in the face of risk, or b.) showing guts and acumen in the face of risk and seeking positive, supporting reactions, subtly or overtly depending on their social tendencies. This is not Gamist play, but as a trend in an individual player's behavior, it's probably an expressed desire for Gamist play.

    If it helps, using any creative agenda term as a noun is confusing, I think. I prefer to use them as adjectives only as often as I can, most often modifying the words "emphasis", "priority", "play", "support", or "wanker".

    \n A lot in identifying the CA depends on what you're playing because players interact and react to different things in different games.     \n It's a group level thing if you're playing coherently. You're playing coherently if the actions of the other players seem purposeful and fun to you while they also respond with affirmation to your actions, pretty much.

    You identify the GNS mode of your play in a coherent group by analyzing your own experience of the play: what was it that you enjoyed in the game yourself? What were the elements that the whole group clearly appreciated and took to consideration in their interaction? If these were challenge-based, then it's Gamism. If the play was about theme, then it is Narrativism. If it was about letting the experience play out with consensual imagining, then it was Simulationist. Think back on what actually was the core content of play and what it was that your group enjoyed about the experience.

Play analysis isn't the simplest of things anyway, as you need to be able to look at what happens and notice the pertinent things over the inconsequential.

    \n I'd say that it is pretty typical for narrativist play to include both premise-addressing moments and moments of pure elaboration - I call it "heavy texture gaming" when the game world is present with particular power in comparison to the story. This does not imply incherence GNS-wise, it just means that the group is exploring the fiction. Exploration does not come in the way of premise-addressing, because they happen in different parts of the process: typically you'd first explore the fiction to find out interesting nuances, and only then start addressing premise, being informed of those nuances.

    What would make the exploration into real simulationism would be if the premise-addressing were not validated when it's time came. This is really quite easy to see in that a player addressing premise with his choices is making a moral decision, while a player enforcing the dream derives his choices from established genre or story facts. For example, he might decide that because we saw earlier that the character is stingy, he'll be stingy now as well. A premise-addressing player will be more likely to choose against prior indications because that makes for a clearer statement. All situational, of course, but that's often how you distinguish sim vs. nar.

    \n I wouldn't put stock to Steve's method, as regards Ron's GNS theory. GNS modes are not about individual feelings, but rather about the focused attention of the group itself. Each of the items on Steve's list can happen in any agenda mode. For example, caring about outcomes - in a gamist game you care about an outcome because you're invested in it as a challenge, but that doesn't mean you don't care about the outcomes in other modes; you might well care about the outcome because of your sympathy towards the protagonist character (a major issue in Zombie Cinema), for example.

    As for whether GNS agendae can mix - you don't see an agenda mix in a single game session (or at least a single united communication situation; a session could shift gears in the middle, but that'd break the currently on-going reward cycle) simply because the agenda requires coherent play from the players to manifest at all. If you're not playing towards the same agenda, chances are that your agenda will only be instantiated as failed overtures towards getting it off the ground. This doesn't mean that you can't switch agendas as a person or as a group, but I've never witnessed this "40% narrativist" phenomenon myself; I don't in fact understand how it could happen, as the agenda modes are discrete units. It makes as little sense as would an aeroplane that was 40% bicycle.

    Concerning Graham's original question, I do admit that it's not simple to answer this if you want unambiguous and mechanical guidelines. Roleplaying is so very rich and varied a phenomenon that the best I can do is give advice about recognizing the CA in individual games. For example, I could make a guess that if players are planning to blow up monsters in Call of Cthulhu and they prepare accordingly, then it's probably the gamist variety of the game that you have going; blowing up monsters, and especially explicitly and concernedly preparing to do that, doesn't really play a role in the very different sim variant CoC which assumes that the players are going to enjoy playing characters who are basically clueless as to the dangers they're going to encounter. Player activity, the mechanical points of contact and reward cycle stress points are so different in different games that I can't off-hand give any better advice than trying to understand what the players think that they are doing while playing, and seeing if those understandings match into a coherent communication between the players. If it does, then you have a CA in the group, and you can look into the psychological satisfaction that is served by that CA - which in turn tells you which GNS mode that CA represents. It's abstract, but barring a concrete game system to reflect on (and one I know pretty well, to boot), that's pretty much the best I can do.

      \n Here's my take on the standard line (Ron's essays) on how to identify the CA of an instance of play.

      At the end of the instance of play, look at what people said and did and how the group reacted to them. Find those things that the group rewarded with praise, affirmation, and collaboration. If there was a kind of synergy among the group regarding a certain class of things, you probably have a creative agenda.

      Which one? Well, if those things tended to prioritize _performance with risk_ (see Gamism: Step on Up), then you have a Gamist instance of play.       \n I try to avoid "instance of play" as a term, as it's pretty deprecated. "Reward cycle" is better, as it pretty much defines the necessary time-span for observing CA in its name already - to observe CA, you need to observe the structural buildup and release of value a given game provides to its players. In most games this takes over fifteen minutes, say, but it's rarely more than one session in the games I play nowadays. You can read "instance of play" as an earlier, more vague term for the same thing if you want.

      (Not my idea, this term - I picked it up at the Forge a couple of years ago, I think.)

      Couple of simple examples of reward cycles:\n
        \n
      • In 4th edition D&D the basic reward cycle concerns winning a battle: it starts when the combat encounter is defined by the GM, and ends when the tension is released around the end, at the moment when everybody sees what the outcome will be. I'd say that the great majority of how a 4th ed. D&D experience pans out depends on what role this combat subgame plays in the group's toolbox. I would be slightly surprised by a 4th edition group whose CA I couldn't observe from when, why and how they set up and resolve combats.
        • \n
      • In an advocation-based narrativist game, like Dogs in the Vineyard, Shadow of Yesterday or Zombie Cinema, to name a few, a basic reward cycle concerns reveal of information, choice situations caused by the revealed conditions, and the consequences of those choices. This basic model creates a dramatic arc; the way an individual game sets up and handles these cycles contributes much to its basic nature. The length of the process varies: in Dogs it's pretty much by definition (caused by the town creation rules) one session per 1-3 arcs, in TSoY it can range from 3 per session to one per three sessions (usually in the quicker end at my table, but I've read of groups who play a much more textured and leisurely game), while in Zombie Cinema it's one per fifteen minutes of play if the players are doing their job. You can see functional narrativism very clearly in these games if you know to observe the existence and functioning of this model; the games tend to fail if these reward cycles do not happen for some reason, as they are the designed main content for those games.
