[Mridangam] Frustrating play

Matthijs
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Actual Play
I'm writing this with mixed emotions. I really wanted to like this game, and had high expectations; however, play was okay at best, and a horror of meta-communication, rules interpretation & debate, and competitive deprotagonization at worst. This is partly due to the rules, and partly due to the players.

The players had all scanned the rules quickly before we gathered. I then explained the rules, we tested out a few threats & blessings, and then started play. During play, it became apparent that we really had no idea what sort of play was expected. What sort of core story is the game supposed to support? What kind of characters, situations and relations work best? We also weren't sure exactly who says what when, and came up with two possible structures: 1) "pass-the-totem"-play where anyone who gets narration (through blessings & threats) keeps narration until someone else takes over (through blessings & threats), and 2) PTA-like round-robin one-conflict-per-scene.

Somehow or other, we ended up playing in a different genres than the rules imply. This was probably because none of us really know much about Indian mythology, history or fairy tales, and we needed a common understanding of the setting in order to know where we were going. We first tried a pulp-era "adventuring party" thing, with the "pass-the-totem" structure, which broke down into pure silliness as we started telling asinine stories about each others' characters. We'd somehow missed the (important) rule of only speaking in your character's voice (or narrating thoughts). There was little character ownership, and less and less investment in the story. Much of this can be blamed on the players. After about fifteen-twenty minutes of actual play I called timeout (fist + open hand) and said I didn't want to play like this anymore; the others agreed, and we restarted.

We now tried to define characters, genre and central conflicts more strongly before play. We aimed for a story of interweaving plots. "Pass-the-totem" didn't seem to work, so we went for option 2: PTA-like play. For a while, it went pretty well. However, the threat system quickly lost its significance, as players started narrating strong threats: "Shut up, okay? This is a RAIL GUN I'm holding to your head." On the one hand, we were coming up with stuff like this on our turns; on the other, we were using the limited threats allowed by the system, which paled in comparison. There are, as far as I can see, no mechanical restrictions on using threats this way. The game worked pretty well for a while; there was dramatic tension, there were exciting plot twists, and the story escalated - though it was a bit bumpy at points, and there was a lot of "what the hell happens now???" after someone had thrown the plot in an unexpected direction. After we'd triggered the two divas & the shiva lingam, it was time to leave the temple map. Two of the players were close to the exit, and just stepped out; after doing so, they narrated an epilogue, which made follow-up hard - I and the last player still had a few scenes to go before we were out, and able to finish the game. Things started getting silly very quickly after the two players on the outside lost interest, and we ended the game prematurely.

In all, I feel we didn't play the game under optimal conditions - but I've played a lot of games with these players under similar conditions, and know it can work well with strong, clear rules. (Bacchanal, PTA, Dogs, local designs). The central problem was a lack of understanding how the game is supposed to be played, which led to floundering and lack of structure.

I had the feeling I was doing a blindtest of a half-finished idea. I'm left with a feeling of frustration both at the group and at the rules. I know this feeling very well - it's one I've had many, many times when playing with rules that don't support play properly. I go into the session believing that I've understood the game's vision, try to convey it, find it just doesn't appear in play, and watch the game disintegrate as people lose interest. I hate when that happens.

    Well, without a threat gesture, someone can't forcibly inflict an effect on you. He can point a railgun at your head and pull the trigger, but then, he has no ability to specify what happens to you. Threat gestures are the way that you make people react in specific ways.

    So, basically, when someone points a gun at me and doesn't offer a threat, I read it as an invitation to disarm him, block/dodge the shot, act injured, his bullets are actually flowers that make me horny, I step onto the bullet and fly up to greet the sun, or anything else I already wanted to do. He's got no power over me. That's the mechanical weight; a gesture sharply restricts the ways that the victim may respond, while without one, the other player is just another dancer, and he's not actually telling me, "I want you to respond this way."

    See what I'm sayin' here? The narration is on one level, and the pushy interactions of player authority are over on this separate level, in your hands and not in your mouth. You should never let yourself be dominated by words.

    These are deliberate gaps in the text because the text is written in a fictional author's voice, toward a fictional audience - specifically, it is Rachana Melwani (a dancer) speaking to other dancers. Rachana doesn't need to tell the dancers about the setting and characters and so forth because they are professionals who know this stuff for a living.

    Giving up this power, or consciously subordinating it to the mechanics, can be hard.

    Sure! I've been there too -- the point I'm trying to make is, though, that that's how the mechanics work, and you're not really playing the game-as-written if you don't play that way. That's not a slur on you; if we didn't change the games we play as we play them, we wouldn't have design communities and so on. But it's hard for me to talk about how the game works and understand what went wrong and make it work better, when you didn't really play the same game, y'know?

    While it seems that in Mridangam, you're supposed to have an invisible line, a feel for exactly how far you can go before it's considered a threat (or, in some cases, deprotagonization or usurping of narrative control).

    Now, um, this is Just Wrong, I'm sorry to say. It's very clear when something is a threat and when it isn't; if you wiggle your fingers, it's a threat. If you don't, it's not. Lxndr said it to me this way: Hands trump words. This means, if someone picks up an elephant and hits you with it, you can throw up Mayura and see what they do, without saying anything. If they accept it, give you the narration, and hit you anyway, you have carte blanche; maybe that was Airavata, Indra's elephant, and now that guy has forever lost the favor of the gods. You don't have to say anything about how being hit in the head with an elephant affected you, because no threat required it. That's how threats work.

    I assumed that the game would have complete instructions for play, for us, the real audience. I'm disappointed that it didn't....

    Did the text fail to dispel this assumption for you? I don't want to be the defensive designer here, I'm trying to figure out what was too sneaky and what was too obvious. It seemed to me, at least, that it was fairly clear that the document was something of an experiment in form-of-text as well as form-of-play.

    The intention of the article is to give the reader something to think about, really, and the tools to make something that he can play, with awareness of the structural and communicative issues the game raises; I no more expect someone to play Mridangam out-of-the-box than I'd expect someone to play d20 system without any character classes and races, y'know?

    How has your actual play been? Have any other groups tried the game?

    I have played the game a little bit, before the gestures and flower economy was finalised (which means, I clearly understand how-exactly-I-play the gestures, but am uncertain about the flowers), and basically I feel like it's essential to set down certain things before you play, most importantly a basis for the color and conflicts you're drawing from and a clear understanding of what the temple map represents; ask John Kim about his (uber-cool) prep work for his game to see what sort of stuff you need to do to make a playable instance.

    Since we're talking about playung stuff from PUSH 1, by the way, Waiting/Tea is playable right out of the box, prep-free, and a billion times awesome.
