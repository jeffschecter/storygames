    \n I'm really, really good at building characters. Most specifically, concept characters. If it's a system I'm intimately familiar with, I can tease possibly game-breaking mechanical efficacy out of the numbers, and still have it all make sense within the fiction, and fit the feel of the character. Usually, the character will be deeply rooted within the setting as well, assuming there's a setting to be deeply rooted in, and I have the requisite familiarity with it.
    \n
    \nIn play, I'm good at getting into character. I know that some people don't see that as important, but some people have three eyes and an ear in the center of their forehead, too. I can usually make tactical decisions without violating character. I can foreshadow and highlight things my character doesn't know without having their actions be based on that knowledge. I can, and frequently do, get so deeply into character that I'm emotionally wrung out at the end of a particularly good session. As a matter of fact, it wasn't a particularly good session if I didn't. Once, a non-gamer who'd gamed with us for the first time said he began to cease seeing me and start seeing the (female) character I was portraying. This is a double-edged compliment.

    \n I'm good at saying 'Yes, and...' not just to other players' suggestions but to my own character. I guess I mean that if it's the sort of game where my character has defining traits or a merit/flaw style sytem, I will go out of my way to play those. I have a friend who says 'never split the party!' when playing Call of Cthulhu, but lately I've taken to saying: 'Why not? It's what my character would do, and it's more interesting in this scenario.'
    \n
    \nTo be fair, while I enjoy the risk involved in this approach and know it can lead to a more interesting story, I -do- have a habit of getting annoyed about character death, particularly when it seems arbitrary, or pointless, or just doesn't satisfy the story in some way. That's part of the reason I'm drifting towards story games, or at least games with more story-focus.
    \n
    \nI tend to do well in Munchausen because whenever anyone has an objection or wager I always say 'yes' to it and incorporate it into the story, and it nearly always makes the story more entertaining as a result. Like when one particular Baron of mine had to argue his innocence in rhyming couplets.
