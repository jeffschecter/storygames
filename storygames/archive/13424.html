    \n Six, for sure.

    I'd suggest ignoring the instruction to gradually introduce the various elements (Consolamentum, the layout of the castle, the origin of the siege, etc) and get those expository bits out all at once before play. It's a bit of an info dump but can really help people understand what's going on.

    Make sure the endgame rules are clear - at most one can escape into the night, everybody else either burns or repents, full stop.

    Be prepared to help frame the introductory scene at the inn as facilitator - I've seen people be at sea about how to approach this, not really understanding the game's dynamic yet. Let them know the important thing is making a thematic statement about their character that they can explore, revisit, and challenge. How far will they go? What will they do? What moral lines will they cross? The ultimate outcome isn't particularly important, but these questions are.

    Have fun!

    \n The sweet spot is 6 players. 5 is pretty good too.

    Advice? Yeah. Make sure you print lots of copies of the relationship map (ideally one per player). Also make sure the players understand the significance of their primary and secondary character before they frame their first scene.

    If there is a player with experience of the game allow them to frame the opening inn scene. I would be tempted to order the players so the most experienced players get to frame scenes early and those who don't know the game get to see it done.

    \nSound advice from Jason & Declan.

    Pushing all the background information at once at the beginning of the game can cause information overload so I usually run it interactively (ask the players if they know about the Cathars and let someone else explain if there is one) and with some reading individually and some reading aloud - e.g. let each player turn over a new character and read the text aloud. An added benefit of this approach is that it is easier on your own voice

    Enjoy.

    \n First, I'd suggest that you play in addition to facilitating. There isn't much for an Montsegur facilitator to do once things get rolling, and this will give you a chance to demonstrate the game's features in actual play.

    Be sure to demonstrate the scene cards in play. There's a lot of juice in their scene-stealing powers, but it's easy to overlook them.

    I second Jason's recommendation about lumping the exposition. It's important that the Cathar material come out as early as possible. The Cathars are weird and consolamentum can be a fun thing to bring up in early acts. I usually just dispense with Raimonde's reading entirely and describe Montsegur simply as a near-impregnable fortress on a bluff. This makes time for Cecile's and Bertrand's readings.

    Because the Cathars are so alien and strange and the Perfects so isolated on the r-map, it is easy for the religious elements in the game to be disregarded, which seems a shame. Except for the Perfects, the game doesn't specify who is really a Cathar and who is not. I've had good times making this an issue in game play by having a character explicitly affirm or deny their belief, or challenge another character's beliefs.

    Good luck!
