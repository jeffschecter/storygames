    People who love both big miniatures games and bargains take note: I just scored a sweet 4.5'x8.5' Chessex wet-erase mondo-mat from frpgames.com for $39.95 + s/h.

    These suckers normally retail for over $100. This was a factory second. The only problems I could see were some very light blemishing on the vinyl and the printing was offset some so that the outside rows of squares were not flush with the edge. I don't know if the squares are flush on a regular full-price mat, so this might not be a problem at all. With 38 sq.ft. of mat, you can always trim off the ragged edge and still have plenty of squares left

    note that Noble Knight also carries these at a slightly lower price -- I bought from FRP because I figured the shipping would be faster from California than from Wisconsin.

    Heya,

    Sandy Antunes talks about three different agendas designers have when publishing an RPG here: http://www.rpg.net/columns/soap/soap116.phtml

    Sandy is a great guy, but never have I seen the Indie RPG movement more misrepresented in a column such as this. This line especially, "The game is often read and critiqued by others, again without actual play being required." The Indie movement, and I credit Ron Edwards with most of this, has always pointed again and again to actual play as the most important facet of design. "Design comes from Play" has been our mantra for several years now. And look at the the Forge, Story Games, and GameCraft. Each has a place to discuss and disect Actual Play. Heck, the Forge has two forums for it now.

    It's also interesting to me that he cites examples for "High Concept" (which should more acurately be called "High Hopes") and Homebrew but fails to cite an example of the sort of Indie game he's talking about. The successful independant RPGs like Burning Wheel, Sorcerer, DitV, TSOY, and InSpectres weren't crafted to be read, they were crafted to be played and came from actual play themselves. I mean, I can't say how much time Sandy has spent at the Forge or Story Games, but from this article it just seems like his only exposure to the movement has been the rants on RPGnet. I mean, do you guys get the same impression?

    Posted By: Call Me CurlyIs the Golden Girls crack really that egregious? I mean,
    if the question is "What's a good game to play with grandma?"

    And the answer is "Primetime Adventures."

    Then the next question is "what's your favorite show, Grandma?"
    I find myself surprisingly uncomfortable at the prospect (even imagined) of playing the role of an elderly person with an actual elderly person.

    I mean ... on some levels I know that it wouldn't be a big deal to get things wrong. "Oh, my poor dear boy, you think your eighty year old man is going to be able to sit through an hour long lecture without a bathroom break? You're so optimistic!" My grandma would totally laugh that off. She doesn't expect me to know that kind of stuff. I'm young. But there's a part of me that is deeply ashamed by my ignorance ... it ties into a sense of neglect of my elders that my mind wants to shy away from, hard.

    I wonder how much difficulty in terms of gaming with people who are not mere inches away from your own demographic (other geeky, often-male, gamers) comes from a sense of shame at the prospect of so vividly admitting your own ignorance of how other people live? You dare not roleplay in their conceptual territory, because you feel like getting that wrong would betray a lack of caring about them and their lives ... and yet, it's not fair to solve that problem by insisting that they roleplay in your conceptual territory.

  • TonyLB
    January 2007
    Well ... newbies aren't the issue. Newbies have a happy place at the Forge Booth, and should take it.

    The harder issue is that there are some folks who, despite 2+ years at GenCon, really aren't terribly good at working the convention. You know I won't get into names, so just imagine your own candidates.

    Now say we had Abby, Brian, Caldwell and Diana who are awesome GenCon hands. And then we have Eddie, Frank, Gina and Helen who are, frankly, "Eh" at best. The best way to pollinate the convention is to have (say) two booths: Abby+Brian+Eddie+Frank and Caldwell+Diana+Gina+Helen.

    The best thing for Abby, Brian, Caldwell and Diana, on the other hand, is to get a booth and staff it all on their own. That leaves Eddie, Frank, Gina and Helen rather critically short of ... well ... talent. Not experience, per se, but rather the benefits that can come from experience but don't always. Do they get a table, and have a hard time? Or do they just look around, see which way the wind is blowing, and not get a table? And if they don't get a table, how do they feel about that?

    I'm all for pollination. More booths is great. At the same time, stepping beyond the nursery of the Forge Booth puts all of us into a world where some more mature (read "NASTY") choices may have to be made.

If you have it in one language you're excluding a lot of people, even if you choose English which is the second language of most countries.
