    \n\nMarch 2008 \n

    \n
    \n
    \n

    Two questions. One is small, one is big. I'll answer the small one first, and if I can't get to the big one, someone remind me later.

    \n

    Re: Composing front to back, editing back to front.

    \n

    OK, let's say you've got a book you're making. Obviously, you make page 1 before page 2. Maybe you do it in chapters separately, but they each start on the right hand page and, though the use of filler, illustrations, and clever typesetting you can end them all on the left. That part's easy.

    \n

    For the sake of the argument, let's say you have a ten-page book. Let's say it's two chapters long. Both chapters are 5 ppg long. Let's say you've marked up some changes you want to make.

    \n

    Now, let's say that you want to make some changes in that book. Let's say you add a paragraph to chapter 1 on page 1. That pushes an equivalent amount of text to the next page. Let's say that it pushes the last page of that chapter over a page. Now it ends on the right side instead of the left, so you need to add filler, use that picture you wanted to use, whatever.

    \n

    Now you move on to chapter 2. Your marked up copy page numbers are off by two. Page 7 used to be page 5, but you know that, so you compensate, right? You make your change, which reduces a paragraph by three lines. Now, on page 8, line 2, you had an edit to make. But it's gone. It's somewhere on page 7. So eventually, you find it. You make an edit that adds six lines. Now you move on to page 9, line... wait, what line is that edit on now?

    \n

    This is all avoided by starting at the end. Your markup stays valid the entire time. Also, as a bonus, it keeps you from getting fatigued by accidentally rereading your entire book forward, and you tend to see errors more easily.

    \n

    It's a solution to a ridiculously basic but time-consuming problem.

    \n

    A for the jobs of a GM, well...

    \n

    OK, let's say we're playing GURPS. I am an Experienced GURPS GM, so it's a good example. Here are my jobs.

    \n
      \n
    1. Make up an environment in which the players can play.
      2. \n
    2. Make up NPCs who do things that the players will want to support or antagonize.
      3. \n
    3. Make sure those characters are powerful enough to threaten the PCs (otherwise, the players won't have fun in their defeat or will resent their weak help.)
      4. \n
    4. Make sure those characters are weak enough to lose to the PCs (otherwise, the players won't have fun defeating them or they'll resent having their contributions overshadowed).
      5. \n
    5. Make sure that the story goes on, irrespective of the failings of the players.
      6. \n
    6. Make sure the players' contributions are meaningful.
      7. \n
    \n

    So, let's see.

    \n
      \n
    • 1 is a fun thing to do.
      • \n
    • 2 is fun, too!
      • \n
    • You can not simultaneously do 3 and 4 with any reliability. The fun of the players (not to mention my own as GM) is based on seeing the whole story, so the threats have to be illusory. But they have to feel substantial. This is one reason many players hide dice.
      • \n
    • That means that 5 and 6 are also mutually incompatible. If the story must go on, the players' contributions are meaningless.
      • \n
    \n

    There are other implications of this stuff that's also nonfunctional, but this is the most direct stuff.

    \n

    I'm totally happy to talk about this more, so if you want, ask questions. I'm a little pressed for time at this very moment, so I probably didn't say something I meant to say, or might have pounded out "OWEH OWGEBHOUPEWF" and didn't proofread.

    Oh, wait, I forgot the big part of the GM conflicts of interest!

    \n
      \n
    1. Give rewards for good play
      2. \n
    2. Present challenges
      3. \n
    3. Arbitrate the relevance of rules
      4. \n
    \n

    This is the equivalent of having the opposing team both keep score and arbitrate the rules. There's a reason games have referees. Now, I hear already the response that the GM isn't your opponent. But if the GM isn't your opponent, then why are they responsible for 8? Do they provide opposition or not?

    \n
    \n
    \n

    Ryan, yeah, it's not necessarily easy, but once you think about it like that, you can start seeing how to structure play.

    \n

    Incidentally, mechanical rewards don't necessarily come without human judgment. Take a look at Fan Mail in PTA or Minuti\xe6 in Shock: They both rely on players "financially" supporting what they like to see happen. It's just that in PTA the Producer can't give fan mail (they're the Opposition) and in Shock: the Antag player can't do it. In both cases, though, everyone at the table is a human, and those players certainly weigh in on the players with the reward power to plead for what they want. I mean, has anyone ever seen a Producer in PTA not encouraging the players to give fan mail when something awesome happens?

    \n

    This is, of course, in addition to getting new Features in Shock:, which is a mechanically determined reward.
