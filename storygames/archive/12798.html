    \n Right, good call on the examples. I'll put some in.

    With the rituals, I guess it's a matter of taste and testing. If it were too much, for me, I'd notice because I'd get impatient during the rituals, thinking "why are we doing this rubbish when we're here to play". The rituals in SoD are very short. Long rituals would displace the balance, making the game less important.

    The rituals in SoD don't break up the game - they're at the beginning and end, like bookends. (Of course, that's a lie. The game itself is a ritual, clearly, with sub-rituals: The dream scenes, for example, and the cooperative creation of the mnemosite, which leads to the creature seemingly taking on a life of its own. Chaos magicians could definitely play the game with intent to create a servitor. I don't, though.)

    All in all, rituals are techniques just like everything else you use in a game; so it's hard to answer the question except to say what works for your group, works for your group. Just like "what's the right amount of dice-rolling", for example.

    \n Thanks!

    Okay so here's another question for you. If I say, "I hit with my sword," and you say, "Okay, roll d20 plus your base attack rating," is that a ritual? It certainly happens a lot when you're playing D&D, and it provides a certain rhythm you come to expect from every session of the game. What makes something a ritual vs. some other game mechanic? Does it only have to do with beginnings and endings of a play session?

    One example of a ritual that didn't feel like a ritual to me (but still worked to the same effect) was when I ran a game of Mouse Guard, in the beginning of each session after the first, one of the players is supposed to recap what happened in the previous adventure. Nothing in the book tells us we have to do this, but the rules offer a reward to the person who does, so of course it happens. I think this kind of opening ritual offers a clear incentive, which somehow makes it feel more natural and less like, "okay, now it's time to do the opening ritual everybody!"

    Also, a common closing ritual, not only in Mouse Guard but in many other games, is to divvy up experience or other reward points earned in the process of playing the game. In Mouse Guard specifically, the process of going through to see if you acted for your belief or against it, for instance, actually helps you review the most important events of the game for you and your character, as well as for other characters. It's sort of an instant-recap of the highlights as something for you to take away.

    Apocalypse World also has a special ending move that requires every player to "choose a character who knows you better than they used to...." In the special "Hx" mechanic of Apocalypse World, giving someone this kind of knowledge of your character can sometimes be like an extra experience point, which helps them to advance more quickly. So in a way, it's like players giving one another experience, except that it's tied to the fiction of the game and the changing relationships of the characters.

    What do you think of these sorts of rituals that people don't even call rituals most of the time?

    \n In my personal opinion (I say this because, well, there are a lot of opinions on what a ritual is - on this forum alone), just about everything can be called a ritual. (And a game. And art. So many terms are hard to define.) But when it comes to role-playing games, there are some distinctions I tend to draw:

    - Do we do it every time? Does the session/social event feel like it lacks something, if we don't perform this thing?
    - Is it something that doesn't actually create something new in the fiction?
    - Is it a social thing, something we do together?

    If so, I'm more likely to call it a ritual.

    So when we eat dinner before the game, and towards the end of dinner, I say: "Okay, let's get ready for the game", that's ritual.

    I'd also say that if we always have a Big Battle, and we know that the outcome is "the heroes win and the bad guys all die", that's a sort of ritual. It doesn't actually create anything new in the fiction - it just recreates something that we always need to be present.

    \n People, I think the discussion is already derailing a little...

    First: nice article, Matthijs. I can think of no way you could make it any better.

    Now, back to actual discussion...
    As someone who's played Society of Dreamers quite a lot, I'd like to point out to everyone that the "rituals" in this game are different from any other "ritualistic" behavior I ever encountered in roleplaying games. Different from the examples Matthijs himself gave two posts above, different from the very ritualized game mechanics in Polaris...
    And the difference is that SoD rituals seem to imply supernatural consequence in-real-life. This is never stated, but there is a strong suggestion of otherwise undefined "spirits" somehow interacting with the play of the game.
    The use of a Ouija board definitely unsettles some players at least a little, and the opening/closing ritual is easily rationalized as a way to make the Ouija thing "safe" \u2014 which of course just becomes a confirmation that it is, indeed, supposed to be dangerous. Much like when enacting a s\xe9ance at a party, people are prompted to partially suspend their disbelief and to set themselves up to be easily scared (in the name of having fun, of course).
    The second opening ritual is modeled after "serious" meditation exercises used as part of "spiritual" practices, thus carrying an underlying suggestion that maybe the author believes such things. It's pretty relaxing and sort-of reassuring. In context, it prompts people to let go and open up: what's going to happen can't be bad, it suggests, but you have "let" it happen.

    My point is: while mechanically optional (they could be done away with and the coherence of the game-design would hold), the rituals in SoD are in my eyes a clever and deliberate technique to affect the participants in the "metaplay" level (as defined by Tobias Wrigstad et al.), putting them all "in the right mood" for the game.

    Posted By: thissentenceisfalseI'm after two games of SoD, and Dream sequences in both them felt very flat and uninteresting (in comparison with "normal" scenes).
    \nIn my experience, the key to rewarding Dream sequences is to fully embrace the irrational and chaotic working of dreams, which may include some very blatant symbolism as well as childlike literal-mindedness.

    In one actual play instance, the Diver had just described herself (as the result of an action which amounted to a change of location) standing in front of a lighthouse, which had a bright light on its top, under a blue sky where the sun looked very large, and a soldier sitting on a bench by the lighthouse door, as if waiting for her. I told the player: \xabTake the sun in your hand.\xbb She described the sun turning into a gold coin in her hand, and was therefore told to gift it to the soldier.

    We knew that "sitting on a bench by the door, as if waiting for her" was a reference to her childhood scene, btw... I suggest you perform this sort of reincorporation (only) when you want to mark that the dream is something personal to your character \u2014 as opposed to something personal for the Dreamer. It was effectively like turning the Dreamer's dream into the Diver's dream, either showing that the Mnemosite is a linking force transcending the singular human being, or maybe that the Diver was a Dreamer herself.
    When the Dreamer in the sequence is also a player character, you additionally have the option of reincorporating material from his childhood and youth sequences, which I recommend you do most of the time and I don't think carries any drawbacks.
    Finally, the "gold coin" in the example exchange could also be read as a reference to a third player character -- not Diver nor Dreamer for that sequence. That's because it was a time, long enough into the game, when we really took off our gloves regarding the weird, creepy, bat-shit insane supernatural crap.
