    \n Very cautiously. I don't play opposite-gender characters very often, because I am a deep-immersion-style player, and there are some things I just can't get into, in that I feel I can't do them justice. I have similar problems with real-world cultures, which is why I tend to play North Americans of European or Aboriginal descent (like me). I don't have a problem with it in fantasy settings, because I feel there's a lot more licence to improvise.

    A lot of this emerges from the fact that in the past, I've been at the table with people playing female characters or characters of a different culture who would do horrible, stupid, or stereotypical things, often to the detriment of the group, and when asked, responded "well, [group x] would do that; they are just like that." I try not to be that kind of player.

    When I run a game (which I usually do) I have two defences against this kind of behaviour: the first is that my NPCs tend to be walk-ons, defined primarily by their relationship to the PCs. I don't spend a lot of time putting their character development front-and-centre, unless the PCs seek it out, because this is time to play a game, not time to listen to me talk about my kewl NPCs. The second defence is that I get to play a wide variety of cast members, so if I feel I am descending into stereotype territory with one character, I can always balance it out by playing another against the stereotype, or by having another character "lampshade" the activity: "Oh, yeah, Col' Dub is great for information, but he's also doing what he can to live the textbook rasta gangster lifestyle. You learn to live with it."

    That all said, there will probably come a day soon where I might start toying with the idea of expanding my character box. I already have been pushing the boundaries by trying to play characters with genuine faith (something I totally don't relate to). Who knows what might be next?

I've enjoyed playing sans Y-chromosome ever since, and haven't had any complaints about my characterization, either.

    \n Like Johnstone touched upon, the story centers on the PC's, not the NPC's. I've never played a female PC before (but then, I've GM'ed much more than I've played). I keep fearing that I'll do it wrong; that despite appearances, some things motivate women that I simply do not understand. I try to avoid the stereotypes, I try to avoid the misogyny, but sometimes it seems so subtle to me that I feel certain that something I'll consider no problem at all, will actually reveal me as an ignorant, privileged male. I've heard the argument many times that, "But you'll play an elf, is that any less alien?" In fact, yes! Sure, in theory, elves belong to an entirely different species, with different traditions and live for centuries. But really, they're just us middle class, twnetieth century American white men with pointy ears. Besides, I don't know any elves that my performance might alienate or offend. I can't say the same for women.

    I do play some female NPC's when I run games, though. But since they only appear as NPC's, they really shouldn't get enough spotlight time to highlight my inadequacies. The spotlight should stay on the PC's. If any of my NPC's, male or female, gets that kind of screen time, I've screwed something up, I say.\n

    \n I think the biggest reason why players in our group don't typically play opposite-sex characters is because we have as many men playing with us as women. We've already got a nice, nearly-evenly-mixed party if everyone plays their own sex (barring the GM, of course, but NPCs tend towards an even mix, too).

    Some of the other reasons are probably influencing it, too, like the fear of "getting it wrong" or "MY IMMERSION!" or "I can't do the voice" or whatever, but I don't think any of those have as much impact for us as simply already having plenty of both men and women at the table.


    Now, the weird thing is this: the most over-the-top, aggressive, macho characters we've had in our games have been, without exception, female PCs played by female players. And the most devotedly romantic, deeply sensitive, nurturing PCs? Almost entirely male PCs played by male players. So there's a lot of bending of stereotypical gender roles going on, even if no one is actually officially playing a PC of the opposite sex. (And this is why I made a point of saying "opposite sex" rather than "opposite gender" in the first paragraph.)

    \n I don't play female characters often, but it's mostly so fellow players don't have to think about switching pronouns, rather than any sort of discomfort with playing another gender. A lot of character elements aren't really gender-specific, particularly for heroic characters who are challenging the world.

    I do GM a lot, and of course I have to play many female NPCs. In such cases, I pay close attention to the culture within the game and how it constructs gender. The more heroic/strong the female NPC is, the more likely I'll deviate from that gender construction and focus more on the character's individual drives. Admittedly, I do this with male characters, too (and other genders, e.g. some cultures don't differentiate between boys and girls below a certain age, they're just children).

    I suppose my suggestion would be to think about how you choose to play any gender, to consider the ramifications of how you play. EDIT: as an extra suggestion, it's also good to be forgiving of those who sincerely try to play well but make an occasional mistake.

I don't *think* I play them as men with different parts (i.e. the gender equivalent of humans with funny ears) though a few old standbys do tend to be tomboyish.
    \n
    \nAs a result of this, I don't ever have an issue with playing female NPCs. I once got the somewhat disturbing compliment when playing V:tM from a first-time gamer after a hours-long roleplayed "interrogation" that after a while, he stopped seeing me sitting there, and started seeing the female character I was playing.
    \n
    \nI still get a little shy of romance/attraction when I play female characters. I'm okay with casual in-character flirtation, but if it gets even vaguely serious, I'm like to start getting uncomfortable. This happens less often when I play online, and even less when the player of the male love interest is played by a female. I think this is a comfort issue, and with a group where I'd be able to play seriously without worrying about anyone cracking a gay joke, I'd probably be okay with that.
