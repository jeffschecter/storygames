    \n Heck, I think that all the games I've ever made fall into this category.


    I made a GMless horror game that is super cool in a lot of ways, except that it has one basic problem: it's not scary. It tells cool stories about ghosts and monsters and people whose lives are in danger, but it never evokes a sense of dread or fear in the players themselves.

    I won "Best Game" in Game Chef several years ago with a game that, it turned out, was completely unplayable. The basic system sounded like a good idea on paper, but in playtest it turned out to be too difficult to set stakes that could actually work within the system. I revised the game to make it playable, by completely removing the core mechanic and replacing it with something else entirely. But it still has a limitation of requiring exactly six players to play. This strict limit on players has made it difficult for me to get in more than one or two playtests.

    I put a lot of thought and effort into Death Takes a Holiday, but in the couple of playtests, it turned out that there was no incentive for players to edit others player's statements. Which was sort of the entirety of the core mechanic.

    It is possible that, with some revision, I may still revise and playtest these games some more.

    \n I designed Brick & Mortar: Last of the Independents for a design contest. It's a satirical game about retail stores wrapped in the guise of a survival-horror-comedy scenario set in a failing retail store. I was trying to do some funky stuff with Apocalypse World-style fictional triggers in a GM-less context. I think I did some clever things in the game, and I think I made it humorous without the game itself being a joke. I ran one playtest session which revealed a few minor issues (there wasn't enough seed content so play tends to flounder a little in the beginning, and there probably need to be some triggers that cause people to escalate tension if it's not resolved, and the resolution system wasn't great), but I have zero passion for the survival horror genre so I haven't had the motivation to develop the game further.

    Heck, I think that all the games I've ever made fall into this category.


    I made a GMless horror game that is super cool in a lot of ways, except that it has one basic problem: it's not scary. It tells cool stories about ghosts and monsters and people whose lives are in danger, but it never evokes a sense of dread or fear in the players themselves.

    \nLooking at Nameless Horror, I can certainly understand why it's "not scary". The rules are all there for telling cool stories but there is nothing in the rules that mechanically inch people close to danger or death, so there's really no sense of dread.

    I can't help but think that you need to add something like this:

    On a series of index cards, write a different number of each, 10 through 20 (the numbers are kind of arbitrary, but you get the idea). fold the index cards to hide the value, and each player draws an index card. This is the player's DOOM number. The player is never, ever allowed to look at the doom number they own, unless the value crosses below zero (at which point it will become revealed).

    When a player asks the two other players to interpret the tarot cards, they each roll a dice, in secret from the rest of the table, which is kept under a cup.

    When a player selects an interpretation, the player hands over his folded index card to the interpretor, who reveals his die roll. The Interpretor subtracts the die roll from the player's doom number, and makes a note of the player's new doom number on the card. Again, the player never sees his doom number (but all of the other players will know what it is to some extent)...he just knows how much it has been reduced at each stage.

    If the interpretor reduces the number to below zero, he reveals the index card. The interpretor now takes full control of the scene, describing VERY VERY BAD THINGS that happen to the player's character. The tarot card interpretation can be ignored, or modified.

    If an interpretor kills off a player, he gains doom points based on the inverse of the value of the dying player's doom number. The player that had just died will mark this adjustment on the interpretor's card.

    \n One of my most well-known works is the survival horror game Geiger Counter, and the main issue with it is that it doesn't do what I want yet. But a lot of other people enjoy it, in either the alpha or beta drafts. I could probably make it work, but my design interests have mostly moved on to other projects, so I'm waiting until the interest and inspiration strikes me again at a moment when I have time to work on it.

    Things I want it to do that it doesn't currently do:
    - provide really good guidance for playing the menace
    - get across the weird semi-protagonism the players should feel for their semi-disposable characters
    - take advantage of the huge advances in GMless play since it was written
    - be consistent in delivering stuff like Alien rather than stuff like Event Horizon
    - be strongly location-based without feeling artificially restricted like a board game

    \n Early drafts of Delve:

    I thought I could contribute a valuable fantasy game to what's out there, by cost-balancing point-buy of useful abilities and integrating positioning rolls into combat. Basically trying to be more fair & realistic than D&D. Turns out it takes more than that.

    I thought broad-scale setting design was more important than designing support for immediate situations. Oops.

    I thought everyone agreed on what "immersion" meant, so techniques for the kind I like would appeal to everyone. Hahahahahahaha.

    Early drafts of Within My Clutches:

    I thought that, if I thought it would be cool for something to happen in play, I could simply write a rule telling players to do that. It turns out, people don't follow instructions unless that gets them something they want. In some cases, it turned out that when I added a good incentive, no further instruction was required.

    A mechanical action that's independent of what was just roleplayed puts players in the awkward position of needing to play two games, one where they roleplay for its own sake, and another where they roll dice (or whatever) to see what happens. I find this less fun than a well-connected game, and most players seem to agree.

    I didn't pay attention to frequency of choices. There were too many steps to go through in set-up and early play before anyone actually got to make any meaningful game decisions.

    \n I have discarded a number of games because they weren't any good. I started off in the indie RPG community when I was in high school, designing a fantasy heart breaker that somehow sought to fuse Alien with D&D. as I recall, the Aliens were a playable class. This was heavily reconfigured into an incredibly boring game with tarot card resolution about humans living in a world where enormous dream beings had invaded. Even attempting to type it up reveals to me how disjointed it was.

    Way back when Ron Edwards did the Ronnie's game design competition, I submitted The Rat God's Girlfriend, which was a three player game where each person took on the role of a colony of rats, the human they worship as a deity, and his new girlfriend. At the time, I considered it to be a serious relationship drama with horror and supernatural elements, revealing my perennial problem whereby I make something I think is dramatic, and completely miss how fucking hilarious it is.

    Other lessons I have learned are: games inspired by Beckett are no fun.

    \n In Monsterhearts, I found that players too readily embrace their monstrous side without fear of consequences so I tried hacking it into an organised crime family setting (Mobsterhearts), where if their mob family found out their true nature, they'd execute them. I had a neat thing where - whenever a PC acted 'freaky' - they could buy the silence of witnesses by giving them a string, but if the number of strings built up to a certain level, the witness would expose the PC unless they were 'dealt' with (hopefully leading the PC to commit more 'freaky' acts and so cause a downward spiral).

    Trouble was, it took one of the coolest things about Monsterhearts (acting like a monster) and then punished you for it. So the game would turn into the MC trying to push the PCs to use their skin moves, but the players refusing to do so because it would get them into more trouble down the line (which flies in the face of the basic MC principles).

    PCs trying to deny, hide, suppress their monstrous side was the right vibe, but they needed to be motivated to engage with the mechanic of showing their monstrous side and buying people off with strings as opposed to their best tactical course of action to be refuse to show their monstrous side at all. There needed to be a bigger advantage to using their monstrous side so the game could be about using it, but managing the consequences, rather than just not using it at all.

    So, basically, nice little mechanic, but it demotivated the players from actually using it.
