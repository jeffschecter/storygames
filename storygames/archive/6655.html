\n Part 1 of 2:
\n
\nDespite the gentle teasing of many, I did get sign-ups for a game of Dogs in the Vineyard set in the Reign of Terror (Les Chiens de la Terreur), so got to run that game. Just for context/terminology, the Chiens are the "watchdogs" (hence "dogs", in French "chiens") of the Committee for Public Safety in this "Terror that never was", as the Committe has created a national system of Commissioners (Stewards) nationally, with the order of the watchdogs to of course safeguard the Revolution (i.e., conduct the Terror, but also of course also grant mercy and instruction as needed).
\n
\nJana was also in this game, playing Jean-Luc de la For\xeat (I may have the last name wrong of the PC), simple and hardy farming young man and revolutionary, salt of the Earth, inspired to become a dog. Jean-Luc is truly in touch with nature and also has a thinly-suppresed vengeance streak, especially against the Royalist enemies of the Revolution, as she believes it was they who burned down her family's farm as her parents were tax resistors as the Revolution began.
\n
\nThis alrready created a great contrast to Matthew's Henri Phillip and Gareth's Emile d'Estaing, both of whom came from Royalist-but-converted backgrounds! Henri's family's lands were, of course, repossessed by the people, and his father killed himself over a tax dispute. The mother raised Henri, who seemed to talk to "imaginery" demons and was soon selected by observant government people as the right kind of person to become a Dog; Henri is a servant of the wrath of justice.
\n
\nMeanwhile, Emile - whose family it turns out was actually behind the tax dispute/financial ruin of Henir's family - is the son of an aristocrat who has totally disavowed and disowned that background, enlightened to join the Revolution, and who actually completely hides and tries to bury his past, and is trying to constantly prove himself as faithful to the cause.
\n
\nTheir Accomplishment rounds were all really strong, the players (2 totally new to Dogs) took really well to that compared to many new to the game, and so we had Henri clear his father's name (and of course face accusations of being a Royalist in his heart; I like how he tied it back to Emile's family), Jean-Luc prove he could be urbane and sophisticated (given his country bumpkin background; this had a great scene and kudos to Jana for making a Trait of "Loud, snorting laugh that's effective" useful, and even graduating from 1d4 to 1d6), and Emile prove he could get by without servants around (great scene as former servants try to lynch him and Gareth gives a great speech pointing out that to kill him now would ruin any potential he'd have that these servants invested into him and which could serve the Revolution now, I can't do it justice at the moment but I really liked that).
\n
\nThe 3 new Dogs go to the town of Landersheim and face a heresy where forces have taken over from the old Commissioner, and an inspired Revolutionary, a latter-day Joan of Arc basically, Stephanie d'Alesia believes she has been inspired by the Lord (remember, Robespierre and his ilk were Deists, so Christianity of sorts is actually part of the Revolution in this game) that the Revolution has truly reached all the people and "anyone" can be a Dog AND Commissioner at once, that the Parisian elitist system does not apply and anyone receives direct inspiration from the Lord to fill these roles. Henri is disgusted with this "anarchy" while Jean-Luc thinks perhaps this town is in fact carrying the Revolution to its next level, at least suitable locally, and Gareth is conflicted, wanting to support the people but wanting to be orthodox as a Dog.

    \n Part 2 of 2:
    \n
    \nWithout going through the whole story, the players did a great job of immediately debating and arguing over their stances, soon coming into Conflicts, and this really got intrigueing when they got into a Conflict over whether to relieve Stephanie of her Commissioner duties, Jean-Luc at first taking Stephanie's siding and trying to stop Henri, while Emile at first is cautious and shifting as it goes, watching the Raises and Sees. But soon Emile at least believes they should relieve Stephanie, even if he draws no greater conclusion, and as Stephanie and the mob threaten and imprison Emile and Henri, Jean-Luc soon moves to keep her fellow Dogs safe as the primary motivation. And finally, having broken out of prison (all during the same Conflict - Dog is cool!), Emile does a very Revolution-style thing, reminding me of the death of Marat in a way, in shooting Stephanie in the back! But it's brutal, and the PCs actually Give, letting Stephanie stay as Commisioner.
    \n
    \nBut that final volley of flintlock fire is so brutal that Stephanie lies dying, bleeding to death, and here the Dogs pass their final judgement - "let nature take its course." Stephanie's husband and the (very) few of the mob with any remaining courage against the Dogs are kept away from Stephanie as she bleeds to death in front of them (how Terror-like)! So justice is served; they appoint a new Commissioner, an old mentor to Jean-Luc who is in the town now and who used to actually be a big supporter of Stephanie's, making sure the old mentor agrees to be orthodox and be "just" the Commissioner with no one else in the town serving as Commissioners or Dogs. Cool beans! I really enjoyed this game and especially the players, and I think the town "works" (it's sort of based on a conversation I once had with Carl about a town of all Dogs, and I think there was discussion on this on the Forge as well).

    \n This game also is starting to echo loudly in my head, and I'd like to share another bit of it. A good bit of detail, I hope you enjoy it, or if not it at least is good actual play documentation for any researchers/analysts.
    \n
    \nOn the way into Landersheim, the Dogs note the peasants of the small farming community (which is now rife with famine due to the failure of wheat, along with a threat of wolves) are wearing hastily-improvised, oft-atrocioius Dogs-like coats and caps and cockades and cloaks and capes (the Dogs of the Terror are a flamboyant sort!).
    \n
    \nThey quickly move to the Town Hall to ask after the Commissioner, whom they are informed has been replaced - and imprisoned. Most of the able males and females of the village are Commissioners, divinely inspired, and from there they learn this doctrine of anyone can be directly divinely inspired and is equal to act. Essentially, the ideal of communism' or anarchy, depending a bit on how you look at it (I don't want to get into a sociopolitical argument, I do understand distinctions, I just want to give a broad brush feel). There is a woman who is effectively albiet not nominally in charge, the prior-post-mentioned Stephanie d'Alesia.
    \n
    \nAsking after the imprisioned prior (depending on how you look at things) Commissioner, Pierre Gaston, they find that it turned out the was sending love letters with revealing information to a Royalist woman in the Bastille. The Dogs debate amongst themelves but as I recall while some were more vehement than others all were united in agreeing it was deplorable if true and he would be unfit to be Commissioner if anything were revealing in those letters. I can't recall exactly, but I think on the matter of having just sent them at all there were divergent views but it wasn't something discussed too much. I might be wrong here in some details.
    \n
    \nAnd the Dogs heatedly argue over this notion of direct divine inspiration bypassing the state authority, the expression of the people's will. The players were SO awesome here, they TOTALLY glommed onto the little bits of Robespierre and Jacobin philsoophy, and they threw around comments on the "social contract" and an "enlightened minrotiy," only a couple times even pausing to ask me "hey, what was that bit you told us before where some people could act...", those sorts of things. I was really struck, I should say, and when the game was over, now that I realize it, I never had said anything to the players, so if you read this, folks, you were totally awesome here, you blew me away, I was tickled pink! It's a great memory.
    \n
    \nAnyway, as Henri angrily and loudly uses the word "anarchy" in describing what was happening, it reaches Stephanie d'Alesia's ears as the Dogs are inside a foyer near the hall she inhabits in Town Hall. It takes her a few moments to come out, as the Dogs continue to argue, again more quietly, but soon she comes out, and asks after the accusation. She is a bold and charismatic woman, or at least I think that got across to the players (I mean, I wanted to be clear, so I outright said that's how people would commonly perceive her, not to say their characters can react however). Basically here she goes through the spiel supporting the doctrine and (I hope) demonstrating passion for the Revolution. Jean-Luc is taken with her approach, Henri is outraged, and Emile is receptive to hearing more, but not quite as sympathetic as Jean-Luc (whom, it should be said, is not saying Stephanie is correct, Jana doesn't play him buying all the way into it, just being very receptive to this botoms-up approach).

    \n Whoops, pardon that incomplete thought above, what I meant to finish with was that what supported this was setting up certain Relationships in advance to people in the town worked. I'd seen Carl do this prior, and read of it elsewhere. I asked the players to define any Relationships in advance and then I tried to map those into people I already had in the town scenario, although in particular I let them define the type of people and then I looked through the villagers or if I could inserted them into the village in a supporting role as it made sense. So as Jara wanted a hermit mentor who had helped Jean-Luc learn the ways of the forest, I inserted him as having moved nearby Landersheim and as Stephanie inspired the people to go out and defend the village against the wolves and work harder in farming and using the land, he became inspired by seeing the community so self-inspired. I'll talk more about that later, but for now the point is that having these people inserted into the town whether matching them to someone already in the setting or adding them does help the players have focus and interest and gives the GM ways to create sympathies or challenges or loyalty issues for the PCs and therefore players.

    \n Wilson, thanks for writing this up, and thanks loads for running this for us! As mentioned elsewhere, I was initially apprehensive about the setting, but those misgivings happily evaporated immediately at the table. You did a great job presenting what was needed to get rolling, and the alternate history definitely has enough room for non-history buffs to flex and strut with glee. Props.

    I played Henri at the table, and enjoyed that immensely. I had played my first dog the day before, and that game definitely colored my Chien in this game, so I hope you'll indulge a digression here. Micah, the Dog I played Saturday, was defiant of authority and really fairly forgiving of sinners and tolerant of outsiders. I think I made Henri so obsessed with the power structure and authority of the Council to play out the other side of that coin. Also because it gave me a focused point to tie Henri to the situation in a setting I was less familiar with.

    Some high points for me:
    First riding into the town and seeing all these...peasants...with mock Chien cloaks on. Oh, yeah, that was a bit shocking, and I was sorely tempted to have Henri start rounding them up at gunpoint then and there. But the other Chien just kept riding to town hall, cool as you'd like, which just made it more tense. There was no conflict in that, just the feeling that Henri was getting wound up tighter as he had to turn a blind eye to heresy. For now. I think a lot of that tension was internal for me, so I thought I'd share it here in hindsight.

    The ideological conflict between Henri and Jean-Luc was stirring. I really felt like there was some weight to the arguments. I definitely personally sympathize with Jean-Luc's position, focused as it was on what was best for the people. But Henri's position was very clear for me, that being a Chien charged with enforcing the laws and securing the authority of the Council. The clash of those two viewpoints felt really valid.

    I also loved when Henri threw his body before Emile to protect him from the explosion(helping dice). I was unsure how those two characters would end up, given the complicated family histories, so I was really pleased to have that come up.

    Stephanie was a super antagonist. Her position was strong and empathetic, even though it conflicted with the Council. That was very grippy. In truth, I cannot imagine her sorcerer husband being a more engaging antagonist, even if we had tangled with him more directly. Very nice. And do not forget the red-white-and-blue eyes!

    One thing that I thought was very cool in the last conflict was the way the Chiens(and Stephanie) shifted in how they relate to each other, but without actually changing their positions. Jean-Luc wasn't convinced that it wouldn't be great for everyone to be a commissioner. Henri still believed that a strong central authority is critical. And I don't think either of the two really convinced Emile that they were right. But still by the end all three were united against the strong antagonist. Perhaps that was to protect the people, or the Council, or the brotherhood of the Chien. But we started the conflict on opposite sides(or undecided), and ended it unified. And it is very cool that the system not only accommodates that, but supports it. Neat.

    I'd be very interested to hear Gareth and Jana's impressions of the game and the central conflicts!

    \n Filling in a few more details as I said I would above...
    \n
    \nJust as les Chiens complete the interrogation of Pierre Gaston, they hear the churchbells ringing and a loud hue and cry from the streets. They go out to see what is going on, finding that while some in the mob yield as one would expect to them, others do not, treating them as equals and not giving way. Still, les Chiens are powerful and it doesn't take that long to force their way through the crowd to see a cart with a few men on it and 3 dead wolves. The townspeople are excited given the danger these wolves presented, but also because the famine is so severe the meat from these wolves will be of great value. Jean-Luc recognizes that leading the men is Jean Benoit, the (now ex-)hermit who was his mentor. He does not wear a Chien's coat or such, but it is quickly clear he is inspired by Stephanie and the movement in town as he cheers her for the success of their hunt, and otherwise praises her. Jean-Luc calls to Jean and the old friends are reunited, kissing on each cheek.
    \n
    \nCatching up, Jean-Luc finds that Jean had moved from the old area given the collateral damage from the farmlands' burning down. He ended up near Landersheim, and until just the last few days had retained his hermit ways. But he saw the townspeople suddenly more fervent, more coordinated in their hunts after the menacing wolves, and he saw them otherwise trying as well to do their best to survive with great sense of community - and Revolution. He joined them, inspired by that. He accredits Stephanie with unveiling this revelation of direct divine inspiration to position. And most importantly, he is able to give the townspeople better skills and organization.
    \n
    \nDuring this time, les Chiens listen to the villagers, feeling them out. While the large majority seem truly enthusiastic and supportive of Stephanie, others though do quietly grumble. It also turns out that some people who have opposed this idea have been imprisoned and will be tried and likely guilotined as enemies of the Revolution (remember, this is a common punishment for opposing the doctrine of a Chien, and so in a sense this isn't so extraordinary, but does show the seriousness they're taking this with). Les Chiens also confirm that Stephanie is the central decision maker, with her husband most often present. Apparently there is already a debate among the townfolk as to have more people involved in decision-making, but no conclusions yet as this "new phase" of the Revolution is so new.

Emile agrees he would prefer this, and while Jean-Luc will not kill Stephanie, the point is moot as Stephanie now is seen to be mortally wounded. Henri, as he checks himself, realizes that he's not so bad after all (Matthew had a few d10s and even some d8s and more, yet his highest total was a 10 or 11!), and Emile also was banged up but basically okay, too (no medical follow-up). Jean-Luc was in no real danger. This is where as I mention early in the thread they agree to let Stephanie live or die at God's will, letting nature take its course; she dies, bleeding into the street as les Chiens bar those few faithful who even consider opposing them.
