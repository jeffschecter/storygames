\n Back in the day I played a lot of D&D. In middle school it was Red Box - we played a little bit of AD&D with it, not really grasping that they were different games, so mostly Red Box. One of our first campaigns was very much as Eero described: we wanted to level up as fast as we could. And that was fine but it didn't really feel right. The Dragonlance modules (and high school) were where we stopped doing this. We would level up, explore around, push back on our problems, have adventures doing so, and our goal was that at the exact moment we had done all we could with our current character effectiveness, we wanted to level up then. In other words, each experience level was a new set of character capabilities, giving us (incrementally) new trouble we could get into that we couldn't before, new goals we could pursue that were (again, incrementally) out of our reach before, and nemeses that were too powerful might now be within our reach. If we levelled up too early, we were disappointed in the same way you might be disappointed in Nethack if you're exploring an interesting level and a pit trap drops you down to the next one. This was the reason we implemented training times for levelling up. I think this was a rule in an AD&D book. Basically we would say, "bah, I don't have time to train right now, I have to obtain this legendary piece of jewelry for my hot elf girlfriend" and artificially string out the levelling up process if we played, say, a module, and got more XP than we expected.

White Wolf's XP system was almost entirely pacing. Eero thinks:\n
Posted By: Eero Tuovinenthe purpose of XP is not to be a token bone thrown to keep the players happy (Whitewolf)
\nbut that seems way off base. White Wolf XP was virtually entirely about two things: 1) showing up to the session, and 2) interacting with the other players (including the GM). The XP rules were there to encourage a lively table, but the XP themselves existed for the same reason as in D&D - to give characters the capability to get themselves into more serious trouble, and to dig their way out of less serious trouble. The high degree of player freedom given in spending XP helped with this. "I need to go punch a dude, I want more Punching skill." You decided where you were going before you spent XP, you decided your goals for the character with the XP you had.

    \n Tyler has it - thinking of XP merely in terms of vague "reward" is too superficial. The only "reward" in xp is if you're into power fantasies, but need the game to make you earn it so it feels more real. Actually, not quite right - the other way for xp to be directly a reward is if it's used to keep score, as it is in some D&D versions. All other purposes for it are more complex and only rewarding insofar as the XP system actually drives the game's engines towards greater heights.

    Anyway, my experience with D&D mirrors Jason's. I'm especially enamoured of the notion that the reason for having character advancement in that game is variety and a growth towards new challenges as the old ones go stale, all stapled into an appealing fictional story about growth into herodom/dukedom. D&D is of course such a wide game that there are plenty of different attitudes towards this thing in its different play cultures. Another attitude I rather like is the one where XP is just a score-keeping device, and the system is fine-tuned to make its benefits nominal, enabling fresh characters and veterans side by side. I'm seeing these tones in that new Lamentations of the Flame Princess D&D-spinoff, for example, as it seems pretty diligent about never making the player characters into invulnerable superheroes.

    My experience playing WW games is really quite nominal, but I've played other games with essentially that same xp system - it became very common in its day, I still encounter new designers strutting out those same moves today. My own read of it is that it's vestigial: the system is intentionally made equalizing between different players to reduce competition and it's intentionally made so slow that it has no discernible impact on moment-to-moment play. It's also completely point-based and player-controlled when it does do something so as to not cause any upsets. In a word I find it hopelessly dull, like the glorious D&D system viewed through a stained glass. My pet theory of how this sort of xp system came about is that the game designers during the late '80s when we started seeing this sort of thing really wanted to get rid of the D&D leveling activity altogether, but they were so mired in the traditional game design structures that they were unable to provide players with real alternative play activities. That's where you get a game like Vampire that tries hard to be dramatic and moody and whatnot, but really spends most of its wordcount speccing up character crunch to keep players happy and hungry for more; the xp system is then there simply as something to keep the players coming back and keep the players committed to their characters. Just come back for three more sessions and your character will get another dot!

    So yeah, I don't have a very high opinion of the static xp set-up where you give the same small number of points to each character after each session. It has all the signs of vestigial ritual activity that could be done away with altogether were the designer not committed to making a compromise with his every act of design. Alternatively, it's good for limp-wristed groups that don't want any competition but want to feel like their characters are growing stronger in their hamster wheels. In either case it's only a reward mechanism by the virtue of incoherency: I fail to see why the players should actually care about the xp, and as it comes to them completely automatically, the game wouldn't really change if they just dropped the whole thing.

    The above contains no hate for any real people, I'm just telling you what I see when I look at a static xp-dole system.

    \nPlayer Tasks are the main resource of progression, they can be used to try and find an item, gain health/spirit, or gain a trait level.     \n
    \nTrait levels gained with a player task, trait levels are ability packages that can give players new actions, more dice and special powers, fairly standard rpg stuff. in addition to spending a player task they also cost a character some spirit.
    \n
    \nSo the game's character progression is somewhat decentralized, there is no real exp but a few currencies and game events that can effect your character's progression.
