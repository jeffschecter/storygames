    \n I just finished a local con, Weathertop.

    Because I GMed 6 games, I got to take a massive amount of swag (unlimited "low swag" and several pieces of their "premium swag"). Since d&d books no longer appeal to me, I got a Dreamblade starter and 3 Dreamblade boosters.

    Two of my friends (Daniel Wood and Kirk Mitchell) each GMed a game and thus each got a Dreamblade booster as well.

    Since we definitely have enough to play, our first and primary reason for having them is to play Dreamblade.

    Other uses, thus far:
    1.) I've used the more humanoid ones in Polarpunk playtests, so far. There's a model that looks sort of like a blue bear, and I used it as polarbears. Swordstriders became the Gadgeteer's droids. Forgeblind Punisher and Blind Spikemauler and others became the human gangers that were encountered.

    2.) miniatures for any other minis-based or minis-optimal game. Agon, d&d, Savage Worlds, etc.

    3.) I want to do a Dreamblade/RPG hybrid. Play out a game of Dreamblade, and have the results effect the session of Don't Rest Your Head that follows (maybe the surviving dream monsters become statted as Nightmares, and whoever wins the Dreamscape gets to narrate in some effects for the Don't Rest Your Head game, etc).

    \n Put a bunch of them on a table, covering each one with a paper cup.
    Shuffle the cups all around.
    Then play 'Concentration'.

    Play Concentration thusly, alongside a story game.
    When a player unveils a mini from under a cup, that particular mini-characters' aspects influence the story.
    If the player is able to match the mini with an identical one, the influence is in the player's favor.
    If player fails to match, the influence result is against the player.

    \n\nCall Me Curly \n\n\n

    \n\nMarch 2007 \n edited March 2007
    \n

    \n
    \n Chased across the frontier with pitchforks and torches,
    a settlement of gullible hicks have followed their cult-leader into the desert.

    Their odds of survival seem scant. Buzzards circle over starvation and hopelessness.
    The leader's pronouncements were always meglomaniacal, but recently they've turned incomprehensible and horrific.

    The leader confesses that he was all-along a charlatan, preying on his flock.
    But insists that, nonetheless, he is now truly an instrument of the divine presence: servant of an immense, ancient god
    who resides in the great dead Salt Lake.

    He baptizes a few chosen followers in the murky brine, and empowers them to enforce the will of their diety.

    They emerge as walking (slithering) proof of the leader's supernatural ranting.


    The PCs have become inhuman shape-shifters, and the Dreamblade minis indicate the various incarnations the PCs may take.

    Draw a large relationship map (such as a DitV town), with nodes representing NPCs and paths representing relationships.
    Then place the minis atop the map nodes. (Such as blob-monster atop Farmer Steve).

    To change form, a player must travel on the r-map to the location where a mini has been placed.
    This is done by sending the PC-- in the game fiction-- to encounter the NPC designated by the map node.

    Then the player uses the Dreamblade combat system to battle the Dreamblade mini located at the destination node, using the Dreamblade mini they already inhabit.

    Victory means the PC has prevailed in their conflict with the NPC, and has also taken the new form. Defeat means the NPC prevailed, and the player now plays that character-- as in the new Dreamblade monster form. Win = change of form. Lose = change of POV + change of form.

    Or... something like that.
