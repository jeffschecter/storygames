    \nMouse Guard

    - Comes with sample characters and missions.
    - Rules for creating an adventure in 15 minutes.
    - Character sheet summarizes most of the major rules.
    - You can play in 2 hours.
    - The book is full of beautiful art that can be used to easily sell and inspire new players.
    - Uses D6s.

    PTA

    - Easy concept to understand... let's make a TV show.
    - No GM prep.
    - You can teach the rules in 5 minutes.
    - You can play in 2 hours.
    - Uses a single deck of cards.

    Dogs in the Vineyard

    - You can teach the rules as you play with Initiation scenes.
    - Character sheet summarizes most of the major rules.
    - Easy to sell "teenage paladins with guns, ultimate power, and no life experience".
    - Lots of sample Towns / Adventures online that I can download and use.

    The downside to Dogs in the context of this thread, is some people are irked by the content and it requires a lot of dice.

    Other Games

    Action Castle would work. I've never run InSpectres but I suspect it would make a great go-to game. I'm working on a Shadowrun Hack to act as a go-to game for my old gaming group. When Castle Ravenloft comes out, if it lives up to the hype, it may become my go-to game.

    \n I've got more-or-less three groups I play with, each with a different go-to game.

    Group 1: Apocalypse World. We all really get the way the systems work, and I feel like our play styles mesh really well when we're playing it. I've played other games with members of this group (though never EVERY member, I think) and our Apocalypse World games invariably go better than the others.

    Group 2: Exalted. We don't really engage the system much, and it's been heavily house-ruled, but it's how we met (!) and how we think of fantasy, and there's a lot of history there. Where our visions of the setting depart from the books, they depart in unison, which is big fun. There's so much shared history and vision there that it's just much easier to reach for it than a brand-new game where we all have to reach a consensus again.

    Group 3: It's Complicated. People come and go, and it's more along the lines of people hanging out and itching for a one-shot than a formal weekly group or anything, but It's Complicated is the go-to game. It's easy to explain to new people, you can finish it in two hours, and it supports pretty much any genre. It's good because, for the most part, everyone likes systems which have "just enough" but not too much\u2014 so when Char's structured freeform One-Rule System isn't quite enough crunch, IC is it.

    \n Man, I haven't had a stable enough play group since I moved to Seattle to really have a go-to game. There's so many great people around that I tend to shift between various semi-related, semi-overlapping crowds a bit.

    \n We have tried many times (just my Tuesday group) to land on our Platonic ideal of a "go-to game". In all cases, there was something missing from the secret sauce.

    We tried Reign, because it looks on the surface to be BWish. But the Company rules don't work as we thought they should (under all of it, it's still the GM telling you how many dice to roll), magic is a mess, and the default setting is hard to sell. There's also just the faintest hint of strong social conflict procedures baked into the game, and we greatly prefer to have that tool at our disposal.

    We tried a couple iterations of Fate3 (SotC, Diaspora) but there's something missing there as well. I think we don't feel the Fate economy strongly enough in our play. You can arrive at a BW-like style of play but you have to bring that to the game. In our experience, the system itself doesn't do that.

    Oh man, WFRP3! We really wanted to like this one, mostly because of the money I'd spent on it. But it's pretty rough around the edges. This is its charm for many players I think. There's lots to like about the conflict system, the die are great, but it's a high-handling-time game.

    We've played lots of small single-premise games (Inspectres, Lacuna, 3:16, Dogs, Agon, DRYH) but the problem with those is the single-premiseness of them. They're not robust enough to handle a wide variety of ongoing games. I think we always go into our games with a long campaign in mind, even though we all acknowledge we'll probably only play 6-8 sessions of anything.

    So for us, our go-to game has to:

    * Be familiar to everyone, or at least learnable (BW fails on this count, truth be told -- it's a hard sell to those players who are showing up for the lulz and not To Play The Fucking Game)
    * Not too slow
    * Compact and generic-ish, so as to handle a big variety of play ideas
    * Have social conflict rules

    p.

    \nLOCKED ON TARGET! Have you been hacking into my notes?

    Probably the #1 phenomenon I'm interested in.

    In my specific personal experience my more traditional groups aren't against "indie stuff" but "new stuff".

    They don't want to play D&D in general, they want to play the D&D that gives them the biggest emotional payoff from a nostalgia perspective. That's why so many trad groups I know won't touch D&D 4E.

    Or they don't play a lot and have built up a backlog of games they want to try so anything new would be an impediment.

    Or they don't want to learn a new system because it's perceived to take a long time or

    Or they want to play something they've heard of before.

    Or they don't care about the system but associate specific systems with specific settings so if the setting is wrong, so is the system.

    In some of these groups, Vampire or Mutants and Masterminds might be considered "new stuff" or mistakenly "indie stuff".

    When I want to pitch a game to these groups, if they have a specific game in mind, I'll ask them what they like about that specific game. They might end up selling everyone else on it or we might find the thing they like exists in another game we want to play. Or if they mainly care about the setting, then their favorite book or TV show might be a terrible fit for D&D and if we focus on finding the right game to fit their actual interests... it's not too hard of a sell.

    Ultimately a lot of these groups aren't even playing D&D or Pathfinder in ways I recognize, so even specifying a game isn't enough to understand what's really going on.

    \n So, I posted earlier and said that my group's doesn't really have a go-to game, but mostly just plays those games that we have traditionally played the most. But I just realized that observation it not at all true; in fact, interpreting "story game" broadly, we do have go-to games. The thing is, they aren't roleplaying games (in the traditional sense) at all.

    We we want to play a roleplaying game, there is often excitement for DnD, TSOY, Werewolf, etc., but more often than not rpgs don't even come up. Instead, the first suggestions are inevitably for Bang!, Citadels, or Gloom. Gloom is, imho, one of the best story games there is (when "story-game" is broadly interpreted), and we also play Bang! and Citadels in a narrative fashion, which is to say that we augment the competitive nature of the game with involved fiction. (Everyone narrates, sometimes extensively, on their turn.)

    My initial impulse was that adding on narration to another game doesn't really make it a story game, since (in my personal formulation) the fiction must influence the mechanical elements as well as vice versa. (That's why I don't think chess, for example, is a story game, though you can certainly narrate along with a game.) What I realized after last night, though, is that-- at least with how we play Bang and Gloom, and to a certain extent even with Citadels and Risk-- our fiction actually significantly influences the play of the game. Because of our collective buy-in to the fiction, we began making sub-optimal choices (in the game theory sense)-- that is, the goal of "winning" was to a certain extent redefined and subjugated to the fiction.

    That was a bit of a ramble, but it struck me as significant that our go-to games, even when we want fiction-centric play, tend to be agon-centric games played in an unconventional way (i.e. with multiple goals-- someone wins Citadels by victory points, but some else wins by building a city with only unique buildings, or only military/religious buildings in support of their theme of theocratic autocracy, and so forth). Our go-to games aren't story games from the rpg tradition, but they sure as hell feel a lot like story games when we play them!
