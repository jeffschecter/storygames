All the love on the Car Wars thread inspired me to package up some rules I wrote a couple of years ago: Alpha Destructicus.

This game came about when I demoed Warhammer 40k for John, Phil, and Brandon. Everyone thought the vehicles were the best part of the game, so we put together a vehicle racing and combat game emphasizing fire, explosions, and spectacular crashes. It's kind of DIY, so you'll need to figure out your own minis and terrain.

    \n HA -- I've been saying they'll be going after the text book market since the day Kindle came out.
    \n
    \nThey'll start with College Text Books because people are already used to buying their own text books for that.
    \n
    \nNext it will be Private Schools...cuz if you can afford to put your kid in a private school, you can afford $500 for a reader that will let the school provide all your text books much cheaper. No doubt they'll play up the "better for your kid's health not to lug around 40 lbs of books every day" angle.
    \n
    \nThen on one sweet blissful day, it will be public schools...and at long last it will be possible for the nation's children to learn from texts that aren't already 10 years obsolete.
    \n
    \nDavid, actually the kindle CAN surf the web. Its more primitive than my blackberry at the moment, but I can read this site quite comfortably from my kindle...without needing wi-fi or (currently) incurring other charges. It CAN annotate texts...I don't think (yet) I'd want to do classroom note taking with it...but the potential is there...to put the notes right into the book. And it DOES have MP3 capabilities and audio book capabilities. I don't think mine (gen 1) has a mic, but that would be a trivial addition if there was a demand for digital voice recording for class room use.
    \n
    \nThe biggest weakness, and it doesn't look like they addressed it with this version, is that it doesn't do color. Gen 2 kindles can do much better grey scale than the measly 4 shades mine can do, but it would be hard to do, say, an Art Appreciation text book on a kindle yet. The second biggest weakness is that the software on my kindle lays out the text so that it freely wraps based on whatever font size the reader picks...which makes its layout perfectly fine for continuous text like novels but really wonky for anything that requires layout. For reference I uploaded my 3:16 PDF onto my kindle and, while legible, the layout is all jumbled up. Presumeably they've found a solution to that for text book use, cuz just having a bigger screen won't cut it.
    \n
    \nSorry...I REALLY love my kindle...everyone should have one so that Amazon makes silly amounts of money with it and is thus motivated to develop the technology even further...I want 1 page "flimsies" in my life time.

    \n [3] David, I'm a smoking cessation provider.

    I was too broad in my comment - it's a damn stupid way to stop for those people who, each time they smoke again, resensitize their brains to nicotine. The alcoholic parallel is one I often use. There's no such thing as "one" for such people. You have a cigarette, and then the cigarette wants a cigarette, and two good cigarettes get together and it's cigarettes all round (to paraphrase Terry Pratchett on drinks for alcoholics).

    For the (rare) people who can just stop without help, it's a damn stupid way because they don't actually need to keep having the occasional cigarette. This is about 5% of the population, though.

    For some people it may work, though the general advice in the literature is that "cutting down" is not as effective as setting a quit date and stopping, by whatever method.

    But we digress.

      \n John Wick waxes poetic about his love for indie darlings OctaNe and InSpectres.

      Excerpt:\n
      The Wick
      Jared's keen insight into roleplaying games changed the way I wrote games. He didn't change the way I played or ran games, but he made me realize I could write games the way I ran them.

      See, if truth be told, first edition L5R looked absolutely nothing like the way I ran it. My style of Game Mastering has always been very loose. I encouraged player feedback. I seldom, if ever, used dice. In fact, I only ever used dice if I didn't know what should happen next. I gave players NPCs to run on their own, encouraged them to contribute to the world with suggestions and used narrative techniques that would make most roleplayers wig out. In fact, most did wig out until they figured out what was going on, and then, they jumped on board the train and never looked back.

      Reading Jared's work taught me that I could write a game with the same philosophies I was using to run them. I could not have done this at AEG. In fact, the staff at AEG was moving closer to a more traditional RPG approach while I was moving in a far less conventional one. I was tired of making compromises in the games, tired of putting in rules that would discourage cheating or wankery, tired of forcing cliches into the world to satisfy D&D tropes.

      from today's WSJ:

      "Google Inc. previewed a new communications service that combines features of email, instant-messaging and document-sharing to facilitate multiperson conversations online.

      The Google Wave service caps a years-long project to come up with a way to break down the barriers between different types of online communication services, said Lars Rasmussen, the Google engineer who led the development of the service along with his brother, Jens Rasmussen.

      The service, which won't be open to the public for several months, will allow users to start a conversation, called a "wave," and to invite their contacts to join it. Any member of the wave can put photos, notes or other content into the group, which updates in near-real time.

      People see an "in-box" with the most recent waves they have joined and whether others have added any new notes or content to them. They can click to scroll through the wave and see what content has been added to it.

      ...

      Google, which announced the new service at its developer conference in San Francisco on Thursday, envisions Wave as not only a way to share messages with friends and colleagues, but also to share and access a variety of information online. For instance, a user could create a wave that is an article he or she wrote and then invite people to read and comment on it.

      Mr. Rasmussen said it is too early to tell how consumers will use Wave, but he expects that the various applications and services Google hopes developers will build on top of it will be a big draw. Videogame developers could build a game that began as a wave, for example, and Google will allow other Web sites to embed waves into their Web sites.

      He added that Google hasn't thought much about how the service could generate revenue. "One of the great luxuries of Google is that we get to not think about that yet," he said."
