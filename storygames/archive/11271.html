\n As you probably know, because I talk about it every five seconds, I'm laying out A Taste For Murder. It's a murder mystery game, set in 1930s England.

At the end of the book, I'm going to have recipes. I believe, fairly firmly, that understanding food is a pretty good way of understanding a culture. So, there are recipes for roast beef with yorkshire pudding, shepherd's pie and so forth. There are also instructions for making a cup of tea.

However, I'd like to have recipes that people will actually cook. With that in mind, which recipes would you like to hear about?

Here's a bit of guidance. One thing I could write about is Hot Desserts. These, in the form of a pudding, are a very British thing. There are things like Bread And Butter Pudding, which is the most astonishing dessert: it's a leftovers dish, made from stale bread and butter, milk and sugar. The astonishing thing is that it's delicious.

I could also write about Indian-derived foods. British food used to be surprisingly spicy: we lost much of that, during the war, when rationing came in and making do with poorer food became a virtue. In the 1930s, though, we've got chutneys, kedgeree and lots of British interpretations (or bastardisations) of Indian dishes.

And then there are thrifty dishes. It was traditional to make a joint of meat last three days: it would be roasted for Sunday dinner; made into cold meats for luncheon; then be incorporated into various leftovers dishes. The bones would be boiled to make soup. Actually, I could definitely write about soup.

And there's also a particular spice/herb combination that's distinctively English. I could write about how to use spices and herbs in that distinctive way.

What sort of thing should I write about? What would you cook or like to hear about? Thanks for any ideas you can provide.

Graham

    \n The recipes suggested so far are scrumptious but rather English. Also, they don't strike one as being any good as a murder weapon. Aye, I know Bix Barton was once haunted by a Full English Breakfast in 200AD, but I'm talking about Real Life here.

    Let's hear it for recipes that you can use to kill people. Scotland - like Texas I hear - has a proud tradition of creating food that kills you and tastes awesome. Deep Fried Pizza and Deep Fried Chocolate Bar are two national delicacies. I present below my maw's recipe for pure proper Scottish Corned Beef Stovies. Braw stuff that sticks so hard to your guts that it'll be years before you finally shite out the last of it.\n

    Ingredients:

    \n
      \n
    • 2 large onions
      • \n
    • 3 large potatoes
      • \n
    • 1 small container (a teacup) of roast beef dripping (either your own or bought from your local butcher.)
      • \n
    • 1 tin of corned beef.
      • \n
    \n

    Recipe

    \n
      \n
    • Melt the dripping, enough to cover the bottom of the pot.
      • \n
    • Chop the onions (not too finely) and add to the runny hot dripping in the pot. Be careful not to brown the onions: you just want them to be soft.
      • \n
    • Peel the potatoes and cut them into nice big chunks (1/8ths)
      • \n
    • Rinse the potatoes under cold water - do not dry the potatoes - and add to the soft onions in the pot.
      • \n
    • Stir the mixture together and then cook very slowly with a lid on the pot.
      • \n
    • You will know when the potatoes are ready by testing them with a knife: push your knife into the centre of one of the potatoes. Pull the knife out of the water with the potatoe hanging off the blade and the potato should gently slide off the knife back into the pot if it is cooked.
      • \n
    • Don't worry if the potatoes stick to the bottom of the pot: mine always do. Just don't scrape the pot when stirring otherwise you get not-very-appealing potato-gunk in your stovies.
      • \n
    • When cooked, stir in the corned beef for a minute or two and then serve.
      • \n
    \nCheers
    Pete

    Posted By: NeilIf you're writing about making tea, what's your position on the MIF-TIF divide?
    \nWhat a superb question. Milk in first or tea in first.

    What's fascinating is that this becomes a class issue. You will find people who argue, vehemently, that putting the milk in first is an upper-class habit. Similarly, you will find people that argue that putting the tea in first is a sign of good breeding. There's some justification for the latter: it used to be the the case that only porcelain could take hot tea being poured directly on it. Poorer china would crack, so you put the milk in first, so that the tea was at a lower temperature.

    This is what I like about food discussions. They implicitly reflect economic and class issues. Passing the port is the other classic one: there are lots of rules about how you should pass the port, after a meal. Everyone agrees you should pass the port to the left (that is, to port). However, there are then various traditions: one says the port must never leave the table as it passes around; another says that it should never rest on the table.

    Graham

    Posted By: joepub*Rules about cutlery and table setting? Were those important?
    \nOh, man, I could talk about this all day. Yes, definitely. There were different sorts of cutlery: a fish knife was significantly different from a steak knife; a soup spoon was not a dessert spoon. Using the wrong fork or spoon was an indicator of low class. People would laugh at you behind your back for it.

    The rule was (and is) to work from the outside. Use the cutlery on the outside of your place setting to eat the first course. Then, the table is cleared, use the next set in to eat the next course. This, of course, meant the servants needed to lay places exactly. They had to know what every piece of cutlery was.

    The impetus behind chutneys was more technological. In the 1930s, British country houses still didn't have refrigerators: they had larders, pantries and perhaps ice rooms, instead. So you couldn't keep fruit and vegetables. Instead, you made them into chutneys, pickles and preserves.

    You didn't do it yourself, of course. You got the housekeeper to do it. (Note how the cooking was divided on gender lines. Baking, chutneys and pickles was done by the housekeeper; formal, showy dinners were done by the chef.)

    Chutneys are easy and rather fun to make.

    If you're writing about making tea, what's your position on the MIF-TIF divide?
    \nJust to jump in here (and probably a bit late), but there is a very boring and scientific answer to that question. Cold milk proteins like tannins. In other words, if your tea is strong and very bitter, pouring it into cold milk will reduce the bitterness significantly. The proteins stretch with the heat and entangle the tannins, changing their shape (and flavour). Bitterness goes away, but the strength of the tea flavour persists.

    However, if you put milk into hot tea, the proteins are cooked before they have time to mix with the tannins. Tea remains bitter.
