I've been noticing lately that there are two kinds of rule invocation in roleplaying games - by judgement call or by obligatory procedure.

\n

Act Under Fire in Apocalypse World is a pretty textbook example of a judgement-call rule; its guidelines basically say, "MC, use this when you feel like it." (There are occasional other points in the game that invoke AUF obligatorily, but that is beside the point just now.) There aren't any RPGs I can think of that have zero judgement-call rules, although there are a few that have only a very small amount. There are some very 'light' games that rely mostly on judgement-call rules, and I happen to find that pattern very effective for short-form gaming.

\n

Again using AW as an example, we have the "at the start of session" moves from several of the playbooks. You don't get to make decisions about when these occur - they happen exactly when the circumstances calling for them are met. IME many trad games of the past are written suggesting that all rules are obligatory to invoke, but in play they drift to judgement-call use over time.

\n

What I'm wondering is, how do we use these different sorts of rules invocations most effectively? What circumstances call for what kind of treatment?

    \n Here's how I look at it:

    For any procedure in any game, players need to know when to do it. That "when" comes with a huge spectrum of ambiguity from "totally clear" to "totally ambiguous", as your examples illustrate. In many games, when to use a rule is only ambiguous (i.e., requires a judgment call) due to poor presentation of the procedures. In RPGs, this is often true: perhaps what "roll to attack" really means is "once you get within range, roll to attack".

    Of course, that's not the whole story. Any RPG procedure whose use stems from a particular fictional situation begs the question, "Okay, does right now count as that situation or not?" I'm not talking about "Roll when your Humanity hits 0"; though that represents a particular fictional situation, the rule is invoked by a formal game quantity. I'm talking about procedures you use when your character is outnumbered or your character is agitated, etc. Some such situations manage to avoid judgment calls and ambiguity: either you're outnumbered or you aren't. But other situations are simply ambiguous: how agitated counts as agitated?

    So, if you want to attach rules use to particular fictional states, and any of those particular fictional states might ever be ambiguous, then you can't have a game free of judgment calls on which procedures to use when.

    Generally speaking, not knowing which procedure to use when is a sign of a shitty game. So attaching mechanics to ambiguous imaginings is a risky proposition. Our hobby shows that it can be done! But I'd be inclined to avoid that hurdle if I could. How to avoid it?

    - Ignore ambiguity. Let players resolve ambiguous stuff by just talking. Avoid mechanics until the players get to the next concrete thing that you, the game designer, care about. Provide a framework and logic for play that enables them to in fact get to that concrete thing by just talking.

    - Destroy ambiguity.
    - - put a point value on any and all fictional elements as they're introduced
    - - restrict players from introducing content outside of defined parameters

    - Avoid rules. Provide rules to help players navigate the social dimension of the activity (basically, collectively inventing and communicating something they can all enjoy), then leave the fiction itself alone.

    Other games' goals necessitate ambiguity, and some of them try to bridge the procedural gap with lots of guidance. Perspective, attitude, orientation, rules of thumb, advice, examples -- these are an effort to help the players to quickly determine, "Yeah, this character is worked up enough to impede his performance on fine motor tasks, so we should roll Agitation now."

    I think many good RPGs do a little of all of the above, to minimize the amount of procedural ambiguity in play, without going so far as to make sure it never happens. For example:

    - cull rules that are ambiguous and non-vital
    - list game-mechanical ratings of fictional elements expected to be most important
    - outlaw known problem content (no teleporters!)
    - teach players how to resolve stuff themselves
    - provide principles and media metaphors to guide arbitration

    Whether the end results look like shitty games or not seems to depend on whether the players bring the skills to make the judgment calls easy, and/or the attitudes to make the judgment calls fun.

    \n Ooh, interesting stuff!
    I think another angle to think about is consistency of play experience, and intended style vs. emergent:

    - in a game like Labyrinth Lord, there's a high degree of spontaneity and customization. Turning rulings into rules, swapping ideas for new rules (like classes or custom dungeon details), etc. puts the ruleset in the realm of being more of a toolbox for a variety of play situations, rather something more sharply-focused.

    - in a game like, I dunno, My Life With Master, there are definitely judgment-call rules (when to award the sincerity die, the intimacy die, etc.), but the structure of the game is codified strongly by the various formulae: when stat x is greater than stat y, or when stat z reaches 0, event A happens.

    In LL and games like it, there's a sort of style that comes up where GMs and players are encouraged to adjust, tweak, and change things on the spot, which in turn encourages them to do so on their own and develop a little culture of doing so. It's Metallica mix tapes all over again.

    In MLWM and other games with more explicit structure in play, there's sort of an "Am I getting this right?" feeling that can develop in play - I consider this potentially anxious-making scenario the price of unlocking higher levels of play-experience consistency.

    At the same time, it's not at all difficult to rely upon judgment-call rules (if applied in narrow circumstances) to create a particular play experience, or aspect of the experience. In Kagematsu, for example, a great deal of the game involves the Kagematsu player deciding whether a given act by a Woman player inspires love or pity. Given that, tactically, the Kagematsu player is best served (I think) by handing out lots of love instead of pity, it creates an interesting element of trust: will Kage-san uphold consistent standards of feminine propriety, or will he be understanding, tolerant, and gentle, his heart open to all?

    \nI guess one reason is that the "trigger" for using the rules is pretty ill-defined; what does "uncertain outcome" mean for a social situation, anyway? And if it's unclear, there's greater incentive for the gaming group to ditch rules in favor of roleplay. (Which isn't necessarily a bad thing, assuming no one in the group invested heavily in social skills under the impression they would be useful.)

    At the same time, there are also substantive characteristics of the rules themselves to consider, as in when are players likely to submit to the rules? Elements like tactical choices, a focus on creating an interesting process of conflict, and emphasis on making the outcome interesting win or lose, or even redefining win and lose altogether, make it more likely for players to subject their player characters to the rules. While these issues aren't strictly about when to use the rules, they do have a lot to do with whether the players are like to use the rules, which is a related issue.

    \n Rules that aren't fun or that are too complex seem to be the most likely to be ignored. Of course, there's always the tendency for players with very firm preferences or expectations to decide to edit a game on the fly, so to speak.

    In the case of social interaction rules, the specific history of D&D has been such that they really did feel tacked on in 3rd edition. More precisely, they were something very new, at least in them being so explicit.
    NPC-reaction rolls and the like have certainly covered that kind of terrain for D&D, in previous editions, but actually making, say, Intimidation rolls was definitely a new approach (at least for D&D).
    It doesn't surprise me that Pathfinder players would ignore or gloss over those rules, given the historical precedent of doing so.

    From a design perspective, there's not much you can do about that - you can't control the degree to which people follow the system as-written/as-they-understand-it; all you can do is be very explicit in your goals on the level of something like GM principles or an MC Agenda.
    When you do that, you can make the case for playing the game in a certain way, or from a certain viewpoint (such as "Don't plan the outcome in advance!").

    \n The only game I know of that formalize precisely how and when the rules occur is Apocalypse World : rules are defined by Moves (eg. Seduce or Manipulate Someone), which come in play when a) the player explicitely announce their character is doing such a move, provided they also describe what they're doing ("I try to manipulate him" "Cool, what do you do?") or b) the character does something that's described as a Move ("I tell him \u201cI'm pretty sure a big guy like you isn't affraid of a small fry like him. Why don't you go deck him?\u201c \u201cCool, you're trying to manipulate him?").
    \n
    \nThat's the only moment you roll the dice in AW.
    \n
    \nMechanically, Moves define moments in the shared narrative when the outcome isn't automatic. You roll the dice to see how much things go in your character's way. Moves are defined like this:
    \n
    \nWhen you try to seduce or manipulate someone, roll+hot. On a hit, they ask you to promise something first, and they do it if you promise. On a 10+, whether you keep your pthey need some concrete assurance right now. concrete assurance right now.
    \n
    \nMoves make some things interesting by providing suspense to the situation: the outcome isn't sure.
    \n
    \nMoves conditions and results are pretty concrete. They are explicit things, actions that you can see from the outside. It's never "when you fall in love" or "when you feel moody", but things like "you try to take something by force" or "read a charged situation". Also, Moves are for the players. As a player, it's up to you to push the narrative in a precise situation if you want to make a move.
    \n
    \nThat tends to reduce judgement calls to a minimum.

    \n Ultimately, any game that is truly an RPG and not a boardgame is going to have to have some human judgment calls in it somewhere. It's a G\xf6delian problem\u2014even in a game with really clear guidelines for judging when the right thing has happened in the fiction to trigger a certain mechanical event, someone, somehow has to judge when that's happened. And if the rules tried to go a step farther, like "Only if everyone at the table agrees that the move happened in-fiction," then we'd still run into problems, like "what does it mean to really agree? What if one person is still sort of uncomfortable agreeing that the move happened, but is going along with it for the sake of group cohesion, but we can all tell that's what's going on?

    It's judgment calls all the way down, I'm afraid.

    \n I completely agree: winning a typical RPG is like winning a party. There are definitely things you want to achieve, but you can't actually "win".     Maybe one or two or a thousand games arise in the course of a given RPG session! It's all up to the play group, and the way they relate to the play tools they have.

    So, from that perspective, we should probably assume that the players are quite capable of enjoying free play, and that the rules should serve to support free play, rather than to fight or supplant it. Rules that inspire, for example. Or rules that organize who gets to do what. Or rules that can be fallen back on as crutches when free play gets stuck.

    Hey, I just described trad "games"!

    Take White Wolf games. You use the char-gen etc. rules to get inspired. You divide up into Storyteller and character-players to structure a unique social interaction. Then you free play, and roll a shitload of d10s when free play is insufficient to quickly reach mutually acceptable outcomes. My preference has always been, if you can reach good outcomes without dice, don't roll 'em.

    If WW phrased their games this way, maybe they wouldn't get so much hate from the indie crowd. They're perfectly fine as inspiration, organization, and crutches for free play. It needs to be only roughly set out in its supposed physical relationship. Interior sets and exterior sets don't need to be in proper consistent scale to one another, and they only need to be representative of the stuff that is likely to be relevent to the film.

    There should also be some space available for re-arranging as play develops.

    One table edge or all edges can be considered to be Casting/Props room areas. That's where extra stuff can be stashed, whether it's toys that might be sued or dice or extra index cards or whatever. Things in those areas are just there to be convenient and aailable. They don't represent stuff physically there the way tey might in a wargame.

    Optimally, there should also be a lounge area where food, conversation, and so on is encouraged and people can take a breather from the game activity. It could be for extra people not directly playing or for the presumed/scheduled break periods.

    Time outline:
    As a conceit, the production crew ( that is, all of the players) have a total of 6 Weeks ( six real time hours) to shoot the project from start to finish.

    As a further conceit, there is only enough film for 4 hours of filming ( although naturally that will be cut down in editing to make a proper film for release). Again this is 4 hours of real time.

    This is a working concept. The idea really about keeping things relatively focussed for a one-day event, like a house party game, so maybe something that starts at 1pm on a Satruday and ends by 7pm ish. It could go longer or shorter and I'm playing with ideas for that related to the type of film ( A pilot for a series on TV vs. a Feature Film vs. A Saturday Morning Serial movie episode, for example).

    Real world time:
    The folks show up more or less randomly as they tend to and you let 'em look at all the cool stuff and talk freely about concepts while settling in. Catch up for a while, have drink, and slowly ease into general socializing. This is pre-production and doesn't count against time limits. People can start brainstorming a bit too, and write ideas about characters on cards/board and post them or whatever you have. Make sure they initial anything they write down. Don't take too long though.

    Official start of brainstorming
    Start on an easily rememberable time mark (write it down) and begin the official brainstorming session. This is just semi directed bull session related to the film project: Some character development concepts, ideas about sets, placing toys around the backlot and so on. Take maybe 15 minutes. Remeber to write ideas down and put initials by them.

    When it looks like something has cooked pretty well, go on to the next production step: Casting Call

    Everybody who is playing tries out for parts. You should have some characters by now, certainly your lead characters, heroes villains, rivals, etc. Everyone should "auditon" for one character they'd like to play. Take turns however seems best. The person auditioning should give a sentence or two about how they imagine the character. Then, they should give a short example of how they'd play them. Other players can call out suggestions or ask questions or set up situations. Other participants can be called on if need be to step in as another already reated character or one made up on the fly. Auditions should only be maybe a minute or two long. Players should ham it up a bit, using voices, body language, speaking patterns, whatever.

    It's not only perfectly okay if multiple people audition for a given character, it's encouraged. Just make sure that all characters already created have been covered.

    Second round of auditions: Players now choose a different player and a different character than the one they originally auditioned for and have them try the role out. Otherwise, as before. Encourage players to pick some less developed characters to hae their pals audition for. Choosing wildly inappropriate seeming player to character comboes for this round should be incredibly enlightening and thus encouraged.

    This might take up to 30 minutes. Don't go too much longer!!!No one inherently gets a role from the audition. The goal is to see what the group collectively is thinking and gets some further character development, as well as get some audience feedback from the group on who does a good/interesting interpretation/portrayal of the characters the project is working with.

    Wrap this part up and write down the time used and announce it. This comes out of the total Production Time ( the 6 hours).

    Take about a five minute breather for Real World socializing and get ready for the initial scene shooting. This doesn't count against Production Time. Let people schmooze and grab drinks, but also encourage further brainstorming and chatting about plot and characters.
