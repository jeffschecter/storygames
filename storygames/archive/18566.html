\n Hi,

I am currently DMing a Donjon game, though this question came up for me in other games too. How do y'all deal with players trying to cover up their tracks, essentially trying conceal an action from NPCs or the world at large in order to avoid potential negative consequences?

Example: During our last session, the players killed an assassin who worked for a powerful family. This took place at night in the city where the said powerful family operates. After the deed, one of the players stated a fact that "It is very dark." and all the players wore hoods. Given these facts, I feel like to preserve player agency (is there a better term?) I cannot treat the murder as having been witnessed by anyone, so the players essentially suffer no consequences because they were clearly trying to cover their tracks. However, this seems like it precludes interesting things from happening, for example the said powerful family (Donati) finding out that their favorite assassin was killed by the players and deciding to make an example of what happens to people who interfere with their business.

Is can perhaps see this being a good opportunity for some sort of an ability test. In Donjon, let's say the player's Cerebrality + pertinent hiding Ability vs. an Easy (or even Very Easy) difficulty to not be recognized at night while wearing a hood. If they fail, I get to state a fact "Someone recognizes you." However, in the very likely chance that they all succeed, that still means a whole branch of potentially exciting play (dealing with whoever the Donati send after the players, perhaps trying to negotiate with the Donati to end hostilities, etc.) is crossed out based on one roll. On the other hand, disregarding their obvious choice not to have such consequences feels like Railroading. Or is that just an instance of overly-cautious players who have not yet realized that more drama and more danger mean more fun for everybody, and I should not be a party to their turtling?

    \n I can see some possible consequences even if the players weren't recognized. A witness could still have seen a group of figures in hoods (any other distinguishing features? Is one of the characters tall, short, or carry a polearm?). The Donatis put pressure on the city watch, and now the city is on high alert, the watch is questioning everyone (especially strangers and travelers) about their wherabouts that night. Maybe the roads and city gates are even closed. The patrols double, and the city is now better lit at night.

    I don't know what kind of magic is available in Donjon, or common in your setting, but the the assassin's ghost could finger the PCs as his murderer. Could the Donatis potentially hire a spirit medium or diviner, or even a non-magical tracker or detective to find the killers? You don't even have to have this detective actually find the PCs, just have them hear that this awesome detective is coming to the city to solve the crime, and let the players react.

    Maybe someone else gets blamed for the murder. Will the PCs care if an innocent man is going to hang for their crime?

    If the assassin was a valuable asset to the Donati family, then rival families might make their move now that the assassin is dead. The balance of power in the city could be disrupted.

    Most of these ideas have to do with consequences to the world around the characters, rather than consequences to them directly. This way, they still got away with murder (preserving player agency, since that's what they wanted), but their action has ripple effects on the story.

    \n So many great suggestions. And if you really want to put PCs in a spot, consider making them aware of some other evidence they could have plausibly left behind \u2013\xa0something they dropped, or blood spilled from them (if they were harmed at all in combat). Maybe this game doesn't have DNA evidence, but is there magic that allows you to track them with a bit of their blood? This (or even the rumor that this is possible, concocted by the authorities to draw out the murderers) may encourage the PCs to try to tamper with the evidence.

    I had a group of PCs in very similar circumstances in a game of In Nomine (caught on security video using supernatural powers to burn a man to death who turned out to be a human cop, not the demon they suspected). It nearly kicked off a street war between angels and demons, and the PCs had the new goal of trying to find and destroy the video evidence (which they failed at). In the end, the only way the PCs could use diplomacy to prevent a battle with scores of bystander casualties was to turn themselves in to appease the other side. The tensest scene in any game I've ever run involved a room full of immortals realizing they were about to accept life imprisonment just to "do the right thing." (They got off easy in that case, with an ally providing non-sentient body doubles to take their places, but I could see a "prison break" storyline being pretty cool too.)

    \n Lots of great advice here. But don't forget the other side, too:

    "Wow, that was clever. Well done, you've gotten out of a near-impossible situation. What's your next goal? What do you do now?"

    Maybe the assassin was supposed to kill someone else soon... someone who really would be great (from the players' perspective) to get out of the picture, but only this assassin had the means to do it.

    So, maybe a tangential question: Where do you draw the line between "let them have their victory" and "show them the consequences of their actions"?
    \nI try to ask myself a few questions whenever PCs do something major and "get away with it":

    - What do various NPCs think about what happened? Or if they don't know what happened, what changes would they notice in their situation, and how would they respond?
    - What plans/relationships/delicate balances were disrupted when this happened? Will anyone try to fix things or take advantage of the situation?
    - What, if anything, will make it hard for PCs to keep their role in this a secret? What opportunities can I give the players to show how their PCs are feeling about this?
    - What reactions (from the NPCs, the PCs, and/or the world) are going to give the PCs something interesting to interact with? (Preferably, something that they could react to in so many different ways that I will be delightfully surprised by what they do, because that is one of the greatest things about GMing a game.)

    Generally that serves me pretty well; it's rare that I can't find something good to work with just with the materials on hand in the game setting, so I'm satisfied to draw my line there and steer well clear of more arbitrary-seeming comeuppances or cheap shots.

    \nThat sounds about right to me (although you lose me with the storytellers/power gamers bit). No matter what, your job as the GM is to show interesting, impactful, exciting consequences to their actions. You decide whether that means they get into deep trouble (didn't think of how this would affect the ________ of the city), or whether it means the whole landscape of play changes, instead. It can be about new threats or it can be about new opportunities, instead.

    The important part is that they've made a plan and carried it through: now the ball's in your court to show how things have changed as a result. The worst answer is that it hasn't. Show how their actions have changed *everything*, whether for better or for worse. What are the ramifications? How are other people affected? How does it impact their business, or their families, or local politics? That's what the players will want to see: how has what they've done changed the world?

    \n That's right. The consequences are what they're interested in: that's up to you to provide. But they can be negative (blowback, collateral damage, getting into more trouble) or positive (the world has changed, new opportunities exist, someone who needed help benefits from the change). I don't think there's a rule for which you should pick at any given time, but a good game should have a balance of both, I think.

    As for the whole "power gamer" thing, I follow what you're saying, but I'm not sure how it should impact your own decision-making in this case. It's your job to create interesting but believable consequences, either way. Of course, it's great when the players help you out like that! (A good middle ground is to lead them there, by the way: tell them that a problem exists, but ask them to help define it. For instance, you might ask: "So, who do you think is most pissed off by what you did?" Most players won't hesitate to say, "Oh, man, the mayor will probably be enraged when he finds out about this! What are we going to do?" And they'll usually come up with something you didn't think of, so it's great material for the GM.)
