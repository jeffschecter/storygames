\n On Saturday I played a game of Shock with Jesse and Laura. Neither Jesse or Laura had played or read the rules before this weekend, but both were familiar with the concept and how good scifi works. I explain the rules as we go and I throw out my one request concerning the shock. I don't like the shock to be any sort of immortality or extreme longevity. Those are not bad shocks, but they come up a lot and I've done them enough for my taste.

The shock we settled on was robots, and we make the Laura the owner. We each come up with a minutiae to flesh out the shock some and chose robots have free will, it's easy to tell robots from people, and robots are humanoid. Next we moved on to issues. Jesse picks relationships, Laura chooses equal rights, and I decide on the decay of society. We then decided on some minutiae concerning our issues to bring them to life. Some notable examples were robots have human emotions, robot brothels, and class society. The we had upper and lower cities with the wealthy living high above the pollution and the poor and those displaced by robots live in squalor on the ground level.

We moved onto protagonist creation. All of us chose to make protagonists in the issue we chose. Jesse was a Hugh Hefner type, except with robot playmates. I can't remember his story goal exactly, but it involved being accepted. Laura was a robot fighting to have robots accepted as people in here local city. I played a U.S. senator who was trying to get a constitutional amendment through congress and to the states for ratification that would only allow robots to be use for dangerous jobs and jobs humans could not do.

Jesse's antagonist was a former "employee" of his, now, all robot operated brothel. Laura's was actually my protagonist, who mostly interacted with her character through an aid. My antagonist was a wealthy robot industrialist.

Our story had people operating on very high level, a local one and a deeply personal level all tied together through links and antagonists. In the end robots were recognized as people in Laura's city, the amendment limiting the role of robots was passed along with one creating a third robotic house of congress, and Jesse's character's wife left him and he hired back his former employee.

While the story had a fairly grim setting, the tale didn't seem too dark. I think our choice of praxis had a lot to do with this. We had manipulation/honesty and anger/love.

I think the game went well. Jesse and Laura both enjoyed it, but Jesse commented that we created a lot for what was a fairly short story, so we decided to play another session next week.

    \n There were two things that I found really interesting. First of all, when I chose "relationships" relative to robots I originally had in mind a character who had completely isolated himself from people. Why deal with people and all their complications when I can custom build my entire social circle? But I went the total opposite direction. I want with a very human oriented person who saw robots as an extension of the human experience rather than a replacement. I liked that. I think it was your minutia contribution of "Robot Brothels" that sort of pushed me over the edge. Oh, and my Story Goal was, "to not be seen as an exploiter."

    The second thing is that I now thing Shock is extraordinarily sneaky. Probably the most significant thing I didn't know about the game was that it pretty much forces you to tell short stories. At first I thought the large amount of material generated at the start of play was a problem relative to shortness of the game. Then I thought about it some more. Then I realized it's doing it's job. It makes me want to play again.

    I read the rules and paid particular attention to how you continue the game. It says you make up new issues for sure and you can add another Shock if the generated fiction warrants one being created. I realized that Shock does a very, very sneaky thing that I think will only become apparent over multiple sessions of play.

    Growing up I never much cared for science-fiction and the reason for that was it always seemed so... sterile. We have these characters but these characters aren't really all that interesting because they're eclipsed by these big ideas. The characters are there mostly to illustrate of explore these big ideas. That why I stuck with mostly horror stories growing up and why I was so excited when I discovered the Swords & Sorcery stuff Ron presents in Sorcerer & Sword. It's so much more personal and inward focused.

    Shock forces you into this short story mode in the face of a lot of big ideas specifically to keep the game focused on the ideas and not too much on the turmoil of the protagonists. The stories are about the protagonists but the protagonists are always subordinate to the ideas. They're short burning candles existing long enough to shed illumination on some points before blowing out and we have to get new candles. I like it for all the reasons I hated it as a kid and I want to play with it a little more.

    Jesse

    \n Yes, the book says that you do issues first and I agree with Joshua's assessment that it's over compensating. When you have people playing who are committed to making good science-fiction they will naturally extract issues from the shock presented. In fact, that feels more natural to me. The other method feels more like a training exercise for people who are used to using Shocks as cool color on an otherwise bog standard action flick.

    To answer Joshua's question about robo-bunnies and free will. My thinking on the issue was similar to ideas behind psychoactive drugs. A person suffering from major depression, still has free will but they're disinclined to make certain decisions because of how their brain chemicals make them feel. The robo-bunnies work the same way. They're factory made to feel "frisky" but they still have free will within that emotional state of being.

    In fact, that was that the point of change for my character. In the end I sent the robot bunnies back to have their emotions "normalized" because, "It's no different than when we used to give the girls drugs in the old days."

    \n I think requiring issues to be contemporary / real-world is a bad test -- it cuts off lots of ideas. A game can be set on an alien world in the far future without that being any kind of Shock. Slavery is a great issue even though we don't have a lot of slavery in our society.

    It's not common but I could see cases where an idea could be an issue in one story but a shock in another.

    Being an issue or being a shock says more about where the idea fits in the story. If the shock is environmental collapse, that means we aren't really concerned about environmental collapse, it's just the thing that impacted the issues we are concerned with (can I still be Altruistic once all the food is gone). And vice versa: if the environmental collapse is an issue it's the thing we are addressing via the shock, which could be something as simple as space travel (do we leave this dying earth behind).
