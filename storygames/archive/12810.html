    \n I'll take a slightly less dismissive crack at this...

    You're not writing and you're not reading. You're not the author. You are a participant, one among many, and that's pretty cool! But if you come to the table with your writer skills and mindset, you are likely to irritate people who came to improvise a story, as you recognize. Improvisation is a separate skill and a different process. I'm going to go ahead and break the glass and get out the awful identity politics words: What you're asking, I think, is "My friends dislike it when I Story Before. How do I Story Now?"

    If I'm right, well, maybe you would benefit from not making your own character? Or from playing a game with a very collaborative chargen process? Might be a useful exercise. Maybe you should GM a game that shackles you pretty tightly, something by Vincent, or perhaps InSpectres? Or something that rules out prep altogether, like Penny or Fiasco (Fiasco hint - don't decorate any of the cards adjacent to you, only the ones across the table). Or try a game that celebrates characters' disposability and incompetence, like Paranoia or Call of Cthulhu. One way or another, if you want to learn improvisation, you need to go in raw and get out of your comfort zone from the beginning - and if your friends tell you that you're doing that thing that irritates them, take it in a constructive and fun spirit and resolve to do something different.

    Or play with people who think your style is more fun, of course. Or meet your friends partway. Lots of options. Fun!

    Posted By: masqueradeballbut when I talk about where I want things to go with my character in the game, its made clear (in subtle ways, mostly just by being ignored) that those kind of interests/expectations are ignored.
    \nYeah, if you can't get anyone else to buy into your idea, you're basically shit out of luck. In a game with a GM, you really need GM buy-in at a minimum, although another player can absolutely help with that; in a GM-less game, you need at least one other player. Otherwise the best you'll ever manage is an occasional monologue...and if you're at all aware of the other people at the table, you'll know what a terrible idea that is.

    So anyway, the first rule of buy-in is know your audience. If the GM is dead set against incorporating your ideas for your character's arc, then either there's no interest in "pre-planning" a character arc, or your ideas of what makes a character arc interesting...aren't.

    So try changing things up. If you've been hammering on specific beats you'd like to see ("...and then he would confront his old master!"), try making it more general and more attitude-focused ("...so the more good deeds the party accomplishes, the more my character will realize that he needs to let go of his past, and he'll stop being angry and taciturn and start enjoying himself more!"). Or vice versa. Try just putting out an ending point, with no other details. Try not mentioning an end point at all, and just focus your attention on what can be done in the current session ("My character is getting more desperate; after our failure at the mill, he's convinced that if we don't stop this guy NOW we'll never stop him at all...and his reckless streak is really starting to come out.") If you've been doing one particular type of character, try a different one. Try keeping your idea a secret, but design your character to make the arc you want to create almost inevitable (a character destined to turn evil just happens to have features that really benefit an evil character, etc.). Basically, keep throwing new ideas out until one sticks and you finally start getting a feel for what kinds of arcs would actually get traction with these people. It would be weird if ALL your ideas sucked, right?

    Also, try talking about it with the GM outside the game sometime, just when you're hanging out doing something else. As a GM, I always found it easier to work in player suggestions and desires when I had some prep time to think about them; if you dumped your idea on me five minutes before we started playing, I probably wasn't going to do much with it...but if you'd mentioned it a few days earlier, I'd have probably come up with something interesting for it by the weekend.

    Worst case scenario: you're right, and you'll never get what you like gaming with these people. Bummer. It happens, but that never seems to make it any easier.
    \n
    Posted By: masqueradeballI wanted to say... how do people use or not use story arcs when creating characters, Everything else was pretty much a "this is why I ask."
    \nSometimes I do, but I generally keep it very vague and focused on who the character IS (personality, beliefs, goals, etc.) rather than on what the character DOES (actions, relationships, milestone events). I generally throw that shit in during character creation -- "I'm going to play a grizzled, alcoholic PI who starts off with a fear of responsibility...but as the game goes on, he'll have to start taking on responsibilities, and form real relationships with people, and generally become a better person." I'll also stack the actual character sheet to make that happen -- the PI in that example actually started off with good leadership skills and empathy, because the whole idea was that the only thing keeping him from being a better person was his fear of making real connections with the world around him. If he sucked at that stuff, there wouldn't be much incentive for him to step up and take responsibility, right? But the way I played it during the game, he was just discovering that he could handle things if he made the effort.

    I do a lot of reinforcement and refinement of "big picture" ideas like that outside game sessions, too...but our gaming group is made up of friends who hang out and do non-gaming things together all the time, which makes it easy to have conversations about the game: how everyone thinks it's going, what we like about our characters, what we hope to do next, and so on. We get psyched about what other people are doing with their characters, and we conspire to set up situations in-game that will let people go after what they said they wanted to do when we were watching TV two nights earlier; the character who wanted a romance will find other PCs are pushing eligible NPCs in his direction, or the barbarian struggling with her bloodthirsty nature will find herself shoved into the spotlight when the party has to decide what to do with the surrendering bandits.

    \n Hello Masquerade Ball.

    You are going to need to find a new group to play with. That may not be easy, it might take a while, but ultimately, it doesn't sound like your current gaming experience is satisfying to you.

    Also, I suggest you drop BESM. I suppose you CAN have character arcs with it, but there is nothing in the design of it to suggest so. If you play games with the idea of character arcs written in the design, it'll be easier for you to make it happen with your group. (Not with your CURRENT group, I think, don't try to stealth Story Now into your group, it won't work.

    Having an end to your arc in mind isn't so bad as you'd think, as long as you are ready to change your mind forty times in play. Those games go in all directions at once, players each have much more impact to push the story in the direction they want. The plot is often at their mercy. If you have a character arc in mind, it often includes some kind of events that may not occur. What then?

    Say you play a war vet with post-traumatic shock disorder. Your first thought is to play through an arc in which he gets better. That's fine. Your advocating for your character, that's cool. But he might not. He might stay the same. He might get worse. He might turn to drugs or become homeless. He might hurt someone he loves. He might get better just long enough to save everyone and then die in the process. Or he might get better and live forever after.

    But don't choose before you play. Play the PTSD to the hilt, react to the situation in the way that makes most sense to you or that seems most interesting. Let the conclusion write itself naturally.

    Now I have some game suggestions :

    In Hero's Banner, you'll play a young noble who must pick his life. They are torn between three influences (hero, blood, conscience), each exemplified by three different ideals, and three different goals. You should strive to make the three goals equally interesting to you, and the GM, if she does her job, will try to make the choice as difficult as possible in play. At the end of your session, you can only achieve one of those goals, the other two are lost. You then epilogue your character, describe how that choice affected his life, and then you describe his death.

    In Primetime Adventure, each character has an issue (grief, romantic troubles, teptation, etc.) and a Screen Rating that varies from episode to episode. You get one Spotlight Episode, in which is going to beabout your character and his issue. You can resolve his issue, or not. But this is the only place where it can be resolved in-game.

    Don't Rest Your Head : You get asked a few questions as part of character creation. One of them is "what just happened to you?" You get to pick your own story hook. "My baby brother was kidnapped by goblins and its my fault. I need to find him before mummy and daddy return from the movies." "I was reading some boring old book while on acid and I saw a white rabbit with spectacles and a pocket watch running through a hole, and I followed it. I could have sworn it talked, too. It said "you're late! you're late!"

    Then you get asked another question : "what is your path?" What are your character's goals, both short and long term? "I want to find my baby brother, but I wish my parents would pay a bit more attention to me. I also hope to be a better big sister in the future." "I wish to find that white rabbit, and figure what he meant; also, I'd like to find the strength to kick off drugs."

    (I don't think my examples are very good. Aw well.)

    Another game you could check out is The Shadow of Yesterday, there is a free wiki out there. Check out keys. You choose how you character is rewarded. Say your character meets a villain, you can say she falls in love with him, take the key of unrequited love. Whenever this love is displayed in the story, you gain xp. The more trouble it brings you, generally, the more xp. If the love is returned, or if you opt to abandon your love, you can also decide to buyoff that key forever, for a whopping 10 xp (yes, 10 xp is a big deal). You could also buy a new key. In fact, you may have as many as 5 keys in play at the same time.

    Also, in the same game, check out transcendance. You have five ability level. Unskilled 0, Competent 1, Adept 2, Master 3, Grand Master 4. A Grandmaster can never fail. Also, a perfect success at Grandmaster level translates into Transcandance. Your character effects some great change to the campaign setting, and then exits play forever, somehow. Could be death, could be ascending into godhood (also often known as death) could be something else entirely. You aren't ready to transcend? Don't use an ability at grandmaster level! Indeed, just don't ever advance to that level until you are becoming ready.

The Rustbelt is especially innovative in that it pays special attention to the character's internal makeup and how it changes to it due to outside events. Not as mandated effects as in MLWM (e.g. Self-Loathing goes up when you do X and Y), but as a set of choices triggered by certain events (e.g. when you act counter to your Faith, either feel bad about it and take on Woe, or lose the Faith, or feel good about it and take on Vice & lose the Faith).     But to look at the issue pragmatically: defining a character's arc means defining the Conflict that they face, how they deal with that conflict, and how it resolves. Somebody has to have authority over these different things. Call of Cthulhu works because it punts all of those straight to the game itself: it's expected that you'll make characters in X range that handle problems in Y ways, and they'll face the Outer Dark and lose their minds over it eventually. Your typical railroading GM also handles all of those things in getting his story to work (but, hey, it's only railroading if the players resent it). In The Rustbelt (and Sorcerer, Trollbabe, and Dogs) you only define how you deal with the conflict (you might have a bit of input on the conflict itself, but that's primarily the GM's territory), and then how it resolves is filtered heavily through system techniques that cause unanticipated outcomes and prompt decision points that nobody could have seen coming.

    Posted By: DeliveratorWhen you don't have mechanics to resort to, guess what, you don't get the kind of play you describe yourself as wanting here. Play games that support this desire of yours.
    \nI have to agree, with the caveat that social mechanics count just as much as game mechanics in this case. A group of people who want the kind of play you describe can (and will!) make it happen, even if the game they're playing has no specific rules to address it. Likewise, a group of people who explicitly do not want that kind of play will find ways to avoid or minimize it, even if there are plenty of important rules involving it.

    So switching game systems isn't necessarily a magic bullet; if they genuinely do not want what you want, you're still going to be left looking for another group more in tune with your tastes. Still, trying out a game with rules that support your chosen playstyle makes it much, much easier to tell whether your current group is open to that playstyle, so it's worth a shot. And if they try it and like it, you're not going to be locked into playing only games with rules to support that style, because you guys can fill in the gaps yourselves.
