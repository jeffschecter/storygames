    Really? I... I think it's a pretty poor argument against CC. There is no CC license that removes an author's rights, period, end of story. All CC licenses simply share some of the author's rights with the audience. Authors who tend to use CC are also likely to be highly interested and negotiable about sharing or licensing rights--at least as much so as authors who use full copyright.     First, a mini-rant, conveniently quoted so people can easily skip past it.
    Okay, pet peeve time. Brendan's comments speak of "copyright" as if it were something opposed to CC, GPL, etc... but in fact, copyright is what enables those to exist. Without copyright, your only choices would be to let anyone copy your work, or keep it secret, showing it only to those who you can trust not to copy it.

    Copyright simply states that a creator has the right to say who can make copies of his/her work, and the conditions under which they can do so. That's it. Without that, no licensing scheme can exist, because there is no right to state conditions in order to copy.

    If you want to oppose something to CC, GPL, etc., the thing to oppose to them is "All Rights Reserved."

    I can see Ben's point in the artistic and game design arenas -- as he points out, copyright does not prevent copying the processes, mechanics, and tone of a game, only copying the text itself; and it does not prevent taking inspiration from someone else's work.

    The GPL was designed for a different situation -- namely, computer programs, where rewriting someone else's code to make yours "original" can be difficult and time-consuming, since computer code implements a process, and a "paraphrase" of the code will not implement the exact same process. Thus, in that case, you're stuck with searching for another process that has the same result, which makes copying the idea from computer code without copying the code more akin to working around a patent.

    September 2006

    Huh. A lot of the reactions here seem to be "nu-uh" so that's a little strange. I'm trying to decide if it's a failure on my behalf to write my points cogently or just running up against a lifetime of lies and falsehoods about copyrights. So I'm just going to re-iterate some points, hoping that a new phrasing and some clear examples will make what I'm talking about more apparent.

    I'm aware that some people here are very big proponents of virally restrictive liscenses, Creative Commons share-and-share-alike especially, but also GPL, OGL, and whatever else. Please be aware that I'm going to have some very vitriolic things to say about these licenses, because I think that anything that restricts artistic freedom, especially so violently, is morally wrong. Uhm... I hope we can handle the conversation civilly, without getting ourselves attached to our beliefs. I do reserve the right to withdraw from the conversation if I get dogpiled or overwhelmed.

    I'm also going to talk about law a bunch. I know a lot about copyright law, but I'm not a lawyer, and if you go to court based on my statements without talking to an IP lawyer that's on your head.

    Okay, so I think that there's three points here.

    1) The big lie. Copyright law is not nearly as wide-reaching as you've been lead to believe. The law as written reserves broad privileges for parody, free use, inspiration, and appropriation of creative content. Not only that, it's getting better -- there are some orphan works bills right now which will make a great deal of things legal which really ought to be.

    (This whole bit is not to talk about the length of copyrights, which have been getting longer and longer. But the length of copyright is a topic full of right and wrong and emotions and I don't want to get into it right now, because it will certainly derail the thread.)

    Now, there is a problem with the law in practice, because of the cost of civil law in the US, but that's a problem with the entireity of US civil law. Essentially, if you can bluff the other side into think that they're in violation of the law, you can enforce your own law as such, because no one wants to get tangled up in the courts (this is how the RIAA works). To that end, you have been the victim of a massive dis-information campaign to make you believe that such reasonable things as taking inspiration from a previous work, using small snippets of text or sound, or making copies of tapes for friends are illegal.

    Creative Commons, in both development and in practice, plays directly into these myths. For most uses that CC partisans champion, ordinary fair use is more than sufficient.

    As an example of this locally: The Story Games Names Project is a set of lists of names under a creative commons license. Why? "so that people can use it." Well, as it turns out, names are public domain (a fact which helped the development of the book). So there is no restriction to the use of the contents of the book, by anyone, at all, for any purpose that isn't in itself illegal. But the license is trying to claim that we don't have ownership over these names -- that we're required to put a little CC stamp and comply in other ways to use them.

    (Yes, in theory, you can apply a copyright to the arrangement of the names. But that's not what the presence of a CC license implies now, does it? I mean, how many people are going to use the exact layout?)

    (I'm not dissing the excellent project. It was a really cool project and I'm glad it was done. I'm talking specifically about the fact that it is placed under a legal license that it has a shaky legal claim to and no ethical claim to. No one owns names.)

    Here's a second example. I remember back in the day when Clinton was stumping for CC, and said "Hey, Vincent, you should put your old unfinished games into CC (otherkind was the one, I think), so someone else can finish them."

    There are two key errors here:

    The core content (the mechanics and processes) of Otherkind fall under copyright at all. They don't.

    That a potential re-writer would need to fear not getting Vincent's permission. He wouldn't. (As a personal example: I appropriate big chunks of otherkind. After a few months, I said "Oh, Vincent, I use the otherkind dice for Bliss Stage." And he's like "Sweet! How?")

    continued.

  • Ben_Lehman
    September 2006

    2) Okay, if you agree with the last one (which I somehow doubt, but sport me the bit), you might go "So maybe we don't need Creative Commons, but it doesn't really hurt anything, right?" And, again, I think that the answer is no.

    My concern here is not with the primary creator -- someone who comes up with his totally whole-cloth ideas. My concern is with secondary and, mostly, tertiary creators. You know, the people who are actually putting this theoretical resource of ideas to use.

    Okay, so let's say that the Alfred writes a game. He wants to release it under a Creative Commons non-commercial sharealike license. He uses material from the following artists, all under similar licenses: Betty, Carl, Dan, Emily, Fred, George, Harrieta, Isabel, James, Kevin, and Laura.

    Now, let's note that Alfred has now produced a game that he does not own in any real sense. While he may own the IP in some esoteric legal sense, he can't make the key decisions around the game, such as the decision to use it for commercial purposes, or even the decision to let someone else use bits of it for their own commercial creation.

    So, right away, as humongous believer in creator's rights, I'm not cool with this. But it's fair enough to argue that this is consensual -- he decided he didn't want to use his game for commercial purposes, and got to use a lot of work in the bargain, without the hassle of writing and asking permission.

    But let's say that Zeta wants to write a game. She wants to incorporate elements of Alfred's game, and also Amy's game, Andrew's game, Adrianna's game, and Alison's game. Let's assume that all these A games are CC non-commercial share-alike, and that all of them have a list of references at least as long as Alfred's. And let's say that Zeta wants to sell her game. The A's are all cool with it. If they could, they would give the same "Cool! How?" response that Vincent gave. But they can't, because they're tied by the CC license. So Zeta needs to contact on the order 50 people, securing their permission to sell her own creative work. That's fucked. I would go so far as to say that that's wrong.

    This is all because none of the A's actually own their game. Rather, they can use it under license, a license which necessarily restricts their creative freedom.

    (There's an aside here about how viral licenses in software are built precisely to create this sort of paradox, and it's good for code, but crappy for writing. I'll fill it in if there's interested.)

    Let me offer a different model: Complete creator ownership.

    Alfred is working on his game, with all his sources. He contacts the different creators that he wants draw on, and tells them about his project. "Awesome!" say most of them, go house. A couple of people want a pay-off to use their stuff (say $50 or whatever) and one guy doesn't like role-playing games and would rather not be involved. Alfred has now completed his game. He decides to give it away for free, but he's totally in his rights to sell it or whatever.

    Let's say that Zeta is now working on her own game, and she wants to use some bits from Albert's. "Cool," say Albert, and that's that. Now Zeta has written her own game, which she owns in its entirity. If Omega wants to use some for his own game, he doesn't have to ask Albert. He only has to ask Zeta.

    Much easier, yes?

    3) So, so far, Creative Commons takes away your rights if you use it (as a secondary or tertiary creator) and it claims to restrict rights it has no legal standing to restrict. Oh, and it's of no particular benefit to our community, except I suppose as a bit of free press. As far as I'm concerned, that should be enough to make my case that it's a bad idea. But, in all honesty, that's not the reason I dislike it (they're reasons I think it's stupid, but that's different.) I dislike it because the assumptions it brings are socially toxic.

    We're artists. We should be able to freely take inspiration from each other, but we should also be (and are) a community pulsing with creative feedback -- which necessarily runs in both directions, both for the person inspired and the person who inspired. We should respect each other as people and as artists.

    In a situation of mutual respect, all that's required for any manner of appropriation is "Hey, I'm going to use this, cool?" And the response is almost always "Cool! How?" This is totally removed from any legal standing -- I wasn't legally required to talk to Vincent about using Otherkind stuff, but damn, I wanted to!

    Creative Commons, and indeed any licensing, assumes a situation where this environment of mutual respect does not exist. The very basic "right" that it grants is the ability to use the material without telling the original artist about it. I mean, fuck! If we get into a place where that's ever an issue, ever, I'm washing my hands of this community as a whole and spitting on its grave.

    As it is, every derivative work that has been done based on a CC licensed game (I'm thinking of TSOY here) has been done with the permission and love and respect of the author. As it should be, of course, but is it apparent that, in that case, there's no reason for the license to exist in the first place?

    So, I have a thing, which is basically creator ownership + basic social niceity.

    I'm not trying to convert you here, Ben--you should absolutely have the right to publish your work however you see fit--but since we're discussing the usefulness of an entire concept, I want to provide a solid counterpoint. I hope my responding point-by-point won't be considered rude; this is mostly to help me organize my thoughts.

    1) The Big Lie: Ben, I think you kind of talked all the thrust out of this one yourself.

    a) "This whole bit is not to talk about the length of copyrights." But pretending expiration terms don't exist is disingenuous. Without getting into the issue of whether they're absolutely too long or too short, some authors believe they're too long for their own work. Creative Commons provides a way for those authors to open their work to redistribution or derivation before the term is up, without forcing them to put it fully (and irretrievably) into the public domain. I have to squint really hard to see that as anything but a benefit.

    b) "There is a problem with the law in practice" and "there are some orphan works bills." Bills aren't laws, and tort reform has so far been nothing but talk. I'm an artist working right now and I'd prefer not to wait for the state of US civil law to change--if it changes--before I publish. Creative Commons sidesteps the whole mess with clarity and ease: I publish my work under a license, and thereafter you know that I want you to use it in at least one set of ways and won't sue you for doing so.

    c) Your SGNP example fails the test you set for it: since the book will contain considerably more material than just names (non-name lists and art), the aggregate work is copyrightable and a valid subject for a collaborative license.

    As for otherkind, you're a poor test subject: the vast majority of people reading and potentially building on Vincent's works are not people who have slept on his couch. I know Vincent's not the type of person to sue over abandonware even for non-Ben users, but I've been reading his blog and talking to him on the Forge for years. If Clinton's (and Vincent's) intention was that a writer who didn't know Vincent or anything about him should be able to pick up the existing text of otherkind, add to it, and republish it, then a CC license would accomplish that.

    2) I'm going to respond to your argument about negotiation with a couple diagrams. Dotted lines are inheritance without negotiation. Solid lines are inheritance where negotiation is required.

    Here's the way it works if all the people except Zeta publish under Attribution-Noncommercial:



    And here's your case for all rights reserved:



    If you count up the solid lines, you'll see that the sum of points of contact is the same; all the Noncommercial restriction has done is move the effort downstream to the person who wants to sell the aggregate, which is exactly what it's supposed to do. Furthermore, this whole scenario only works as an argument against the Noncommercial clause. If those upstream licensees published under Attribution or Attribution-Sharealike, the points of negotiation drop to zero; if they published under NoDerivs, it's exactly the same as the full-ownership scenario.

    Finally, you make a false distinction between "licensed" and "owned" games. Making an agreement with an author to reuse their work, whether or not it's under CC, is called licensing--unless Alfred bought the actual copyrights to Betty's and Carl's work, which you say is unethical. In both examples you presented, Alfred's game is a licensed creation (presumably in conjunction with some original material that he owns outright).

    3) This is where it becomes obvious that dodging the issue of copyright expiration is impossible. A community of mutual respect and communication among creators can only exist in a situation where the creators themselves are perpetually present and communicable. But we're not always instantly available, and we're hardly immortal. If I have an aneurysm tomorrow, I don't want potential collaborators for the next eighty-five years to have to track down my next of kin and secure her permission to use my stories--I want a license slapped on the front that says they're free to do so right now. CC accomplishes that nicely.

    Just two bits:

    1) It goes sometimes unnoticed, but I find that one of the most useful properties of the CC licenses is that they allow (and encourage) the re-distribution of your work.

    With a CC-By-NC, for example, I can print some pages of your game for a convention, mirror your PDF in a webpage, translate your game to my own language and post it in a forum, etc...

    Using a CC license not only simplifies A LOT the act of creating a hack of your game, it also simplifies A LOT the act of distributing your game AS IT IS.

    And I know that you can always post your game in your own website for free but sites go down, games get lost and I'm so tired of going to the Wayback Machine to look for old stuff that I just wish that all games that were freely offered by their creators in their own webpages could have been mirrored by fans so they will be available nowadays.

    For example: I cannot mirror GURPS-Lite even if the rules are free for everyone to grab. This makes GURPS-Lite less popular than it will be if they just used the most restrictive CC license (say CC-By-NC-ND) which is pretty much the same deal but without free re-distribution of their unmodified document (where they can put their name, website, etc...).

    2) Licensing a PDF by parts (images are Copyrighted, but text is CC-By-SA except the basic rules that are OGL...) is a nightmare and goes against the spirit of free licenses.

    If you cannot license the whole thing using a single type of license it is better to create a "lite" version (without images or without setting or just with the basic rules or...) that others can use without surgery. Or at least license whole pages of your document, so we can print or re-distribute them without editing it (from page XX to page YY the content is CC-By, the rest of the document is All Right Reserved).

    This is perfectly compatible with dual licensing (this game is CC-By and OGL) and with the idea of wanting to get paid for the "complete" version, I just want to point that sometimes I would rather see fewer parts of a PDF getting a free license if that means that the free parts are easier to use, share and remix.

    For example: The fact that Fate text is CC is offset by the fact that I cannot share the Fate PDFs because the illustrations are copyrighted. I know that there is a SRD freely available and that I can share it, but, again, I wonder if it wouldn't be more productive in marketing terms to publish a completely CC version of the FAE PDF.

    And I'm fully conscious that it is really generous to get a nicely illustrated PDF for free and that the companies that publish games are not charities but, again, simplicity trumps everything else in terms of spontaneous and genuine word-of-mouth.

      A post with a link to the new thread is probably the best choice, although even then I would personally prefer it if the forum software had a way to ignore such managerial posts in thread dating - as it is, old threads are dragged up by the user interface and presented out of chronological order when new posts are made.

      What forum software actually needs is support for blog-like linkbacks: whenever somebody (on-forum or off, using the protocol) links to a thread, there's no particular reason why forum software couldn't do like blogs and insert the linkback into the thread, all without considering it a "real post". A blog and a forum are rather similar things, there really isn't much reason for the latter not to adopt the innovations of the former.
