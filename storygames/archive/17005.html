    \n Fastaval is basically a tabletop/freeform convention, though there's probably much more standing up and portraying characters and much less dice-rolling in European (especially Nordic) than in American tabletop. Montsegur 1244 premiered as a Fastaval scenario, IIRC.
    Knutepunkt/Solmukohta is a conference about larp, but it's been branching into freeform a lot in recent years. Games played at KP are most often freeforms, because of time/space constrains especially, while games talked about at KP are mainly large-scale larps.

    In the context of the Nordic scene, btw, "freeform" is best understood as a game coming from a tabletop tradition of design (all people in the same room, no costumes, short duration, etc.) which got closer to larping by embracing stand-up, full body portrayal of characters and by discarding some crucial TTRPG fetish-items such as dice and scores, but is still fundamentally tabletop-like \u2014 e.g., still fundamentally a conversation in which people take turns and everybody is constantly involved as an audience to everybody, with talking over each other very strongly discouraged (as opposed to the simultaneous action and multiplicity of subjective view-points of larp proper), much of the action and all of the backdrop is verbally depicted and imagined (as opposed to WYSIWYG), and a "game master" (if present by design) is actually a player with a distinct role (as opposed to an event organizer/designer).

    \n Rafu is mostly spot on, but I'd like to point out whether nordic roleplaying has less dice rolling or not depends on who you ask. 90 % of roleplayers here still play DnD, just like everywhere else. The most active people in the roleplaying scene just generally comes from the Fastaval-scene (as is much the same with the indie community, I figure) and is thus the vocal minority you'll see in places such as this (hi!). Fastaval isn't even the biggest con in Denmark, that would be Viking-con, which is far more traditional and more "american" like, scenarios usually built on american games. The organizers at Fastaval also strive to maintain a diverse variety of games, so there's usually a DnD, or DnD inspired scenario or two every year, and a good amount of scenarios with homebrew rules involving dices, cards and/or numbers. That said, there's still a slant towards structured freeform, that style probably being the most numerous.

One point about definitions: when people ask 'What is freeform/Jeepform?' the Anglo answer tends to be 'It's a kind of larp', but in fact Nordic freeforming appears to occupy a sort of middle ground between TT and larp, in that you can play freeform TTRPGs but don't have to sit down, and it's very immersive, whereas actual larping involves scenarios and a playstyle that necessitate the wearing of costumes, and a very expansive meta-gameworld, i.e. not just one table in a big hall somewhere but a whole forest, field, building etc. As such I'm very keen to try it. There's a con here in the UK, Consequences, that seems to cater for it; I haven't been there up until now, partly because it's usually only a week or so after a big and growing TTRPG con, plus I've been put off by the fact that I'm not really interested in larps (anglo definition, as stated above).     \n In our own vocabulary, we actually tend to talk more about 'semi-live' than freeform. Semi-live being tabletop at heart, but with full body language, walking around and the like. It's great for immersion. Any game can potentially be played like that, but it's problematic in games that use dice and other stuff that requires constant, physical interaction. Thus free-form games are also quite often played semi-live. I think Jeep-form are always played semi-live, but I'm no expert that.

    \n Well, if you're standing up and moving about to increase emotional intensity, naturally then the design goal becomes "to further increase emotional intensity." Ergo jeepform, and all of its toolbox.

    Emily Care Boss and Matthijs Holter are currently drafting a game called Playing with Intent which makes this toolbox of tabletop/freeform techniques accessible to any group for any style. It is our hope to decouple the notion of intense, moving play from associations with only specific groups. Many standard Fastaval games enable us to push our boundaries through different manifestations of "semi-live-ness."
