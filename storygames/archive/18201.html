\n Have you ever done this?
Twice I have GMed an impromptu game. No system, no rules, no character sheets, no dice. The first time was as a teenager; the players couldn't grasp the concept and it fell apart. The second time, in my twenties with two strangers, no less! (friends of a friend who went to bed). It was a success! I think at one point a coin came out to determine an event. We knew the tropes of the genre, we bought in to the premise of Characters having to do Something. It was actually a good "bonding" experience.

Have you ever heard of (or experienced!) a session in which the "Rule Book" was never consulted or the dice (cards/Ouji board) never consulted? Have you ever played a game without The Game?

    \n I'll clarify my own reasoning for not wanting to do it on a regular basis. I think I could pull it off, but my players enjoy having the rules there to give them a structure to fall back on when they don't know what to do. On my side of the game, I like having a system there to introduce complications and situations I wouldn't normally have considered. In particular, the Fiasco setup comes to mind as helping to generate great ideas and situations that I probably wouldn't get from just our own imaginations.
    Apocalypse World resolution also does this for me, since even when you don't get what you want, interesting and DIFFERENT things happen, things that change the story in ways I wasn't expecting beforehand. You can get surprise out of freeform from the other players, but rules can provide things that were unpredictable to everyone.

    So for me having the rules is a way to enhance the experience I might have during freeform, while at the same time providing a safety net to fall back on when we're directionless or when there's disagreement between players over what should happen.

    However, at the same time, the occasional session without it can be very interesting because it gives the group a greater level of control over the interesting things we want to see happen. Plus, there's no structure the group needs to understand or have explained, which makes it easy for new folks.

    \n I very much like this kind of gaming, and it is a bit under-explored in terms of text, at least in the case of text as item for sale.

    Matthjis Holter and Emily Care have been working on tools for structured freeform, along with other folks. Somewhere on the interwebs they have a pretty nice text well worth searching out.

    There are a number of problems talking abut this kind of thing though.

    1) There actually are a whole bunch of rules to this stuff
    If you played anthropologist and watched people involved in this sort of play, and could put aside what you already know, you'd pretty quickly be able to suss out the rules involved. We generally tend not to write this stuff out, or we gloss over it, or we write it up in a way different from the "proper" bits of the rules. Some of it just seems way too obvious to bother writing down. Some of it probably is, but it could be worthwhile to do an experiment writing out the instructions clearly and briefly.

    If you ever had a grade-school teacher who made you do the "Write Instructions for making a peanut butter and jelly sandwich" exercise as a kid, you'll know what I mean by this. (Hint: It's a lot harder than you think it would be, as the teacher in the exercise will basically play the part of a completely uninitiated reader of the instructions).

    2) Hobby gamers have a fairly different idea of what a game must be to be a game than lots of other folks
    The vast, vast majority of games in this world are extremely simple. OTOH, most also have some kind of clear goals which at minimum act as an "off-switch" to the activity.

    3) At some very basic level, we all know this stuff is Play Pretend.
    We need to be able to find a way to convey that, along with the idea that Play Pretend itself is fun, worthwhile, and perfectly acceptable as an activity for adults. Mechanics do lots of positive things, but one of the things they do is act as a shield to criticism that this stuff is really an activity for children only. That attitude is bunk, but I really do think sometimes there is an attitude of "Baffle those critics with bullshit" in the love for complex mechanics.

    4) Social recognition by turning stuff into a for-sale commodity
    One of the great successes that came out of The Forge days was a whole lot of info about becoming an independent small game maker and seller in a reasonable and do-able fashion. Awesome! I think there was a downside though: It really cemented the idea that not much was worthwhile, unless you could turn it into a commodity, and that doesn't fit well with this type of gaming.

    5)Factions: System Matters! vs. All this stuff is a bunch of optional tools!, and everyone in between.
    As noted in point #1, there actually is always a system in place, even if we don't recognize it. Having said that, the type of gaming we're looking at in this thread tends to fall closer to the second attitude up there in italics. In fact, most of the tools are so very optional, that you could play successfully without them.

    The classic "We played all night and never opened a rule book or threw any dice!" line is a nod to exactly that. Thing about it is, as discredited as that line is in lots of places, those people actually were having a good time, the fundamental point of all of this activity. Clearly, those folks were using some part of a text. What part was it? How did they use it?

    edited to add:

    6) Extremely light or freeform-ish play is easily explained when face to face, but much harder to get across in text.

    \n Dave (or anyone else with similar experiences),

    I want to ask some questions about the "magic room" freeform roleplay experiment.

    * Was it like: "Here, play my game, although you don't know what the rules are, you'll find out as you go"?

    * Or was it more like: "Here, we're playing, and we're slowly establishing the rules/System as a group as we go along"?

    I would imagine the significant markers would be how players deal with "unacceptable" actions. For instance, did a player ever say something which led you (or someone else) to say, "Hang on, that's not how this works. If you want to find out what's behind the door, you've got to open it, and then I'll tell you what's there"? Or was it all, "Cool, this just turned into a game where we're all making up the contents of the rooms and trying to see whose idea is more interesting. I didn't expect that, but this is fun!"

    I know that's not a line that can be clearly drawn in the sand, but I think it's interesting to at least guess how far along that continuum a given instance of play might land.

    \n I've done some of this at least at two points in my life.

    First, in high school. The invitation was to friends already hanging out/gather for game playing of various sorts: "let's do free form roleplaying". We had a GM, and it was really just a more pure form of some of the play we engaged in, but without the rules of D&D to obscure GM fiat (e.g. turning a 20 on a to hit roll to bash down a door, into you getting your sword stuck in the door). In that way it was much more functional and saved time (especially in character creation), while allowing much more flexibility. I remember playing a lizardman who could run on walls.

    More recently, I GM'd a couple asynchronous games with friends online. There I just launched into it directly, invitation was play. Maybe because I was GM'ing, but here I felt that there was a lot more exploring what the rules were. I was occasionally uncomfortable having so system to tell me when to make hard moves, and there was occasionally a player asking if what he introduced was ok (narrating the existence of things beyond what his character was doing). My tendency was to always allow everything and incorporate it.\n

    \nThe trick with talking about this kind of stuff is that rules in fact already exist, from pretty much the jump off point.

    "Freeform" as used is more of a comparative term. This type of play is vastly more free in form than other, related games which are more familiar, but the suggestion that they are without rules is a red herring.

    Most of the rules are not written out, and that's a bit problematic, for many of the same reason's you have talked about in other threads.

    For purposes of functional play, the most important rules may well be simply assumed to be understood, and that can be a very poor assumption when it comes to transferring the concepts to someone not in direct face-to-face contact.

    A practical example of this:

    I was messaging with vulpinoid, trying to get some insight into setting up a kind of miniatures using/LARPish hybrid type thing, similar to what is known in Australia as a multi-form which is out towards/in freeform territory. He has a great deal of experience with that, as a player and designer.

    I wanted to know how, as a designer/ref/helper one prevented PC A from simply going and killing PC B, especially since the set up for the scenario certainly indicated violence was a viable solution (and there might be some mechanism for that). Back and forth messaging occurred until he told me the most important part:

    In Australian freeforms/multiforms of this sort, it's simply considered extremely bad form to go and kill off someone else's character straight away, even in a fictional set up with deadly rivalries and capacity for violence. Everyone else had also paid to participate. It was something understood socially by the players that if some kind of terminal violence was going to occur between characters, it didn't happen until well into the game, and then usually only near the end of the time slot.

    That is an incredibly important rule.

    Unwritten, understood if you're part of the play culture or you encounter it face to face, amazingly non-obvious to me initially. Also a rule that is vastly more important than any later additions of fiddly nonsense about miniature positioning or dice rolling and whatnot.

    To me, that's a really good example of the sort of unwritten mechanics that are way more important to play than all of the stuff we tend to traditionally think of as important. We tend, I feel, to focus on writing rules that are two or three categories down the scale of importance in proper, non-freeform games.

    I do think there is an entire realm of development worth is exploring in writing down those higher level, more important rules and even an entire unexplored/poorly explored path in the presentation of those higher level rules.

    \n I'm most familiar with Archipelago, so I tend to think of it when I think of structured freeform.

    Right away, I think of the ritualized phrases. They're a tool, written out and made official, that cover tons of useful behavioral ground quickly. They also cover lots of ground that in other games is never truly put in the spotlight but is terribly important to functional play.

    In my own experience, most RPGs are transmitted orally with only one player actually reading the text and then explaining to the other players.     \nI've spent some time talking to Jay Willis about his long-running Lord of the Rings RPG, which I think fits this model pretty well. The structures seem to be:

    The GM is the judge of what contributions are valuable. The GM's basis for this is not spelled out as explicit rules, but is consistent enough that anyone who plays with him for a while gets a good sense of it.

    The GM provides the setting, and the players' goal is to traverse that setting and make valuable contributions.

    When a player attempts to contribute:

    - If the contribution is not deemed valuable, the GM will look annoyed, ask for a d20 roll, and only grant success if the player rolls a 20.

    - If the contribution is deemed valuable, the GM will express approval, interact with the player for a bit, ask for a d20 roll, and grant success on a difficulty number that seems appropriate to the fictional task and conducive to fun results. The interaction will then continue, elaborating on the results of the attempted task.

    There are lists of things you roll for, and the player characters have stats in those things. These represent the characters' fictional abilities. The GM will factor these fictional abilities into the difficulty number, though less so than the roleplay.

    When rolling the d20, a 1 is always a problematic failure, a 20 is always a useful success, and a 10 always gets you an experience point to boost a stat.

    That's the structure and mechanics. The rest of the game is "the GM's basis for judging contributions", which is all colorful portrayal, clever problem-solving, and riffing off Tolkein. If you can do all three at once, the GM will love you, and the other players who've bought into the GM's taste will love you. You and your contributions will receive attention and enthusiastic response, and that's the game's primary reward.

    Many will find this social set-up a huge turn-off, but it's one example of a system that's pretty freeform and pretty stable (been running for years, and Jay seems to have standards and enjoys it).

    I, uh, don't have any brilliant takeaways from this, but hopefully someone else will...
