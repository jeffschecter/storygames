    \n Note: I've never actually played the game.

    Might want to start here:

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_of_Warcraft_Trading_Card_Game

    Basically, the card game is just like a normal card game, along the lines of Magic: The Gathering and so on. In it's normal form, players build up their hero and its allies, and then fight against their opponent's hero and allies; last one standing wins.

    However, the raid decks (referenced in the Penny Arcade comic you linked) are a sort of special co-operative multiplayer mode. One person acquires the non-customizable (as far as I can tell) raid deck, and plays it against a group of players using their normal decks. The raid deck contains monsters and difficult bosses and so on that amount to a challenge for at least a few players. It's not necessarily meant to be played in a story game style - Tycho here is taking it to extremes - but there's no reason that it can't be played with some narrative flourishes.

    \n I think that those things are permitted because the players understand that the raid deck is supposed to be harsh and eat the opposition alive. It's the same reason no one throws a fit in a game like Descent, where the dungeon master is instructed to KILL the player characters at any and every chance they get within the listed rules. There's no story to be told, no big reason behind it. We know they are out to get us and we have to survive their little sadistic game of cat and mouse, people accept it because it's a card game or a board game where winning is the goal, far different than most RPGs where players are often told "There are no winners or losers. The ride is the destination."

    All that's needed to achieve that in a RPG is to notify players ahead of time that once combat hits or dice resolution pops up, it's going to be brutal.
    I think there's some card decks made by Paizo that add in new problems to critical hits or other situations which can also help because "Hey, it's in the text on the card, the GM isn't being a jerk."
