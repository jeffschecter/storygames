    And the Finnish:
    "Is\xe4nn\xe4t ovat aina naimattomia. Ehk\xe4 leski\xe4. Kenties kihloissa. Mutta eiv\xe4t koskaan juuri nyt naimisissa."

Maybe Janne misremembered because in Finnish, unlike English, the corresponding female word "em\xe4nt\xe4" means the female master (or co-master) of a household, unlike the English word "mistress", which is more often used in a subservient way.     But anyway, that's a different topic. The point to me seems to be that Maid seems to get a lot of defence by the way of nit-picking the text of the game. I don't think this is very fair towards Judd and others who dislike the game - it's like saying that they don't know how to interpret what they read. I find that if I have to choose between considering Maid a perverse sex game or pretending that it doesn't really mean to be kinky, I'll have to go with the former as being closer to the truth. The fact that you can use the game for non-kinky purposes, and the fact that the game's text has no overt pornographic tones, do not together mean that Judd is unfair in characterizing the game as having erotic overtones. The same goes for the whole maid phenomenon, by the way - you'd need to be blind to the subtext to claim that it's not a sexual fetish, and you'd similarly need to be blind to claim that the Maid rpg doesn't gesture towards that same fetish with its contents. It's all well and good that actual play is not pornographic, but rather comedic and adventurous; that's what I'd expect of any product in the ecchi genre, and that's what I'd definitely expect of a roleplaying crew - it'd be pretty weird to gather the guys together for a shared session of overtly sexual maid fetish narration, frankly. But saying that the fetish is not a motive force behind the game's imagery and genre is just stupid.

Might be just a culture thing, but I don't expect each and every product that makes a passing reference or even bases itself onto a sexual subculture to have a large "adults only" warning plastered over it. It's just not that big a deal, and I personally doubt that children are going to get hurt by it. (Could be wrong, just my opinion, etc.) Human sexuality is and has always been a strong driving force behind all sorts of artistic creativity, and the results tend to make their way into mainstream polite culture in time. The Maid roleplaying game is just a couple of naughty non-explicit jokes away from such mainstream phenomena as Bratz dolls; definitely still recognizable as being based on a sexual fetish, but not directly sexual itself any more than dryads or succubi in D&D are.

    (The child porn angle is more troublesome by far here - if it's OK with you, I'll just skip it in favour of arguing about the general position of sexuality in polite society, unless you want to concede the above point and focus on how wrong and sick Maid is for mixing children with sexual imagery and situations. They're a bit separate issues, as I see it.)

    In summation, though, let me say that I'm 100% OK with Judd disliking the game for its kinkiness, and I do recognize that we differ only on a matter of taste. I do agree with him that it is kinky, no amount of selective reading and creative drifting in actual play really gets over the sexual innuendo evident in the game text in many places. I just don't personally see any great aesthetic or ethical problem in Maid's kinkiness. Something like Bacchanal is much more problematic in that regard - the latter has a maturity warning in my webstore, while the former does not.

      \n Yes, Brian, it is problematic. That seems to me to be the entire premise of the series - how people handle such a problematic situation. It's nothing I wouldn't throw out as a bang in certain sorts of roleplaying games... in fact, that's a shock for Shock:, isn't it? "There are genetically engineered variant human beings used as slaves" seems text-book to me. Heck, Blade Runner uses that. This particular anime is just more straightforward about the primal psychology, namely the supposed male desire to advocate for a social model that allows them sex slaves. Blade Runner might not be that explicit about it, but the impact on human relationships (and human sex) is the thing that is really the crux issue of that particular shock, isn't it?

      The point being, I find it positive and interesting if an artistic work is problematic and powerful in presenting its theme - I well might disagree with the conclusions, but that doesn't make the work bad as art.

      Now, in case somebody who's actually seen the show wanted to tell me that it's disturbingly... positive and affirming towards the set-up, I'd have to agree with them. I would probably add that Dears clearly presents its image of powerful, physically hyper-perfect superhero love slaves from the stars as a glamorous fantasy replacement for real human relations. If I had to get all media-analytic and try to divine whether the authors of the work themselves supported and idolized female slavery, I wouldn't have any ready replies. This particular work could be a very stylish ironic statement, or it could be made by horrible, civilized but degenerate reprobates who wanted to advertise the idea that women are happiest in chains (or perhaps the idea that we should do some genetic engineering to make ourselves some improved women). I don't know, I can only speak of the work itself, not the motivations behind it. Whatever the intent (and if we're being frank - this is mainstream commercial anime, the real motivation is always that it sells, nothing more), the work itself is thought-provoking regarding gender-relations, the socially acceptable forms of love and the lines between liberal and fascist forms of society.

      Actually, here's the anime's opening sequence from Youtube. I think it's pretty frank about the topic and style of the series. As you can see, the series has a high sexual tension to it and a clear authoritarian theming - the name of the title song is "Love Slave" and the lyrics are pretty explicit, if you had any doubts about the topic. Still, the work is not psychologically simplified or instantly gratifying, it does really discuss its premise from a variety of angles.

      Still, calling that "creepy" - maybe it is if you're unused to that sort of thing. I guess I would consider it creepy if somebody made it a hobby to collect and obsess over this particular subgenre of anime (female slavery is, indeed, an extreme subgenre of harem comedy), but considering the individual show on its own merits instead of painting an imaginary picture of what goes on in the heads of the fans... it's an intentionally provocative and extreme image of gender relationships in a fantastic scifi setting, I'm not going to get any more bend about it than about the latest Hollywood blockbuster or something. All sorts of things are done in art, and God bless the variety, especially when it's done in a fresh and intelligent manner. If our convictions about human equality and mutually respectful romance as the basis of carnal relationships can't stand some speculative fiction, then I should really be worried for our civilization.

      \n Eh, this will probably be taken as offense by somebody, and I thought that something like this would be a given as a basic precept, but maybe it'll help some people to see why considering Maid somehow peculiar could be seen as strange.

      Reductio ad Absurdum coming up: if Maid or the maid genre of anime is wicked and weird because it refers to sexual urges as a source of comedy, then we should also conclude that numerous immensely popular American blockbuster movies published this decade are similarly wicked as well, as those movies make use of the same exact cinematic techniques for the same exact purpose of making fun of sexual mores and gender images. Picking a completely arbitrary example that to my knowledge sticks pretty close to the average of naughtiness in the Hollywood genre of naughty comedy, American Pie 2, a movie that is certainly more grotesque than the text of Maid, grossed something like 300 million dollars according to Wikipedia.

      Now, keeping the above in mind, the way I see it, we only have a few logical conclusions here:\n
        \n
      • Somebody who considers Maid nasty would be similarly offended by my completely arbitrary pick of an American naughty comedy blockbuster. Fine, that is your pregorative. However, why should I not consider your opinion that of a prude, exactly? Most of the civilized world at this date has no problems with naughty comedy, so aren't you and your minority opinion prudish by definition? Why is this not the case if you insist on condemning what is essentially the core of mainstream cultural mores at this point? Note that I have nothing against American Evangelical fundamentalism, to pick an ideology that would indeed stand in judgment of American Pie - but if something marginal like that is going on here, how are we to understand and appreciate each other's opinions if we don't discuss the ideological underpinnings frankly?
        • \n
      • Ye who complain of Maid perhaps did this in the mistaken impression that the game involves something more sordid than your perfectly average naughty American comedy. I might be tempted to cite this as the result of ignorance or some sort of special pleading for American culture - it's somehow more acceptable for an American to joke about masturbation or peeking under women's skirts than it is for a Japanese, I don't know. Why should I not try to educate and correct your view, if it is indeed predicated on you simply not knowing enough about the topic to have an informed opinion? Many people in this thread have engaged the topic based solely on the game's website. I don't dismiss these opinions off-hand myself as you can still have an opinion on the genre and the website even without reading the game, but it always seems that people go from looking at the website into wild flights of fancy concerning the contents of the actual game text. Take that sexual slavery thing for instance; the only way Maid is involved in sexual slavery is the same way Wonder Woman comics with their busty damsel in distress theme are. Even if you don't care about the topic enough to look at the actual game, it'd be nice if you at least limited your critique to the material you actually have some knowledge about.
        • \n
      • This, I think, is likely the most likely reason here: a person arguing that Maid is offensive on a rpg board is very likely doing this because he feels that rpgs should be held to different criteria than movies, comics, novels or primetime television. I'm not trying to put words into anybody's mouth, this is just what this whole discussion seems like: you people must be aware that getting offended about naughty comedy in the media is something that was hip over fifty years ago, you can't be unaware that this sort of comedy happens in the television every single day. But in roleplaying, those discussions we actually have relatively often: gamers have a high degree of ownership feelings for the media of roleplaying, they would like it to accord with their own tastes and preferences. We all are likely much more provincial about roleplaying as a media than we are about television, simply because we feel more attached to it. But stop to think about it, is it really a valid argument for condemnation that roleplaying should be kept more pure and more in accordance with my personal preferences than any other media? Is it just because roleplaying is small and my word should thus have more weight? Is it because I like it more than television or movies? Is it because I think that I'll have more support for my prudish (relatively speaking, no offense intended) opinions with the demographic that enjoys roleplaying? Am I worried that I will get stamped with the pervert badge in my own, prudish non-gamer community? Am I still hanging onto some Satanism scare that happened 20 years ago? Thinking about it, is any of the above justifications really valid for requiring roleplaying to follow a different set of rules than other media does?
        • \n
      • The last reason I can think of is that a person might accept American Pie but not Maid specifically because the naughtiness in American Pie was of acceptable kink, while that in Maid was a bad sort of kinky fetish, something that is to be judged evil, no matter how implicit the reference. I personally don't see Maid as any closer to the BDSM scene than superhero comics are - both involve sexually suggestive character types in fetish uniforms, but doing very non-explicit things that don't really have anything directly to do with sexuality, alternative or otherwise. However, if you happen to think that BDSM or similar sexual fetishes are bad and implicit references like Maid anime or superhero comics are also bad, then I fully support the notion that you should judge the Maid rpg as well. To me this is another reductio ad absurdum, I find it absurd that somebody would judge maid anime, superheroes and sexual roleplaying as so harmful that anything, however tenuously associated, should be ostracized. There have been stages like that in western history, though, so perhaps it is possible.
