    \n I think IaWA is aptly suited for pulp, given that the sword-and-sorcery brand of fiction it is based on is a direct product of pulp. Conan the Cimmerian, the writings of Jack Vance and Tanith Lee. Both Vance and Lee have sci-fi novels penned in the same style. I think one of the hallmarks of the sword and sorcery style is a lack of explanation/support for some of the epic or spiritual elements. For example, there's rarely an exposition of where gods, demons came to be nor an explanation of how sorcery works. It just does. It's just assumed. In contrast with high fantasy which often actually states it's cosmological reasons for those things and (for some reason I'll never figure out) attempts to make them plausible to the reader. Tolkien's writings are a prime example-- all the origins of various things, the Valar, Istari, Sauron are all spelled out.
    \n
    \nPulp sci-fi has the same elements as sword-and-sorcery fantasy. It rarely goes into any sort of exposition as to how FTL travel works, how blasto guns work or any such thing. In contrast with Star Trek which constantly is explaining things by way of analogy.
    \n
    \nAs long as you have the same genre "buy in" from the players as you'd want when doing sword-and-sorcery In a Wicked Age, it should work great for campy pulp sci-fi serials.
    \n
    \nI'd also recommend consulting the Evil Overlord list to find out some classic cliches and blunders you can possibly include in the game.
