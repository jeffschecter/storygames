    Posted By: QHudspeth what you think needs to be in a fantasy RPG for kid's, and what doesn't.
    \nOn this subject, of what needs to be in an RPG for kids and what doesn't, I think it could all be boiled down to this:

    Because the imagination of children can vary so much more wildly than adults, and you never know what to expect, the system should be flexible enough to handle whatever comes its way, without having to add any new rules or exceptions. While this can be a pretty tall order, it may just be a matter of having a simple dice mechanic, and boiling down actions to like five or six different broad abilities. That way, no matter what a child might dream up, they can define it as "an attack," or "problem-solving" or something like that.
    In a way, story games are perfect for this, since they already think outside the "box." D&D and other more mainstream games, though, often try to place too many definintions and exceptions to things (move THIS far, can use this EXCEPT when..., affected ONLY by...).

    Again, this is only my opinion...

    Posted By: wburdickInteresting -- I hadn't thought about that!
    \nItr opens up a lot of possibilities, and also removes a lot of constraints at the same time.

    For one thing, the game part doesn't have to be perfect or even complete.

    For another, it can be short and even a bit vague and that's okay.

    For a third thing, whatever game you come up with doesn't need to be definitive. A given property could have one or three or ten games related to it, each a bit different and focussing on a different aspect of the property. And each game could be short and focussed and still true to an aspect of the property.

    Last, it opens up the game design on the property end in the sense that a designer no longer has to either buy rights to the property and gamble on acceptance by a paying public. That's all off the table. One need not write something like Redhurst Academy and promote it; Instead, you just write a Harry Potter game and get a check cut by the company for your efforts.

    \n I've learned a lot - the hard way - trying to game with my daughter. At 4 she was playing Warhammer Quest, which is pretty easy to play (move and swing) but hard to win. Back then attention span was an issue - we'd play for 15 minutes and then she'd be more interested in stacking the dice into towers. This is before she even knew what death was. ("you knock him out"). Just recently we've been playing Faery's tale which has a lot of good advice for playing with different ages. She's almost six now and in a sore loser phase - play Sorry with her? Forget about it. So faerys tale suggests diceless and that works pretty well - "you can't hit him"
    \nWe can use dice whenever the stakes are low, like if the faerys are in a tournament or something.
    \nUsually I ask first - "do you want to roll dice to see who wins?"
    \nOnce with FT she chose to use the last of her essence and fall into magical sleep to save the day - that was pretty awesome - but I don't think she could handle it if it was done to her.
    \nShe's usually passive "what happens next? What happens next?" but sometimes if I tell her "I'm out of ideas. What do *you* think happens next?" she'll come up with some cool stuff, like: "I draw a picture of a cat and it becomes real."
    \nshe loves character sheets - she likes writing down her abilities and drawing pictures of her characters.
    \nThere's a couple "RPGs for kids" web sites that have advice that I've found to be really true, like: kids love to pretend already, the hard part is getting conflict and dice in. So sometimes we'll play "soldier" and pretend to shoot aliens (tranquilizer bullets, I tell her) and then we'll roll dice a la 3:16 to see how many we got.
    \nShe likes universalis, too - she likes bidding for scenes but usually doesn't know what to do once she's won - I've gotten carried away a couple times "the wizard banishes your cat to another world" and made her cry - it's tough to tell what negative outcomes will set her off and what she's ok with, so lately I always ask first.
    \nSo... That's my report on gaming with 5 year olds. I think my daughter's pretty awesome, obviously. (yesterday she asked a friend of ours "do you play role playing games with *your* son?"). My biggest problem gaming with her is that *I* get bored much quicker than she does: if you can make a game that's as engaging to me as, say, Fiasco, without scaring her off, big win.

    Posted By: PotemkinWildlings isn'treallymy bid for "intro RPG for kids." It has veered away from that direction somewhat
    Why not? And, how so?

    I don't think a narrativist game would work at all for young kids. At that age they want explicit rules, since they don't have the experience yet to make judgement calls, and it would be up to the book to make it for them. That's one area in which computer games work really well, is there's no arguing with the computer, although in multiplayer there's accusations of cheating. If you have a very flexible framework for the game, there's always going to be a problem with cheating. That's also where having dice rolls for everything, and very limited GM judgement calls help. There's no thought that the GM is playing favourites, and any bad result can be blamed on the dice (therefore not ruining friendships). If we're looking at combat, keeping it simple, Final Fantasy 1 style helps, because it's just an attack roll, you know it's coming. If you have strategic combat going on though, a player could again feel their character is being singled out. Combat focus is also itself satisfying for kids since they're always told they're not allowed to fight in school, so it's something where they get to be grand heroes saving the day, rather than kids being told what not to do.

    \n Speaking from my own experiences of working with kids in a play environment for many years, I'd have to say that 'narrativist' style play is almost the natural mode for children and it is usually very hard to get them to accept most kind of explicit rules, which they more often than not find very constraining. Cases in point:
    \n
    \n- We have a home corner, which is a collection of child-scaled play furniture and utensils, such as a toy fridge, oven, washing machine, etc. They will happily play houses and shops in there all day without any kind of adult input at all. By contrast, we have a whole cupboard full of boardgames, but they won't ever play those without an adult supervising to make sure the rules are used... though they do often grab the money out of the Monopoly to use in their games of houses/shops.
    \n
    \n- One of the games that a particular group of children always ask me to play is 'Monkeys', the rules of which are: the children are all monkeys and I'm the zookeeper. From this set-up, the children create tales of the monkeys escaping, dying, having children, being scared of visitors, accepting new monkeys into the zoo and so on, all with me following their lead and playing along with what they want to happen. If I ever try to inject any kind of order, logic or 'rules' into the proceedings, I generally get ignored, which is fine as I shouldn't be adulerating their play anyway.
    \n
    \nSo, if you're looking to get children into role-playing, then I think the more narrativist it is, the better: children want to create and imagine, not to be told "Your character can't do that."
