    \n 13th Age also has a pair of unreliable narrators explaining the rules. In places, the two disagree on what the rules actually are. The bicker occasionally in sidebars and examples. In one place, they each argue for a different rule and each manages to convince the other tochange how they play the game.

    But they aren't fictional narrators. They're just the two designers of the game having different ideas on how it should be run.\n
    Wait. Where the author of the rules is unreliable? (Not the GM.)

    Are you trolling?
    \nI don't know if the original poster was trolling or not. But my examples above all are examples of rules presented by a fictional, unreliable narrator. Baron Munchausen is written by the fictional Baron himself, and he's wildly unreliable (also drunk). In a few places, he contradicts himself, or refuses to write a section or argues with his editor. A Penny For My Thoughts is also written in voice. The unreliability is subtler, but it's definitely there. I don't really trust the Orphic Institute entirely.

    So it's totally possible to have an unreliable narrator explain the rules. It's just tricky to do so and still have a playable game on the other end.

    \n Not sure if this counts, but one of the long-running White Wolf gimmicks is to have all of their splatbooks (guides to playing a particular faction or character type) written partially from the perspective of that faction: so if you read the splatbook for the group of vampires that wants to kill all the other vampires, you get lots of explanations for why that's reasonable and logical, along with the special vampire-killing rules they use and a lot of in-character fiction about vampiric vampire-killers trying to kill vampires. It sounds pretty stupid, but when well done, it sometimes made me suddenly interested in playing or running a game with that character type, because it suddenly made sense and seemed intriguing.

    There's actually a name for this effect on White Wolf forums, the "Chupp test", named after some White Wolf designer of days gone by. Some of the particularly good splatbooks, like the Guide to the Technocracy for Mage, succeeded in converting a fair percentage of the online fan-base to the belief that the formerly villainous faction were actually the good guys of the setting (even though, if you read closely, it's pretty clear that the writers are taking the piss).
