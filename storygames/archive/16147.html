\n A couple of years back I posted a topic talking about the rich story/theme material in the works of Manowar, one of the most durable power-metal bands out there. Some interesting discussion was had, and pictures of loincloth-clad metal men were shared, so it's worth a look.

I was set to thinking about similar material today when I heard Iron Maiden's "Powerslave" on Ozzy's Boneyard. That's a channel on SiriusXM, if you're wondering. It reminded me of how much good stuff can be extracted from metal music in general, not just from Manowar's discography.

Someone in the earlier discussion brought up Metal \xd6pera, an RPG by Zak Arntson that tries to distill the power of metal into a science-fictional narrative and is a prime example of what I'm talking about now. It's not tied to any particular song, but it is doubtless informed by many hours of dedicated metal listening.

Anyway, I started wondering about what metal songs people thought were good fodder for adaptation. "Powerslave" is a solid choice, though the song I've long wanted to make a game out of is Mot\xf6rhead's "Orgasmatron." I have no idea what system I'd bend to this purpose, but the story of an immortal futuristic dictator who may actually be a god is too interesting to pass up, as the characters' struggle against supernatural tyranny is fertile story-gaming ground.

So what are your thoughts? Do you have a song that pops to mind? How would you do it?

    \nTrue Story: This is the thread I've been waiting for (I'll be the first to boast that I have been playing the bass for 15 years, and metal is my indulgence...)

    The first thing that comes into mind is not so much a song, but a concept album. Check out Warp Riders by The Sword. Here are a couple reasons why:

    First and foremost, listen to the music. If you are a fan of metal's progenitors, this album will inspire. It draws from the pedigree of classic metal (Sabbath, Deep Purple, Iron Maiden, etc.) and is musically interesting throughout. My personal recommendation is the song "Lawless Lands". Embedded in the crazy, flanged-out riffs is a chorus based on the blues progression with brilliant stanzas ("No woman will have him, no man calls him friend").

    "Lawless Lands" is just one song - part of the vaguely revealed storyline, but it's probably easy to draw "oracle" type ideas from J.D. Cronise's lyrics. Here's another example that is basically a game premise in and of itself:

    "Upon the forsaken world of Acheron,
    the Archer is exiled from his tribe.
    In his solitary wanderings, he makes a
    singular discovery within an ancient ruin..."

    ***
    (forgive the disarray of the next part\u2026it\u2019s kind of rough, but it is based on a mechanic that works)

    So with all that said, here's a full system that might work with this album. I'll start with a mechanic my friends and I play with from time to time. I\u2019m modifying it out of the blue, though, so it probably won\u2019t work perfectly. Anywho, we like dice pools and d6s, so we based it all on that...

    (only roll when you are uncertain of the outcome, and remember that there is always something dangerous involved).

    1. identify the goal and dangers around your action
    2. roll a handful of d6s equal to the trait you use
    3. take the highest roll and compare it to the degree of success.

    Highest Roll Chart (Degree of success)
    1-3 = No success (the dangers come true\u2026the GM will assign you a freeform setback that\u2019s appropriate to the circumstances)
    4-5 = Half Success (always a step closer, you can try again and add 1d6 to your next roll for free)
    6 = Full Success (you get the goal you want)

    There are four PCs and four traits based on what each PCs career path is. Give your career path a rating of 6, and rate the others at 2, 3, and 4.

    The Archer (ranger-like things; strong in instinct and resolve)
    The Witch (sorcery; strong in magic and intellect)
    The Kinsman (the wary kinsman of the Archer, socially adept, especially in leadership and diplomacy)
    The Marauder (the hired gun of the group \u2013 tough as nails and physically menacing)

    Okay, so there\u2019s the system. Here\u2019s what you do with it:

    \u201cJust as the ancient prophecies foretold, the Archer has discovered an artifact \u2013 an orb of great power. However, the evil Chronomancer wishes to seize this device for his own wicked machinations. Your motley band \u2013 the archer, the wise witch, the hired marauder from the Night City, and the kinsman (who banished the archer) \u2013 must work together to bring forth the power of the orb to defeat the armies that lay siege to your homeworld of Acheron.\u201d

    Maybe some questions will help\u2026

    \u201cWhat can the orb do and why does the Chronomancer want it?\u201d
    \u201cWhy has your group been chosen to defend Acheron? (what powers do your career traits give you)\u201d
    \u201cHow can you use the orb to stop the Chronomancer?\u201d

    Anywho, I know this was a longwinded post, but it really got my mental gears in motion! I hope someone can scrap something out of this\u2026

    Best,
    ~Klaus

    MASTODON!

    Blood Mountain and Crack the Skye are incomprehensible vivid psychotronic acid-soaked weird fantasies and they are way influential on me when I am planning D&D adventures. I want to make adventures that feel like this music sounds.

    Blood Mountain is the more straightforward of the two; it's about a dude carrying a crystal skull up a mountain, consulting prophetic Cysquatches for wisdom, dodging Birchmen and reptilian hunters of the sky.\n
    HUNTERS OF THE SKYFly, reptile!
    Leather wings
    bones, hollow
    petrified
    \nDespite the fantasy content of its album, Bladecatcher gives me a fever dream don't-rest-your-head vibe.

    Crack the Skye is about a paralyzed boy astral projecting too close to the sun, Icarus-like. He is rescued by the assassinated Rasputin, who needs to hitch an astral ride, and then Rasputin defends him from Lucifer and tries to get him back to his body. Like I said, acid.

    There's also Seabeast from the Leviathan album. The video should offer an obvious gaming vibe.

    \n To type an umlauted U on any Windows machine:
    Hold down Alt and press in order, on the NUMPAD (NumLock, thus, is on) :
    Alt+0220 = \xdc
    Alt+0252 = \xfc

    (My gosh, how did he learn that?!?! Character Map; lower-right corner of the Status Bar, when a glyph is selected.)

    You can also use ampersand HTML codes, as follows:
    &Uuml;
    &uuml;
    Like so: \xdc \xfc

    Or COMBINE the forces, using HTML ampersand codes with the ASCII numeric value:
    &#220;
    &#252;

    (I won't bore you with the Hex code equivalents....)
