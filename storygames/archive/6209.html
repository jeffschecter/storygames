\n I know, right?

I'm thinking about this a lot, because I'm designing an edge case. I'm inclined to have a very liberal definition of roleplaying, and "I know it when I see it" has always been enough for me. I've seen people treat Shadows Over Camelot as a nebulous proto-RPG, and that's pure fun. I've seen people play explicit RPGs as tactical exercises - also fun. I don't really care about definitions, but I really care about that "I know it when I see it" zone, because I want my current project to live there happily.

Right now it's a pretty functional game that has an over-arching plot, defined characters, compelling situation, a juicy little set of mechanical gears and wheels that force players to make difficult choices, and would fit comfortably on the shelf next to Shadows Over Camelot, for example. It's a game you could bring roleplaying (that thing I know when I see it) into, but that you probably wouldn't. It's fun without it and it plays fine. Right now any roleplaying I introduced would be of the "step 3b: Now roleplay until it is time for step 3c" variety, which is worse than no guidance at all.

I'm not really satisfied with that, though - I want all the cool interplay and some organic, interesting, fun and useful roleplaying as well. I recognize that retrofitting my current design might be futile, and that's cool, but let's talk about the intersection of activity and mechanics a bit.

Some questions:

1. Is it enough to design systems and procedures that allow roleplaying to occur and trusting your players to follow their own instincts?

2. Is the act of roleplaying so central that it must be designed around? Or can it be firewalled away from system entirely?

    So, my preliminary (and semi-contradictory) thoughts:

    --I really don't like games that claim to be about roleplaying/storytelling but simply do so by tacking it on. I'm looking here at Lunch Money and Gloom. IMHO, if you can play the game in a satisfying way without roleplaying, then it's not *about* roleplaying. As such, any roleplaying that comes out of such games tends to be unsatisfying, or at least disconnected from the actual events of play.

    --I once dressed up as a Servant of Cthulhu to go to an Illuminati tournament. Was that roleplaying? And if I sat at the table, muttering "Cthulhu fhtagn", am I roleplaying? I think so. So, it's possible to tack roleplaying on to almost any game and have it be a fun part of the broader activity.

    So, I'd answer your questions as follows:

    1) Yes, so long as those systems and procedures function without the roleplaying.

    2) If you want the game to be about roleplaying, then yes, you need to design for it. Otherwise, clever players can bring it in anywhere, but it won't really be central or (likely) significant to the game being played.

    \n A good answer to these questions would have to tie together all of the things that have been said about immersion, stance, "parlor narration," and other ways of describing the relationship between game-play and the game-fiction. You can imagine a continuum in which one end is occupied by abstract games like chess or go (in which a move has tactical value but no referential import--it stands for nothing but itself) and the other by free-form games (in which a "move" is pure symbol; it stands for something in the game-fiction but is not bound by anything at the game-table). The ground in the middle--which is what we're talking about--is harder to make sense of.

    If we start with the notion of parlor narration, which I take to mean adding fictional color to a self-contained game-mechanical system (so that the "roleplaying" is a dispensable adjunct to play), what do you have to do to make the roleplaying an essential component?

    I think I'm playing with this in The Perilous Realm, a game I've just finished drafting. The game-play is all about dealing with the ebb and flow of your bodily humours, with an eye toward how that will influence both the end-game and your own individual capabilities. But there are two places where roleplaying is built in to the game, explicitly: when your humours are unbalanced, you have to discard a card or roleplay the imbalance, and when your character is in a building scene (a "prep") you have to pick someone to roleplay with you and then declare what the outcome of your prep was at the end of scene.

    The answer implicit in the game is that roleplaying isn't automatic; it has to be built into the game if you want it to happen. And the way it does that is by trying to link game-mechanical outcomes to "symbolic" moves.

    \n Its also hard to really judge what the best course is without having knowledge of the material you're dealing with. That's not a cry for you to divulge anything, just the issue I deal with when trying to think about this topic in the framework you're dealing with. What I mean is, I can't think of a boardgame I've ever played that really pushed me to want to roleplay or act in "character". One of the problems behind that is that there is such a finite amount of complication within most boardgames that it seems that roleplaying those out would get pretty repetitive. There can be numerous combinations of tactical complications that make for different gameplay each time but the roleplay would still be limited to the underlying core complications that are repeated.

    I think you increase the grist for the roleplaying mill by injecting your game with highly charged game situations where the decisions being made actually lend themselves to being expressed through roleplay. The more drama inducing decision points that the game has the more likely you'll find folks getting into character to portray it.

    If you however want to have roleplay of the nature that actually drives and helps to create the game as it happens, you'll need some mechanics to outline how the game is affected by the players wishes. I think that's where part of the "cool" comes from in roleplaying, knowing that you're no longer limited to a set of finite complications but instead have a means for interacting with and defining the direction of the game in a very real way.

    \n Ok, I'm going to start from the premise that you can't just mandate roleplaying because that would be lame. So if you can't _force_ it to happen, you want to encourage it, and that means you need to provide incentives for doing so, or disincentives for not. Now, the disincentive angle doesn't appeal to me too much, so I'm going to swing in from the incentive angle and say that the examples and ideas are just as easily inverted if you want.

    Ok, so I want to provide incentives for roleplaying. That's a tough place to be because I now need to decide what atomic actions, present in the game, give me opportunities for that. Worse still, I am faced with the question of what roleplaying _is_, and as we all know that is a painful debate with no right answer. And that means i can sit arond and try to come up with an answer that fits every definition of roleplaying, or i could say to hell with it and just accept my model will be imperfect.

    So given that, I have a few models to draw from. First, let's assume I wanted to incentivize some other behavior at the table - let's say profanity. If I swear during my turn I get a cookie (Which is to say, a card, a reroll, a bonus, an extra turn - whatever it is, something mechanically meaningful). Now, this is pretty clear cut whether I've done it or not, so It polices itself well - If I violate this and try to take the cookie without swearing, one of the other players will call me on it. This is less formal than making them the arbiters of whether i swore enough or not, they are simply a check against abuse, as is the case in most games.

    Now, if I codify a few behaviors as roleplaying - like speaking in character or describing things with color - then that's easy to plug into the rules, and rules like this already exist in some RPGs, from Over the Edge's 'Take a penalty if you're boring' disincentive to the incentive of stunt bonuses from Exalted and other systems. Now, I would simplify it - exalted's system breaks in places because it's tiered, so there's also a question of how well you came up with stuff, and some arbitration on how good or bad it was, and that decision is non trivial. However, the decision of whether or not yo u did it at all is far more trivial.

    So that's a start. Now, you could jazz this up some, make the rest of the table a more active audience, have a robust fan mail style economy and such, but that has a basic fallacy in that it depends on the table as a whole being inclined to RP, and since we're depending upon rules, that's not much of a solution.

    Anyway, what we have so far makes it easy to introduce roleplaying into a game. It adds funny voices and cool descriptions, and that may be all that's _really_ needed. But my know it when I see it feels like this falls short because there is no depth to it. There is nothing mandating any sort of continuity or connection between these bursts of roleplaying.

    There's a definition of roleplaying which I , while far from comprehensive, covers a nice broad swath with the idea that roleplaying means making non-optimal mechanical choices to support your RP priorities. Now, what those priorities are can be hotly debated, so right now we will just call them doughnuts. So when faced with the option to protect the queen or interrupt that mage's spellcasting, where interrupting the mage is a much smarter idea, I protect the queen because doughnuts are more important to me than that tactical advantage. That is, in the abstract, an RP choice.

    There are a lot of ways to take this, but at its simplest level, this is not hard to codify in any system where accomplishments can be measured. Any time you can make an optimal choice, but you make a less optimal one for doughnuts, you get a cookie. Probably requires a bit more feedback from the table to check for bullshit, but for the most part, this is still pretty easy to judge.

    But this is where it starts getting crazy, and the cracks in the big system show. Doughnuts do not exist in a vacuum. There is some rewards within the doughnut which the character is choosing over the optimized path, and every time he chooses the doughnut, he is promoting that agenda and moving towards that reward.

    Which suggests a parallel reward system exists (not unlike the practical/ethical split presented earlier in this) which is, to be frank, usually poorly systemized at best. And that's not necessarily a bad thing, because it's the part that is usually the most dependent upon human behavior (since the doughnut is usually either an internal issue or tied to NPCs). Now, such a system could be systemized - when I chose the doughnut, I get milk instead of cookies, and milk can be accumulated towards my larger, doughnut related goals. But if that system is too transparent, then it stops being about the doughnut, and becomes all about the Milk - the doughnut is just a means to an end, and since RP lies at the empty center of that doughnut, I have just overshot my goal of seeking roleplay, and come back down to a purely mechanical exercise, and I have to start all over again.

    I don't know that this cycle is perpetually doomed, but it mostly suggests that roleplay is something give tools for, leave room for, then leave the hell alone after a certain point.

    Now I'm hungry.

    -Rob D.

      Not to be a lamer, but the only part of those three that *might* generate roleplaying is the third, and that third element could be dropped as readily as dropping the use of an outrageous Scottish accent when playing a D&D Dwarf.

      Look at Long Live the King: personal goals (no need for secrecy, but that's neither here nor there), and enough goal overlap (actually, enough initial reason to ally, early in the game) that people will collude. But do they have to use English accents or wear Renwear or do a bunch of melodramatically hand-wringing, if the Ambassador? Not at all--in fact, the "roles" are so generalized that one could drift LLTK to any hierarchical organization: Mafia, Corporation, Priesthood, etc. And though the functional roles don't change one jot, the acting which one may or may not do would change radically. Or, again, could be totally ignored: Mafia LLTK with no Sicilian accents or bad suits; Color without reciprocated Acting.

      The closest I've seen System come to actually enforcing role playing is Trait evocation in Dogs. And even then, it's enforced on the level of the social contract, by the players and GM calling BS on weak/invalid evocations or pushing each other to act-out the evocation, rather than just say, "OK, it's a gunfight; I evoke Sharpshooter 2d10." The Point of Play is that inter-player, social stroking of "cool narrative" and "wow" moments, when someone takes a boring Trait like Sharpshooter and evokes it into a seemingly inappropriate situation with no objections.

      So think more about what it means to you, vis a vis what the Point of Play is for your new project. Do you want/have merely functional roles (goal-based identities; the "UN Ambassador to the US" mentioned above; the Brick fighter in a standard D&D tactical grouping); or do you have mechanics which reinforce and/or reward acted roles?

      \n I wanted to jump into this thread to say exactly what Josh BR said. I'm also very tired and out of it, so I hope this post makes sense to somebody. I'm just going to brainstorm a little...

      One way to force/encourage roleplaying (however you want to define it) is to design your game in one of two ways:

      1. The game is not fun unless you roleplay.

      Make sure the game doesn't provide anything that's fun, fulfilling, or challenging on its own. I think GURPS would be a good example of this (with the possible exception of character creation). If you're not roleplaying, what are you doing? There's no other real activity in there.

      2. Doing stuff in the game (i.e. interacting with the rules/system) does not make sense or cannot be communicated to the other players without roleplaying.

      Examples include games like "It's Complicated" or maybe a game where you combine names or elements, with the result creating some sort of narrative structure. For instance, you combine fragments of sentences together to tell a story.

      --If there's nothing to do in play except create and enjoy the imaginary "stuff", then that's what people will do, or they won't play the game. (Again, this is like the GURPS example.)
      --If important decisions must be made during play, and cannot be made without roleplaying, people will have to roleplay. (This is like the Dogs example--the rule is that the narration must make sense, which can only be judged within some sort of roleplaying-like context.)

      One example might be some kind of advanced tactical combat rules (as one would find in, say, GURPS). If you try to play out a combat, you'll HAVE to define who's involved, possibly where they are. If someone takes a "trip" maneuver, or a "knee to the groin" maneuver, you begin to define some fictional space where stuff is happening. Someone has just been tripped, or kneed, or whatever. Some made-up story is taking shape.

      Second example: Imagine you're playing a game where one of the things you can do is spend some sort of points for a bonus. But the rule is: you are only allowed to spend those points if the target of your action is someone your character is in love with. If the player wants to spend those points, the group first has to decide who those two people are, and whether one is in love with the other. This is roleplaying if your definition of roleplaying is something like. "making decisions for, or about, a fictional character and their actions/beliefs".

      The other thought this brings us for me is:

      If you can make some rules (a subsystem of the game, for instance) that is just plain _interesting_, that may motivate players to roleplay. If something is cool enough that they want to try it, but they can't without making up some characters, situation, etc, then they'll have to, won't they? For this to work, you have to make sure that some aspects of the imaginary stuff are necessary for the rules--for instance, your game has some detailed Car Chase mechanics. But, before the race starts, you have to decide, "Is the ground wet?" "Does the driver hate the driver in front of him?" If that determines how the rules will interact, you're well on the way to roleplaying/story gaming.

      Do you know what I mean? Have you ever picked up a game in an RPG shop, and just thought, "those Space Combat rules sound cool! I've got to try them!"

      Note that for something like my example, above, to work, it should probably be explicit that those important decisions have to "stick" and can't just be changed nilly-willy. For instance, imagine playing Monopoly with an optional rule that you can avoid paying rent if your character hates the character who owns that street/hotel. But the decision has to stick for the rest of the game, or until some other rule comes into play and changes it. If the argument between the two characters involves some stuff that is not hard-coded into the rules, but important for their adjudication, I think it would be hard to avoid roleplaying (at least by some definitions). For instance, the rule could be, "describe your character's belongings, and how they were acquired. The other player can pick any number of belongings and describe how his character now owns them." For it to work, the belongings then have to tie back into the regular game.

      Yeah, crappy example. But it's just for the sake of illustration.

      \n Some academics (e.g. George Lakoff, _Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things_) are noting that creating definitions that try to completely encompass a set is a futile act -- it's not the way people use language. Consider "mother": apparently a simple word. But can you define it cleanly? It is possible to describe someone as a "mother" who did not give birth to a child, or did not raise a child, or did not emotionally support a child; although probably not all three. "Female" seems to be required, though.
      \n
      \nInstead, it's worth thinking about "prototypical" versions of something (e.g. a prototypical mother would give birth, raise, and support a child). There are set members which are not prototypical (an ostrich is definitely a bird, but not a prototypical bird), and there are items which are not clearly in or out of a set (sometime in history, there was something like a pterodactyl was that neither a bird, nor a non-bird).
      \n
      \nGiven this thinking, rather than try to cleanly "define" the category of RPGs (lists of necessary/sufficient qualities), it might be more productive to list RPG-ish qualities. The more a game has, the more prototypical it has. E.g.:
      \n Story narration
      \n Non-tactical decision-making

      Posted By: Moreno R.This touch on a question that interest me, but I never found an answer: who came up with the name "role-playing games" for these games, and where? It's my understanding that OD&D (1974) didn't use it (even as late as the AD&D player's handbook, the name is very seldom used. I don't think it's used more that a couple of times there, maybe less. It was much more used in other games like RQ at the time).
      First use of the term "role-playing" in a TSR D&D product was I believe toward the back of either the Greyhawk or Blackmoor supplement to original D&D, where it appears in a price list of TSR products.

      Traveller (1977) uses the term "role-playing game simulation" in the blurb on the back of the box.
      AD&D PHB (1978) uses the term "fantasy roleplaying games" in the introduction. I'm skeptical that RQ uses significantly more or less than AD&D since the term really isn't necessary outside of blurbs and introductions. Maybe RQ III does, but RQ II (1978) has a more straightforward approach, largely assuming the audience knows what it's getting into or will infer it directly from the rules themselves.

      Dave Weseley, who in the 1960's either invented or discovered the form of the modern RPG (depending on your perspective) when he did Braunstein, has an interview over at the Acaeum where he states he disliked the term "role-playing game" because role-playing already had connotations from improv theater and psychotherapy; he preferred "Adventure Game" but it didn't catch on in the sense he wanted. (The term did get used for a while to mean wargames + RPGs.)

      Looking at the Tome of Treasures website I see from some product lists included in early D&D sets that "swords and sorcery [war]gaming" was also experimented with along with the semi-famous "Fantastic Medieval Wargames". The 1977 6th printing of the white box has the aforementioned reference to "game of swords and sorcery role-playing" in the pricelist at the back of Men & Magic (the first booklet), while the 5th printing (1975) just has "swords and sorcery wargaming" in the corresponding location. Eldritch Wizardry (1976) uses the older formulation. Same applies to the 1975-76 printings of Blackmoor, and up to the April, 1977 3rd printing.

      So it seems that TSR began using the term no earlier than 1976 and no later than 1977, and it's probably the vagaries of printing timing that determined exactly which product it appeared in first--definitely not the first printing of any of the OD&D + supplements.

      Aside from that I think I've been told that the term appears in the Strategic Review, the predecessor to the Dragon, which was published 1975-76.

      [EDIT: rereading the data, I think we can pretty definitely narrow the year down to 1977 for the first use of "role-playing" in the D&D price lists at the back of the D&D books. I.e., 6th printing White Box (1977) uses the term, but 3rd printing Blackmoor (1977) doesn't. Whether it appears inside the text of any of the books--probably not, but I'm not 100% certain.]

Is the reason for preferring inclusiveness over exclusiveness that it just keeps people from yelling at each other? Or is there some other benefit?

      Komradebob does got it, except I'd add: the line between RPGs and wargames is just as arbitrary and invites exactly the same sort of deconstruction. Back in the 70's, "adventure gaming" was purposed to mean pretty much "all those unusual games that broke the traditional board/card game mold". It included wargames, sports games, RPGs, and funky games like Acquire, Cosmic Encounter, or Quirks. For me at least there was also an overlap with things like Conway's game of Life. These were all innovative forms of play, largely linked by an audience of geeks and grinds--with a strong indoorsiness I might add. The divergence was as much a product of the audience as it was of structural qualities. RPGs attracted an influx of non-grinds and \xfcbergeeks, consequently became less grind-y, and the cultures split apart and diverged. Years later a similar process occurred with Euros, so that looking back we might now see some of those 70's games such as Acquire and realize they'd be considered Euros if they were published today. (My experience is that the audience for Euros is weighted toward people with Engineering Personalities.)

      But back then it was all the same stuff.

      \n I want to begin by saying that I am probably the last person who should be writing this post, because I have a limited exposure to RPG theory in general and I have only played a handful of games myself. I am open to constructive criticism, suggestions, and comments in general. Forgive my arrogance.
      \n
      \nResisting a definition of an RPG only posits it as a non-reality. Everything that exists has a definition.
      \n
      \nI would alter Eliot's list a bit by taking a different perspective.
      \n
      \nWhat is an RPG? When we define something we have to look at the most pure and simple form of the thing and identify its parts.
      \n
      \nIt seems to me that the absolute bare minimum components that are required for something to be considered an RPG are five in number. The first is group. If you are sitting by yourself, it doesn't seem to be role-playing, but rather authorship or daydreaming. Your writing or thinking by yourself. RPG video-games still include \u201cthe other\u201d in the sense that the game writers and designers are not the player and something outside of himself is involved in the game. It is not a solo activity. The second component would be progression. Quod Scripsi, Scripsi, what is written is written. If you can go back and rewrite or represent a previous account, it becomes a sort of collaborative story-telling/ writing. You can't move backwards. It has to be in a sense \u201clive.\u201d Even play-by-post RPGs follow this rule. Game mechanics that allow a player to \u201cre-do\u201d something can be considered cohesive in a sense that the previous action never really entered into \u201cshared imagined space.\u201d (Forgive me if I am misusing this term!) Freedom is the third component. If we aren't free to choose our actions in game, we are either actors on a stage with a script or simply watching a movie. Both can be rewarding, but neither seem to be role-playing. The fourth component is story. Without a story, we are simply playing chess or another board game. I will reference this as Ron did: \u201ca series of caused events.\u201d The last component is system. Without system, we are merely competing egos in a stream of consciousness.
      \n
      \n1.Group
      \n2.Progression
      \n3.Freedom
      \n4.Story
      \n5.System
      \n
      \nThese are the five components that, if one is missing, role-playing ceases. I have explanations of the terms written out if anybody wants them.
