\n So, last Saturday was my first session of Marvel Heroic Roleplaying. It was a fun, but rough, ride.

Here are the facts. I was Watcher, and I had three players. We played the first Act of "Breakout", and the players chose Captain America, Wolverine, and Iron Man as their heroes. They faced Count Nefaria, the mob of villains, and Living Laser. The preamble and defeating those three opponents took us about four hours of play.

We began with Cap on his way to DC in a helicopter, Iron Man at a fundraiser on Long Island, and Wolverine at a dock on the East River; all as suggested in the Event text. Cap's helicopter took a hit of lightning and had to land on the Raft helipad. Iron Man suited up once the blackout hit and flew over to the Raft. Wolverine, alone with no X-Jet, opted to swim to the Raft; we did an action roll vs. the doom pool, and he passed it pretty handily.

Once all our heroes were at the Raft, it was a pretty straight-up fight vs the above villains. Nefaria appeared first along with the mob; Living Laser showed up a round or two later.

Overall, I made the key mistakes of: 1) not following John Stavropoulos' Watcher Cheat Sheet, and 2) not paying enough attention to the details of the Event (e.g., there were boats at the dock Wolverine could have used, I didn't make Scene Distinctions clear enough, etc.). I biffed a lot of rules, so we had a lot of discussion bookending most of the die-rolling.

Since I did not adequately explain stunts, complications, and assets, the battle saw players rolling the same combos over and over again. They questioned why Cap would ever do anything other than throw his shield, for example.

Overall, their impression was that the game moved pretty slow. They advised me later that I should have made less use of the Doom Pool initially, letting it build up for use later so that things would move faster at the beginning. Not sure I can fault them on that, honestly. I was just trying to get used to the rules.

And this happened about five minutes in...

Watcher: "Okay, Wolverine, you pull yourself up from the East River on to the landing pad at the Raft. Captain America is there and you see a number of S.H.I.E.L.D. agents."

Cap: "Hey, Wolverine! Do you want to join the New Avengers?"

Wolverine: "Sure."

Cap: "10xp, please."

This was pretty indicative of how my players felt about Milestones. We even had a protracted email debate afterwards where they made the case that Milestones were inherently "bad design" and "got in the way of the story". E.g., "What if one player's Milestone makes them take actions that prevent another player from hitting their Milestone? That's broken." While I agreed that many of the provided Milestones in the book are very weak, I didn't think that invalidated Milestones as a concept. Still, it made me wonder how other gamers raised on trad RPGs are going to feel about Milestones.

Anyway, while I'm bummed that this group now has no interest in ever playing MHRP again, I do feel a lot more confident about running the same Event at Gameday 31. I am definitely going to: 1) use John's sheet more strictly, 2) pay better attention to the Event details, and 3) teach the rules more through examples in play than any sort of info-dump at the start of the session.

    It wasn't till my 4th game (each with mostly different people) that I felt I did an ok job GMing. I suspect you will do an even better job in less time!

    Although to be fair, the act of creating cheat sheets helped me internalize the rules. Those first 3 games I did so many things wrong! Actually, thinking back, even that 4th game had plenty of mistakes!

    But once you get past the initial learning curve the game becomes RIDICULOUSLY easy. I wish there wasn't such a learning curve (and the text was clearer in parts and easier to reference). But now I feel I can run this game with almost no effort. It's to the point where I've been able to turn players into GMs by the middle of the game and then walk away and read a comic book! But the ride to that point was bumpy.

    I'm hoping my experience will mean you'll have less work to do.


    If someone has played Cortex+ games before, Marvel Heroic will feel familiar. But if they're used to Mutants & Masterminds, Marvel FASERIP, or Champions... oh man, culture clash! That said, all the people I played with who had no RPG experience seemed ok with everything.

    Two Champions GMs I ran for HATED, absolutely hated Marvel Heroic. But then a week later they were asking if they could play again now that they realigned their expectations. One even bought the game!

    But no game is for everyone. Even if the result of your game is that someone decides the game isn't for them, you've done them a service!


    Another light at the end of the tunnel... the Villain Creation rules are amazing and easy! I'm working on a cheat sheet so that I can make characters on the fly mid-game. They are wonderful tools for improv gaming.

    \n For the record, Buzz, I had stunts, complications and assets explained to me and ran through a combat and I would give exactly the same critique as your group.

    You've got this German bean-counting math board game every round (sometimes you want d10, sometimes you want 2d8, elsewhere, sometimes you want d8 sometimes you want d4+1 plot point which you then spend to get a d8--you have to care which). During or after the counting of the beans, you turn that into story. It's slow.

    Unless you love that process--which I'm sure some people somewhere do--it's tough going.

    And, unlike a lot of games you do have to know all the rules just to play--huge barrier.

    Every round, if you're Captain America, you really do want to get that d12 for the hardness of his shield into play--yes, you have to manipulate the story to bring it into play in a different way every round, but that's not everyone's idea of fun.

    However, I would like to try it again.

    Marvel Heroic Is A Difficult Game.

    \n One question -- my sense of a lot of games in the Marvel Heroic vein (like, uh, Danger Patrol, to pick a totally non-random example) is that you're only supposed to approach them semi-tactically, that is: you're not meant to always push for the biggest chance of winning and so forth. Instead, things are kinda... tactically emergent, if that makes any sense. It's kinda like "play to find out what happens" but from a mechanical standpoint. Like: do whatever it is Captain American does in this situation (guided by a few signposts like milestones and stats) and figure out what happens based on the mechanics, rather than always tailoring your action to get the most bang for your buck. Your drive for excitement and sense of character are supposed to be the main motivation for your fictional choices, not the mechanical bonuses, but the bonuses are written to support you and generally back up the kinds of things you're likely to want to do as Captain America.

    And then sometimes, when it's REALLY IMPORTANT that you succeed (big stakes, somebody's injured, etc.), you look down at your sheet and try to figure out where that extra die or two is coming from (like you constantly do in Burning Wheel). But that's not intended to be the default way that you approach the game.

    Is that potentially part of the disconnect? Is Marvel Heroic really in that vein (I suspect it is, from what I've read so far)?

    \n I think Marvel works well, even when you power game. Power gaming in Marvel is fun!

    I love it when players grab as many dice as possible. In most cases, grabbing those dice reinforces the character they are playing (Captain America Leading By Example D8, flipping around with Enhanced Reflexes D8, using his shield as a Weapon D8, and using his Combat Mastery D10 to take down Villains is awesome). And more dice means they may win now but it also means they are more likely to roll 1s which grows the Doom Pool! As the Doom Pool grows, the story gets much more tense and exiting. And if the Doom Pool grows to 2D12, the GM can end the scene before you have a chance to accomplish your goals!

    A problem with Milestones is certain Milestones are written poorly. But some are amazing. Take Iron Man's BLEEDING EDGE TECH:

    - 1 XP when you first shutdown a power.
    - 3 XP when you\u2019ve shutdown all the powers in the Powered Armor power set, or you recover your Weapons Platform power set after using your EMP SFX.
    - 10 XP when you choose to abandon your armor or defeat a villain with all powers shutdown.

    We had a player Power Game the above Milestone and it made for amazing fun!

    But Captain America's "when you either convince a hero to join a new Avengers team or disband your existing team" 10 XP Milestone makes a lot of sense in terms of following the Breakout Event which mimics what happened in the comics but has felt strange in play as an RPG. What works in comics doesn't always work as an RPG. Iron Man's Milestone works great for game play and emulating comics. Captain America's has felt cheesy. I know it is supposed to be a big deal, and it is in the comic, but not at the gaming table.

      Posted By: Zak SI am not assuming this is how Heroic has to work. Jenskot is, I believe, saying it does not.
      \nI'm not saying that Marvel Heroic does not have beancounting as you describe it above. If I said this or gave this impression, I miscommunicated. What I am saying (or intended to say) is that I have run Marvel Heroic for players who love beancounting, and for those who don't care about beancounting, and it worked reasonably well.

      For example, I've had players who get all excited by the idea that they can micromanage their resources and split their Specialty D10 die into 2d8. I've had other players who don't want to do that, so they don't. Personally I wish their was a fictional reason for it. Since it's tied to your Specialty, I see it as working patiently and carefully but maybe that's me stretching to find a justification. My default preference is for the mechanics to be tied to the fiction. Generally playing with mixed groups has been more work for me as a GM, but has worked well. With 1 exception!

      If you don't use Plot Points you've earned, it can be a significant disadvantage. That said, I've had players who have both never read a comic, and never played an RPG, and they had no problem with Plot Point as long as I was there to help them. For example, I'll say... you defended so well, and momentum is on your side (I often describe Plot Points as momentum), you can spend a Plot Point to counter attack right now! Usually people get all excited and jump at the chance. Then they get frustrated when they don't have Plot Points and want more!

      I have to also say, we've had plenty of confusion over the name Plot Points. You can't edit the Plot with them. It makes players who dislike narrative control outside their character nervous, and confuses new players (so far). A lot of comic book fans don't think of comics in terms of plot. Karma as a name made more sense (to me). Usually saying Power Points or Momentum has solved the problem when it comes up.

      \n When I designed the hero datafiles in the Basic Game (Cap, Iron Man, et al) I tried to provide a number of different "ways to play" despite the fact that every hero takes care of things in more or less the same way (grab dice from categories on sheet, roll dice, pick total and effect, etc).Iron Man's SFX and Milestones work to make him a more resource management character. Luke Cage is great for direct play with safety nets. Mr Fantastic rewards people who love to game the system. Spider-Man is exactly what it says on the box.

      I've had people play one hero and then try another and suddenly enjoy the game a lot more. Others love it or hate it equally. But I did put a fair bit of thought to how these bits fit together, especially with SFX, and how some "builds" are more encouraging of certain kinds of play than others.

      Kind of like wizards vs fighters vs clerics vs monks in D&D.
