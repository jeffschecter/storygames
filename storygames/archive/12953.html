\n Film nerds assemble!

I'm putting together a filmography consisting of movies that represent softer fiascos and I'd love your suggestions. The best examples are ensemble pieces involving stupid disasters and embarrasing tomfoolery in which people probably do not die unless they are old and it is funny.

If someone gets handcuffed to a toilet it might belong. If someone gets handcuffed to a toilet and drenched in muriatic acid, it does not belong.

Dumb high school caper comedies belong. Ten Things I Hate About You is in, Brick is out (it is great but too hard edged).

I'd especially love some wedding-themed examples and non-contemporary examples (Buster Keaton's Steamboat Bill, Jr. works.)

Thanks!

    \n Part of the reason I'm asking is because I am not well versed in the sub-genre/s. I'm rebuilding the Tilt and Aftermath to handle, say, John Hughes movies.

    * Ensemble cast, at least four characters you could imagine being Fiasco protagonists
    * Ill-considered adventures at the intersection of status, fear, and lust.
    * A lighter tone that does not preclude violence or mayhem, but tempers it with humor.

    That last point is important, because if a film is a vicious, brutal black comedy, the core game works just fine and the original mediography is sufficient to guide people toward edifying entertainment.

    \n I'd like to help, but I have a concern/confusion that might be "too meta": Would someone please distinguish any movie that follows the traditional comedic arc--starts in tension but OK, then shit goes down hill fast, then a twist helps to get back to starting happiness or higher--from a fiasco? I look at a lot of the suggestions above and can't fathom why they qualify as fiascoes and not just bog-standard comedic arcs.

    When in such doubt, I turn to The Authorities (dictionaries):
    fiasco (n.)
    1a. (M-W) - a complete failure.
    1b. (Wiktionary) - a ludicrous or humiliating failure. Some effort that went quite wrong.
    2. (Wikipedia) - the straw-covered flask historically associated with Chianti. Never mind that one.

    OK, so 90% of comedies are NOT complete failures--quite the opposite. Humiliation can be in the offing in a comedy, but it's not requisite; and often, there's non-diagetic 'humiliation'--we might, as viewers, find something cringe-worthy while the character is blithely content with him- or herself--viz Michael Scott, The Office).

    [Oh, yeah, by the way, Jason: The Office. Most episodes are Epic Fails on Michael's or one of the supporting characters' parts. And Dwight is the perfect example of someone accumulating a ton of black dice to roll for a positive Aftermath.]

    Conversely, a "mere" tragedy is not by default a fiasco--there's something more than the failure involved (the "complete," the "ludicrous," the 'humiliating"). And those adjectival elements seem to suggest comedy (though only ludicrous *should*, and even then not exclusively).

    Seems to me, then, that a fiasco sits somewhere between traditional comedy and tragedy, with an additional element to bring the "WTF?" moments (both laughable and shocking). And (again) I'm not seeing that bridge, that balance, in most of the above suggestions.

    Anyhow--not to derail, but I think the target is too broad, judging by the shots being fired. Can someone enlighten me; or is Fiasco really "The Screenplay System," at its heart?

    \n I have a hard time classifying a lot of these movies as fiascos, since I feel what makes a good fiasco is some kind of plan or scheme, that goes wrong, and the fun part is how the characters deals with these unintended consequences.
    \n
    \nSo, as much as I love movies like Blues Brothers and Fandango, I don't see where "the plan" goes off the track. Sure, Molly Ringwald gets a lot of crap dumped on her in Sixteen Candles, but I have a hard time seeing any kind of planning going on there, aside from hoping that the hunky guy notices her. But to each their own, I suppose.
    \n
    \nI don't know if this has been mentioned yet, but possibly in that same time frame, "Weird Science" fits the bill. The crooks in "Home Alone" definitely.
    \n
    \nAs far as older stuff, a lot of the old screwball comedies probably work well, ala "Some Like It Hot." In that vein I'd suggest:
    \n
    \n "The Front Page"/"His Girl Friday"/"Switching Channels" (re-makes of each other).
    \n
    \n"Arsenic and Old Lace" is what Fiasco would be based on it it was created in 1940. Old ladies who kill old men, mobsters, and a newlywed finding out about the truth of his aunts.
    \n
    \nAlong the same lines, the classic "Bringing Up Baby" would work, too. Two different leopards on the loose, lost dinosaur bones, people in and out of jail, and a million dollar endowment at stake.
    \n
    \n
    \nOn another note, I'd swear that "Kiss Kiss Bang Bang" was an actual Fiasco session merely filmed. While the people do dark things, it's quite funny in a more playful manner.
