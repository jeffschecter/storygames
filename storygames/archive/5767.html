    \n Okay, Willow.

    a) You have too many items on your list. Like, way too many items. You really shouldn't get past three, or five at the outside. Anything further, and the reader's eyes glaze over. What you can do is compound them (Nazi zeppelins on a collision course over Antarctica!), but even then, you probably can't get past three bits in each item (Nazis, crashing zeppelins, Antarctica). So my first bit of advice: pare your list down.

    b) What you have here is a big long list of color and content. You have nothing about the play experience. If you're selling a book, you need to sell its features and the benefits that it will give its consumers. You need to tell them that they will play this, and that the play experience will be... something. Is it fast-paced? Is it exciting? Is it crunchy-fiddly-complex, or easy-simple? I'm also a little bit of a fuddy-duddy here, but I strongly believe somewhere on your back cover the word game should appear, since that's what is being offered, right?

    \n See, this is tough, because I don't want to disparage Spirit of the Century or FATE, since my game wouldn't exist without them. (In fact, I highly recommend them both, in general, and specifically in the text.) And my target audience is people who like SotC and FATE already.

    On the other hand, I wrote Awesome Adventures because I felt SotC had some clunky parts and extraneous bits that got in the way of the fun. Character creation is faster (no stunts), combat is faster (no stress tracks), and play in general is faster (less crunchy hard-coded mechanics, like difficulty tables, and more emphasis on the feel of each difficulty level.)

    So, how do I emphasize what I feel I improved, without dumping on a game I love?
