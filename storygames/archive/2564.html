I've been giving a lot of thought to social combat in BESM lately, and have been testing out this system lately. Have at it with pointy sticks, if you will.

SOCIAL CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Step 1: Declare social action and set stakes

Declare your character's social action and what they hope to accomplish through it.

Extended example: "I tell the Nightmare Prince I love him to redeem him!" The magical girl Faye, the Nightmare Rouser, will attempt to redeem Alexander the Nightmare Prince (stakes) by declaring her undying love for her (action).

Step 2: Determine if a conflict occurs

The targeted character may allow the stakes, in which case there is no conflict and they "say yes," or resist, in which case they "roll dice."

Extended example: Alexander is not about to be redeemed that easily: a conflict does in fact occur and Faye's player and the GM must roll to resolve.

Step 3: Determine applicable Attributes and roll

Add all applicable Attributes to Soul for the acting character's roll and to Mind for the resisting character's roll.

Extended example: Faye has Appearance 3: Cute, and Seduction 2, and a Soul of 6, making a whopping 11. But Alex's mind of 7 and Composture (new skill, used to resist all social action) 5 means that he adds 12 to his roll.

Faye rolls a 10 for a total of 21. Alex flubs, rolling 4. for a total of 16. Faye's MoS is 7.

Step 4: Resisting character chooses to accept stakes or deduct energy

The resisting character may accept the acting characters stakes, or deduct the MOS of the roll from thier energy point total.

If they are unable to deduct energy points from thier pool equal to the MoS, then they must accept the stakes.

Extended example: Alexander must lose 7 energy points or be redeemed to the side of dreams. He elects to lose 7 energy points from his pool of 35 energy points (it's been a long battle and they've been throwing energy blasts) and now has 28.

Step 5: Edited needfully to say: The resisting character may counteract. Repeat steps one through five for them, as needed.

    \n You'll hate me for this. But what does "betray" mean?

    Using stakes in these sort of social conflicts is interesting. It seems to me you can either:

    1. Make them external actions ("If I win, he resigns his post at court"), which would work, but isn't as interesting.
    2. Make them internal actions ("If I win, he is filled with bitterness at the court"), which leads to interesting stuff, but leaves it vague as to what the character actually does.
    3. Make it somewhere in between ("If I win, he betrays the court"), which I personally I think could lead to clashes of player expectations:

    "But you said you'd betray the court!"
    "I did! I broke all the procedures when spoke out of turn!"
    "That's not betraying them."

    I bet Tony has answered all of this in a previous thread about Misery Bubblegum.
