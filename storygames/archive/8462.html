\n I know, I have like a million thread projects sitting here needing to be updated, why start another one? Well, a recent thread got me to thinking. How did I come to the belief that all games are toolkits for play/all RPG play is customization of written material/and so on? Yeah yeah, everyone's sick of hearing it. But actually I haven't told the story behind it before. So maybe you'll be interested. If you're not that's okay too.

Return with me now to the THRILLING DAYS OF YESTERYEAR

1989. Solidarnosc. Ted Bundy electrocuted. Salman Rushdie bounty. Keating Five. Batman. Pete Rose banned from baseball. George Bush is President, he bought cocaine across the street. Alien Nation. Quantum Leap. Baywatch. Seinfeld. COPS. Doogie Howser. Berlin Wall falls.

And young JDCorley attends 9th grade. High school. Hangs out with his gamer pals. We play D&D. Of course we do. We each have a stable of 3-4 characters that we bring out for various things. At one point we were over at a peripheral friend's house. We all have brought a random character and as it turns out we all brought thieves. (I think one guy had a houseruled thief-acrobat.) We had some reservations, but nobody was willing to back down on the character they brought - they were all "legal" characters, weren't they? Nobody had a problem when I played this character last week, right, so why now? We go through the scenario and it's a blast in the same way that watching silent comedies is a blast. People run into each other and fall down and it's funny. We were constantly laying traps, sneaking up, yelling like idiots to draw the monsters down into the traps, then ambushing them. And robbing each other.

At the end of the session we were hanging around yacking and someone mentioned "That was more like the Godfather than Lord of the Rings" and everyone laughed, it was such a ridiculous notion.

At home later, I started thinking, "what would it be like to run our D&D game more like the godfather and less like Lord of the Rings?" I even made a little list: family, established loyalty, outside threats, all thieves of various sorts...and I discovered the alignment system actually provided a lot of juice for plots based on backstabbery and loyalty - the lawful evil guy won't break his word to his underlings and won't ever rat to the cops but the neutral evil guy will every time he thinks he can get away with it and gain something from it. I drew up a few outlines for potential D&D crime games and set them aside for the future.

But it was at that game that I realized something - we had made virtually no changes to the rules of D&D. The only thing, the tiniest only thing we had done was to ignore the admonition about a balanced party mix, and that admonition said (basically) "just a thought, do what you want!" after it, so we didn't even feel like we were ignoring the game text at all. (This was super important to us because we were 9th grade males and therefore idiots.) "And," I reasoned, "they really all were completely 'street-legal' D&D characters." Nobody had cheated, nobody had made their character "wrong" in any way, they just all happened to be thieves. Nobody had colluded to make them all thieves, but so what if we had? We were still playing D&D, we were still following all the rules...

Yet the difference in experience between that and "regular" D&D play was so pronounced that I felt like I was playing in an entirely different game. Something so outrageously different that it wasn't even in the same ballpark. We spoke differently, planned differently, played differently in every single way.

Because I was a scientifically minded young lad, I wondered if this could be replicated.

So the next time we planned a get-together, I said "everyone bring your wizards, or make a wizard" and we did an all-wizard game about a dastardly insidious demonic presence at a beloved mentor's spellslinging academy. And holy shit, it was different AGAIN.

Now I'm really starting to wonder. Why the hell is "regular" D&D regular and why is this "irregular" D&D irregular? And then I started to think back to our "regular" D&D and compared a game I ran to a game my friend Brian ran. In my game the main badguy was a dragon - he had a few minions and a lot of magical protections but basically, it was all preparation and setup for One Giant Fight, comic book style. Brian had run a game in which there wasa giant horde of orcs and our characters' job was to blind the horde by wiping out scouts and outliers. (We incorporated a new goal of spreading disinformation to the horde as well based on what players were trying to do.) And those two games were completely different in feel and in play and it had nothing to do with the rules. The rules didn't change (or if they did, it wasn't remotely as significant as the changes in play experience)...so even "regular" D&D was so amazingly different with a different set of players that it was actually more different than a game where we actually changed a rule or two.

More, as they say, later.

    First off, authority's a big deal in any game group, right? Like, who decides on the final draft of the ongoing string of events in the game? What's the process? What are we agreeing is the bottom line? Well, we can draw that bottom line in all kinds of ways: We can agree it's up to the GM, or that it's a group consensus thing (even though it probably still resides with an alpha player), or whatever.

    Intersecting with that you've got credibility. Even though everyone has agreed to X arrangement (verbal agreement maybe, or implied by playing a particular game), in their dark little hearts they really believe Y: I know we said it's up to the GM, but I would rather look to the rules for an answer. Hence you've got the bad kind of rules-lawyering.

    I really think that's the fundamental disconnect between the Jason approach (every game is a toolkit, every act of play is customization) and the shall-we-say fundamentalist approach (RPGs are games like any other, intended play experience emerges when you follow the rules).

    For me at least, my personal authority/credibility settings vary by person and by game.

    Some games I find to be low-credibility games. I first try the rules as written, find they don't work or they don't produce the play I expect/want it to (or that I've been told it will produce), and then I start mucking around. Full disclosure: I find indie-designed games to have a low level of credibility for me.

    Now to complicate things: These self-same indie-designed games, the ones I grant low credibility? They're written in a way that assumes the rules will be granted a high level of authority: follow the RAW, the engine is so tightly integrated that customization is (deliberately?) difficult, etc. The problem is, when you've got authority without credibility, the whole thing falls apart.

    Anyway, my two cents.

Many years later I realized that we actually probably were holding this guy back from getting some kind of prize. We didn't actually grasp the competitive aspect of the tournament. We just thought we were there to play!

    This wasn't the last time I played with an "RPGA dude" either. We had one come to our group for a while (transfer student, military kid) and in contrast to cheese-smelling-Santa, he was just as open to goofing around even in competitive situations as we were, but never ever spoke in character, it was all "Elberon tells you he wants some jerky". Which was weird because we always switched back and forth. He told tales of the RPGA that sounded great, but again, they all sounded different. He even took me to a RPGA-only event and the game we played in was a lot more like our house game than it was like Santa Claus' ideal "shut up and cast your spell" game.

    "What the fuck is going on here?" thought young JD, not for the last time in this story. "The RPGA actually has real-live rules that are about making sure every game is as close to each other as possible. And they're still as different as night and day."

    I started getting sensitized to differences in game experiences. I noticed that if one person showed up who was comfortable with talking in a funny voice, everyone was comfortable with it, but if there wasn't that one person, nobody would do it. I noticed that two Monty Python fans was a very different experience than one or none. I could tell the difference between people who had read Tolkein and who hadn't, and people who had read Conan, and who hadn't, and people who had read The Screwtape Letters, and who hadn't. (It was one of the most popular books in our group and all our devils and demons were super smooth and...now that I think of it most of them actually wrote letters!)

    It got to the point where I could tell the difference between a dungeon crawl run by GM A with players B, C and D and a dungeon crawl run by GM A with players B, C, and E. Virtually all the actual game rules were exactly the same, but the experience...I started to see more and more differences everywhere I looked. And I could draw judgment calls on each of the differences! I liked funny voices but also liked when we used them for non-comedy things too. I was cool with "Elberon says..." for inconsequential conversations or when we just needed to get a piece of information to get us to the dungeon. I thought just saying "standard operating procedure" when we came to a door or a new stretch of dungeon corridor as a shortcut for searching for traps, listening for ambushes, and so on, was a cop-out. ("Either you think something's there, in which case you should describe what you're doing, or you don't, in which case you're fucked if something's there.") And each of these things provided a positive or a negative to the experience - and if enough positives lined up you had a great time and if enough negatives lined up you had a shitty time - all under the same ruleset, all doing "the same thing"...what the fuck was happening here?

    I think a long time before anyone ever said 'social contract!' I was getting some idea that social aspects of gaming had a big impact, but it was more than just social aspects. If we all showed up and wanted to fight, we fought a lot. If we all showed up and wanted to work on our strongholds and describe our hot half-elf girlfriends, the game was about castles and charmeuses. Those decisions were all about the content of the game, which was so broad as to permit all kinds of things, and which, naturally, we pushed even further.

    \n BATTLETECH

    Oh fuck yeah, you know you love it.

    Okay, maybe you don't. Actually there's a lot of very weird things about Battletech if you look back on it from the distance of 20 years. Battletech is a very Western look at what is essentially a Japanese genre - giant robots fighting. So much so that it is entirely divorced from the source material in virtually every way. There's no teenagers with colorful hair. There's no quasi-mystical hoohah. There's no familial drama (in the game itself).

    It also was the first game where I was struck with the disconnect between "color" and "game". Now, I still believe today that in the vast majority of cases the two are intertwingled so thoroughly that it's folly to try to pull them apart. And even Battletech would not have been Battletech if it had giant monsters fighting instead of giant robots fighting. But Battletech has an enormously detailed universe, with complex, well-written military histories of this far-future set of wars that crisscross the galaxy, and none of it, none of it matters even in the least to play. Even when they finally came out with the Mechwarrior RPG, the only thing that mattered was the mission, the scenario, the campaign (in the military sense of the word). This fascinating universe was made but nothing happened with it. Keep that in mind, because young JD sort of forgot it and he ran into a lot of problems because he forgot it.

    What I wanted to talk about with Battletech was some of the scenarios we played out.

    Now, Battletech can be played in many different ways, here are the various variables that you can use:

    * Map - is it preset by the scenario, picked by one side or the other, or randomly selected?
    * Forces - are they preset by the scenario, selected within a certain "tonnage" (i.e. your side has 1000 tons of giant robots, you can pick 3 100 ton robots, 4 25-ton robots, and so on), are they selected within a certain "price" (including building your own robot using the price structure given), or some combination
    * Victory conditions - are they preset by the scenario or just "wipe everyone on the other side out"

    And one day while hanging around playing Battletech I devised the smallest, cheapest mech known to mankind. Codenamed: The Putz, it clocked in at 10 tons (actually the parts were lighter, but the rules required that 10 tons is the minimum), it had a single gun and (a little fuzzy on this) I think it ran on an internal combustion engine (all other mechs used a fusion plant). The Putz II was a 15 ton mech, had a fusion engine, and a giant gun that, upon firing once, would overheat and destroy the mech carrying it. I decided I would use these two designs in the upcoming 500-ton-limit battle. After double and triple checking my math, the other players agreed that the Putz was a legal design. When, in my first turn, I moved 30 mechs onto the field and critical-hitted 7 times (just due to raw damage output, each successful hit from a Putz I barely hurt the target but there were so many firing that criticals were crippling them), the cries and bawwwww-ing of my good friends were sweet sweet music to my 15 year old ears. I had invented the Zerg rush long before Starcraft was ever invented. kekekekekekeke

    But on the way home that night, because I had always thought of Battletech as a fascinating universe and one I would love to roleplay in at some point (if I could but figure out what the fuck to DO there), I started comparing the Putz to some of the things that had happened in our D&D game. The Putz was "street-legal". There was no doubt about that. It had been repeatedly rechecked as I swarmed across the battlefield murdering everything (I'd say "like a swarm of locusts" but actually as I recall, Locusts were pretty cool semi-light mechs!) Just as the "group of thieves" was street-legal. Just as all the differing ways to play D&D were "legal".

    Even in Battletech, where inputs were highly constrained, in fact, where your input was limited to "what giant robots do you pick to fight", I had created something that completely changed the entire feeling of the game, the entire experience of play.

    I then thought about chess. At least in chess, I thought, the experience of play is always the same no matter which moves you make. You're just making moves on a chessboard. Even in Battletech you're imagining the missiles going off and the lasers going off (we made laser noises when we shot lasers - did you?) but chess is so abstract and SO limited that the experience has to be of the same "type". It can't be that different.

    And that was true.

    Until I actually started playing a lot of chess.

    I already did a fucking huge thread about my experiences with Vampire here and I always invite questions about the game there, because it's completely great, but for the purposes of this thread probably the most interesting thing was the urging, even in Vampire 1ed, to pick a "theme" for the game.     This was a new axis of customization I hadn't seen before. A Vampire game with a theme of "old vs. new" would be a very different thing than a Vampire game with a theme of "family crises". By picking a theme, explaining it to the players, and working it into the details of the campaign and the game world, the GM could create the stages for very different sorts of play experiences. When, sneakily, I changed the theme to "splatterpunk chainsaws", and discovered that the game functioned just as well, I was both highly impressed with the product - it still worked when I fucked with it - and my own cleverness. Of course I was in the 11th grade. It didn't take much for me to impress myself then.

    The essays at the back of the book on how to use the game were also helpful, though in a different way. See, by this time we had developed, if not fully and not explicitly, how we liked our games and house rules of a half dozen sorts. The essays in the back, contradictory as they were, conclusively put the last nail in the coffin containing the dead idea that the designer had some clue about the best way for our group to game, because for the most part we had no interest in the sorts of things the designers went on about. LARP? Haha, right. (Yet we did cheesy accents with gusto!) Multiple GMs? Uh, there's another way to do things?

    But let's be clear - I liked Vampire (I still do) - I liked the game these people had made, I enjoyed what they had given me and used it and used it and used it. The only possible conclusion was that game designers were a bit like idiot savants. They were capable of making really cool things but incapable of recognizing what was cool about them or using them in cool ways, or at least, incapable of doing these things as well as WE did.

    \n No, I didn't forget about this. Better than the last chapter, it's THE NEXT CHAPTER, entitled....

    THE INTARNET I mean the Internet

    I went off to college and discovered Usenet. Yes, you can find embarrassing posts from me there. So much for aspirations of public office!

    I am usually very skeptical of people identifying big turning points in gaming history. I snicker at them, I make nasty comments about them, in prediction threads I always say "nothing will change" and I am righter than everyone else, and more of a killjoy, every time. So I am going to be a hypocrite here, and identify something that changed not just for me, but for everyone:

    Usenet was the first time that game developers and their audience had a continuous conversation.

    In the past, we had talked only through letters columns, or the 5-10 minutes we could buttonhole them at conventions. All those "let me tell you about my character" conversations were actually leading up to something, some kind of commentary on the game they had created - and the reason that they were so painful was that the developer was aghast at the content the players had made and did not want to hear about it. So it was inevitable that when an actual conversation took place that it would immediately go badly. Game writers hated what people had done with their games - they had hated it all along - they just didn't know they hated it because there was no means of communicating it.

    Usenet was the first place that a sustained conversation could take place and everyone fucking hated everything everyone was doing with the game. Developers flamed gamers who posted "fixes" to games. They harangued people who misunderstood sections. Gamers yelled at developers for "mistakes". There was one point at which I was in the killfile of every single White Wolf writer who posted on Usenet, because I was relentlessly posting about White Wolf games in a way they didn't like at all. Even setting variations ("Wouldn't it be cool to play Changeling in a Cyberpunk 2020-style world where intellectual property and thus creativity are tightly controlled by media conglomerates?") were greeted with howls of frustration and anger from the ones that had actually written Changeling.

    Nobody had yet figured out that someone who constantly posts about a weakness in your game and all the things they did to fix, avoid or subvert it is a giant fan of your game who has sent you a pile of money. Developers expected consumers. What they discovered on Usenet were handypeople. "Thanks for the hammer and the instructions on how to build a doghouse, I made this great birdhouse with it!" "Birdhouses are lame, check out this sweet go-cart, by the by, the hammer is not so great!" "EXCUSE ME IT IS A DOGHOUSE HAMMER YOU ARE ALL STUPID I HATE YOU SHUT UP".

    Usenet was the first time I realized that game designers themselves have no idea what makes their game any good. They love parts of their game that are stupid. They think parts of their game are really important when I could care less about them. They completely overlook badass stuff.

    And the reason for this is that the things in a game that make it good are different for every group for which it is good - let alone the ones for which it is, on net, bad. The only possible value in a game is in play. That is the only place it matters, and play is so different, with such different goals and such different inputs, that the actual game text is a relatively blunt instrument. It's a canvas and paint, a typewriter, and it makes a big difference whether you're writing Moby Dick, or just, ya know, Ivanhoe. (Yes, I also learned Laurie Anderson in college.)

    Everything that has happened on the Internet since Usenet has proved me righter and righter and righter until my rightness shines like a burning beacon. In fact today, the smart developers will encourage people to post about how they fixed the game for their groups. They'll encourage setting customization and campaign-specific discussions. What if someone else runs into the same snag? What if someone else wants something similar in their game?

      Now, after I'd been on Usenet for a while I refined the old position of my old friends. You remember, they had laid virtually all the changes in the gameplay experience at the feet of the GM. I was not willing to do this, I put it on everyone (though of course the GM was proportionately more important than most of the players due to the increased decision-making power they had.) But one of the biggest draws for White Wolf fans around that time (and I was and am one) was this really detailed and evocative setting. I (as you can see) leaned more towards the evocation than the detail, but to the horrified persons who responded to the setting-crashers like myself we could always make a distinction: "Look, there's nothing about Mage that requires it to be set in 1997 America. This idea we're working on of playing it in Cuba in 1940 isn't changing the system, it's changing the setting."

      But then of course we had to change some minor parts of the system. ("Replace the Computer skill with another mental skill..." or whatever.) And doing so improved the adaptation of the new setting dramatically. Sometimes we would even do a GIANT change to the system as our centerpiece and then work backwards to the setting that system change got us to. There was a palpable feeling of difference between setting and system, though, and eventually this feeling came to be demonstrated to be absolute bullshit and completely made up in our heads. There were two things I experienced at about the same time, one theoretical, one practical:

      1 - a game writer, whose name I have forgotten, responded to one of our setting/system hacks with something along the lines of: "Look, I have no idea why you want to put together the game you're putting together here, but the game I wrote for was very specifically a modern-day portion of a larger game..." (and here he said Vampire or Mage or whatever it was.) "...if you set the game in 1989, or with six factions instead of seven, or with different superpower loadouts, you're really playing just as much a different game from what I wrote as if you played it with GURPS. I wrote what I wrote for the game that exists on paper. You're doing what you're doing for a game that you hope will exist at a table." And that distinction was, A) very insightful, quite mature, and a precursor to the more progressive position a lot of designers today take, and C) completely at odds with the idea that by changing the setting we weren't really changing "the game". Combining this with my previous experience that just by legal character creation choices the entire experience of gameplay can be forever altered, it was a huge eye-opener and solidified my theoretical views finally in their complete form. Everything you do in playing a game is customization, and the act of customization destroys the written setting and system equally. (So to belatedly answer CK's question, there's a distinction, but it is a distinction without a difference - the decision to use dice or go diceless in Vampire changes the game less than the decision to play a game in modern-day Seattle or 1801 Poland. There can be huge degrees of customization in setting but not system, and vice versa.)

      2 - White Wolf came out with a really great and underrated science fiction game, Trinity. I liked it a lot but was put out that there was only one skill for Science and a very detailed setting that could be better explored through more specialized researcher/investigator characters. So for the first time I turned to GURPS for an actual game rather than just for inspiration and being a "collection of bits", which actually I think summarizes what a lot of people did with GURPS books back then. Pawns move one space forward, they capture one space diagonally, and sometimes en passant, and on the first rank they have the opportunity of moving 2 squares forward instead. Rooks move horizontally and vertically, bishops diagonally, blah blah. You win by attacking and trapping the opponent's king.

      Shouldn't a game like chess, with its constrained inputs, be impervious to my previous thought that all play is inherently customization? As it turns out, the answer is no, not just "no", but "NO" in letters 400 miles high. I got to play a lot of chess in a lot of different contexts in a lot of different places. And I quickly found the rules of chess don't define all of the inputs into the play experience. If you're quietly playing a club match, that's one experience. If you're trash talking the shit out of your opponent in a street game for five dollars, that's another experience. If you're in a 3-round competitive match, that's another experience. Heck, just the decision whether to offer or accept a draw completely altered everything, when I moved from "let's play and see who wins" to the competitive scene.

I didn't like positional play, in which you crept forward bit by bit to try to conquer one square at a time with perfect defenses. I liked dynamic play, where you went diving in and tried to shake things up and create opportunities. Some openings were cool and fun, others were boring and lame. So I played the cool openings. Others in the club felt differently, they liked the lame openings - a matter of taste even here. So playing against them was a very different experience from playing against someone like myself. And that wasn't even taking into account skill level!

      Chess was an amazing experience, I highly recommend it to everyone.

      \n Some thoughts that struck me while reading this...

      Imagine five stereotypical middle school girls (A, B, C, D and E), all "friends". One day everyone but E are together. Early in the conversation, someone makes a catty comment about someone, and this provokes an enthusiastic reaction from the others. Because of this reaction, others are soon adding their own catty comments. Before long, they are having a grand old time, ripping people to shreds verbally.

      A few days later, E is in the group, but D is not. Early in the conversation, E mentions something about the desirability of a certain actor, and this provokes an enthusiastic reaction from the others. Because of this reaction, others are soon adding their own accolades for hot guys. Before long, they are having a grand old time, lustily pining for pin-ups.

      Even later, E and D are there, but C is not. Early in the conversation, A mentions something fairly erudite about a current world event and this provokes an enthusiastic reaction from the others. Because of this reaction, others are soon adding their insights on the troubles of the world. Before long, they are having a grand old time, solving Problems That Matter.

      Most social interaction (cocktail parties, cooler talk, watching the game, gaming) is like this: without anyone really meaning to, there is a sort of "cred" that is being competed for, but what exactly gains that cred is an emergent property of the interaction.

      This "emergent cred" has some interesting properties:

      Uses positive feedback. That is, gaining cred increases the desire to gain cred for all in the interaction and more deeply defines the sort of actions that result in getting cred. It also leads to an escalating standard, a one-up-man-ship, as time goes on (actions snowball, in other words). In your all-thieves game (probably), as people did cool/funny theify things, it led to people wanting to do more cool theify things, with the baseline for what was "cool/funny" increasing as people had more success with it.

      Sensitive to initial conditions. Usually, the first action that really "hits" with the group deeply colors the shape of the cred and how to win it. I'd bet that the first really cool thing that you can remember happening in the all-thief game is something that "set the tone" for the rest of what was cool about the game.

      Unconscious. While most people in a social situation are vying for the "cred", almost none actually realize they are doing this, and would probably deny doing so if questioned about it. This is one of the main reasons the nature of the cred changes so much from situation to situation: because people don't know they are doing it, it cannot be planned in advance.

      Subversive. Since it is not planned or realized, but happens anyway, vying for cred often undermines what might, at the outset, appear to be the "social contract". This is why the game as written often diverges from the game as played.

      Inscrutable. If the emergent cred-chasing was "fun", it might not be obvious why, and the experience itself might not translate well to people who were not there, particularly if it diverges from the emergent cred they were chasing when they played. Thus, you'd expect a lot of "those guys are mucking up my game".

      \n This is an interesting conversation to me.

      I mean, there are games, games have rules, focus, example text and usually a theme to follow ("Vampire games should be dark and moody games of personal drama and social games - Sez Designer").

      Then, there's this phenomenon. It's called "You're playing the game wrong." Or "You're not playing it as the designer intended".

      I don't see it leveled around here as much, but it is leveled.

      * Sometimes we all (including designer) view it as awesome: Using Game X for Setting Y.

      * Sometimes some people view it as awesome, while others view it as an abomination: See "How JD runs Primetime Adventures, with a GM role who /acts/ like a real Hollywood Producer, and not just simply a GM with another name". He's been called out for "Playing it wrong" by some horrified fans, while others think its edgy and awesome.

      * Sometimes only the person making the changes view it as awesome: Adding bullet tumbling/lightsabers/"realistic initiative" system to Dogs in the Vineyard, or whatever.

      There's a movement... well, nothing as organized as a movement, but it's kind of a thought I've seen shared around in the small press design community which says: "Let's try to write the rules in a way where people can reliably recreate this fun experience". But then people inevitably change or ignore those rules... Or they play in a way that isn't backed up by the text. Is it the same game? Again, some people, and sometimes even the designers, issue the "You're playing it wrong"/"You're not playing as intended" Yellow/Red cards. Ex: See the Adept Press forum, circa all the time (it's rarely ever Ron blowing that whistle, tho; usually fans).

      JD's coming from a view of "This is why you can and should expect people to Play Your Game Wrong, where "Your Game" is the game you designed as a designer, or a game you love as a player.
      From that point of view, he's kinda kicking the wheels as to how far you can expect someone to go before it no longer is the game that you designed (as designer) or played (as a fan in another group, who had a much different experience of the game). He's also kicking the wheels of how much of the "playing in a way not backed up by the text" is a responsibility of the designer, and how much they can expect/rely on people to play it differently anyway.

      Now, I'm not sure about the recent customization posts (haven't read through them all enough to settle in), but the concept is an interesting one, particularly something that designers should read, know and try to come to grips with when the first play reports come in from their game where the group didn't have fun; or the group played differently (by setting or rules or theme) than intended (and then did or did not have fun from there) when the designer's pen hit the paper in writing the game; or when two groups emerge with totally different experiences (good and bad; dark themed and light themed; funny and serious; etc).

      In the end, I don't think there's a right answer: This is like Nature vs Nurture, and trying to play devil's advocate for a 100% leaning on either side (Is it Nature or Nurture? Is the group responsible for a fun time or the designer?) is folly, but it's interesting to consider both angles when thinking about writing a complete game. Or as a designer when someone doesn't like your game after having tried it. Or in discussions of games where people's experiences with Your Favorite Game differ wildly from yours.

      Posted By: sageSituation #1: A player chooses that character and decides he'll pursue both, even when his son betrays him. He never buys off either.

      Situation #2: A player chooses that character but says, before play even starts, he isn't interested in the father/son thing too much, he buys off the key pretty early, but stays loyal to his old buddy to the last.

      Situation #3: A player chooses that character and buys off both keys. He takes the character in an entirely different direction.

      \nWell, all three are choices, right? So the designer didn't know which one they'd take, right? So the designer doesn't know what kind of play experience will result from a player choosing one or another, right, or at least, didn't tell us? Like, there's nothing that says "pick this way, it's the best!"?

      And we can agree that in all three cases, the player in question (or the other players) might have strong, or not so strong, aesthetic preferences about which one is the best one for the play experience? i.e. the player in #1 thinks, "Ugh, no, why would I resolve such a delicious dilemma, I'm keeping it forever and ever!" and as a result chooses as he does. Or perhaps the player to #1's left thinks, "Why is this idiot stringing us out on which way he's going to jump on this issue, does he not see this is completely ruining the story and his character?" And nothing in the text (it's been a while since I read/played Lady Blackbird, great game) says which way someone should choose, so we reach the following conclusions:

      A - The designer has no particular intent that the players take any of these actions in any of these Situations. The designer's intent is thus useless to the players in making this decision, it's already gone, we're past that, trampled right over it to get to these.
      B - Nevertheless the decision may be very significant to the experience of play, so significant in fact that it might make or break a game for someone who has narrow preferences and strong feelings on this subject.
      C - The decision gets made - in all three situations, the player takes action when the decision point comes up.
      D - The result gets into play through the normal operation of the rest of the game.

      I don't have a better word for this than customization. Nobody has yet offered me one that isn't too mechanical - remember, this decision point, while mechanically supported in the game, is not itself mechanized - the straightforward "application" of the rules won't make this decision for the player.

      But let me throw a couple of more Situations in that will help undermine my position much more than all the bikes in all the Queen songs ever written:

      Situation #4: It's someone's first time playing a game like Lady Blackbird and they just don't exactly get Keys. They buy off one, but are really not sure what that means.

      Situation #5: It's someone's second time playing Lady Blackbird and they kinda get Keys, but are really not sure how they fit into the fiction. They buy one off because they get that they're supposed to, and it has to do with their characters' decisions, but don't have a clear picture of how that feeds back into the game or the fiction.

      Situation #6: The player gets Keys, but really doesn't have a significant preference as to which way the character goes - either way will produce a fiction equally facile for them. They pick one at random and buy that one off.

      In these, there's a much stronger case to be made that no real customization is taking place. The player lacks the knowledge, skill, or desire to make this a conscious decision to maximize their own enjoyment. 4 and 5 are more akin to the Candyland player - along for the ride. 6 is an even bigger problem for my position. Someone without strong opinions as to which way something will go may have all the skills necessary to customize the outcome, but no real desire to do anything more than come along for the ride. What's worse is when 6 is sitting next to someone with narrower preferences. That can be a big problem for a group. By the way, it's a problem that's inherently beyond the reach of the designer. Replace the "Keys" aspect with any aspect of the game with player choice involved. See?

      Here is the takeaway from all this, and it really says very little about design except in passing:

      As players develop knowledge about the game and skill in the game, they will take action to make the game experience personally enjoyable to them. This is the best definition of player skill I can think of.

      Design stops well before play begins. Whatever the designer's intent, it's often opaque and is, except in Candyland situations, incomplete with respect to play.

      I'm really surprised to see all the worshipers at the church of design pissing on their altars and setting fire to their banners at the smallest provocation. I mean, saying "I went to all these different places and people were playing games in ludicrously different ways" and then adding "but they weren't, you know, CUSTOMIZING the game, let's not be silly JDC", that means the designers must have designed them to be played in many different ways, and they succeeded in their goal and are to be lauded and patted on the back instead of sneered at. We should be celebrating that amazing accomplishment! Imagine making a bike that with no customization could accomodate both a 300 pound obese man named Francis and ...wait, no, I still don't understand that metaphor.

      I mean, "incomplete" design? Lady Blackbird is incomplete because it doesn't tell you which Key to buy off. "Oh, JDC, you're so fucking stupid, you're the dumbest man on the Internet, don't you see that's the player's choice? Don't you see that the designer clearly intended for that to be a choice for the individual player, based on their aesthetic preferences for the fiction, at least once they understand what's going on mechanically?" Then, when GURPS told us to pick a premise for our campaign instead of delivering one, clearly intending for it to be a choice based on our aesthetic preferences for the fiction, once we understand what's going on mechanically... "THAT was an incomplete game, now you're getting it."

      I think the complication comes into play here:

      Your examples of customization range all over the map, from choosing a premise if the game doesn't provide one, to limiting the classes available to a game of D&D and sticking to the lower levels, to choosing a new strategy in a game of chess.

      I would offer that some of the se things are expected of any game and that your definition is so tautological as to be useless, while in other cases it might be a big deal to make a change to a game. And, further still, in some cases it might really screw up a game.

      In other words, there's always customization. But some customization is either:

      a) workable and expected: the chess example you gave; choosing a premise for a game if none is offered.

      b) maybe workable but maybe not expected: the D&D school for wizards game example you gave; deciding as a group that Sorcerer is all about playing horrible people who do one thing after another to drive their Humanity to zero. It's "maybe" workable, because if someone showed up wanting to play a fighter and level up as hard as he could, there'd be a clash of expectations of play. If everyone is on board, it works, if not, someone feels like he's being cheated of the chance to play D&D. In the Sorcerer example, as long as everyone is enjoying that, it's fine.... but doing that kind breaks so much of what the game is about and closes down many avenues of play that make it worth playing. "Customized" yes. Effective customization, maybe not.


      So, you've made a bunch of statements that can't be refuted, since yes, all play demands players having input into how to play.

      But you've glided over any problems that might arise from customization that doesn't work. All of your examples are full of unicorns and sunshine.       The issue is clouded because when "customization" works, it works great. And when it doesn't, it doesn't. And you're only focusing on the great.

Why am I buying a book that does nothing but give me permission to go walking?

      Purposeful design does all that work FOR you so you can get at the activity faster. But even that isn't quite enough. Because I can imagine a box labeled "For Walking" and in it are a pile of running shoes, a pile of comfortable flats, a guide book, some hand weights, some sweat pants, an umbrella, a pair of binoculars, a device for tracking speed and millage. Well, that's not particularly useful to me either. I have NO idea how to use all of those things in concert.

      At this point there are two options. You provide a guide book that tells you how to sort all that stuff for different effects. If you want EXERCISE then use the running shoes and the sweats. The weights and the device are optional but can be useful. Everything else is not useful for the purposes of exercise and will likely get in the way.

      My *preference* though is to have unique kits built for unique purposes. I want TWO boxes. One clearly labeled "Walking for Exercise" and "Walking for Sight Seeing." Each one containing ONLY the tools and instructions necessary for achieving the desired result. That way I can safely ignore one or the other if either exercise or sight seeing doesn't interest me. Or I purchase both and use as appropriate.

      That obviously doesn't mean I have no decisions to make. If I'm using the Walking For Sight Seeing box then I have to make choices about the guide book. I have to decide when and where to use the binoculars. I have to decide if the rain is heavy enough to use the umbrella. But the one thing I'm NOT doing is sitting there wondering what these weights have to do with Sight Seeing. Because the "buy in" for the activity and the tools provided for that buy in are 100% aligned.
