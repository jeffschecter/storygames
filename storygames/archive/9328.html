\n

\n
\n
\n
\n I was just thinking about that weird, fruitful time in the early to mid-eighties when there was a small press RPG diaspora. Many of the games that crawled into print back then are still around in some form, which is crazy and cool.

For whatever reason Detroit was a hotbed of small press publishing. Besides Palladium, the metro Detroit area was the birthplace of such great games as The Morrow Project and Fringeworthy. As an absurdly young kid I helped playtest these games at local conventions and gaming clubs. My brother and I spent $20, a fortune, to buy one of the 100 typewritten first edition copies of The Morrow Project, and we played the living hell out of it for years.

The very, very best things about The Morrow Project were an absurd fixation on realism (weapons were rated according to real-world data, including inches of penetration in white pine or ballistic gelatin) and the inclusion of Soviet ICBM targeting data that wasn't even available domestically. Your character had Blood Points.

Local company Tri-Tac games was super visionary - they produced a series of settings using the same unbelievably terrible core system, and each was genuinely unique. They did dimensional portal exploration years before Stargate, off-kilter paranormal hunting years before Ghostbusters, and hard-ish science fiction years before ... OK, nobody has ever really tried that again. Transhuman Space, maybe. They even had their own little home-brewed supplement treadmill, with game-universe crossovers. This all peaked around 1984 I think. Dot matrix typeset, GBC bound, truly labors of love. Really cool stuff.

So if Detroit had this going on, what happened around the same time in your neck of the woods? Was there a comparable flowering of small press hotness? I know this is antediluvian, but the information and games must be out there. Share what you know!

    \n Roleplaying really came into Finland as a major phenomenon at the end of the decade, when 1:500 Finns apparently bought the newly translated red box D&D. Being as roleplaying was rather fringe before that, there wasn't much of an indie scene during the '80s. Still, the first Finnish rpg, "\u201dSecret of Raguoc In Acirema Dungeons" (yes, the title was in English), came out in -85, and it was just about as indie as you could get in its photocopied pamphlet glory. This was also the year of founding for the greatest Finnish rpg store of all time, Fantasiapelit.

    Later on, all Finnish rpg publishing has been indie, excepting the translations.

    \n Ringwielder

    It was a crazy sort of "Dark City" thing where a giant generational ship has an accident and the computer goes crazy and rewrites the environment and everybody's memories and forms to match fictional characters and locales. It seemed pretty gamey. Each section of the ship was mapped on a grid and PCs had like 27 separate stats (I'm serious) like Radiation Immunity, Alcohol Tolerance, Anger, Vertigo and (I kid you not) Virgin Status.

    The PCs had magic knock-out rings, and the game urged a heroic "non-violent" approach.

    The game was a "shareware" game for free on the internet, but you could buy a print copy.

    Hmm... It looks like this was copyright 1990. But still.

    \n Holocaustic Dungeons (1982,1986) - A straight up random dungeon crawl game by Silverwolf Games. It's a neat little game and we played the hell out it usually after a night of Car Wars or AD&D. Basically stripped off all the crap on AD&D and let you get on with exploring and killing. Had some major influence from early computer games as well. I want to hack this game some day (well I have notes kicking around somewhere).

    From the website (Silverwolf Games)
    "The concept for Holocaustic Dungeons originated in 1982. The idea was to create a character or a group of characters-in the case of multiple players. Thrust into a futuristic "dungeon" designed to protect certain artifacts while providing media coverage of the dungeoneers as entertainment for the masses, characters are forced from room to room in an attempt to locate sections to keys and treasures. The game consists of a small instruction booklet although supporting books are also available including character sheets, logs, maps, and other dungeon grids.

    The books came out at the time of the first generation of Silver Wolf and had severe start-up difficulties due to the market failure of the time. Yet, the game still exists and I will be releasing the new publications, in time. The rules will be the same, and be backwards compatible. "

    \n In front of me, I have a game called Abbeta: Fantasy Role-Playing Rules For In The Twinflare Solar System. On the front, it has map of the Solar System, which includes the planets Quelk, Gomb, Juffux, Cawth, Rygon and Tantia. It's copyright Martin Hackett 1984.

    I loved this game, when I picked it up at Armageddon, our local gaming convention. It has attributes POW, FIT, AGI, LUCK and LEARN. It also has skills such as FAUNA and FLORA. Everything is percentile.

    Oh, yes, and you have pages of tables to calculate your character's background. These really are good. Roll a 3? You're a Chief Apprentice. Fabulously detailed and wonderful.

Its basically a minis wargame turned into an adventure game, with random encounter charts for things you meet along the way (monsters, sorcerers, townspeople, the Black knight). They have some fun looking jousting rules as well as a Courtly Love table to see if the Lady of the Castle makes you her champion.

    Was that common? I have Ankh - Kalevala Heroes in my bookshelf, also in Finnish, with a title in English.
    \nThat's a good question, I hadn't thought about that before. Those two are pretty much the only Finnish games with English titles. They've had their share of ridicule for it - I don't exactly know why the designers went for English, but I suspect that it has something to do with complete cultural domination of English in early roleplaying around here; perhaps they just thought that the game's name sounds cooler in English. I've met many of those Finnish roleplayers and other nerds who've been in the hobby since the '80s, and some have pretty... "interesting" notions about cultural politics and language, to the tune that English is the only worthwhile language in the world and everything should be in English and so on.

    \nBlood Points! That takes me back! We only played one or two sessions of Morrow Project - we really hated the system. But ideas from the game crept into so much of our gaming. My friend produced this beautiful map of Ireland for a Celtic fantasy setting, with a big crater/harbor where Dublin once was...

    I have a digest sized, type-written, saddle-stapled copy of Space Quest, published 1978 by Tyr Games. DnD stats and a mish mash of scifi stuff from Heinlein, Pohl & Niven, with some Doc Smith and Alfred Bester thrown in. Chargen was a bitch - I think the rules call for taking a cube root at some point. WTF? Calculators were like $200 back then (ironically, for collectors they have held that value), and my junior high school math book didn't have a table of cube roots in it...

    \n I remember Space Quest, and have a copy somewhere. I recall the fantasy game Ysgarth used cube roots, too. There was a small booklet Authentic Thaumaturgy that applied the principles of "real world magic" to roleplaying, with laws of contagion, similarity, etc. I have fond memories of early Morrow Project, in particular running a short campaign for military veterans and the loving care they took when setting up camp for the night. The thing I liked best in Fringeworthy was that the ability to travel through the gates was rare and random, explaining why the PCs weren't always the people you'd prefer for such an important task.

    There was a booklet whose title I forget, about painting minis and playing princes on Barsoom, with charts for your medals and jewels and a hilarious chart for how badly you bungled your first meeting with the princess, who would then get kidnapped right away by the villain and you'd pursue.
