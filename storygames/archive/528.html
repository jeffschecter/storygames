    It's real easy to be enamored of the potential of the system in your head. Like a drawing that has not yet been drawn, its every detail is perfect in the abstract. It isn't something that anyone else can have an opinion about, and so there's no chance that they can not like it. But it's also not real.

    When you have to make an actual system, one that is written on paper, one that will have to run without you being there to fill in the gaps and smooth over the bumps, it gets less perfect. It becomes a combination of your ideas (cool or not) and your craft (refined or not). It becomes an imperfect artifact that other people can have an opinion about, and even dislike intensely. And it's real.

    That's a cool scary feeling. If you don't think it's cool then designing and publishing a game probably isn't for you.

    I make role-playing games because I want to help people tell awesome stories, and I want to give them good tools with which to do so. I want to help them express their own creativity in a way that they couldn't without my support, and to know that someone is out there having fun and telling stories because of me.

    I make games in general because my career is as a game academic, and if I'm not also in the trenches designing games, I risk becoming an ivory-tower recluse who's just blowing smoke. It's also because I'm a manipulative bitch (which is how I describe to my students the core skill that game designers must have): I love to make systems and structures that get people to behave in a particular way, and I love reasoning about how the rules I create might inspire both 'obedient' and 'alternative' forms of play.

    The thing that I make is games because I love to play, and I love to get other people playing, and I believe that Play Matters - which is kind of my dissertation.

    Worth it? So far, in some ways. I'm poorer in money (though I'm supporting myself, between consulting and academia and design) but much richer in happiness and community. I have the opportunity to spend basically all my time hacking around with things that I'm passionate about. I get to be playful and creative in my work every single day. And I can theoretically justify my time here on story-games as a professional activity . . . though I doubt my advisor would buy it!

    Also, it is a great way to meet the ladies . . . but that may be because I'm actively involved in recruiting women in my school to try gaming, and teaching game design to both men and women, and helping put together a series of game design events for highschool girls.

    I did meet my partner of ten years through the gaming/game design community, though, and he's always seemed pretty happy about it. So it's worked for at least one guy.

    --Jess

    It all started when I was in grade school and little Ricky N, invited me over to play legos. What I didn't know until I showed up was that he had designed a lego wargame, and it was the coolest thing I'd ever seen, but with his rules, he beat me every time, so I had to design my own to beat him. and beating him was worth every second it took me to design my lego wargame.

    I did my first rpg, because we couldn't find an rpg to play over a dial-up 28k bbs run from a C64. It was called Yellow Dragon Inn. The next few were improved versions of YDI, and then when my group got tried of the games we were using we made our own. We liked to play different games instead of sticking with just one. It was worth it, because we got to play what we wanted to play and didn't have to spend a dime to do it.

    And then I wanted to play with plastic toy soldiers, so I wrote the game I wanted to play. And that one was published in a magazine. I got $10 for it, and that was pretty cool at the time.

    I think that as long as I get to play something I wrote once, it has all been worth it. I bet it must feel really good for those designer that sell lots of their games. I know the feeling I get when I sell a print for display or see an image I took in a magazine, on a website or on TV. You just feel warm and fuzzy and all the hassle of getting to that point seems to disappear.
  • Josh Roby
    April 2006
    First: being a game designer or being a game publisher? There's an important difference there that often gets glossed over.

    Designing games is all sorts of fun. It's an excellent hobby. There's this awesome community that you can participate in. You get to play your stuff with your friends. It takes a pretty significant investment of time, but that's what hobbies are for.

    Publishing games is not 'fun' in my book -- it's gratifying and satisfying and exhilerating, but it's not fun in the same way that designing games is. There's risk involved in ways that just design does not entail. It's got financial risk -- it's either a business proposition or a money pit; your choice -- and it's got some pretty heavy emotional risk, since you're putting your stuff out for others to approve or disapprove. But when it works out, it's a great sense of accomplishment.

    I don't consider myself to be "a game designer." I define myself primarily in other ways and hope that I always do. I decided, about a year back, that I never wanted to design games for a living. I want to keep designing games, but I want it to stay a hobby, something I don't necessarily get paid much for, but a place where I invest a lot of my time. I'm not opposed to freelancing or designing games for pay if people offered that kind of work to me, but I'm not going to go out of my way to seek it out.

    I also don't want to own my own small indie publishing business, really. I'm gonna publish all my books through Lulu so I don't have to deal with print runs or distribution or all of that stuff. I just organize people to make the product and then, when it comes up, tell people about it and how cool it is. And I can handle customer service and all that, but I feel like I write and publish games because I believe in the value and possibilities of roleplaying and, more than that, I believe in what roleplaying could be, which is a modern preservation and re-imagining of the oral storytelling traditions that have meant so much to humanity over the course of 50,000 years.

    Yes, I'm an idealistic nutcase. Why do you ask?
