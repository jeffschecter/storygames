\n I open a new thread on this topic, to keep it from flooding the Murderland thread (great premise for that competition, thanks Jonathan).

It is natural that a lot of people (including my wife and me) react to the game I posted there: RAVEN - claw and beak. It makes use of quite laden symbolism, and encourage a kind of violence that is far from the happy go-lucky hack'n slash of old.

Sage says this about it:\n
I tend to like games that in some way create and resolve conflict via mechanical systems, and while I think your entry does a really good job of making people think about their choices (and probably make them feel very awkward in the process ), that doesn't make it a game for me. I think your piece has a lot of value as a way for creating creative responses and communicating a theme.
\nThank you, Sage, for reading the game and giving it such praise.

I wonder about your wording: I tend to like games that in some way create and resolve conflict via mechanical systems.

Have you actually tested other ways of creating and resolving conflicts in games? Are you talking from an informed position? I ask this due to the generalization you make here. It always makes me suspicious when people do that: are they distancing themselves from my game by saying that, or is it for real ...

Do you know that you would not like this game, if you ever played it? Could it be that your hypothesis on this is faulty, building on a preconception of games that don't really suit this special game?

Could it be, actually, that if you played this game you would be morfe likely to call a game afterwards?

I know the design is challenging, so I'm giving you all these questions to make you think through the statement you have made on it.

That was my answer (questions) to Sage.

To all of you: I make games for people. I like it when people read them, and enjoy it when someone is playing them. I challenge you to read my game and to play it. I dare you! ;-)

My game is made in earnest, in a spell of inspiration. I had little control over the outcome, but acted on inspiration and whim, mellowed by my experience as a games designer. I acknowledge that most of us are challenged by this game. I am challenged by it myself. I know for a fact that I could not bring my own teddy to the game (my wife would kill me), so when I eventually play it, it will have to be with a "proxy" teddybear. And still I believe that it will be a tough game for me.

But in spite of this I also believe this game will be fun. Fun in a challenging way, an intriguing and meaningful way. So I am really looking forward to playing it.

Any comments on the game is welcome! Don't be shy! I really don't know how the game would play, as I wrote it the way I did and posted it immediately, so any speculation and/or reaction is valid. :-)

    So, little story ... when my (then) four-year-old son was going through a particularly picky eating phase, we insisted that he wasn't allowed to say that he didn't like something without trying it. He comes up to me on the playground, and hands me a small rock covered with dirt and moss and what I can only describe as slime. "Try this, daddy!" he says, "You might like it!" Then he stared at me intently, clearly waiting to see whether I had some excuse (which he would later use) that would get away from the whole "You don't know until you've tried it" argument.

    For my son, to prevent an argument with a toddler about nuanced concepts, I licked the slime-covered rock, made a face, and said "I've tried it. I didn't like it."

    Grown-ups shouldn't need to have the nuances pointed out to them. We are, in fact, able to extrapolate our general tastes and preferences, and apply them to particulars without having to actively sample.

    I have not played Raven - claw and beak, but I can see the structure of it: It is sequential viking hat GMing, in which the only "game" is a freeform round of kibbitzing between a vested-but-disempowered audience and a single central actor with absolute fiat authority over the story, and only tenuous social incentives to listen to any kibbitzing. I have seen that dynamic many times, both in innumerable home-brews and in published games (Amber leaps to mind).

    I am perfectly willing to hear a case that there is a dynamic I am overlooking, which makes this more than what I've just outlined ... but right now, I'm basically of the same mind as when I'm offered someone's special eggplant risotto: I don't need to try it. I already know. I've tried all sorts of eggplant dishes, and didn't like any of them. I dislike eggplant that much.

    Posted By: Number6intheVillageI think that one of the main underlying purposes of role-playing games is to enable us to explore life from alternate perspectives through the creation of alternate conceptions of the self..
    Wow! This is very close to how I would describe the workings of this game. So how come you think this is arguing what this game is not?

    I do not postulate that there is any "single self" hidden in you or me that we need to come in contact with. Raven is not created for such a purpose. It is created for players to have fun with serious issues, while exploring alternate perspectives to known conflicts, and perhaps trying out alternate conceptions of ourselves. Now you know that, so how can you resist the urge to try this game, John Kantor? It must be made for you, man!

    As for Tony's allegation about "viking hat GMing": if you choose to look at it that way, it will be, of course. I do not work within the limits of prejudice, I challenge them. That stance enabled me to create one of the most successful railroading scenarios ever for my game Fabula, while everybody else in my clique of players were agreeing to how bad railroading were. "Railroading" as a term was coined from GMs misusing their power, but grew into stopping all use of heavy GM- or narrative-power in the service of other goals (goals not achievable in other ways).

    I expect the use of total authority for the center-player in this game will have a very benign effect on the single scenes, and on the game as a whole. It is a postulate by me, but I do believe this is the case. Tony is still right in his assumptions, I think, but only to the degree that you play this game with his stance. Shed the stance that this will not work, and try the game with an open frame of mind, and you may very well experience something quite different from what you express here, Tony. I do think there is a dynamic to this game that you are overlooking. The dynamic is partly lying in how the PC vs NPC set-up is reversed. In this game most players play the NPCs, and they are close friends and families to the PC (playing on such relations is something most of us do easily), and the PC is alone. So: there is indeed powerful social dynamics present in the game. The friends and family are instructed to oppose the PC, you know. The fact that the PC-player has total power over the outcome makes this interesting, in my eyes.

    I know for a fact that RAVEN is not free-form or magic-ritual or anything other hocus-pocus you may come up with. I am a bit irritated over repeated allegations that this is therapy, and that is obviously a magic ritual, and over there we have a typical drama-excercise, etc. I meet such allegations all the time, even on games that I know are great games. Such "stamping" smells too heavy of people trying to place challenging games in a box marked "BLUFF!"

    This is a methodically stringent game laden with powerful dynamics. I do not say that it is a successful design yet, but it is certainly a powerful text, and my experience with similar games indicates that this game may very well turn out to be a gem. And until the game has shown itself to be faulty in actual play, I will maintain a positive stance towards it. Any design of serious intent deserves as much, in my eyes.

    Tony: your tale about the 4 year old miss the mark, although it is a nice tale. I have children too, and love these small tales of exploration, inventiveness and bravado. I am in fact in the process of writing a book on the theme, with a friend of mine. I believe the simple answer to you son would be: This is not food, so we don't have to taste it. Food is something we respect to much to put in the bin without tasting it first. Go play now, little one!

    I respect the complicated nature of games too much to say they don't work before I have tried them. If you dare to try out this game in earnest, some of you may very well have your eyes going wide open! ;-)

    I might note, however, that FMA has far more "mechanical" hooks (pun intended) for exploring an individual's personal issues, through the agency of maiming a plush toy. Introducing other PCs by stabbing with a knitting needle has two strong mechanical effects: it determines who is in a scene; and it directly leads to either Damage (crochet hook to an existing Scar, yanking out stuffing as a metaphor of how an issue can erode your strength) or a new Scar (which is typically created to further illuminate or engage or interrelate issues that might be getting "soft pedaled" by a player).

    Anyhow, not wanting to thread-jack--rather, I using FMA as, perhaps, an example (for Thomas) of how (Tony thinks) RAVEN has insufficient mechanical impacts and constraints to qualify as a game and not a freeform/coffee-klatch with teddy bears.

    [Disclaimer: Today is the first time I've ever read RAVEN; it had no influence on FMA at all. FMA was designed to fit Ashok Desai's artwork and the "story" I saw in it while also trying to blur the lines between PC and player (almost null difference), eliminate conventional gaming tools (your doll is your "character sheet", you "current status" is shown literally, as a record of inked-on Scars and withered--or restuffed and patched--body parts), and drive cathartic play to 11).]

    Posted By: sageHowever, I still can't see a non-PC trying to make sure a painful solution is reached unless the person is just mean or really buys into the role they play (and even then, what made them chose that role?).
    \nI went back and re-read the thing. If the non-PCs aren't pushing for a painful solution then they're not doing their job. They are effectively breaking the rules.

    Are you familiar with Seth Ben-Ezra's game "A Flower For Mara"? It's about dealing with grief. All the main characters are people with an immediate relationship to Mara, a woman who has died suddenly and unexpectedly. In the game you hold a flower. As long as you are holding the flower you are ruled by your grief. You put the flower down when you feel as though your character is moving on from their grief.

    Here's the thing: Mara is a real live player in the game. The text makes it clear that she is not a ghost but instead is the collective memories of Mara. Her JOB in the game is get the players to NOT put down their flowers. Her job is make sure Mara is remembered....AT ALL COSTS... and that the characters DON'T move on from their grief.

    If the Mara player is not willing to be an emotional antagonist then she is not playing the game correctly. I see the same thing here. If the other players are not trying to anger you. Not trying to push, and push and push to make you do something painful then they're simply not performing their role in the game.

    I also suspect that "painful" does not have to be anything over the top. You don't have to strangle your mom and bury her corpse in the backyard or anything. I assume that gritting your teeth together and saying, "Fuck you, slut" to your girlfriend before slamming the door is sufficiently painful.

    \n"Always different" does not mean "always mutually exclusive." You might want to clarify your intent there, because the passage taken as a whole indicates that, while the other players always have to have different ideas than you, they only might oppose you.

    Suppose Bob is recalling his teenage years when he hated his life with his parents.

    Example 1:
    Bob wants to drop out of high school and join the army. Mary wants Bob to get his GED first. Mary's proposal does not overlap with Bob's course of action, but is not mutually exclusive with Bob's course of action. Mary has fulfilled her obligation to the rules, though Bob can satisfy her desire while still executing his own course of action. Bob may very much not want to take the time to get his GED, so the combination solution may seem unpleasant for him, but it is possible. People very often go with these sorts of compromise solutions.

    Example 2:
    Bob wants to drop out of high school and join the army. Mary wants Bob to finish high school, marry her, not join the army, and get a job at her father's company. Bob's course of action and Mary's proposal are mutually exclusive, and Bob cannot satisfy her desire and his own simultaneously. Bob is now in a true dilemma, assuming that he fears the consequences of telling Mary "no."

    Example 3:
    As 1 and 2, but Mary simply doesn't want Bob to do that. She offers no alternative, but simply opposes Bob's intended course of action. Bob can't simultaneously do and not do the same thing, since Bob is a macroscopic being ruled by classical mechanics rather than quantum mechanics, so he is again faced with a true dilemma.

    I think you're driving for situations like 2 and 3 to occur, but the current rules make 1 also reasonable.
