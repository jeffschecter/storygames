    \nA Poem for Mr. Morning (play-test)

    Ingvild sets the scene:
    Even and his grown up daughter Ingvild walks out from the doctors. The doctors are suspecting cancer, so they have taken some tests. Nothing is conclusive yet.

    Interaction:

    Even is bleak and coughs. Ingvild is visibly unsettled, but keeps a brave face.

    The two of them goes down the sidewalk, talking about ordinary things. But then they stop, and Ingvild makes her father promise to phone her when he gets the results of the tests. He claims it to be nothing, just a flu. And she says: "Well, call me anyway".

    They enter the car. Even is cold, so he takes a blanket over his shoulders, and tries to be calm about the prospect of possible cancer. He lights a smoke. Ingvild sits there, looking at her father. None of them speaks.

    End of scene.

    \n Played our 1st session of Apocalypse World over Skype.

    Remi, the MC, frames a flashback scene between Shade, my Skinner and an NPC male prostitute named Missed. Shade is this androgyne who always wears masks and her art is theater. We have a hold, a whorehouse in the middle of the ruins of a big city. Shade gets by doing plays for the staff and customers, its her one light in the world.

    We'd decided Missed was the one person Shade spoke with about the history of theater. So there we are, in what passes for the playhouse in the hold, talking. (MC) Remi cuts in with Missed trying to kiss Shade and she stiffens. He professes his love for her. That's when I find a way to express the hook for Shade. Shade spurns his advances, assuring him that he doesn't love her, he doesn't know her. He loves the art, he loves the characters she plays. Nothing else really matters, it's all going to die and rot away.

    Missed gets upset, he starts walking around the stage, throwing things around. Shade screams at him to calm down, then she manipulates him to back off. So he asks her to give him a chance someday, that he's good at doing things, he would be good for her. Shade agreed, but since I rolled a 10+, I knew I didn't have to do it. We moved on to present time, but I saw this tiny crack in Shade's facade. Did she care about Missed? Why did she only share her art with him if she didn't? Would she give him a chance?

    \n I played Spellbound Kingdoms last night.

    It's kind of a weird hybrid fantasy RPG, that mixes a lot of traditional elements (classes, levels, a fantasy setting specific to the game, six playable races, etc) with some new-school stuff (aspect-like "histories" instead of skills; "fears" and "loves" you can tap for bonus dice), plus some genuinely innovative things I've never seen before (like the combat system - which is a little mini-game where you move a counter around a sheet to unlock different fight moves as the scene progresses).

    One of the best parts was the magic system. A spell-using character has access to multiple (and colorful) disciplines. Each discipline has "high spells" - which work pretty much like any other "list o' spells" systems - and also "close spells", which are just a paragraph covering the kinds of things that discipline can do, like mending broken pots or sensing things nearby, from which you invent spells on the fly.

    Oh, also, whenever you use magic, you have to roll to see if bad shit happens. Your target number to beat is the number of other magic-using characters in the immediate vicinity (including yourself and your familiar). This is a fun thing, and makes magic kind of unpredictable and dangerous, and also makes wizards dislike each other.

    \n Ran AW for the first time at PAX (though I've played it a bunch), for some folks who were interested but had never played it. It was terrific, as expected. We did character creation (Saavyhead, Battlebabe, Chopper, Driver) and decided that they were a gang of salvage specialists in a flooded NYC where people lived on monstrous trash barges and the tops of buildings. There was some cool backstory about the gang being formerly run by this asshole named Scorpion who had been murdered by the Chopper. They got a job from this douchebag Millions who had a yacht and was really pretentious, to find a way into a water-sealed basement beneath a completely underwater house in the Hamptons. The operation was very cool, involving the Saavyhead doing a lot of talking to the Psychic Maelstrom and jury-rigging inflatable rafts as airlocks. At one point the Battlebabe cut a hand off a floating body to get through the handprint lock (she also seduced Million's goon bodyguard into switching sides). A strange high-tech black drone was knocking about the Chopper's minions with a crazy vibration weapon, but the chopper ended up headbutting it until it exploded after he was pulled into a death grip with it when he started ripping its guts out. And the Driver got an advance and took "My Other Car is a Tank" to make his submarine into a well-armed battle machine. Good times.

    \n Got a chance to play 'A Taste For Murder' (murder mystery in a 1930s British country house) again, this time with all improv actors. We truncated the usual scene structure quite a bit, but it still seemed to work great.

    I played Chef Francois, the prissy but obsequious French chef who was bitter at having to cook all English cuisine.

    He smoked and scowled constantly every scene, and everyone (including myself) grew to hate him. So it should be no surprise that he was the one who killed the Scottish nanny (Miss Kitty) with a cheese knife in the brain, and it came out that Francois had a three-some with Miss Kitty and the Master of the House (who was secretly a cross-dresser). Your usual upper class English family story.

    This game is definitely up there with Fiasco for "games you can pull out and play in an evening with anyone who wants to tell a cool story". The rules seem designed to give some clarity and structure, and then get out of the way of the scenes developing.

    Posted By: nikodemusWere there any surprises or discoveries of note?
    \nNot really, I think we pretty much just cemented what we already suspected or knew instinctively.     I now have empirical (if subjective) proof that actual play, as such, is about fulfilling the individual players' creative agendas.

    \n Lady Blackbird. My first chance to play instead of run the game.
