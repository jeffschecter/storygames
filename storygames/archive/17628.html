\n There's been talk, at our conventions, of running games for children.

Which games do you think would work well for kids? I'm not particularly interested in games that are written for kids (e.g. Happy Birthday Robot, Dread House). I'm more interested in good games that would work well with children. And I'm interested both in children and teenagers: so, let's say, any age from 5 to 13.

So, for example, Apocalypse World and Monsterhearts have sex moves, so they rule themselves out of contention. Similarly, Witch involves burning people at the stake. And various games sound a bit too complex, dull or gritty for children: Durance, What You Wish For. I wouldn't want to try those with children.

Would Fiasco work for children? What about Dungeon World? How about Marvel Heroic? If I wanted to take an established game and try it for children, which one would it be?

    \n I'm not going to be helpful here with 'pretty much anything'.

    I started my son off with (original) 2nd edition Runequest, messed about with a bit. We also played Monsters+ probably some Cthulhu, and some other stuff too. When he was in to his teens he played some Don't Rest Your Head.

    It'd be easier to create a list if child inappropriate games (as you said, Monsterhearts, Apocalypse World, etc.) but don't underestimate kids abilities to roll with stuff that seems quite complicated. As a GM for kids your focus changes. There seems to be a lot more showing what the system can do and how things work, or don't. There seems to be a lot less needing to egg them on to be pro-active or imaginative. All depending on the kids I guess.

    If you let them come up with the bad stuff, it will be age appropriate by default. They will have fun just being the characters and playing about so don't need complex plots. Don't be surprised if they become quite involved/attached, and expect to be surprised by some of their reactions and methods. Young people do not game like old people and the definitely don't game like old people pretending to be young people. From my experience at the outset there is little resource hoarding. There is often no real consideration of consequences.

    Two examples that come to mind with my son was in a weekend long freeform (LARP) when he was 10. He had a card that was basically 'you use this thing to solve any problem that can be solved with force, One Use' an adult would hoard that until really necessary, he used it within the first 90 minutes because "this had to be done, this would do it" the GM's were really pleased (once they'd stopped being surprised!) When he was 8 I ran the RQ2 for him and had him playing an adolescent dragon (this wasn't Glorantha) I wanted him to be pretty powerful so it wouldn't be lots of 'no, you can't do that' or 'try and fail'. At one point he'd been told off and flew away from the party. I had a group of hunters come across him. They had bows that could barely scratch his character, his first reaction, flee from the encounter like a scalded cat back to where the rest of the party was. 'There are bad people there that might hurt me'. A much more visceral reaction than I'd ever expected as the GM.

    \n I have played Fiasco with my two daughters, at the time aged 5 and 8, and it went very well - I recommend it.

    We used Main Street, and I do remember doing some real-time editing of the playset. Need 6 is 'To get Laid' and there's a porn stash under Untoward Objects, but that was the only change that I can recall feeling necessary. The editing was easier because the youngest, not being a quick reader, was rolling her stuff randomly rather than browsing. If we did it again, I'd probably bring an edited playset to the table (they're stronger readers now).

    The game itself went extremely smoothly, way better than I expected. Kids at play seem to be in a constant process of negotiating a imaginary context, so establishing scenes was dead easy. I made the decision to keep the scenes really short, the long ones running about two minutes. (Really.) This was really important, as it's a long game for a 5 year-old otherwise. Her attention waned only in the last ten minutes or so.

    The fiction was a little gonzo - my eldest played a vet named Dr. Eggnog - but the story was still surprisingly coherent and enjoyable. Our characters (former friends) came to blows, and my character wound up dying. I had a scene left, however, so we decided to do a flashback, and we chose a moment when the two were in school together. There was no conflict, but the juxtaposition was surprisingly moving for me.


    I have also played old Dungeons & Dragons with them. They enjoyed that quite a bit, mapping in particular until we all discovered how bizarrely whiffy the combat is. (They had very good armor, which led to one fight where whole rounds would pass with no damage being done.) My eldest was right into the mapping.


    We've also played Mouse Guard. MG is a great system for kids of their age, except for character generation, which takes a long time as there's a lot of browsing for skills. (I'd use the pregens next time.)

    My youngest, who insists on writing everything herself has a hilarious character sheet, with all of her write-in skills written in her phonetic best. This leads to exchanges like:

    "Honey, you have a wise about 'OTM' - what is that?"
    "You know, the season before winter. (duh)"


    By far the most successful of our role-playing games, though, is 'Action Play'. We strip the sheets off the double bed, which they then use as a sort of stage/trampoline, and I stand next to it. At the start of each 'session', they get to choose three 'spells' (favorites include 'friend spell', and 'magic net'). We have three resolution mechanics - avoiding 'arrows' (I try to grab them, they dodge), leaping over things, and 'wrestling'.

    The main reason this one works so well, though, is that it gives them an outlet for the wriggles, so the main session limiter is my attention span, not theirs! We've played out fairly lengthy campaigns with it. (A particularly memorable highlight was the arc where they had to defeat the nine heads of a dragon, all encountered separately, each of which had a special power. The power of the eighth was lying, and it managed to convince them to do a whole mini-quest for them - you should have seen their faces when they figured it out. But the dragon got its just desserts in the end.)

    I suspect this format is not ideal for a convention!

    \n World of Dungeons has been working out well for my kid groups.

    I've played World of Dungeons and Faery's Tale with kids of different age groups, and I've found that the younger ones (6 through 8) tend not to handle procedural rules terribly well, and their attention flits quickly from one thing to the next, so you're not going to get terribly far with lots of abstract rules or even rules that require much in the way of simple math. The context matters too, especially for the older kids. My twelve-year old daughter, for instance, likes World of Dungeons better because the high fantasy milieu strikes her as more inherently content-rich than the fairy forest of Faery's Tale.

    The leveling structure of WoD play also seems to work better than Faery's Tale's sort of ethereal Boon-driven advancement, because the progression of WoD's reward structure ties in a concrete way to character advancement, and the kids like that (at least, that's what they say). With both age groups, however, I've hit a wall where greater mechanical complexity would hinder interest in playing. I don't think 4E or Runequest would have flown in any form, for instance. I'm not even sure Dungeon World would work, certainly not for the younger set. It's not that I think they're incapable of engaging with complex mechanics, just that they don't particularly want to. The kids want to act without poring over their character sheets trying to figure out what options they have.

    For kids, I think it's especially important for a game to be light, intuitive, and accessible, but also provide enough structure that the rules don't feel arbitrary. World of Dungeons has proven ideal in this regard, at least for my groups. The central mechanic is clear and dirt-simple, fictional results flow organically from the dice results (i.e., no additional interpretation of what 1 success versus 4 successes means), the special abilities and skills allow for enough combinations to make each character feel unique, and the magic system is evocative and full of interesting tactical potential.

    \n I have a 10 year old son that I've taken to cons with me for the past year or two.

    As a generic system for modern, pulpy "adventure play", Savage Worlds works really well for young people. Use pregens for con play. My son has played about 3 different con games using Savage Worlds (even using different editions) and he's had an easy time engaging with the mechanics and the story.

    Also, I used a slightly modified Savage Worlds campaign at home for a year. It went well.

    We played in a game using GURPS that went great, but only because the GM hid most of the system stuff from us and just told us what to roll. None of the players felt like they knew what they were doing, system-wise. The only thing we did was try to make use of some of the skills on our sheet that we were obviously good at - like I had a good disguise skill so I put on a costume and tried to talk my way through one of the challenges. I think the game would have been even more great if it had used Savage Worlds, which is much easier to get a real feel for.

    For dungeon crawly monster bashing play specifically, Dungeon World has been a huge hit with my son. He played 2 sessions. He died both sessions, but still likes the game and wants to play more of it.

    The biggest challenge my son has with Dungeon World is getting his head around the possibilities of fictional positioning. For example, it never really occurs to him that he should narrate that he's lining up his enemies so that he can shoot 2 at a time. He needs lots of guidance toward that sort of possibility - he is more used to letting a list of skills drive his choices rather than using his imagination to his advantage.

    What about Primetime Adventures? I think that could work quite well for kids. They're immersed in TV now.
    \nI haven't tried it, but having read the game, it doesn't seem like an easy fit at all. You have to spend a lot of time and energy thinking about how a TV show is structured, rather than just thinking about what a character would do in a given situation.

    I can't imagine my 10 year old finding satisfaction in playing it. He's a consumer of television, not a producer. PTA pushes you to think like a producer.

    My assumption is that I'd have to play it like that GURPS game mentioned above - where I'd basically hide the system from him and just resolve what he wanted to do. And PTA doesn't seem like the kind of game where that fits what the game is trying to accomplish.

    It's way too meta for kids, I would think.

    That said, if someone comes back and tells me they had great experiences playing it with their kid, I'm not going to call them a liar. I just can't see an obvious path as to how to introduce the game effectively to young'uns.

    \n I ran Witch for a mixed group of adults and three teenagers in the top end of your range. It went surprisingly well. Two of them needed occasional prompting but they had no problem with the concepts involved and the freeform nature of the play had no barriers for them.

    I've run AD&D for kids before and it's fine. Everyone seems to know what generic fantasy and an adventure are. Rolling dice soon becomes part of the fun and I haven't really noticed and whiffing problem, as long as the descriptions are exciting.
