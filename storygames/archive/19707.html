\n I've been doing some thinking regarding RPG design and I came up with 3 different sets of player behaviors I have observed in my own gaming groups.\n
    \n
  • Creation
  • Exploration
  • Participation
\nI tentatively propose that all of these elements are required for an RPG to really be happening. Different systems may utilize these principles at different ratios or formally distribute these elements to specific players, but these 3 things are always happening.

Creation
Creation is about determining what happens. Creation is prominent with players who think about things at the narrative and scene level: They want certain scenes to occur, or certain story elements to be true in order to make the experience more interesting and dramatic.\n
    \n
  • Answering questions about the world
  • Creating backstory and history.
  • Weaving independent stories together.
  • Determining facts about the imaginary world.
  • Ensuring that drama occurs (bangs).
  • Filling the imaginary world with conflicts.
  • Taking pleasure in creating an experience for others to enjoy.
  • Concern with meta-concepts like drama and pacing.
\nCreation takes place at the meta-level, with a focus on the fictional world.

Exploration
Exploration is about finding out what happens in the imaginary world. Exploration is prominent with players who want to have a lot of agency in their actions, but don't want to determine things that their characters can't influence. Exploration is usually focused on at the level of characters or exploring through the viewpoint a specific character.\n
    \n
  • Asking questions about the world.
  • Finding trouble, action, and drama.
  • Getting immersed in a character.
  • Talking in character with other characters.
  • Taking pleasure in actively guiding what is created (specifically the interactive aspects).
  • Separation of character and player knowledge.
  • Concern with things "making sense".
\nExploration takes place in the fictional world, with a focus on the fictional world.

Participation
Participation is about what things happen at the meta-level (outside the imaginary world). Participation is prominent with players who are most concerned with having a good time with the other people at the table while playing the game.\n
    \n
  • Talking with players (out-of-character).
  • Engaging the mechanics.
  • Developing strategies based on the game mechanics.
  • Taking pleasure in passively viewing what is created (more like a movie than an interactive medium).
  • Taking in-character actions to get a reaction from the people at the table (laughs, groans, etc.).
  • Enjoying a conversation with the NPC or PC more as a conversation with the GM or player than with a character.
  • No separation of player and character knowledge.
  • Recognizing when players are having fun or not.
  • Concern with meta concepts like "fairness", "spotlight time", and "winning".
\nParticipation takes place at the meta-level, with a focus on the real world.


I'd love to hear some feedback regarding this breakdown and how it works or how it fails to describe your experiences.

    \n I like the distinction between Creation and Exploration. I've long found that an important axis to define for players in games I GM -- "make stuff up" games vs "just play your character" games.

    I like the concept of participation -- the behaviors you've listed are undeniably a big part of gaming practice -- but not the term itself. "Participation" sounds to me like "engagement with the stated activity", not "engagement with the people, beyond the stated activity". I think "socializing" or "facilitation" or something like that would be more on-point.

    I also wonder why you're grouping mechanics-engagement with interpersonal engagement. In my experience, there's not a particularly strong overlap between the two (in individuals or groups), and the fact that they're both "meta-level, with a focus on the real world", while true, doesn't initially strike me as useful or significant. Where do you envision the utility of this "Participation" category?

    \n I think Participation is something that's perhaps under-examined. There's a general exhortation in most more free-form story-game type deals to share spotlight, and more traditional games (and some indie games) often have a turn structure in combat that more-or-less ensures equal spotlight*. But I think something to really divide up spotlight time, especially out of combat, more equitably could be quite valuable.

    Matt

    *This sort of thing actually hasn't been working well for me lately. I make decisions very quickly and as a result spend less time talking/acting than most players.

    The original intention of the "Participation" model was for it to include everything you need to play a non-roleplaying game (ex. Risk, Chess, etc.). Typically these games involve opponents who interact socially and mechanically to create the full game experience.

    Exploration and creation are meant to be the elements largely unique to role playing games which set them apart from other types of interactive entertainment.
