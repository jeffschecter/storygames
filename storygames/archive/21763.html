Resolving Significant Uncertainty [Follow]

moconnor
August 2018 edited August 2018 in Story Games
Played my first game of Follow yesterday and had a blast. We took on The Dragon, which in our instance was a Wolf from Hell here to devour the Earth.

We defended a village by convincing Ellis, the sole survivor of a previous attack, to wield a magic sword powered by his will to protect the innocent.

We found the beast's weakness by finding a sword powered by innocence and letting a twelve-year old, Prior, lead us into battle. His father Anthos, was not happy with this, but swore to stand at his son's side.

We cornered the beast by facing Ellis, now an enemy of the fellowship, and disrupting a ritual meant to give the wolf even greater power. Ellis wielded a sword powered by his will to protect, but he had lost that will and the sword was powerless. Prynn faced him with a sword powered by his need for revenge, but he had overcome that obsession and his sword was likewise drained. Prynn won and Prior was able to enter the cavern to face the wolf.

The one moment of confusion was when this fight broke out mid-scene, with player characters on one side and non-player characters on the other side. We wanted to smoothly determine consequences in this scene without waiting for the resolution of the whole challenge. We kind of resolved this ad-hoc, with proposed actions and consequences being floated. It worked, but it lacked the smooth flow of the rest of the game. There were a few exchanges like "I attack.", "He leaps out of the way" that felt like the narrative wasn't moving forward.

What I would want to do next time would be to formalize how risky actions like this are resolved:
- The acting character states what they want out of the situation and asks 'what price would I have to pay to achieve this?'
- The others set a price of the proposed action.
-The acting character decides to accept the price or abandon their stated goal.

This is, perhaps obviously, taken from the escalation system in Kingdom. I think it would weave together nicely with scenes in Follow.

I'm wondering if others have had similar stall-outs with GM-less games without in-scene conflict resolution systems and if you have ideas for mitigating them.

    2. The group says, “but, only if...your sword gets stuck in the dragons scales.”
    3 The active player, “Okay, but only if...[the character who my character is in love with] is impressed with my courage and heroism and sees me in a better light.”

    There are lots of "soft" resolution techniques.

    I'm not a huge Follow fan (it's rather freeform for my tastes, and we had some similar issues with moments of conflict/uncertainty when we played it). That said, the lack of resolution procedures is clearly intended as a feature of the game here. It may be worth carefully exploring the way the game is intended to be played (and my impression is that something like the fight choreography you're describing is ultimately not the game's focus at all - best to describe it quickly and move on to the draw of the tokens).

    I like the technique described above.

    Here are some others:

    * As a group, brainstorm two or more possible and plausible outcomes, then randomly choose one (roll dice or draw from the bag).

    * "But Only If..." - another player suggests a cost or complication (as in the post above).

    * Other players each suggest a possible outcome/consequence; the acting player chooses between them.

    * Assign a player (perhaps randomly or arbitrarily) authority over the relevant element of the fiction. They get to decide what happens.

    * Consult some oracle - dice, cards, tarot cards, imagery, opening a book to a random page, etc... - and interpret the outcome yourself as you wish.

    stefoid said:

    Doing away with uncertainty does away with the possibility of failure except as an intentional decision by the narrator. Good or bad thing?

    Most likely irrelevant, in the specific game we're talking about (Follow): the possibility of failure is deeply enmeshed in the rules of the game and is, in fact, the biggest plot point - but it's elsewhere, later. Failure of the quest as a whole - and then, why? whose fault was it? - as opposed to binary success/failure of every small bit of action.
    Our fixation on reading individual actions as binary pass/fails is one of those all-pervasive legacy elements in RPG design which are seriously holding us back, I believe.
    Hence, the sword-fight as a conversation: the point isn't who gets a swing through the opponent's guard, the point isn't even "who wins"... The reason to have the fight in a scene is to state something about the characters, to communicate. Don't fret about who's winning, don't "play to win" - play to show us who your character is and who the other character is.
    When engaging in a conversation, in most games, it isn't about who wins (those games exist, too, but I don't believe they're a majority, right?). We all have this engendered trigger, as players, that talking is "just talking", but when swords come out it's all real and you make it with the dice... Except, when your game settles something else with dice, and does that well, how is a sword fight different from a conversation over drinks? (And there's, of course, the possibility of games where the tongue is mightier than the sword, where you "just talk" through a fight but resolve actual talking to each other with hard dice mechanics).

    For resolving situations like combat without resolution mechanics, you generally want to follow the golden rule of freeform which is:
    "No one can do something to your character without your consent."
    Thus, they can't stab you unless you say they can, they can't charm you unless you say they can, they can't convince your character of something unless you say they can, etc, etc, etc., because absolute control of your character's story is in your hands, and the only things that happen to your character are the ones that suit their story and aesthetics.

    You're welcome!

    It definitely probably should be in the books of all games that don't have resolution mechanics!

    Chuubo's Marvelous Wish-Granting Engine directly mechanizes that through giving you mechanical veto power over things that happen to your character that you don't want to happen to your character, or that don't fit the aesthetic of what's going on with your character.
    If someone narrates something happening to your character that doesn't suit what you're going for, you can take a Wound, which is a mechanical mapping of narrative veto power. When you take a Wound, you either negate what happened, or you reshape what happened however you want. This could be anything from "The bullet didn't hit me because it broke the pendant ocarina hanging around my neck and bounced off" to "I was too durable for it to hurt me" to "I wasn't turned into a frog, but I took on some frog-like qualities like for instance now I want to eat flies and grow gills when I'm in the water" to "My character's girlfriend doesn't really break up with her. She's angry though, and I'm going to have to resolve this situation, but in the end she's going to forgive me."
    Then you gain some temporary abilities until the Wound goes away (which takes anywhere from 1/3 of a session to 5 session depending on the severity of what happened, and you can choose to keep a Wound longer because you like the powers it's giving you and find playing up to the Wound interesting and like what it's doing for your character's story in this moment).
    You technically have a limited number of Wounds per block of time, but it's a large enough number that you rarely use them all in play before you get some back, so you effectively have as much veto power as you could need, especially since there are several simple ways in play to get more Wound slots if you need them, and to clear away Wounds you've taken. The average character can theoretically veto something like 16 things per session, which is typically far more than you have need to veto (and if you're needing to veto that many things consistently, there's something going horribly wrong that needs to be discussed with the group).
    It's super super cool. ^_^

    Yes, the basic (but often unwritten) freeform rule of "Your Liberty To Swing Your Fist Ends Just Where My Nose Begins" is pretty key for this kind of play.

    In IIEE terms, for those who care, basically the target or victim of any action gets to say what the Effect on their character is. You may pursue your Intent, Initiate and Execute an action... but the other person says what the Effect is.

    I really like the sound of how Chuubo's handles that, Emma. Sounds like a well thought-out approach to the problem, and with fun outputs, too.

    However, games like Follow are also distinct from pure freeform in that they do have specific points of uncertainty/resolution. I think that, in those games, the players need to be mindful of them and consciously drive towards them as they act: "How is what I'm narrating or doing contributing to getting us to the next resolution point?"

    I've seen Fiasco fail many times, for instance, because people may forget about that "target" entirely, or, perhaps worse, have different ideas of what it might be. Jason Morningstar writes in the book that "it will be obvious when you try it", but, in my experience, that's not the case for everyone, particularly when people come to the table with different expectations of what "resolution" is for and what it looks like (usually based on previous gaming experience).

    Follow is better in this sense - it presents a clearer target.

    Sometimes, in a game like this, you'll find places where "your freedom to act ends where my nose begins" is too loose ("No, you can't narrate your own character jumping off the boat, because that depends on the beads drawn at the end of the scene") and places where it's too strict ("Sure, you can narrate getting my character expelled from the school - he's not the Primary character and that will make it easier to play out the next scene, anyway").

    Getting everyone on the same page about what the decision/resolution points are and building towards them is always key. (It's arguably one of the main skills involved in playing a game like this, in my opinion.)

  • Rafu
    August 2018
    stefoid said:

    How is achieving goals uncertain in Follow?

    When you start playing, you choose a goal as a group (the "Quest"). In fact, this choice is what defines the setting for the game, thus you make it before even creating characters: everything is defined by and measured against the Quest.
    Each round of scenes (of which there are three to a game) begins with one of the players stating what the next step is in the Quest - the next goal to be achieved or threat to be averted. Then you play out several scenes to demonstrate how your characters deal with the problem or goal at hand, how do they feel towards each other and about the Quest as a whole, etc. (crucially, the details of character creation will have established the characters as working together, but also more or less at odds regarding why). Usually, players each frame a scene per round, though this also depends on how many players there are.
    Finally, after each round of scenes colored stones are drawn from a bag to establish whether the fellowship (as a whole) succeeded or failed at their goal for this stage of the Quest, as well as how much their success cost them (i.e. whether they lose a member) or whether the reason for their failure is one of them betrayed the others (or was betrayed by them). The players as a group decide on the exact implementation of this "oracle" (such as, which of the characters is lost) based on the events of play so far. Crucially, how many stones per color are put in the bag also depends on the events of play, as evaluated by the players both in terms of objective refereeing ("Have your characters done what it takes to succeed?") and of subjective character feelings ("Are you dissatisfied with what the fellowship has done? Do you actually want the Quest to be successful, as of now?").
    The final resolution of the Quest is also affected by whether the previous two challenges where won or lost. Thus, all of the events of play ultimately feed into the final overarching goal.
