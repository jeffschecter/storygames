    \n What makes me want to GM is the ability to bring new games to the table.
    \n
    \nIf I didn't GM, I'd be stuck playing Game X and Game Y. Game X and Game Y are fun and all, but I don't want to play them all the time. If I want to play Game Foo, I'm out of luck. Bringing a new game to the table takes some effort, starting with a) reading up about new games, then b) sinking some dosh into the game, then c) reading it, and then d) communicating it to the table. That's a lot of work right there. I personally do not find it a lot of work. Other people do. I want to play Game [a-zA-Z], so the GMing/facilitating role falls on me. Which is fair enough, I'm not moaning. But the push to GM comes out of that, and not something perhaps more positive.

    \n\nAccountingForTaste \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nDecember 2009 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n To begin with, I have one inviolate rule: I only GM a game if I really want to be a GM. Wanting to bring a new game to the table, feeling like I "have to" GM because no one else will and it's game night...no, uh-uh, not going to do that ever again. I've gone down that road in the past, and it leads to not having fun and eventually burning out, so fuck that. I'd rather GM less if it means that I'll really enjoy GMing when I do it.

    What makes me want to GM is pretty much wanting to do the things you get to do as a GM. You get to play the world: switching between NPCs, creating opposition, reacting to what the players do, describing the scene, trying to control the pacing, setting a plot in motion and seeing where it goes, giving everyone a chance to get involved and have fun, and so on. It's not really that much more work than playing, but it is more intense work a lot of the time, since you're almost always in the spotlight and therefore really have to keep your energy up. Doing it well is a major ego boost and makes you feel awesome for days afterwards.

    I also want to GM because I like prep work. I like sitting around in the week before the session thinking about what we'll be doing on Saturday. I like preparing things and I get a great feeling of satisfaction when that preparation pays off in the actual game, and that goes double when it pays off in an unexpected way because of something the players did. Also, the prep work I do as a GM is different from the prep I do as a player: more intense, more comprehensive, more organized. GM prepwork is its own kind of fun.

    And really, it's wanting that different "GM fun" that moves me to run a game. It's very, very rare that I run across a system or a novel or a movie that makes me think "Oh damn, I totally have to run a game of that": instead, I come at it from the opposite direction. I think "Oh damn, I totally want to run a game," and then I go looking for something that would be fun to GM. When I do find something that clicks with me, I'll pitch it to our group and we'll play it.


    (As it turns out, GM-less games don't actually scratch the same itch for me...probably because I don't like mixing my "GM fun" with "player fun." I want to concentrate on one or the other and fully enjoy it, not split my focus between them.)

    Designing challenges is a lot like being a teacher, a profession I hope to enter in a few years. Your goal as a GM is to help people grow in some way, to gain some new understanding, practice problem-solving reasoning, or whatever. Roleplaying games present unusual opportunities and difficulties in presenting challenges. Also, part of the prep work is learning something (ie growing myself) in some way that might also challenge the players. This may not seem to fit with the player-driven approach that is popular at Story Games, but believe me, there's a lot more options to bring in player input than the two stylistic extremes (shared-GMing and railroading) might suggest.

    \n Lately what makes me want to GM is a game that clearly outlines what role the GM has and how that makes the game cool.
    Apocalypse World did this for me most recently, but I'm also still riding the GMing high I got from reading Burning Wheel.

    Lots of games seem, to me, to be written about how the players get to do cool stuff and and provides tons of rules, advice and examples to show the players how to do their cool stuff then it will throw in a quick note about how the GM is supposed to give them cool stuff to do and doesn't give you any support (beyond some vague advice maybe), as if the GM's job isn't really important. The games I've mentioned above both seem to provide the GM with the same kind of support that the players get and make an effort to point out how cool being the GM is.

    Basically, I don't want to be a facilitator, I want to be a participant and I get pumped when the game treats me as one.
