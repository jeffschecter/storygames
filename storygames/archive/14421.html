\n Over the weekend Andy, Mark, and I played a short session of Technoir. I enjoyed the game, and I could really rave on about how much I like the character creation and plot creation systems. Instead, I want to present a couple issues I have with the game.

Whiff Factor
When an attacker fails to exceed the defense rating the conflict and the verb in question is already primed, then nothing of consequence happens. The conflict is not moved in any direction. This doesn't feel satisfying.

PC Death
When the GM chooses to spend three push dice to kill a PC, who gets the spent push dice? The PC's player won't have any use for them if his character is dead. I think I'd like this rule better if it were "Spend 3 Push dice to mortally wound."

Task Resolution
At one point during our session, I described my character's attempt to jump a motorcycle (using a pile of trash and a dumpster as a ramp) onto a fire escape to rescue one of his contacts from a blazing building. There were no characters opposing my jump - just the laws of physics. We attempted to personify those harsh laws as my opposition in the conflict, but it didn't feel satisfying. Should we have just rolled against a flat target number? Is there another way to handle this conflict that missed? I suspect that we would have enjoyed using a single target number and resolving the jump with a pass/fail outcome, but I suspect we would have been flatly breaking the rules to do so.

    \n Andy mentioned that I'm a habitual hacker. I can't help myself. I'm a hack addict.

    But, as I was just trying to get back to work, I had an idea. It might be a bit extreme, but hear me out.

    1. You don't recover from locked adjectives. You Replace to turn locked into sticky.
    2. Reduce the TN to recover from sticky adjectives. Make it very low. Like 2 (seriously).
    3. Failing to recover from a sticky adjective means the end of your PC. Death, jail, retirement, as appropriate to the adjective that finished him.
    4. Remove the 3-die option entirely.

    Now, every time the GM hands out an adjective, of any kind, the player starts wondering when their luck is going to run out. Sure, it's really unlikely that their contact with level 4 Treat is going to roll all 1s, but it could happen. And the more wounds they have to treat, the more likely it's going to happen soon.

    Whatcha think?

    \n Hi, I'm the aforementioned Mark. My thoughts on the game are as follows:

    I was given a description of the transmission and I thought up the protagonist I'd want to read about if it were a novel. The system did not limit me or force me to rewrite my initial concept at all. By pseudo roleplaying during chargen (choosing alliances and making calls on them), I not only got a feel for what my character was but was also given a new spin by the subsequent choices (going into debt to Loren and having to buy three weapons from Syd instead of just the one). This is awesome to me! The more of this kind of thing the better.

    The adjectives for my relationship with these contacts/alliances, however, were weak. I struggled to choose them for each alliance - they were too passive and didn't make an evocative feel for the relationship.

    On the character sheet, I'd like to see a section for cost directly under the name of the equipment. Base + Upgrades - Discounts would be nice. Also, you might want to add story tags that make them cheaper, too - stolen is already in there, but traceable, loud, beta (glitchy), etc.

    I struggled to be able to see how we were going to personify non-NPC forces. A ton of advice would be necessary if I were ever going to run this instead of just play it.

    I would like to put an NPC or PC down on my contacts list, but I don't think that was mentioned. An initial adjective during our first encounter would be cool, too.

    Implant as a tag - some items make sense, but some weapons do not. But if I want a neural link to my gun, I have to take implant. What is being implanted? Maybe a series of interface devices for equipment would be helpful.

    There was no cost for lifestyle. Is this a conscious choice? I thought it was one of the best parts of Shadowrun - you were going to continue to lose money if you didn't do anything or while you were healing. Great pressure, there.

    Making it clear that if you want to give a sticky adjective, you have to narrate your own adjective or tag and use the die in the roll would be helpful.

    Back to work, if I think of other things, I'll pop in.

    \n CONTENTION: Maybe try to draw diagrams of how push dice work. It took us a bit to grasp the relationship between Unspent, Spent and "Dice that move to the GM/Player" Push Dice.

    * Re Eric's comment on failure: We actually had about 4 Contentions, about 6-8 rolls total? But we didn't complete the full game. If we completed the full game, it might have worked out differently. So just note that we did do about 3 hours' worth. Might have just been cold dice, though. I think there were only two conflicts with Hurt dice used.

    * In the game, the GM starts out with zero Push dice and players start out with 3 each: So by the first Contention in the game with one GM and 3 players, there are 9 Push dice on the table, 3 per player, correct?

    * "Increase the Adjective's Severity": Can you only spend (that is, give to the GM) Push dice that you added through use of more Adjectives and were spent, or can you use any of them you have? Rules say the former, but wanted to confirm.

    * The second item of Resolve the Roll is hard to consider: "If the attacker's result was equal or less... the attack happened as described but was not effective enough to inflict a new adjective".
    ...but not sure how that works out with even some of the common attacks:
    Trying to outrun someone: Do you catch up to them anyway (in fiction), just can't add an adjective?
    Give someone the slip: Do you still completely get away anyway?
    By that description, it sounds like in the fiction that anything you try to do, you actually succeed at it no matter what, but "failure" simply means "I can't stamp it with an adjective".

    Because of that, we couldn't figure out the best way to resolve some contentions.
    For example, in a secuction scenario, Coax vs Coax: "I make her fall in love with me".
    You roll but "fail": You still "make her fall in love with you", but you can't put an adjective on it.
    ...but what does the antagonist do next? How do they get a turn next? The contention in the fiction is pretty much over at that point: The player didn't get an adjective, but they made what they want to happen Happen, so it doesn't seem to matter at that point.

    Another scenario that came up: Anything that uses the Detect verb seems to be problematic:
    * If you want to for example determine the history of the hidden cult (faction), what do you roll against? Does that mean you have to stop and basically create one single new NPC, stat them out, then use them to represent the faction? Or use numbers to represent the faction? This is in a scenario where me as the GM simply didn't care if the PCs learned the faction history now or find it through connections later (it didn't kill or disrupt the scenario either way, both were valid), so Success was as good as Failure... but what does the PC roll against?
    - Also, as above with the "the attack happened as described... no adjective" piece, does that mean the PC automatically succeeds?

    * A PC was looking for an NPC: I wanted to make it difficult, and didn't think that it needed to land one way or another (failure would not stop the game, etc) They were using Detect, but I couldn't decide the difficulty. I ended up using that NPC's natural Prowl, even though they weren't particularly trying to be avoided. Naturally, I couldn't think of a way to have the target "attack back" against the Detect roll.

    I... think that was it actually. The Transmission we chose ended up building itself out like a murder mystery/LA Confidential type, so naturally there was lots of Detecting going on, moreso than fistfights, guns, cars, etc. There were a few Coax conflicts and one Move one as well.

    I'm sure we just had a mental misfire or something of some sort in reading the rules and it's all a lot more simple than it looks, but when we read the rules we kept getting hung up on the "Failure is actually Complete Success, you just don't get to add an Adjective". It led us to come up with ideas like:
    - Detect vs Prowl to find the Faction Headquarters building address.
    - You fail. OK, my turn to attack back: You find an address and drive out there, but when you show up to the site... Gangsters show up (sent by the faction) and are going to beat you up...
    ...but this sounded like REALLY hippie, moreso than the rest of the game feels, so I think that interpretation was too liberal.

    Which parts of the session were the most fun? Why?
    Everybody seemed to enjoy character creation for the most part. It's relatively quick yet dispenses nice little bursts of color and situation as you create your character. Not unlike a much simplified, and non-random, version of character creation in Mongoose Publishing's edition of Traveller. The beta pdf is not the most easily referenced thing right now, but that's sort of the nature of the beast. There were some minor issues with understanding how the tagging system works when buying gear and splicing an implant being a separate, added cost.

    The contention we played out was enjoyable and the mechanics felt like they were a fun multiplier and not an ungainly yet necessary anchor. I feel like there is a definite improvement here over the resolution system in Chronica Feudalis for ease of use and return for time invested.

    Which parts of the session were the least fun? Why?
    Hmm, besides my spotty internet connection giving me the vexed adjective? I think that despite the enjoyment of character creation there was a general sentiment that there could be just a little more meat on the bones. A little more flavor, personality, or background for players to work with in getting the feel of their characters. That didn't stop the players from portraying their characters as distinct and flavorful in play though, not at all.

    Did you play out a full contention? How did it end?
    We did! A pretty simple, but fun, Hacking contention. It was a good, non-critical, situation that I could use so that everybody could get familiar with the mechanics. It consisted of 5 rolls all together, 4 by players and 1 by myself in the role of a banking system's firewall and countermeasure software.

    The adjective system was fun and quick, giving an easy sort of short-hand language for hard modifiers that was plugged directly into the fiction. We had overclocked created with an Aid roll by one of the hackers to back his partner's play, outmanuevered created by the other hacker as she danced her way through security protocols, flagged created by the banking system as it attempted to lock down some accounts, drained as the hackers emptied the accounts they were hacking, and disconnected in order to pull back out before the system could try and snare them with a backtrace.

    The contention system helped created a beneficial tension for even this minor situtation. I'm interested in seeing how it holds out over extended use. I could see how altering the turn order would have altered the dynamic of the contention as well. In the end the PC's got away with the goods free and clear, but it took some teamwork to do so.

    As the GM, I'd like there to be a little more meat in the Transmissions. Not much mind, just an extra bite or two. Maybe a general situation chart that I could then apply to my seed nodes in a way that makes sense or works for me. The seed nodes provide me with raw materials, but usually what I have trouble with is coming up with an initial situation that is dynamic and engaging. If I had some situations presented to me, along with the material already on the Master Chart for a Transmission, I'd feel a lot more confident in being able to just pick up and run the game.

    Yep. Los Angeles Sprawl.

    \n I have a thought on the Death thing, and to avoid bogarting Eric's new thread I'll post it here.

    We haven't gotten into any do or die Contentions yet (hopefully next session!), but if we had I might have come out feeling unsatisfied as well. One reason I dig Otherkind-inspired resolution is that you have somewhat of a safety net in being able to decide which die results to assign to particular outcomes. The player gets to pick their own poison.

    So I started thinking about what sort of hackage I might apply if the mechanism for PC death falls flat for me. The easiest solution I thought of was to just institute the option for an active defense roll for PCs when death is on the line and they have no Push dice. The defending player would roll the appropriate Verb dice + any Hurt dice. If their roll was equal to or greater than their opponent's roll they would avoid death and all the Push dice used by their opponent would transfer to them. That puts some risk into going for the kill and adds the possibility for some interesting table turning. I'm not sure if there should be some adjective fallout for the defending PC, I'm leaning towards not but trying it in play may change that.
