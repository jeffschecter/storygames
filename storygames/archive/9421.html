Your game will be played by people who are trying to recruit their non-gamer friends into their gaming group...

    \n Your game will be played by people who don't want to browse the rulebook during play. Include any summary sheets or other aids that would help this.

    Your game will be played by people who're used to other kinds of roleplaying games. To the degree your game differs, make sure you include the unrules (thanks Robin Laws) that break them of preconceived notions that wouldn't work with your game.

    Your game will be played by people who learn differently (visually, by examples, through explanations, etc.). Write your text to accommodate many different learning styles.

    \n I'm guessing the idea here is things to think about that will help improve your game/product.
    \n
    \nHere's some I've thought about:
    \n
    \nWhether or not they want to, people will end up browsing your book in play. Make it as easy as possible to find things quickly. Indexes, callouts, subject tabs, visually distinct pages, etc. will help, a lot.
    \n
    \nDetractors and supporters of fiction and art in game texts are a mixed bag. Do whichever makes *you* happy, but make sure you do it well, and make it support the rules text and the game play.
    \n
    \nAny level of jargon can turn off prospective players from reading your book. The less people are reading your book, the less opportunity for play you will have. If you must use jargon, don't assume that the readers will know it. Explain the jargon, and stick to plain english (or whatever language) as much as possible.

    Posted By: Andy* However you want your game to be played, there are people who will play your game in ways that you never intended. People who don't care what you (as a designer) intended, or how much you meant for folks to follow the rules. Just be ready to deal with that.
    \nAs a corollary, players are always going to drift your rules, and that's okay. Just make sure that in addition to explaining what to do and how to do it, you've told them *why* those rules are there. How do the rules support the driving themes of the game? In what ways is the system mechanically balanced, or not? This way when they drift the rules, they will understand how significantly they are changing the game.

    \nJonathan Protip: Most players of your game may never read the rules at all. Maybe the GM will read the rules; maybe not. Especially if you have a document that's more than 20 pages, most likely even the GM will skim your text rather than read every word. Have I read every word of Mouse Guard or Bliss Stage? No. Have I run the games a bunch and even written supplements for them? Yes. Also, there's a good chance that the GM or players will have learned the rules secondhand from someone else. They may not even own the text (or have owned the text in the past an no longer own it). For example, I have no idea where my print copy of Dogs in the Vineyard went. I loaned it to someone and never got it back, so all I have is the PDF, which I didn't re-read before running it last week. It went fine anyway.

    So... creating really great texts only gets you so far. Ultimately, the goal is to create great play of your game (in whatever manner you can) and create a community of people supporting and talking about your game. Doing that involves more than releasing a really good text, which is something many designers (including myself, at first) aren't really prepared for. I mean, once you publish a game, it's out there, right? People are going to read and play the game, right? No, not necessarily.

    \n Also, as a corollary to Andy's last post (which I fully agree with): don't take money for your game (frex: pre-orders) until you at least have a proof back from the printers. You have no idea what kinds of delays -- several of them taking months or more to resolve -- can arise. Maybe your artist or layout person takes your money but then disappears. Maybe the printer can't understand several of your fonts. Maybe it just takes you longer than you thought to resolve some issue and in the meantime you get ill or have a kid or become burnt out or uninterested in the project to the point that you can't complete it. You don't want people upset with you because of some commitment you made to them.

    Why should the player playing the game care that the author expected Rule X to be considered Very Important, and not discarded because It Didn't Work for Us?
    \nThe player, having an iota of sense*, knows that any new activity is a skill which may need developing, and will conclude upon a rule Not Working that the group rather than the rule is in error. This should be true if and only if the culture of design of that activity is known for tight, well thought out and broadly playtested designs. Eurogames for instance. If one of Reiner Knizia's rules seems to Not Work, it's almost certainly because your group hasn't yet found one of the strategies for winning, not because his design can be easily improved by something you think is obvious that somehow he and his playtesters missed.

    If players routinely take a different tack? That is some evidence at least that the culture of design is not known for one of a) tight, b) well thought out, or c) broadly playtested designs.

    To answer your rhetorical question: the player should care because in some cases he should believe the design he purchased is better, even for his unique snowflake of a group, than almost any similar design with minor tweaks. He should care because he should know that patchjob rules (in the main) at best treat a symptom of whatever caused his discomfort, and at worst don't even treat that due to design being a hard task.

    *this is the problem with many reasonable maxims

    \n Game Design Protip: Be clear and deliberate in your usage of the word "if."

    Good lord, I did a quick search through my latest draft and found my usage of that word really buried and inconsistent.

    Now I'm going through all the procedural documents and using that word at the beginning of a sentence only when it pertains to some instruction for actual play. In any other context, I'm revising the sentence for another format.

    Any "If" sentences that remain, I'm bulleting and listing out individually to make them more obvious.

    \n Designers also use the word "will" (i.e., the future tense) way too much. Don't say what the player will do. Say what she does.

    Also, please avoid passive voice unless you know what you're doing with it. Especially in rules, saying what someone does is clearer than saying that something gets done. In the latter, the reader must guess who did it.

      \n Protip: Make it clear to players how to start.

      That is, you need more than setting + character to reliably create fun situation. Make sure your text includes rules or guidelines that help players create a fun situation. It sucks when you're jazzed about the setting, you've created an awesome character that you're dying to play, and then the game grinds to a halt because no one knows what to do.

      Look at games like Dogs in the Vineyard or Sorcerer or D&D 3.5e or 4e for excellent rules for creating engaging situation.

      \n Protip: Do the math.

      Math is hard. Let's go shopping. But it's likely that some part of your game relies on the math of currency or dice. Understand the mathematics of your system.

      Probability and statistics is not the trickiest math in the world, but it's not the easiest either. Spend some time to learn how to compute probabilities of various dice scenarios. Or spend some time to learn how to use tools like Troll (or some programming/scripting language) to compute probabilities for you. As a last resort, ask for help. Do not ignore the math.
