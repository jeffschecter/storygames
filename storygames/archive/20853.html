    I'd be very interested to hear your examples of what you believe other PbtA Games do "wrong".
    \nSo, it's worth clarifying that when I say "wrong," I don't mean to say, "there's one true way to do PbtA." I do mean to say that, in my opinion, a lot of work went into AW to make sure that the various mechanics mesh together, gear-like, to give rise to a cinematic experience in which authorial power/protagonism is shared among players in a particular way (emphasizing giving them information and fictional potency, so that they are put in a position to make hard choices, and have their choices matter), and that other games often don't have those gears mesh with the same precision.

    That's often okay: it's why gears have tolerances in real life. How tightly you need the rules to mesh depends on what you're running.

    I generally hesitate to criticize PbtA games, because I feel like a large fraction of their authorship can be found around here. I may very well find them flawed to a greater or lesser degree, but writing games and game engines is /hard/, and in this sort of context I feel less like I'm offering constructive criticism and more like I'm pissing on someone's hard work. I prefer a little more distance between my critiques and the author's ears, yanno?

    But, I am willing to give one example, because it may be totally wrong (!) and therefore I feel more comfortable that the author may simply shrug it off.

    So, take a look at the City of Mist Starter. They chose to use a core PbtA mechanical base, because, as the author says, "FATE dice rolls felt bland" (I paraphrase slightly). It was originally meant to be a FATE game. Now, let's look at some core things that make PbtA work, and some mechanical bits of CoM. Caveat: it's just a starter set, for an as yet incomplete game, whose full text I haven't seen. I can be wildly off! So this is half-baked criticism of half-baked material:

    The core PbtA mechanic has a three-track outcome system:
    (1) got what you want, how you wanted it
    (2) got what you want, but not the way you wanted it (complication arises - what do you do about it?)
    (3) didn't get what you want, and definitely not the way you wanted it (complication arises - what do you do about it?)

    The point of the mechanic is the core gameplay loop: "players make choices. choices have consequences. players make choices about dealing with those consequences. New consequences!" Choice -> Consequence -> Choice is the core, snowballing, engine of PbtA. That's why players need to know what the general consequence of their choice is, and why the players are narratively empowered - their choice has to actually make an impact on how things play out. The key isn't "here's a die roll for great/okay/shit." It's a die roll to enforce the Choice->Consequence->Choice loop.

    PbtA games generally have a philosophical/mechanical bar on removing player choices. Thus, when you see Mind Control mechanics, they're generally implemented as something like "Mark 1 XP if you do what you're told and/or take -1 ongoing if you're acting in direct opposition to orders." You're basically incentivizing people to incorporate the "mind control" into the core gameplay loop of Choice->Consequence-> Choice. I kill that loop if I take choice out of your hands.

    So let's see a couple of the core mechanics from CoM. These mechanics are meant to enforce the core themes of exploring your mythic/mortal identities, and trying to balance them by not going against or ignoring them:

    In City of Mist, you're the physical embodiment of legend, and it's supposed to be very difficult for you to act "against" your legend. It has the Make A Hard Choice move, which includes the text "On 7-9, this aspect of you somehow deteriorates. Choose a new weakness tag. If you fail, your character is unable to muster the willpower to act against this aspect of herself and the MC makes a move as usual."

    So, first, this dice roll isn't "here are consequences for your difficult choice," it's "roll to see if you're /allowed/ to make this choice." On a 6- it's not that you made a valiant effort and you didn't achieve your aims, now deal with consequences. It's "nothing happened; your character didn't really want to try after all." That's not just narratively boring, it undermines the core Choice loop of the engine.

    A 7-9 isn't much better: "You gain a new weakness tag." It's not a fictional complication that requires the player to the answer the question "What do you do?". It's a mechanical penalty. Literally, 2/3 of the outcomes for a mechanic embodying the game's central tension are *fictionally irrelevant* for the players: in one, nothing happens. In the other, you did what you wanted, and your character gets a bit weaker. It's not actually a fictional complication. It doesn't snowball the plot.

    Another example is Stop. Holding. Back. In this move, a player attempts to " try to use your abilities in a way or scale you've never attempted before".

    So how does Stop. Holding. Back. work? "The MC will choose one stake and write it on a piece of paper, out of sight. Then, roll+logos. On a 7-9, you do it and pay the price. On +10, you can take a look at the stakes the MC wrote and then choose - do it and pay the price or hold back. On a miss, you lose control over your powers - the MC will say how." And the consequences are things like "you burn out your tags" (resource drain that recovers with downtime), "you're acting in contravention of your identity" (you take a resource hit), "you take a status" (debility, or not - but probably, since all the other choices here are negative), "you lose your theme" (you lose one-fourth of your superpowers, and have to replace them).

    On a 10+, you actually get the transparency to make a hard choice. You didn't "get what you want, how you want," you "get what you want, or avoid a bad consequence," which is traditionally a 7-9 in PbtA. The 10+ here isn't "what you want, how you want," it's "now the MC gives you the transparency to make a choice." "What do you do?" doesn't really follow, because most of these outcomes don't have a direct fictional consequence, either (e.g., you mark a crack, or it burns you out).

    The 7-9 doubly undermines the core mechanic: there's no direct fictional consequence to respond to /and/ you're not given the transparency to make a meaningful choice. ("Lose a Theme" is the best one: roll a core gameplay mechanic, and risk *unknowingly* losing a significant portion of your character's abilities/customization). Can you imagine something more divorced from the PbtA, "So, I think what you're describing is X. Is X what you're trying to do? If you do X, the drifters are definitely going to turn hostile." "No, I actually meant to do Y." "Oh!" That is, making sure the player choice is clear, establishing a general sense of consequences, etc. than making sure the player doesn't know what choice they're making, what the consequences will be, etc.

    It has the *appearance* of the PbtA mechanic, but in cargo cult fashion. It doesn't actually produce the core gameplay loop of PbtA. You're not really choosing a fictional course of action, you're just choosing a mechanical one, and you're not really dealing with consequences. You're just rolling 2d6 and getting 1 of 3 outcomes.

    That's just two moves, but since they're the moves central to the game's thematic focus, I thought that would be a good place to look. Fundamentally, the game *looks* PbtA-ish, but it's actual core mechanics completely forego the fiction<->mechanical interaction that makes PbtA work for driving consequential plot-heavy play.

    \n (Jeff, I never received an e-mail from you, but I got a private message, so I'll send you one!)

    As far as "any fertile ideas" go, each PbtA game tends to have its own. My recommendation is to start with AW, the "Beginner's Guide to DW", and Monsterhearts. Then you can look through the freely available materials for other PbtA games to see which ones you want to explore further. Most of the good ones have some neat thing which differentiates them from AW, and which might be worth "grabbing".

    For example, Vincent's work-in-progress, "Freebooting Venus", has a cool approach to treasure and experience, where you "examine treasure" and that determines how your character's life improves - whether you living situation gets better, or you have a chance to start a business, improve your skills, or some other thing. But there's nothing *fundamental* to *World games in that.

    Simple World and Dream Askew are free and really worth looking at, as well.

    For example, Vincent's work-in-progress, "Freebooting Venus", has a cool approach to treasure and experience, where you "examine treasure" and that determines how your character's life improves - whether you living situation gets better, or you have a chance to start a business, improve your skills, or some other thing. But there's *fundamental* to *World games in that.

    Simple World and Dream Askew are free and really worth looking at, as well.
