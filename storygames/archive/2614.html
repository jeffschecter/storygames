    \n The hard part for me, Andy, is that the Z'bri, the Fatimas, the River of Dream, and everything else were so loosely defined, most of what is being suggested could have been true in any given game of Tribe 8. I mean, we killed Joan and raised a new Fatima in our game.

    So here, I'll try and stretch it:

    After the transhuman society that spanned the galaxy fell in a great cataclysm, the planet Laurentia has been cut off from all communication. Whether this means that the rest of the galaxy is fallen to barbarism, still paralyzed by massive wars, or simply disinterested is unknown. The people of Laurentia have had little opportunity to find out. In the midst of the cataclysm, slavers from beyond the rim descended on the planet. They called themselve sthe Z'bri, and they stripped the people of their man-machine interfaces, boiled away their longevity gene therapies, and toppled their harmonious planetary government. The Z'bri went further, concocting terrible retroviruses and cybernetics to replace those "inferior" technologies, and twisting the people into grotesque minions fit only to serve their disgusting needs. The people of Laurentia began to experience generations for the first time in centuries -- and experienced those generations under the heel of the Z'bri.

    Then mysterious figures appeared among the people, spreading the word of hope that rescue was coming from the great, lost world that they had all but forgotten. These figures -- the Guides -- were hunted and persecuted by the Z'bri overlords and their twisted lieutenants, but the message endured. Finally, one night the Guides disappeared, and the Fatimas came to humanity. Animating the wreckage of the forgotten world, ten ghosts in the machines brought old robots and cybernetics to life for the first time in memory. They identified themselves as the Fatimas, compiled from the cultural memories of humanity and sparked to sentience by a force beyond the galaxy. They were here to liberate mankind from the yoke of the Z'bri.

    The ten Fatimas appeared in the ten labor camps of the continent Vimary, and they led a bloody crusade that liberated the slaves, slaughtered the Z'bri, and finally brought real hope to a world. After the crusade ended, they settled and began to reteach their people -- their tribes -- the ways of civilization, and to reteach them the right ways that would keep the Z'bri from returning from their homes beyond the rim. But all was not well: one of the Fatimas collapsed, her tribe collapsing and scattering; the remaining eight turned on their brother and slaughtered him. The collapsed Fatima returned, much changed and twisted into a shape only slightly less disturbing than the Z'bri that still lurked on the edges of Vimary and over the waters on the rest of Laurentia.

    Things got worse, and the Fatimas began to exile members of their own tribes for crimes real and imagined. Those exiled -- the Fallen -- gathered on a small island off the coast of Vimary, and came to call themselves the Eighth Tribe, and sought out the lost lore of the murdered Fatima.

    \n I actually liked the metaplot in Tribe 8, and comments in this forum made me realize why: the element of Fate/Destiny. When I consider the metaplot events as fated rather than scripted, it adds an entirely new dimension to the game. As a (related) aside, the most powerful (thematically) character I've ever played was a guy with the Dark Fate flaw in an otherwise disposable Vampire game. The text for the Flaw warned playing it seemed to railroad on the face of the description, but that the actual play with it was liberating. I found this assertion true in spades. The idea of running under a darkly fated setting just oozes with Awesome.
