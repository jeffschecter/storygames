\n For the Indie Game Company design challenge last year, I made an "investigation game". In Jared's critiques at the end of the contest he said, "An investigation game? Really?" A while later, I met him at Dreamation or Dexcon and asked him what he meant by that.

He said, "Every game's an investigation game."

I replied, "Yeah, but they're not real, good investigation games."

Not much to work with there. But now I think I have a better way to put it. In the player's search for the GM's facts that will move the story forward, there are two basic play styles that show up.

Style #1:
The players should actually do what an investigator does. Look at this, then look at that, then refine what they're looking for and look some more, until they're satisfied that they're fully equipped to move on. At the extreme of this style, a GM might do what I did in middle school: put the clue on the ceiling, and feed the players an increasingly frustrating stream of "no luck" until one of them finally says, "Where haven't we looked?! Anywhere?! What about the fucking ceiling?!" The GM is not actually trying to be a dick (or at least I wasn't). He's trying to support the group agenda of "cleverness pays". The players are going along with it because sometimes they decide to check the bottom of the beet jar, and that's where the clue is, and they get to high-five over their accomplishment.

Another facet or version of this play style is non-obvious clues. "Well, we found the key, and we found the map. What do we do now?" The GM tries to feed the players a reasoning challenge they can overcome. Sometimes it works. "The key number is 705. There are no 7-floor buildings on the map, but there is a post-office. Let's check the P.O. boxes." Sometimes it doesn't work. "Uh... I guess we enter from the North and start checking everything on the map as we move south? I know the apocalypse has nearly arrived, but we're out of ideas!"

Whether it be in an "investigation game" like Call of Cthulhu or a "game with investigation" like D&D2, a lot of new GMs and players leap at Style #1, because the challenge is familiar from puzzle games. Unfortunately, this process doesn't lend itself toward a satisfying narrative experience. If you're actually playing because you like role-playing, it's hard to get consistent satisfaction.

Style #2:
The other major style of investigative play takes the player effort and challenge out of the process. You will find something that will productively move you along through the unfolding plot. Maybe the GM will make you search briefly, but he's looking for any opportunity to hand you success. The group may eventually say, "Okay, the characters search the place very thoroughly. What do they find?"

Sometimes this is a functional part of a functional game. Other times, it leaves players who liked being clever feeling superfluous, and GMs who manage all the discovery feeling unappreciated. This play style is particularly doomed to failure when folks haven't made a conscious decision to not do style #1.

When done right, I think the "investigation" in style #2 is most about telling stories that resemble investigative fiction -- in any way except the part where the protagonist brilliantly solves things. Which is fine, because you still have all the other fun stuff from that genre.

I'm thinking of this now after re-re-reading Trail of Cthulhu. The book says, quite clearly, "This is not style #1. This is NOT style #1." It even goes on to say, "You can enjoy the fun of our style because this isn't style #1!" The problem is that, at that point in the text, "our style" is still described by talking about how players get to "investigate". "Investigation" is slapped all over the back cover too. So, the first time I played, the GM and I were both in this weird halfway place, where we knew what was bad about style #1 and were going to avoid it, but hadn't replaced it with a new M.O., because, to us, "investigation" was style #1. Despite knowing rules like "announce an ability use and receive a relevant clue", I approached play as if I, y'know, needed to cleverly find stuff and figure things out. I didn't realize that, instead of focusing on finding the trail, I should have just followed it easily and directed my energy to the sights and sounds along the way.

Anyway, to wrap up, I think there's no shortage of games that produce investigation-flavored fiction, and wherein the characters do plenty of investigating. Style #2 works. However, I've yet to see a play procedure set that adequately supports Style #1. I'm working on it, though.

Unfortunately, a lot of people really have little ability to engage in deductive reasoning, and so any game that does a good job of encouraging and supporting it might hold clues that could improve training methods.

    Things I've learned about how people fail to use deductive reasoning:
    - the most common failure is, "if at first you don't succeed, give up."
    - the second most common failure is, "if at first you don't succeed, ask someone else for the answer."
    - I also have to help people who are struggling with learning disabilities that make it difficult to interpret clues (e.g. a book spine labeled "romantic suspense" may seem clear, especially since we have a shelf for "romantic suspense", but a reading disability makes even such obvious clues difficult to use for some people)

Is the publisher known for producing a certain genre? Does the cover flap or back cover tell you anything about the book? I also teach what false clues to be cautious in over-using, such as the cover's aesthetic.

    \n How about considering pieces of information, rather than clues?

    For example, the piece of information the players are trying to discover is: who is the murderer? There are many ways they can deduce this. The GM does not have a list of clues, but knows how the murder happened.

    Some players follow a suspect; others analyse fingerprints; other question witnesses. The GM has a broad idea of where fingerprints would have been left and who would have witnessed what. Eventually, the players will deduce who the murderer is.

    \n I use a version of #1. Only I have a lot of clues strewn about that if pursued, will give the players a general idea of what's going on. In the off chance that they don't, play will go on because there's a kind of deadline to the situation. Example:

    My last Dark Days game. The characters are invited to a school reunion, only they have no recollection of every going to that school or ever living in that particular town. Slowly, they regain memories of the place and their classmaters. They arrive at the town and get more flashbacks (these are ''clues'' handed over, but they're mostly flavor) and eventually meet people they're supposed to know. The reunion is in 3 days and they suspect something sinister is at play.
    If I dissect the story, these are the clues ''provided''.

    Locations triggering flashbacks. If they investigate some particular areas (they're not specific, one of the players asked about there being a lake and then a beach, he found the beach) they are rewarded with memories of flashbacks that pad the plot.

    The people in the town. It's obvious everyone in this town is either old (50+) or young-ish (30 or below). This doesn't add up. Furthermore, everyone above the age of 50 doesn't speak English. Following any of them or breaking into a house will uncover clues that will make the plot more obvious.

    The core NPCs. Through discussion or investigation into their homes you can get some major information.

    Traditional research. Checking out the library or the town records should uncover some weird information that can help out with the mystery.

    Now in case none of this works (in my game my players investigated the core NPCs, did traditional research and investigated a few locations), the reunion will take place in 3 days, at which point things will be revealed.
    I also always make sure my players know that no action is not allowed. No action will ''break'' the game or my enjoyment of it. I think it's important because players will often hold back on actions for fear they're doing unrealistic things (breaking and entering, intimidating someone, stealing, etc).


    I find investigation games easy to run. I wasn't impressed with the Call of Cthulhu (at least my understanding) kind of investigating (library research, diaries and notes, that kind of thing) but I realized the important thing is, to just use logic when creating the story and the clues will fall into place.

    \n This breakdown is artificial. I was talking to John about this when we went drinking on Friday.

    You don't need to create a mystery in order to GM an investigative RPG. You just need to create characters who have done something that they're trying to hide or get away with, have some PCs whose best interests involve getting that information or finding out about the deed, and then turn them all loose on each other.

    This is what happens in Dogs in the Vineyard for example, which is effectively a cop game mixed with a Western. You come into the village, people tell you what's wrong and you investigate it and try to make it right. Meanwhile, you -- as GM -- don't have to either 1) feed clues to the players, like you're told to do in the GM advice in some investigative games, or 2) be especially secretive and make the players find stuff out. You "just play your NPCs" (as both John and Vincent like to say) and you push back against the players' investigation with your NPCs, not with some weird de-characterized GM manipulation or something.

    Honestly, I expect that Jared means something like that when he said "all games are investigative games." This kind of thing is, honestly, no different than what you do as GM in many, many other games. You just set up a premise and starting situation that pushes characters towards each other in an investigative fashion.

    Anything else, and in my opinion, the investigation feels really artificial. The NPCs become only the means by which characters gain information related to the investigation and the GM is often tempted into teleological framing where the outcome of the investigation is pre-determined, even if the order in which PCs acquire information can change. There's some right result that the PCs are driving towards finding. "The truth is out there," to quote the X-Files. However, in reality, there's no one truth out there. In an investigative game, it should be totally possible that the PCs come to the wrong decision, shoot innocent people, lock up the wrong guy, follow false leads, get caught up in red herrings, all that stuff. The situation should be complex, real, and changing, not some step-by-step deal. And I think the best way to do that is to "just play your NPCs."

    \n I've been thinking about the stuff David brings up here rather a lot lately.

    I think that his styles #1 and #2 are really just instances of two different problems people often have with how story games are run. The problem with games of the first type is that they're too "gamist," to borrow an overused expression - the solving of the mystery is treated as though it were a sort of linear, cumulative process like "levelling" in D&D. Whereas the second types of games always strike players as being "on rails."

    I've come to the conclusion that games based on the traditional model of mystery fiction simply aren't doable. If the GM starts out with the thought "The players' goal in this session is to discover that X did Y to Z," he will just always run into one or the other of the two aforementioned problems.

    A better approach for GMs is to be much more flexible about the significance of a particular mystery that the players will be trying to solve to the overall narrative. It's OK if they start out trying to find out where the lost explorer disappeared to, or who murdered Mrs. Wiggins, but there has to be some other plot device on the back burner in case they either figure things out too quickly or totally screw the pooch in attempting to.

    Two of my favorite narratives that work this way are the Robert Altman film Gosford Park, where the central mystery ultimately loses most of its significance, and John Le Carre's novel Tinker, Tailor, Soldier Spy, where the central mystery is solved surprisingly early, quite a bit before the still-highly-suspenseful denouement.

    \n My hint-dropping process goes like this:\n
    Mentally identify what I'd like the players to figure out next. Call this "C".

    Mentally identify whatever they already know that would help them figure that out. Call this "A".

    A + B = C. Create a "B".

    Introduce "B" into the fiction immediately, usually either by NPC speech or by "Oh, I forgot to mention, in this room there's also...".
    I dunno if writing it out that way would help anyone else actually do it, though.

    \n Here is where I've seen Stake setting really shine. The PCs are going to get the clue, no matter what, but the Stakes change the framework the clue exists in.

    Example: If the PCs make their investigation roll, it leads to a mob guy, who feels like it is in his best interest to cooperate with the investigation as long as it doesn't upset his apple cart. If the PC do NOT make the investigation roll, it leads to the mob guy, who is surly and will not cooperate, but the PCs know he is invovled. How much they want to push on a mob guy is now part and parcel of the drama.

    \n In Trail, the distinction between core clues and other clues is very important. Non-core clues do not have to be leads. They can be puzzles to be solved. For example, a point spend in a library might get you book, but there can be an actual problem to be solved in the book. What you won't do is go into the library and not find the book if you look for it. It's a very strange idea that there's "cleverness" in succeeding on a die roll.

    The core clues will only take you to the final scene, not necessarily solve the mystery (if it's there to be solved). Certainly, you won't have ever to do that rather dull thing where you have to give a shopping list of places to search. The other vital, vital point is that Trail ensures you get the core clue, but any other clever way of getting it is also fine.

    So, you can fine tune the level of investigative work to the needs of your adventure. If you want an adventure where you breeze through the mystery, where it's background to a narrative of increasing horror and dread you can do that (The Dying of St Margaret's is one such example), if you prefer just to make the core clues direct leads in themselves, and make the other clues an actual investigative problem for the players, you can.

    The breadctrumb trail idea is often scorned, but it's actually quite sandboxy and open; you think of an event, and work backwards away from it considering what traces might be left. Of course, you can improvise evidence, and incorporate player suggestions.

    \n Interesting thread, but you're missing an approach:

    Style #3 - Induction
    As GM, you figure out who did what, where, to whom. That sets the crime (or whatever) scene, upon which the PCs will soon stumble. Thereafter, your only task as GM is to decide--on the fly--if the hypotheses that the players suggest would or could lead to that scene. If a clue for which they decide to look fits the situation, then, sure, the players "find" the clue. If it doesn't, they don't (or find a red herring, if you want to torture them). You don't have to seed a thing, hope the PCs find it, and then hope the players can connect the dots; nor do you have to feed them every clue to "craft a story."

    Hell, you might even like their "conclusions" more then your prefabricated notion of the situation... and with an inductive approach, you can just switch to their idea on the fly, if you want (so long as their "conclusions" are not invalid given the body of evidence).

    This idea was first floated, IIRC, on the RPG.net LARP board, talking about how to run a mystery in a LARP, where seeding clues can waste a lot of time and money (and be seen OOC, which can ruin the mystery). The idea was "why seed? Let them find whatever they look for, so long as it doesn't violate the facts of the crime scene." Someone came along and explained to us that we were talking about inductive logic.

    \n Jaywalt, I guess I should provide a good example. Okay, let's say there's a town plagued by giant dogs. The adventure includes some fighting them and tracking them.     1) They solve the riddle. Then there's a dramatic but ultimately manageable scene where they fill the vase with holy water, smash it, and save the town.

If they flee, the town is irreperably damaged, and the PCs don't get the reward for saving it.     \n Dave,

    My personal take on investigative games (if indeed the investigation is the focus, and not just a backdrop, as in some Lovecraft-style stories) is that one or more players are putting in the work to create a mystery, and the game is a failure unless that mystery is resolved and revealed. Just like writing a ten-page character history and then never having it become relevant to play would be a waste of time.

    (I do think that this has much to do with available time: if I had an unlimited amount of time at my disposal, and so did my group, it might be worthwhile to create this sort of information simply to invest the game with a little more richness. But in real, practical terms, it seems to be very much a case of diminishing returns to me.)

    (You'll also note that my game Land of Nodd often creates unresolved mysteries, and I'm OK with that. But that's largely because no one had to waste their time writing up the solution to that mystery. It's just a point that's fine to speculate about: in our Imperial China/Wushu game, where did that plague, and the dead corpse in the wheelbarrow, come from? We never found out. We might have fun hanging out and comparing theories, but no one feels like their effort was wasted.)

    My only attempts to run investigative-style games were while playing traditional, task-resolution-supporting-type games (like D&D and GURPS). My experience with those games is that unless the GM willingly revealed information to the players (as in your Style #2), the mystery was NEVER revealed. A "simple" mystery would last for months (sometimes years) of game sessions, with each session adding to the mystery--which was enjoyable--but the big reveal would never come to the open unless the GM gave up on "Style #1" play and gave out more information than was warranted. What happened in those games, instead, was that the stories of the characters gradually outgrew the importance of "the mystery", until the mystery was pretty much forgotten. In many cases, the GM would now and then try to bump it back into people's consciousness ("Hey, guys! Remember that funny statuette you picked up?"), but that would rarely have any effect for more than 10 minutes or so.

    This happened in pretty much every game of this sort I can remember, both as player and GM. Inevitably, the players were more interested in what was happening to their characters (or _between_ their characters) than any kind of overarching mystery created by the GM. I suspect that was sometimes just a way of making sure the game didn't get too boring ("We're stalled on this mystery thing? How about my character falls in love with the same guy as yours? Now we can have fun roleplaying with each other for the next hour instead of banging our heads against the table.").

    I also saw a lot of (generally short term or one-shot) games where a complex background created some interesting and unexplained situations and behaviour in play ("Why is the Duke disappearing every night?" "How did this young pipsqueak inherit his title?" "Why won't the young doctor agree to my very favourable deal?" etc...). These games were fun, but, to be fully satisfying, involved the players hanging out with the GM afterwards and basically asking questions: "So, who was behind the murder? What was that thing with the needle we found all about?" Because the actual mystery was rarely revealed in play unless there was some heavy-handed Style #2 going on, whether from players or GM.

    My successful investigative-type games went something like this:

    Create a mystery, figure out what kind of clues it might leave behind. Now strip out everything except those clues. In other words, create a small setting within which every possible element the PCs might interact with provide some clue to the mystery. This is exactly like the Dogs Town Creation process, I'd say: every NPC you interact with will tell you something about what's going on, even if it's a lie. From what I can tell, this is also what made George's (northerain's) high school reunion scenario successful. Literally everything in that town the PCs enter is abnormal: a clue to Something Wrong.

    If every single NPC is involved in the mystery, you CAN simply play the NPCs as you would any invested character and the game will go, as Jonathan (jaywalt) suggests. But if many of the NPCs are completely unrelated to the mystery, you can't (unless I'm missing something, Jonathan!).

    In those games, there would also have to be either a strong goal the PCs were driving towards or an imminent threat. Something to keep things moving. Sooner or later a climax of some sort would be reached, when the mystery would be revealed (because it's tied to either player goals or the imminent threat). The satisfaction for the players was to be able to say, "I told you so!" (or, better yet, make preparations to deal with the reveal) when the mystery was finally revealed. Perhaps "less successful" in this case would be total surprise on the part of the players and "more successful" would be grim satisfaction at having figured things beforehand. ("Ha! I knew he was the killer all along!")

    \n [It's not my form, it's Charles Pierce's, but I'll try to make a clear example.]

    Scene
    The PCs come into a room where a window is open, a husband and wife are bloodied and dead, and the room is in disarray.

    That's it. I'm done prepping--are you? Now, I happen to know:

    Solution
    A werewolf killed them, coming and going via the window.

    And that's what the PCs need to learn, to prepare for the Final Confrontation (which is the fun part of any investigation, right?). How do they determine that? Do I have to scatter hairs about the place, make claw marks up the wall outside, etc? No. The PCs--the lot of them with their creativity and capacity to imagine FAR wilder shit than I could--instead ask stuff like:

    "Was the room's disarray caused by the people struggling?" - I know the answer is Yes, because I imagine the wolf busting in, startling them, and some struggle. I might have that PC make a check to see if he or she could tell that from the room--after all, I haven't actually put the room into disarray (in a LARP, I might not even have enough props to furnish and accessorize a room!).

    "What do the wounds look like?" - Medicine or Forensics check (and maybe a secret Animal Knowledge check for a PC or two) and I might reply, "Tears and rends, not cuts and stabs" or even go so far as to say, "bite and claw marks, larger than any you've ever seen," depending upon their ability.

    "We go outside and check the house under the roof--are there claw marks on the wall?" Why yes, yes there are.
    "And in the soil below the window, where the beast leaped down?" Yep.

    ...And so forth. It will take some ingenuity on the PCs' part to make the beast -> werewolf connection, and then to figure out which (N)PC is the werewolf; but the cool thing is that the GMs don't have to seed the world with enough stuff to point all that out. The players ask, a GM considers, knowing the Solution, and replies based on logic and the PC's abilities. Should the PCs get stuck--not able to imagine a hypothesis and thus can't test it for evidence availability--then the GMs resort to any one of a host of tricks one would employ with breadcrumb (deductive) or railroad (guaranteed solution, "investigation" only in service of story or color) methods in the OP: The Observant And Helpful NPC (AKA Look HERE You Tards!), The Reluctant Witness (AKA The Finger), Another Crime (AKA This Time With Feeling!), Close Enough For Government Work (AKA Say Anything So We Can Move On!), and any number of other tricks. But I would think it more rare for the PCs to get stuck with abduction than with deduction--they're making up the case, after all!

    And, thus, it is a Third Way. Not a One True Way. Just well suited (in particular) for large LARPs on public land and for lazy/poor GMs.

    So Paul, there's an assist to your "lotsa wasted prep" dilemma. As for your issue of playing for hours without solving the mystery, surely that's a sign of poor mystery-adventure design. Which is probably the bulk of the problem a good #1-style game design would need to solve. Let me throw out an example. It's what I've been doing on and off in Delve. It compromises #1 as soon as the going gets tough, but starts out with #1 in mind.

    There is a problem. There is a one right way to solve the problem. That is Task #4. There are 3 clues that will get you to Task #4 with enough info to have a chance of solving it. These 3 clues can be acquired by performing Tasks #1, 2 and 3. Everything in the fiction is set up to point at one of these 4 Tasks. The players can wander around and flesh out the color of the situation if they want, but everything they look for ends up saying "go do a Task". If the 4 Tasks are of a reasonable difficulty level (usually this means easy but not immediately obvious), the process of solving the first 3 will not take hours and hours. Especially if there's some vague clock ticking -- a simple sense of "things here are getting worse!" is usually quite sufficient. So far, all this is in keeping with the spirit of style #1, but is really just fodder for it. So now we get to the crux. The players have 3 clues. They know what they have to use that information for -- Task #4: solve the scenario's problem.

    Did the GM design his big scenario puzzle well enough? How tall an order is that? Well, here's the thing -- in playing through everything up to this point, there's been plenty of opportunity for the GM to make sure his clues have been properly received. To realize, "Oh, they breezed over that important detail I mentioned, and may have forgotten it. I'll have an NPC remind them." So, the GM makes sure the players have the needed information. The scenario ensures that the players know they needed this info, instead of banging their heads against Task #4 before gathering the clues.

    So, at this point, success or failure hinges of whether the GM can devise one satisfying logical task. I've been calling this "deductive" because of the cultural familiarity with that term in this context, but it's really inductive and then deductive. "We have 3 clues. What does that tell us about the scenario? Given what we've induced about the scenario, what can we deduce about how to fix it?" I don't yet have a system to help GMs do this. Just trying it myself, sometimes I succeed, and sometimes I don't*. But this doesn't seem an insurmountable challenge.

    *When I don't succeed, then I wind up helping, starting with improvized extra clues. Sometimes this even works without ruining the challenge! Other times, I do enough of the work for the players that it really isn't much of a challenge, and style #1 gives way to style #2, where most of the fun comes from fixing the problem in a dramatic way (e.g. "Just in the nick of time!") rather than having earned it with logical skills. This only works sometimes, and only if I have players who are flexible enough to enjoy style #1 or style #2, and only if they can forgive the clumsy transition.
