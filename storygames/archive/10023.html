So, Michael, you ended your last comment with this:\n
Something about the way skill challenges are described eats away at the very soul of role-playing and seems to turn what used to be role-played scenarios into a half hour of die rolling.
\nCouple of things:

1.) So, as much as you don't like how skill challenges work out, can you imagine how much more they would suck if you were just sitting there for a half hour watching it?

2.) You're bringing that pesky roleplaying thing into it again.

I think your original criticism is out of scope for 4E, but I absolutely agree that it's a poor assumption to use in other games without designing for it. In something like Primetime Adventures, I actively would not want my character to contribute to every scene and every conflict, because I have certain expectations for the fiction - there's a lot of character development that you can't do when everyone's present, and the mechanics reinforce a shifting "screen time" emphasis among the protagonists.

But in PTA, the content that's produced in other people's scenes is interesting enough that I can be engaged as an audience member. It's fun to see how other people's characters are going to deal with their Issues, and I can still participate by giving Fan Mail and contributing dice to a conflict.

4E is precisely the opposite of that. All of the mechanical action of the game is geared toward a group of people working together to overcome an obstacle. You can either overcome an obstacle by playing a minis battle game or participating in a simple, round-robin dice game where you try and roll high a certain amount of times before you roll low a certain amount of times.

In either case, there's nothing really going on outside of the application of those mechanics to keep me interested - it's like watching two people play checkers or Yahtzee. You can't even really sync the fiction to the mechanics in a meaningful way - I mean, you're talking about a game where as a cleric, hitting someone in the head with a mace magically makes one of your buddies feel better... except it's not really you doing it, because it usually just means that they have permission to use a healing surge, which they spend.

There's *certainly* no human drama involved unless the group heavily drifts it into place; it's simply not a system where "free-play" roleplaying is encouraged or even necessarily desired, at least according to the structure that the rules put forward. So in that case, I absolutely do want to be able to participate in and contribute meaningfully to every encounter. If I can't, I'm going to sit there being bored to tears.

    Ultimately my issues with 4E are personal ones. And I accept that. Whatever the purpose of the mechanics, whatever the angle of the game itself (historically or otherwise), all I can go on, when discussing things like Skill Challenges and At Will Powers, is my experience with using them.

    Prior to 4th Edition, I was never in a D&D game - Basic, AD&D, 2nd Ed, 3rd Ed, or 3.5e - where I felt that the mechanics were actively working to stifle role-playing. Whereas every one of 3 personal experiences with 4th Edition (and 2 anecdotal ones) has been quite the opposite. Rather than role-play, talk to NPCs, or strategize in combat, players now seem to simply play the same Powers in the same order (turning combat into a board game) and use the same skills in the same order in Skill Challenges, turning what used to be an opportunity for RP into a game of Yahtzee.

    With 3.5, I've seen players sit by for a half hour or an hour waiting for their turn. I've been that player. And I was never once bored, because what was happening was interesting and moved the story along. With 4e I always have somethng to do, and that something is generally picking from a list and rolling a die. Sure, there's more I could do, and I do try to do those things. But the base mechanics, while solving one problem, cause another. And I prefer the previous problem.

    Now, all that said...

    I think an important piece of this is what the game itself is meant to replicate (as you suggest with the mention of PTA). 4th Edition is apparently not meant to replicate the Swords & Sorcery Conan the Barbarian Fafhrd and Grey Mouser Lord of the Rings Thieves World Gord the Rogue style gameplay any more. It's meant to replicate some sort of Wushu Exalted-style heroism where everyone has awesome Powers right out of the gate. There's no character arc. The creators have said as much; to paraphrase, "a character is as interesting to play at 1st level as he is at 30th level; there's no more sweet spot." Well frankly I liked the sweet spots. I liked the imbalance. That's the sort of thing that drives narrative. Having weaknesses and overcoming them. Having a part in some situations but not others. Being the rogue who has to pick locks and dare poision needles instead of becoming a DPS monster. Being the cleric who has to carefully dole out healing spells instead of everyone having 87 Healing Surges to work with.

    Imbalance breeds story. I don't want a game where going from level 1 to 10 to 30 changes absolutely nothing except all the numbers get bigger. I want a game where the wizard has to struggle to stay alive until he can cast fireball, and then he becomes something ELSE.

    If LOTR had been based on 4th Edition Frodo would be smoking Ringwraiths all by himself at first level instead of suffering from a grievous wound through most of the story before barely succeeding only through the actions of someone else and then never recovering. Because the Ringwraiths would be scaled down to an appropriate level of difficulty. And afterward Frodo's player would get to pick the +1 item he wanted to receive from a wish list.

    I like what was. I do not like what is. I loved Decks of Many Things and Girdles that changed your gender and wandering monster charts and save or die checks because it was the great unknown that made things interesting. I used to be HIGHLY engaged. In every 4E campaign I've been in, the players are on autopilot.

    Previously, it was "You're in a maze. You can go any direction you want. Which way do you go?" and now it's more like "You're in a maze. Here's a map to the exit."

    As to "heart and soul" and suchlikes, I'm not a drama queen romanticist and I tend to hyperbolize to make a point. RPGs aren't going to save the world from itself. I simply think that role-playing games have tended on an arc towards encouraging more role-playing - yes, I even include 3.5e in there - and I think that 4th Edition is a huge step back towards D&D's wargaming roots, with more gaming and less open freeform "make stuff up" role-playing. And in that regard, it is less of a role-playing game than its predecessors.

    You used the right word. Mechanical. Every version of D&D has had mechanics, of course. Only in 4E do they feel mechanical to me. It's an artificial heart where there used to be something organic.

    Insofar as D&D is still the flagship for RPGs I think that's a travesty. It would be like selecting The Fast and The Furious to represent Hollywood in its entirety.

    Insofar as it's D&D and we all (mostly) know better than to expect more of it... c'est la vie.

    These are just my opinions and I enjoy sharing them.

    \n My group's issue, quickly identified even before we started playing 4e, was that the design of skill challenges pre-selects what skills are useful. That is like being told, "cleave, power attack and fireball are what you use, go to it". It is disempowering for players.

    I adapted the system to a "freeform the skill you're using to fit what narrative action you take to advance in situation". We still only let 3 skills be Primary as a rule and have rules for freeforming it others as Opened etc. I track what skills are used and people are not allowed to keep "going to the same ones as last time".

    This has lead to more creative play and empowering PC's. We've had the entire "dungeon crawl" free formed as skill challenge and gone straight to the "big fights".

The main difference is that the previous editions can handle that kind of drift okay, and 4E really can't, from what I've experienced of it.     Posted By: Leonard BalseraAlso, man, I'd have loved to see what your groups had going on with 3E fights.
    \nActual 3.5 Example

    The party of 4 is heading for a dungeon they've heard about when the DM rolls a completely random wandering monster up. A pretty powerful one. We're not prepared for it, and neither is he, but we go with it. Now we're in completely unexpected and dramatic territory. This encounter was not totally balanced beforehand. We might very well die here. Unless we think through this and play our cards right. We've got a healer, a rogue/fighter, a scout and a mage. We talk through our plans and decide that the fighter is going to rush one and try to draw it onto him so the others can get to better positions because they can't handle a full on assault. The scout tries to stay mobile to get his damage bonus while the healer backs towards the river and the mage tries to plot a way to use an AOE spell that won't also incinerate me. It's my turn as the fighter but everyone else is chatting and involved in the strategy that's unfolding. Every round counts because a bad die roll could end one of us. Turns out that the encounter generated TWO creatures and one comes out of the river and tears the healer in half. Now we're in for it. But we manage to strategize and maneuver and take out two of the three baddies while the third takes off. Then we collect the remains of the dead cleric and return to town. We're in a shambles now, and our plans to go to the dungeon for treasure are for the moment dashed. We're humbled. We have to negotiate with the head cleric to raise our friend, and the expedition is now costing us money. Now we HAVE to go get that treasure because we're in debt. During that negotiation, I (the fighter) have little to contribute, but I don't feel bored that there's an RP narrative happening for a few minutes that doesn't involve me because the story still involves me.

    Most of the above involves things that aren't hard-coded. The base d20 mechanic carries us through, but while we've got miniatures on the table there's no grid, just rough positions and estimates. The GM is winging a few things, and we're chattering and talking through the entire half hour fight. There is no need for mechanics to cover the cleric negotiations because the Role Playing handles that part. A few skill checks when appropriate come up, briefly resolved and then gone. Because of how things went down, we've got a story arc going now, an unexpected one that forces us to rethink the story and plan ahead. There's drama. That's how my 3.5 experiences have gone.

    4e COULD do all that too, but you'd have to more explicitly be ignoring a whole bunch of the rules to do it the way we just did it. The way Powers work would have completely changed the way that encounter went. Instead of making up maneuvers and discussing strategy, I would have used a power and moved 5 squares, and then the scout would have used twin strike every single round, and the mage would have cast his daily and then an encounter and then fallen back on the same at will over and over, while the cleric healed us from afar. After the encounter, which we probably would have all survived, we would magically find exactly the +2 longbow that the ranger had been waiting for. We would each earn an action point and continue on our journey. The entire combat would take about as long, but there would be no creative decision making in there. Just power usage. Assuming the cleric still died somehow, the negotiation with the high priest would have been a skill challenge. My fighter would have repeatedly tried to intimidate him because that was his mathematically best skill. Because everything comes down to die results, the whole role-played discussion becomes meaningless. The game is pushing us to get it done and move on to the next dungeon room. That's how my 4e experiences have gone.

    At this point in the argument, Page 42 usually comes up (see Simon's link, above). And that's all well and good that there's a page that tells you how to handle "everything else." But I haven't seen it used. Why? Because the players don't try to stunt in 4e. They have Power Cards and those are the most mathematically appropriate things to do for any given encounter. You COULD do anything you wanted, but why would you when you have a list of suggestions in front of you?

    It could also be argued that 3.5e also required miniatures and a grid but that's patently untrue. Spells have ranges and AOEs given in feet. With that you can always freeform it and guesstimate things. 4e essentially requires that everyone in the world move in squares.

    Heck, even in my 1st edition game we had more creative combat scenarios going on. The rogue climbed walls and dropped things on people's heads. The cleric took cover behind the mule. The mage ran out of spells and started throwing darts. We had to think on our feet using limited resources and there was an inherent power imbalance and an element of risk that just is not there (in my experience) in 4e.

    I have been and remain constantly engaged in D&D, in a manner I never was in 4e. YMMV, but that's the way I've seen it.

    \n One thing that's come up in this thread that I've seen in a number of criticisms of 4E are things in the neighborhood of "there's not as much challenge in 4E because encounters are all perfectly balanced."

    As someone who's both played and GMed 4E (and, to come clean, likes it better than previous D&D editions, but not as much as other games), I find this criticism confusing. Every version of D&D has had some method for trying to figure out how much of a challenge various encounters pose. And all of them have advised the GM to, more or less, throw things at PCs that they can handle (or at least warn them if things are going to get too dangerous).

    Things like monster level are essentially information, not a straightjacket. Arguably, the 4E system of encounter balance is more accurate than previous editions'. But - and this is an honest question - why do you feel it somehow prevents you from having games where the PCs can't be assured that they can handle everything. As a GM, knowing roughly how much trouble the PCs are in seems like an asset, even if I'm not going to stop them from getting in trouble.

If you're paying ANY SORT of attention and following the rules, you always draw.

    I would rather run Tomb of Horrors a hundred times than run a perfectly balanced 4e module a single time.

    \n I've been running skill challenges more or less the way I run the GM's turn in Mouse Guard. Here's an overall goal, and here are the success/failure conditions.     \n Heck, you have to have certain skills (I wouldn't call 'em special) to play 4e with story in mind.
    \n
    \nLet me ask a question. Let's say you're playing PTA, and the fiction is about trying to escape a mysterious island. Do you feel compelled to always make choices which most directly get your PCs off the island, or do you make the choices which serve the narrative and which highlight the flaws, strengths, and personality of your PCs?
    \n
    \nHere's the money quote from this thread for me: "Given specific options, I choose from those options, and because there are so few optimum choices, I can choose them on autopilot." That's a valid way to play 4e and it's certainly a strategy that some people will enjoy while others won't. But you absolutely don't have to always make optimal decisions. I find, both as GM and player, that making dumb decisions improves the fiction during both combat and skill challenges.
    \n
    \nConcrete example time!
    \n
    \nI was running H2, and my party ran into this big lumbering automaton monster that was hard to hit, didn't hit too hard, and had a zillion hit points. I played the monster with all my tactical wiles. It was an intensely boring fight. The PCs took down the rest of the monsters in the encounter, then spent like six rounds hammering on the automaton. Yawn.
    \n
    \nThe next time they ran into one of those, I remembered to avoid perfection, and the automaton acted without cunning. It provoked a lot of opportunity attacks, lurched towards whoever had last threatened its master, and so on. And wham: now it wasn't a token on the board, it was a part of the fiction. Through its actions, it reinforced the picture the PCs had formed of the master's plot. It wasn't an isolated thing, it was a dumb servant, and that made a ton of difference.
    \n
    \nI'll note that this is not in any way an obvious skill, and I don't know of any D&D; texts which teach it to you.

    I completely understand the argument that D&D4e is an RPG like Diablo or WOW is an RPG wherein you choose a class and race and run around bashing in skulls and using powers over and over whilst gaining experience and leveling up so you can bash in larger skulls and use different powers. I completely understand the case being made for making all classes more or less equal in some regard such that they all have some cool power to use at every mouse click cum round of combat. And to some degree I also understand creating a system wherein the powers are more balanced and mathematically optimized to the point where a novice can more or less figure out which powers to use during which phase of the combat.

    And I likewise understand the argument being made that there are ways to circumvent that, but I don't think circumventing the system to get a desired outcome is saying great things about that system.

    The fact remains (for me at least) that prior editions played notably differently. It felt like there was more choice, and that it was more encouraged. There was a notable imbalance to the classes which meant that you could pick the one that matched the play style you wanted to emulate. Are you a newbie? Play a fighter and learn the ropes, then maybe swap to wizard when you want the bookkeeping to handle too. There was no obvious framework to everything that happened outside of combat - you used a few quick skill checks here and there, but for the most part it was a big open world where the GM would have to come up with things on the fly, where narrative flowed and I feel creativity was more called for. There were fewer rules, and less obvious mathematical choice, and while (in 3.5 especially) there was plenty of room to min-max and play Diplomancers and whatnot, there was also a lot more room for story and creativity. Not only because it was encouraged, but because it was necessary.

    Some say I'm crazy and that D&D has never been like that, but it was for me, and has been since I got that Red Box set for Christmas in 1984. From the very start, I would tell people who asked that Dungeons & Dragons was like a game in your head where you could do anything you wanted and there was really no winner at the end of the game session. "Why would you play a game you can't win?" they would ask. And I would just shake my head. They just wouldn't understand.

    But I can't really say any of that about 4e without wincing. The game is necessarily played on a table now, not in your head. You can theoretically do anything you wanted, but the game encourages you (sometimes subtly, oft overtly) to use specific powers at specific times because they make the most mathematical sense. And frankly, you can win at D&D now. Every little combat feels like a little "Ding" and a pat on the back. You get WIN POINTS for it. Powers reset. You can almost see the little POWER UP icons floating over the miniatures. The edges around encounters used to be hazier, and a dungeon (for me) never felt like a sequence of blocked-in encounters handled one at a time all in a row. The edges are sharper now, the goals clearer, the win states more obvious, and the paths to victory are brilliantly clear. And all of that SHOULD make for a better game, and I think as a GAME 4e is far superior. But none of those things, in my mind, improves upon what always made D&D (and role-playing in general) so interesting to me. I never played it because it was a game; we had Monopoly and Sorry and Dark Tower if we wanted to play games. I never played it for the mechanics. I played it because it was D&D. And now, for me, it's not any more. I used to be engaged, as a player and as a DM. Now I'm not.

    Of course, D&D has evolved. Basic D&D was a crippled thing missing a leg. AD&D added three arms and two polearms and cut off the other leg. 2nd Edition gave it legs but put them on backwards, and then added a few more arms which promptly fell off. 3rd Edition got the legs and arms figured out, but it added rocket launchers to the shoulders. But through all that, it was still the same core game. With 4e, it feels like D&D was dragged out back and shot, and now there's an imposter wearing its skin.

    Like you said, there are plenty of other gaming options, and I've chosen them. But I still think talking about 4e -- though I've probably given up on it for good -- is appropriate when it relates to specific issues (such as player engagement). Like I said, I didn't used to play D&D for the mechanics, but for D&D, and now I'm in the reverse position where I'm not playing D&D because of the mechanics. And I think that's worth discussion, as a game design issue. I don't play Exalted any more either, but I'll be happy to discuss that game's ridiculous dice pools with you. And I'll never watch "Austin Powers in Goldmember" ever ever ever again, but if you bring it up in conversation I'll be sure to tell you why.

    \n Jenskot (and others) \u2014 I ran an awesome skill challenge at the end of the session of 4E I DM'd earlier tonight.

    The PCs had to rescue a group of Eladrin civilians who were trapped in a neighborhood overrun by Far Realm creatures. These survivors include the family of one of the PCs, long ago exiled from the Eladrin city but now returned. The tower they were holed up in was the last one standing in the district, and was at the edge of one of the floating disks that comprise the Eladrin city.

    First, the PCs had to fight the besieging monsters, which wound up being quite a cool fight. But, then the tower was collapsing! Oh noes! Get the civilians out safely before it does, or they will all die!

    I started with five suggestions for them, and said that many of the options presented would have hard DC's (but that would net them two successes, which I admit is a bit of a house rule but I think a damn good one). Others had moderate DC's. In addition to taking some of my suggestions, the players got really creative, both with primary and helping rolls. Successes #4 and 5 (out of 6; it was Complexity 3) came from the Dwarven Warden setting up ziplines, using the Tiefling Warlock's climbing kit, to move some of the survivors from the collapsing building onto a nearby building that was damaged but not destroyed. And the final success came when the Warlock used her Bluff skill to lie to the Eladrin children, the final group trapped, about how it really wasn't that bad, the fire wasn't that hot, the shards of crystal weren't that sharp, etc.

    That last example is interesting because Bluff is far and away the Warlock's best skill, and she does use it quite a bit in skill challenges, but it doesn't feel munchkin-y or inappropriate because she can always make it work in the roleplay. And in fact, I think in this case in particular it actually strengthened the fiction\u2014by lying to the children about how the situation "isn't really that bad," she was in fact establishing just how dangerous the situation actually was.

    The fight took awhile, of course, though not excessively so, but the Skill Challenge took about 10 minutes.
