\n
\n So, there was a thread in the forge or whereever about how in some games (you know which these are), the biggest challenge was creating an character build that could kill stuff efficiently before actual face-to-face play began, and actual play would be just you exploiting this pre-built character's strengths to the fullest in a forseeable and repetetive way. "Play before play" - most of the significant choices you could make are made long before your character enters the playing world so to speak.
\n
\nNow, that probably sounds pretty horrible, right? But I LIKE that kind of play. In fact, I'm playing a game of this type right now (it's an online flash thingy, not an RPG, but still...) and it's been super awesome. Looking at what I have and trying to build death on wheels from it, building from a general idea to what a build should be able to do, testing it out and adjusting accordingly, getting new abilities to work with that increase the number of possible build combinations to ridiculous levels... My little munchkin heart is full of content.
\n
\nPlaying this game, I realized that there are fun ways and unfun ways to do 'play before play' type games, and I think there are some general principles here that it might be useful to talk about.
\n
\nSo here we go:
\n
\nEase of changing builds: In the game I'm playing now, you don't really play a character - you play a pilot of a vehicle that you pull together from different parts, and the main abilities are associated not with you as a pilot, but with your car as a combination of parts available in your garage. This means that, when you get a new 'ability' in the form of a new part, you're: 1) not tied down to using it forever; 2) not stopped from taking different abilities/parts later on. Compare to: Welcome to level 2! Pick carefully between Slash, Stab, and Tickle, because what you pick will stay with you forever.
\n
\nLow cost of failure: When you lose a game and it's the end of everything, it's frustrating. When you lose a game and you can spring back to the same battle with a different build, a loss can be interesting in that it lets you see how your opposition works - and works succesfully.
\n
\nA paper for every rock: There should be an easily identifiable counterstrategy for every strategy taken. This way, you don't get stuck in a single repetetive sequence of action, but instead get stuck in multiple repetetive sequences of action (never said play before play could be perfect, no?).
\n
\nVariable challenges: It's easy to create a build that will level all oppossition if you have good information about what you'll be facing in advance. Your rivals should have a fair chance of adapting to your strategies, and you should, too.
\n
\nThe right to decline: For the game to be about skill in making the best build possible and not about getting the "best" stuff and using it to straightforwardly smash the opposition, it's extremelly useful if both parties involved get to choose whether to engage into the battle or not. This is probably the best way to ensure that the game is fair - that both parties involved have a chance to win that THEY think is good enough to risk it.
\n
\n
\nI'm not sure it's possible to make a roleplaying game under these principles... But I'm fairly certain that it wouldn't hurt to try.

    I struck this out because it's not a binary proposition, so that sentence is hyperbole.

    What I *mainly* want to comment upon is that I've expressly designed my LARP rules with the very opposite notion: survivability, defense, and healing are all cheaper to buy (as character abilities) than harming. Obviously, the rules I'm building assume a group wants to play toward long-developed characters (but that said, if a group wants a Team Fortress-like experience, they can just toggle off or limit the Maximum Up Cost for defense and restoration abilities).

    You're making some good points regarding game play, though, in particular addictive game play. But every game will have its own particular pacing, be it to suit styles of the players, genre appropriateness, or a notion of balance--and the relative efficacy of survivability versus lethality is one of the bigger knobs to twiddle.

    \n Staying power <> immortality. They could end for any number of reasons: run away faster than the aggressor; all opponents unconscious or dead; various special abilities (e.g. bind) that make fighting impossible for long enough to escape or talk it out.

    The main point behind building GLASS with a defensive slant is to provide for more fluid and varied conflict/combats amongst folks with little actual martial skill (or, more so, amongst folks with varying actual ability to spar/shoot). One-hit kills, while realistic for the vast majority of weapons, don't make for very exciting combat--in fact, high-lethality systems tend to discourage combat significantly, especially with long-term plots and multiple sessions. So I build into GLASS the basic notion that it's cheaper (and therefore easier) to tool up on defense than offense.

    But nothing grants invulnerability. Every paper has a rock, and even if you put every build point into Natural Armor and other defenses, you'd still only have AT BEST an order of magnitude more net "hit points" than someone who spent a handful of build points on defense (i.e. you could tool up to about 30 while a "normal" person has only 3, butt-naked). Testing might cause me to adjust costs a bit here and there, but I doubt I'd get away from the basic "defense is cheaper than offense" notion.
    -----
    I want to add one:
    Only allow one side of an opposed dichotomy to scale - Don't allow arms races: if one can increase damage output and defense equally, then an arms race ensues, which can cause significant imbalance between veteran players and new players, or which can narrow a players options in character design--e.g. "everyone MUST have at least 5 Damage reduction to even survive a round of combat" and similar localized, game-cultural trends (i.e. the "metagame").
    (That's a very LARP-oriented notion, but it could apply to long-running table top games that admit new players from time to time.)
