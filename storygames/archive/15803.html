\n Here's a question for those who have published their own games, coming from someone who hasn't, but would like to:

How tightly do you control access to your games before you publish them, and why?

Sharing your game with potential playtesters seems to be a foundational part of self-publishing RPGs, and it seems like there are a lot of possible ways to do this, from making your design doc freely available online to having those interested in playtesting your game request a copy by email. There's also the question of what level of availability helps to generate interest in a game without suppressing sales.

I don't know. I have no clear opinion. That's why I'm asking.

    \n In indie publishing, the most important thing is that people know you and your game exists at all. Nobody, as far as I've ever heard, has ever lost sales from a freely available product or draft, even if the print product they release is exactly the same. In fact, it's usually the opposite: people find out about your game and start playing it because early drafts are free. This is especially true if nobody knows you from a hole in the ground: not all of us are Vincent Baker or Ben Lehman. Putting yourself out there is part of the process of building name recognition and a reputation for making cool stuff.

    I design everything in the open, even half-finished things that never get finished, because I like being honest about the design process and sharing the design experience with others, especially if nothing comes of it. As somebody (Vx?) said, the natural endpoint of every game is abandonment, so if I design all by myself, nothing may ever come of it but the stuff in my head. Out in the open, it could inspire somebody else, and sharing ideas and building off others is how the medium advances.

    \n One potential downside of being totally open about drafts - those drafts will be out in the wild and people will play them in lieu of purchasing the finished game sometimes. And they will be disappointed. This happens occasionally with The Shab Al-Hiri Roach, and it is going to happen with Durance. I don't think this effect should be considered serious enough to make you change your behavior, but it is a known thing - every so often someone will say "that game sucks and is totally half-baked" because they played the version you wrote in a week.

    If you find yourself sharing a draft looking for comment and players, hopefully you will have enthusiastically commented on and played lots of other games in similar circumstances already without any expectation of reciprocity.

    \n The counterpart to Jason's concern is that sometimes people will like an earlier version of your game better than later versions, so you can kinda have edition wars with yourself. I know a bunch of people in Seattle who still play the alpha version of Geiger Counter, for example. Sage and Adam will probably have similar issues with Dungeon World, but that game is meant to be easily hackable anyway. Honestly, I don't see that as a problem, really (at least not as much as the issue Jason mentions). People are still playing your game, whatever version they're playing.

    Jason's other point is right on too. It took more than a year after the alpha of Geiger Counter was released for it to get significant play, and I was pretty active in the indie games community for 3-4 years before that. It takes patience and time to gain people's attention and for them to spent their valuable time on your game when there are a bunch of others out there that they'd probably prefer to be playing.

    Posted By: mease19I'll be curious to see how the DW app turns out. I could see having the ability to update everyone's version, simultaneously, being really useful.
    \nOn the flip side, any automatic revision system can be maddening if there aren't detailed change notes included. You'd think you were being Gaslight'ed: "I just read this rule yesterday! I could have swore it said I got +1 vs orcs!"

    Which relates to releasing multiple drafts of playtest documents: there were four major (and by major, I mean very different text) playtest drafts of Microscope, and then several smaller patches along the way. In hindsight I'm surprised the playtesters didn't murder me (even though I did include version change notes). It's very hard to read text and spot what's changed from a previous version because your brain is full of presumptions.

    Or to look at it another way: every single playtester is someone who might have a really hard time adjusting to the final version, because they're still carrying around the playtest versions they tried in their heads.

    \n Ben - whether PDF or App, we've tried to take a software approach (Sage and I are both, by profession, computer nerds) to our releases, gathering changes to the beta in point form and releasing patch notes whenever we update the game.

    Once the full version of the game is out, the Red Book and Hack will likely become unavailable and only the final version will be around for official access. People will still have Beta X PDFs floating around, I'm sure, and maybe some of them will choose to play that instead of the full version, but the full version will have polish and changes that aren't in the Beta, too. *shrug* We're finding, so far, that the benefits of a semi-open system (the Adventurer's Guild) outweigh our general concerns of people getting our game for free.

    \n I made my second game, Anima Prime, available from the first alpha version. Conplete beta versions as well as the final full text are available for free under a Creative Commons license. The result has been people playing, enjoying, and talking about it. I was also contacted by an artist who had played a beta and was excited to contribute art. So it was a total success story.

    If you want to see a much bigger achievement, look at Evil Hat. They put FATE out for free, creating a huge fanbase over the years, which translated into making Spirit of the Century and later FATE games massive successes.

    \n For the record, the system that Adam and I use:

    -Free "Hack" version which requires AW to play.
    -Basic version available for pay while we finish up the full thing.
    -Full version in Beta available to anyone who shares thoughts about the Hack or Basic version

    It's been marvelous. It's a trade of a sort: help us make the game better and/or spread the word and we'll give you stuff in return. The real test is going to be when the full game comes out, but I'm not worried.
