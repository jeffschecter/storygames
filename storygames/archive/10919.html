\n "Carnage amongst the stars"

"Fighting the forces of darkness so you don't have to"

"Play the greatest TV show that never was"

Great games seem to have great tag lines. But is it important?

If you have good artwork, then the mood of the game will be set by the cover, while the tag line either supports tone, explains content, or both. In the abstract titles, like 3:16, the tag gives us our first understanding of what's to come. In that respect, it seems important. But I get the feeling, more as I bang my head trying to come up with something myself, that whether or not I conjure something catchy, it is less important than whether or not the tag line is useful.

For example, in Hell for Leather, the closest I've made it to a decent tag line is "a team vs team roleplaying game" or "a roleplaying game of deathmatch hyperviolence." The former illustrates game play, while the latter illustrates tone. Even though the first one is a bit dull, I think it's more useful for a new audience. But I feel like I'm missing something. Maybe I could harmonise the two concepts.

Do you think that you have been influenced to research a game because of its tag line? Can you give an example?

    If you're marketing a thing, you need to be able to market it in the media you have available. Unless you're going to post a picture or icon \u2014 and expect others to do it, too \u2014 every time someone mentions the game online, you need to do something you can type. If you expect someone to talk about it in a podcast, they have to be able to say it.

    \n

    It's hard to work out your elevator pitch, but figuring it out is exactly the same as figuring out what you're doing.

    \n

    For myself, I don't care if a game is a team vs. team roleplaying game. That doesn't tell me about the kind of fun I'm going to have. It just describes a technical fact about the game. "A roleplaying game of deathmatch hyperviolence" tells me what the game's about. If I want to play a roleplaying game of deathmatch hyperviolence, my eyebrows will shoot up at the mention of your subtitle and I will throw money at my computer screen to get it.

    \n

    There are other elements, too. The colon in Shock: (which isn't optional \u2014 just ask Remi) is part of that. It has a meaning, but it's a typographic logo, first and foremost. carry uses lowercase letters to talk about the small, human scale of the stories, despite the geopolitics and wartime heroics involved. FreeMarket has the caps in the middle because it references a particular technologically-based utopianism. In both of these cases, I inferred correctly about the game before I knew much about them. It so happens that they're both things I'm interested in.

    \n

    So, that's what.

    Posted By: whiteknifeYou're right that it can be really hard to boil a game down to one line though.
    \nIt's often easier than boiling a game down to one short title, though...which is what I notice when I look at the examples of good taglines. First we have the title, which typically tells me squat about the game -- then comes a tagline, which in one sentence usually at least gives me an inkling of what the game is about (and sometimes the tone of it).

    Obviously, the ideal would be if you could tell everyone who looks at your game exactly what it was about in a way that attracted their interest. But very few people would sit still for that even if you were capable of doing it, so...yeah, a tagline. One sentence, easily stuck on the cover or on the spine of the book or in big letters across the back, that gives them a tiny bit of context to hang the (otherwise meaningless) title on.


    (If you just tossed "Hell For Leather" at me, I'd say it was a Western game with sixguns and horses. Second guess would've been a biker gang game. I would never in a million years think "team deathmatch game." A good tagline prevents me from mentally filing your game in the wrong place, and also pops it out from the background noise in a way that the title alone never could. Now I'm thinking, "Oh, I totally would've said that was the name of a cowboy game," and you've just earned a second glance.)
