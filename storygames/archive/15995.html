2009:D&D4E: "The game informs the fiction informs the game informs the fiction."
\n...with D&D4E, the game felt so utterly disconnected to fiction, and so very board-/tactical war-gamey that I literally cannot grok this statement. And I'd really like to.
I think your reaction is a reasonable one, given how the 4E rules focus on running well-ordered encounters and ignore any notion of story. But I believe that every mechanic in D&D (or any game, really) can drive fiction, and a large part of my DMing project is to identify the fictional opportunities within the 4E rules. I give 4E credit for this revelation because of the huge number of fictional opportunities contained within it.

Specifically:

1. D&D4E quantifies risk extremely well, and thus supports fiction about risk and jeopardy very well. When facing a risky encounter with a time constraint, does the party care enough about the fictional stakes to press onward, or do they fall back? As the DM, I want to tempt them towards risk, to place fictional stakes and opportunities in conflict with optimal game behavior.

Twice in the campaign I've granted gift powers to the PCs -- very potent new abilities that can settle encounters, but also impose fictional consequences. Again, fiction is placed in coflict with game optimization.

2. D&D4E has a huge number of mechanical touch points that can be used to inform fiction. Race, class, paragon paths, religion, powers, falling damage rules...

Our dreadnought fighter was dumped by his infernal girlfriend because she didn't like his paragon path choice. Since he has the ability to fall from a great height without dying, his paragon path test was to fall from a slightly greater height than he was certain to survive. He has a Tide of Iron power that allows him to shove people on the battlefield; he has settled at least one pure-fiction scene by busting that power out at a critical moment.

Our battle captain attracts followers whenever he's in a civilized town and is naturally regarded as the leader of the party. He took a Diplomacy buff feat, which informs my encounter design. Whenever I lay down an encounter I have to think about negotiation points. He has a teleportation power and is a magic hacker with a crazy Arcane skill bonus. Once he used those in clever combination to teleport a devil chain-parasite out of a sky whale and completely short-circuit a line of fiction that that I was planning.

Somewhere along the line we figured out that our druid lives half in the spirit world and half in this world. He's got information-gathering opportunities that the others don't, and that's a fun fictional channel to play with.

We have a dwarf avenger of the Raven Queen. If any of those character choices were different, his fiction would be different. For instance, if he wasn't a Raven Queen dude, then the kenku flock tagging along with the party would not love him so much and cackle with glee whenever he does something badass.

3. Fictionally odd situations are potent fiction-creation opportunities. Since the rules are about encounters and not about fiction, occasionally they make for odd situations. For instance, our fighter has a Come And Get It power that sucks surrounding enemies into neighboring squares. The power works on any kind of enemy. How do we fictionally justify this power working on brainless oozes? That's a super-interesting fiction-creation challenge to me.

And once we've settled it, we know more about oozes and more about the fighter's power, and we can reincorporate those details down the road. In this way, the game really becomes our game, a game unlike any other D&D game that has ever been played.

    \n This is so weird a perspective that it's interesting. I myself can't take 4th edition seriously as anything but a skirmish war game, but it's nice that it's working for others. This definitely has mostly to do with me not finding the fictional detail of the game compelling: it's exactly the sort of paper-thin American computer game fantasy stuff that elicits only frustration from me. No emotional connection, no creative agenda, and thus no roleplaying. This is a bit better when I actually start playing and putting in the detail, but then the game kicks me in the nuts by forcing everybody to fight life and death battles all the time, with no graceful handling of the very real possibility that the party loses a fight.

    (Considering 4th edition a skirmish war game doesn't mean that I don't like it - I like wargames! I just don't have the social space for playing them seriously at this time of life. I've definitely considered starting up a 4th edition campaign several times, but it's always been in wargaming terms - we'd set up fights and see who wins and who loses, and in general put the system through its paces. Probably no non-combat scenes or the like.)

    \n My own view on 4E is that nothing really important should happen that's not on a battlemat. When you incorporate Rob Donoghue's insight that, if you give everything hit points, the successful use of a skill is just another excuse to roll damage, then you can create interesting and complex situations more or less on the fly by laying out a grid, describing a situation, and giving the PCs things to hit (with weapons, with spells, and with skills). The interstices between those "on-stage" scenes are procedural and expository: they advance the plot by fast-forwarding through the slow parts and providing an in-game explanation for how the PCs make their entrance. Once on-stage, though, the PCs drive the fiction with their choices, and success or failure have consequences for what happens next.

    \n I love your ideas, Johnzo!

    Perhaps my favorite idea for incentivizing non-game-optimal behavior that's tied to the fiction is from Rob Donoghue, called momentum. The idea is simple but powerful: for every consecutive encounter beyond the first that you do without taking a full rest, the group gets a cumulative +1 to hit and damage.\n

    \nThat was me! I was the GM!

    I think in the early part of the game, we did, as a group, engage reasonably well with the game fiction. As time went on, I felt like fiction-first moments started to decrease, for a couple of reasons:

    -Settling into the mechanical certitude provided by the 4E framework / subconscious realization that
    -Narration fatigue, given how often you're using your powers in that game
    -It's fun and cool to describe a power the first time you're using it, but less cool the 78th time.
    -Laziness/GM burnout on my part, which lead to both less mechanical engagement from a moment-to-moment point of view, but also simply more encounters that were simply a bunch of monsters stats thrown together in accordance with the matrix in the DMG
    -Some changes in the player base (notably your departure, Abel\u2014not only are you a very fiction-first sort of player, but your character was the mother of one of the others, which created a nice and purely fictional tie-in*)

    One technique I adopted later, but still fairly early on, was this thing with +1 Tokens. I've mentioned it here on S-G before, but it's been awhile and it bears repeating, because it was a good and successful innovation. Every time you missed a d20 roll by 1, narrate some bit of color to explain why/how you missed, and take a +1 token. On any later roll where you miss, you may spend one or more +1 tokens to instead hit, but again you must add some color.

    Given that you will earn +1 tokens 5% of the time, this largely solved the narration fatigue thing\u2014it added more or less just the right amount of color-narration requirements to keep things feeling grounded in the fiction without requiring everyone to narrate every swing.\n

    \nI've run a fair amount of 3E, and played it less but some. Here is my take: some portion of those people liked that 3E was well-balanced but that it kept Vancian spell-casting, and missed it when it was gone (which is very silly in my view). Regarding combat, 3E non-caster characters have a narrower menu of options during fights, but tend to develop more of a signature style\u2014people liked that, because it meant you could be rewarded for good build choices, without having to make good tactical choices turn-by-turn in a fight. (And caster builds could, at least potentially, work similarly, like my friend who built a Charm-focused Sorcerer. He buffed the hell out of the save DCs against his spells, and spent fights turning enemies into friends. It was hilarious--his save DCs were so high that he could often overcome the -5 to the save DC for being in open combat.) Whereas in 4E, build optimization is important, but the system expects you to make judicious use of your Encounters and Dailies no matter what your build.

    So in short, a lot of 3E players love imagining this cool character who can run basically on autopilot during combat. It allows you to concentrate more on imagining your guy doing stuff, even though the combat system itself is equally if not more fiddly\u2014that's just processing, not decision-making.

    As an aside, what both games (3E and 4E) suffer from, IME, is any incentive at the character-building level (as opposed to the in-game level) to make choices to take "non-optimal" Feats and such. Even though the game could, in theory, support this idea, in practice everyone just went for combos. For example, my first-ever 3E character was a bow-wielding Halfling Cleric. I just thought it would be cool to have a bow! So I spent my Feat on Martial Weapon Proficiency: Bow, because I was so in awe of the idea of a system that would just... let me make the concept I wanted. And the game does allow such coolness (archer-Cleric, stealthy Paladin, or whatever). But nowadays, I would frankly never do that in a d20 game; I would take something much more optimal, like Improved Turning or whatever.

    I'd love to introduce some sort of rule like, "When you take a Feat that isn't in a Feat-chain with any of your other Feats, and/or branch out into a new Skill, gain a get-out-of-death-free card." And then run a really deadly, RAW campaign with lots of hard fights.

    Or, more easily, simply give people more Feat slots, but require that every other Feat be unconnected to their main class role. (You could do something similar with Skill points.) I'm planning on doing that if I ever run my "You're all Specialist Wizards!" campaign: I'll give Feats almost every level, but half of them will be meta-magic or item creation Feats, and the other half will have to belong to other categories.

    \n Abel, I think that people are really sensitive to the narratives that a game affirms.

    4e really affirms only one narrative -- the party uses tactical play to face challenges of appropriate difficulty. Because the rules are tightly focused on this narrative, the game feels brittle, like it must be engaged wholesale or not at all. So if you're not on board with 4e's narrative and gameplay, the game will frustrate you. It's a design that serves a narrow range of interest very well and alienates others.

    3e didn't really strongly affirm any particular narrative aside from the narratives encoded in its DNA. In attempting to provide rules for many situations, it superficially affirmed a lot of narratives. And because it was not a focused design, play groups were more empowered to disregard the parts of the rules that didn't serve their needs. It's a design that (IMO) serves a broad range of interests, but none in a very deep way.

    \n I think your point #3 is kind of like what I was saying about fighting-men and patriarchs in OD&D in the MIA Driver thread. Sometimes being jarred by the assumptions of the system can spur creativity. Explaining tripped oozes and no-girls-allowed warrior traditions can take you places you wouldn't expect!

    It is interesting that #2 is kind of the opposite of what OD&D does - since class is a mechanical touchpoint giving it a gendered name kind of encodes fiction into the mechanic but in a way that is orthogonal to the rest of the design (which probably means it's unintentional if I let myself care about that).

    Matt's thing about settling into the mechanics is like what I meant by the educational component. Often this is "I say I care about what kids are being taught by this game to cover my real agenda" but it's definitely true that I can be taught bad habits even as an old dog. Things I observed myself doing back when I was playing 4e and Basic/OD&D in about equal proportion:

    - Not bothering to choose equipment for my 4e characters because I assumed only what was written on a character card matters, but obsessing over this in OD&D.
    - Not running away from encounters in 4e because I assumed even when a fight seemed unbeatable that the solution was a special kind of fighting, (George Stayton cured me of this) whereas in OD&D I would run from encounters that maybe could have been beat.
    - Making self-destruction a common drive in my 4e roleplaying because I was frustrated by the padded sumo challenge level once I developed a taste for OD&D wasabi lethality.

    Also like Matt I worked out a trick in OD&D roleplaying to fight against some of the learned effects - rolling a d6 to determine character age and gender. I wasn't aware of this as a reaction to the male dominance implied in the mechanics, and I can't say I wouldn't have started doing this in the ungendered 4e environment if it killed my characters every session so making new ones that would be interesting in a short lifespan mattered.

    Finally I am fully down with point #1, the loosey goosey way I incorporate different rules texts in OD&D means risk is hard to quantify which frustrates my players as we all want to tell stories about braving lethality that is hard for all of us to gauge.

    My take on why people think 3E supports fiction more is that heist novels are one of my favorite genres. When I say "and then you go over there and fireball the troll" in the hours-long planning sessions that we liked in 3.5, I am doing just what I imagine my character to be doing. When I say this during a combat round it is instead getting in the way of me imagining myself being in a tense combat situation.

    Posted By: johnzoPosted By: Bill_WhiteMy own view on 4E is that nothing really important should happen that's not on a battlemat.
    \nBill, this is super interesting. Can you give me examples of how this might work?

    Say the fictional stakes are the time-honored "convince the Baron to bankroll your expedition." How would that encounter work?

    I agree that we need to stipulate for the sake of the thought-experiment that I've decided (or the table's decided--you know what I mean) that "convincing the Baron" is important, for some reason. We want to play it out, in other words, and with more than just a Diplomacy roll. This means that there's more than just exposition ("Here's why what you're doing matters") or establishing the mise en scene ("Here's where you are and what you have with you") happening in the scene. Fundamentally, we play it out because there's a chance to see our characters in action.

    That means the opportunity for physical action has to be present. I'm doing this on the fly, so I outline part of a castle on the battle mat and I say, "This is Hawkmount, where the Baron holds court. It's surrounded by a moat, and there are guards here, here, and here. The drawbridge is up, of course."

    "Can we get in?"

    "You can't just walk in, no. Here you are right here, outside the castle." I put their minis on the edge of the map, on the road that leads to the castle.

    So now I'm beginning to see the basics of the encounter: they have to (a) get in the castle somehow, and (b) convince the Baron to help them.

    For getting in the castle, I do what what Rob Donoghue says to do and give each element defenses and hit points.

    Actually, it's worth quoting Rob at length:\n
    When faced with a challenge, there are a few ways to approach it, but in the abstract, they break down into four different approaches:

    First, you can try to circumvent it. You can elude it, go around or otherwise avoid engaging it on its terms.
    Second, you can try to manipulate, control or wrestle with it. You meet it head on and try to bring it to heel.
    Third, you can try to understand it. Study it, and use that knowledge against it.
    Fourth, you can hit it. Hard. Possibly repeatedly.

    #4 is easy. D&D already handles that really well.

    #1-3 is the trick. You will generally use a skill to do one of these things, but there's no 100% correlation between which skill is used what way. There are some logical limitations, but they're situational. When you need to travel through enemy territory, Survival might be used to get around enemies (circumvent) or it might be used to try to find the best route (understand). Viewing actions through this lens of what they accomplish makes them more versatile and interesting, and definitely its more satisfying than the "skill first" approach where a player says "I use Survival!" and upon being asked "Do do what?" has to scramble for an answer. [3]

    Experienced 4e hackers probably have already seen where I'm going, but I'll lay it out here. The 4 approaches correspond to the four defenses used by 4e. Thus:

    1 - Circumvent - Reflex
    2 - Manipulate - Fortitude
    3 - Understand - Will
    4 - Smash - Armor
    \nMy PCs are 1st level, so I give a group of guards AC 15, Fort 13, Will 11, Refl 11, and 12 hp. This means that it's pretty hard to fight them (attack vs AC), and almost as difficult to strong-arm if I try to forcefully manipulate them (e.g. bull rush or strong arm them--attack vs. Fort), but not too hard to control (i.e., trick) (attack vs. Will) or avoid them (attack vs. Refl). I give the guards an attack, too: maybe +3 vs AC for 1d8+1 damage, and they can Sound the Alarm to summon more guards if the PCs directly confront them (i.e., attack vs. AC or Fort). I stat out the moat and the castle walls, too, 20 hp for the moat, 30 for the walls let's say. I think it'll be easy enough to swim the moat, so Fort 12, but hard to jump across it or tunnel under it (Refl 20) and pointless to "attack" or try to trick (although I might have to worry about a magician using Ritual Caster to try something funny like turning water to ice, which would be cool, so I set it at Will 15). When you're in the moat, the thick mud and weeds threaten pull at you, attacking Refl +3 for 1d3 damage plus immobilize (save ends).

    You swim the moat by "attacking" it with your Athletics skill; if you beat its Fort 12, you do damage by level per the table on damage expressions in the DMG; I don't have it readily accessible, but lets say its 1d6+3 for "normal attacks" (i.e., you have the skill but it's not trained) and 1d10+3 for trained swimmers. So it'll take 2 or 3 rounds to cross the moat, unless you get stuck in the weeds (its attack is +2 vs. Refl, 1d3 damage plus immobilized, save ends). The castle walls are handled similarly.

    Guards have a chance to spot you in the moat or on the walls if you don't roll your Reflex to beat their passive perception of 11 (same as their Will, really).

    Smart PCs will probably try to talk their way in, in which case you're rolling Diplomacy and Bluff versus the guard's Will defense; do 12 hp of damage and they roll over. But now I know how to deal with them getting in. Maybe some PCs talk and others sneak; it's all one.

    "Here's the Great Hall where the Baron greets petitioners; here, we'll use this mini for the Baron,"

    Next I have to worry about them convincing the Baron, but here too I just give him some number of hit points (let's say 45 hp, because I don't want him to just roll over). I give him whatever defenses I want, interpreting them as his resistance to being browbeaten (Fort), misdirected (Refl), or psyched out (Will). His AC is high because his bodyguards intercede when he's attacked. His stern disapproval of frivolity and wastefulness lets him do "Psychic" (i.e., morale) damage to anyone trying to convince him to part with his hard-earned wealth. He can summon guards, too, and I bet he has a court jester who can mess with the PCs minds (attack vs. Will) and bemuse them (1-3 dmg, plus dazed, save ends).

    Of course, there's no urgency to any of that, no reason why the PCs should want to race into the keep to beard the Baron in his den. To create that, maybe there's a host of other petitioners who want the Baron to spend money on their needs: improving a mill here, helping out an impoverished relative there, and so forth. I put half a dozen of these on the map and say, "These guys are looking for the Baron; if they make too many demands on him, he won't have the money to fund the expedition." They have a Beg for Money attack they can use on the Baron; every time they hit him, they do 1d8+2 damage, but their damage isn't additive to the PCs, and they go away after they've made one successful attack. If they get him down to zero before the PCs do, the PCs fail and whatever benefit was to be obtained from the Baron's patronage is lost.

    Now, the only reason to do something like this is if the players are going to enjoy it: maybe they've been begging for a chance to show off their social skills, or something. But all your 4E combat moves have a place in the adventure; you can use your Sly Flourish on the Baron to show off your martial finesse and convince him that you've got the skills you need to make your plan succeed, or something. It's all measured in hit points anyway. And if your players surprise you, you can create new elements of the situation--the Baroness, the court magician, the Baron's daughter's mysterious illness, and so forth--fairly readily on the fly in order to keep players engaged.

    And so that's what I'd do: put things on the map so that anytime our attention is fully engaged with the fiction, we've got opportunities for everyone to participate in the scenario, explore the setting, and showcase their characters.

    Posted By: salvagebarWhat I can't understand specifically is people who criticize 4E on the grounds that the rules of 3E tie the game more firmly to the fiction, especially the combat.1I do see how 3E's matrix of feats, skills, and abilities are more complex and satisfying to navigate, in that math-crunchy way. I don't see how they are better at driving the narrative, so if someone who has that point of view can point out a few places to start, I would appreciate it.
    \nAbel, I have made this claim on several occasions. Despite this, I think 4E is a much better design, and for me, more fun to play. I have a very similar stance to Eero - I appreciate the skirmish war-game design of 4E, and if I want to engage with a game on a different level, I'd play a different game that's not a version of D&D at all.

    The reason I feel this way is quite straightforward - like most RPGs, D&D 3.x associated a game element (e.g. Fighter, Fireball, Bull Rush, Otyugh) with some numbers representing their usefulness in some algorithm-driven simulation of a fantasy world, but the name of the game element stood on its own in the fiction. A Fireball was a big ball of fire, conjured by trained wizards through reading arcane incantations from a spellbook and doing something weird with bat guano. Often, these game elements had lengthy fictional descriptions associated with them, explaining how they worked in the world fictionally. The few times 3.x was more abstract than that, as with Hit Points and Alignments, it spawned decade-long flame wars and tens of thousands of house rules.

    Furthermore, the nature of the numbers and algorithms driving the game always appeared to me to be more driven by the need for simulation than the need for game balance. A wizard started out green and useless, but 10 levels later could transform a dragon into a bunny rabbit and step on it. A warrior started out armed and dangerous, but 10 levels later they were still hacking and slashing, though maybe with a magical sword. Big scary monsters could be killed by a team of greenhorns with a smart plan and a complicit DM. In one particularly dull session, my 5th level half-orc monk killed an entirely settlement of orcs by essentially standing in the centre of town with his fist out and letting them run into it, because his AC was so high they just had no way of injuring him.

    DnD 4E isn't relatively speaking an abstract game, but as johnzo so aptly demonstrates in the OP, it is a lot more open to interpretation than earlier editions... and as Eero points out, it-would-be-nice-if it would mean something when I use or learn one power over another. Sure, those aspects can all be layered onto the game by individual groups, and in a long-term campaign with the same people, I have no doubts that eventually you will reach a shared understanding of how the world you play in actually works. But in an early game, when the lazer Cleric smites a foe with the power of his glorious lord in the first combat with goblins outside the city gate, how do NPCs react to that? Is that special, or commonplace? How do you keep the magic of that moment from becoming routine and dull when the Hand of God has smoten your foes for the 50th time?

    At its core, I think the (excellent!) approach to focusing on balance over simulation, and letting the players decide for themselves how to deal with the situation where Come And Get It is used against oozes (and what their choice means for the nature of how the world works), has led to most players simply ceasing to try to make sense of it fictionally. The books no longer feed you descriptors, the mechanics are more abstract, there's more focus on artificially imposed 5' squares, and combined this leads to players' attention being focused on the things that don't make sense (and some that never made much sense, like Vancian magic and their 8-hour sleep cycles). Instead of layering on a really interesting creative meta-game as johnzo evidently has in order to make sense of it fictionally, they either artificially restrict PCs' action options to whatever is listed on their sheets (my friend built a mega-dungeon and said "sure, there's a world outside these gates, but you'll agree to never go there while we're playing this campaign") or simply play the game as a series of skirmishes with mostly handwaved freeform what-do-you-do-now scenes (or even cut-scenes) in between.

    ...also, no offense meant to Bill W. or Rob D. - I think the idea of all-encounter-all-the-time is pretty cool too - but am I really the only one to think that the approach of assigning hit points to everything just sounds like a slower, more tedious way of resolving Skill Challenges? If the moat has 40 HPs and the PCs do on average 10 damage when they "hit" with an Athletics skill, how is that different from saying "you need 4 successes on Athletics at DC20 to cross the moat, every time you fail you take 1d8+3 damage"? Most uses of powers and positioning wouldn't really apply, or seem necessary - and without those, DnD 4E combat is just a boring JRPG where you trade hits until one of you dies.
