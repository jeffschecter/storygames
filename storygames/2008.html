So, here's a separate thread.

FIrst off, let's knock of the "nice" jokes, ok? That's fucking stupid, and really annoying to me particularly. Andy asked me if he could use my comments about nice-ness to start a new thread. That thread became a loaded gun, one that fortunately didn't go off. This is my urging to you to knock off those flippant "nice" snarks. I'm already tired of that shit. It's been, what, four days?

Now, on to my next grumpy old man post:

The idea that we would want games BECAUSE THEY ARE COOL is profoundly disturbing. I am not interested in a hobby where owning a particular game, or a particular edition, or a particular rarity, equates in some kind of thrill in and of itself, or -- much, much worse -- a badge of cool among a subculture.

The idea that role-playing is who someone is rather than what someone does is really backwards. I keep seeing unconscious references of this kind.

(I'm waiting for replies to call me out. I have identified a post or two at least, but I'd just as soon not insult individuals, which me pointing out would effectively accomplish. Can we be honest about this and agree that this happens to some extent? Here's to hoping.)

I think it drives people from our hobby who don't want anything to do with that lifestyle. I think it's hurting us more than helping us.

It's a fine line, isn't it? Remember when Nathan said he was tired of Awesome? Well, how much of Awesome was raw thrill of seeing something new and getting excited about it because it was something new you could do this weekend? And, how much of Awesome is the bizarre thrill of knowing that just having it in your hands makes you better somehow? I don't think it's honest to say that NONE of the enthusiasm is this second thrill.

I mean, Jungle Speed? I can take it or leave it. I played it once. It's fun to play. I'm pretty bad at it. But, seriously, am I a less cool person for suggesting it's not worth all the damn noise? I mean, I get it. It's funny. I watched Keith and Mayuran wrestle like tomcats. Funny. But, really, who cares?

Here's what I am saying: If you support story games as a concept (and if you don't, this sure is a poorly named site!), then can you agree that story games as a concept are quite poorly reprsented in the traditaional publishing industry? In other words, the games aren't many and reasonably hard to find. And, if that's true, then it makes a whole bunch of sense to say that this is at least a small activity, if not a niche.

Why, then, behave in such a way that newcomers won't comprehend the hidden language of cool, not understand the subculture, and run screaming for the hills when they don't get all this.

I think this is nuts.

I'd much rather see us talking like regular joes about an activity we do. Sort of like my wife who talks like a regular person about scrapbooking, or my brother about golf, and so on. They are passionate and excited about their hobby, of course. But, they don't revel in a culture of cool based on the hobby itself.

    Matt, the roleplaying publishing industry seems, to me, to be a pair of conjoined twins:

    1) Selling books that are pretty, fun to read, and neat artifacts to own.
    2) Disseminating games that are fun to play.

    If someone was only interested in doing the latter, they could release their games as free text files. But hardly anyone does this, because a lot of people seem to believe that things that you pay money for, or things that are printed, or things that are really pretty are more valuable and probably more fun to own and play. That's marketing for you. Sometimes you try to get people to buy your game on sight alone, when they know nothing at all about the content or how much fun it is to play, based solely on its qualities as an artifact. But people will certainly feel cheated if the game is prettier than it is good. It'll just sit on their shelf for a while and then get sold on eBay.

    I'm all for turning roleplaying into "just another thing that we do" like the way I play the banjo and do graphic design and enjoy reading comics. But those things aren't free of these tensions either, right? I feel the commercial desire to buy a tricked-out banjo, get the newest version of InDesign, and, sometimes, pick up nice hardcover editions of comics I've already read, just so I have them around "in case I want to read them again." So, yeah, I do feel like the commodification of game products has gone overboard, but it's probably impossible for us to escape that entirely.

    Still, more efforts to "normalize" the hobby would be very welcome. I'm all for that. Rah-rah etc.

      Hey Jonathan,

      ...the roleplaying publishing industry seems, to me, to be a pair of conjoined twins:

      1) Selling books that are pretty, fun to read, and neat artifacts to own.
      2) Disseminating games that are fun to play.

      If someone was only interested in doing the latter, they could release their games as free text files.

      I think the selling=commerce=industry meme warrants some examination, in that it's a particularly stingy meme that doesn't like sharing "dollars change hands" with the hobby meme. The actual difference between industry and hobby isn't the selling activity. A hobby is a community of appreciation for an activity. And in a pure hobby, there is no stratification of the participants. There are no lessers (people or entities) who've ceded power to other people (or entities). All participants are equal within the context of the hobby. Industry, on the other hand, is about stratification. Industry is about consumers and producers, and about extracting profit from the exchanges across the strata. But just because industry is about commerce doesn't mean a hobby can't have buying and selling of goods or services. Let's say you and I are into remote controlled cars. And you're an awesome pinstriper. In no way are we on some slippery slope to industry if I kick you a few Hamiltons to pimp my car with your awesome pinstriping skillz. Not if we aren't stratifying into consumers and producers.

      And that's my problem with premium editions of games (and comics). The indie design community is an assertion that we are all equals (and that this is a hobby). We come into conflict with the counter assertion by traditional publishers that this is an industry, and premium editions of games and comics are part of this assertion. Personally, I'd love to own the hardcover premium edition of The Watchmen. It has a section in the back of the book with character concept sketches and a chronicle of the development. I'm not immune to having my wants tweaked by products with premium features. But within a hobby premium features and manufactured collectibility have to be regarded as an irritating status game. Why wouldn't you just publish the game true to your vision, exactly as you'd want to buy it? To reserve important content for a premium edition is artistic dishonesty. To add production features for a limited premium edition is a maximizing revenue technique that works by tweaking the fetish instincts of others. It treats others like programmable objects. It's something you do in an industry. It tells me the publisher thinks this is an industry and not a hobby.
