\n Hi all
\n
\nI've got to put out there my love of FU, or the Free, Universal Roleplaying Game. It's an incredibly rules-light system with elegant mechanics.
\n
\nIn a nutshell, it consists of Descriptors and Conditions. To resolve a task/scene you add a Bonus die for all positive Descriptors (including scene based), and take away those dice (or add Penalty dice) for negative Descriptors and Conditions.
\n
\nWhen you roll the dice you take the best, for total Bonus dice, or the worst, for total Penalty die. The dice themselves give Yes and..., Yes, Yes but..., No but..., No, No and... results.
\n
\nThe other element is Conditions, these are simple adjectives like Angry, Dazed, Injured etc. and can be anything, like Fate's Consequences. These are factored into the Penalty and Bonus dice and altogether make a great little system.
\n
\nYou can get it from RPGNow or from the Peril Planet website, and as it's free I'd suggest you have a look.
\n
\nI love it so much I created a Yahoo Group and a Pirates hack for NaGa DeMon available on the group or at RPGGeek.
\n
\nIt really is simplicity personified.

    \n I've found it plays great. The text advises against rolling more than three dice at once, so you're not expected to tally every possible modifier in the scene, just the most relevant. Also, note that even if you roll double fistfuls of dice, there's always a (admittedly very small) chance of still rolling low across the board, so there's always at least an element of suspense when the dice are thrown.

    The thing I don't like about FU: the name. Its at best a goofy joke that doesn't get any better with repetition.

    Things I like about FU: pretty much everything else. The system is staggeringly elegant, "light as a feather but firm as oak." Just enough support in just the right moments to give play structure, while still allowing for all the inventiveness of free-forming. The text itself is very shrewdly written, succinctly stating principles of good play. There is no mechanical indoctrination needed, no period of fumbling through clever rules of obscure intention. Hand anyone a character summary and its clear immediately whot it means and how it works (in other words, its now the default system I use for gaming with casual-to-non gamers).

    \n Interesting. It seems a bit of a mash-up of Risus, PDQ and FATE*, with a weird dice mechanic.

    To echo Wightbred: it sounds as though the effects of modifiers are very strong. With no modifiers it's even odds whether I get a Yes or a No. With one modifier it jumps to 3:1 odds of a Yes. With two modifiers it's 7:1. Moreover, the distribution isn't anything like a bell curve \u2026 it's a hockey-stick, where the extreme results become *more* likely. With two + modifiers, the chance of each value from worst to best is (1%, 3%, 9%, 17%, 28%, 42%). That's pretty weird.

    It's not like probabilities are hugely important to a story-game, as long as modifiers generally change the odds in the right direction. So I'm sure FU is fun to play. But the dice rolling seems like the most novel thing in the game, and it actively does a *worse* job than the other systems I mentioned above. So I don't know why you'd pick this over the others.

    (* OK, granted that FATE in its full form is a much larger and more complex beast than any of the others. But the core of it is pretty simple, and there are a handful of simplified variants around like Free FATE and Wheel Of Fate that are pretty comparable to FU or Risus or PDQ.)
