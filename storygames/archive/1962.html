  • Josh Roby
    December 2006 edited December 2006
    Posted By: Mark CauseyWhat's locked content, in a tabletop RPG experience?
    In Sons of Liberty, there will be about sixteen Figures (George Washington, Ben Franklin, Fanny Campbell) that you can play as a PC. Each one has a list of "favored circumstances" which are used as stats. These circumstances are in three broad periods, based on time: Stamp Acts, Shot Heard Round the World, and The Revolution. Some Figures do not have any circumstances in the early periods, because they weren't active yet (Deborah Sampson doesn't enter service until 1778, for instance).

    If you play in "Campaign Mode," then the first game you play is set in the era of the Stamp Acts and Townshend Acts. About a third of the Figures are unavailable, and what Figures are available do not yet have their full complement of circumstances. Each individual game has three Objectives and one Secret Objective which is revealed in the course of play. If you fulfill the Secret Objective, you can "unlock" the next period, which means that you'll have more circumstances available to you (you remake characters each game) and more Figures to make characters from (you can switch characters game to game). Proceeding to a new period may or may not give the Tory Player more resources / increase the difficulty (there is no character advancement per se, outside of unlocking new circumstances).

    Outside of Campaign Mode, you just ignore the periods and play any Figure with any of their circumstances.

    I have absolutely no idea if it works, but that's the plan.

    Interesting thoughts on Locked Content.

    Back to the part that Mark was talking about, where you explore the rules in gameplay.

    Most of the time this is a conscious decision that some GMs have made as they introduce a game to new players. Especially at a convention setting (this is how I do Tenra: I put the character sheets in front of people, but only get to those parts in play: Zero Act Scene One I try to have JUST be about the Harmony Coins. Scene Two I may have a skill roll or two, but try to stay away from combat. Scene Three or Four may have a brief combat in it. The first act is where I begin to talk about Kiai, etc.

    I've not really seen it worked into a game, the closest is maybe Dogs in the Vineyard.

    I'm totally on the same page as you, though: I remember well Final Fantasy X, which has you start out (after a cool cut scene) with a sword in your hand, fighting some really weak dudes. Auron instructs you how to hit this guy or that guy. You take out one monster, then go to the next fight. Auron tells you how to defend, or use Overdrives. There's about four fights in a row, after which you've got down about 80% of the game system (the next tutorial comes when it's time to level up).

    To do that in a game, I think you'd have to write a mandatory (but fun!) introductory scenario (when I say Mandatory, I pretty much mean, "Compelling, and integrated into the rules section somehow") that specifically tells you what scenes the characters should play out, the setup for those scenes, what they should be doing, and what the GM should be teaching them. Heck, doing a game in this format can teach the GM the rules from the start, too, not just the players!

    So, again: We haven't seen it explored too heavily yet, but it would likely take the form of a very brief scenario or scenario introduction that is very well defined as to what rolls are made, what to do for each roll, etc.

    -Andy
