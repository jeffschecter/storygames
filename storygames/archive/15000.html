\n Hey everybody! So, as I mentioned in my intro, I have been writing an RPG for awhile now. Its honestly going over 15 years. Now, that IS a bit crazy, but its been an ongoing, evolving project. It started out using other peoples rules and adding bits and pieces from a myriad of different games. I am on the third (possibly fourth, its hard to keep track) version of the system, each looking less like the one preceding it. It is a skill based game. No classes or levels. It is built around a d10 core that is modified by attributes and uses opposed rolls. Its still very much in the testing phase. I am working on making it presentable for people outside of my group. I call the system "Multiversal". SERIOUSLY hoping that name is not yet taken! Anyway, I have rough files on Google Docs, but, as I said, they are UGLY and very much WIP.
\n
\nAs for the setting...well, that is where I have done a LOT of solid work in the last few years. What started as a generic fantasy setting has slowly evolved into a post-apocalyptic fantasy world with a bit of Revelations for the overarching plot. The apocalypse happened due to a cabal of dark mages working to cause a nuclear war. Things spun out of control and the entire world was thrown into chaos and the four seals began to break lose and with them, magic returned to Earth. After the third seal opened, the three riders stood facing the gate, awaiting the opening of the fourth seal. The lamb said unto them "Look." and they turned and beheld their fourth brother, Death, in a mortal guise upon his pale horse. Around him was not Hell, but the kingdom of man, wreathed in steel and steam, lightning, thunder and fury. The three were beaten down and spread to the winds, never to be seen in their immortal face again. Now, each of them has taken mortal form and formed their own kingdoms, the three trying to destroy man to bring Hell to earth, but Death resists them, siding with humanity, spreading his word of resistance so that man may beat back the forces of hell.
\n
\nThat is the BIG plot. Their are lots of little things I have written as well, such as the Reborn which are undead who are reanimated by unknown forces but still have their souls. Something about "when their is no more room in hell..." and all that. ;)
\n
\nThanks for reading. Hope you enjoyed this teaser.

    Posted By: KaneBlaireauI wanted a slightly crunchy system without classes or levels and nothing else was doing it for me.
    \nThat doesn't really tell us anything. I've played quite a few games that could be sincerely described as "slightly crunchy, without classes or levels" and they're all really different from one another.

    What people are trying to get you to articulate is what the point is of playing your game. What are the interesting choices that people playing your game get to make, that (and this is the important part) they don't get to make when playing other games on the market? Your list of tabletop games doesn't include anything from the "indie" or "story-game" spectrum, but game design in the last decade or so has really moved away from setting details as the thing to distinguish games from one another.

    Setting design in support of a focused game with a clear point to it is great, mind you\u2014take a look at Fred Hicks' game Don't Rest Your Head, f'rex. But DRYH works so well because the mechanics also help the group hone that feeling of intense personal psychological horror, because of the way the dice pools work.

    All the games you listed, even though they are in some minor ways diverse, contain numerous core cultural assumptions: there's a GM, the PCs are extraordinary individuals in some way, there's a plot that is essentially external to the characters' personal concerns, and the rules are basically there to model some combination of the physics of the world and the tropes of the game's genre. (There are probably other embedded assumptions but those are the main ones that jump out at me.) Many of the most active posters on this forum have played and even written games that toss out one or more of these core facets of RPG design, and invent others to take their place, all to achieve specific effects.

    I'm not saying that you should write some hippified story-game without a GM or dice. What I am saying is that the choices that a designer makes about all these various aspects of a game have a huge impact on the experiences of people who play it, and all of them need to be carefully considered. Right now, you're not in a position to make informed choices about where to set these different "dials," because you've only ever played games that had each of them in a certain, traditional location.

    Posted By: KaneBlaireaudo I keep it a generic system or start pushing it towards on specific setting?
    \nCommon wisdom around here has it that focus in game design is always a good thing. That doesn't mean that all good games are necessarily so closely tied to a specific setting as DRYH, I was merely citing that as *a* good example. There are games that support an infinitude of settings but provide structure in other ways; there's no better example of that phenomenon than Matt Wilson's PrimeTime Adventures. That game structures play around the idea that the game is a dramatic TV show; obviously TV shows can be part of many different genres, so what's similar from game to game are the structural elements like the way scenes rotate, the way characters are called on to confront their Issues, and so on.

    Anyway, the reason that tight focus in game design, whether it be tied to a single specific setting or a usefully-constrained dramatic structure, is popular around here is because there are so many RPGs that already do so many things very well. I can think of quite a few games that have the characteristics you say you want your game to have\u2014and not just indie games. I mean, why not play GURPS?     \nUgh. Now I am going to be dismissive and even more blunt than before: if you have a story to tell, write a novel or a play or a screenplay or even a video game, but stay the hell away from designing RPGs or GMing. When people writing and/or running games talk about "the story I want to tell," I run the other way. If RPGs are about story, then they are about creating that story collaboratively, in real-time, at the table. "Collaboration" doesn't mean that players (including the GM if there is one) can't play adversarially\u2014the way in which adversity is provided is one of the major considerations a game designer has to account for.

    Posted By: KaneBlaireauWhat is different or better about non-standard RPGs? Can you give me examples and opinions?
    \nI could, and it would probably be 10 pages long. But the question itself is fallacious: the spectrum of games I've played and run and facilitated is so wide that there is almost nothing I could say that would be universally or even close to universally true. Non-standard RPGs have been developed to address an incredibly diverse spectrum of interests, and more are coming out all the time. The one thing that the games I like and recommend have in common is something I already mentioned: focus. That is, focus on making the game itself carefully tailored to doing its particular thing well; the flipside to that is it means very few of the story-game darlings are broadly applicable for all groups and all possible desires and social arrangements. (Just to give a simple example, many only work as single-session games, for example, and so wouldn't be useful to a group looking to play a campaign. Others are unsatisfying when played for only a single session.)

    So, no. I'm not going to answer your question. What I'm going to say is this: go download Lady Blackbird. Read it (it's only a few pages long), and play it with your group for at least one session. Then come back here and post again.

    \nThat's cool that you want to publish your game and sell it! It's a big difference from making it freely available, and even beyond just the rules you'll have a lot to think about in time. Asking people for money means you have to deliver something beyond "download at your own risk." As soon as you attach that price tag, you're responsible for something. How do you make sure your game is worth $5 or $15 or however much?

    Everyone above is saying "play all these games." It's a good idea, mostly because what you've listed includes games that share a lot of concepts. If that's exactly what you want, you'll have a hard time convincing anyone to try your game when they could just play the games that they already know. You've probably seen those discussions elsewhere on the internet, people saying they don't want to learn another new rules system. By and large they have a point, if the new system does pretty much the same thing as the old one.

    There's every possibility that your game actually does fit the more traditional RPG model and maybe just needs some additional rules to handle X, whatever X is. That's something to really think about, making your game as a supplement/setting that's compatible with an existing game. You have immediate access to that game's fanbase. But that's up to you of course.

    And just to clarify, a lot of people here have done what you're doing and want to help you avoid the stupid shit we all did. Publishing a game is pretty exciting, but it can also end up being a terrible disappointment after all the work you put into it. I'm excited about your setting. I think it has merit.
