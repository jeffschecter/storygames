\n "how much we've heard about the 'character'! Moreover, I fear we haven't heard the last. Fifty years of disease, the death notice signed many times by the most serious essayists, yet nothing has yet managed to knock it off the pedestal on which the nineteenth century has placed it. It is a mummy now, but one still enthroned with the same - phony - majesty..."
\n
\nalain robbe-grillet, one of the lead proponents of the mainly (and perhaps rightfully) forgotten 'new novel,' wrote this 50 years ago in his essay 'On Several Obsolete Notions'.
\n
\nHis argument was that:
\n* great 19th century writers created memorable characters such as the Kazamazovs
\n* so writing fiction came to be conflated with adding new figures to literary pantheon of characters
\n* characters need 'character' (their nature, history that shaped them, etc) so readers can judge them, love them, hate them
\n* this persists (in 1957) despite the fact that many of the best recent novels deemphasize, obscure or provide competing interpretations of their characters
\n* but the preeminence of characters arose when society was marked by the 'apogee of the individual'
\n* the present period is one of 'administrative numbers', so personality and character are less essential
\n* the novel staggers without its best 'prop,' the hero
\n* this decline holds the promise of new discoveries
\n
\nthe rpg version might be:
\n* rpgs have always focused on or at least through characters
\n* players cherish memories of their first and/or favorite characters, like my beloved ranger Thor Wolfshire III
\n* characters with something to care about & that players care about generate better stories
\n* characters are essential to almost all roleplaying/story games despite the diversification of the form
\n
\n>> So, with the caveat that character-centered games are plenty good fun, what would games that dragged those withered mummies off their golden thrones be like?
\n* you could deemphasize characters' 'character,' make them ciphers or pawns, say like Lacuna or a hack/slash game
\n* you could have non fiction games, documentary games, games about beauty pageants or herring or the overthrow of the Hawaiian monarchy or the hydrogen bomb, which would use characters from time to time but not be limited to them
\n* you could carry the story line through an object- like a fantasy game where play follows a coin as it stolen, spent, looted etc by people/monsters
\n* you could follow a place as it changes- a street corner in year X, X+5, X+20 etc, what's going on there
\n* you could aggregate characters - DITV as a report by the temple on trends from the last 500 missions rather than 3-5 dogs hitting a few towns
\n
\nAny ideas on character-deemphasizing games that would actually be fun?

    \n
    \nI like this. I'd use something like PDQ or The Pool for this:
    \n
    \nA modern day game where a Gun is the central "character". The GM begins play by describing the location/situation of the gun. Is it at the pawn shop, or on a cop's hip? It might be laying on the ground, next to a dead body, in a dank back alley. The situation should be in media res, at the edge of conflict. The GM turns to the first player and says simply go. The player then carries the narrative, creating a character on the spot. Play moves around the table, player to player, in the same way. Players shouldn't become too attached to their Characters, as they won't remain in the narrative for long. This game should follow the Gun, with the players creating new characters as the Gun moves.
    \n
    \nSimple Example: Player 1(decides to play Tom, the unhappy husband) picks up the Gun, carries it home. Player 2 (Becky, the housewife) catches him about to commit suicide. There's a scuffle, Tom accidentally shoots Becky. But Becky was the cause of Tom's misery so Tom decides he wants to live. He gets rid of the body and tries to move on. Player 3 decides to play Sammy, a thief. He breaks into Tom's house and steals the gun. Play would now follow Sammy. Players 1 and 2 would create new characters more appropriate to Sammy's world.
    \n
    \nI'm going to have to give this a try. Wasn't there a show on cable similar to this? IIRC, it was a western.

    You know, even though I think this is worth the experiment, I caution that, central to the story are things that the players care about. And people only care about people. If they care about something that's not a person, it's because they think of it as a person.

    \n

    So part of this thing is, I think, figuring out who the "faces" are, from moment to moment. Following a gun around (as in the eponymous Altman movie) or whatever is only interesting because of people we care about. And fictional people are called "characters".

    \n

    You can't make a story about the weather without relating to the participants/readers/audience.

    \n

    (If you figure out how to do this, by the way, I'm all over that shit. That's a challenge, not an actual assertion of impossibility.)

    \nWell, this dawned on me in a dungeon crawl thread on RPG.net. But Most of the classic modules and D&D games were really about the dungeon, not the characters. It was about discovering what this place was and what used to (or still does) go on in it, and exploring it. Your wizard could get eaten by a geltanious cube, and it didn't matter too much because that character's story was unimportant. You could make a new character as a replacement and still explore the dungeon.

    \n
    \nWhat if each character has a Relationship list similar to other games? Only in this game, instead of these people coming into play as situational modifiers, they actually become the player's characters. It takes a bit away from the "Object as Character" idea, yet it should add a sense of "this is what I care about."
    \n
    \nLet's say Player 2, from my earlier example, began the game with the character Becky, who died during the scuffle with her husband Tom. Player 2 then chooses George, Becky's High School sweetheart, from Becky's list of Relationships. Sometime later, after Sammy the Thief steals the gun (which Tom should have gotten rid of to begin with), Becky's body is found. Sammy recognizes the house on the news coverage, and on a hunch, attempts to blackmail Tom...
    \n
    \nActually, now that I look at it, this strays too far away from the Gun, becoming more Becky's story. Well, at the least, you can play with building a story through the Relationships.
