\n Certain games bandied about on this site, and several of those presented in the early days of the New Paradigm/Forge Break-Out period were what I'd call algorithmic: they were based on a definite set of procedures that yielded a pre-determined mathematical outcome.

My Life with Master is a shining example of this style of play and I find it's one that I'm drawn to strongly. I think the argument could be made that Fiasco also has a clear algorithm/play rubric. Penny for My Thoughts, Dirty Secrets and to some extent Umlaut and Shooting the Moon have well encoded moves that lead to semi-predictable endgames. I'd like to see more games like that and here what others think of this kind of thing.

    \n Ahh, in other words the whole "Games With Endgame Mechanics" thing. It was a very unique and novel idea at the time! And indeed, the games themselves can be really fun. But I've noticed that too-too procedural games for me sometimes fall into the traps that Jeph mentions.

    There was even an oblique thing I noticed, Games With (for me) Too Much Procedure (again, for me): Agon and Nine Worlds fell into this, despite being both fun-as-shit games.

    in 9W you have this effect where you want to interact with the world, but to do so requires constant manipulation of cards. Everything that can be done in the world is ruled by the hand of cards you have and your intention. So you're constantly sizing up everything against what you have in your hand and what you can do. It can be a little overwhelming at times.

    In Agon, to do Anything, you need to have a scene about that Thing. So if you've gotten the crap beaten out of your characters, and you're looking to face off against a "boss battle", say for the reason that "We wanted to play this game for X Sessions/Y Months and we're at the end of that cycle", you still need to go through the procedure of, say, each character requiring their own 3-5 competitive scenes in order to restore abilities they're based on. In play, we had to say "fuck it; just heal up 3 abilities and we'll be good", but it really felt like we shorted the game, broke out of it in some way.

    Maybe there's something to be said about games that are /too/ internally consistent?

    Sorry, a little sidetrack from the Endgame mechanic thing, but still relevant I think.

    \n I really like having an ending or endgame mechanic hardwired into a game, for a number of reasons. I think it's an easier sell for casual gaming because it can help the pacing of the game match the allotted real world time. It encourages playing games to completion because there is an end in sight. Sometimes, too much freedom in a game can be paralyzing but the hard structure gives you something to play off of. I generally like the decision between exploring more of what's going on in the current fiction or taking one more step toward the end (for example, if things slow down in Fiasco, the option of moving to the next player's turn gives you an easy out but you could keep going with the scene if it was interesting). I love that endgames keep everyone on the same page about establishing fiction in the early game and then everyone can see the tipping point and start wrapping up the plot threads in time for the ending. A lot of games with an endgame built in also seem to have good replay value and/or allow you to wrap up the game and then play a sequel. I'm not sure that having an endgame and having story structure are necessarily the same (e.g. I see Lady Blackbird as having an implied ending without imposing structure), but they often seem to go hand in hand. I'd like to see more games with structural mechanics.

    \n The rules feel different to me in RPGs or in boardgames. (I know I'm forcing an arbitrary separation here, but take that for what it's worth for the moment.)

    It feel like (to me) in a boardgame, the fun comes from not knowing who will win, or the uncertainty of it. And I HATE that in a roleplaying game. I don't want a "win" condition, and I don't want to take a long walk down a narrative path, get in to character and build myself into something to have it obliterated out from under me by some side-rule or trick I didn't have the time to pay attention to.

    I hate that more than anything.

    In an RPG, I love the ones (in particular) where we have a good deal of "how this should go" already decided. That's not to say 100% pre-played. I don't mean to simplify it to "well, we know the outcome of the scene, so just improv and build to that point."

    I do love a game like MLwM or Kagematsu or The Mountain Witch, or Misspent Youth, where we can see the points adding up, where we can see that we are all creating a thing, where we have feedback on the health of that thing (Medicine in Ganakagok, Love/Fear/Pity/Self-Loathing elsewhere, Trust/Wounds). These are board-gamey elements and they make me happy in an RPG.

    I hate games that are so bloated and obese with side-rules or "fiat" that they can turn upside-down unexpectedly. In my opinion, it sh*ts all over my investment into the game, erases any impact I thought I was making.

    Back to the original post, I love those games too. I would like to see more of them. Carry, Grey Ranks, Fiasco, Annalise, Zombie Cinema, Panty Explosion, Hot Guys Making Out, and the countless others I can't name at the moment.

    (/rant, and thanks to anyone who actually read that soap-box nonsense)

    \nI didn't get a chance to comment earlier but a "story-making machine" is the way I do think of some of these games. I'm not a good GM and although I would like to improve, the reasons I run games is to give another GM a break and to help out for various other reasons, or simply to expose new players to a game. I would prefer if other GMs ran most games, but when I do run a game, it's nice to have some assurance that it's going to flow well, and that it's going to wrap up well (as that's mainly what the players remember). I've played Fiasco many times and Penny for My Thoughts and Dirty Secrets once each, and I like the aspect that once you are out of dice or whatever, an automatic end game is triggered. The first time we brought 2 new players in to a Fiasco game, the 5th player didn't even know what kind of character she was going to play until it was her scene, and then it was obvious who she should be, as there had been foreshadowing earlier in the game. It feels like we fold all the ingredients for a recipe together during the first and second parts of Fiasco and then we see what we pulled out of the oven with the Aftermath, which we know will be a sum of most of the ingredients and will come at a predicted time. Since so much of the rest of the game is flexible, wild and crazy, it's kind of nice to have that structure.
