\n So Janus, the Italian RPG publisher and all-around nest of genius vipers, has a Renewable Games Initiative. The deal is - if they release a revised edition, you can return your well-loved copy of, say, Don't Rest Your Head to them. They will "cancel" it and trade it for a new version at a discount. The canceled game is then given away to a library or game club, of which Italy has many.

This is a tremendously cool idea and as a Janus-affiliated designer I really, really like it.

I only read Google Italian, so I have some questions, vipers - what's the discount/rebate? How will you determine which library/club gets a used book? Does the cancellation have to happen in person? Tell me more!

    \n Thanks for the shout-out Jason!
    \nWe're very proud of this idea... and really, being first and foremost a nonprofit organization with the purpose of spreading the love and culture of gaming, it seemed us to make a lot of sense.
    \n
    \nHere is what this particular viper knows (I've been horribly busy and some details might have escaped me, more info will come), in random order:
    \n
    \nThe amount of the discount depends on the final cost of the printing of the new edition, and believe-it-or-not that's still a BIT in flux. We'll give a more definite answer soon, but in the meantime we can say that it'll be a very substantial discount: it'll save the people who "upgrade" quite a bit of cash... and the new edition is (dare I say) fucking cool: not just a reprinting but new graphics, new contents and more.
    \n
    \nThe swap has to happen in person: people that come to our booth at cons and fairs will see their copy being canceled before their eyes, stamped as "not for sale" with a spiffy rubber stamp (and maybe a corner cut in the interior flap of the cover).
    \n
    \nBasically, clubs (and libraries) will need to contact us to make arrangements, plus the "scene" is pretty well connected over the internet, and we'll seek libraries on our own, too. It's a bit clunky, but hey, Italian bureaucracy, we have standards to keep up to!
    \n
    \nOh, one last thing: Don't Rest Your Head will be our "test case". We'll see how it goes, if there is interest from our customers AND from clubs and libraries, and see how it goes. We'd like very much to be able to extend this initiative to future editions, too, obviously (new and improved editions just don't happen very often among these games, so...).
    \n
    \nI think other vipers will chime in ^__^

    \n My first thought (and other people's first thought as well) was "why aren't we doing this as well?" A couple observations:

    1. The Janus crew is very clever.
    2. Italy is full of gaming clubs and has a meetup culture - it won't be hard to hand your old book to the publisher, in person, without a huge fuss.

    For me, in the United States, this is a little more problematic. We are not clever and don't have a ton of events where people will be guaranteed to meet with publishers. We don't really have game clubs on a wide scale.

    Doing this through retailers does not seem like a good fit - what is the retailer's incentive? How do they get compensated? Where does the old book go at that point?

    Doing this through the mail introduces costs and handling. American libraries don't want used materials. So I'd love to hear other ideas about how to reproduce this very excellent idea in our country.

    Other countries will obviously differ - this seems like it might work in the UK, for example.
