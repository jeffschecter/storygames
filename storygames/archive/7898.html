\n An idea I've been kicking around for a little while:

Each player, including the DM, gets a token at the beginning of the game (poker chip, coin, whatever). If during a combat scene the players seem to be in danger of a TPK, the GM can lay their token on the map and offer a deal. The deal is always "You escape, but ..." with the player characters losing something.

So, say the party's cleric takes a tumble, and the others are quickly running out of hit points. The DM slides a coin onto the table and says "You escape, but you leave the cleric behind." Anyone at the table can take the token, including the guy/gal playing the cleric. The heroes automatically escape to the next room, and whoever takes the token narrates the escape. So lets say, in this initial example, the cleric player grabs the escape token, forcing all the other players out of the room and leaving his character behind. He can describe the other characters grappling with the fighter, dragging him out of the room as the goblins surround the fallen cleric, the scramble to spike the door as the goblins try to get at the adventurers,etc. The cleric is still alive, but now the player characters must regroup and (hopefully) go back after him.

The players also have tokens, which allow them to offer an escape deal to the DM. For example, same fight as above, but this time the Fighter slides a token onto the mat and says "We escape, but I lose my sword." The DM can take or not take the deal, or other players can put their own tokens into play and offer deals. Again, even the guy who got knocked out can offer a deal. If no one accepts any of the deals, the tokens come off the table and the fight continues.

The tokens really are only meant to give a physical representation to the deal, and indicate who gets to narrate the outcome of the deal.

    \n I like the idea, and I can imagine it being useful. Sometimes a fight is dragging on, or the outcome is immediately obvious, or it's miscalculated.

    I'm not sure what the tokens add to this idea. Here's a similar idea, that kind of cribs from "Raising the Stakes":

    Before every round of combat (including the first) the players and the GM are allowed to make offers. For example, at the start of the fight, a player might offer "The goblins run away - we win, but we might have to fight them later". Or the GM might offer "You retreat, unharmed, but the goblin scout will follow you". Later in the fight, when the odds become more clear, the offers can become more one-sided. The players might offer "We win, and kill them all except one who runs to warn the others", or the GM might offer "You lose, and are captured by the goblins." Players and GMs can offer healing surges and action points as part of an offer. "We win, but the fighter loses a couple of healing surges". Or "You escape, but you'll lose an action point each". Offers can also mention specific characters, like "You escape but the cleric gets captured" or "We win, but the fighter gets killed". In all cases, there's no obligation to take an offer. The players have to be unanimous to take an offer.

    I can see this playing out a bit like that bit in Fellowship of the Ring, where Gandalf falls into the pit, and the others escape. They've paid the price, we don't need to see what happens after that. I like the idea that you might get an offer that seems really tempting, and decide to risk another round of combat, which changes the situation for the better or for the worse.

    \n If you don't have trouble with TPKs in your game, or you don't feel the need to add a storytelling element to your 4E game, then you wouldn't care for my idea. Fair enough.

    In several 4E games I've played, we've gotten into combat encounters that have gone bad. Usually through some DM fudging the party survived, but there was a level of frustration by the players (and sometimes the DM) that you had to keep fighting until you either won or lost. Also, if the DM doesn't figure the threat level correctly or over-estimates the ability of the players/characters, you can end up with a very one-sided fight in either direction.

    The Escape Tokens aren't meant to "solve a problem," but rather are intended to give DMs and players a fun little tool to liven up a combat encounter that seems to be going downhill. I think I made a mistake of mentioning TPK, because that seems to be what everyone is focusing on. You also could have an encounter where the DM purposefully has the villains outclass the PCs, but gives them the chance of escape just to add a new story element.

    I love 4E. Not trying to fix anything. This was just an idea I was kicking around.

    \n I've encountered the same problem (fight till you win or lose) in many games with a combat focus. They key is finding other goals to a fight than just kill or be killed, which in turn gives you other ways to end the fight (e.g. the goblins run away, the heroes get through the game, the bandit leader is cowed ... etc.). This is sort of what you are doing here, by allowing these alternate end conditions to be arrived at mid-combat when things go wrong, but you can always approach it from the other way too, by building these alternate victory / loss conditions into the encounter to start with. A different thread was talking about scripted things to happen on different turns, but this is more like a trigger condition, and I often plan things like "the goblins will fight bravely till they have lost half their number, and then retreat to their cave. If their leader is wounded over 1/2 hit points, they will retreat at once"

    \nToo many games show creatures as being suicidal or fanatical to the death. That kind of stupidity/devotion should be rare. i recommend that a DM plan scenes, rather than battles. "You come upon a group of orcs, they appear to be on the move". This gives the players a chance to avoid a fight. They can let the orcs pass, or attempt to parlay. Let the bard have a crack at negotiating, build a battle of wits ala Burning Wheel. Make surrender a real option for both sides. Combat should be a serious matter. Don't be afraid to let PCs die. When arrows fly and swords swing, people die.

    \n To add to what Apeiron was saying, it is about creating balanced encounters and relying on something other than "fights to the death." What I intended with the escape tokens was to give players and gms a simple and fun way to do that if they hadn't planned ahead or if an encounter goes in an unexpected/unwelcome direction.

    It is a bit of a GM fiat, except you all have input.
