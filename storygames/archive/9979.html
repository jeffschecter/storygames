What do I say when random people ask what we are doing?

\n
\n
\n
\n\njenskot \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2009 \n edited July 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n When people ask you what you are playing (say Dogs or any RPG), what's an engaging, quick, and clear way to describe it?
\n
\nIn Manhattan we don't have many game spaces. Neutral Ground, a popular game space, closed down. We have nerdnyc's GGG but that's on specific fridays where we rent space. And with many lay offs, more and more groups are losing office spaces to play in.
\n
\nWe've seen a huge influx of gaming groups in indoor public spaces. I've been playing random games at Citigroup Plaza on the occasional Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday and at times there are easily 2-6 other different groups playing as well (usually playing D&D; 4E or Pathfinder and there are also lots of knitting clubs).
\n
\nOur tables seem to attract lots of random people who don't know what roleplaying is to inquire what we are playing. I think when we play Dogs, the massive pools of dice usually attract people. Or maybe our loud gestures and expressions. Either way, people usually say: What are you doing? What are you playing? It looks like fun! Or it looks like you are having fun! It happened twice tonight (we pointed them to lumpley.com). And four times last week. Not including the other people playing D&D; who wanted to know what we were playing.
\n
\nIt almost makes me want to organize (I'm already organizing so many things! ahhhr!) permanent weekly games in public spaces where when people ask what we are doing, we can say... sit down for a minute and find out!
\n
\nEach time people ask, I usually spurt out something like, it's a game where you play stories using dice like a boardgame. Or it's like improv but a game. I rarely describe the actual game (Dogs or otherwise)... it's a game where you act out teenage mormon virgin priest cowboys judging good people doing bad things for good reasons!
\n
\nI think what attracts people mainly is that we appear to be friendly people socializing in fun ways and there are colorful things on the table.
\n
\nWhat's a good way to hook or excite people?

    \n I'd say "We're using this game to make a story together. It's pretty fun," and expand if they have more questions. Over-explaining will probably just muddy the waters.

    If they seem really really interested, you could have a handout pointing them towards your local gaming group website or websites for the games themselves, maybe even a little spiel you put up on your own site about story-gaming.

    If they begin arguing about the meaning of "game" or "story", then they're probably from this forum. Run.

    \n We're making up stories, the way kids do, but because we're adults we like things more complicated. Haha. So we use some rules to help us make up characters, set up fun situations for them, and then we figure out what happens. It's exciting! Sometimes it's tragic or funny or scary.

    The dice? Oh, that helps make things more exciting because we don't know what will happen next.

    It's more complicated than some games, like RISK or Monopoly, sure, but you can pick up the rules pretty quickly. Most people learn by jumping in and winging it. We're not competing against each other so you can't really lose. If you tell a cool story, everyone wins.

    \n Anytime someone asks me about games or my hobby, and this might sound really strange, I give them a short, friendly answer and act as normal as possible. No lingo, no over-explaining, no debating the merits of games, no telling them about my character, etc. Basically I say "This is the basic idea of the game, it is a lot of fun, you should check it out." Chances are, if you are someone they feel they could hang out with, then they are more likely to play.

    \n One major barrier to effective communication is that the game is "closed" (you're already playing, you aren't prepared to take on an additional random stranger as a player). Another is the time commitment (hours to be a part of a real game).

    If someone walks up and asks, "what are you doing?", it would be awesome (after the elevator pitch and signs of interest) to say, "Do you have ten minutes? Have a seat and I will show you." And let them play. Maybe they do a "ride-along" where, like a reporter in a police car, they attach themselves to one of the role-players, who answers their questions. Better, give them a really simple character sheet. That requires prep, of course.

    Maybe let them play the role of an NPC for a short time. Give them a teeny bit of information and let them go wild. If the game has dice mechanics, give them a chance to roll something. Give them a chance to make a decision. Show them how their decision affects the rest of the story. That is the awesomeness of role-playing, after all.

    Or you switch gears. Everyone at the table plays a simple game like The Pool for fifteen minutes.

    If I wasn\u2019t always busy (or rather prioritizing other things higher), my dream scenario would be:

    - organize 3-5 games, twice a week (with the days rotating monthly), to be held at a different public space each week
    - people interested in playing can jump right in
    - have a stand up sign that lets people know they can jump right in and what they are jumping into
    - provide handouts where they can learn more or share with their friends
    - point them to a central website with an intro to story games, a downloadable free intro story game (or mp3 file or video explaining how to play), how to find players, where to play, and how to fit story games into your busy schedule
    - then try to expand it to cafes, book stores, and libraries

    I have no doubt we could introduce at least one new person to story games every week.

    Hell, we could design games specifically for this experiment. For example, games where the audience has a powerful and entertaining role. Or we could tie games to relatable ideas. Maybe games that are set in the actual place we are playing. Or games that can help people build skills for job interviews, dating, asking for a raise, negotiating contracts or whatever. Just trying to think big and different. At that point we would definitely be picked up by the local news. Then grants, sponsorships, create some sort of interactive art installation out of it\u2026

    I need to stop before my head explodes. But it\u2019s a lot of work and time lost not having fun or not making money. It\u2019s tough. But I see the potential.
