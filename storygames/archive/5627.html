\n One thing I always do with d20 is implement Players Roll All The Dice, because it makes the GM free to do other stuff like mime dragons, act out getting violently slain as an orc, circle the group as an inquisitor, etc. That' s what I want to do with the Red Box Hack.

This all came from looking at the numbers. In combat, you roll 2d10 vs. a fixed target number, and out of combat you roll 1d12 + Stat vs. the GM's 1d12 + Stat.

What I found interesting was how easy it would be to unify those mechanics. Let's assume for a second that we can get away with squashing that d12 to a d10. (to compensate, we might have to max the stats at +4, but I don't think it's really necessary).

So we have:

1d10 + PC vs 1d10 + NPC
= 1d10 - 1d10 + PC vs NPC
= 2d10 - 11 + PC vs NPC
= PC + 2d10 vs NPC + 11

So you have two situations: PC's stat plus 2d10 vs. NPC's stat+11 (conflict resolution) and PC's attack modifiers plus 2d10 vs NPC's Target Number (combat). You can also spin the armor target numbers into flat numeric bonuses, and give the PCs an armor roll of 2d10 vs. NPC's Target Number.

If you do that, then you've got Players rolling all the dice and the GM's hands are free for notes, pantomime, and generally being really excited. The GM isn't even tied down to the table if they can remember a few NPC / monster numbers. I've occasionally found myself realizing after the scene that it's time to sit down again, because I've gotten so excited describing things that I'm on my feet gesturing and generally having a blast. Those are my favorite scenes when gaming.

Now, the only problem is that this implementation breaks the monster talent Natural Weapons - now the players have to roll 3d10 and drop the best die, which is awkward. So I'd house rule that. But besides that I think it's a pretty good way to get all the math lining up in the same way.

    \n Yeah, I'm another person who, as a GM, enjoys rolling the dice. But clearly some people, like Ryan, prefer being free to focus on other stuff. Of course, if the math works out, you can just start with an assumption that both sides will roll dice in any conflict, and then give them the option of taking the average roll at any time. If the GM takes it, the PCs are rolling against a set target number. If the PCs take it, the GM is rolling against a set target number. If both take it, it's a simple higher-trait-wins game. Which, with expendable resources (like awesome points or whatever) can still be pretty fun.
