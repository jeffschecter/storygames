\n This thread is inspired by Grahams skill-love thread.

You and your party are exploring the dungeon. You come to a branch in the corridor. After some thought you decide to follow the left corridor. For whatever reason, you never come back and explore the route to the right.

Does it make a difference to play whether there was content to explore behind the un-explored branch? Does it matter to you as a player?

Let's say, for example, that the game has GM, and the GM has prepared the next encounter. You'll get that encounter no matter which choice you make at the crossroads.

For a coutner-example, you're playing a store-bought module. There are rooms behind the branch that are detailed, but are never explored.

    \n Seconded. Irrelevant answer. If a kobold falls in the forest and no PC cut him down, does he make a sound?

    A game world is like a field or a forest at night, with the player characters all holding torches. Everything that is out of their sight has only theoretical reality - it can, will, and should be changed to suit the needs of the story. While you might have detailed huge swaths of the setting that the players will never see, it doesn't really matter until it impacts them directly. Even if it impacts them indirectly - and it should, or why the hell did you bother writing it? - it's reality is vague and amorphous until the moment it enters their perceptions.

    That is: gaming is an ephemeral medium of art. Only what is real in the moment is real.

    \n Your though experiment is mis-leading in that you're leaving out the crucial, absolutely unconditional component that is required for such a situation to even make sense - some meaningful difference between the two choices. In real play what you propose wouldn't even be a choice unless you gave some hints about the nature of the corridors before the choice were made. The only meaning two identical routes offers is that one might backtrack and explore both, but even then you're not actually giving the players a choice over the order of exploration if you don't give some information about the destinations of the two routes.

    (Note that the players can generate this sort of information themselves in real play, if you allow it: they might send a scout to look at one of the routes and come back with a report, for instance. Then the choices would become real, as the players had at least some basis for making the choice.)

    Barring that sort of reality-based argument against your set-up, though, I'd have to say that whether there were genuine content in the never-explored branch only matters as a basis for the contract of play - if we were playing a game where the GM has an opportunity to cheat and switching stuff around like that would be cheating, then it most certainly matters to me as a GM and player whether the GM was actually cheating. In some other games it wouldn't matter, or would even be expected. And no, I don't buy it for a second that cheating doesn't matter if it was never found out - the cheater himself knows what he's doing, and it affects himself even nobody else knows about it.

    You ask about differences in play, though, and that's a bit trickier - of course our trust in the procedures of play matters, so if we're suspecting the GM of illusionism, say, and he's actually doing it or not doing it, that influences how we react to his play. Or if we're playing a store-bought module, that influences things quite a bit - we have reasonable faith in there existing solid preparation for the dungeon, and thus we're going to take the choice of corridors seriously. On the other hand, we're aware of how fragile such modules are, so we're not going to pick any third route - we pick a route that keeps us comfortably in the module space.

    \n Loads of difference!

    What was that thing Vincent said on his blog, a while ago? I'll try and reconstruct. Imagine a conversation like this:\n
    Graham: I shoot you in the face!
    Simon: Like fuck you do, actor-boy. Let's roll dice.

    [Rolls dice. Graham loses.]

    Simon: Hah! I knock the gun out of your hand!
    \nVincent's point was that "I shoot you in the face" is an important part of the fiction, even if it didn't happen.

    This is similar. That something behind the door, that you never discovered, is a strange, shadowy, important part of the fiction. It's more important if you know it actually was something.

    And you know this intuitively from playing D&D, right? When the GM says "Ah, if you'd been through that door?". And you say "What?". And he says "Ah, we'll get to it another time". That's much different from saying "Ah, if you'd been through that door, we'd have made something up."

    \n There is an interesting tactical consideration here. GMing for some experienced dungeon crawlers, I've noticed the tendency to try to explore every branch in order to avoid exposing themselves to an attack from the rear.     So in this case, there's definitely an unspoken assumption that what you leave behind you remains alive and active.

    I feel that point two really is the point. The branching hallway thing is actually a pretty poor starting point, because it's such an irrelevancy. The question we're really debating is this:

    Should a campaign world be fully realized, where things are happening in a fully internally consistent way, or should a campaign world be a narrative construct keyed to respond to whatever the players of the game think is fun? Or (and this is the real question - both extremes are pretty much straw men) should it be some combination of the two, and if so, in what proportion?

    Now, I will never call your fun bad - it's against my religion - but I am curious as to how you respond.

    My opinion? It's about the illusion of consistency. I don't want my game world to be really happening, with my character simply plugged into it and hoping for the best. I get enough of that kind of crap in the real world. I want the campaign world to be about me and my issues. However, I agree that a game needs the illusion of consistency. Even though I know full well that X happened to my character because I used a narrative system to make it happen, or even because I said the GM "hey, wouldn't it be cool if..." there need to be compelling in game reasons for it to happen, otherwise my suspension of disbelief is strained, and everyone knows what happened if your strain your suspension of disbelief...

    On some level, this may be a GNS question (for all that I don't 100% approve of leaning too heavily on GNS theory...). Narrative play, as a rule, tends to require that the setting respond to the demands of the story, whereas Simulationist play is more interested in consistency and verisimilitude. I want just enough realism to beat that little voice in the back of my head that says "stupid stupid stupid! Horses can't fly! No one will ever love you!" to death, but I don't need any more than that. Other gamers might be different. Remember, no one's fun is bad.

    \n This is very similar to a discussion I'm neglecting on the Forge right now, about no-prep/low-prep gaming.
    \n
    \nI think an important thing to take away from this discussion is this: You're offering a choice. Don't offer a choice unless it means something to choose, and both choices are meaningful.
    \n
    \nSo if you've got one encounter planned (or none!) don't offer a choice if either choice leads to the same place.
    \n
    \nIn this case, if you offer the choice, you should at least have considered what will lie down either path, even if it's not a fully planned encounter.. and there should be some indication. One path leads downward, and the faint smell of decay drifts up toward you. The other path has a faint flickering light, and you hear the distant sound of rough merriment. This gives the players a frame of reference. Sure, they don't know (and you might not have really decided) what's down either path.. But if they go down the first path, they can reasonably expect to face undead, or some sort of darkness loving creature in it's den, scattered with the remains of previous victims, and the other path shouldn't surprise them if they come across a bunch of goblins feasting on human flesh, or another party of adventurers having a good laugh around the fire.
    \n
    \nThis can still allow for a completely improvised game, if that's your thing. You had no more idea of what they may encounter than they did before you presented the choice. Once they make the choice, it's meaningful, even if you then make up the specifics on the spot.

    \n One interesting side point to this whole discussion:

    In my gaming experience, I've seen many, many instances of GMs working hard to maintain the illusion that "there was something behind every door". It was a weird sort of pretense, since everyone (usually) knew it wasn't likely, or maybe even possible.

    The players would say, "Oh, man, I wonder what was behind door #2!"

    The GM would answer, "Well, I guess you'll never know now that you've left the island!"

    ...but really, the GM was thinking, "Oh, thank goodness. I couldn't think of anything to put there," or maybe, "That was a close one! I would have had to make something up on the spot, and they would surely have caught on this time."

    And from the players' perspective, it was a bit like calling the GM's bluff: well, maybe she did have something prepared, and maybe she didn't... I wonder which?

    I remember the day when I realized that, as a GM, I didn't have keep up this sort of pretense. It was very liberating.

    I think now that the players will generate their own suspension of disbelief, if they're into the game. If the game is awesome, no one seems to wonder or care so much about this stuff.

    (That said, let me also second the "Black Keep" idea: always, always give some kind of hint or taste. That makes those choices meaningful and interesting, and brings the world to life. It also limits the GM a little in terms of railroading power: it forms a creative constraint to further developments. Very powerful tool! You'll notice Tolkien uses this all the time in Lord of the Rings.)
