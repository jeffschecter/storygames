\n I played them extensively when I was younger and I didn't have a tabletop group. For me, my most successful game was one that went three whole scenes and lasted for almost two weeks before going moribund.

I moved on to play-by-voice games, which is mostly what I do now, and I spent a good long while playing those, and absorbing a lot of game design theory along the way. When I went back and looked into play-by-post games recently I was sort of hoping that someone would have solved the problems that I experienced when I was playing them back in 2003-2004.

But it looks like nobody has.

A quick scan through every play-by-post I've been able to find will show that nearly all games die early, and with almost nothing getting done. Most stall within the first scene if they ever manage to start at all. Many of them die before people even manage to introduce their characters.

Anyone have any idea why this happens? I mean, people clearly WANT this to work, those forums stacked with endless piles of dead games are full of people constantly trying to make this work.

To the people who have made this kind of thing work, do you have any idea why your game survived when the vast majority of others fail so quickly?

    \n I have been active and a moderator in a play by post forum for a longer time. I have to admit I do not really play there anymore, but I can say it worked for years.
    The players had similar rythms in their life. That worked for them to post at similar times and commit to a similar rythm. We had slow going games and ones with multiple posts each day. Both worked with the players being happy with the same commitment.
    They need to stay at the game for a long time too, can be month and years till a scenario is finished. The players should know that.

    But on the other hand not too many scenes will be done so the scenario should be somewhat short. Something you can do in one session I would suggest.

    Starting somewhat in medias res is a good thing or starting with the characters together at least, maybe getting to know each other right then. Individual starts for each character have been the bane of my failing scenarios.

    You should be willing to get new players into the game and have some leave. If after a few month one player has to get out that should not fail the game for everyone. So not hinging the whole scenario on a certain constallation of players is bad. If you need the same characters that is fine, but have other players take them over or if you are a GM be willing to run them as NPCs if you do not find a replacement. Also if you are willing to bring in new characters and take out old ones, you should not run a scenario that seperates necessary information.

    All of these have multiple sessions, hundreds of posts, an incredible depth and tapestry of story.

    An episode of the Geeky and Genki podcast where they brought me on to talk about play-by-post gaming:
    http://www.geekyandgenki.com/gamecast-23-rich-rogers-on-play-by-forum-gaming/

    I am NOT an expert, but I've had more hits than misses in the last few years. Some of my thoughts on how I'm successful in PBP games I play in and run:
    * Play games that have conflict resolution mechanics (Apocalypse World and Lady Blackbird are my faves, Monsterhearts and other games Powered by the Apocalypse are great at this), this way players are resolving conflicts quickly and moving on, not plodding through maps or rolling tit for tat
    * Set up alternate communication methods with players and get to know them as people a bit so we can talk about more than just the game
    * Learn WHEN the other players post, anticipate it and set up a post schedule around it to facilitate quicker play (FREX: if Joe only posts weekdays while at work, don't set Joe up in a new scene with Jane when she posts on weekends, instead try to get Jane involved in something, ANYTHING over the weekend by herself or with other weekend posters)
    * Have a GChat / text-only Hangout going with the group and we chat about the game throughout the day, check in, high five, BS, all that jazz
    * Set expectations for post rates and reach out to players (and the GM/MC) if folks fall behind.
    * Constantly talk with the group about the awesome story bits, talk about the fun, remind them of the awesome thing we're doing; the hype pays off
    * I have been incredibly blessed with the gamers I play with, they are cool people and they make awesome games happen with me

    \n There are definitely certain allowances that need to be made.
    I've played in a few PBPs, all of which fizzled out.
    On the other hand, I am now in the final two scenes of a year-long DramaSystem PBP with - astoundingly - no player drop-outs. Was it perfect? No, but I think the framework I set up at the beginning has more or less worked out OK.

    Key features:
    - a rigid scene structure
    - a GM willing to say 'it's time for the next scene'
    - posting deadlines. Flexible if need be, but they're there.
    - a posting model. At the beginning I outlined the 'ideal' scene: establishing shot, PC 1 asks (N)PC 2 for something, other character says yes/no, done. Of course, we almost never did so few exchanges, but more than 4 or 5 posts and it's too long.
    In PBP you've got the time to think and then load a single post with a lot of important stuff - posting a single line doesn't speed up the process.

    Biest is right to point out that the scenario should be short. A lot of time can pass in the story, but the number of discrete 'things' that can happen is necessarily lower.
    In our game each episode had 11 scenes - and our series finale is... episode 5. We still got a lot done, though.

    There's an RPG.net thread on the subject of system that might be useful: http://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?704702-What-Would-the-Ideal-Play-by-Post-RPG-System-Look-Like

    One thing I got out of that thread was the idea that a player should be able to declare and resolve their action in a single post if possible.
    I never quite managed that with my game, but we got closer to it, and I have plans for next time.

    \n I don't have time to respond right now, and there is some great stuff here, but I wanted to say that I think that simply linking successful games without commentary isn't super useful. There's too much data.

    It's like saying "read my novel to understand how English grammar works." It might help, but the information is not in a digestible format.

    Part of my issue with fixing the problems that I've observed while playing this kind of game, is that failed games often resist analysis. It's difficult, often impossible, to determine what you did wrong because of the WAY that these games die.

    If a tabletop game self destructs, you all sit back from the table and then you say "Jee, that really sucked, lets not do [REASON_FOR_FAILURE] again."

    But when play-by-post games fail, there can be no analysis, because the way that they fail is that everyone just... stops talking. Information can typically be gathered only by archaeology, by digging through the threads, which is, I've found, an inefficient method for acquiring usable procedures.

    \n@Jack the Quick - I don't expect you to read through hundreds of posts from my past games. I was trying to establish a bit of credibility.

    I agree that it's tough to analyze why PBP games fail. I've done some post mortems on failed games and had conversations with players after they dropped out of games I was running.

    A few reasons I've found:
    * Life/work schedule changed for an ongoing period (got new job, stress at home, moving, etc.)
    * life/work schedules change for a brief period (vacation, got sick, travel) and return to the game is a post debt, leading to the player falling behind and not catching up or unable to re-engage
    * Player post rate heavily out of sync with the playgroup (posting too fast and becoming frustrated with the rest of the group, posting too slow and falling behind)
    * Players not "doing enough", meaning their posts are introspective without moving the fiction forward. I've seen games stall out because players were busy posing and angsting without do-ing
    * A playgroup has wildly different expectations out of the game/setting and the slow pace of conversation stalls out, leaving it unresolved

    \n Ooh! That IS an interesting one there orklord. The concept of post-debt.

    The feeling that the longer one has been away, the more work one has to catch up on, leading to more avoidance.

    I've noticed this trend with other things in my life, and it is a dangerous cycle if one wants to get anything done.

    Your last point in your second post is also important. I've often thought that the reason that play-by-post games often go badly, is that it is so difficult to do a conversation through asynchronous text.

    Games are conversation, and in a live conversation we're constantly aware of the general emotional state of the other people we're playing with, but over an asynchronous medium like a forum, we don't have that connection.

    I can't tell, if what I'm doing is boring, or annoying, or confusing you, while I can tell that instantly in person. Due to the lag between communications, asking people to change something they've already posted (because it's going in a direction you don't like, or contains incorrect information about something that you created) is a significant burden, and people are less likely to make on-the-fly adjustments to the shared fiction as a result.

    This is aggravated by the medium of forums, as the conversation that generates the fiction also becomes the -record- of that fiction, and so things are 'set in stone' far more then they tend to be in a real-time game.

    Often, when playing in real-time, I'll find myself in conversations like this:

    "OK! So I go light the torch and -

    "Wait! Wasn't the torch already lit? I mean, Torvald was waving it at those zombies."

    "But isn't it really dark in here? We have an aspect Deadly Shadows."

    "Right, yes, ok, lets say that it went out when Torvald fell down and you just re-lit it"



    Whereas in a play-by-post game, this kind of continuity editing is very difficult or impossible.

    \n Orklord covered most of what I would have said but:
    -Pacing works more like a con game. Better to plan around a short game and finish strong than plan for a long game and fizzle during a slow start.
    -Players should keep a loose rein on their characters, letting other players narrate trivial details for your character gives everyone the power to move the story forward.
    -It's OK to be choosy about picking players to give the game its best chance for success, keep track of players that worked out for future games (different players work well for different games though).
    -Not all games are created equal when it comes to PbP, some systems will work against you while others will stay out of your way. Choosing a game is your first chance to shoot yourself in the foot.
    -Games that have an expected ending or duration are great because, when things start to slow down, you can see the end in sight and play toward it. There's nothing worse than flagging interest and no end in sight.

    \n@JDCorley :

    I suspect that our personal experience doesn't overlap a lot here. In the places where I played PBP games (the Megatokyo comic forums mostly, and RPOL), it was common to have a game master, though they often also had a PC character.

    I think PBP communities diverge wildly from each other in terms of culture and expectations.

    I remember reading an article or thread about early dungeons and dragons, and how 'the game' of D&D played by any specific group of people was a sort of cargo-cult amalgamation of the various books, magazines, home-brew, and whatever else was available at the time.

    The "PBP Scene", if one can even call it that, is the same. There are a massive variety of different Play-By-Post cultures, and they are all very different.

    For instance, I played mostly on comic forums. These forums contained a lot of people with a wide variety of interests, almost all of whom had no tabletop RPG experience.

    Games were created by one person, and took place mostly in a single forum thread. The number of players differed, but usually started with between three and six.

    The GM typically held authority over the world, but not over the narrative, each player having absolute mastery over their own character. Most of these games never went anywhere, often fizzling out after one or two posts by the players.

    The post format flip-flopped between first and third person, with various game masters attempting to apply one standard or another, but they were almost all written like media, not like a conversation, and were typically written in past-tense.

    One would write:\n
    "Jim walked over to the table and picked up the gun, looking at it carefully."
    \nOr:\n
    "I walked over to the table and picked up the gun. I peer carefully at it."
    \nNever:\n
    "I walk over to the table and pick up the gun. What does it look like? I'm trying to figure out if this is the same gun we found earlier on the floor in the lobby."
    \nThe last example is interesting because that's the kind of dialog I expect in tabletop games, but I never even considered using it in PBP. I only realized it was even an option when when looking at OrcLords monsterhearts game. Many of the posts in that game look like conversation, talking about the game, and not AS the game.

    That seems to be a missing piece of technology, along with having a dedicated game master to drive things forward.


    I also noticed that all the successful games that have been posted so far have had multiple threads, often -dozens- of threads, while my experience had the entire game contained in a single thread. Has anyone done both? Is there a reason multi-thread games appear to work better or do you think that is just a small sample size skewing the results?

    \n This is some really cool stuff, @Jack the Quick!

    Yes, the convention, or style, of talking "about the game" sort of like a player slipping in and out of portraying the character, is a method that Michael Loy does with amazing success. I have tried to ape his style, but often I post, as a player, 90% in character, first person perspective, only slipping out of the fiction when, like in Monsterhearts or Apocalypse World, a Move happens.

    \n I've played years of PBP. Most of it in my case has been freeform and GMless, though I don't think adding a light systems like *W would change the experience significantly.

    The most useful thing for running a PBP in my experience is an out of character chat that runs at a much faster rate than the game itself, ideally with some means of saving a history so those people who miss conversations can still read up on them. This lets you hone down to the experience that you actually want to get "on screen", whilst also giving you a meta game that spreads a lot wider than it likely would in a tabletop game where you can try ideas on for size before committing to them. Talking in advance about how to frame the next scene, or being able to tell you during the game that I'm okay for you to post that my character follows your out the door rather than having to write something separate to that effect, speeds things up a lot and avoids burnout from writing loads of words about pointless things.

    In my experience, a single thread game works best for something that has a fairly traditional "one epic journey undertaken by an adventuring party who mostly stick together" format. As long as everyone's posting at a fairly equal rate, it gives everyone a chance to contribute and lets you easily keep on top of everything that's going on.

    A multi-thread game by contrast is better for something with an open world and a less linear timeline. As long as players are careful with how they treat scenes that could have a large impact on their character - which they should generally know in advance because of the OOC chatter - you can run multiple scenes with the same character at once. This means active players can do more without keeping less active players away from the main story, and can lead to a feeling of an expansive, continuous world that's pretty much unique to PBP games.

    \n My favourite tactic when I've been away for a bit is to get on the group chat and ask everyone what's been going on. It is no secret whatsoever that gamers love to tell you about their exploits, so for the slim price of being an invested 'listener' for a little while you can get a highlights reel very easily.

    Generally though, it's fine as long as you've got a decent out-of-game atmosphere. If you know you're going to be away, you can do the team-player thing and write your character falling asleep or going off to scout for a bit to get them out of the way, or just ask people in slower threads if they're cool with putting the events on hold for a bit (and if the slow threads get culled afterwards because you don't feel like going back to them, no biggie as long as everyone feels like they've got a satisfactory understanding of how it would end). Likewise, if you come back, you should be able to trust your fellow players to cut you some slack if you haven't read every post in detail and get things wrong - if we're all adults, they'll correct the mistakes in good cheer and you'll accept the help with good grace and make edits.

    In a bigger group, you might want a formalised policy for absences which is basically just writing out the above so everyone feels comfortable doing it. I personally like it when the out of character chatter is preserved somewhere so I can skim through to see if I missed any big community events or in-jokes, which is good for feeling part of things again after a while of being disconnected.

    \n So are we talking about play-by-post (e-mail, forum, or some other asynchronous "slow" venue) or by IRC or other "real-time" venue? There are definitely differences, with real-time you can ask lots of clarifying questions as long as people are relatively responsive. With asynchronous, "slow", you need to be prepared to make assumptions and just move forward.

    As to the problem of post debt, poking my nose into the OD&D IRC game that spawned from discussing Eero's playstyle, I definitely could not jump into that, way too much volume to read. But the play-by-post games that I'm involved in, the post volume is not more than 20-30 per week, and someone who missed a lot could just read the last few and probably get enough of an idea of the situation to contribute. If they make assumption that contradicts posts they didn't read, it's easy enough to correct if actually necessary.
