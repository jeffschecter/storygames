    \n Heh, different people are recommending quite different games for Sim. I agree that playing PTA sim-wise seems quite near impossible without taking some serious freedoms with the Issue as described in the game text. Of course, the same could be said of how I hardly seem to manage to get Nar out of Universalis.

    Anyway, to answer Filip's question, I might explain Dead of Night in length, but I'm going to do something simpler. This is how Primitive facilitates a certain sort of Sim:

    When you first start playing Primitive, you have to make a choice: are you going to take the setting of primitive cave men seriously, or are you going to put in those dinosaurs, vikings, lizard men and other stuff? Should you go for the former, which is what the Finnish groups seem to always do, certain specific forces start to push for Sim mighty hard. Specifically:
    • Nobody at the table knows too much about prehistory, but presumably somebody knows enough for you to want to go for a "historical treatment" instead of the nazi dinosaur option. In fact, the setting is so sparse that the game immediately asks you to answer some immense questions about it: how do these cave men live? What are their daily routines like? Do they love, go to war, pray? Do they have marriage customs? How long do they live? Are they happy in general, or wretched? These questions are of such import that you cannot really play the game without answering them at least implicitly. A committed group of players is thus, by necessity, going to focus on the minor details of the setting right from the start simply to get everything straight and explore enough to make some room for something to happen. This process lays the perfect ground-work for sim, should the group be so inclined.
    • The communication system works its magic in this process as well, as it further limits the play experience and sets barriers for efficient and purposeful action. Should the players draw pictures like the rules recommend, they will find that the pictorial supports a simulationist interest in the setting as well - the process of play itself pretty much creates a living album of places, people and items that clutter the cave man lands. This whole process has a strong doll house feel to it, and that's pretty much the way to appreciate it, I've found - you're just going to get bored silly if you're not interested in creating this slowly expanding world. This is world-creation sim, pure and simple. Every time I've played the group has spent most of its time tinkering with the fictional details of simple construction and hardcrafts as they've strained their brain to figure out how a cave man would build a functional raft or some such.
    Of course, Primitive is an immensely incoherent little jewel, the game practically begs for a hard-ass gamist treatment with its communal resource pool - and certainly there's Nar possibilities all over the place as well. But Sim is something I've found unproblematic and natural with the couple of groups I've played the game with. I've yet to play a longer term game, so I have no idea what'd happen after the players got over the constant need to expand the setting and determine routines, but I suspect that the momentum of exploration would carry for quite a while.

    I agree, though, that it'd be easier to recommend specific Sim games to Filip if we had some hint of what he might find interesting. I personally suspect that he might enjoy Dead of Night - if for no other reason, then because it's a very clever design. I have many of the same reservations Filip listed when it comes to actually playing even well-designed sim games, so one of the reasons for why I like DoN is that it sidesteps all of that and just provides me with compact B-movie horror in one-session packages with hardly any effort. Like having your own movie factory in the backyard.

    \n Re: Sign in Stranger, if "explore alien environments" fills your head with fun premises to address, you could perform all the acts of interpretation the game requires from that POV. You might have to fudge a lot, though, to get "Story Now" rather than "story eventually".

    If you just want the Right to Dream "exploring alien environments", though, the rules facilitate that extremely well. (Unless you're trying to immerse, and watching your fellow players invent the world on the spot would ruin that for you. It didn't for me...)

    Forgive the long-form GNS terms, I just think they apply better to my points this way. Calling Sign in Stranger a "simulation" of world-exploration would be much less accurate.

    My personal favorite version of "Sim done right" is the game I'm designing, Delve, which avoids all of your no-nos except "procedurally incomplete", which I'm working on. What's "right" about it to me: situation is emergent from player choices, not scripted by GM metaplot; yet, at the same time, the fiction is opaque and it's quite easy to convince yourself that the setting is real without ever being reminded otherwise during play. Within that, you get to do a lot of learning, induction, and deduction, as you discover secrets and figure out how they work... which is close to Gamist, except that you don't have to show any strategic competence to keep the secrets coming. Color is hugely important: there's a dial for the players to communicate desired levels of detail to the GM; player rewards for contributing color; and character sheets that track color-inspiring stuff.

    Of course, this is in many ways opposite to Sign in Stranger. I just happen to like 'em both.

    Posted By: bunnypundit
    What exactly makesPrimitive's actual play not go the Sim route if the group puts in those dinos and stuff?
    \nWell, this is just conjencture, as I haven't played this way, but every time I've played the game I've personally felt very strongly against the dinosaurs, and the people I've played with have felt the same. Essentially, the pulpiness seems to undermine the process of setting exploration I describe above. If you're going to make the setting blatantly fantastic, it's even odds that you're also going to get humoristic, and at that point there's no group pressure at all to not make your character Grug-the-man-with-the-large-club and go do something with the actual high-order mechanics of the game. This is not conducive of wondering about the issues that are in the core of the setting exploration - I'm not going to speculate about the sexual mores or dress code of the cavemen if we're actively trying to pulp up the setting - this shows very clearly in the Record of Threats, which is sort of a gamist prism for the original game: if the GM frames the situation of play with something like "your tribe has been driven out of their home and now you're on the coast, wondering how to get to the lush island just across the straits", it's pretty clear that the game will not be about group-wide setting exploration of the sort I described.

No idea why we like the genre, but ever since I played Primitive I realized that I want an even more comprehensive game that takes a more coherent stand at exploring the primitive man. I got Og basically because I wanted to see another cave man game, but it was a sort of a disappointment for being another ultra-light treatment. Interesting how you can get hooked by a genre like that.

    So take the same game and give it to a group that is intensely interested in chaotic pulp fiction and who appreciate genre-mashups and just trying to figure out how cave men and nazis go together, and you're going to see a different sort of game. Perhaps it'll be sim - as I said at the beginning, Primitive is really, really incoherent, it's basically only going to cohere if the group happens to have a strong shared vision of what a cave men game should be "about". For us that shared vision has, perhaps due to my own personal influence, been all about serious armchair anthropology (not science, you understand; more like science fiction); for some other group it'll be dinosaurs. I can't explain how the game supports sim without a strong setting focus the way I can explain the setting-focused play we've experienced, but that's not to say that it's not possible with the right group doing the right things. And of course, if you're interested in the setting of that dinosaurlavamenlizardfolk game, then I'd imagine that the same or similar mechanical forces would drive the game as have been working when we've played. I can't offhand understand myself why anybody would play setting-focused sim in such a frivolous setting, but people have widely differing tastes.

    \n There's a lot of effort put here in what boils down to attempts at deconstructing Filip's original, straightforward request. It's like some people have a great need to participate in the discussion despite not having any useful knowledge or experiences about the actual topic. I've been biting my tongue a bit to not say this, but frankly, I'm not seeing the value in telling a guy who wants to learn more about modern simulationism that the whole concept is wrong-headed and he should be focusing on his own fun, anyway. I know that when I'm researching new vistas of design, I vastly prefer that people engage my question instead of trying to convince me that I'm a bad man for daring to explore the concept in the first place. In other words: if you don't yourself even believe in the existence of a simulationist creative agenda, perhaps you're not the right person to instruct Filip in how modern games go about fulfilling that very purpose. If being vaguely defined (if that should be what Sim is) made discussion of a concept into a grave sin, perhaps we shouldn't discuss this vaguely defined thing called roleplaying at all. And finally, if having different ideas about the concept appear in the thread is proof that the discussion itself is wrongheaded, then that's one mighty facile way of shutting down any and all dialogue.

    To get back to the original question, which I happen to be able to answer by the virtue of having actually played a number of simulationistic games instead of just criticizing the concept in the Internet, one interesting type of simulationist attention that some games make a good effort at is character-centric concept sim. I'm thinking of games like octaNe, Full Light, Full Speed, Houses of the Blooded and Spirit of the Century, for example. What is common to these games is that the basic onus they put upon the individual player is essentially that you have to fall in love with your character during the character generation process or early play to enjoy the game. These games also have a systematic method for enabling the player in this process: each game, while differing widely in the scope and mechanization of their resolution mechanics, involves a very heavy-duty character generation system that essentially allows for lots of player creativity and requires the player to visualize a full literary character concept akin to how comics-writers work for the character generation process to go anywhere. The actual play of a game that centers on the character concept in this way can often vary widely simply because the goal itself is very flexible: it doesn't matter to us what the game's practice actually is as long as it allows the player to experience and express his powerful character vision in the playground the GM sets up. Perhaps looking at some of these games might be useful for Filip? Of course it's notable that this sort of sim is also front and center in many traditional designs, so these particular games might not teach anything particular in this regard, except perhaps how one can cut most of the unnecessary setting and mechanics and still leave intact a quite valid platform for character expression.

I'm also sure that many games work better if the player has an appreciative audience for his character, but immersionist games seem to work differently - or perhaps they just don't express their interest in ways I'd notice.

      \n Filip:
      I totally do not have a game I can't point at for you, but I can tell you about a little orphan mechanic/artifact thing I've been noodling around with, and maybe you can work it into another game somehow.

      Background:Where I'm coming from on Sim:
      To me, Sim has to do with staying true to the settting, situation elements, and style of some source material.
      (Weirdly "staying true to..." can also mean parodying the source material really well).

      I'd note that the source material in question can be something created by the players, or the GM. It need not pre-exist from someone else's art/writing/comic/film/whatever ( although I'd expect it to in some for reference other work).

      So, the artifact/mechanic: Idea Collages/sheets

      These sheets are simply like the sort of things you see young people, especially teen girls, make. A largish sheet of paper covered with snippets of cut-out writing and probably some images.

      Except, instead of pics and phrases from Teen Beat or Cosmo or something, you're using stuff related to the game you want to play.

      If you're the GM or game initiator or whatever, you'll probably want to make these up, at least one per player, maybe more.

      You may choose to use something like index cards instead, then stick them to a bigger board.

      Make it messy. Cover all of the stuff you think is important for the right feel. It could be combat results, or scenes, or locations, or ways people act. Whatever, let your brain go.

      Make sure each sheet has a variety of types of stuff ( or not; a heavily focused sheet could be interesting, too, although I only just considered that possibility now).

      Leave room around the cards or snippets of images, so people can make notes and scrawl on the thing.

      Place the stuff chaotically around the sheet. Turn stuff at weird angles or upside down and side-ways.

      Hand this stuff out before the game kicks off. Let the participants have time to digest all the things you've picked out to put on the collages.

      Keep in mind, the idea sheets have no direct impact on play. Instead, they're meant as a springboard for ideas central to the type of game being played.

      I suppose you could experiment with having particpants create their own collages, or bringing elements for collages, and making them there at the table or before the first official game session.

      Players can trade them around, too, swapping if they wish. I could see "scenario specific" collages as well, or collage elements tacked onto pre-existing collages for the same setting/on-going game.

      Hack up the collages and used them to make new collages after a story arc if you wish to better reflect the evolving understanding of the setting/situation/style/character development.

      One key thing is that they are messy! They don't completely, individually tell everything about the setting, etc. The goal is to create a soup of ideas, and then let group pattern recognition emerge.

      Is that non-trad enough? ;-)
