\n So about a week ago Laura Bishop (who you can hear in the Actual People, Actual Play podcast with me and Will) and I were sitting around a bit tired of playing World of Warcraft and trying to decide what to do. I said off offhandedly, "We could play Houses of the Blooded since that other thread here on Story Games was fresh in my mind." To my surprise she said, "Sure." So we went through character creation.

I had created a character for a short-lived play by Twitter HotB game before. I noticed the same thing here that I noticed there. Character creation creates little in the way of Situation. Which is odd since John obviously worked so hard to create a system that produced connected social characters rather than D&D style lone wanderers with no ties. However, there's nothing like a Kicker or Beliefs or Best Interests. Despite being connected to other people, the characters have no motion. On paper, they're not going anywhere and nothing is coming at them.

So you have to read between the lines. Laura's character had the ritual The Circle, and she has a Court Scholar and she has some Ruins on her property. Oh and she has a Artifact that lets her define people's futures as well as some Aspects related to that kind of thing. So, all I have to go on is a vague notion of "The invisible world is important."

So a week after we created this character, we played. It was not a total disaster but I did find myself "pin wheeling" in a few places.

I had one of the Suaven Laura's character is devoted to show up in a dream and tell her that her cousin (her Contact) was in grave danger. That his past was catching up to him. Opening this way felt ridiculously old-school but at least I was drawing from elements on the character sheet and not just making up random relatives the player had never heard of.

I needed seed material so in my head this is what was going on: In the past Laura's character's cousin had murdered his rival to marry his current wife. A Spectre was haunting the cousin in the form of the murdered rival. About mid-way through the session I suddenly had the idea that the cousin's wife might be poisoning him. I was prepared to change any and all of this based on the result of Cunning and Wisdom wagers but as I said I needed a BASE LINE of content to introduce. Now, I wasn't working particularly hard to hide any of this content and what I found interesting is that Laura often choose to reinforce and back up my implications with her wagers rather than change direction even though she could have. In fact, at one point she used a wager to say that the Spectre had, in fact, been deliberately summoned by the cousin's wife which I thought was a nice touch.

Where I really began to pinwheel is when I discovered that so much of the "social drama" laid out in the book is predicated on the social presence of *other ven*. Throwing Insults only makes sense if you have an audience there to pick up on your subtle implications. The Duel is a giant formal process involving appealing to others to justify your grievance. The rules of Hospitality only make sense in the context of multiple ven who might want to harm one another hanging out in the same social space. Debate is about gaining favor with a political audience.

So much of Ven culture is about ME making YOU look bad to THEM. In my games (even when there are more than one player) there is no "THEM." I like intimate scenes with very participants. So this is how I kept getting tripped up.

Laura's character confronted the cousin's wife alone and out and out called her LIAR and demand she own up to her own plot. I thought WOW she called a Ven a LIAR in her own HOME. That can't stand I should.... I should... wait....

Trading insults doesn't work, there is no audience.

Convening a jury is.... well... over kill and kind of a mood killer as far as the current in progress scene goes.

Revoking Hospitality makes no sense since there isn't anyone else in the house whom you would now be threatened by without the host's Hospitality.

Basically there's no social mechanic for when I want to just grab a character by the collar, slam them against the wall, and scream, "Tell me the truth!"

So I just had the character snarl, say, "How DARE you call me a Liar in my own home! Get out! Leave at once!" And storm out. Which felt like emotive drama with no teeth.

This happened again later.

Laura decided to take her cousin to a Suaven temple to pray for guidance and salvation. On the road I had the Spectre materialize and demand that she not interfere with his Revenge. Again Laura's character stepped forward and demanded that the Spectre reveal to her who is behind manifestations. And again I was like..... so what happens now..... I like this idea of this bold ven commanding the Spectre answer to her..... how do we resolve that.....

Here I at least had an idea. I remembered that things have a Terror score. So I had Laura roll Courage and I roll Terror. Each wager adding a fact the Spectre revealed to her. This was okay. But felt like I was making up rules as I go along which I hate feeling like I'm doing.

Then Laura says the most amazing thing ever to me. She wants to use a Ritual to merge the Spectre and her cousin together. Thus the Spectre gets to live out the life robbed of it and her Cousin is punished for his crimes. I give her Style.

But... uh... how to handle this. There certainly isn't a Ritual in the book for that kind of thing. It felt wrong for Laura's character to suddenly know this Ritual because you have to buy them at character creation and spending a Style to just pile on Sorcerous knowledge in light of the Season actions makes no sense.

And as I've always said I think the rules in the first part of the book get muddied by the rules in the later half of the book. The following solution I think is 100% in line with the rules outlined in the first part of the book and totally violates several rules and principles in the later part of the book.

First, I had Laura spend a Style to say that her Court Scholar knew of such a ritual. Laura took her cousin to her rank 2 Ruins to perform the ritual. So I had her roll Wisdom and gave her 2 dice from her rank 2 Ruins which I think is consistent with the "bonus dice from items" rule. I also rolled dice for her Court Scholar. I gave him a Wisdom of 4 and an Aspect about "Things you were not meant to know" or something like that.

We rolled and traded wagers that described the effect of the ritual. In short her Cousin is now a totally different person. Neither his former self nor his long dead rival but some weird thing in between. We ended the session there. We will probably play more.

So there's my experience playing the game.

Jesse

    \n Hey James,

    Mulling it over I eventually came to the conclusion that I could use Courage as kind of the "bold presentation of self" stat. So yeah, Courage vs. Cunning to interrogate a liar seems right. But I had to mull it over a really long time before I felt "okay" with that. Don't know what that says about me.

    So your conclusions about how I handled the Courage vs. Terror situation the Sorcerous rituals are exactly my point. They are 100% Rules as Written if you "weight" everything that comes before the Poisons Chapter as "more important" than everything that comes after it.

    Terror seems to have some highly specific functions when it is presented. It's not presented as: "Orks do not have Virtues. Instead they have Terror. Whenever an Ork engages in a risk roll it's Terror regardless of the risk at hand." Terror is instead presented as having a very specific use and function. No, I don't think it's a stretch or out of the "spirit" of the rules to use Terror in the way I did but it does go against the presentation of Terror's purpose.

    That's a bit nit-picky I agree. My issues with the Sorcery Ritual is less nit-picky.

    First Issue:

    If ALL Sorcery really is at the end of the day a normal Risk with Wagers being used to describe supernatural facts then why is there a whole chapter dedicated to it? Why are there individual Rituals with specific mechanics that modify the basic wagers game? Why do you have to spend points to know individual Rituals?

    Second Issue:

    It takes a Season Action to learn a new Ritual. If all I have to do is spend a Style Point to say my Court Scholar knows a given Ritual then what is the purpose of Season Actions? The book does say you can accomplish a Season Action by making it the focus of an adventure. Spending a Style point for your Court Scholar to know a given ritual is hardly "the focus of an adventure."

    So my issues are (1) I treated Sorcery as normal risk and not as a special subsystem as the book present it as and (2) I allowed the accomplishment of what would otherwise require a Season Action with the expenditure of a Style point.

    Jesse
