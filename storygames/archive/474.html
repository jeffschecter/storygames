Hello there, Story-games.com

My name is Eric Bennett, and I have a problem.

I love percentiles. I adore them. My most positive experiences introducing new people to the hobby have been with percentiles. They are easy to play with, transparent in their probabilities, and rolling two dice is [i]just[/i] about the right number to feel right.

I like the way percentile stats look on a sheet. They look...respectable. Odd aesthetic? Maybe, but they have class, dammit!

    This is only a partial answer to your questions, and come right off the top of my head. Caveat lector.

    The good and bad thing about percentiles is their containment--as soon as you invoke the percentile, you know the system has a very defined upper and lower limit. You aren't going to get negative percentile rolls or rolls that exceed 100%. That allows for some quick on the fly estimations (hmm, I think you have about a 50% chance of succeeding...).

    Now, you could structure a system with percentile dice to exceed the 1 to 100, but I suspect you would need to be careful with that. If you break the containment provided by the percentile, you end up with a system with enormous variability (roll, get a number anywhere from 1 to 100, add related modfiers...).

    If you want to go with percentile dice, I would think the resulting system should be relatively fine grained so that there is some difference between 54 and 57, not just between 51 and 61 (otherwise, why not just use d10).

    Rolemaster did some neat things, but, man, I would want to avoid that kind of chart craziness--that seems like it could kill a narrative premise. What if the even/odd status of the result mattered--either the number as a whole ('61') or its components ('6' and '1')? To keep the flavor you would probably want resolution tied to the percentages, but you could color that result based on something like its even-oddness. Allow someone to make a certain kind of narrative move based on the nature of the result? A well-developed 'parasite' like this could even stand in for a card mechanic--the interaction between the two dice invoking/allowing someone to invoke effects that alter the narrative of the game.

    Perhaps my least favorite application of percentage dice--1st Edition D&D thieves skills. For the character progression laid out by the levels system, the percentages always seemed way to low for a majority of their career. Who would try to pick pockets if more than half the time they were caught? Other pecentage mechanics have gone awry with this as well--they retain a large range for character growth by making new characters remarkably ineffective. I would be careful with character generation, watching for this trend (unless the premise is such that character incompetence plays an important role...).

    You don't have to constrain the dice. You can do a "Mastery" thing like HeroQuest. 132% is basically 1 Mastery + 32% chance of something cool in addition, or additional actions in a task-based system, etc. "I'll use my Fighting 150% to take a 100% attack and a second 50% attack." "I have 150% Diplomacy, so let's assume I avoid war, but I want to take a 50% chance of convincing the ambassador that I'm actually the Galactic President."

    You can do opposed rolls pretty easy with percentiles. Highest roll that still succeeds wins. If I have Fighting 85% and you have Fighting 65% and you roll a 64 and I roll an 89, you win (I failed my roll). If I roll 80 and you roll 65, I win (we both succeed but I rolled higher). If I roll 12 and you roll 50, you win (we both succeed but you rolled higher). This makes a 100% roll not a sure thing, since you can still roll crappy.

    My problem with percentile systems is that they tend to get mathy. I've seen a lot of +10, +25, -30 type modifiers. I've seen a lot of "divide by 2 for a challenging action, divide by 3 for a really hard action, divide by 10 for a super hard action." Division is a pain during play.

    Avoid trait + skill systems because it slows down play to have to add 22+91 before you roll. You can do interesting things by giving players additional rolls instead (make a 91% roll, then make a 22% roll).

    Don't forget other things that you can do with the Object Called Dice:
    ------------------
    You can use them in conjunction with other objects. You could stack them on cards for some sort of meaning.
    ------------------

    You could split up a conflict into three tasks: The player rolls percentile, then "divides the result" three times and places those against the tasks of the conflict:
    GET TO THE LAB WITHOUT BEING SEEN
    Requires:
    Sneak 20 to get past the guards
    Sneak 20 to avoid the cameras
    Perception 30 to watch the keycode being entered into the lab pad.

    My Sneak is 10, My Perception is 12. This makes 22. Then I roll percentages, adding it to this total. I get a 34, for a total of 56: I split that up into three sets of percentiles, 20, 30 and 06, and match those up with cards representing tasks:
    Sneak 20: I put 20 down, I sneak past the guards without being seen, but just barely.
    Sneak 20: I put down 6, wihtout my knowing a camera spots me, alerting someone deeper within the compound.
    Perception 30: I put down 30, again just barely making it. It looks like a 9 digit code, I caught the last 7 digits and just happened to guess/infer the first two numbers correctly.
    ----------------------

    You could involve color: Something involving associating attributes or skills to colors, and rolling the dice or picking dice depending on the attributes or skills. An example is, say a game where the ten die can be flipped: Peace is white dice and Violence is red dice. You're trying to get your teacher to let you out of class. For ones you roll a "colorless seven". You also roll a Red dice for 7, and a White die for 1. You can decide to take the one, try to talk your way past the teacher and fail, or jump on the violence die, kick her teeth in and get past her that way... but which will you choose?

    Just a few thoughts.

    -Andy
