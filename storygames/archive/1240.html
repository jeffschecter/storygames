So I got Rob Donoghue's sheet.

I've got that nice yellow square in the middle. I want to slap a pad of PostIt notes right there -- peel off the back sheet, right, and affix it right onto the sheet. Then in the course of play, STUFF can be written on the top sheet, used temporarily, and then pulled off and discarded in favor of a blank slate on which more STUFF can be written.

I just dunno what kind of STUFF to write in there. It needs to be something that is (a) important enough to write down, preferably mechanically significant; (b) totally discarded ten or fifteen times in the course of a game; and (c) never comes back.

Here's some of my initial ideas:
a) roles or masks that the player-characters take on, allowing the PCs to be actors... or spies or con men.
b) setting details in a very serial sort of game, like different islands visited in an island-hopping game.
c) "status effects" like posthypnotic suggestions, genetic modifications, downloaded skills, etc
d) transient goals, oaths, or bets -- "I will kill the monster with one hand behind my back!"

Any other ideas? Any of these sound particularly grabby? Love to hear some other points of view...

    Just added a centering mechanic -- Judson, I'm starting to really like this concept -- and I am all fucking excited about the game, now.

    Last phase of conflict resolution, you go around the table one last time. If you want to squeeze out a few more points, you can "Tap the Globe" and explain how all of your actions in the present conflict are actually forwarding one of the things that you wrote into the Globe. The Globe, if you don't recall, contains your ostensible destination, your apparent purpose, what you believe your purpose is, and what you suspect your true purpose is. So if you play towards one of those, you can add a number of points equal to the unspent rows in that quarter -- 1 to 5. You spend a point from one of those unspent rows to do this, so it's a limited resource.

    Even if you aren't planning on using those points, though, you'll still be roleplaying towards it, just in case one of the other players pulls out the stops somehow and tops your score. It's like a safety net, there if you need it, if you roleplay towards it.

    It will also be pushing the game forward, since the quarters with the most unspent rows of points will be those that you've only recently unlocked. Your ostensible destination will be tapped out first; it will give you no benefit to keep up the pretense, so you can drop it. It will be much more lucrative for you to be working towards what you believe or suspect your true purpose to be.

    So it was me, my wife Laura, and Jesse. I claimed I was going to the Royal Palace; Jesse was going to Geist Mortua, the island of the dead; Laura was going to the throne room of Atlantis. So.

    Hesperides
    I started us off in the Hesperides, to kind of ground the beginning in something solid. We very quickly determined that +10 difficulty would be unplayable, so switched over to +5 per island from the get-go. I'm very glad we did this. As we started putting down the first pages and framing scenes that went nowhere, we also improvized a rule that said you could slap down a page and immediately pass the Captain marker if you didn't see a conflict worth pursuing yet. This was also a good addition. In the Hesperides, we added a Marketplace and some Theives, who ran off with our stuff. When we gave chase (first conflict), Laura and I staked getting our stuff back while Jesse planted a totem on Laura's character. Jessee, see, was already planning. Once we caught the theives, they pleaded with us to let them go or else they'd be executed. I slapped down Law, the fourth page of the island, and narrated us to conflict where I demanded local justice. I got to claim two pages and Jesse and Laura only one each, so I managed to beat them out on that one. Then the theives escaped, anyway, and we proceeded on to the next island.

    Misthaven
    I happened to start off the next island, as well. We did the quick round-robin thing where we just slapped down pages onto the island until it was interesting; Jesse put down a Blood Temple and a Goddess Ritual because he's creepy like that. Also, he was thinking ahead. Oh, and he had directed the theives to meet us here, so he had a little retinue. Jesse tries to get Laura's character to spill her blood into the spring that came out of the catacombs where the temple was located. His stakes were "get the blood in the water" and Laura's were "don't spill the blood in the water consciously." I supported Laura; everyone won their stakes but Laura won narration, taking control of how the pinprick scratch she had picked up got washed in the spring. The next scene jumped to that night, with Lunacy being added to the island and the townspeople swarming the inn going after the ring with which Jesse's character had pricked Laura's earlier. Lunacy was my page to play, and it worked to force Jesse to reveal his agenda: to summon a goddess-demon into Laura's character's body, then proceed to the land of the dead to resurrect his dead wife. Laura and I went for stakes "Leave the island" while Jesse went for "Perform the ritual." Everyone again got their stakes, with Jesse netting narration control. He tricked my character into finding a boat and then tricked Laura's character into the catacombs. He then used his theif minions to tie her down, and the ritual was completed: Laura's character was carrying a goddess-demon's essence. They got to the boat with Laura's character fuming. Jesse takes souvenirs and puts Goddess Ritual on Laura's cornerstone, making what had been her Talents into supernatural abilities.

    Geist Mortua
    By happenstance, I then named the next island. I decided not to pull any punches and sent us directly to Geist Mortua, the land of the dead, where Jesse had claimed to be going to raise his wife. The characters chase each other off or slip away. Jesse's character heads to the graveyard of Geist Mortua, where everyone who has ever died has a headstone. Laura's character makes a connection with the goddess and performs a ritual to incarnate the goddess outside her own body. My character finds Jesse's in the graveyard, steals his precious ring and compels him to help him find the tomb of King Menelaus. About this time a giant column of light explodes out of the forest, and Jesse knows that the goddess is now coming for himfor vengeance. However, my character pries the King's crown off of his dessicated head, puts it on, and strides out to meet the goddess. Now, I was planning on just having some sort of binding or control over the goddess, but Jesse goes and slaps down a page titled Wedding. So I say fine, and go whole hog: my stakes are to marry the goddess and become the King of the Underworld. In roleplay I let on that this had been my plan all along, and thank you Jesse for summoning the goddess and bringing her here as I manipulated you to do. Jesse's stakes are promptly declared as "Get out of here." Laura debates on whether or not to directly oppose me, but decides to support Jesse, thereby pooling their resources. Both stakes are won, and I gain control of narration thanks to the Lunacy +7 page Laura used on my cornerstone on the last island. As the boat's about to leave and Laura's character is naming herself captain, the King of the Underworld steps on board and promotes her to admiral of his fleet. For Souvenirs, I take Sorcerer-King and Fisher-King off of Geist Mortua and I slap them on Laura and Jesse's Logs.

    Edge
    I then get to create the next island, as well, and this is getting ridiculous. I name it Edge, and make it the island on the edge of the world, with the ocean pouring over the edge of the world to either side of it. Laura and Jesse must put the Sorcerer-King and Fisher-King pages onto the island or nothing at all, so they're added in shortly. However, they're going to bail, so Laura immediately sets stakes for leaving the island; meanwhile, I'm drumming up a fleet. There aren't enough pages on the island for divided stakes to both be won, so the island-leavers take the day. I don't get my fleet, and they fly off into the sky to their next destination. Which I, for a change, don't create.

    Kai Durrel
    This time Laura gets to define the island, which turns out to be a not-quite-real easter island sort of thing, blockaded by an unknown fleet. Laura's character convinces the captain of her ship to run the blockade and they skate over a reef and catch a faster current to get past the intercepting ship. She knows -- through her research and her oft-used Maps trait -- that on the island is the last signpost to Atlantis. So she heads uphill to the stone monuments, only to find Jesse's character, all Colonel Kurtzed out, leading the local natives in the battle against the invading marines. They fight off the marines thanks to some divine power channeled by Jesse's character, and then he tries to approach Laura's character to either replace his dead wife or at least accord him some acceptance. She flees, he follows; they find their way to the Keystone which opens the portal to Atlantis. The King of the Underworld is perched on top, waiting. He tells them that it's all come to pass as planned, and all they have to do is step through the portal. Stakes become "Accept that everything has been fore-ordained" or "Believe that everything has been the result of personal choices." I actually lose out against the two of them, and in order to prove his point Jesse's character rams his divine staff of office through his chest, spattering the keystone altar with blood, thereby opening the portal. The fourth quarter is unlocked, so the next island will be Atlantis. I lay down my stakes that Laura and Jesse's characters go through the portal; Laura supports; Jesse bows out, satisfied with martyrdom. Laura and I beat the island (at this point a difficulty of 40, with only five or six pages on the island) and so Laura's character picks up Jesse's and heads through.

    Epilogue
    Laura had the least unspent points, so she went first, narrating how she was the last scion of Atlantis, and was returning to Atlantis from the outside world to be crowned Queen. She resurrects Jesse's character to ask if he would prefer to be dead or to live in Atlantis as her advisor. I went next, and narrated the King of the Dead descending to the Underworld, his new Queen welcoming him home, and him pronouncing that a new age may now begin. Jesse goes last, narrates that he accepts life, hands the Queen of Atlantis his signature pocketwatch, and tells her that if she ever needs him, she can contact him through that, and sets off to wander the world.

    So as far as scope and escalation go, the game performed admirably. We started on a scuffle with theives and we end up with this gigantic plot where there's some conspiracy manipulating the underworld in order to restore Atlantis. We sailed off the edge of the world. We had mythic lineages and terrible purposes. It was fantastic.

    Amusingly, not letting the players write their true natures and purposes on the character sheet immediately made everyone suspicious of each other. Jesse's character Sebastian was creepy from the get-go, but neither Laura's nor my character was at all trustworthy, either. We warily watched each other the entire way to Geist Mortua, and then it was all running and chasing and craziness.

    In fact the three of us came to the game with some very different strategies. Laura knew from the outset that she wanted to be the last descendent of Atlantis, and she managed to hint about that and work towards it the entire game. Jesse came to the game with an initial situation and agenda, which got totally overturned halfway through but provided us good grist. Jesse said in the aftergame that he did not feel at loose ends afterwards thanks to the developing goals laid out in the Globe, which was good (Jesse, care to elaborate?). I played the game as a complete blank slate, playing some guy in a navy uniform and only later developing him into this conspirator willing to take incredible steps to inaugurate a new age. All three approaches seemed to work out, although Laura's was probably the most difficult; in a few places she had to work to maintain her planned identity.

    On the other hand, an emergent property that I totally should have seen coming was that we started transforming each other's cornerstones in ways that were appropriate to the character. Jesse got Twilight and Wedding and Imprisonment; Laura got Bloodline and Goddess Ritual and Ancient Decendents; I got Sorcerer-King and Fisher-King and later Reflection (turning from the Lunatic conspirator to the contemplative version). We either helped each other or challenged each other to take the step further constantly, which was totally awesome.

    In terms of currency and economy, we had a little too many available points towards the end of the game. In the last conflict I was able to tap my fourth quarter for the full five points because I hadn't spent anything out of the last 25.

    We didn't see many pages get slapped onto Logs and then returned to the island with a point value, but Jesse suspects (and I agree) that we were gunning for the end of the game, and trying to unlock quarters by transforming cornerstones. If we had planned to play the game over two or three sessions at a more sedate pace, more elements would be recycled into the islands. (Additionally, we might have spent more points on traits.)

    I was surprised at how slowly the character sheets filled up. After we got about five or six traits at the start of the game, we only rarely bought new stuff. I suppose this is because the baseline of the character had been established and everything else was development, which comes slower.

    I have a whole lot of little procedural fixes to add to the rules (and I'll get an amended version posted as soon as I can), as well, but that's the meat of it. Love to hear your thoughts and suggestions! I've only got a couple more days to polish this one off!

I knew that reason was likely to change along the way but without an initial reason I feared the game would flounder.

    The whole character "transformation" thing was neat and is probably the game's primary reward mechanic. It's an opportunity for me to say, "this is what I like about your character, do more of this." I was thinking more about putting the souvenir on the log pad. Here are the problems I see with it.

    I don't need an element on the island for it to exist in the fiction. Even though the "Theives" sticky wasn't anywhere on Mist Haven I could still interact with them and use them in narration. So the whole idea of "I want to see these guys again" element of that mechanic is kind of shot because I can see them whenever I want.

    However, I think that mechanic might get more play in larger group going at a more leisurely pace. The idea that there is the potential for a mechanical boost gives me incentive to bank situation elements I know I can narrate into conflicts easily. For example, I KNEW I wanted to use the Theives in a sacrifical ritual so it might have been in my interest to keep slapping those guys back on log books so that I can snag them for extra points anytime I was doing anything with the ritual.

    But! There are problems with that as the mechanics stand because I can't control when they come into play and I can't control how long they stay there. Of all the mechanics in the game I think this one could do with the most rethinking. It's a great idea but there needs to be more of a...dare I say it...strategy element to it. I need someway to bank those theives and have SOME say in when they come back into play so that I can draw on them when I want.

    Hmmm.... It might be sufficient to say that Souvenir's go EITHER on another character's corners OR on your own log. That way if I'm working up to something I can bank elements into my log at the cost of not being able to contribute anything to new islands. Then when I'm ready to make my big push for what I'm working up to I can pop everything off my log stack onto an island and go for it. This also incentivizes stepping down and letting others pursue their agendas AND thinking of ways to bend those agendas to your own so that you can bank things from their island contributions.

    Just thoughts.
