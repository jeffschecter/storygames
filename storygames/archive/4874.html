\n At least I think that Sim and immersion are the right terms...

I watched Darkon last night (Hail TiVo! Hail IFC!) and found it fascinating. There were some social/psych aspects that kind of weirded me out, but as far as gaming goes, it made me contemplate the following.

If you're heavily invested in your PC, self-identifying with them and/or really becoming them ("getting deep into character"), how are you ever supposed to gain any enjoyment from, say, that character's death? Or even setbacks in general?

I mean, the "Inheritance" scenario for Burning Wheel in which I played is a total blood opera. Almost everyone dies glorious or ignominious deaths at the end... and they have fun doing it. The deaths are a perfect ending to the story. But, BW is one of those indie/hippie games where even the book asserts that your character is just a piece of paper.

Thing is, I don't see how this can be enjoyable if you're not looking at the game from this sort of macro, story-level perspective. If I'm playing the game from the perspective of people like the Darkon players, how is this going to be any fun for me? If my goal is the "be there" and "be in character," all that story-appropriate death means is that I'm a) no longer there, and b) certainly not in character any more, unless I enjoy lying still on the floor.

It reminds me of a guy I used to game with who would get incredibly emotional whenever bad things would happen to his PC. He'd get angry at NPCs, even though it was, of course, the GM who was thwarting his PC via those NPCs. It just seemed crazy to me (and was a factor in why I left that group).

Or, to get back to Darkon, the scene where Skip and his friend are having a heated discussion at Denny's. The friend decided to have his Darkon PC become an evil lich, and Skip is totally pissed at him for it. The friend is literally saying "It's just a game!" Skip refuses to take that as an answer.

Again, I can see your buddy's PC becoming a lich being a really cool story. But if you're so heavily, personally invested that you see everything through the eyes of your PC, and take this as a betrayal... isn't that sucking the fun out of it all?

    \n No. When viewed solely as continuous gratification fun is vapid. When an intense, even painful event occurs, there's an opportunity for any reasonably mature group to view it as a process that's ultimately gratifying, because it lets you look at the narrative (by which I mean actual narratives, not Narrative) without special expectations and view it as an artifact in its own right, instead of a mechanistic process that creates pleasure. And *that* is enjoyable on a whole different level.

    \n There is one player in my group that bears out your theory, although she doesn't particularly immerse. It's just a matter of taste, it has nothing to do with maturity. Her preferences for what to do on a Saturday afternoon go, from worst to best:

    * I game, and my character suffers significant setbacks and difficulties.
    * I watch TV
    * I game, and my character doesn't suffer significant setbacks or difficulties.

    It made me tear my hair out (and I even said that 'maturity' line once or twice), until it dawned on me that I was being stupid. We don't have a competitive group. Others in the group are not diminished if her character doesn't suffer major setbacks. I as GM am fairly ambivalent to PC emotional states. As game organizer, I am interested in how players feel. So why not just give the player what they want? She'll have fun, we'll all have fun. If her tastes change up the road, they'll change. But for right now, why make her miserable if it won't make others more happy?

    \n There's a difference between immersive playing and wish fulfillment. You can enjoy being immersed in character and experiencing strong emotions, even bad ones. But when your character is a vessel through which you pretend to be stronger, smarter, more handsome/beautiful, and all that, then you don't want anything bad to happen to your character.

    Some people like to watch scary movies. How could anyone possibly enjoy that? It makes one afraid, which is a negative emotion, and the characters we care about get slaughtered in evil ways. But it's also cathartic, and it's sometimes a good experience to have these negative feelings.

    However, if you go into the movie expecting a wish fulfillment fantasy (like, say, a Steven Seagal movie, or (disappointingly) Live Free or Die Hard), then it would piss you off if your favorite character gets shamed, defeated, killed, etc.

    That's one aspect. Of course there's the issue of how you continue playing if your playing piece is removed, but that's an issue not just for immersive players.

    \n I've got exactly this issue playing out in my local group. The game that triggered my massive split was, ta-da!, Burning Wheel.

    The game pushes almost every correct button for me, and I'm the only GM in a rotating group of 6-10 players. Several design elements and play philosophies come together in that game (and BE, to a much stronger extent) to produce what I feel is a near-optimal RPG experience.

    Half my players agree, and we're enjoying the best gaming in 25 years.

    Half my players disagree so strongly that they have withdrawn from the group and refuse to even discuss roleplaying with us 'til we drop Burning-anything and get on with something, anything, else.

    The following is strictly my own theory and is probably both inaccurate and incomplete.

    The hater-half just isn't wired to be able to see the story side of the game. They want immersion and achieve it easily; so easily, in fact, that they experience a lot of anguish when I put their characters through the wringer. Unfortunately, since it's me, the GM, inflicting the anguish on the players/characters, they take it personally. Why are you punishing meeeee?

    Meanwhile, the story-focused half is climbing over one another to get another piece of that delicious high-drama spotlight time they feel I'm squandering on the half that wants a string of tactically interesting and visually intense combat scenes.

    It's a real problem, because this is the first time I've ever had a situation where players' mutually incompatible needs were so clearly highlighted. Once I wrap up my current Blossoms are Falling game, we're gonna need to take a little break and play something else.
