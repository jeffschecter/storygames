    \n Because there is an apostrophe in the middle of the word 'well' for no reason? Because the majority of the activities listed are stereotypically female-gendered? Because it suggests that people come to GenCon with no interest in gaming whatsoever, which makes no goddamn sense, significant other or not? Because it sounds vaguely condescending in a way you can't quite put your finger on?

    I think it's a good idea to have some non-game events. Why they have to be framed in terms of gamer widows is not clear to me -- as though someone who was there mostly for the gaming wouldn't also maybe be interested in participating in some non-gaming workshops? But no, yoga is only for those who do not like gaming!

    Because you're also not able to attend GenCon, and feel the need to channel your bitter envy into pointless, pedantic spite?

    \nI dunno about that, whoever they are probably sat down and said "what non-gamer things can we actually get some people to put on, that have some vague popularity with non-gamer people?"

    It's the part after that, where they wrote all this promo text, where things seem to have drifted off the rails. I mean, like I said, a yoga class or whatever at GenCon seems like a fine idea -- a few hours of physical exercise in the middle of 4 days of mostly-sedentary 'activities' probably does wonders for humanity. What makes someone think this is best pitched to imaginary 'gamer widows,' instead of just 'people who don't want 100% gaming' is less clear.

    Whatever happened to that dance class idea?
