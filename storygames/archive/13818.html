\n So I was MCing Apocalypse World, and Matt was playing an Angel, and it was like we had two entirely different visions of what an Angel is and how they work. He was pretty clearly imagining this hardass combat medic who pulls metal out of people, and I was thinking of Angels as post-apocalyptic doctors kind of like Doc Cottle in BSG.

So I kept having this parade of people show up like, "So I got this RASH on my ASS" and Matt is like wtf.

It is, in retrospect, kind of hilarious and embarassing. Way to make his life interesting, Bret.

    \n Thats why I found that specific way of presenting the premise so useful. "a team of superheroes who are basically super security for world class banks" is wide open to interpretation, as you have found out.

    If you followed the what we try to achieve and how we try to achieve it and/or what is our major obstacle then it really informs. Those last two phrases are one in the same thing really, just a different way of stating the same idea. "How we try to achieve something" is equivalent to "we will try to achieve it by overcoming this major obstacle."

    The fact that you work for a bank is more like situation - doesnt really come into that premise in the above format at all.

    So one premise might be "Try to make the world the world safe by battling villainous super heroes and supernatural beings" -- justice league

    while the other premise might be "Try to hunt out the truth despite the conspiracies of immoral corporations" -- watchmen

    \n Thanks for trying to help, Steve, and you're right on, but I'm already kind of there. I'm trying not to derail this thread talking about my game, but the breakdown was really a matter of communication, not of lack of premise. Basically, a couple of the players and I had talked about the game enough that we had already communicated some of the game's premise, so they built the group premise inside of it. Walton and I hadn't had the same communication, and have different comic-reading backgrounds, so that didn't make it across the table to him. The mentions I've given of the premise so far have been brief and incomplete, the full premise would be something like "You are The Agency, superpowered security consultants who protect the strange and extravagent from superpowered theft. The game is about the supervillain plots that you stop, to defend your clients, and the repercussions your actions have."

    What I really figured out as a result was how to communicate what the game is better, and to make that communication a clear part of the setup process.

    I completely agree with those being very different premises. In fact, only the Justice League premise is really supported by the game, Powers For Good doesn't try to emulate large parts of Watchmen. If you look at Watchmen as just being an adventure about finding who's killing masks, I guess it could work, but the deconstruction of superheroes and Cold War mindset isn't this game. This game actually started with reading a lot of comics and narrowing down the type of stories it does.
