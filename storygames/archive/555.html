D&D... it's SciFi!

Eric Provost
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

I was in my hotel in Atlanta this morning, shaving in front of the mirror and pondering over D&D. I was thinking about how I'm often dissapointed with the magic of the system & setting, just because it's so pervasive. I was thinking about how the magic looses it's luster when everyone and their brother has a +1 short sword, and you're not possibly cool 'till you hit +3 or better.

Then it hit me...

In the 3.0 and 3.5 versions of D&D, anyone with a better than average intelligence and a few years of study can manufacture magic items. Staying strictly by the book, with very few exceptions, the manufacture and use of magic items is reliable and predictible. It's effects are repeatable, testable, and measurable. Damn! That's not magic, that's science!

One could argue that the Divine Magic of D&D remains magical and not technological, because it's defined as originating from a diety. But that just doesn't hold true for any of the wizardry bits. I suppose you could argue that, in the Forgotten Realms setting, it was Mystra who supports the Weave for all those wizard-guys, and therefore it's supernatural, but that line of logic would also support the claim that gasoline makes cars go fast because the god of fast-things put the oil in the earth for us to refine.

Oh yeah! D&D Magic = Science, baby!

-Eric

Now, you may read this as meaning that the commoners have plenty of Dire Chickens to combat, thus raising their ability to do um... common things (that's what commoners do, right?). Therefore, I assume that these commoners are coming to their levels by means of overcoming challenges other than the gizzard-eating variety. Furthermore, I posit that the larger the community, the greater the challenges a Commoner has to overcome.

    So, with the idea firmly in hand that XP for NPCs must totally be available by means other than mass murder, let's take a look at the XP requirement to achieve 10th level. That's 45,000 XP! Plenty enough to spare. I mean, WTF is a Commoner gonna use them for anyway??

That's good enough to cast 1st level spells + Cantrips.

    So, what keeps a Commoner from being a wizard? A little luck, some cash, and lots of devotion. Doesn't sound any different from trying to become a scientist to me. Just because 91% of the population is content to live in a shack and farm dirt doesn't make the sciense any less of a science. Demographics have nothing to do with it.

Maybe even as good as a 10th level wizard! Oh yeah, there's totally already a market here in Small Cityville for some low-level wizards. Hell, there's gotta be a guild, right? So there's his stack-o challenges to bring him up to 5th level (2 COM + 3 WIZ). Cuz guild-rivalries totally produce challenges. Like trying to get the dirt on the other guild to make sure that your guild gets the new Detect Poision contracts for that merchant caravan. Usual stuff for the world, but totally challenging.

    Right, so now we've got our dude set up to manufacture potions, right? He's gonna make bank off of manufacturing Detect Poision potions. You know how it goes, you're an important magistrate, you're eating with some people who may want you dead, if you're feeling ill after dinner it'd be nice to have a potion to determine if you're poisioned, or just got a bit of gas. It doesn't matter what level Detect Poision is cast at, so the DeVry grad can totally cast it at 1st level. A 0-level spell, cast at 1st level, costs 1 XP (pg 80, PHB 3.0). So, if he overcame just one 1st level challenge every year, even as part of a 4-person 5-th level party... he'd have 300 XP to play with. That's a potion a day, taking two months vacation off to travel somewhere warm in the winter (which he'll be able to afford to do, with all that extra GP you'll have floating around). Heck! If he takes up some dangerous rock climbing during his two-month vacation, BANG! There's his 300-XP inspiration for another year's worth of cranking out Cantrip potions. Dang, he rocks.

    So, not only is the amount of mass-production a moot point (it doesn't matter how many rockets you can make in a year, it's not magic), but also a non-issue. Anyone in the D&D world with the time, devotion, luck, and half-a-brain necessary to become a wizard can mass-produce items of 'magic' which are reliable, repeatable, and have effects that are measurable.

      OK, dig, here's what I think one should do. This is material for Absinthe, but it's abstract, so it's probably applicable here.

      I like this definition: Magic is the process of using social rules on natural phenomena in order to have an acausal effect on a phenomenon.

      Piece by piece:

      Magic is a process. In that sense, it's gamable. Anyway, the point is, it's something you do.

      Social rules are rules that use things like words, gestures, bargains, names, and other symbols, as opposed to "I hit it and eat it" or 9.8m/sec/sec.

      Acausality is to differentiate it from our naturalistic viewpoint of the billiard ball universe. We've all felt this: you wish someone ill, (and maybe feel a little bad about it) and then something bad happens.       The systematic nature of magic as well as the mysteriousness of magic can both vary a lot, depending on what kind of magic you need in your game. I like Joshua's suggestion for some kinds of magic, specifically the more shamanic, demonic, or religious kind: you've essentially got someone playing the role of the spirits you are trying to propitiate.

      I've had a couple different magic systems, each for a different feel. Sometimes, I just want magic to be like any other action except for its description, so I just use a "magic" skill, maybe with a rule that it lets you do anything you could normally do, but violate one restriction, like "persuade someone, but without speaking" or "open that door, without touching it". Or, alternately, magic is a bonus effect to ordinary skills, if you roll an extra target number, or have unusual manifestations occur whenever specific numbers are rolled on the dice.

      Sometimes, I want magic to be very subtle, sort of like "realistic" medieval magic instead of zaps and pows. So, I define a small number of bonuses or simple effects (heal, harm, change weather in next few days) you can potentially get from magic and have a requirement that magicians need to perform some kind of physical act that is symbolically linked to how they are applying the bonus.

      Sometimes, I want magic that's more like super powers, maybe just a few kinds, like Glamour, Turn Ethereal, and Shapeshift. Everything a magician can do has to be done with that small list of powers.

      Sometimes, I want a more mechanistic kind of magic, one with simple lists of components that you combine to create a wide variety of effects. In this case, I may link specific material elements, gestures, or behaviors to specific modifications of the base spell. This is a pretty standard Chinese Menu approach that you see in things like Ars Magica or GURPS Improvised Magic.
