From the d20 side, the parameters:\n
    \n
  • Characters are defined and conflicts played out in a format that's recognizeably d20 (a cozy blanket for D&D players)

    • \n

    \n

    • \n
  • A "woah hold up" veto mechanic for things that squick.

    the basic mechanics

    how to read the die

    Reading the die is simple. In order to save space, a hobby abbreviation is used to refer to the twenty-sided die from here on out, classifying the die by referring to the number of sides on said die preceded by a lowercase \u201cd\u201d \u2013 thus a d20.

    When you are asked to roll a d20 and read the result in these rules, you will merely roll said die and read the numerical value that is showing on the side of the die facing up. For example, if you roll a d20 and the number three is facing up, then the result of the die roll in question is three. Simple, no?

    how to roll the die

    When a player wants their character to perform a certain action under duress (be it striking a character in combat, deciphering an ancient scroll, or trying to shrug off the effects of a bad cold), they will need to roll a d20 as outlined above and add some numbers to the die roll result that they generate.

    The numbers that a player adds to the result of such a die roll are their character\u2019s level and any die roll bonuses granted by their character\u2019s relevant traits. Broad traits grant a +1 bonus to die roll results, while narrow traits grant a +3 bonus to die roll results. All such bonuses are cumulative.

    A player always wants to roll as high as they possibly can. Specifically, the player will need to generate a final die roll result that equals or exceeds a difficulty class (or \u201cDC\u201d for short) assigned by the GM to the action that the player\u2019s character is attempting to perform.

    If the player generates a die roll result that meets or exceeds the DC of the action whose outcome is in question, then their character successfully performs the action, with the GM narrating the outcome accordingly. If the player fails to generate such a result, then the outcome of the action hinder the player instead, with the GM describing the exact nature of the hindrance.

    Finally, die roll results of natural (i.e., unmodified) 20 always indicate an automatically successful action attempt, while die roll results of natural one always indicate an automatically disastrous action attempt. Naturally, you want to roll more results of 20 than you do results of one during actual game play.

    when to roll the die

    A player only needs to roll the die when their character is trying to perform either an action under duress (i.e., when a character, monster, or force is bearing down on them) or when what they are attempting to do falls outside of their areas of expertise (e.g., a Saxon warrior attempting to decipher a Latin scroll or a 15th Century warrior trying to drive a 25th Century Battle Wagon).

    If a player\u2019s character is not under duress and the action they are attempting to perform is specifically related to an area of expertise that they have ranks in, then it is automatically successful (treat the situation as if the player had generated a die roll result of natural 20, as discussed previously).

    combat

    The term \u201ccombat\u201d in this system could refer to a sword duel, gun battle, or even a heated public debate \u2013 so long as there are two or more parties attempting to achieve a clear victory over one another, a given situation qualifies as \u201ccombat\u201d.

    Combat is, for the most part, simply a series of actions, resolved using the basic rules discussed previously. Having said that, these actions must be structured in order to alleviate any confusion during play.

    the combat turn

    During a given combat, time is divided into combat turns. During a combat turn, a character may attempt to perform one action (e.g., strike their opponent with a melee weapon, cast a magic spell, deliver an impassioned plea for mercy, etc).

    After all characters participating in a given combat have performed their allotted action in a given combat turn, that turn is over and a new combat turn begins.

    the order of action

    During a combat turn, the players of all participating characters must roll a d20 and add their character\u2019s level and any bonuses granted by related traits to the die roll result. Characters act in descending order of the die roll results generated on their behalf, from highest to lowest. Any ties should be re-rolled before proceeding to the resolution of actions being taken.

    Acting order can be determined once at the beginning of a given combat or, alternately, at the beginning of every combat turn. Ultimately, the GM and the other player should decide which of these two methods works best for the game being played.

    striking an opponent

    Striking an opponent with a weapon (e.g., sword, handgun, or offensive spell) in combat is a simple matter of a player stating that they wish to do so, rolling a d20 per the basic rules, and meeting or exceeding the DC assigned to the strike attempt by the GM (in combat the default DC to strike another character is 13).

    ending a combat

    A given combat is over when one side of the conflict, be it an individual or group of individuals, has been defeated or forced to concede victory. So long as two opposing forces are still actively engaged in conflict with one another, combat continues.

    The Caveat

    This is a quick copy and paste of EZ20's core rules. Character creation is also complete. Still working on the rules for the GM (assigning DCs, etc). The final product will be 100% OGC, even the title. If any of this (or subsequent snippets) make into the quilt, I'd prefer that any kind of formal publication (free or otherwise) credit said snippets as being lifted from EZ20 (per the OGL).

    \nFrom EZ20 sidebars. . .

    About Equipment

    Mundane equipment has no mechanical value. Your character will need to be holding a sword in order to swing one, though it is merely a prop. Likewise, in order to pick a lock, your character will need a set of lockpicks handy, though they don\u2019t otherwise impact the action attempt.

    What about classes, feats, etc?

    Classes, feats, races, and skills are simply traits that perform specific functions in the games that utilize them. Under these rules, classes and races become broad traits, while feats and skills become narrow traits. A class or race converted to a broad trait bestows its bonus upon all action that said class or race is well known for in the source material. Similarly, a feat or skill converted to a narrow trait bestows its bonus upon the action that said feat or skill specifically applies to in the source material.

    What about Saving Throws?

    Saving Throws can be easily incorporated into these rules, if you so wish. If a certain spell effect (see Magic for more details) or attack type specifies that the target is allowed to make a Saving Throw, the player of that character simply needs to make a die roll per the basic rules and add the bonuses of any traits which may help them resist the effects of the spell or attack in question. The default DC for such a roll is the die roll result that was generated by the attacking character to resolve the strike.

    \nIf you're interested in making individual task-based resolution into conflict resolution, you'll want to check out my Raising the Stakes .pdf (somewhere like http://ebrys.pbwiki.com, scroll down).

    Once you've got players being the ones always rolling the d20, a player can "raise the stakes" whenever they would roll a task-based action by putting a good and bad condition on the roll. For example "If I hit the goblin, he falls unconscious and his buddies are shocked into hearing us out, if I miss, they swarm me and I've take three attacks on me right away." If that's acceptable to the GM (or in Quilt's case, the table), then you resolve the action normally with the new consequences. The GM / table becomes the filtering mechanism if stakes are too favorable or odds too one-sided... they just decline.

    Anyway, the stuff I've cooked up for this uses Raising the Stakes a lot and guts most of the rest of d20.

    \n Moving right along, there's a table of standard modifiers, including a whopping -5 Untrained penalty. That penalty is used quite frequently, and shedding that penalty is the major function of your Features from class and race.

    Features are essentially feats from d20, but they also encompass magical effects and magic items. All of those fit under the heading of Features, which are a one to two sentence rule snippet plus a prerequisite. A Race and a Class are collections of Features.

    The great thing is that just about everything can be defined in the same language. A big stone wall:

    Level 6 Wall, Fortitude 15, Hitpoints: 54 Features: Made of Stone - Ignores the first 10 damage from any attack.

    \nThat's what I'm talkin' about! It gets right to what matters in the game and dumps the superfluous stuff that only gets used once. Incidentally, I went the other route with Simple20 (I included base stats, though only the actual modifiers ended up getting used in actual play) and, in retrospect, after much feedback, I realize that it was a mistake. I originally went with that option for the same reason that you initially decided to go with two sets of stats (i.e., the familiarity angle).

    What I found was that those people who were already familiar with d20 didn't need the stats -- they knew what was up and the blatant hand-holding put a lot of them off. They liked the point-buy, they simply wanted the point-buy to apply directly to modifers, side-stepping the stats altogether (as True 20 did it). If I did it again (and there are some plans for that waaaaaaay down the road), I'd go this latter route.

    \nPatterns and Power

    Power is the currency of Game Mastery, and everyone starts with 20 at the beginning of the campaign. First, there's a general round-robin chat. Then, going clockwise starting with a randomly determined player, players can pitch their ideas. These ideas make up the Quilt's pattern - they are the rules for how the rest of the game will proceed.

    A player pitches an idea by telling other players about it and seeking support. Before a pitch is accepted the player proposing must spend 2 Power and convince 2 other players to spend 1 Power. If any player objects, the idea is not accepted and everyone keeps their Power, but the turn continues clockwise. If no one objects, the idea is added to the Pattern document.

    Ideas for the Pattern document can go from "We're using this extra page of Features" to "There is an ancient elven empire" to "We aren't going to swear while playing the game. If you swear then you lose 1 Power." Usually, however, entries on the Pattern are major facts about the game world.

    At any time, a player can ask for the game to start. If no one objects then the game begins, otherwise making the Pattern continues.

    While making the Pattern players should create their characters. These are made by agreeing on starting Level and rolling stats. Players should talk about how the characters are related to each other and to any parts of the Pattern. Note that the Pattern trumps these rules - for example, if the Pattern says that there are no Dwarves in the game, there are no dwarves even though there are rules for dwarves in the Features section.

    \nGame Starts

    At the beginning of the game, players reach for a single die. So players roll d4, d6, d8, d12, or d20, or pass. Highest roll frames the scene (Where, Who's There, and Agenda). Lowest roll GMs for the scene. This gives players a chance to say "Hey, I'll GM" or "Hey, I know what should happen next" or "I have no idea, I'm sitting back for a bit."

    The Scene Framer has the power to end the scene if the Agenda has been met, after which the GM may award XP and players roll for a new scene. If everyone passes, go back to Power and Patterns.

    \nHold that thought
    At any time, any player can put the brakes on the game by holding up a card sideways, cardback out. This is used to Object in the Patterns phase, or to indicate something that violates part of the pattern, or that violates some unwritten agreement between the players about the game's content, mechanics, or social contract.

    When this happens, there are a few options: \n
    1. The person who was talking can rephrase / re-pitch / re-frame (The best way to carry this off is in a casual way: someone accidentally crosses a line, sees the sideways card, fixes something, card drops back down, and then play continues).
      2. \n
    2. A new Pattern idea can be proposed and added to the Pattern page to resolve the situation
    \nIn case of an impasse, first, remember it's a game, everybody wants to have fun, and there's probably a Pattern idea somewhere that can direct play so this butting of heads doesn't happen in the future.

    Note: Players should not use this mechanic to bypass the other conflict resolution mechanics in the game - either the effect of task mechanics (such as attacks or critical hits) or to back out of agreed results of Raising the Stakes. However, it can be used to object to how those results are described (i.e. exceptionally gory depictions when the players have agreed not to do a gory game). If there is consensus (excluding the Objecting player) that the player Objecting is doing it on unacceptable grounds, they are overruled. This is a rule to be used only as a last resort.

    \nEnding a Session

    When it's time to wrap up, players can add things that happened in the game to the Pattern document. Like the Pattern and Power procedure, players must pitch an idea, gain help from 2 other players, and face no objections. However, if the idea pitched is directly from the play that just happened, no one needs to pay any Power. If the idea is a possible but not necessary result of play (i.e. a conspiracy involves someone the players just met as a friendly NPC), then Power must be spent normally. This process means that really important facts are included in the game's Pattern, which is important if no log is kept of the game.

    \nThe Power points

    Power points are represented by cards in Gold-backed card protectors. There's no mechanical difference between these cards but they each have a helpful tip for the game printed on them.\n
      Is there something you want to know more about? Why not set a scene about it?
      GM Hard: Dare us to earn the XP
      Ask for scenes where another player can shine
      Keep an eye out for things other players are interested in; you can frame scenes about that.
      Remember, it's OK to use the Hold that thought rule. You can use this card to do it!
      Want to Raise the Stakes but can't think of a downside? Wager some Conviction.
      You can spice up the game as a GM by adding extra conflict.
      You can spice up a game Framing a scene by asking for a dicey scene.
      You can spice up the game as a player by Raising the Stakes.
      When you frame a scene you can assign NPCs to other players.
      If you have a cool idea, spend a Power Point when you set a scene and give Gming power to someone else\u2026 with a secret note about your idea. If they go with it that proves your idea is cool.

    \nXP
    When any Player Character resolves a conflict that involved a real challenge, the GM can award them a point of XP at the end of the scene. Conflicts and challenges can take a lot of different forms, but this XP must be awarded by a GM. This award is subject to the Hold that thought rules, and players should use that rule to encourage exciting challenges and conflicts. The player who earns an XP also gets a Power Point that they must give to someone else besides themselves or the GM.

    If multiple Player Characters were engaged in the conflict then they can each receive a point of XP, but only if that character was legitimately challenged during the conflict. Even so, only one Power Point is awarded (Any player who earned this XP can award it).

    Feedback on this one is more than welcome. Basically I need the XP rules to say "Player Characters can only grow by experiencing conflict and challenge."

    \n I'm considering this rule, to get even more Universalis-style reuse of materials. My worry is that this could make the Power Point currency too lean but it's hard to imagine without playtesting.

    Power Points and the GM

    To introduce a new object to the game, the GM of the scene must spend a Power Point. Objects include monsters, NPCs, obstacles, and other features that have stats. Features in the form of items are also objects. Some objects are "master" objects, such as "goblin" These cost the exact same amount to create and can be reused again and again even if an individual instance of the master object is killed. Thus, a GM can spend 1 Power Point to create a Goblin object - thereafter, he or anyone GMing can create Goblins for free.

    \n Summary of the game's 3 major currencies:

    Conviction is a PC resource, it helps buy extra actions, re-rolls, and special abilities. PCs get a point of Conviction for every scene they're present in.

    Power is a Player resource, it helps buy Pattern and Patches. Players get Power when they GM a scene (also see XP, below).
    * Pattern is setting world parameters, like "There are no dwarves" or "Elves can have beards" or "The Dark Lord is alive and plots his return."
    * Patches are objects or master objects like "Kevris Kilraven, NPC thief" or "Goblin"

    XP is another PC resource. PCs get awarded a point of XP at the end of a scene when the GM thinks they've resolved a conflict where that PC was challenged (conflict including both combat, quest, etc.) When a PC receives a point of XP they also receive a point of Power which they must give to another player (not themselves or the GM). XP can be used to purchase Features or to increase a character's Level.

      \n Absolutely, but here's a shot at the fighter trait:

      Fighter
      Fighters are versatile combatants, familiar with all the weapons of their culture and other weapons of similar construction, as well as typical unarmed combat skills. They are skilled in the mobility and terrain control required of a soldier, including jumping, running, climbing, digging trenches, kicking down doors, and so on. Fighters are natural in social situations involving hierarchy, either the explicit hierarchy of command or the implicit hierarchy of intimidation.

      Grappler
      You get a +3 competence bonus when you try to grab another character in combat.


      Here's the tricky bit: Right now, what you've got are quick, easy rules. The GM (i.e. the table) has broad discretion to interpret "grabbing another character" - there's no "you suffer an Attack of Opportunity on the way in" which would embroil us in the definition of Attack of Opportunity. In this case, it's all up to the GM at the time whether it's reasonable to be able to grapple someone. That means not importing feats directly - just importing what they imply. I'm not sure which is better, but I know I don't want to be 5-foot-stepping across the battle or getting "bogged down" by the combat system.

      \nModifiers

      These rolls are applied to the PC's roll, since all rolls are on the PCs' side. You would figure these out by reading the text of a character's features - for example, if a feature said "Expert crossbowman" then you would use the +5 modifier.

      Skill Modifiers
      Untrained / Inexperienced: -5
      Competent / Trained: +0
      Skilled : +2
      Expert: +5

      Action Modifiers
      Daunting: -10
      Difficult: -5
      Challenging: -2
      Straightforward: +2
      Easy: +5
      Ideal Conditions: +10

      \n This is along the lines of what the classes will look like, although I will have to insert damage notation in some of this, which I regret.
      \n
      \nBarbarians can move swiftly, enter a rage that makes them stronger and harder to kill, and remain standing after dreadful blows.
      \nBards can manipulate the emotions of others through talk and song, inspire their allies and beguile the weak-minded with their music.
      \nClerics can take up arms to defend their allies, heal the injured, terrify undead and remove fear.
      \nDruids use their potent combat skills to champion nature, and move quickly through natural terrain. Along with spells that restore vitality and turn plants into allies, they can converse with animals, and even call upon a wolf or other animal to aid them.
      \nFighters are skilled in combat with heavy armor and weaponry.
      \nMonks are spiritual unarmed combatants that use their focus and discipline to deliver powerful punches and kicks - some of which even leave the enemy dazed and unable to respond.
      \nPaladins are holy warriors, who can detect the presence of evil in their enemies and strike back against it. They are also initiate healers, capable of sending holy light from their hands to heal the wounds of the downtrodden. They are courageous, wearing heavy armor to defend against their foes and wielding mighty weaponry to strike back against them.
      \nRangers are skilled hunters that focus on a single hated foe. They are also woodsmen, able to follow spoor and tracks, and fight with either two weapons or a bow.
      \nThe [rogue] excels at performing acts of larceny. They can detect and disable traps or build traps of their own. In addition they can provide reconnaisance and they can deal additional damage by striking at an opponent's weak spot.
      \nSorcerers have the blood of dragons in their veins, and call upon this legacy to create magical effects. Sheets of flame, protection against the influence of fiends, and a grasp that zaps its recipient are all effects the sorcerer can manifest.
      \nWizards can read and detect magic, create magical light and sound, and move objects at a distance by concentrating. They can strike foes with magic missiles or shield themselves from harm.
