\n What is it? Katana? Gyrojet Rifle? "Power Word: Nuke"?

For me, in games that give liberal freedom over what you can carry around and wield (that is: 1) not strictly historical, and 2) doesn't factor into the system that the weapon is more trouble than its worth), I like 'Anything with a chain on the end'. Take a kukri knife, attach a chain on the end and maybe another kukri on the other end of that knife. Or take a sword, add a chain on the end and attach it to your arm (which I did in a D&D campaign long ago, even before God of War).

Others:
3-section staff

No knives, always kukris


Fists and feet, in games where cinematic kung fu Wins. Anything from drunken boxing to wing-chun on crack.

When it comes to pistols, rifles, or SF weapons I really don't have a preference, though. Depends on the setting a lot, I'd guess.

Anyone else? Axes? Particular swords? Rifles? Crazy stuff that you've only seen in RPGs?

    \n In general I like things that are actually close to historical or real, though giving them a fantastic edge is always cool. Things that are too fantastic tend to less cool to me just because they feel like they're trying to hard.

    I love kukris and jambiyas so much that I bought like 5 of them while I was in India. Had I enough money I totally would have loved to pick up a Shamshier, which I've always loved. I mean, sure, maces and axes are more effective, but I love a sword that only has to whisper of mastery because it is too elegant to shout.

    However, my favorite hand-to-hand weapon is probably the Urumi which I would only ever have the guts to actually use in an RPG. All hail the whip sword!

    Pistol wise I'm currently loving to go all over the top and hit the Smith and Wesson Model 500, with the Performance Center options, of course. Gotta love a pistol that weighs more than some rifles, for all you Feng Shui "this is my boom stick" needs.

    In Sci-Fi weaponry I'll take anything that uses magnetic fusion plasma. Because really, shooting someone in the face with something heated to the temperature of the sun is just the coolest thing ever. Screw lasers, I want the plasma.

    \n Speaking of khopeshes: In my first AD&D 2nd Ed campaign way back in middle-school days, our DM had a recurring villain -- a short man in a mask, dressed all in black, armed with a khopesh. He kicked our asses, over and over. *shudder* When we finally beat him and took off the mask, it turned out he was a she! To a group of twelve-year-old boys, it came as a shocking revelation.

    My favorite weapon from a fantasy game was a pretty unlikely feat of weapon-smithing. I called it the valspyr. It consisted of a three-foot staff with a two curved short swords that interlocked into the ends of the staff. Both short swords could be removed, so my character could have the following combinations with one weapon: a pair of short swords, for close quarters fighting; just the short staff, when I didn't want to leave gaping wounds in my foes; a single-bladed halberd, which was great for holding people at bay; or a double-bladed halberd, which turned me into a swirling, twin-edged engine of death. For frame of reference, the other combat-heavy characters in that game were an one-handed elf with an axe and a guy with a dagger that generates light (which he would use to blind foes at the start of combat).

    \n Even if Katanas weren't overdone, there'd be the fact that John Clement and the ARMA have largely debunked them, and pointed out that a European Damascus Steel Longsword can defeat a katana any day of the week (and that's without Western armor - with such it's no contest).
    \n
    \nYeah, I know you weren't serious. I'm that sort of geek.
    \n
    \n
    \nMy faves? You want SMG love? Then I give you the American 180: http://world.guns.ru/smg/smg62-e.htm
    \nLearned about this one from the game Aftermath! where the authors go on about having seen a film of the weapon used to cut a Volkswagon in half with sustained fire (I can neither confirm, nor deny...).
    \n
    \nIf you just want the maximally silly volume of fire power available on the small scale: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_Storm
    \nKinda makes the "Sci-fi" weapons that we sometimes see seem kinda low-tech by comparison, no? Not that it's all that high-tech. Just that you can do a lot with the tech we have right now. More than most people imagine.
    \n
    \nIf you're not allowed to use nukes, but still have to blow it up real good: http://www.globalsecurity.org/military/systems/munitions/moab.htm
    \n
    \n
    \nFor a sci-fi weapon, just from pure nostalgia, and personal firepower, I love the FGMP-15 (Fusion Gun, Man Portable, Tech Level 15). For when you absolutely, positively have to be sure that there's nobody left threatening you from that city block over there, there's nothing like a weapon that burns with the heat of the sun in a three meter wide cone blast area. Just how the firer survives, we're not sure. But anything that does 6D6 x 20 points of damage in GURPS terms is all right by me.
    \n
    \n
    \nFor a hand-to-hand weapon, the longsword, as I say above. Why? Because it's really the ultimate development of the HTH weapon. It's an axe, but the edge goes all the way from where you hold it to the end (and it won't turn in your hand as often as an axe will). It's a spear if you need it to be. Used half-swording, it's a war-pick capable of puncturing or prying open heavy armor. And it can leave big bloody rents in people.
    \n
    \nWhy aren't all historical armies armed with swords? They're expensive. Put another way, where cost is not a factor, they are, in fact, all armed with swords.
    \n
    \nPeople think that guns should do more damage than swords in RPGs sometimes... just think about it for a minute. What makes guns dangerous is that they're easy to use because you don't have to get too close to the person you're attacking, and they're "point and click." But they make pretty small holes. Swords, OTOH... there's really no comparison.
    \n
    \nThe fact is that weapon damage is really not so important to how fights end. Hence why guns are so effective in real life (most people give up after the first injury). But if you really have to hurt somebody as badly as humanly possible, well, that's what swords were developed for.
    \n
    \n
    \nMy absolute favorite weapon system, however, is one that I love due to having been one of the few people in the world to learn to operate it. The M109A6 Self-Propelled Howitzer: http://www.fas.org/man/dod-101/sys/land/m109a6.htm
    \nWorked with M109's for 15 years in the National Guard, culminating with the Paladin. Field Artillery, King of Battle!
    \n
    \nPeople have asked me why they don't see this sort of weapon system operating in Iraq (they do, actually, on a very limited basis). To which I always respond, "Because the US goal in Iraq is not to make it into the world's largest parking lot." Using FA when there are civilians around burns up political capital faster than just about anything else.
    \n

      \n So I'm mildly obsessed with eastern Mosesia, the Roman designation for what is now northeastern Bulgaria (and encompassing the little blob of Romania south of the Danube that includes Constanta, which was called Tomis at the time.)

      As far as I'm concerned, using this region, in about 450 AD, is the ideal semi-historical fantasy game setting, for lots of reasons.

      One of those reasons is that the Dacians (north of the Danube in what is now Romania) used a particularly interesting weapon - the Falx.

      http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falx

      It's like a naginata, but with a heavier, forward-curved blade. Apparently it was particularly effective at pulling shields out of position when used in groups. And the point was apparently effective in piercing helmets, which is pretty nasty and badass.

      From what I've read, the effectiveness of the falx was one of the main reasons the Romans had extremely limited success in expanding north of the Danube.

      Also, the people of the region tended to wear the Phrygian Cap, which is a whole other kind of awesome. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrygian_cap
