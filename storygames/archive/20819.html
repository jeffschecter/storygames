    First, I don't completely understand the question. What's "hard" scene framing? How do "hard", "harder" and "hardest" differ from each other?
    \nThe "hardness" of scene framing refers to how sharp, immediate and unnegotiable the dramatic demands of the scene are upon the protagonist character set in the scene. A scene is framed softly when it is established in gentle dialogue with the character player and allowed to develop organically towards the actual meat of the scene. A hard frame is one that moves directly, quickly to the core issue, and does not include negotiated niceties.

    For example, let's say that the GM or whoever is framing the scene gets the idea that it would be cool to have your character accosted by a crazy homeless person claiming to be their father, and maybe the character remembers just now that this is, indeed, the case. (We're playing a psychological horror game of some sort here, I guess.)

    A soft frame would be something like "What do you do with your days normally? OK, let's say that you're on your way to the mall. The day is sunny, birds are singing. What stores will you visit? Cool, on the way to Gamespot you see this homeless guy sitting at the curb..."

    A hard frame would skip the preliminaries and take liberties with placing the character into a relevant situation: "One day, while shopping, you are accosted by a homeless guy..."

    (The very epitome of a soft frame would be when the GM doesn't even have an idea in mind for the scene - they just start talking about it with the other players, and perhaps some sort of a point emerges as they do. This kind of framing is all but non-existent in dramatic terms.)

    The different degrees of hardness would refer to the amount of pressure put upon the character by a hard scene frame. A "hardest" scene frame would presumably be one that leaves the player no room for maneuver: the character is in a fixed situation, forced to make a single choice, and all that's left is to tell how they feel about it and what they will do. In the context of the above example the hard frame could be made even harder by having the GM drop the entire dramatic idea of the scene in monologue before allowing the character player a word edge-wise; only after they've introduced the NPC fully and established that the character is fully committed to facing an awkward social situation in a public setting is the player allowed to decide how they will react; all opportunity for prevaricating is taken away by the framing.\n

    \nScene framing is democratized in games that give everybody a voice in how the scenes are framed, of course. Specifically, I think that for a process to be truly democratic it needs to somehow take into account multiple voices; taking turns to set a scene is not necessarily democratic, it's just dictatorship that rotates

    I do not remember a simply democratic game out of hand, but I suppose there might be some somewhere. By "simply" democratic I mean something where the players outright vote on the next scene to be played - that would presumably be the epitome of direct democracy as regards scene framing

    Scene framing is negotiated when multiple players have a real say in which scene will be played next, and what it will be like - we might well consider this a subset of democratic framing. The players literally negotiate about the direction of the story. Ars Magica features highly negotiated scene framing due to its creative agenda leanings, for example. Other games that give explicit doctrinal nods to negotiated framing are Primetime Adventures and The Mountain Witch. It is notable, though, that in all of these cases the negotiation aspects are more or less informal advice, hardly enforced by systematic means. (This is important to me personally because I like all of these games while not being much of a scene negotiator myself - I play the last two particularly with rather hard, dictatorial framing.)

    In general I don't think that Vincent necessarily has much basis for being tired with democratization, considering how relatively rare it is. Then again, I've no idea what he's been playing through the last few years, so maybe this is a common phenomenon in his neck of the woods. Many games are certainly easy to drift towards either more negotiation or less, so I can easily imagine how "storyboarding" (a type of democratic hard scene framing, sort of) and such can occur for some people in games where they don't for some other people.\n

    \nWhat is naturalistic? How are unproceduralized+naturalistic and dictated by procedure/fiat mutually exclusive with democratized? Can't I have procedural rules for scene framing and still do round robin? Or procedures for handling negotiation?
    \nScene framing is naturalistic when we pretend that there is no artistic decision-making in what we discuss next in the game; basically, whatever scene occurs next is whatever of significance would happen next in the fictive world, according to the players at hand. Nobody gets to make bold assumptions or focus the story with framing choices, and bringing it up at the table is an effective veto if somebody does. This is how games with "no scene framing" work - everybody gets to demand scenes provided that they fall logically into the chronology.

    Democratization is exclusive with these ideas because both of these methods cause scene framing decisions to be made without hearing explicit artistic or partisan choices that individual players might wish to be made in this regard. In naturalistic framing we constructively pretend (or really believe) that no framing decisions are taking place, and thus it is impossible to be democratic, because we refuse to discuss the decisions that are taking place subtextually. In procedural framing we cannot make democratic choices because there are no choices to be made: the procedure tells us what shall be framed next, taking the choice away.

    Specific to your suggestions, "framing by procedure" is different from "framing by negotiation regulated by procedure". The former is like in My Life with Master: the procedure says that next scene will be about your minion doing the Master's bidding (as per the last scene). The latter is like Primetime Adventures: you get to choose focus and characters, I get to say where and what.

    \nWait a moment... I believe you're getting two things confused. What could be called "round robin" in Fiasco is just whose turn it is to set the scene, not the actual framing of it!

    In Fiasco, procedure dictates which character is central to the next scene, in that the scene is "about" them or has consequences to them. This is part of framing the scene, of course - it tells us, for example, Rafu's character is in the scene - and the procedure we follow here is simply taking turns - we focus on Rafu's character because Rafu is sitting next to Eero, whose character we last focused on.
    Then, in Fiasco, I get a procedural choice, because it's my turn: I can either frame or resolve.
    If I choose to frame the scene, there's no further negotiation involved: it's just my decision, my fiat, informed by what makes sense in the fiction, what "naturally" follows from previous scenes, etc.
    If I choose to resolve, though, I'm outsourcing the job of framing the scene to the other players as a collective. Being more than a single individual, they necessarily have to negotiate it somehow - even when the negotiation is sometimes implicit and actually boils down to a player framing the scene according to their individual idea and no one else stopping them.
    Overall, the whole game can be said to be democratic in that the procedures afford everyone equal opportunities.

    By contrast, it's conceivable there's a game where the actual scene framing procedure is "round robin", in that everybody takes turns adding to the framing. I've once designed such a game, actually, though it's meant to undergo significant revision.

    \n While the stylistic details are a part of how the scene framing is executed, I think that the main issue regarding "hardness" is how obscured the GM's dramatic intention for the scene is. The framing is soft if the GM separates the act of beginning the scene from introducing the scene's raison d'etre, and hard if they bring that core issue front and center.

    As I remarked earlier, the very softest imaginable scene is framed by a GM who does not even have an idea of why that scene exists; they merely set the stage and let the characters do their thing, hoping that it leads somewhere. Such a scene can't be hard-framed, as nobody knows a priori why the scene exists, so everything executed in it is by definition emergent. It's the absolute softness

    The very opposite of that is a scene that goes immediately into the crux. Let's say that the crux is a Bang (this is a narrativist game), the GM's notes have "this hated NPC is left to the PC's mercies - will he pull the trigger?" The very hardest possible frame for this would be something like so:

    You're holding a gun to the head of this NPC that we talked about earlier. Will you shoot?

    This is not hard because it lacks narrative detail so much as because it does not obscure the GM's intent in introducing the scene in any way. We could discuss what the NPC looks like, or other narrative details, but gilding the setpiece would not obscure the abruptness of how the issue is introduced. A softer way to frame the same thing would be something like this:

    You drive to the abandoned warehouse, holding a gun. You hear Him puttering about inside. What do you do?

    That's still pretty hard, but not nearly as much; the dramatic intent, the Bang of it, is still clearly visible to the player, but there is leeway. The scene could turn away from the GM's intended Bang by e.g. the player deciding to leave their gun in the car or whatever. However, we can go even softer while preserving the dramatic intent:

    Next week nothing much happens in your life. However, one day you get a phone call from your informer - they've found Him, his hideout is in this abandoned warehouse near the docs. What will you do?

    Note that the GM still possesses their own idea where this scene is going - he's going to leave the hated NPC on the mercies of the player character. However, the player no longer perceives this intent from the start of the scene - he'll need to decide whether he'll pursue the lead, perhaps take friends to help him, or whatever. Depending on the game and GMing methodology the GM may use various interactive ways to bring his Bang on (maybe the scene morphs and he ends up bringing the NPC to the PC, instead of vice versa), or it may be subtly evaded due to the relatively soft frame. I would not call this frame "hard" anymore, but that doesn't mean that we can't go any softer:

    After the last events are over you return to your life. What will you do next, speaking of next week or so?

    This is even softer because the GM is inviting the player to bring his own input to the scene's direction first. It is still not the absolute softness I introduced above, because the GM still has their agenda of bringing the NPC to the mercies of the PC, but he's willing to work for it, weave the idea into whatever the character player gives him. This is a traditional acme of scene framing, by the way - so soft that a player simply cannot know whether you have an agenda or not.

    As can be seen, all of those scene frames are about as sparse in fictional detail. I do not deny that abrupt narrative style often accompanies hard scene framing, but I think it's not direct causation so much as a psychological feature of why we produce verbose narratives in the first place: colorful narration is usually used to set the tone of a scene, which is then used to provide emotional context for the crux of the scene. When we frame hard, we reveal the crux early, and this simply makes colorful narration less useful - the moment when we would have benefited from setting the tone has already passed, and now we have this provocative issue on the table, so who wants to hear about the birdsong, anyway.

    Incidentally, a little technique that I find myself using often when hard framing: you drop the bomb, whatever it is (in the above example, the reveal that the NPC is now at your mercies, and you have to choose how to deal with it), and then narrate descriptive detail largely afterwards. This has the advantage of lingering in the moment and leaving the player some natural space for musing over their choices in the scene; while the GM describes how the NPC is sweating, or perhaps monologuing about the past wrongs of the NPC for the benefit of the group, the player can immerse in the situation and think about what their PC wants to do about it. So it's not like hard framed scenes necessarily lack in lingering lyrical narration, it's just often beneficial to put it in a somewhat different position in the scene's structure.

    I do not remember a simply democratic game out of hand, but I suppose there might be some somewhere. By "simply" democratic I mean something where the players outright vote on the next scene to be played - that would presumably be the epitome of direct democracy as regards scene framing
    \nI have played ~15 sessions of a game our group calls DD&D, or Distributed Dungeons and Dragons. I forget the precise numbers for the word limits, but the rules go something like this:

    0. Get ready to play D&D 4e. Remember to play fast: avoid being too clever, and when in paralysis just write something meh down and hope it doesn't get voted for.

    1. Everyone writes the next scene on an index card. No conversation. You get an absolute maximum of 16 words.

    2. Starting with the (rotating) start player, read what you wrote. No other words. Go around the table twice.

    3. Each player puts one token of their choice into a cup secretly, indicating which scene they wish to have occur next. Token colors correspond to players, so vote black for Eric, green for Guy, etc.

    4. Start player shakes cup, pulls out one token at random and shows the color. Now reveal all tokens. Chosen color is overruled if 3/5 of the total tokens were a single other color (we played with 5 total people).

    5. Player whose color was chosen becomes GM for the scene and start player rotates. Frame the scene you described and go. Feel free to use or completely ignore all other written words.

    6. If it's a non-combat scene, play it out and goto 1. If it's a combat scene, set up the encounter. (We had techniques to make this rotating improv 4e encounter prep not suck, often they worked). All enemy initiatives must be the same. Whenever it is the enemy's turn, do steps 1-5 but your word limit is 11, chosen player becomes GM only for that turn, and when the encounter is over revert GMness to original GM until the scene ends, usually very quickly or even immediately.

    This worked surprisingly well. We started with a somewhat inferior version for a one-shot, refined it over a couple of sporadic one-shots, and actually played a short campaign with it.

    \n It's a real phenomenon though, that for some roleplayers the "why we are playing" is very much in the soft underbelly of explorative play. I've met plenty of people who strongly dislike the entire idea of scene framing, preferring to pretend that no such thing is occurring, and being most comfortable with scenes that do not have any overt "subject matter". (This is more common for certain types of simulationist and gamist agenda than narrativist interests, obviously.)

    A player with this sort of preference might well feel that a scene framed hard has "already been played" in the sense that the exploratory, implicit discovery of how the events are unfolding has been skipped. Maybe there's one big choice left to be made, but for a player who finds the choice obvious and uncompelling, the real meat would have been in how we got there.

    I personally like to alternate soft and hard framing in many games, mostly because it's a way to distribute opportunities for story direction between players. A soft frame, in addition to its stylistic effects, makes more room for the non-framing players to contribute to story direction. It's not an insignificant advantage, although there are games where such interactivity is unnecessary.

    \n

    \n Very interestin topic! Since I prefer GM-less games, scene framing is essential for these kind of games.

    I would like to involve GM involvement as a parameter:
    1. Authoritative GM: GM frames and dictates all scenes (classic RPG)
    2. Participitative GM: GM frames scenes, mechanics allow players some collaborative involvement (i.e. FATE points for narrative changes)
    3. Shared GM/Player control: a designated GM and players have specific roles within scene framing
    4. Active Moderator: Shared scene framing, designated moderator overrules or re-shapes scenes occasionally
    5. Passive Moderator: Shared scene framing, moderator just makes sure the game structure is followed, assists with problems and intervenes in exceptional situations

    Personally, I favor a variation to the round robin scene framing similar to Elective Action Order: Anyone can be next, still you make sure everyone had a scene before you go again (I give each player a card with a different color on each side. When it is your turn, you flip the card. When all cards are flipped, we start a new "round").

    \n Player preference plays a big part in how people engage with different scene structures. I know people who play like distracted wallflowers in party based games, but are on fire in their own spotlight scenes and vice versa. In my experience these people are often more casual players. I've also noticed that the most hardcore indie folks seem to play actively in most games whether it's round robin scene framing or party based dungeoneering. Based on this I'm suspecting there's a skill factor involved as well.

    When I'm playing a game with individual characters that get spotlight scenes, I use my audience time to plan what to do next when I'm given the spotlight to avoid slow scenes and indecision. Likewise in challenge oriented D&D it's essential to have a swift decision making process for the player group or play grinds to a halt when there's a tough or ill-defined decision to make. Unfortunately I haven't found that much advice on these kinds of soft player skills in game texts. I can think of many reasons for this, perhaps it's because designers feel that casual players ignore them, or that players don't read rulebooks, or that generic advice is easier to write than precise techniques.

    AW was a notable leap to the right direction for clear GMing advice and it's much praised for it, so I wonder if there's room for clearer player advice on these topics as well. Efficient play varies wildly depending on the game's structures so generic advice doesn't cut it. It'd be useful to analyze how different kinds of scene framing techniques and driving forces mesh with player psychology. The least we can do is set the right expectations for the players, though I don't think that's enough.

    \n As far as Apocalypse World goes...

    The scene-framing rule for the MC in Apocalypse World is:

    Give every character good screen time with other characters.
    Bring them onscreen in pairs and triples, in obvious groupings and unlikely ones too. Play with their natural hierarchies and bring them into circumstances where they might have something to say to each other.

    ...Followed by some concrete suggestions.

    So by default, the MC should never let the PCs scatter. Apocalypse World's rules presume that most or all scenes will include at least 2 PCs, in changing combinations as play proceeds.

    Now, this rule isn't absolute, it's subject to the MC's agenda. In particular, it's subject to the MC's judgment about what will make the characters' lives interesting. So when the MC thinks that the characters' lives are more interesting if they're isolated, that's her call to make.
