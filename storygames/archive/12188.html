\n The people who first built the laser had no idea what it was for. As one engineering professor told me "at the time, it was a solution looking for a problem". To my mind, this is the more pure form of invention (and the most gratifying, when someone takes your invention and does something with it you never intended).

I'm curious about similar ideas in games: gaming inventions that sit around without a specific purpose, waiting for someone to make use of them. Along these lines, three questions:

1) Have you ever built a gaming "laser": an idea you didn't know what to do with, but someone else used to good effect?

2) Have you ever used someone else's "laser"?

3) Can you identify any unused "lasers" lurking about in the gaming sphere now?

    \n 2) A friend came up with a mechanic for samurai combat by rolling dice at the start then spending them for attacks and ripostes. It was cute, but it could only handle one-on-one confrontations, and some of the considerations for range and weapons were a bit fiddly. He wasn't sure whether it was better for an RPG, a wargame or some other table-top game. A few years later, the very basic idea became the central mechanic for Rivers and Lakes; now with multiple combatants and stunts.

    \n I have a directory full of lasers. I tend to keep them under wraps, in hope that I'll come up with a CD player that needs one of them. This is probably counter-productive, but occasionally it works out. A few years ago I wrote an entire game, playtested it once, and abandoned it as spectacularly terrible. But recently I realized that I could Lady Blackbird-ize the whole mess to good effect - the situation bones were strong, the system wasn't. If I'd just put it out there it would have been a bad game forever.

    \n My favorite example of this is Robin Laws' GUMSHOE system. Laws designed it to solve the "problem" that investigation-based RPGS could be thrown off the rails by a few bad die rolls, a possibility that any non-idiotic GM should be able to get around without the need for a whole new system. But I'm convinced that Laws' mechanism for the allocation and in-game usage of skill points could be the basis of a whole new paradigm for how GMs and players communicate and build a story together. I wish I were smart enough to find a way to demonstrate this.

    \nYeah! That stuff!

    Also, I think that it has the potential to cause a paradigm shift in how adventures get designed in advance by GMs. Instead of a simple set of tasks, some location descriptions and a bunch of stats for adversaries, the GM's job would be to come up with something more like a complicated flowchart, with different branches for each possible interaction between objects, people etc. in the players' environments and particular point spends. This would be much closer to how the plots of some types of computer games get designed - old Interactive Fiction games like Zork, for example, or point-and-click "adventure games" like Myst and Grim Fandango,

    I have a crude, untested intuition that GMs who relied on prefab adventures might be motivated to engage in a much more imaginative, freeform style of play if the Gumshoe system ever got used in the way.
