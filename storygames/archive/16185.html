Choosing to Fail

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJames_Mullen \n\n\n

\n\nMarch 2012 \n edited March 2012 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n There are numerous games whose advice about setting stakes in conflicts is to make sure both outcomes are interesting: for example, in Primetime Adventures, the player sets a stake of what will happen if they win the conflict, whilst the GM sets one for what will happen if the player loses.
\n
\nBUT... if both potential outcomes are interesting and can advance the story, why do we weight the outcomes towards the character\u2019s success? It\u2019s almost like the text is taking an author or director stance, where what happens to your character is less significant than what happens in the story, but the resolution mechanic is in a pawn or actor stance, where the character\u2019s success means everything to the player. Back to PTA for an example: the character in the spotlight for any episode is automatically more likely to succeed at everything they do, and has more resources to spend to further promote the success of their actions, yet the text emphasises building a collaborative story where success or failure exist only to serve the developing plot.
\n
\nSo, to scratch this itch, which has been bothering me for a while, I\u2019ve come up with this as a basic model for a new system:
\n-\tThe player opens a scene, stating what their character is doing.
\n-\tThe GM (or another PC) can interrupt at any time with a challenge: they present the acting PC with an obstacle which will cause them to lose something.
\n-\tThe acting PC then chooses whether their character prevails or not. If they prevail, they narrate how they overcome the obstacle and carry on; if not, they narrate how they lose whatever the opposition has stated and they gain a Drama point.
\n-\tIf you have any Drama points, you can spend one when you choose to prevail, so not only do you not lose anything, you also gain something as a result.
\n
\nIt\u2019s always up to the target of the challenge what happens: obviously, they can narrate a string of successes if they want, but they\u2019ll never earn the Drama points they need to achieve anything really important. A random element could be reinserted if Drama points were coins and players could choose to toss one to see what happens, rather than choosing their own success or failure.
\n
\nThat\u2019s the rough basis anyway: I could do with some more suggestions of things PCs might lose. So far I\u2019ve got:
\n-\tTime: They can be delayed by a critical amount.
\n-\tCash/Possessions: They can be forced to part with things.
\n-\tConsciousness: They can be knocked out.
\n-\tThe trail: They can come to a dead end.
\n-\tRespect: Their reputation can be damaged.
\n
\nAny feedback appreciated.

    \n So, you've identified something interesting. In games where the rules more or less tell us that we should be looking for interesting outcomes regardless of a character's success or failure, it is still the responsibility of the player to advocate for the success of their character, as it has been since the beginning of the hobby. I've thought about this too! It doesn't always make sense, but it's a hard thing to get around while still having players invested in the stories of the characters.

    Your system doesn't really change this, though. It just takes the chance out of whether a character succeeds or fails leaving it to group negotiation, and pushing the advocacy to 'how much will it cost the character?' The player is still advocating for the character to suffer fewer consequences. I mean, can't we carry your original question on to this system as well? If we're just interested in interesting outcomes, shouldn't how the characters suffer consequences be based on what's interesting?

    That's not to criticize the system! It sounds cool. But I don't think it answers the question you're posing.

    Posted By: James MullenThere are numerous games whose advice about setting stakes in conflicts is to make surebothoutcomes are interesting: for example, inPrimetime Adventures, the player sets a stake of what will happen if they win the conflict, whilst the GM sets one for what will happen if the player loses.
    \nThis is not only very, very, very bad advice, but it's actually against the rules of the game.

    In PTA, the player set the stakes, what his character want to achieve. Nobody decide at this point what will happen if he fail. (or if he win the conflict, apart from the fact that he get what he wanted: the rest in up for grabs).

    It's the narrator (the player or producer who win the narration of the results) that decide what really happen.

    For example, you want to try to sneak undetected into a hotel room. You draw cards against the producer (always) and you lose. The producer AT THIS TIME can say that you are discovered by someone before you can pick the lock, OR that the lock is too difficult for you, OR that you hear someone coming from the elevator and flee, or anything that he want to narrate that is compatible with the scene and with the result of the conflict.

    Posted By: James MullenBUT...if both potential outcomes are interesting and can advance the story, why do we weight the outcomes towards the character\u2019s success?
    \nI don't think this is accurate. In at least a few stake setting games (I don't recall if PTA allows this, but Smallville and Dogs in the Vineyard does - and I think PTA works if you play it this way) you can set stakes where a "winning" result for the play can result in the character losing something they want.

    In a Smallville game, I was a pilot trying to convince another character to come join us in an air raid. The other character just got his ego wounded and was at the bar drinking his sorrows. He didn't want to go. I wanted him to go. As it escalated, I switched tactics and stated that my roll confirmed that he didn't go, that he's stay in the bar and miss a chance at proving his experimental airplane to be a success (which was something I did not want either). I was playing to the PLAYER however. I knew what the player wanted for this character, despite what the character wanted.

    So, by the same token, most stake systems work to drive towards what the players want - which is usually good things for the character, but not always. In fact, giving other players what they want through storytelling and roleplaying is a way where you might avoid the conflict system altogether.

    But systems lean towards favoring the players - if not always then when it's important - so that we have the power to guide the game "when it's important" to us. Otherwise, you run the risk of "missing" when you really REALLY want to make it. Those moments aren't often, and are usually the story moments that a player has been building towards.

    To go back to PTA, the spotlight is more powerful for this reason. The producer does have the power to fight back, as Jason points out, but those spotlight episodes are the explicitly that time where the player can shape the story to fit the vision that's been building up in the episodes prior to that one and/or so they can enjoy the fallout from it for the episodes that follow. The player will probably not get complete say, but they get enough power where they have a good chance to prevent things from going in a direction they do NOT want. Maybe someone has been building to have their cop promoted to Detective. The episode might play out where she doesn't end up getting detective, but she has enough power to pivot to something else she finds interesting (maybe she joins IA instead, or she quits in protest of corruption, or she gets kicked out so she can explore a darker side).

    Why? Because it's the difference between a role-playing game and a story workshop. And about wanting our own character to fail, I can't put it better that Ron Edwards here: Players wanting their PCs to fail?

    That post works as a reply to the opening post of this thread, too. If you can decide by yourself if your character succeed or fail in every single conflict, why play at all?

    \n The problem is not "choosing to fail". Many games have moments where you compare what you would get if you succeed and what it would cost you, and you leave the conflict and choose to fail.

    Dogs in the Vineyard entire conflict and balance is based on this, for example: the GM has to try to get the player to choose to leave the conflict (losing) by their own will to avoid a very costly raise.

    The problem is that the character has to TRY. If the player choose stakes that equal to "hosing his own character" ("If I win the conflict, my character is defeated and robbed") going against the rules about stakes, the conflict rules don't work anymore:

    PTA has the same kind of stakes, but after the drawing of the cards, the conflict is over. There are no raises, back-and-forth or other follow-up mechanic: the conflict is over, you get the stakes or not. But even if you win the stakes, an hostile narrator can still hose you. So in PTA the choice about "what is more important" is not done after a raise, but right at the moment you choose the stakes. It's the most important part of the conflict.

    Posted By: James MullenBUT...if both potential outcomes are interesting and can advance the story, why do we weight the outcomes towards the character\u2019s success?
    The drama has larger potential for emotional content if the players act as advocates for their characters. We do this ourselves, you know, in real life; try to make the best out of our lives.

    So trying to make the best out of the character-life, is one way of stepping into his/her shoes.

    To put the story on top, as the greater aim of the game, makes sense in many ways, but it is always in danger of making our game-play into an intellectual exercise, with lesser engagement. Such games may still be great games, and entertain us. They're just one step removed from the immersive experience that is the hallmark of most great role-playing.

    \n In octaNe you roll for narrative control so you chose what outcome you want.     In Hillfolk, Robin Laws' new game, in a drama scene you can accept the other player's deal and you get a token. Once you've got enough tokens, you can force a deal on someone. Failure is definitely a reward.
