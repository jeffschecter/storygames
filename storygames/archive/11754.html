    \n The reason I stayed with Exalted 1E as long as I did was the fiction. And the little blurbs in the front of the Apocalypse World minisplats are great.

    I will buy a game for great fiction, and will roll my eyes and possibly skip a game if my perception is that it's full of poor fiction. I feel like a jerk for saying so, but I kind of assume most RPG fiction is terrible, sight-unseen. Probably because game writing is filled with people who tried to be novelists, but then decided this was easier. There's a big difference between a good technical writer and a good fiction writer, and it's much easier to find the former than the latter in this genre.

    \n I agree with RichD -- if there's going to be fiction, I prefer microfiction to anything else. For one thing, I'll actually read it, which is not at all true of any RPG story longer than about six sentences. And I like that microfiction has simple, modest goals -- illustrate this setting element, evoke this mood, etc. -- and sets out to accomplish those goals as quickly and seamlessly as possible, without wasting words.

    Honestly, I've never seen the point of longer bits of fiction in a game text. We buy these books so that we can make up our own material (characters and plots and everything), so why the fuck are they wasting pages giving me someone else's story? I don't want it, I don't need it, and even if I did I'm not sure it'd be a good idea to give it to me: I mean, if it's an awesome story with awesome ideas in it, now I can't actually use those awesome ideas without feeling like I'm ripping them off. And if it's a crappy story, why would I want to read it at all? It's nothing but wasted pages.

    \n Nearly all game fiction I've read (within a sourcebook or not) is at the level of fanfic, i.e. embarrassingly bad. It can be useful, though, if it adds flavor to the setting or illustrates some of the mechanics.

    The best game fiction I've ever seen is the "flavor quotes" in the Stunt descriptions in Spirit Of The Century. These are deliberately clich\xe9d purple prose that immediately bring to mind dozens of examples from film & fiction of the stunt being described. Many of these made me literally LOL upon first reading. A few examples:

    Gift Of Tongues: \u201cAncient Martian Frang\u2019Twa dialect? I know a few phrases.\u201d
    Mighty Leap: \u201cHahahaha! It is too late, Jumping Jack! There\u2019s no way to cross! The bridge has collapsed, and I shall escape w\u2013\u201d
    Unsafe At Any Speed: \u201cI think you just put an entire row of street vendors out of business!\u201d
    Martial Arts: \u201cAh yes, he is using the Leaping Toad technique. My Rising Crane should be proof against that!\u201d
    Impossible Detail: \u201cI detected a faint hint of lilac in the wine \u2013 not at all appropriate given the vintage. I suspect it has been tampered with.\u201d

    Second place goes to the stories in "Swashbucklers Of The 7 Skies", which are very evocative.

    \n Well-chosen and evocative epigraphs, as Jens points out, serve that valuable purpose of getting everyone thinking about the same kind of fictional possibilities. They can be novel and fictional, or quotes from other real-life sources - White Wolf had a good run doing that for a while, and Jason did a dead perfect job for Fiasco. You can be at sea when you start considering how Fiasco will play out, and then you read Dignan's sales pitch from Bottle Rocket ("Here are just a few of the key ingredients: dynamite, pole vaulting, laughing gas, choppers - can you see how incredible this is going to be? - hang gliding, come on!") and it all snaps into place pretty neatly from there.

    \n Screw all y'all haters. I love me some decent fiction in RPGs. Micro/mini-fic or short story format, it's all good, so long as it helps flesh out the world and the feel of the game.
    \n
    \nShadowrun was a big one for me. I would sometimes re-read the short story in the core books just for the hell of it. The quotes from fictional characters really helped portray the attitude of the gam (and the discussions from several of the gear books! Those were fucking awesome!) I can still flip out quotes from the various Shadowrun books at random.
    \n
    \nWhite Wolf did a good job with their fiction, too. I especially liked the slanted opinion snippets from members of each Clan about the various other Clans from Vampire.. The ongoing story, told both in fictional blurbs in each chapter, and in the examples of play from the 2nd Ed. Werewolf book were really cool, too. I especially liked the graphic novel section with fiction on one page, and rules on the mirroring page. I even bought a no-rules sourcebook that was all short stories and vignettes from the V:tM world.
    \n
    \nJake did a good job with his little chapter heading blurbs in The Riddle of Steel. They captured the pulp fantasy feel, and gave little peeks at the world of Weyrth.
    \n
    \nNot to toot anyone's horn unduly, but I really enjoyed Johnstone's micro/mini-fic from when we worked on Sexy Deadly together. They gave a respectful nod to the source material, but where they really shone was in illustrating the types of characters you might play, the relationships between them, and the various situations that may occur within your games.

    \n So I loved Tribe 8, hated White Wolf, loved parts of Unknown Armies and was bored by other parts, was alright with Universal Brotherhood for Shadowrun but was annoyed by the fiction in Earthdawn.

    As I look over these cases, its always about the fiction playing a role that is important to the nature of the game.

    In Tribe 8, for example, the fiction presented the world. The fictional world was presented through fictional characters within its reality. It did wonders for unreliable narrator-presented material, tone, function, and structure. If anything I wish they'd given less OOC information.

    In most White Wolf products, in comparison, there is a story at the start of the book that maybe has something to do with what the book is about. Kinda. They're rather hit and miss about actually conveying theme, and even when they do they do it almost accidentally. Some of them are actually written pretty well, but they don't do anything to sell, enforce, or structure the game. They just sort of sit there like my ultra-hot salsa at my wife's Irish family's dinner.

    In Unknown Armies any of the micro-fictions, and the one page example characters in the 2nd ed core book, are fucking brilliant. They convey the game, they convey character, tone, story ideas, pacing, theme and about a million other things. I've run whole campaigns based off the page and a half of rumors in the core book. But the books that have stories that sit at the front of the book White Wolf style? Yea, nothing. It isn't that they're badly written, mostly they're pretty skillfully done (Stolze is the best prose writer in gaming today). But they don't sell the game for me, they don't convince me to play, they don't make me give a shit about the book. Mostly, they get skipped.

    In Universal Brotherhood there was basically a novel that was packaged together with an adventure. I found this tolerable because the novel was really that -- it was a book to be read. It also tied together with the adventure, and gave some tone and background to it. And the cover told you that was what you were getting. I was fine with that. Maybe not the most excited I've ever been, but fine with it. Earthdawn, otoh, ended up with the same problem as White Wolf -- fiction bits floating for no good reason at the start of a book.

    So for me its less if the fiction is good fiction or bad by some abstract aesthetic standard, and more about what it does for me in relation to the game. Games that actually use the fiction effectively and for a purpose, I like.
