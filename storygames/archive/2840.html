\n
\n Assume you're running a game where-in the PCs are exploring a dungeon, wilderness, ruin, etc.
Assume that a map will be drawn by the players (I don't wish to debate whether the players SHOULD be mapping).

How do you make drawing the map fun for the players?

How do you make the act of exploration (apart from specific encounters / challenges faced) enjoyable and rewarding to the players?

How do you avoid slowing down play (e.g. how do you keep mapping a simple enough process to avoid sucking up a lot of game time)?

I'm interested in the ideas people have for what the advantages of a physical artifact, created by the players as a record of the character's journey, are. And how this artifact can be made an interesting and rewarding part of play. I'd love any ideas on how to re-use the map during play in some fashion. I'd prefer that there to be a ongoing pay-off for the process of mapping (e.g. the map gets used again and again, not just as a reference but as some sort of mechanical resource during play).

Any suggestions and thoughts are welcomed.

    \n I consider maps to be "fun" as physical objects, charts of play, and encapsulations/summaries of story - whether as actual geographic maps or as relationship-webs. They're of great help in tieing one arc of play into the next! But it is still definately work to draw them - especially in play...

    As far as easy mapping in play goes, i'll advocate a "non-realistic" method of rendering.

    For geographic scales i've taken to using a blank sheet, and labelling the rough spots where something, like a mountain or town, goes without drawing all of the details or borders or things.

    For something like a dungeon flowcharts are the way to go - each circle is an encounter, connecting lines are connecting passageways. A complicated dungeon is just like a relationship map!

    For tactical maps, it's best to use a pre-ruled battle board with counters for both characters & terrain. I've never done this though, preffering to just do a quick sketch on paper, which gets increasingly messy as we draw each turn's moves over top of the last's...

    \n Depths of Kanthe, my solo/collaborative dungeon crawl game, uses six-siders rolled on a blank sheet of paper to create the map. The location and orientation of the die indicated the location and orientation of the room or tunnel, and the arrangement of the pips tells you whether it's a room or tunnel, and what type of either. Exact scale doesn't matter. It's quick and intuitive.

    I enjoy this, because I enjoy mapping, and this technique gives a lot of detail for little effort. I don't think you can make mapping fun for people who don't like mapping, though.

    \n Give your players a sheet of "parchment" (this being different from parchment in that it's cheap and only looks like the real thing) and some calligraphy pens. Have them put all their campaign notes for puzzles and battles fought right there on the map. make sure the adventure has lots of puzzles that require piecing together clues from different parts of the map.

    Don't make your map too "dense" that there isn't room to draw things out.

    Have lots of "photographs" of things you can show the players, but don't let them have them... require them to sketch out any visual clues they want to record right there on the map.

    make your visual clues obvious, so that they want to sketch down everything they see.

    At the end, you have a rather unique adventure record. Heck, if done right, this would be something I'd definitely want to keep.

(If no one wants to run a game where mapping is important, lo and behold, there will not be any reason for a map.)

    Posted By: FlynnHow do you avoid slowing down play (e.g. how do you keep mapping a simple enough process to avoid sucking up a lot of game time)?
    \nI'd absolutely follow Sempiternity's suggestion: the less realistic the map, the faster it is to deal with and the less likely that the non-mapping players will be bored to fucking tears as the party's map-bitch counts out squares on his graph paper. For indoors maps, go with circles and boxes connected with lines (like the maps from old text adventures), and the wilderness can be handled with vague, sketchy, not-to-scale depictions. It's enough detail to keep the players oriented, which is really all you need.

    \n I only used this once, and it was a long time ago, so I can't honestly remember how well it went. But I do recall having a dungeon where if you mapped it correctly it obviously spelled out a message, which the guy doing the mapping caught before anyone else did, giving him a little bit of nice spotlight time. I suppose you could do the same by having the rooms and corridors mimic the design of the correct amulet that they'll need to pick out of the lot of decoys or what have you. But these are pretty much one-time tricks, not real ways to make mapping fun for people who don't normally enjoy it.
