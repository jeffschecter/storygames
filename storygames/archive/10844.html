"Meaningful?" No.
Addictive? Hell, yeah. It fits a packrat mentality (I play Mouse Hunt and Myth Monger).

There's little in the way of raw "fun" in any of them that I've played. A bit of strategy in the better ones, a bit of resource management in most of them. I think it's mostly about Keepin' Up With The Joneses and showing off the rare catches on your feed.

Hell, some folks think that STAMP or SPORTS CARD collecting is fun. Define fun. :P

Posted By: jenskot[3 Social Success]
I'm very impressed by Zynga.

They are a huge well oiled machine.Check out their job lists. That's a lot of specialized people working together! Edit: And apparently they may be making close to $100 million in revenue this year.

The interview is with their VP of business development which is probably why the focus is more on business.

I know a lot of people who play Farmville. And they love it. They mention how people's children are managing their parent's farms. It's definitely true. My friends 5 year old excels at it. It scratches the same itch that Sims provided for many people. In fact most of my friends who play are the same ones who used to play Sims and stopped gaming since (outside of cell phone games).

I find these quotes interesting:

"I think the days of the indie developer are beyond us."

"We've actually made investments into some innovative games that were incredibly hardcore. If you look at Guild of Heroes, for example, we did roll that out. It was a version of Diablo built in Flash, and it wasn't successful, and we didn't support it any longer."

"The truth of the matter is that we kind of did, and were mindful of it, and we tried to build specifically for that casual, social platform."

That said, indie developer can definitely break ground in these spaces just enough to show the potential of an idea without having to go all the way through production, marketing, sales, maintenance, and customer support. Looking at their job descriptions for game designers, they ask for people who have experience testing ideas in Excel first before going into full development. That's incredibly smart.
\nAnd, sadly, I am not so impressed with how some (Zynga included) play ball, after reading this:
Scamville: The Social Gaming Ecosystem Of Hell
basic argument, for the TL/DR folks: "Game developers who monetize the best (and that\u2019s Zynga) make the most money and can spend the most on advertising. Those that won\u2019t touch this stuff (Slide and others) fall further and further behind. Other game developers have to either get in on the monetization or fall behind as well. Companies like Playdom and Playfish seem to be struggling with their conscience and are constantly shifting their policies on lead gen."

As someone who knows better than to take an "offer," I'm not surprised. Then again, I don't get the l33t c077ect18lz, either.

    \n Great topic! Thanks for spinning this off (and linking the Scamville article).

    I'm not a fan of Zynga's game. They make me seethe with boredom. That said, Zynga\u2019s very clever at what they do. If they're as smart as they seem to be, I'm guessing they'll realize that scambait is a dead-end business model and keep refining their approach into a real sustainable model for success. Even without the lead-generation scams, there look to be some pretty compelling revenue models for casual online gaming. Advertising revenue and real cash for in-game loot both look encouraging.

    But this Zynga interview paints a picture of an industry that's more excited by its prospects for revenue than the games it's making. That's not a bad thing. What they're doing is pretty damn exciting from a business perspective. But it's also a recipe for disaster for the indie developer. Zynga's approach is all about making games more addictive. As a creative path, that leads eventually to a dead end.

    My takeaway from the casual games industry is that when people start waving cash around it's doubly important to focus on your goals and stay true to your conscience. As an indie designer, I think I'd probably be better off creating a little viral Facebook game that breaks some of the rules and makes me a few grand than dreaming of $100 million payouts.

    So study these guys, learn from them, and then use your creative juice to make something completely new of it.

    \n Ralph, I think the problem isn't so much ignorant Americans as it is ignorant kids and, generally, unsavvy net users.

    Good follow-up, John. Looks like Zynga makes enough to take the high road.

    Pay-for-content does just fine in the MOO space, and in these socnet games, so it's clearly worth a look to us (e.g. free system, paid supplements or customizations or modules). The "vicious cycle" of ad-supported games having more customer, making enough to dominate their own advertising... that's kind of scary for the indie, in general. It feels like so many other indie niches overwhelmed by Big Bidness: indie PC games (from three-person operations to full teams), cell phone games (now requiring large teams and iPhone Dev Licenses, etc), and our own board and book game markets (fighting for eyes and shelf space against Hasbro and WW and others--and, no, not all are, of course).

    Just worth pondering a bit: How can a small production crew for any creative endeavor succeed once the big boys swamp the consumer space and marketing channels?

    \n Kids...good point...although I think I read somewhere that FB's main demographic is the 30 somethings.
    \n
    \nAs for the question to ponder...I'll link to my own recent musings on this topic where my answer is essentially "they don't".
    \n
    \nIndie production will grow where mainstream "big boys" are missing an opportunity or have become calcified and out of touch. They will thrive as consumers flock to products that better suit their desires. And then their own success will be their undoing as those with scale move in to do the same thing bigger and cheaper. They will then shrink until the next cycle.
    \n
    \nThose few that survive the indie-bust part of the cycle to seed the next indie-boom I think will be those who employ "throw the spaghetti at the wall to see what sticks" strategies of trying things no mainstreamer would waste time on. Their efforts essentially become "proof of concept" endeavors for larger producers and they'll do best to have a business model that capitalizes on the success they find before they get squeezed out.
    \n
    \nSo I suspect...quick hits and move on, rather than attempting to build an extended tail or subscription type revenue stream. If their business model requires achieving an economy of scale to work, they better be able to do it before someone who already has a machine in place to build scale passes them by.
