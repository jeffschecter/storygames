\n Hello,

I'm a former student of music. I've been incorporating live music to RPG sessions for some time now. As far as I know nobody else does it and it is very experimental (with emphasis on mental). Essentially the concept is: the addition of an improvising musician to the GM+players system. Maybe it sounds incredibly posh, I don't know. But I've been having fun as the musician... I've never been that much interested in individual characters, but rather in atmosphere, for which music is an excellent tool. I have used either a clavichord or a synth keyboard.

Before each game, I and the GM agree upon what kind of appropriate musical style I will prepare, and I use tools provided by that style, to accompany the courses and turns of the story and dialogue, amplifying their emotional impact. In practice this means that whatever people describe verbally, I describe musically. As a spiritual backdrop I have baroque opera with its recitative and aria; I'm told that the genre of musical is not completely unlike it. On-site response has been enthusiastic. Of course, this does not mean that there aren't any problems. There are constraints on what kind of verbal communication I'm able to follow:

1) I can't really play anything to dice-rolling, rule talk or tactical/positioning negotiations. Naturally, this rules traditional adventure RPGs as rather unsuitable.
2) I can't play anything sensible if the characters' lines are short, consecutive, even overlapping bursts. This is especially the problem with ad-hoc player groups where the players' struggle for power over the story can be a detriment to actual storytelling.
3) Off-character talk and violating the IC/OOC border in general are very counter-productive.

I like to impose these limitations to the rest of the group:

1) As a rule of thumb, make sufficiently long and meaningful lines I can take hold of, eg. 1-2 sentences.
2) No off-character talk. We make frequent 5-minute intermissions to catch up and ask and make plans. And I want to cool off my fingers from time to time.
3) Don't talk over each other! I had success with playing a sign, eg. a clear V-I cadence, once a player has finished, to signal that another player (or the GM) can take the scene.

Other problems, inexhaustively:

1) An atmosphere game works best from my point of view, however, according to my GM of trust, it can be very straightforward and not entirely gratifying for him.
2) Not everybody knows a willing musician...

So far we have played ia.: 17th century horror with baroque music; Metro 2033-style apocalypse with doom ambient; space exploration with sci-fi film music pastiches... We have used the Flow system, which has served passably (perhaps just because there are no dice). However, a helpful soul very recently pointed out that there are potential benefits of creating mechanical rules for using music in a game, and that the 'story games movement' could be in the power to provide a good model or platform. For example, players could form a first-in first-out queue to take turns in storytelling and I could better anticipate what to play. I should look for a system that is more drama- and aesthetic-oriented than Flow, he said.

I'm not at all familiar with story games, which is why I'm here now. If anybody else has any similar experiences, I'm all ears... but I'm equally happy to be the one and only.

My question is in the subject line. Hopefully I didn't forget anything important. Responses are welcome.

    \nRibbon Drive was also my first thought for music+roleplaying.
    But the game I know which could make best use of a live musician is Sea Dracula!

    However, going back to Ribbon Drive, a game which I'm pretty well-acquainted with\u2026 I think the main take-away lesson in it is that you can use background music as a suggestion of a mood, one extra layer of input, but like any other suggestion/input in play there is no fault in ignoring it from time to time.

    Now, I'm of course extrapolating from what you wrote, but it looks to me that you're taking your role as a game musician mostly in a reactive way: you take inputs from the play conversation (a conversation in which you don't actually feel you belong) and weave those into music; by playing such music you send back to the players, amplified, the emotions you believe they were already feeling. You're trying to act as a mood amplifier for others, not to affect the conversation with an agency of your own. You define yourself as part of the GM's toolbox, not as a player.
    I will resist going into the whys of the above, but I can't help noticing you're experiencing issues with it. Most notably, your task being a 2-step process (get input from players, send it back to them woven into music) it's slow, and many of the problems you highlight probably stem from this rather simple issue of time logistics.

    Conversely, the road you probably haven't beaten yet is that of becoming a proactive participant in the game, with music being your own share of unbridled creative input. This will work better for a much more "improvisational" game than what you're describing above, of course \u2014one without any single "GM", for example. I'm picturing a 5-players game of Polaris with all players being musicians\u2026 They take turns, not just "sitting out" as a 5th player (if you're not familiar with it, Polaris is basically a 4-players game in which players take turns covering 4 separate and equally important player-roles), but playing whatever music they like, and of course the mood of the music is going to affect the mood of play.

    \n There are a number of live games that use music as the core mechanic (though rarely live music, I think). The Nordicans in particular are very into using music to set mood and emotion, and sometimes as a core mechanic of the game.

    - Love in the Age of Debasement by Erlend Eidsem Hansen (larpsfromthefactory.com), in which player couples choose theme songs that control their play.
    - Stereohearts by Maria Ljung and Helene Willer Piironen, a game about love where the players interact solely through playing songs to one another (http://scenariofestival.se/scenarios/stereo-hearts/)
    - Superheroes' World by an international team, which uses songs and dancing as conflict mechanics. (http://www.grenselandet.net/p/the-larps-2013.html)
    - on the flip side, there is Before and After Silence by Matthijs Holter and Frederick Hossman (larpsfromthefactory.com), which uses the absense of noise as the core game mechanic.

    Games such as Screwing the Crew by Trine Lise Lindahl and Elin Nilsen (larpsfromthefactory.com) also use playlists to help queue the intensity of the playing.

    I also once heard about a Danish larp for four players during which they all contributed music from their high school experiences to a playlist. This act of the larp took place in a car. When a song they'd chosen came on the radio, play focused on their character.

    I once played a tango larp where the number of songs denoted the length of the act. That seems like it would suit itself well to a live band.

    I also played a poetic blackbox larp recently. It had no words at all, just physical mechanics and mood set by a playlist of Johnny Cash, Tom Waites, and Nick Cave. I wrote a play report of it here (http://lizziestark.com/2013/11/06/stockholm-scenario-festival/)
