Designing around Jerks

\n
\n
\n
\n\nDanielSolis \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2007 \n edited September 2007 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n We've all had to deal with with unpleasant player behavior channeled into in-game actions that are, to all appearances, within the bounds of the game's rules. This is a potential problem of all games, from Chess to story-games. In designing board games, I've discovered that I work under one unspoken assumption: Players want to win.

To put a finer point on it: A player will engage in this activity in order to pursue victory according to the activity's defined victory conditions. Once that assumption is altered, like one player pursuing a stalemate instead of victory, the game breaks. If one player just gives up and doesn't put in any effort at all, the game breaks. Some games are more susceptible to this than others, but even with my admittedly limited experience I'd say it could be common weakness to all games.

This is most definitely a concern I'm facing in RPGs, where in-game actions are far less strictly controlled than in the board game medium. A designer can place firewalls and fail-safes in a game's design to prevent jerky behavior. Or she could just throw up her hands and refer to the "Don't be a dick" mantra. The former can easily lead to lots of needlessly complicated exceptions-based designs. The latter, to me, has a certain sense of resignation.

Where do your designs commonly fall in this spectrum? Do you put a lot of effort considering potential dickish behavior, then designing rules to prevent that from happening? Do you just design what you want to design, assuming that players won't be jerks?

I've done certainly done both in a variety of games, but I'm curious how you handle it.

(Oh, and we're assuming here that players have already made an effort to follow the axiom, "Don't play with jerks," but some circumstance has nevertheless brought a jerk to the table.)

    \n Here's my problem with "don't play with jerks."

    At times, what at first glance may be jerkish behavior may in fact be a flaw in the design itself. There could be some gear in the system encouraging the jerky behavior. If you go to this axiom too quickly, then it seems like you could miss a real bug in the game.

    I do see a value in considering some jerkish behavior. There's a certain point in the development process where you recognize the difference between a player being a jerk and the game encouraging a latent jerk.

    Finding where those two split off is a skill that I've not quite yet honed, but I'm working on it.

    \n Jason, Dev pointed out how it can occur in a dungeon crawl. In board games, I've occasionally run across a problem where a player can choose to go to a stalemate and his opponent has no recourse except to trust that the stalemating player will at some point choose to pursue victory once more.

    For a storygamish example, I've got a section in Do's character creation where another player-character remembers an anecdote of your childhood exploits. I intended to make this anecdote the source of your traits, a la the 50 word paragraph in The Pool.

    However, in considering potential jerkish behavior, I realized you could get screwed pretty bad if that player either wasn't feeling inspired or if you had contrary ideas about the character. (Nevermind if the player writing the anecdote was just a plain jerk.)

    I implemented a couple solutions to this:\n
      \n
    • Distribute anecdote duties: Instead of one player remembering big-ass anecdote, three players take turns writing little mini-memories. This mitigates a single player's influence, allows more group contribution, and gives you some more variety in choosing your character's skills.
      • \n
    • Structure the anecdotes: Each player's anecdote focuses on one of three words. The first player remembers your Courage, the second remembers your Power and and the third remembers your Wisdom. (These words were totally ripped off of the Triforce in Zelda.) The words are clear enough to provide a groundwork for the little mini-story, but ambiguous enough to give some creative breathing room.
      • \n
    • Explain what the anecdotes are meant to communicate: This wasn't so much a design adjustment as a matter of more clearly writing the goals for this step in character creation: "These memories reveal how your companion showed Courage, Power and Wisdom worthy of leaving the temple and going on the Pilgrimage." That line provides further structure, focusing on the positive, heroic aspects of each character.
      • \n
    \nI could've just said "don't be a jerk when you're writing an anecdote for your friend's character," but then I wouldn't have found these very fun little mini-game elements and it wouldn't have shown me how I could more clearly write my rules.

    EDIT: Christopher makes a good point, I'll refrain from using "jerk" henceforth in this discussion.

    Posted By: DanielSolisI've discovered that I work under one unspoken assumption: Players want to win.
    \nDaniel, I may be off-base with this, but I'm not sure your core assumption is the same thing as handling "dickery" in games. Everyone is assuming here that you're talking about playing with people who are just being jerks and ruining everyone's fun (intentionally or not). I think that's different than someone who simply isn't playing to win, and thereby breaking your game.

    As an example, Jason and I often end up playing in games where our friends play to lose. They are not "success oriented", which can make it difficult for other players who are. So we're playing a team of secret agents, trying to infiltrate a high-security facility. We've slipped past the lasers, hacked the retinal scanners, and we've got the secret plans in hand. Then Mike says "uh, as we exit the room, I trip over the door jamb and roll down the stairs, landing at the feet of the now alerted guards! They start kicking me!"

    Now is that dickery, or is he just complicating the situation for fun? Is it fun for everybody, or was he just personally bored with success and trying to liven things up for himself?

    Put another way, sometimes playing to lose is a legitimate player strategy if the ultimate goal is to have a good time. If you're looking to design rules that don't break under that kind of pressure, you'll have to set your game up to give everybody room for their own kind of fun.

    \n My design philosophy: I specifically ignore jerks in my game design. I design games that assume everyone is cool and there to have a good time. Get the whole party in trouble because of my misdeeds, and you've just guaranteed that the whole party will be focussed around me (and trying to blame me) for hours. You couldn't offer a better incentive for me to go out and commit more misdeeds.
    \n
    \nBy contrast, Capes punishes jerkiness by ... not rewarding them, or even noticing them, in the slightest. I've seen it happen. Someone comes in, does something completely out of keeping with the game, everybody shrugs and goes back to what they were doing. The people who are playing the game right get rewarded with resources that literally give them more time in the spotlight, the people who aren't get side-lined until they either shape up or ship out. Heartless, but effective.

    \nGoofus: Man, that game ... it looked like you guys were having fun, but I just never got to do anything.

    \n Quick anecdote:

    My first game of D&D in college, I'm playing with a group of people I don't know. I've just come from high school RPG experience almost completely consisting of running Traveller in a highly "secret agent mission" mode: here is the mission, go do it. One of the people in the group is Chris (a person that now, 20+ years later, is one of my dearest friends in the world). He is playing a thief. I'm playing a Paladin (because that was me, then). We fight some monsters. We get some treasure. Chris says "I'm going to pick Hans's character's pocket and steal some of his treasure." I totally blow my stack...what the?! Your stealing from me? We are team, for criminy's sake! Chris says "hey, I'm a thief...its what we do." I appeal to the GM, and the GM does NOTHING about this! Man I was playing with total jerks...

    Except...on reflection I think I was the jerk. Or, perhaps more charitably to me, no one was a jerk. All this was was an example of most everyone at the table playing one game, and me playing a different game and suddenly realizing I wasn't playing the same game as everyone else, and moreover, HATED the game that everyone else was playing. My REACTION to that realization was probably jerky, but the fact that I hated it was just an item of my then personality. I was an "accidental jerk", as JZN and Mikael discuss above.

    I'm guessing this isn't what Daniel is talking about. Let me define "jerk" in the context of role-playing games:\n
    JERK. noun. A player who values their own input into the game at the expense of the enjoyment of the other players.
    \nGiven that definition, I'm not certain what mechanics COULD exist to alleviate the effect of a jerk on a game.

    However, another anecdote:

    I'm running TROS on Monday night for my regular TROS group. The players in that group are all people that come from very sim-heavy traditional game backgrounds, mostly sim-style D&D, with character immersion being the primary definition of role-playing. Two of the characters in the game have radically different religious perspectives, as represented by their Spiritual Attributes. I, either mistakenly or otherwise, framed a situation in which these perspectives were in conflict with each other. And then...things got out of hand. Both players were heavily into "what would my guy do" and had very narrow intepretations of that, and I could see that we were heading for a bad situation, TROS style (i.e. throw your red or white die between two players). We were playing through things, and then I sort of held up my hand and said (I paraphrase) "Ok, guys, I'm being honest with you. You are both role-playing perfectly. You are doing exactly what your characters would do in this situation, which is fantastic. Except...if you keep doing it this way, we are going to end up with a situation in which NO ONE WILL HAVE FUN. The thing you think is fun is...well, not fun in this situation. I'm going to ask you both, as players, to talk this over, and see if you can come up with some resolution that both satisfies your thoughts on what your characters will do AND makes sure that both of your characters survive this." I think suggested what seemed to me to be a high-level narrative compromise to the situation as an outcome, which both players accepted.

    Were these players being jerks? In a way, they were, by my definition above. They were valuing their own input ("What my guy would do") at the expense of the enjoyment of the other players ("What my guy would do is leading to a no-fun place"). But I honestly don't think they were being jerks, really. Their intent was not to be jerks, because they were NOT THINKING ABOUT ENJOYMENT in that moment. Really, they weren't. Enjoyment had nothing to do with what was going on. I could see in both of their faces that they weren't ENJOYING where they could see things were going, but they honestly felt they had to keep going there to be good role-players.

    Much of this was my fault...I know very well that TROS doesn't handle conflict whose ultimate arbiter isn't sword play to the death very well, and I knew that inter-player conflict wasn't really what the group was into. So maybe I was the jerk in the situation above; I valued my own input (a cool scene frame) over the enjoyment of the other players.

    \n Don't be a dick is part of the social contract. That's where it should be addressed if at all possible. Someone who's consistently a dick is someone you shouldn't play games with. No game rule is going to fix a situation like that.

    On the other hand, there are always going to be ways in games that allow players to do something that has the potential to make another player really unhappy -- without intending to do it. Rules can be written to smooth over these danger spots.

    Let It Ride is an example. Many GMs don't realize that they are massively affecting a player's chance of succeeding by calling for a series of tests, all of which must be successful. Let It Ride just cuts that out.

    The Connection Die in Burning Empires is another. Psychologists in the game are given the power to invade other characters' minds and even rewrite their Beliefs, Instincts and Traits. The Connection Die is there to create a set of Permissions and Expectations. The player has to give permission to the possibility of a rewriting, and is rewarded for doing so. But it happens with the expectation that the character may get screwed over in the future.

    \n [JoshBishopRoby and I talk about this all the time -- designing to meet your needs vs. the audience's needs. My opinion is most likely unpopular.]

    The jerk exists, even in the best players, whether or not you acknowledge it. The jerk is the part of the gamer that looks for an opening, an attempt to gain a foothold in the story over others. Some games encourage this without knowing it. A fair amount of the games that get discussed on this site barter in the sale of alpha-dogism, the player who talks the most or the fastest gets to "win."

    The session of Executive Decision I played in this weekend had a huge jerk at the table, who just about ruined the experience, but could not be abridged in his crappy behavior because the game didn't say, "shut up and give everyone a chance to talk." Toward the end of the game, I was outright yelling at him to shutup, but that didn't fix the previous 3-hours of fun that he had run over with his truck.

    And not every gamer wants to argue about every violation of the social contract, every time. The GM or the rules should guard against a player's fun and involvement being slighted by the rest of the players.... or one specific jerk... but often this is not the case. Task resolution aside, everyone gets a turn every round in tradition games. Some story games are extremely free-form, providing room for the marginalizing of others.

    A designer that does not at least consider this during design is forgetting that not every gamer is him or her.

    Paul Tevis was telling a story at Gateway about a group of Polaris players who always talk so loud that the one player who "brings the awesome" never gets to talk. None of them are "jerks" in the definitive sense, but if a free-form game allows you to talk over me, then the core rules are already in violation of the social contract. Right?

    As an aside, GMs are guilty of jerkism too, removing the fun from a game by railroading their own agendas, ignoring rules they don't like, calling for too many "spot checks," or forgetting about all the players in the pursuit of the one (player) agenda at the table that they like.

    Games that allow me to "interrupt" with my action points or resources are vastly better in this regard to stopping a player from being ignored by the game play.

    \n Jim, while I agree with you that having resources to accomodate player participation can help with problems like screen-time, etc, I can hardly agree with your more general notion. This got discussed in some detail in a heated debate between myself (and Ralph Mazza) with Tony LB, with regards to whether or not something like the challenge mechanic in Universalis is a good idea. Tony's argument was (and he'll correct me if I'm wrong), that the challenge rule isn't neccessary, because if you don't play with jerks, you can always just accept each other's contributions.
    \n
    \nBut I agree with you that you can't always do so, and still have fun. Even if the other player's are not jerks. And that's because, despite even the best attempts by players to accomodate the needs of other players playing, sometimes people make additions to the game that we don't appreciate for whatever reason.
    \n
    \nNow, yes, sometimes this is specifically beacuse we're being selfish, and not paying attention to the needs of the other players. I'd argue, however, that this really isn't being a jerk. At best, it's unintentional jerkism, if you will. A true jerk, one that's really violating the social contract, is one that does so because he really doesn't care about the needs of the other players.
    \n
    \nYou can't hold a player to be 100% accountable for successfully taking into account the needs of the other players. Or even 80%. Why? Because we're human. And to that extent, I argue, it's important to have methods of feedback that allow players to communicate well to each other what they want, and give players an equal voice. The system should be democratic.
    \n
    \nCreating a rule to facilitate this sort of communication, a rule like the PTA fanmail rule, for instance, is not a rule to prevent real jerkism. Because it won't actually fix real jerkism. A real jerk won't care if they get fanmail in PTA, they'll relish the fact that they're getting away with whatever they're getting away with.
    \n
    \nThis is where the line has to be drawn in terms of these behaviors. Is the player somebody who, if they understand the needs of the other players will negotiate to come up with a result that's satisfactory for everybody? Or are they somebody who either can't be communicated with, or can be, but who won't accomodate the desires of the other players?
    \n
    \nYou can write rules to addess the player who is well-intentioned, but imperfect (though Tony might argue that such rules are unneccessary, and basic communication skills will always suffice). You can't write rules to address the true jerk. You just have to avoid playing with him (or, if you're somehow amazing, change their behavior).

      \n I'm going to second Jim's notion of designing to smooth out the wrinkles caused by "unintentional jerkism". In my experience, if you're designing a game to facilitate understanding between parties that may approach the game from different angles, you don't need to put quite as much effort into finding gamers that think exactly as you do.

      For example, there are some problems with this in our GURPS game because GURPS has no explicit mechanics for flagging or inter-player rewards. But we play Spirit of the Century with roughly the same group, and such problems are nowhere to be found. I'd like to attribute this at least in part to the fact that we have a much better understanding of what the other players want out of the game when we can see (at least) ten very clear flags on their sheet.

      I don't like the advice "just avoid all the jerks" because I'm convinced most people aren't jerks. Design and play games that facilitate a shared understanding of the Social Contract, and you won't have to switch groups as often.
