MikeRM

By ploy, I mean a course of action you can take which may alter the situation to your advantage, but will cost you if it fails.

Trollbabe has several ploylike things (not by that name) available to the player: An Unexpected Ally, A Remembered Spell, etc. These are not true ploys, however, because they are more like "fortunate coincidences" than "risky things the character tries". They are ploylike in effect but not in content.

Examples of ploys:

A Dangerous Shortcut.
An Untrusted Ally.
A Desperate Leap.
A Wild Burst of Firing.
A Last-Ditch Defence.

I don't have a mechanic for this worked out yet but I'm sure there are many possibilities. The Trollbabe ones give you a reroll, if I recall correctly. But there should be some downside (either potential or inevitable), apart from "you can't use it again this session".

Any thoughts? Idea have legs?

    Okay, so game X has two types of Traits: Simple and Problematic.

    Each Simple Trait has a Ploy.
    You can use the Ploy in the same way that you can simply Buyoff a Key in TSoY.

    Utilizing a Ploy jeopardizes the straightforwardness of a Simple Trait.
    If the ploy fails, that trait is now permanently problematic.

    That way, the game isn't killed, there is a risk, but there is also a benefit - if the ploy works, you get to keep it and the trait stays simple.

    There's a John Wick (I think) trick that I'm only faintly remembering that handles this pretty nicely. You start play with a bowl of white stones which players can take and spend for some positive effect. Players are free to take stones any time they want, but every time a player takes a stone (or possibly under other circumstances) the GM tosses in a black stone. Since draws are blind, the more often you pull from this, the more likely you are to pull a black stone, which has some specific mechanical bad effect. This gives you the opportunity to do risky things (ploys) but with escalating risk. Not a 100% match, but I think it's in the spirit.

    One other option is to (Stealing a nerdword back to it's real use) allow players to stake mechanical elements of their sheet on on an outcome. This doesn't work for every type of game, but for games that have elements like aspects or other personal elements with a mechanical meaning, it allows for genuine double or nothing giving the character a greater bonus than usual, but removing the element from their sheet (Temporarily or permanently) if some condition is not met. Now, "Failing" is technically a viable option for not meeting conditions, but I think that runs the risk of being mechanically unsatisfying - the player is doing this with a reasonable expectation of success, and failure is probably already bad enough that it doesn't need something else. There's a gambling issue of perceived cost that tends to make greater consequences of failure decrease in impact once the initial cost is established that I think outlines this issue pretty well. If players already don't like failure enough to be looking for a ploy, making failure worse won't move their needle much.

    If, instead, the stakes are on something specific - some subset of the conflict, or some secondary issue - then you get some juice again. Perhaps something akin to the declaration and qualification of Mortal Coil or Polaris would serve here: "My Faith in My Father gives me the strength to survive this..." "...But only if the child is unharmed".

    I admit, I like taking the price directly out of the player's hides. Unless you're playing a mechanical opposition game, like Rune or Agon, adding complexity or challenges are briar patch punishments. I get to take a big bonus and my punishment is that there's more cool stuff to fight? Oh, no! Not that!


    The exact nature of the bonus is also an interesting question. Consider: is the player's concern that they succeed (that they desire a specific outcome) or that they not fail (Thatthey wish to avoid a specific outcome)? That question alone opens up interesting negotiation possibilities.

    One last option that worked really well was by using a currency for it, but making it insanely rare. The old WEG did an awesome job with this with Force Points in Star Wars. They were _crazy_ powerful, but you had *1* and if you used it in a non-heroic fashion, you wouldn't get it back. You had to do something awesome just to break even, but if the awesome thing was blowing-up-the-deathstar awesome, you'd get it back with a bonus. How tightly this was controlled probably varied from group to group, but my experience with it was very tight fisted, and it really managed to produce the desired outcome: The ability to pull it out of the fire when you _really_ needed to but strong incentive to try everything else you could first.

    Kind of rambly, I know, but it's an issue near and dear to my heart.
