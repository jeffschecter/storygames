    \n Yes it is true that marketing of RPGs has become stuck in a niche, and it's a niche that seems to be shrinking in profile as the term RPG becomes ever more appropriated by MMO computer games. I remember, as James Wallis does fondly, the time when Virgin stores had huge roleplaying sections and compare that with the three 4E books that are now stocked by borders.

    It occurs to me that the big shop presence that we've lost didn't really have anything to do with RPGs, though. Virgin stocked piles of RPGs because they wanted something to go beside their CCGs. The high profile period was driven by Magic (and VTES and Battletech, and Netrunner) not any RPG. So it's not really that we have failed in our marketing since then, rather that the wave we were riding has subsided. Sadly what we have largely failed to do is hang onto the coat-tails of computer RPGs in the same way, and get things like the Warcraft RPG into computer game stores.

    Could we do it in our own right? Could we deliver any style of tabletop RPG to the masses in a way that gets it sold in high street stores regularly? I suspect the answer is simply no, we couldn't, because the truth is that this is a minority passtime, and most people will never ever go for it, no matter what we do. We an try, and we can push the boundaries (as 4E does by profile, and Ron is trying to do by getting Spione put into German bookshops), but we already know where the avenue of success lies ... turn our games into computer games and they *do* sell just like that.

    I think maybe what James doesn't say is that the RPG hobby has already made the leap to mass market appeal. From D&D and Fighting Fantasy we have grown an industry of computer games that dominates the entertainment industry now. Fantasy RP is now a normal thing, spin off movies from computer RPGs dominate the film schedule every year (along with fantasy children's movies that also would likely never have been made without the RPG industry). We've done it. We've won, we just found out that pen and paper and tabletops weren't the best format to do it in (and even there you *do* see Murder Mystery games, which are a form of RP poem, in most high streets).

    Instead, we both set out different directions even from before the start of the discussion (designing wanting to reach mass-market vs. designing for fun and for self.) and that could indeed shift the topic entirely towards that single issue.     My gut feeling is that gamedesigns are subject to diminishing returns. In more then one way, but I'll try and keep on topic. Indie games design creates games that by their very specific nature don't allow for big target audiences. Generally its designs havent been based on the needs of a large target audience but instead on either the very specific needs of the designer and his community or in relative isolation. To reach that "massive" target audience you'd need to base your designs on its perceived wants and needs. These target audiences are like bubbles in a ...well ... bubbly landscape and they intersect and overlap. I think I could design a game for a target "bubble" (an audience with the same wants and needs im aiming to fulfill) and THEN try and reach all the people in that specific bubble. But as soon as I try to reach other bubbles i have to make concessions and this is were the "diminishing returns" kicks in. I could put in alot of effort to make my game accessible to all these different bubbles of wants and needs, but the energy invested in its design beyond its target bubble will yield less and less appeal in return. Of course one could design a game targeting the huge bubble of "male power-gamers" and hey thats cool! but i think you need to be VERY aware of your target audience, its bubble and designing beyond the wants and needs of that specific group.

    Hell I wish I could say that in a much clearer way, i hope you get what im trying to say.

    Hi Matthijs, I don't have a huge amount of time but...

    Some great books to read:
    The Tipping Point: How Little Things Can Make a Big Difference

    The Art of the Start: The Time-Tested, Battle-Hardened Guide for Anyone Starting Anything

    Good to Great: Why Some Companies Make the Leap... and Others Don't

    Word of Mouth Marketing: How Smart Companies Get People Talking

    How to Make Millions with Your Ideas: An Entrepreneur's Guide

    The E-Myth Revisited: Why Most Small Businesses Don't Work and What to Do About It

    A little know fact is that you can buy AMAZING case studies on many of the mass market successes you mention through Harvard's Business School. Operation and Marketing plans are also available. I haven't looked into this in great detail yet but I plan to.

    I put together a Small Press Marketing How To guide here for Gencon. The ideas are targeted towards people with small to no budgets and are very specific to indie RPGs. But the information on clarifying what you are selling, why it has value, who you are selling to, why they should care is fairly universal.

    Ultimately, to truly be a mass market success, not only does it take doing all of the above well, it takes many years or decades, persistence, and diversification. Many known successes didn't make it big trying to push 1 product or idea. They push forward many till one broke through. Don't get too invested in 1 idea. The best way to have a great idea is to have many ideas first. It also takes money but in most cases if your idea is great and tested on a small scale, you can find investors to fund you.

    Many music companies won't sign beginning artists unless they have been able to sell at least 1,000 CDs or downloads on their own. And then will only put in limited marketing resources (online marketing, social networking, sponsorships, press, community building) if they expect to sell 10,000 CDs or stick to digital releases only. If they can expect to sell over 10,000 CDs, then they will put in a modest amount of money for advertising. My point is that it is a slow build. And rather than put everything into 1 idea, it's best to diversify and then focus in when the numbers reflect the growth needed for further investment.

    Just look at Apple! They've been around for 30+ years and 10 years ago ago they had less than 4% adoption in the computer market (which is still huge). The climb towards the iPhone involved many steps and many products and many failures!

    The iPod itself was a long term plan. They had a basic plan for an iPhone like device when the iPod first came out. They released versions of the iPod strategically to have each new version slowly teach you how to use what would eventually be the iPhone. And all their entertainment deals over the years is partly what helped them lock in the major labels to release iTunes which was a huge component of their strategy which also includes eventually turning their desktops and laptops into large version of the iPhone + entertainment center + TV.

    We scream about playtesting all the time but another factor is market testing. Once you know your product works, you have to test a sample of your market and how they will react and talk about it. Which may involve making changes and will greatly inform on how to market your product. When I go to Gencon, most people can't tell my why I should buy their game. You also have to make the first step easy. Don't want to buy? How about a demo or take away or contact info. Bought the game? You still need to make sure they play it. Hell, if they play it at the con... awesome... more sales. But wait... why don't you have character sheets to give them? Or quick start rules? How are they supposed to play right away? But it's not just RPGs. Huge billion dollar companies make these mistakes or deprioritize these techniques.

    It's also important to ask why reaching mass market appeal is important. For money? How much? At what cost? Awareness? Awareness by who? My fiance is a best selling author who has her books available in Borders and B&N. Even at that level you would be amazed how many sales she compromises to keep her integrity and stick to her vision. She's passed on starring on TV shows where the cost to her vision was too high. And reaching a mass market is one thing, staying there... oh man, that's another :-) I think for story games designers and publishers, the question might be what are your goals? If they involve reaching a wider audience, then how do we double their existing audience let alone times it by 100! What's the next step? I don't think we've fully tapped smaller niche markets, or even the ones we are in. Trad gamers, people interested in improv, theater, teachers and students that use simulations in schools.

    I hope that helps!

      \n Hi Tony, I don't do this for a living but I partially work and manage many people who do as one of the many things my job requires (the list is very long!).

      Very generally speaking we:

      - decide how we will sell and distribute a product before producing anything
      - look at our existing brands, skills, interests
      - look at the types of products we ourselves buy related to the above
      - look for markets that also buy these type of products
      - research websites and magazines dedicated to these markets
      - see what products are advertised on these websites and magazines
      - brainstorm product ideas that we can offer
      - something whose main benefit can be described in a short sentence (my Gencon Seminar above has tips for this)
      - look at the competition, how can we differentiate our product?
      - choose something that stands out (a better, new, innovative, an unmet need, or highly in demands product)
      - make sure the potential profit margins allow for savings, growth, and mistakes
      - it can be manufactured quickly in small amounts at first and then at large amount with significant cost savings
      - use Google Adword keyword tools to see if people are searching for similar products, in what volume, how much those keywords cost and write down the results
      - keep testing the above, trying different keywords till you have enough traffic that a conversion rate of 0.5% (different people use different numbers) would cover all your costs
      - build a website advertising your product (which doesn't exist yet)
      - include a form that people who visit the website can sign up to for future updates
      - use Google Analytics to track who visits your website and how they use it
      - use Google Adwords to advertise the website (sometimes we initially use Myspace or Facebook targeted advertising)
      - analyze the results
      - optimize your advertising (wording, keywords)
      - analyze the results
      - optimize your website (wording, colors, fonts, layout, graphics, links)
      - analyze the results
      - if the response is poor, start the process again
      - if the response is good, run with it!

      This is a gross simplification but it probably nails a few of the basic ideas. This is only one way to test a product. There are many more. There is a science to pricing alone!

      Edit: We also usually restrict this to quick products with short life spans. With many of our artists, when we try to build them up as a brand, we often do things that would contradict much of the above in an effort to stay true to who the artist is and look for monetary success in the long term not the short term. But stick pretty close to this process for things like t-shirts and posters. Using my fiance as an example, she will often turn down short term monetary success for a slow build towards a lifelong brand sticking to her visions, integrity, and beliefs. Money is far from the only form of success.

      \n Clearly some in the indie gaming scene lean toward prioritising the personal artistic vision as the basis for creating a game. The hope here is to express something you find meaningful (note: fun is meaningful) on your own terms, and to impact those others that share or are open to that perspective.

      On this thread, some people have expressed a desire to approach things the other way, using a mass-market led approach to game creation. The hope here is to penetrate the consciousness of (some parts of) society and potentially transform how people play together.

      (I guess the hopes could respectively be 'to revel in niche artistedom' and 'to make a killing', too. That's fine.)

      I can't help but see an excluded middle, and perhaps one that even bridges directly between the two seemingly binary options. We talk about doing it, but I'm not sure we do it enough or go far enough:

      Design for the people you know.

      Yes, yes, I know you already design to quicken the pulses of your online crew, or with your game group in mind, and even in rare cases for your non-gaming friend who would totally play if there was only a game in that genre.

      Nice! Not enough.* I'm talking about subordinating your design impulses to the task of delivering games that work with the people you know - not necessarily your grandpa and your anarchist pomo pal and your boss all at the same time, but a game that suits the desires and definitions of fun of your hockey crew . Another game that you could definitely sit down with at the family table on boxing day.

      How to do this? Seriously explore their wants from fun. Get you sister to describe an imaginary game she would definitely play: would it be more like monopoly or hide and seek? Would it combine the two? What about winning? Is it important to get better as you play? Would it be revealing? And as one small piece of the questioning: if the game was supposedly 'about' something, what would it be (monopoly is about being a property person)?

      By gathering this data, you begin to draw a map of fun in your social environment. This is a map that points to buried treasure.

      As an aside: John's posts have been awesome. Really, all I'm doing is standing on his shoulders and waving to one side: I think we could invest a fraction of the effort he mentioned (for gathering market data) into synching what you do with the people dear to you.

      I think this could be a worthwhile approach for some people, and for the health of the hobby, for the following reasons.

      By designing for ourselves and other gamers, known, unknown, or imagined, I think we substantially curtail what we could be doing with the medium.

      By designing outwards rather than inwards, but doing so with a modest target audience in mind, that of people you know, I believe that you are likely to produce something authentic that will be played.

      I ultimately believe that something authentic and distinctive, and at the same time directed towards other rather than towards the needs of the gaming community, is most likely to gain traction beyond that community.

      I also believe that writing games to bind you to other people can only be a good and beautiful thing.


      With a flourish I shall now volte-face and submit that there may already be examples of this going on, and if you are one such it would be interesting to hear from you.


      *in case it needs saying, it may be enough for you. It's simply not the limit of where this approach can be fruitfully taken.

      \n There are storygame texts that remind people that banter supports play, so don't be afraid to shout whatever. That is so backwards from mainstream perspective. When my folks get together to play cards or dominoes or even basketball, the game dies quickly if it fails to support banter and conversation. We might use the game lingo, but we aren't really talking as part of the game or about the game, but about each other. And when I go into that mode of thinking, I can't help but wonder, Why play a game that has the purpose of producing conversation about itself?

      Storygames are about sharing stories, and non-gamers will understand that one shouldn't carry on an unrelated conversation when someone is telling a story. That is true enough. Think about when adults tell stories to each other in mainstream contexts. People listen politely and respond appropriately, and then maybe someone else takes a turn telling a story -- it is a ritual, maybe even a game -- but nobody ever says, "Wouldn't it be better if your character did this instead?" In part because we are afraid to interrupt, but far more because the story itself isn't the point. The story, and the entire ritual, is just supporting a broader conversation.

      We could take a step closer to storygames by thinking about those moments when a parent and child share a story. A child almost certainly will make suggestions, or at least ask suggestive questions. But the child often also identifies the protagonist with someone real. The story becomes a conversation about that person. The story continues in this new, shared context, but it supports the conversation, rather than the conversation supporting the story.

      A slightly older child may make the conversation about people in general rather than people in particular. Great, that's theme or something! We must be really close to storygames now. Maybe. The thing is, for all that people talk about using narr games to explore premise; in practice, I get the feeling that storygamers here more often use premise to create better stories, which is a bit different. I'm on iffier ground here, since it's still easier for me to put head in a mainstream hat than in storygames hat. But my gut is to see the parent & child as in something closer to a conversation-supporting mode than a story-supporting mode, while storygamers are more about using vocal interaction to support the story.

      All in all, I think a huge obstacle between storygames and mass appeal is that it's not just about convincing people that sharing stories is fun. The hard part is convincing them that sharing stories is worthwhile for it's own sake, to the exclusion of other things they could be doing, like playing a game that supports conversation and banter for their own sake. I'm not sure marketing can do much to get past that obstacle, unless we are talking about marketing an entirely different product, which may or may not change the broader culture to be more accepting of storygames down the line.
