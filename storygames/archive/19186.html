\n Thought I'd skip the three post rule with this one and break out a new thread as what this KS is promising sounds pretty revolutionary: at the very least, it's at the forefront of virtual gaming technology. What's its USP? Well, from what I can gather, it's mainly the fact that it's designed to work with iOS, Android, Mac, Windows and... whatever that penguin icon represents (anyone enlighten me there?).

What I'd like to know is, what makes it different from Obsidian Portal and Roll20 etc.? Is it the platform-independentness? Still, why would you choose it over OP and R20? And what exactly is this 'scutwork' they're claiming to obviate?

I'm interested in this because I'm just about to take my first steps into online gaming, via G+, which seems a perfectly adequate platform, but if the gaming experience provided by creators such as the ones behind this KS is likely to be so much better, I need to know about it, as I suspect the greater part of my gaming in future is likely to be via the Internet. (True, I'm mainly talking about playing games with my buddies over Hangouts than online games per se, but still...).

Thoughts?

    \n Yeah, it's using Unity, which is a cross-platform game engine.

    It's not the clearest at exactly what it is, but the closest comparison seems to be Neverwinter Nights. It's taking a ruleset (In NWN, D&D, in this example, an unpublished design by Robin Laws), and making a set of tools for running a game in this ruleset. The GM is given tools like scripting and creating NPCs, tilesets, and such.

    There's a lot that's weird about this: it's not designed around online play (see the FAQ), so unless they reach a hastily-added stretch goal, you'd have to be running your own voice thing. (Reasonable on a computer, trickier to work well on phones). Unless this has magically solved a lot of very difficult UI problems, it'll probably require a bunch of prep to do anything but pre-canned adventures, and be hard to improvise at the speed of face-to-face play when they go off (other than simple things like: "Uh, two orcs appear!"). The KS doesn't mention whether it's real-time or turn based.

    So, it's a computer multi-player RPG that requires a device for all players (most likely, for face-to-face games), that comes with a bunch of GM tools for creation, that'd you probably need to use in prep to be effective. Lots of people did get a lot of fun out of NWN and NWN2 for sure, but I'm not sure why the pitch isn't oriented that direction instead of at tabletop roleplayers.
