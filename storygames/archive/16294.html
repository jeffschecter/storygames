    \n We define roleplaying because separating form from content frees you from cultural bondage. We saw this happen in the field of comics; it is not coincidence in my view that around ten years after Scott McCloud's Understanding Comics, we suddenly had graphic novels in every bookstore. (It's not sole causation, either, but it's not coincidence.) When form and content are bound together, as they are when we avoid the hard work of definition and stick to tradition, people can dismiss one on the grounds of the other, or be blind to new content on the grounds of similar form, and so on.

    The Big Model is most certainly not the last, best word - I think everyone who's inclined to do theory is just thoroughly tired of arguing on the Internet right now. Hopefully this won't entirely lead to reinvention of the wheel by the next wave of folks who show up.

    Posted By: Snake_EyesWell then why do some people *not* define it, or make up there own terminology and lexicon?
    \nThis happens when people don't find the answers that makes sense for them. For GNS, it got a lot of exposure through an evangelical approach, and argumentative. I was a part of the problem (although a small part, I hope) as I found it really helpful and would try to explain to others how it was Right\u2122.

    Part of the problem of trying to be right was making those general categories harder definitions. A game with mostly Gamist elements had to be Gamist. A player who played it was also Gamist. That wasn't the intention of the theory, but the cat was out of the bag and a lot of people encountered it with someone telling them that either their game was broken or damaged because it didn't have a clear premise, or having their favorite game or themselves given a label (with Gamist probably being the most common, something that was often painted as the most unfavorable of the three)

    The problem is on both sides. Is there something not covered by GNS? Yes! Probably every game! I've played "broken" games with focused simulation or narrative. I've played narrative oriented games (intentionally, forge style even) where I do very little except min max and try to get the best results - a gamist attitude. Categories and theories are an attempt to define reality. They aren't reality. Even in the scientific sense, theories are the best picture of what we know to be true. But there are gaps and nothing is every "proven" completely.

    Also, the dominance of the theory colors future discovery. Is it the best? Doubtful, but it's not bad either. It can certainly be improved and refined. But what other ways are there to theorize about gaming that are completely different? Maybe, but it's hard to look at games and think of theory and also not let GNS influence me. Even taking something like Magic's Timmy, Johnny, Spike player archetypes and that's not far off from a GNS basis.

    And then there's the fact that some people don't like theory. The abstract and/or objective look at things is either not interesting, not fun, or not helpful. It's easy to self-justify by making value judgements ("Well, some people aren't smart enough, I guess") but that doesn't add to the dialog. The truth is people have different goals with games, and different perspectives on life, and sometimes that means trying to avoid categorizing and defining everything they do. Critical analysis is something that people who like a huge variety tend to do. It could be a coping mechanisms for people with a hunger for new things and new experiences.

    Like I said, I can't answer for you, and I know I can't answer for a collective. But I can do more than ask you your own questions in return .Let me answer your questions from my perspective and see if that helps.

    I define games because it helps me look at them objectively, and helps me understand what they might be like to play. I am not satisfied with just a handful of games. I love playing new games, and love the thrill of maybe finding a new favorite game. Theory helps me do this. Also theory helps me investigate my own style of play.

    I define RPGs based on a rough GNS model. I'm not happy with the model. Each category has a problem with it, and when applied to games, they seem to address different things. In other words, I can imagine having a game that is Gamist, where the mechanics focus on creating story (now!), and simulates a fiction in a way to allow players to fully explore. It could be I misunderstand the theory. But these are still valuable as objectives for play. In fact, I like games that have the potential for all three of those. I find more fruitful is having designers who are aware of the possibility of theory, and even if they dislike all the current ones, it still can prompt designers to think about the intentions of their design.

    I use english words when talking about theory. Unless GNS is part of the topic already, bringing it in is too much of a potential powder keg. Or rather, I don't tend to talk about theory. I try to focus on experiences instead. If someone is having a problem with a game, it doesn't matter if we can define one of the players as Gamist and another as Narrativist or not. They aren't going to fit the archetypes anyway. I focus on the experiences at hand, and then learn from that. I reflect upon that. Sometimes I see patterns and connections, and those help me build a picture of game theory, as it were. But when I need to communicate that, I go back to experiences and English. I might be able to write up essays to establish theory and lexicon, but then people would need to know my essays to understand my new lexicon. But by focusing on my experiences, I think I get theory across without alienating as many people, and the dialogs themselves can result in a lexicon that is more natural and easier to understand as a result.

    \n Forge Theory / TBM is full of all kinds of jargon. I know the word "jargon" is a loaded term these days, and I mean it a couple senses: both the "way of talking meant to confuse someone" and the "powerful technical term meant to convey meaning in a condensed form" senses. People seem to rail against the idea that game design theory overloads the meaning of a lot of everyday words. I don't consider that a terrible problem. If you want to talk about game design, which is a hard subject, then you have to learn some special terminology, which is kinda expected for when you're learning something hard. I'm not saying that game design is brain surgery, but when you learn to play football, there are new terms that overload everyday meanings of words.

    The thing about TBM is that is (mostly) focuses on observed, actual play. A lot of people assume it does more than that, and that's where most of the big problems start. Creative Agenda doesn't try to categorize players or individual play preferences or what's going on in a single person's head. It's an observed phenomenon about a group property, generally after the fact, based on observations of hours of play (one "instance" of play that touches on the entire reward cycle a least once). Sure, you can have theoretical discussions about CA in theoretical play, but if you follow Ron Edwards on The Forge for a while, you'll see he avoids that and will jump at people to provide descriptions of real people playing real games. The man is a behavioral scientist, and that's how he rolls.

    The biggest issue with Forge jargon is that it changes and it isn't really defined anywhere central. The Forge Glossary is the best start, but even it is outdated. It's more like the statute law that gets overridden by case law, but replace "court cases" with "forum discussions." If you don't follow the forums or disappear for a while, meaning can change and then you're lost because you mean 2001-Simulationism and everyone else means 2012-Simulationism. [Mind you, I don't think the definition of Simulationism has changed significantly in the last decade, but it's been explained better using examples or re-castings like "constructive denial" (another jargon term).]

    Remember that Forge Theory came about as a way to diagnose actual play and fix some problems people were having in real games. It wasn't exactly a design tool. That is, it wasn't for designing games in a vacuum. It was for looking at games that weren't working for some people, at least not consistently, and changing them or making new games or just playing the game differently so that people could have consistently fun play experiences.


    To address your specific questions:

    Why do we define games?

    I'm not sure who "we" are. The Forge? I'm not sure we define games at all. We have a large-scale model--not a definition--of an instance of play for a role-playing game: The Big Model, with its layers of Social Contract - Exploration - Techniques - Ephemera... and Creative Agenda
    spiking through all four layers. Lots of people have attempted defining "RPG," including myself.

    There's a lot of disagreement about these definitions, but I'm not sure a good definition really matters that much. I used to argue that being able to design an RPG requires that one knows what an RPG is. I'm not sure it's that important anymore. If you design something that isn't an RPG, but it's similar and still fun and people will play it, who cares? Definitions can be limiting. Sticking to definitions could have stopped games like Everway, Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple, Happy Birthday Robot, or Montsegur 1244 from seeing the light of day.



    How do we define RP design?

    Again, why does it matter? It's that process we go through when we make a new RPG. Do we need to know anything more?

    We can wax philosophical about what "design" means in any context, and maybe that will inform us about aesthetics and elegance and such, and perhaps that can make us better designers.


    What can I use to describe RP theory and lexicon?

    Anything you want. Jargon is a language shortcut. Instead of saying "a shared priority about how to measure the value of player input among everyone at the table, so that everyone is on the same page and is pushing the game in the same direction instead of working against one another at worst, or taking turns getting what one wants at best," we say "Creative Agenda." Do people get confused about what Creative Agenda is, maybe because the word "agenda" suggests a single player's personal preference? Sure. All the time. But I think that's an issue with jargon in all fields.

    If you want to start new discussions about RPG theory on your own blog or whatever, just define your terms and start from scratch. You don't have to use Forge terms, and you probably shouldn't unless you really, really understand them. Also, don't try to have these discussions on TheRpgSite, because my impression is that a lot of the more vocal regulars there feel that there isn't a lot needed for RPG design and that it's all up to the GM anyway (that is, System Doesn't Matter), and look down on the entire idea of design. If you want to talk about RPG design on other forums, talk about it in terms of your own actual play and what worked and what didn't work. You don't need jargon for that.

    If you are looking to expand upon the field of RPG design theory, then pick a model and learn it well, use it to analyze your own actual play, and write some games and analyze your own playtests using the model.

    \n Victorian, to be able to use a language for communication, you have to SHARE a language with your interlocutor.

    Forge theory is not widely known, and what is known is 99% of the times not what the theory really says, but what somebody misunderstood years ago and posted hundreds of times everywhere, smothering any real use of that terminology to communicate with anybody that you are not really sure is using these words with the same meaning you do.

    So, outside the Forge and very few other places, Forge Jargon is used almost exclusively to confuse communication, to appear more knowledgeable, or to pass as an "insider".

    It's not a failure of the language, is a failure of the forums social culture, but the end result is the same: don't ever use Forge Jargon without first be sure that you are talking with someone who will not misunderstand what you mean. Even better, don't use it at all, just to be sure. Talk using real play examples.

    This is even mandatory at the Forge. And it's a really good rule (we copied it for our own Italian forum). Talking in actual play terms give you credibility from the beginning, it says that you play (it's not a given in rpg forums), that you think about your playing, and you want to talk about it. When you talk using forge jargon, people don't know if you are really trying to communicate or you are showing off or posturing.or trying to confuse the discussion.

    As an example: just a few hours ago I compiled a list of the best actual play threads to learn forge theory (and GNS in particular). You can find it here: http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forge/index.php?topic=32931.0

    They are a good example of how the use of actual play examples simplify the communication and understanding of the theory.

    And about most of the people who play rpgs... they simply don't care. And they are right not to care. They want to play, not to learn some theory and a new language. Do you want to have a car engineer and a mechanic on the backseats of your car explaining to you how the car was built and how it works, every time you take the car to go to the store? If a game works, you don't need to learn any theory to play it. You need some knowledge of how the damn thing works only if it doesn't really works and you have to fix it by yourself (this last part describe my first twenty years in traditional role-playing by the way...)

    Don't try to force people to be interested in things that they are not interested about. Play good rpgs with them instead. This is the best way to help people understand better what rpgs can do.

    \n No, the 3-step model is not based on GNS. Far from it. I try to give game-smiths and players an alternate perspective on role-playing games, to help them with words that convey what we do without the haphazard connotations of old terminology (especially the connotations that flourish arounds the terms "system" and "mechanics").

    Here's s short summary; with key-terms in big letters:

    The core of the 3-step model is VISION -> INTERACTION -> FICTION

    Vision is where the game-smith is active, having an idea of a game, inventing method and setting, writing the game.
    Interaction is where the players are active, using the method and their human skills to make inspired game-play.
    Fiction is the product of the interaction, being the narrative each single player experience, and his emotions when experiencing this narrative (mind this; the fiction produced will vary from player to player, due to their singular perspective on the narrative; their characters).

    If the game-smith is any good, the fiction produced by the players in the interaction will connect to his vision. If he is lucky it may produce something even better. ;-)

    The METHOD invented by the game-smith, is defined as "how we play the game" (the full tool-set, and our use of it), and is usually divided into
    - TECHNIQUES of dialogue, story-telling and leadership
    - MECHANICS of numbers, resolution-rules, dice, cards and whatnot
    - but not limited to these; anything that may be used to strengthen the game is indeed part of the method

    - SETTING is a part of the method too, in the way it is presented for use in the game, as elements of play, to inspire the players and help their interaction produce high quality fiction (in other words; a very important tool in presenting an inspired vision, in the hands of great game-smiths).

    I have invented this model for understanding role-playing games, after years of discussing rpgs, to bring some clarity to my thinking on these games. It has helped me a lot.

    \n Here's the quick list of motives I wrote about. The whole article is 20 pages long, so it's not ideal for pasting in. But the main gist is this - people are looking for things in games, you can identify what things someone is looking for by observing behaviors, you can place affordances into a game (opportunities for a player to find those things) to help a player find what they're looking for.

    And these are the types of things I've identified that players look for (keep in mind that this is a list that focuses on live combat larp play, and adjustments are necessary to apply it to tabletop, but I think that's probably obvious):

    Audience - Experience a satisfying narrative
    Catharsis - Experience emotions through the character
    Comprehension - Figure things out. Solve problems and puzzles.
    Competition - Win at something, or at least enjoy the act of competing.
    Crafting - Create non-ephemeral things (costumes, props, documents, etc.)
    Education - Take away new knowledge or understanding as a player.
    Embodiment - Make decisions based on character background, knowledge, and motivation.
    Exercise - Enjoy physical activity and movement.
    Exploration - Experience the fictional setting.
    Exhibition - Show off (costumes, props, acting chops, mad skillz, etc.) and get kudos.
    Fellowship - Enjoy time with friends (also includes flirting and such).
    Flow - Enjoy losing oneself in the moment.
    Leadership - Be important to the player community.
    Protagonist - Be important to the story and impact the game world.
    Spectacle - Experience the awesome stuff (pretty costumes, elaborate sets, funny NPCs, etc.)
    Versatility - Collect important things (spells, lore, favors, etc.) and have the right thing at the right time.



    Keep in mind that these motives are intentionally atomic. Many things that people talk about enjoying in a cluster and give one name to are broken down into components in this list very much on purpose.

    Many of the disagreements and misunderstandings I've seen come from people assuming, for example, that a bigger idea always contains all of the smaller ones for everyone who enjoys any of them.

    For example, people who are classified as "Gamist" are often assumed to like Competition, Comprehension, Versatility, and, in a live combat game, Exercise (usually sword fighting or sometimes physical challenges). But I can easily think of a friend who likes Comprehension and Versatility but avoids Competition and Exercise. He's also big into Exploration - he knows the lore documents of the game inside and out and is disappointed when NPCs that represent a given in-game culture don't know their lore well. By using this list of motives, I can describe him as a unique individual (not a person who fits into one of three camps) and know how to put content into an event that will make him happy. I find that EXTREMELY useful in a day-to-day way, whereas I find TBM to only be good for abstract ivory tower analysis.


    Here's the thing - TBM assumes (or, to be fair, I assume after reading it) that the players in a game all need to be working toward the same creative agenda in order to have productive play. But when you're working with groups of 20-50 or more players, that level of agreement just isn't going to happen. With that many people, you're bound to get some amount of incoherence.

    I think that by using a vocabulary of motives, players and organizers can have a more fruitful dialogue about what people are looking for. Organizers can advertise more effectively about what elements they are planning to support heavily and how. Players can more effectively describe what they are looking for, and perhaps help other players get what they are wanting. And organizers can observe their players and identify more effectively what motives are present that are not being addressed.


    Discussions of The Big Model (I've started reading some of Moreno's linked threads at the Forge) tend to assume that the mechanics and ephemera and color and character and all that are just means to an end. That by focusing on those things, people are not seeing the forest for the trees. But trees, man. Those are important. Some people really like elms a lot more than pine. Some people like climbing and others like turning them into cabinets and still others love to watch things burn. Sure, it's a forest, and that's great. But man, look at all those trees. Think of what you could do with them...

      \n Here's the thing.

      According to The Big Model, if you have functional play that doesn't have incoherence, you have one and only one creative agenda. That doesn't mean that you don't have competition or narrative or simulation elements present. But you always only have one creative agenda.

      That's what the model says. If you're looking at it like a 3 bubble Venn diagram, you're not using the model as designed. You're using something else inspired by it and misrepresenting what it says.

      Look at Moreno's links to Forge threads. I've started reading them but haven't finished. But Ron's pretty clear and adamant on that point.


      ---

      That's why I made a different list and gave it a different name. Because I don't want to confuse the tools I find useful with another set of tools that are vaguely related but similar on some key points.

      Personally, I think my list of Motives is much more useful in designing events, especially for a known group of players whom one has been able to observe at play before.

      And also, I think it's possible to have productive play with a group that has different priorities. It's easier if the group recognizes each other's priorities and is willing to feed them, even when they differentiate from one's own. I think that's what Levi was trying to express in his initial thread with Ron (in the first Frostfolk thread Moreno links to). And I don't think analyzing his group in terms of identifying their Creative Agenda did anything at all to help him express that idea and pick apart how it was working for his group. I think my Motives list is a tool far better suited to that task, though arguably not perfect, largely because it was designed with larp in mind rather than tabletop. Like using a flat head screwdriver on a combo slot/phillips screw - it gets the job done but can result in some slippage you might not get with a different tool.

      - theory is, to me, a way to explore what I have done; role-playing games. I have played them, led them, and designed them. Being so engaged in role-playing games, as a professional leader and designer of them, I need to understand them. Theory is one way to do that.

      My theoretical efforts comes second to my relentless practice and uncompromising experimentations. It is inspired and informed by my extensive catalogue of design, play and discussions through the years.

      The model I have made present the quintessence of what I have done. And it is simple;
      - The 3-step Model consist of what I have given you here, nothing more. It is, first and foremost, a system of terms, interlinked to give a good overview of what how any and all role-playing games pass through three main stages;
      - from the moment of creation (vision)
      - through actual game-play (interaction)
      - and towards the product of it all (fiction).

      The three terms "VISION -> INTERACTION -> FICION" is key to understanding how the full form behave in the various stages, and as a whole. I can say a lot about how it behaves in the vision-stage (the design of games and scenarios, and how players create their own visions within the parameters of a game-method, changing the method in lieu with their own ideas) ...
      - but have chosen to focus the model on the second stage; interaction, as that is where most people in rpgs have their practice.

      So the model is supported with a set of terms on interaction too;
      - the method (how we play) consisting of (but not limited by):
      --> the mechanics (material elements)
      --> the techniques (human elements)
      --> the setting (fictional elements)

      We need more words than these to talk about role-playing games, but we do not need much more of a system to get real. We need to see some totality of what we talk about; something akin to the large picture of role-playing games. Until we take into consideration the full extent of practice taking place in role-playing games, from design to product, we will not see what we are talking about. As a result we will discuss ourself into foggy positions time and again. As players and designers invariably do.

      A designer or player, or any other man/woman thinking and talking on role-playing games, need to develop a consciousness of the totality of the form. They need to experience the clarity of thought that follows from adopting such a position. The 3-step Model is a way to keep your head clear, when talking role-playing games. It is a reference point, a sound and solid base from which to venture forth in exploration of the form. It is a good position to start your journey into understanding.
