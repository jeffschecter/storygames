\nEdit almost four years later: Wow, it took me three and a half years to come up with a good system? Talk about stubborn. But the following thread is only historical, the good system is here: 5e "tapping" to keep track of action econ

So the primary (of several) reasons why I don\u2019t like to MC games that are Powered by the Apocalypse such as Dungeon World is that I found that I really missed the initative order. Without it, the game is more like a group conversation i.e. super difficult. With it, there\u2019s a clear order that\u2019s easy to follow.

But, I was rereading some DW play examples and they really highlight something I value \u2014 seamlessness between the standard combat actions (\u201cattack\u201d, \u201cdodge\u201d etc) and new, weird, on-the-fly actions.
I\u2019ve seen some of this seamlessness in 5e already, with some monsters chasing part of the party while the thief hid in the water, or characters climbing up bridges, and doing other weird things (which the game mechanically rewards with advantage).
But I want to kick it up.

As I\u2019ve mentioned, I currently keep track of the initiative order with a little piece of paper with 30 lines (descending), noting the first initial of each participant as their rolls come in.

So the idea I got was to ask for init rolls on a need-to-use basic, adding participants into the initative order one by one. Roll your init to join the fray.
And also, to the best of my knowledge, init is equal to dex at all times.
And also, in the RAW, you roll init before you roll for the surprise round even though init is meaningless in the first round if there is surprise. I\u2019m thinking of changing that.

I haven\u2019t thought this through yet, or tested it. I have a game tonight, but let me start by writing up some hypothetical exchanges.

Before: \u201cI shoot the wumpus.\u201d \u201cOK, everone roll for initative.\u201d This enters \u201cinitative mode\u201d and is a very clear line between it and \u201cstory mode\u201d.

Now: \u201cI shoot the wumpus.\u201d \u201cOK, roll dex first to see if you\u2019re fast enough, it\u2019s coming at you\u201d, and I\u2019ll add the W and the shooter\u2019s first initial to my 30-lines as they both roll. \u201cI want to get in there with my shield\u201d \u2014 \u201cHow fast? Dex roll.\u201d \u201c13\u201d \u201cThe wumpus is too quick, it mauls the shooter with\u201d ...roll, roll... \u201cit\u2019s teeth! Take 11 damage as you struggle with it, and it tries to sink it\u2019s claws in you but your armor has that covered\u201d. \u201cNow, shooter, you\u2019re tangled up with the wumpus, what do you do?\u201d \u201cI\u2019m too close to shoot now, I use my sword and take a swing at it!\u201d \u201cOK, roll for it! It\u2019s AC is 15\u201d ...roll, roll... \u201cIt takes 8 damage\u201d \u201cIt\u2019s barely broken out a sweat. OK, shieldy, it\u2019s over there, biting into your shooter pal. What do you do?\u201d

Another (obvious) example: \u201cWe both reach for that sword.\u201d \u201cOK, both roll dex to see who\u2019s fastest\u201d and the rolls will be their positions in the 30-lines.

    \n To clarify what\u2019d be going on:
    in normal 5e, every person rolls initiative before combat begins. Like a ritual phrase, \u201croll for initiative\u201d. Every number that people report I place on a little chart, the \u201cinitiative order\u201d.

    Here, instead of a formal \u201clet\u2019s go around the table, what did everyone roll\u201d, the rolls would come in more piecemeal as people entered the fray (or fled from it, or did something else).

    So let\u2019s say in the normal game, we\u2019d be in story mode, and then boom, a fight, let\u2019s enter initative mode, everyone rolls as normal and it\u2019s

    Wumpus: 17.
    Shooter: 15.
    Shieldy: 13.

    The wumpus would go first and then the shooter and then the shieldy.
    I want to mix it up, tangle everything up. (The purpose and goal of this is to encourage action beyond the standard \u201cattack\u201d, \u201cdodge\u201d, \u201cdash\u201d, \u201cdisengage\u201d etc, and instead see more weird things like climbing trees, grappling etc.)

    So in the \u201centering the fray\u201d-game, we\u2019re in story mode. Suddenly I find the need to have some initative order (because fighty stuff is going on). Let\u2019s say someone is aiming their bow at the wumpus, I want to know if they\u2019re fast enough because this creature is wildly angry at this party.
    The two roll it off in a dex context but I save the results on the initative tracker, let\u2019s say they roll a 17 and a 15:

    Wumpus: 17.
    Shooter: 15.
    When Shieldy wants to enter the fray, they\u2019ll roll another dex roll and we know which combatants they can go before, and which they have to go after. Let\u2019s say shieldy rolls a 13, and isn\u2019t in time to stop the wumpus from running up to the shooter and engaging in melee.

    We\u2019ll have
    Wumpus: 17.
    Shooter: 15.
    Shieldy: 13.
    and we\u2019ll stick to that for the rest of this fight which will hopefully be short. The idea is that there\u2019s less of a transition from \u201cstory mode\u201d to \u201cinitative mode\u201d. I can go less \u201cOK shieldy, it\u2019s your turn\u201d and more \u201cthis is what\u2019s going on, shieldy, what do you do!\u201d

    Another example, let\u2019s say that shieldy would\u2019ve won:
    Shieldy: 18.
    Wumpus: 17.
    Shooter: 15.

    Shooter starts aiming at the wumpus but the wumpus is faster (dex contest) and starts fighting the shooter. Shieldy wants to enter the fray, rolls dex and gets 18, and is alert enough to do something before wumpus has the chance to even move.
    Now I have all three numbers and can stick to that, but in the same \u201cwhat do you do\u201d-fashion as before. Translating all the actions to 5e rules rather than DW moves, and, importantly for me, having an order.


    That\u2019s the goal. I\u2019m not at all convinced that it\u2019ll work, I might be setting myself up to fail here, but I kind of want to give it a go.

    \n I think this is definitely worth experimenting with!

    To me, the question would be whether that feeling of freedom would carry through past the first round: after that you're back into locked initiative anyway, right?

    As Caiomhe points out, the "declare, roll, then have to re-declare" is a potential issue, because there's an extra step in there. But maybe it won't feel so bad, or you'll figure out a way to make it more interesting (e.g. your action declaration affects your odds of success, or your initiative roll - so maybe declaring an attack is more dangerous, because, if you lose initiative, you don't have your defenses ready, or something like that).

    Other models I like for initiative:

    * Free-and-clear

    The idea is that everyone declares their actions all at the same time, and only then we play through the action and see how it turns out. This can be exciting and chaotic.

    You can handle it one of three-four ways:

    - Assume that all actions are simultaneous. (So, for instance, two opponents can kill each other on the same round if both hit.) If two actions should contradict each other, whichever action rolls a better result takes precedence. (e.g., If you are trying to shoot Bob, and I'm trying to step in the way and block the shot, we compare my defense roll and your attack roll, and whoever rolls higher gets their way. If we're both trying to kill a monster, whoever's attack roll is better hits first, etc.)

    - The AW way (Vincent says AW is a conversation, but in some of his combat examples, he goes "round-by-round", with everyone declaring actions, and then the MC resolves them in whatever order seems most reasonable or most interesting). So, basically, just GM's call, in logical order according to the fiction or whatever seems most interesting dramatically.

    - Everyone declares at once, and we resolves in whatever order we like, but we use Dex rolls (or whatever) to handle any actions which are time-sensitive (like two people trying to grab something at the same time).

    - The Sorcerer way: everyone declares at once, and *then* we roll to see which order the actions take place in. (In Sorcerer, you just roll once, and that determines how fast AND how well you carry out your action, so success and initiative are based on the same roll.) This can be fun, especially if you declare that you're going to launch a fireball at the door, but in the meantime a monster pushes your friend in front of the door, and you hit them instead.

    * The Marvel way

    Here, we find some way to determine the first person to start, and then that person chooses who goes next. A little meta, in a sense, which could bother some D&D players, but simple and works really well. Good for strategic players, who can try to pull off convoluted plans more easily (compared to, say, the chaos of the Sorcerer method).

    * The players go first, always.

    This is the old D&D method, and it works surprisingly well. It basically feels like AW/DW: the GM announces what the opposition is doing, and then says, "what do you do?" Then we resolve the players' actions.

    * Reverse declaration and initiative

    Everyone rolls initiative, then declares actions in *reverse order*. The worst initiative has to declare what they're doing first, before they know what anyone else is doing. Then, resolve actions in initiative order. So the best initiative gets to hear what everyone else is doing and THEN choose their action and pull it off first.

    Makes initiative really, really valuable.

    In case you're not familiar, there's also Eero's take on B/X D&D initiative, which I've used and really enjoyed:

    Both sides roll 1d6. The winner "has the initiative" for a number of rounds equal to the difference between the two rolls.

    Especially interesting if a) you extend your interpretation of "initiative" to things beyond simply who-goes-first, and b) you come up with a fun rule for ties. (I use a players' choice, choosing between first action and careful positioning.)

    Another way to run initiative in B/X D&D:

    The group assigns a leader/caller. That person's initiative stands for the whole group. However, particularly fast characters can use their personal roll, instead.

    This allows an unusually fast character a chance to always act first, even if "his side" is losing the initiative.

Some of the most tense, emotionally charged scenes of violence were resolved through a single move, plus maybe one help/interfere move.

    You could make this feel different by having some kind of key phrase to announce it: "I try to react faster than my opponent!" "Ok, roll Dex."

    And only then do you say what you do. In other words, you've now attached a fictional action or trigger to the attempt to take the initiative.

    The default case is that the opposition does something... but you, being a hero, can attempt to act faster, which requires a roll.

    2. You declare and then roll, as you have it. But the declaration is *important* anyway. Don't throw it away once you roll the dice. In this case, you have to carry through with your announced action, or some such.

    You could have both sides roll their actions at the same time, but apply the effects one-by-one, for example.

    Or the declaration affects you in some other way: there's a penalty to choosing a different action, or some other reason it is important even if you lose initiative.

    3. Have some version of free-and-clear, where everyone announces their actions, but the Dex roll determines the order in which things happen. (This is kind of the opposite of what you're looking for, but fun in other ways.)

    4. Maybe another way would be to make it a choice: do you want to follow your reason, or your instincts? In one case, you get to roll and act first (or at least potentially act first); in the other, you cede initiative to your opponent, but maybe you derive some bonus from that, or you have a wider scope of available actions (this could be represented by receiving more information about the current situation from the GM, for instance - waiting and delaying your action allows you to act on better, more thorough information).

    Of course, you may be perfectly happy with it as is, anyhow!

      \n I don't know if this will be helpful or not, but this is what I've come up with over time.

      There is no initiative. Everyone acts at the same time.

      Rolls determine if your character's action (not intent) is successful or not.

      I apply modifiers to the rolls based on what the characters are doing. For example: If someone is shooting at you and you decide to duck or run behind a corner, you apply some penalty dice to the attacker's roll.

      The way it works is like this:

      A player (usually the DM) declares an action for a character.
      The player of the character targeted by that action declares a response; that same player declares an active action. (This is more of a table/person thing than anything else.)
      Repeat until all actions are made.
      Anyone can change actions until everyone's good with what their characters are doing.
      Modifiers are applied.
      Rolls are made.
      Outcomes are determined.

      "Who goes first" isn't important; what matters is who is successful. If your success relies on you going first, then that's what a successful roll means.

      \n So this whole thing started out with wanting to bring the good parts of Dungeon World's (lack of) initiative to a system that still had some sort of formal spotlight dividing (or action economy) system. I'm currently running D&D 5e so that's what I'm working with.
      What are those "good parts"?

      1. A sense of urgency and directness: "The golem is running towards you, axe in hand, and he comes closer -- what do you do?"
      2. A sense of "tactical infinity", anything is possible, not just seeing the world through an interface of predefined at-will attacks and canned actions. "I dive underneath the axe and go for the dam, trying to get to the water".
      3. Primarily to foster 1 & 2 rather than for its own sake, but: a seamlessness, eroding the boundaries between "story mode" and "combat rounds", a directness. Describing the events in the shared fictional space as they happen, not before they would happen. This '3' is specifically what I haven't liked about "everyone first say what they want to do, then we resolve it". It introduces a dissonant time gap.

      Now, such a two-step, "free and clear"-system isn't what you (Dave L) are proposing. If I understand it correctly, instead of divorcing the temporal aspects of actions (initiative) from the qualitative (hit or miss), you use the timing to inform the narration of failure/success of the to-hit. I.e. I already narrate successful to-hits as creating a tactical or positional advantage (especially when hp is still high) and failed to-hits as a positional stalemate or "evenly matched fighting" with i.e. mutually successful parries.
      It seems to me than the system you are proposing would work great.
      I see two (solvable) problems with it. One is that things like movement, diving into lakes etc often don't require any rolls in 5e. A lot of the "Defy Danger"-type actions fall into this category -- their success or failure is instead dependant on position in the initiative order. (The more I think about it, the more genius the six-statted Defy Danger from DW looks, and I realize that I want to codify practices for my self that closely match applications of Defy Danger while still hewing close to the D&D raw.) But this doesn't seem like that big of a problem. Character A wants to get into the lake, Monster B wants to get a bash in with the axe before that. Just let (as you suggest) the monster's to-hit resolve that.
      Problem two might sound trivial, but it is that this system (I'll call it "temp-factor" for now) still doesn't give me a turn order for where to turn with my "What do you do?"-questions. I'd have to remember who have acted in a "round" and who hasn't.

      That brings me to the "bad parts" of DW for me. It's initiative system reminds me of turn-taking in a very informal group conversation. Which is a very difficult thing. When I've run it, sometimes everyone in the group feel that I gave everyone else more spotlight. It can feel like your turn is never coming up.

      \n Here is where I am currently:
      I want to have a fixed, "eternal" initiative list to start with. 10 + dex.
      From there, I want to follow it, going across the table describing DW-like situations and going "What do you do?"
      I can mix it up occasionally, if a particular situation seems more urgent, but only when I feel confident enough in the current state of "information juggling" that I'll be sure to get around to asking everyone in the round. I also want to foster a seamlessness between rounds, instead of a sharp divide between one round ending and another beginning, which is also an argument against mixing it up too much.

      Here are some of the "moves" (defined as "cloud talk with consequences in the dice talk") to be on the lookout for:
      Anything that obviously triggers a canned D&D action. For example, applying positional pressure by threatening or attempting physical harm with a weapon is already codified in the game as "making an attack" and causes a roll vs AC to happen. Similarly, I as DM rather than the players, need to be familiar with the other predefined actions such as dodging, helping, shoving, grappling etc.
      Anything that triggers a codified reaction. This both means actual Reactions (such as getting a free swipe at someone moving away from you) as well as things like saving from a blast spell etc.
      Moving around. This is tricky because it's so simple. "OK you do it" is unsatisfying after all that Defying Danger I was spoiled with in DW. There are a couple of different resolutions available. First of all, here is where 5e's simpler Opportunity Attack rules shine, they can be really evocative and give us that danger-defying feeling. But let's say it's a ranged situation. The elves are shooting arrows at you, what do you do? "I dive under the bridge!" In DW, it's a dex-based defy danger. In Dave L's system (as I understand it), the elves get a shot in against your AC -- if they miss, you dove quickly enough. In D&D raw, it's up to the initiative order, maybe they can try to hit you, maybe not. I think the Dave system implies a lot more free attacks, making "disengage" meaningless and almost gives even ranged combatants opportunity attacks. Also it disincentivizes things other than attacking: if you're getting shot at anyway, why not just shoot back? It also means fewer opportunities to create Advantage or Disadvantage.

      Here is where I previously suggested to have a dex contest for it (in the early rounds -- your roll in that contest also placed you on the initiative order for future rounds). My worked examples in this thread used this method. At the table, this proved confusing for one of my players. In the case of NPC shooter, PC jumper, it's clear enough, but not in the reversed situation since the player would be asked for both a dex roll and, if successful, also a to-hit roll. This confused him, he was all ready to make an attack roll and he was jarringly interrupted and asked for a dex roll.

      Let's look at the "static initiative" solution to this. If the PC is later in the initiative round, it's fair to give the npc archer's a roll so it's isomorphic to Dave's system here (and no extra attack has been granted to them, making it isomorpdic to the RAW as well). In DW terms, it's a "DM-facing AC-targeting Defy Danger move" (a.k.a. a normal boring attack roll from an NPC).
      Or maybe I'll have the player make a dex save instead. (BTW, I kind of miss "AC saves". Your "AC save bonus" would be your current AC minus 12, and the DC would be the opponent's to-hit bonus plus ten. E.g. a +4 Goblin hits your AC 14 character on a ten or higher (55%) so your +2 AC save would save you from a DC 14 attack on a 12 or higher (45%). But I digress.)

      What if the player is ahead in the init order? They'd just succeed with jumping in the water, perhaps this counts as a "dodge" towards the archers giving them a shot with disadvantage, or it just works ("the game fiction either triggering or not triggering a move. Sometimes, a player can just do something without rolling- attacking a helpless or unsuspecting enemy is a good example, so are common-sense situations like the one above." from a DW guide).

      Be on the lookout for things that can be represented by "dodge" and "delay". Dodge and delay are both very good at coping with temporal wonkiness. Anything slightly defensive? Boom, instant disad to the opponent. Anything that can't be done Now now? Bam, they just set up a delay.

      Anyway, I long to give this "static initiative" some table time. I'm committing to trying at least three full sessions with it and will report back with data.

      I've used this for many many sessions now. As a DM, I love it, love it love it love it. It absolutely succeeds in the goal of making the transition between fighting and non-fighting more seamless and it has encouraged doing some more non-combat action, mixing things up, making it more narrative.

      However, I've received consistent pushback from all the players. They don't like it. They want the "game-like", classic, roll for initiative, be in a turn order, is it my turn, what is going on style of 3e and 4e.

      As a compromise, I've started using a bowl (I use an upturned lid) with various colored wooden discs for the players and white stones for the monsters. If you're inside the bowl, you haven't used your action that round yet. If you're outside, you have. As the last person acts, instead of removing them, I add the others back in. Since I'm still jazzing around with the turn order (according to what fits best diegetically), it's not always the same person that's last.

      This has satisified the players; they no longer suspect that I give the monsters way too many actions (which I didn't), they have some insight in what's going on in my mind. I get the benefits of the system I've discussed in this thread but it's clear that "one action, one reaction, one bonus action and one movement per round" is enforced. I've used the bowl system for maybe half a dozen of sessions and it's working good so far.
