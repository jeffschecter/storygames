\n It all started with an Okkervil river song.
\n
\nI've got this crazy idea in my head for a game set entirely within the space of one man's death as he jumps from a bridge to take his own life.
\n
\nA series of scenes detail the man and his life and his death and his beliefs, with the players assuming faces and roles from his life. The whole thing is driven forward by questions, two of which are 'Why did he do it?' and 'What happens after?'.
\n
\nOne main inspiration is 'An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge' by Ambrose Bierce.
\n
\nAm I crazy, or could it work?

    Posted By: Graham WDavid, if we played this around the table, what would be happening?
    \nSomewhere in between those two. I imagine each scene has a theme or question, with some predetermined and some chosen in play. The trick then is choosing roles that might be appropriate. They might choose to play a scene with his wife or the memory of his wife or the 'voice' of his wife that rattles around in his head (or even all three if we want to get crazy).

    Then the scenes play out, with a combination of playing out the roles and of making assertions about what events actually happened or will happen.

    So it might be like:
    Me: Ok, in this scene we'll be dealing with why he did it.
    Player 1: I'll be the guy.
    Player 2: I'll be his wife
    Player 3: I'll be the guilty voice in his head over something he did.

    And they invent the backstory wholesale.

    These are just my vague ideas so far. I'm trying to figure out the structure and how much players can do in a scene mechanically speaking.

    \n Just on a side note, the 'fact' that the guy jumped can be messed with latter. So if he decides he doesn't want to die, the fact of his jumping is turned into 'he was just thinking about the act of jumping and what it entails'. Or if you want to add some fun mystical twist, he thinks he was just thinking about it, but actually there was some benign supernatural creature who saved him when he figured he wanted to live, but has made him think he just imagined jumping. Further, don't even make the mystical creature a fact, just a suggestion - a fruitful void so no one really knows if they are narrating the last seconds of a life, or a life reflected on in safety.
