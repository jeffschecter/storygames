\n I'm interested in exploring what games that embrace a "system matters" approach have done to emulate or capture the spirit of big summer blockbusters, the "cinematic" style of narrative as opposed to, say, episodic TV. I'm sure many games would have advocates who say their Savage Worlds or D&D4E; sessions are just like a Michael Bay movie or Jerry Bruckheimer production, but I don't know how much of that is baked into the system or if it's just stuff brought to the table by the players and GM.
\n
\nWhat are the essential tropes and expected play outcomes? What's irrelevant? What's still largely genre-dependent and thus moot? I'm especially interested in games that already exist in some fashion to model this kind of narrative, but hacks and tweaks to games to edge them that way would also be good.
\n
\nCheers,

    \nThe Shadow of Yesterday (and descendants like Lady Blackbird) have a few things they do to make the game embrace a big, cinematic structure.

    The first is keys: you have keys which flag and reward certain behavior. This helps players create three-dimensional characters with ambitions, weaknesses, obsessions and desires. Because you earn XP for specific actions, the audience learns about characters in a "show, don't tell" way. I think that's essential in a visual medium - to not describe a character's traits, but to prove them through their actions. Finally, you get XP (10!) by buying off your Key, which creates an awesome character arc: demonstrate who the character is, put stress on that character, witness them transform under that stress.

    Another part of it is gift dice. There are moments in most Hollywood movies where you know that there's absolutely no way that the hero will manage to complete their task, but you root for them anyways, and they somehow manage to squeak through. Gift dice are a mechanic that echo that.

    \n Cam, do you mean games that emulate the visual and narrative style of blockbusters or the form and structure of them?

    Because, as far as I know, aside from a few games like Geiger Counter, Mist-Robed Gate, and Fiasco, there are very few games that are structured to resemble a single movie, meant to be played to completion in 2-3 hours, with character introductions, rising action, climax/twist, and eventual resolution. Seems like you could definitely make a "ridiculous summer world-saving blockbuster" game in that model. Jason's Game Chef 2010 game, Danger Mountain!, is probably a good place to start for something like that. Another movie-focused game that comes to mind, also from Game Chef 2010, is Mike Olsen's Action City!, another game with a bang in its title.

    If you're talking about the roleplaying style that is often called "cinematic" -- where does that originally come from anyway? Feng Shui? HKAT? -- then that's something else and I'm less certain how to talk about it. It has to do with games where the description of actions matters to your success, yeah? Rather than just your raw stats? It's also the style that gave us "hero points" of various types and other player-authoring techniques, yeah?

    \n I played a Big Eyes Small Mouths hack where the GM gave us points every time we added action-movie narration onto our die rolls. "I shoot him" just lets you shoot him, but "I backflip through the window, firing upside-down as I pass" lets you shoot him and gets you a point. This was all the reward we needed to do what we'd already agreed to do anyway by playing a game pitched as "cinematic action". (The points were intended to be spent on cool team super-moves, e.g. Colossus throws Wolverine, but the GM never got around to creating them.)

    \n Each character has a unique flavor or ability that makes them integral. (Most movies and TV shows lack the internal monologues that give a character any real depth, so this is an artificial shortcut.)

    Each session ends on a cliffhanger. (Or in a movie, you get the tail-snipe after the credits.)

    All enemies are black and white. (Plenty of civilians, but the enemies are actually evil.)

    The object of conflict is to return the group to normal, i.e. even if a change in the political landscape or scenery takes place, the object of the game is to keep the group dynamic and the cast relationship in balance.

    ((These are all built in to Misspent Youth's design, first in the traits, then in scene 7. The last two points are the premise, but they're backed up by the "relationship questions" and the "authority figures."))

    I'm not sure if Misspent Youth is designed to be cinematic, but it has felt that way at the table.

    \n I think tweaks and hacks are helpful but it is really hard to offer players imagery in an rpg.

    The problem is how open ended rpgs are. It's easy to make board games cinematic because board games usually only tell one part of a story. My giant ape in Monsterpocalypse is never going to need to be in a tense negotiation, a struggling romance, or try to navigate a dangerous dungeon. This allows a board game to fill my head with plenty of words, models, and rules that inspire a fantastic fight scene from a monster movie.

    Offering solid imagery in an rpg is something that more or less requires a re-think of roleplaying. codification of plot structure, information presentation, resolution, all of it.

    \n Been thinking more on this

    When I say imagery I need to clarify what I'm talking about. I'm thinking of how words and writing use imagery. but I'm also talking about how systems and rules create their own sort of imagery and physics.

    So I'm not really talking about pictures, illustrations, sound effects, or bean bags hurled at high speeds. Though they help quite a bit.

    I'm talking about what players put into a system and what it spits out. In many rpgs the players will insert a context and the game will spit out a simple PASS, or FAIL result. It's then up to the game master or hippie to create the imagery for this result.

    Systematically these games only offer imagery that helps build the context of the pass or fail check, some games build on this imagery by offering, "attacks" "moves" "talents" ect, These features have little systematic hooks that build the imagery in a player's mind. The resulting effect of a sex move, the AOE template your mage drops and the resulting fire damage that last for a few rounds. These sorts of things are systematic imagery.

    a fantastic example are conditions in mouse guard, hungry, angry, tired, injured, sick. have way more imagery then an abstract number like HP-5

    Again I will point toward the challenges rpgs face because they are open ended. Systematic feedback is hard to make work in a multitude of situations, if you assign something more specific then \u201cpass\u201d or \u201cfail\u201d it's going to cover fewer situations.

    This asks the question. What situations do you cover with the system? And then leads to \u201cwhat situations are likely in the story I want to tell? This is what I mean by codifying a plot structure.

    Can you make \u201cplot\u201d a system? I think so, I think plot needs to be a system. I use systematic plots in my own game, and I have seen them in plenty of other story games. I write mine a little more specific then most because that makes it easy to plug systems inside of the plot, and helps biuld stronger context for the story.
