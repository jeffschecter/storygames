\n When I arrive, either Steve or Dave usually offers to make me tea. Usually, it's Steve who makes the first cup of the evening, and Dave who makes the cup after dinner.

There is a list, inside the door of the cupboard, which explains how different members like their tea. They are idiosyncratic: one likes a tiny amount of sugar, approximately a quarter of a teaspoon; one likes their tea black. I personally like herbal tea, for which I receive an appropriate amount of stick. I think it is because of my preference for herbal tea that I am not trusted to make real tea.

How do you arrange tea-making among your group? If you are foreign, you may tell us about the beverages native to your country, if you prefer.

Graham

    \n I'm freaked out by Graham's intense interest in these social conventions. However, it's fun to talk about. At our regular games, it works like this:

    - As the guests arrive, I ask if anyone wants coffee. Regardless of the answer, I start brewing coffee, or ask my wife Monica to brew coffee. When it's done, we ask who wants coffee, because we forgot, and most of the guests didn't hear us the first time anyway.
    - During dinner, there are bottles of coke and sprite on the table, as well as water. People usually help themselves to this, and make sure all the children present get what they need. (The children are good at letting us know).
    - Sometimes we might have red wine and/or beer at dinner. I ask who wants some, and give it to them, trying to ignore the sullen silence of the poor souls who have to drive that night.
    - In general, I _think_ I'm in charge of making sure people get coffee and alcohol. Sometimes it happens without my initiative or intervention, because I'm busy acting like I'm a real GM and people think what I say is important.

    \n In my old gaming group we made of game of being the "brew bitch".

    Starting from the moment you set foot in the house, if you uttered the sound "tee" you were immediately hit by a chorus of "milk and two sugars", "really milky please", "I'll have coffee", "yes please, thanks for offering", etc.

    IC or OOC, if you vocalised "tee" you were nailed. Words like "dexterity" (we were playing a lot of AD&D 2nd edition at the time), "beauty", "charity", "nasty", "TV" and suchlike were thus out of bounds. It was surprisingly easy to forget what you were saying when you were engrossed in an RPG moment, so someone always got stung every session. Meta-games such as trying to provoke someone into saying the sound were encouraged, especially if you were thirsty or otherwise gagging for a brew. You could always offer to be brew bitch, but that was considered weak. Substituting the sound "tango" for the "tee" in words was allowed, leading to nicely mangled sentences such as "You want a cup of tango", "That bird is a real beautango", "Anything on the tangovee?", etc.

    Once you were branded as the brew bitch, any beverage could be ordered and not only tea. But we're British so it was mainly proper builders tea with the odd heretic indulging in coffee.

    Cheerio

      \n For my group, it depends on the game.

      If we're high-trust game, then I'm happy to put the kettle on.       If we're low-trust, like a competitive game, then we're in a "go fuck yourself" attitude. Bring your own kettle, your own mug, your own tea. Or hope you have allies and have something worth offering. And then there's the issue of getting up in the middle of your game to freshen your cup. In short: don't unless you're already losing and want to be a cock about holding up the game.

      Which means that on Monday nights (my board game night), the thought of tea doesn't even come up. But it's cool on Fridays (my indie RPG night). Thursdays are weird -- depends on the mood, since we play 4/e.

      On the other hand, even in the most competitive game, beer bonds us together. Or hard liquor. Last Monday, we all enjoyed Manhattans while playing Race for the Galaxy. Clearly in our group, tea is a more violent drink.
