    I think that as games grow smaller, at least in certain circles, we'll begin seeing more and more patterns emerge. This may, however, require us to have a more flexible idea of 'published games'.

    So, let's look at two examples I'm fairly familiar with: Jonathan Walton and Shreyas Sampat.

    Jonathan Walton has 'published', in some sense of the word, a significant number of games including (but not limited to): Seadog Tuxedo, Kazekami Kyoto Kills Kublai Khan, Waiting for the Queen/Tea at Midnight, his unofficial game based on Avatar: the Last Airbender, and most recently his Exalted-inspired Anathema. That's five games off the top of my head. I'm pretty sure that every single one except for Seadog Tuxedo was written/published within the past twelve months.

    Shreyas Sampat has officially published even less than Jon, but he's got a lot of stuff out there for people who want to look: Torchbearer (arguably two games if one examines the original and current drafts), MSKR, Snow From Korea (again, two games, maybe three), The Golden Chain (working title), and The Garden (working title). Again, that's five games, more if you consider drafts rewritten from scratch with significant changes to be separate works.

    Part of any analysis I do of their works is going to be colored by personal relationships, but I do believe that both Jon and Shreyas exhibit extremely clear and consistent styles.

    Now, it may just be that they're part of a very small sub-group in RPG publishing less worried about finishing and perfecting games for comercial publication, and that the majority of designers simply aren't this prolific (mostly due to sharp focus on current projects, you'll note that many of the games listed above are either extremely simple, incomplete, or both; which is fine, really). But, for at least some selection of game design, there are people who are producing enough, and doing so in such a distinctive style, that I think auteuristic analysis is possible.

    (This is a digression into film-crit-land that can be ignored).

    Well, here's why we should use a French word when we're talking about the auteur theory (and why we probably shouldn't if we're not).

    The theory was developed by Francois Truffaut and his buddies at the Cahiers du cinema (mostly before they were filmmakers themselves) and popularized in America by Andrew Sarris. The reason to stick with the French word rather than switching to "author" is that these guys were talking about something very specific to filmmaking - and, really, specific to the studio-style filmmaking we associate most closely with 1930s/40s/50s Hollywood.

    To simplify (and to a certain extent parody) their argument, Truffaut et al. proposed that certain directors - "auteurs" - were able to sneak their personal vision into the films they were making, despite the fact that they usually didn't write the screenplays, were almost always working under tremendous studio-dictated constraints (with regard to casting, for example), and were often making B-movies for lowbrow audiences.

    The auteur theory was not really aimed at filmmakers who we might actually consider the primary authors of their films - like Ingmar Bergman, for example. (Granted, Truffaut, Rivette, Sarris, etc. all seem to be pretty arbitrary in their judgments of who's an auteur and who isn't).

    The theory was criticized almost from the beginning (by Andre Bazin and Pauline Kael, among others). One of the major points against it was that the authorship of movies - especially the studio movies that the auteur theorists were writing about - is a much more complicated affair. (See Pauline Kael's "Raising Kane" - a landmark anti-auteur piece).

    Now, I think the auteur theory, at least in its strongest form as articulated by Truffaut and Sarris, is bogus, but even if I did buy it, I'd argue that you can't apply it willy-nilly to other kinds of creative endeavors (especially solo-ish ones, like indie-RPG design or singing-songwriting or novel-writing). Its raison d'etre, after all, lay in the fact that movie-making is a collaborative process and it's often difficult to unravel the specifics of the various participants' creative contributions. (You might be able to talk about, say, old-time super-hero comics in this way).

    But do game designers have a certain personal style that they express from game-to-game - yeah, I think so.

    Ben, Truffaut had the excuse of actually being French, so his saying it in French seems OK to me. Also, he had the excuse of being an artist, so that his theory was really an excuse for doing what he was going to do anyway. When people like he and Godard changed their minds about how they wanted to make movies, they changed how they made movies, and so much the worse for the auteur.

    But really, I agree with you. The critics who adopted his manifesto as an actual theory were being really, really dumb. I understand their motivation -- a film is a really complex creative collaboration, and it's just easier to write and think as if the director is the author and the writers, actors, cinematographers, and production designers etc. are the tools the director uses to execute his or her vision. As a critic, you can then adopt all the techniques that you use when analzying a single-author novel. It's easier, but actually supposing the writers, actors, cinematographers and designers are not active creative collaborators is just amazingly dumb.

    We don't have to be that dumb.
