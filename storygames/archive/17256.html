\n Once a year I run a weekend long session for my 35 year-old AD&D campaign. That weekend is nearly upon us.

Last year our heroes saved the Jaw Peninsula (now Island) by diverting a tsunami intended to destroy all the coastal cities over the path of an invading army which numbered in the hundreds of thousands. For the duration of the flood, the Interstitial Guild suppressed teleportation which meant even the most powerful invaders were swept away.

My question is, what happened to the necromantic energy of those 500,000 souls?

    \n Everybody waits and expects something to happen, but nothing will because the universe is an uncaring place and the soul is a lie. Every religious figure the adventuring party encounters prophecies and waits in vigil for some awful consequences; churches are built and concecrated in the memory of important leaders who died in the event; singular survivors develop manias, becoming serial killers with psionic powers; signs and portents related to the event are seen all over the world; the land seems bleak and grey in the eyes of anyone who participated in the events, and they cannot sleep well, for their consciences are bothered by what they did.

    But nothing, nothing whatsoever is behind these events, and nothing major happens as a consequence, except if the players should make it so. Perhaps some godling or demon will claim otherwise, but always for its own benefit only.

    \n Well, everything seems fine until a few weeks later, when reports start coming in to town of all of that waste, that extinguished life. It has covered the floodplain, getting into the rivers. The physical decomposition is causing a flux, and it seems to be spreading across the land fast. A last hurrah of a dead army, perhaps?

    And meanwhile, the PCs find themselves hounded by a group of spirit talkers who exist only to fulfil the last wishes of the dead. Though the army might have sought to do evil, not every man who marched under its banner was bad themselves. In communing with them, whilst some might wish to exact vengeance upon those that killed them, maybe others merely wish to keep their families safe from the loan sharks they contracted to pay for their armour, or the problems plaguing their home, that were the real reason that they joined the military. Even a cameo, locket or family lucky charm that they want to be passed on to their next of kin can be a really engaging plot hook that can get the PCs to think about their actions, and put faces to nameless NPCs.

    \n Hey there! I'm Maraciel, and I'm a necromancer who was possessed by an angel of death, but we're cool now and get along just fine. Anyway, good question!

    This sort of event is going to attract a lot of psychopomps. Most of them are going to be very distracted and overworked, it'd be an easy time to try and abduct a few for your own grim purposes, and let me tell you, a few thousand souls is pocket change compared to a full-fledged psychopomp shackled to your will. Most psychopomps who deal with disasters and wars aren't used to operating in marine environments, so they'll be at a disadvantage and easier to bind, and it'll also be a rare opportunity to nab some spiritkrakens and anglergeists. I've already got two of the latter. TWO! Half of my peers don't even think these things are real! And let me tell you, if you're looking for spooky lair ambiance, one of these in a giant water-filled pillar of rune-etched glass can't be beaten, even completely disregarding the awesome power over life and death etc etc.

    Those trying to claim the souls of the dead directly are going to run into some logistical issues. Like, the prices of black onyxes and nightdiamonds are going to skyrocket over the course of the next few months, if not years. If you're working with making a ghost-behemoth, the question of where you're going to draw a binding circle large enough to amalgamate a few hundred souls, let alone tens of thousands, is a nontrivial matter. And how are you going to get them out of that now ludicrously haunted sea in the first place? I mean, sure, you could build a necromantic beacon somewhere along the coastline (expect to see a lot of those soon FYI) but those things attract adventurers like cockroaches. Not exactly metaphysically subtle (they really can't be if you want them to work as intended).
