\n Hi, new to role playing and LARP (female and never played D&D or anything like it) but I really want to get a group together to play immersive theater-style games. NAME: The Conclave: Trouble in the Hidden World

GENRE: Blended fantasy (werewolf, vampire, fey, witch/wizard, elf, etc) in modern day setting.

NARRATIVE SO FAR:
A group of Others meet in a dungeon or cave. It is dark, full of comforting shadows. *Me*, the wolf, has summoned them to conclave, after everyone having noted these events over the last several months. They discuss the growing danger in the world. The danger is as yet undefined, yet unsettling for most at conclave. There is a sense something is coming, but it is unclear what or why.
There are signs of things amiss in the hidden world. A raven fell out of the sky. A group of tadpoles died en masse in the river, before becoming frogs. Fires take longer to stoke, and are more difficult to keep burning. Humans sometimes seem to "see" us, to recognize us, more so than is usual for them. The night is long, but somehow not as safe.
And now...rabbits and deer are not as plentiful for the wolves. More humans are wearing crosses, making it difficult for the vampires to take nourishment from them. Flowers are not producing enough nector for the fey to make good honey wine. Spellcasting is becoming burdensome, taking more energy with lesser results.
What is causing this? It seems to be affecting the entire Other world. Who or what is behind it? (GOAL OF EVENING)

STORY ARC:
Weird and unsettling things are happening in the Hidden World. It has gotten to the point that a conclave is called, a gathering of Others who do not usually mingle with each other, in order to discuss the growing unease and what or who might be behind it. The goal of the evening/game is for the Others to discover the Villain-in-the-shadow, perhaps via video email announcement from the Villain at end of evening (video email could work because the setting is modern day).

PLOT ELEMENTS:
Why is Villain casting a pall over our world? What is Villain\u2019s motivation?
Subplot: One Other (unknown at first?) suspects, then accuses, another of being behind the weird and unsettling happenings.
Subplot: Vying for power or control regarding who is in charge of the plan to oust or defeat the Villain

SOUND EFFECTS:
General cave background \u2013 non-threatening
Heavy sub-bass thrum for announcement of VIS, or at various times to build ominous atmosphere
Wolf howl \u2013 "Franceso", annoyed at being left out of the conclave

    \n Hello! This sounds lovely. I'd like to play.

    One good question for players is: what do the players/characters do? If I've just started playing this game, what do I do? There's a hint of talking about the problems affecting the wolves, which I like. There's the overall goal of hunting for the villain. How does that translate to what I do in the game?

    I think it's useful to think about games that might serve as a model. For example: is your game like Werewolf, in which you must guess who the werewolf is? Or is it like Fiasco, in which all the characters have relationships and the game emerges from those? Or is it like a British freeform, in which all the characters have goals, and the game emerges from them pursuing their goals?

    What does it feel like to play this game? Is it a witch hunt, tense and fast, in which we hunt for the villain? Or is it slow and thoughtful, as we explore the problems affecting the wood?

    Lots of questions! I hope you don't mind.

    \n Yeah, I like the ideas here. Two questions that spring to mind for me:

    1) What are good reasons for the groups keeping apart?

    If the groups are supposed to be coming together, there need to be good reasons to stay apart (so there is tension), but ultimately stronger reasons to make an alliance.

    2) What is the method by which they will find out more about the Villian-in-the-Shadow?

    The traditional would be to scatter clues about it among different characters, which will add up to more if they pool their information - and possibly have further hints to be given later by you as organizer as the game goes on. A more story-gamish possibility is that the players can create their own information and create who the villain is through improvisation.

    I have an old Knutepunkt article on creating larps based around different factions, though it's not an easy read. The article is here:

    http://darkshire.net/jhkim/rpg/theory/narrative/immersivemethods-larp.html

    \n Not that I want to discourage you, but you seem like you're learning to swim by taking a cannonball into the deep end.

    Before trying to craft a game from scratch right off the bat, I would highly recommend getting your feet wet first with some pre-crafted games to get both you and your players used to some of the things you can do with the medium.

    There are some excellent repositories of free one-shot games that I would recommend:

    Mike Young's page has some great games. Humans Vs Monsters Diplomacy is a great game for 6 players, and Trapped is excellent for 4. Either would be a perfect way to host a first larp.
    http://www.interactivitiesink.com/larps/

    The RPG.Net list of free larps is also a good place to look. The one's from Mike's page are repeated there, and there are many others. The ones from Shifting Forest Storyworks are particularly worth checking out.
    http://wiki.rpg.net/index.php/LARP_Scenarios

    For anyone trying out the medium of larp for the first time on their own, especially someone interested in dramatic games (as opposed to sword-fighty games), I highly recommend starting with some well-trod ground rather than trying to do everything from scratch on your own right from the start.

    Also - campaigns (multiple, linked games) are hard. Start with one-shots. You need to learn about how certain things pace out before you can plan for a campaign arc. You would be surprised at how certain plot developments can happen lightning fast in a larp, while other things will drag on forever that you thought would take 10 minutes. And, in the end, some things are unpredictable and you need to learn to recognize how things are going and adjust on the fly.

    \n I think you've got a good start here.

    I've run LARPS for many years. I've written three and it took me several months to write the amount I did for the one shot games. The key to getting LARP to work is that you want to minimize the amount the players are dependent on you for information. I usually accomplish this in my games by writing character summaries for each character motivations and affiliations along with knowledge they have about other characters. I include these thing:
    *many different groups with motivations that can't see eye to eye.
    *information in different parts to different players who want different things from the information.
    *reasons for the players to interact and not to want to interact in interesting combinations.

    You've got a good setup for a lot of these.

    I would draft half page sheets for each player including:

    1. Name

    2. Affiliation with Villain (You should have more than one on that side and I also agree with one of the above posters that you should let a PC be the villain) and possibly two groups that want different things from resolution of the mystery... perhaps.. a group that wants to kill the Villain and another which wants to capture and use the power for their own ends. Strife is the key to any LARP and the more disagreement the more discussion and character interaction you will have.

    3. A small backstory with some events you've been privy to and some omen you saw in relation to the coming evil. Possibly a couple of bits of information on how to stop it.

    4. List of other characters and one line with what you know about that character (that character should know as well what they are not necessarily that others know).


    I wrote a dinner party one shot larp and went to the thrift store and picked up some cool knickknacks for a few dollars. Each of these were used in a final ritual to banish an evil type thing. The trick was getting them and getting them all on one side. It's kinda important to have timed events in a one shot to pace the game. For example, if the game will take 6 hours. Have 6 events with pertinent information distributed every hour. You should not be playing however. You will be needed to answer questions and allow PC's to enact plans and off screen action. I promise you will be too busy to play the main villain character. Running a one shot like this is a LOT of effort in game as well as before.

    Hope this helps to give you some ideas. Use what you want and damn the rest!

    The Golux

    \n So if you want to have all the players working together to solve a mystery, then what your mystery needs is roadblocks - specific things that are in the way of the answer.

    From the player's perspective, these things may appear as steps that lead toward the answer. But from a game design perspective, they're more like locked doors - things that need to be overcome to get to the answer.

    It's good that you're thinking about player creativity and wanting them to have lots of input into where things go. But it's hard to be creative in a vacuum. So it's probably best to have a certain amount of planned structure that you can make use of. You can always decide to drop some or all of it on the fly if players come up with something better.

    So you have initial information that you're giving to the players so that they can share it. Information about natural things dying or failing or whatever.

    So brainstorm a list of places that the information they already had could lead. Things that would get them closer to the big bad guy. In particular, try to make these be things that actually feel like they are making progress - things that feel like they are hinting at who/what the villain is. Have several of these, and be prepared to implement various ones depending on how the players are going.

    One way to structure it - no matter which thing they investigate first, they get Clue 1 out of it. Maybe Clue 1 is a letter with instructions to do some bad thing, signed vaguely (like with a single letter or something). So Clue 1 hints at what is coming, but doesn't really tell you what to do next.

    So you randomly investigate one of the other initial pieces of information. This leads to Clue 2. Maybe Clue 2 is something a bit more specific. Maybe it's a matchbook for a bar. So now they have a specific direction.

    So maybe when they get to the bar, someone approaches them and lets it be known that it has been noticed that they have been snooping around. So we start to have more of a direct threat.

    And then at this point, you should come up with multiple ways that the players could push toward some sort of confrontation and conclusion with the villain.

    Using this kind of a loose, flexible structure allows you to prepare some props in advance that guide the plot toward an interesting result, while still giving you tons of room to react to and involve the players in the creativity of the story.

    --

    Also, you could add in a physical puzzle for the players to work on solving. Those are often fun. And with a group of maybe 5 or so, it's not terribly hard to find something they can work on together.

    An example for something like that - you could take a game like Perfection (also sold as Scramble), and hide the pieces around the basement. Once the players find all the pieces and put them in place, they have solved the puzzle. Maybe completing this will summon the villain to them?

    Ok, so results of my parlor LARPish thing, The Conclave. Well, basically it went ok but not great. I had 6 players show up out of expected 7 players. I have been avoiding posting since I feel embarrassed and fear getting slammed, but I have a responsibility to you all who helped me. Read it all below, or skim and read only what you like.     Story and effects (short version): Secret meeting of supernatural others to discuss signs of trouble in their world. Discussed observed signs and speculated on what might be going on. Riddles arrived, and stones arrived when each riddle was solved correctly.     \n Congratulations on getting your first game going! It sounds really cool with the special effects and all and I really would love to play this game, plans on uploading it somewhere?
    I understand that you are not completely happy with the outcome and I recognize most of the problems that you point out from my own games. Below are what I try to keep in mind when I design a game:

    *The players must know what do, their characters need clear goals and ways to work towards them. In many larps I as a player feel lost at the start of the game, not knowing what to do or how to do it. Giving my character an important and clear goal to strive towards helps alleviate this and I can ju,p straight into the action. How to give characters goal?:
    - Goals in the backstory. Doesn't get much more straight forward than this; just state the goals of the charater in his/her backstory.
    - Goals through outside interference. What happends during play threatens the character or creates an opportunity to act on. A bit more unreliable than putting stuff in the backstory and also more open to interpretation from the players.
    - Relationships as agendas. My favourite. For this I use a map of the relationships of the characters indicating characters with "dots" and relationships with "arrows connecting the dots". I divide possible relationships into three categories: "active", "reactive" and "passive". "Active" means that the relationship in itself creates a clear goal for the character (for example: "Loves and wants to marry") to work towards. "Reactive" is in itself not a goal but can be activated through what happends during play (for example "Wants to protect"). Passive relationships can often look like active or reactive ones but really gives nothing to play (for example "brother/sister of"). Passive relationships could be interpereted by the players into being active/reactive but I prefer not to gamble with that. On my relationship map I create triangles by drawing relationship arrows connecting the dots. I then fill out the relationships opting for at least one actve and one reactive relationship per triangle, always considering what could lead to dramatic conflict ("love triangles", etc). ((side note: active relationships can probably be divided into the three subcategories "wants possession of", "wants to destroy" and "wants to preserve" (this last one often lads in the reactive category though)))

    Losing or achieving your goal can be very detrimental to play as it removes the characters' raison d'etre. Make sure goals can not be achieved until the end or even not at all.

    If you want characters to interact in smaller groups then you must give them strong incentives to do so. Playing in smaller groups is scary as it outs more focus on the individual players and getting a group to separate can be problematic (this has happened to me more than once when I added content that was only useful in smaller groups but no incentives to split up). Create strong goals aimed at this.

    Those are the things i try to keep in mind.

    Also, as you may already have done, if you want your players to be part of creating the "story" then tell them that beforehand to prevent situations where players are just waiting for the "next event" and just being reactive instead of taking the initiative and pursuing their own agendas.
