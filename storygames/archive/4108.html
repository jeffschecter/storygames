\n Hi! I might be starting a PTA series in a couple of days, it will be the third one for me and I feel very comfortable with the system and general techniques we will use. I have been the Producer for both of the earlier games we played, and now that I started thinking about it I realized that I never really thought that much about the way I prep a PTA game. Now obviously you can't go and prepare too much stuff since everyone around the table has more or less equal input into what's going on in the fiction, but perhaps there are some tricks or techniques that are more suitable than others for the game? I think I usually just come up with a couple of bang-like scenes that I can use to get the ball rolling, but more often than not I find that they're not really needed.
\n
\nSo, does anyone have any neat little tricks or just hings they always do that seem to work? Things you've tried that definitely did NOT work?

    \n

    \n
    \n Usually, my prep involves looking at the Issues and where the scores stand, and talking to the cast about an opening scene that hits on the issue of anyone with a score of 2 or more for that episode. Once I get buy-in, I think about how to frame that scene for maximum effect. So, the players pretty much know how the episode's going to start before we even sit down to play.

    I find this kickstarts the momentum from the get-go pretty nicely. If we're all like, "So, this week, Lord Coxstar totally has to blow up Nikki's parents' house and touch off her Uncontrolled Rage issue," in the pre-game powwow, the cast has had some time to think about where to go from there.

    Here's an interesting thing, though: I break this pattern regarding spotlight episodes. Usually, I'll make up Bangs for each person's spotlight episode pretty much after the pilot or first episode, and not consult the players about them at all. Of course, if a player gets a strong idea of what they want to confront in their spotlight, their ideas take precedence, and I always tell each group that I'm deliberately keeping their spotlight Bangs from them, in case they have a problem with that.

    Like, I had one game where a protagonist had a self-loathing issue, because since his wife died, he turned gradually into a person he knew she'd have hated. So I was like, "Clearly, he has to find out that his wife had a daughter he didn't know about, who shows up in his life to call a lot of his assumptions into question, and/or give him the opportunity to start redeeming himself."

    Usually, it's worked out to decent effect, because the big confrontation with their issue comes as a complete surprise, which can create stronger visceral reactions.
