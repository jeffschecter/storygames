\n First question: Do you ever sign up for a convention game that sounds interesting, but is a system or setting that you're unfamiliar with?

Second question: If you're sitting down to that game, what are most helpful things for you, as a player, to get your head around the game?

For sake of argument, I'm assuming that the person running the game is passionate about it, and able to display knowledge and confidence with the rules.

Are artwork and/or pictures helpful to give you the feel of the setting? Is it helpful to have a simple relationship map of the other characters? Would a name tent card that had a small cheat sheet on the back of it (facing you) be useful?

Do you prefer to get a quick verbal run-through of game mechanics, a one-page reference sheet, or learn as you go? Do you like games that have a kind of tutorial section to them in the beginning?

Assuming you've got a one page character sheet, and a one page background sheet, what other handouts would be helpful? Rules cheat sheet? Relationship map? Illustrations of relevant setting elements?

I haven't run a convention game in a few years, and I'm trying to get a feel for how to bring people into a new game in four hours without overwhelming them. I'm a visual person, so I love character and setting illustrations and handouts and all that jazz.

    \n I prefer to learn as I go, subsystem by subsystem. Ideally the session will be structured so that they can be introduced gradually. If there's something you need to know for the whole thing to work (like spends in Trail of Cthulhu), obviously that needs to be laid out right away, but the intricacies of conflict probably don't.

    I like handouts and play aids that reduce the cognitive burden - relationship maps, map maps, reference sheets, etc. Unless you can make up a guy in ten minutes or less, pre-gens, hopefully interesting ones pointed at the situation and eachother. If my dude has special stuff, include a page number reference on the pre-gen character sheet.

    \n Goals

    One of the biggest things that make me feel comfortable in a game, especially one in which the setting or mechanics are foreign, is to have my character strongly pointed in a direction at the beginning.
    So give my character something to work at while you explain the mechanics. Give me something setting appropriate to try to achieve while you tell me about the setting. Once I'm setting literate and mechanics knowledgeable, I'll be able to make my own choices for the character. But at the start not knowing where to go, along with not understanding how to get there or where I am, is a bad hat-trick.

    If you don't feel right about railroading players into goals on pregens, then you can always ask leading questions and help get the character set on some appropriate direction.

    After that its subsystems for mechanics and limited exposition on setting.

    Don't give me big setting dumps of information. Sliver it up and feed it to me in scenes where it makes sense. Help players win at interactions with settings until they're comfortable with the setting.
    'you know that he's a brigand of band X by the ritual scar above his left eye', rather than just 'you see a brigand with a scar' type of thing.

    \n I would totally dig all of those things as aids in getting into a con game, Darren.

    One thing that would be handy to me right off is to have it laid out what roles there are in the game (Is there a GM, or a Facilitator, or is there a round-controller that switches mechanically somehow?) and especially say what we're meant to be doing as players - like, is it kind of a two-player-game-while-sitting-next-to-others like Don't Rest Your Head, or is it stab-the-other-players like In A Wicked Age or etc. Like, answer the question "Where is the fun in this game?" And that'll tell me what to do. Danger Patrol: It's fun to describe wacky reasons why other players' actions endanger them, which actually helps the other player; and it's also fun to layer everything in the fiction with a retro-future sheen. Now i know what i'm supposed to do.

    Also, yeah totally, lots of guidance cards can't go wrong. Unless it's impossible, let me make a few choices for my pre-gen character - like, the first time i played 4E D&D was at a con, and i got to name my guy. That was enough for me. Don't make me make mechanical choices off the get-go if i could make stupid or less-effective choices, compare 4E D&D to Apocalypse World in this case, i feel like AW doesn't have "bad" choices in character creation, except maybe "Oh, i made a character that doesn't fit well with the others in a short con game, or one that i don't actually want to play."

    I've gotten a bad habit of assuming that i can just make anything up in settings, so guidelines around that & pictures and stuff would certainly keep me on track. (for the record, the bad habit is in the assuming.)

    i prefer a quick runthrough of the game procedure, like a tour through the halls, pointing out the bathroom and the kitchen without going in and standing near the toilet and showing me where the toilet paper is hanging. So, procedure and rhythm but only a nod to the mechanics?

    i def. want a rules cheat sheet if i'm in competition with other people, 'cause i don't want to go tipping my hand about my plans if i need to ask about a rule, but otherwise i'd be fine without. But i, the individual, can always just hand that sheet back to you, whilst Jason (seated to my left) would really appreciate his.

    Yay! What are you running, and where?

    \n My attention span tends to wane during those initial "run-throughs", so I prefer a quick "here is the feel of the game, here is the level of complexity, and here is what is/isn't cool to do during gameplay" before diving in.
    \n
    \nI learn by doing, mostly, but having visual aids is awesome! Take the cheat sheets and other visual aids for Fiasco for example; I totally dig them! Anything that takes a game and breaks it up into easy bits (BW pun not intended) is my friend. I hate being taken away from the story to dig through pages just to find that one skill, power, what-have-you.
    \n
    \nIf we're going with a more traditional game, give me a card with the important powers and/or skills (maybe a brief description if it isn't self-explanatory). Better yet, put that card on the character sheet (and the less crap on the character sheet the better). Illustrations are pretty, but not all that useful for me.
