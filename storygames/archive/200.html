Does my narration have to be disliked in conflicts?

jhkim
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
OK, so here's my dilemma.

Some games define a conflict as solely between players. That is, there isn't a conflict unless another player objects to your narration. A simple example is Soap. The mechanic is that anyone can enter any sentence of narration on their turn. The only way that it doesn't happen is if another player objects to that sentence.

Here's what I found when playing Soap with a few friends -- play could go around for several rounds of all the players without an objection. Even if bad things were happening, players were often content to let them occur. In order to create a conflict, I had to narrate something that was not only bad for another character -- I had to narrate it such that the player didn't like it. This worked in a sense, but I was troubled by this. It's much much better in Polaris, because one player (The Mistaken) has the designated job of coming up with narration which the player opposite won't like. In Polaris, I'm not really playing for what I want as a person. Rather, I'm sort of role-playing an abstraction ("What the forces of the Mistaken want") rather than a specific character. So I (John) don't want the game to turn out that Bill's PC suffers as much as possible and never succeeds. However, as a player of the Mistaken, that is my job and I do it. "Drifter's Escape" is also good for this -- two players are "role-playing" the Devil and "the Man". That gives a direction for what to play. If I'm playing the Devil, I call for a conflict if something happens which the Devil doesn't like.

In some other games, rolls are called for at defined times. For example, whenever a minion wants to do Violence or Villainy to a townsperson, the player has to make the appropriate roll. In "Breaking the Ice", there is an attraction roll for each scene. Really, calling for rolls at specific times is common in many RPGs.

So how do we define conflicts, or more generally the need for resolution? I see a few functional options here:

1) Define conflict as between characters, and give ownership of certain characters to different players. When a character of one player comes into conflict with a character of another, then the players may agree to a roll.

2) Define abstract roles for different players, like the Mistaken. A players "role-plays" this force and what it wants.

3) Define when rolls should happen independent of the wishes of the characters.

What are the advantages and disadvantages of each of these?

    Hmm, I just see myself disagreeing on principle fairly often, not because I dislike narration, but because I can think of something else interesting that could happen.

    I think this is just an off-shoot of stakes-setting. What you're looking for is not moments of disagreement, but moments when the act of choosing between two divergent but interesting alternatives will be fun for the group as a whole. Of course, if they are blowing you away with their narration of how things go wrong for their character, fine--but sometimes it is interesting to act as the advocate for the other side. Give them a chance to get out scott-free, to get away with murder for now. Force the possibility of being the only survivor, or the person who doesn't care about others on the character--just to see if it gets them riled up.

    If antagonists are not against the character but against the player, then you can be the antagonist by being against the player and for the character.

    It seems like we're getting caught up on the word "disagreement." I'm not actually seeing much, um, disagreement about the issue, though. Anyone?

    John, I stick by my claim and by that wording. If you have a different idea than another player, and you want to see which one happens -- even if both are cool -- I term that disagreement. I call it that because agreement would be picking one mutually and going with it. "What happens next, this or that?" Advocating a position necessitates that you "disagree" with at least one other player about what's going down.

    In my experience, Nine Worlds usually works something like this:

    Player: "I my Archon want to go to Mars. My character has a Join the Free Spartan League muse."

    GM: "Ok, you go to Mars."

    (The players agree. No conflict. Then, later ... )

    Player: "I want to join the Free Spartan League. Remember that Muse I have?"

    GM: "Um, neat. But, they won't let you in. You have to prove yourself first. Kick Leonidas IV's butt first in their new arena, and you're in."

    Player: "Awesome! Draw cards, man!"

    BUT, nine worlds can work like this:

    Player 1: "We're going to Mars."

    Player 2: "No, Mars sucks. Remember what happened last time? I don't want us to go there. Besides, we've both got Muses on Jupiter, let's go there."

    GM: "Sounds like a conflict to me. Draw. The winner decides where the aethership goes."

    Player 1: "Damn."

    I call both of these situations disagreement about what happens next. In the first example, when the player wanted to join the Free Spartan League, the GM could have just as easily have said "Right, you're in." The Game Master had a choice (as could other players in similar situaitons). He could have agreed to the player's desire to join, just like that. It would have resolved a Muse (which means I sorta lied eariler -- you can get rewarded in Nine Worlds outside of a conflict). The players would have been agreeing about what happens next. Agreement/disagreement, narration/adversity.

    Similarly, in the second example, Player 1 and Player 2 could have come to some kind of negotiation. Maybe Player 2 relents and goes to Mars. Or, maybe vice versa. If they can't resolve their ideas about what happens next in the story, the GM did the right thing there.

    Like I said, I term both disagreement between players about what happens next.
    Others do not dig this use of the word disagreement. (shrug) Seems to me we're talking about the same thing. That's cool.
