    \n Come to think of if, yes, every game has been about something political on some level when I've played or run. Even when I've used it with dungeon environments it's been in service of affecting someone with a political nature.

    I'm having a think about my history with the game. Most of these are one shots but three are campaigns. Even the least likely were political somehow.

    I did a Harry Potter-like dungeon crawl that was all in service of the politics of the house heads even if that was just the motivations and those characters didn't get much screen time.
    I did an orc game that became political when the baddie at the end started talking of his agenda to rule the tribe for the greater good and PCs turned on each other and died once they'd heard his words.
    I ran an orc campaign that was tribe politics from the start.
    I played in a dungeon crawl in which I became trapped but surviving in the dungeon but was never found because of one little thing one of the players did that plunged to outside world into war, causing the dungeon to be forgotten.
    I ran a city watch game that saw one of the characters hunting politicians until they all entered politics themselves.
    I played in a policially focussed game about the survival of a city with poor land and powerful enemies.
    I ran a dwarf game that turned into Doctor Strangelove.

    \n Two thoughts:

    Although some of the high-end (hard to reach) lifepaths are martial or sorcerous ones, most are at political - bishop, (dwarven) prince, (elven) althing, etc. That primes everybody to expect politics.

    Duel of Wits is great fun, simple to use, and can take the game in unexpected directions. Fight! can be fun, but is much harder to use and can quite easily leave your PC with a massive all-tasks penalty for a year.

    \n Glad you posted this, Dave.

    Even low-lifepath BW is pretty political, IME, it's just that the scope of what you can do is smaller at the outset.

    By the way, I think our earlier discussion got sidetracked a little by the issue of how tough it is to make sure you're playing "correctly"--following all the guidelines for how to write Beliefs found in the Adventure Burner, and things like that. The truth is, even if the game is played clumsily, it will still be *exactly* the game you described, Dave\u2014having to choose between personal and political gain, balancing the needs of various factions, and so on. It's just that the choices will be less compelling (or compelling choices will come less often), advancement may be slower, whatever.

    I mean, I think back on the very first time I played BW (outside of a con with Luke). Jonathan White was running, and I was a Dwarven Engineer. All I really wanted was to build giant trebuchets and other awesome war machines and fortifications. Which the system let me do! But inevitably and inexorably, I was drawn into all sorts of interpersonal and factional politics. There are all sorts of ways it wasn't an ideal game\u2014our Beliefs weren't well-structured, we didn't really understand Fight! positioning, etc. However, it absolutely and completely meets all your requirements, Dave.

    \n The game is guided what your beliefs are about but the conflict systems are there to further guide things. Eventually, an NPC is going to push on your beliefs and Fight! and Range & Cover won't be right, so you'll try to hammer the mofo with words ala Duel of Wits.

    \n That's a smart analysis, Matt. My guys "like" high-LP games but our low-LP games generate way better, more involved storylines. There's also the failure thing that comes with fewer LPs.

    I wanted to bring up one more thing, and that's the nature of Beliefs themselves. I think the artha cycle is a really flexible economic tool, but I've found that Beliefs mostly facilitate ego-driven heroes. I believe this big thing, and this is the big thing I'm gonna do to make that happen. That to-do element of "good" Belief writing, which I think kind of evolved over time as a BW best practice (and is obvious from the way the artha payouts are structured), I believe also leads to that ego-driven play. It's exciting to be self-actualized, and the game itself rewards you for it. Not a lot of hapless-heroes-floating-along type action in BW as a result.

    So, yeah. I think a lot of small elements feed into an overall tendency toward big politics.\n

    \n Well, here's the full story. I'm working on a game for Mendel's long-term-play endeavor (here). My inspiration was to take the "character actions ripple out and produce various significant consequences out there in the larger world" feature of some campaigns and really highlight that. So, my PCs are ambassadors, negotiating with various factions in the kingdom, trying to end wars and such, but also trying not to screw themselves and the kingdom in other ways by generating bad blood with unpopular actions. Being nice and being everyone's friend only gets you so far; sometimes it might seem like mercilessly crushing the opposition is the way to go, but then you have to worry that (a) they'll seek vengeance on your sister, (b) they'll start training warriors to wreak future havoc, (c) the next faction you meet will be wary of you, etc.

    I was excitedly telling Matt about this cool thing I'd provide for gamers, and his response was "anyone who plays Burning Wheel already gets that".

    So in this thread I'm trying to get a better idea of what BW provides, and what I might provide that'd be meaningfully different.
