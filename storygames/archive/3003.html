A good game can be played wrong

\n
\n
\n
\n\nClinton \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2007 \n edited May 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Three times in a week, I've played one of my own games. In every one, I could see where people broke the rules. Twice, this was fun, and a third time, it wasn't. The third time was with a game I have never really promoted, and I finally figured out why: it's not fun if you don't follow the rules exactly. In the other two cases, the game still worked and was fun, although I know if other rules were broken, it wouldn't be fun.

So here's what I learned:

- Expect people to play your game wrong. If you don't, you will design a brittle game.
- If you have rules that are inviolate - and these should be a small subset - make them prominent and easy to remember.

This seems really simple, but it's been a big insight for me.

    \n A brittle game! I really like that term.

    Nine Worlds is a brittle game. I have, to date, had a hard time getting people to play it. That's because it's a game that's basically impossible to play unless you follow the rules pretty strictly. Now, this is intentional. But, it also means the game is a tough sell for one person to convince his/her game group to play.

    I'm ok with Nine Worlds being brittle. It is precisely the game I wanted to make. I'm very pleased with it. But, it also helped me learned lessons -- some bad, but many more good -- about how people use my designs and designs in general.

    So, yeah. Kick butt term, I think.

    \n This is sort of a group answer to Jason and Doyce:

    Once was in "Inuma," which is something new. I have all these very symmetric areas in the game, and in one part of the character sheet, you're supposed to write action phrases (verb - object). Someone I was playing with wrote down something like "great cook," which isn't an action phrase at all, and I started to correct the player, but I realized it wouldn't be fun to do that.

    In TSOY, Jason was running a Bringing Down the Pain, and he did something totally strange to me: he determined whether a conflict was parallel or perpendicular by whether the abilities being used were associated with the same pool or not. It's not a bad rule, though, and it didn't really screw anything up.

    You guys are already getting the term wrong. Christian, Beast Hunters isn't brittle because there's one inviolate thing. Having the entire game be an inviolate thing is brittleness. Jason - keeping the inviolate rules with the GM is probably smart, in that less people have to remember them, but expecting your GM to remember everything is a bad idea.

    Matt, I never knew how to articulate what I don't like about Nine Worlds, but I think you're right. I look at it and think, "I can't play this right. It's too complex and I don't know what to do." That's a little different, in that I can't get a hand-hold on the rules, but it's related.

    The various PDQ games (DI, T&J, ZoZ) seem to be pretty damned flexible -- I've had players/customers change just about everything with the core engine [1] and have it still run pretty well.

    While I think this is strongly related to the fact that the system is extremely rules-light/rules-tight, I do think my constant yammering about "You bought the game, it's yours now: go koo-koo nuts with it, then come back and tell us all what you did, so we can ooh and ahh over how awesome you are!" helps a bit.

    [1] While changes are generally piecemeal, they've:
    * Expanded the PDQ Master Table
    * Renumbered the PDQ Master Table
    * Contracted Quality penumbras greatly
    * Expanded Quality penumbras greatly
    * Added Attributes/Traits
    * Replaced the Damage to Qualities system with a straight Hit Point system
    * Modified the Damage to Qualities system to only affect certain Qualities per conflict type
    * Rewritten Powers (shifting MOD-based effects to TN-based effects)
    * etc...

    In our crew, we've been using "brittle" and "flexible" to describe sets of rules. In Mechaton, for instance, there's a lot of shit you can do to change things to get the kind of experience you want to have, so long as you retain the core principles of the game. Dogs in the Vineyard is less so: skip a step and things start to rot, at first imperceptibly, then later they fester and consume all the fun.

    \n

    (I use Vincent's games because they're the ones I know best but aren't my own. 1001 Nights, Breaking the Ice, Shooting the Moon, and Sign In Stranger all have their own points of rigidity and flexibility, but I don't grok them and I grok V's. I can address the points of flexibility of my own games elsewhere.)
