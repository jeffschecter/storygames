\nThe Flowers of Evil is a game that can best be described as Lord Byron, Vampire Hunter meets Dangerous Liasons. It's about opium-addicted, syphilis-riddled, absinthe-drinking libertines who kill monsters (who are usually aristocrats who live in manors overlooking terrified villages) and take their stuff, but manage to severely piss off the locals and seduce the burghermeister's daughter in the process.

I'm nearly finished with the playtest document, and I need some folks to play it and give me feedback. If you're interested, you can whisper your email to me in this thread.

Any questions about The Flowers of Evil are welcome.

    Posted By: JesseI put my email in the pot because the idea in intrigues me and I'm willing to take on faith that the ideas mesh. I admit I'm not really excited by the prospect that this might be a little bit like a Gothic flavored Sons of Liberty or a Monster Hunter version of The Committee For The Exploration of Mysteries. (Note: Those are both really good games but are not the reason this material grabs me).

    I would like to SEE it though and judge for myself. I think it sounds neat.
    It's not at all like The Committe for the Exploration of Mysteries. I haven't played or read Sons of Liberty, so I wouldn't know.

    The system is a flavor of FATE, that is similar to Chronica Feudalis in that it uses polyhedral dice (for the moment). I've tweaked it quite a bit to get the feel I want. Even though you'll be able to see it's lineage, it's definitely a new game. There aren't a lot of new ideas, but I think they're combined in an interesting way.

    I'm designing it to be fast to pick up and play. I want people to be able to sit down with the book, follow the procedures for character and scenario creation, and be playing within 20 or 30 minutes.\n
    Posted By: spookyfanboyI wonder how much of the drama/tension in the game derives from your characters seeing just how far they can go without becoming monsters themselves...or worse yet, getting scared into conformity!
    That's a large part of it. I was partially inspired by that Nietzsche quote: Battle not with monsters, lest ye become a monster, and if you gaze into the abyss, the abyss gazes also into you.

    It's really hard for me to keep the game from getting too complex. The last think I want is a lot of tables and flowcharts and having to reference the index. My target audience is my wife, who is a non-gamer. If I can give her the book and have her run the game and have fun, then I've accomplished my goal.

    In between making clouds of smoke from popcorn and melted sugar, I've managed to simplify the game a good bit without losing much of what makes me love it. It's really important to me that it be accessible to non-gamers and a quick-and-easy pickup for more experienced players. It might be a little later than expected, but I'll have it to you guys pretty soon.
