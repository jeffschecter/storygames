    Mark, one pregnant source is anachronistic science and philosophy. If you're going back to 1500, you can discard germ theory, the periodic table, any understanding of sexual reproduction, the heliocentric solar system, and so on. What's the world look like when it's actually flat, diseases don't come from microbes but the wrath of God, where the female is simply the vessel for the male's generative seed, and all material things are made up of the four elements? Take a look at correspondence and association as the basis for thought, rather than causal relationships; that changes everything. If "as above, so below" is the unifying axiom of the universe, what effect does that have? And so on.

    Great topic! I have some notes for a rennaisance game I was working on. I wanted to capture the virulence of the Catholic - Protestant wars but to defuse the political touchiness of that topic.

    I had a pagan religion that was the successor of the ancient Roman religion, but had become centralized, i.e. with a Pope, Carindals, monasteries, and the trappings of the Catholic faith. The pope receives authority to work magic from the gods and passes it down to the cardinals and so on down the line. They use this heirarchy to enforce obedience (but there are always factions, heretics, and rogue branches out there).

    Then I had an emerging form of scientific magic or "natural philosophy". It's workings were a complex mix of alchemy, astrology, and science, but anyone could use it with the right training, knowledge, or experimentation. Of course the Church has done its best to outlaw and denounce it as demonic it as a potential threat to its foundation. The natural philosophers, of course, engage in their share of anti-Church agitating too.

    The upshot is a setting riven by magical/political conflict, but where the characters don't necessarily have to take sides. You could be a rogue bishop working for Queen Victoria, the Pope's own astrologer, or anything in between.

    "Hedge priests": this was about the period of the Friars (Franciscan and Dominican). Whatever their later integration into the hegemony, the early Franciscans in particular were a very discomforting group (like the Hebrew prophets) who were looked at very askance by the hierarchy and criticized it in no uncertain terms.

    Later, of course, and not necessarily much later, friars (who wandered around loose without a lot of oversight sometimes) were notorious for their scandalous lives and preying upon the gullible. Which is pretty much what they were accused of, falsely, early on.

    Something else for you to transform into the terms of your setting and play with if you wish.

    EDIT: I beg your pardon, I managed to slip that by 300 years. By 1500 the Friars were long established and had fallen a long way from the radical piety of their foundation in the 13th century.
