[M-B] Prizes vs. Threats

TonyLB
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Play Advice
So, I'm sitting here trying to figure out what the GM's role in Misery Bubblegum is.

Basically, the players are in charge of their internal and relationship conflicts. Literally, the cards are sitting right in front of them.

And, as the GM, I'm usually holding a bunch of "External Conflict" cards. But, really, those aren't singing ... like, at all. When you're dealing with "Relationship: You don't deserve what you have" a conflict like "External: Your world is under attack" can never be anything more than a grace note. At least that's what it's looking like in play-test.

And, looking at it, I think it's because the external conflicts are all phrased as threats and constraints. They're meant to inspire players to react, but all of the internal and relationship conflicts are meant to inspire players to act. There's an incompatibility there, and people are consistently choosing the active rather than the reactive course, and pushing the external conflicts to one side.

I'm thinking that something as simple as rephrasing the external conflicts as prizes rather than threats could have a substantial effect on game-play. Isn't that strange? But something like "Prize: Peace" (or "Prize: Safety" ... two difference conflicts there!) looks like it will work a lot better with the rest of the game than "Threat: External attack."

Does that make sense? Do you think I'm making too much of such a subtle change?

    Is there a way to make it a free choice of goods somehow? IOW, real life and fiction both produce outcomes in which any of the following could be the case, right?

    1. I get to go to the prom with Lisa and it makes me feel less alone.

    2. I get to go to the prom with Lisa and it makes me feel more alone than ever.

    3. I don't get to go to the prom with Lisa but I feel less alone anyway.

    4. I don't get to go to the prom with Lisa and it makes me feel more alone than ever.

    5. Feeling less alone causes Lisa to go to the prom with me.

    6. Feeling more alone causes Lisa to go to the prom with me.

    Any of which can be satisfying story outcomes. Picturing the game as meaning that an "I don't want to feel alone" card and a "I want Lisa to go to the prom" card are sitting on the table, the player should be able to "play for" either or both and "win" either or both.

    Sky High is a story where all the "good guys" win both cards. Spider-Man I is a story where Peter "wins" the external card - Mary Jane declares her love, but not the internal card (something like "I feel a crushing burden of responsibility".) In Spider-Man 2, Peter wins both cards, but there's a suggestion in that Helga-picturesque final shot that Mary Jane has picked up an unpleasant new internal one.

    While I can't think of an immediate example of "spurns the external card, wins the internal," it seems like a staple of a whole class of coming-of-age stories. For that matter, so does, "spurns the external, embraces the internal."

    I do that already, but the ties are just there in the creation. They don't carry over into the game itself. That's deliberate. It makes sure that the things are grabby enough that they could be tied to something, which means that they're often grabby enough to be tied to something completely unexpected later.

    For instance, we create "Where do I belong?" together with "You don't deserve what you have" to represent someone jealous of a newcomer. Then we create "I can't lose control" together with "Duty Calls" to represent someone torn between their duty and their passion. But in the actual play, we quickly find that "Where do I belong?" gets brought up more often in connection with "Duty Calls" than anything else, and so the story turns out to be about someone trying to define their place in the world by what they can do for those around them.

    Still trying to figure out how to mix that with Brand's "key to resolution" idea, which is a lot easier if they're resolved separately, one after the other.
