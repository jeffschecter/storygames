\n This is a rant.

Okay, so I've decided I can't take another night of roleplaying where the players all assume they're on some kind of fucking "team." Seriously, it's completely killing my desire to facilitate any sort of rpg night any more.

To be fair, this is an issue that's very explicitly restricted to a couple of my players. Frankly I think the issue is (lack of) trust and hypercompetitiveness: They're afraid that the moment two players enter a conflict, it's not only a fight to the death but also utter destruction of whatever facade of friendship they might have had outside the game as well. That is perhaps melodramatic. But it's how it feels from where I'm sitting.

So both these guys have begun to insist, nay demand, that we restrict their play to team-oriented stuff. They're perfectly fine directing their energies toward defeating whatever the GM cooks up, but God help the GM who wants to play facilitator and not Chief Punching Bag. Give them a mission and they're good to go. Give them a mission upon which to hang their personal character stories, the second those character stories take up more spotlight time than the mission, they get jettisoned as "irrelevant to completing the mission."

I look at team-oriented, mission-driven play and all I see is shitty fiction. I see weird, artificial stuff about niche protection and character build optimization, snuffing out any hope of individual character desires or interesting drama. I see the total destruction of any sort of self-actualization in the game -- wouldn't be good for the party, now, would it? *rollseyes* What I don't see, unfortunately, is any desire to play out the best team/mission stories in regular fiction: team members with conflicting agendas, team members who are loyal to each other but cannot rely on that loyalty, missions that put the team to the test, etc.

As a GM, I think I'm really goddamned good at neutrally facilitating play. My strong suit is setting up provocative situations where players can make statements about their characters through their decisions. Half my Group A players love this -- I mean really, really love it. My entire Group B appreciates this. For those guys, this is the Golden Age of roleplaying. For the other half, their reactions to my desire for them to "make statements" range from itchy discomfort to outright anger. The anger is the most surprising, and dismaying, reaction I can count on. How do you get pissed off playing a game? They must think we're all playing a different game, or we're playing it wrong.

I think this has been building for some time.

In reaction to my second, sadly unsuccessful, go at Dust Devils the other night, one of my players suggested he run Cyberpunk. I literally got sick to my stomach at the thought of it. Already I can hear the discussion of which guys should carry which kinds of cyberwear, and how to ensure we have an airtight spread of abilities so the GM can't screw us.

So I come to you asking for help: Help me not hate "teams." How do you build team-oriented, mission-focused play that alleviates the PVP fear (or whatever the fuck is going on), without crushing any hope I may have of individual stories and character-driven play?

    \n It's a tough situation, because in fiction, a well-functioning team is like a loaded gun. No gun should go unfired, and no team should go untested. But these players are telling you they don't enjoy it when the team is tested.

    I think you're taking the right approach here, trying to find a way to get what you want out of the game, without making the game all about stuff these guys obviously hate.

    Here's my best shot:

    When a team starts out as a functional, compatable unit, the most interesting thing that can happen is that something comes along which threatens that harmony. The more these guys work together, the more your GM instinct tells you to drive a wedge between them. There's interesting play there, and it drives a lot of game sessions.

    When two characters start out at complete odds, as total enemies, the most interesting thing that can happen is that something comes along which forces them to work together. My advice would be to talk to these guys about creating two characters who are at odds, and then seeing how well they can make them work together. The interesting stuff then happens when the characters are working together, not when they're driven apart. You can talk this out beforehand, so that you all know that it's safe, and they're not really going to be working at odds. The interesting stuff is happening where you all want it to - when they're working together. The trick to this is to make it so they never get comfortable together. As soon as they decide they're actually working well as a team, you've got to throw something in that drives them apart again, and that's going to bring it all crashing down.

    \n My group also loves teams. Hell, I love teams. All the bad experiences I had as a kid with dysfunctional intra-PC relationships, all the goddamn boring Mexican standoffs, all the divisive and petty one-upsmanship, all the lame and predictable melodrama, all the backstabbing and note-passing and "I stab the paladin when he's asleep" bullshit...I'm just over it. I remember 1988 well enough and I don't need to relive it. But seeing PCs working together, reserving the "serious" stakes for external conflicts and keeping the stories of the main characters largely supportive of each other (maybe with a little surface friction, but always pulling together in-character when the chips are down, always with the players being concerned out-of-character about including and supporting everyone else's efforts)...that's fun stuff! That's the kind of thing that makes me feel good about playing a game with my friends!


    So, here's my advice in a nutshell: either figure out what you can love about a cooperative, team-based game, or bow out gracefully and tell someone else to run a cooperative, team-based game to meet the needs of group A without you. If you genuinely can't enjoy running or playing in the style of game that they like, then don't: be honest with them about why you can't have fun the way they have fun, and step aside. It's really your only option -- the odds of you magically converting them to players who like to have their characters working at cross purposes is basically nil. (They know what they like and they've been telling you what they like, and that's something you should thank them for instead of telling them that you hate their preferences and think it can only produce "shitty fiction." Seriously, man, that's not cool.)


    You might -- might -- be able to generate the kind of interpersonal antagonism that you crave by farming out antagonist NPCs to the gang, so as to allow the players to really square off against each other (which they might enjoy) without forcing their characters to butt heads (which they definitely do NOT enjoy). And maybe that would take some of the "Chief Punching Bag" heartbreak away, too. Or play in a smaller group -- with only two or maybe three players, the competition for "spotlight time" is much less intense and there's a lot more room for personal stories without anyone feeling like they're wasting everyone else's time. Or negotiate a settlement -- the people who like playing teams make their characters a team, but the non-team-players can do their own thing.

    Or (and personally, I think this is a really long shot, but whatever) you could try to take deliberate and highly-telegraphed baby steps towards less cohesive "teams." Use analogies: "This game, we're all like 'The A-Team' -- BA and Murdock work together fine when the bad guys are shooting but never get along under any other circumstances." "This game, we're like 'Dexter' -- we're working the same case, but everyone's got their own agenda, from stealing the credit to covering up their own crimes, and we're going to have fun with seeing how everyone approaches the same task from different perspectives." "Okay, now let's try 'Reservoir Dogs' on for size -- you're a team in name only, and apart from the ONE TASK that you had to do as a team, you'd gleefully shoot each other in the face." Slowly work your way from optimal teams down to dysfunctional ones, and then see if it makes it easier to take that final step and abolish teams altogether...or maybe just luck out and find the intermediate level where you get enough of what you like without having to shit on what they like, and vice versa.

I'm still exploring in myself why I had such a visceral reaction at the thought of atavistic play; probably the same reason my two outliers have such a visceral reaction at the thought of character development and exploration of themes.

    Everything in this thread is absolutely reasonable and, of course, has been repeated and echoed around the web for years and years now. I know all this stuff. I do. But it's also really good to hear it all again.

    New blood: Honestly I couldn't take any more blood! I've got two very active, viable groups, maybe 12 people total I can tap almost at any time for any kind of gaming I'd ever want. 1.5 of those groups are having the time of their lives. The 0.5 guys are the ones digging in their heels. My short-term solution: Back off on RPing nights when those guys are involved, and do other kinds of gaming (their board/card/video game tastes are well established so it's very easy to keep everyone entertained). I won't tolerate being whined at, and that's what my outliers are fundamentally doing.

    I do think there's fertile ground within the concept of "team". I mean, we've had a grand time playing Lacuna -- but only because it can be a fun puzzle to solve. I don't believe I'll ever see agent-on-agent violence in Lacuna unless Control orders one of them to shoot an agent. We had a good time with InSpectres as well.

    The Stargate vs. BSG example made me smile, because that's precisely the matchup that was in my head. On that note, my personal perfect "team" dynamic would probably be something along the lines of Farscape: Everyone's forced together and have mutual interests, but individual agendas also crop up and threaten the team. Seven Samurai is in this vein as well.

    Andy: Terrific question. They'll argue in-character but, in-character, the emotions get amped up fast until it's obvious that it's the player's emotions, not the character's, we're feeling at the table. Author-stance play is, frankly, a deal-breaker for one of my two most ardent team/mission players. When a system requires they do so (Burning Wheel, Dust Devils), that's when he gets angriest. In DD, a player sided with an NPC in a conflict between that NPC and one of my team/mission players. The team/mission player took it as a personal slight that the player helped an "enemy" NPC and won, thereby causing his character Harm, thereby fucking up his effectiveness. i.e. "your helping that NPC was mechanically identical to shooting my character yourself."

    So...IMO they'll play in-character but not in-character with any sort of emotional investment. They'll go along with it when they're RPing with an NPC.

    Teasing this apart, I think I've got a good profile on my two team/mission players:

    T/M #1 has a social-contract problem with any sort of conflict outside what he feels is the "okay" one with the GM. PvP is not acceptable.

    T/M #2 is strongly competitive, and would happily go PvP against everyone...so long as he's winning due to tactical decisions. Tactical victories are okay, character-driven victories (I lost the fight but I stood by my ideals!) are not. This means he enters conflicts on a completely different basis than everyone else. In DD, he "won" his conflicts when he got more chips than everyone else, for example.

    And, KBob, of course you're right about all that (again). This is all talkable with my folks; we'll figure something out.

    Posted By: Paul BI think the issue is (lack of) trust and hypercompetitiveness: They're afraid that the moment two players enter a conflict, it's not only a fight to the death but also utter destruction of whatever facade of friendship they might have had outside the game as well.
    \nThis is an echo of a situation I went through with one of my groups about six months ago. Two players in the group had obvious personality conflicts and problems in their personal life. It occasionally came up as snide remarks at the game table, and more often in out-of-game conversation where the other player was not present, but usually there was a facade of strained politeness in place.

    At one point, perfectly reasonable fictional events led to an in-character overreaction, and guns came out. You could tell the players in question, who were also among the instigators, were visibly distressed. Push came to shove with some MyGuyisms, and one character got their arm blown off. Some heated out of character discussion later accompanied with some backpedaling weirdness, and suddenly the fiction has been rewound and altered such that the argument never happened. After the session, the player whose character got their arm blown off stressed how he really doesn't like inter-party conflicts, how he loves Shadowrun and mission-oriented RP, and so on. He never came back to that group, but joined us with a different set of people who play anything but, and is loving it.

    The other player, whom we still play with in that group, has since stopped a game of The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach mid-session and canceled a scheduled game of Paranoia because she "hates competitive games". Yet, her character seems to have no problem competing with others - in a sample of four, none of them have been particularly friendly with other "party members", and are often overtly antisocial and cruel with the requisite systematic backup in terms of Disadvantages, Traits etc.

    Judging from my own experiences, could it be possible - given the quote above - that you're not dealing with players who love team play, but players with such bad relationships with the rest of the players that they cannot handle even a fictional conflict between them? Or in Mike Holmes' terms, to prevent the bad blood which Nar play might reveal, they want to strictly immerse themselves in the rules of the Game?

    Team play can be cool, but as Simon said, a fully functional and cohesive team makes for bad fiction. More commonly, the team's goal is a McGuffin for revealing the fiction which lies within the team itself. If your players have issues with each other, these issues will arise and ruin any game, whether it's constrained by artificial cohesion or not.

    Perhaps this will help. Ask yourself if the players who "favour team play" actually play as a team - that is, their characters enthusiastically interact with others in a positive way - or if they play solo, and interactions with other characters are mostly dismissive or negative? If they actually interact in a positive, excited way with others in a team environment, then the suggestions others have given above are gold. If they try to play solo, and ignore other players, then they're not into team play at all, they just want to hide their social flaws behind a game system. This latter will probably mess up your game regardless, and all the team play advice in the world won't help.

Only on the third session did we kill the gnoll and return to the village to collect our reward.

    No character development. No back stories. No plot twists or betrayals. No lead-ins to some epic campaign. No fecking "story". Just a group of ass-kicking level 1 adventurers trying to collect a bounty.

    \n Well...there's "niche" as in "the job skills I bring to the operation". That can suck if that's all that ties together a team: I'm the thief because we need someone sneaky! Duh!

    I'm thinking there's also "niche" as in "the drama/personality/conflict I bring into this group of people." And I do think there's room for the concept of "protecting" that niche. Like...how many people in your group really need to be avenging the murder of their father? How many different ways can one group do "I'm devoted to the cause through obedience, but my cowardice may keep me from fulfilling my duties"?

    So...good teams. Stargate got me thinking, because while everyone's on the team via shared loyalties to their CO, as well as mutual loyalties forged through several seasons of friendship, each character on the show has his own character-driven thing. There's the guy who needs to balance honor against group loyalty. There's the guy who needs to be in charge. There's the guy who needs to do the right thing, even if it's not the expedient thing. So in game terms, they all share a job: go through the Gate and deal with the alien dickheads on the other side. But in story terms the "going through the gate" thing is just an excuse for them to work out their individual stuff.

    The team structure is there to ensure everyone has a story reason to share scenes, and that's valuable. Sometimes it can feel strained when players find themselves needing to look for reasons to stab each other in the face. This was, in fact, a Dust Devils thing: someone needed chips, so they looked at their Devil ("cruel") and started casting about for reasons to be kind while in a conflict. Since they didn't have an existing team thing in place (we're on in the same posse! we're employed by the same rail baron!) it felt like kind of a stretch to require that much authorship on the parts of the characters.

The idea being that we each had our skills and resources we brought to the coterie and we worked out various backstories and so on, had a good old time.
      \n
      \nIn play, though, the GM semi-rewrote a lot of the characters, their interests, their goals, and so on, leaving us uncertain as to how to participate in the world he was creating. Worst of all, he emphasized the divergent goals and de-emphasized the common goals, so all the work we'd done in creating relationships and backstories, which was itself there to support the fact that we like to banter and argue in-character, was pretty much wasted.
      \n
      \nAt one point several of the characters had collected a few plot tokens and wanted to share them with the rest of the group, so they called a meeting. We all pounced on the idea of having all the characters meet up, since they hadn't in three months of playing the game. My guy was the last to arrive. I described him coming into the meeting, the GM backed things up and said "As you are crawling out of the sewer, your cellphone rings." The group was so frustrated and pissed off that this team they'd done so much work on had not even been able to be in the same place, let alone work on things together, that the room exploded, almost in unison, with "NO IT DOESN'T!!!!" I naturally am a much calmer personage. I looked the GM in the eye, and said firmly, "I don't answer." Finally noticing how angry we were, the GM backed off and let us have the meeting.
      \n
      \nUnfortunately, too much had been laid in place for the team to 'stick together', even on a common goal (we don't mind splitting up a lot so long as we're all working on the same thing), so the game drizzled out a few sessions after that.
      \n
      \nThat was a situation where the "team" was where the interesting story for one group of people was, and where someone else did everything they could to block that.
