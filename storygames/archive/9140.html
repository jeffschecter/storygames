\n In light of the contest fever in the hobby, these past few weeks, I give you:

The RPG Contest Contest
1) Come up with a RPG Contest, or a contest related to producing a component of RPG play (e.g. one-level dungeon contest).

Submit the idea to this thread, with basic rules (no need to get all detailed with it, for now).

III) After some period of time (i.e. the thread sinks), I will declare that the Contest judging may begin: quote (or whisper to me to quote, if you're paranoid about anonymity) the NAME ONLY of the RPG Contest that you think would be:
* the most innovative in concept;
* the most likely to produce a sellable product;
* the best for bringing in new players to the hobby; or
* the damnedest fun regardless of outcomes.
ex) For one of my votes, I might quote "The RPG In A Brochure Contest" and vote it as "Best for bringing in new player."

four) Folks may also award special recognitions for Contest ideas that deserve them. For instance, I might present "The Emo, Misery Tourism RPG Contest" idea the "Ah, just give it to Morningstar and skip the hassle" award.

last) No prizes are offered, except the recognition of your mad genius... and maybe folks clamoring to have someone run you Contest idea, so they can actually participate in it.

    \nNumberless RPG Contest:

    It's a RPG design contest, where the major design constraint is that your game cannot use numbers to quantify things. No statistics, no ranking traits relative to one another, no expendable resources. No numbers on a character sheet. It might be necessary for a game to involve no randomizers at all to qualify, but I'm not entirely sure.


    (Very nearly every non-Scandinavian game I've seen relies on quantifying abstract things and comparing them. But I know it is possible to design games without any numbers involved. So I want to see other people do that, and see what wealth of options people can come up with in a different mode. What sort of conflict resolution can you do if you cannot compare the values of the opponent's stats? That sort of thing. I may not be explaining it very well, though. And it may work better worked into a larger design contest... say, used as a design constraint for some Game Chef like thingy, where you'd also get some sort of non-rules based constraints.)

    \n The thing I hate about RPG contests is that they are invariably a contest about the part of design that's already the most fun.
    \n
    \nI mean "creatively brainstorm an awesomely fun idea, then throw it together into some semi playable form that could usually be described at best as a pre-Alpha and call it a day"...wow...lets socially reward people for doing the FUN part of game design. Shouldn't the fun part of game design be its own reward?
    \n
    \nSo here's my entry.
    \n
    \nThe "Get that game published and do it right" contest.
    \n
    \nRules
    \n
    \n1) The game MUST be a game you already submitted to some other design contest at some point (ideally for your sake, one that's already made it to Beta level testing)
    \n2) You have exactly 1 year from the date the contest begins to submit your entry.
    \n3) Your entry will be judged on:
    \na) Proper editing...not just proofreading...EDITING
    \nb) Art Direction...how well does the art fit the game, how effectively was it used in the layout.
    \nc) Layout and Graphic Design
    \nd) Format...how well does the format of your design fit its intended distribution model (i.e. a game to be distributed solely by PDF can (and should) be laid out differently from one intended to enter the Three Tier).
    \ne) Expressing the Hook...for games intended for retail distribution this would include the effectiveness of the cover image and back cover blurb at getting people jazzed to take a look. It can also include Website effectiveness and online presence.
    \nf) Clarity of Text...the judges will play at least 1 session of your game in which they will evaluate the degree to which all of their questions about how to play are answered by your text.
    \n
    \nIf this contest is picked and someone organizes it, I will personally offer a $500 prize and a full page ad in a future Ramshead product to the winner.
    \n
    \n
    \nSee, to me THAT's a contest...recognizing the shit, sucks all to hell gruntwork that kicks in after the fun creative part ends -- The blood sweat and tears of actual craftsmanship is what seperates a game-design from a game-design-idea
    \n
    \nIf you don't hate and despise your game by the time its finished...it ain't finished. If you're still having fun working on it...you ain't even CLOSE to being finished. If you're so sick and tired of your game that you wish you'd never ever even thought of the idea and the thought of playing 1 more session of it makes you ill...now you're getting warm.

    \nThe Non-Mechanical Resolution Contest

    Entries may include procedures, guidelines, and advice, but no formalization of what is allowed to enter the fiction.

    Contributions enter the fiction via player agreement only. Your guidelines can lay out the bases for such agreement (e.g. "accept contributions that inspire you" or "accept contributions that seem logical") as long as they don't grant any specific authorities that impinge upon "free social agreement among players".

    Your job as a designer is to enable the play group to decide "what happens now?" at every instant of play despite the lack of a structured arbitration process.

    Entries need not include traditional player-character relationships. "Storytelling games" are fine. GMs also optional.

    Here are some ingredients to choose from when concocting the purpose of your game's fiction. You do not have to use one; this is just to help get you thinking:
    assemble
    deconstruct
    emulate
    innovate
    mash up
    pervert
    satirize
    simulate
    worship

    Submissions may not contain images, but feel free to do whatever you want with type and layout thereof.

    Judging will take place in two stages. Stage one evaluates presentation, stage two evaluates play.

    In stage one, 2 "official" judges will get the ball rolling by announcing which of the entries they'd like to play, in order from "most want to play" (10pts) to "just barely want to play" (1pt). Stage one judging will then remain open for one week, during which anyone can vote. At the end of stage one, the judges will tabulate the votes and list the vote totals for all games. The top vote-getter will win "best presentation", and the runner-up will also be acknowledged.

    In stage two, having seen which games generated the most interest, people will play the games and rate how well they played. The official judges must play the "best presentation" winner. Anyone who plays a game and rates it (again, 1-10) gets a free copy of the game they played if it is ever published. Ratings should be based on (1) how playable the game was, and (2) how much fun was had, tempered by judgment of how much of the fun was really supported by the game itself. Stage two will last for one month after stage one. At the end of stage two, ratings will be tallied and a "best game" winner will be announced.

    Rule I'd include if surveys found it to be non-prohibitive: to be eligible to win in stage two, you must play another contest game and rate it.

    \n Well, Ralph already killed a lot of my thunder, because my idea has some similarities to his (basically the year-long length), but here it is anyway:

    The Path to Publication Contest

    Rules
    1) The game must be a new idea for you. You cannot previously have posted about it online or talked about it at cons, etc. It's fine if you've had the idea in your head before the start of the contest, though. You just can't have done any sort of work on it yet.
    2) You have a year at most to design and publish a complete game. The contest starts on a specific date in one year, and ends at that date the next year. People can join at any time during the year -- in fact, no one even has to announce that they are participating, though they are free to do so.
    3) You are free to use whatever resources you have at your disposal in creating the game, including online discussion. However, any public discussions should reference the contest, so that other people can hear about it and join in.

    Finalist Qualifications
    After the year of designing, your game must meet the following conditions in order to be judged:
    1) You must have a published game. (The exact definition of "published" will need to be hammered out before the contest starts, but will almost certainly include free PDFs.)
    2) You must have a detailed document of the creation of your game from start to finish.
    3) You must have at least three examples of actual play posted online.

    Scoring
    50% - Depth and subjective quality of the documentation of your design journey.
    25% - Number and subjective value of actual play examples.
    15% - Subjective quality of game mechanics and setting (if any).
    10% - Subjective quality of the game as a product.

    That's all I've got at the moment. I'd really like to offer to front a prize on this one, but my current financial status is pretty horrible, so I certainly can't commit to anything like that. If things change, so would that.

    \n HOW TO FISH

    A contest in which each game is judged primarily not by content, or how well it teaches you how to play the game, but how well it teaches you how to teach other players how to play the game. Providing simple resources for the matter like one-page handouts, "quickstart" scenarios, example questions and answers or a list of common misunderstandings, something like BW's modular design that you can introduce in increments as players get used to each subsystem, etc. This includes explaining the details of a novel setting to them, as well as explaining how the system works. Also, complexity will be used as a tie-breaker: if two games explain how to teach themselves equally well, the one that has more to teach will be judged better.

    (Inspired by thinking about how many gaming groups work under the assumption that one person will buy and read the actual book, and then be responsible for teaching everyone else everything about the game.)

    \nOff the Cuff game awards.

    This contest is for games designed using thread titles on a roleplaying forum. There are four awards:
    - Swine Award using threads from Story Games.
    - The Man-Up Award using threads from theRPGSite.
    - The Brain Damage Award using threads from the Forge.
    - The Jared Sorensen Award using threads from RPGnet.

    The title of the game must be from two consecutive threads (e.g. My White Whale already have a name, thanks!) with documentary proof in form of an image of the threads. The writer of the game is welcome to start threads in the hope that they get an interesting juxtaposition but cannot be the latest poster in the thread.

    The game must also use at least four further juxtapostions as inspiration. These must come from the same forum (see above for acceptable sources) and must be no more than one year old. Juxtapostions can come from the main page, sub-pages or the new threads page. There must be no more than a week between subsequent posts. Again, documentary proof is required of each juxtapostion. Image alteration is not allowed.

    Points will be awarded on the basis of how well these juxtapostions are integrated into the game rather than just used as colour.

    \nThe Happy Song Game Design Contest

    For this contest, you pick out a children's song and design a heartwarming role-playing game that is in some way inspired by it. Entries will be judged based on the following criteria, in descending order of importance:

    1. Producing heartwarming actual play.
    2. Being heartwarming to read.
    3. Creative use of a children's song as inspiration.

    Winners get hugs from the judges and anyone else who wants to give them.

    \n With most of the credit to Adam, here's a version that doesn't treat the judges as gods who take half your moneyz.

    High Stakes Game Design and Judging Contest

    Rules:\n
      \n
    1. There is one Organizer who may not be an Entrant or a Judge. The Organizer will collect and distribute all money by some means, declared beforehand. Examples: everyone pays up front, the Organizer pays prizes at the end. No one pays up front, the Organizer figures out who pays who how much at the end, and harasses people until he gets confirmation of everything.
      2. \n
    2. Anyone but the Organizer may be an Entrant. The fee is $100, refundable only at the Organizer's discretion.
      3. \n
    3. Anyone but the Organizer may be a Judge. The fee is $100, refundable only at the Organizer's discretion.
      4. \n
    4. Entrants may be Judges!
      5. \n
    5. Upon start of contest, each Entrant has two weeks to deliver a finished game to the Organizer.
      6. \n
    6. Game rules should address the idea of incentive structures guiding a product without becoming the product.
      7. \n
    7. The games should use at least two of the following ingredients: casino, racing, markets, altruism, and espionage.
      8. \n
    8. After the games are delivered, the Organizer will give them to the Judges who have two weeks to deliver a finished critique to the Organizer.
      9. \n
    9. Each critique should include a paragraph or more of constructive feedback about each game design entry, and a pairwise comparison of each game. The easiest example of this is a ranking of each game from best to worst, but A > B, B > C, A = C is perfectly acceptable.
      10. \n
    10. A Judge who is also an Entrant may not include her own game in the ranking, but is assumed to set it equal to each other game.
      11. \n
    11. The Organizer will give the constructive feedback portion of the critiques to the Entrants, who have one week to deliver to the Organizer a pairwise comparison of each critique.
      12. \n
    12. An Entrant who is also a Judge may not include her own critique in the ranking, but is assumed to set it equal to each other critique.
      13. \n
    13. The Organizer will use the Schulze method, aka beatpath winner (online tool), to determine the overall ranking of Judges and Entrants.
      14. \n
    14. The prizes for the Entrants will be proportional to $100 and the number of Entrants (E), and the prizes for the Judges proportional to $100 and the number of Judges (J).
      15. \n
    15. The prizes for placing Nth will be proportional to 1/2^N as long as $100E/2^N > $10 for Entrants or $100J/2^N > $10 for Judges, otherwise 0.
      16. \n
    16. The Organizer will organize prize payouts in the real world, and publish the critiques and rankings to Story Games.
      17. \n
    \nDunno what constraints are good for the game, like at most 1k words and 4 pictures, or anonymous, or whatnot. But there's a prize structure I think may be workable.

    The Controversy Contest

    Games are created in a two week period. After that period, there is a two-week discussion period. After the discussion period, scoring is done as follows:

    * The words 'privilege' or 'cultural appropriation' are used in reference to your game. +1 point
    * Kynn makes a post about your game. +5 points
    * Someone makes an ad hominem attack on you because of the content of your game. +10
    * Andy has to sink a thread about your game. +25
    * FATAL and your game are mentioned in the same post. +30

    The Gateway Game Contest

    Make a game that uses 4 out of the following 6 items. Your goal is to make a game that the players, despite never playing an rpg or story game before, can sit down and easily play and have fun.

    1. No prep.
    2. Easy to learn the basics or it doesn\u2019t take twenty minutes trying to explain how to play. \u201cAppealing and quickly playable.\u201d \u201cFew minutes to learn, lifetime to master.\u201d Not \u201crules-lite\u201d or \u201cdumbed-down\u201d
    3. Add a board or some physical representation of the imagined space. \u201cWhat kind of game is it?\u201d \u201cOh, it\u2019s a little like a board game?\u201ds a game.
    4. Somehow reduce or remove a central power figure or no GM.
    5. Include more items to spark creative imaginings or you don\u2019t have to make stuff up and play pretend.
    6. Shorter playing time, and no expectation of multiple session play.
