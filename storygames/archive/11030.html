\n From Ralph, re Dragon Age:

=====
I'm hoping that [Dragon Age] manages to capture all of the information revealed through the CRPG codex and in-game dialog because I haven't found a fantasy world that hits all my buttons*...

*For me that would be:
1) Familiar enough to relate to (as opposed to the bizarre for the sake of being bizarre of some of the Elder Scrolls titles)
2) Refreshingly different enough to not be boring (as opposed to the tedious "been there/done that"-ness of Gothic 3)
3) Believable enough to actually feel like a place that could exist (as opposed to any of the Final Fantasy sillyness)
=====

Has anyone else played a tabletop game set directly in a world pulled from a computer/console game? (and not just "inspired by it": God of War--->AGON etc). Anyone have a setting that they want to at some point pull and turn into a tabletop experience?

In my case, I believe that the only true CRPG to TRPG crossover that I've done was Fallout, played with Unisystem (Buffy/Angel-inspired).

I like the weirdness of Morrowind (Oblivion was a snorefest setting-wise), but the shlocky "a dozen races all mashed together" is kinda blaaah. I prefer settings with 1-3 playable races, tops.

Dragon Age is kinda cool. I mean, it's basically LOTR made sliiightly edgier. And every male in the kingdom wears hairstyles that I've seen at one time or another on Kevin Sorbo (save for the Cornrows, the only one).

I really like the dark fantasy feel of the game Demon's Souls, but there's not a lot of deep "setting" there, just a tone (dark monsters in a light realm, with a bit of mysticism), a cosmology (multiple realms, The Nexus), and badass monsters that will murder you over and over until you overcome: The only way to properly emulate this on the tabletop is for every single roll roll a companion 1d8: If you roll an 8, you die, the end: Start over. However, out of all of these on my list, I think that Demon's Souls is the one I'm most attracted to actually running/playing.

Mass Effect I believe has some appeal: I have not played those (I have PS3, not XBox) but they seem pretty tight and sufficiently fantastical.

Thoughts on other console/computer settings?

    \n Rune was a direct tie in to the horrible-I-heard-but-never-played Rune PC game...which IIRC was a First Person Slasher. So if you've played the Rune RPG (which I have) you can answer yes to Andy's question.

    There's definitely some LoTR features in Dragon Age...you'd be hard pressed to push my "feels familiar" button without touching on LoTR somewhere. But I really like the details. I like the Bannorn being a region of freeholding Bans (barons) who were subjugated by a number of warlords (some T-word that equates to a duke), one of whom declared himself king, and weaned the T-word guys down to just two subordinate to him. They then elevated certan Bans to the title of Arl (Earl) to help control their neighbors, but didn't quite manage to snuff the independence of the Bans (who are free to choose which Arl they'll serve and change at will) and who wield some outsized political power when they gather for a Landmeet. All of which is familiar enough to work with but I've never seen that precise arrangement before in a fantasy setting.

    I'm similarly fond of the relationship between the Chantry, the Circle of Mages, and the Templars. Again, not revolutionary, but really well done, and even deeper when you start to uncover the history of Andraste in the game and how she (and thus the Chantry) intersect with the Teventors and the Dalish

    As a setting it has a lot of believable groups of people behaving in believable (and neither black nor white) ways with lots of grist for conflict beyond the actual story line of the CRPG game. Alot of other CRPG settings have cool imagery but the actual conflict is so focused on the game's story-line that there often isn't much else to do there.

    What makes the setting interesting for me is that nearly every historical event you can find 2 or 3 different perspectives on in the game, which makes the fictional history feel much more like real history. Too often in an RPG setting the history is presented as Fact From the Game Designer Delivered Unto Thee, which winds up feeling totally artificial because no matter how many different opinions you put into characters you know unequivicably which characters are canonically right and which are wrong. Presenting the history of the world through actual character voices or books written by fictional characters feels a lot more solid to me, even if that's just a pure psychological effect.

    Andy, are you playing Dragon Age? If so did you go through the Soldier's Peak premium content adventure (where you reclaim the old Grey Warden Keep)? If so...the story of the Sophie Dryden and the siege of the castle by the king 100 years ago is a great illustration of this...also the core Dalish / Werewolf story though that was a little more clipped.

    \n If I had to pick a CRPG to make into a tabletop RPG I'd pick The World Ends With You hands down. I'm not quite sure how the hell you would transfer the crazy game mechanics to the tabletop, but the setting and the story are just so awesome that I just want to explore them more.

    Actually, most of the video games I'd love to see as RPGs (paper mario, shadow of the colossus, pokemon, no more heroes) would be exceedingly difficult to map to the tabletop, but if they were pulled off well they'd be excellent for sure, because then I'd be able to call up the awesome experience of playing that game whenever I wanted, and to be able to explore the world as I want to, not as the game progresses. Because ultimately, those are the reasons I prefer tabletop games to video games, and if you stick a great video game onto the tabletop, I think that you can potentially get the best of both worlds (although of course if you screw it up you can get the worst, but so be it).
