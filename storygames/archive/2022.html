    thematic opposition - the characters and circumstances thrown at a character which challenge the character to make thematic choices.

    Note that the "thematic choices" do not need to always be big, hard choices, as Matt points out. Which brings us to:

    callibrating thematic opposition - selecting and later modifying the characters and circumstances thrown at a character to ensure that the choices the player must make are engaging -- not only relevant, but varied, and making the choices exactly as "tough" as they need to be for the player's enjoyment (and probably the rest of the table, too)

    This is a big, big concept, and describing it simply is probably impossible. The SotC thread is probably as compact an example as is possible.

    Christopher, this is that "magic" step when the GM looks at the back of the Sorcerer sheets, sees a dozen different concepts and then somehow ties them together into a nice, satisfying gordian knot for the players to poke at. For my money, Sorcerer leaves the GM high and dry on how to actually make that happen; Dogs in the Vineyard took a couple steps in the right direction. We still have only the most rudimentary described procedures for this process, whereas D&D 3.5 can reliably produce satisfying tactical opposition by following the dungeon construction rules or whatever they're called.

    ICE, there most certainly is stuff that qualifies as "thematic help" and "thematic support" -- the most prominent place it comes from is the other players. Now often big IC talks complicate matters rather than simplify them, but take a look at the shorter IC talks, and I think you'll find a lot of them having the characters simplify choices for each other, and make things easier. Exchanges like, "Look, Merwin. I understand he's your brother, but you saw that village he slaughtered. We can't just stand by and let him continue that." The players often work as a group to unravel the choices they are presented with -- in fact that's very close to the essence of all this Story Now stuff -- and that's what I'd pin as thematic help.

    The GM-figure can get in on this at times, too, and I think this is probably a piece of calibrating thematic opposition. How many times have we had an NPC do something to be more sympathetic or more despicable, thereby making the players' choices more straightforward?

    I'm not sure if I'd want to split hairs, but thematic support might even be considerd as something slightly different than thematic help. If a character was really all about, say, duty and loyalty, the GM or another player can contextualize a situation in those terms, thereby supporting that character's theme. Sort of like: "Look, Merwin. I understand he's your brother, but we've been sent by the King, and by the strength of our oaths, we must stop him."

    The scene's on, the characters are variously reacting to what Arjuna's trying to do, he's in his element and on his feet running. It's time to bring in some opposition. The kid who's seen him kill the gang leader stands up in the meeting and although obviously afraid of him, challenges him right there.     He threatens to reveal to the group that Arjuna's not a hero, but a monster. Brand responds to him powerfully and in character, Arjuna realizes that the confrontation with the kid is going to be the thing that makes or breaks the meeting, and so a debate ensues as Arjuna has to fight hard to calm the kid down, contain his reaction and defend his theme. He does so successfully, and wins the group over, forming a neighborhood watch group.

    (Daniel: There is also a difference between opposition within a theme, like the kid above that poses the question "How hard will you work within your theme?" and opposing theme altogether, like if the neighborhood watch started working together to perform vigilante killings that pose the question "Do you even *want* this to be your theme?")

    Thematic reinforcement: Providing followup to the results of the opposition to cement the accomplishment that the character made in game and showcase the effect the character has had in the game.

    Several days later, Arjuna's been out of the neighborhood and he comes back into the middle of a scene where the neighborhood watch is in action, doing the stuff he asked them to do. There's a rich kid in the neighborhood cruising to buy drugs, and one of the women in his group has told him that there aren't any gangs or drugs in G Block anymore. Nobody will sell to him here. When the kid gets physical, there are whistles from the watchers in the windows, and the Guardian Angel dude leans out a window with a gun to make him back off.

    What those things *are* or how to *do* those things is not what I was looking for in the other thread when I asked about calibrating thematic opposition. Instead, it was about developing skills or tools to help GM's make sure that the thematic opposition was calibrated optimally to the situation at hand.

    I have no trouble recognizing a character's thematic flag, explicitly (via a character sheet) or implicitly (via character action or player discussion). I have no problem coming up with situations that reinforce, challenge or acknowledge those themes, what I'm looking for is ways to tell, heading into the conflict that the thematic opposition I'm bringing is calibrated for frequency, quality, intensity and scale to bring it in the most optimal way for that particular character/player and his connection to his theme.

You can't just pick one and hope it works out -- or, well, I guess you can, sort of, but it's harder to do. I think it makes thematic play more like the craps table and less like the roulette wheel.

      On the other hand, I think that the risk of too much/too soon/too little is necessary in order for this kind of play to be surprising and fun.
