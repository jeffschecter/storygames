    I took a quick look through your rules and have some thoughts - I'm not sure what you're looking for.
    It feels like a fairly strong effort, but could use some tweaks depending on your audience.

    One immediate suggestion: Look for the Four Color project (http://www2.seraphim-guard.com/content/view/42/77/) for an open-content take on the Marvel Rules. If you are looking to use your rules for anything other than personal use, you may want to take a look and port to Four Color to avoid licensing issues.

    My other first instinct: if you are going to add rolls based on details Wushu-style, you might want to convert from percentiles to d20s or some other single die resolution. Then the player could roll a fistful of dice and look at them all at once, rather than re-rolling.

    I've grabbed a couple of the pdf and will look at it more in detail later if you want more input.

    Blue

    \n Blue: Yeah, I saw that 4C system a while back, but I'm only interested in doing stuff with Marvel (and on a purely fan, non-pro level.) And another "yeah" about the percentile dice-- it might be repetitive, but that's why I "flipped" the table, so there are easy to identify roll-under numbers. Typical Rank has roll under 50 for Green, 25 for Yellow, 10 for Red.
    \nI really appreciate you taking a look, though! Thanks!
    \n
    \nBoulet, Number6: Thanks for chiming in. And yeah, I *did* think about the whole added-crunch thing. In fact, I did a pretty straightforward Marvel write up in purely Wushu Open-style at some point in my thinking about this. (It's been percolating off and on for about a year.) The advantages I see:
    \n1) It makes Wushu's Principal of Narrative Truth the focus of a Marvel game. This is the most revolutionary part of Wushu that I just fell in love with, and it lends itself well to the Classic Marvel system, which also emphasized stunts (power stunts) and teamwork/action, etc.. I like the ideas of Target/Threat Ratings and Chi (in my mash-up, Drama Ratings and Reserves) and the idea that you don't get hurt, you just have narrative control or you don't.
    \n
    \n2) It makes use of Marvel's descriptive Ranks, Intensities, and Karma. On an abstract level, it's taking semantics that work well to reinforce the setting and making them matter, mechanically. On a more concrete level, it helps me understand how to better make Wushu threat ratings and pacing stuff. Using the ranks, you have more concrete guidelines in how to call a veto, how to set a Drama Rating, how difficult a Nemesis will be (while still allowing for freeform gaming, of course.) This also allows for other things that Wushu doesn't, like character advancement. As I'm typing all of this, I realize that this doesn't come through, necessarily, in the condensed version of the rules. The unabridged version talks about all of these things.
    \n
    \nand 3) It's fun. At least, it's fun for me, so in some ways I'm falling into that "write something that you want to play" kind of thing. I guess the bottom line is-- if I wanted a Wushu game, I would just play Wushu. If I wanted a Marvel game, I would just play Marvel. I kinda wanted to play a mash-up of the two, and so that's what I made. It would be hard for me to admit, but if there's a consensus everywhere that it's absolutely no fun for anyone else, or worse, has ended up too "broken" for its own good, then I want to know that.
