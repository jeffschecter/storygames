\n I'm a little unclear on something.
\n
\nIn Prime Time Adventures, I always thought the Producer counted out the fan mail tolkens at the start of the show with the budget, leaving them in a big pile in the center of the table for all the players to get at and distribute freely.
\n
\nI keep running into guys who, as the producer, count out the fan mail tolkens, but then won't let anybody else get at them until the appropriate cards are played. I thought when the right cards were played, the fanmail pile increased, rather than it only becomes available when you play the right cards.

\nLet's say the producer has a budget that grants, uh... 8 pieces of fanmail.

    \n Your second idea is almost correct.

    Here's what happens. The Episode starts with the producer counting out the points which becomes his Budget which sits in front of him. The middle of the table is empty.

    When a Conflict happens, The Producer gets 1 card by default but has to spend Budget to get more cards. As he spends Budget the points/tokens move from in front of him to the middle of the table. This pool in the middle of the table is called The Audience Pool.

    From the Audience Pool players award Fan Mail to each other.

    So, yes, if you don't get in to Conflicts that The Producer is willing to spend Budget on, no Fan Mail is available to anyone.

    \n There's an unhealthy dynamic that can develop with fanmail, as it is a mechanic based on social-level evaluation of what someone is contributing, and there are toxic social interactions. If one person is the most popular person in the group who everyone like the contribution of, they will get a ton of fan mail and others lesser on the social food chain will get less. If you're doing something you love and everyone else despises, you will get less fan mail or you will stop doing what you love. It's not a perfect mechanic. (Nothing is!) What I try to do as Producer is pick up on characters we haven't seen in a while or aspects to a character that people responded to when pitching them and say "well, we haven't seen this yet, let's do something with that, what do you think?" and getting buy-in from other players for the "unpopular" person's contributions that way - but in the end even that is artificial. It works sometimes, other times not so much.

    The great thing and the scary thing about collaboration is that people's tastes differ and what 4 people at the table love, 1 may find dull, and vice versa.

    \n I sort of thought the whole point of fan mail was to highlight and reinforce "toxic" social interactions. Bob keeps doing stuff no one likes, so no one gives him fan mail; Bob stops doing stuff no one likes, and either finds a different way to have fun or quits the game or is miserable (probably even more so when he realizes everyone else is happy that he stopped doing that stuff).

    Likewise, if lots of people like it when Robert does something (throwing in another ninja attack, making a snarky quip, having his character get caught in bed with someone else's wife, whatever), all the fan mail he gets for doing his shtick tells him "Do that again!" Thereby fitting into the whole catchphrase phenomenon associated with genre TV.

    It's sort of a shame that this...er...public shaming system is tied so intimately with the Budget system, and therefore to the overall length of a given session, but if the basic inequality of fan mail isn't meant to pat "well-liked" players on the head while shoving "unloved" players in the corner, then what other options are available to tell Bob that he needs to cut that shit out, or tell Robert that he should hit that beat again? TALKING? That'll be the day!

    \n In my experience the more fanmail is given the better is the flow of the game. It gives the incentive to come up with something new and better. It's also good to think of fanmail as a method to make shy/less creative players to become more open and willing to contribute to the game. I.e. giving fanmail to them for their attempts and stating what was fun rather than shunning them for things you didn't like. Negative approach is death spiral to the game - makes players go competitive and/or makes them play in their own bubbles and not interacting with each other .

    Posted By: ArpieIn my experience, players tend to get into conflicts, anyway... but they don't hand out fan mail readily, especially when it's such a limited resource.
    \nWhen there's still tokens in the Audience Pool at the end of the session, all of that goes away. Forever. It's kind of like reverse hit points. It all gets lost if it's not turned into Fan Mail, which on the other hand is permanent. If they feel like hoarding, they should be giving it out as quickly as possible and hoard the resulting Fan Mail points. And if they don't see the value in spending those points (or having them) start spending a bigger budget on conflicts that they really, really do not want to lose.

    As producter, I compliment them, the actors and writers, on not getting big egos about their work as I scoop up the remaining Audience Pool and boast about how this will help pay for the new yacht.*

    *I just realized the beautiful symmetry about the token economy. Money (Budget) is spent to present the show to the audience (Audience Pool) which then become fans (Fan Mail) of events and actors which gives the actors and writes more clout in the production of future episodes (spending Fan Mail) the success of which can result in the studio putting more money into production (some Fan Mail returning to the budget). Up until this point, I chalked it up to just being a really cool game mechanic, not an actual representation of entertainment production and feedback.

    \n More about GMless PtA:

    I used the Umlaut rules (which are basically GMless but use a similar resolution system to PrimeTime, albeit with black scoring instead of red) and did some tinkering (primarily applying Prime Time's cool ideas like the Pitch Session and Fan Mail) to make an Ensemble TV Show hack.

    The downside is that I lost the cool Issues/Story Arc rules from PrimeTime and replaced them with the more competitive aspect of comparing scores to see which player gets the Denoument at the end of the episode. The upside is that winning the Denoument resets your current progress towards victory to zero meaning that everyone has a chance to get their spotlight ending (but not an entire spotlight episode. *Sigh*)

    Here's what I use in my games. Keep in mind, I wrote these for myself and to help players along. Without a copy of Umlaut AND PrimeTime at hand, they probably won't make any sense at all.
