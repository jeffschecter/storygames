    \n On Wednesday, Rachel, Rachael, Rob, and I started out Burning Empires-via-Skype game in earnest, after about a month and a half of planning and prep, and it went really well (despite my concerns over the zombie space pirates introduced at about minute three-and-a-half). We've got a whole thing going on with a series of space stations in the Casiguran Matriarchy that have been taken over by a revolutionary but repressive and paranoid workers' Commune, but the core of the game has to do with a pretty tangled omnisexual love triangle (love hexagon?) among most of the main characters.

    We avoided Duel of Wits and Firefight for the first session, since two of the players are either entirely or mostly new to the system, and I'm rusty, and it turned out (IMHO) to be a really good idea. It felt like we got to stretch our legs a bit without any huge conflicts, and we had really cool scenes that might have gotten lost in the build-up to a complex conflict scene, like Rachael's Hammer Captain sharing some memories with Rachel's Psychologist about their parents, and one of my FoN's watching his son sleep while clutching a plush Hammer toy (just like Mom's!).

    \n Second session of Tenra Bansho Zero last week, which focuses on the lives of three girl Armour (mecha) pilots who pose and shoot demons during the day, and have complicated emo relationships at night.

    Here's the thing: There were certainly awesome moments, but nothing really stands out. I think this is a mixture of:

    1) Lack of sleep: I was awake from 5:30 AM, worked a brutal and distracting day right up until gaming, then ran.
    2) Lack of prep: I've gotten by before, but I think this last session was to my detriment.

    The lesson I learned was that in small conflict-centric or explosive games like TBZ and other games with *very* aggressive scene-framing, lack of prep can really derail things. I noticed that when I had lack of prep in other games, like L5R or Savage Worlds, I could often stall for time by having "tavern talk" or a quick combat. In aggressive scene-framing games, you have to be on the ball or you might as well pack it in. It was a lesson learned.

    Luckily, my work schedule changed so I won't be pulling any more insane no-sleep work-then-gaming runs for a long time.

    \n I ran some Living Forgotten Realms on MapTool (online) this last Friday; that's always interesting and keeps me on my toes because you never know what or who you'll get. It's kind of a loosely coupled community and the lack of facial clues can make roleplaying tougher.

    This session, one guy was playing Richard von Frankenstein, a revenant artificer. Revenant means just what you think it might mean. Undead, unclean! Artificer means, more or less, magical engineering type. Part of the artificer schtick in 4e is that you make little healing powder/elixir/poultice thingies during the course of the adventure and hand 'em out during combats.

    So about halfway through I twig to what he's doing: his story is that he removes little bits and pieces of himself, which are charged with necrotic lifeforce, and distills those into his healing elixirs. When he's healing the party, he's feeding them his own flesh and blood. The player was marvelously understated about it, and it was tremendously fun to watch.

    Sometimes you get crappy LFR tables, and sometimes you get little nuggets of brilliance.

    \n Fiasco: Not to slight our very enjoyable play with Happy Birthday, Robot, but immediately afterward, my group had a best-ever game of Fiasco tonight - as in, perhaps the best session of any game I've ever played - when we figured out how to do the Lucky Number Slevin/CONfidence whiplash con style of story with it. Our game started out slowly, and (using a technique we've come to love) I decided to fast-forward a bit in my second scene, to a point after the fiasco has gotten underway. The setup: My character has a replica Star Trek bridge chair that John's character wants like nothing else in the world. My character won't sell it to him, though, partly because he harbors a dream of selling it to William Shatner, who is said to collect them, for an exorbitant sum, and partly out of spite. His character schemes with Kathy's character, a thief named Brenda, to somehow extract it from my guy's possession ("I don't want to know how"). She talks my guy, Courtney, into taking it to a major Trek convention in San Diego to sell it when the Shatner thing falls through, which will put it outside of Courtney's vault. I skip over the convention and establish a scene where I'm menacing John's guy, Luke, with a gun, since I know he's stolen the chair - he either needs to give the chair back, or give Courtney the $100K that Shatner was "going to pay me." So, I'm already lying. Two scenes later, someone suggested that maybe everything about that scene, from my character's perspective, was a lie. So in that scene, Courtney and Brenda are at the convention, when Courtney proposes that they should both sell the chair, and then shake down Courtney's geek cousin Luke too, by accusing him of stealing it.

    The group built on this by setting many of the succeeding scenes before the main fiasco happened, revealing that the events we knew had to happen, in the future, were all part of a larger con. Detailed AP will be forthcoming. The interesting lesson, for now, is that by doing a flashforward early in the game, then revealing that someone was lying in that scene, then revealing that someone else was conning the liar in the scene where the original lie was revealed, you can achieve this kind of heist-movie story with Fiasco, and it's totally awesome. We kept surprising ourselves by finding new ways to recast the meaning of previous scenes, and captured the feel of something like Lucky Number Slevin perfectly, with fake double-crosses that, we discovered, were pursued in the service of the master quadruple cross - one that we only realized had to have happened as we narrated the penultimate scene.

    The best part? We had no idea what was going to happen until it did. Every betrayal and lie and hidden plan was a delightful surprise to all of us; we really were composing at the keyboard, in the best way, and the system pushed us there. I love this game.
