    I have always felt that task-resolution is (as Brand says) optimal for immersion. The keys are as follows:

    1. The GM and players should be in synch as to what tasks will accomplish what objective. Often it's (IME) pretty clear what is wanted--sometimes it isn't though. As a GM I will usually talk with the player about what I think the outcomes of an action will be before the dice are rolled.

    2. The case where you "do everything right" and it "still doesn't work" (the most common example I have seen being breaking into a safe to "get the goods" on someone and the safe being empty).

    I think this is actually fine for most immersionists so long as the situation is not completely furstrating. In a Conflict Res system either the goods would be in the safe or something else would happen to yield the character getting a good outcome (the safe is empty but the phone in the target's office rings and an incriminating message is left).

    In an immersive game, this outcome is still pretty valid if it matches up to other priorities (i.e. it's way more "dramatic" than "realistic" and if getting the goods is understood by the participants as a *challenge* then using a poor approach should yield poor results).

    However, outside of these two caveats, I don't see a whole lot of difference in the execution of actions, just in the mind-set of the person taking them (the person who sets up a conflict is certain what's at stake--the person who tries a bunch of tasks and sees what happens can't be sure).

    Hence, I think that so long as other priorities align, there should be no reason for discord between people in a system who want to immerse and those who don't.

    -Marco

    This is going back to the "Conflict Resolution for Immersionists" thread, but: here's a hypothetical for Conflict Resolution compatible with (some forms of) Immersionism. Suppose we have a Task resolution system that's basically roll a d10 under your Task Number to see if you succeed. We let the GM roll the die, but only for convenience (rather than fudging or anything like that).

    If there is a larger Conflict going on, there's some chart the GM can reference to see that X successes or X failures are needed to finally resolves those stakes, and each Task Resolution roll devoted to the larger Conflict accumulates successes / failures towards that final resolution, and the GM will not describe a resolution of the conflict until the threshold is hit. Tricky part: who sets the stakes in this Conflict Resolution system? The GM does - but he pays very careful attention to what the players are intending / flagging so he knows stakes to keep in mind. The players should have some IC / RP techniques to help make clear what kinds of stakes they want (without saying so explicitly), while the system should have something allowing for stakes to change ad hoc, since stakes tend to drift especially when they're not tightly defined.

    This is a kind of mechanic that would not require more from my friend, in terms of consciously interfacing with the system in a meta / OOC way, but it would also satisfy me knowing that there was CR underlying what was going on in the game. If we found a GM who was cool enough to read us that well, then I think this would be fantastic for both of us.
