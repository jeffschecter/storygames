\n Inspired by Helene Wecker's The Golem and the Jinni, I'm thinking of running a game in a genre that I\u2019m calling Fantasy Comedy of Manners. (It might be more accurate to say that portions of the book feel like Fantasy Comedy-of-Manners, though the overall story takes a different turn.)

I'm interested in running a very rules-light game. It should facilitate a comedy of manners story in traditional (Aristocrats in Regency England) or unconventional (immigrant communities of 1899 New York) settings with some fantastical elements. Probably only looking at a handful of sessions, so I don't need strong support for long term play.

Can anyone suggest a good system for a game like that, or would anyone care critique the current frontrunner, which is to modify John Harper's Lasers and Feelings? The mechanic would either be renamed Sense and Sensibility or Practicality and Passion or similar.

Why I think I want to tackle this by converting Lasers and Feelings: I noticed that in many comedies of manners, there is a great deal of attention paid to the tension between the passion and sense, ambition and practicality, creativity and reliability, proscribed behaviors and transgression. Also, that it\u2019s not so much a war between the two as it is an interaction between opposed but complementary themes. And that many of moments of success rely on finding a way to accommodate both sides of this yin/yang dynamic.

In the one game of Lasers and Feelings I ran, I noticed that it did a great job of keeping the central thematic elements, the yin and yang of rationality and emotion and the dynamic tension the generate, at the forefront of the story. I thought it might be applicable to games where one want to emphasize that same tension, as seen from a different perspective.

I'm considering adding Keys from Lady Blackbird and perhaps a very small pool of bonus dice. Perhaps two of the former and three of the latter, to start. The bonus die pool wouldn't refill from refresh scenes, only from hitting or abandoning your keys.

I'll have to re-flavor \u201ccharacter creation,\u201d \u201ccreate an adventure,\u201d and \u201crun the game\u201d, but the central mechanic should work, I think.

Ok folks, rip me apart. Where am I going wrong, IYHO? Right idea, wrong modifications, or is there already a better system out there? I'll mention that, as far as I understand, both Cortex Plus Drama and Drama System are substantially more rules-heavy than what I have in mind.

    \n My own approach would be to first consider what you want the overarching dramatic structure of play to be (as opposite to the narrative structure of the media you're referencing, though a great degree of similarity between the two is probably ideal).

    Would this work as a Fiasco playset, say? Or would Shock-like rounds of intertwined but individual stories, protagonist vs. antagonist, get closer? Or do we need something slightly less structured, the kind of play you can get out of a Sorcerer or TSoY-like setup with a "bass playing" GM, maybe with individual character Kickers and "story bullseye" diagrams?

    The GM/character players split of most role-playing games, with an at least partly GM-driven plot, tends to work best for stories where the tension is between a somewhat fixed cast of main characters and somewhat changeable, ultimately disposable external forces: stories such as ensemble-cast action adventures, expeditions toward the unknown or police procedurals. Though exceptions abound, of course.

    \n Thanks for the votes of confidence.

    As to Rafu's analysis (for which I'm grateful, as it contains good ideas and is helping me clarify my thoughts):

    The dramatic structure that I think I want is the one that I usually see come out of "Powered by the Apocalypse" games. PCs have relationships to things in the game world, there are things they want to accomplish, they take actions to do so, and the world reacts to them. They often interact with each other, but also have plenty of solo scenes. The GM plays the world and the NPCs.

    Lasers and feelings has the following options for resolving "risky" actions:
    - It goes wrong. The GM says how things get worse somehow.
    - You barely manage it. The GM inflicts a complication, harm, or cost.
    - You do it well. Good job!
    - You get a critical success! The GM tells you some extra effect you get.
    - You have laser feelings. You get a special insight into what\u2019s going on. Ask the GM a question and they\u2019ll answer you honestly.

    So that's sort of a very light version of PbtA's outcomes, plus the Read a Situation Move.

    My most successfully GMed games have been (IMHO) Monster of the Week, Monsterhearts and the one game of Lasers and Feelings I ran, which felt like running Monsterhearts.

    I expect NPCs to be less disposable than described as typical for GM/character games. I agree that this is a common pattern, and possibly the one leads to the other, but I expect this will be one of the exceptions. I've played in a several games with that division of responsibilities, in which there was a recurring cast of "Friendly" NPCs who provided challenges in the form of complicated social relationship requiring management and negotiation, rather than obstacles to be overcome in a short time. Or rather, the NPCs were a mix of temporary and ongoing sources of opposition and story complication. In any event, I have reason to believe a GM/character split will work at least adequately well.

    Fiasco's system of setting up relationships and needs and MacGuffins isn't far off from what I have in mind, but otherwise it's not the narrative structure, or game length, that I'm looking for.

    \n So I've been around from the ground floor on the discussion of this game and I'm trying to work through my thoughts about it. I must confess to a general block here because I'm an English Major Geek and my hackles rise--in an undeserved fashion--at the thought of reducing Lizzie Bennett, one of the most fully realized, humane, and captivating characters in English literature, to a single stat of opposed poles. But that's me; it's not going to advance the conversation. Instead, let me confine my remarks to some general observations about the nature of "Comedy of Manners" and the specific obstacles that should be considered in creating a game around it.

    (1) What exactly do you mean by "Comedy of Manners"? The term is broad and can conceivably envelop the comedies of Terence, Much Ado About Nothing and other Shakespeare comedies (possibly Midsummer Night's Dream, but not Taming of the Shrew and All's Well That Ends Well), most of Moliere, the Restoration comedies of Goldsmith and Sheridan, Austen of course, but also Wilde, Coward, and Wodehouse.

    This is somewhat important to consider, because if people come in with different models you're going to have a jarring experience.

    (2) How does the fantasy element interact with this? Are we talking Elven courts, Alt-Italy intrigues among the great merchant houses, or English estates with gnomes? Again this is mostly an issue of shaping expectations--if one person comes in expecting "Frozen", another the Winter Court from the Dresden Files, and a third Lizzie Bennett as an elf, there's issues.

    On a side note--because it can and should impact the fantasy bits--a Comedy of Manners is inextricably caught up in matters of class. (And for Austen, the Woman Question as most of her novels are about the need of women to secure enough money to live on.) You can remove issues of class, but it's like cutting the strings on a marionette--it will still be pretty, but it won't dance.

    (3) On rules issues: how is the comedy going to play out? Are PCs expected to pair up with NPCs? Is this going to be one of the "central couple needs to be united" stories with the other PCs providing support/opposition? How often are PCs expected to oppose each other? My own estimation is that PvP is going to be a major component of a game like this and needs to be planned for. (Apocalypse World is fruitful reading here, as it also assumes PvP.)

    (4) What exactly is the Number expected to test? What questions will it answer? What kinds of obstacles does it overcome? Do you roll it to stand up to Lady De Brough when she tells you Darcy must marry her daughter? Or is that handled by table talk?

    (5) I think Lasers & Feelings rather awesomely captures the madcap and rather loosely plotted nature of a Certain Classic Sci Fi show, where plot holes and sudden reversals could be swept under the rug of Awesome Concept and cast chemistry. I'm not as sanguine that it can capture the more subtle nature of the internalized conflicts of the source material--especially Austen. There's also the need for really tight story structure, generally, in this genre (in addition to being one of the English language's great prose stylists and wickedly funny, Austen was brilliant at structure; Pride and Prejudice is so taut Ginger Baker could do a twenty minute drum solo on it.) Again, * World games are helpful models here, as they lend themselves to consequential structuring.

    Part of me wonders what John Harper's minigame for Austenesque stories would look like, but part of me says he already wrote it and it's called Lady Blackbird.

    I think in the end a lot is going to come down to the kind of game and most especially the tone you're shooting for. If it's a light-hearted fantasy romp of marriage and intrigue, I think you're pretty spot on. I don't think the system is complex enough to do real justice to Austen; for one thing, I don't think her more developed characters work along a scale of opposed values. (Certainly true of Pride and Prejudice; if nothing else, this thread convinced me to start reading Sense and Sensibility, and you may be on more solid ground there.) If you tried to do Tolstoy with it I'd probably have to hunt you down ;-)

    Finally, after all this bloviation it behooves me to make my own recommendations. Keep in mind that my English Geekery is going to push me towards the serious end of things:

    For full-blown Austenesque stories: Cortex Plus Drama, which may end up my go-to system for almost everything. (That's part of what the Midnight's Voice experiment is about.)

    More nuanced but simpler than C+ Drama: a hack of Lady Blackbird. All the elements are there, it's just a matter of coming up with the right elements.

    There's probably a World hack in here somewhere, but differentiation of the niches may result in less playable characters.

    Fiasco could work, with the Soft Tilt from the Companion. It has the enormous advantage of allowing the setting of tone via the playset choices.

    \n Last bits first: I'm not familiar with the Soft Tilt variant, but a Fiasco like setup that plays more like more of a romantic light farce than a Coen Brothers comi tragic dark farce isn't a bad idea. That might be what I'd use for a one shot.

    I might do a pros, cons and question list for Fiasco, C+ Drama and Lady B in a later post, but first I'd rather respond to the stuff about the genre.

    I'll start with talking about the primary source text.

    The Golem and The Jinni. 1899, New York City, dozens of classes and communities and cultures and languages and religions all piled next to and on top of each other. The story concentrates on two immigrant communities at the southern end of Manhattan, and while the interactions across communities and classes is infrequent, it's a significant part of the story.

    The appearance of supernatural is rare, and invariably kept secret from the populace at large, but the story revolves around supernatural characters and events and the small number of mortals who interact with them. Not like the Dresden files where the supernatural is hidden but common enough to have a significant influence on the world at large. The supernatural is primarily a metaphor to heighten thematics, and to introduce complications and be a disruptive influence.

    Despite the wide variety of cultures, preoccupations with propriety is ubiquitous, as is the need to earn enough money to keep body and soul together. Actually becoming financially successful in the New World is a minor theme, but present.

    Other themes include:
    How one fits into one's community (being the newcomer, the pillar of the community, the outcast/charity case, etc)
    The old ways vs the new ways
    Schisms in the community
    Interacting with people outside your own community vs staying within it
    Conflict and cooperation across class lines
    How class lines run between communities, and through them
    Bigotry and tolerance (this is a smaller issue than it might be)
    Ambition vs prudence
    Making a good love/marriage match (including versions of "The Woman Question")
    Freedom vs interdependence
    Artistic expression vs diligent productivity
    Falling prey to sin and temptation vs never living your life
    Relationships with the city itself
    And secrets, lots and lots of secrets

    A Civil Campaign by Bujold probably makes a good secondary source text, though I'd rather focus on the middle and lower middle classes than the affairs of the High Vor. Bujold's take on the influence of scientific innovations in that particular book is a reasonable facsimile of the effects of magic that I have in mind.

    \n Re: point (2) - Fantasy elements and the setting generally. I've described the setting of The Golem and The Jinni, but I'll want to return to setting again. Briefly, I think I want to move away from small neighborhoods of English Gentry in the Regency, and toward settings with more moving parts, such as cities. At a guess, the closest we'd get to Austen might be something along the lines of an English Village, small but vibrant and sprinkled with oddities, with Bennet-like estates on one side (possibly with gnomes), an enchanted forest on the other, and a main road that leads from somewhere else to the big city.

    Re: point (3) I'm not imagining that every character's issue will be about making a love match and/or a good marriage, though I do expect that to be a common plot element. Some characters may be more about having their business succeed, or securing enough funding for their charitable organization, or escaping the ancient curse that forced them to flee their home, or reconciling with their children.

    I envision the PCs opposing each other, cooperating, or working orthogonally to each other in very roughly equal proportions. So more or less like most of the MH games I've been in, or the AW games I've heard about. PvP will probably be decided by consensus when possible, going to the mechanics only when necessary (though I could see that changing if the system becomes more PbtA-like).

    Re: point (4) Good question.
    The number represents which side of the Practicality vs Passion spectrum you fall on. The Lower your number, the better you are at doing things that require Passion. A higher number means you're better at Practical things. The instructions in L&F say to roll whenever you do something risky. I this case, I think you roll whenever you do anything consequential in pursuit of your goals. Yes, you'd probably often roll to see if you score a victory in mental/social/verbal "combat" against an NPC, unless it's obvious that the scene should work out in a particular way, and that the interesting bit of story is in the next scene.

    I think this will take a little working out at the table, to make sure it's fun for everyone. My experience with L&F is that all the results of the rolls tend to lead to more story.

    My impression of C+ Drama is that it requires some fairly substantial investment of time and attention to build character and the relationship map, particularly in comparison to the L&F setup. Would you say that's true, and if so, is there any way to bypass that?

    Likewise, my impression of the C+ Drama resolution system is that it's in the middleground of the rules heavy/rules light spectrum, in the general neighborhood of FATE. True or false? Any way to lighten it?

    \nYes, full setup in the Smallville style requires a decent amount of time. This is either a feature or a bug, depending on your goals; it's guaranteed to create a well-detailed world with a lot of good conflicts built in. For example, my own C+ Drama project had really no set up other than what the players did on the relationship map (plus my own knowledge of the Autumn of Terror), but we've had some nice dramatic conflicts from the start.

    Mechanically, Cortex Plus is about where Fate is. The main issue is Distinctions, which have bennies & drawbacks associated with them that are worked out in advance--usually.

    You can simplify Distinctions somewhat by using the Action methodology of "d8 if it helps/d4 & plot point if it hurts". You could also create the bennies on the fly based on play, or do it with rulings, although at that point you're drifting from the rules as designed.\n

    World games have a pretty strong focus on resolving stuff through violence, which is something I'd like to de-emphasize.
