    \n This bums me out a little, because I love steampunk and I would obviously love a game about Asian traditions and cultures, but there are some issues that make me uncomfortable about this one. The title is one issue, obviously (along with the repeated use of the term in multiple places in the description), but I would also ask: is there a structure to the "Asian" language sprinkled through the description? Is it based on some existing language, or built around a reasonable linguistic theory that happens to sound stereotypically Asian? (This seems a little unlikely since there are words from different Asian languages used as part of it.) Similarly, is there some core organizing principle behind the melange of Asian cultures in the book, or is it mostly just what people thought was interesting from a wide range of Asian civilizations, mixed into one unwieldy pot? Are there any people of Asian descent working on the game? Do you have any concerns that the storyline of a backwards Asian country encountering a much more advanced "traditional" civilization reinforces some colonialist narratives? What does the relationship between the "foreigners" and the "Rosuto-Shimians" look like -- what do the foreigners want? What motivates the Rosuto-Shimians to abandon their "old gods"?       \n Rob, that was intended as an emoticon for surprise, not an insult. I apologize if that came off in the wrong way.

      History is a complex discipline, but -- at least in a modern context -- in has to do with a lot more than just collecting stories uncritically. Newspaper reporters can't even get away with doing that. They have to back up their findings with actual facts (often corraborated from multiple sources, not just one), so that when people challenge the accuracy of what they wrote, they have some ground to stand on. And historians are generally held (or, at least, should be held) to higher levels of accountability than reporters, who work under much tighter deadlines and have fewer traditions about properly citing their sources. As a historian, you live and die by your ability to convince other people of the usefulness (and hopefully the factual accuracy) of your interpretation of events. Telling an interesting story is often beside the point, because it can actually hurt your ability to descern the truth --- actual historical events are typically not as cut-and-dry as stories, and following a pleasant-sounding narrative can lead you to assert things that may not, in fact, be true. The entire field of history is mostly concerned with arguing over different interpretations of the same events or finding new interpretations or facts about things that change existing views of the broader picture. And that means you have to be able to back up any claims you make, especially if they challenge Status Quo understandings (and if they don't, why bother?).

      Collecting stories from people, in both news reporting and history, is generall considered "hearsay" unless it's backed up by other more trustworthy sources. Some historians and anthropologists examine not what actually happened (historical fact) but what people believe happened (historical memory), in which case, sure, gathering that kind of evidence is super helpful. And doing things like conducting interviews can lead you to interpretations and sources that you wouldn't have found otherwise, so it's an important research technique. But you have to do much more work than that if you're going to assert something you heard as being the actual truth.
