\n So I want to play in a game, but I'm not sure what system would be smoothest. Dice rolling isn't a problem (http://www.catchyourhare.com/diceroller/) so it doesn't have to be totally resource-based (e.g. Universalis). I'd also like to do something GMless, which reduces the field somewhat.

I'm thinking mostly asynchronous (i.e. not planning to all be online very often, if at all), but with a firm rule of "reply at least once per day" so nothing stalls too badly. I could probably reply repeatedly, most work days (yeah, yeah, I'm a bad person) but not more than twice a day on weekends. I kinda, really, very much wish Fiasco was out, because it's damned perfect for such play!

I also think it would be good to do two waves: narration and discussion. The idea being that the narration wave would be (mostly) a full, fluid story, with minimal or no out-of-character or mechanical blips.

Anyone else game? (No pun intended.) Pitch something, if you are!

    \n The four play sets come with tables, sorted by white dice (1 through 6 values) then with sub-lists for black dice (1 through 6) for a total of 36 elements, times four: there's a table for Relationships, Objects, Places, and... something else I forget, but will see when Jason provide the rules.

    During setup, you pick a black die and a person to your left or right: which is why we need "seating order," even if we can play loose with turn order in scenes. Once you have a black element on each side, you can only populate them with a white element each. Once we all have a black + white thing on each side, the dice are used up and we go to Scene One.

    So you see that we each will have to make four selections, before we can even play, and they are in turn order. That can make for a LONG setup, if folks are doing one per day.

    After that, it's basically freeform narration, for two scenes each. On your scene, you can either frame (meaning the others will close iit) or elect to let the others frame (which means you will close it). At the close of a scene, a die is traded--white or black, limited to the original pool (but values are ignored, after situation creation). White means a good ending, black manes a bad ending to the scene; and who gives the die and to whom is based on who framed. I am still not 100% clear on how that goes, which is the main thing I need to review in the rules. I *think* if I frame, then someone else give me a die; if y'all frame, then I give one of you a die. Or vice versa (if I close, I give a die; if y'all close, I get a die).

    After two scenes, we all roll the dice we were given, and select from the Twist chart: these elements show how the situation is going from bad to worse. Two more scenes each, and we do closing scenes. I don't think the dice matter, then (I was sleep-deprived by that point at MACE, and don't remember rolling before the closing scene).

    So, really, the dice economy is very basic, with only the need to pass them around (i.e. sort them on the dice roller) at scene endings, and only roll again once (IIRC).

    When Jason links in his rules, we can start to choose play sets. But here's their names:
    A Nice Southern Town
    McMurdo Station, Antarctica
    A Wild West Boomtown
    A Safe Suburban Community

    It might even be good to choose one blind, without pouring over the elements. I've done the Antarctica one, so unless other's are really jazzed, I'd like to try a new one (even though, yes, there's a TON of stuff we didn't use in our game at MACE). Being in a not-so-nice southern town, I could riff off that pretty easily, and the suburban one just screams "desperate housewives," sight unseen! Wild West will probably yield Deadwood-ish play, and I'd be more out of my element there... but I am but one player; no pointy hats, here!

    We can take this to the OOC Wave, now, ya? (Except for my last player ID!)
