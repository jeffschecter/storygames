\nBefore we begin, just so everyone is clear:

These games don't actually exist. No one mention games that actually exist.





I will forever hate Brilliant Aura, the game that purports to be a cross between Exalted and modern supers.
It's broken, because the set skill list is vastly overpowered by the freeform, user-defined Powers system.
This makes it impossible to play common folk well, and yet the premise and structure REQUIRE some players to play common folk.
This is total bullshit!
They make a game where you need some people playing common folk, and then punish those who are willing to with mechanical disempowerment.

That's not even what annoys me about Brilliant Aura. Has anyone read the endgame rules?
Before the Master Villain's Immunity lifts, the table needs to Dot The Sun three times and unlock two of the Master Auras.
Dotting the Sun once takes between 5 and 7 sessions.
To Dot the Sun three times AND unlock two Master Auras would take roughly 20 sessions.
And yet Brilliant Aura calls itself a "pick up and play game".
No one would play this shit for 20 sessions. TOTALLY BROKEN.

Also, most of the listed skills are completely irrelevant to both supers and the whole faux-Exalted vibe.
Accounting? Academics? Scavenging?
I hate this game.

    \n The game that I'm never going to play again is The Friendship Game from Walt Disney Industries. I don't know who designed it; it says "Walt Disney" on the cover.
    \n
    \nAfter a first skim of the rules, it all seemed nice enough - lots of little things like how to set up the room for perfect play, how to introduce players to each other, ritual phrases of courtesy etc. I gathered some friends in the old tree-house, and gave them the handouts. Everyone was smiling as they read their lines out loud.
    \n
    \nAfter a few minutes, I introduced the Joyful rule, as per the instructions. Happiness increased in the room; we could almost feel it physically in the air. It was as if the sun had come up.
    \n
    \nThe player on my right, Erika, one of my childhood friends, was the first to understand the Conundrum Option. I'd thought the procedure was pointless, but as she started playing it hard, I started seeing how it worked. And that's when it clicked.
    \n
    \nWe all looked at each other and understood the game. At the same time, we understood we couldn't put it down.
    \n
    \nI don't know why I was the first to take action. But I knew we were locked in the game. Some emergent property, some strange interreaction of procedures and rules that we hadn't foreseen, was choking us with its clammy hands.I reached out and picked up the fork. I knew - we all knew - that there was only one way to get out. I didn't really feel anything as I pierced Erika's eye, nothing but desperation.
    \n
    \nThen there was the sharp pain as someone bit my leg. It wasn't one of the players. It was some neighborhood kid. He'd been hiding in the garden outside the treehouse, listening to the game, and got caught in its spell. My blood was running, warm, I could smell it. Dripping on the floor of the tree house.
    \n
    \nI don't want to talk in detail about what happened. The night was long, long. In the morning, I climbed down from the tree house, exhausted. Hanging by the only arm I had left.
    \n
    \nI just wanted to warn you all.
    \n
    \nThe Friendship Game is broken.

    \n Has anyone else here played "The Glass Bead Game"? It's based on Hesse's novel, so I was all hyped up about it, but that's where the excitement ended.

    First of all, the setting is crazy. Instead of just using Europe, they made up some pseudo-Europe bullshit "Castalia" setting with the serial numbers filed off. But then, instead of giving me cool details about the setting, it just lists other resources I need to read: novels, magazines, albums, movies, textbooks. Great. For my $20, I expected a complete book!

    But where it really falls down is the rules. There are mechanics strewn about the "setting" section -- or at least allusions to the mechanics -- but nothing really solid. I can't figure out how actually to play this thing! The game plays out on a giant relationship map, I think, but it seems you're not supposed to write it down. The rules say that the truth is expressed in music. You connect all kinds of weird cultural and scientific ideas together. The mechanism isn't clear, and I have no idea how the bead currency works. Apparently, to be able to play it well, you need advanced training in mathematics, too.

    The book ends kind of abruptly. It's like the designer got bored with it before he finished. I'd say it is incomplete but it was already too hard to understand, so I dunno. Oh, also, I think calling the GM the "Magister Ludi" was a really dumb idea.

    I spent $20 for the damned PDF, and I can't even figure out how to play it, so I totally feel robbed. I hate this game!

    \n I was really excited when Fabricated American was delivered to my door. The age old theme of an uprising of plush animals against their human oppressors has long been a passion of mine. The cover was evocative as well, showing a partially damaged Teddy Ruxpin (The Gwar cassette tape in his chest was a nice touch) defending a group of Cabbage patch dolls, his little bear arms holding a can of Raid and a lit match.

    The Full Felt edition comes with a sewing kit to add "Battle Damage" to your characters, which is a cost saving measure. However, problems began to crop up during character creation.

    First of all, I'm not comfortable with the first step, to "liberate a fabricated American (FA from here on in) from the cruel child overseers". My two year old nephew put up quite a fight, and I still have bruises from where he head butted me.

    Once you have your FA liberated, you have to cast off it's slave name and take up a new one. This is done using a bag of letters from a Scrabble set. You can only draw five times, so Binoo was reborn as XLGRU . The value of the letters in your FA's name are the number of traits your FA has.

    Now, the idea of adding additional traits to the FA is to literally sew on pieces of felt, glue on googly eyes, as well as glitter and paint. The problem is that the rules don't give you any kind of limit on where you can sew on these traits. I ended up covering my plush cat with felt, googly eyes, glitter, and bright orange paint, which turned him into some sort of Cthulhu disco dancer.

    From here, play begins. And here's where the mechanics get in the way. Combining the Tarot with an Uno Deck may sound like a good idea on paper, but in practice it creates insanity. When I drew a "Reverse" and "the Tower" I was able to shatter an M1A1 Abrahms Tank with XLGRU's felt screwdriver. What kind of balancing is that?

    Now, battle damage is completely broken. Considering the material composition of most stuffed animals, burning off the pieces with a butane torch just didn't fit our playstyle. There are alternate rules involving a bandsaw that we ended up using, and it did make the staplegun healing mechanic a lot easier.

    The endgame mechanic involves filling the insides of our FA with fireworks and setting it off, signifying their final act of defiance against the human oppressors. We hadn't read far enough ahead to realize that we could gather pieces of our FA afterward to sew onto our new FAs for the next game, but really, there wasn't going to be a next game.

    \n Man, the worst game I ever read was called \u201cStructure, Sign and Play.\u201d I think maybe it was French. Anyways, the settings was French, in the 1960s where you all played grad students. At first, I was pretty stoked about a French twist on the old classic \u201cBeckoning from Y\u2019tdkkyxxc.\u201d What with monsters and mysteries and discoveries of sublime Truths. How I was mistaken.
    \n
    \nThe first thing that I noticed is that the first fifteen or so pages of the text rigorously detailed the interests and backgrounds of five grad students at the University of Nanterre. At first, I thought maybe it was like fluff fiction or like an example of a party but no, it seemed to be the actual lives of the authors of the game. Weird, I know. Then the second chapter titled \u201cPraxio-Phenomenal Embodiment: An Introduction and Notes Toward a Holistic and Critical Understanding of Immigrant Disabled Lebanese Lesbian Transvestites in the Great Toronto Area; a Memoir\u201d started stating characters with both Greek and German terms. I mean, sure, Ousia or Techne may not be as intuitive as dexterity but I figure most people can see what they\u2019re getting at. But Dasien was somewhat ambiguous. However, the footnote explaining and justifying that these concepts will be co-constituted/embodied during practice and furthermore that quantitative mechanics where indicative of hegemonic, patriarchal and positivist epistemologies started taking up more than two-thirds of the pages I started to wonder if this was the game for me. At around page 214, the main body text seems to split into columns wherein the text starts to argue with itself. After ninety or so pages of this the whole section on what I presume to be character creation abruptly stops.
    \n
    \nI consider myself a man who both enjoys, nay, loves skill-lists, spell-lists, feat-lists, lists of any kind in a game. They calm my taxed nerves after having to come up with things like name. But this was too much. I think \u201cskill-list\u201d here would be a too generous term; perhaps \u201cactivity-list.\u201d Every conceivable skill was broken into component parts. For example, Scrap-Booking was not just one entry but several: glue-gunnery, photo-scavengery, &c.; These seem to all interact with other entries, like Collage and Scissor proficiency, in arcane ways.
    \n
    \nThe core mechanic seems to boil down to talking over the other players in using ritual phrases and referring to your splats\u2019 (here called \u2018paradigm\u2019) power. There were not clear rules on how the various power interacted. I mean, could a Marxist take a Feminist? Did it depend on tenure or species? The rules were mum. There was a splat that was "after" splats and they seemed to have some sort of mysterious edge.
    \n
    \nWhat follows appear to be some meteorological or geological charts of Southern France. Maybe. The printing wasn\u2019t very good so I can\u2019t really read the font. These are covered with all sorts of symbols which I assume to be trade routes. Maybe highways. What I can tell you is that this is followed by meticulous descriptions of thirty-five French sounding towns\u2019 economic activity. What relevance this section has I don\u2019t know.
    \n
    \nThis section is dove-tailed with what seems to be a list of supplements published by either the authors or the same publisher; maybe recommended reading.
    \n
    \nI would not recommend this product to a friend.

      \n I cannot fucking STAND the travesty that is Organic Superhero Revolt. Sure, I'm all for the reclamation of the food industry from the factory farm regime, sustainable alternatives, love the hearth, blah blah blah. . .but this game just does not give you the tools to GM it. I mean seriously, there are no stats for pesticides, no material strength indexes for common farm fortifications. . .not even so much as a difficulty factor for chicken coop padlocks! And don't get me started on the superpowers. Each power is a single-line description. That's IT. No guidelines on movement speeds, damage dealt, or relative potency vs. different Agricultural Agent types.WHAT THE FUCK? I know, I know, this whole game is supposed to be an exercise in the inevitability of agricultural collapse, but at least give me the tools to challenge my players a LITTLE bit. If I wanted to listen to another night of some opportunistic prick explaining to me why possessing an Aura of Permaculture means he's immune to all Hormone Injection attacks, I'd have kept volunteering with the Estacada Food Co-Op.

      And dont say One. Fucking. Word. about the Vegan supplement. OSR: Meat is Murder is fucking DEAD to me.

      \n Have you played SIMple SIMon? I tried it with some friends last weekend. Let me say in my understated way that it SUCKED ASS.

      It's a generic, super-light rules system. Everything resolves around a single roll. There is one stat, Goodness, rated 1-3. In conflicts, you roll d20 and add your Goodness and compare it to the opposition's d20 + Goodness roll. High roll wins.

      That last paragraph? That's the rules. I don't mean I paraphrased them. I mean it's a direct quote. I understand some people like systems that get out of the way, but this is ridonkulous. I couldn't figure out what to do with this. I mean, the character's Goodness hardly influences the roll at all. I might as well just be flipping a coin at this point.

      I'm never playing SIMple SIMon again. I hate that game.

      \n I will forever hate Cook God.
      \n
      \nThe game billed itself as a cross between Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon, House of Flying Daggers, Hero, and God of Cookery. Sweet sweet wuxia action I thought.
      \n
      \nI pictured it as maybe being something like PCs are doing Iron Chef like competitions, only they have to duel other chefs to get ingredients, deal with their mixed loyalties to each other, the desire to win versus maintaining their honor, describing beautiful colors of the food as it cooks and the area that they're cooking in, serving the dish to the randomly chosen customer and convincing them to try it and of course... hardcore wushu smackdown against not just the other rival cooks, but delivery food driver mooks, Applebees management types secretly sponsoring cooks and trying to rig the win, maybe even some sort of drug-company hit squads that have been dispatched because the cooking competition has stumbled onto various cures for some ailment or another over the years.
      \n
      \nInstead, what do I get?
      \n
      \nChartmaster 17000. You roll to start your "career". Just about every single one of them sucks, being some variation of "fry cook" or "supermarket sushi chef". Not that anything is wrong with that in and of itself; I've had more than a few inglorious jobs myself. It's just... wow... international wushu/kungfu chef that started out working the DriveThru of Burger King? Really?
      \n
      \nBut I was willing to let it slide. Until I discovered the usual "fun" of a lifepath system... getting your character rendered non-viable while still making the little bastard. It's amusing if it happens once or maybe even twice... but it shouldn't take me 9 @#$%#@$%#$ tries just to get to "wushu initiate cook". I mean, ok...slipped in the walk-in freezer while having sex and had hands crushed by frozen chicken... amusing. Eyes lost due to towel snapping fight...I haven't seen a single eye lost, let alone both, but..still a giggle. Accidentally locked in the freezer? Fingers lost in tomato cutter accident? Groin shot by 80 year old vegan protester that's making a stand for animal rights? Bit by a presumed rat (actually a prairie dog) and dying of Bubonic Plague? WTF?!?!
      \n
      \nThere's _5_, count them _5_ levels of this crap. "The Call", "Initiate", "Proving", "No matter what", "Graduation". "No matter what" is just.. c'mon guys, it might be a standard trope in some 80's action movie, but... at least make an effort.
      \n
      \nThe Lifepath (called "Career") wraps all the skills, recipes, special ingredients, cooking moves, wushu techniques, and environments, into the usual smorgasbord of nonsensical combinations. For example, did you realize you get "knife" technique points _only_ from the 6 soup paths? Yup. Apparently, a chef _only_ uses a knife when making soup. Tea ceremony is where you pick up the Frozen Fist throwing technique.
      \n
      \nI could deal with all of this, except when it comes to conflict. It's an unholy nightmare. They do this scripting thing, which is done every so often in rpgs. It can work, but Cook God slips and falls down hard. First, you roll a contest to see if you get the Environment that you want. Then, you have to script out a mix of Ingredients, Cooking Moves, Wushu Techniques and Recipes. Depending on the order you set it up, you might wind up actually cooking something, getting into a fight, stealing one of the 4 scripted moves from an opponent and adding it to your current Cook-Off contest (not a permanent addition though, just for this particular Cook-Off) or simply Botching. How do you Botch? No clue; apparently, they managed to forget to actually explain that.
      \n
      \nNow, take all that and multiply it x5. Or however many people you've got in your group.
      \n
      \nThose 36 (!) different cooking skills? Completely worthless. Charmaster 17000 renders it into a deeply stacked Rock, Paper, Scissors thing. The equipment list? A whole _other_ nightmare. It's like gun-porn, only for chefs. While it might matter desperately to someone, I for one don't give a crap whether my timer is a sand, dial, or electronic. I really freaking don't. Likewise, I don't see the need for 15 different paring knives. Especially if you're only going to offer 3 bread knifes. I mean... who the hell thinks this stuff up? Would the game have broken if it'd only had... oh, say 7 paring knives and maybe 5 bread knives? What about the sharpeners?
      \n
      \nYou get laundry list after laundry list of crap like this.
      \n
      \nThe GMing advice is worthless. "Encourage your players to be creative in their descriptions." I almost started slamming the book on the table I was so pissed. How much more generic can you get? What's even the point of being "creative" when they're spending all their !$%@#$%@#$ time rolling on the stupid @!#$%@#$% chart after they've spent 2 hours staring at the equipment lists? It's not like being "creative" actually matters one whit, and considering how long it takes to script the Cook-Off, I'm not sure I really want the player to say anything more than "I chop" anyway. I'm not even going to discuss the advice given to GMs for "entertaining" mishaps when a character Botches.
      \n
      \nThe lack of an index is just salt in the wound of your lost eye from the chip that flew into it from your cutting board (one of the "entertaining" Botch results).
      \n
      \nI think I've sufficiently described my hatred of the game and why the broken rules suck. I'm pretty sure I hate this game forever. I mean, "Heavenly Razor Carrot Fan Technique"? I can accept the whole flinging wasabi powder into someone's eyes to blind them and all, but some things are just...
