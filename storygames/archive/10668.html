\n It occurs to me that a thriving growing vibrant indie scene comes about when the establishment has become calcified, painfully orthadox, hide bound, and stuck in a repetetive rut.
\n
\nThe indie scene explodes onto the stage to challenge that rut, revitalize creative energies and point the way to numerous possibilities...some of which may actually have merit.
\n
\nBut when the establishment becomes shocked out if its complacency by this unexpected competition, or its members simply get inspired and reminded of the potential that is out there, it will respond. It will shake off the crust and accumulated detritus adopt or adapt some of the more promising ideas proposed by the indie scene and reimagine itself.
\n
\nAt that point, the indie scene is no longer needed and no longer really in a position to compete effectively with the scale and resources of the establishment. Then the scene dwindles to a handful of the hardest of the hard core practicing their craft on the fringes until the next time there is a need for the scene to blossum again.

    \n You could consider indie gaming like fringe theatre.

    Fringe theatre challenges the establishment. In response, established theatre adopts the ideas of fringe theatre, in exactly the same way as you describe the gaming establishment reacting. But that doesn't mean fringe theatre dies away. It keeps bubbling away, challenging in new ways.

    You see similar things happening with, say, art and music. Just because mainstream music takes on aspects of underground music doesn't mean the underground scene disappears.

    So it's likely, I think, that indie gaming will keep bubbling away.

    But if it does die away, it won't just come back, the same as it was before. As with theatre and music, the next thing to come along will be completely new. There would be a new sort of indie gaming, with new theories (certainly not the Big Model) and new ways to play.

    Graham

    Posted By: Mark WUnfortunately, your last clause is probably impossible.
    \nHow so?

    Conversation becomes too noisy, with an overload of games overwhelming the signal people actually are engaging with. People flee those conversational groups for others and attempt new conversational groups that re-spin that noise into a different signal, or exclude it deliberately. They accumulate failures and successes (mostly failures, of course), and the old groups get adjourned or reconstructed (socially speaking) as part of a broad trend once the pattern of the successes is obvious. And mostly, people don't realise that this is happening, even as they do it.

    The light control version already exists on RPGnet - it goes "It's rude to talk about your own game unless you're doing it in a thread that's for that kind of thing. If it's obviously a good place to mention your thing in some other thread, apologise for the self-pimping". It's not a rule, and not a sweeping norm, but it's gaining traction slowly.

    It's not what I'd call a tidy deal, but this kind of process is happening pretty much all the time.

    Now, getting a lot more real, that's not likely to happen to the mainstream of gaming - there will be a tall head of games that gets a lot of individual attention, games discussed as games. But it might very well happen way out here in the long tail.

    \n A while back, I took the position that no story games player or creator has the obligation to evangelize, unless they promised that they would as part of some kind of larger arrangement (e.g. Luke, as a full-timer, has people to pay). But I am seeing a way in which maybe I've impaled myself on a fork.

    The thing that pisses me off most about these games, and the way we talk about them, isn't the elitism, the insularity, the echo-chamber fora, or any of that BS. What pisses me off is that I didn't find out about InSpectres, which came out in 2002, until 2009. I wasted seven years of opportunities to run a supernatural mystery game where I, the GM, didn't have to know the Answer in advance, where I could be as surprised as everyone else. No one told me I could have been having more fun. There was no master I could go to with my woes, who would tell me that "System Matters!" and bam! enlighten me.

    Keep in mind that I was a reasonably well-informed gamer. I was aware of the existence of games from small publishers; I had a complete set of the New Style games acquired as they were published. But I never found out about My Life With Master, a game that would have been perfect for me and the people I played with. I played D&D with a DM who, in retrospect, I can see would have loved to run a real monster. There is no way I can see, in that same retrospect, how I would have found out that the perfect medium (MLWM) existed.

    So having figured out what I need to do to have fun every time, maybe, annoyingly, I do have a sort of bodhisattva-esque responsibility to convey salvific knowledge to people who could be having transcendent kinds of fun, on the grounds that if no one does, then everywhere GMs are condemned to ride the Wheel of Prep Samsara eternally, and players to suffer from creative frustration. If I act on a rule where I do nothing, then universalizing it, everyone winds up in the position I was in, which is an unendorsable result.

    When is this particular indie scenes necessary? Until all beings are enlightened, I guess. Until every group knows that there might be a solution to their dissatisfaction with their current palette of games. Until the understanding that System Matters (and only matters) penetrates into every table. Until every gamer's repertoire of technique is stocked with the tools that he can use to build the stories he wants to tell and hear.

      \n Are indie games defined solely by the fact that certain aspects of the games won't appeal to trad gamers? No. 3:16 is just as much of an indie game as SAJ, after all. But I think that Trait--Unappealing to Trad Gamers 4d10--is pretty essential to the "scene"'s definition. Not that designers are going out of their way to be unappealing to trad gamers, mind you! (That *would* be super elitist and stupid.) But simply because people are writing the kinds of games they want to play.

      Look at L&L for an interesting example. It's this weird hybrid of uber-traditional dungeoncrawl and uber-modern shared narrative. It might actually be a good bridge game for grognards\u2014but maybe not. I mean, it requires that everyone rotate GM responsibilities!

      Indie designers are pretty much going to continue designing whatever the fuck they want\u2014*that's* the essential element of the scene. Sometimes maybe there will be a breakout hit with a lot of crossover appeal, like MouseGuard and 3:16 and Panty Explosion and whatnot. But I don't think it's something we can deliberately aim for with our design processes\u2014think of it as the Fruitful Void of the Designing RPGs RPG.
