\n This is a micro-RPG for 4 players. Assign the following roles: Cardinal Walter Kasper, Pope Benedict XVI, Father Christopher Jamison and Andrew Copson. Pick a country that the Pope is visiting for this game.

Play begins with Cardinal Kasper making a statement. The Cardinal must make a vicious and unpleasant speech about the country being visited. He should make it as insulting as he can without actually telling any falsehoods. The Cardinal then leaves the room.

In the second scene, Pope Benedict XVI makes a speech. The Pope must make a speech that tops the Cardinal's speech for insult and petulance, and his speech can include blatant falsehoods.

The third scene consists of Father Christopher Jamison attempting to rehabilitate the Pope's comments, layering an interpretation on them that makes them less insulting. However, he cannot directly contradict anything the Pope said. At some point during this scene, Andrew Copson holds up a piece of paper with the words 'technical difficulties' written on it which interrupts the speech. Seeing this, Jamison says "I think I'm losing touch..." then Andrew Copson nods his head slowly.

The game is now over.

    \n As a game, this is less interesting, but as a political game-text, it is.

    I would say that the insulting bits don't quite convince me, as a reflection of the cardinal or the pope, but then I do not follow these figures closely, so I can't really tell. It may be that the game (text) would be better if there was some background for the characters in play, referring to their behavior in the real world. Thus the players of the game may be strengthened in their interpretation of the characters.

    \n Some context, now I've tracked down a youtube link and got my backside in gear.

    Scene 1.
    The meat of scene 2.
    Scene 3 is inspired by this, from about the 4:00 mark.

    The game is as much a critique on how the visit was reported on as of actual events.

    As for specifics, I'm in love with the idea of a participant who only watches in bemusement until the very end, and says nothing, but I guess if you only have 3 players you can double that role.

    The progression from insults to lies may not be entirely true to the source material, but I like the sense of raising stakes it provides. Trying to keep the lies plausible is a nice addition, although again not in the source material.
