\n Further to Jonathan talking about failure in conflicts.

We played Forbidden City tonight, which is my take on the Opium Wars. In this game, you can't fail in fights: you just succeed in one of four different ways.

Specifically, there's four different dice, and you decide how you win by seeing which one rolls highest:\n
    \n
  • If Wealth rolls high, you win by superior firepower.
    • \n
  • If God rolls high, your superior morality crushes the unbelievers.
    • \n
  • If Smoke rolls high, you lose your grip on reality, but come out winning.
    • \n
  • If Cowardice rolls high, you win by sheer luck, probably while soiling yourself or crying like a baby.
    • \n
\nAnd, if you win by rolling a six, you overshoot: you win, but you lose control and go too far. For example, if Wealth rolls high, you win by superior firepower, but you carry on blasting them manically, long after they're dead.

It's fine. So conflicts where you can't fail: they're fine and still fun.

Graham

    \n Fiction level conflicts have never been the same as game level conflicts...I'm not sure why that distinction would seem bizarre.
    \n
    \nAt the game level we always filter potential fiction level conflicts into those that are worth using game mechanics to resolve and those that aren't. You're a GM you have 2 NPC characters who in the fiction are fighting. Do you pull out the game mechanics to resolve the fight? Or since they're "just NPCs" do you simply resolve the outcome and move on? At the level of the fiction those are two characters same as any other in a conflict same as any other...at the level of the game you may well judge that conflict too unimportant to be worth rolling for. Many games have Mook rules for unimportant characters. At the level of the fiction are their physical bodies any less capable of taking punishment than a villain's body? No (well, sometimes, but not usually). Its at the level of the game that we decide that conflicts with THOSE characters aren't interesting enough to use the full game mechanics for so we'll fast forward through them.
    \n
    \nWhat I'm saying here is that while who wins the fight may well be a conflict at the fiction level, its not at the game level. At the game level the conflict is over how you win. Point being, that claiming the game has conflicts where you automatically win is not an accurate representation of what's going on. The part where you automatically win isn't the conflict, and the thing that is the conflict you don't automatically win.

    • Take a Wealth die, when you show your superior firepower or knowledge.
      • \n
    • Take a God die, when you show your superior morality.
      • \n
    • Take the Smoke when you narrate the Smoke (which is a supernatural, weird smoke that covers the land)
      • \n
    • Take Cowardice when you take something from the land or its natives
    \nThen, when it comes to a fight, roll all the dice in your hand. Highest wins, in the way described above, and then goes back in the pot.

    \n Well, you always win every fight anyway. The only control you have is over how you win.

    So you want dice because they'll help you win in the "better" ways: Wealth dice for superior firepower, God dice for superior morality.

    Otherwise, you'll win by Cowardice every time (which is the default option, because if you have no dice, you take a Cowardice die). Winning via Cowardice is an unpleasant way to win: sure, you shoot the enemy, but you scream and soil your pants while doing so.

    So you don't want Cowardice dice. However, if you ever accept anything from the natives (including food, directions or assistance), you'll have to take one. And so you'll need other dice, to make it less likely you'll keep winning by Cowardice.

    Oh, and no, you don't start with one of each by default.
