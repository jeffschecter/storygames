Miracles in my TSOY Game

tony_dowler
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games
I'm working on a game using the Solar System. The settings is an alternate magical rennaisance Europe. One of the features of the setting is that Christianity never really got off the ground as a big social leader. Christians live in small mostly autonomous communities holding their possessions in common, forgiving their enemies, and loving their neighbors as best they can. It's as though Christianity stayed more or less in the form we see in the Acts of the Apostles. The big church in Europe is a centralized pagan religion with big rituals and divine powers handed down from the gods.

Eventually, someone's going to want to play a character who gets tied up with these strange folks who call themselves Christians. So what do I do when one of these characters sees a sick guy on the street and decides to heal him by God's power? I'd like my game to have a mechanic for miracles, but I'm not sure how to do it mechanically without having it suck or be ridiculous. Any insight?

    In mine I just assigned the labels "virtuous" and "sinful" to some of the Keys (included the Seven Deadly Sins and their virtuous counterparts as Keys), and stated that the Pray ability check is at -1 for each sinful Key and +1 for each virtuous key that the character has.     Sorry to necro this thread, but I totally just figured out the answer to my problem, and it's already built into TSOY. I want this concept of mircles in a way that retains some of the feeling of the legends of the early church. I don't want miracles to be "just another sort of magic", nor do I want them to be too open-ended. So here's how it works:

    There are a few secrets that represent fairly simple miracles. For example, the secret of tongues lets the character be understood in any language when they preach the gospel. The secret of healing lets a character heal wounds, possibly with a hefty pools expenditure. These secrets represent the miracles worked by someone who is essentially a minor saint. They include the caveat that the character has to live according to a loosely-defined code, probably requiring them to be poor and not gain power over their fellow people. So far so good. The player has a way to explore a particular kind of play experience through their character.

    But what if the player wants the character to become one of the great saints of the age? That's exactly the kind of thing that Mathematica is meant to allow. The answer to the problem is transcendence. Once the player reaches the point where they're done exploring this character, they have the option of transcending and narrating how their character works a great miracle, becomes a great evangelist, is martyred, or whatever they desire. The character is, in a sense, too much a saint to be playable.

    I may even work in specific special narrations that are only available to characters with specific secrets. Only Christians can become saints, only pagans can ascend to godhood, only alchemists can discover the philospher's stone... the possibilities are endless.

    This works even better in Mathematica because the game is meant to be played in relatively short series with defined endgames. Playing a character through one or more series then transcending them to remain an NPC in the setting is completely appropriate to the game.

    On the other hand, it still leaves the player room to decide that their saintly personage has fallen from grace and play out that experience, which is also exactly the kind of gameplay I want Mathematic to include.

    By the way, thanks for helping out everyone. I don't think I could have figured out how I wanted to do this without someone to bounce the idea off of.
