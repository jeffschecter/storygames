    \n I don't play indie games with the people that I roleplayed with before I was "converted."     Which is to say \u2014 it's pretty probable that the guys you play with now don't want to play indie games any more than your baseball team would like to suddenly become a quilting circle. As others have implied, don't try to convert your friends. Find other people interested in playing "indie" stuff.

    To use the British slang (probably completely incorrectly): Bollocks.

    \n

    More my style: T'hell with that. Recruit, convert, whatever. Go for it. They're your friends, and you want to play with them, so give it a shot. Just don't be a dick about it.

    \n

    First, try to figure out what they're objecting to. Is it the crazy differentness of the games? Resolving a whole conflict on a die-roll may seem unfun to people who like to get down into the nitty-gritty of the blow by blow. Is it that the games are physically smaller, so how could a decent game be put into such a small package when D&D requires 2+ full-size (letter-size, 200-300 pages, hard bound) books to be able to play? (This is a real issue for some people; The sheer tininess of the books made them easy to discount when I first encountered the scene) Is it just that they really like D20-esque rules?

    \n

    'cause there are indie games that satisfy all of those objections. Some games (like the aforementioned Shadow of Yesterday, or The Riddle of Steel, if it's still able to be found in print) are nice, crunchy games with a very traditional feel. Some games (again, The Riddle of Steel, and at least some others) are big ol' solid books with supplements even. There's even at least one D20-based indie game out there.

    \n

    Indie doesn't mean Dogs in the Vineyard, Sorcerer and It's Complicated. There's a wide, wide variety of styles that fall into the indie/small-press and "Story" games categories. Sometimes, it's not even a matter of the game as the techniques used. Look at some of the play discussions about D&D here, and over on the Forge, where certain techniques born (or at least popularized) in indie gaming have been introduced successfully to traditional games like D&D, Shadowrun, L5R, etc.

    \n

    As for me: 3:16 helped. It was a game I introduced by saying "Hey, I've got this game that I can explain to you, we can make characters, and play a full session in 2-3 hours. It's all about space marines shooting up aliens for Terra." I received lifted eyebrows, skeptical expressions and grudging nods of acceptance to my proposal. Two hours later, I've got people laughing and asking where they could find the game for sale as we clean up to go home for the night. It's a test-drive with option to buy, and it takes no more commitment than playing a boardgame.

    \n

    The Riddle of Steel also helped me get some trad players into trying indie games. It's a big ol' game full of pulpy fantasy goodness, with crunchy rules that include such mechanical "comfort food" as encumbrance rules. There are lists of weapons and armor, a nice and flavorful table of general equipment ("wolf, not happy" was something you could buy). Then when you play, you find that your contributions to the fiction matter, and that by playing proactively rather than following the bread crumbs the GM lays out, you do better. The game doesn't work as written any other way.

    \n I don't think anyone I personally know is actually "converted," if by that you mean "playing only 'indie' games and scorning anything else."

    But yeah, we play some games from time to time that I guess fall into what you're calling 'indie,' although like JDCorley's group, I don't think any of us gives a crap about anything other than whether a game is good and if we're going to have fun with it.

    How we added those games to our lineup is pretty straightforward: someone got the game, looked it over, and said "This looks like fun." Then came a pitch to the group -- "Hey, this game looks pretty fun, I'd like to run it, let me tell you about it." Then came general nodding and approval, because the game that was pitched sounded like it would be good. Then we played, and had a good time, and decided that we'd like to play it again sometime.

    No long, drawn-out discussions of theory, no expectation that the new game would be anything other than a fun thing to try playing, and no lingering resentment on the occasions when the game turned out NOT to be fun; basically, we just used the same process that went into deciding to play, say, D&D3 for the first time, or nWoD, or whatever. We're really never more than one good pitch away from playing ANYTHING.

    \n My advice (and it echoes a lot of what's already been said) is:

    A) Don't be the indie games inquisition. People don't like to be "converted," they like to play fun games. If you make it an ideological conflict (which is silly anyway), you will breed unnecessary dissent. You also have to be as open-minded as you're expecting them to be. If you roll your eyes about "those primitive trad games" expect the same reaction when you bring up "those crazy indie games." In short, don't bother making a distinction between "indie" and "trad." Games = Games.

    Create a group culture that revolves around playing short campaigns and one-shots. You'll cycle through a lot more games that way.

    C) If your group flat out doesn't want to play the games that you do, look for some new folks to play with. Check out nearbygamers.com and hit some people up. It doesn't mean you have to drop your old group, just broaden your horizons a bit.

    \n All the "find new folks" and "ease in" and other advice about the social situation is great--follow it, Seth.

    But I'll go ahead and read the OP as I think it was intended: a poll to find which games have a great "grab" to attract folks to regular play (regardless of whether they leave "trad" games, or continue them with another group or at another time, or even have never been gamers). I'll use myself, as I was a "nothing but HERO" guy for years, until....

    My first "indie" experience was Dogs in the Vineyard in a one-shot con game, run by someone with a lot of experience with it (*ahem* he's in this thread), and it ran well and I loved the scope of the narrative power that it granted. Here was a game that let me be the sort of kook that I like to role play and, dammit, that had leverage (i.e. it wasn't epiphenomenal to the "actually important" crunchy bits that the "trad" games gave me to affect the SIS). But if I had not read a fair bit about DitV, I might have struggled to get into that mode of play (at least for a while). This is the game that I would introduce someone who has demonstrated disaffection with charts and graphs and classes and feats--someone wanting their character to be more than a functional role.

    Not long afterward, I played Contenders, and again it gave a lot of room to tell story and have it stick, but it *also* had this fun and easy-to-learn "mini game" of the boxing match. Great resource management, tough decisions, a bit of strategy, a bit of luck--GREAT intro game to anyone who's seen Rocky or Raging Bull. This is the game that I would introduce to a non-gamer or to someone who wanted "just to give it a try, no commitment."

    By that point, I'm collecting indie games, playing a few at the local game store or friends' houses, and exploring the boundaries of the "indie" design space. Anyone who knows me (and the games on which I am working) will know I'm a freak for the toolbox, for the universal engine, for the "one game book I'd take to a desert island." Enter Universalis, with all its world creation, challenges, fact creation, and mechanics to make those facts matter. Flexible but structured--this is no freeform, and yet I could see the potential for ANY story to be modeled with it. Though a bit leery of losing the direct coupling of player to character, I could see the potential that such a break from "tradition" opened up. This is the game that I would introduce to actors, improv-ers, writers, and other creative types who might never play a "trad" game, with its mechanical minutiae, genre conventions, and rituals.

    And then there was Burning Wheel (though I'd bought and read Burning Empires first). *Glee* A combination of all the crunch you care to employ, with a "points build" system that reflected the game world without pigeonholing a PC into one of ten archetypes, and a powerful reward and resource mechanic that made character matter and gave players agency. It's my go-to game, now, for any but the most easily distracted, easily overwhelmed, or stubbornly competitive. This is the game that I would introduce to a group of gamers who were curious about the "indie" thing but who I though would likely balk at something that gave them a high degree of narrative agency.

    I hope this helps you, Seth, with what I imagine was your intent all along when you sought advice in this thread: continuing to play with these friends, these free nights, while branching out to a style of play that is very hard to get at with "trad" games. Or making new gamers out of other friends, with systems and setting that don't overwhelm anyone not already into genre fiction and the like.

    \n Something has been bothering me about responses like "You can't convert people" but I couldn't put my finger on it.
    \n
    \n Then it hit me. This is not about Indie vs. Trad. You can't force people to play a game they don't "get".
    \n For instance, I was playing with a group and we were playing CP2020. I thought they dug some of the Asian vibes and we had been playing the Legend of the 5 Rings CCG.
    \n So, I suggested playing L5R RPG.
    \n It was an unmitigated disaster. I wanted to play 47 Ronin, they wanted to play D&D with karate.
    \n
    \n This doesn't have anything to do with converting, or the old "indie vs. trad" dichotomy. This has to do with, we wanted something different from L5R.
    \n
    \n What I am trying to say is, if you try an indie game and the group doesn't grok it. It might just be the setting, genre conventions or something else that isn't clicking. It might have nothing to do with it being indie.
    \n
    \n Also, sometimes, you can oversell something. I can get most people to try DITV if I just say, "you play a wild west paladin with a pistol instead of a sword." And that communicates a lot more to most players than, "You play in a fictional Mormon state in a wild west that never was."
    \n
    \n Finally, if you say, this game is indie and I want to try more indie games. And it flops, you have just co-mingled every game you like into this one bad experience. And, honestly, most indie games have very different look/feel/playstyles. Playing DITV produces a different experience/feel than PTA, no? So why lump them into one non-existent genre?
    \n
    \n I mean, if the people you are dealing with are the kind that root for small business over big box chains, then its a selling point that its indie, but if the don't know or don't care who makes their games, its just a useless label that may work against you moving forward, no?
    \n
    \n I dunno, that's just my thoughts, good luck getting a group of indie players and have fun games man.
