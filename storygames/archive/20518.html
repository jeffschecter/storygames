    \n Is success at a task a tangible reward? If not, why isn't it?

    Here's what I think the deal is: rewards exist on a spectrum from 100% extrinsic (compensation has nothing to do with the task) to 100% intrinsic (the only compensation is the satisfaction of the task).

    On the extrinsic extreme, you have the Skinner box: a meaningless action that has no innate rewarding consequence, given a totally extrinsic reward that has nothing to do with that task. You push the button, you get a cookie. You open the box, you get a goodie. It should be obvious why this is a degenerate design.

    On the intrinsic extreme, maybe a good model is the fantasy heartbreaker: an RPG that probably nobody will ever play (thus you don't receive the extrinsic reward of applause from your peers), which is only satisfying because you've done it. It's up to you to decide whether this is a good sort of reward.

    I personally think it's very okay for a thing to be partially extrinsically rewarding. We do it all the time. Sure, you might do something for the love of the thing, but there's nothing wrong with adding an additional reinforcement to that, as well. In fact, one of the basic building blocks of teaching is positive reinforcement, which is a sort of extrinsic reward.

      Assuming we have built a game with a core activity that the player enjoys, how do we keep them playing, and what makes them stop? It's all about the rewards the player experiences, and the manner in which those rewards are structured.

      What sort of rewards can the player experience? Without attempting to define a taxonomy:\n
        Fixed Ratio Schedules: these provide rewards after a fixed number of actions. They produce a high level of activity and are easy to understand, but after the reward is achieved, there is a pause. XP in CRPGs is an example - although the gearing of XP systems is exponential, the intent is that the player is constantly moving up to tougher foes, thus keeping a constant ratio of kills to level. /.../

        Variable Ratio Schedules: these provide rewards after a random number of actions - like a slot machine. You keep putting in coins, because at some point it will pay out. These also produce a high rate of activity and interest, but they tend to block exploration - as the player will stick with the reward schedule until it is exhausted, or until they burn out on it. Effective with all play styles - but burnout is always a risk. /.../

        Fixed Interval Schedule: that is, a reward is provided after a set amount of time. This provides better control over the rate of reward, and comes with the same post-reward pause as a fixed ratio schedule. Indeed, pauses are inherent to fixed schedules of all kinds. An example is the new items in the shop each day in Animal Crossing - the player comes back on future days to see what's new.

        Variable Interval Schedule: like the variable ratio schedule, this produces a steady rate of activity with no pauses - but its not as intense as the variable ratio schedule, because players quickly learn that their actions are independent of the reward. Good for encouraging a player to come back to certain places in a game, however, if a reward appears in certain places 'at random'.
      \nIn general, ratio schedules produce high rates of activity - "the more you do, the more you get". Variable schedules produce constant activity - "everything has a chance of reward". When these combine, (variable ratio schedule), the player will eventually burn out. Conversely, fixed schedules create a pause - which needn't be a negative matter.

    \n Here's another thought about game design goals: sometimes the momentary enjoyment of one player is not the most important thing to consider.

    I have a game design in progress where supervillains pursue a variety of needy, neurotic goals. Over the course of playtesting, I have found that mutual enjoyment benefits from everyone at the table seeing how each villain's need is driven by suffering. If we see the villain lamenting his loneliness before he goes on his mission to seek adoration, then everyone else is better prepared to invest in what the villain player is doing. More roleplayed suffering = more fun for the whole group.

    The problem, though, is that in the regular flow of the game, there isn't always a convenient and natural time to show this suffering, and any given villain player may have their mind on other things. I am still working on an ideal solution for this -- I have plenty of idea that just need to be tested -- but in the meantime, I decided to try simply giving villain players points for showing suffering.

    So, what happened? Well, the introduction of the reward was mostly irrelevant. Mostly -- but not entirely. Here's the breakdown:
    - some players who didn't show suffering before the points were added still didn't after the points were added
    - some of the players who were already showing suffering continued to do so but forgot about the points when they did so, only looking back once the time arrived to spend those points -- "Oh, hey, I have an extra one from suffering earlier, don't I?"
    - some of the players who were already showing suffering were happy to mark down a point after doing so; this did not affect their (or anyone else's) enjoyment of the experience in any perceptible way (suffering generally only comes up twice per player all game; it could be that "demotivation from points" could occur if there were more repetition?)
    - a very few players who didn't otherwise show suffering saw "oh, I get points for that!" on their character sheets or in other villains' scenes, and proceeded to show suffering themselves

    This was a far from an ideal outcome, but I do think it was a net positive. Most players' experience was not impacted (or perhaps very slightly negatively impacted by having a game feature to consider that they didn't actually care about), while a small number of other players found a new incentive to do a thing (show suffering) that made the game more fun for everyone, themselves included.

    I think a lot of game design is like this. As long as you're not trying to get people to do something harmful, or something they don't enjoy, then why not use whatever techniques are effective, "tricky" or otherwise?

This is of course false - not because the science is false, but rather because you can't just decide arbitrarily that this or that complex phenomenon is the same thing as this seemingly different thing researched in a different discipline. I'll welcome a treatise written by a competent psychologist on how Burning Wheel (or some similar game) uses extrinsic rewards if somebody here wants to play the science game, of course.

    The concept of "reward" in rpgs is commonly pretty muddled, and it doesn't help that Forgite theory uses the same term to mean something pretty different. Specifically, traditional rpg language uses "reward" to mean things that the GM distributes to the players as a technique of play that involves GM judgment, while Forgite theory uses the word for the satisfaction of creative needs. These are obviously different things, and sometimes things that are rewards in the common language are not rewards in the theoretical sense, or vice versa.

    As an example, experience systems are often called reward systems and even handled as pavlovian reward machines by some GMs. This is not the ideal function for experience in most games, though. In old school D&D, for example, the GM does not give you experience levels as a reward for being a good boy or something like that; the xp is merely an objective metrification of success that has already occurred. The xp is not even a reward in the theoretical sense, it's merely a symbol and acknowledgement of the real reward, which is the satisfaction of success in winning the challenge. The points are then further converted to experience level gain that basically glorifies and rewards the success by allowing you to play a more powerful and important character in bigger adventures later on; however, it is not the points that are the reward, it is the opportunity to play a bigger game thenceforth.

    Similarly the experience system in a trad game - e.g. Vampire - is not intended to be an extrinsic reward; the xp is supposed to enable you to make changes to your character that make his story more tangible on the character sheet and bring him closer to what you want the character to be. It would be fair to argue that the "show up to play" xp reward common to these games is an extrinsic reward of sorts, but it's minor and ultimately just part of the general notion that the GM paces the campaign by deciding how much xp he doles out.

    Finally, the various xp and hero point systems in Burning Wheel and similar games are not extrinsic to the activity the game assumes as the core of play: narrativist games use similar mechanisms where the players judge each other's play all the time, and it's almost always interesting because we want the players to talk to each other about the meaning or meaninglessness of their decisions. Burning Wheel, by making certain in-game resources dependent on player judgements of character behavior at regular intervals, regulates and defines a discussion space where this critical reflection occurs. This is not extrinsic at all to the game's narrativist goals.

    I'll note that I can see how one could conclude that certain of Burning Wheel's systems are extrinsic if you're not in line with what the game is trying to do. For instance, my experience is that immersionist players of various stripes absolutely abhor these types of rules systems, largely because they seem to miss the point: you should play your character and act for them because that pure act is a value in itself, so having rules tell you what you must do and rewarding and punishing for behavior hampers and twists the game. Maybe this is why Rickard thinks that BW's mechanics provide extrinsic rewards?

    Regarding motivation and psychology, I think there's one area where this research does apply, and that's campaigns that keep going even when they're not fun anymore. Here's what I think is happening:

    There's this psychological phenomenon where you experience something and your brain doesn't know why you're experiencing it, and misinterprets the situation. For example, you're attacked by a man in a beard, and you develop a phobia of beards. Or you're on a date, order a decaf and get caffeinated coffee instead, and your brain interprets the shivers and increased heart rate as affection for your date (yes, this has been shown to happen). Or you are in a hazing ritual that's really humiliating, and you stay in it because of peer pressure, but you then think you must REALLY want to be in this group, because you're obviously prepared to endure a lot to be in it.

    This phenomenon is, I think, widely studied and somewhat generalizable. And here's something we might want to be wary of as game designers. If you're doing something that's fun and you get a reward, your brain might think that you're actually doing it for the reward, and if you stop the reward, you'll likely find it less fun. So if you're playing D&D and stop giving out XP, players might well tire even though they'd have enjoyed it without the XP if that was the deal from the beginning. And, since the XP never stop coming and you keep gaining levels, you might keep playing because you associate those XPs with the fun you had in the beginning, even if you're not really having as much fun anymore. So that might be a thing.

They're pacing mechanisms, they're thematic statements (you get better at magic by killing people), they're signals from the author as to what the game is about, etc.

    \n I'll make a separate constructive post, too, because this is a topic that merits some more writing than just trying to explain to Rickard why I am not convinced by his claims.

    As I've hinted earlier, I think that it is necessary to be careful about what you describe as a reward extrinsic to the activity itself - you need to understand the activity to make the judgement, for instance, which may be non-trivial. I do think, though, that the actual phenomenon of ineffective extrinsic rewards - and even lazy game design using such - is probably real, even if I disagree with Rickard about every one of his examples so far.

    As I think I mentioned earlier, I think that difficulty in determining what's "bad design" in this way goes back to understanding the actual instance of play. The same game can provide extrinsic or intrinsic rewards in different instances, for example, if it is being utilized for different purposes.

    To illustrate this important point about how subjective our judgements can be, a practical example: in Ars Magica it is possible for your characters to gain experience points and thus increase their skills in a variety of ways. One of those ways is adventuring, and adventuring is in fact a trade-off in some ways, because the character could as well spend their time reading a book at home, accumulating xp that way.

    I would argue that the experience points you get for adventuring are an extrinsic reward from the perspective of the adventure, and the ethos of adventuring for the sake of adventuring; they are a bribe the system uses to somewhat balance the choice and thus make it easier for characters to commit to adventuring when provided a passably promising adventuring hook. Everything Rickard says about the problems of extrinsic rewards potentially applies: a player may adventure for the wrong reasons, poisoning the experience, or they may lose their interest in the contents of the adventure when given the choice of cutting the adventure short to go directly to scoring the "prize" in the form of the xp reward.

    However, from the perspective of a long campaign those experience points are very much not an extrinsic issue: you could put them into a rare skill that cannot be trained by books or tutors (the Adamic language from one of the core supplements of the current edition comes to mind), for example, and thus the points could contribute to the character's long-term goals. The fact that the character learned some important insight while travelling abroad becomes a tidbit of detail for the campaign lore - treasured color for the game. It is an intrinsic feature of the game for characters to accumulate lore and learning from a variety of sources, and in this way it very much is an intrinsic matter whether your character gets this or that skill to level 10 during the campaign or not. In fact, Ars Magica the game would disappear almost entirely were it not for the pacing and regulation that raising skills provides: almost everything in the game is about characters accumulating experience points to become proficient enough to move on with their plans.

    As we can see, even a single game can be at cross-purposes with itself about the intrinsic nature of its elements - about whether they are rewards at all, in fact. Whether the adventuring xp in Ars Magica is a good idea (this is what it boils down to, here) depends on how the group construes their campaign and what their attitudes to the various stages of play are, and so on. It is impossible to say in general whether that rule is a good one; the best we can say is that when utilized in the spirit it is intended, it is a logical and valuable part of the game.

    So OK, we've hopefully established that this is a subjective issue that needs to be considered in the creative context of the play instance, not as an abstract mechanical question. This goes for all sorts of games, including Once Upon a Time, on which I actually agree with Rickard somewhat - it is at horrible, horrible cross-purposes with itself due to how the game encourages you to both win and tell a story in style, simultaneously. Yet even there we know that there are groups for whom OUaT works fine and well, with no problems whatsoever. (We also know why this is the case, incidentally: the group either has a strong creative commitment to a style threshold - such as a single player who has enough social capital to demand that the others uphold the standard - or the group only uses the victory condition as a pacing device to end the game rather than the player actively competing for it.)

    I think that all this comes back to an old Forgite saw-horse, namely the notion of cohesive game design rooted in clarity of the creative agenda. We cannot even say whether a given rule is an extrinsic reward system without choosing what the creative purpose of play is supposed to be, but what we can do is to attempt to create games that service our chosen creative purpose well. A well-designed game being played in the same spirit it was intended by its creator won't have the lazy design Rickard abhors (and which I, too, abhor, although we seem to have rather different notions of what constitutes lazy design if he thinks that Burning Wheel passes for it) because the designer is not in fact bribing the players to do what he wants - he's giving the players tools to execute what he, the designer, feels is a worthwhile endeavour.

    Meanwhile, there are lazy games out there, a multitude of them. I think that extrinsic rewarding is very, very common in trad adventure games, for example: every game from MERP to Exalted and beyond runs off the idea that the GM can reward and punish the players with combat competence (xp to level up and purchase powers) to keep them in line and properly appreciative of their storytelling; you play along with the storytelling or you don't get the xp rewards to make your character a badder mofo. This is true lazy, schizoid extrinsic reward bribery game design, and it does exist. (Although, in all honesty, I have to say that many of the games doing this stuff are just copying others or being timid about innovation and whatnot - I'm not suggesting that all these designers actively think about their game working in terms of bribery. Besides, if the group is actually on board with the GM's plans, there is no bribery because they're doing it anyway, right? It's all subjective.)

      \n Y'know. I'm thinking about this a bit more, and the more I think about it, the less good I think the sports metaphor that is being thrown around is.

      Because all of this stuff is being designed INTO the game. So it's not about "playing basketball is the game, and giving out trophies is the extrinsic reward" at all.       Viewing Fanmail/Aiki/whatever as "rewards" at all is perhaps the more fundamental flaw than thinking of them as "extrinsic rewards" but both are equally wrong. These are goals, not rewards.

      \n TSoY Keys are very intentionally multifunctional, it's part of the (lazy?) design spec. Specifically, some Keys are intended as dramatic pacing flags ("Is it already time for this unstable state of things to combust and resolve itself?"), some are thematic communication vehicles ("Does this event that just now happened count as murder, what do you think?"), and some are character projection flags ("Should I maybe project my character's core nature more strongly into the inter-character interplay?"). The game allows a very high level of customization in this regard in an attempt to be inclusive of a variety of personal needs and interests regarding the role you and your character might play in the game.

      The foremost TSoY rule that "balances" XP take is scene framing: because you cannot buy more screen time with XP, the only thing you're actually doing by raking in more XP than some other player is positioning your character in a different way against the setting and other characters. For dramatic purposes it is just as good to be weak as it is to be strong. There is no value associated with fast XP gain per se in the game, except the cultural tendency of the target audience to be anxious to hurry up with character growth before the GM comes to their senses and closes the bank. (As one might imagine, the game plays very differently in your second campaign, once the players get the XP hoarding out of their systems.
