\n So I'm greedy, and I want to make my PTA games the best they can be. I ran two good sessions at Nerdly, but as I understand it that Remi guy, kicked some serious PTA butt. So I'm calling him out, and anyone else who has any helping hints to making PTA better. What you got? How do you do it? What makes it sing for your group?

Personally I always

- give everyone index cards, and after the concept has been established, let them throw out NPCs, locations, situations, tropes, character ideas, into a big buffet to use to seed the start of the show.
- make an explicit R-map for the characters, and try really hard not to start until the characters are reasonably linked together

Jason has suggested to me to use a slow reveal/chase kind of turn over of the cards on critical conflicts to heighten the tension, and thats worked well.

It seems to help, but lately I've been wondering if I spend too much time in the meta game, or too much time setting the scene agenda, and not enough spontaneously seeing where the game goes.

So suggest something that'll help me bring the awesome....

    Posted By: forlorn1It seems to help, but lately I've been wondering if I spend too much time in the meta game, or too much time setting the scene agenda, and not enough spontaneously seeing where the game goes.
    \nSounds like it, sorry to say. Usually I find that the players are telling you what they want the scene to be about through their chosen issue and how they play the scene. You don't need to do a lot of scene agenda setup and, in fact, it's easy to do too much.

    One thing I do to promote "seeing where the game goes" is to really listen to the players during each scene and make the conflict in the scene about their character's issue. Rather than set the stakes of a conflict as "do you succeed" at some action, I make it "do you overcome your issue" to do or not do what you want in the scene. Quite often, they give in to their issue so that they can do whatever action was intended and that adds depth to their character and add tension to the story even though they "succeeded".

    \nBefore I offer up my examination of PTA and how to make running it better, let me say this... Your game was the first game of PTA I had ever played, and I don't want to take away from the awesome that it was. Because of you I really wanted to jump into Remi's game, almost messing up his mojo. Neither game was bad, but both were definitely different.

    Ok, ... deep breath.

    I was in "Unit of Worship" and Remi's game "Eve of Destruction" so I guess I could toss my 2 centimes in. Big differences I noticed 1. Our relationship map in both games was totally different, probably just a fluke but I think they seriously influenced the direction play went and here's why. Instead of setting up a lot of conflict between characters (and there was a little) our R-map in Eve of Destruction centred around our relationship with an NPC, Eve. Practically everyone had some sort of connection to her either directly or through someone else. In Unit of Worship a lot of players were tightly tied to other players, but not effectively tied to others. In fact we had two opposing groups of characters Primus's Cult and Bacchuses Mob.

    Tangent- In Eve, we had PVP conflicts, and as long as we kind of agreed beforehand it worked pretty well. I think we shied away from one or two great conflicts in Unit because of the fear of losing free agency. Maybe thats something that could be dealt with prior to playing? I don't know but its worth some thought.

    Ok, going back to the R map. I think that mattered because of the way that we were handling episodes. Remi was running under a mini-series hack where 3 characters had the spotlight, during the whole thing but not at the same time. Everyone else had a 2, and if your spotlight was over you got a 1.
    Unit of Worship was the season finale. For con play as exciting as a finale can be, your almost forced to do a lot of playing before you play. In fact I'd do episode 1 or 2 for con play instead of doing a finale, because at least one character has a spotlight, without forcing players to establish a whole season of history before you even start setting scenes. That worked against us because we had 2 opposed groups with a big history of conflict between them.

    I liked the mini series hack because it gave a lot of folks a spotlight and took place in acts, each one actually ending pretty organically. Act One, Johnny Lightning turning his back on the Punk rock crowd and becoming a defender. Act II Lance Shooting Johnny's sister in the face and act III the whole compound getting overrun from the inside and the outside at the same time.

    It was pretty exciting, but I dont know if it was the right combination of ideas and players that did it or the mechanic. Im tempted to say mechanic, because Crisis of faith had some excellent players. Anyhow for a one off it was brilliant.

    \n I was in Remi's game and have played PTA quite a bit. I hope this will be helpful to you (some of it overlaps with Jeff's post):

    - Remi was great at making sure that everyone was 100% bought into the show's premise and was willing to throw it all out until it worked.

    - We not only set up the show's premise but we had several large conflicts established as well. Military state trying to recruit the punk rock community. Punk Rock rebellion. Zombies trying to invade the city. These conflicts fed into each other and created a lot of subtext for us to work with.

    - Remi created an NPC to symbolize the overall conflict and had us all form a relationship with them.

    - We then each established 1 other relationship. Then the player to our right linked to that relationship somehow.

    - We ditched personal sets.

    - He also condensed the game into 3 individual episodes of PTA where 3 people were spotlighted. In their spotlight episodes they had a presence of 3 and then a 1 for every other episode. The non-spotlighted characters had a presence of 2 all the time.

    - Our issues were less issues and more goals or beliefs like in Burning Wheel. Mine was, "kill punk rock." We still had issues but we just never stated them. If we did issues traditionally, mine would have been "hypocrite." I was actually obsessed Punk Rock. But instead of being a hypocrite off the bat, we let our beliefs reveal our issues in play.

    - We didn't keep track of edges. Instead we played it loose to what felt right in a scene.

    - Players set up scenes. We never explicitly detailed focus, agenda, or location but instead described a scene and implied those details. Then let the GM go crazy by introducing elements on top of that to create tension.

    - The game was set up to be Player vs. Player. We started out forming 2 sides. The police state and everyone else. But then had PvP within those sides as well. Every conflict reflecting the tensions of the overall conflicts we setup.

    - In creating the show or setting stakes... when anyone started "playing before we started playing", meaning describing content that would be better played in game, Remi would stop us immediately and ask us to save it for the game. The stakes were much tighter this way. And we stuck to stakes that involved our characters' actions rather than more meta level stakes.

    - In PTA you can't have perpendicular stakes. Because it isn't actually player vs. player (even when the conflict seems that way). It's all players vs. the producer. Anyone who beats the producer gets what they want. So 2 players fighting could both win a conflict. Additionally the Producer doesn't have stakes normally. They can only set difficulty and narrate if they draw the highest card. All this can lead to flat situations. The game is waaaaaaay more interesting when the Producer has stakes. Otherwise it sometimes feels like we are just playing to see who gets to say what. Where the producer has stakes, not only are we trying to get what we want but also trying to keep some horrible unforeseen results from occurring. Which adds a lot of tension. So what we did was have the Producer have stakes, allow true PvP, and have who ever draws the most hearts wins vs. just beating the producer.

    - We played narrating very loose. Instead of the person with the highest card narrating, they more acted like a GM and allowed the players to describe how they won or lost and adding details as necessary. We actually ignored who drew the highest card several times.

    - We ditched the limit on awarding fan mail. Instead of each player only being able to award 1 point of fan mail per scene, we loosened the restriction so that each player can award fan mail to multiple other players in a scene as long as they don't give a single player fan mail more than once. Fan mail flowed quite often!

    - If a scene didn't look like it would hit a conflict, we didn't force a conflict. We simply played till we hit conflicts rather than artificially putting them in. Although Remi did a great job adding so much tension that conflicts came very naturally.

    - We used the "car chase" rules quite a bit to add drama when flipping cards. I love this optional rule!

    - Remi made sure that everyone had something to do. He would look around the table, spot the person who has done the least, and target the drama at them.

    - Remi also encouraged everyone to throw out suggestions any time while not blocking each other's input. Everyone really helped everyone else shine.

    - Remi also constantly inter-cut between multiple scenes which was awesome!

    I think that's most of it.

    Rock,
    John

    \n Wow, guys, thanks! I lost a big long post last night due to a power outage, and haven't had time to recreate it today.

    A couple things:
    1. Eve emerged out of your Issue creation organically. I pushed a little on it once three people in a row had latched onto her as a central character, but it wasn't my intention to create a singular interconnected NPC. It actually made it a little difficult to access her sometimes because she was SO meaningful to everyone at the table.

    2. We only used the Car Chase rules twice, both for arguments, I think. I save the mechanic for situations which require a little back-and-forth or expanded narration. An argument between two people fits this as well as a car chase.

    2. John, thank you for listing all the ways I drift PTA. It's good to have that catalogue to examine. I never realized PTA had so much overhead! I can't imagine setting up all that stuff at the beginning of a scene.

    3. Jeff, you didn't almost disrupt any mojo that I can remember. You rocked out while I kicked your ass!

    Jeff/forlorn1, is there anything that I can expand on? To break it down a bit, my PTA style comes from several sources.
    Improv: Intercutting scenes, setting up situations quickly, encouraging agreement and group input, the 'no denial' rule in the Pitch (which wasn't mentioned, but is an important difference for me).
    Application of Actual Play : The change in the Issues, the fanmail, and the high card responsibilities, the Spotlight shifts.
    My Neuroses: Getting through the pitch as fast as possible, making sure everyone at the table is getting solid play time, discomfort with the traditional GM role translating to differences in scene framing.
    Not knowing the PTA text all that well: Ignoring a lot of the scene framing and conflict guidelines.

    I do want to say that I've tuned PTA so that it is easy for me to run. Not all of these things will work for everyone, in fact, sometimes it doesn't work 100% correctly for me (see: My Dreamation game).

    When I say Remi polled us above, he didn't just do so by asking people how they felt but also by paying attention to our facial expressions, body language, energy level, and how excited we seemed. So in the case of someone who thinks:

    Remi would try to determine their interest based on non verbal cues. And even if the results of brainstorming don't produce results that everyone is 100% happy with, at the very least there should be one aspect that everyone is excited about. In those cases, calling on someone's character's issue can be a huge help. Say Friend X didn't love the show's premise. Then use their issue to retroactively alter the premise enough to make the game interesting specifically for them. The premise is only the starting point. The issue is where we can really refine things and make sure everyone is happy.

    \n You guys keep beating me to the punch with posts that are much clearer than what I was writing!

    Maddrjeffe has the heart of it. Basically I don't want to alienate player input. The Producer should be totally willing to throw out the combined pitch if it isn't hitting with the group, because their Pitch shouldn't input anything new, it should just be a synthesis of the ideas presented, straining out the bits into a coherent whole. John's right that I ask, "Does that sound good?" and if I don't get at least some kind of reaction from everyone at the table, I start building again.

    This is a very Producer-centric technique. It's the responsibility of the Producer to combine what people are excited about into a coherent whole. If the Producer abuses this, I'd imagine it could get very ugly. You want to try to honor everyone's suggestion, at least a little. I try to be as clear as possible to the player WHAT I am drawing out of their suggestion. My hope is that if I haven't gotten any part of what's exciting to them about their suggestion, they'll tell me or indicate it in some way. I also try to be clear how I get to my Pitch suggestion, so people can see where they want to fill out more, or what aspect of the Pitch they'd like to concentrate on.

    Also, this is totally a technique for Con scenarios where time is at a premium. Still, I think encouraging people not to shut each other down and build on one another's ideas is useful anywhere. Another common technique is the "No, But" technique where you are allowed to turn down a suggestion, but only if you have a suggestion to take its place. This takes a lot longer, though, although it does create a 'smoother' pitch where the constituent parts are a little less obvious.

    \n Well, I don't "stay in character" for the whole Pitch, just enough to lend some energy to the process when things start to founder on the 'you can do aaaanything' reefs that eventually crop up. And for some parts (character concepts, for example), I stay more low key, it's the player's satisfaction with their protagonist that is of primary importance there rather than group buy-in (as it is with show concept and episode planning.)

    But basically here is my advice on getting great PTA ideas:

    Watch a lot of good television
    Listen to a lot of commentary tracks for television and film
    Incorporate as needed

    And here is my advice on how and when to yell:

    Always, always incorporate something into your yelling that makes it clear that you are a little stupid and wrong, so everyone can laugh.
    If someone suggests the same idea a second time, LOVE it the second time as much as you hated it the first time.
    Muse out loud about how awful this show is shaping up to be, then leave the table and issue a general threat to everyone to have it be better by the time you get back. That puts a time limit on idea-fleshing-out.
    Get episode concepts along with show concepts by yelling "SO WHAT, what does that do for us?"
    Setting and plot ideas are good but they'll never last more than a ten second blurb if you don't tie it in to a character
    Brag about your clout and promise you'll "go to bat" for an idea with the networks/sponsor/blogging nerds on the Internet if it needs to get a bit more attention

    \n Commercial breaks are cool.

    When someone delivers the perfect line to button up the scene and you fade to black... commercial break! It releases the tension in a nice way. People can ooh and aaah, laugh, pat each other on the back, sigh, and generally reflect and appreciate the game, outside the flow of play.

    Go get a drink, bathroom break, whatever. Then, when everyone is back at the table, and the chit-chat dies down, the show comes back, and it's game on again.

    The art of the perfectly timed commercial break is something the best shows master. Buffy, Angel, and Firefly are really good at it. They're a good tool for rhythm and pacing, and help reinforce the feel of television.
