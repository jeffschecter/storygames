Too Many Ideas, Not Enough Me

Jason_Morningstar
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
So I've got a metric ass-load of ideas, some still in my head, some a few pages of notes, some actually finished games that don't work for some reason or another. I think this is sort of the normal design ecology, but it seems like a waste. I strongly believe in the independent publishing model and absolutely don't want to spend my time on - or, God forbid, ask others to think about - ideas that are going to die on the vine due to laziness, neglect, or pernicious design roadblocks. On the other hand, how am I to know which ideas have merit, have legs, and ought to be pursued past the idle fancy stage without some feedback?

Related question - when and how do those half dead but once-promising ideas get released into the wild, for others to pick apart for good bits? Somewhere there ought to be an elephant's graveyard of RPG concepts, mechanical components, and fossilized chunks of hubris.

    Practice. Then some more practice, and then keep practicing.

    A general feature of game design is every game you design is pretty crappy, once you look back at it from a dozen or so designs later. Your best ones will stand out by then, and those are the ones that teach you what works and what doesn't.

    You don't want to focus exclusively on one game, it's slow down your personal development, and paradoxically make improvements to that game slow in coming. Of course if you do too many, too quickly, you'll end up with a huge playtesting backlog...

    I'm rather interested in contributing to an elephant graveyard, since half of my goal in design is to improve the general quality of design, for myself and others.

    - Mendel

    RPGs should not aspire to be art or fiction, they should aspire to transcend such things.

    Well, I think it's a serious consideration.

    Before putting the LARP on there, I felt I had to take out the Terry Pratchett references: I don't want to claim to be distributing a Terry Pratchett LARP under any license at all, because there's things in there that are his work, not mine.

    What I wanted to do was put the standard disclaimer on it: "This is a LARP based on Terry Pratchett's works and lots of character names and references belong to him. Please don't sue us, it's a fan thing, we really suggest you buy his books."

    But, because the wiki insists on that license, I've taken out all the Pratchett references and it's just become a game "inspired by Terry Pratchett".

    Personally, I'd rather you just took the general wiki license off and let people put their own licenses on their games, if they want them.
