    \n Ok, we've been talking about this on (iirc) rpg.net, so here are your options, as far as I know.

    Completely Free:
    Scribus: this is a full DTP program, is not completely mature yet but it can produce 1.6 pdfs, with color management and forms, if that's your fancy. Pros: free, powerful, multiplatform. Cons: if you are new to layout has a steep learning curve, non completely baked in some functions.

    Inkscape: not really a layout program, but could be useful for the cover and the charsheet. Much easier than scribus, roughly an Illustrator equivalent. Pros: free, easy to get the hang for basic stuff, multiplatform. Cons: not a DTP program (but could be used with a little patience).

    Very Cheap or Mostly Free
    Serif Plus A full and (apparently) professional DTP program. I don't know it personally. Pros: there is a free version for non commercial stuff. Very low cost (as in 9 UKP) for the for-pay version. Cons: only runs on windows. I cannot really say anything about it, since I've never seen it in practice.

    ...and then, you have the option of using OpenOffice: for an ashcan it could do the trick.

    \n Here's some references to things people haven't mentioned:

    I know someone who used to use a product called "PageStream" (because way back when there was an Atari hotbed locally, there was a lot of people using I knew using Atari software). PageStream is still around, and is, apparently, supported on Linux, Windows, Macintosh, Amiga(sic). It is not free, it costs 100 bucks. However, I do know that it has been around for a long time and I presume therefore it's reasonably robust. My friend quite liked it for doing small DTP type projects that were gaming related (character sheets, reference sheets, small "newsletter" type handouts).

    Back from the days of NeXT there was a company that created graphics and layout software for NeXTStep called Stone. They still offer Stone Create for the Macintosh (OSX). To get just Create (the layout/illustration tool) costs 150 dollars. It, too, has been around for a very long time. I know of folks who have used it for years to do simple layout tasks. But it's definitely OSX only. (That's not entirely true -- they'll still sell you a NeXTStep version if you want.)

    For incredibly robust type-setting, focussed towards technical publishing, and absolutely no cost, you can use (La)TeX. It is not easy to install, configure, or use. But thousands of academics use it for their layout and publishing needs.

    Thanks for the warnings about Pages, Clinton: much appreciated.

    Posted By: Jeffrey StraszheimA couple of you mentioned ConTeXt. I've done quite a bit of work with LaTeX, have both the books, and am getting to the point where I usually get it to do what I want to do. What extra features does ConTeXt bring to the table? Is it worth moving away from LaTeX if I've already invested the time to learn it?
    \nI think it's actually a matter of personal preference. Me, I chose ConTeXt when I first saw Pragma Ade's awesome manuals some years ago, while all LaTeX documentation was geared towards an academic style and -- it seemed to me -- left little space for visual customization. I dabbled with LaTeX a little, and I remember a lot of struggling to get what I wanted. Somehow, it seemed that ConTeXt macros were more friendly to me.

    I have to say that, capabilities-side, I was wrong about LaTeX: Gregor Huttons's ERP templates and Runequest De Luxe SRD are shining examples of what one can do with LaTeX, and XeLaTeX. Moreover, LaTex coupled with XeTeX engine, which greatly enhances and simplifies font support, is a big winner: it's got more widespread support, more macros, more plugins, etc. etc.

    In the end, it's not a matter of this sucks vs. this does not suck. It's a matter of choosing what more suits you. ConTeXt + XeTeX fits my typesetting needs when I have to produce coherent and yet sophisticated layout.

    ConTeXt, while less famous, is by no means less mature, and Pragma Ade folks keep on improving it.

      \n For casual stuff, character sheets, handouts, and short booklets along the lines of Lady Blackbird, Pages would be more than fine. In fact, for speed of use and simplicity, it actually does a hell of a lot. There are some features it won't handle, like setting type on a curve. And its graphics manipulation capability is absurdly limited compared to the Adobe software. With large graphic-intensive files, it has an intermittent bug that chokes on transparencies and corrupts the file so it can no longer be opened. I learned that the hard way on a 50-page training manual I laid out for a client. But at the end of the day, Pages is basically a jumped up word-processor, not intended for hardcore print publication. You can produce an Adobe PDF by saving it off to an Adobe format and then working through Distiller to produce your final PDF. It's a clunky, multi-step process. I use Pages for casual stuff or short professional documents that aren't terribly intensive from a graphics standpoint. I've made some character sheets I'm really happy with, and basic attractive layout is a snap.

      It's available on Apple's online app store for about $10.

      On the other hand, the Adobe software goes out of its way to be completely arcane. I know the stuff is complex, but Jesus THAC0 Christ, my eyes are pissing blood after a half-hour trying to sort out how to do something I could figure out in Pages in 30 seconds.
