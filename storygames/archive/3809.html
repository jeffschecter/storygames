\n Hi!
\n
\nExcuse my English, it's not my native language.
\n
\nTalking about indie games and criticism in a forum thread, some days ago, someone (I don't remember who, sorry) said that to really criticize a game with some objectivity, you must study and play it, and nobody do all that work for a game he doesn't like. Well, it's true, but someone could do this for a game he would like very much... if it worked for him!
\n
\nCase in point: Nathan Paoletta's CARRY
\n
\nThis is a game that I am really interested in, a game that I wanted to play very much, a game that I played enjoying very much some moments of play, so much to fan my enthusiasm... but that after looking at it (and playing it) I think it's not complete, and has so many issues that it should still be in playtesting until they are solved.
\n
\nI will try to explain why I think so, and what I would like to get from this thread.
\n
\nFirst, I would like to get some discourse going about the game. Because, while it seems much played at cons and gatherings, There isn't a lot of posted actual play after it was published. Really, sometimes it seems that, after a first period of interest (with a very enthusiastic blog post from joshua a.c. newman and a Forge actual play thread by Ron Edwards that was very positive, but signaled some of the problems of the game) nobody talk about it anymore if not in passing ("and I played Carry, too..."). This is really disheartening thinking about the potential of this game, a potential that I believe unfulfilled because of a premature publishing of the book.
\n
\nThe second objective of this thread is, simply, to be able to fulfill that potential playing the game. I hope that someone will explain to me why the game it's all right and I simply didn't notice the solutions I needed, or that the game is playable if I simply do some thing (not explained in the game) when I play it, or, if the game is indeed not fully baked, will help me bake at least a temporary solution (also called "house rules") to be able to play it until a revised edition is published (or an "official errata" take care of all the problems) (and yes, I already tried doing this talking directly with the author, but with very little results, as I will explain later I was often simply ignored). So, this is not a review. I am asking for answer from people who already know and played the game
\n
\nLet's start with a little history of my interest in the game. It was brought after last year's gencon by a friend of mine, who was very interested by the setting and the idea of the game (my friend has even less experience with story-games than me, we are both very old-time roleplayers that discovered these games less than two years ago, but we are both very enthusiastic about them). After hearing his description of the game, and after reading the blog post and the actual play thread cited above, I was very interested too. I brought my copy of the game book, I wrote Nathan to ask for the most recent errata (at that time, Febrary 2007) and some advice (that I received quickly. Among the advices was a different endgame rule, suggested by Ron Edwards in his actual play thread), and we scheduled a game session with some other out-of-town friends that was interested too.
\n
\nWe made a lot of errors during the game, but we still enjoyed it very much. So much that my friend decided to organize a game of Carry at the next Italian Ambercon to show this little gem around. The little glitchs and the many problems, we ascribed to our errors and inexperience. I talked about that game, and I posted a lot of rule questions to Nathan, in this forge thread
\n
\nThat thread list some problems we had with the unclear text of the game. Nathan answered (after a while) most of the questions, but not all. He didn't explain how the endgame and the multiple-rounds action scene (two of the most important mechanism) worked. This even after I asked him again, in the forum and by e-mail. At that time, reading his blog, I found out that he had decided to leave the internet, so I gave up on getting more answers. My friend tried to GM the game at the Ambercon anyway, and it was a bad day for story-games reputation at that Con. Both of the unresolved issues with the rules came out in play (Murphy rules...), and all the glitch and the dice complications of the game showed their worst aspect. This in a convention for people who like to play diceless or with very light rule sets and distrust dice (we hoped to show how dice don't have to detract from immersion and story, but it backfired).
\n
\nMy friend was maybe too enthusiastic about playing the game even without all the clarification we needed, but after the Con game showed us that the problems we encountered in the first game weren't caused only by inexperience or errors (and this showed the first game in a very different linght), and discouraged by the author's disinterest in our questions, he simply said that he was done with the game, that it was broken, and he didn't want to play it anymore.
\n
\nI was uncertain. After the first game, I was very enthusiastic about some of the system features. I really liked the way the dice made for a close ecology, how they forced personality changes, how that system worked. After the second game, I still like some part of the system, but I am not sure they really work. Unwilling to drop the game after only two tries, but without anybody to play it with, I began to search for other people's s comments about the game. As I said at the start of the thread, I didn't found any. Nor I found any new thread with actual play or rules discussions.
\n
\nAfter a while Nathan Paoletta returned, posting about the second printing of the game, and asking for questions. I replied reminding him about the question he still didn't answer in the forge thread. But still, no answer.
\n
\nThen, he posted a new version of the endgame rules (the third one from the publication of the game, in less than a year. The fourth, counting a suggestion for playing the endgame in a somewhat ruleless way that Ron said he received by Nathan, in his actual play post. As far as I know the new printing of the game has still the first version), saying that he would START to use it (playtest it) after then. In his words, "Thoughts? I'll post once I run it this way and I have a chance to see how it goes...."
\n
\nAt that time, I understood that he didn't have really have a playtested endgame rule that worked. (and this maybe explain the reason because he didn't answer my questions about the endgame). Thinking about the "ashcan" discussion at the Forge and Story-game from some months ago, I read again his old playtest thread at the Forge. Looking at the dates, this time. He posted about a playtest where his game was called "Broken and Boring" (forcing a complete revision of the rules) at the end of march, only a few months before publication. Some other playtest thread from that period show the big differences with the published systems, and that he was at the time still struggling with the engame.
\n

    \n In his thread about the game history at the forge, he say that after the revision he played THREE games at Dexcon (three games with the game author doesn't seem to me really a lot of playtesting) and then "The three games showed me the final tweaks I needed to make, mechanically (mainly dramatically streamlining Endgame and jiggering a couple of things about how Burdens work in Action Scenes), and also showed me that the game was good. All I had to do was get the physical labor done.". So he didn't do more playtesting, even after having done some rule change (I think there wasn't the time, if he wanted to publish in time for Gencon), and printed the game.
    \n
    \nAnd after one year, he is still playtesting the endgame rules.
    \n
    \nThis, in my opinion, is a good example of how much an initiative like the Ashcan Front is praiseworthy and needed. If Carry had been published as an ashcan, with some good blind playtesting (without the author at the table), it could really have been a great game. It's already is, from what people say, when the author is GMing it. But in his actual published form, it still isn't. It's an half-baked game.
    \n
    \nAnd these elegant rules with the flowing of a closed ecology of dice... I am not sure anymore if its beauty serve the game's purpose, or not. I don't trust very much anymore the answers of the game author (or him giving them to me) so I am asking feedback from the people who played the game. Do you think that the game work well as printed? How? Can you explain it to me? (Ron Edwards, in one of his replies in the "half-baked games" discussions, said that in his opinion "In the case of [..] or Carry, it means the book ended up having small gaps or flaws that made it a wee bit harder to use, but the book can indeed be used and the game itself is solid". But "can be used" I think take a very different meaning when said by Ron Edwards than when said by someone who doesn't have many years of experience with narrative games or didn't talk in person with the game designer). Do you have some tweaks to suggest? Do you have a functional endgame rule to use?
    \n
    \nSeeing that I talked about the game "problems", all this time, I think I should list at least the ones I noticed more until now:
    \n
    \n- 1) the choosing of the dice at the start. It's really a little thing, but I noticed it got the game to a bad start at the Ambercon, because the players didn't saw the reason behind the complication. It told them "too many dice complications" right at the start. OK, it would not be a problem with a less diceless-friendly group of players, but I would like to get some feedback on this. Do any of you can explain the reason for this complication?
    \n
    \n- 2) During the Squad scenes, if the players don't gift dice to the GM, the GM is soon without dice. The players have no "game-reason" to give the GM any dice. They have to give them to him simply to have a strong opposition and don't win too easily the conflicts in the squad scenes. Both times we played, the GM was always without dice. Nathan about this wrote "In all honesty, it's not particularly bad for the GM to be low on dice, it just tends to mean that the game tends to be more about how the characters overcome adversity than how they are divided and crushed by it.", but after playing again the game I don't agree. To overcome adversity, there should be some adversity. A guaranteed win is not adversity.
    \nAnd if the GM don't receive dice, the players get too many, and they don't change profiles.
    \n(by the way, the book text is wrong - and the game author agreed about it afterwards: the gifted dice are not at all like fan mail, they are given to people for tactical reasons, not to award "good play")
    \n
    \n- 3) this was one of my never answered questions:
    \n3302.b)"the GM frames the scene, explaining the situation. The situation should include enough information for the Ranking Officer to be able to give orders to the other characters. After the GM frames the scene, it is divided in a series of rounds.
    \n(3302.c) Each round, the Ranking Officer gives each grunt an order, as well as giving general orders to the squad as a whole. [...]. The GM then summarize the round, and narrates the consequences of each Grunt's action
    \n(3302.d) An action scene can take any number of rounds. It is the GM's responsibility to pace the scene though his narration, and to call the scene at an appropriate point"
    \n
    \nOK, let's say I am the GM, and the Ranking Officer order Big White to kill a sniper behind a street window. I decided to pace the scene in two rounds, and this is the first.
    \nWhat can I tell to Big White's player? I can resolve his fight with the sniper using total GM's fiat, narrating what I want (even that he is shot and wounded by the sniper), or these are constraints? What can happen and what can't happen until the dice are rolled?
    \n
    \n- 4) During the Action scenes, people can decide pretty much how many wounded or dead will be at the end, by choosing the dice to give, and to who. The GM and the ranking officer can at most add one dice, against all the dice from the players. At the end if the players don't want to be wounded, they aren't, because whoever get to decide at the end has a pityful amount of harm to give around.
    \n
    \n- 5) Am I alone in having some problem in categorizing and differentiating the different approaches? (many times we could have used two or three different approaches declaration, narrating the exact same action)
    \n
    \n- 6) The limiting power of the approaches depends of dice scarcity. If the Players have too many dice (because the GM don't have any) even the approaches seems an useless complication (this is what happended in the Ambercon game)
    \n
    \n- 7) The Endgame: I will not even try to summarize here the three different rules given at various times. You can read them in the game manual, in this actual play post, and in this post from Nathan. Every one of the cause confusion in every endgame if the character don't neatly divide in pairs in different locations at the time. Two of them have someone decide for another's character (Ron's version tried to avoid this, but the new one return to the idea). I really don't like this.
    \n
    \nFor the Endgame, I will add a new condition. I will not be satisfied by any endgame rule where I (or any player) will not narrate the denoument AND the epilogue for our chartacter. The endgame rules should give us the order or narration and the condition we have to follow with our narration, not force us to have the last scene of our character narrated by another player.
    \n
    \nAny idea? Comments?

    \n Hi Moreno:

    My experience with the game went very well. It was a game with Nate as GM and included Ben Lehman, Luke Crane and Michael Miller. And a guy I just met at the table whose name eludes me now. I'm terrible with names.

Doesn't this align with your concerns about adversity? If you make the scene more challenging, it's less likely that there will be significant fallout.     The only observation I have about carry is that there's nothing in it to apportion spotlight time to different players. You can't really wait for your turn. You have to speak up and go after things, and that rewards more outspoken players.

    \n Hi Moreno

    A few points I note that might help you.

    3203: You, the player, pick the approach that you want and narrate it (if you think it's honourable then it's honourable -- I don't think it's up for group debate). So you choose which one you want. If you think it could be more than one approach then just pick one way of looking at it.

    3206a: If you then push you choose a new approach to add, maybe this will fall pretty close to your original approach or maybe it will twist it further (say you now push it with violence, for instance, which gives the situation a whole new face).

    3205-c: If the GM has no dice the Grunts then must give the highest die in their pool to the GM (...they do get to win that conflict without rolling but at the expense of reloading the GM's pool).

    As a player it can be a better choice to give a die to the GM rather than another grunt that you may soon be conflicting with too.

    I think the GMs dice are there to provoke conflict when the players are holding back. Mainly the GM is aggressively framing the scenes and framing conflicts between the grunts. To really get at the burdens and drive the story.

    I don't know if this helps you any.

    All the best.

    \n Hi Moreno,

    First of all, I want to humbly apologize for my lack of communication with you over the late winter and spring. I had a lot of personal stuff happening; I didn't have the time to devote to playing games, let alone writing them or even writing about them online; and I did you a great disservice by not answering your questions promptly and as clearly as I could have. If there's anything I can do to make this up to you, up to and including refunding you for your purchase of the game, let me know - I really want to have the game stand on its own, apart from my personal failings at internet communication in the last couple of months.

    I know that you want to hear from other people who have played the game, which is more than fair. But I feel it's incumbent upon me to talk a little about the game in response to your post, if thats ok with you.

    Publishing carry

    Here's the thing about carry...it's a short-form game. Every time I playtested it, I playtested it to the full extent of the game, through it's longest reward-cycle. Over it's development time, I had 3 games with my local group, 4.5 with groups at conventions, and 2 "blind" playtests where I wasn't present. Is that enough? Maybe. maybe not. I don't think you can put a hard number on "how many times should you playtest". But the entire game was playtested, through it's entirety, 9 times. Of course, once I decided to publish it in time for Gen Con, that became my driving goal - and let me tell you, it's a bad goal. The game suffers for it, and I suffered for it at the time. It's why I've decided to never set release dates for anything, ever.

    However, I was confident at the time that I was happy with the game as it ran, as of the text that went to print for Gen Con. And I still am happy with it, for the most part; 90% of the game has stood up as doing exactly what I want it too when I play it. Endgame is that other 10%. And it crept up on me. I was happy with it at first, but as I've played the game more, it's begun to stand out more and more as not really working as I intended it. This is a creator issue, I suppose - I don't honestly know if it works for other groups (though, as Ron's post made clear, it was weird for him). But it doesn't work for me, which is why I'm still poking at it. And I would rather have my pokings be out in the open, where other people can try them out too. That's all.\n
    And these elegant rules with the flowing of a closed ecology of dice... I am not sure anymore if its beauty serve the game's purpose, or not. I don't trust very much anymore the answers of the game author (or him giving them to me) so I am asking feedback from the people who played the game.
    \nAgain, I'm just going to answer these with how they should work according to the rules. It's how they work for me in play; there may certainly be things that I do at the table that aren't in the rules. I tried really hard to get everything into the text, but I am imperfect, so, who knows?

    Anyone, the next post has those answers.

    (by the way, the book text is wrong - and the game author agreed about it afterwards: the gifted dice are not at all like fan mail, they are given to people for tactical reasons, not to award "good play")
    \nAdversity can (and often does) come from other players. I've run games where I never got dice, and I've run games where I've never had a low dice pool, as the GM. There are mechanical stopgaps to keep the GM from going the whole game without dice, as Gregor points out. I, in all honestly, think that the dice distribution throughout the game accurately reflects the interests of the group, whether they're interested in being united or divisive, in facing strong adversity or not.

    Also, if you have too many dice, and you want to change profile...well, start pushing conflicts and giving your dice to the GM. It's the only way to drain dice out of your own pool.\n
    - 3) this was one of my never answered questions:
    3302.b)"the GM frames the scene, explaining the situation. The situation should include enough information for the Ranking Officer to be able to give orders to the other characters. After the GM frames the scene, it is divided in a series of rounds.
    (3302.c) Each round, the Ranking Officer gives each grunt an order, as well as giving general orders to the squad as a whole. [...]. The GM then summarize the round, and narrates the consequences of each Grunt's action
    (3302.d) An action scene can take any number of rounds. It is the GM's responsibility to pace the scene though his narration, and to call the scene at an appropriate point"

    OK, let's say I am the GM, and the Ranking Officer order Big White to kill a sniper behind a street window. I decided to pace the scene in two rounds, and this is the first.
    What can I tell to Big White's player? I can resolve his fight with the sniper using total GM's fiat, narrating what I want (even that he is shot and wounded by the sniper), or these are constraints? What can happen and what can't happen until the dice are rolled?
    \nThe way I run it, everything that happens in Actions scenes is obscured by a sort of "fog of war" until the dice are rolled. That is, I narrate the scene, the RO gives orders, the Grunts choose to agree/disagree and obey/disobey, and then I narrate how things change based on those narrations, from the perspective of the characters. In your example above, if Big White says "I obey, and I rip a machine gun burst through that window." then, to set up the next round, I might say "All right, you're firing through the window, and theres smoke and gunpowder pouring out of it. You can't tell if you got the sniper or not, but the rest of the attacking force are laying down suppressive fire...."

    Once the scene is called, and we roll, then if the Squad wins I would probably say "you check it out, and you find the sniper sawed in half by machine gun fire." If the Squad lost, I might say "The smoke clears...and then there's one more shot from the sniper. By the time you get there, he's gone."

    I tend not to narrate any actual wounding until the dice happen. It's like an action scene in a war movie - the protagonists are doing their thing, there's a lot of gunfire, shouted orders, and then when the smoke clears they realize who's been shot and who's not coming back to base. Once the points are spend on wounding Grunts and Fodder, then I'll narrate who took wounds and why.

    The exception, of course, is if you use a Burden Die in the scene - then I have no problem narrating how you hit because of it, because whoever wins is going to have to give you fallout regardless of the dice.\n
    - 4) During the Action scenes, people can decide pretty much how many wounded or dead will be at the end, by choosing the dice to give, and to who. The GM and the ranking officer can at most add one dice, against all the dice from the players. At the end if the players don't want to be wounded, they aren't, because whoever get to decide at the end has a pityful amount of harm to give around.
    \nRight...

    In my experience, no group has ever tried to balance the die pools in order to minimize harm to the squad. And if you did, that means that a significant portion of the soldiers need to disagree with the Ranking Officers orders, which provides great material for follow-up squad scenes. Also, Action Scenes are the easiest way to up your Burden die, by putting it in and thereby requiring the winner of the Action to give you fallout.
    \n
    - 5) Am I alone in having some problem in categorizing and differentiating the different approaches? (many times we could have used two or three different approaches declaration, narrating the exact same action)
    \nWould it be helpful for me to try to give some more examples?\n
    - 6) The limiting power of the approaches depends of dice scarcity. If the Players have too many dice (because the GM don't have any) even the approaches seems an useless complication (this is what happended in the Ambercon game)
    \nThe approaches inform the fiction, nothing more, nothing less. If you have a bunch of dice, then you get to freely choose how you want to do things; if you don't, then you have to make choices among a subset of options. Thats all.

    8. Structure. The scenes seemed disjointed because the squad wasn't really doing anything. There was no structure imposed on the story so that it went from a to b. It just went round in circles with the dice from one type of scene to the other. I think we all felt that we could have benefited from a structure that would carry us from scene to scene toward some goal we cared about and that would make victory in the action sequences more than just color, but something that impacted the narrative through-line. I definitely felt like there should be incentive for the PCs to strive for victory as hard as possible, to counteract the effect of the dice mechanic that encouraged balance.

    Well. that's all I can think of at the moment. But given the fact that some people have played this game and had great experiences, I'm wondering if I just didn't get it.
