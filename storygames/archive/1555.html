Indie but not "Indie", "Indie" but not Indie

Warthur
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
So, I was thinking that there's actually two definitions of what an indie game is that float around in the various RPG forums.

There's indie games, which conform to the definition as used by Ron Edwards et al; they're creator-owned, self-financed labours of love which aren't put out by mainstream roleplaying publishers.

And then there's "indie" games, which in the minds of many are all tied up with Forge theory, and in particular exploring interesting Narrativist approaches to gaming (seeing how Gamism and Simulationism is more than adequately catered for by mainstream publishers, in the main).

Now, the two things are actually entirely separate from each other, but easily get conflated in people's minds: if you described your game as being an indie RPG, many would think you were going by the first definition, a few confused souls would plump for the second, and a good many would assume both.

On the other hand, Ron Edwards pointed out in the original (and excellent, even if you don't buy into Forge theory) Fantasy Heartbreakers essay that most small press D&D knockoffs qualify as indie games, as does The World of Synnibar and FATAL.

And what's more, there's been several instances of mainstream publishers putting out games with an innovative approach to Narrativist play, and which - if you didn't know that they'd been put out by a mainstream publisher, you'd guess that they were indie games. Hogshead Publishing reprinted Nobilis its New Style imprint put out Baron Munchausen, Puppetland/Power Kill, Pantheon and De Profundis. Amber Diceless enjoyed mainstream success (heck, even the RPGPundit loves it), both Pendragon and The Dying Earth introduce personality mechanics to de-emphasise the "player controls the character, GM controls the world" mindset and encourage PCs to behave in a manner true to Arthurian/Vancian narratives, and who could forget Over the Edge?

So, what other examples of "indie" but not indie and indie but not "indie" games are there?

    I thought the point of Indie as a term at the Forge was about support, helping people bring their game, their vision, to the market.

    I'm totally, 100% with John. The independence of a publisher with regard to outside financial interests makes a game something different, whatever the content winds up being.

    Shock: has a lot of words in it by Ben Lehman. He owns those words. By our agreement, I get to use them in all versions of Shock:, but anything else I want to use them for, I have to ask him and make an agreement, probably involving the trade of resources.

    That means some important stuff. It means that I can choose to use other game fiction if I want to and take his out (fat chance). It means that he gets to say if it gets put in an anthology or whatever and gets to say under what terms that happens. It means I get to figure out the pricing on Shock:, the color of the cover, and whatever else.

    Neither of these things would be true if we'd done it for someone else. And the products of our labors would have come out differently because we'd have been performing the will of another party because then it would be their money and effort on the line.

    It's totally legit to publish something independently and then sell it. At that point on, someone else owns it and makes all the decisions about it, even if they're real nice to you about it, and that makes it no longer independently published.

    Now, if I'd bought those words from Ben? I dunno. By word count, it's very close to 50/50. It's a long short story and a densely written game (yes, OK, there are some things that need clarification. Make believe there aren't). Would I have to write filler text to make it "indie"?

    I dunno! Let's find a real world test case and worry about it then.
