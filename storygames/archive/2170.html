I dreamed this last night and woke up thinking it was pretty neat.

So you've got players, and they are a bridge crew. Command, Tactical, Sensors, and Helm. Everybody has a skill, from one to six, at doing their job. That's how many dice they can roll at the same time.

Your spaceship encounters another vessel! Helm rolls 3d6 to get the bearing. "Contact, captain, bearing ... 256!"

Command starts a stopwatch.

Helm, Tactical, and Sensors each have a different sheet or booklet with 36 situation elements in it. They each parse the bearing differently to determine what's going on.

Helm looks up 2,5. Tactical looks up 5,6. Sensors looks up 2,6, based on the bearing roll.

So they've got info - Sensors finds out who it is, Tectical finds out how they are armed, and Helm finds out what they are driving. But they can't relay that information until they roll a bunch of sixes - based on (I guess) the difficulty of the potential encounter. So Helm, Sensors, and Tactical are all furiously rolling dice (the number they can roll in one toss is based on character skill), and when they reach the target number of sixes, they get to report to Command what they know.

When all three have reported, Command stops the stopwatch. The longer it has run, the more advantage the enemy ship has. Hopefully you'd get the tense "come on, Helm..." - "Working on it, Captain..." interplay that is so fun in space guys shows.

Now that I am not asleep it occurs to me that rolling one die over and over may actually be faster than rolling six dice and parsing the results. Also, I have no idea how long it would take to achieve specific results, but the difficulty/time scale is easy to create. This seems like it would be a fun mini-game embedded in some larger deal.

    Reminds me a heck of a lot of the Star Trek Tactical Combat Simulator. We always played with a stopwatch. Each player has their own layout on which they make adjustments, and then when time is up, the turn is performed and evaluated.

    Here's a pic of the combined form for a single player (from which you can extrapolate the player forms): http://www.boardgamegeek.com/image/56566

    I've always been fascinated by this sort of thing. Another case would be Space Hulk, where the Marine player has only X seconds to do his turn (depends on the edition, and whether he's got live sergeants, etc). I play my WH40K version this way, too. Loads of fun, and mistakes do get made. Which is what you want.

    My ultimate example of this, however, is the "pseudo-real-time" Zombie Shopper game that I've run twice. In this game, the players move their tokens on a map of the mall, and can do anything that they like in that time...they just have to pay for it between turns. The turns are 60 seconds long, representing 60 seconds of in-game action. 120 turns to the game. In between turns is a short time spent calculating your endurance loss and all 500 zombie figs (taken from Zombie!) move. Game runs, on average, 4 hours with breaks.

    And it's insane.

    Best error ever:

    Player A, grabbing the M-16 counter off the map: I point it at the swat cop.
    Player B to me as ref: Is that the same M-16 I dropped before?
    Me as Ref: Yep.
    Player B: I grab the gun out of his hand.
    Player A: I counter by shooting.
    Player B: It's out of ammo.
    Me: Click...
    Player A: Awwwwwww!
    Me: turn is over...

    It can work, and work well.
