\n Help me love bards. I just don't get them. All I can think of is Cacophonix from Asterix, the guy in Animal House whose guitar gets smashed, and some idiot tuning up his lute (it's invariably a man) while his three buddies fight for their lives. In my original D&D campaign, all the bards were rounded up with the gnomes, hobbits, thief acrobats and cavaliers, and put to the sword.

So, help me story games! Give me good fictional, historical and in-game role models. In particular, when the fighting kicks off, what's a bard to do?

    \n The bard thing in D&D is weird, no bones about it. You can make it work by setting your game in a localized bronze-age setting, turning priests into shamans, removing wizards and removing sages. Then just say that bards genuinely are the keepers of the lore, and revise the bardic lore rules of whatever edition it is you're playing to ensure that players get relevant, up-to-date tactical and strategic information out of their bard. Assume that the bard belongs to a semi-formal institution, celtic-style, with schooling and massive amounts of rote memorization involved. The song-singing thing should be down-played, while sage advice and the notion that bard = wizard emphasized. The mythical celtic bard is a member of an occult cult, essentially, brethren to the druids and wielder of magic alongside his role as traveller extraordinaire and advisor to the kings. Do that, and he'll make sense just fine.

    What you end up with in practice is something that's called a bard but that actually acts like a wise wizard, perhaps with less overt arcane might and as full an ability to grab a sword and join the fray as anybody. Fun to play and makes perfect sense in the sort of mythic celtic setting the bard class assumes. Similar figures who wield mighty magics, sing songs and keep the lore are found in the myths of the northmen and Finns as well, so one might say that the class fits quite well in a Northern European pre-Christian setting.

    Take away all of that celtic context, and of course the bard looks idiotic. I for one don't have a clue what the early D&D developers were smoking, they seemed to think that you could just pluck stuff out of history and legend randomly and mix it all up. It still works if your heart is iron and you can just play everything in the setting straight - it'll be comedic as hell for the players, but perhaps the characters who live in the setting are used to having lute-swinging lite-wizards running around.

    \n Yes, really. The last bard I played was a story teller. Whenever I used some magic music type ability, I told another character a story (e.g. distracting someone from their wounds until they felt better, inspiring them with a tale of a dwarven hero in a similar situation, etc). In fact, he couldn't even sing. I tried to give every power a mundane explanation but told the mage in the party that there was obviously some latent magic involved, whether I knew it or not. I did my best to know something about everything without being a know-it-all, though occasionally I just ran out of material to author. I discovered melee was definitely not my thing, so I avoided combat and/or threw rocks. Except when it was my thing and I threw myself blindly into combat against something I decided to hate (remember the bard's motto: Bards start fights, they don't end them). Social combat, there, I said it. This is your friend. No one is more fit to talk to NPCs than a bard, shine here and let the others have their pointy sticks and awesome earth-shattering power (though bards will suck all the more in games with extended combat rules and shine in rules-lite games). Lastly, print out a good list of Shakespearean insults but use them sparingly - appropriate heckling is part of your job.

    \n If the campaign is nothing but combat, bards (minstrels, troubadours, entertainers, etc.) traditionally don't have much of a role. D&D makes them support, with their songs and such that boost their allies...but I, personally, had never been fond of that whole idea.

    I've actually loved playing Bards, when the campaign has an equal share of interaction and combat, or when interaction is more important than combat. Bards can open doors for the party that might otherwise be closed. Sometimes they can affect change or influence events in ways that a sword cannot.
    My favorite character of all time (at least for D&D) was a Jester (variant Bard from Dragon magazine) in Eberron. He wasn't very combatty unless he was throwing knives, but he could certainly distract the enemy and get away from them quickly (sometimes leaping onto the shoulders of the warforged fighter when things got sticky), thus giving his friends a tactical advantage. Through his performances, he was able to gain them admittance into two otherwise invite-only events, and he was able to improve his group's standing with the commoners of one village by lampooning a local priest they all disliked.

    As for historical bards and how they should fit into the fantasy setting, check out Terry Jones' Medieval Lives series. Very enlightening (actually, all of it is pretty interesting, especially the stuff on peasants and monks).

    \n Historic bards are awesome! D&D/Generic Fantasy Game bards... not so much. D&D bards ARE the Animal House guitar guy. They're thieves with weak-ass Song Magic.

    If I were running a Celt or Norse themed D&D game, the bards would be warriors/classic "fighters", but with their skill spends in music stuff. If there were a party of a ranger and four fighters, and two of those were self-proclaimed bards, I would maybe give the other characters all maybe +1 permanent attack or something (to indicate their focus on martial training over everything, if that's what their background was) and the "bards" +10 free skill points in music and performance-based skills.     \n When we were playing D&D, I think we all just tacitly agreed to not make a big deal out of the whole musical thing. The bard's player would announce, "I'm Inspiring Courage," and we'd all remember to add +1 to our rolls until he said to stop. At no point did anyone ask what that sounded like or what instrument it was played on, and no one ever included any reference to the song when describing what was happening in combat; it was treated like any other buffing spell, like Bless or Protection from Evil.

    Our version of the bard was pretty much a fighter/sorceror who traded a smaller spell selection for actual social skills, some curing magic, and the aforementioned party-buffs. Basically a utility player, great at making other people better and in social settings, passable at fighting and spellcasting, and not terrible at anything...an asset to any party that already has the main fighting/sneaking/spellslinging niches covered. Oh, and he also comes with a lute, but we're not going to dwell on that.

    \n My longest running D&D character was a 3rd ed bard. I went from level 4 to level 32 before everyone moved away. Early game: Led a militia of gnolls against a Derro attack. Mid game: Led the army of a tiny human kingdom against the incursion of a neighboring undead kingdom. We managed to set up some magical range amplification and flight so I was flying over the battlefield inspiring the entire army while providing aerial fire support. Late game: set up an organization of hostels for travellers that doubled as the center of the resistance movement to overthrow the evil empire where bards were anathema. I was the party healer for a while early on, and later kept our ranger stocked with +5 arrows. The GM cooked up two prestige classes for me since he didn't like the WotC bard ones. The first, Herald of Voices, let me sing with three voices so I could maintain 3 bardic effects at once. The second, Vandalblade Cartographer, was more knowledge- and combat-oriented so I was more useful in melee.

    In 3rd ed (fixed in 3.5), the use of the bardic songs was based only on your Perform skill, there was the slightly bizarre outcome that you could take 1 level of Bard and then go anything else but still get all the songs.

    Edited to add more about music in battle:
    In the situation with the gnoll militia... The gnolls were the construction workers on the town walls. We'd just entered town, seen them working, and on a whim I decided to use my "Inspire Competence" to help them build the wall. Later that night, we were attacked as a party, and when I used "Inspire Courage", the gnolls recognized my singing, sounded the town alert, and came to help. I also gave concerts in most towns we passed through and wound up with several status in my honor.
