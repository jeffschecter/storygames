\n So last night we had a last minute open spot in our weekly game, and I had about fifteen minutes to put something together. I'd just read Dust Devils again and it was my first choice. I used Hans' clever wedding massacre situation generator, cadged NPCs from the example in the book, and we were off to the races. My contribution to the chaos was the setting - Lebanon, Missouri, 1867. The bride, Corinne, was the daughter of an ambitious southern plutocrat eager to get in the good graces of the occupation forces. Corinne's Present is "Gettin' sold off". The groom, Jake, was a local boy who was a Jayhawk during the war and is now a carpetbagger, lording over his former friends and relatives. Jake was definitely a bastard. The final NPC was a nascent Klansman sheriff who once rode with Nathan Forrest.

Character generation is really simple and fun. We had a good crew. Clyde, whose Devil was "selfishness". Silas, whose Devil was "Self-Righteousness". John, whose Devil was "Guilt", and Burkey, whose Devil was "Indecision". Three of them gravitated to the groom - the groom needed Clyde (to kill John, as it turned out). The groom was in Silas' way (because Corinne promised Silas all her land if he could stop the wedding). John was in the groom's way (because his land bisected the plantations he would inherit by marrying Corinne). And finally Burkey had been spurned by the bride - and still carried a torch for her. That didn't end well.

I wasn't super prepared to run Dust Devils, but the game's instructions are almost luminously clear, and we had no huge problems. We kept finding ourselves in archetypal, cinematic western scenes, which was very satisfying. At one point Burkey was being strung up in the cemetery by a mob led by the sheriff, and Clyde was already there, busy digging up his father's grave. The evil sheriff looked down, tipped his hat, and said "give my regards to your pa, Clyde" before returning to the business of the hanging. This turned into undiluted awesome when Clyde pried open his father's casket and removed the tarnished tin star from his rotting lapel - establishing that his old man had been the real sheriff, murdered by the new sheriff. Cue the gunfight! We got a new sheriff now! The game was stuffed with moments like that. Dust Devils really delivered.

Oh! And Corinne didn't get married. The groom beat Burkey to death with a fireplace poker (Burkey's winning stake!) in front of the assembled wedding guests, after which Clyde, the new sheriff, took him away to jail.

    \n Hi, Steve -- what you guys did sounds completely "normal." Some people don't like that Dust Devils plays out well in one evening, and that they want to stretch it out more. I've talked about techniques for doing that on my Chimera Creative forum at the Forge.

    Dust Devils should play out in 1-3 sessions, usually. Going longer is possible, but some groups find it difficult without some rules changes (and they can vary -- things like wiping out harm each session, or "reverting" harm that doesn't hit zero, or reducing harm originally inflicted and so on).

    So, I recognize that concern. But, Dust Devils as a text and a game is right where I want it -- punch you in the gut, knock you on your ass play that will, in many cases, get you to come back for more. As in "Last time was a mess, but great. Let's see this time what happens if we do it this way ... " Stuff like that.

    \n Did you guys use any chips during the game? Did you begin with any? Earn any?

    The major revision in this new edition is the use of and reward/earning of Chips. You can use them to Fold, which people probably don't think about much in the thick of it. They can also fend off Harm. Depending on how you want to play, using Chips to fend off harm can be a big deal. That said, it sounds like you guys had a great time, and quick-and-dead play was great.     \n Jason, I think you lost me on that one. Would you mind specifying what the scene was, and which player won the conflict? It's very possible you've found one way where the "must" thing is No Fun. I'm curious to see how I'd "handle" it.

    FYI, what you probably did was the way Dust Devils "used" to work. It wasn't broken, really.     \n I'm glad you got some enjoyment out of my idea, Jason and Steve. I freely admit that the idea owes a lot to Grey Ranks, so I guess what goes around comes around, eh?
    \n
    \nAs to multiparty conflicts: the rules only state that the hand that loses to everyone cannot do Harm. It is up to the narrator whether the loser's goal succeeds or fails. Therefore, Jason, your situation is not really a quandary. The narrator MUST let Clyde get his goals, and the fact that Berkey shares those goals is just lucky for Berkey. The narrator DOES get to decide whether the Sheriff's goal succeeds or fails, because the Sheriff beat at least one of his opponents. The narrator also decides whether the Sheriff's hand does harm to Clyde and/or Berkey (Clyde must do harm to the Sheriff, and Berkey can't do any harm).
    \n
    \nThis demonstrates both the problem and the advantage, tactically, with "teaming up". Because Berkey teamed up with Clyde, Berkey gets his goal. But because Berkey teamed up with Clyde (instead of, say, sitting out of the conflict), the Sheriff might do harm. If Berkey had stayed out of it, even if the Sheriff had won narration rights, he could not do harm to Clyde (as he would have beaten no opponent).
    \n
    \nAs the number of participants in a conflict grows, the more important it is to be very explicit in each conflict round about who is actually opposing who. You can only do harm to your opponents, and you only compare your hand with your opponents as well. This can end up with, for example, more than one truly "winning" hand and more than one truly "losing" hand, if the goals line up the right way, as in:
    \n
    \nClyde: "I want to gun the Sheriff down" (opposes Sheriff and Berkey)
    \nSheriff: "I want to gun Clyde down" (opposes Clyde, Berkey, and Cletus)
    \nBerkey: "I want to get Clyde out of town and on his way to Mexico" (opposes Sheriff and Clyde)
    \nCletus: "I want to convince the townsfold that the Sheriff should be kicked out" (opposes Sheriff)
    \n
    \nIn the case above, both Berkey and Cletus could end up with "winning" hands (i.e. they beat all of their opponents) since they do not oppose each other in any way.
