\n Story Games is many things. But it is not Zen.

The moderation team here likes to refer to the "zen" of Story Games. As far as I can tell, what this seems to mean is something akin to "peace" or "calm", presumably the sense of the word "zen" you'd extract from the kind of tabletop "zen gardens" beloved of executives with huge desks.

Zen is not about peace or calm. Zen is about the achievement of enlightenment. This may involve an environment characterised by peace and calm. But it may also involve other things. Notably, it often involves being confronted with things that alarm and perplex you, in the hope this will lead you to the abandonment of previously fixed ideas.

In this sense, Story Games is very not-Zen.

    \n In zen meditation, the student may be struck with the keisaku to shock them into wakeful practice and encourage them on the road to enlightenment. The jikijitsu strikes the student, not to relieve themselves of their own anger and frustration, but as a tool of guidance for the student.

    In this way, shock, alarm, and perplexion may be offered to the learner, as a tool for their benefit, while still retaining one's personal inner calm.

    Even anger and frustration can be used as tools of instruction, when the instructor maintains control of their own anger and frustration, rather than letting the anger and frustration control them. This is a difficult instruction technique to learn, and generally requires building a level of trust over time between the instructor and student before it can be used effectively.

    \n About "harshing the zen", it's an old phrase of Andy's (and of similar new age hipsters in general, of course), he's been using it since he started the forum. If there's some ideology there, it's informalism: instead of trying to set in stone a bunch of ever more detailed laws, Andy just has this idea of a nice and positive environment for people to encounter each other in; whatever contributes to this environment is "zen" in a very ironically distanced choice of words. I find it an effective choice of words for myself, as I read it as a hipster self-ironic tone - he knows that he's not laying out law, but rather just using a gut-check, and he calls his gut "zen".

    I admit that I find the above feature of Story Games annoying myself when it makes the moderation feel weak on the face of aggression, causing it to buckle basically because Andy doesn't want to take sides for right or wrong - whatever choice amounts to least amount of public argument goes, and this is usually some variant on "you guys stop engaging each other now or my dinner party is ruined". I don't pretend to have a better general strategy, though, considering the goals and nature of Story Games; we all would obviously love it if everybody felt like coming to our forums where all social engineering solutions would obviously be so perfect and never flawed, but that's freedom of congregation for you - apparently we prefer Andy's solution more than the alternatives available in the marketplace of ideas. I know that his approach has been very successful in creating an environment where most people most of the time don't feel a need to carry on grudges or congragate into permanent cliques; either he's attracting relatively mature people (perhaps because of the Forge backstory involved here), or the forum management strategy somehow causes people to act more sensibly than your average forum clusterfuck.

In this kind of environment the only moral goods available are human respect and truth/beauty of ideas. I'll go for the truth/beauty myself, many others will go for being nice first, but the vast majority of time there is no conflict between these values whatsoever, so we can be both truthful and nice at the same time.

    Reflecting on the above, and remembering that anger can be rightly construed as simple passion for the subject matter, I'd say that being angry about a topic is OK as long as it's socially responsible and constructive towards the topic. I think that people find being passionate about a topic easier to take when the intense passion or anger is clearly directed at the subject matter and not at other people. My own impression is that I'd have been forbidden from discussing D&D over the last few months if merely being passionate and absolutist about a topic were enough to be socially unacceptable, that's how fundamentalist I currently am about it Fortunately the vast majority of topics are such that nobody feels like I'm threatening their personal identity by being intent upon figuring out the truth about it. Some are, and that's when I get forumarily moderated, too.

    Also, it goes without saying that respect begets receptive audiences. This alone is a good reason to keep a calm head even when driven by passion. The (admittedly European, probably racist) ideal forum citizen since when talks were held on actual agoras has always been one who has great passions, but also controls them well.

The one you join a discourse for is yourself, and you're the one who needs to learn from what you participate in. Or rather, to be entirely exact: the process of discourse, when engaged with rational purity, should benefit everybody participating in it with intellectual honesty. The point is not to sermonize others about "the truth", but rather to lay out the subject matter for consideration, joining your wisdom to others so that the truth might emerge from the meeting of the minds. Without this feature of learning just about my only motivation for participating at a public forum like this would be to throw in a marketing message when I've got something new to sell.

From my viewpoint it's clear who the bad guys are in these discussions - they're the people who bully others into silence and compliance without being willing to uphold an open and trust-based discussion environment. Discourse degenerates when you start to feel that you're in a debate with hateful enemies, and that you can't share your experiences and thoughts honestly because they'll be mocked and discarded on essentialist grounds. As I said earlier I'll go for the truth when and if a choice has to be made, but as a matter of process I don't see these two virtues as opposed. Rather, what seems opposed to both truth and virtue in difficult forum discourses is uncaring intellectual hubris: people troll and bait and write clever putdowns, refusing to engage others in good faith; they push their dogma, apparently in an attempt to convert others by sheer force of social pressure; they ignore and hurt others emotionally and say it's for a good cause. As far as I'm concerned this is acting directly in opposition to a discussion forum's purpose, so the logical thing to do is to decide what it is that's even attempted here: is this a place where people engage each other's viewpoints in discussion, or is this a place where the bestest people tell others how to live. The dogmatic and intellectually dishonest discourse tactics would be right at home in the latter kind of place.

This causes two modes of communication to clash, as people requesting respect and honest debate get in the way of the sermon. It seems to me that people like myself are directly contributing to social tension by trying to discuss these interesting social justice topics, so somehow removing them from the realm of discourse into the realm of pedagogy could be a powerful means of simply ensuring that we don't get mixed up in this stuff. I mean, I'm not stupid - tell me that I shouldn't participate in something, and I won't.)

      \nI like this suggestion, Graham, because it alters what the forum authorities do, changing it from moderation, with the implication of regulating extreme views, to facilitation, which suggests enabling disparate voices to be heard. "Moderators" in general clamp down on things, trying to keep them on an even keel or from running off the rails, with a general goal of homeostasis (i.e., keeping things the same); "facilitators" on the other hand help a process reach its ultimate end-state or object, with an eye toward productive change.

      \n Hmm, I guess I would only find that sort of thing palatable if we called it presiding or chairing over the discussion instead of moderation. Throwing the Internet police on somebody who's only crime is that they're more invested in and better at debate seems backwards.

      An experimental forum might see interesting results if each and every discussion thread involved voting for a thread chairman, with the thread starter as default. It wouldn't need to be a big deal, and ordinarily you wouldn't need to care about it, but where pertinent you could easily see which of the participants is currently responsible for maintaining order. Maybe some sort of karma-count system, if you believe in those. One can already see the germs of this type of behavior e.g. here at Story Games where people starting new threads attempt to control discussion on the strength of the argument that they started the thread so they get to decide.

      Another possible solution would be to simply limit everybody to posting only once every however many hours. This would work if you're mostly concerned by the superficial issue of who posts the most.

      I guess I find value-neutral solutions more palatable than increased moderation involvement.
