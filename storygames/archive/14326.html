    I think there are a number of issues here that can make hacks different from full games:
    - Lower bar to entry in writing a hack may encourage more people with less experience (which is good, but may mean less finished product);
    - People may be more likely to write hacks to play rather than publish, so they dry up once that campaign is finished or may have rules gaps that are understood at the table that the designer can't be bothered writing out;
    - IP concerns, as you are using someone else's work to leverage off; and
    - Smaller potential fan base, as they may need to own/like the original game first to get into the hack (as mentioned above with Terminator Grey Ranks).

    \n The AW Forum issue is partially the fault of the people who get too excited about their hack, request a forum from Vincent (which is the way you get one), and then don't have enough sustainable activity to really make a subforum meaningful. Certainly I'm guilty of that with Firmament. Honestly, my other AW hack, Ghost Opera, is likely to be in a playable state first, but I haven't bothered to request a forum for specifically that reason.

    Most game design ends in either limited play (design for a particular session or campaign) or just getting used as building blocks and fodder for future design and play. After all, the point of Game Chef isn't actually to produce good games. The purpose is for participants to have the experience of designing a game, which is beneficial to future play and design even if they don't finish or the game turns out to be terrible. Hacks are the natural starting point for design, since you can build on things you already like. That makes them easier in some cases, but they still take some follow through and persistence to finish and, even then, nobody may care but you, just like any other bit of design work.

    \n I may be partly responsible for popularizing the term, though I think the Red Box Hack is really to blame. In my mind, it means a game clearly derrived from a single other game, a variation rather than something that's more its own standalone thing. So the distinction between "hack" and "unique, other game" is vague by design.

    It's also come to refer to the verb, "hacking" a game together from the parts of several other games, Ghost/Echo and Lady Blackbird being the earliest prominent advocates of this style of game design that I can think of. Other games were assembled this way before, but haven't openly suggested this kind of design approach in the way John's games did, I think.

    Note that, according to these general concepts, a game that's "hacked" together is not necessarily a "hack." That's hilarious. Language is so dumb sometimes.

    \n To me this is a classic where a binary approach (hack / not a hack) to language fails. Given that games use varying amounts of concepts from other games it is more like a continuum which is something like:
    - Completely orginal design (are there any? Even back to Chainmail?)
    - Original design, but with some ideas from other games
    - Original design with some elements borrowed from other games
    - ...
    - Complex house rules for an existing system
    - Simple house rules for an existing system
    - An existing system played straight (never actually seen this either?)

    You'll have to fill in the "..." above in your head, but I think the point is clear without me typing the whole thing. Look at the way that we often explain new games to other gamers: "It's simple like Lady Blackbird. The character creation is lifepaths, but simple like Mouse Guard. Resolution is a single roll like AW, but uses a d12..."

    \n What I'd think of as a hack is something that probably requires the original to run. Say there's a hack of Dogs in the Vineyards for a Supernatural game, it's tweaked some stuff to account for the fact that Sam & Dean don't give two shits about Mormon Religion, and some monsters have been statted out. You'd still need DitV to play it. It might at some point get released as its own thing. This would be similar to Counterstrike, which originally required Half-Life to play, but then was so popular that it got re-released as its own game.

    Arms Law and Spell Law originally required D&D to run, but they were popular enough that Rolemaster got released. That's what I'm thinking at least when I hear hack. It's an add on for a game that you already have, and then you perform the same hack that someone else did.

    Otherwise I'm thinking it's just a derivative. Vampire: The Masquerade is obviously a derivative of Shadowrun (for mechanics) and Anne Rice novels (for theme), I don't think anyone would call it a hack though. I also don't think when Werewolf: The Apocalypse was released that it would be considered a hack of VTM.

    \n I've been using Dungeon World as my definition of what a hack is since it is the principal example cited in the original post starting this thread.

    Dungeon World doesn't require Apocalypse World.

    I think if you use DW as your definition of a hack, it's clear that there are an impressive number of hacks that are amazingly successful.

    If your definition of a hack is something that requires another game to run but isn't an official supplement by the same publisher... I can't think of too much within the Story Games scene but there are many in the OGL scene that are moderately successful (selling in the 300-1,000+ range).

    So to answer the original question, "Has a hack ever been truly successful?" I would say that in almost every definition of the word hack in this thread, the answer is yes.
