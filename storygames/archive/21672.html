This is why I like the idea of guaranteeing high level characters with hit point cancel, but only against inferior threats.

\nI use a heavily modified version of DCC's Luck with somewhat similar goals:

Every character has a 3-18 Luck stat (in lieu of Wisdom) and an equivalent pool of Luck points. These are re-filled only upon reaching a new level (and not, as per original DCC, by being awarded a few points for playing according to alignment, as determined by the GM at the end of each adventure).

At levels 1-5, Luck points may be added to attacks, ability checks and skill checks (after the d20 roll).

From level 6 onwards, Luck points may also be used defensively, i.e. they may be added to saves, prevent the character from falling under 0 hit points, reduce critical hits and so on (always on a 1:1 basis). DCC-as-written allows defensive use from the get-go, which makes low-level lethality too low, in my opinion. Level 6 is a very big deal in my rules and also constitutes "name-level".

In my view, the disadvantages of this mechanic are that (a) it's a metagame mechanic (atypical for old school D&D) and (b) it creates or deepens the divide between PCs and NPCs (as Luck is only for the former). However, I wanted to have the following effects:

1. Increasing the survivability of high-level characters to get to the fabled 'end-game' (building castles etc.). This has definitely been achieved, though a natural 1 cannot be alleviated by Luck, so save-or-die effects are still plenty scary. We've had several high-level deaths recently so that the current quest is on hold while the players try to level up a new party to get reinforcements.

2. Allowing players to retire a cherished character when Luck starts to run out. So far, none of the players have done this.

3. Discouraging high-level characters from going on low-level adventures (with or without low-level adventurers) because these do contain grave threats (e.g. bottomless pits) and Luck is just too valuable to blow on a low-level dungeon.

Luck points work like a kind of 'permanent' hit points, i.e. the same old dramatic clock on a different scale (per quest rather than per combat).

An unlooked-for side effect is that Luck makes low-level characters much more viable in a high-level party. In these circumstances, low-level character gain levels quickly (on account of the high amounts of treasure) but usually several tries (i.e. characters) are needed to get to mid-levels because high-level combat is very deadly to low-level characters. Even when they are not seen as worth targeting, many die to area effects (breath weapons, cloudkill etc.). Luck does not help with this, but with the next level just around the corner, it can be spent with abandon, occasionally bridging the gap in competence (to some extent, anyway).

(So I'm not such a radical killer DM after all\u2026 )

Anyway, what are your experiences with the survivability of mid- or high-level characters in non-illusionist D&D? Do you allow raise dead, have a mechanic similar to Eero's proposal, rarely reach high levels etc.?

    \n My experience is that I have relatively little experience with mid-level play, and none at all for high-level [grin]. As I've often said, it seems pretty consistent in my experience of legit play that a fresh group takes like 20 sessions to achieve 2nd level, and 50 sessions for mid-level play to emerge. My longest-running campaign arc was still clearly mid-level at session #100.

    In general I think that survivability is a largely mechanical matter - it depends on what I consider the lowest-order concern in D&D; the actual, practical mechanical conceits your campaign uses. Your Luck mechanic is a good example of something that affects survivability greatly. I have a similar conceit in my house rules in the form of "death save crosses" (not a great name, I just call them "cross-marks" in Finnish) that allow higher-leveled characters to essentially get more grievously injured before they give up and die. So unlike you I don't decrease the probability of the save-or-die, but I rather allow the character to survive more "dies" without actually dying - different feel but the same ultimate outcome, as mid-levelers are more unlikely to die. (I also get legit retirement with my mechanic, as characters suffer and survive grievous injuries like loss of stats, limbs, senses, etc. that make adventuring unfeasible.)

    With that in mind (and of course different mechanizations cancel out in various ways - my homebrew is in some ways more lethal and in some ways less than orthodox D&D), I would characterize the robustness of mid-level characters as meaningful. The big, fat, dramatic edge they have is that they don't simply quit and die the first time an enemy succeeds in an attack and lands a powerful blow. They also often have better adventure hooks due to strategic positioning (this is my real gauge for when you've "arrived" as a mid-level adventurer), but on a strictly tactical level the better hit point total pays off constantly.

    Our historical D&D stuff doesn't feature raise dead as a pre-existing thing in the game world. If anybody ever got a cleric-type character to a high enough level to cast something like that, I probably would allow it - the PCs are doing stuff that doesn't exist in the setting all the time anyway. I would be likely to rethink the spell somewhat, though, because I abhor the idea of building a mechanical "backstop" that cements high-level achievement. Maybe require a short-but-risky adventure to Hell to retrieve the dear departed (the spell merely opens a portal to the right place and takes care of the divine paperwork, so to speak), and they lose 10 000 xp even if they're successfully retrieved. (Implies that you can't resurrect a low-level character at all - not enough xp.) Something like that.

    (We've had characters speculate about resurrecting comrades, but nobody's seriously tried so far. The play culture is not sufficiently rooted in D&D for the players to seriously entertain the idea that you could just cast a spell to do it - they pretty much assume that it's going to be like Orpheus and Eurydice or something like that if they'd try it. The closest we've come has been when a character got taken to the afterworld alive once, resulting in them having to adventure their way out.)

    Considering the general design position, I think that high-level characters should be much more robust than low-level to ensure that their deaths are more meaningful - not in a dramatic sense, but strictly as a matter of resource expenditure. It should not be trivial to neutralize a high-level threat, because "high-level" doesn't mean much of anything aside from this very particular wargaming concern. Going against a high-level character in D&D should mean that you prepare to triple-kill them, hack the body apart and bury the pieces with garlic, if that's what it takes to make sure of the job. Everything in the game and the typical rules and rulings builds in this direction. (I do not wish to imply an absolute formalism in this: if you can be clever and get a cheap kill, by all means - but you have to work around the intentionally formidable mechanical privileges the system grant to high-levelers, and I don't want to leave trivial short-cuts.)
