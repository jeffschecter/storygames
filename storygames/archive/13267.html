    \n Freeform "tags" that give bonus dice (or other rewards). In particular, Character Traits and Relationships. These are starting to get applied as uncritically as Attributes, Skills and Target Numbers were once applied uncritically. Before you go throwing these blindly into your design think about whether they're necessary and how they actually fit into the cycle of play.

    Total ground up design for everything. I think Luke has admirably demonstrated that very basic structural changes can result in VASTLY different games. Mouse Guard doesn't play ANYTHING like Burning Empires. Saying they're totally unrelated is idiocy but saying that they're "just" burning games is equal idiocy. That said, critical thinking about right tool for the job still applies. Are you SURE Apocalypse World or FATE is the right starter dough for your idea?

    Jesse

    \n Hmm, tricky one. The very nature of Indie games and their diversity makes it difficult to pick out specific trends. The ones I see the most of are games with extremely minimal or entirely absent background information. You're presented a rough genre and a set of rules, but no definite characters, history, places to visit and so forth. Another thing that crops up a lot is shared narration, along with GM-free games and extremely abstracted or indeed absent combat systems. I used to see a lot of Indie games talking in way too high-brow a language for their own good, what with social contracts and psychological analyses, but that trend seems to have died down now.

    Note that these are not necessarily BAD things, just common within the genre.

    -Ash

    \n When I think "indie game" I of course think "oh, they must be talking about creator-owned works of a small press nature." And then I laugh and laugh because, well. And then I think the following cliches:

    * Mechanics that facilitate emergent drama
    * Incomplete explanation of how those mechanics are supposed to facilitate said drama
    * Hyperdefensive Ardent fans of said games who immediately go to "then this game isn't for you" in lieu of actually 'splaining.
    * Intraparty/PvP tension as the preferred source of challenge
    * Stats/abilities/metrics based on dramatic/story considerations rather than simulative/physics considerations.
    * Short form play
    * Rejiggered procedural authority, either in small ways (-wise rolls in BW) or in big ways (GMless)
    * Games built around very specific setups (you are: agents in Blue City; guardmice; teens fighting Nazis; soldiers in the 3:16)

    I really like most of these cliches actually.

    Posted By: hans chung-ottersonSure, more indie games than conventional games are one-shot compatible, but I think "indie" games get tagged with the "one-shot" adjective more than is deserved. Many, many indie games are built for longer-term play. I think the thing that's more interesting (and innovative) is indie games that are built for a certain amount of sessions, like Grey Ranks or Polaris or My Life with Master, rather than "one-shot" or "long-term" play. Basically, roleplaying games with an endgame.
    \nI can think of at least three reasons for this.
    1. The vast majority of very long games that I've seen have been relatively unfocused zilchplay, while indie games tend to be more focused and thus less flexible in the long term.
    2. Most indie games are focused on the game/challenge aspect or the storytelling aspect. Challenges need an endgame, a final score, stories can't drag out conflicts forever, they need to wrap up, reach a final resolution.
    3. Most very long games that I've seen have been with a strong GM-authority focus. Basically the old "GM is responsible for the fun" mandate. In such a game, the GM can keep on evolving the "plot" and creating new challenges and locations etc. while everyone just sits back. Such a game can last for as long as the GM can keep cranking out stuff that's interesting to the players (so, indefinitely, in theory). Indie games redistribute that responsibility, and thus become harder to maintain, because they need everyone's imput. Hence they gravitate towards a "let's play this for 10 sessions" approach instead of the "let's play Bob's new game...(cue a 3 year long campaign)" approach.

    \nThis is funny. There are plenty of games -- "indie" and not -- that I have passed on because they were in 8.5"x11" or equivalent format. I have no problem looking stuff up in digest size or smaller books, whereas the bigger books are really inconvenient for me.     For me it's more a question of thickness than face size. For instance, I passed on Burning Empires because it was like a goddamn brick, I just couldn't hold it comfortably. That game *needed* to be letter size, and possibly even in two volumes. On the other hand, I am often put off thinner digest-sized books (like the supplements for Cthulhu Tech) because of their flimsiness. My rule of thumb has always been 'if it's going to be less than 120 pages in letter size, make it trade'.

    Smaller books are definitely a firm trend within the Indie sector though, both in terms of page size and word count.

    \n

    Also, keep in mind: retailers are the very last people indie publishers sell to. Their tastes and observations are of tertiary interest. Like, hey, you notice how we're talking about the format of Burning Empires? And that we're not talking about D&D, except to note that it now comes digest-sized?

    \n

    When diversity is a detriment to a type of product, the market is closed and will choke. Time to make a new one.
