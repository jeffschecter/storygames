\n Joshua (GM/organizer) Fred, Bill, Dave, Mel
It was about 1220 am and I know I was ready to get some sleep but Joshua\xe2\u20ac\u2122s enthusiasm to organize a pick-up game of \xe2\u20ac\u0153Beat the Clock\xe2\u20ac\x9d just couldn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t be refused. We cleaned off the remnants of \xe2\u20ac\u0153Rex Rich and the Warlord of Mars\xe2\u20ac\x9d and sat down to play. Fred came in as Joshua was explaining the rules and pitched in at a few points where the rules were changed from the previous night\xe2\u20ac\u2122s playtest. \xe2\u20ac\u0153Beat the Clock\xe2\u20ac\x9d (BtC) is a \xe2\u20ac\u02dcGM-less\xe2\u20ac\u2122 game where each player provides the opposition for the player to their left. BtC uses a small digital timer to track round length, with each round equal to the number of players in minutes (5 players equals 5 minute rounds). The timer\xe2\u20ac\u2122s \xe2\u20ac\u02dcbeep-beep\xe2\u20ac\u2122 at the end of each round had us jumping every time! As characters escape or die, the length of the round decreases. At the end of the game, one minute goes by awfully fast when you are the only player still trying to escape! Right, Joshua?
During each player-turn, a character attempts an action to get closer to escape. Basically this is done by setting an obstacle difficulty, modifying the difficulty by facing fears (harder) or using edges (easier) and then rolling below the obstacle on a d12. Rolls higher than the obstacle lead to either pain or panic, but also buy more time to escape. So a player can seek to set up an action to fail, which will lead to pain or panic, but also increase the time to escape (by reducing the doom level for that character). Sometimes it doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t work, though\xe2\u20ac\u201dif a character fails too badly, pain or panic increases by 5\xe2\u20ac\u201dwhich leads to death as sure as the doom counter!
The situation we set up was New Orleans during Hurricane Katrina. The five characters included a couple of gangsters from rival gangs, a drug dealer, a down on his luck resident, and a little old lady who\xe2\u20ac\u2122d lived her life in New Orleans and wasn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t going to leave now. This was a fantastic game in a totally \xe2\u20ac\u02dcun-fantastic\xe2\u20ac\u2122 setting. The dread around the table when the National Guard said, \xe2\u20ac\u0153You\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll be safe here\xe2\u20ac\x9d and deposited the little old lady at the Superdome was palpable. And yet, through it all, she cleaned off her glasses to read the street-signs, and was glad for her good shoes as she walked her way to safety. The most vivid death I can recall is Fred\xe2\u20ac\u2122s character hanging from a live powerline as the water rose below his feet. His attempt to swing free came to naught as he grounded himself on the rising flood, was electrocuted, and shorted out most of the city\xe2\u20ac\u201dincluding the Superdome! The little old lady, however, found a boat and poled her way out of the flood, picking up her unfortunate neighbor on the way! Joshua\xe2\u20ac\u2122s white wanna-be-gangsta wound up as the last character in play, trying to escape a Mississipi Sheriff\xe2\u20ac\u2122s roadblock by hiding in the undercarriage of an Army truck crossing a bridge, but getting swept into the river as a wall of mud destroyed the bridge, then fished out by the police, who discovered that the gangster had the radio of a police officer he had killed, and then they shot him as he ran for freedom. Awesome.
