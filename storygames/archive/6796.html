\n I'm designing a game about a zombie apocalypse. Essentially, it's sort of Zombie Apocalypse: the Movie: the RPG. I have this idea I want to use.... I know, bad idea, but the idea is just too cool, and I'm trying to get past the detail of implementation.

So in each Act (the game is divided into three Acts, either one per session for three sessions or one per one-third of one session), each player gets to make one Exposition about something. It's a thing the player is declaring to be true, about their own character, their relationship to another character, to the rationale for zombies in the setting, whatever. It's very much related to the dictum that "If the audience sees a gun in Act I, it needs to be fired in Act III."

So, anyway: all the time in movies you hear characters... expositing. Bits of info dumping. For instance, one character says, rather clumsily, "As you know, Bob, zombies are vulnerable to <insert technobabble here>." The player who has dropped that bit of exposition has for this game defined that as the zombie vulnerability, with the added implication that <insert technobabble here> will be found somewhere by some character between now and act III and used to end the zombie menace.

I want these Expositions to have some degree of... reality is the only way I can describe it. Like, say, if a player describes his character's family as being important to him, without using that bit of Exposition, it's just a bit of background noise.... but if that Exposition (token or whatever) is used, then the family is flagged as being important to the plot in some way. Like, without using the token or whatever, the zombies can eat the family out of hand, but if they are explicitly Exposited, it takes a fight to take them out. (Gawd, describing it like that sounds like I'm reinventing Capes, not that that's such a bad thing.)

Am I overthinking this?

    \n Well, mechanizing a particular piece of input can be a way to say "I want this to be explored in a particular way". You can definately do that by just talking it out, but it offers a way to formalize it.

    One example off the top of my head: You could formally state a fact about the setting, then mark it as "Build up" or "Tear down", and then have mechanics that allow people to work towards one of those goals. You can do the same thing by group consensus, but something like that can offer particular direction. Mechanics as shorthand, I guess. Kind of the same way every combat system is really just saying "We could figure out who hits and misses but we don't feel like doing it ourselves", maybe?

    \n OK, I get you. And my comments illustrate just one approach, sorry guys. I think what you are saying makes sense, Chris, and the really important piece will be how the economy functions. Those statements are going to be epochal, because the introduction of the secret base creates a framework for substantial in-game activity. They need to be issued at a rate that matches the sort of game you want to run (which is a nice group-set dial I think), and there needs to be some understanding about what the parameters are. Maybe only a practical upper bound, because players will latch onto smaller stuff (we're playing TSoY zombie holocaust, and my guy is obsessed with his family bible. Obsessed!)

    The "zombie swarm between things" principle is rock solid! You could even quantify that - "You want to rekindle your love for Sally? There are 1,400 zombies between here and there. Go."

    \n There are several games that do something kinda like this, in different ways that may or may not suit your purposes. Off the top of my head, magic in Mortal Coil is defined in this fashion, virtually everything in Universalis is built this way, the GM's budget in PTA and Agon is spent to establish the mechanical strength of things, and Spirit of the Century (along with some other games) allows you to establish information through skill rolls. In the latter case, if you make your History roll, for example, you can declare stuff like, "Wow, these ruins are millions of years old, way older than human settlements in this area." Seems like any of these approaches might work.
