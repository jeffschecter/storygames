    \n Pulling your table back doubles the floor space you occupy. The con organizers may not be so keen on that, you will probably want to check if they will allow it and if it is even physically possible. Maybe the table just disappears and your product is displayed on another chair.

    The formalized distance between browser and seller - a table width - is a reassuring marker to people. Take that away and you increase uncertainty. Maybe people who would approach you now won't. The opposite may also be true. Your fellow sellers will probably hate you for messing up the traffic flow, grandstanding, or whatever.

    Were it me in this situation, I think I'd work hard to arrange myself within the established parameters and rely on social cues to put people at ease and draw them in. Let the table work for you by being attractive and inviting. Don't stand behind it but remain in motion. Cooperate with your neighbors and know their products. Be clean and energetic. I'm sure you can think of stuff.

    Okay, thanks to this thread, I am convinced that my message should be "sell." Here is my plan:

    * Stick to the traditional rig (to keep the customers comfortable)
    * Keep moving around (standing up, smiling, waving and nodding), engage with the gaming folks (seeing what's going on in the con, offering suggestions) and be enthusiastic
    * Learn about the games for sale at other booths and be enthusiastic about those too
    * Make the stall scream "Hell for Leather," schedule short demos at regular periods and have those announced on the intercom
    * Post a schedule on the booth (so I can take breaks and refresh)
    * Bring a stack of indie games and play the shit out of them with strangers away from my booth
    * Use the selling advice from Graham's other thread

    That's the initial rig. If I feel that it might benefit from a more hippy vibe, maybe I'll shake things up on the second day.

    Jason, in answer to your 'disingenuous' comment, you're right, people can smell insincerity a mile away. But I'm not kidding. I don't care about sales. You know why? I'm terrified. I'm terrified that if I try to shmooze people, they will end up buying something they don't want and hate me for it. I worked in marketing for an international telecommunications company for nearly two years. It was all a big lie. All their products were shit, and we, me and the sales teams, had to push them at ignorants. Every month as I saw the lies get bigger and the products stay the same. I saw them delivered and, after some months, we started to see the complaints. I could feel the avalanche coming. Suddenly, the bubble burst. Thousands of calls were coming in everyday with complaints. We had to outsource two call-centres just to keep up. Why? The sales, while hot, sharp and sexy, were built on lies and opacity. Then, of course, there was the fallout. Total, fucking, mayhem.

    I still work in the selling business, but now I do it the other way around. I sell for the long term. I work in a restaurant and I tell people what I think they'll hate, what I think is bad value, and the best ways for people to enjoy their experience both fiscally and otherwise. Short term loss, long term profit. It's not altruistic. It's just a mixture of terror and common sense. So, when I say I don't care about the sales, I'm not saying "I'm not selling." It's not my priority to move the produce quickly. It's my priority to move the produce properly.

    I started this thread to find out out what that means. I guess the answer is 'with personality, honesty and enthusiasm.' And gimmicks.

Salesman! -- you are just SO cheerful and SO happy that he's here, because you have SO MUCH TO TELL HIM about your product and the products of the people around them and SO MANY QUESTIONS TO ASK about who he is and what he likes and what he's interested in buying today!

    Being friendly and polite and really, really intrusive doesn't leave a rude chair-filler with a lot of options. Either he engages in conversation with you and becomes a potential customer (there's a reason pushy salespeople still exist, after all -- as terrible as they are, a lot of people really do feel obligated to buy something from them), or he thinks "god damn it, sitting down isn't worth THIS" and fucks off to the bar.

    Being a pushy salesman is basically hassling the customer...so while you probably won't want to do that to the nice people who wander in and wander out, you should at least consider it as an option for the rude ones who really NEED to be hassled.

    \nTo be professional, you want to be open for business during the entire time the room you're vending in is open.

    I'm going to have to disagree pretty strongly with Jared on the floor-sitting thing. It might, maybe work in some very limited circumstances, but there are simply too many ways to make people feel uncomfortable when you force them down onto the floor like that, including whether they're dressed for it, how clean the floor stays the whole time, and even whether they're physically ABLE to just plop up and down like that.

    You might consider swinging that table 90 degrees so it's along the SIDE of your booth, allowing you to put chairs or stools inside but inviting people in anyway. We never do the table out front thing, we try to create a 'mini store' that people can browse, but it's always a challenge to get them to cross that threshold.

    As to being a place to just 'hang out', ask yourself this: If the least desirable person at that show chose to sit in your booth for 2 hours and it wouldn't bother you, consider it. Otherwise, find a way to engage people for a specific amount of time and transition them on if it becomes necessary.
