I've just this moment released the Kickstarter to Shock:Human Contact into the wild! If you want to play some Transhumanist, far future, hard science-based, serious science fiction about anthropology, go donate! There are a limited number of hardcopies at $25, and everyone who donates will get pre-production PDFs to play with along the way!

\n

If someone really wants to make sure the game happens and donates $100, I'll hand-make them a Kodrek board! Kodrek is a betting boardgame discovered by the Academy in the Kotaht Archipelago of dirty, alliance-shifting space combat, co-developed by me, Vincent Baker, Rob Bohl, Epidiah Ravichol, and Carrie Bernstein!

\n

Whoa! $135 before I've even finished the announcement! Thanks, everybody!

    Steven, thanks! I'm really excited about how the book's going. My take on the Culture itself is eyebrow-raising: I don't think they've got a real anarchy. They have an... unimpeachable benevolent oligarchy? or something. Player of Games makes that pretty clear. The Academy is a digitally mediated democracy with strong principles of not fucking with each other built in. Some people game the system for personal gain, but that's better than inscrutable hyperminds gaming it for uniterrogable purposes.

    \n

    But the way the Culture interact with other cultures like Azad is really, really interesting. The Academy tells itself that they're not visiting to meddle, that they want to learn from them, but can you really do that? And, once it turns out that the culture is as dynamic and complex as your own, what's the difference between observing closely and meddling anyway?

    It's open for 30 of its 30 days. My minimum goal was met within the first day of opening. Man. I'll be doing other stuff in support of the project over the month, too.

    \n

    It's precisely a variation on pre-order. There are some really big benefits over doing just a regular pre-order, though:

    \n
      \n
    1. It's with a central, trusted organization, so people you don't know will trust it with their money.
      2. \n
    2. There's an approval process, so you have to pass a minimum bullshit test to even run one. (This has had some weird effects; I've seen really cool projects get rejected for reasons that are a mystery to me, and I saw a hilarious pitch once that must have been a joke*.)
      3. \n
    3. You can donate below a pre-order level, or donate out of raw support. Several people have just given money because they think it's cool. Most are friends who want to help but don't particularly care about RPGs or science fiction. They've given between $10 and $33. A rabbi gave a traditional $18.
      4. \n
    4. It restricts your pitch to an existing, lucid format. That's a little frustrating for me, since I want to use more formatting than is allowed; but conversely, helps people who aren't web and graphic designers to make something clean and clear.
      5. \n
    5. You get the thermometer effect, where donors see success: there's a feedback loop. I'd bet that there's a WordPress plugin for that kind of thing, but I bet it's much less dynamic.
      6. \n
    6. The counterpoint to the thermometer is the day countdown. More feedback.
      7. \n
    \n

    On the other hand, Amazon takes 3-5%, and then Kickstarter takes 5%. For a $2000 project, that's ~$200. That's a lot more than the 3%+30\xa2 that PayPal takes.

    \n

    There seem to be strategies, too, and when this is all over, I'll be happy to discuss what I did so that anyone who wants to can learn from my better ideas and not make the same mistakes.

    \n

    Sure! Hebrew letters are also numbers, in alphabetical order:

    \n

    \n

    So, to say "L'chaim", which is a traditional toast that means "To life!" you send the equivalent of "chai", or Chet(8) plus Yod(10) = 18.

    \n

    So, for weddings, birthdays, and b'nei mitzvah, you get presents of 18 dollars or 180 dollars, or 36 dollars, or whatever's somehow 18-related.

    \n

    You've probably heard that this is a big mystical thing in kabbalah, which is true, but it's also just a way of embedding well-wishing in presents.

    \n

    You will note the absence of zero, by the way. That flummoxed Jewish mathematicians for millennia, until it traveled along the Silk Road from China and India and was translated into Arabic, which most Jews spoke at the time anyway.

    Tyler, in the Capitalist system, everything has a cost. Fortunately, 10% is relatively painless to just work into your goal. It means that if you need $1000 for something, you set your goal to 1000/1-.1, or $1,112. (don't forget that it's not 110%!). If you can get funded at $1000, you can get funded at $1,112.

    \n

    Also, as you say, it's transparent. Unlike a gallery that wants you to understand that gosh, we'll give you a shot but don't blow it, and we're taking such a huge risk on you we'll have to take 60%, and in the end who did you think was going to pay for the new lights, instead it's 8-10%.
