    \n Here's my take based on running a year-plus of Wushu-hacked Exalted.

    - Wushu is optimized to produce Dramatic Color. It's only a resolution system in a broad sense. There's a binary pass-fail at the end of the rainbow, but all the details come from player and GM descriptions. It's no good at anything tactical because it just doesn't model that. But for color and dramatic detail, it's damn good.

    - The simplicity is a strength. I bolted on all kinds of extra rules, FATE-lite Aspects, etc., and I regret doing so. Things would have moved a lot faster had I used the basic system with a couple of minor rules mods. As it was, I made it way too fiddly. Run it straight or very close to straight first before you get kinky.

    - Die limits are important. At certain points, I had players rolling up to 14d6 thanks to my hacked rules, and the die-counting alone bogged things down. Plus players didn't care as much about narration when it yielded only three or four dice out of the pool. Since Wushu's main point is to generate Tha Cool, that pretty much dragged it down to a mediocre die mechanic.

    - All that said, it just fuckin' sang for Exalted. We had a Dragon-Blooded group with an Immaculate, a Sorcerer, a Court Assassin, and a reckless ex-Cavalry officer. When we were on, especially in the early games before I'd completely Frankensteined it, they rocked the house.

    - I don't know if subtly stated win conditions would apply, given the potential scope of the PoNT, but we never had a problem with having to veto anyone. Eventually, the players got burned out on having to supply the details, though. Some players found it really hard work and wanted to concentrate on other things--method acting priorities, etc.
