    \n I'd agree with the "buzzword" theory. I wouldn't expect any two given people to agree on what it means.

    For instance, in my circles "fiction first" may or may not mean "the narrative is more important than the mechanics", depending on what 'the narrative' means and what kind of mechanics we're using.

    I interpret is as something vaguely like this:

    Rules-first: it's the mechanical or procedural parts of the game which create fictional content.

    Fiction-first: the established fictional content we're imagining comes first, and the rules or procedures come into play when that fictional content demands or triggers them.

    It's not a strict line, because, depending on the particulars, they can blend into each other or merge in weird ways. These aren't precise or technical terms at all; just a short-hand between friends.

    For instance, most "narrative-heavy games" or "storytelling games" are "rules-first" in this view.

    This old post by Vincent Baker might be a really useful one to read on this topic (although it doesn't address those terms or use them):

    http://www.lumpley.com/archive/156.html

    \n I find "fiction first" an extremely useful shorthand. It does require elaboration when the details are important, though.

    Here's how I use it:

    Fiction-first play means the causality for events in play originates with someone narrating fiction.

    Rules-first play means the causality for events in play originates from formal game procedures or rules.

    Fiction-focused play is play where the participants do not need to devote much time and attention to rules use and mastery.

    Rules-focused play is play where pursuing your goals means learning, mastering, and skillfully employing the rules.

    Sometimes the distinction between "first" and "focused" doesn't really matter so they get used interchangeably.

    \n I think 'fiction first' really only applies to fragments of a game. This is how I think about it:



    I think there are two revealing questions:

    1. What triggers the use of a mechanical procedure?

    2. To what extent is conversational, non-quantified state the authoritative description and influence over what happens next?

    In the first sequence, which is fiction first, the authoritative 'state' of the game (what's really happening is chiefly conversational. Somebody notices that the situation warrants a mechanical procedure (say, a move 'triggers' based on somebody's intention).

    Quantified state may be used (e.g. your +HOT), and modified (take 2-harm) by the mechanic, but the fictional outcome is also important. (e.g. the procedure might tell the GM to come up with a hard bargain, and they do so)

    The second sequence is a like a board game (e.g. Mordheim), or Conflict in Mouse Guard. The quantified state (e.g. whose turn it is, following a turn sequence) tells us what procedure to use ("You" choose an action from a list). The resolution procedure primarily updates quantities.

    The changes to quantities get described, (e.g. you rake the troll with your magic claws!) but that description is not authoritative.. potentially even irrelevant. It doesn't matter where the troll was raked in terms of the application of later procedures or quantities. It's just a description of the quantitative change, but the quantity (eg. the troll has 12 hit points left) is authoritative and sufficient state to play the rest of the game.

    These are just two patterns, there are a bunch of others (e.g. updating a quantity because the conversational state warrants it\u2014for instance, you say you 'rest for a long time', so you put your hit points back to maximum).

The game is more robust when, at any given moment, we have some freedom to attack a problem from either direction.
