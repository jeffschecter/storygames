\n I get the feeling this is a stupid question with an obvious answer, but it's come up a few times in my game.

When a PC shuts another PC down, is the "shut down" PC obligated to quit whatever they were doing in the fiction, or is he allowed to continue with his intent and just has to deal with the mechanical consequences (losing strings, gaining conditions, etc.)?

For example:

PC 1: I'm gonna to eat this kitten!
PC 2: Don't eat that kitten, you monster! (Successfully rolls "Shut Someone Down")
PC 1: I'm still gonna' eat this kitten!
MC: No, you can't. You've been shut down. Also, PC 2 gave you "Kitten Eater" as a condition.
PC 1: Boo!
PC 2: Yay!

or

PC 1: I'm gonna to eat this kitten!
PC 2: Don't eat that kitten, you monster! (Successfully rolls "Shut Someone Down")
PC 1: I'm still gonna' eat this kitten!
MC: Sure go ahead, you just have the "Kitten Eater" condition that PC 2 gave you.
PC 1: Yay!
PC 2: Meh.

In the first example, it's the old bugaboo of telling a PC what they can and cannot do, but it gives the move more weight. In the second example, PC 1 can still do what he wants (with a bit of a drawback), and PC 2 feels like she wasted a roll.
This is especially important when PC 1 instead says "I'm going to stab PC 2 right in the face!"

I've been dithering back and forth on this in-game, leaning towards the first example. It only really came to the forefront in our last session where there was more than a little PC-on-PC violence.

I realize, of course, that Strings can be spent and XP offered in either case, but still...

    \n Neither of those are examples of shutting someone down, in my eyes.

    You engage that move when you bully people, silence people, or attempt to shove them away from you. If a PC goes to eat a kitten, and PC 2 wants to stop them, that doesn't trigger a move. At least, not yet. You have to say something like, "Okay, PC 2, obviously you're not cool with this course of action. So what do you do?"

    If PC 2 tries to shame and guilt and threaten PC 1 away from their course of action, and in so doing re-adjust the power balance between them, then they're shutting someone down. Telling someone not to do something? That's just regular conversation.

    \n That's still not shutting someone down. You don't use that move to try to convince someone of something. It's not a "manipulate PC" move. It's a move you use to try to silence, bully, victimize, or shove away.

    PC 1: I'm gonna stab you in the face!
    PC 2: Back off, pal!
    PC 1: Or what? I'm the one with the knife.
    PC 2: You... you wouldn't.
    PC 1: Yes I would. *edges forward, gestures with knife*
    PC 2: Listen here, you little shit. You think you can just pick up a knife and have it turn the tables? You think some flimsy steel is going to put you in a position of power? Over me?!! Gethrazub chose me as his annointed, not you. ME! I'm the one the crows speak to. I'm the one the winds avoid. Go ahead. Put your fucking knife in my chest. It won't do you any good. I'm still the gatekeeper of the abyssal realm, here in Spruceville. You're a pathetic schlub, and a knife can't change that.
    MC: That sounds like you're shutting him down.
    PC 2: Damn right I am. *rolls a 10, chooses an option.*
    MC: So, PC 1, this infernal gatekeeper pal of yours is shouting you down, trying to call your bluff. Is it a bluff? Are you actually going to put knife to flesh here?
    PC 1: Yeah, I tried to reason with him, but PC 2 wouldn't listen. So I guess this is what it comes down to. I put the knife in his chest.
    PC 2: No way, I -
    MC: Uh, you were monologuing at a guy who was approaching you with a knife. I don't think you get a reaction here.
    PC 2: Fair enough.
    PC 1: Okay, here we go. *rolls a 7-9 on lash out physically, puts that knife right in PC 2's smarmy little ribcage.*

    (and in case that example didn't make it clear: shutting someone down doesn't dictate a specific reaction.)

    \n Why would you do it mechanically? In order to strip someone of some of the power they have over you, and/or to make it easier for you to hurt them and control them in the future.

    Why would you do it narratively? Because it makes sense in the fiction. Because your character has so much hubris that they think it's always an appropriate time to belittle and silence people.

    Is it a strong move strategically to try and belittle/silence someone who's currently advancing on you with a knife drawn? No, probably not. A bad idea in real life and in this game. It is likely to end with you getting stabbed. But maybe you've decided that acting like an unflinching badass is more important than emerging unscathed.

    It's worth noting that the baddies that Buffy fights? They are constantly rolling to shut her down midway through a fight. Their tactic is to try and make her cry, hoping that maybe she'll devolve into a mess of self-loathing and forget to stab them. And sometimes it works.

    \n Yeah. I don't see Monsterhearts as a game about controlling what other people do, so much as influencing how they feel (or how others feel about them). When I introduce the moves I talk about them in terms of how they allow you to influence others or yourself. For Shut Someone Down, I say this is where you're influencing others using a verbal or emotional attack.

    I understand the confusion as the example in the book has both a verbal attack and Vanessa succeeding in stopping the bully taking her backpack. What I now understand is that the mechanical (i.e. mandated) outcome is the application of the condition on the bully, influencing how others feel about him. The fact that he then stops trying to get her backpack is the MC's narrative choice, based on the Principles. The MC (or PC if the bully was a player) could have carried on with trying to get the backpack - as anyone could try to keep on doing something in real life no matter how much ridicule it exposed them to - until Vanessa physically prevents it by either usi.g Run Away or Lash Out Physically.
