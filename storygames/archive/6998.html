\n Background: I'm starting another campaign, and want the PCs to do make the world (so I can be lazy), and was looking at mechanics for that (Universalis and so on).
\n
\nProposal: Using FATE/SOTC to tell the story of the "world to date". Each PC makes up a god/force of nature, with a skill pyramid that has stuff like "Socialize with other Gods: Great" or "Move Continents: Mediocre", plus Aspects like "So Mote It Be!" or "Where's my cup?".
\n
\nThat's about as far as I got. What I want is for the setting (the world) to wind up with a bunch of temporary Aspects that will become the axioms and tenets of the setting -- then in session 2, the players make up human-level characters to play stories within that setting.
\n
\nI'm also trying to avoid being the GM of session 1, because I really want to play the God of After-Dinner Drinks.

    \nSince I don't see anything about gods in that, I say drop the conceit of making dieties and have your players make Aspects of the setting which include Axioms and Tenets just before character creation. Dresden does something like this (although I'm not sure what degree its player vs GM involvement) for city creation. Mortal Coil has a fantastic system that could be adapted to SotC, I think. But aside from those resources (one of which is unavailable being in playtest) I would advise coming up with either phases or questions that will help focus thought and promote creativity. Like, if the forces that be are important to build a metaphysical setting structure, well then just focus on some aspects that are about metaphysics for part of your world building. You setting might have 20 or 30 Aspects anyway. That's okay.

    However, if I'm wrong and the gods/forces are critically important, could you elaborate on how they are important?

    \n Burning Wheel supplement Blossom's Are Falling has a "Adventure Burner." There's also a post by Luke Crane on the BW forums a few years back that's a generic "Campaign Burner."
    \n
    \nOK, here it is:
    \n
    \nWhat's the Big Picture? What's going on in this setting that makes it ripe for adventure. What's changing, evolving, declining?
    \n
    \nWhat's the world's culture? What are the cultural analogs? Analogs can be taken from historical earth, current events or fantasy works?
    \n
    \nWhat's the conflict in which the characters are involved? What are the sides? What's wrong?
    \n
    \nWhat physical place does this conflict take place in? What ecology, environment, place?
    \n
    \nWhat's the name of the most important place in this setting? Not the capital or any dumb shit like that, but THE PLACE where all the action goes down?
    \n
    \nWhat's the name of a faraway place that folks talk about, dream about or mutter under their breath about?
    \n
    \nWho are the antagonists? Who is opposing the goals of the characters?
    \n
    \nAlternately, imagine the characters standing at the scene of some great disaster or calamity clearly caused by one of the antagonists. What's the disaster? How did it happen? What are the characters going to do about it right now?
    \n
    \n
    \n(and now you get into the mechanical questions of the game...elves? no elves? magic? how powerful? etc.)
    \n
    \n
    \nPS I really think that having you and the players come up with the setting is THE WAY to start campaigns: it can get everyone invested. Setting and Characters equals Situation....so if they've got any interest at all in what they want to do then they need to get into helping create the Situation.

    \n Oh my God, Z-Dog, yes. How could I forget? I was all gung-ho for Burning Campaigns a few months ago. I like Blossoms' take on it, since it's more specific to "right now" instead of big picture questions. The importance of "right now" is, of course, to have the fuel for story to happen as soon as you start playing. Play is just tossing the match. Although it does not do world creation (the world is made, it's Edo Japan) but modifying the Campaign Burner above to be less "what is the world" and more "what is the world as the PCs see it". So, instead of asking "What is the world's culture?" I would ask "What cultures do your characters come from?" If one person says "Like Ancient rome" and another says "Like the minbari from Babylon 5" then that doesn't mean there's not a pre-columbian Aztec culture. It just means it's not important right now. It also means that you want a discussion on how those two cultures get along and coexist (what's the common ground for ancient Rome and a alien sci-fi culture? What's the compromise to give the setting a coherent feel), and how that would lead to the characters knowing each other. The way Blossoms works was that each answer gave some game element. So here, each answer could give multiple setting Aspects and some will even give potential character Aspects.

    \n Setting + Characters = Situation
    \n
    \nSituation + Characters actions = Plot
    \n
    \n
    \nIt's something I swiped from Luke Crane on the RPG boards a few years back and can't ever forget. It was from a pretty heated discussion that ended with Luke basically saying he didn't give a crap about anything that didn't touch who the characters were and what the issue they were facing was (and I'm paraphrasing from a few years back so don't quote me/him on that--it was the generally gist that I took from it).
    \n
    \nIt was a thought that forever closed my wallet to 80 percent of the merchandise in my local game shop (read: setting books) and REALLY made me stop getting into pointless discussions about "realism" in RPGs.

    \n I don't know if you've run across the "Dawn of Worlds" game before. It bills itself as "A cooperative system for creating fantasy worlds". In it, players build a new setting from the ground up, starting with geography and then moving to races and cultures in subsequent ages. Each player is a god who can spend a limited pool of resources to shape the world to the benefit of his chosen people. I've used it once and thought it did a pretty good job of getting characters invested in the setting.
