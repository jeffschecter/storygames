\n Hello

My name is William and I write games. Not very good games, nor are they very popular, but I write them nevertheless.

I wrote both the first and second editions of the Fantasy Heartbreaker rpg. It's an D20 OSR kinda game and nothing ground breaking. My other games are all thankfully lost in the mists of time, all be one.

My new game is called Domicile. I call it a Narrative roleplaying game, but I'm not sure it is. I am fairly certin it is a rules-lite game. It was written and playtested totally in house (meaning that I wrote it and conned my friends and family into playing it) and as such lacks a lot of outside influences.

Well the die is cast and it's out there for all the world to see and laugh at. But now that for all intents and purposes all the work on it is done I find myself reflecting on it's creation and on why I made some of the design decisions I made. I know only I can answer why I made them, but I was hoping that if I could bounce a few ideas off of you guys and see what you thought it might help me clarify some of my internal thoughts on these subjects as well as help point out the pitfalls in my thinking so as to avoid them in and future project if I were to take up the rpg design gauntlet again.

My first question I would like to present to this most august body is this: What do you think of a game design philosophy of taking the play experience of a number of games you like. Internalizing what you think are their strong suits and best points of play. Then retaining only those parts that you as a GM are willing to actually use in play?

    \n Heh. High marks for committing to negativity there. I kinda wanna try to match it. Let's see:

    Although everyone in the world tries to retain the favorite parts of prior play when planning future play, it's not a totally doomed and horrible endeavor. It's absolutely possible that in between the derivative drivel you'll inevitably employ, a new focus or emphasis for one of your chosen strong points will emerge and not completely suck.

    Tailoring for your own GM preferences, while a navel-gazing ride down an echo chamber, is probably the best you can do, because any rule or procedure you have to force yourself to use is probably even more insipid than the others.

    Of course, I can't possibly give you the brutal deconstruction your failed previous project so wretchedly deserves if you don't post a link to it. Please provide one link and one link only, lest my eyes dissolve from the horrible futility of gazing upon it.

    How was that?

    More directly, I'd say that picking the best and most usable bits from other systems will tell you a lot about your own taste and what's important to you in gaming, which is a very useful step in game design. Depending on your goals, that might be enough, or it might be just step 1 of many.

    \n The process you are describing is, simultaneously, arguably a terrible technique for theoretical game design, and a very effective practical shortcut towards the same.

    Real innovation is not important in any particular game (although, of course, it would be sad not to see it in game design in general). Some of the best games are most innovative, and others are anything but.

    I think a group of designers or design-inclined people (such as many at this forum) are drawn to innovation more than the average gamer. In that sense, it can really help you get some attention for your ideas or your game.

    Otherwise, innovation holds fairly little inherent value; the goal of producing a good game (for whatever audience - making a game you really enjoy playing and no one else does is a perfectly valid goal) should always override it.

    \n Well, now I don't know if you were serious or not! I'm going to treat your original question as serious and tell you what I found exciting (I had no problem accessing the file at first, so I read it, but it is now down, as Tod (AsIf) says):

    * The title and premise grabs me. I can't think of too many games that can do this well (although Dread comes to mind), and the focus on everyday-life-touched-by-the-supernatural is a really good one, standing out from many other "typical" roleplaying games.

    * The idea of Boons is promising. A sort of "Achievements"-type structure, with potential for continuing bonuses if you explore them further. There's some food for thought there, and potentially a really fun tool for game design.

    \n I'm quite opinionated on the topic, you know? A disclaimer: I haven't checked the links, just this discussion.

    Re-assembling bits from existing works into a new whole = game-design.
    Corollary: anybody who touts their creative process as being free of the above is probably a liar.

    Innovation for innovation's sake = useless.
    Historically, in RPGs, innovation-for-innovation's-sake yielded things like: "Look! My Runequest/Gurps clone uses a d36 for skill checks! I've even patented it."
    Actual innovation is borne of problems: you try to solve an actual problem you're facing using whatever tools you already have \u2014 and end up developing a new, customized tool in the process.

    No design is done in a vacuum. Or, rather, design in a vacuum as much as you wish, but those designs are never going to get out of the vacuum-zone.
    For real game-design, there's an audience you're designing for/from (otherwise, there can be no actual problems to solve, right?).
    An anecdote: surprisingly often, game-designers have no idea who their audience is (which doesn't mean they don't have one). A piece of advice: identify your audience.

    \n Rafu all the points you made were good ones, especially the ones about problems and audience.

    My latest game was designed with the intent of providing a more classical style game (a gm running a game for players) for GM's with as much of the games arithmetic transmuted to prose as I could stand.

    The game was to allow character creation in a very minimal amount of time. Insure that the characters were appropriate to the genre (Hollywood ghost films) both in theme and power level. And have game systems simple enough and and written in such a manner that the GM could explain them to the players using common terms without the player ever needing to look at the rule book.

    I envisioned the audience for this game to be GMs that wished to run games for players that were not from a traditional gaming background that might be intimidated, confused (or in the case of my wife) unimpressed with the math to be found in a lot of main stream rpgs.

    Whether I achieved those goals is up for debate.
