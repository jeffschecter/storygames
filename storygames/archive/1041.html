    Legally the situation is no different from my taking the contents of your blog and publishing it in book form.

    I think the question is: is putting an RSS feed online an implied license to use that feed to republish content in a variety of ways? Bloglines is republishing content. So is Feed Burner. And Technorati.

    This is a bit different from the book example. Unless I give you permission to republish my blog in a book, you are infringing on my copyright. [Although in my case, my blog has a Creative Commons license, so I'm already specifying the permissions under which you can legally republish my material. ;-)]

    This contrasts with an RSS feed which is not as clearly defined. Here's a good post going into more detail: Technology & Marketing Law Blog: Blog Content Aggregation, RSS Feeds and Copyright Law
    I'm pretty sure we're going to see some big/high-stakes battles over the scope of any implied licenses in RSS feeds, and I honestly can't predict where the lines will be drawn. In the interim, I suggest to bloggers who care that they put some restrictions near their "syndicate" link, add some restrictions to the RSS feed, register their copyrights and use the DMCA notice-and-takedown procedures when appropriate.

    Yes, that's correct.

    I think the argument is that the RSS format is intended for reuse beyond you looking at my content in your web browser when you visit the URL for my website. The point of putting an RSS file online is to allow other people to syndicate your conent. RSS = Really Simple Syndication.

    By putting material into an RSS file, you are offering it up for syndication by others. That's the implied license. Since you put it up on your site without any sort of login / payment / permissioning (which you could do) you're making your syndication feed available to the world for free. If you don't want to do that -- don't put your feed online, or even better - don't include the full text of your posts in your feed - so people have to link back to your site to read the full article (eg. http://feeds.feedburner.com/designmeme )

    I have a hard time understanding why someone would put an RSS file online, but not want other people to use it.
