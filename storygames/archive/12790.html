\n I had the thought that much has changed in rpg-design the last decade (!). Please bear with me, when I try to describe this in my usual affirmative way ...

When we think of rpgs in general, we tend to think in terms of D&D and other classical games. However; since year 2000 the picture is a bit more complex, with several influential design-traditions broadening the idea of what a role-playing game is and may do. Nothing less than a shift of paradigm has taken place within the design of role-playing games

I believe that the first 20 years of our design history was under the classical paradigm of rpgs; GM/player-divide, scenario and conflict-dynamics, and character-focus.

The last 10 years we have seen the paradigm shift. The modern paradigm frequently see these qualities in games: player-equality, thematic and dramatic-freedom, and focus on player-interaction. At the same time most of the modern games retain some level of character-focus, but the picture in this respect is way more fragmented than it used to be.

I do not believe that my description of the classical and modern paradigms are quite there yet, so please feel free to help me out here.

Any thoughts on the main idea;
- do you believe our design-paradigm has shifted?
- If so; how has it shifted? Do you agree with me? Anything I've overlooked?
- Where do you think the modern paradigm will bring us?

    \n I don't want to sound negative, but I kinda thought this was commonly accepted by now - to the point where we already got over the fact that there was a paradigm shift, and moved on? People have long been talking about "trad vs. indie" or "classic vs. modern" or even silly stuff like "forge games vs. real roleplaying games". A lot of people are still struggling about how to define those things, but the bottom line is: they can't ignore the distinction, it's there, even if nebulous and probably far less shocking than some think.

    I would concur that the design paradigm has shifted, design has matured, it now knows much better what it is about and what it wants to achieve. I would venture to say that it started with the Forge but it was in incubation long before that. I think the peak of that shift has come and gone, and the community has been settling down for the past few years.

    At the same time, I don't think it has been *that* radical. It was just very tumultuous. A lot of the mechanics and techniques that "modern" games use, have been around pretty much since the beginning. Fundamentally, we're still doing the same things. It's just that we've learned to reflect them, systematize and formalise them and identify their bits and bolts. Things such as what constituted "good GMing" was just hearsay and experimentation back in the day, then it was written advice, now it's systemic. The greatest overhaul, I believe, was the fact that the community came to accept roleplaying games as games, without abandoning the roleplaying element (that terrible, terrible old fallacy of roll-play vs. role-play). The definition was broadened, became more inclusive, because of the (deceptively simple) realisation that the keystone of the hobby was play, as a social activity, and the design shifted from the abstract to social - we had to be reminded that it's about these people here at the table, interacting.

    There are still parts of the internet that are kicking against that, but they've long degenerated into a self-parody of hate.

    As for the future. No one really knows. And that's exciting.

    \n I think that part of the issue here is difficulty in defining terms. There is such a diversity of games that my experience with classical games may be quite different from yours. Thus we might make quite different conclusions from our disparate data.

    Classical Games: AD&D, Gurps, Rolemaster
    Modern Games: Dogs in the Vineyard, Don't Rest your Head, Fate.

    In the classical games I have dealt with, the player decides on a general course of action and the details are randomly determined. AD&D 2nd ed unarmed combat or Rolemaster are excellent examples where you choose to "attack unarmed" and random chance turns your attack into a haymaker. Because the player can not modify the details of their actions as well, they cannot flavour them with the personality and flavour of their character and thus the games tend to revert to a mechanistic tactical exercise.

    In modern games, the conflict resolution often leads to situations of "you win, but" or "you loose, and". The outcome is determined, but the details are entirely in the hands of the players. Accordingly, the drunk might win a fight by a lucky wild swing of a chair and having great luck, while a kung-fu master might use precise aimed strikes to take down his opponent. Usually the conflict resolution systems are more open and an argument and archeological puzzle are each using the same variety of resolution.

    I think the issue is that you are lumping the the narrative control games, as being modern. I see the emphasis on character development, agency and player creativity as the key aspects of "modern" games. I view Dogs and My Life with Master as being beautiful examples of the modern design paradigm. Prime Time Adventures also falls into that category, but I don't think it _defines_ the category.


    Your mileage may vary though. This all comes down to personal interpretation of the terms and I think we are coming at this from different directions.

    \nIn my experience, both traditional RPGs and story games have a strong component of do-it-yourself expressed as kitbashing, house rules, on-the-fly rulings, hacks, and/or original system development. For example, we just had a thread "1001 Nights hack for Mouse Guard" where a group adapted the 1001 Nights rules to their story. Small press publications have been common in traditional RPGs, but are even moreso in story games.

    The distinction, then, is that the story games community more strongly prefers to have adjustments be made into formal hacks or variants - whereas traditional role-players often (though not always) prefer on-the-fly adjustments to the rules.

    While this is an important difference in tendency, I wouldn't call it central.     \n To my mind, the notion of story game has to deal with several inter-woven evolutionary threads of RPG design and play which likely stem from before the early days of RPGs to the present and towards the future.       Posted By: Accounting for Taste
      Really, I think I'm just fishing for someone who's deeply invested in drawing this particular line to step in and say why it has to be THIS line, and why it doesn't actually matter that many (most?) games apparently draw elements from both sides of the line.       \nI'll bite.

      Partly, it's the community issues we've been alluding to recently\u2014many of us in the self-described "indie gamer category" know each other and agree with each other about a lot of stuff related to gaming.

      But there *is* something deeper, and it's the reason why, here on S-G and on other internet discussion forums, that one key distinction keeps cropping up.

      Tyler hits the nail on the head when he says that in classical games, resources are distributed according to GM tastes, whereas in more modern designs, they are distributed according to the rules. Now, I think this distinction is especially true *once play begins* (most classical games actually have *more* detailed rules for things like starting equipment and spells, after all). But really, the important thing isn't that resources are distributed in a particular way\u2014it's the effect such explicit distribution has on the power structure at the table.

      For many of us, finding games where it is made explicit the extent and nature of the authority each participant has in the game was really revelatory. So many of our prior bad experiences were made comprehensible. Furthermore, we now knew how to avoid having such experiences in the future.

      Examples: 2nd Ed. D&D, Vampire, GURPS

      3) Indie: no rule 0, there may or may not be a GM, but all participants are equally bound by the rules

It is a statement that says that it's fine for you and your friends to use your best judgment and do what works best and is the most fun for you, even if the author didn't manage to think of it first.

      Since this is going to happen anyway -- the groups who do these things typically don't sit around waiting for permission to do them -- writing rule zero in a game book always seemed to me to be less "our rules don't really matter" and more "your play experience is what matters the most." It's going in there to say that if you and your friends want to change something about the game, the author isn't going to give you shit about it (and hopefully no one else who is not playing at your table will give you shit about it either). The author frankly does not care what you do with the game, as long as you buy it and have fun. If you run into trouble with the "having fun" part, you can come ask on the website for tips about using the rules "properly," or you can find your own solution, it's all good.


      Omitting any mention of rule zero is perhaps a more interesting game feature to discuss: is it a statement from the author that there really is one and only one way to play the game? A step away from the idea that a game text is something you hand to people and let them pursue their own fun and their own goals with, and towards the notion that a game text is a highly-specific tool to create a highly-specific experience, and anyone who doesn't want exactly that shouldn't be playing it?

      Or is it a reaction to perceived abuse in past game experiences? There's no rule zero because your old DM was a jerk who set aside the rules whenever it would screw the players over the most, so therefore THIS game will have no rule zero, and no jerk DM will ever harm another soul. No rule zero, no changes to the rules, everyone has to stay within these boundaries so it's fair for everyone.

      Or is rule zero being left out because the author thinks it simply goes without saying? The people who are going to modify and drift the game in small or large ways are going to do it with or without a Rule Zero to tell them it's okay, so why waste your precious wordcount on it? The people who run into trouble will either ask for the author's help on the website, or they'll "fix" it themselves: either way is fine.

      Or is it something else?

      All in all, I think there are more (and more interesting!) reasons to not write up rule zero in a game text. When someone puts rule zero in, there really aren't many ways that can be interpreted...but when someone leaves it out, it could mean a lot of different things.

      And maybe that's a shift in design paradigm right there: the absence of rule zero in some new games suggests that at some point, different ways to approach game design sprung up, not all of which were compatible with an explicitly-stated rule zero. Older games may simply have never even considered the possibility that there was a market for a game that had to be played exactly the same way by everyone, in order to produce a very specific experience -- but obviously you can't have that sort of game if the author is telling the players to ignore or change rules to create their OWN desired experiences. Older games may not have considered the possibility that it would be fun if no one at the table had the ability to step outside the rules, that working within those restrictions would create more interesting play.
