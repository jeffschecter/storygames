    \n I think you will get a lot of different reactions from people who were playing with these materials at the time. Personally I liked these maps provided the books also came with images of the maps in the core books (which many did). The good thing about them were they were both evocative and handy while prepping for the game. I used to pin them to my wall and found it quite easy to become familiar with the lay of the land by having the map by my desk when I would play. During play I would either use the map folded, rely on the images of the map in the book, or photocopy sections of the map and put them in my folder. These maps were also useful if you assumed the characters had knowledge of the geography, posted it near the gaming table and used it as a common point of reference.

    A lot of them didn't have hexes, and that was I think one of the bigger issues (because you had to use a ruler or similar method to measure distance). Still you could eyeball or measure manually. The Core Map for Ravenloft came with a pretty cool plastic overlay that had hexes on it which 2097 mentioned. One advantage of going without hexes is the map is a little clearer to the eye and is freed up to be more evocative. Personally I like hex maps. But I do see what they were trying to do. Ravenloft even shifted at one point to a quasi 3D map of the core (which I think was good for being evocative but not as good for precision).

    I don't think this was so much about not worrying about live play. I think it was about a general shift away from exploration as the focus and also just a product of experimenting with different approaches. Sometimes these maps landed with a thud, but other times these maps could be awesome.

    If you check out the module Feast of Goblyns, there are a ton of 3D maps in there of all different kinds and I've always found those very evocative and functional at the table. They gave me a very clear idea in my head of what the places were supposed to look like.
