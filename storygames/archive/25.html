    One thing I learned GMing is to give each NPC a distinctive name (try to make sure they don't sound alike) and a distinctive gimmick (certain way of dressing, certain way of speaking, certain way of acting, and so on). This allows players to quickly pick up that the guy in the white gi is the local White Triad leader, but the guy with the booming monotone voice is the technomancer. Because I'm playing so many characters, I find it easier to play "flat" characters instead of "round" characters, and there's a certain enjoyment to such characters as well.

  • eddyfate
    January 2006
    Heh. Sorry, I just started my literature classes. I'm now just as bad about not defining my jargon. [lambasts self]

    The terms come from E.M. Forster's "Aspects of the Novel." Off the top of my head, "round" characters are those that are three-dimensional (what we often associate with a well-developed PC or NPC) while "flat" characters are those that are two-dimensional (what we often associate with a convention PC or most NPCs). In fact, a criticism of a Dickens character for sitting in a room all day wearing the same dress through the entire story immediately prompted me to think of how many NPCs do the exact same thing.

    That being said, it's a situation with two ends. If your NPCs are getting murky, it makes sense to "flatten" them and make them more distinctive and easier to classify. If they're getting too bland, they should be "rounded" more to give them more life.

    New Crazy Ass Roleplaying Idea: A game where everyone must play a stock character -- the only choices the player has are picking the stereotype, picking the clothes, and picking the gear. Only through play can you "level up" and add detail and depth to your character, whichmust be related to the game's actions and events. Hell, don't even give the character a name until session 2.

    So, Player A starts with Elfy-Elf in session 1, and by session 5 has Thurilbrond the Swift, an elven hunter known for his fleetness of foot.

    My suggestion is that you always take a moment to consider why you're bringing this NPC into the game environment. NPCs are either color, in which case they're no more important than any other piece of setting, or they exist to test, challenge, confound, provoke, etc. your PCs. If NPCs are the latter, no one will think they are flat if they pursue their agenda full-bore.

    In my current Burning Wheel campaign, I've got a nasty witch whose sole purpose is to push the norse runecaster/warlock PC. I'm using her to find out exactly how far the player will go in pursuit of his beliefs (I will give my eye for knowledge).

    I've got a devoutly Christian huscarl whose role is to keep pushing at the warchief PC, who has converted to Christianity for political reasons. He's going to keep picking at the warchief's belief: I fight for money, not faith.

    Consider who the players are and how they want their characters to be challenged. Then make your NPCs about that. The players will start caring about the NPCs awful quickly.

    What I usually do:

    Create a set of NPCs, focusing primarily on their motivations and why they're in conflict (and usually want something from the PCs).

    Once you have the motivations, you just play NPCs like you'd play a PCs- what would this character want to do? Figure out which NPCs are going to have the most interesting actions/reactions and frame scenes around that.

  • Iskander

    Hey, Lisa,

    To be specific about My Life with Master for a moment, there's a good way to have the NPC's named and relevant: use the players' connections. If you're not sending them off to capture each others' favourite people, they're surely going to want to have overture after overture. Put the connections in danger, and the villainy and violence flows, in my experience. I ran it recently, and didn't need to institute a 'no consecutive overtures' rule: the players trapped themselves in the Master's commands.

    Also, again specifically for MLwM, the NPC you really need to own is the Master, who is by definition named, invested and present.

    Finally, I would say that, yes, MLwM is a hard one to get down as a first-time GM, though. The rules are only apparently simple: sticking to them is very necessary, and rather hard when you're also managing a campaign of brutal psychological warfare.

    This is a silly question, yes.

    Rather, I recall a moment in the otherwise unmemorable movie "Young Sherlock Holmes" in which Watson and some other character approach Holmes trying to play a violin. He plays a few sour notes and throws the instrument down in disgust saying something like, "Damn thing, I've been working at this for two days now, and I still haven't mastered it!"

    Two hands worth of games? I don't want to scare you, but I only feel that I became a solid GM after about two decades of it, amounting to, I dunno hundreds of games I'd run? How do you get to Carnegie Hall? Practice.

    There's no magic bullet.

    Now, that said, two important points:
    1. In RPGs, OK is good enough. That is, OK, one session of one game, and you didn't have fun. Have you had fun other times in other games? You are probably at the very least "adequate" as a GM, and that's good enough to run. It's a percentages game, and we all have bad nights. Let me tell you about a couple of weeks back...
    2. You're going to learn faster than most. Why? Because you're conscientiously working on it, and doing so in what may be the most advanced forum for discussing the how to of these things in the world. It only took me two decades because we hadn't even invented the language to discuss the salient issues, or otherwise figured out how to talk about it, other than articles by people pulling stuff out of their asses and published in Dragon Magazine. Cripes.

    So, just a bit of encouragement. Keep running games, and, likely very soon, your percentages will be past adequate to "high enough that I don't worry about it any more."

    Mike
