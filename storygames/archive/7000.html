    \n It really confounds me when people continually suggest taht all D&D4 can do is play in the style of a board game or miniatures.
    Yes - D&D4 suggests use of miniatures. Yes - many powers and abilities in the rules are aided by miniature play.

    But nowhere does my imagination for story NEED to be looking down at a table and moving pieces around.

    I am loving the style of D&D4, which in my humble opinion brings back old school original D&D to a nice clean mix of modern rule styles, complete with many Indie type rule functions that aid in storytelling and group dynamic.

    I feel that with the addition of game campaigns (from WotC or other companies) that D&D4 will be a vibrant set of rules to play any amount of ROLL-play and ROLE-play. Earthdawn is a perfect example of what will be a great use of the Points of Light theme of D&D4 mixed with the fantasy story themes of Earthdawn.


    In fact - I'd also argue that D&D4 system suggests to play in any "non"-Earthdawn/story style...

    System does NOT dictate play style in any way.
    And D&D4 is not just a set of miniature rules, no matter how much the nay-saying fanboys try to complain...

    \n I was mixed when I heard about that. I was just thinking about the uniqueness of the stepped rules system for the original Earthdawn, But then thinking more about it, it actually seems like a natural, and pretty hot fix.

    I mean, you can pretty much play 4e in Earthdawn with little deep-level conversion, I think.

    Sky Pirate, Weaponsmith and Nethermancer will be the hardest to convert, I think. maybe Beast Master, too. Thing is, in the few games I've played of it, these classes never really came up too much (I've seen Nethermancer more than the others). Troubador and the like we'll probably have to wait for those classes to be filled out in future PHBs.

    Swordmaster is probably a Ranger, but where "two blades" is simply replaced by "One Blade, just make two attacks with it" (or "Yeah, or you might wield two weapons, if that's your Thing"). I think most of the spellcasters (Elementalist, Nethermancer) could be replaced by either a Wizard or a Warlock (player's choice), just giving the effects to the class abilities: Nethermancer Warlock would be like, "I hit you with an infernal death blast", etc.

    And then of course you'd have to add maybe a general Art skill, which all players must be Skilled in, which represents some art (pottery, painting, dance, etc).

    Most of all, the setting was pretty ripe with stuff that basically makes 4e what it is: Powerful dudes kicking in doors in ancient tombs, killing things, saving the day, gettin' loot, exploring mysteries. Sounds pretty much like 4E to me. Only now you do most of the beat-downs on a top-down map instead of sort of scattered across a half-drawn map.

    \n Out of the can, 4e magic items and Earthdawn magic items don't quite gel, but the tweak is a trivial one. Basically, earthdawn magic items level up with you, growing more powerful as your story grows. It's mechanically easy to implement, so I see no problems there.

    It will need an art skill (or some sort of stand in), as someone noted. One key element of the setting is that demons cannot truly create, and the way for a traveler to prove he's not a demon is to demonstrate his art. It's not hugely important mechanically, but it's a very big deal in terms of flavor.

    A few races to add, no big deal there. The system was level based, so again, no great cognitive jump.

    As Andy notes, I feel like I could run it pretty well with the existing D&D classes. You lose some specific fiddliness, like with magic, and certain classes liek the sky pirate and smith would be in strange positions. But they really have some leeway with this, and I suspect it's an open question how many new classes they'll create. It could be anywhere from zero to all, depending upon how happy or unhappy they are with the spirit of 4e.

    And hopefully it adds cool ass airship rules. Because, man. Airships. Can't have enough of em.

    \n The magic users are going to be the most curious rub, because they ultimately ask the question of how how much give there is between the concepts and the old rules. If I were to run an Earthdawn game with existing D&D magic I would basically be saying "The specifics of magic use were pretty much color, and not essential to my vision of the game." Now, sometimes that's fine - if I'm tempted to run a game in the setting for World of Warcraft, and I don't feel like the specific spells are essential to the setting, I can use D&D out of the box. Is Earthdawn similar? Well, it probably depends who you ask.

    Realistically, I'd be shocked if they didn't just make new classes. One of the interesting things about 4e is that because classes are so power driven, it is much easier to create new magical classes than new martial ones, if only because the special effects are much easier to justify. This dovetails really well with the core principal of Earthdawn that all PC's are magic users, even if they're not necessarily spellcasters. (For those unfamiliar with this idea, it means the fighting guy is magically fighty, the sneaky guy is magically sneaky and so on).

    This, in turn, is one of the reasons this is such a great approach for Earthdawn. Because they have such solid IP, they can practically build the game from the ground up, and if they're feeling ambitious they could do it from scratch. That is, they could make a game that is less grid dependent or with different roles than the default for D&D, since those things really emerge from the way the powers are designed. Of course, whether or not that's how they want to approach it is totally up to them.

    -Rob D.

    I've talked about these ideas a great deal for Earthdawn between the threads I pitched for translating Earthdawn on RPGnet and talking to some of my players about the idea... we came to a couple of ideas on these things. It's only in our opinions, I could totally be happy if they go another way - these ideas just seem like the most simple or clean way to go.


    I came to the conclusion that overall when translating Earthdawn to D&D4 the most clean thing you could do would be to choose your "battles" or rather pick the points that you think will make the best bang for your buck to design from the ground up. From my perspective there are many things that you can translate into D&D4 terms that donot "shoehorn" but rather fit nicely into the D&D4 scheme without sacrificing any Earthdawn COOL... with that here are my thoughts...

    On making new classes.
    While I know classes could be a neat "bang" to create from scratch, I'd suggest that it might be easier for players NEW to Earthdawn to go with using the D&D4 classes straight-away and building "Build Options" into each Class as written in D&D4. For example: looking at the RANGER class, D&D4 lists the Build Options: Archer ranger, two-blade ranger. These are basically suggestions to maximize utility and give new players a hint at building their character.
    So rather than make brand new classes for Earthdawn, I'd suggest that you might build the Earthdawn Disciplines as new "Build Options" under the existing classes. We've noted several of these on the RPGnet thread but the perfect example for Earthdawn is RANGER: Build Option: Archer. and so on...

    What about Earthdawn's uniqueness you say?
    My thought is that building Disciplines as Build Options keeps the connection to Earthdawn but allows non-Earthdawn folks to immediately understand the options for Player Classes without the learning curve. The uniqueness comes back in full swing with the NEW powers you could build within each class to choose from that stack on top of the standard class options. So rather than building a bunch of brand new classes, you'd just have simple additions that would minimize the learning curve yet still remain true to Earthdawn.

    \n Crafting (similar to Rituals) is a key component of discerning whether or not a person has been tainted by the Horrors. Crafting is vitally important to the denizens of the Age of Legend, one skill check does not do justice to the importance. Crafting combines various skills that culminate in the creation unique to adepts. To craft, you need to have studied with a master or developed your skills from a book or a scroll containing it. A craft book or teacher open your ability to create one or more crafts that you can use as often and as many times as you like, as long as you can spare the time and the components to perform the craft. A craft scroll contains a single ritual, and you can perform the ritual from that scroll only once. After that, the magic contained in the scroll is expended, and the scroll turns to dust. Anyone can use a craft scroll to perform the ritual it contains, as long as the appropriate components are expended.

    Before you can perform a craft, you need to acquire the knowledge of the craft passed down from the masters through a teacher or book that contains it and master the craft, or you need to acquire a craft ritual scroll.

    Owning a craft book or having scene a craft being created isn\u2019t enough to let you perform the craft yourself. You must first master a craft by studying it for 8 uninterrupted hours. You must meet two requirements to master a craft. You must have the Craft feat of the craft you wish to develop (similar to Ritual Caster feats), and your level must equal or exceed the craft\u2019s level (based on the intricacy and artistic level of the craft). If you meet those requirements and spend 8 hours studying a ritual, you can add it to your list of mastered crafts. There\u2019s no limit to the number of crafts you can master.

    To perform a craft that you have mastered, you spend a certain amount of time (specified in the craft description) performing various actions appropriate to the craft. The specific activities required aren\u2019t described in most craft descriptions; they\u2019re left to your imagination. Each craft specifies the cost of the components you need. If a craft requires a skill check or challenge, the check usually determines the craft\u2019s effectiveness and artistic value. Even if the check result is low, a craft usually succeeds, but if the result is high, you can usually achieve better effects and artistic value.

    Primary Skills: A craft\u2019s key skill determines the type of components required to perform the craft, and if a craft requires a skill check, the key skill is used for the check. If this entry ends with \u201c(no check),\u201d then the craft does not require a skill check. If a craft has more than one key skill, you choose which skill to use. Your choice determines both the components you use and the skill you use for any checks required by the craft. Unless a craft\u2019s description says otherwise, you make your skill check when you finish performing a craft. You can\u2019t take 10 on one of these skill checks.


    Skill Challenges
    A skill challenge is an encounter in which your skills, rather than your combat abilities, take center stage. In contrast to an obstacle that requires one successful skill check, a skill challenge is a complex situation in which you must make several successful checks, often using a variety of skills, before you can claim success in the encounter.

    Noncombat encounters focus on skills, utility powers, and your own wits (not your character\u2019s), although sometimes attack powers can come in handy as well. Such encounters include dealing with traps and hazards, solving puzzles, and a broad category of situations called skill challenges. A skill challenge occurs when exploration or social interaction becomes an encounter, with serious consequences for success or failure.

    The difference between a combat challenge and a skill challenge isn\u2019t the presence or absence of physical risk, nor the presence or absence of attack rolls and damage rolls and power use. The difference is in how the encounter treats PC actions. Skill challenges can account for all the action in a particular encounter, or they can be used as part of a combat encounter to add variety and a sense of urgency to the proceedings. The Basics To deal with a skill challenge, the player characters make skill checks to accumulate a number of successful skill uses before they rack up too many failures and end the encounter.
    Example: The PCs seek to find a Horror believed to be hiding within a Bazaar by carefully watching the vendors and customers. Achieving six successes means they spot the person not displaying the craft ability or appreciation inherent in beings not Horror tainted. Accruing three failures before achieving the successes, however, indicates that the Horror has escaped the Bazaar before the adepts find it.

    \n Brew Ales and Wine
    Liquids brew and bubble, coalescing the barely and hops into a form you can drink, commonly thought of as a social lubricant.

    In this skill challenge, the PC is attempting to create a brew of ale passed down from his ancestor\u2019s. It involves research in the journals of your ancestor\u2019s notes on this art craft as well as attempts to use your own experiences and knowledge of natural flavors to blend your unique tastes into the brew. The challenge might take a matter of hours, or even days crafting the most tasty drink. You create a brew of Ale or Wine passed down generation to generation. The brew\u2019s component cost is equal to the price of the brew\u2019s potency you create.

    Setup: To develop the brew, the PC must follow the recipe of their forefathers and find new ingredients to test for creating their unique blend. This may also involve speaking with other brew masters or searching the area for interesting herbs.

    Level: Equal to the level of artistic difficulty that the PC wishes to create. Complexity: 3 (requires 8 successes before 4 failures).
    Category: Water + Earth
    Time: 1 hour minimum
    Duration: Permanent until consumed
    Component Cost: Special
    Market Price: 2 sp per pitcher of Ale; 5 gp per bottle of wine
    Primary Skills: Nature, History, Diplomacy, Arcana

    Nature (moderate DCs): You seek out flavors and herbs that will aid in the crafting of your brew, searching for the components of the recipe as well as finding new ingredients to add your own unique blend. Success means you\u2019ve found proper ingredients and come one step closer to fruition. Failure indicates the local area does not have the ingredients needed for the brew, meaning you must seek other means of finding the ingredients such as Diplomacy or Streetwise for tracking down the components from local bazaars or stores.

    History (moderate DCs): You delve into historic recipes of your ancestors and other brew masters to further your research. Success brings the research one step closer to fruition. Failure indicates that this particular line of research ran into a dead end or an unavailable ingredient.

    Diplomacy (moderate DCs): You visit a brew master or potion scholar, hoping to curry favor and learn a useful bit of information that will bring you one step closer to discovering the secrets of brewing. This approach requires a character to spend gold pieces equal to the level of the challenge. You pay the cost, whether the check results in a success or a failure. A success provides a +2 bonus to the next Nature check or History check, as well as counting as a success for completing the challenge. A failure likewise provides a \u20132 penalty, as well as counting toward the completion of the challenge.

    Arcana (moderate DCs): You visit libraries and sift through vast stacks of lore in search of a useful bit of knowledge. Success brings the research one step closer to fruition. Failure indicates that this particular line of research ran into a dead end.

    Success: The PC creates the brew with the Difficulty Class resulting the potency and artistic value of the flavor.

    Failure: The PC uncovers flawed or incomplete information or fails in their creation of a tasty beverage. As they proceed forward, they operate at a disadvantage. Perhaps the ingredients are lost from improper mixing or other accident in the process. Or they might translate the recipe incorrectly, leading to all kinds of mistakes and misadventures from an overly addicting drink to a poisonous concoction

    Posted By: AndyPosted By: Alvin Frewerwhat is it about Earthdawn magic that makes for interesting story hooks?
    \n...not much, from what I recall. I never really read all the supplement background-book-throw-it-on-your-shelf-and-never-use-it material. In the book, it just seemed that there were different types of magic (Elemental, Death/Nether, Arcane) and that was that. There were other interesting stories that happened in the setting, like where the Blood Elves came from and the like, but nothing really tied to magic that I can recall. I might be misremembering though.

    -Andy

    Most of the magic in Earthdawn was tied to threads, threadweaving, pattern items, and true names. Smart PCs (just speaking strategically, not even story wise) wouldn't ever go in against a strong foe until they'd found his true name, some of his pattern items, woven threads to them, researched his magical protections and found the ways around them. All of those things, finding names, discovering history, learning the human stories of their enemies, very easily moved into making stories. That some types of magic could corrupt the threads you'd tied to someone else to gain power over them, allowing them or horrors to gain power over you, made it all the more juicy.

    Earthdawn spells were, more or less, just spells. But the threads, named items, pattern stuff, and name rituals all were awesome for story hooks. Most of that can probably be ported to 4th Ed rules, it'll just take a little work and thought.

    As for the general topic, I think the whole thing is going to depend largely on how much effort goes into the back end. A simple "Play Earthdawn with the Players Handbook" will probably lose a lot of the magic that is Earthdawn. But a good, well designed and playtested game could probably give a lot that is the best of both worlds and maybe breathe some new life into the IP. I mean, after all, much as Earthdawn had many rules for things other than combat, the most detailed and extensive parts of the rules were those for combat, and fighting made up a big chunk of game play, and getting read for fights with research and rituals the other big chunk. All those things can be done with 4th ed -- especially if they fix the rituals rules.

    P.S. Andy, no Skyraiders? WTF? I've never played an Earthdawn game without at least one Skyraider PC. Skyraiders are the dopest shit in the game.
