\n
\n
\n
\n So here's the thing:

I've run PtA a couple of times and played it a couple of times. Whenever I've played it, I've had a ball. Whenever I've run it, I've had very mixed results, but I've never actually enjoyed it.

The first game I ran was disastrous. We came up with a pretty cool sounding show, but the three players were all pulling in different directions. The game didn't work and we quickly aborted it.

The second game is running at the moment. We're going to be running the 3rd episode tonight. The game is running fine and everyone is having a great time, except for me. I'm just kind of sitting there, trying to point the players toward the conflict in each of their scenes and drawing cards when I need to. I play the odd NPC, but to be honest I'm not really doing much of anything. The game's pretty much running itself, and frankly I'm a bit bored.

So what do I do? I'm reluctant to step in and direct the plot in any given way. I suggest stuff to player who are stuck in setting a scene and whatnot, but to be honest the players are pretty creative and don't really need my input.

I think the problem comes down to this: I ran games for a great number of years where I was the GM and therefore ruled as a god. Which is how most people played to be fair. I was a benevolent god and pretty much let the players do whatever they wanted as long as I thought it was cool, and I'd write "plots" for the characters the players presented to me. So in jargon terms, it was distinctly Story Before, but the story was heavily influenced by the players. I'm now (I think) overcompensating by being very, very hands off and pretty much letting the players create the story themselves, just touching them along every now and then when they get stuck, but (and here's the problem) not actually creating anything much myself.

So how do I find that happy medium?

    \nMy answer is: you're a player too. Add your ideas. Give NPCs agendas and run with them. Don't be afraid to push story points that interest you. Sure your role is to support an overall story that helps the players focus on their issues, but that doesn't mean you don't get to push plot -- just that you have to give up control of where it goes. The PTA rules won't let you dominate the game, and it sounds like you have a robust group of players who aren't going to suddenly become passive. Your players may well be pleased to have the pressure turned up a notch.

    \n Rich: If you haven't already done so, draw an R-Map of the PCs, their relationships to each other, and to their Connections and their Antagonists. Include their Issues on this map.
    \n
    \nLook at this map. Make sure that every NPC wants something from at least one PC (this doesn't have to be negative, it can be "I want to escape the doomed planet with you"), and that the PCs want at least one thing from one NPC. Then, whenever a PC is in a scene with this NPC, push hard with this desire. Have other NPCs come in with desires that are partially in line with the PC's.
    \n
    \nSuddenly you're playing interesting, vibrant characters who are an important (if not central) part of the story, and capable of increasing the pressure on the characters, but without simply decreeing the plot.

    \n i think one of the things about PtA that people miss is that while the players set the Location, Agenda and Focus of the scene, the Producer is suppposed to frame *every* scene. This gives the Producer TREMENDOUS power to add twists to every scene. We're on season two of our PtA game for which I'm the producer. Part of my job is to push the agenda of the NPCs as hard as I can, and the fact that I have framing power over every single scene is my primary method of doing this. (Note: I don't always USE my power if a player really has something specific in mind but sometimes a player will have just an idea of what they want their character to be "up to" so to speak and then I use my framing power to twist a knife in it).

    Example from own game. In the game Violet is a brilliant clockwork engineer and she's currently investigating a mysterious message her dead father left her. Her player gives me: Focus: Plot, Location: CIty Library, Agenda: Violet researching the mysterious map she's found.

    As Producer I narrate that the scene opens with Violet walking through the stacks, pulling books off the shelf comparing drawings inside with her maps when suddenly she hears familiar voices on the other side of shelf. The voices turn out to be Stephen (Violet's love interest) and Mademoiselle LePointe (a mysterious young woman who attacked Violet earlier in the story). Both are NPCs with strong agendas driven by me.

    Also remember to use narration rights when you win/lose a conflict to spin the ball as hard as you can. A good thing to keep in mind is that winning Narration means you get to say WHY someone succeeds and that doesn't have to be direct 1-to-1 with character action. The last conflict of the last episode a player was trying to stop a wedding. He succeed but I, as Producer, won narration. He was able to stop the wedding because he snuck out the back door with the bride while everyone was distracted by the fact that the police had just arrived to arrest a different player character.

    The Producer has power in PtA. It's just in weird places and you have to learn how to use it.

    Jesse

    \n Another thing to keep in mind is that you don't necessarily have to chuck your inner "strong GM" out the window, you just don't get the carte blanche anymore. The rules say, "achieve consensus," not "wuss out". The role of the Producer is vital for providing strong suggestions when the rest of the group is floundering. I've played PTA with folks who don't have a lot of GMing experience, so sometimes there have been hitches where people aren't sure what scene to frame next, where to take the episode from here, what to narrate, etc.

    Those are all good times to remember the so-called godhood and go, "Hey, you know, I think that it'd be pretty cool if *insert suggestion here*. What do you think of that?" As long as you can keep in mind that everyone can go, "Nah, that's not what I really was looking for," or "dude, that's lame", you can still bring all those trad-GM thought processes to bear.

    \n For what it's worth, part of the problem I've had with PTA play in the past is that there's not enough explicit exhortation in the system for the GM (bear with me, that's shorter to type than Producer) to really get in there and push stuff at the players. I mean, I'm all for hippy let everyone do their thang stuff, but not at the expense neutering "here's some story and IT'S HAPPENING RIGHT AT YOUR FACE" stuff which I rely on from a GM and *need* a GM supply to me as a player in order for me to truly enjoy the game.

    I've played in several PTA games at this point and always had fun, but it's been very, very rare that I've walked away feeling like I've had an emotional connection to any character at the table. I think this was because I felt too much in control of my destiny; some element of that equates to the death of discovery-delight and surprise in terms of how I emotionally experience the game.

    All of which is me saying, dude -- if you know you're being a "weak" GM by your old standards, STOP BEING THAT. If there are any players at the table who are like me, there's a decent chance that they may be having fun but they're wanting some of what you've got to give, all the same.

    \n So I read this thread, and got some good advice, and ran Episode One (did the series premiere already) of Strange Allies on Saturday.

    FIRST SCENE with a conflict went like this...

    I roleplay the NPC, keeping in mind what the NPC wants ("I want you to watch the others and report any wrongdoings to me.") and generally playing toward that.
    Player roleplays the character.
    We get somewhere in the vicinity of the conflict, and I stop and say "okay, I think we're at the conflict..."

    And then we hem and haw for a few minutes, trying to figure out what that conflict is, and narrowing it down a bit, and realizing that we're talking about the FRIGGING NPC'S GOAL in the scene, not the players, so we back up and come at it again, and figure it out, and finally get it down, and draw cards, and resolve it.

    ((This has been, up to this point, pretty much how many of the conflicts have been going.))

    I frown to myself, think on a bit, and we move on.

    ----------------------------

    SECOND SCENE:
    I roleplay the NPC, keeping in mind what the NPC wants ("I want some sign that you care about me.") and generally playing toward that.
    Player roleplays the character, who's issue is self-destruction and his sense of inescapable doom.
    I get in the general vicinity of the conflict, and I PUSH THE NPC RIGHT AT IT, IN THE CHARACTERS FACE. (As he's leaving the room, she says "I love you." Just HANGS it out there, between em.)

    Then I turn to the player and say "Okay, given this situation, what do you want?"

    ----------------------------

    So. Much. Simpler. So much CLEARER. SO much faster. SMACK! Right in the face!

    And holy cow was the scene suddenly electrified.

    After that... instead of stopping and looking for what the conflict was about to me, I drove the NPC right in there and smacked the PC with something.

    Then it was just "Okay... given that, what do you want here?"

    In other words, I rediscovered that great adage: "Show, don't Tell." It was like realizing "Oh, I can do Bangs in this game, too!"

    Had a fantastic session.
