\n This is a thread to talk about Until We Sink, a curious and excellent game by Magnus Jakobsson.

Have you played? What was your experience? What worked and what didn't?

If you've played multiple times, what advice would you offer? There was a question about Until We Sink's replay value, and having played numerous times I can assure you that it is high. Despite the rigid sequence of events and fixed characters, every session is interesting and unique.

Here's an old thread about the game, sorta funny in retrospect.

Anyway, Until We Sink!

    \n One thing - and this is pretty minor but strangely compelling - when you leave a scene, leave the scene. Get out of sight in a different room. For reasons that are probably suspiciously larp-y this is way better than just getting up and signalling that your character has departed. Partially this may be because it allows for some hidden knowledge among those you leave behind. It seems like a lot of important stuff happens between the first departure and the second, and the lack of transparency for those few moments seems to work well.

    \n I've played it multiple times, and I disagree with the advice in your second post. Being clear about leaving the scene = yes. Leaving the room = no, no, no. I just walk past some imaginary, arbitrary "out of game" line and then sit quietly in a far corner of the room, listening. If you leave the room for real, then it's like if you left the room during a game of Fiasco: people will need to fill you in on what happened while you were away, because you need to know (as a player).
    The game's not a "larp", in the sense that synchronous play and partial player knowledge aren't part of the format here: even with full transparency, it's already hard enough to tie all of the loose ends into a "story" in the end.

    \n That old thread about the game was pretty hilarious. I guess we did know how to argue back then;)

    Looking back on playing Until We Sink on four occasions, and how it has helped me grow as a player and a designer; I would still say that it was a ground-breaking game. and honestly, just as fresh as it was four years ago. I wouldn't hesitate to put it in my Top Ten Influential RPG list.

    Re: leaving the veranda; I think I have played all of my sessions in the same person's apartment. And if memory serves me, the person getting up would just want into another room (the apartment has an open layout) and either listen in on the remaining convo, or not. Or use the bathroom, or fix a drink or whatever. It just never came up as an issue for me.

    I see the first person leaving the veranda as being a big point in the scene. It's a sign that "hey, the main part of this scene is over" or "we've been rolling with these scene for awhile. And the unsaid message from the first person to get up is "let's wrap this up". Sometimes it takes awhile for the second person to get up (and thus for the scene to end). But occasionally, the second person gets up almost immediately. It's a fascinating mechanic.

    \n Tomas makes an interesting point here.

    From playing it with different groups (and my own experiences the first time), I see a lot of aimlessness of purpose in the first few scenes. A bit of unsaid "what is this game really about?" There are no real mechanics and just a few cards with a small amount of info on them. Nothing to really hang your hat on.

    And then eventually everyone realizes that the game is really about the tension between the characters. Most everything about the game; the dead body, the island slowly sinking, the islanders vs the visitors, the scene cards, the character differences...they all feed into that tension.

    \n I'm glad to hear about its replayability - this is a game I think I'd like to bring to some of our local cons, as there's a few people I know who I think would really dig it. For those of you who've played it more than once, how long do your games last, on average. (I think the game we played on Tuesday night went for about an hour and half, plus some time at the end to reflect on what'd happened.)

    My favourite part of the game is that an entire day happens off-screen between each evening's conversation. I found that gap inspired me to flesh out my character's life, and it created two reversals I found interesting:

    - During Night 2, my sullen teenager had started to enjoy bantering with the backpacker and obviously wanted to get stoned with him. But at the start of Night 3, I found myself acting coldly towards him. Something had obviously happened between us during that day, but I had no idea what.

    - My dad and I ended up exchanging some pretty hurtful words at the end of one evening. I decided that my teenager would start the next evening happy and considerate towards my dad - obviously we'd had a good talk during the intervening hours and sorted some of our issues out.

    Another thing that became obvious as we were playing it: we treated the situation a little bit like an analogy for rising sea levels (a situation that island nations like Kiribati are already experiencing).

    And a good point about the social divide between islanders and guests: as we played, I became aware of a real hostility from the two characters who were native to the island towards towards their slightly obnoxious guests.

    \n You are so right, Jason; actual play reveals the treasures of the game.

    Magnus and me were involved in a project before Until We Sink, with another bloke, where all of us tried to unlock the secrets of modern game-play, each in our way, in three games meant to be published together. The secrets were the way of non-mechanical and GM-free play; a modern design of a very different type.

    Magnus made his part of our co-project a genre-game on explorers discovering a hidden valley, called The Forgotten Valley. That game had some of the elements you find in Until We Sink, but the method was hugely refined and simplified in the later game. We eventually gave up on our co-project, but the secrets we were searching for, were unlocked by Magnus when he went on to design Until We Sink.

    I'm very grateful to him for doing that. It has opened up whole new venues of design to us, and new venues of methodical game-play.

    I were astounded by the simplicity of the final principle. To make a game so simple, and then to have that game deliver such nuanced drama, was mind-blowing! It seriously widened the possibilities inherent in role-playing games as an art-form.

    \n I got a chance to play this for the first time last night at GoPlay Thursday at Red Castle Games, with Joel, Tyler, Joe McDaldno, and ...uh, Abby? (lack of sleep making me forget her name...SavageHominid's girlfriend).

    Anyhoo...

    It was really, really fun. By limiting the character interaction to a particular time and place each day, as they are gathered on the veranda, makes it feel like we're playing out a scene from an art theater play. The curtains rise and the lights go on to reveal to the audience a piece of a story.

    It was also interesting to see how the revealed Event cards sometimes match up perfectly with things that were brought up in play on the previous day. I was playing the hotel owner, Lon Fata'ai, and had revealed on the previous day that there were the ruins of an old temple on the island, and it was kinda dangerous to go in there, trying not to really explain anything like any good tight-lipped, cranky old man would. Sure enough, the next event was the newspaper clipping about the death 20 years prior, which seemed to fuel EVERYONE's suspicions about what the significance of the temple might be. It was AWESOME.

    It was only at the end where things started to drag out just a bit, as we had three unanswered questions that no one was really willing to own up to (the wet radio, the dead fisherman, and the death 20 years ago). But I think that's because none of us really tried the whole pointing at someone else, and saying "Hey, I saw you doing..." or "Why did I see you over at such-and-such place?" Not sure if it was because we weren't really used to that bit of the rules, or if maybe we kinda forgot it as the game went on, but I think it would have really pushed the scenario a bit more. It needs to be a big, bolded bullet point in the rules: Tell other players what you saw them doing.
