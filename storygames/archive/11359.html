\n So having recently downloaded and read TRAIL OF CTHULHU, I find myself falling in love with the Gumshoe system.

Problem: all of the products associated with it seem to be in the horror genre. Having run a 9-month World of Darkness Chronicle and then invented and playtested my own horror game (THE BLACK SPOT - available at a website near you! - ahem...) I have worn out the patience of all my friends for the horror genre.

What I'm looking for, then, is a systemless (or easily adaptable) commercial setting in which mystery-solving is the chief component. I can come up with an ad hoc list of abilities for characters to use once I know what kind of game I'm going to be running. But designing a whole setting from scratch looks too much like real work. Plus I want to see what my group's general level of tolerance is for the type of gaming that Gumshoe encourages (though I sincerely hope that it's high) before I go scribbling out a bunch of maps and whatnot.

Any help for me out there?

    \n I have thought for a long time that Gumshoe would be an excellent system to use with a campaign setting based on the Judge Dee mysteries.

    Plusses:
    - a reasonably lengthy, in print, set of books that can act as background
    - a non-modern setting (which Gumshoe doesn't yet have)
    - something to add to the Gumshoe rules -- rather than making "The Magistrate" a character, I'd set it up like this: all the PCs would be "Magistrate's Assistants" (as in the books), and abstract their boss as a shared resource pool they could draw upon to lend them direction, find clues when he's present, provide needed authority when needed, and so on.

    Cons:
    - reasonably tight and not very well known setting
    - books not in public domain, so presumably would either need a license, or a kind copyright owner, or a "serial numbers filed off" carefully written book (given Pelgrane's experience with Dying Earth, I'm not sure they'd be tremendously keen on leaping into another licensing arrangement, and a third party having to license both the setting and the rules set would seem to be a recipe for disaster)

    Would make excellent meat for a "house rules" campaign, and finding resources for it would not be hard:
    - the Judge Dee books themselves
    - Paul Mason's excellent, although incomplete, "Outlaws of the Water Margin" rules set
    - added films, books, etc, to taste
