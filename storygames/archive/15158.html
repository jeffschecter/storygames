\n I'm guessing that most of us at S-G already know about the downsides of the GM determining what'll happen in a game before it's played. There are many bad things that can happen, that have in fact happened and ruined everyone's fun for many a session.

So. Let's just start with that as a given, and not argue about it here.

What I want to ask here, separately from the bad stuff, is what's the good stuff?

What types of fun have you seen a GM's planned outcome create?

    \n Fun NPCs whom the players really like are my favorite outgrowth of GM preparation.

    In large-form games (that is, big complex games with many sub-systems and rules), GM prep can be a fun and efficient way to introduce new content and features. The GM also gets to familiarize themself with complex rules in advance, making them easier to initiate at the table and requiring less look-up and argument time.

    GM prep sometimes results in multimedia and visual aids, such as detailed maps or soundtracks.

    A preset plot can be convenient if your players happen to be tired or in a receptive mood, even for the most adamant "play to see what happens" advocates.

    The feeling that a GM did a lot of prep to make a story thread just for you, tailored to your character and your play style, is charming.

    \n My favourite DM is also my oldest friend, and he doesn't always do a lot of pre-determining, but on occasion he does, and it usually works out really well. The best example I can think of is a d20 Modern steampunk campaign he's running right now - it's a sequel to a campaign I played in a few years ago featuring some of the same characters, and so I made a cameo while visiting home as my old character. The party had travelled back in time in order to ensure that certain events happened as planned and did not disrupt the space-time continuum Back to the Future style. But the brilliant thing about it was that the whole adventure was based on a PREVIOUS adventure they had played almost ten sessions ago, so they had to make the past versions of their own characters do the things that they had already done. They had encountered my character as an NPC in the original adventure, but this time I got to play him myself, interacting with the time-travelling future versions of the party, later the same day. It was too fucking funny (and just as brain-hurting as every other time-travel story ever).

    At the end of the original adventure, the party's aether-ship had been blown up by sabouteurs in a spectacular explosion. This event was highly traumatic for both the characters and the real-world players, as the ship had been around since the original campaign that I played in and it was essentially their home in the game-world. The party had never found the real culprits, and had since built a new, crappier version of the ship and used it to travel back in time. So, naturally, they ended up having to blow up the ship themselves for complicated macguffinny time-travel reasons - but they stole back the original, better version of the ship and blew up the new, crappier ship instead. My favourite part was the party gathering on a nearby rooftop to enjoy the explosion, and watching the shocked reactions of their past selves, which they had themselves roleplayed ten sessions ago, before sailing back to the future in their beloved original ship.

    It wouldn't have worked without extensive GM pre-planning and railroading, but it was just glorious!

    \n Some advantages that come to mind:

    1. Preparation

    If you pre-plan outcomes... you can better prepare them. Let'say you plan a big emotional speech by the Big Bad Guy. Well, you can sit down and carefully write exactly the speech you want, then practice reading in front of a mirror until you get to sound just the way you want.

    If you know what's coming... you can make sure you're ready. Which leads me to:

    2. Challenge

    In a game built around challenging the players, you may be able to raise the bar higher by preplanning events. If you know they'll be in a dark room with five sides, and jumped by two tall three-armed troglodytes, you can take your time to prepare the surroundings and the monsters' stats to be really really challenging to the players (which can be hard to do in some games - maybe you have a group of super-powered munchkins in your game, but they love a harder challenge?).

    This one skirts your line of "do we care?", though, which might make not qualify as pre-planned for your purposes. (I like that definition, incidentally.)

    3. Acting/Emoting

    When you know where things are headed, or at least that they ARE headed in a specific direction, you might be able to focus more on delivery, acting, voices, emotional details, etc. This may be what happened in Todd's game (in that link, "4 out of 7 cried"). By knowing pretty much what was going to happen, the GM could be better able to improvise details or descriptions (or whatever) for maximum impact without having to worry about where it was all headed.

    Same thing from the players' side: when they trust they're "on rails", guided carefully by something-or-other (be it the GM or not), they can put more work into their portrayal of that kind of drama. In a railroad where you know you'll be the heroes, you can be more confident in doing brave-but-foolish hero-like things, and give big grandiose speeches. If you're playing Polaris, you know it'll probably be cool if you start foreshadowing your character's tragic downfall from the start.

    From what I understand, some Nordic LARP/jeepform takes this to another level by letting everyone know where things are headed, and leaving the player's only job the thorough and high-level portrayal of that path. Someone whispers into your ear, "You are falling in love with her," and the player can dedicate him or herself to portraying that as skillfully as possible.

    In the same way, if your goal is "immersion" inside your character's head, and in their emotions, maybe playing a strongly pre-planned story allows you to immerse yourself in the current moment where your character is standing, and really "feel" what the character is experiencing.

    ("worm" alluding to armies on the march, snaking through the lands. And of course; alluding to the poison of war)

    This scenario is pretty railroaded. The characters are scouts/warriors under commando, as part of an army. Follow orders, or be considered a coward and/or traitor.

    - The first part is your farewell to the village you live in, your close family and friends. Then you don your sack of provisions, and your snow-shoes, take up your weapon, and march towards a village in enemy country. Hard march in winter-conditions, and secrecy is of the essence. No witnesses to the march must be left alive. Preplanned scene: the characters happens upon a mother and small boy in the woods.     \n- The second part is the attack on the village. You don the tabards of an ally of the village, and attack the unsuspecting village in pre-dawn, burning roofs, plundering essential food-stores, hacking down anyone. The fight is given as a pre-planned list of situations, that the GM's throw at the players, in furious tempo. We demand answers to threats the very second they are perceived (to simulate the stress of real combat). There's next to no die-throwing; only shadows, movements, cries and brutality, and characters/players making dire choices to survive. In the darkness and chaos they hack at anything that comes upon them; old men, naked women, screaming children, a dead man hanging on a wall by a spear, their own bloody comrades, etc-etc-etc. It goes on for a lot of rounds. It is supposed to end when a horn is blown, but we pack that info into more stressed and bloody situations, and predictable; not all players realize the horn has sounded. So the bloody killings go on a bit, after the attack has been blown off ...

    - The third part is you gathering for the march home. Now we slow down the tempo to a crawl; giving the characters/players ample time to consider what they have done. Describing how they avoid the eyes of each other, how some vomit in the snow, and how some sit and stare at bloody hands, making no move to wash it off. We let one soldier be an coldhearted idiot, asking the characters how many they killed ... bragging about killing 23 adults and 9 children himself. No one touches him; all have the same blood on their hands. And we keep that mood throughout the march home. Setting scenes with fellow warriors despairing over their own guilt, hanging themselves, crying, trying to put a brave face on it, etc. This dreary march is mirrored by the brave march they made at the start of the scenario.

    - The fourth and last part, is you coming home to your own village. You are received as heroes. Your younger brother wants you to tell how many you killed. Your father thumps your shoulder, and your mother wants to hug you (but she looks like a woman you killed, so you involuntarily steps back from her, eyes wide). And a great feast is organized, to celebrate the heroes and the victory. You all have to participate in it, listening to speeches, getting heroes gifts, being looked up to ... and all the time there are details reminding you of the village you burned and brutalized.

We GMs came out of it with full honors, but feeling dirty and evil and mean, by having put our players trough so much shit. I've never played a scenario that made me feel I should apologize to the players afterwards, for being a good GM.

The scenario was a well orchestrated attack on all their moral self, putting them in the unforgiving shoes of a soldier in the chaos of battle, having them, by their own hasty choices, perpetrating acts of brutality that went against all they believed in, and against what they really wanted to be.     Here's what's clicking for me:
    The core conceit of all roleplaying games is pretending, playing as-if. It is, in its most basic meaning, play. But there has to be a point to it, some payoff (just pretending is aimless). The payoff is different for different games. I'd suggest there are a handful of types of payoff, and different people, different groups enjoy one, some or all of them. Coming into a game you expect such payoff, consciously or not. It's what we find satisfying when sitting down and playing.

    When you don't get that payoff, whether you've consciously identified it or not, the game is boooring (yes I'm using that as a term).

    In the sort of game Thomas describes (norwegian-style, jeep?), pre-plotting definitely serves a certain kind of satisfying payoff. But it excludes others. Like, if I came into that game expecting the payoff of a tactical challenge, that would suck for me. You need to get in tune with what the game is offering to enjoy it. Similarly if Thomas and his team of GMs actually allowed a chance of the players, I dunno, deserting, or rebelling and turning away, then the whole scenario would have been ruined, right?

    I think the payoff of Thomas' game and, for example, Dogs (or perhaps more accurately, Poison'd) is very similar, at least superficially - they are both concerned with brutal, troubling imagery, violence, emotional gut-punching, both seek a focused narrative. But the war scenario is concerned with giving the players an experience, quite literally, of doing terrible things and leaving them with that where Poison'd is (I believe) concerned with letting players decide what to do, and then showing them the consequences. I think those are very different payoffs.

    So I think the thing to do is:
    -identify and clearly communicate the kind of payoff your game offers
    -figure out which parts of the game will bear pre-planning and which won't, to allow that payoff

    Going back to D&D - to provide appropriate dramatic elements, the DM has to plan story arcs, but the payoff of play is in the open-ended combats. As long as the DM's story only serves as the frame for the players' strategic and tactical decisions, that enriches the experience while supporting the payoff of tactical play. But when the DM's plots begin to intrude upon the player's decisions (like pre-determining the outcome of a combat because "that's what the story demands") then the tactical play payoff is killed. You can totally begin to drift the game in the direction of a different payoff, and turn that game into something like Thomas' scenario! But you've lost a different kind of payoff.

    So, as a bottom line so far, I'd say pre-plotting (I actually prefer pre-deciding or pre-determining), is definitely a technique that can be applied to support (or block! it's neutral) different payoffs of play.

    Identifying the specific payoffs of pre-play determination is harder, because it's reliant on actual play examples. But I'm pretty sure that the main payoff that pre-determining stuff can provide is the accuracy, or correctness of the experience (much as I said in my first post). Even more specifically, I think this implies an overall passivity on part of the player, who is being led through a dream like a somnambulist, being-john-malkovich-ed, put-in-a-VR, sensorium-stoned, immersed, walked-in-another's-shoes, body-swapped, is getting a transfusion (bleed) from the fictional persona. I don't think we've developed a functional terminology for this yet.

      When the tempo is on, you have no problem coming up with new situations, so you may go on spreading blood and gore on the table for a period that may seem indefinite to the players (as you do this, players really loose their sense of time).

      As the dreadful slaughtering goes on, the GM asks the players to help frame situations too;
      - You stand by a burning house; what do you see? Is anyone attacking you? How many?
      - What is happening to you, right now? Where are you? Tell us what you see!
      - The two of you is doing something! What?

      3 ESSENTIAL TECHNIQUES

      --> Killing tempo
      The battle is done in ferocious tempo. The list helps you gear up to really fast tempo, so no one can relax between situations they take part in. We end encounters by simply turning to new players, starting new encounters. This keeps all players on their toes, ready to go. The players get pumped up, with adrenaline, by the tempo. And that in turn, keeps the battle alive and acute. We do this to mirror the crazy tempo and chaos of a real fight in the night, and to mirror the panic of humans in such conditions.

      --> Postponed reflections
      The overall effect is that each and every player make a lot of choices, in the heat of the moment, and none of them gets to ponder anything very much (except for the pause-situation, which is thrown in for contrast). The full weight of the choices is left for them to ponder in the aftermath, when the tempo is dropped to a slow pondering crawl. And the painful emotional implications will begin to show when they return home, to families/friends/lovers, and a heroes welcome ...

      --> Using instinctive reactions
      The players instinctively react to the shouting and gesticulating GM, building up adrenaline, and thus they share in the fear and aggression of the characters. This is done to mirror the adrenaline of real soldiers. The technique is simple, and effective.

      --> Bring the character to the crux
      No character dies in these encounters. If a player rolls abysmal, we let the character have a scar or some bad trait for it. We do not explain how they survive. They do. Miraculously or shamefully. The characters are important vehicles for the aftermath. Without characters living through the aftermath, feeling the pain and shame, this battle is meaningless (in the context of the narrative, that is).

      \n There has got to be a better way to develop this science.
      We've gotten really good at playing a game rich in detail by making up the fiction on the fly. But we're forgetting or skewing a lot of the potential for pre-planned, prepped, or pre-plotted games. Railroad has become a four letter word.

      I love a pre-planned game. I'm fine with outcomes that are inevitable, even when i know what they will be (though i'd prefer to find out). I see the strengths of planning a game giving you richer detail and a stronger GM control, and even more than that. But it's still being associated with a person manipulating the people around them. And so Illusionism becomes a new four letter word.

      You don't prep a game, or it's plot points, in order to manipulate people. You don't prep a game to impress your crowd. You do it as a gift to the people playing your game. Prep is bad when it's manipulation. It's good when it's the act of crafting something for people, like a gift. Bad prep is showing off and not letting people participate. Good prep is creating something heavily tied to what your players love and want to see. It's the art of building sockets into the game they're playing that they can plug into. A good designer-prepped game system will build generic sockets for people to plug into - and those can be all you need. But if you're good at prepping a game you'll do more than just uncover the game-designed sockets. You'll build your own. They will be shaped perfectly for the friend you've got sitting at your table. And that's what will yield the real "Ooohs" and "Aaaahs." That's when the big reveal isn't just a railroad, it's a friggin rocket ship going straight where they want it to go.

      There's a lot of discussion about this in theater improvisation, and i wish it was talked about more here. About how to take a cue from someone, how to build with someone instead of blocking them, how to understand and communicate in a way that doesn't have to be taken out of the fiction and explained. A good pre-prepped game is going to work well if the GM is building something for their friends, but it works even better if the players are looking for the big reveal, if everyone is cooperating. It takes trust. It takes focus on what the other people around you are interested in. It takes the willingness to just communicate what you want to see.

      There's gotta be a better way to do it.

      There's this other medium, called interactive fiction, that, like RPG, started out as a form meant purely for disposable, poorly respected entertainment, and has, in the past couple of decades, had a community that has striven more for artistic merit.       \n I use this technique on a fairly regular basis. I'll give two examples from my convention games and then offer what good I think comes of each.

      [Background that may be helpful as you read the text below: I run all of my adventures as if they were movies. We talk about camera angles, voice-overs, cut scenes, the whole nine yards. One of the systems that I currently use is Hollow Earth Expedition (HEX). HEX characters have something called Style Points, which can be used to augment die rolls, reduce damage etc.]

      Example the First: From my HEX scenario for this year. At the start of the session, I explain to the players that this adventure focuses on action, a la an Indiana Jones movie. Their job is to keep the audience on the edge of their seats. I then open the adventure with bad guys stealing a crate of something from a university. The PCs see the bad guys from a distance as the bad guys load the crate into a truck and start to drive off. My intent is to have an exciting car chase with lots of over-the-top stunts.

      About half the time, it doesn't occur to the players to chase after the truck. Some will react as if it's a real-world situation ("I write down the license plate number"). Some will attempt to bring the action to an abrupt halt ("I shoot out the truck's tires"). In those cases, I usually say, "Rewind. Remember two minutes ago when I told you to make an action movie? A truck is driving away with stolen property? What would Indiana Jones do?" That's always been enough to make the lightbulb go on. I've never had to tell players, "Chase the truck!" Although I would tell them that, if I had to.

      The Good That Follows: I want an opening scene that shows off what the game system can do and sets the tone of my movie. That's my job as the GM of a convention game. I've planned events within the car chase that will give the players opportunities to be creative and will give them something to react to. This scene will also let them discover how I apply the rules to over-the-top action, because no two GMs use the rules of a game in exactly the same way.

      But here's where the real GM fiat comes in. As soon as the car chase starts, I say: "Here are the rules for the car chase: (1) The bad guys are going to get away with the crate. That's just the way it is. (2) Pretend you don't know that. Make the car chase as exciting as you possibly can. If you come up with an ingenious idea that should stop the truck from getting away, you'll also need to show the audience why that idea fails. Any of the resources that you spend this scene (Style Points, ammunition, whatever) will be returned to you at the end of the scene -- plus additional Style Points, the number of which will depend upon how exciting a scene you make this. (3) The car chase will last exactly as long as you want it to. When you're ready to move onto something else, the bad guys will get away."

      Most of my players have played out the car chase scene for about an hour and a half. It's almost half of my adventure.

      In case it's not obvious, the second half of the adventure is the recovery of the crate. The reason the bad guys' escape is by fiat is so there's something to recover. Happens all the time in action movies.

      The Good That Follows: Once those rules sink in, my players have a lot of fun with the car chase. Because they don't have to think about tactics (since the bad guys will get away anyway), they focus on making the scene fun and exciting. Some players will come up with stunts that hinder the PCs, thereby helping to explain why the bad guys were able to get away. I reward those players with extra Style Points, of course.

      Example the Second: This is from the Unknown Armies adventure that jenskot mentioned above (which I've permanently retired, by the way). In his character write-up, one of the players is told that his character brings several board games to the first scene. One of the games is Ouija. The player is instructed to ensure that the Ouija game is offered for play.

      As soon as the player has his character bring out the Ouija board, I step in as director of the movie and explain to the players that the Ouija game is the Maguffin -- it has to be played. Not all of the PCs have to participate in the game, but none of them can stop it from being played. A character can try to stop it, but that player has to come up with a reason why the effort fails. This discussion is necessary because one of the PCs has a very superstitious mother, and the player of that PC might feel that it would be in character for her to prevent the game from taking place.

      The Good That Follows: The adventure is about a haunting. The Ouija game is the primary factor that starts the haunting. It's required in order to keep the storyline tight and to avoid plot holes. The only benefit the players get from this particular GM fiat is that the adventure makes sense. A tight storyline is hugely important to me.

      That's all I've got for now.

      Posted By: Deliveratorwhat OSR and Trad have in common is a resistance to overt/explicit structuring of narratives.
      I'm not sure what "OSR" stands for (old school role-play; but what sets that apart from Trad?), but I am quite certain that classical role-playing games don't have an inherent resistance to techniques that structures the narrative (I term games with GM "classical", them being "trad" or whatnot).

      Classical role-playing games have a great field of dialogue techniques to explore, largely untapped or in unconscious use in todays game-practice. These techniques may be explored and developed further at the courtesy of skilled game-smiths and inventive GMs. There is nothing stopping you from having more tools at hand when playing a classical role-playing game, the tools that focus on the dialogue between players, aka; the psycho-social field where we actually create the fiction * of our games.

      * A strong and engaging fiction is the final goal for any role-playing game. "The fiction" being our self-made narrative arc, of course, and then some more; it is the small tidbits too, that gives us some experience throughout the game; every small scene, every character involvement and dialogue, every mood-swing related to the character and/or happenings of the game. "The fiction" of a role-playing game is the great total sum of our game-experience. It is both something we share, as a product of our interaction, and something we produce internally, as our imagination and emotions are spurred by our interaction.

      I've made a simple 3-step model to get to grips with this:
      1 - the designer gives us a vision, and a method to go with it ...
      2 - that we the players use in our exploratory, dialogue-based interaction ...
      3 - to create a fiction (that may be an expression/exploration of the initial vision).

      I find this way of thinking, simple and stringent as it is, quite liberating. It clarifies the fact that we have a lot of leeway in how we organize and play our games, and says very clearly what our gaming aims at. We may whisper ourselves towards that high goal, or talk ourselves into it, or scream our heads of in our quest to get there. The tools at our command are ours to make, and is as versatile as anything we may invent.

      The interesting thing is that a lot of GMs place themselves in a middle position in this model. They are visionary designers in their pre-planning of a game-session, going further than the game itself supports (tweaking, inventing setting-elements and new methodical tools), and then they are interactive players in the execution of the scenario.

      A lot of GMs do a lot of work to make a lot of players have a swell time. They are the brave and silent (mostly) artists of the modern age!

      \n This doesn't quite count as "GM pre-play," but it may come close enough for this discussion.

      I mentioned earlier that I'm currently running Hollow Earth Expedition, and that HEX has Style Points that players can spend to benefit their characters.

      When I'm explaining the rules at the start of the session, I tell players the different in-game benefits that they can buy with Style Points. I then tell them the four ways they can earn Style Points. Ways 1 through 3 are basically rewards for a player's making the kind of movie I want us to make.

      I explain that Way 4 is different. It's not initiated by the player. "The fourth way to earn Style Points starts with me saying something like this: 'Ann, I'm giving you two Style Points for what I'm about to do to your character.' " The players usually laugh at this point. "Whatever I say after that cannot be avoided. Trust me that whatever it is, its sole purpose is to make a better movie."

      I've used this technique to have characters get caught in traps, get knocked out, fall down holes, have their weapons jam, fall victims to mind control, all sorts of stuff. Most, but not all, of these obstacles are made up on the fly. (That's why I'm not sure this belongs in the "GM pre-play" conversation, because they're rarely planned.) To the best of my recollection, I've never had a player complain about any of them.
