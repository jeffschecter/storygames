    \n Okay, this is a weird-ass perceptual thing. Or, it is to me, at least.

    In a serious majority of RPGs, every character has some "pool" that either gets added to or drained away. When manipulating that pool is capable of causing catastrophic character change (like, say, death), those are hitpoints. Sometimes a bit of mental effort is needed to slot them in, but that's cool.

    A couple of reasons I think this matters:

    Hitpoints are easy to grasp.
    When a game does not have something that can be thought of in this way, and puts the potential for catastrophic change into a totally different deal, it's being counter-intuitive. Which isn't necessarily bad, but if it's going to be understood and used, it needs to 'splain it really, really well. To me, one of the things that works smoothly in Dogs is that while it fallout generally acts as a different deal, a reader can still think "hitpoints" as a placeholder and carry on.

    Hitpoints are points.
    And, as such, are manipulable. There's all these cool things that can be done with points, and Dogs does a whole bunch of them. Delaying the "damage" rolls until a pause in, or the end of, the fight (what happens if you stuff "pauses" into D&D combat, those moments where there's a natural break in combat, and only roll damage at those points?). Creating different ways to regain / get rid of them (Choices! What if I had a list of scars available in a dungeon crawl, and could kill damage from a hit by taking one?).

    So... Fallout is hit points. Or, if you like - Fallout is an example of what Hit Points can become when fine-tuned.

    \n Hit points are a "health" mechanics, where you start with some stuff, and losing it causes consequences.

    Dice in Dogs are like that, but the consequence of losing them is losing the conflict, not taking fallout.

    Rolling for fallout is a "damage" mechanic, where you start with none, and the more you get the worse the consequences. This is like taking damage in Burning Wheel, or Consequences in Covenant, or whatever other stuff in games. This is the opposite of HP. You don't start with a number that goes down and causes consequences, you accumulate wounds or whatever that cause consequences.

    The actual effects of that fallout are consequences like dying or falling unconscious, or suffering a temporary insanity when you lose 5+ Sanity in one roll. But suffering a fallout effect is not the same as either taking a number of "damage" or losing a number from your "health."

    \n Just to make a REAL hash of it: what if a game's character Attributes are levels of effectiveness AND measures of player freedom to act? (*ahem* GLASS)

    So, say I have Health, Alacrity, and Psyche. Each are a class of "hit point," but they are ALSO triggers to "fallout" AND measures of effectiveness. My Health 4 lets me pass Health Threshold Tests up to level 4; it also determines when my character becomes unconscious (Health 0) and, thus, when I am forced to portray that--pretty straight-forward.

    But Alacrity 0 is different: I can't use Tiring Abilities nor can I run or jump ("Drained") nor can I pass any Alacrity Threshold Tests. But I'm not "out," just suffering from some (temporary) "fallout."

    And at Psyche 0, I must either (a) behave as a Catatonic, if I did it to myself, or (b) portray an extreme Phobia of the entity/object which did it to me, both until Restored to at least 1 Psyche (with encouragement to RP lingering effects until full normal value). But in addition to that, Psyche determines how long I am at risk of death, if I Commit to a conflict: One of the main Toggles in the system is what happens at Unconscious. Either you become Killable by anyone who is willing to take the time (World Priority); or you can only die if you Commit--which lets you regain Consciousness and restores you to full normal Health--and then are again knocked to Health 0 within (15 - your current Psyche value) minutes (Player Priority).

    Lots of data dump there, but it sums simply: sometimes, "hit points" only dictate behavior, not viability or participation; and, further, they can be directly coupled to efficacy at "skill-like" activities (which sort of makes sense: if I am knocked down to 1 Health, why would I still be able to Bend Bars and Lift gates?). Going to 0 "(Alacrity or) Psyche Hit Points" gives me a significant "(Drained or) Insanity" temporary trait. Even Unconscious is a sort of pseudo-trait, not "Dead" like in D&D (which is only Mostly Dead, as we all know).

    Thus, there can be a complete overlap between d20-like "HP" and DitV-like "Fallout" inherent in the system: neither one is necessarily a sub- or superset of the other.
