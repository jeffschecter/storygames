\n Our gang played DitV for the first time this week. None of us have played enough to understand the reasons behind some of the rules. We did a good job deciphering most of the rules and enjoyed them alot... Our question:

WHY are traits with d4s bad?

In character creation it explains that most of the time d4 traits are negative traits for the PC, suggesting that most opponents will be rolling higher die values and your d4 would be at disadvantage... This makes sense to some degree. However in any conflict you can STILL add that d4 to your pool when it comes into play. And (as far as I understand) you can bring in multiple traits in conflicts. So even if you did bring in that d4 you're STILL getting another die.

Why is this a negative??


In losing a conflict you recieve "fallout" adding a new trait with a d4 and others seem like benefits rather than negatives.

What are we missing??
anyone?

    \n It's not "bad" per se, as it'll help you win conflicts. Any extra die is going to help you there. However, having d4 traits will make it difficult to see with two dice. Since the most common die is a D6, you'll often need to use three or even more dice to meet a raise when you're using d4s. That means you're taking the blow. So while you're still winning the conflict (if you didn't have the d4, you might have to give), you're taking fallout because of it (if you had a d6, chances are you could meet the raise with only two dice).

    So the d4s are not bad, but will create trouble (=fallout) for you. Does that make sense?

    No, seriously ... they're totally not bad. They're good. They're more dice. What's more, you can keep taking fallout, and ramp up to (like) 6d4 Relationship with Fellow Dog Brother Ezekiel because you guys just hate each other's guts that much ... then with one experience check you can change the die size, and have a 6d10 Relationship with Fellow Dog Brother Ezekiel, because you guys worked it out. There's a saying: "In Dogs, friendships are formed over the barrel of a gun."

    Taking fallout from people talking at you hard, that's just a general all around good thing ... it means you're listening. It means, quite literally, that you've taken one of their points and said "Okay, yeah ... I can't just shrug that off. That moves me."

    What you don't want is that gun-fightin' fallout. That's downright unhealthy for a person. You can get yourself a serious case of lead poisoning.

    \n Kevin, cool. I asked because my advice is different to players and GMs.

    My advice to GMs is, you gotta find raises where they really don't want to take the blow. That's when those d4s create hard choices - "oh crap he raised 'he shoots her dead.' If I take that blow, she's shot dead. Crap. If I give, she's not, but then I won't get the stakes. Crap. I wish I had an 8 showing but all I got's these 2s and 3s!"

    My advice to players is, yep, there's no downside. Take all the d4 traits you want. Each d4 is an opportunity to make a choice, where without it you wouldn't get the opportunity.

    Look out for what John (jenskot) said, though. Don't be easy taking d8 and d10 fallout as though it were d4 and d6.
