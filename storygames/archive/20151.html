\n In the April 'Stuff to Watch' thread, @Coalhada talked about laid-back gaming.

I've defined that as a style of role-playing that:
- encourages scenes that don't immediately leap to crises or conflicts
- takes its time exploring characters, moods and ideas
- takes its time to explore the scene, and doesn't use aggressive scene framing to either start or finish a scene

I know about a couple of games that are trying to capture this vibe: Left Coast and Until We Sink. @Coalhada also said:\n
I'm working on a game that's at one point described as "a sunny afternoon, sitting outside a cafe watching passersby kind of game"\ufeff, and Graham Walmsley has talked about "slow gaming" in reference to The Tavern.
\nFrom what I've heard, Golden Sky Stories and Ryuutama might also be in this zone. And Still Life by Wendy Gorman, David Hertz, and Heather Silsbee (from the Golden Cobra challenge) could be another example.

So, what techniques are games using to create that vibe?

In Left Coast (payhip link), I'm using two specific techniques. The first is to figure out what scene is next by letting the group decide who they want the protagonist to hang out with. Everyone names a supporting character (people can choose the same person), and the protagonist's player selects someone from that list. That gives the game a very natural, drifting feel.

The second technique is how to end a scene. @Abstract_Machine summed it up pretty well in their review:\n
The other great piece of the system is the double-knock on the table to end scenes. ... Left Coast encourages you to ramble on a bit. ... If one person knocks, they think the scene should end. The moment the second person knocks, the scene is over.

It\u2019s the best of both worlds: a collective edit with creative freedom for each and every player. Aggressive knocking could, of course, occur \u2013 but at least it\u2019s negotiable without breaking the flow of the story.
\nI took that technique from Until We Sink, a (I don't know the technical term) 'contained LARP' set on the patio of a tropical resort on an island that is slowly sinking into the sea. The game has 5-7 scenes, each of which take place at the end of successive days. I love this game, I love the conversations it produces. It ends scenes using this process:\n
Every evening, conversation ends as soon as two characters have left the patio. You can declare that your character is leaving at any time (you yourself may remain in silence, listening to the others). Usually another character will leave at the same time or shortly after, so no one has to wait for very long.
\n@Coalhada, what about your game (is it Malandros?)

Anyone else got examples of games that are doing this?

Edited to add the games that have been cited in this thread: Golden Sky Stories, Chuubo's Marvellous Wish-Granting Engine,\tSpione (manuevers), Imagine, Okult, The Shadow of Yesterday (refreshment scenes), The Tavern, Burning Empires (Color scenes), Ribbon Drive\t

    \n My Golden Challenge contribution Imagine.

    It's built upon improv techniques, uncommon use of uncertainty (where kishotenketsu is one of them), and my interpretation of how basic human interaction works. The structure of play is easy to learn but there are heavy unspoken and underlying principles that comes forth in play through these structures.

    The game creates a shared atmosphere by letting the participants explore memories and hopes for ordinary fictive people in a mundane and contemporary world. It has statements to get everyone on the same page. "Be boring", "say ordinary things", and "no conflict" are a few examples. Scenes starts with hand gestures, and ends when all but one leans backwards.

    \n Lots of good stuff here, to dig into. I also will have to pop back later, but I just wanted to say to @kksimons: I'm a New Zealander (we're basically the Canadians of the South Pacific). In every game I playtested here, the two-knock rule seemed to work fine and didn't set off our 'Am I being rude?' alarms.

    The first knock is usually just a signal that it feels like we might be approaching the end. After that, the group is usually more invested in the details of the scene. They become more aware they need to 'achieve' anything they want to do in the scene, and they start collectively looking out for a good place to end it.

    That time between the first and second knock is usually filled with pretty awesome or fun moments.

    By the time the second knock happens, it's usually with group approval and a sense from everybody that it's time to end the scene. It's also not uncommon to have people say to the rest of the group, "I want to knock here. Is that going to be okay with everyone?"

    \n The two-knock rule sounds pretty good to me.

    I've used a similar rule in my game Land of Nodd: in any given scene, every uninvolved player has a single Token at stake, which she places in the middle of the table.

    Any player can withdraw their stake at any time. When the last one is withdrawn, the scene ends. There are usually only 1-3 uninvolved players, so, in practice, it's similar to the "knocking" rule.

    In unWritten, a player actually got a benefit (some kind of point/token) for ending someone else's scene, and it was called "buttoning" (a film-maker's jargon, I believe - the ability to spot a good dramatic spot to end a scene and cut to something else).

    Cutting a scene is a great way to emphasize a decision, bit of foreshadowing, or, more typically, a particularly powerful line of dialogue.

    (However, it's also arguably quite the opposite of "laid-back gaming"!)

    \n Much of the laid-back content in Malandros is advice rather than rules, such as "short scenes are OK", encouraging the GM to err on the side of addressing the PCs' quotidian lives rather than their goals - which are for the players to focus on, if they choose - and so on.
    That said, there are a couple of concrete mechanisms there to encourage a more relaxed pace.

    One is, in fact, refreshment scenes.Malandros uses pools of skill points that you spend to add to die rolls, and you refresh them by calling a scene in which your character relaxes, practises that skill, has dinner with someone, etc.
    (I always liked the refreshment rules in The Dying Earth, where different styles of each skill refresh through different activities. Malandros isn't quite as strict about this, but you do have to tie the nature of the refreshment scene to the ability in question.)

    This ties in with another rule: you are not allowed to skip your turn to call a scene.
    In Malandros, everyone at the table gets to call an equal number of scenes.
    What I found in playtesting was that some people become very anxious about "wasting" their scene - and when PCs start to get on a serious project, or PvP play emerges, the scene economy can become quite sharp-edged.
    At first I did allow people to skip a turn or to say "come back to me later", but the side-effect of this is that players almost exclusively call scenes when they have something they want to do. The story becomes a cavalcade of people relentlessly accomplishing stuff.
    Whereas the question to the scene-caller in Malandros is not so much "what do you want to do next?" as "what is your character up to now?"

    Oddly enough, knowing that you can't have your go later seems to ease the pressure on the scene-caller somewhat. I think it's because where before it would be OK for someone to say "hey, can we do the scene where I try to get out of going to my dad's birthday party and we'll come back to your scene later?", everyone now knows that no, you can't. So the question doesn't arise.

    It helps that you'll usually have the fallback of a refreshment scene.


    Finally, and with thanks to Rickard's Imagine for the inspiration, taking your time is also a rule. Specifically, if you draw a blank when it's your turn to call the scene, lean back and take a moment to think. No one else is allowed to offer advice or suggestions until you ask.

    \n On double-knocking: someone has to cut the scenes anyway. To me, a double-knocking technique is a more democratic formalized way to do this: it's effectively a non-intrusive negotiation, and I'd expect it to be easier on everybody's feelings compared to most other ways to do it I know of ("one specific player has to end scenes" or "any player can choose to end a scene", with the more laid-back alternative being "unstructured consensus that the scene has served its purpose").

    In my group - which I would promptly describe as "laid back" when compared to the style of play I can infer as the default from most American rulebooks - we're currently playing Polaris, specifically in the Thou Art But a Warrior setting. By the rules as written, either Heart of Infidel (Mistaken) can end a scene by uttering "And so it was." But in our group, I noticed that "And so it was\u2026?" is usually proffered in an interrogative tone, waiting for the other player to nod and confirm: "\u2026And so it was." This is no deliberate rules-hack on our part, it just came up spontaneously and - though we do from time to time remind each other that's not how it's supposed to work - we keep falling back into the habit. Since the habit is not game-disruptive at any rate (that we can perceive), we don't fight it hard, but rather let it be. It is, to us, the most natural and logical way that ritual phrase should work -- because we're more used, from a number of games we played together, to ending scenes by consensus.

    \n So, I guess I'll cover Golden Sky Stories, since I have a much more solid play experience with that than with Chuubo's. There's a few things that contribute to GSS' pastoral tone, and I'll try and parse them out.

    The Setup
    From the getgo, GSS tells you to create situations that don't rely on conflict. As in, that's how it outlines scenario creation. You're supposed to keep heartwarming ideas, not conflict-filled ideas, and the game strictly tells you not to build scenarios with antagonists. I know it's not technically mechanics yet, but this actually goes a long way towards creating that feeling. It upends a lot of preconceptions that GMs have about scenario creation. How do you build a story without an antagonist?

    The "Dreams" Mechanic
    Okay, so Dreams are fanmail. That's pretty straightforward. They're not the core of the tone engine, but they really grease the wheels. The book says "hand a dream when someone else does something sweet or cute". Sounds a little hokey, but it really works as a reinforcer of the mood.

    They directly work with your characters' relationships. Your character's relationships to other people give one type of resource, other people's relationships to your character give another type of resource, and you increase relationships by investing Dreams.

    The Powers
    In my opinion, this is where everything comes from. The setup provides context, the Dreams grease the wheels, but it's the characters' powers that really make everything work. For the one part, characters don't have dice involved; they can do pretty much whatever they want by spending enough Feelings. But that's not the big part. No, the big part is the cool powers that the players get for their characters.

    Obviously, none of them are combat-oriented; they're all cool, weird stuff that acts as utility magic...but none of it has obvious uses. So to use your magic effectively, you need to figure out creative ways to apply it to your problems. Or you can just do cool stuff with it. And that, I think, is the lynchpin of GSS: you have to spend your time ferreting out the details of a situation, then figure out neat things to do with your magic.

    If you have a little time to spare, I perpetually link people to my convention one-shot game, which may be the absolute best roleplaying experience I've ever had.

    Avoid introducing conflict. In Imagine, that's to help merge different situations together. Is that hard to stick to, when you're playing. Does the group help reinforce it?
    \nIt's repeated on several places in the game text. It's one of the things the person who explains the game will read out loud, and it's also a thing that the participants will read (in the "Knowing One Another" part - Yes, I actually made a rule that had a side effect that the players read through parts of the game).

    It's also reinforced through the kishotenketsu structure of how the story is going to be told. I only played the game once, and the main thing is that Imagine builds an atmosphere for the whole group, and that can't be captured in text. But I will write a summary of what happened. This session took about 40-60 minutes.

    FIRST SITUATION
    Present time: a woman on a bike on the country side.
    switch to Memory: an empty house on the country side. Dust everywhere.
    switch to Hope: "Congratulations, you got twins." But where is the father?
    switch to Present: "Here are the keys to the house".

    SECOND SITUATION
    Present time: a man in an empty apartment wanting the phone to ring. Walking out of the apartment is a huge thing.
    switch to Memory: a car crash. Wife and daughter killed.
    switch to Hope: the man taking a cup of coffee at a cafe.
    switch to Hope: an android app made.
    switch to Present: I will leave. I'm going now.

    THIRD SITUATION (bringing the people and their hopes together)
    Present time: the man and the woman waiting for a job interview. Starts to talk. The man is looking through his drawn images in a portfolio.
    switch to Hope: the man is working on his game about his life. He's late for the birth of their twins.
    Present time: more small talk and thoughts.
    switch to Hope: the two are getting a cup of coffee.
    Present time: the man is coming out from the office. The interview is over. Small talk, resulting in a date over a cup of coffee.
