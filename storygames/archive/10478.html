    \n I'm really contemplating how to model the fiction of The Wire the television series. A deep theme throughout the series is how institutions affect those involved.

    I thought about hacking Dust Devils but the "Devil" concept is too internal and not exactly what I'm looking for. I'm contemplating using a hacked version of Lacuna's Static where certain actions create "static" which aggravates your superiors. At certain points things happen (like on your custom Static chart in Lacuna) as command responds and they apply pressure to you. How to relieve that pressure? Put dope on the table and arrest suspects. That intense struggle between satisfying your superiors and hanging on to the case long enough to make it a true career case is what I'm after.

    \n My game Drift deals with a very similar dynamic in the (veiled) context of colonialism and losing your native culture. In Drift, you are one of the best and brightest of your world, and are recruited as new blood for a Circuit Ship, which travels a series of worlds, bringing technology and governance at sublight speeds. As a result, by the time you return to your home an entire generation will have gone by.

    In Drift, you have Truths, things you know and rely on - like Virtue: Honesty or Sin: Pleasure. These are also your abilities, which help you perform on missions. But when you don't have an appropriate Truth to what you are doing, you start a process which requires replacing one of your Truths. This is where the institution comes in, this new Truth must come from the Circuit Ship's own culture (in Drift it can also come from the world you are currently on).

    For the Wire, I suggest having a similar clock - which gets incremented by failure, can be increased by pushing for success, or by calling upon the resources of the institution involved. And at each long scale increment, some unique part of the character becomes replaced by a trait of the institution.

    Posted By: wyrmwoodFor the Wire, I suggest having a similar clock - which gets incremented by failure...
    \nFor some (I'd say most) types of institutions, I think it is just time that increments the clock. Failure might accelerate it, but most institutions, particularly those run on lots of hierarchy, work on a notion of "how long will the superior let you muck around before he has to get involved to save his own ass?". For a while, it is sufficient for the chief to know that officers Dick and Jane are out there working the Spot case. If they don't have results by some time, however, then he has to step in and exert more control over them.

    This time is also not constant. If the mayor is all over the chief's ass about the Spot case, he gives Dick and Jane a lot less leash. In other words, it matters how much interest the institution has in the outcome.

    Lastly, maybe the only reason the mayor cares about the Spot case is that the public (or just the press) is in an uproar about it. In other words, other institutions have the ability to alter how much interest the player's institution has in the outcome.

    \n I'm deliberately doing this in Quincunx, and used an earlier version of it in Rajah Spiny Rat.

    A character only improves through the course of the game by facing complications derived from the traits they identify with. During scene framing there is a 50/50 chance that the player will be able to choose a complication relevant to their character, otherwise one of the players to either side of them chooses a complication from the character's sheet and introduces it into the scene.

    Players may choose to accept the complication associated with their trait, thus gaining strength over that trait (and potentially better powers), or they may deny this aspect of their personality (and thus lose some of the benefits associated with this trait).

    Traits may link to virtues, vices, paradigms, occupations, mystic powers, hobbies, virtually anything...as long as their is something that might cause issues for the character and something that might provide them a benefit, it has the potential to be used in this fashion.

    The concept here is all about dealing with the outside world and identifying oneself in context with the events that happen beyond.

    Of course, a series of educational sessions run at Gencon Oz over the weekend have meant that I'll be making some dramatic changes to the Quincunx rules-set. The concept described above will remain intact as it worked quite well, but quite a few other changes will have to occur around this concept.
