\n Thought it might be an idea to gauge interest in some of my games-in-progress. Please have a dekkho at the list, dudes and dudesses, and see which ones (if any) take your fancy. I can't guarantee to speed up work on any of them unless interest is very high, but it'll give me an idea of the direction I need to be travelling in over the next 1-2 years or so.

We The Jury, about a group of jurors unable to reach a verdict, with various of them having their own agendas. It'll be not so much like the Henry Fonda original, but the more recent film on the same theme.

A is for Anarchist, about a group of anarchists/terrorists holed up in their hideout in the woods/mountains/an East London squat, with tensions mounting and tempers fraying, and the Police/Feds closing in. There'd be a spy, an emotionless intellectual, a fanatic, a guy who joined the group just for kicks, a spoilt rich girl etc.

Disgraceful is a game set in the 1920s which explores the conflict between ambition and morality at a time when seemingly anything was acceptable (\u2018Anything Goes\u2019, to quote the song title). Players have to negotiate the moral maze of 1920s Hollywood, and succeed in their ambitions without falling prey to the many temptations the city has to offer. Link to outline concept here.

Chaotic Evil is a hectic, fast-paced game about ever so slightly incompetent apprentice demons, with a high level of PvP (and GMvP) as the characters attempt to sow havoc without either messing up and being hauled over the coals (literally) by the Boss (trust me, you don\u2019t want that) or having their mischief foiled by the other players. It\u2019s a high stakes game where the loser loses everything, and the winner doesn\u2019t lose quite so much. Link to outline concept here.

Great Minds is a game about scientific geniuses who can solve the mysteries of the Universe but between them can't boil an egg. Link to concept doodle here.

MotorMouth is a game about automobile salesmen in late 1950s/early 1960s America, a time when people had 'never had it so good', jobs were secure and plentiful, and there were customers waiting to buy the latest model as it rolled off the production line. Except that behind the picket fences the reps' lives are slowly imploding, with marriages under strain or on the rocks, reps sleeping with each other's wives and/or stealing each other's customers, their wives on amphetamines, their teenage daughters getting pregnant and having abortions, and their sons modelling themselves on James Dean, loafing around in leather jackets and crashing their Dads' cars in illegal drag races.

Influences:
Tin Men
Mad Men and
Rebel Without a Cause.

No name 1, a game about a secret Government unit set up to exploit trans-dimensional travel (vaguely similar to Sliders), and catch the villains who use it for their own nefarious purposes.

No name 2, a black powder game about a land riven by civil war between powerful clans.

No name 3, a game set about 50 years after the galaxy has been taken over by orcs, there being secret resistance cells on Terra and other planets that plot to shake off the orc yoke.

Cheers
Leo

    \n Hmm, none of these grabs me immediately - hopefully that's a valid answer. Most seem like they're entirely valid concepts to investigate, but I'm not seeing any hooks that would immediately grab me. I would also be extremely hesitant of "voting" on the basis of genre familiarity or fandom leanings - not only because I don't have many leanings in that regard at this age, but also because it's a really bad heuristic for you to choose your next project based on whether I happen to be a fan of a given literary genre.

    Maybe you should write up proper pitches, or even design documents, for the ones you actually believe in yourself the most? Chances are that most people, myself included, will get what excites you about a given concept if you write about them in more detail. It is also likely that you'll clarify for yourself which of the projects is most worthwhile if you write about them a bit - basic concepts, rough outline of content, market strategy, artistic and economic goals, schedule, resources, and so on.

    (I already know what I'm going to do when I'm next in your position of having the luxury of choosing my next project: I'll be writing a "brainstorming bible" of all of the prospective projects for our studio, sort of like you've done here, except that mine will be about 5-10 pages about each project, examining their strengths and weaknesses. It'll be a bit of an effort, but once I have that on hand not only have I documented my inspirations in a snapshot useful years later, but I also have something that I can give to others to look at when I want this sort of consultation about whether my projects make any sense. Much easier to evaluate that from a proper project pitch than a one-line elevator pitch.)

    For context, consider how a "design document" precedes game design in video game development; I've found that this is also useful in roleplaying, despite so much of the design and development work being easily unified.

    In addition to the concept of "design document", consider the "sales pitch", which can also be illuminating, and is much less work. For an excellent and timely example, I find that Ron hit it out of the park with his pitch page for Circle of Hands recently - that's the sort of explanation that immediately sells me on a game. Basically, if I can see why I would like to design a game myself from the pitch, then chances are that it's a worthwhile project.

    \nUnfortunately I tried that approach and it didn't work . The games I want to write tend to fall on stony ground, witness the less than rapturous reception that greeted Dead Man Talking, which I still think has potential, but folks aren't interested in it so that's that. So I figured, rather than continue to indulge myself by writing game after game that I want to play, I should do what I've done here and see what games my customers want to play.

    I considered writing a post discussing the difficulties of determining precisely what kinds of games people want, given that 1) tastes are likely to change between polling and producing, and what was popular when you started writing a game may no longer be so by the time it's published, 2) people may not necessarily give the question a great deal of thought, and might mention some genre or theme on a whim when if they thought about it it's probably not what they'd want to play after all, and 3) you are likely to end up with conflicting results, where a third of respondents favour an emotionally-charged story game, another third prefer a quasi-historical mythic Europe game, and the remaining third would like to see a light hearted slapstick-style game, which is kind of what I got when I posed the question on RPGGeek, although on UKRoleplayers the results were much more conclusive.\n

    \n I think that We The Jury sounds like an excellent subject matter for a game (based on that single-sentence description upthread); depending on the treatment and productization it might well be a very, very interesting game! That's the weakness in polling, particularly without proper scientific procedure; you're relying on people more ignorant than yourself, who might not even bother to give the matter any thought. Besides, I have no clue if you'd make the exciting game that I see when I imagine what I might do with the topic; your approach to this topic might be a dud from my viewpoint, I can't know without seeing a full-length pitch

    (The game I'd create of that topic would either be something similar to Zombie Cinema - very small, tight, well-productized - or something like In a Wicked Age - expansively tackling its topic, enticing of the imagination, branching out to embrace the entire legal genre. Both of those have certainly achieved reasonable commercial and critical success, so I'd be quite optimistic about this one as well. Goes to show how differently we can see these sorts of matters.)

    Considering your stated goal of creating something that actually interests people (a worthwhile goal, I think; I myself am frustrated about writing something that nobody cares about as well), have you considered writing and developing for a pre-existing game line or brand? If your own artistic passion is currently at a low ebb, and you'd basically just want to do some craftsmanship on a subject matter that the audience wants, then an existing brand seems like it could ensure attention and make the product development easier as well, as you could rely on others working on the brand to support you.

    \n If your intent is to gauge commercial viability, I doubt that forum polls for interest are a good proxy for customer dollars. I can tell you which of your ideas sounds most fun to me, but that does not equate to me giving you any money. I think requests that involve effort are a better metric -- "playtest this" or "review this manuscript" or "help fund this" etc. Of course your audience wants you to do the work first before they'll truly engage with you -- ideas are plentiful and free. For any of the 9 ideas you listed, why should I assume you can and will pull it off better than I could? I need proof!

    That said, since polls are fun and easy, I will happily respond. I just wanted to throw that caveat out there first.

    Chaotic Evil is the one idea here where I can guess what it might be like to play it. Loss-heavy PvP/GMvP with inept demons sounds fun. I am curious about the flavor of "demon" and the mechanics behind winning and losing. If these were good, I would play it.

I already wrote a game that creates the A is for Anarchist dynamics, and no one ever showed any interest. I think the situation is too specific and limited. Same for We the Jury. The only thing that'd grab me here would be novel takes on how these concepts are explored (probably in the form of innovative rules).

    No name 1 reminds me of the Continuum hunting the Narcissists, and No name 3 reminds me of Season 3 of the recent Battlestar Galactica series. No idea if I'd play either one.

    \n A year on, and I thought this thread merited an update, so here's where we are.

    Unsurprisingly, most of the games mentioned haven't had any work done on them beyond the initial concept doodle, nevertheless it's somewhat disappointing that I've gone beyond that stage with only one of them, to whit Great Minds. However, that's now definitely playable so please give it a spin folks, if you're interested.

    Following last year's Game Chef I now have a rather bizarre, fantasy-based storygame called All That Glitters. I'm actually quite excited about this game, and would really like to recruit playtesters for it. It has a few kinks, and is possibly a wee bit bloated, but I'm sure all the kinks can be ironed out in playtest.

    Where the Heart is I didn't mention as that was even then almost ready to publish - in fact the beta version is scheduled to go up on DTRPG at the end of April. I've also designed two new games since the above post - SkipJack and Dead Man Talking (the latter of which came about as a result of this thread in fact, a gamer buddy having sent me some suggestions for games I should work on) - and I've done concept doodles for a further two, Priceless (which uses Sci-Fi Beta Kappa's d:merit system) and Burn Baby Burn, which arose out of a casual remark in a thread on G+ a couple of days ago.

    All these games are on my indyhippo design lab G+ community. Please join me there, talk about my games, other people's games or your game ideas, or just start a discussion or two.
