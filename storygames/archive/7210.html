The supply and demand of time, money and games

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2008 \n edited August 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Economically, I think it's like this for many role-players:

It used to be that we were kids with little money, and lots of time to waste - we could sit around for hours trying to squeeze a little fun out of incomprehensible rules. And role-playing games were rare and expensive. We'd rarely get new games, and it was a big deal to try out something new. So:
- High demand for new games
- High supply of time
- Low supply of money
In this environment, spending time instead of money on getting your fun was a great deal for a role-player.

Now, we're grown-ups with jobs; we have more money, and less time to waste - we want fun now. And there are lots of good, cheap (even free) games out there. Game publishers are more or less begging for attention. So:
- Low demand for new games
- Low supply of time
- High supply of money
In this environment, spending time instead of money on getting your fun is a terrible deal for a role-player.

It's no surprise that the indie designers who are successful, are those who play their own games a lot with a lot of different people. It makes it very easy for others to try out the game (low time investment). This isn't necessarily positive for the state of RPGs as a whole, since being an active social networking person with single-minded focus on one single game doesn't necessarily mean you make good games.

It's also no surprise that word-of-mouth is so important: People want to know that a game is actually fun before they spend even five minutes looking at the rules. This is positive, as it helps good games get the attention they deserve.

It's no surprise that short games with high "bang-for-buck"-ratio are popular. With little time to spend, people want faster fun.

And, of course, it's no surprise that famous game designers sell more games. It's safer to buy from people who've given you fun in the past, than from unknowns. This might be positive, as it helps good designers keep on designing.

    \n Matthijs, I agree with some of your generalizations, but not all of them. If a game takes relatively little time to go from reading -> playing, the folks I know are all over new games. If I can grok a new system in 15 minutes, I'm sold. If it's more than that, there has to be a big payoff that easily foreseen down the road; otherwise, it's much less likely to happen.

    Posted By: Ben Lehman
    Getting from "bought" to "played" is the largest marketing challenge for an RPG company.
    \nAn excellent point! I run a lot of one-shot games at conventions and Endgame, and countless times I've had players tell me "I bought this and wanted to see how it works before I try running it myself" in addition to all the "I wanted to see what it was like before I bought a copy." I've bought far more games than I've played.

    Paul Tevis' podcast of his Polaris game convinced me to try playing it, although I'd owned a copy for some time. Heck, even Dogs sat on my shelf for six months before I got around to reading it, although I ran it very soon after that.

    I think it might be useful to have more podcasts of groups playing a game well, especially if it were a GM who really knew the game well, teaching it to new players. Some people can learn a game by reading the text, and others are visual or auditory learners, or learn by doing.

    I think part of marketing a game should be providing a good demo kit so that enthusiastic GMs can show others what it's like. It'll come as no surprise that Don't Rest Your Head has such a kit, and it would be an excellent model for others. Dogs has a great demo, but as far as I know it's just tucked away in some random thread somewhere, so good luck finding it. It comes with a great example town (Tower Creek), though.

    One of the things that's kept me from running Sufficiently Advanced yet is the lack of a demo. Sure, I could make one myself, OR I could spend that time running several games of DRYH instead, with guaranteed fun instead of maybe-fun.

    Seth Godin and Clay Shirky both write very well about the advantages of leveraging the enthusiasm of your core supporters into bringing the good word to others:\n

    "People don't believe what you tell them.
    They rarely believe what you show them.
    They often believe what their friends tell them.
    They always believe what they tell themselves."
    -- Seth Godin
    \nProvide more ways for people to show their friends how good your game is!

    \n Minor quibble: in economics, demand refers to "the amount of goods that buyers are willing and able to purchase at various prices." (emphasis mine)

    Wants, needs, and demands are different in this model. So, "low supply of money" actually means "low demand for new games" in the economic sense - you can't "demand" them economically if you can't afford them. Likewise, there's "low demand" for healthcare in the U.S. since lots of people can't afford it at all (even if they need it), and those who can are generally adverse to buying more of it at the same price. Of course, healthcare can be thought of as a sort of Giffen good, so it doesn't automatically respond to supply and demand pressures normally. Games, however...
