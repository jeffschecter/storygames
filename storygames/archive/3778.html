\n Awesome! A whole bunch of shit explodes about being too nice on Story Games and I have jack and shit to do with it!

Until now, I guess.

This is crazy. Three or four years ago, an average Story Game-type person either bought or was VERY famililar with, say, 80-90% of the games available in the scene, maybe more. While there certainly were instances I know about where person X thought person Y's game sucked, and kept hush about it, there were also many, many instances where person X contacted person Y and said "Hey, this is what your game needs to improve." And then Y went on to publish Dust Devils or Conspiracy of Shadows or TSoY or MLwM and so on.

Today, it is basically impossible to own all the games. I'm pretty sure it's impossible to know about all of them very, very well. And, I know for a fact it's impossible to play them all. This is great! It means we have to start making choices as hobbyists about what we want to spend money on, and especially about what want to get around to actually playing. It also means someone's gonna lose that choice, consistently, and fail to succeed.

We actually have a market! It's tiny and it's prone to weird burps. But, it's working! It means that publishers will have to get their shit together to survive. Someone remarked in today's threads that a game might suffer if people critiqued it.

Um, GOOD!

Games do not deserve to survive because they are creator-owned. Games deserve to survive because they are valuable games. Thinking otherwise is poisonous to everyone's success. Only a little bit, I imagine, but then we're little, aren't we?

I get it. It's messy. Toes get stepped on. Our market is so frickin' tiny that our publishers are simultaneously our customers. If a game can't survive Graham bitching about them, then that game didn't deserve to survive in the first place. Fix it or get it off the teeny-tiny market. We don't owe you publisher status. You have to earn it based on the merits of your work.

Many of my publisher peers long ago said we determine our own success, whether it was just getting in print for free or making a zillion dollars. I can't see anyone who defines success as inadvertently avoiding criticism because others are afraid of hurting your feelings.

    \n "Smack talking" is bad. It's what other Internet forums do. I don't want smack talking of any games - storygame or otherwise - on SG, uh huh, no siree.

    "Smack talking" is stuff like:

    "This game sucks because elves are phoney"
    "This game uses too many dice"
    "We didn't have any fun playing this game"
    "Everything just ran slow and Paul fell asleep"

    "Critique" is good. That's what good, supportive forums do. It's helpful on every level. If people on SG didn't critique, I would not hang here.

    "Critique" is stuff like:

    "I'm not a fan of elves, and I felt the game text focused too strongly on elven culture."
    "Prepare to acquire a whole bunch of dice of different sizes and colours for playing this game. I thought it was too many and would have preferred a more streamline resolution mechanics. It felt a clumsy in play because of the sheer volume of dice that had to be matched up against each other."
    "Our group felt there was too little direction in the game and we were flopping around trying to come up with cool story lines, but failed. A bit more setting information would have benefited us greatly, I think."
    "The GM was going on very little sleep and since the game placed a lot of narrative power in the hands of the GM, it really depended on the his high energy to keep the game moving along quickly. It fell flat that time so I can't really give you much feedback. I will let you guys know if I get a chance to play it again, this time a little earlier on in the convention schedule before everyone runs out of caffeine."

    Just like many of us are working to becoming better game designers, we all have to work to become better critiquers, players and designers alike. We have to know when it's us that's the problem, when it's the mechanics that's the problem, when it's the text that's the problem, when it's the layout that's the problem. We have to be honest when critiquing and we do not own the game, have not read the game, have not played the game, played the game with people we don't like, played the game with people who aren't gamers, and so on. Set, what state you're in and your mood at the time, and Setting, what environment you are in, play a large role in our enjoyment in experiencing anything. I know I would have a much better time playing D&D with a bunch of typical storygamers (as I know them) than I would playing Dogs in the Vineyard with a bunch of typical D&D gamers (as I know them). Being aware of these factors and stating them is the cornerstone of any fair, unbiased, constructive critique.

    Like many designers on SG are awfully humble about their games (with exceptions as needed), I think they're also painfully aware of this in themselves - they've already had to deal with people who smack talk games with no consideration to the above caveats. Not wanting this fate to befall their fellow designers, they (falsely, I believe) assume that they may make the same mistakes. But they are the least likely to make those mistakes, because they are more aware of the subjects than anyone else.

    It's not an "honesty" thing at all. It's a matter of "are we qualified enough to accurately transmit why we honestly didn't like this game". If we are, then we should do it. I've previously purchased games based on the fact that people whose opinions I do not agree with accurately transmitted to me why he hated the game.

    Posted By: Joshua BishopRobyWhat was going to be a comment here became a blog post:The Pride and Pain of Publishing

    There is no correlation between any standard of quality and actual sales. Let me repeat that: there is no correlation between quality and sales. Sales are not derived from quality, they\xe2\u20ac\u2122re derived from utility. Utility is, roughly, how useful a given product is for a given customer.
    \nI second that.

    A co-worker recently tried to turn me on to Harry Potter books. The conversation went like:
    me: "What does Harry Potter do that is groundbreaking? That changes things?"
    him: "Nothing. That's the point of the books. It's all derivative, stuff that's been done before. But it's entertaining."
    me: "So then what would be the point of me reading it?"
    him: "I told you. It's entertaining."
    me: "But I've already read it before? In other books?"
    him: "Yes. But it's a good read. You can read it quickly."

    And on and on it went. The disconnect was this:

    me: "I read slowly. I have a ton of books on the shelf, books I have been recommended as breaking new ground and expanding one's world view, but I have not had a chance to read them yet. My time is very valuable."
    him: "I read very quickly. I constantly run out of things to read and I get bored. I like to read new stories that deal with familiar subjects, so I can keep turning pages and lose myself in the story. I have lots of time on my hands to do that."

    I couldn't see his viewpoint. He couldn't see mine.

    Look at any best seller list and you will see books that are by anyone's standards derivative, but well marketed and accessible. Not "good", in some objective, qualitative way. Not groundbreaking or original. Like Joshua says, there is no correlation between quality and sales (perhaps you may even argue there is a negative correlation, but that is a touchy subject).

    Posted By: CalvinoPosted By: lachekthere is no correlation between quality and sales
    What is quality?

    Quality is subjective. It's meaningless to try to specify the relation between quality and sales, because quality is immeasurable. It means different things to different people.
    Joshua describes what he means by that - utility is subjective, while quality is, as you say, largely immeasurable and meaningless. One person's idea of "quality" literature is so different from somebody else's interpretation that conversing about it is nearly futile. On the other hand, when you use the word "utility" you filter your perceived quality through your wants and needs as a person, and it becomes easier to communicate about it with people of different utilitarian needs. However, once you start including "my wants and needs" into the equation, you have to accept Joshua's statement - "quality", as defined by the work the author put into the piece, has no correlation to sales. This is why, when art is produced on the corporate level for the exclusive purpose of profit maximizing, you rarely see any groundbreaking, mindblowing works - they understand that the current market generally wants things that are easily digestible, and make their art for the purpose of selling straight towards that utility.
