\n Here's my idea: When I make a game, people pay me what I want.

So, for a limited time only:

Society of Dreamers: $50.00

If you buy this today, you will, by definition, own a more valuable product than others who have bought it previously. It will be a rare and exclusive book - something your friends will (silently!) envy you.

Not something to flaunt, but something to savor.

    \n I'm really interested to hear people's thoughts on this whole thing. I've gotten a lot of surprises already.

    To clarify a few things:

    I'm doing this as a playful experiment, to see what happens. If I analyze it and try to find out my motivations, I think part of me is saying "look, our way of putting value on things is ridiculous and inconsistent". Part of me is saying "hey, look what I can do! I can pretend my game is worth 50 bucks just by saying it is!" Part of me is saying "actually? it IS worth 50 bucks, and more, too."

    The reason I'm using SoD for this, is because it's a game I control, and a lot of people already know about. I think it's an interesting experiment to do, and to do it, it has to be visible to people. This isn't primarily a marketing thing for my game - that is a possible by-effect, but it's just as possible that people will think the whole thing is stupid, hate my game, and distrust my pricing for years to come.

    The act of putting a $50 price tag on a game that has cost a lot less before - including being given away for free to hundreds of strangers - is a question. I like to see what people are answering.

    Posted By: cwilburWhat's the qualitative difference here? Why is someone who willingly and knowingly pays extra for a finished product a boob and a sucker, while someone who willingly and knowingly pays extra for a work in progress a supporter of the community?
    \nTwo things: the honesty of the original offer, and the spirit in which the money is offered. In the former case, the $50 price tag is being presented as if there were some benefit to paying more than the required price, and the purchaser should be happy about doing so. That's what we call 'proselytisation'. In other words, it has all the hallmarks of a typical internet scam. In the latter case, the advertiser is openly asking for donations and assistance in producing something that they wouldn't otherwise be able to complete at all. If the total doesn't come up to enough, nobody gets the product (and nobody pays any money either). Furthermore, with a Kickstarter campaign you can donate as much or as little as you like, notwithstanding any rewards being offered. It's the difference between putting a wad of cash into a charity collecting box and being ripped off by a back street salesman with a fake Rolex.

    Now certainly one could take the original advertisement as being ironic, but if so why accept the $50 when it is offered? Surely that negates the irony inherent in the sentence because it shows that the salesman is willing to take advantage of that situation and did not mean it simply 'as a joke'. Similarly, yes, a Kickstarter campaign could be immorally used to gain money which is then never spent on the project in question, you just have to take your pick and hope that the advertiser is honest.

    -Ash

    \n Ash, neither you nor I know the motivations of the buyer. It could be...:
    - That the buyer thinks this is a fun experiment, and is in on the "game"
    - That the buyer thinks I deserve the extra money for creating a cool game book
    - That the buyer mistakenly believes the book is physically different from the cheaper (and otherwise identical) versions
    - That the buyer hasn't read this thread (!), but is someone who decided to buy the game at that price anyway
    - That the buyer wants games to be more expensive (though that sounds unlikely, doesn't it?)
    - That the buyer has been seduced/inspired by my ironic/playful talk in this thread into seeing the $50 book as more valuable than the $15 book (and, honestly, this is how human psychology works; if you see something as more valuable, it IS more valuable)

    Personally? I don't in any way believe that the buyer is a fool. I think he or she knows exactly what they're doing.

    But in order to make sure nobody feels I've fooled them in any way:

    Dear buyer,

    If you want to, I will return the $35.89 I made on this deal. That means you will have received your book at a discount of $0.89.

      \n The thing is, if Matthijs were writing business software instead of story games, it would be a common and accepted practice for him to sell what is basically the same software at several price points. He'd produce at least three editions of the software, varying mainly in artificial restrictions.

      The reason for this is price discrimination. The basic notion is that some people (companies) can afford to pay more for the product, and you want them to. But other smaller companies can't, and if you try to charge them the price you charge a megacorp, they won't buy. So you find some flimsy pretext to charge them less, and you make a sale to them, although at far less profit than you make to the megacorp. Also, an individual person can't afford what even a small company can, and so you find some pretext to give away a nearly-free version (or even a free version), because some of those people are going to work for companies later and you want them to buy your product.

      This should really not come as a shock to anyone here. This is a well-understood and commonly practiced part of microeconomics and marketing. Marketers have been doing it for as long as any of us have been alive. Every time you see a students & seniors price at the movies, or have to choose between Windows Home, Windows Ultimate, or Windows Professional, it's the same thing.

      There are two problems when it runs into small-press games, though. In most other markets, it's fairly easy to find ways to distinguish between people who can spend a lot of money and people who can't. Airlines, for instance, discovered that people travelling for pleasure tended to stay over a weekend, while business travelers wanted to get home for the weekend. So fares that don't involve staying over a weekend tend to cost considerably more, because businesses will pay more. But there are very few easy ways to distinguish between the story gamer with a lot of disposable income, who could pay $50 for a game and not feel it, and the story gamer who's squeaking by, who has a hard time justifying $10 for a PDF. The other problem is the common misconception that community and commerce are somehow antithetical to each other; when done right, they reinforce each other, because the relationship between creator and satisfied consumer can be as strong as any other relationship.

      \n Yesterday, I was at the post office, mailing out some games.
      At the post office, there is a display case of merchandise that you can buy from the Canadian Mint. Collections of newly-minted coins.

      There's this one set that includes one of each of the Canadian coins, priced at $25. It included the rare, not-in-regular-circulation fifty cent coin. In total, the collection included these denominations: $2, $1, .50, .25, .10, .5, .1. Now, I've done the math. The total value of these coins is $3.91. But they sell them, complete with a plastic display case, for $25.

      There's another set that doesn't include the fifty cent coin - just the standard, in-circulation coins. It sells for $19.50. The total value of the coins in the collection is $3.41. Plus the value of the plastic display case.

      People sell money at like 4-5 times its face value.

      Given that anecdote, what Matthijs is doing hardly seems ludicrous. Matthijs would only be more ludicrous if he sold you a copy of Society of Dreamers for the low, low cost of 5 copies of Society of Dreamers.
