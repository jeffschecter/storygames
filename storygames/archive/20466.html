\n One of the intriguing possibilities presented in the 5E DMG is that, rather than a world with thousands of years of history, oodles of dungeons full of treasure hidden there centuries before, and ancient beings, imagine playing in a young world, newly created by the gods of the setting.

One thing that I think would be cool to do in such a setting is to take a page from AW and say that each PC is the only member of their class in the world, at least the only one who gets access to the full suite of abilities. (This is largely how NPCs work anyway, this approach would just lampshade that convention.) So if you choose Fighter as your class, well, there may be other warriors in the world, but you're The Fighter, and there can be no others.

What's particularly interesting to consider is that, in this "young world" setting, most of the classes simply don't exist yet. Only the ones the players choose have actually been instantiated.

I also wrote up some Sex Moves for 5E classes, which goes along with this...

Anyhoo, what do you all think would make for interesting primary conflicts in such a setting and using 5E's mechanics?

    \nNot exactly. In Apocalypse World fashion...there are many warriors, and they may call themselves fighters, but there is only one person who is The Fighter, there are many burglars and cutpurses and scoundrels, but only one person who is The Thief. (I was almost 100% positive that there was a verbatim quote to this effect in Dungeon World, but I can't place it.)

    It's an interesting conceit of the game, but I like the fact that it acknowledges that the only characters who really "level up" and get super things are the PCs, which avoids wild worldbuilding implications such as those you find in D&D, where you've got Elminster around the corner who knows way more spells than you ever will. Other characters will often be powerful and important, but only the PCs have such immense potential. It's a way of making them "chosen ones" without the hard-coded "The One" approach.

    \nI don't think the proposed character class concept involves "all this work of hacking". It's simply a choice about setting, and it should add less complexity and design work (mainly making classes more vague and generic) than it removes, because history and many traditional D&D complications like powerful NPCs or special magic do not exist yet in a "young world"; whole ecosystems and civilizations require less effort than, say, picking Elminster's magical items.

    On the other hand, further hacking to get appropriate rules for world creation seems in order. Apart from the tried and true world-building method of just asking the players (another typical PbtA feature), there should be, for example, some mechanic to "create a precedent" when the characters do something for the first time ever.

    Dungeon World is a game already designed to have applied Vincent Baker's AW design objectives into D&D.

    -Player Characters are unique and the only ones in the world with that set of abilities.
    -Players have an in-game ability to create fictional fact about the world and establish setting. (Spout Lore move).
    -Bonds as a form of Sex Moves between characters.

    DW also takes a very novel approach to the longstanding issue of campaign conflicts through its use of Fronts. The most poignant conflicts I'd like to see in a "fresh" world as OP's would be the Players asserting themselves in either newly formed or established communities and the ramifications of choices they make. (EX: do they promote industry causing a gigantic industrial boom on this side of the world spurring war for resources and poverty? Do help discover magic crystals in the far hills? Do they mine the magic creating an abundance of it? Is it stockpiled by the rich? etc).

    But I do like the idea of limiting those setting-defining moments to things that PCs spend Insp on. The way I run 5E, Insp flows fairly liberally, so it's not a huge constraint, but it means we don't have to be adding setting details with every skill roll.

    Here's a sketch of an idea for the setting:

    So you have your Good Gods and your Bad Gods, right? (Really just Team A and Team B, because I dislike alignment.) And after they create the world, they start squabbling, and their battles threaten to destroy this new creation before anything really interesting can happen with it, like diverse ecosystems, different forms of government, or Dwarven cocktail parties. And Team C (the "neutral" gods) are like, "Uh uh. Nope. Go stand in your corners, Teams A and B. You can still create creatures in the world, but all the intelligent ones have to have free will. And you yourselves may never enter its planar sphere."

    So the PCs are the "good" team's scions. They don't get missions from Mom and Dad so much as problems that need solving.
