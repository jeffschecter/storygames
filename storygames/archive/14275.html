\n The challenge went down like this:

&#149; I asserted that any game writing could be made interesting with a little work.
&#149; JD said that D&D3 reads like an algebra textbook, but it was still solid.
&#149; I said I could re-write any boring section of 3E to make it exciting and inspiring to read.
&#149; He said to pick three SRD feats in a row, by alphabetical order.

Here's the first one:\n
Blind-Fight [General] (169 words)

Benefit
In melee, every time you miss because of concealment, you can reroll your miss chance percentile roll one time to see if you actually hit.

An invisible attacker gets no advantages related to hitting you in melee. That is, you don\u2019t lose your Dexterity bonus to Armor Class, and the attacker doesn\u2019t get the usual +2 bonus for being invisible. The invisible attacker\u2019s bonuses do still apply for ranged attacks, however.

You take only half the usual penalty to speed for being unable to see. Darkness and poor visibility in general reduces your speed to three-quarters normal, instead of one-half.

Normal
Regular attack roll modifiers for invisible attackers trying to hit you apply, and you lose your Dexterity bonus to AC. The speed reduction for darkness and poor visibility also applies.

Special
&#149; The Blind-Fight feat is of no use against a character who is the subject of a blink spell.
&#149; A fighter may select Blind-Fight as one of his fighter bonus feats.
\nZatoichi (150 words)

Pick one:
&#149; You were born blind and raised by a cabal of warrior monks, but have recently been kicked out of the order. Tell us why. (pick this one only at the start of play)
&#149; You were cursed with blindness by a dark wizard for seeing something you shouldn\u2019t have. Tell us what.
&#149; Your eyes were gouged out by a rival who was threatened by your abilities or ambitions. Tell us how it went down.
&#149; Some other circumstance that happened in play, leading to blindness.

You can fight in the dark or against invisible foes with no problem. It\u2019s not any different from what you already do! The only exception is ranged attacks, from foes that are too far away for you to sense their movement or aura.

Also:
&#149; Reroll misses due to concealment.
&#149; Halve the movement penalty for not being able to see.

    \n I like Zatoichi, but:

    * The speed penalty shift (from 1/2 -> 3/4) isn't there, nor is the blink spell exception.

    * Also it's important that this be on the fighter list of feats, cos fighting people have to look for those things? That's like one word, though, just put "Fighter" with (General) in the classification.

    * You don't list how many times you can reroll, but that's easily fixed too.

    * Also, why can't someone train with a blindfold on or fight a shitload of invisible badguys and learn how to do this? By attaching it to blindness-in-play you kinda cut the shit out of some of the fun of shopping for feats to make a badass fighter. Or do you want to do that?

    * 15 year old me is hitting me on the side of the head saying "what the fuck is a Zatoichi, do I even pronounce that "zatt-toy-yee-chee" or is it "zatt-toe-ee-chee" or what the fuck, why can't they just say I'm good at fighting shit I can't see, I dont know, fuck these guys". I don't know if this is a fair criticism (i.e. like ours, your alternate-world D&D3 is meant for a super-wide audience including 15 year old dopes who have no clue what that is), or if it's a bad criticism (i.e. your alternate-world version of D&D3 is more flavorful and is no longer a fantasy-adventure toolkit, but instead a game tied directly to presenting a world and culture, so "Zatoichi" is totally normal and anyone reading the rest of it will totally get what "Zatoichi" is supposed to be about.)

    I do think this example sort of proves my point insofar as this version is a cool analogue of the Blind-Fighting feat, but it gives up the very straightforward, pretty damn concise explanation of what it is. For example, the original version of the feat contains information about what a normal person who is trying to fight blind has to do, reminding us whether this is a "good buy" or not. Yours doesn't have that, it assumes we remember from elsewhere in the book what the normal situation is. Now, is this information strictly necessary? It is not. Surely we remember with absolute 100% clarity every other rule in this book. But on the off chance someone is 15, or drunk, or just doesn't want to page back to double-check, the original text puts a reminder here. This is good teaching, the textbook analogy was picked for a reason.

    I think I see what you're after here. You are trying to use the feat to get across a vivid, unique fictional world, whereas D&D3 firmly puts that in the hands of the DM (and puts the tools for it in the DMG). I think both approaches are valid and can be used to great effect.

    \n Okay, I'm gong to take this head-on: three feats, alphabetical order. And, even better, three boring as hell feats.

    Negotiator
    Your calm demeanor gives you a +2 bonus to Diplomacy and Sense Motive checks.

    Nimble Fingers
    Your light touch gives you a +2 bonus on Disable Device and Open Lock checks.

    Persuasive
    Your forceful personality gives you a +2 bonus to Bluff and Intimidate checks.



    Each of these is 1 word longer than the previous version. Not bad. If you count the [General] tag, which I dropped since it seems implied on anything that's not otherwise tagged, it's even, same length.

    First off, I condensed the wording a little, removed some redundant phrases. These feats read like legal text, designed to be bulletproof, not something to prompt gameplay.

    Then, I added something to actually say what the feat is about. There was an entire article in Dragon a few years back about doing just this. It boiled down to "I took the Negotiator feat, what does that mean about me?" So I just baked a little bit of that into the feat, so taking it says something explicitly about the characters.

    They're not much of an improvement, but at least there's some phrase there that's not purely mechanical. The original text feels like code:
    pc.DisableDeviceBonus += 2; // I don't think we lost any information from the original here.
    // At least with my rewrite there's some flavor that we can't reduce to code.


    Looking at a lot of these feats, they'd be less painful to read if they just edited them down some. They all read like everyone playing is going to be trying to misread them. I mean, take Eschew Materials:

    You can cast any spell that has a material component costing 1 gp or less without needing that component. (The casting of the spell still provokes attacks of opportunity as normal.) If the spell requires a material component that costs more than 1 gp, you must have the material component at hand to cast the spell, just as normal.

    I think you can rewrite that much more briefly, but with very little loss of specificity:

    If a spell requires a material component costing 1gp or less, you can cast the spell without that component. Any component costing more than 1gp is still needed.

    \n Sorry it's taken me so long to get back to this thread! I didn't mean to just post and run.

    I really dig the direction that Jason is going. My "narrative requirements" were intentionally written so that they could apply at any point in the game (aside form the one about being raised in a monastery), so we could probably merge his and my feats to make one that's really solid.

    To answer JD's questions more directly, this is what I saw when I looked at the original feat (i.e. the "boring" parts I was trying to fix):

    First, it repeats a lot of stuff that should be succinctly listed on the character sheet or a condition card regarding the rules for fighting or moving in the dark. While JD likes this, since it shows that the feat is valuable, 1) ALL feats should be valuable, not just some; and 2) just thinking about what fighting in the dark or against invisible enemies must be like, you can understand how hard that would normally be. The text of the feat takes something that's intuitively cool and breaks it down into fixed, boring stats and modifiers, which is one of the things I loathe about "traditional" games. Like how Batman's power in d20 is apparently to be able to attack every enemy adjacent to a square he moves over. WTF. He's the GDBatman! Instead, I tried to stick to real language for the most part (much more exciting and evocative than numbers!), descibing what the move does and assuming that the details for fighting in the dark or against invisible things would be listed elsewhere.

    Second, there's no sense of how or why the character does this. Do they have special training? Are they a blind samurai? Do they have crazy Daredevil senses? Lots of interesting stuff there, but the game suggests that it doesn't matter. Just pick your feats already; we're all waiting for you to finish your character so we can start killing things. This also means that if you pick the feat later in the game, it can just come out of nowhere, with no fictional justification or fictional requirements you have to fulfill before you can take it. Lame. Sure, the GM could set some, but the GM has enough to do in games like this already.

    Third, what good does this "Benefit / Normal / Special" distinction do anyway? None that I can see. It's just more jargon that's opaque to new folks.

    Fourth, I really wanted a break from the names of 3E feats, a good 50% of which seem to be "that's what she said" jokes. This HAS to be intentional because otherwise people at WOTC are way stupider than I thought. Clearly this is some kind of attempt at appealing to middle school boys. Rather than making the middle school boys giggle, I thought it would be neat to direct them toward some badass media that they wouldn't otherwise encounter. Hence, "Zatoichi." They can Google it and find the reference instantly. Boom, a little bit of culture or trivia that they can lord over their friends.

    Fifth, I wanted a place for the players to actually interact with the text and the content of the feat, especially if that involved them generating imaginary content that would feed play. Hence, all the "narrative requirements" also specifically call on the players to generate content.     \n I worry that it was my feedback that killed this thread, which I'm actually very interested in. I put some thought into it over the past month or so, and I have come up with a few ideas myself. I hope it's not bad form to resurrect this thread with a few ideas.

    PRESTIGE CLASSES
    This idea's straightforward: use the test-based prerequisites from Unearthed Arcana

    MAGIC
    This requires doing a lot more violence to the system than the other changes, but the basic idea is: sorcery and magic never fail. Where you would fail, you succeed but in a nasty or unpleasant way. Spells and powers have suggestions for 'fumbles', but the DM is also free to invent his/her own.

    I think this fits in with conventional role-playing because magic has always been given more narrative flexibility than skills or combat. For example, wizards are expected to invent new spells whereas fighters aren't expected to invent new feats. Spell misfires aren't part of the core rules, but they commonly pop up.

    FEATS
    To qualify for a feat, you must have done something amazing. Obviously, what\u2019s amazing or not is subjective, so you need to make your case to your fellow players. If they agree that it\u2019s amazing, you can take the feat.

    You don\u2019t have to select a feat right away. You can save that option for as long as you like. You could even wait until you do something amazing and then immediately take a feat.

    Comments
    I think there's some cool stuff hidden here.

    Players have their decisions limited to what's actually going on in the game. For example, I'd be surprised if many people benefited from Far Shot because few battles are at such long range. By requiring a successful attack at long range, the game subtly stops people from selecting that feat if range is never an issue.

    It highlights what the characters have done rather than what they will do. In this way, it replaces generic training rules but still gives a sense that characters are learning from their actions.

    Feats (I'm working through the SRD ones here)
    Agile
    You found your way out a tight spot.

    Alertness
    You noticed something everyone else missed.

    Armour Proficiency (Heavy)
    You fought in heavy armour for a day. Requires: Armour Proficiency (Medium).

    Blind-Fight
    You fought bravely and well despite being unable to see.

    Cleave
    You killed three creatures in three turns.

    Endurance
    You were the only one to resist an environmental effect.

    Extra Turning
    You used all your available turning attempts.

    Far Shot
    You hit an enemy at far range.

    Greater Two-Weapon Fighting
    In the one round, you killed two enemies \u2013 one with each weapon. Requires: Improved Two-Weapon Fighting.
