\n It seems that a lot of people around here aren't too familiar with the genre of Magical Realism, or they don't quite get what it means. Let's start a list of books that fit the genre. Then let's argue about the list.

1. One Hundred Years of Solitude by Gabriel Garcia-Marquez
2. The House of the Spirits by Isabelle Allende
3. Daughter of Fortune by Isabelle Allende
4. Labyrinths by Jorge Luis Borges

I'd also say that Hopscotch by Julio Cortozar and many of Flannery O'Connor's short stories could be included. Perhaps even The Unbearable Lightness of Being by Milan Kundera, or quite a few Fellini films.

    \n As I mentioned in the other magical realism thread, I don't think you can really do magical realism properly unless you have some idea of magic as realistic\u2014and by that, I mean that you can, at the very least, appreciate the viability of traditional epistemology, even if you don't accept it yourself. I've done a lot of reading on this over the past year or two, and though I wouldn't claim to have any more than the sophistication of a little baby animist, these books have helped me greatly in getting that far:\n
    • The Spell of the Sensuous by David Abram
    • Animism by Graham Harvey
    • The Perception of the Environment by Tim Ingold
    • No Word For Time by Evan Pritchard
    • The Lost Language of Plants by Stephen Harrod Buhner
    \nIf you lack the time and/or funds for books, though, you can get a pretty good start just with Joe Sheridan & Roronhiakewen "He Clears the Sky" Dan Longboat's article, "The Haudenosaunee Imagination and the Ecology of the Sacred."

    \n Also worth mentioning: Black Water: An Anthology of Fantastic Fiction, ed. Alberto Manguel

    Manguel read to the blind Borges as a boy (as he never tires of telling us), and the collection gathers together 700 pages of short stories, from Poe, Stevenson and Wells, through South American magic realists such as Ocampo and Marquez, down to the present day. While what he calls the 'fantastic' is not exactly the same as Magic Realism, they are much closer kissing cousins than Magic Realism and what we think of as main-stream Fantasy. What they have in common, and what Fantasy lacks--or more exactly, deliberately eschews--is an emphasis on the inexplicable, the unknowable and the mysterious. Although they may write fantastic literature, personally, I'd claim that writers like Jonathan Carrol and M. John Harrison aren't magic realists.

    \nNothing I've ever read of Gaiman's strikes me as magical realism. Characters in Gaiman's stories tend to acknowledge the fantastic, either with wonder, or by expressing shock that anyone would find the fantastic unusual. Also, if we adopt this rule (more of a guideline, really) that the magic in magical realism never solves problems, existing mostly to call attention to the human elements of a scene or character, that's not really fitting with Gaiman's fantasy style.

Also, if we adopt this rule (more of a guideline, really) that the magic in magical realism never solves problems, existing mostly to call attention to the human elements of a scene or character, that's not really fitting with Gaiman's fantasy style.I agree. Gaiman isn't writing in the magical realism vein. Most of his protagonists find themselves in surprising, fantastical worlds, worlds that exist in the wainscotting of the real world. In MR, there's usually no distinction drawn between real and magical. A woman's tears result in enflamed passions among her guests. A man wakes up as a cockroach but no one reacts with great astonishment. These things just occur. They're not explained by Gods walking among us or a hidden world underneath London.
