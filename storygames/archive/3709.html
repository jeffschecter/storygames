\nOver here Paul Tevis got people talking about convention play, and the discussion got a little out of hand. Let's talk about conventions - specifically, the ideal gathering. Let's limit this to 50+ participants, all the way up to Dream Shadow Gen Con.

How long should the perfect convention run for?
How long should game sessions be? How should they be organized?
How many people is too many? How many is too few? Any particular kind of people? Who shouldn't come?
How should time be scheduled, if at all?
What sort of organization is in place?
What sort of interactions should be facilitated, if any?
What's the venue shaped like?
What are the organizer's responsibilities? The participants?
How much should you pay, and for what?

Let's discuss.

    \nHow long should the perfect convention run for?

    Three days. All day Friday and Saturday, and part of Sunday. Long enough to matter, not too long to get everyone sick and tired (literally!). Also, relatively easier to schedule for.

    How long should game sessions be? How should they be organized?

    A mix. Some people like the schedules, some do not. So, schedule slots in those hour blocks. Then, simultaneously, just provide ample, relatively quiet space for open play. (Games on demand -- that kind of thing.)

    How many people is too many? How many is too few? Any particular kind of people? Who shouldn't come?

    For me, personally, I'd like to be able to know several people there. The more there are, the fewer I know among the crowd.

    Once you start to "sink" below 25 people or so, I think you start to lose cohesion and energy as a worthwhile con.

    BUT, my dream convention is the Cannes Film Festival, the Sundance festival of Indie RPGs.

    Bring on the people. Hundreds!

    But, please, leave out people with whom I'd not eat dinner with my parents. That's my new slightly humorous measuring stick. If I'd be embarassed to bring you home to meet my mom and dad, I'd prefer you stay away from the con. That means hygiene and socially acceptable behavior. It doesn't mean everyone must conform and be "normal." It means we don't put up with bullshit in the name of accepting. Accepting dysfunction and disrespect of others isn't tolerance. It's stupid.

    How should time be scheduled, if at all?

    See above. Slots for games. BUT!!!

    Insert some nice breaks. Breaks for food, with plenty of time to make that happen. Let's be people, socializing. Not robots gaming nonstop. Maybe an evening session at the bar for drinks and soft drinks.

    What sort of organization is in place?

    I'm not sure I get the question.

    What sort of interactions should be facilitated, if any?

    Allowing people to both:

    1) Play with friends, particuarly long-distance ones (Maybe somehow have a "buddy system" sign up for game slots).

    and

    2) Meet and play with new people who are enthused about games you're enthused about.

    What's the venue shaped like?

    This one's tricky. Big, loud rooms with everyoen talking like a bumblebee hum stinks for good gaming. That means hotel meeting rooms are often a pain. More private rooms would be ideal. Good luck finding such a facility.

    Also, since this is my DREAM convention, there's a show floor for Indie publishers. The booths or tables or whatever are not a zillion dollars. They have games for sale. This is something people want, despite a handful of people mentioning they don't want a "sales" thing going on at a social gathering. Cool games I can buy from the creators! Fucking right I say!

    What are the organizer's responsibilities? The participants?

    The organizer has to plan and reserve the event. And, especially, make it crystal clear what attendees can expect and expect to pay.

    Surprises suck. One aspect of this is that you need to set up a good pre-register / RSVP system that's reliable. People duck out at the last minute. So, figure out some way to not leave someone holding the bill.

    Since I'm advocating indie sales as well, the organizer has to come up with an equitable system and payment process for handling the publishers' sales efforts (tables, sales room, etc.)

    Given my druthers, I'd want only indie publishers and their fulfillers. So, creators and folks like IPR. Retailers, no thanks. Dice companies, etc. No thanks.

    How much should you pay, and for what?

    I'd pay upwards of $50 for a quality event, possibly higher. Other people have a harder time justifying such expense. I respect that.

    I'd say that there's no way ANYONE should expect a quality event like this for less than $20. So, somehwere between $20 and $50?


    Oh! One last bit -- I'd love to see some time slots for seminars on relevant topics -- how to get a game group in your area, story-game techniques in play, how to publish and/or design games, maybe even in-con design competitions for fun.

    \n As may be evident, I've been thinking about this lately. My dream convention scenario is that GenCon becomes a non-event in terms of the indie scene. YES, of course, the games would sell there. But, instead of it being The Scene, a new, strictly indie-RPG con would take on the role of The Scene for the year.

    I keep thinking, very loosely, of the Cannes Film Festival or Sundance (a big, annual indie RPG con) INSTEAD OF the big summer blockbuster premier party (GenCon).

    Man that would rock. Some day, maybe. Maybe.

    \n[Convention]
    \nlength: Friday morning to Sunday night
    \n physical layout: 1 large open area (60 people, 14 tables), 4-5 separate rooms (6-12 people each, 1-3 tables)
    \n location: Speaking strictly out of selfishness, 2 hour flight or 4 hour drive from NYC. Speaking less selfishly, somewhere 50% of storygames can get too easily.
    \n transportation: 30 minutes from airport by car, or maybe 10 minutes to public transportation
    \n food: meat, vegan, and vegetarian options via delivery or 10 minute drive that can sit 10-20 people together
    \n special events: speed dating except switch dating with games, art gallery or show, game design + improv seminars, lots of ice breakers, game demos, places to buy games
    \n living/sleeping arrangements: hotel or camp site, 2-4 people per room, everyone has a bed, 1 shower to every 6 people.
    \n other: name tags, lots of white boards, pickup board/card games, pickup RPG with printed modules/scenarios
    \n
    \n [Games]
    \n length: 1 hr, 2 hr, 4hr, 6 hr
    \n scheduling: overlapping, with clean breaks for registration, ice breakers, lunch, and dinner
    \n registration: no pre-registration, on-site registration twice a day, can't register for events in later registration time slots
    \n organization: 1 sheet game, scenario, and rules descriptions available on a wall
    \n
    \n [Attendance]
    \n min: 40 people
    \n max: 100 people
    \n
    \n [People]
    \n want: interested in playing multiple games with new people, variety (age, gender, ethnicity, sexual preference, beliefs, politics)
    \n don't want: people who smell, don't want to try multiple games, don't want to play with strangers, are racist or sexist
    \n
    \n [Responsibilities]
    \n organizers: make sure everyone knows where everything is (events, food, transportation), answer questions, help people get into games, clean up
    \n participants: organize games, introduce people to each other and to new games, break up cliques, clean up
    \n
    \n [Admission]
    \n amount: max $150 a day, max $450 for Friday-Sunday? (lower is better, this includes sleeping arrangements)
    \n for: sleeping arrangements, events, minimum food (breakfast, snacks), convention supplies, pay organizers, discounts for people running games, no swag, no transportation
    \n
    \nEdited: for layout

    \nHow long should the perfect convention run for?

    Three days.

    How long should game sessions be?

    A variety: mostly four-hour slots, but some two-hour and, well, six-hour ones if people like that sort of thing.

    How should they be organized?

    Sign-up on the day, an hour or two before the game starts, no signing up in advance.

    How many people is too many?

    Ten billion.

    How many is too few?

    Three.

    Any particular kind of people?

    Not particularly. I trust that the other kind of people will go and play different games from me.

    Oh, wait! Adults. It should be adults. No kids.

    Who shouldn't come?

    Kids. Jason Morningstar.

    How should time be scheduled, if at all?

    A morning slot, an afternoon slot, an evening slot, different lengths.

    What sort of organization is in place?

    Benevolent dictatorship.

    What sort of interactions should be facilitated, if any?

    Talking in the bar. Talking before games.

    I think there should be some way of getting people to talk to other people. I did a board-game drive at a small convention where you'd play lots of really quick board games with lots of different people. It was great.

    What's the venue shaped like?

    Lots of little quiet rooms.

    What are the organizer's responsibilities?

    Make it run smoothly and strictly on time.

    The participants?

    Not be tossers or prima donnas.

    How much should you pay, and for what?

    Not more than, say, 75 quid for the whole convention, and it'd have to be good for 75 quid. Don't do a combined accommodation/convention fee: I might want to arrange cheaper accommodation.

    Graham

    \nHere's an idea I've had for a while - provide activities for kids (maybe 5-12 years of age) at the con whose parent(s) are playing. This would probably only work well at a minicon, with people who know each other, and where the physical location is small enough that parents would feel comfortable leaving their kids. Possible activities could include group storytelling exercises, imagination games, The Princes' Kingdom, Faery's Tale, simple board- and cardgames, depending on age group available.

    I've always thought it important to involve my kids in a hobby I consider very stimulating for young minds (and they'll grow up to buy games someday, too) but it's not always easy to bring your kids to cons, for you or for other players.
