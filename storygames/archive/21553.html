\n I'm posting this here because the site is called story games, plus i can't think of a better forum anyway. I'm sure I'm not the first to wonder this:

Is it better to take your idea and write a book/novel/short story/long story than to try and write an rpg?

Better in the sense that it would be more productive and better served as one not the other.

I have ideas that may well be better as one not the other (whether they would succeed is entirely a different question). But as much as I like stories and fiction, there is something particular to rpg's that is more satisfying: you aren't just creting a single story, but a world that can be shared. As a creator that is something I would like to do a bit more than writing a story.

Unfortunately while neither are especially easy or quick to do (I mean in the professional-get-published sense, rather than homebrew for your own entertainment - which is perfectly fine), fiction is easier. This is because to publish an rpg you need to not just write the text, but lay it out, find art, find a way to at least print it electronically. None of those things are as important, if at all, with fiction. YOu don't playtest a novel; fiction doesn't require rules and dice.

If anyone else has considered this question for their own work, how (if) did they resolve it.

    \n I've done/am doing exactly that, and I'm sure others have as well.

    You have great ideas about the game and wish that the story would unfold as your vision allows, but minor obstacles like the other players and rules get in the way. This is especially true if you are a storyteller gamer and many of the other players are not.

    I have started novels/short stories for both my GURPS Saduria gameworld and around the character I developed for Trail of Cthulhu (a campaign that floundered mainly due to GM inexperience). My imagination was swirling with the ideas that I'd come up with in the initial creations but the chances of those ideas seeing the light of day in play were practically nil.

    The solution is to give an outlet via creative writing. My stories are highly unlikely to become available to others and I would be amazed if anyone else found them interesting, but I am able to develop those ideas I had without needing to crowbar them into a game.

    \n I agree with yukamichi, trust your instincts on how functional one choice would be over another.

    However, If you purely thinking in terms of which one will reach a wider audience, or be more successful in terms of being published, I think that's the wrong way to approach it.

    But also I don't see a reason you couldn't do both as well. Really though they are very different animals. While they might, depending on your project, share an element of world building, a story is about creating compelling characters that navigating some form of orgazed plot.

    RPG design on the other hand is about cultivating a play experience somewhat out of whole cloth. Your honesty more worried about how human behavior interacts with each other and with fiction than just creating one compelling narrative.

    \n Layout, presentation and visual appearance are very important for RPGs. Otherwise we'd read RPGs in epub format, instead of pdf; but we don't, right? Why?

    I think that the fundamental reasons are two:
    1. An RPG text is structured: it contains rules, pieces of advice, examples, maybe some setting, playing material like PC sheets\u2026 A good visual layout should support this structure, helping the reader to memorise and navigate this sea of heterogeneous information.
    2. The more an RPG text tries to convey the tone and describe the setting, the more the rules exposition becomes confusing and hard to internalise - or risks to. Therefore an RPG text is usually focused on teaching the rules well, not describing the setting and the tone. The visual and art are important to fulfil - or supplement - that role.

    Novels are almost always "just" a linear text with very little structure[*], so the layout can be really simple: you basically just need to separate the chapters and apply very basic formatting for paragraphs and dialogues. A novel text is also completely focused on describing a world and conveying a tone, so you don't want extra media for that[*]. (Art can actually spoil the experience if it conflicts with the reader's imagination.)

    * For a notable exception on both points, read Ursula Le Guin's "Always Coming Home".

    \n I'm a writer too. Have been a lot longer since I've been RPG-ing.

    I think if you have a story pre-planned, you should write it as a story rather than an RPG. An RPG is a tool to tell stories. In fact, I tend to playtest my RPG designs solo--using the rules to write short stories about the same group of characters. The stories themselves don't usually go anywhere, but they really help me see where the rough patches are in my rules.

    I guess if you have a rough story in mind, solo-playing an RPG could help you flesh out the moment-to-moment action?

    I don't know if this is helpful or not, but it's an interesting subject.

    \n I think it\u2019s important to keep in mind that there\u2019s a more nuanced continuum of things between \u201cwrite a novel\u201d and \u201cdesign an RPG\u201d. You could create an adventure for an existing game system, or a custom campaign setting, or a rules hack, or a collection of NPCs, or a map \u2013 and that doesn\u2019t even get into whether you should write your fixed story as a book or a play or a short story or a podcast or something else. As others have said above, it really depends on what you want to do and what you\u2019re interested in.

    Here are the main considerations that I can think of at the moment:

    Worldbuilding
    Do you care about creating a fictional world and not much else? Create a custom campaign setting.
    Do you have ideas for a kind of world you want people to experience, but no specifics? Design an RPG.
    Does the world you\u2019ve made have a lot of backstory for specific fictional people? Write a book.

    Characters
    Do you have specific characters in mind whose story you want to tell? Write a book.
    Do you have specific characters in mind but they don\u2019t feel like protagonists? Design an adventure and use them as NPCs.
    Do you have only character archetypes in mind? Design an RPG system or hack an existing game.

    Plot
    Do you have a specific story in mind with a beginning, middle, and/or end? Write a book.
    Do you have a series of problems for characters to solve but aren\u2019t tied to a specific outcome? Design an adventure.
    Do you have a setting without any story threads? Create a custom campaign setting or a rules hack.

    Narrative Control
    Do you want complete control over what happens in the story? Write a book.
    Do you want some control over what happens? GM a game
    Do you want little to no direct control over the story? Design an RPG

    Collaboration
    Do you want to work alone or with only 1-2 other people? Write a book.
    Do you want to collaborate on a story with a group of 3+ people? Play an RPG.
    Do you want to enable others\u2019 collaboration? Design an RPG.

    Audience
    Is this for a broad audience of strangers? Write a book.
    Is this for a specific audience of people who you want to have a group experience? Design an RPG.
    Is this for a small group of friends? Write and run a custom adventure in an existing system.

    Obviously this is not an exhaustive list. Maybe someone wants to make a flowchart?

    \n Oh yeah -- once I figured out the "it happened before the PCs arrived" trick, things got a lot easier. I think I may have learned it from larp writing.

    Another guideline for gming: If you have to stop your players from playing, you're doing it wrong.

    There was one sessions where I wanted A Thing to happen. So I started making notes.

    Hm, that would require this PC to do this amazingly stupid thing, and that PC to do this other dumb thing -- okay, so if I did X and Y...

    But wait, then what if the PCs did this logical thing? Okay, how could I stop that?

    Right, and if they did this other thing, how do I stop that?

    Okay, at this point, I realized that I was trying to stop my players from, well, playing. Not cool. This was not a plot I could run. So, I started prepping a different plot.

    Then, one of my players couldn't make the session. I looked at my notes, and said, "Hey, would you mind if I specified that your PC did [stupid thing] for [magical compulsion] reason winding up in this [damseled position] but winding up just fine once the others rescue you and it's the session after?"

    Player: My character will be fine by the session after?

    Me: Absolutely.

    Player: Sure!

    In other words, since the player was not making the session, I could use the PC as if she were an NPC so long as she went back to being a PC when the player returned. The set up happened off screen, the PCs found out what had happened, and the players got to do whatever the heck they wanted. But without that? I would not have run that plot.
