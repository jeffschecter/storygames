\n To start with, let's stat up the Superman-analog.

DER UBERMENSCH: Found in Ultima Thule as a child preserved in ice by the Thule Society in the early 1920's, Der Ubermensch is the first Superpower on the stage of history. Stronger, faster, smarter, more charismatic than the Fuhrer himself, and possessed of a skin not even bullets can penetrate, it is not known if Der Ubermensch has even begun to tap his full potential. He has singlehandedly handed the Nazis victories where defeat should have been certain, and thanks to his efforts, Britain is largely rubble. Though he will wear a traditional military uniform for state functions, he is usually seen flying under his own power into battle wearing nothing but a customized red cape bearing the swastika, unconcerned as he is by environmental extremes, or bourgeois notions of shame and modesty.

But underneath his relentless optimism and almost otherworldly attitude, there is a sense of growing unease. He has begun to realize that the worst threat to The Reich is not external, but indeed the very leaders of the Nazi Party themselves! Their bizarre obsessions and refusal to engage the realities of the situation have, if anything, been exacerbated by the successes of Das Ubermensch, and he is rapidly losing his patience with them. He has no time for these "camps" that have been set up, and sees them as a sign of mental illness, of weakness, and perhaps a sign that he should take control...but to what end? What is this thousand-year empire supposed to achieve?

    \n Hmm. Now I'm going to try to do the Imprint scores. These separate the Imprints into themes or geographic groups. Since I'm doing a WWII-based Imprint, I'll divide it into theaters of conflict.

    THE EUROPEAN THEATER (6 points)
    1) The war rages on, changing what it does not destroy outright.
    2) The darkest elements of the supernatural hide in the shadows of the conflicts.
    3) The darkest side of human nature easily bubbles to the surface.

    THE RUSSIAN FRONT (3 points)
    1) The Russian winters seem to go on forever.
    2) Cracks in Stalin's Russia are widening.
    3) Russia does not believe in tears.

    THE PACIFIC THEATER (4 points)
    1) The supernatural conflict spills into the open.
    2) Japan's brutality towards its neighbors is legendary.
    3) There is a doom that will fall, to pay for the injustices of this war.

    THE UNITED STATES (2 points)
    1) A looming dread of the future begins to grow.( +1)

    I put the most points in the spaces where I suspect there will be the most player interest, and wrote down the three elements of each space that I think should be emphasized. The US is different, because I want to emphasize the effect of the war in ending American innocence and also the fear that superhuman beings engender in democracies, where the whole system is based on everyone being equal. The Human Atom has a far larger impact on the US' psyche than he could ever realize.

    \n Let's try a new one, anyway:

    THE JACKDAW: In our world, Franz Kafka was an emotionally troubled writer of dubious health whose stories of angst, fear, doubt, existential dread, and self-loathing in the face of a nightmarishly impersonal and bureaucratic world ended in 1924, when he succumbed to tuberculosis. In this world, the illness forced Kafka to stare his own mortality in the face, and he pushed through his doubts and personal demons to come through the other side, miraculously cured and more physically and spiritually whole than before...just in time to see the Nazi menace begin in its full dark glory.

    Reclaiming his family crest as his own, aided and abetted by his sisters and those who follow his lead, Franz Kafka churns out pamphlets by day that are a cross between Viktor Frankl and Thomas Paine, giving those under the heel of the Reich the will to fight back as best they can. By night, he is the Jackdaw, dressed as a man sized raven, whose strangled "KAW KAW!" fills Nazi soldiers and their accomplices with dread. His acts of sabotage throw a wrench into the well-oiled machine of Germany's expansion, and he is Public Enemy Number One in Germany.

    However, his discovery of the camps has unhinged him a bit, splitting Kafka and The Jackdaw into two personalities, neither of whom realizes they share the same body. As Franz Kafka, he motivates the Underground and decries the terrorism of The Jackdaw. As The Jackdaw, he strikes terror both psychological and, with his research into mesmerism and mind-altering chemicals, physiological into the Nazi Empire, mocking Franz Kafka as a mewling appeaser and idealistic weakling. The Jackdaw's recent alliance with The Golem has also begun to alarm his followers. He is a dangerous hybrid of Rorschach, The Scarecrow, and V.

    Rebirth Incarnation
    Beast (formerly Vigilante)

    Awareness: 3

    Survival: 3
    Maintain Split between Jackdaw and Franz Kafka: +1

    Bonds:
    The German Underground, made up of prisoners liberated from the concentration camps, whom he leads in guerrilla strikes against the Nazi government (6)

    Code:

    Note that there are four advances in there, which I assumed was okay due to his rebirth as a split personality from a vigilante.

    Keep in mind: Kafka is still the upbeat Underground voice of warmth and optimism, and the Jackdaw is still it's sinister, disturbing bete noire. Only his sisters understand that Kafka has basically split in two; the rest of the Underground assumes that Kafka wants plausible deniability, or has passed on the Jackdaw legacy to someone extremist enough to continue on with it. Thus far, his mind has been remarkably flexible in filling in lost time or explaining away one personality's connection to the other.

    In early 1919, a 35-year-old Benito Mussolini was given the gift of a very young girl by a female admirer of his, who said of the child, "She will bring you luck, and she is possessed of the gifts of the Benandanti, but she is herself ill-favored." Benito had no idea what the woman meant: the child was as a porcelain doll, and so sweet and bright! By no coincidence, he found out, his luck began to change, and he was able to form The Fascisti, and ride it's message of traditionalist populism all the way to the head of Italy itself. By 1925, thanks to her advice and her little "blessings" (which Mussolini did not believe in until later), he was firmly in control of Italy.

    However, things took a turn for the sour by 1940. Angelina was adamant that Hitler and his flying male prostitute were not to be allied with, that it would only bring disaster and undo all the work that Mussolini had done. She and Mussolini had many arguments about this, some approaching violent. This was exacerbated by an unspoken and unacted-upon sexual tension that neither one wanted to acknowledge, let alone consummate.

    One night, late 1940, things came to a head. Drunk and angry at the visions she described for him, he assaulted her, and vowed to show her what a woman's place is. To his horror, he found out something he had never even suspected: his beautiful little angel was a fully-equipped, fully-functional hermaphrodite. To her horror, it did not stop him from forcing himself on her.

    She was locked in a cell in an undisclosed location, where she alternately wept and begged for forgiveness, and raged and lashed out at Mussolini. Almost overnight, Mussolini's campaign in Libya stalled and had to reconsolidate. He visited her many times, sometimes beating her, sometimes begging for forgiveness. still he never let her out of her cell, and demanded she perform for him as she always had.

    By 1942, she could not take it. The broken bones and bruises healed over immaculately, but her heart was ready to break under the strain. She arranged for a band of Italian communists to find out about her, and help her escape. This is where she met Father Marco Antonioni, who declared that she was an angel fallen to earth. Now, while the Italian Underground aids the Allied invasion force and hides her, she gives them visions of the future and blesses their efforts. Soon, she predicts that Mussolini will fall, and will be killed. She does not know how to feel about that.

    She has found faith in God, and it guides her actions. She feels unloved and alone, and has had thoughts about Father Antonioni, but fears to act upon them. In her dreams, she speaks with a Chinaman about the futures of their countries, and she takes comfort from those dreams. She has had one opportunity to converse with the Flying Man, and found him surprisingly sober and thoughtful about his country and its leadership. There is an odd kinship she feels with him, the kinship of outsiders. But nowhere does she find love, nor what she really wants: respect and acceptance.
