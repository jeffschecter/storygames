How do we discover common ground?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2007 \n edited March 2007 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n It is easy to find out the things that are unacceptable for your game. When something pops up, and someone says "No, no way, uh-uh, I veto that," then you know you've got an issue. Then you can talk about it, and you're likely to be doing something productive. Negative evidence (of the "I did it and nobody bitched about it") is good enough to operate on.
\n
\nIt is hard to find out the things that everyone likes for the game. There are two problems: First, negative evidence (of the "Nobody's said they wouldn't adore playing gritty Care Bears in a world of thaumaturgic slave-feudalism") is pretty much worthless. Second, you really want something that everyone enjoys, so having one person unilaterally say "Oh, I think this would be cool!" does not necessarily mean that you've got something that has any decent chance of being productive.
\n
\nRandom, unilateral shots in the dark followed by group discussion are one tried and true (and inefficient) method. Another way is to try to go from broader categories ("I like Sci-Fi") to narrower ("I like Cyberpunk!") Also inefficient, IME.
\n
\nI also think that huge laundry-lists of what you can do are a game-design method to let people decide on their favorites by process of elimination. Everybody removes the things they aren't stoked by from a finite list of options, and eventually you narrow down the field: "No, I don't want to play in Greyhawk city again ... how about the Sword Coast?"
\n
\nOtherwise, though ... how do we do this very important thing? How can a game give structure that helps people to create/discover their common interests in order to pursue them together?

    \n My gaming group has exactly the same problem: very limited time available for gaming, and a wide array of gaming interests. On top of that, we are geographically dispersed, which makes it difficult to have a discussion involving everybody.
    \n
    \nSo the solution we came up with is that whenever we are planing setting up another game, everybody pitches in a private forum whatever he is interested in playing or GMing. Once the list is drawn we have a votation following the condorcet method lasting one week: you vote for all the games you are interested in a wiki, giving priority to those you favor most. Once the week is over we compute the winner (given our group size, you can do it on the fly), which is the current group's consensus game.
    \n
    \nWorks pretty well, never heard a complaint about the procedure. The results are often interesting, because they give a detailed description of the current interests of the participants. Sometimes a game we thought had limited interest in the group turns out to have a strong support, which gives us motivation to prepare something in that setting.

    The players pro-actively introduce ideas paying a coin. Any tenet may be challenged by the others, spending coins, if they don't like them.     \n I'll tell ya how I set my current campaign up, with a crew of players that are all new to each other basically.

    First I went fishing in the local pond (FLGS forums and a local gamers' list), "Who would like to play X? Here're some campaign premises I'd be up for running." I tossed out about 6 quick concepts (one or two sentences, only bare bones stuff).

    Interested players emailed me. I asked, "Which of those campaign ideas intrigues you?"

    We narrowed it to two concepts we liked, and then finally down to one. "Let's meet and talk about it," I said.

    We met. We didn't play anything, just got to know each other a bit. We talked about what we each brought to the table interms of game experiences and playstyles. We talked about what we wanted out of the game, and kicked ideas around. The result was an agreement on what the campaign would be about, tropes, style, mood, all that stuff.

    So far it's going very well. We did our chargen as a group and came up with some great stuff, and we're getting to know each other better. As the GM, this gave me a raft of ideas and the ability to tailor things to their characters more, and give everyone what they want.

    Our first session is coming up soon! :-)

    \n This reminds me of how I order pizzas for a dozen people.

    Typically we go around the room for five minutes having people say what they want on a pizza, and having other people disagree, suggesting other toppings, wanting different combinations and half-pies and such. I don't talk at all, just bringing up "no anchovies," to which everyone says "duh." Then, just as some people are ready to opt out, I suggest something entirely different that no one else brought up, and everyone goes for it.

I think it's just watching for the "holes" in their suggestions and seeing where they line up.

    \n I'm with Frank (err, about games, not necessarily about sex): Something that makes one personally feverishly excited and that everyone else is willing to try is likely to produce more fun for EVERYONE than something that everyone thinks is pretty cool but no one is on fire about. Fire spreads. So does enthusiasm. If one person finds something that really jazzes his or her imagination, that person will start sparking creatively and coming up with neat things that spark other people's imaginations, which means those people will start coming up with neat things, which will spark other people -- it's a virtuous cycle.

    This, by the way, is proably why the unwieldy traditional hybridization of rules-referee, antagonist-player, world-builder, and real-life social organizer that we call "the GM" has lasted so long.

    Here's a deeper thing: What people get enthusiastic about is rarely something as broad as a genre ("let's do cyberpunk!" in Tony's example) or even something as specific as a particular setting-and-rules combination ("Ohhh, Underdark!" in Andy's). If you push people, they are usually responding to something crazily specific and idiosyncratic that strikes a profound psychological chord -- maybe particular image they find cool (e.g. a two-sword-wielding reverse-albino leather fetishist with pet panther), maybe a specific story-pattern (e.g. for me personally, certain kinds of parent-child revolving around withheld approval, the struggle for independence, and forgiveness).

    You can't get to common ground by narrowing down from a general concept that everyone finds something appealing in somewhere -- especially since a general concept will mean radically different things to different people: "cyberpunk," for example, may strike one person as cool because of ghostly, super-sentient AIs, and another person as cool because of the snickety-snick fingerblades and implanted mirrorshades. You can get there by broadening out from from a specific idea that some specific person finds strongly appealing -- especially if you give everyone else opportunity to contribute their own idiosyncratic coolnesses that mesh with the original trigger idea, and above all if whoever contributed the original idea is not just willing to tolerate other's contrbutions, but outright eager to change and evolve his or her original idea in response to the coolness of others.

    \n Making this a procedure -- a "setting burner," or more accurately but less euphoniously a "setting and situation burner," to borrow Burning Empires terminology -- is crazymaking hard. I do not think you get it by going around the table and have everyone add one story-element (color, character, conflict, setting element, whatever) in turn, because that begs for disconnection. What you need is a process that pushes people to listen to and riff off of each other.

    Perhaps something like Universalis (maybe even exactly like Universalis) where it costs fewer resources to riff off something someone else has already done than to make something up totally from scratch....

    But, as I think about this, I think a better (notional!) system would be for everyone to throw out ideas and do a kind of iterative weighted vote, where everyone has two types of resources: (1) resources you have to divide among your own ideas to say which ones you care about intensely and which ones you're just, y'know, tossing out there; and (2) resources which you can only give to other people to bump up the importance of their ideas. (Probably you should be able to spend type-1 resources to vote down other people's elements you just can't stand). Then -- err, somehow -- the actual game-mechanics would build off the set-up: A story-element that got resources below a certain threshold would just go away, like a political party with too few votes in a parliamentary election, and other story-elements are more or less powerful when invoked in gameplay (e.g. conflicts) depending on how many "votes" they got (type 1 and type 2 combined) in the initial setup.

    Hey, damn, I think I'm going to try to use that for apocalypse girl.

    \n Sydney, what you're talking about with the whole iterative weighted vote sounds a little like the "Load"/network-creation phase of Verge: there, the players are each taking turns, spending story tokens to create new stuff and ratifying/vetoing stuff other people created. It's an interesting way to build a setting.

    Still, I look at it ("it" being Verge, in this case) and don't get a "this is a solution to the problem" vibe from it. If anything, it seems more likely to produce a setting that no one hates but no one really loves, either, which is precisely the kind of thing that I'd be happy to never see again in our own games. Ideas that someone hates will get vetoed, which is good...but ideas that only one or two people genuinely love won't get ratified, which (in the case of my group, judging from our experiences with PTA and other games) means the game will suffer badly as a result. If you've already got a group that's guaranteed to love the same things in roughly similar amounts, it could work; but if not, it seems like you're just coming up with a really pretty and kinda fun mechanic for developing a setting you will never, ever want to use in a game.

    \n I don't think it's necessarily always a problem if only one person is "holy shit YES!" about a particular thing, either...so long as including that thing isn't something that turns everyone else off. (It also helps if there are one or two other people who like that thing even if they don't love it.) This may even be true for a shared-narrative game, but I don't have any proof of that.

    For example, one of the guys in our gaming group is really gung-ho about intrigue. If he's running a game, there are elements of intrigue all over the place in it. They make for fun games, because even though the players don't all have the same love of intrigue that he does, he delivers great intrigue and there's always room for individual PCs to connect to different parts of it in different ways. For a traditional GM-player setup, this is ideal: he's getting what he wants, the players are getting what they want (or at least are having fun without putting up with stuff they emphatically do not want), the world keeps on turning and the songbirds chirp merrily all around us.


    (As a side note, things weren't nearly so idyllic when we tried taking that trad-game intrigue success into a shared-narrative setup or a setup where the setting is developed by group consensus. When we did an "Alias"-esque action-spy show in PTA, we found that either the people who weren't as good at intrigue as him were inadvertently blunting his best material with their contributions, or--more commonly--people were totally opting out of getting involved in his intrigue-related contributions because they just weren't as into it as he was and didn't want to mess up his flow (surprisingly, these were often the players of the PCs in his traditional games who were always coming up with awesome schemes and pushing the story along!). A couple of our players made the point that while they really like playing in an intrigue-based game, they aren't so hot on running an intrigue-based game: a lot of the pleasure they take in 'em is a direct result of our intrigue-master's creative input, and if there's going to be any intrigue they'd rather cede most of the control over the plot/scene/framing to him. What I'm wondering is whether this is something that anyone else has seen in their own games, and if there's a solution other than "don't even bother trying it" when dealing with a game element that everyone agrees they enjoy but most would prefer not to have creative control over.)

    Posted By: JDCorleyMath's a language, what you say with it still is the domain of the heart.
    \nActually, J.D., in part inspired by this observation of yours, I've actually already come up with the second generation of the concept I came up with yesterday, which breaks the process into two phases, the first being qualitative and only getting to quantitative stuff in the second. I realized that a voting/auction/point-buy process only works once everyone is on board with all the story-elements being considered; you have to select the elements themselves and make sure everyone's cool with them before you start weighting them, because no numerical weighting system that I can make simply enough to use around the tabletop is any good at distinguishing "one person is enthused about this and no one else cares, 1x10 points + 4x0 = total 10 points" from "five people think this is kinda okay, 5x2 points = total ten points."

    So, step by step:

    1. Free discussion and brainstorming until everyone is satisfied.
    2. Write down a list of story-elements (aspects of the setting, motifs, themes, etc.) that have come up in the brainstorming (and that anyone still cares about).
    3. For a story-element to become "canon," it must have (a) one person who enthusiastically likes it, (b) a second person who likes it a lot, and (c) no one who really dislikes it. In other words, you need a nomination, a second, and no veto from anyone in the group.
    4. Once you have the list of canonical story-elements, each player (including the GM) gets X number of points to divide among them. The more points a story-element gets from all players, the more mechanical impact it has in the game when it's invoked.
