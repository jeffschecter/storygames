\n So, I recently roped my girlfriend and her flatmate into roleplaying, something they never did before. We're playing a high fantasy campaign in High Valor (a hobbit treasurehunter and a winged, magic-corrupted outcast from society meet in a chance encounter in the woods and go find out what the evil wizard's deal is, in short) that's going pretty smoothly. And then, one thing led to another and I mentioned to her, while she was watching Nip/Tuck that there are probably roleplaying games out there that do soap operas. While that was mostly an off-handed comment made to get her more interested in the hobby, she responded with such enthusiasm that it now seems downright evil not to make good.
\n
\nAfter asking her what specifically she likes about soaps, this is what I got (warning- stream of consciousness!):
\n
\nDRAMA
\nand true love
\nand an evil twist
\nand horses
\nand some spanish countries
\nand all the last kisses (when they think they will never see each other AGAIN)
\n
\nSo, basically, an absurdly overcomplicated plot powered by overflowing emotions, and beautiful people in beautiful environments. On top of that list of ingredients, it'd also be good if the game was low crunch and one-shot capable. Like keeping the entry barrier low and the rewards immediate.
\n
\nAnything like that out there?

    \n The Smallville RPG does soap opera drama very well: all the characters are defined by how they feel about particular values (truth, love, justice, etc.) and especially by their relationships to other characters, so every conflict is basically saying what they want and who they care about.

    Just drop out the superpower stuff (or don't, if you're going for one of those freaky-ass soaps with vampires and whatnot), and you're basically ready to go.

    It's not perfect for a one-shot, but you can do a lot of the mechanical heavy lifting for her if you're willing to. Check some of the Smallville threads here, they give a good overview of what the system is like and some excellent advice about how to run it well.

    \n There's a rules-lite spanish game called libido. It's already been commented here:

    Libido

    What I like about this game is that it has a mechanic to mimic the escalation of relationships, even into the sex stage. You can certainly drop that part if it makes you or the players uncomfortable, though I would keep the relationship escalation part, since it would make good material for the emotional flow of the soap operas. Hack it with a relationship map and keep the rest of the rules lite. When you play it, just throw whatever situation you like (you can even use the high fantasy setting you were already playing) and have players/ characters roll for reactions so they can keep adding/substracting points that take the relationships into different states and trigger different emotions.
    Oh, and whatever you do, keep describing the emotions in detail, whatever they are.

    If you can fit that into a script for a soap opera, you'll got a beautiful experience. Heck, I'm gonna try this one with MY girlfriend NOW...

    \n The issue with specifically soap-related games (like Invitation to Love, say) is that they're basically building on a concept that only exists for deconstructive purposes. Doing "soap" is the same as doing "colonial adventure": you can't actually capture the spirit of pulp adventure literature while simultaneously acknowledging its political outdatedness, as doing the concept "straight" requires believing (for the purposes of the exercise) in those values the deconstruction uses to criticize the phenomenon. Similarly doing soap opera with a game that acknowledges the genre as somehow separate from good drama is counterproductive: a soap opera from its own viewpoint is just good storytelling. This is why soap opera games tend to be ironic and comedic: it's difficult to say that you like something when that something is being defined as critically and misogynistically as soap opera is.

    Primetime Adventures totally does soap opera, and it does it with flair. The main reason for this is that the game does not try to be soap, it just allows you to do the sort of television drama you like. If what you like is soap operate in style, then that's what you get. This same reason is why it often does better anime than an anime-specific game, to pick yet another example where the label is actually counterproductive to capturing the genre creatively.

    \n I'll second the nomination of Smallville. We've played a couple of campaigns of it and they've both ended up really soap-like. The key thing is that the mechanics force everything to be about the relationships between the PCs, which leads to some wonderful character-based drama. Plus the fact that you're encouraged to challenge and change what each PC thinks about the other PCs, and you get a game where the relationships are always changing.

    But yes, it works better over a few sessions than it would for a one-shot. However, the R-map creation phase is wonderful fun itself, as you stitch together this massively complicated knot of relationships.

    Posted By: Eero TuovinenThe issue with specifically soap-related games (likeInvitation to Love, say) is that they're basically building on a concept that only exists for deconstructive purposes. Doing "soap" is the same as doing "colonial adventure": you can't actually capture the spirit of pulp adventure literature while simultaneously acknowledging its political outdatedness, as doing the concept "straight" requires believing (for the purposes of the exercise) in those values the deconstruction uses to criticize the phenomenon. This is why soap opera games tend to be ironic and comedic: it's difficult to say that you like something when that something is being defined as critically and misogynistically as soap opera is.
    \nAlthough I see the point, I think this goes a little too far. Certainly there's more than one middle ground that will work:

    * A game/fiction that's serious, but the players aren't. In other words, we faithfully recreate the crazed stupidity of the source material, and the characters never wink or indulge in any irony, but we ourselves are yukking it up and being ironic and poking fun.

    * A fiction that's mostly serious, but occasionally gives you a nudge. This actually is how many American soaps are these days. The characters will remark on the ridiculousness of the situation, sometimes plotlines will abort in a non-genre way, and so on. In short, while it still has some of the traits that make it satisfying to fans, it also plays with the expectations not in any systematic way but just to spice things up.
