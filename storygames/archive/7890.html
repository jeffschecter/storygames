    Posted By: Jason MorningstarHow do you make a video game scary? I don't mean that facetiously - surely that's a topic of discussion and I'm wondering what tricks you used.
    \nThis is a very good question. The shortest complete answer I can think of is: "Think of everything in the game individually, and then ask how you would make that scary." Modern videogames tend to be very big and complicated, so you need to break them down into manageable chunks or you'll just get lost.

    Some basic techniques you see in a lot of games that we used to some degree would be things like

    - alternating periods of solitude and combat
    - visuals that mix organic and mechanical styles in unnatural ways (this just looks "wrong" to the human eye)
    - spooky audio (distant childish sounds contrasted against horrific visuals, etc.)
    - create an expectation in the player's mind, then don't follow through, to make the player feel uneasy
    - limited player resources (the "survival" in "survival horror")

    Of particular interest in Dead Space is our much talked about lack of HUD. That is, no little info-graphics floating in the corners of the screen. Player heath and ammo count are built into the player model. Entering the inventory system does not pause or slow down the game, rather it brings up a holographic display radiating from the player character. Our goal is to keep you, the player, as immersed as possible in our world.

    Posted By: WirebrainCan you talk about why he doesn't talk or... do anything?
    \nHe doesn't talk because it was decided very early on in the development process that he wouldn't talk. Really, that's it. Half-Life was cited, but also a silent main character is much cheaper to make than a talking one.

    Having animated reactions to what's happening around him was talked about quite a bit, but the fact is animating all of that would have required time and money that we needed to put into actual gameplay. It simply wasn't something we could finish on time to quality for this project. One of the reasons Isaac wears a mask that covers his entire face was so we wouldn't have to animate his facial features. Not to mention that if you go too far with that kind of thing, you're taking control of the character away from the player, which is something we almost never do, even during IGCs.

    Posted By: Paul BOne of my favorite things about this style of game is how the game itself acts as a tutorial for play. They hardly come with documentation any more. Sure wish there was a way to introduce that idea into a tabletop RPG -- it often feels, to me, like the players are supposed to have a comprehensive grasp of every mechanic in the first minute of play.
    \nIt's quite unproblematic, and has been done by board-games for decades. Structuring the rules as a series of tutorials of increasing complexity is done in a lot of old wargames - first a small scenario with just the most basic units, movement & fire; then a bigger one with e. g. supply lines & attrition; then an even bigger one with e. g. airstrikes, submarines and military commanders, etc.

    In a role-playing game, I think the easiest way is by integrating a scenario with the rules. For a traditional RPG, you could start off with a presentation of the characters, and a few simple skill rolls and choices; end the first scenario with a few minutes of equipment shopping. Next session, move into combat. Session after that, magic and experience/advancement. Etc. (This is what we did with L\xe6relyst, and it works especially well with inexperienced GMs - they can start playing right away).
