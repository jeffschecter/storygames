    \n In Kerberos Fate, Our Heroes were off to Lake Country to investigate something. This raised the question of a chaperone.

    Sophronia (the 9-inch clockwork fairy: )I make a marvelous chaperone. I don't need to sleep. What other chaperone can you say that about?

    Victor: You can say it about any, but then you'd be Gregory.

    Next came the question of whether the invisible flying carriage they traveled in to speed things up would or would not have horses.

    Sophronia: Fine, I will bewitch the damned horses. (to the horses) You are by no means flying. Those horseshoes were removed for a Good Reason.

    That reason was that fairy magic doesn't work when there's too much cold iron around.

    Later, there was a discussion about families, not unusual given that one of the campaign Aspects is Family Can Be Complicated.

    Reginald / Gregory: All of us have problematic families -- let's not go making comparisons.

    Sophronia: My family is perfectly wonderful.

    Reginald: So is mine, but that doesn't stop it from being problematic.

    Later:

    Sophronia: Your sense of responsibility will be the death of you.

    Reginald: Quite possibly, but I hope it will be the salvation of many others.

    And, Our Heroes found an ally in Sir Daniel.

    Daniel: Are you freemasons?

    Victor: No, but that's a good idea.

    Regiald: We're Kerbereans. It's like Freemasons, but with less badges and more world threatening dangers.

    Victor: And better coffee.

    Sophronia (whose creator made the Kerberos Club Coffee Machine): Oh yes, we have excellent coffee!

    And, we learned that this is perhaps not the best group of heroes to send on covert operations.

    Sophronia: Do take care of Alice!

    Alice: I don't see why I have a secret identity at all! (pulls off veil which conceals her identity, having earlier switched into the veiled lady costume when her friends blew her Other Cover Identity as a man)

    Reginald (whose identity was blown by his fiance, Sophronia): Welcome to the club.

    \n Dungeon Planet got deadly last night. Every character faced Death at some point; one still lived, barely, at the end of the game.

    It was an interesting session. Things definitely didn't go as planned. I set up a destroyer spacecraft as a dungeon, with 2 really tough bad guys and 1 lieutenant that was pretty rough on his own (statted as an Orc Breaker). I placed equipment around the "dungeon," and left some friction between the two main villains, hoping that would give the PCs an advantage, but they split and took on each villain separately, which resulted in total carnage. They did achieve their goals, though! The Pirate Dragon was not able to take his destroyer to Titan to try to overthrow the Solar Emperor, and the evil robot M10 was not able to take the Princess of Titan to Earth, as everyone perished when the PCs managed to send the destroyer plummeting to the surface of Tethys.

    It left me wondering if I made this session too hard. I'm not sure; things could have gone really differently, I think, but I'm not sure. Anyway, we still had a blast! I feel like I finally got fronts and how to use them this session, which was cool. Previously, I had a hard time with Grim Portents, as I wrote them as things that would happen in the fiction that the characters would see. I never could use them, as the fiction wasn't ever set up to reflect them. This time, I made them things that could happen without the PCs present, and they were easy to use. Having one front be a ship in the process of falling apart made this super-easy: any time I had a hard move, I could destroy the ship a bit more. As you could guess, we got to the last Portent: "Boom."

    \n Playtest of my naval adventure game, Devil and the Deep.

    The Weazel, 28 guns, was in pursuit of the Huntress, 44 guns. The Weazel used a cunning ruse to appear less dangerous, then closed suddenly to avoid the worst of the Huntress's heavier gunnery. Yardarm to yardarm, they pummelled each other, but the superior bravery of our sailors won out - the Huntress struck her colours rather than face boarding. Brave action by the lieutenant of marines and second lieutenant made the difference at critical moments.

    (In plain words, the naval battle mechanics worked well - the battle was quick, exciting, and gave a chance for the characters to do cool stuff.)

    \n Played a game of Golden Sky Stories using the demo rules this weekend. I'm utterly charmed with the way the system worked out and how the game played. In a nutshell, you have two piles of resources (feelings and wonder) that are used to power your actions/abilities. At the end of each scene, you get more feelings and wonders. So, you can spend wisely throughout the game or save up for big mojo later. I find that this easily works as a good spotlight/pacing mechanism for the players.

    \n Ended our Lady Blackbird miniseries with session 3 last night. PCs were the crew of the steamship known as The Owl (Captain, 1st Mate and pilot). The final scenes happened as The Owl landed on a platform outside the hidden fortress of the Pirate King deep within The Remnants. Captain Vance professed his love for the Lady Blackbird and asked her to stay, then turned away from her before she could answer him. Then as Lady Blackbird stood on the platform just outside the ship awaiting the Pirate King, a puzzled, emotional look on her face, Snargle the pilot ambled down to "talk sense to her". But still, Lady Blackbird stood between the unknown and her own past, undecided.

    The final two "shots" of the game (camera shots like a movie) were
    1) The camera pulling back on Lady Blackbird with her bodyguard Naomi standing by her side as she looked between The Owl and the Pirate King and his retinue slowly walking the long walkway to her.
    2) Then, a shot of The Owl zipping up and away from the platform to head back into The Remnants, tracking the Owl as it flies right towards the camera and past it, the last picture is the empty platform: no Pirate King, no Lady Blackbird. Just empty.

    It was really fun to leave it completely open. What life did she choose?

    \n Dungeon Girls on the high seas, using World of Dungeons rules:

    The ship is attacked at night by vampire pirates. The guild assassin Fox is on the crow's nest, and uses her magical black arrows (they never miss) to pick off two vampires climbing the rigging to get at her. She then decides to shoot another arrow at one of the badass pirate captains, but (failure) he grabs the arrow out of mid-air and hurls it back at her! The kids gasp. Those arrows never miss! Fox throws herself backward off the crow's nest and (success) manages to intercept the arrow with her arm. It pierces her arm, she manages to (partial success) break her fall by clutching at the rigging, but slams into the deck, dazing her. The magical arrow has become a black feather transfixing her arm.

    Meanwhile, the Death-aspected druid Valavanora unsuccessfully tries to defend herself against a vampire and (failure) falls to the deck with the vampire's fangs buried in her throat. The next round, as she tries to pry off the bloodsucker, her hands (cold with the touch of Death) lock on the vampire's arms and (crit!) a deadly frost spreads over the vampire and brings sweet Death to the undead. She pries the frozen corpse away and climbs shakily to her feet, blood still streaming from the wound in her neck.

    \nDeath Takes a Holiday playtest

    So the Auditor from the Celestial Bureaucracy is annoyed that our substitute Grim Reapers screwed up. We failed to make sure the fake cult leader died as preordained. Now he's spooked and hiding out in a cardboard house in the theme park where he works, with a couple armed guards keeping him safe.

    About a minute and a half through the immortality, something dawns on me. As I'm being repeatedly shot, I yell "There's a flaw in the plan! It takes more than two minutes to suffocate!"

    \n The past 2 weeks at STURP I've run Agon, in part inspired by Harryhausen's passing and a fond remembrance of Jason and the Argonauts. We did the Temple of Hera adventure from Ben Robbins (this here) and it really helped me to learn the system. I'm a big fan of systems that reward me for playing hard as a GM in conflicts, and (despite my limited knowledge of the system to begin with) it felt very natural, and cool, to be able to hit the players with all my NPCs had. I just wish I'd realised the sorceress had a Gaze attack earlier...

    They blew the Horn and caused the satyrs to flee, then had an anxious moment when they remembered Hera's instructions... we did a little flashback and I read the boxed text again from the adventure as they tried to remember her exact words.... and realised their quest was to slay the defilers, not just let them run off! An aced Athletics challenge later and they'd cornered the satyrs in a mountain pass, from whence they slew them mightily.

    I still had lots of strife left at the end (probably my fault from being too timid with minions early on) so it made me think I should probably have some 'stock' NPCs ready - I could've easily added in a brutal satyr leader, or even Pan himself, as an additional opponent, but couldn't face statting him up on the fly.

    Really cool game though - I've already put down to run it at Furnace (con in Sheffield, UK) in the Autumn - and it could well become a con/one-shot regular for me.
