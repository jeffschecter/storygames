    Are you looking for actual play, or techniques for incorporating, or both, or what? Actual play is never a bad thing.

    Both! On the one hand, I'm curious about how the distinction can function in play but also how it can be built in to the system at the design stage.

    On thing I'm wondering: I have been running a 3.5 D&D game which, to my mind, is designed to be fairly push-centered. The mechanics don't really give you a whole lot to encourage players to pull--I think part of that may rest on its emphasis on in-game mechanics rather than 'metagame' negotiation where a lot of pulling gets done.

    Now, I have four players (P, R, T, K--they will know who they are if they ever drift on in)--one of them is a woman (K), the rest are men. K is new to tabletop gaming, R is an rpg veteran, the rest of us somewhere in between (but all more than 5 years of gaming experience).

    In a disappointing session (one of the watershed sessions that really got me thinking about alternative games and led me to start digging around places like this), you have the following dynamic:

    Party kicks in door and begins to mow down baddies (in this case, a Cleric, her devil guards, and two monks). The cleric and devils go down hard, the monks do not, but it is clear that they do not pose a real danger to the party. This is all very push, push, push.

    P & T get into the smackdown, starting to taunt monks with their immanent demise. R begins to voice some concern about why the smackdown is happening--they sort of burst in there and went at it without trying to talk to them. At this point, we are reaching a pull--he's trying to solicit a similar response from the other players (very much, 'hey we're the good guys, what are we doing?'). K picks up on this and hesitates. Other players keep up with the combat, leading R to push, namely withdraw his character from the combat, trying to 'force' the matter.

    I pick up on the fact that both K & R have become distressed with the current direction of the session and throw in some description to really humanize the monks, to 'pull' the other players into thinking of an alternative route.

    P & T are a mixture of frustrated and confused by the change--they just want to get on with the smackdown and wind up. They keep up with the smackdown (essentially pushing back on R), and K joins the fray reluctantly so as not to drag things out. I let them ignore my pull and finish the smackdown.

    There is some interesting post-game stuff that surrounds this, but I'll stick with the session.

    Things that jump out at me:

    1. there is a way in which a lot of good players (and GM's) begin to 'telegraph' dissatisfaction by first pulling. If that pull is ignored, they often follow it up with a push. I think they do this because they have good social skills, even if the game doesn't have the best mechanics for facilitating them.

    2. the confusion seems to happen, in part, because players and GM's don't do a good job of switching up to a metagame discussion. We stick to the mechanics which discourage tabletalk, discourage metagaming. Because of that, (back to example) R doesn't have a platform to really establish his pull and so he gets shouted down (no real shouting, just metaphorical). That leads to a little resentment and a push back--a push that the game allows but which it can't easily 'recapture' for game fiction purposes--sort of overflows into the player level with potential hurt feelings.

    It really seems like the game would have gone much better if there had been a mechanic in place that would allow a player to explicitly flag their pull. The combat rounds made it easy for him to flag a push (my character steps away, my character voices frustration, etc.) but not so easy for him to flag his pull ('hey, let's do something besides beat these guys down--what if we could talk them down, appeal to their ideals, something...'). Something real simply could have worked--'hey guys, I just want to flag something: we strolled in here without a lot of forethought, maybe we should..."

    It seems like a lot of really bad gaming disasters (this definitely wasn't one of those) occurs when someone's pulls get overlooked again and again, so that they feel 'driven' to a push or outright withdraw. Now, it would be great if the GM could flag their pulls, too--it might help break the habits of players who think of every GM action as push (more or less covert).

    an irony: it seems that a lot of good pull players flee these push games--not pushing back, but withdrawing (in big or little ways). They want more cooperation, more give and take, and so when that is frustrated they vacate.

    In the game of Truth and Justice that Mo and I were playing the other night, there came a moment where Mo's character was facing off with Matador, an evil but sexy bastard who has been her lover and enemy for ages. They've always played this cat and mouse game and ended up just barely getting away from the other at the right moment. You all know the drill, I'm sure.

    So, this time things are different. This time they are fighting to really hurt each other, to end everything once and for all and crush the other in spirit as well as body. It's a seriously ugly fight, and Mo and I are slamming at each other back and forth, doing our level best to destroy each other's character.

    Then I narrate this one action where Matador gets the upper hand and just molly-whomps all over Mo's character. The way we describe the layout of it on the page (we pose everything like pannels in a graphic novel) is that it looks like a domestic assault, with all of his nobility stripped from him as he just beats her up and down in the most vulgar, ugly way.

    Mo then picks up her dice to roll, to stop the above brutal beating of course, and then stops in the middle of figuring out what she's going to use and says, "What if I want that to happen? Like everything you said happens, but then at the end when her face is all bloody and she's spitting out crap she's still standing and then he has to face what he's done."

    And then I'm like, "Well, what if he knocks you on your ass and stomps on you, and then has to stop because he's out of breath and you're sitting there panting and looking up and him and the both of you have to stop and look at yourselves and what you've both become."

    And then Mo goes, "Huh, but what the hell would I roll to do that?" And we both look down and realize that Truth and Justice resolution doesn't work that way. Neither of us really has the authority to do what we just described, and it's pretty clearly something important. So instead of rolling we shrug and say, "Okay, what we just said -- ink it."

    So Mo narrates getting monkey stomped, and then I narrated him looking into her eyes, and then they got really nasty as they stopped fighting and escalated to talking. Then we go back to rolling and using the system normal style.

    So there's the thing about it, we switched from a fortune (mostly at the end) system to a drama in the middle system right there in mid stream when we hit a place where the mechanics in the book wouldn't let us do what we wanted to. Later, I realized we do this all the time, though often we bandy about Hero Points as currency in the exchange.

    So, when you're desining a game, I think it might be an interesting thing to consider where and when you see your system working and how it gets subverted when people want to agree on something rather than when they want to contest something. Rolling for what your character wants is one way to go, but is it always the best? What about when you want to lose, but lose in a specific way? What about when everyone agrees what should happen before you get to the dice? Does that short-circut what you're doing with your resolution, or can it handle that?

    the players will - and SHOULD - always have a layer of 'free play' operating outside of that structure.

    2. I think yes, too, but what I want to take away from this: the freeform moments help us get to what we think is really cool in a storied fashion, and encourage us to break out of system molds holding us back. However, once we find a place where that system mold is holding us back, why not start thinking about ways the system might not hold us back, might in fact help us get there to begin with?

    I tend to think of most activities as being a kernel of habit surrounded by an experimental bubble of variation, the variation providing the 'cushion' necessary for adaptation. When the habit fails, the experimental (and this isn't 'raw' randomness, but 'partially' habituated) kicks in. If the experimental really works, then it should (in most cases) make its way into the habitual kernel. We have freeform so we can grow, but we also want to bring that freeform back to the habit.

    In gaming, the central feature of that kernel is the mechanics that the system provides us. We take that system, use it, creating the 'experimental' freeform bubble on our own. We can find what was cool about that freeform bubble and bring it back to the kernel. To some extent, sure, the coolest thing about the bubble is its experimental capacity, not the particular results, but bringing back particular results to the kernel means that you get an opportunity to enrich someone else's bubble when they pick up your addition to the kernel.

    Which is a long-ish way of saying that I'm not sure any designer can entirely show where their elements are and aren't useful, because you find that out by applying them, and there can be surprises along the way. It isn't that you 'want' to use a resolution that doesn't work but, in fact, contribute to the resolution system so that it does work--for other people in a similar situation.

Player to player interactions seem one of the best routes to rules changes being assimilated--they make the habits being developed more explicit and actually involve folks 'getting together' and 'getting behind' them.
