\nWelcome to the Hood
It has been 27 years since the nine Wu-Tang masters arose to dominate Shaolin and the surrounding countryside. For a brief but glorious period, peace and plenty reigned across the land. But now the Wu-Tang masters have retreated into seclusion. It is a lawless time. Gangs have divided the land into countless shifting territories and protectorates, turf held only by the power of a crew\u2019s style and the force of its rep. From the fortress island of Shaolin in the south, to the Boogie Down \u2014 the ancient homeland \u2014 in the north, from Chilltown in the west and Strong Island to the east, 60,000 soldiers slang, bang and battle across the city.

Without the guiding hand of the masters, it\u2019s all precariously balanced, ready to topple into a storm of fire and blood. Everyone senses it. War is coming.

Set it off
From the hearts and minds of John "Jenskot" Stavropolus, Thor Olavsrud (Burning Wheel), and Kevin Allen Jr (Sweet Agatha), comes an all new roleplaying game titled The Five.

In The Five you play a crew of gangsta soldiers slanging, banging and battling their way across a crime-ridden vision of New York City. It's "The Warriors" meets "The Last Dragon" with all the action of "Double Dragon" and "Grand Theft Auto." With a soundtrack by the illest east coast hip hop of past and present.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarHey sneaker pimps, how did you divide up the work? How did you collaborate?
    \nThe three of us got together on January 8th and decided we could kick out a game before Jared's Indie game company challenge ended. We brainstormed for a few hours talked about what we liked and didn't like in our recent play, a picture began to emerge of what sort of game we could craft. Then we talked about what was a reasonable workload goal to accomplish. We did all this work in an office conferance room, with a whiteboard wall. We took some pictures of the wall when we were done. Those'll get posted sometime soon i imagine.

    Thor was in charge of physically writing the thing, a herculean effort given the timeframe we were working with.
    John was lead developer, keeping us on track, editing Thor's writing, working out kinks in mechanics, pushing everything along. He also built the website.
    I art directed and designed the layout.

    We all had a pretty strong hand in the concrete design of the game itself, that was a good group effort. We stayed in pretty constant contact, trading a lot of emails, meeting a little more than once a week. We also did the second phase concepting/rough drafting/writing/editing in a google doc so we could all comment on each other's work. Really the trick was to maintain as much transparency as possible.

    I'll let my partners add any insights they may have gleaned.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarHey sneaker pimps, how did you divide up the work? How did you collaborate?
    \nI'm probably X-posting with Kevin RIGHT NOW.

    Basically, it went down like this:

    Three weeks ago, on January 8, we had our regularly scheduled D&D game. But a bunch of players had to cancel at more or less the last minute for various reasons. John, Kevin and I found ourselves sitting around a table in a conference room in my office, where we play.

    Jared had just announced the first of his three wrinkles, so I suggested we design a game in the next few hours rather than playing the board game John had brought.

    Kevin and John were into the idea, so we jumped into it.

    One of the nice things about this particular conference room is that one of its walls is a whiteboard. We made sure to take advantage of it. We did a brainstorming session with several steps. Each round was slightly different and I don't remember exactly how it went, but basically we took turns listing things we liked about games, things we didn't like, things we wanted to include, things wed didn't want to include, etc.

    Then we started taking the stuff that was written up on the boards and used it as inspiration for our concept. We knew what we wanted the game to be about (The Warriors and the Wu-Tang Clan's music), we knew that we wanted the system to use dominoes and that we wanted a music playlist integrated with the system.

    At that point we figured out the roles that we would need to fill. We knew we wanted a strong lead developer to keep us on task, and we saddled John with that responsibility, as well as responsibility for creating our Web site and marketing. I wound up as the writer and Kevin took on the graphic design and layout. We were running out of time for the night, so we all agreed to play with some dominoes and generate some system ideas for our next meeting.

    At that point we set up some documents in Google Docs so we could start listing our ideas, inspirations, thoughts, etc. Everybody contributed a lot there, whether marketing thoughts, system ideas, lists of music, a breakdown of The Warriors conflict by conflict, etc.

    The following Tuesday we met up at a bar after work and hammered out the basics of the dominoes mechanic and powers.

    On Thursday, we again couldn't get the D&D group together (very unusual!) and so John and I sat down with Dave Turner and played with the dominoes a lot until we got a sense of what worked and what didn't.

    John started another Google Doc dedicated to just our system information.

    That weekend, I sat down and hammered out the text of the game. Explaining how to play dominoes with words is a tricky thing! I put the whole manuscript up on Google Docs.

    John and his wife Terry then read and edited the document.

    While that was happening, Kevin built the template for the PDF. John provided lots of great criticism and helped Kevin make it look as awesome as it does. I basically just contributed that it looked awesome.

    Once John and Terry were done, Kevin flowed the document.

    While Kevin worked on the layout, John built the Web site.

    And that pretty much brings us up to today!

    Posted By: Ice Cream EmperorWell the layout pretty much eats all the competitors for breakfast, and the writing was spot on, but I had some trouble working through the dominos mechanics in my head -- probably because I don't think I've played dominos since I was six years old -- and so the interaction between the resolution mechanics and the overall game economy was kind of opaque.
    \nThanks for the kind words!

    As I discovered while wrestling with the text, while I can show someone how to play a basic dominoes game in two minutes flat, describing it in text is tricky! I think your best bet is to get a set of dominoes and work through the text step by step while laying some out for yourself. I think it will become clear very quickly.
