Design Contests for Games Already in Development?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nwhduryea \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2011 \n edited February 2011 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n This topic is inspired by conversations I've had with a friend who enjoys designing games, but is frustrated by the fact that all of the significant game design contests--Game Chef, 24 Hour RPG, Ronnies, etc.--require you to start from scratch and create a new game. He has a pile of in-development games that he would like to draw attention to and find playtesters for, and is discouraged by the prospect of having to create yet more games in order to cultivate attention for his work.

Are they any RPG design contests for unpublished, unfinished but not entirely new games?

If not, why not? Do most game design competitions ask you to create an entirely new game just to promote fairness, or are there other reasons?

    \n To your first question, The Thousand-Year Game Design Challenge is open to any unpublished game. It doesn't need to be made from scratch, just unpublished before submission. I'm curious how an RPG designer would approach this challenge.

    To your second question, I decided against a from-scratch restriction for this challenge. As you said, I figured a lot of people already had ideas for games that would be eligible, but had no major incentive to polish them up into a final form. I didn't want to exclude those designers, so I left the challenge open to them.

    \n I floated the idea of a design feedback contest for in-progress games on Praxis a while back, but the response was a bit tepid so I didn't pursue it. I think part of the problem is that finishing games is a lot harder than starting them, so it would be hard to find a critical mass of people who are serious enough about finishing to all participate in something simultaneously. Another barrier is that it's a lot easier to look at the "contest games" people produce without the kind of investment that it usually takes to look at a "real game", since they tend to be longer and can't just rely on single stark ideas. I think there's room for more types of design contests than the ones we've got, but there are some structural barriers that would have to be overcome to make them happen.

    There's two issues: when a game is finished, and when you're committed to finishing. Some stuff, I realized it's just going to be a PDF, then drive to that PDF (Old Mesilla, for ex.). Other things, I realized there's something more, and drive to that. (Disclaimer: I haven't actually hit that yet. This year.)

    \n In my experience, discussion on a game in development without actually playtesting it is damn near useless. It involves a lot of speculation, positioning by comparing it to games you HAVE played that might not actually be like the game in design at all, and some projection from the reader in terms of what they think the designer should be designing rather than the game's actual rules, fun, playability, etc. I don't want to discourage such a cool community thing, but I do want to encourage actual playing in addition to talking about the games, and if you do just talk about the game keep the designer's goals foremost in your mind rather than turning the game into the game you wish you were designing but aren't. And designers, have a vision of your design and don't be swayed. And from my own experience, it is a lack of playtesting that has killed more of my half-finished designs than anything else.

    Right now I have two games I'm developing. One took nearly three years of development to complete. The other has gone from random idea to nearly-polished game at a speed so rapid that I'm startled - about a month. The difference? The former I had a difficult time playtesting. The second hit the ground running as far as playtests go and has external playtesters who have played the game more than I have. Discussion has helped me to get through certain thorny problems or roadblocks, but it's the playtesting that has actually moved the game closer to completion.

    I am not trying to poo-poo this endeavor, but since I am standing right now on the edge of going from endless, perpetual development to actual publication and being able to see with hindsight and comparison to another game I have in development, I wanted to throw this out there. I realize that talking about a game is a much easier contribution to make to a game's development than a playtest, but I think doing external playtesting is the most significant single contribution one could make to the development of a game.

    \n Brett, of course you're right. But I'm not trying to replace actual play, or keep people from getting actual play. If anything, I'm promoting actual play.
