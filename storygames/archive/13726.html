Calling them "rpg" where The Forge itself stresses and analyzes the social nature of rpgs activity, makes me think "no, wait. These have nothing social about them. They basically feel like methods for fiction creating/writing (and excellent ones at that!)

I don't mean to tell anyone what they should call things, especially when it's people whose cultural authority I deeply respect like the people who are promoting and participating to this activity, but don't you find this definition an oxymore? And not an aesthetically appealing one, but a semanthically confusing one...

A trivial question, but I wonder why I haven't seen anyone making it so... please indulge me.

    \n So say I've written a book. And I decide not to share it with anyone. I've still written that book, yeah? Engaged in a creative act? Imagined a world, some characters, a story? Some people might say that sharing your story is the only point to writing one in the first place, but that fundamental act still happens regardless of whether or not there's an audience, yeah?

    So we're roleplaying, instead. The GM describes the situation, turns to me, and asks "What does your character do?" All the other players wait with bated breath to hear my answer!

    OR maybe I'm playing a solitaire game, the system provides me with a system, (perhaps randomly-generated, perhaps established by the system's designer) and prompts me to ask myself "What does your character do?"

    To me, at least, it's the same sort of situation. I respect that some people don't think there'd be a point to solo play... but I think you can engage in all the same creative processes, given a good enough system that provides interesting prompts and challenges, and that reacts to your input in a clever enough way.

    \n The weight that the Forge placed on the social aspect of RPGs was there because Forge theory was largely intended as "hey we should all be on the same page to enjoy the game", it was a result of investigation of broken actual play. If you're playing alone, then you don't have to agree or compromise with anyone but yourself, that's considerably easier to do. In fact it's neglectable. So the Forge theory doesn't really take solo play into consideration. That doesn't mean it's not possible.

    People have been playing solo for ages. The old Red Box included a solo introductory adventure. I'd venture a guess that people have been just writing up characters, creating worlds and rolling random encounters all by themselves since the 70's. It depends what you consider to be "play" in this case. RPGs most certainly include plenty of opportunities for "lonely fun" from the get-go. Is that integral to the whole, or something you can ignore?

    As I said in Per's thread, I think RPGs don't need two or more people, but they do need a dialogue (and an imaginary space). Having more than one person is the easiest way to get a dialogue, but you can have it with a game text or game rules or some randomly generated feedback or even yourself.

    Going by the lumpley principle, system is how we agree to what happens in the game and a replacement of the (group's) default interactions. It's super-easy to agree with myself, so I could just agree with everything that happens in my imagination and then I guess it's simple daydreaming. So the role of the system here is to "disagree" with me, to replace my default agreement with myself, to introduce elements that are against my natural overwhelming unity of consent. So me and the game are now in a dialogue: I can roleplay, put myself in the shoes of a character and decide what he does and the game must respond with things that are not what I'd otherwise simply imagine, and I respond to that, etc.

    So I believe solo play is entirely possible (interestingly enough I don't consider crpgs to be "real" rpgs). I don't think it appeals much to me, and it will never be big, but as Graham says I think it's useful to think about it and explore this territory.

    \n I've been working for a while now on an article identifying player motives. It's a little more focused on LARP than tabletop play, primarily because I think there are a lot of potential player motives in LARP that haven't been identified and discussed well to date.

    I've got a list of about 16 so far. They're sort of a combination between Stance and Creative Agenda, because I find those to be too high level.

    Anyway, several of the motives can be satisfied in solo play and several can't.

    So mostly I'm reiterating the idea that people don't identify solo RPGs as valid because they are driven by motives that can't possibly be satisfied in solo play.

    And yes, I'll post the article when it's ready. I hope this group finds it worthy of discussion, even though it isn't developed from a StoryGame-centric perspective.

    In fact, I might post an in-progress version soonish to see if the StoryGame crowd has some ideas to help firm up some of the concepts I'm presenting.
