\n Having wondered why I lost interest in Warhammer 40K the miniatures game long ago, I have continued to wonder why I still am interested in the setting and current minis. Warhammer 40,000 Rogue Trader was my entry into wargaming back in 1988. I had collected minis for RPGs before, but Rogue Trader was the first game I bought minis for to move around on a board with measuring tapes and fists full of d6s.

Alas my interest faded even before Warhammer 40K 2nd Edition came out. Rogue Trader had become 2E and was no longer a small skirmish game, but now a battle game. I still bought fluff and new minis although I no longer played. Necromunda came out, but it was gangs set in 40K, not 40K proper. I wanted me some piratical space elves not Shakespearian Italian gangstas.

Then as GW brought out the Lord of the Rings game, they brought out Inquisitor. Ignoring the beautiful, but limited 54mm miniatures range, the game squarely aimed at people like me. But the rules were dodgy, needlessly complex and incomplete. It looked and played like someone had tried to take Rogue Trader and mix it with Warhammer Fantasy Role-Play with the WD Confrontation rules sprinkled on it like like glitter at a princess party. Nice big minis though...

Then last year GW released Shadow War Armageddon, Necromunda with real 40K armies. It was self-contained and not just an add-on like Kill Team. It was exactly Necromunda and all that entails (swingy mechanics, weird momentum,...). Still it was a squad with XP and skills, but...

Still reading? Want me to get to the point?

It was time for my yearly flip through Rogue Trader and convincing myself that you couldn't play it today because...reasons. While doing a bit of research I found a bunch of interviews with Rick Priestly (the author and person most responsible for 40K). Seems he always intended Rogue Trader to be run as an RPG--with multiple figures. Your squad was your character or party if you will. It's not a mistake Rogue Trader needs a game master.

Then I looked back at Inquisitor. Also requires a GM, also harkens back to the origins of 40K as an RPG. Forget SWA for now, we'll get back to that.

Seems 40K was always meant to be played with your mates with dice in one hand, brew in the other and tongue firmly in cheek. This was the part that tied everything together. I had fallen in love with a game where each miniature had a name and we cheered or lamented the death of characters rather than models. Each figure had their own history and sense of continuity. Then 40K became a war-game proper and I lost interest in it.

What I yearn for is a game with the ideas of Warhammer 40,000 Rogue Trader, but some how better realized. The closest would be Shadow War Armageddon, but it is still rooted in competitive battle games, the skill list giving away what the focus should be about.

Somewhere between Rogue Trader, Inquisitor and Shadow War Armageddon lies a great way to tell stories in the 41st millennium of mankind with little plastic elf dollies. 30 years after the event , at least I now know what I'm looking for.

    \n While there are other issues, the real world expense related to the elf dollies is part of the problem in creating minis-using RPGs vs. making viable minis wargames.

    ( And for a long time, GW minis were at the more expensive end of the spectrum for readily available minis. Now other companies have caught up with them, so they all tend to be rather pricey, with a few notable exceptions)

    I've spilled a lot of electrons over this issue, but truthfully have never found even a vaguely satisfactory answer that uses actual miniatures.

    The best, closest thing I can find is something like:

    Start with a wargame genre/period that people are already interested in, that uses individually based minis at least at 28mm scale ( or larger).

    Now use that as the basis of the RPG.

    The second best thing I could come up with is: make an RPG that uses cheap Heroclix (and other Clix ) minis as the bulk of the desired minis.

    However, the fundamental issue I was getting at is that in minis wargames, especially GW made games, the SOP is that every player will buy the stuff for their own personal team, and thus players provide the opposition for one another.

    My Monster Menz fight Your Monster Menz.

    The SOP for RPGs is... the players provide their personal character and associated NPCs, the GM provides everything else.

    Generally speaking, this is a non-viable set up if miniatures are involved for reasons that should be fairly self evident.

    If it isn't actually self evident, I will break it out a bit more.

    \n The barrier to entry that miniatures pose is something that I've been thinking about a lot.

    What about designing the game so that NPCs are represented by a chess set's pieces? This gives you two factions of 16 characters each, including six different "classes" of character.

    You could play with a chess set, although I appreciate that that's no better than chips and papercraft. But you could also assemble a couple of "factions" of miniatures (16 miniatures each) and I think you'd have enough for several games.

    Keep a chess set on hand as well for odds and ends.

    Players would be responsible for providing their own character's miniature.

    For example, I don't know much about Rogue Trader or the like, but from the bits I've picked up about Warhammer 40K, you could have:

    Black: The Planet's Authorities
    King: Archivist
    Queen: Inquisitor
    Rooks: Space Marines
    Bishops: Commissars
    Knights: Alien Ambassadors
    Pawns: Imperial Guard

    White: The Underbelly
    King: Crime Boss
    Queen: Sewer Monster
    Rooks: Smugglers
    Bishops: Psykers
    Knights: Genestealer Cultists
    Pawns: Ruffians

    I feel like that set of 32 miniatures would be able to cover all kinds of shenanigans and battles, and also situations where the PCs might hire some Ruffians or befriend a Psyker or have to escort an Archivist, etc.

    Then if you wanted to introduce a third faction, you could start using the chess pieces, and then as you build up the miniatures you could sub them in.

    This model would also work well if each player has a miniatures-collecting responsibility: you could say each player is responsible for one of the factions, and they would know that their contribution is capped at 16 miniatures - they can plan ahead and not feel ambushed if a subsequent adventure suddenly involves 30 kobolds.

    \n While I would disagree with using chess pieces for the reason you already mentioned, the overall concepts of

    1) Building around a smallish, flexible collection that can be used for lots of related things, repeatedly, and as-needed, and

    2) Dividing up the collecting duties in some reasonable, organized fashion are both very solid concepts.

    From looking at these kinds of questions a bit, the types of games that are problem children for using minis are

    A) Settings with a very broad universe of critters and/or significantly rising power levels ( D&D is the obvious poster child for this problem).

    [If you truncate the critter list and have minis that can be re-used, this is less of a problem. 3rd/4th Age Middle Earth or the Game of Thrones universe would both be vastly simpler to do than generic D&D for a variety of reasons. From a slightly different angle, Lankhmar or Sanctuary would also be easier]

    B ) Settings with a very specific visual style ( To an extent, the 40K universe has this problem, as does something like Star Trek)

    [This can be mitigated simply by being a bit flexible on look and going for the more small scale and behind the frontlines stuff, more Inquisitor than Death Watch, for example]


    Things that keep a budget down:

    1) Games that have a lot of civilian interaction and where minis are produced for those civilians at a reasonable cost ( hopefully by a few different companies)

    So, some historical or semi-historical genres ( historical + weird elements would be a common example). Stuff like superheroes ( especially street level supers), or semi-horror monster hunting, Victorian SF/Mystery, some kinds of espionage, that sort of thing.

    For almost anything modern/near future, plundering cheapo-Heroclix/ Other 'Clix and repurposing them ( through repainting and kitbashing as needed) is a good starting place for everything that isn't a PC ( and some things that are).

    2) Settings where we can easily mash different eras and aesthetics

    The Firefly/Serenity universe with it's frontier/Old West look combined with a lesser amount of near future look, and very little obvious SF elements save for the ships themselves would be really easy to put together. A big smashed, kitchen sink universe, like a Gamma World-ish setting would also be very easy to do on a budget, since you really can use almost anything at all, and that opens up all sorts of bargain hunting possibilities.


    3) Settings where the universe is relatively "small"

    If you were to constantly emphasize the villager NPCs from something like Beyond the Wall and Other Tales and have limited "monster" based adventures, you could do that fairly easily on a budget. If the monsters make repeat appearances, even easier.

    Really anything that mainly focusses on a relatively small population of characters ( PCs/NPCs/Monsters) in a relatively smallish geographic area ( a single town/city/spaceship) is going to be easier to get going and to expand on.

    Okay, so why do I keep mentioning that one game?

    GASLIGHT is a pretty generic Victorian SF ruleset. It's nice enough, fairly light weight and meant for 2 player up to maybe six players, with forces about the size you'd be used to from the later Rogue Trader minis game rules era. Basically 2-3 squads and maybe a vehicle per player. Like RT, GASLIGHT does have some recognition of miniatures as individual characters, especially leader/officer/hero types ( who sometimes have some distinct special abilities as well).

    Those are the core rules. Like RT, it drops them in your lap and tells you to figure out how you want to use them.

    To Be Continued By Gaslight takes those core rules, modifies them a bit, drops the action back to something similar to Necromunda/GorkaMorka/Inquisitor/Legends of Blank scale of action. Players are generally using a main character or two and some lesser, good guy mooks. There are some rules for going with fewer but more competent characters, or more lesser characters ( Sgt So-and so and his squad, for example). Again, the core rules are solid, bland-ish skirmish rules. Functional, but not really anything to write home about.

    Where it sings is in its conceit that the campaign being played is an old serial movie, to be played across 12 chapters.

    It is those rules that I think you should check out. They suddenly make everything else viable.

    Within a chapter of the overall story/adventure/movie/campaign/serial, you pretty much just plain play it like a one-off skirmish battle. You have a GM who is coming up with the overall, broad strokes of the adventure, but really only has broad ideas ( and a related set of toys for terrain and maybe some baddies).

    It's up to the players and GM collectively to make the main characters, certainly the Good Guys, but maybe also some player played baddies or rivals as well. Or separate teams of Good Guys. Whatever.

    The Gm is really supposed to come up with the road outline of the serial and an opening chapter or two, then pretty much just use random charts, events in the previous chapter, some random tables, and the constraints of their toy collection to build the rest of it largely ad hoc, from session to session. And, possibly with help from the players, to string the developing narrative together, at least enough to make one skirmish/chapter tie in to the next one.

    Also, as the campaign/serial starts to near the final chapters, to start pulling the various strings of previous chapters together towards a satisfying and climactic final pair of chapters.

    Main characters can "die", but mostly in the old serial, cliffhanger sense, as paired with basic minis skirmish rules. In other words, if the mechanics of the game say that Indiana Jones takes a bullet and dies, he's dead. For that chapter/skirmish. He isn't dead-dead though unless it's the final chapter. And maybe not then, Mostly it's up to the players. If they want to stop playing that character that "dies" they're dead until the player decides otherwise. Because, hey... Source Material Emulation!

    Beyond that, there are some other great ( or at least neat) things going on. Players
     can compete on the campaign level for Victory Points. Yes, they get them through their miniatures/characters, but they belong to the player.

    This means that if one player is always playing the heroes, but other players are bouncing from various NPCs and/or villains over the course of the game's chapters, they still personally get victory points.

    No surprise, Victory Points can be traded in prior to the end of the game for character upgrades and rerolls and such, so there's a balancing element ( some may find it frustrating. I think it's neat).

    What I'm pretty sure this means is that you could have Indiana Jones win in-fiction, but have their player use up VPs to do it. The in-fiction result is that IJ and pals have once again beaten various villains and ultimately recovered the magical artifact ( satisfying for the emulationist urge) but Bob whop played IJ and pals has 20 VPs while Fred who played various premade/NPCs villains and rivals has 21. Fred has gained the real-world player victory. Weirdly, that should work to influence spending almost exactly in a way that mimics the inspirational materials. Towards the end of the story, the heroes start pulling out all kinds of unlikely successes.

    Anyway, there's a bit more than that to it, but certainly could be great inspiration for an RT inspired minis using RPG.

    \nGood tip, komradebob. It can be tracked down via the Wayback Machine if people are interested.

    The procedure in In a Grail Epoch is:

    1. Whittle your miniatures collection down to 12
    2. Each player gets one miniature for their character (it's a three-player game), and names them.
    3. The remaining miniatures are the NPCs. They get names and factions (there are three factions, but not necessarily with an equal number of NPCs)

    There's also a map with nine locations, and nine connections between locations. As characters are eliminated from the game, new locations are created until the fourth character is eliminated, at which point new connections between locations are created.
