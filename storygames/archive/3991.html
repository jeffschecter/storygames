\n So, reading the various GenCon threads, there seem to be four published games that are generating the most buzz:

1. Bliss Stage, Ben Lehman's robots-and-relationships game (which, as I understand it, comes out in its full form later this year)

2. Grey Ranks, Jason's teenager's-at-war game

3. Steal Away Jordan, Julia's game about slaves and kidnapped glamour models.

4. Poison'd, Vincent Baker's game about pirates (although I'm not quite sure whether this is an ashcan or not).

If ashcans counted, then Acts of Evil would leap into that list. (In my head, ashcans are games-in-development, so they're not game games yet, although I'm excited about lots of them.)

Fair summary? Anything else that should go on there: not because you think it's great but because everyone's talking about it?

Graham

    \n Everyone, from all walks of life, seems to have purchased Colonial Gothic. I want to talk about it (especially its Fate mechanic) soon.

    Also, thanks to Ian Noble, James Brown and me throwing down some Dragons of Kir and being really vocal about the awesome experience (I literally just blurted out how awesome it was to random passersby), about one metric fucktons of people from all walks of life picked it up. It's seriously that cool. All the fun parts of Tsuro (fastness, ease of learning) and the logic-fun of Robo Rally, and the instructions are so tight that we could not find, in play, a time where we didn't know what to do ("What happens if a dragon hit two Area Effect tiles at once?", etc) where the rules did not address it. Which puts this game far above any Steve Jackson game I've ever seen, which is basically a litany of hole-filled rules.

    Posted By: AndyThe instructions are so tight that we could not find, in play, a time where we didn't know what to do ("What happens if a dragon hit two Area Effect tiles at once?", etc) where the rules did not address it. Which puts this game far above any Steve Jackson game I've ever seen, which is basically a litany of hole-filled rules.
    \nSpeaking as a frequent board game designer, I can point to this as proof of rigorous and frequent playtesting. It's all too easy, particularly in an abstract game like DoK, to just write up some rules, do a playtest or two, and leave it at that. (Hell, I've done it for most of my games, but they're released into the wild free-of-charge.) These guys obviously figured out their game inside and out, looking at every permutation and polishing everything until it became an elegant work of design.

    We played a four-player session, which totally voided the warranty and broke the game, but even in that session we could see how excellently all the gears would work together for a proper two-player session.
