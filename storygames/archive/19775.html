    \n Is the d6 meaningfully more accessible, then? I agree that it is somewhat more familiar from boardgames and such, but I'm not sure if that makes it much more accessible. Stealing d6s off old boardgames seems like a 1st, 2nd world kind of thing to do (who else has many old boardgames just lying around?), and I haven't really seen dice sold on their lonesome outside a gambling (a fringe industry everywhere, surely) context, except alongside other gaming paraphernalia, including gaming dice.

    I suppose that a game that only requires one or two d6 and no complex character sheet has a bit of a leg up in material terms on the conventional tabletop roleplaying game. A d6 pool game (which is what a significant number of d6 games are), not so much - having a batch of 20 identical d6s for convenient pool manipulation is just as exotic as having a set of gaming dice, it seems to me.

    The traditional design solution to this issue is the boxed set, which of course is irrelevant for global digital accessibility. On the other hand, when digital access is presumed, you can just use digital randomizers; presumably using a computer application to generate random numbers wont' be an issue for somebody willing to access the game text itself via the Internet.

    For what it's worth, my own "maximally beginner-friendly" tabletop game uses arbitrary randomizers (a single d6 being the textual case) and no character sheets, using the book itself for notes instead. You still need to provide your own pencil, though.

    \n@Eero_Tuovinen
    I wasn't intentionally ignoring your point about computer applications for randomization. It's just that most people here access the internet through feature phones, so random number generators aren't really realistic. Smart phones are wildly popular but still mostly in the hands of upper-middle class adults. Desktop computer access is available in public spaces like schools, libraries, internet cafes, and even family-oriented bathhouses; but you're not going to be able to take one home to play a game with your friends. Desktops in the home are a fairly rare thing. Laptops are available everywhere in business settings and distributed to key employees, but still seen as too valuable to put in the hands of a young person; and private ownership is beyond the means of most adults.

    The usability, or not, of an electronic device for the replacement of dice at the gaming-table is another question beyond the topic of this thread, I think.

    \n As a player, I love rolling the dice. I love the way they seem to have moods, personality, prescience and psychic sensibility.

    As a designer, I love bell curves and consider them vital. The only reason I would use poly dice in a game design would be to generate radically different bell curves without forcing people to do math.

    Of course, not everyone has poly dice, so that's a trade-off, not to be taken lightly. Easier if you're working on a boxed set, you can include the dice with the game. But of course that's not usually the case.

    \n I don't really see something like a bell curve having an objective value: it may be good for some situations but bad for others.

    However, any designer should be aware that there are lots of ways of building in curves and other probabilistic devices. Some are more or less obvious than others.

    For instance, if a game requires you to roll a d6, while I roll my own d6, and we compare results and note the difference (e.g., you won by two points), there's a bell curve in effect. But that fact might not be obvious to the people playing.

    Similarly, having each player draw a card from a deck, and then assembling all the cards drawn into a hand, creates a sort of bell curve.

    And so on.
