\n I'm finishing up my first hobby game and plan on releasing it early to mid next year. I wasn't going to talk about the game's actual mechanics until much closer to the launch... mostly because I thought this was a somewhat novel die system.
\n
\nIt includes a variable dice system rather than a single die unified mechanic like d20. I hadn't seen any other RPGs like this... until this morning.
\n
\nAfter seeing Savage Worlds mentioned on The Evil DM's blog, I decided to Wikipedia it to see what all the fuss was about:\n
\n
Wikipedia wrote:
\n
A character's traits are characteristics that are rated by a single polyhedral die. The more sides the trait is rated in, the better the character is at the trait. So a character with a Strength trait of a ten-sided die (d10) is stronger than a character whose Strength trait is rated with a six-sided die (d6).
\n...
\nDice are rolled to determine the outcome of character actions and interactions in the game. Usually a trait die is rolled against a target number of four. If the roll equals or exceeds the target number, the action succeeds; otherwise it fails.
\n
\n
\nBoth the attributes rated by a single polyhedral die, and the base target of 4 have been central to my game for well over a year now.
\n
\nDoes anyone know of any other games that work like this? How do you like this mechanic compared to games with a unified die mechanic, like d20?
\n
\nThanks!

    \n Both Earthdawn and Savage Worlds have some odd probability problems. So the first thing I'd do is calculate the percentage chance in a number of situations. I've seen quite a few games (most recently JAGS-2) that get horribly slaughtered by mechanics that stop working so well when you look at them closer.

    I also kicked around the idea of a system that went from 1d12 for absolute beginner down to 1d1 for absolute master. It was based around everyone succeeding, you just roll to see when you succeed, so the lower the roll the faster you succeed, and the smaller the die the more likely you are to have lower rolls. Cool idea, the problem was that from 1d1 to 1d2 there's a 50% drop on suceeding on your first try. That just didn't jive particularly well.

    In retrospect I could just have the top ability be 1d2 and the odds would look better, but it illustrates that you can run into some problems with variable dice that aren't obvious on a first glance.

    \nHello Stuart!

    There are many RPGs featuring multi-die mechanics, as others have said. To answer your question, I don't personally like them much, as I often feel like the designers "squeezed" the scale based on the existing polyhedrals. But that's just one guy's opinion.

    Forgive me but I have to share this with you and hope you won't take offense.

    I'm a tiny bit worried that you didn't know there were indeed such mechanics. They've been there for a long, long time. This might mean you've been incredibly unlucky and never stumbled upon one, or it might mean you've not been exposed to many games yet. May I suggest visiting RPG.net and reading lots of reviews? Trust me, it's a good start and will expose you to many games, if only as core concepts. There's also John Kim's list of free RPGs, where you will see all sorts of RPGs in full details, from robust games to lite, from trad to avant-garde. Hanging out on the various forums (RPG.net, RPGsite, The Forge and of course Story Games) will also point you to all sorts of commercial games as well.

    What worries me even more is the fact you were not going to talk about your mechanics until close to launch "because you thought they were novel". That's usually a sign (for me) of a game that will be bland and ultimately disappointing. I mean, even if we assume the core mechanic had been novel, what exactly did you think would happen should you describe it?

    Trust me on this, Stuart. Every day, the various authors and designers are breaking new ground and sharing it... which provides them with feedback to further refine the concepts. The great games nowadays are often those who are designed completely in the open. Heck, more and more authors are actually giving part (or even all) of the game for free and still manage to sell a lot of copies. So the next time you have an idea you think is novel, put it out in the open with a great community like this one (or another) and you might just do yourself and your game a favor, not to mention attract a customer base in the process.

    Hope you don't find the comments above insulting. They aren't meant to be. It's just that in 27 years of gaming, I have seen a lot of people claim to have super secret, super novel engines only to see them bust.

    Nobody is going to steal your ideas. Your ideas don't mean much, like pretty much any ideas anyone might have. It's all going to be in the execution of those ideas.

    Take care and best of luck with your game!

    \n I think Serenity the RPG, Ironclaw (and it's cousin Jadeclaw) use an attribute/skill rated as a die system. I don't own any of these games but this is what I gather from forums and reviews. I'm not sure if they have set target numbers.

    As for the system? I like Savage Worlds. I haven't played it too many times but it is rather fun and I would definitely use it as a stand in for D&D games. In Savage Worlds dice can "ace" meaning that if you roll the max value you can roll again and add the two dice together. Multiple times even. I liked how this turned out in play. We were all pretty competent but there were times when even a lowly NPC gets an amazing hit in.
