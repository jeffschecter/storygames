    \n Those interested in maps as inspiration for world-building in their games, you may want to look at the Strange Maps blog.

    Each entry has an image and explanation of someone's strange map. The choice of maps breaks down as about 10% completely fabricated (e.g., a map of Oz), 20% - 60% interstingly deluded or exaggerated, and the rest documentation of the results of historical anomalies.

    Frankly, while I like browsing through these, I've not found maps as real useful tools in playing story games. I'd be very interested to read (perhaps in a different thread) someone else's experience to the contrary.

    Now if you had the players drawing up the map as a group activity at the game start, complete with all these little bizzaro bits,thatwould be neat. (In fact, I have this in Console Legends already, though with much less initial detail.)
    \nJack Aidley's Angles of Tengut, a Reverse Engineer Challenge game started the players off by drawing a map of the realm, complete with villages, castles, roads, rivers, and so on. Then the players proceeded to populate the villages, and castles with people and problems. Game play started, and the heroes went about trying to solve those same problems they just created. Pretty neat actually. I can think of many extrapolations of this idea for other games.
