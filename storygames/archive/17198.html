\n "The orc stabs you, you bleed and might die."

Is one more inherently obnoxious to have to go through as a player? Is the second crossing a boundary that the first does not?

    \n The first one is about what happens to the characters physical body. It is very plausible that the characters themself does not control this situation and can be killed against their will.

    The second one is about what happens to the characters emotional life, and also tells the players that the character has acted on that emotion. (Betraying the secret.) A character may not be in control of their emotional life (who is?) but they are in control of what they tell other persons, despite of those emotions. Emotions might tempt them to take certain actions but do not force them to do it.

    Hence, a player might be okay with something happening to the characters body.

    But not be okay with the rules having control over the characters emotional life and/or what actions their character chose to make.

    \n Jaakko Stenros has talked about this (in @lizzie_stark's book):

    --Violence is entertainment only because your average gamer has very little traumatic experience with actual violence that resembles that implemented in RPGs. Most violence is statistically sudden, drunk and domestic. Violence in war is alternatingly chaotic and boring. Thanks to the demands of players, violence in RPGs is both cool and objective as well as providing short-term emotional rewards (in the rolling of dice). So both kids and veterans can play D&D 'cause they feel like they're in control.

    --Sex is not entertainment only because your average gamer has all too much experience with emotional entanglements, so love/sex has this "everyday" feel to it, despite the allure of sex as an apposite story element in many settings. So both kids and veterans don't quite know how to place the erotic within their fiction, because they don't have a lot of control over that in their own lives.

    \n I don't think either roll is inherently objectionable. It depends on what kind of game the players want to play. Clearly, we can define boundaries that the second one crosses which the first one doesn't, and vice versa.

    What both rolls have in common is that they introduce a fictional element, and do not require the player's agreement. One way in which they differ, as @w176 indicates, is that one addresses the body of the PC, and the other addresses their power of choice. That might make enough of a difference for some players. This isn't the distinction that hits my squick button personally, though.

    Another way in which they differ, is that one element has a violent theme, and the other has a sexual theme.

    Someone who is disturbed by violence but has an open attitude to sexual themes might be disturbed by a graphic stabbing, but not by a seduction.

    On the other hand, someone who is disturbed by unwelcome and un-agreed-to sexualizing of social interactions might be disturbed by the second move, even though the fictional situation it describes is a consensual one, because the theme has been introduced to the game without their agreement as a player. This is where my squick boundary lies, personally. (I'm perfectly happy with AW's special moves, because those require the mutual agreement of the players.)

    As long as players understand the moves of the game before they agree to play it, what kinds of rolls can come up or are likely to come up, I don't see a general problem with games that include either kind of rule. But I think no one should be surprised if there are people who are okay playing a game that includes rolls like the first one but not okay playing games that include rolls like the second.

    \n First, Adam, I am picking up what you are putting down.

    Second, I am working on a game that addressed that very question. Also, this "Most violence is statistically sudden, drunk and domestic. Violence in war is alternatingly chaotic and boring."

    Third, fundamentally, neither of those is all that different. We lean on contemporary myths about personal agency, despite two hundred years of social science quietly chucking at the notion, whether that's in sociology, psychology, neuro-science, economics or political science. We are not in control of our emotions or actions; during episodes of trauma, we dissociate from our psyche.

    Even when brutally faced with our precarious mortality and physicality, we'd rather dissociate our bodies from our being than admit a lack of control. You know, Matthew 26:41, Watch and pray, that ye enter not into temptation: the spirit indeed is willing, but the flesh is weak.

    RPGs don't deal with that, especially traditional ones. Mostly they reproduce Hollywood action films, with all the incumbent cultural baggage.

    Forth, when one starts running games for non-gamers, non-nerds, one starts to find that the permissive violence is what bothers people, and less the sex.

    \n In the example I offered in the other thread, quoted above, there were a couple different things going on, and I think it might be problematic to mix them up here.

    First, there was the player's annoyance with having agency taken from him in a manner inconsistent with the rules and generally understood principles of the game.

    And second, there was the player's discomfort about being led into a sex scene with his friend.

    These were very different things. If I may elaborate:

    On the topic of rules, principles, and agency: The game in question was In Nomine, in which characters play angels and demons. This scenario involved a demon using a totally non-supernatural skill (Seduction) against an angelic PC. In Nomine's rules are quite clear (though we didn't recall this at the time) that Seduction CANNOT be used against PCs; players always get the final say in what their characters feel or believe, as long as they have control of their own mind. Of course, you aren't always in control of your own mind. If this NPC had used a supernatural power to force the PC to fall in love with her (which she definitely could have done if the NPC had been built that way), the player would not have been mad about the loss of agency any more than he'd be upset about losing in a combat scene. My players are great about rolling with the punches whenever demons manipulate them, and roleplaying accordingly.

    This is not an unusual principle of game design or gameplay: Apocalypse World is a great example of this being codified in the rules, as there are totally different versions of the Seduce and Manipulate skill as used on PCs versus NPCs, and the one for PCs lets players DECIDE whether they're persuaded/seduced/manipulated/whatever. Anybody can shoot or stab you without your permission, steal from you without permission, or spit in your food without permission, but nobody can definitely say "this is what you think/feel/believe." That's not a distinction between sex and violence; it is a distinction between "things external to my mind" and "things internal to my mind." Some games ARE built such that emotional manipulation is mechanically indistinguishable from physical conflict, but this wasn't one of them.

    On the topic of sex making the player uncomfortable: Yeah, that still would've happened even if it were the result of a supernatural power. But I think that says more about my friend's GMing style and my other friend's sense of personal boundaries than it does about anything generalizable in terms of game mechanics.

    \n Also, a brief follow-up to address the example from the original post:

    It is a false comparison to draw a parallel between "the spy seduces your secrets out of you" and "the orc stabs you, you bleed and might die." The former scenario presumes how a character would react; the latter does not. For this to be parallel, the latter scenario would have to be something like, "the orc stabs you, and you grab a nearby child to shield you from its next attack." In most games I play, at least, I'd say the player would be within her rights to say in these scenarios, "Uh, no. No, that's not what I'd do at all." That's the GM overstepping a boundary and narrating what PCs do, which is just as problematic as a player overstepping a boundary and narrating what NPCs do.
