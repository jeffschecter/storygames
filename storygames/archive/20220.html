So if I understand the book, inspiration isn\u2019t a number, you either have it or you don\u2019t have it.

This caused the problem that when the players already had insp, you
couldn\u2019t hand it out again. If player\u2019s are regularly using their
insp, they can do things to re-gain their insp, but if they aren\u2019t,
the inspiration can stop flowing. Which kind of makes it so that the rule doesn\u2019t exist.

Here\u2019s my solution. I have a huge bowl of tokens (I use black go
stones) that represent insp. I give them out to players and they can
have any number of tokens. Already inspired but does something that
deserves an insp token? Here, have it.

However, when you *use* your insp or when you give your insp away, you
give away all of it!
So it\u2019s still the same as in the book: you have insp or you don\u2019t have insp.

But that one insp can be represented by one or five or fifteen tokens,
or any number.

I use the mental model of the insp being like jelly. You can get jelly
pieces but when you receive them, they \u201cstick together\u201d.

This way of representing insp is only cosmetic. There\u2019s no rules
changes. It might seem that it doesn\u2019t do any difference, but it has
made a lot of difference. It\u2019s easier to remember to hand out insp
once you can do it freely, without considering whether or not the
player already had insp. I\u2019ve also seen players grab insp from the
bowl and give to each other.

    \n I played the first part of my 5e campaign without using too much BIFTs nor alignment, and now I'm regretting it. Except maybe for alignment. Last week I had to talk to the players about their BIFTs, because I realized that if players don't compromise into playing a stable character, it's like the GM not compromising into creating a story that makes sense.

    While that's doable (and probably incrediby gonzo-like fun), that's entirely a different game. Conversely, if everyone compromises to keep coherence (players keep their characters coherent and the GM keeps the world coherent) it's okey if the players go full murderhobo: the GM can just follow them popping up interesting consequences for their acts and yes, give them inspiration for creating scenes in a "Natural Born Killers" fashion. Of course, as long as everyone (including the GM) is comfortable with that.

    Or, they can play LG or CG heroes in any fashion they want.

    I'd certainly like to use the Inspiration rule and this is a cool option, but I'd need to find a convincing way to explain the players that more inspiration tokens doesn't change the effect of the current Inspiration rule at all. Otherwise they will probably roleplay only when they have spent it... which in turn may make some players less prone to hoard the spotlight.

    \n Good thread.

    I've been mulling over tokens. Maybe a different colour tokens for each attribute, so players can choose to gain Inspiration by dumping all their red tokens for advantage on that STR roll but, in this example, keep their blue INT tokens in reserve?

    But really, the beauty of the system is in Keeping It Simple, Silly. Multiple tokens are satisfying so long as they don't make a sprawling sub-game out of the process.

    A satisfying play-tool could be to have Inspiration indicators (green/red cards players can flip, or something) which would add meat to the process of checking/unchecking the INSP tickbox.

    And if you were looking to add a little more to it you could even go as far to rule that no one can spend their INSP until everyone has it: So, hypothetically, players should be keen to flip their card and then move the spotlight to whoever's struggling to "go green." And players who don't want to spend their INSP when all the lights are on get rewarded by having their cards already green when the next round begins.
    The fictional context for this, if you're asking Richardo, would be the party's teamwork and coordination getting results - and I was always into D&D being about The Team rather than The Hero anyway.

    \n I found out that dungeon crawling without BIFTs nor alignment is totally fine, but by the time the sessions become a campaign, players should really start to fill those or the game runs the risk of going the way of the murderhobo fest, if players decide that their character motivations change from one moment to another and that in-fiction consequences of their actions aren't subject to moral or natural consequences.

    Bear with me for a moment, I'll try to explain. This happened to my party, composed half by newcomers and half by hardcore D&D players. One of the hardcore players (now absent from the group because of work) was a natural born murderhobo. I'm not sure how much of his behavior became the norm for the rest of the players, even when he was gone; but I believe that as we were playing a campaign without BIFTs nor alignments, even without him it would have been the natural course of the game seeing the PCs start to play as chaotic evil guys. One or two of the other hardcore players started to play as if he was chaotic good in reaction to that, but left to their own devices, probably all the new players would be now pillaging through the setting instead of doing quests.

    This idea of mine has its origin in the real world: criminals are usually people whose principles doesn't consider respect for others in many different levels, nor think much of the consequences of their actions. Take principles out of people, have them relax about the seriousness of the consequences in the short and long term and probably half of them or more will find hilarious to release their stress through a life of crime and gratuitous violence. At least until their needs are satisfied, being the more important for them, their need for fun.

    Despite my impression on what happened with my group (I might be wrong, probably their reaction was just the result of miscommunication and my lack of skills portraying non-threathening NPCs), I actually agree that this is something that shouldn't be pushed. Even playing the game in murderhobo-mode isn't "playing it wrong" (some people just love GTA and Saints row) but another form of fun. It's just that BIFTs do help a lot the GM to provide material to create a consistent story around the PCs and keep giving the players quests and situations that actualy catch their interest. I take them as a compromise the players make to have somewhat stable characters, whatever they choose to be.
