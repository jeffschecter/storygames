\n This is not strictly a TSoY question, but TSoY answers are very welcome:

I'm designing a system where one roll normally settles things, but the loser may choose to "bring down the pain" and extend the conflict to two or more rolls.

Here's my question to any of you who have played in similar systems (though TSoY is the only one I know--maybe Sorcerer as well):

In such a system it seems like someone with a very high ability has a huge advantage: whenever someone with a low ability enters conflict with them and wins, the high ability character can extend the conflict until their luck starts to even out (many more rolls mean closer to average results) and the weaker party has expended whatever resources they can bring to bear. After several rolls, and once the weaker party is beginning to run out of steam, the stronger party is very likely to win in the end (assuming they haven't been knocked out of the fight yet).

Is this how it feels in play? What are the ramifications of designing such a system? Is it good or bad?

    \n Yeah, in TSoY that's intentional. The basic conflict system is pretty quick and goes over fast, so the inherent randomness isn't so good at all times. TSoY in general puts a lot of stock to after-fortune result manipulation, which I like myself: the players often only really consider whether they want to win or lose a given conflict after they've seen the dice and know whether they need to think about it.

    A more important point is the matter of pacing the fiction, though. Extending conflicts is something you only do for conflicts you care about. In practical play I've felt that this has impacted player choices more than the fortune-lessening effect.

    \n I have a related (i.e. consciously informed by BDTP) dynamic in The Rustbelt. Conflicts are run in a series of exchanges, in which each person does one thing, and each exchange is resolved by a two step process of dice and a mechanic called the Push, which basically is buying success when your roll fails, and the Price for it hurts. A success puts the hurt on the other guy pretty seriously; Pushing puts the hurt on you, sometimes quite seriously (depending on the margin of failure). In most combat situations, both sides can succeed, if one makes a successful roll and the other Pushes, so then they're both in a world of hurt.

    The conflict, as a whole, is not resolved by the dice. It's resolved by the players, through voluntary acquiescence. F'rinstance, a fight ends when one guy decides to stay down and the other guy decides to stop kicking him (or, more often, when one guy runs away and the other decides to stop chasing, or one guy begs for his life and the other decides he doesn't want to kill him). What the dice and the Push do is leave lasting effects that compromise the character, thus putting the issue of acqueiscence in a certain perspective. It's all bargaining, pressure, and threat.

    What it comes down to is resolve. How much are you willing to lose to win this fight? The bottom line of this is that, regardless of the involved characters' actual power levels, the winner is 90% of the time the guy who is willing to suffer the most in the process, the guy who needs it the most.

    EDIT: What I'm saying here is, to equalize between different levels of power, make MOTIVATION centrally important.

    \n Yes, the more skilled character is almost guaranteed to win an extended conflict. The question is, is he prepared to take the 2-3 points of Harm he's likely to collect in the process? The dice penalties (or even the loss of Pool needed to "heal" them) can be crippling.     I think that's the answer to the question. Like Burning Wheel "Duel of Wits" compromises or even just D&D hit points, the question isn't always IF you can win, but at what PRICE.

    \n Only PCs (as I recall) can BDTP, so if a more powerful character is bringing down the pain, that means a PC lost and wants another shot at winning. If I'm GMing, that's awesome! The only time you might really encounter the issue you're describing is in a PC vs. PC situation, since that's the only time both sides can potentially BDTP. And, in those situations, ideally your social contract -- and not the incentives in the rules -- will help decide if BDTP is appropriate or if the less powerful PC deserves to win (if temporarily). This holds true if people are fighting to the death or just playing poker.

    \n In the Rustbelt, either:

    1. Both die at the same time
    2. One dies, and then the other goes into shock (and will die shortly if he doesn't receive emergency medical attention)
    3. One dies, and the other has been quite terribly maimed (but is at least alive)

    The procedures that differentiate between these are all Drama mechanics, informed by ablative resources. See, you are mortally wounded/go into shock when you hit 20 "Blood" damage. More than that and you're dead outright, but you actually decide how you allocate your damage (the attacker determines how much), so that doesn't happen unless you're complicit with it somehow.

    Now, at 20 Blood, you're dying, and you can't do anything, unless you do a Last Push. The Last Push, like all Pushes, requires that you meet one of the following criteria, all but one of which draw from non-ability stuff written on your character sheet:

    1. You are acting to pursue your Hunger (the character's defining conceptual goal)
    2. You are acting to get a fix for a Vice
    3. You are acting to uphold or prove a Faith
    4. You used Vice or Faith to cope & steady your nerve
    5. You are acting to achieve absolution or redemption for a Woe
    6. You are acting to save someone you care about

    The Last Push lets you accomplish any one action automatically, but you have to die in the process.

    Nobody has actually used it yet, though.

    \n I can only speak in terms of TSoY on this one, but the text of that book points out that "human motivation" is important for the system to work. That's why you can't BDtP against crocodiles or cliff faces or tentacular horros. Just "people" (however weird they might look). Part of that is becuase generally, people don't want to kill each other. They want the other guy to stop trying to kill them. They want the other guy to stop sleeping with their spouses. They want the other guy to give them their wallet.

    So if the fight goes on for a bit and one side starts losing the advantage, maybe he starts running, or begging for forgiveness, or he tosses his wallet to the ground and cowers beside a garbage can.

    On the other hand, if the fight really is between two guys who will happily die if only they can take the other guy with them, then it's probably going to be a drawn-out affair and the less-skilled guy is probably going to bite it.

    EDIT: And that's what he gets for being stupid.

    \n Paul, I've played in 2 TSoY campaigns and didn't enjoy BDtP as an extended conflict mechanic for exactly the reasons you bring up at the start of this thread. Although I would like to note that I enjoy the rest of TSoY.
    \n
    \nPotential solutions for a BDtP type mechanic when sides are not evenly matched really depend on the style of game you are designing. In a game where you generally want to see the underdog get their come back, you could reward them resources for failing rolls. So they build momentum that would eventually allow them to topple a more skilled opponent. In a game that rewards player skill, you could involve some sort of rock, paper, scissor mechanic where hidden tactical choices can even the odds. Alternately, I like Dimfrost's solution of requiring a change of arena when extending conflicts. And then allow competitors to try to shift the focus to play off each other's strengths and weaknesses.

    \n HeroQuest has this in its extended contest mechanic. For those not in the know HQ has two contest types, simple contests and extended. Simple settles the whole thing in one roll, in an extended contest we play out each action and counter-action. In an extended contest you have a pool of action points equal to your ability. Each round you bid a number of points from your pool. If you win your opponent subtracts a multiple of your bid from his pool. Lose, and you lose a multiple of points from your pool. Your bid represents how risky the action you narrate is.

    Now the same logic applies, the longer the contest the greater the benefit to the higher skill. Over time they should average more wins. But the bidding system lets you alleviate that disadvantage. If you have a significantly lower skill you want to make high bids every round, so keeping the contest short. So it rewards throwing caution to the wind and launching an all out attack instead of settling in for a war of attrition.

    The draft of HeroQuest 2 drops the pool based extended contests in favor of what is call the scored mechanic. In the new system there is just one roll for each round instead of action and counter-action. The results are compared on a table. The loser gets 1-4 strikes against him. The first person to accumulate 5 strikes loses. But the system of risky action still pervades and you can up the stakes on your action, so that your losses and gains are doubled (risky behavior does not need to be symmetrical)

    \n One thing I ran into with Dirty Secrets when I was playtesting (not sure if this ended up in the final document), but if someone lost the initial conflict, they very rarely "pushed" into a second conflict because our reading of the rules were such that they would enter the second conflict with far less resources than the first. Death spirals, in other words, will stop people from escalating/proceeding past the first conflict.
