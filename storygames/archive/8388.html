\nIn this post I pointed out that I thought that Jesse's understanding of Sim was occluded by a body of theory that focuses on types of play other than what is rather formlessly considered to be Sim.

Well, I say considered to be...

The problem, from where I'm sitting, is that the heritage of the Forge both in terms of theory and game design has created a perspective that focuses upon tightly focused play (with narrativist and gamist sympathies by and large). However, as someone who games regularly but has never really enjoyed many indie games, I think that the non-focused, conflicting games that most gamers engage in deserve some thought other than "they're broken and need to be fixed".

So I thought I'd share some of my observations on my regular game and see what you people think. Apologies if they're not completely clear as I've only really started formulating them recently.

To start with, I consider myself a 'casual gamer'. By this I mean that as a player, my main aim in most games is the social side of things. I like cracking jokes, tossing ideas around and I like the structured environment that a regular game brings with it. By making this clear, I'm hoping to make clear my perspective on games as well as what my 'creative agenda'. I don't really get on with immersing myself in my character and the 'game' aspects of roleplaying such as combat and rules tweaking bore me.

This means that I tend to think about my game not in theoretical terms or even in terms of pre-theoretical gaming folk wisdom such as 'railroading vs. freedom' or 'role vs. role'. I think about the dynamics of my game in terms of the social dynamics of the group. When things go right I think in terms of people being in good moods and when things go badly I think of people being unhappy, of key personalities not being at the table and of clashes between personalities.

For me, this is the level at which sessions are made and broken. Not at the level of failures in social contract, clashing creative agendas or any of the other diagnostic theoretical concepts that are floating around out there. Despite having a background in philosophy and a largely theoretical PhD, I've never really embraced theory because it has always struck me as an attempt to reinvent the wheel; a dressing up of straight-forward intuitively understood human problems in language about games. I think that whether you are bowling, swinging, sewing or playing D&D, Vampire or DitV the same social forces hold and so the same types of explanation are required. In short :


System does not matter. Rules do not matter. Content does not matter. People matter.

That's observation one.


Observation two flows from the fact that most gaming is unfocused and features conflict between the players and GM as they try to impose upon the game their expectations for the genre or for what will be fun.

Forge theory describes this as malfunctioning gaming. I would argue that it is standard at most tables and as such is normal gaming and I think it is unfortunate that most theory has come to focus not upon understanding this type of gaming but rather upon understanding the more narrowly focused type of gaming you see embodied in most indie games.

For me, any group has internal power struggles. The balance of power within most social groups is forever shifting in accordance with people's moods, people's energy levels, people's recent history and the situation at hand. When a group makes decisions it feeds all of these factors in along with a host of others and some people decide to stand their ground, others decide to argue and eventually a decision is made but once that decision is made, the situation changes and the balance of relationships is adjusted again.

As this is normal in most social groups, it is also normal in most gaming groups.

The GM arrives at the table with an idea in mind, one player just wants to kill things, another wants to crack jokes, another thinks they want the same thing as the GM but actually is drawing on different source material or has just misunderstood the email describing tonight's game. When the group sit down to play, the balance of power representing the dominant creative agenda enters into a state of constant flux. Out of this chaos, the game's flavour and themes and quality emerges. Or, to put it another way...

Games emerge solely from group interaction. A game has no existence in the head of the GM, the players, the designer or in the text of the rulebook. Its character, form and quality is entirely the product of the group in play.

Nobody is really writing articles describing the current state of indie game theory in this community, but it's certainly at a very different place than it was several years ago. The only way you can really keep up with it is following a variety of forums, blogs, playtests, and newly published games. Even then, it's really, really hard.

    \n Sorry for going silent. Deadlines to meet. I shall respond to some of your comments :-)

    Mike -- I am as flexible as a reed and if people want to 'talk me down' from those assertions I'm happy to discuss the matter.


    Jonathan -- I'm a big fan of the Lumpley Principle and it has always struck me as the best idea to come out of the Forge. However, I think that to a certain extent it does not go far enough as it is still invoking these nebulous concepts such as "social contract". That's a concept ported across from political philosophy but it's not clear to me the role played by that idea in every day social interaction. My view is that if games are all about social interaction then a separate body of theory (separate from social psychology and sociology) is muddying the waters at best and re-inventing the wheel at worse.

    I don't think that conceptual analysis or abstract talk can resolve any of the questions you raise. I think they vary from group to group and the answers lie within the wider tapestry of human social rules. For example, just how much a GM can mess with you after fumbling an etiquette role seems to me precisely the same question as to how much your friends can mock you after you do something stupid like spill your drink down yourself or accidentally call the teacher 'mom'. There's a point at which 'playful teasing' becomes 'being a dick' and I don't think that line is determined by what a group is doing. Hence my view that system, rules and content of game do not matter.


    Graham -- My practical view is that system doesn't in fact matter but as part of a self-fulfilling prophecy it does. In other words, people arrive at the game with a set of preconceptions and they play those preconceptions. It's like the idea that Cthulhu is all about the slow decline into death and madness or that Warhammer is all about grubbing a living as a member of a fantasy renaissance society. In truth, both games can be played as heroic combat games but I think that people read what they want into the text of the game. The author is dead and so on...

    As a result, when I GM I treat system a bit like kosher food. I don't think there's any real difference between kosher food and non-kosher food but I realise that as with religious custom, there's a set of symbols at play that means something to someone and I am happy to pay lip service to their irrational beliefs ;-)


    Logos     -- I think that there is some conflating of game stuff and people stuff or at least a belief that talking about game stuff is the same as talking about people stuff. For example, the idea of focused thematic play is supposed to do away with conflicting creative agendas but this idea completely ignores that there's still going to be conflict in the group regarding whether or not a game is actually played. I don't think there's a meaningful difference between "Steve wants to track up in the hills and hunt gnolls but I want to roleplay flirting with prostitutes" and "Steve wants to play Gnoll: the Hunting this week and I want to play Hooker: the flirting". I think that focused systems merely kick creative agenda conflict upstairs. Nothing is resolved.

    Creative agenda conflict is more a question of personality clash than anything else.


    Accounting     -- Quite so. It's the same as literary criticism; 'system matters' is a form of essentialism suggesting that buried within the words that make up the rulebook is some true form that can be understod or misunderstood. I'm more of a modern critic who thinks that the only thing that actually exists is how people interpret the text of the rules. I don't think it's possible to misunderstand a system as system has no meaning and no existence beyond the people using it.


    Sean -- I'm not sure that's helpful as it's a rather slanted view of the debate. A better way of thinking of it might be that there's a town in which people wander from restaurant to restaurant tapas-style having a dish here and a dish there until a couple of chefs say "what's with this wandering around garbage? if you want to eat french then eat a French meal. If you want to eat Chinese then have three courses of Chinese food". My view is that the wandering around having a bit of this and a bit of that is not a failure to eat a style of food, it is a style of dining in and of itself.

    But I tend to find that similies such as these rapidly spiral out of control and before long we'll be discussing the difference between tapas and dim-sum and then the point is lost :-)

    \n Jon,

    I think a lot of your argument that system doesn't matter falls apart when you consider this: a game is a book. A books is made out of two things: the words inside it and the experience you have while reading it.

    Saying that the system doesn't matter except as a self-fulfilling prophesy is like saying that Stephen King is only a horror writer because his books have creepy covers and everyone knows he's a horror writer. By that logic, Stephen King would be just as much a horror writer if he wrote books about happy bunnies, as long as the books had the same covers and people had the same expectations, which would be silly. In fact, Stephen King writes horror because A) he writes creepy words in his book which produce creepy feelings in some of the people who read his books.

    In my experience as a gamer, a gaming book is actually more like a really great cookbook. It is a set of instructions, anecdotes, examples, and abstract advice for taking certain ingredients and producing a certain effect. Of course, you don't need to follow the advice. You can take the advice in the book and do something else with it, but that matters.

    Finally, it matters what you do at the table. Little GM tricks like, say, how rigorously to apply a psychological degeneration mechanic, how often to call for rolls, how powerful you allow starting characters to be, how deadly you make combat... these things work together to produce an experience that we call a game session. A gaming book includes a given set of standards for all those factors and more. If you can produce a given experience by how you apply those things, doesn't it follow that a game can do the same thing through the advice it offers prospective players and GMs?

      I think there are a few situations in which system does matter and resolving the outcome of combat is likely to be one of them simply because it's the one aspect of games that pretty much everyone signs up to when they sit down at the table. A different example might be something like the personality mechanics in Pendragon where people treat them as more or less binding upon their actions depending upon their moods. Similarly, GMs enforce those rules more or less depending upon their mood or their devotion to the system.

      The Zenobia game is actually a good example of what I was thinking of as Runequest is a system where the possibility of a 'total party crippling' is generally counter-acted by healing magics but those parts of the system were not bought into. Similarly, I thought at the time that one of the reasons why that game was playable was the fact that you tended to grant us initiative so we'd charge people down, do 30 points of damage with our lances to people's abdomens and the rest would run away or be sucked into a melee where they were outnumbered. Had we bought into a formal initiative system then I think the game would have been different. But in either case, the system as written, was only a starting point. What sculpted the game was the interpretation of that text.

      As for WFRP, you can read the game as a completely traditional fantasy RPG : a group of normal people including rat-catchers, bawds and students get sucked into an unpleasant mess and, after surviving for a while, stop being rat-catchers and start being artillerists or knights or assassins. It's just a variation on the theme of a lowly peasant or beggar who becomes a great hero. The game was clearly written with the intention of creating a heroic FRPG but ultimately, the interpretation that presents it as a game in which the working class struggle to survive and to advance socially proved to be more popular in certain circles.       \n Okay, I'm sure this is an old analogy but I'll make it anyway, and I think it will satisfy all but the most radical viewpoints.

      Virtually all RPGs are tools, the reason being that they don't provide an objective purpose within the game. (Or if you want to be pedantic, an objective way of evaluating the purpose. It's well and good to say that the purpose of an RPG is to have fun or tell a good story, but there's no universal and objective concept of either goal.) I can't really speak to RPGs with objective win/loss (or similar) conditions; that might have to come in another reflection, although it's my opinion that, if you have objective win/loss defined for all the participants, you can't also have rigorous mechanics (that is to say, dispense with discretion/"fiat") without turning the activity into a board game. In any case that's all a digression from what Jonathan M is talking about since his games don't have objective win/loss or rigorous mechanics. Both the goal within the game and large portions of "what happens" are socially/subjectively determined.

      So: tools. Most tasks can be accomplished using different tools and methods; there's more than one way to skin a cat, and more than one way to paint the Last Supper. It's an objective fact that you can reproduce the Last Supper as a digital photograph, and therefore it can't be denied that, if someone really wanted to, and had the time, they could create digital paintings from scratch, in the style of Leonardo, which would at least match what you could get by painting them on canvas, wood, or plaster, and then photographing them. Jackson Pollock, too. But people don't, by and large, because the tools available tend to shape the types of expression that are possible.

      The same thing is true with RPGs; you can always overrule, ignore, or avoid the rules if you choose. For that matter if the rules contain an escape clause like "rule zero" or "say yes or roll the dice" (note that the latter does make it possible to play an entire game without ever applying the mechanics), you don't even have to say you're bending the rules let alone breaking them. I suppose this would be like creating a new Mona Lisa via a pixel editor: it's doable, yes, but just because it is doable doesn't mean that people won't take advantage of the brush tools, or 3d modelling, rendering, and shading to do something that clearly bears the mark of the new tools.

      Why this is, I'm sure I can't describe exhaustively, but several reasons are: because the new tool shapes expression in a manner that's interesting the user, because it's easier to do some things with tool X than tool Y, and because human beings, unlike God, do not have unlimited agency: our conception of what is possible is itself limited and suggested by the tools at hand.
