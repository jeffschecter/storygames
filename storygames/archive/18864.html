\n Okay, I love Dread; have had fun playing it and even more fun hosting it. I've hosted for various scattered groups and always had a good time. Then I bumped into a new group of Dread players and discovered something strange and scary... they were playing it completely different from the way I had been taught and had then gone on to teach... horrors! So, once my senses returned, I had to find out more... so here I am.

The games I've played have had a "you touch it, you own it" policy when it comes to pulling blocks: you can look at the tower, move around it, but once you make contact with a block, that's the one you've stuck with. This has led to a lot of tension via trying really hard with tough pulls or amping up the failure because of many failed pulls.

This other group seems to play with a "probe, then pull" approach where a player can actively check out the tower until they find a "good pull" and then they go for it. This seems to amp up the tension via "easy" pulls rapidly disappearing and leads to people watching the others probe, hoping for clues as to other easy pulls. And it sounds like it's still a tense, fun game.

So, my question is, how do *you* play it? And, as a follow-up, are there even more variations on pulls?

I'm hoping this isn't something Man Was Not Meant To Know!

    "Probe, then pull" is definitely the way I've played it. If the player had to commit, my Dread games up until now would have been very lethal which really isn't the point.
    \nThat's the thing, I haven't found it "more lethal"; just a lot more "failures" and consequences as people elect to fail their pulls... so there's lots of maimings, damage, and complications to compound the trouble the players are in; I've found it also leads to more heroic/sacrificial deaths when the going is tough.

    I'm going to try the "probing" next time, and I suggest people try the "own it" method as well!
