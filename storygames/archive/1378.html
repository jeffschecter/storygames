The Book as Object: Let's talk formats

John_Harper
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
So, my game Agon has been out for a while now. People are playing it, and consequently are handling the book (It's a 9x7 landscape softcover). For those of you that have it, I'd love to hear honest criticism about the size, the binding, and generally how the format "feels" in use. Mine is the only game book of this format, as far as I know, and I'm curious about how it's being received.

I'd also like to talk about this as a more general issue. Which book sizes most appeal to you? Is there a connection between book format and content, in your opinion? What are some physical book qualities that turn you on, and which ones turn you off?

    I like smaller books over bigger ones, dimension wise. I like digest over letter, and if you can get it smaller than digest and still have it practical, I like that even better. For some reason I also seem to prefer horizontal layout books to vertical ones, but I think that might just be me.

    I like books that have had some forethought into how they are going to be used and handled, such as easy to find sections and nice friendly layout. If the book looks like it's usable, that's a turn on. Books that don't look like any sort of thought about someone picking the book up and reading it, then that's a turn off. Text that vanishes into the spine or horrible fonts, for example, as well as bindings that come apart as soon as you open them!

    John, we were actually just talking about Agon at our last playtest circle. I really, really like the format; I find it very flippable and a very friendly format. The borders, line art, and use of white space give it a very open feel. It actually feels less like a "roleplaying book" and more like "a book describing a game."

    One slightly amusing note is that, with that open sort of look, and with the greek gods and goddesses in line art, the book keeps looking and feeling like one of those books on greek mythology that you might find in a middle school classroom -- the kind that I, personally, used to love to find and devour. I don't know if that was intentional, but it's a big plus for me -- it's saying, "Remember those slightly kooky stories about heroes that you used to read instead of do schoolwork? This game is about that." And that's pretty cool.

    As for the cover, yeah. Black covers are almost universally a bad idea, especially when you're talking softcover. This can be somewhat alleviated by using a texture (see every single World of Darkness fatsplat ever), which will obscure the prints.

    I'm really digging on the small-format books. I find them far more natural and friendly than the unweildy 8.5x11 game books of my youth. The only drawback is for games that really need to be referenced often in play, the small formats and especially the digest make it hard to impossible to lay the book out on the table open-faced. That said, I have a couple copies of Capes that I printed four-up and had comb-bound, and they are just startlingly useful and convenient to use. I'm sorely tempted to do Agora in that format, although the nice thing about Lulu (where full-color Agora will be going) is I can offer the same book in multiple formats, so this is less of an issue.

    Posted By: LxndrPersonally, I reallyreallylike coil bindings. It helps a book lay flat, and has other very nice features.

    But everyone else in the whole world (or at least a majority) has said to me variations on 'coil bindings look cheap and dingy andlesser'. So I've abandoned the nice coil binding for Fastlane's second printing, since what matters is the game I'm trying to sell, not the package in which I'm selling it.
    My experience with coil binding is still influenced by the weird zig-zag wire coil binding on the computer manuals of my youth. Especially the old Apple IIe stuff. Here are my particular issues with coil bound:
    • the pages turn only with difficulty because they have to slide along the coil
    • the holes wear until pages begin to fall out
    • the tops and bottoms of the binding wear and look scruffy
    • the zig-zag means that you can't fold the pages over easily
    • when open the book looks mis-aligned
    • all you can see on the shelf is the coil
    Now, more recent plastic coils overcome most of these issues (especially the wear issue), but not all. 1984 Prime still disappears into my shelf, which is probably the worst thing I can say about it - otherwise it's a cool game.

    The other thing that continues to confuse me: O'Reilly has been publishing books in a lay-flat binding for a long time now, and no one seems to want to emulate that. It's essentially a perfect binding, with a flexible spine and a separation between the cover and the spine. Word from them is that they don't hold any patent or anything, but I'm sure there's opinions about the lay-flat as well...

    The actual content of the book tells me if I want coil-bound or perfect.

    A game with many tables that I will reference frequently MUST be coil bound so that it can be left open. A game with few tables or rules I can just as easily hold in my head is better perfect bound so it can be found by it's spine when I need to dig it out to check on it. Quick reference sheets can reduce the need for either, but they are necessary if you've perfect bound a book with more than a few look-up tables.

    My ideal book-as-artifact layout for Contract Work is a landscaped letter format in Boston Screw binding with a hard cover. Cover graphics printed portrait and the game title printed on the page edges through a full bleed so that it is visible from the side. The goal is to emulate an old-fashioned financial ledger. This will never happen.
