\n I was chatting to a friend over a pint the other day, and I started talking about the eternal game I'm 'just about to go finish', and how it was to be a fixed length camapign, and potentially have more scope to be epic because of it. Then I got to thinking - what makes a game epic? We chatted and came up with a list of sorts, and now I'd like to ask you: To what degree do the following factors affect the epicnicity of a game?

1. The scope of the plot: a plot that encompasses things from the little tiny details all the way up to the major power structures and which have total repercussions on the whole game world - is this important/always true?

2. The length of a game: Can a game be short and epic? Can the necessary experiences and revelations be ondensed into a short run game, or does it have to last for 7 years? Many people talk about their favourite games and say "Man, we played that game for X years! It was so Epic!"

3. Engaging with your characters: Really feeling your characters are more than stats or genre clich\xe9s - the feeling and knowledge of how the events change your characters; familiarity with the World and the things going on in it, and how your character feels about that too (classic example: character comes to know and love a city, which is then destroyed, and player is distraught and vows revenge)

4. A real, personal Nemesis: Is it necessary to have someone to hate, someone who's defeat is a challenge worth sacrificing for?

5. Revelations, reversals and betrayals: Making the plot personal, then playing with your heads using classic storytelling techniques.

So which of these are necessary or unnecessary, and what examples can you give? What other things make for epicnessicity in gaming?

    \n I started writing, then I saw the RPGNet thread, then I saw the Wikipedia entry, which pretty much summs up what I was thinking.

    For me, Epic is world-changing events, larger than life issues, etc. Doesn't matter if you start off as a farmhand, or as a 20th level paladin: If you're single-handedly saving the nation, the world, or the universe, that's epic:

    auf 'pedia:
    -----------------------
    Epics have 6 main characteristics:

    1. the hero is of imposing stature, of national or international importance, and of great historical or legendary significance
    2. the setting is vast, covering many nations, the world, or the universe
    3. the action consists of deeds of great valor or requiring superhuman courage
    4. supernatural forces--gods, angels, demons--interest themselves in the action
    5. a style of sustained elevation is used
    6. the poet retains a measure of objectivity

    The hero generally participates in a cyclical journey or quest, faces adversaries that try to defeat him in his journey, and returns home significantly transformed by his journey. The epic hero illustrates traits, performs deeds, and exemplifies certain morals that are valued by the society from which the epic originates. Many epic heroes are recurring characters in the legends of their native culture.

    Conventions of Epics:

    Praepositio: Opens by stating the theme or cause of the epic. This may take the form of a purpose (as in Milton, who proposed "to justify the ways of God to men"); of a question (as in the Iliad, where Homer asks the Muse which god it was who caused the war); or of a situation (as in the Song of Roland, with Charlemagne in Spain).
    Invocation: Writer invokes a Muse, one of the nine daughters of Zeus. The poet prays to the Muses to provide him with divine inspiration to tell the story of a great hero. (This convention is obviously restricted to cultures which were influenced by Classical culture: the Epic of Gilgamesh, for example, or the Bhagavata Purana would obviously not contain this element)
    In medias res: narrative opens "in the middle of things", with the hero at his lowest point. Usually flashbacks show earlier portions of the story.
    Enumeratio: Catalogues and genealogies are given. These long lists of objects, places, and people place the finite action of the epic within a broader, universal context. Often, the poet is also paying homage to the ancestors of audience members.
    Epithet: Heavy use of repetition or stock phrases: e.g., Homer's "rosy-fingered dawn" and "wine-dark sea."
    -------------------------
    ...which I pretty much agree with.

    \n What Andy said. "Epic" means it's gotta be big. I don't always take it to mean the world, literally has to change, but the characters' world has to change, and the characters are the ones who changed it. "The Godfather" is an example of what I mean in this regard - the "world" of Michael Corleone changes because of what he does, and his actions have big repercussions within that world (the mafia), and in the larger world around it. It's an epic story, with big plots, high stakes, lots of characters, but it's also intensely character focused on how those events shape and change those involved. Characters at the end of an epic should rarely be the same as when they started off, and (IMO) there should always be some price they've paid to reach their goals or achieve success.

    \n Well, not to pick a nit, but I think an epic can be the stories of more than one hero, as long as you have that sweeping backdrop, big issue stuff.

    I don't think epics need to be narrated in the past tense. The Lord of the Rings is perfectly valid epic fiction without past tense narration. Guy Gavriel Kay is a particular favorite of mine for epic tales and they aren't past tense.

    Edit: serves me right for rushing. Those books do use past tense (frodo walked, gandalf said etc.) but i dont think its what makes them epic.
