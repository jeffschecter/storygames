    No, no. The "Chosen One" is the fantasy story with a "normal guy" you're supposed to relate to in the Protagonist's seat. It's fucking weak, is what it is. Like, the astronaut in Farscape, or Major MacGuyver in Stargate who has to have all the big words explained to him.     \n My feeling is no, he's not. The Chosen One may have bad things happen to them, but only along the path to success. Buffy can get beaten up and mope around all she wants, provided that she eventually gets her shit together and kicks some vampire ass. Luke can get his hand chopped off and find out that he nearly got it on with his sister, because in the end he's going to overthrow the Empire. Peter Parker gets crapped on by the entire universe on a daily basis, but always lives up to his great responsibility and defeats the supervillain.

    Ultimately, what makes you The Chosen One is not just that fate is screwing with you, but that you will be triumphant in a way that makes the audience say "Fuck yeah, you just saved the day!" And really, that's not Baltar. As perennially screwed by fate as he is, he's not marked out to be the hero in the end. The best he can hope for is a pyrrhic sacrifice that gives an actual Chosen One the opportunity to win.

    Edit to add that I don't think The Chosen One and the "Newbie Protagonist" are necessarily the same thing. TV shows definitely love to have a Newbie Protagonist, because the conventional wisdom is that audiences will need to have elements of the setting explained to them and it's easier/more comforting if that explanation is given in-character to someone they can identify with. The Chosen One is a person of destiny, the special snowflake who has the mojo no one else has and will ultimately win where anyone else would lose. You can combine those roles into a single character (and many shows do), but they're not the same at all.

    I asked the question because I'm trying to separate the "Chosen One" thing from broader ideas like "Make cool stuff happen" or "Stories are about people."     \nThis is very true. Having a 'point of view' character that allows the audience to have a window into the fiction is a perfectly valid strategy. In fact, nothing gets made in Hollywood without one of these to some extent. However, the lame-ass cop-out is when you make the newbie protagonist also the chosen one -- because then you're saying to the audience, "Hey, this guy who's a clueless fuck just like you? He's actually totally awesome. Don't you feel all warm and happy inside, knowing that you can be a clueless fuck and still be totally awesome?"

    For a rather good example of clueless newbies that aren't chosen ones, check out the hobbits that aren't Frodo in LotR. They all have wide-eyed adventures and make big, significant decisions, they're hella compelling, but they don't save the world.

    \n For a really well-done Chosen One story, which isn't just derivative though it recycles a lot of standard tropes, try David Zindell's Ea Cycle (starting with The Lightstone). He spends a whole (thick) book on the question of whether the viewpoint character is or is not the Chosen One, and does it really well. Credit to Mike Sands for putting me on to these.

    Having said that, yes, the Chosen One is almost - but only almost - as tired (and as likely to put me off a story) as the Dark Lord. We get them both, ultimately, from Zoroastrianism via Judaism and Christianity, so they've been around a really long time. This, in turn, means that it's hard to do anything new with them. Whenever I pick up a book and read the blurb and it tells me that an unlikely Chosen One must battle the Dark Lord to prevent a terrible fate befalling... I put it back.

    (I didn't think much of the Wizard Knight, by the way, but I can't stand Gene Wolfe generally. It's a personal taste thing, obviously, because some people I really respect, like Neil Gaiman, think he's great.)

    \n Well, one of the reasons why Chosen One stories tend to be so strongly popular is because it tweaks a particular psychological fantasy -- that any old schmuck, yes, even you, could be Really Special. Considering the fact that one of the many common themes about living in the modern world is its homogeneity, people like the idea of vicariously living through some schmuck who, through no fault of his own, Becomes Someone Special, Nay, Heroic.

    So, yeah, apparently Heroes Journeys or Chosen One Tales are popular with the public -- but, you know, just because a work actually uses the trope doesn't mean it's any good. Sure, Harry Potter has a host of problems that detract from its quality -- but it's pretty good, or, maybe, you know Good Enough For Those Plebeian Bastards.

    It's overused? Certainly. But so what? When you get down to it the reason why you stick to a good piece of work is because it's good, not because it's not clich\xe9. If it's good and it sells, excellent. If it's good (oh, and of course there are so many ways to define what is 'good') and it doesn't? Oh well, too bad.

    \n I think that the author is full of it, and that her nonsense will take her far.

    Paul Fussell once observed that TV shows aimed at the lower class are often concerned with magic:
    flying nuns, housewife witches and genies, highways to heaven, incredible hulks.

    He suggested that the appeal of magic--- was that it implied that the world operated according to
    superstition and luck and wishful thinking, rather than according to complex forces beyond the
    experience, comprehension, or control of powerless masses at the bottom of society.

    I think the article's author mistakenly conflates genre science fiction and fantasy with
    use of sci-fi and fantasy elements in a non-genre manner. Small Wonder, Mork from Ork, and ALF had
    robot and alien family members, but remained family sitcoms, never gee whiz escapist technobabble.

    Which would suggest that the real key to getting fanciful stuff on TV-- is to not rub the audience's nose
    in their ignorance of intergalactic propulsion or mythological beastiaries.

    Is The Chosen One =really= a magic spice that renders niche tastes broadly palatble?
    Here's your answer: Wesley Crusher. Boy he sure gave Star Trek mainstream appeal, didn't he!

    Still The Chosen One will serve the author well in pitching to network development execs.
    First of all, it's a catchy buzzword. Also, it's a dumbed down version of The Heroes' Journey,
    which has been a fad among Hollywood's no-nothing middle management for a couple decades now.
    So she won't be threatening -their- ignorance with her pitch.

    Also, there's a huuuuge sappy soft spot among guys like George Lucas and Spielberg and Tom Hanks--- for schmaltz about
    boys who go on magical journeys of vague wisdom and self-discovery-- and bond with their dads and crap.
    TV execs probably have the same nostalgic soft spot. Jane asserts that it's the broad audience that
    loves The Chosen One. I am not convinced. But who cares? The guys who give the green light to her
    projects will lap it up.

    \n I hate the Chosen One trope so much. The reason I hate it is that it makes so many of the peripheral characters look like worthless chumps. Oftentimes, as stated, The Chosen One is just a schmucky everyman arbitrarily thrust into a position of greatness. They start out as being weak and under the protection of some secondary mentor characters that have obviously striven to become competent, but soon outdistance this mentor in power, usually with the most minimal of effort. I'm thinking of characters like Morpheus in The Matrix, or Obi-Wan Kenobi. The mentor characters soon end up becoming sacrificial lambs to pull the heartstrings of the protagonist, despite the fact that the mentors usually have more character and more interesting drives and histories than the cipher that is the Chosen One. For me it's a weird paradox. Main characters in stories, especially if there's just one central protagonist, are almost always the ones I find most dull.

    And in Star Trek, it wasn't the crew that were The Chosen Ones so much as it was humanity. Star Trek was all about making those other alien races look bad for their craaaazy cultures, and having the crew show them how much better humanity did it. Those silly Vulcans with their obsession with logic! Don't they know that it's important to be in touch with their feelings, like us humans? Those crazy aliens with asses growing from their foreheads, and the way they mandate the killing of their old people! They should cherish the elderly for their wisdom, just like us humans, amirite? Hooray for humanity! That's why most of the aliens that were part of the central cast needed to either be half-human or raised by humans, otherwise they'd just be lost souls, I guess.
