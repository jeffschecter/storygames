\n
\n Over in the Flags in Flux thread, some folks (notably Georgios and Max) argued that the players should always be able to over-rule any constraint placed upon their character. The argument was that if the players found the constraints not to be fun then the only productive option would be to let them override those constraints, in the interest of seeking fun.
\n
\nI think that's utter nonsense. Freedom can lead to fun, yes. Constraint can also lead to fun, by helping you to flex your creativity. The benefits of freedom are in no way an indictment of the benefits of constraint.
\n
\nTo choose a specific example: I think that choosing to play a Fighter at level 1, and continuing to play that Fighter up to level 20 (even if, sometimes, it seems like it'd be more fun to switch over to a Thief or some such thing) can be a powerful tool for helping players to create fun. The very fact that they KNOW that Fighter is what they have to deal with can help them to explore that concept more thoroughly, and to find unexpected facets. What does the Fighter do when he's become a king, and is now in charge of keeping the peace, for instance? How does Conan, sitting aged but still deadly upon the throne, still embody the virtues of the Fighter?

    Posted By: Max PHow do you handle it when it doesn't?
    In various ways, depending on the details and context. As stated, your question is so abstract as to not admit of any specific answer.

    If I were playing a Fighter, and the fighting started to wear on me, but I couldn't change classes I might handle it as follows: Figure out something that I do find exciting (like puzzle-solving) and look for ways that I can apply the game-resources that the Fighter gives me toward the goal that I find exciting. I could, for instance, try to figure out a new monster's weaknesses in combat by utilizing various maneuvers ... and then capitalize on it to win the fight.

    That sorta thing.

    \n Tony, I always find your discussions very provoking.

    I have an idea for a TSoY hack floating in the back of my head which I think is suggestive in this context:

    What difference might it make if you set a predefined and limited set of Keys for a TSoY campaign before you began? For instance, for a Feng Shui game, you might decide on a set of 16, something like this:

    Key of Faction
    Key of the Traitor
    Key of the Outcast
    Key of the Independent

    Key of the Mission
    Key of the Guardian
    Key of Vengence
    Key of the Impostor

    Key of Bloodlust
    Key of the Coward
    Key of Power
    Key of Glittering Gold

    Key of Love
    Key of Unrequited Love
    Key of Fraternity
    Key of the Guardian

    Now, this group isn't much smaller than the basic TSoY set, but I think the combination of explicit declaration and implicit constraint might have an interesting effect on the game. Like the set list of Descriptors in Sorcerer, this is in part a set of genre restrictions. In addition, the set list makes buying off Keys place increasing constraints on the player's actions. At the beginning the field is quite open, but as the game goes on a player's choices will narrow--and I think it is this combination of opportunity and constraint that really seems productive. If the players buy in early on, increasing constraint appears as a result of their own actions and is more likely to be productive rather than frustrating.
