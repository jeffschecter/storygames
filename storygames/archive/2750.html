    \n The podcast, GeeksOn, did an interview with actor Richard Hatch, for their latest show. Richard played Apollo on the original BSG, and plays Freedom Fighter/Terrorist Tom Zalek on the modern BSG. He is also releasing a media thing-ma-bobber called The War of Magellan, which apparently will be novels, a tabletop RPG, a video game, etc. He also discusses; how he came to be an actor, original BSG, telling Lucas how he could have improved Star Wars, the Heroes Journey, Storytelling, Roleplaying, Video Games, Improv, how he was involved in getting the new BSG off the ground, and many other things. A really fascinating interview. I had no idea about this guy.

    \n That's a nice article, Rob! Great for reinforcing the basics of improv.

    Of course, as you get further into improv you can break any and all of those rules. For example, I'm currently on a monoscene team. We play a single 22 minute scene, and the slow-burn of discovery is much more enjoyable if you don't immediately come out and label everything. It's really fun, and I think makes for an interesting show, but operates on slightly different principles from a faster-cutting Harold.

    Right now I'm at a point where I want to question as many of the rules in that article as possible. One of the big ones at my theater is 'NO PLOT', and I really, really want to find a way to challenge that.

    \n
    \n OTOH, there are clear reasons for it. The more you're in your head, the worse your improv is. Having to juggle character, plot, and reaction is too much, something has to give, and reaction is the thing that goes. Plot restricts yes-and and justification. It's a wonderful thing to surrender to the world you've created, and great improv comes out of that surrrender.

    I think all of these things can be overcome (by internalizing character and game, perhaps by incorporating ritual and direction), but I also think that being at the table and playing is much different from being on stage and playing, despite the superficial similarities.
