\n Suppose there was a database of every RPG ever created, whether produced for free or for commercial publication. Suppose this database described each RPG perfectly, and thus if you were trying to find an RPG you already knew about but had forgotten the name or you were trying to discover new things you could do so by searching for particular categories or types that interested you.

How would you search for them? What kinds of descriptors would you want to see used for RPGs to enable them to be located by users of this hypothetical database (it IS hypothetical, right? if this thing exists and I've missed it, someone needs to tell me immediately)?

    Posted By: iagoIPR has a "people also bought..."
    These are cool, but there's a problem with them. This problem is obvious on Amazon, and this problem probably isn't as severe as IPR. But that problem is that when you're looking at games as games rather than as books and products, knowing what else someone bought is less likely to be helpful. Look up a D&D manual on Amazon and the list of other books people that bought that also bought is probably filled mostly with... other... D&D books. Many or most games on IPR are stand alone products, so this is less likely. But still, I imagine if I pull up the page for BW some of the "people also bought list" is going to be other BW books.

    Since RPGs are generally sold as physical books there is a tendency to treat them and describe them in the same way one would any other book. And while it's important to be able to find information about them as books, it's even more important (at least to someone approaching this gathering of information as a player of games rather than a buyer of books) to be able to get information about it as a game. This is something that, to the best of my knowledge, hasn't really been done yet.

    Retailers have sunk millions into research on purchasing trends, and it turns out that the reasons that people buy things can be pretty random, so picking out winners logically can be complicated. But trends can be found; and no matter how unlikely the trends are, and how little logical sense they make, there are still underlying reasons behind them. I bring up an example elsewhere of calling a mail-order store to buy a lampshade, and having the operator ask if you want to also buy winter slippers: And in many cases, the person will actually buy those winter slippers: There may be a reason for the similarity in buying habits, but at the end of the day traditional logic fails, and only the weight of the trend can be analyzed.

    The trend says that if you like Maid, you also might like Dictionary of Mu or Reign. Makes no sense. And yet, I have all three on my shelf as well.

    \n Y'know, I went to library school, so I should have been all over this one. But I wasn't. The features I look for in a game are just too subjective and vague to be defined, so I held back. I just wouldn't use a database to find new games, but I would use a database to find more about games someone had told me about.

    So, what I need in a game database:

    Name of the game
    Name of the game designer
    Publisher's company name
    blurb (from the back of the book), just to help confirm I'm looking at the right entry
    link to the major website for the game
    links to reviews and actual play examples
    link to the designer's page, if one exists

    Only a few of these things are actually searchable (name, designer, etc.), but that's okay. Librarians make lots of databases that point to other sources of information (catalogues, bibliographies, etc.).
