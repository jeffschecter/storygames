\n This is a little story about what happened yesterday in our campaign, when the group confronted Lamentations of the Gingerbread Princess. As this is one of the best LotFP modules, you may wish the consider the following to be SPOILERS - this one's well worth playing if you are into this sort of thing at all.

So here's what the tactical situation looks like from a bird's eye perspective:

The mission target is the relief and rescue of a couple hundred hostages, plus finding a way out of a hostile fae magic pocket dimension for the party and the hostages. The hostages are held in a village on an island, with a big ivory wizard tower in the middle. The hostiles consist of a few dozen demonic teddy bears (clearly subhuman in combat abilities), five wardog-like poodles (sort of dangerous) and a dozen or so flying, archerous cupids (subhuman, but flying and shooting). The hostages seem ill-treated and mentally defeated, under constant surveillance, but basically free to move about in the village. There's a 5th level magic-user in the tower, plus all the mission-critical mcguffin stuff is in there, too. There's a single entrance to the tower, plus a balcony 30 feet high from the ground.

And here's what our party decided to do about it:

The adventuring party of seven people goes up to the single bridge leading to the island-village and requests an audience with whoever is running the place. The two teddy bears guarding the bridge request them to leave their weapons, which they decide to do, and follow them into the village. The magic-user appears at the top balcony of the tower and addresses the newcomers, welcoming them warmly into this (nefarious, horrifying) place as new residents, but refusing to come down to meet them in person for now. The party decides to attack the tower unarmed in an attempt to reach the magic-user; their plan involves breaching the doors and taking control of a hypothetical internal stairway. A sudden skirmish erupts as the adventures start breaking in, with the enemy forces converging on the adventurers as they fight their way into the tower; the party forgets to secure the stairway as they'd planned, and gets bogged down individually, each adventurer fighting several halfling-sized teddy bears; the party is wiped out, one by one.

The specific concept that motivated the party to attempt to breach the tower was that they had concluded that the magic-user in the tower was the demonic linchpin of the entire pocket dimension; if they could exorcise the devil, they could save everybody in one swoop. As it happened, the assault on the tower proved that their basic concept was faulty: the magic-user was not, in fact, vulnerable to Turn Undead, upon which the entire plan rested. The last member of the party to go down was the priest, after they realized their error.

The peculiar bit in our newest TPK was that once the party was established in the middle of the fae/demon village, with the magic-user unwilling to come down from their tower, the players actually had a long-ish strategic discourse wherein they fielded a number of ideas that would seem in hindsight to provide them with more of a chance for success. For instance, the following concepts were brought up:
* The party could wait for night and try to climb up to the tower balcony stealthily.
* The party could live in the village for a while to observe and learn more about the particulars of the place. They could perhaps foment a rebellion among the prisoners, create some weapons, and so on.
* The party could continue interacting with the magic-user, perhaps over the course of a longer time period, to gain their trust and attempt an assassination when their guard is lowered; the magic-user doesn't seem very smart, after all.
* The party could distract the frankly quite stupid teddy bears and whatnot, attract them away from the tower, and then attack the tower with a momentary tactical advantage when the majority of the village's overseers were not in the immediate vicinity.

So afterwards I'm left to ponder - what went wrong here, exactly? The party starts with at least three viable strategic approaches (stealth, diplomacy and brute force), they pick one (diplomacy, wherein they declare their presence and disarm themselves), but then decide to follow up with an ill-planned impromptu assault on the enemy in the exactly most disadvantageous time and place. All this, in a context where the players spend lengthy amounts of time discussing their options; they even voted on the immediate assault, which vote went 5-1 in favour I think.

This sort of thing is not unique at all - and if you have your own stories of herd stupidity, feel free to share. After thinking about it a bit, the way I'd formulate my thinking is that it's actually pretty difficult to produce smart decision-making in the iterative committee context that D&D offers: there's a complex strategic situation, and there's lots of chefs in the kitchen, all with their own unique ideas and preferences, and the situation is changing all the time, and you need to separate the meaningful tactical and strategic facts from a compex game world narrative. There's some effort at chairmanning the proceedings, but it's not super disciplined by any means. This is an environment in which herd stupidity has a great chance to flourish, causing specific cognitive faults in the proceedings:
* The party may come to lack strategic foresight: they make decisions moment-to-moment without considering their long-term goals.
* The party may come to lack strategic memory: they outright forget what they were supposed to be doing, and thus waste their positioning as they jump tracks.
* The party may get stuck on a singular strategic conceit to the exclusion of all else.
* The party may outright miss important strategic particulars; not because characters fail some checks, but simply because the players do not think to ask obvious questions, or ignore pertinent details as they are narrated.

Lots of things happen in old-fashioned exploratory wargamey D&D, as it's a quite varied game, but alongside tactical combat scenarios and daring guessing games this is one of the classics: a complex strategic situation where the players have great initial freedom of approach, but every move they make rapidly narrows down their choice set (solidifies the situation towards a resolution, in other words). While making smart strategic choices is an excellent skill to have here, I think that simply having some handle on the group debate dynamics would be even better: first train yourselves to act and decide things intelligently as a group, so as to avoid herd stupidity, and then perhaps there will be some room for actual strategic brilliance. But the first thing, before anything else, needs to be to get a handle so that you're not collectively dumber than you are individually.

    \n This is a problem intrinsic to the type of game you're playing. LotFP is, like D&D set up as a wargame, and rewards good wargame thinking and punishes poor tactical choices, like making a frontal assault on the tower. From that perspective, what happened in this game was perfectly predictable and gave the results you should have expected. The session should be considered a resounding success by all players because of that.

    But it sounds like that's not the perspective you're taking. You wanted the story to continue, to result in something other than TPK (which often happens simply because there's no rules about surrendering or running away).

    My suggestion? Either accept that sometimes these things happen, invent rules which support surrendering, or switch to game from a different RPG subgenre. Even then, poor tactical choices will likely still occur from time to time. It's just usually easier to escape fights in games with more narrative-based combat systems.

    \n That's a real phenomenon, I agree. With players who are on board with the agenda this sort of thing isn't even defeatist, it's just a player's conviction that they've done all they can, and the rest is up to luck, so let's stop fuzzing and start rolling the dice, and if luck is bad then let's make new characters and adventure some more.

    Here's an interesting article about herd stupidity in a non-gaming context, perhaps it clarifies the concept a bit. It's a relatively well-established idea in the social sciences that people are not always smarter as a group than they are individually.

    I think it's really interesting that you don't have experience with this thing in D&D. I mean, your D&D crews always play smartly in strategic terms, and they only fail because of bad luck or individual foolishness, and not because they fall into a herd mentality and just go with the flow? I find that really unexpected, because I'm seeing this stuff all the time. So much so that it's one of the defining characteristics of clever, high-quality strategic play for me when a group manages to avoid it and respond intelligently to complex strategic problems.

    Let's look at another example of herd stupidity from an earlier adventure, with a different group, just to have a broader view:

    The Block Party

    A few years ago we were playing "Blocks of Quox" (a fine puzzle adventure published in Fight On!), an adventure with large, tunnel-filling, 10 feet to the side stone blocks that were moved around the dungeon with the aid of magic. I was GMing for a group of four or so adventurers.

    Two adventurers crossed a pit in the floor into a tunnel which was blocked by just such a magical cube, which they could perhaps push to have something useful happen in the dungeon. Unknown to them, beyond the cube was a room with bugbears in it. When the adventurers started pushing the stone cube, the bugbears soon reacted and started pushing back, so that the cube started moving inexorably backwards. The speed wasn't anything special and there was like twenty feet of room between the block and the pit, so it wasn't a crisis situation or anything like that.

    The way the party reacted to this situation was quite memorable, as they realized that it would be pretty bad to fall into the pit at the mouth of the tunnel, and therefore something needed to be done. The two adventurers chose to continue trying to force the cube back by sheer strength, and, to my amazement, the rest of the party hastened to help them, crossing the pit specifically so as to stop this run-away stone block. They got the entire team there to push back on that block.

    After a few strength checks it seemed like the block wasn't going to stop moving (those bugbears are pretty tough!), and the party realized that they'd need to figure out something else. Specifically, they didn't want to fall into that pit. It would be so bad to fall into the pit.

    This being a funny TPK story, of course two party members fall into the pit as they're crossing back to the safe side. They drag down the rope the characters had been using to help them cross, leaving the others to try jumping. One of the other characters went voluntarily into the pit, I think, so as to help the fallen ones out before the cube would inexorably fall on their heads. The jumpers were pressed for running space by the approaching cube, and thus failed to reach the other side, falling into the pit with the rest.

    At the end we were amazed to realize that yes, the players had managed to get the entire party killed by the combination of bad luck and a slowly moving stone block that fell on them in a pit, all because they had gotten so stuck on the idea of stopping the block that the entire party hurried to join in the effort, despite having a clear picture of the architectural situation. In hindsight nobody could explain why the party could not have just taken their time to help the two initial block-pushers cross the pit, and then let the block fall into the empty pit, thus clearing the way to whatever was behind the block (the next room with bugbears, as it happened). The party had nothing riding on that block, so why was it so important to stop it from moving in the first place? Obvious in hindsight, but somehow the real-time-ish decision-making, the 1st person perspective and the group decision-making process combined into one of the lamest TPKs ever.

    Another common D&D example: only players who are familiar with the principles of siege warfare ever suggest and enact a siege plan when their characters need to take down a keep or other fortified position.     And that's where we come back to the skill of actually governing the strategic discourse: if players were more skilled about explaining to each other what their options are, and in what way those options are entertaining and effective and otherwise worthwhile, then the group would be less unlikely to vote on essentially superficial basis. It's the same thing we get in political democracy: the only good decision-making body is an informed one, and therefore the quality of the decision-making is directly dependent on the officials who prep the decision-makers. For D&D, that's individual players prepping each other to make smart decisions; you cannot get a group to make a decision that's smarter than the smartest individual decision would be unless you spend effort in sharing knowledge and wisdom among the group. If they had a dictator who decided what to do, I doubt that they would have first given away all of their weapons only to decide to follow-up with a frontal assault; the dictator would have stuck to a single strategy throughout.     Given that the party seems to run on a pretty democratic basis, the players would probably find advantage in assigning more explicit briefing responsibilities. The way it worked in this particular case was that the players asked each other for options, and thus amassed their list of potential courses to take, which they then voted on to discover an acceptable concensus. However, no serious exploration of the alternatives was engaged in, and nobody really argued for particular solutions over others. The one player in the group who had serious misgivings about the frontal assault was particularly notable in that while he made concrete suggestions for alternatives, he never went as far as to take a firm stance and explain to the others why he preferred a more circuitous strategy. I imagine he was following his general approach of letting the other players gain leadership experience by not interfering too much.

    \n These are great stories, Eero.

    I have seen this kind of "herd stupidity" numerous times, and especially so in in this kind of gaming idiom. I agree with your analysis of it.

    I think that a big part of it is the high potential for boredom in this gaming style - very often making *something* happen becomes a priority for the group, and suboptimal choices are made.

    However, it's also worthwhile to remember that choices in such games are not made in a vacuum. There is a deliberate context of limited information for the characters and players, and it's not hard (in that context) to become certain that you are doing something clever (e.g. trying to kill the wizard in the tower) even while you are totally misguided.

    In my opinion, this happens in part because logical leaps ("The wizard must be the linchpin!") are necessary in this style of adventuring, and in part because it's impossible to predict with great certainty how a given GM will handle a given fictional situation.

Because that decision-making impacts character success at such fundamental level, I find it part of the strategic decision-making in the game.

    For example, consider a character who gets lost in fairyland. What do they even want from the situation? To know that, one would need to understand the situation. What if it's a chance for great wealth, shouldn't the character take that chance? Or what if fairyland can only offer them delusion and the loss of their immortal soul? Could they gain great magics or powerful friends? Should they try? I think that seeing the possibilities and choosing to pursue them is also a strategic choice.

    As another example, consider an outright bad adventure hook: say that a 1st level beginner party decides to pursue a greatly over-leveled challenge, such as slaying a dragon terrorizing the countryside. This is probably a bad choice, because the odds of success likely do not balance out against the size of the reward (particularly if none is on offer and they're just acting on heroic instinct). A more strategically minded player realizes this and instead casts their character in an ancillary role more suited to them - perhaps they'll take on the quest to find a True Hero to slay the dragon, for example, which sounds much more like something a low-level party might accomplish.

    (This second one is actually a pretty good measure of just how sandboxy your campaign really is, I feel: if the group takes it as a given that when the GM offers a dragon-slaying they should take it, perhaps it's not much of a sandbox after all.)

    This sort of smart analysis of the possibilities, and allocation of goals, is the first step in intelligent sandbox D&D strategy, I think. After you know what you're even trying to accomplish, it's time to figure out the options you have for advancing your cause.

    \n Three methods I've seen:

    * Based on fictional elements

    In a "character stable", sandbox-style game, each adventure tends to be "organized" by one of the PCs. They find an adventure hook, convince others to go with them, plan and/or pay for the venture.

    Sometimes it might just be the only character who survived the previous attempt, or the only one who *knows* about the adventure in the first place (for instance, the one who found the treasure map!).

    In this case, the leadership role falls quite naturally to them.

    * Formalized/rule-based

    I like the idea of establishing a formal way of declaring a leader, as well, though. It might be as simple as assuming that the character who rolled the highest starting wealth is the leader of the current outfit, for instance.

    In my own dungeon crawler, you roll for social standing/background, and the character with the best outcome is the leader. You might get an expedition formed of two escaped slaves, an army deserter, a mercenary, and a noble's son trying to prove himself. The rest naturally suggests itself...

    * Social contract

    In other groups, it's not about the characters at all, but the *players*. Someone is the most veteran player, or the clearest manager of actions, someone knows the rules best, someone has been at the table the longest. We agree to allow that person to take the lead, and play our characters accordingly.

      \nSure. Consider these snippets of wisdom (or perchance lack of wisdom - either way, they're issues on which I believe it is possible to have good protocols, it's not all just roll of the dice).

      * Did you know that adventures that involve savage tribes are common at low levels, very easy to spot in advance (as in, you know that there's goblins in this adventure before you'll actually have to deal with them), and tactically very predictable? The ideal dungeon tactic is disciplined closed-order melee (think hoplites or legionnaires) with 6+ combatants, capable of bringing more force to bear against loose-order enemies, breaking weaker lines where needed, and scattering a superior number of savages. If an acceptable front line cannot be mustered (often the case for a first outing crew), I recommend leaving that adventure for the fools - intelligent savages are very dangerous, a dungeon tribe generally has well over a dozen combat-capables, and nothing in particular prevents them from gathering the whole posse to crush you if you can't control chokepoints and bring the battle to them in short order. If you absolutely have to despite not having the strength for it, then do stealthy scouting, retreat at the alarm, develop a lure, attract the tribal warriors out of the dungeon, and then get in and out before they come back.

      * Did you know that after the savage tribes, the second-most common low-level monster is the non-intelligent undead? (Skeletons, zombies - ghouls generally have low cunning and therefore don't count here.) These are also generally easy to spot well in advance. The favoured tactic is to build and transport portable fortifications that provide the defenders significant advantage, or even prevent the undead outright from endangering the defense, and then just poking them to bits with heavy polearms from a safe distance. (This works because of the "non-intelligent" part - the enemy chooses to attack even against bad odds.) The traditional "portable fortification" is a mantlet, which I recommend to be built in sections about 3 feet wide, with robust backstops preventing enemies from pushing it over, and with a height up to a man's chest or so; it is important that three pieces block a dungeon corridor and that retainers can carry and operate each piece, and that fighters can operate long spears over-hand over them.

      * Did you know that adventurers who don't carry their personal light-sources cannot search effectively underground unless Light magic or similar is used? Your own shadow blocks any central light source, and centrally positioned ordinary torches and lanters shed much too little light to enable you to check the room corners for intricate details such as hidden treasures or passages - you need that torch up close and personal, either in your own hand or that of a personal retainer. A party should, therefore, have lightning flexibility: in marching order a single lantern up front but low (helps preserve dark vision, easy to find the grouping point if anybody lags behind), and when exploring a room everybody lights a personal torch off a central source. You can always drop it in an emergency, it's moronic to fight over who has to carry the light. Remember to post guards when investigating stuff - and the guards don't carry torches, they need their dark vision.

      Playing this game is basically about thinking this stuff - I believe that anybody who's wasted a few hundred hours on this could talk your ears off about the art and science of dungeon commando operations. Saying that it's all about play group communication or something like that seems rather opposite to my experience.

What if following the orthodox anti-skeleton playbook leads to a catastrophe one time? It'd be difficult to be the GM in that situation, when you're the one who recommended that strategy - essentially you promised that it would be the smart play to make. Much better for the GM to stay scrupulously out of it and let the players figure out for themselves how to do things.

      Regarding "story based thinking", by the way, one shouldn't knock it entirely - it's not worthless in D&D, I think. There are three basic reasons for why players with a good grasp on cultural traditions often have an advantage:
      * Stories include lots of things that are outright true observations about human nature. Things in reality - or D&D - are more often "like in stories" than not, really. Story-based thinking at least gives you a null hypothesis to work over when pondering about how some complex situation might turn out.
      * The GM is influenced by stories, because he's just as story-based an animal as the players are. The campaign may be predicated on cultural sources that are pretty far from mainstream entertainment television, such as history, military history, alternative history, historical re-enactment, folkloristics, pulp fantasy literature, esoteric religions and whatever, but those are still all stories, and they're not completely alien to whatever the player might have in his head. We're still on the same planet with the GM.
      * D&D is structurally predicated on mixing realistic and thematic thinking: character classes and character levels, random encounters and hitpoint mechanics, for example, are all intended to emulate romantic storytelling conceits in contrast to the hopeless fantasy vietnam gameplay. A player who understands those conceits can make cynical use of them within the constraints of the game. It would be a misunderstanding to think that the game would privilege story logic, but it is in there, and all the more so at higher levels.

      One of the best and most successful wargamey old-school D&D players I've met is very drama-based as a player. They've gotten a character to 6th level (I'll tell you, that's much more impressive in context than it sounds) and their characters generally end up doing interesting things and succeeding more often than not. Part of it is that they've got a good general grasp on military history, but I'd say that most of it is because they establish character identity for their characters quickly and identify story patterns early; this enables them to cleave their character into a central position in a scenario, which in turn gives them access to high-reward opportunities that sometimes pay out greatly.

      As an example of what I mean: in 2012 said player had a character in Three Brides, the LotFP adventure set in the idyllic little town of Pembrooktonshire. He came out of the events with a marriage to a rich carpet weaver's daughter, thanks to his unhesitatingly dramatic style of play; where the other players made boring choices, his character had bold motivations and he dared to play the social situation in town. The player doubled down on the situation by playing a bit of SimFactory with the carpet weaver's shop over the following in-game months, and the character only came out of the downtime when he got an adventure hook related to stealing some Persian carpet designs for the factory from a merchant in Amsterdam. Having accomplished that, his character took his savings (and the gratitude of his father-in-law) and started his own mercenary company, suitably in time to join the Italian Wars on the imperial side.

      \n I think that's all very sensible - similar questions are central to my philosophizing about this sort of game as well.

      My core theory about what is supposed to be going on in a game like this skill-wise is that it's a communal effort towards cohesive armchair understanding of various forces that reign in dynamic systems. The game is not actually realistic because Gary Gygax wrote such amazingly realistic rules (he didn't), or because the GM is such an expert on medieval warfare (he isn't); rather, the game is realistic in the sense that the procedure the players use for determining what happens is one that should, given patience and earnest effort, help them understand how things work and why plans succeed or fail. The process of play is a truth-discovering one, not truth-obscuring.

      Obviously the outcomes are more or less realistic depending on where the play group collectively and individually is in their engagement with wargaming; beginners probably won't achieve as deep, complex or precise simulation as players with more knowledge of the issues and skill with the gaming procedures. Still, even beginner play of this type of game should feature cohesive striving towards realistic outcomes, rather than mere genre-based conventional thinking. I think that this kind of play is more about the journey than the destination, and everything will work out in the long run even if the players do not start from a position of absolutely unassailable expertise. The play process enforces this.

      For example, the reason for why mantlets work against zombies at the local tables here is not some sort of gestalt judgement made by the GM. Rather, it's an idea that we've tested through a process of truth-seeking where somebody first invented the idea, and then after that numerous groups and GMs have attempted to establish various reasoned arguments against it, so as to circumscribe the limits of how much you can get away with by simply shouting "Mantlets!" at various problems. Establishing this tactical idea as something that "works" in the game has required players to work out, to the satisfaction of the groups in question, details such as the following:
      * Does your character have the cultural background to conceptualize and invent the item in question, and even an inkling of appropriate tactics for using it? If not, are we engaging in unrealistic metaplaying by introducing this idea in this campaign context? Where does our mantlet come from, diegetically speaking?
      * What are the construction materials, and is there sufficient crafting skill in the party for managing to build a mantlet? How much wood, leather, rope and metal does your design require, precisely? How much time, work and money?
      * How much does this mantlet weight? Precisely how encumbering it is for a character while dungeoneering?
      * Given that you've managed to bring the mantlet with you to a dungeon combat encounter, how do you use it as part of your combat tactics? What if the enemy simply goes around it? What if the enemy does not approach it? What if the enemy tries to push it down, or pull it down? Do you even have the time and the mental wherewithal to worry about the mantlet in the heat of battle? Do you have the extra men and discipline to form mantlet crews to keep the mantlets upright?
      * Finally, given that everything else has been dealt with, how much of a stochastic advantage should the presence of the mantlet give you in combat dicing? It turns a generic melee into something of a siege situation. Should you get a hefty AC bonus, or should certain types of enemies be simply unable to attack you as long as the mantlet prevents their approach to range? Should you get an attack bonus or penalty yourself, being somewhat limited by the mantlet as well?

      All of the above questions, and more, have been considered and investigated as part of the process of play, multiple times, under several GMs. There is no pre-conceived notion that mantlets "should" work - there cannot be, because I at least hadn't even considered them before Sipi I think suggested them one sunny summer day as the solution to cracking Tomb of the Iron God. The idea that mantlets give X points of circumstance bonus against zombies did not rise full-formed from the brow of some genius, it came about as an inescapable conclusion after we tried to figure out why it wouldn't work, and came up with merely circumstantial reasons. Thorough testing seems to indicate that not only does the mantlet work, but that it's a very good plan with a wide variety of applications once you establish it as part of the tactical doctrine.

      (Just about the best reason for why a mantlet shouldn't work against unarmed D&D zombies is that they may be supernaturally strong, and thus capable of e.g. pulling down the mantlet in the right conditions despite facing active resistance. This is not a critical issue, as you can shore up your mantlet in various ways - my personal favourite is to have a retainer whose specialized job in the combat is to crouch behind the mantlet and keep it upright with their body weight. What is critical is that both intelligent enemies and small verminous enemies beat mantlets in various ways, and thus mantlets alone are not sufficient to "solve" low-level D&D.)

      I could totally see the kind of play culture that you postulate, where the players simply have different aesthetic sensibilities and a strong conviction that their aesthetics are somehow superior to more conventional ones. In fact, the "grim and gritty" fantasy aesthetic isn't even that dissimilar in this regard - a big part of the charm seems to be a belief that it's a more realistic depiction of the world as it essentially is. There's more going on in wargamey D&D than that, though, as the decision-making about what we do and whether it works isn't aesthetic; it tries to be more.

      \n Regarding the consistency of tactics, it seems to me that the only thing underpinning them from group to group is the inquisitive method used to discover truth. Your question, Paul, is no different from asking why would we think that two different courts of law would judge the same case in the same way. The answer is that there is a good chance that two courts would arrive at the same conclusion in most cases, but that obviously they would also disagree on difficult ones.

      Note, however, that whether a group would rule for or against mantlets is not nearly as important as the fact that they rule at all: introducing the idea and gaming it through to find out how the group plays it has value even if they somehow would manage to conclude that a mantlet would be useless. I think that a tactical manual written by somebody else for some other campaign could be quite interesting despite the fact that a different group might judge certain issues differently - it would still be a source of ideas and arguments, challenges for the gaming. It is better for the group to have to face the challenge of the mantlet and to discard it, than to never consider the issue at all.

      In my personal experience good ideas are relatively consistent from group to group. There are several reasons for this:
      * If a player has gone over a given idea - e.g. mantlets - in a prior campaign, they're presumably well prepared to argue for it in a new situation as well. Their excitement is contagious, and assuming that they get along with the other players, the others are likely to go along with a well-presented case. The fact that the idea has a champion already committed to it at the table makes it much more likely to pass, obviously.
      * If an idea passed muster at one table running a high quality wargaming process, then that idea probably has some objective merit to it, which makes it more likely for another table with a similarly rigorous process to pass it as well. The basic precepts of judgement are the same, after all.
      * While two campaigns well could have different conditions that cause different ideas to be feasible, it's a fact that most of everything is the same from group to group: the game worlds tend to have things like humans, gravity, trees, animate skeletons, 10-feet dungeon corridors, medievalisms... any strategic doctrines that rely on such common precepts are likely to cross over well from campaign to campaign.

      Wouldn't you say that those factors are likely to cause a certain degree of cross-compatibility between campaigns? That precisely is my experience: I can talk tactics and strategy meaningfully with players from different campaigns, as long as their game has a genuine strategic environment (that is, they really game out these things instead of just relying on the GM's aesthetic sensibilities or similar to determine what is permissible and what isn't).

      If I had to pinpoint reasons for why tactical objectivity wouldn't cross over from campaign to campaign, then I'd place most of the blame on the GM. Particularly, if the GM has an impure method (e.g. they labor under the misconception that their job is to force the game to resemble a Gygaxian campaign, rather than working things out independently), they might well make judgement calls that would not stand well under rigorous examination. This is an avoidable problem, but obviously only if the group is interested in improving in that regard.

      Here's a funny cognitive tendency common to many GMs: it is usual for a GM to reward inventive on-the-spot thinking (because they like active players), while discouraging thinking out of the box on a strategic level (because they're afraid of change). This is the aesthetic process that gets you lamp oil problems: a GM delights in it when a desperate party improvises burn bombs out of lamp oil, but then they come to regret their initial call when the adventurers start equipping themselves with carefully constructed oil bombs for every expedition. This may lead to a funny dip in tactical effectiveness, where you got a d8 area effect damage or something similarly ridiculous out of the lamp oil first time around, but later on all sorts of difficulties start to crop up, and the mechanical impact starts to go down, as the GM tries to discourage oil bombs from becoming a standard feature. All very understandable when viewed as a process of fine-tuning the simulation, but if the motivation is simply regret for a mythical "right way to dungeoneer", then we can't really call it a robust wargaming procedure anymore.

      I could see a GM judge on the mantlets the same way: when first in a desperate situation you detach a dungeon door and use it as an impromptu shield against enemies it's excellent tactical play and merits a huge mechanical impact, but later when you improve on the idea and start developing actual hardpoint defensive tactics, the GM turns on you simply because they're conservative and don't want to see standard gygaxian tactics change on them. I haven't personally seen mantlets being treated this way, but I imagine that I would be somewhat cross as a player if I witnessed a GM be so flippy-floppy with no good reason.

      Those kinds of cognitive leanings are a threat to tactical compatibility between campaigns, I agree, but I do not think that they're an insurmountable issue, any more than similar features of human psychology prevent courts of law from providing a reasonably reliable service. The other players just need to keep an actual hand in the process instead of just letting the GM wallow in the responsibility - keep the GM honest by challenging their reasoning, and they should arrive at something resembling truth.

      \n Heh heh, I like those rules. Solid.

      I've gotten into the habit as a player to incorporate the adventurer party as a company, which tends to formalize new inductions in somewhat useful ways in addition to handling logistics much better than people would do it by themselves. It's also an useful short-term goal for a new character to get a chance to buy into the company (as opposed to having a hireling position), which requires proving oneself on a successful expedition.

      Aside from the robber-capitalist paradigm, military simile is also obvious in adventuring parties - a permanent leader of the party tends to turn into a "captain" sooner or later.

      Whether a given adventuring party is more like a corporation or a military unit (or a circus, as the case may be), it is favourable to pair newbies up with more experienced adventurers - in truth it's useful to pair everybody up if you can, but the newbies in particular benefit from sitting next to somebody who knows what they're doing.

      These types of intra-party social constructions help orient new players, as the natural urge towards character roleplay is directed in an useful way when we establish hierarchical in-character roles like boss, employee, officer, grunt. The player in the subordinate position is generally better prepped to take in advice and act towards meaningful task completion when compared to an anarchic group where nobody in particular is responsible for acclimatizing the newbies.

      \n The adventuring company ("warband" may be more apt, given the game's roots) is how I start the vast majority of D&D games. It provides a solid foundation for starting a new campaign:

      1. An excuse for the party to be together.

      2. An authority to provide structure (adventure hooks) for the game.

      3. An emphasis travel and exploration.

      4. A business need to gather treasure and investigate dungeons.

      5. An expectation of combat.

      6. A convenient method for replacing deceased characters and introducing NPCs.

      7. Potential loyalties, entanglements, and conflicts (oh, my).

      It is probably the most solid adventuring foundation next to "you meet in a tavern."

      \n OK, so my friend Heikki asked me if I'd been in any herd stupidity situations myself as a player. I've been thinking about that myself as well, but nothing really emblematic has surfaced. The closest I could get was the time when we armed and equipped a tribe of goblins in a megadungeon, only to have them turn against us, but we never finished the scenario, so it was left up in the air as to whether we could scramble our way out of that particular stupidity. Plus, I don't think that quite qualified - I, among other strategists in the party, thought long and hard about what we were doing, over several weeks, and it wasn't really a democratic stupid-concensus at work at all. At most one could consider that the stupidity of an adventurer who is trying to be too clever by far (a separate kind of stupidity, that).

      But, Heikki could remind me of another case earlier in that same campaign (Heikki was GMing this) which totally counts. I had just joined the campaign, going on its second year at that point, I think - this was in I want to say 2014 or something like that. I'd forgotten about this because it sort of wasn't my fault and blah blah because I wasn't on the map yet as to our strategic situation, and thus when it all backfired horribly I didn't really feel the enormity of it as it occurred [grin].

      So, we were playing Rahasia here, a TSR classic. This is, I think, one of the highlights of Heikki's DMing career, as the lengthy adventure morphed and devolved in unexpected ways as we went, but Heikki kept an even keel strategically and played the NPCs in a very inventive, life-like and merciless manner that made for a good series of challenges that extended far outside the original scope of the adventure. I have written about a slightly later sequence in that adventure earlier, in fact - let me drag that up for those interested... So yeah, it was in the spring of 2013, as we can see from the thread date.

      Let's quote a bit from that thread, because I see that I've already written about the event that Heikki reminded me of in there, near the start:\n
      Eero_Tuovinen said:

      As for the temple and dungeon, it appeared that the local Baron of the town of Roman had garrisoned the temple "so as to prevent pagan gatherings" within. This, combined with the sudden paranoia of the Baron (he'd closed the gates of the town proper) and the strange rumours about the death of his wife, were something of a concern for the party. Fortunately we had a back route inside: as a proper '80s TSR adventure, Rahasia features plentiful arbitrary magic, so there are even secret teleporters that can take you in and out of the dungeons just like that, at the snap of the fingers. Quite eldritch against the otherwise relatively realistic backdrop - very similar to how I swung in my own campaign last year.

      Of course the players who actually knew from prior experience that the in-going teleporter was faulty due to hijinks from an earlier excursion did nothing to prevent us relying on it for entry, which led unlucky dice rolls and two high-level characters plastering themselves all over the walls as ugly red paste. So that part didn't go so well. Plucky as we were, we located the under-dungeon jail cells and released a few new player characters to join the party before advancing further

      \nSo I think that's an excellent example of herd stupidity in action: we chose, for some minor reason of convenience (as far as we knew, anyway - in hindsight the baron's weird actions were a huge deal for the scenario), to use a known faulty teleporter to get to the 2nd level of the dungeon in a slightly "safer" way. Nobody really questioned the plan, it was just something that we were going to do as "a matter of course". (Was my very first session with the group, I learned from that old actual play report, so no wonder I didn't say anything - but I could easily have been there for twenty sessions and still gone for it, the feeling of consensual sleep-walking was that strong at the time.)

      To make the hindsight stupidity of this course of action perfectly clear, consider the following points of fact:
      * Heikki tells me that the malfunctioning teleport failed on a 1/6 per use, in which case he rolled off a Teleport Mishap Table to find out what, precisely would happen.
      * Fully three characters managed to get a teleport failure out of that table that fateful day. Two were 3rd-level party veterans, while the third was my shitty throw-away character.
      * In an amazing stroke of bad luck the two 3rd-levelers got '20' on that teleport mishap table as they stepped in together, and both failed their saves - instant death. My worthless character got in separately, and of course saved on his mishap result of '13', which would probably have killed him as well.
      * Being a 3rd level character in our campaigning style is a huge deal. It takes either exceptional luck or a few dozen sessions of careful and successful play to get there. I've only ever gotten one character to 3rd level myself, and never beyond that. Before that hit the party was practically into the mid-levels, but after that we were collectively back to 1st level, just like that. I am actually impressed by how calmly Peitsa and Taneli (Heikki and I think it was them, anyway) took it when the mains from their character stables were snuffed out like that.

      So yes, the dice rolls were somewhat unlucky, but frankly we were asking for it by going into that teleporter. Herd stupidity at its finest, and much worse than a 1st level TPK. Never trust an unquestioned, convenient consensus like that. We should have actually gauged the odds, you can't call that an informed decision when we never even considered the difficulty of getting into the dungeon in the old-fashioned way.
