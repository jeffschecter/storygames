    \n Okay, fine. You've given me an excuse to talk about one of my favorite games.

    In Nine Worlds, the gods have stats precisely because you are supposed to get all up in their business and challenge their power. So... yeah. It is a lot like your 30th level wizard, actually. The gods have set up the universe to be a certain way. Don't like it? Challenge them to see who's boss. Do like it? You better step up and champion their cause, because there are a bunch of super-beings lined up to kick them off their thrones. That's the "core story" of the game, and the mortal/god relationship is a key driving force of play.

    Oh, and the gods are powerful, but they are not invulnerable. A PC has a chance against any of the Immortals, if he has passion (his muses), a little help, and luck on his side. In Nine Worlds, the god write-ups are a catalog of the characters you are eventually going to beat the crap out of.

    I haven't played a long-running 9W game yet, but I imagine this around session ten: "Remember back when Zeus was running things? Man, those were the days."

    The other thing that 9W does that makes dealing with the gods awesome: Matt's write-ups convey the deities as people. You can really picture them as characters with interests and quirks rather than just symbols of power. Sure you could fight Apollo, but you could also just make him feel like a dick for all the shady crap he's done.

    Let me know if this is what you're looking for.

    \n The gods are not that powerful in most myths. Human badasses can and do take them on regularly.

    One thing I've found to get this across is to have the gods have very mundane concerns in addition to being gods. A paternity lawyer is after them for impregnating his client while disguised as a sunbeam and they're ducking service. The golf course where they are the pro is cutting back its hours and they're worried about getting another job. The scene is not like it was back in the seventies, you know, those were the days...

    \nYes and no.

    If I understand you correctly, you're warning me that gods are just antagonists after all. Focus on them as antagonists, not on the fact that they're labelled "gods".

    For "god", read, maybe, "the most powerful thing in your setting that can still be an NPC." My current thinking is that "most powerful" means can mean something important. It seems to mean something in Nine Worlds, like maybe the decision of where you stand in relation to the gods is the most important decision your character has to make?

    I'm not quite sure.

    Here's where I'm going with this, though. In Principia I've just added the sentence "The players are more powerful than the gods." That seems to me to be an important statement that says what the players want out of the game is more important than what the gods, or GM for that matter. And it's backed up mechanically.
