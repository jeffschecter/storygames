\n Jason's "Help Me Make One Note Superheroes" made me want to post, but he appears to be done there, and this isn't directly relevant, so... new thread!

I've seen a lot of verb/noun magic systems over the years, and there's two verbs that I think would be really useful for them, but none of them seem to have: Join and Sunder.

Join magically links the target to something. For example, Join Space can be used to create a gateway between two places. Join Fire could be used to create the typical "flaming sword".

Sunder is the reverse, separating two things that were attached. Sunder Life "detaches" the soul from something -- and a subsequent Join Life can attach it to something else, thereby doing a "Magic Jar"-type spell, or a "switch bodies" spell. Sunder Space can turn an area into a pocket dimension, with no way in or out without using "Join Space" to create one. Sunder Magic "cuts off" an area magically, so that magic and magical things can't go in or out -- think pentagram. Join Magic can be used to take magical energy and "attach" it to something, to create "power reservoirs" for magical wands and the like. Alchemists could Sunder and Join the elements, removing, say, the Earth from something, then applying it elsewhere.

What uses can you see for them?

    \n Not a lot. I mean, when I played Zork and other games of that style (Infocom things), they had a simple parser that handled imperatives more complex than verb-noun, and it was still infuriating to tell the game what you wanted to do.

    > GET BOX
    I don't see box here.
    > GET CRATE
    You get the crate.
    > PUT CRATE INTO SLOT
    It won't fit.
    > OPEN CRATE
    You take the lid off the crate.
    > PUT CRATE INTO SLOT
    You slide the crate into the slot in the wall.

    Any manipulation system needs more than verb-noun to provide any kind of unambiguous control. Just looking at your examples points out places that things can go wrong. Imagine if the Infocom parser were interpreting your verb-noun spells.

    > JOIN SPACE
    You join space. There is no air here.

    The problem is that if you want to join two things, you probably want to specify which two things, so you need two objects.

    > JOIN THIS SPACE OVER HERE WITH THAT SPACE IN CLEVELAND
    You create a wormhole to Cleveland.

    Certainly, you're not being literal about spells being verb-noun, but I assume you're talking about using it as a system for circumscribing the powers a magic system might offer. JOIN SPACE is the spell that connects two spaces. But once you've left the literal behind, it becomes just another spell system. "JOIN SPACE" doesn't mean "interpret JOIN as the verb and interpret SPACE as the direct object of the verb." It means "do some joining stuff and do some stuff with space, but I'm not sure how they relate, so wing that." Maybe that's enough for a spell system, but I doubt it.

    Look at Ars Magica for a verb-noun magic system, by the way. In the end, it's not a literal system. Creo Ignem (create fire) lets you create fire in general, but the words themselves don't say where or how or when or how much.

    \n Cool -- glad people are riffing on the idea! :-)

    I used join and sunder along with the more traditional verbs (create, destroy, control, move, transform...) in a "universal RPG" I wrote back in the early '90s, when those were still all the rage. They got used in ways I described above, and I found them invaluable for working out some kinds of spells.

    I later came up with the idea of using "join" and "sunder" to imitate the Laws of Similarity, in a more "mechanical" way. Basically, things had OtE-style traits, and you could join or sunder those traits. Thus, a bird has the trait "flies", so you could Sunder that trait from it, then Join it to yourself, to give yourself the ability to fly. I never actually ran a game using that, though, as that was around the time my group back then split up.

    I also threw in "know" as a verb, but it didn't work out as well for me.

    Riffing off of Adam's post above, and combining it a little with Jacson's, I remember an Internet-distributed magic system document someone made called "Mage2Mage". It basically had a "magic programming language, with programs like:

    Spell Fireball:

    Create Fire with name "MyFlame"
    Move MyFlame to PointAt
    Resize MyFlame to 20' Sphere
    End

    ... which would be your basic D&D-style Fireball spell. The fun parts came in with two things. First, loops:

    Spell FlameStaff:

    Create Fire with name "MyFlame"
    while True do
    Move MyFlame to MyStaff
    loop

    and second, interrupts, which let you interrupt an existing spell and change its "code". Thus, instead of casting the fireball spell above, you could do:

    Interrupt FlameStaff
    change "Move MyFlame to MyStaff" to "Move MyFlame to PointAt"

    to cause the flame to leap to a target. You could also do this to another mage's spells, and there were rules for "reading" someone else's spell, which determined how fast you could figure it out (so many lines per round, as I recall). There was also an "On Interrupt" you could use to booby-trap your spells:

    On Interrupt:
    If (caster != me) then
    ... do something to make it blow up in the guy's face ...
    end

    You could also use On Interrupt to give a spell a secondary capability. For example, taking the FlameStaff above, you could add:

    On Interrupt
    If (caster == me) then
    Move MyFlame to PointAt
    else
    Move MyFlame to caster
    end

    So now, I can interrupt the spell without providing any further instruction to make it throw the flame where I point... but if someone else tries it, and isn't careful, it'll throw the flame onto them.

    \n Back around 1995-6ish, some friends and I brewed up a somewhat complex magic system based around verb-noun, for the pretty-obscure Manhunter RPG. It was pretty crunchy, taking a fair bit of calculation, but most of that was front loaded; once you cast the spell it was just a matter of adjudicating the effects. We were all GURPS and Hero veterans so this wasn't an issue. The basic idea behind it is that magic is some kind of extradimensional resource, and you can only handle so much of it at a time but that you can hold it and build it into larger effects; we had ways to equate points of mana to levels of damage and other things, and modifiers (basic multiples) to represent improving range, volume, and so forth. We had a pretty small list of verbs (five sets of a word and its opposite, so you'd have Bind/Loose, Sense/Cloak, and a few others) but a pretty freeform set of nouns. It used a skill difficulty system similar to GURPS, and wider applicability of a noun meant a higher difficulty to learn it; taking required "methodologies" (effectively limitations on your casting, such as requiring gestures or incantations) reduced the difficulty of the noun. It was, IMO, unreasonably fun to play with.

    \n Ever since taking a Linguistics course focused on Syntax, I've had this dream of defining the rules of grammar for a magical language, then giving the players a bunch of made-up words to play with. The idea is that they would try to construct sentences with those words, and, by figuring out the rules of the grammar, they would master the magical possibilities in that gameworld.

    For example, most languages have a simple and repeatable rule for turning a statement into a question (often it's "switch the subject and the verb" or something like that). I can imagine magicians in the setting trying to change the order of the words in their incantations, and then try to figure out why they got the different effect that they did.

    I've never actually put it together, but I would still love to someday.

    \n I have stab at a magic system that combines verbs, adjectives, directions, types of targets, and types of relationships between caster and target. These parts of speech are all symbols, and altering the adjective symbols adjusts the amount of "oomph" in your spell.

    Each symbol has an element and a number. Within a spell (magical sentence), the numbers interact with each other, and the elements interact with each other, and these interactions determine stuff like how easy it is to get the effect you're aiming for, and what side effects you risk. This determination is further mediated by the positions of the sun, moon, and stars at the time of casting.

    The idea was to blend "logic-puzzle toolkit" with "magicalness". However, my one playtest showed that "magicalness" was really about the color given to playing through casting, and not at all about factoring in the moon or whether you had a sample of your target's hair. I think it still might be a fun system to piece together in play, though.

    \n Let's reskin this a bit. This is fun. I am a gamer, a game designer, software developer, a student of lots of programming languages (and parsers), and a builder of online RPGs.

    Spell Fireball:

    Create Fire with name "MyFlame"
    Move MyFlame to PointAt
    Resize MyFlame to 20' Sphere
    End


    No, no.

    You want this:

    Spell Fireball: At 30 feet in the direction I point, I open a hole to the Plane of Fire for one second. Do as I command!

    One could write a parser that understands that. It's far more interesting to say what you do but have the effects be implied rather than explicit. For example, one must know that opening a small hole for one second causes fire to burst in all directions from the hole, and it will reach about 25 feet before ending. If you had said "a large hole," then you would have immolated yourself. If you'd said "a small hole," then the fire would have spread about 5 feet in each direction.

    Of course, these things cost energy. Pointing with a staff is cheaper than pointing with your finger. If it's a magical staff, all the better, and it's more accurate, too. Causing effects further away is more expensive. Larger holes are more expensive, and of course, opening them for longer durations is more expensive.

    The "Do as I command!" is a sort of "go!" that tells the magical program to execute. Without it, you can't as easily set up effects that require concurrent effects, like this:

    Spell Steambath: Right above my head, open a very small hole to the plane of fire for ten seconds. In exactly the same place, open a small hole to the plane of water for ten seconds. Do as I command!
