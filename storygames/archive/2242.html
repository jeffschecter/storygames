A New Game Platform

talysman
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games

There are these predictions for the game industry in 2007 we've all seen linked from John Kim's LJ and from the Threads To Watch thread here. In it, Ryan Dancey talks about hobby games, meaning games that require a lot of out-of-game setup which players find as enjoyable or as necessary to the play experience as the actual play itself. He claims that hobby games are in decline.

This isn't all gloom and doom, if for no other reason that maybe a hobby games industry should be allowed to die, to allow everyone to become hobbyists again instead of obsessing over international recognition. I'll allow other people to argue over whether story games are doing exactly that, in another thread.

But I'm thinking that we may have seeds for a new kind of game, similar to RPGs, but not hobby games as Dancey defines them. We've seen several threads about cutting down prep time, aiming for the goal of zero-prep. Open the book, write some stuff down quickly, then start playing. There's a couple ideas out there i like as potential components of "non-hobby RPGs". For example, although I haven't played Tony Lower-Basch's Capes, I read up a little about it and like the Click and Lock method of character creation, particularly the part about crossing out and replacing one item on each half of the character for quick customization. It's clearly evolved from D&D's race and class system, but makes things quicker, and has potential for other applications. Like: what if you combine "medical condition" and "personal situation" halves to create a conflict for a Medical Center/St. Elsewhere-style game?

Another component: playmaps. Lots of people have been investigating using common playsheets to create more of a boardgame feel. I think this idea hasn't been properly exploited yet. I'd like to see the playmaps become more than just a place for group notes and maybe actually let the playmap alter play in a way only a map could, like using where game tokens, dice, or coins land on the map to create new information.

I also like games like InSpectres putting the basic rules on the character sheets so that the game book is used as a training manual, then abandoned.

What I would like to see here is: more brainstorming. What would the non-hobby successor to roleplaying games look like? What tools already exist that could be developed further to create a new game platform?

    In the world that Ryan Dancey operates in, any given product is defined by what it does (play), what it's made out of (components), and how you get it to the consumers (marketing). A CCG product is different from a non-collectible card game product because while play is roughly the same and the components are very similar, the means by which the producing company gets it into the hands of players is vastly different.

    Honestly, I think the distribution thing is less important than the audience. After all, look who sells CCG games now: not just game stores but comics shops, places that used to sell baseball cards, bookstores, Wal-Mart, everyone. If you build an audience for something, stores will decide to carry it. Beside, having we been talking about the end of traditional distribution for the past 5 years? Sell stuff through your own internet store. Deliver the components electronically and let people print them out themselves. Boo-yeah!

    As for what the "new school" games will look like, I think everybody's on the right track here. I definitely think it's not a question of if but who and when. Game designers have learned a great deal from each other in the past 5-10 years, and so I have faith that many people will eventually start making games that fall along the boundaries that currently separate various game genres, including:

    - Board Games
    - CCGs
    - Roleplaying
    - Larps
    - Interactive Fiction
    - Fan Fiction
    - MMORPGs
    - Video Games
    - Cosplay
    - etc.

    Syncretism is pretty clearly the future.
