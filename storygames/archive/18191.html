    \n Hi guys -- and hi Jason! Thanks for the interest in the book, and the support. If you want to know a little bit more about our process, I recently did a short podcast interview about it here (first 10-12 minutes of the cast), a comics blog interview here, and there's some info on our project's website (along with more page samples).

    The basic steps of our process were:

    1) Play Fiasco using a custom playset that plugged our Vermont railroad town into the "Small Southern Town" playset, recording the session digitally.
    2) Assign each contributor a chunk of the recording to transcribe.
    3) Create a unified, formatted transcription.
    4) Break the transcription into scenes, and assign each scene a page count, mindful of the fact that the entire story has to come in at 72 pages.
    5) Assign each contributor 3-5 scenes to "thumbnail" (think storyboard for comics), usually scenes that include the character played by the contributor.
    6) Edit the 5 sets of thumbnails into one cohesive whole. This was the biggest "writing" step, the step where we changed/shaped the raw play session the most.
    7) Establish the page production line: lettering > foreground pencils > background pencils > inks > colors.
    8) Assign each contributor one or more positions in the page production line.
    9) Go!
    10) Design and packaging.

    \n Hi David --\n
    Thanks for answering questions! I'd like to add a few:

    When transcribing:

    Did you literally type out every word said by every player? Or every word other than out-of-game stuff ("I have to go to the bathroom") and abortive attempts ("My guy says, um, he says that, uh...")?
    \nThe instructions I gave were to turn the audio into a rough movie script during the transcription, so most of the hesitation and incidental chatter was cut at that point. Each transcriber turned in dialogue and stage directions, focused on what was said at the table and trying to edit as little as possible. So the term "transcription" may even be pushing it a little. \n
    What was your method to distinguish character speech from player speech?
    \nIt was purely up to the interpretation of the transcriber. But everyone at the table knew each other already, so they were familiar with each other's personalities and speech patterns, and each person finished his or her transcription within 48 hours, so the session was still fresh in their minds.\n
    When thumbnailing:

    Was the working assumption re: balloon text "We'll use the unaltered character dialogue from the play recordings"? Or did the contributors do things like, "Well, that went on forever in the recording, but the character really only made one point, so one bubble in a standard panel ought to suffice"?
    \nAs noted above, we tried to keep the first draft of the transcription as raw as possible within reason. Most of the kind of cutting you're describing in your second example happened when I made my first editorial pass on the transcriptions, at the end of which we had something much closer to a formatted film script. And it's great that you bring up the idea of what a "standard speech bubble" can hold, because that is a huge concern when writing for comics. A basic rule of thumb I teach is to try to limit dialogue to one sentence per balloon, editing to fit and/or structuring an entire scene around that limit.\n
    When editing:

    What did you find most necessary in order to bridge the gap from play transcript to comic? Removing scenes? Removing parts of scenes? Re-ordering scenes? Adding material of any particular sort?
    \nThat's a hard one, but in thinking about it now I would say that, before I think about what to cut, I ask myself what the essential elements of the story are, and how to best retain/reinforce them over the course of the editing process. For this project, the primary concern was character: how do I retain the distinct personalities that emerged from the play session? This is not only a great strength of the Fiasco play model, but what in the end will distinguish our comic from most other comics. Each character really feels like an individual, and the story emerged from their interactions -- from the bottom up, as opposed to from the top down.

    So in editing the dialogue, my focus was on maintaining the personalities of the characters. Secondary to that was hammering out the plot specifics, so that there was continuity and consistency. As a Fiasco player, I'm sure you're aware of how details can shift and change over the course of a session, and that doesn't really matter so much in a live session (in fact, it can be part of the fun). But once things start to get nailed down in a book aimed at a more general audience, that stuff has to line up and play out in a way that doesn't wreck the suspension of disbelief. And on that note, one thing we ended up doing was toning down the level of catastrophe a bit from the actual play session, in order to keep the proceedings believable and grounded.\n
    P.S. I'm happy to discuss this wherever works best for you. I'm a backer, so if you'd rather I post a KS comment, just let me know!
    \nNo problem, this is fine. Thanks for the interest, and thanks for the support!

    I think perhaps you answered that by saying that the transcribers wrote movie scripts. I know that movie scripts use a certain format to clearly denote location info, events, actions and speech.     \nWe adhered loosely to screenplay conventions. Since no one else was going to read the script outside the team, there just needed to be enough information for everyone on the team to get things like character actions, etc. And throughout the process there were these thumbnail drawings of each page, which could usually answer any questions about framing and staging that arose (i.e., how wide or close a given "shot" needed to be). \n
    That's great to hear about the distinctive characters produced by the bottom-up approach; I'd been hoping for that, and that was one of the reasons I went in for a copy. Does preserving that distinctiveness mean that you cut fewer lines than you would have otherwise? I'm actually pretty surprised that 4 hours of talking could fit in anything close to 72 pages, so I've been assuming a lot was cut.
    \nIndeed, a lot was cut. It was a great exercise in editing for me and my managing editor, Donna Almendrala. I would say it was roughly equivalent to boiling a single paragraph down to 1 sentence. The goal was to distill while losing as little of the story's essential character as possible. I think that a 96-page comic book maps pretty well onto a 90-minute film, and the average Fiasco session (in our experience) feels equivalent in terms of ground covered by the story. But due to conservative strategizing on my part, we only had 72 pages to work with, so from those numbers I'm going to ballpark that we cut 20% of the raw story that was created during that first session. Not 20% of the dialogue -- we cut way more than 20% of that -- but 20% of plot development, side plots, etc. Learning what I have from Bingo Baby, I'm considering upping the page count to 96 for the next book (which we're starting June 1).\n
    Replacing a gonzo catastrophe with a more believable one makes sense to me. Were there any other additions you deemed necessary? Any added scenes?
    \nGosh, let me think -- I would have to go back and compare our raw transcript with the final book, it's all lost in the haze. I know we added a couple of "color" scenes, just visuals to give some context or to create a shift in the rhythm. We came up with one or two of those during the live session (as per the suggestion to do so in the Fiasco rules), and they were great, but their inclusion meant we needed to integrate similar scenes in other places for the sake of consistency and pacing. And if memory serves, we added some stuff near the end for the sake of clarity.\n
    I hope I'm not grilling you excessively here! I have some practical and non-practical curiosity in answering whether the material of an RPG can merely inspire a publishable story, or whether that post-play sensation of, "Hey, if that thing we just did had been a comic, people would totally have loved reading it!" might ever be accurate.
    \nI firmly believe that that feeling can be accurate, but of course it depends on the specific story being told and the audience you want to reach with that story. Fiasco is structured in such a way that it is really good at producing stories worth sharing, and since a Coen Brothers movie is the basic model, the potential audience is broader than it might be for a story generated by a game more locked into a particular subculture. For our next project, we want to maintain an improvisational approach, but since one of our goals is to experiment from book to book, we need some other tool besides Fiasco. I've been playing the Dungeon Crawl Classics Role Playing Game a lot lately, and I love the stories that emerge from that, but it I would be reluctant to lock our next story into the D&D subculture. Not because it would hurt us in any way -- we'd probably sell more books, in fact -- but because that ground is already pretty well-covered.

    And on that note, I'd love to hear any ideas anyone here might have for our next collaborative modus operandi!\n
    As a creator, how much of the work I'd ordinarily do has already been done?
    \nIn our experience, nearly all of it. Creating a story from nothing can sometimes be a painful, solitary, frustrating process, and what I love about role-playing games and improvisational acting is that, when the story-making is a joint venture, it comes alive and becomes entertaining and fun in its own right. It's one of those things where the whole is greater than the sum of its parts, and what you end up with is something that no single creator could have ever conceived. As a creator, my main contributions to Bingo Baby were choosing the approach (Fiasco) and editing. And I really enjoy editing.

    As a prospective player**, how much is reading this comic like being in the room for the playing of the game?
    \nGood question. I would say they are very different experiences. I think the comic is funny and entertaining, but it is also poignant and sad. All of those elements were present in the play session, but "funny and entertaining" drove the experience, which, like most sessions in our experience, got pretty raucous at times. The comic is not raucous, so the poignance and sadness rise in the mix.

    Some potentially disturbing story elements were played for laughs and absurdity in the live session, which does not come through as easily on the page. For instance, the character Goldie is a 68-year-old retired train conductor with a drug problem. That was funny and profane in play, sort of tragic and potentially exploitative in translation. But just before we started playing, I reminded everyone to treat their characters like real people, to treat them with respect, regardless of how stupid or absurd they may end up behaving. Luckily, I think that worked (you'll be able to judge for yourself), because when the pleasure of the players is absent, the strength of the characters comes through. Another reason my editorial focus was on the characters.

    In any case, reading the comic is a markedly different experience from playing the game, but I think anyone who reads it and then discovers that it was created using a game (information we reserve for the credits page at the end), will probably be really curious about the game.\n
    If I were ever able to give people a comic of my game that was fun to read and a genuine example of what play was like, that'd be awesome. I don't think I've ever seen that -- most mere transcripts of play bore me, and most games I've played that were based on published works felt nothing like watching/reading those works.
    \nI think you'll be able to hand people our book, wait for them to read it, and then say, "Did you like that? Good. Because that was created using Fiasco, but playing Fiasco is even better."\n

    Good point. I wonder, what is it about the moment of play, where Goldie gets laughs? I think it's something about the process of seeing holes, and filling those holes with unexpected content. It's the difference between:

    there's a person with a drug problem who is a retired 68-year-old and they used to be a train conductor and their name is Goldie

    and

    we need a person with a drug problem... edgy teen? stressed banker? no, wait, let's have an old woman!
    does she have a sordid past? illness? no, she's retired from her glamorous life!
    her glamorous life as a... secret agent? racecar driver? train conductor!
    yes! old Goldie misses her days on the rails!
