    \n I've always played it pretty traditionally - there's a strong GM who throws adversity at the players. Ideally conflicts that will leave the players with a lot of choices about how to approach and resolve them. Here's one:

    The pterodactyls are nesting on the cliff over your village all of a sudden and, sooner or later, they are going to steal a baby.

    That's a problem - do you want to go kill them all? Do you want to put frickin' roofs on your huts? If the players dither too long, steal a baby. That's a whole other problem. Have it rescued from the pterodactyl nest by another tribe and adopted. Enough of this stuff and they'll start making their own problems among themselves, trust me. Give them problems that are essentially social, group-cohesion testing problems. Create NPCs with strong points of view.

    I'd approach it like any other game - look for what the players care about (ask them and look at their characters) and then challenge that. It's totally OK to talk when describing stuff, or explaining stuff - just no direct communication. Leave space to get up and thrash around, and demonstrate that physicality is encouraged.

    Is that helpful?

    \n Jason's pretty well hit the nail on the head. I play the game with a sort of light adventure bent, i try to get the players going on little missions, and then presenting them with complex situations (like dealing with other tribes who have different ideologies) they have to overcome as a group. The more open-ended the solution the better things are going to be.

    The biggest lack of the book is that it doesn't really tell you what makes cool games (from a GM's perspective). Record of threats corrects this a little by giving you a bunch of NPCs and settings and stuff, but you don't need to buy more to make Primitive work. Just think of situations where normal play groups all express opinions and try to meet in the middle, then plunge your cave men into THAT problem. A few dinosaur attacks and crazy caves full of glowing crystals or bear people also works pretty well.

    Thanks for picking up the game. I hope this helps, if not, keep asking (or if you have other questions) and i'll tell you whatever i can.

    \n You don't really want to overthink Primitive. I don't think Kevin's trying to say anything especially deep about civilization and violence. I think he wants you to take your group of cavemen and fight giant sea kraken represented by packs of chewing gum, so you can use the gum pieces to make tentacles (this is what we did, last time Nathan ran it). It's basically a fun miniatures combat game with the grunting and communication issues thrown on top of it, plus an invitation to engage in culture-building and social manuvering, like the baby-adoption stuff Jason is talking about. I Primitive.

    \n You can set the dial to the level of seriousness you want, or spin it wildly during the session. It works pretty well however you play it.
