\n This Wednesday I'm getting a few people together to try out Danger Patrol (beta), and I'm pretty sure at least one of them will want to play a Mystic. However, the Mystic style card has a power listed that simply says "Ritual Magic: Should do something cool." So... I was wondering if anyone has had any ideas on this front. I've been thinking of maybe allowing the Mystic to cast a ritual during a scene by checking off any number of their [+]s, and anyone in the next scene that does something empowered by the ritual can spend from those [+]s to add hits to a roll instead of bonus dice.

Does anyone have any other ideas that we might try out?

    \n I see. So it's providing an expansion of what a character is normally capable of by saying that most characters can't do big flashy rituals to affect threats and change the stuff that is happening in a scene because it doesn't fit with them thematically (so they have to describe it in a different way, if they want to close a dimensional rift, for example). But you can do magic, assuming you are willing to sacrifice the time necessary to do so, and thus have more freedom with how you explain your approach.

    That seems interesting, if unlike the other Style powers. I definitely don't want to lean on hard mechanical effects as a crutch, but recent experience with D&D 4e (for example) has me seek out mechanical effects as a representation of what happens at the fictional level. And since the game seems to involve a lot of tactical usage of dice and powers, it seemed like it would be appropriate to have something more similar to the mechanics from other powers. But this kind of thing works... it reminds me of some of the Secrets from the Solar System, which don't really provide bonus dice or new effects but rather expand the purview of a particular ability (e.g. "You can use your Artist ability to cause harm in physical conflicts" or "You can use your Knife Fighting ability instead of your Cooking ability to make conflict rolls").

    \n What about an effect like: Target any Threat, you get +1 hit against it cumulatively for each consecutive round where you target it. Take a reduced hit if you end the ritual (by taking a different action) before the Threat is defeated.
    \n
    \nSo the ritual becomes something you are locked into and becomes more powerful the longer you keep it up for; it will ulitmately defeat the Threat, regardless of how many hits you actually roll, but you might acquire a lot of Danger along the way. Also, it allows the ritual itself to take any form that is appropiate to the situation.
    \n
    \nThe problem is, its only really useful against a high level Threat where there are also other Threats on the table, otherwise its likely to be destroyed anyway before your next turn comes around, so all you've really gotten is +1 hit.
    \n
    \nMaybe a ritual just lets you ignore a Threat's ability, like you can hit something that is staged behind another Threat, or your attack is not reduced by its resistance?

    \n Can I resurrect this thread? I think I am gonna resurrect this thread.

    If I can wrangle enough players, I am planning on running Danger Patrol sometime in the next couple weeks (cross fingers). I went looking online for precisely this question, and I love these answers. I really like the general idea of "spend some time doing a ritual to do something especially cool," but I suspect some of my players would want something like this a bit more spelled out.

    James's suggestion is the first place my mind went too, but I wonder if it might be neat if using ritual magic actually CREATES a threat that the mystic must face alone (binding fickle spirits or elemental forces to your will and whatnot), and defeating that threat allows you to toss a bunch of hits toward some other problem. I'm still getting the hang of this game's mechanics, but something like: "RITUAL MAGIC: Treat your ritual as a major threat that can only be faced with mystical powers. If you defeat it, deal twice its level in hits to any other threats or questions on the board."
