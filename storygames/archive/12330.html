\n Hey guys! I'm not entirely certain of the protocol for press releases here, but I figured the news was likely to be of sufficient interest and wanted to make ourselves available for any questions you guys might have. Here's the release:
\n
\nDOJ INC. ANNOUNCES ACQUISITION OF MAJORITY SHARE IN INDIE PRESS REVOLUTION
\n
\nJune 17th, 2010- New York City, NY: DOJ Inc. is proud to announce that they have closed a deal to acquire a majority share of Indie Press Revolution, the industry's leading direct-sales network of high-quality small press game publishers, and will be taking over administrative operations of the company beginning July 1st, 2010. The minority shareholders have all approved the sale and will be retaining their ownership shares in the company.
\n
\n"I'm delighted to be taking over a sales organization with the brand strength and reputation for quality that IPR has built over the last several years," says Darren Watts, President of DOJ Inc. "IPR has a very strong business model and has done an outstanding job representing its client creator-publishers, and we intend to carry forward with the same philosophy in order to bring great new games to the public from the cutting edge of game design."
\n
\n"I'm excited to be working with Darren, Jason, and the rest of the DOJ team," said Ryan Macklin, General Manager of IPR, who will be remaining in that position. "They have an excitement about IPR that I relish, and I'm looking forward to the future they'll bring to this company."
\n
\n"DOJ Logistics has already been providing IPR and its publishers complete shipping and warehousing services since August 2008," said DOJ Logistics Director Jason Walters, "so both the publishers and the customers of IPR should receive the same fine service they've come to expect."
\n
\n"DOJ Inc has been providing IPR great service and over the years we have developed a close relationship with the warehouse staff. I can't think of any better partner for IPR to help the business grow and thrive in the years to come," said Brennan Taylor, owner of Galileo Games and outgoing President of IPR. Mr. Taylor is retaining a minority share in IPR, as well as remaining a client publisher, and will be assisting DOJ in the transition.
\n
\n\t\t\t\t\t###

\nABOUT INDIE PRESS REVOLUTION
\n
\nIndie Press Revolution is a consolidation house and direct-sales network of quality small press game publishers, dedicated to establishing a community in which creator-publishers can flourish and share their ideas and visions together in a cooperative environment. IPR sells exclusively to retailers and the public through their website, at www.indiepressrevolution.com. They currently represent over ninety outstanding independent small publishers, including such industry standouts as Cubicle 7 Entertainment, Evil Hat Productions, Arc Dream Press, Pelgrane Press, and Galileo Games, to name only a few.
\n
\nABOUT DOJ INC.
\n
\nDOJ Inc. are the owners of Hero Games, publishers of Champions and other roleplaying games using the award-winning Hero System, and of DOJ Logistics, providing warehousing and shipping services to the tabletop gaming industry.

    \nDarren: Brennan's definition of "indie" and what IPR has carried has always been broader than any one scene. I think sometimes this was masked by the IPR site being somewhat nontransparent to navigate, so folks tended to mostly stick with games they were specifically looking for or had been linked to. But I think that IPR has always been theoretically open to any games produced by individual or small groups of designers. I can imagine the potential for some of the more hardcore pro-"creator rights" indie designers to passively or actively resist perceived shifts in IPR's mission, but, in my mind at least, those shifts are nothing new and represent a natural broadening of the indie community, something that is already happening in other areas. Honestly, if you can make the site *more usable* I don't think anyone will have much to complain about as far as content, as long as there isn't a flood of mediocre products that make it difficult to find the good stuff, as on RPGnow and other places.

    The biggest challenge for IPR is maintaining and expanding its quality of service. With the shift by many individual publishers back to handling much of their own distribution (the Unstore, the Burning Wheel store, etc.) and some mixed feelings about IPR's initial foray into selling to retailers (which I think a lot of publishers, including myself at the time, weren't adequately prepared for), IPR is still doing really well, obviously, but may no longer represent the default distribution choice for many current and upcoming indie publishers. Instead, it's a significant option within a still limited cloud of different distribution options, and many publishers are choosing to combine multiple different methods at the same time (maybe due to the collapse of Wizard's Attic and the closure of Key20, warnings not to place all your eggs in one basket). IPR may not have any strong challenges yet, at least in its core services, but it would be a shame to rest on your laurels, especially in a relatively volatile business environment, with the potential for major shifts nearly constant.

      \n Go to Amsterdam and browse the shops, each of them selling the exact same souvenir t-shirts and hash pipes and shot glasses with marijuana leaf iconography. Browse the shops in the city in Cancun, or in Fort Lauderdale, and you'll see the same effect, except it's brightly painted ceramic reptiles and "bikini inspector" t-shirts. There's no reason to buy from any given shop if they're all selling the same sweatshop clutter. An online RPG shop not dealing in used games has three options: 1) sell everything and compete on price, because there's no other reason than price for a customer to choose you over RPGshop or whoever, 2) drive to have a more complete product catalog than anyone else so your business lives in the long tail (ala Amazon.com), or 3) sell a curated catalog of games and work to be significant via the social media space.

      As an indie game publisher, the one to be afraid of is #1.

      Here's a curious thing I noticed:

      \n

      For a long time, Shock: was the #8 seller on IPR. Eventually, there was more stuff (like Don't Rest Your Head and Don't Rest Your Head PDF) that pushed it down the list. Then the list got shorter. As soon as I bumped off that list, my sales dropped. That says a lot about the site's interface, and the power of front-page presentation.

      \n

      When Vincent and I decided to do our own sales, my sales numbers picked up quite dramatically after a brief lag while my own promotion got folks pointed the right way (I don't know about Vincent's sales). Retailers are contacting me themselves. I sell several copies a week to individuals and just sold several dozen to retailers. I'm going to be out of this print run in a week or two.

      \n

      I ship anywhere for five bucks. It's not difficult or expensive to do that. When an overseas retailer buys my stuff, I charge real shipping, which is usually not onerous. Larger volume is obviously cheaper.

      \n

      For any retailer who only buys through IPR, you're not getting the whole picture. You're not getting Dogs in the Vineyard or Apocalypse world, and you're not getting Shock: but your customers are getting them anyway direct. If ever there were a reason to return to IPR, I'd take it. But the decreasing volume of sales coupled with the increasing percentage cut wound up not making sense for me. I appreciate that the company needs to take its percentage, and this is in no way acrimonious. But IPRs interests turned out to not be my interests. IPR wants to sell copies of lots of games. I want to sell lots of copies of my games.

      \n

      I've got no idea how one would go about rectifying that, though the "curated store" thing seems like an intriguing avenue to explore.

      \n I don't know about JACN, but these are a few of the issues I had with selling through IPR, issues that -- along with personal choices and some other stuff --- contributed to me pulling out of publishing Push in print format and ceasing to be a commercial publisher entirely, at least for now.

      1. I priced Push very carefully for the direct market and selling to folks at conventions, so that I wouldn't be making much profit per copy and could keep the price low. In my case, this was because it mostly was a labor of love and I didn't -- and still don't, really -- want to think about what I was doing as a business. This was a "vanity" project in that sense, for me. But I think this same issue holds true for folks trying to keep their prices low for other, business-savvy reasons.

      2. I was only making a few bucks a copy on Push, so when IPR started selling to retailers at a steep (but I guess standard for the industry) discount, I ended up losing money on each sale. It wasn't a lot of money and it was counterbalanced by the bucks I was making through direct sales, so I didn't even notice for a while. Initially, direct sales far outweighed retail sales, so the money was coming in and the hardest thing I had to worry about was calculating profits so I could pay my contributors.

      3. However, over time, retail sales picked up as a percentage of my total sales, both as IPR was pushing that angle more (because it meant more sales for IPR, increasing their profits) and as the initial spike of direct orders -- which for me, mainly happened in the first few months after the initial release -- crested and began to decline. Finally, I discovered that I was actually losing money on retailer orders, especially with discounted or free shipping deals and the cost of shipping books to IPR's warehouse. Fuck. But it was hard to know what to do at that point. My options were basically:

      -- pull out of IPR, because it wasn't clear to me that I could opt out of retail sales (though Ben Lehman and a few other folks told me later that they did just that)
      -- raise the costs across the board, because I couldn't raise the cost just for retailers, since IPR advertised their discount from the cover price
      -- eat the costs for the rest of the current print run and move to PDF only publishing

      4. I ultimately decided on the latter, because I really didn't want to raise the price of the book and really didn't want to handle distribution on my own, which was the only real option if I pulled out of IPR. The PDF ended up being released for free (with the permission of my contributors) and eventually pretty much dropped off the radar entirely, partially because I was burnt out and frustrated with the publishing process. I didn't want to treat this as a business anyway; it was a hobby for me. But it seemed as if it was impossible to make this sort of "vanity" or charity project work without putting a lot of time into and treating it like a business. And, especially at that time, I wasn't prepared to do that.

      5. At the time, IPR's push towards retail sales was very discomforting to me and I think several other member publishers. IPR was pushing for sales that would generate basically the same amount of profits for IPR (since it took the same cut on retail and direct sales, at least as I recall) but would make much less money for the smaller independent publishers that made up the bulk of IPR's clients. Even the big sellers like Ron, Vincent, etc. ended up moving on to another distribution model and ceasing to work with IPR, which I think indicates that NO ONE was really happy with that business direction, even the folks who stood to make the most money through distribution. It very much seemed, and honestly still seems that way, that IPR is moving to becoming much more like a traditional distributor and losing a lot of the personal touches and dedication to publishers that it was previously known for. And maybe that was necessary -- then and now -- to make IPR profitable, but I think it lost a lot of love in the process, which is unfortunate.

      So, overall, there were a lot of mistakes and misconceptions on my side, as a first-time publisher, and I take responsibility for those, but in the end the experience left me wondering if it was even worth bothering with print publishing at all, which is pretty unfortunate. I wish there had been more resources and communication with publishers about the direction that IPR was moving and tools and advice for making us all more prepared and on-board with that. Even with all of IPR's recent success, I look back on what was lost along the way, especially the products and publishers that no longer work with IPR, and wonder if it was worth it.

      P. S. The other thing that bothered me about IPR at the time, as opposed to doing distribution on your own, was that there was zero contact between publishers and the folks buying our games, which is not really how the indie community has traditionally worked. Generally, most designers welcome contact and interactions with the folks buying their games and, while the VOR podcast and a couple other outreach efforts were cool, I really just wanted an email list of people interested in my products, even if there was an option to sign out of that when making orders. Not feeling like I knew who was buying my product felt really wrong.

      \n When I quit IPR, I lost sales, no doubt about it. I was, after all, a full beneficiary of IPR's promote-the-top-ten approach to marketing games.

      It's really significant, as Joshua says, that IPR's interests and an individual publisher's interests aren't the same. It means that it's not just a decision about money. As a publisher, I also have to make decisions about giving my business to a company whose interests are sometimes at odds with mine, and sometimes at odds with my fellow publishers'. It's an ethical, or ideological, or at least business-mission-ical decision too. "Is IPR a good value for the price?" and also "does giving IPR my business align with my goals as a publisher?"

      I think that IPR is neat and good. IPR carries my wife's game, for goodness sake, and it causes us no strife whatsoever.

      I think that the circumstance we find ourselves in, where IPR can presume itself to be an essential middleman, is terrible. It's in IPR's best interests to maintain this circumstance just as long as possible; of course it is! But it's in lumpley games' interests -- and my own personal interests, as a mutuality-minded creator smack solid in the indie hardcore -- for this circumstance to be done with, and the sooner the better.

      \n Seems clear to me that Joshua's just explaining why IPR doesn't work for him. I can see how the proportionate fees can be seen as distasteful - they detach the IPR workload and expenses from their rewards, making IPR a commission retailer instead of hired help for the publisher.       Perhaps it would be worthwhile for IPR to explore alternative client models, such as allowing sales channels other than its own main website. As it is, selling through IPR is effectively retail sales - the best the publisher can do to facilitate is to tell the customer that he should go to this IPR webstore to get his game. This works fine for those who aren't in it for the direct sales, but some like the advantages of communicating directly with your customer base and making the commercial transaction personal, so that the customer knows to come back to them for further business, and so their customer relationship has an opaque back-end - the customer doesn't need to know where you stock and who does your fulfillment, those are all functions IPR reveals by the virtue of its webstore model of operations.

      A potential problem in any alternative sales channels that affect the IPR profit sharing model is that they cause competition that might be found distasteful. For instance, if I'm given an opportunity to hook up my own orders page into the IPR fulfillment system and I'm told that IPR only takes 15% instead of 35% out of any sales (or whatever) I can cause through my own orders page, that'll reduce my interest in marketing IPR as a venue sharply - my links to buying my game no longer go to IPR. This sort of thing would ultimately reduce the value of IPR as a store to its owners, considering how the best marketing IPR has comes from the fact that there are dozens of indie publishers out there marketing their own game and directing customers to IPR.

      For my own part I don't have a particular problem with the retail nature of IPR per se. It's a good deal for the retailer and customer who want to buy many things from a set of closely associated publishers, much easier than buying them separately one by one. The model does cause what is effectively a subvention of passive and slow marketing schemes among the publishers to a degree: IPR gets the same cut regardless of whether the publisher drives customers to the IPR or if the sales only happen accidentally or even due to IPR's marketing efforts. Thus the "easy money" IPR gets from fulfilling popular titles goes in part into the expenses of warehousing and marketing slow moving stuff. Same as any retailer, except the publishers have a stake in the proceedings, too, due to the commission sales model.
