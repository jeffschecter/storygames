\n Traditionally RPGs give a list of skills/abilities/traits and players pick from that list when designing their character.

However more recently some games, indie games in particular, allow the player to choose which skills the character has be it inconspicuous interrogation1, intuitive leap2 or "I ain't sure about that"3.

So now I'm wondering which was the first published game with free choice traits. I think it was Tweet/Laws' Over the Edge (1992). Anyone think differently?


1 you can interrogate someone without them knowing you are doing it (HQ)
2 you can make a mental intuitive leap to get round any problem (HQ)
3 DitV 'natch.

    \n In the 1988 TSR Bullwinkle and Rocky Role Playing Party Game, there are 3 modes of play:
    \n
    \n- The Narration Game
    \n- The Everybody Can Do Something Game
    \n- The Graduate Game
    \n
    \nIn the Graduate Game, you can create your own characters by selecting positive/negative traits and powers using a spinner randomizer or you can make them up with group consensus.
    \n
    \nThey also have rotating GM play, narration rights, failure = consequence and more!
    \n
    \nThanks to Eppy for bringing this game to my attention!

    \n Never played the Dallas Game. I definitely think it is an RPG. You advocate a persona. You have skills and powers, there is a rotating GM, adversity, and a resolution mechanic to determine if you succeed and add complications / consequences if you fail. As I mentioned it has 3 modes. The Narration Game may be less RPG like. But the other 2 are very much an RPG i believe.

    \nI would be cool to trace back this innovation to TSR, 1998, and Bullwinkle and Rocky.
