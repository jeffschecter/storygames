\n Elsewhere someone turned me on to "swim lane" diagrams. In a swim lane, you divide your diagram into columns representing each actor and the vertical scale is the order of events. In this way you can assign single and group responsibility, see parallisable tasks, etc. I re-wrote a combat system flow chart for Spirit of the Far Future as a swim lane and got a nice piece of information out of it accidentally. Here's the swim lane:

See how the "referee" column (previously GM -- getting to that) never has a box alone in his lane? That means in this subsystem he is always acting as a facilitator of negotiations. He's an arbitrator. He never produces anything without consultations whereas, by contrast, the subject and object do. The "Arbitrate results" box might credibly be alone in the referee column, I suppose.

So, falling back again on thirty-year-old game ideas, he's the referee and not the GM.

Anyway, what's cool here is that using a different methodology for displaying information gives us insight. Anyone have similar revelations when they took their text to the drawing board?

    \n I don't have a diagram to share, but I did recently lay out all the elements of a game design on index cards (one element per card) and spread them out on the floor. Then grouped them, un-grouped them, slid them around, re-ordered, removed cards, added new ones, etc. It was very fruitful -- especially in showing where there was too much stuff. I think Daniel Solis mentioned trying something similar recently, which was probably what spurred me to do it.

    I find that getting out of my head and/or the rigid flow of text is a big help to me during a design process. Probably one reason why I spend so much time on sheets and play aids even in the early stages.
