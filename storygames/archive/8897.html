    \n A modern real world setting.
    Without strict real world rules.
    No fantasy elements.
    Maybe subtle supernatural elements.
    I'm playing a normal person thrown into an insane situation.
    I'm normal, but I'm doing things no average person would.
    And I'm changing into something more.
    Dealing with complicated problems.
    With consequences I can't walk away from.
    Where there is no right answer.
    But I have to act.

    A familiar world where I dare ask myself... what if?

    \n Interesting thread idea. Are you asking for a perfect setting from the perspective of a single character within that setting, or from the perspective of the entire game world as a whole?

    When it comes to RPGs, I've always loved games with fully-developed, multifaceted settings that can be played from a multitude of angles. For example, the setting for White Wolf's Aeon/Trinity had a variety of different ways in which it could be explored: the PCs could be a military strike force (sanctioned or otherwise), secret agents for world governments, investigators, hackers, freedom fighters, doctors, diplomats, etc. The feel of the game could be anything from hardcore beat-em-up to murder mystery. The locations included a fascist North America dominated by giant arcologies, a post-apocalyptic European wasteland, a xenophobic Japan, mafia-controlled South America, and a shining, quickly-developing Australia. Throw in lunar colonies (some high-class, others slums), and extrasolar colonies that have been out of contact for decades, and you've got an almost unlimited choice as to how to play the game. And I'm the type of person who would love to explore all the different elements of that setting. The genius part? This was detailed in the core book, albeit at a high level.

    From a personal perspective, I'd probably say something similar. To narrow it down to a personal perspective: some sort of cyberpunk setting, with rogue AIs, giant mega-corporations, instant access to data, secrets and mysteries and conspiracies, with a little bit of grunge and grime thrown in. Sexy razorgirls, augmented cyborgs, jacked-in hackers, and high-class decadents who indulge in technology for fun and leisure. Ideally, the whole thing would have a bit of film noir feel to it. My character would definitely be someone who has to struggle, in some capacity, to survive: eking out a living doing odd hacking jobs, security details, private investigation, etc. He probably ends up thrust into a situation which is above and beyond his understanding. Seemingly unconnected elements all connect together to form a larger, and incomprehensible whole, and my character has to deal with working out what's going on, and decided whether or not he wants to affect the outcome (if he even can).

    • Sci-fi or Fantasy - Basically in another world or another time
      • \n

      • \n
    • "People" are the primary obstacle, be they human, robot, alien.
      • \n
    • We each have something unusual about us, be it psychic powers, cybernetics, magical abilities, or just being the best at something. In other words, we are cool.
      • \n
    • Some of us move in different fields than others, "fight" in different venues, but we all have fun. One guy could play a bartender, another a legendary knight. They can show up in each others scenes without getting totally steamrolled - they are dramatically "protected".
      • \n
    • We are not the biggest movers and shakers, but our actions and our choices influence events at critical points.
      • \n
    • We have to make a lot of choices that show what our characters are like as people, and the consequences of those choices drive the game at least as much as any pre-existing situations the GM has chosen
      • \n
    • We might see...The woman raises the glass to her ruby-red lips and takes a sip. Her jaded expression turns to one of wonder. She whispers, "Perfect...". The bartender just smiles and continues wiping the bar.
      • \n

      • \n
    • Sometimes we are dealing with "big-picture" stuff, other times with very personal matters
      • \n
    • There is a long-running but subtle romance between two people (PC-PC or PC-NPC), or possibly a love triangle involving two characters and an NPC
      • \n
    • Alliances shift. People must re-examine their loyalties - sometimes reaffirming, sometimes changing.
      • \n
    • Someone runs up a wall and does a backflip. Spaceships collide. Sparks fly from swords. A man is beaten to the floor but refuses to yield. A prince trods his own crown underfoot.

    \n Mine are more setting parameters than setting details:

    There are no bad guys. Everyone has an understandable motivation to the audience, even if characters in the fiction don't understand it.
    All acting characters are human. (Cylons are great, right up until they're the focus of the show.)
    Magic is either low-powered or non-existent.
    Insanity and madness are debilitating and destructive disorders, never character motivations.
    Love, money, and creativity are as powerful motivators as greed and jealousy.
    Nobody is trying to take over the world.
    There is a social network that tightly binds everybody. Family matters \u2014 for more than a source of adversity.
    Violence is not the only solution to problems, nor is it never a solution.

    \n A stereotypical party might include a "human" (with fey overtones), a dwarf, a Froudian hobgoblin, and a ghost-like brownie. You are something between grail knights, midwives, exorcists, grave robbers, and trash collectors. You are budding young or wilting old. There is no state of balance.

    Crafted tools, jewelry, etc. tend to be someone's ancestor, yet-to-be-born child, or a stolen or traded soul, and as such are only so inanimate. A shovel, a pick, or a brush may be as much an heirloom as a sword. Souls neither enter nor leave the world, and you make sure whatever happens to them happens legally, caringly, or expediently.

    Temple-dwelling godkings abound. They have little extended power beyond the wealth of their offerings, but that counts for a lot, particularly considering the spirited nature of objects.

    Promethean space giants are expected to cause world destruction in the far future. One particular such giant, bound in chains, has over the millenia become the common man's almanac and a mentor to would-be knights.

    A coral palace rises from the shore, a home for fishermen and kelpies, but the wise women say to avoid it.

    A dark wood smothers much of the world. Here the blackest, oldest things entrap and obscure the brightest things. It is a necessary evil.

    A city on the plains, full of politicians, theologians, skeptics, and their labor force and audience, is where the guns come from, and where they are used most frequently. A jungle grows up around the city each year, and it must be burned off for crops. The crops in turn seed the next jungle.

    A copse of trees with such inner heat that they burn from within, each a forge that sends orange sparks and yellow-green leaves rising into the air.. Dwarves scoop embers with flat shovels, or craft delicate items with twig-carved tweezers.

    A soul and a story are the same thing.

    ***

    I guess, in general, I want a setting that can be dialed at a moment's notice from dark, grim(m) horror to dazzlingly bright high myth, with emphases on rich imagery and ecology.

    \n If I can ever get a few projects out of the way (including a short campaign I'm running), I'll get back to making the game based on my perfect game world.

    - The setting is something of a fantasy urban setting, but with technology developed in a slightly different direction (no computers, more boats, fewer vehicles overall, neither dark nor gritty, and with the magic hidden
    - the PCs are: just people, but people who have taken the time to open their eyes to the world, to pay attention to what is going on around them, and discovered just how wondrous world really is and have the heart to want to make it even better
    - PC activities include: exploration/pilgrimages, creating a family/home/community/subculture, helping others while remaining hidden from the world, trying to survive with very little
    - I mentioned fantasy: no spells, no powers, but beings who are otherworldly, who can do that which we cannot, but cannot do those things we can
    - Violence is avoided by setting up problems such that violence simply cannot solve them
    - communities: everyday people, events, subcultures and places that can be dropped into a game as characters walk down the street

    \n I want people who are talented and gifted in the action movie sense, but not with weird special powers.

    The setting is fantastical, but just enough to get the job done. There's FTL travel, say, or subtle ritual magic that allows special healing. There's not a unicorn in every garage. It's a game about people, so nothing exists that can distract from that. "I want to play an intelligent ceiling tile" does not apply.

    There's a budding sense of urgency--a larger quest that must be resolved. It doesn't consume the characters, but it provides motivation, and it's go-to material for both players and GM. It's like exercising. You don't have to do it every minute of every day, but putting it off has consequences. It's a thing that basically all characters want to see resolved, unless they're psychotic.

    The characters are all sympathetic. They might do bad things, but you have reasons to care about them.

    The characters have complex relationships in that their motivations draw them together and pull them apart.

    Your choices in play impact the setting. Things change because of what you do. Cities prosper, children get sick, Jason Morningstar becomes CEO of Proctor & Gamble. Be careful.

    As in Star Trek, gadgets only get you so far. You have to use the Mk. 1 noggin to get you out of trouble. And maybe do some flying kicks.
