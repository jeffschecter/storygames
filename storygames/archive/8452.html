    \nPrimetime Adventures

    Winning narration rights means suddenly being responsible for narrating out a really cool, twisty-turny action set piece.

    This would make make brains melt. It's simply too much narrative responsibility to dump on someone.

    I think cool, twisty-turny action set pieces are best built in discrete units, built one piece after another, often with the use of multiple rolls along the way to provide results no one at the table expected or even wanted. This is what will make it twisty and turny.

    I think HeroQuests extended conflict system would do this in wonders. I think think your suggestion of InSpectres is on the right track as well.

    I'm assuming that the question is about the types of characters and situations that appear in the movies, particularly the ones I like.

    \n

    So, going through my shelf:

    \n

    Jenga dread also wouldn't work. Too much is defined by the deaths of the characters, which isn't how Star Wars works at all.

    \n

    The Mountain Witch, for all its shared samurai stuff, also is no good. Too much about the characters being driven against each other.

    \n

    Spione is very, very wrapped up in its own color. The realpolitik of the Cold War is totally alien to the world of clear good and evil of Star Wars (... even when you compensate for some very suspicious hand waving about good and evil in the movies.)

    \n

    Cold City, the spiritual successor to The Mountain Witch, has the same issue with character relationships, but with even more alien color.

    \n

    Hot War is even more alien. There aren't even putative bad guys. Just guys.

    \n

    Polaris would work fine for the end of the Republic.

    \n

    Carry, while also being about a war, is precisely the war Lucas was trying not to talk about.

    \n

    Beowulf, based as it is on the Anthology Engine, could kinda be used, since it covers a lot of the same themes. The rules tend to incline one toward selfish violence. I guess that's a Star Warsy theme. There's a single character everything else revolves around, too.

    \n

    In a Wicked Age could be done, I suppose, like Beowulf could, with the proper oracle, but frankly, that's kind of pushing it. The floating cast really kind of precludes the "ensemble of friends" element of the movies. Not to mention that things are only ever done coercively.

    \n

    Shock: Social Science Fiction, ironically, is really inappropriate. People have joked with me about doing space opera stuff, but it's too "ripped from the headlines" to work, I think. Plus, the amount of control that you have, moment-to-moment, as players of the game over the color is really important. It's really a game hostile toward existing canon.

    \n

    Dogs in the Vineyard would be fine. Ensemble, moral authority, the whole thing. The key is to make it so that Towns are both Star Wars colored and morally challenging.

    \n

    Poison'd Yeah, no. Not even a little. It's all about driving at each other to scrape your dignity off the floor where it resides in a puddle of your own blood and semen.

    \n

    Breaking the Ice. Totally. Han and Leia's first three dates: The Space Slug, Cloud City, and Jabba's Palace.

    \n

    Shooting the Moon. Star Wars is lacking serious romantic rivalry and you could patch that with this game.

    \n

    Under My Skin. I don't think Star Wars is really an effective environment for multi-limbed romantic relationships.

    \n

    ... and now I have to get back to work, so I'm going to stop linking:

    \n

    PTA: of course.

    \n

    Some games that don't have spines, so I can't read them: Who knows? Consider that there are flaws to spiral bindings, people.

    \n

    The Shadow of Yesterday? You bet.

    \n

    Sorcerer: Demons can be Force techniques, I suppose. Sometimes, they own you. Sometimes, you own them. So I guess you could.

    \n

    Psi Run: Ensemble cast, psychic powers, persecution, and wounds that lead to complication? Sounds like Star Wars to me!

    \n

    Some more spiral-bound games: C'mon, guys. It's not a good format.
