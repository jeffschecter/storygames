\n Hey, do any of you speak German? If so, you could really help me. I am rather desperate.

I am writing a Trail of Cthulhu scenario, called The Watchers In The Sky. In this scenario, there is a description of a poem, which is described as a single German word written in a circle:\n
Your research of cosmic entities is fascinating but inconclusive. However, there is one constant in your research: a reference to a 19th Century Bavarian poem, "The Great Cycle". Written in a spiral, as a single German word, it translates roughly as follows: "They travel to the pulse of the wheel and music that turns at the heart of space as they leave and return and leave and return to the beat of the wheel as it turns as they travel". Interpretations of this poem vary, but most agree it concerns a regular cycle of visits to the Earth.
\nI am really hoping someone could write that poem/word/thing for me. It needn't be an exact translation of the words: bastardise as much as you like. I am willing to repay you in beer, German or otherwise, or will happily read through your game in return.

Graham

    \n So like maybe "middle-of-space-moving music wheel return traveler?" And it's poetry so there's poetic license, and interpretations vary?

    So maybe: weltraumherzverschiebungmusikradfolgenr\xfcckholreisender

    weltraumherzverschiebung is heart-of-outer-space-shifting, or you could use weltraumherz-Bewegung (-moving).

    musikradfogen is music-wheel-following, or you could use tonrad or stichhaltigerad (both are sound-wheel), or ger\xe4uschrad (noise-wheel), and add fogen (-following).

    (beat is schlag, so maybe musikradschlangfogen?)

    r\xfcckholreisender is return traveler.

    I don't actually know how to combine all these words properly or how to order them for the proper meaning, but hey poetry, right?

    \n Steve is right that German composite words can't tell a story; they can only drill deeper and deeper into detail of one specific noun. That is, you can't do "leave and return and leave and return". The closest you can do would be:

    Radrhythmusrueckkehrverlassungskreisweltraumherzensmusikpulsreisende

    Doesn't actually make much sense, but it includes the words you wanted I put "circle" after "return leave" to indicate a circle of returning and leaving, and left out one mention of wheel in turn (there's one right at the start).

    \n Turkish is an agglutinative language so it might be able to express the concept in one word.

    My favourite German compound noun is Dampfschifffahrt and not just because it has 3 fs in the middle and pronouncing these things is quite easy if you know where the breaks come. And it's right at the root of this baby:
    Donaudampfschiffahrtselektrizit\xe4tenhauptbetriebswerkbauunterbeamtengesellschaft
    "association of subordinate officials of the head office management of the Danube steamboat electrical services" (the name of a pre-war club in Vienna).

    It's right up there with Hottentottenpotentatentantenattentat (Hottentot potentate's aunt assassination)
