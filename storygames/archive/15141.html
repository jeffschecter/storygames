    \nAdding extra Keys as a way of increasing XP gain does not work as a solution after the first three-ish Keys. It's exactly as you suspect: the practical medium TSoY functions in only allows a character to be involved in one event at a time, so as long as you can get a Key to activate each time you do something, you're not deriving any benefit from the rest of your Keys. This of course assumes that Keys do not stack - you cannot just get lots of nigh-identical Keys that tend to apply to the same situations and activate them all at once. For the dramatic purposes of TSoY not having stacking Keys makes perfect sense, especially because the Story Guide is supposed to coordinate towards the Keys in framing scenes; maybe some other game that used Keys within a different framework could have Keys stack and thus derive more use out of them, although I guess it would make for boring character builds to encourage players to stack as many variations of the Key of Bloodlust as possible.\n

    \n For the sake of conversion I'm going to refer to all advancement mechanisms as keys, since that is essentially what they are.

    Presently, there are five keys active at any given time. One is the key of alignment, another the key of bonds, and third the key of circumstance (situational moves that earn xp). Additionally, there are two active stat keys. What we're interested in doing is replace the stat keys, which are tied to game mechanics and not character behavior with a different mechanism.

    My suggestion is to replace the stat keys with keys from the five factor traits. They could still be highlighted like stats were, but they each describe a different way for the character to act that is not directly tied to moves.


    Openness to experience \u2013 (inventive/curious vs. consistent/cautious). - Mark XP when you show appreciation for art, emotion, adventure, unusual ideas, curiosity, and variety of experience.

    Conscientiousness \u2013 (efficient/organized vs. easy-going/careless). - Mark XP when you show a tendency towards self-discipline, act dutifully, and aim for achievement; planned rather than spontaneous behaviour.

    Extraversion \u2013 (outgoing/energetic vs. solitary/reserved). - Mark XP when you exhibit energy, positive emotions, surgency, and the tendency to seek stimulation in the company of others.

    Agreeableness \u2013 (friendly/compassionate vs. cold/unkind). - Mark XP when show a tendency to be compassionate and cooperative rather than suspicious and antagonistic towards others.

    Neuroticism \u2013 (sensitive/nervous vs. secure/confident). Mark XP when you show a tendency to experience unpleasant emotions easily, such as anger, anxiety, depression, or vulnerability.

    \n One thing to consider:

    How do more advanced characters interface with less advanced characters?

    In AW and TSOY, "beginning" characters play successfully alongside "advanced" characters because there is a fairly quick story arc built into the system, where characters reach their peak and write themselves out of the story. In a long-running campaign, these characters are then replaced with more interesting "fresh" characters.

    In D&D, however, "beginning" characters cannot play very well with "advanced" characters. I think that may be one of the reasons for the exponential XP gain model: you can bring a "new" character into a D&D game and as you gain XP with the group, you will soon reach their level, or something close to it. (A low-level character will level up much faster in a high-level group, and, in the same way, a high-level character will be quite stagnant in a low-level group until they catch up.)

    There may be a good reason not to have "flat" advancement in a game where you might be mixing "advanced" and "beginning" characters.

    Dungeon Keys

    Key of Teamwork
    You see the value in camaradarie and helping your allies
    1XP Help (Aid?) a party member
    2XP Exposed to Danger, Retribution or Cost as a result of Aiding a party member
    5XP Take mortal harm in the course of helping a party member (?)

    Key of Exploration
    You live to discover new vistas
    1XP Open a dungeon door or be first into a new dungeon location.
    3XP Step through a portal into the unknown without hedging or investigation.

    Key of Home Invasion
    Might makes right of way
    1XP Enter the dungeon lair of an (intelligent?) being uninvited
    3XP Initiate conflict within the lair of another being, at risk to yourself

    Key of Extermination (Victory)
    Leave no enemy behind
    1XP Defeat (slay) the last of your foes
    2XP Draw down unwelcome attention through your pursuit of the enemy past the point of victory
    5XP Slay the very last of a kind

    Key of Looting and Pillaging
    You're here for the clink and heft of gold
    1XP Loot bodies for treasure
    3XP Loot bodies for treasure at personal risk or cost

    Are these on the right track? I don't think I've quite got the feel for writing these (Motivation? Dramatic?), though I think they'd encourage the level of sociopathy traditionally associated with dungeon adventurers. Whether that is the right principle, I know not.

    I'd see other players and the GM each highlighting one, in the manner of the current highlighted stats. They are communally available, so I've not written a buyoff.

    Hmm... on reflection, burning a static list of keys could lead to an interesting retirement mechanic (burnout)...
