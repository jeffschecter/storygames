[Improv and Gaming] "Take a Step"

Jason_Morningstar
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
Last saturday in my improv class we played a game that I really want to repurpose as an RPG mechanic. Here's the setup:

We're working on relationships in class. The exercise is intended to force us to establish very solid, complex relationships with a partner. We start on either side of the stage and begin a scene, but movement is linear and unidirectional - our goal is to meet center stage, and we're only allowed to take a step when we've added detail to our relationship. The instructor says "take a step" when he's satisfied with a statement we've made. So:

***
PARTNER ONE: I didn't even know 'crashing a yacht' was possible, Maurice.

INSTRUCTOR: Take a step.

PARTNER TWO: I've ... always been clumsy. It's my curse.

INSTRUCTOR: Take a step.

PARTNER ONE: And yet, somehow, I still love you.

INSTRUCTOR: Take a step.

PARTNER TWO: I can't seem to avoid screwing up.

(Stony silence from INSTRUCTOR. Not good enough)

For a Nazi you're very gracious, Emily, and very forgiving.

INSTRUCTOR: Take a step!
***

A silly example, but invariably we'd meet at the middle with very rich characters brimming with options after five or six declarative statements by each player, building on what had already been said. I love this idea for establishing relationships among characters in-game, but can't imagine the "instructor approval" model of validation being useful. Let's talk about ways to implement this either mechanically or within some clearly-defined social contract parameters.

    What about requiring the player towards which the connection is directed to respond to the connection? What about requiring an exchange--i.e. no one gets anything until player one reaches out, player two responds, player one responds, and player two responds. That way you get the "we meet in the middle" moment rather than just "ooh, I like that connection."

    As for judging, perhaps a rule like this: "Repetition cannot occur in an exchange--each response must add substantively to the previous." If it merely rephrases a previous response, the 'judges' get to withold rewards for the whole exchange.

    "I'm clumsy, it's my curse" is very, very similar to "I'm always screwing up" but "I feel I always owe you something because you still love me when I screw up" repeats *with* development--i.e. takes the love, takes the screwing up, and establishes a new attitude based on their copresence (I owe you...).

    Could that additive structure be coded to the rules with other players being the judge? It gives them a pretty straightforward rubric to work with and it would allow the nazi move, as well--it takes her forgiveness and reframes it, extracting an implicit sadism (*somehow* I still love you) into an explicit one (for a nazi).

    Rule: If everyone at the table affirms exchange, each player gets rewarded. If only some affirm, allow majority to carry the decision? If the exchange is affirmed, then it gets 'scripted' into the story.

    One thing I like about this mechanic--it has a very pull quality, since the other person can always 'drop' the ball and keep the exchange from becoming part of the story.
