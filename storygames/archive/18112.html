\n Can you help me list more concerns and more solutions?

Status: You have an ongoing story. You\u2019ve done some world building, you are playing characters, and things are going well.

Problem: A new person wants to play in your game.

Concerns:
The new player might not follow the tone the group has established.
The new player might feel uncomfortable about speaking out and acting, never knowing if they are breaking something from earlier.
The new player might play a character the group has dealt with and change them.
The new player has different creative agendas than those present (I think a lot of people aren\u2019t great at accurately explaining their creative agendas, and that these change all the time).

Possible solutions:
What if we blow up character monogamy and make everyone switch?
What if we let the new player create fiction so they establish some buy in and ownership?
What if we do the \u201cnew season fast forward\u201d and we advance the timeline?
What if do the Dawn Summers thing from Buffy season 5, and we just add the new player\u2019s character, act as though they were there the whole time, and then play to find out why no one noticed the change?

Compounding the problem: What if we do these things, but then the new player doesn\u2019t work out or they quit or something?

What\u2019s the cleverest way to add a new player to an ongoing story? Is there a play culture solution, or a game design option here? Are the solutions provided superior to never letting a new player join a game?

    \n It depends a great deal on the nature of the game, I think.

    In many games it can be very fruitful to find an "in" to the fiction that takes advantage of the new player's lack of knowledge. For instance, is there a character who has just been wounded, or is in the hospital, or the subject of some dangerous magics? How about they wake up/recover, but have no memory of what came before? Or maybe even different memories altogether, which don't match up with what's going on.

    Or maybe we find out that someone is actually possessed, and when the demon is chased out, the person who remains is this new character.

    If the characters have just encountered some kind of "outside" force or faction--a different nation, a new species, an underground guild they were unaware of previously, a new Defense of the Dark Arts teacher, an exchange student--then this is a good opportunity to bring in the new player, and give them total say over what this new faction represents, where they're from, and why they're here.

    Another approach I've seen work is to take a relatively minor character we don't know much about and develop them into a PC. A kid at the back of the class, who's seen fairly little screen time up until now. One of the characters' father. I once joined an ongoing campaign and it was explained to me that I would be playing the part of a thief/burglar that the PCs had apprehended while he was trying to break into their home. I had total leeway to decide who the character was, and why he was there, as well as a reason why they might be interested in cooperating, so this worked really well.

    In a D&D dungeon crawl-type situation, playing a recent captive (or local "guide") could be a fun role, I think, if the new player likes the idea.

    I think it's also fine (if the player is into it) to include a new player in a specific role. "Ok, they've been dealing with this Duke, who's encroaching on the local Lord's territory with an army. I put him into the game in order to put pressure on their relationship with the Lord. Would you like to play him?" If the player isn't sure what to do, they can go along with that NPC's current 'purpose' in the story, and feel like they immediately have an "in" to the story and in-game goals. If they develop their own ideas along the way, even better: who's to say the Duke didn't have different plans all along?

    In any case, I think it's important to give some room to the game to breathe, and not to be afraid to change details or reinterpret backstory so that the game makes sense for the people playing, even if it means some gaps in continuity. That's the main thing I'd keep my eye on. You don't want the new player to feel like they're walking on eggshells, no matter what.

    Can you help me list more concerns and more solutions?
    \nMore possible concerns:
    The old player characters are a tightly-knit group, and the old players might use this to unconsciously circle their wagons against the new player ("My character just met this guy, so I don't trust him", etc.).
    The new player might be struggling with the system and having to split his or her attention between trying to keep up with the game mechanics AND fitting the new character into the game.

    More possible solutions:
    What if we all openly agree to just roll with having a new character in the group, without worrying about how or why the new character arrived?
    What if we all play a short arc with pregenerated characters to familiarize/re-familiarize ourselves with the system and the setting on an even footing, before returning to the player characters?
    What if we pause after two or three sessions and talk about the new group dynamic to make sure we're all getting what we want out of the game?

    \n Have the new player join as a new character who provides a side quest to the existing party.

    1. The side quest introduces new NPCs and context, so the new player isn't penalised by not knowing the existing context and NPCs.
    2. It's a *side* quest so if things don't work out everyone can go back to the main storyline without much loss.
    3. Opportunity for the whole team to do something together for a while and bond together over new experiences.
    4. A reason for the team to start interacting with the new character and find out that they can trust them.
    5. The side quest provides a sandbox for testing how the new character changes party
    dynamics.
    6. Perhaps you can have a guess at how the existing team will react to the character by checking how do they generally react to quest givers.
    7. To avoid changing the existing context in unpredictable ways, perhaps instead of tying the new character to existing NPCs, tie the existing characters to new NPCs (from the side quest / new character background)?

    Paul_T>
    It depends a great deal on the nature of the game, I think.
    \nAgreed. I feel like the setting, the roles of the PCs, and the type of game work together to imply some solutions over others. When I introduced new people to a multi-year In Nomine storyline, for instance, I leaned heavily on certain premises of the setting to incorporate newbies: When everybody is working under orders from someone way more powerful than themselves, they have no choice but to show "the new guy" the ropes. This also meant discouraging certain PC concepts, though: You don't want to encourage a new player to take on the role of "the seasoned veteran" in an unfamiliar setting, with unfamiliar rules, knowing they're going to feel like they're constantly falling short of what they envision for the character.
