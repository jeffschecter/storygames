    \n The game has randomized situation creation. There's an Oracle that you roll on to come up with session elements (e.g., a blue-skinned orphan on the run from an arranged marriage with her elderly tutor), 4 per session. You then write down all the characters directly or indirectly implied by the elements, and the players pick which ones they want to play. The GM then plays the rest.

    When you go up against someone with better dice than you, you go on the "owe" list. If you're on it, you can cash it in for an extra, very helpful, die. If you're #1 on the list (i.e., the first owed person who wasn't cashed in), that character reappears in the next session. Otherwise, it does not.

    Characters have two "it would be in my best interests to" statements.

    There's more but that's off the top of my head. Oh, and that I've got an interview banked with Vincent that will be on the January 12 episode of my podcast.

    \n I don't know you very well, Seth, so I'll only speak to what the game, in the form it is played here in Durham, has provided.

    1. The situation set-up is really interesting. You get a bunch of elements from the 4 Oracles, and you tease out the characters within those elements. For example, an element might be 'a mighty king, cold and merciless', and that's obvious, or it might be 'a bandit's treasure', from which you must surmise that there's a bandit. This process of finding, and then choosing, the characters has naturally resulted in relationships between them. Part of character set up is saying what they want. We work to make these desires at cross-purposes, and with the setting vaguely decided up on by the elements present, you have an instant situation. It's sort of magical.

    2. The conflict resolution isn't. It's hard to explain, but you start off with just an attempt to DO something, and it's very active. No trying just doing, like "I stab you in the throat" instead of "I swing my sword at you." There's no "If I win X" from both parties, only the active party gets to say what they're doing. The defending party gets to say what the outcome is after the dice are rolled. It seems simple, but the way the resolution works it builds and builds in intensity. When you finally win, or someone gives, usually a lot of information and consequence has built up out of the actions you've taken during the conflict, so you end up with nice, meaningful endings.

    The combination of these two things makes for a very good game. Other things like how magic works (It's just a keyword, giving great latitude for narration outside of conflicts, which makes magic use flashy and epic-feeling), and the episodic nature of the game (only one character MUST pass between stories, and it might not even be played by the same person) lead to a unique and enjoyable experience at the game table.

    The Durham 3 have done 5 podcasts about In A Wicked Age/AG&G. Game discussion is usually the last 5-10 minutes of the podcast. [1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5]

    \n What Rob and Jason said. I only played it twice, and it was like "scattered rules versions collected from message boards and threads", but it delivered the awesome.

    The game setup systematically and quickly does EXACTLY what the players are meant to do in your Legends of Alyria game. The result, I find, is just about the same, but In a Wicked Age does it faster. In fact, if you ever come up with a future version of LoA, I'd recommend fastening an optional IaWA-style oracle.

    Other than that, y'all know that I don't really believe in this thing called "A Story Game" that is somehow different than "Role Playing Game" (the whole title was a reaction to dickishness against good role-playing games that happened to have more restrictive rules in some places and less in others). It's just different shades of a single spectrum... And yet, In a Wicked Age is something that I would truly say, "This is a Story. Game.". Perhaps while slamming my hand on a table, or something.

    To me, with the possibility of generational play and the like, it really reminds me of one of the modes of play that that game ARIA: Canticle of the Monomyth promised to provide but utterly failed at.

    And it's fun, oh God is it fun. Seriously.

    I like playing Dogs. I've never run Dogs. I think Dogs is really cool and all to play between meatier RPGs. But when In a Wicked Age is finally released (and I'll have to play it again to back up this coming hyperbole) I could imagine just clearing about 1/3 of my games off the shelf, never to play them again.

    -Andy

    \n Here's what I'd do for Conan.

    You know how in some rpgs there's one player who, for the whole duration of the game, is the GM? Like, Sorcerer and Shadowrun and The Mountain Witch are examples; there are many, many more.

    If I were to play Conan In a Wicked Age, what I'd do is, I'd have one player whose job it is to play Conan in every session. Everybody else would do the full-on In a Wicked Age thing, with the oracles and the owe list and some characters being recurring and some not being recurring and everyone can GM some sessions if they want to - but this one player would be set apart to play Conan in every session.

    This sounds so fun to me that I can hardly blink.

    -Vincent

    Hopefully Jason will answer too, but I've done IAWA both GMless and GM'd, so thought I'd chime in here. When we played crazy-Durham style, whoever didn't happen to be in the scene would play Everyone Else, and if all the protagonists are in a scene together, the conflicts in that scene are almost always between the protagonists, so no GM needed. Several of the characters ended up getting played by more than one person, but we each had one that only we ever played. In fact, the person we all ended up feeling like the story was really about would have been an Everyone Else character in a GM'd game, and we each had a turn playing scenes as her, which was fun! Having played both this way and the GM'd way at Jiffycon, I'd say I had a lot of fun in both games, with a slight lean towards GM-less - it helps keep everyone even more involved, all the time.

      Posted By: Ryan StoughtonCan I "act" to convince another player's character of something? I.e. Can I say "I convince him to come with me to the mountain." as an action, or is that ruled out as "don't roll dice when two characters are having a conversation."
      \nI think groups will come up with answers to this individually. However, my gut reaction is that in IAWA you can't force someone to do something by talking unless you have a particular strength that might allow it. (Like the passion spirit's in the example.) If you want to convince another character to go to the mountain, they can just say no. If you still want them to go to the mountain, well now you have to escalate and drag their bitch ass up there, or poison their retainer and convince them that the only antidote is on the mountain, or get a whore to seduce them and then blackmail them into going.... or whatever.

      I think I like it that way because it forces characters into tangible, graphic-novel-esque actions. Talk is great for talking and revealing and discovering character and circling and setting up conflicts, but it's only going to be the things that solves things when the players involved agree that it does. Which will probably mostly happen when best interests aren't directly in conflict, and so move the story more quickly to places where they are and the shit gets ugly.

      Alternately I could see a way of doing it where some speech acts aren't "conversation" -- like blackmail, extortion, or other kinds of really pointed talking. You don't roll dice for things that are just characters talking back and forth, even if heatedly, you only roll dice when you are doing something with your words -- like threatening, seducing, or the like. There can, after all, be a world of difference between "I talk to him and try to get him to come to the mountain" and "I tell him if he does not come to the mountain I will kill his daughter."

      \n Oracle Making for Dummies:

      Think of 5 broad categories for your Oracle things. For my cyberpunk oracle, those were City, Corporation, Technical, Virtual, and Criminal. In the Cheap & Cheesy Fantasy Game that gave birth to IaWA they were like City, Country, Ruin, Military, and Magical I think. Now divide up a sheet of graph paper into boxes like this, with your five things in place of the numbers, leaving at least 6 lines in each box if you can:\n\n\n\n\n\n\nWrite at least one thing in each box, being sure that most of your places, events, and things still somehow suggest connected characters. Try to put at least 4-6 in each of your People boxes, and don't stop till you've got at least 50 entries altogether. Then make a new oracle on Abulafia, or if you're not ready to share yet, write your entries on index cards or old business cards, and you're set!

      \n For making your own oracle, Vincent has his own suggestion here:\n

      TO MAKE YOUR OWN ORACLE ON THE FLY

      1. Have everybody write down two elements on 3x5 cards. If there are people around who're going to go to bed instead of playing, get them to write down two elements too anyway. Put them in a bowl. We had 10.

      2. Draw four as usual, play as usual.

      3. At the end of the session, the player whose character's up next goes through and chooses one, as usual. Her selection is limited but that's okay.

      4. After the session, have everybody write down another element or two. Add them to the bowl. I think that for it to work long-term you'll need to add more than 4 elements per session - but I don't forsee your friends having any trouble with that.

      Maybe when I'm home this afternoon I'll post the elements that we created for this game.

      There you go! My big idea here is to play In a Wicked Age Over the Edge using these oracle-on-the-fly rules.

      My big concern is that the events of the session will unduly narrow the new elements you create. One of the best things about the In a Wicked Age oracles is the curves they throw you, the nonhomogeneity of the elements.

      \n Oracle making:

      Open the comic book/novel/dvd that is your inspirational material. Find a character. Describe that character in one sentence, using this format:

      A former freedom fighter, the captain of a ragged starship, keeping one step ahead of the law.

      Make a bunch of characters that way, then change up the format a little bit for variety:

      A mysterious crate with strange markings, jealously guarded by a secretive aristocrat.

      Be sure to do some locations and events, too.

      The betrayal of a long-standing alliance, sudden and inevitable.

      So there's three to get ya started. :-) Once you start writing them, they'll take on some momentum of their own.

      \n Yeah, IAWA is just about the best implementation of setting exposition in a dirty hippie indie style that I've ever seen. It's like a perfect mix of providing enough information so that everybody is playing in vaguely the same style of fantasy game (or whatever a given oracle is meant for), but gives enough room for player interpretation that every game is really unique. It will be interesting to see things like this blossom across the gaming world in the next few years, especially in systems that don't necessarily follow the rotating-protagonists / short story anthology form of IAWA or ones in which there is no GM role. The randomization of game elements (as opposed to resolution) is something I haven't really messed with since TMNT ("Mutant giraffe! Awesome!") and Toon ("Okay, apparently the ray gun turned you into... a cream pie!"). The cool thing about it is it feeds you setting bits one at a time, not requiring players to familiarize themselves with it all at once. I'll be interested to see, however, if the folks I play it with seem more interested in picking their elements instead of rolling for them.
