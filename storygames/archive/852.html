Reinventing the alternator

rossum
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
In the ancient and dim misty pasts, say about seven or eight years ago, I told a friend of mine about an idea I had to improve automobiles. Imagine if cars had a little generator inside them that would touch the ground, or a tire, or the engine itself and reclaim electrical energy from that kinetic energy. This electrical energy could then be used to keep the battery charged, or to run some of the electrical systems inside the car to save wear and drain on the car battery.

My friend then enlightened me and let me know the powers that be had already done this, and they called it the alternator.

I do this a lot, it seems. Frequently enough that the phrase "reinventing the alternator" has entered my social circle's collective slang- not always used on myself these days.

What this means to you:

Over lunch, I found myself noodling along, trying to work out some new take on mechanics for 1984 Prime. Suddenly, I had a great idea about spending tokens for narrative rights, adn then other players could spend to take control, but then you could counter with more tokens, and by the time I had finished thinking out the details, I discovered that I had essentially reinvented the mechanics as used in the Adventures of Baron Munchausen (or Soap, or Universalis [which I haven't read], or any other games you might have on your mind). Disheartened, I starting planning a forthcoming road trip.

I know that parallel evolution (as it were) happens pretty frequently in board and card game design, but I don't recall anyone on the RPG side of things talking about retreading design ideas. Does this happen to anyone else? How many times have you had a really nifty idea, but then realized that it came from somewhere else? Is it really that important to create unique ways of doing things? Where is the line between inspiration and plagarism and homage and laziness?

    Is it really that important to create unique ways of doing things? Where is the line between inspiration and plagarism and homage and laziness?

    No, it is not important to create unique ways of doing things, not unless you're specifically trying to upturn everything and do something totally new. If you're not trying to do something totally new, why should your rules be doing something totally new? Borrow and steal proven mechanics that you know do what you want and the players will be familiar with.

    The line, for me, between inspiration and plaigarism is whether or not you're adding something new or doing something new, and if you attribute your sources (a quick nod on the Acknowledgements page don't cost you nothin).

    Joshua: Yes!
    Everyone else (who hasn't already written, playtested, revised, replaytested, rerevised, replaytested, and published a game), including me: Tattoo that on your foreheads.

    Shamelessly steal from what you love. In the process of combining the set of things that you, as a unique individual, think are worth stealing, you will create a unique combination of existing elements -- which, because they weren't designed to fit together, will utterly, totally, miserably fail to do what you want them to do, until, probably without realizing it, you do something totally original in order to make the darn thing work, already.
