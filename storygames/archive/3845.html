\n What do the Table Custodians do and how is that different from just coming the the G.o.D. tables to run a game?

Table Custodians will
1.\tHelp you find space at a table.
2.\tCollect Generic Tickets from everyone but the GM.
3.\tRecord the names of the games are being played
a.\twhen the game starts
b.\thow many players
c.\twhen the game should end
4.\tMaintain the Meet-Up Board
a.\tHand out Post-its, and Pens for those who want to arrange a meet-up.
b.\tRemove any Meet-Up Post-its that have passed
c.\tKeep board free of non Meet-up Notes.
5.\tRun an Indie Game.
6.\tBrief the next shift GM's on which games are currently in play and when they should end.

As you can see the Custodians will be doing a bit more than just running games, and some of their duties may interupt the game they want to run. This is why there will be two Custodians, so they can cover eachother. Take turns.

If your not the assigned Table Custodian but an enthusiastic Gm you need only bring a game that you want to run for the Table. Last year we did not lack for players and this year I'd be surprised if there were a lack of willing players for any game.

    \n Probably you could round up the free GMs there, let the players know what you can run, and let them decide from there.

    Ex: I'd probably ask, "OK, is there some game that you (pointing to like The Really Excited Guy who Brought His Three Buddies) really want to see?"
    Yes/No
    If No, then "Well, I'm thinking of throwing down X or Y. Which would you guys prefer?"
    If Yes, then check the GMs to see if anyone can run that. If no, apologize and suggest something complimentary game-wise.

    There would be nothing worse for me, as a player, to show up wanting a game run for me, and then having the GM read the book slowly in front of me because he never ran it before. Remember, people pay for these tickets. Better to run a game you know, love and can have fun with, than to go with an unknown that may fall apart in front of you. Your players would rather be in a fun game of something they never heard of, than a sucky game of the game they did hear of.

    -Andy

    \nSince we are collecting tickets for these games, each ticket covers 2 hours of play per ticket.
    Ask the Gm if he thinks his game will finish in 2 hours or if he thinks he is going to run a 4 hour game.
    Each player will then pay a ticket (or 2 tickets in the case of a 4 hour game, 3 tickets in the case of a 6 hour game)
    I will have little paper clocks that you can set to the time the game should end to put on the table near the GM for that game.
    \n
    5. Run an Indie Game.
    How "on demand" is this? What would I do if people come up and ask me to run a game I'm not familiar with? Try to figure it out? Suggest another game? Offer to light myself on fire as substitute entertainment? (I'm out of practice with the latter.)
    \nThe "On Demand" part doesn't mean anyone can demand any game or even any indie game. So don't worry about that.
    Ryan, since your doing 2 shifts as Table Custodian, all you need to do is prepare an indie game (or a couple of indie games) that you have the most fun running. Make sure its a game you can run in 4 hours or less.
    When players come only offer to run what you feel comfortable playing.

    If they are stoked about a new indie game that they just bought and no one can run it, show them the Meet-up Board and give them Post-its and markers. Have them write "Needs GM" on the post-it along with the game and the time they want to play it. There is a good chance that someone will come by and want to run it for them.

    \n That's a good idea in general, Steve. I think that the (sorta unfortunate) fact is that, as Kat says, one Custodian at most should be running at a time, so that he is free to do so without the trouble of admin work. The other person can then also do the watching out for new players and such. Otherwise you really have nobody to give any attention to the organizing aspect of things.
    \n
    \nThat said, it's going to be a real temptation not to grab a table and run something if you're like me and Andrew. I know that when I was covering last year, that I was not nearly attentive enough, and focused on the games I was running. We really just have to bite the bullet, and sit out for a while, I think.
    \n
    \nI'm not on the schedule this year, but I might offer to spend a slot or two doing some extra admin and recruiting should there be a lot of games going on.
    \n
    \nWhat you'll find is that a lot of the people who show up help out in a lot of capacities. Ryan, it's quite social, not at all what I think you imagine. It's not:
    \n
    \nPlayer We've Never Heard Of: I understand that you're offering to run games on demand? I want to play Rifts! Run it for me now!
    \n
    \nIt's more like:
    \n
    \nJason Morningstar, Remi, and Judd: What's going on?
    \nMike: Thomas is about to run some Heroquest for Shreyas and myself. Dude, you guys should totally play.
    \nJason et al: We're in!
    \n
    \n(Cue game where Judd's medusine basilisk ranch owner ends up marrying her own sex slave played by Remi, amongst other developments).
    \n
    \nThat is, there's just discussion about who is present, who is ready, willing, and able to run what, and then games form up. The "on demand" part really refers to you being able to show up at any time and find people to play with in relatively short order. Like as soon as four or five people show up, games seem to just start spontaneously.
