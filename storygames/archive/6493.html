    \n Josh: I use a text editor as well for routine work, but you need a text editor that accords with the alphabet/syllabary your text uses. Notepad, for instance, only started supporting Unicode in Windows XP as far as I know - before that a word processor was your best bet for writing more complex text. Another similar problem is typography - ascii text editors fail in providing typographical special characters such as en and em dashes, which requires its own workaround as well.
    \n
    \nBut other than that, I agree with the text editor recommendation. Ideally you'll organize your text and images by editing the images in an image editor and editing your text in a text editor, instead of using the same program for both. Then you combine the two in a layout program in whatever manner the current project requires.
    \n
    \nThat said, I do use the word processor for short one-off essays with low quality requirements, such as academic papers. Word, for example, actually has better style control than QuarkXPress, my dedicated layout program. It's nigh useless for anything more complex due to the humongous feature creep and hundreds of default settings that might clash with anything that's not a simple Word document, but if that default document is exactly what you need, then Word works just fine.
    \n
    \nAnother thing I've been doing recently is writing directly in the layout program, which has its benefits and drawbacks. Typographical exactness is a benefit, for one.
    \n
    \n***
    \n
    \nNathan: use Notepad and organize your thoughts into several separate text files, arranging them into several folders if need be. Draw any images you need by hand and scan them, or draw them in Paint, whichever seems technically easier to you; save the image with a descriptive name and put a note in the text in the place where you want the image to appear in publication. Later on have a dedicated editor turn the text and images into technically presentable form when and if you know the media and purpose of publishing it. No need to make the writing process itself more technically complex than it needs to be.

    \n The wiki suggestion is actually a really good one. Aside from hosting your own, there are some excellent free wiki hosting solutions. My personal favorite is wik.is, which I use to host my current campaign wiki. A lot of people I know really like Wetpaint for this sort of thing, though I found it a bit limiting. It would work fine for what you want, though. It also has some pretty clever community-building and collaboration elements, though that's outside the scope of what you're looking for.

    If you don't want your stuff hosted on the web, there are some very cool pieces of "personal wiki" software out there, where the wiki lives on your computer. One I've tried and liked is wikidPad. There are plenty of other tools like it out there, but I thought it was pretty clever, and as I recall, it doesn't require you to use any markdown.

    Lastly, to go back to web-based solutions, have you thought about using Google Docs?
