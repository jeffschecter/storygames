    \n 1. While writing the text, listen to death metal. When not writing the text, read gruesome crime novels and horror fiction.
    2. Spend months (or years) researching the material. Take copious notes. Organize them. Study them. Forget them. Burn the notebooks. Make stuff up.
    3. Make sure that the rules actively support the game's core themes (shooting, stabbing, blowing things up, devouring flesh).
    4. Write the book in my 'voice'. The text should be every bit as sarcastic, profane, and irreverent as I am.
    5. Design a system so easy that someone who has never played an RPG can be taught the basics in under two minutes.

    \n Well, I design more board games than RPGs, but have designed both and miniatures games as well. These general principles apply to all of theses game formats.
    \n
    \n1) Clear rules that can be easily understood
    \n
    \n2) Balanced rules. There shouldn't be any single method of play, combination of options, etc, which overpower the other options given in the game.
    \n
    \n3) Keep an open slate of options. The rules or game format should not drive players towards a singular set of choices. Players should be free to make all the choices possible within the game structure (and in RPGs that generally means attempt any damn thing their characters can try, stupid or not) and the rules should be prepared for a means to handle the unexpected choices. Ideally resolution of the unexpected should be easily contained within the same core mechanics that run everything else.
    \n
    \n4) The rules should help capture the theme, genre or setting the game is placed in. Rules by themselves are merely a mechanical means to resolve tasks. The rules ideally should also help to paint the setting of the game or at least give it some of the flavor. Its understandable if non rules text needs to do the bulk of this work, but absolutely the format of the rules should not detract from it.
    \n
    \n5) Know how I am going to produce the game ahead of time. That way I can plot out all of the material components of the game and its price structures before I delve into the design stage beyond initial concept. This way I end up with a game design that is functional for publication and fits the economic structures needed to hit the price range ideal for the game's best placement on the pricing to sales curve and therefore giving my company the best chance of fiscal success in the marketing of the product.
    \n
    \nIf I had to look at 5 critical issues of design, those would be it. Obviously you can drill down a little deeper on what items 2-4 mean once you identify the exacting game type and genre/setting its is being bundled with, but no way I'm going to try and cover all of that. lol.

    \n 1. Make sure your game is about something other people will want to play. This is really important. No-one wants to play that game that's about raping people with your flower-penis, nor do they want to play that one about being like D&D only cutting out all that silly roleplaying and getting to the good stuff straight away (becoming gods). Very few people even want to play the game where you're all drug-crazed criminals who rampage about, kill and murder and eventually get shot to death by angry policemen, even though they're all well written and have good systems.

    2. The system has to be relevant to the story or game you want to play. Poker is cool, but its theme is gambling and bluffing, so it won't work in a game where there's no gambling or bluffing going on. A system which is based around accumulations of chance or time are appropriate to games where time and chance are important (take Stitch, for example) but just feel tacked on otherwise. Dice-based systems aren't evil, because they at least tend not to clash with the feel of the game, being pretty much generic. Don't be afraid to keep in less fashionable items like a GM if they will help the game run better.

    3. Don't skip on the prose. You have a vision for the game and how it should be played, but no-one else has that vision unless you detail it for them. Never presume other people 'get it'; write it down for them, including GM or Player styles, attitudes and goals, and background/setting materials.

    4. Keep your focus. Decide what you're doing and stick with it. If necessary, walk away from the game for a bit to let your accumulated distractions from the core concept drift away, and get back to what you wanted to write in the first place. This includes things like suddenly deciding you want a GM-less game because you were just reading a forum and it sounded great; losing track of your core setting because you had a great idea for other ways to play the game and you're suddenly trying to write the game to allow for that alternative; and re-vamping your whole game because its just too G/N/S, and you don't want to be like all those other games which are already under discussion of that type. Be true to your game idea.

    5. 4-eyes checking - get someone else to look over your game, before you even get to the playtest stage. This is your game, which means you're too close to it to spot the obvious flaws yourself most of the time. Basic Maths can escape even the most intelligent of designers when they're tired or on a roll, and glaring mistakes or holes are demoralising if they're only found once you've shown the game to a playtest group and they come out in front of a bunch of people. You've got to protect your will to continue and get it done.

    Other important issues: Spell checking! Proof-reading! Advertising! Demoing! Advertising some more! And never, ever, be clever at the expense of producing a good game.

    \n Here are five arbitrary rules I may or may not use when I design (and which may or may not be useful):

    1. The rules are not art; they are a instructions for behaviours and interactions at the gaming table which produce a certain experience. This experience can be art.

    2. Do not try to be (too) clever with the rules. If you want some interactions to happen in the game, try to write rules which get you there the shortest route possible. (More indirect rules can be interesting, but too many of them will fast make the game incomprehensible.)

    3. Decide what the players (including the GM) should be able to do in the game for it to work properly and then make sure that the rules support these actions.

    4. Always remember that it is real people with real desires and emotions who plays the game, and, if you can, take advantage of that.

    5. Players are not mind readers!

    - Anders

    1: Write down some rules you think are fun.

    \n

    2: Play with those rules, noting the ones you made up on the spot, the ones that turned out to work differently than the way you thought they would, and the ones you never played with.

    3: Repeat as necessary.

    \n 1. Write what you know. That is, don't tackle subject matter that you know nothing about (e.g., don't write a game about realistic street crime in America, if your sole source of exposure to American street crime is via Hollywood).

    2. Write what you like. That's pretty self-explanatory. When you write about things that interest you, that enthusiasm is reflected in your work. Likewise, when you write about things you have no interest in, this is also reflected.

    3. Don't assume that the reader lives in your head. That is, don't use vague, nebulous, language to convey the ideas that are floating around in your head -- be as precise as possible. The reader has no idea what you're thinking.

    4. If your game doesn't do something that justifies its existence, don't publish it commercially. For example, if your game offers a neat-o resolution mechanic but otherwise produces the exact same end result as GURPS, don't publish.

    5. Be willing to change what you've written based on feedback from playtesters and proofreaders.

    \n Hey Jamey,

    I had 66 drafts of Grey Ranks, many of which contained only small but important revisions. In the later stages there was also editing going on, and feedback from playtesters who were invariably using earlier drafts. Things got very confusing and some time was lost tracking down changes that were unintentionally rolled back by taking text from the wrong draft. I'll never undertake a major project again without some way to attach useful metadata to each draft, and version control software is the right tool for the job. Everybody is - literally - on the same page, each change is tracked, it's harder to make dumb mistakes.

    \n Amen, Jason.

    A plethora of versions is the bane of late-stage design, playtesting and editing. Creating a version-control plan to manage the process is vital for design methods that generate lots of drafts.

    Also, there is no better way to kill an outside playtesting group's desire to help you than to pump out draft after draft of changes week after week, especially if you don't keep a log of changes.

    Ideally, your game will be substantially designed and revised during your in-house playtesting process. The outside playtesting groups won't be there to test the game (which will be running the way you want it to with your home group), but the game text. They'll be playing to make sure your game text provides all the necessary information and context to allow others to understand how to play the game the way your home group does. In this case, changes during the outside playtesting stage are likely to be minor, and to respond directly to the playtesting group's issues.
