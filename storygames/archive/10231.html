\n I'm going to pick on a guy here for a comment in Luke's "definition of roleplaying" thread. I'm choosing him as an example because I like him and he's tough and knows I mean well, I'm sure.\n
Posted By: JesseI don't see how a great deal of D&D play (which includes the way I play D&D, by the way) fits with the definition. Indeed much D&D play is largely indistinguishable from many types of board/card games. In fact there are card games (such as Dungeoneer) that try to replicate the experience in a more streamlined manner.
\nI've seen several comments like this, and every time, I scratch my head and think, "What the fuck are these people talking about?" I particularly think this in regards to older editions of D&D. D&D 4E, ok, I can buy this, even if I think it's kind of ridiculous. 4E has a reasonably good tactical combat engine that could power a board game inside of it, and if you want to play it that way, go ahead. But classic D&D? Really?

Here's the case. First, it would be shitty as a card or board game. There's barely any rules, no real constraints, and no way to objectively judge what's going on in the game.

Next, how can it not be about ethical choices? (Note to lurkers wanting to say I hate D&D or something: I love D&D more than you do.) The whole game is choices. Do I take the left tunnel or the right? I want treasure badly, but I know there's something down there that might kill me, and might kill my friends. How much do I prepare for that? Can I outsmart it? What should I do with this artifact? Should I stab these slavers in the face?

I'll grant you that the answers are often obvious. Who isn't going to stab these slavers in the face, right? But they are questions involving your survival and the survival of others, questions with real consequences. When you're third level with 9 hit points, there's a big consequence to going down into the dungeon hole.

So, I'm actually interested and don't want to totally make fun of others, so let me know: how are you playing this game that it's not about ethical choices? I can't figure out how that would work.

    \n Ralph's right. Let me get back to Clinton's original post.

    When I was eight years old my mother bought Red Box D&D from Toys R. Us. We had to figure out how the game worked in isolation from the rest of "gamer" culture. Overtime we graduated to AD&D 1st Ed. My mother really, really enjoyed making maps and populating them with all kinds of details. Basically, she was a video game level designer slightly ahead of her time.

    When we showed up to play there always some guy waiting to pay us to go do something or other for him. One of my absolute favorites was a guy who inherited a house from his crazy ex-thief uncle who rigged the place with traps and monsters. The guy only got to keep his fortune if he could actually find all of it hidden in the house in under 48 hours. So he hired us to do it for him.

    We literally had to keep track of every six square we moved because that cost us "6 seconds of time" (the length of a first edition D&D round). We had to account for every copper piece of treasure. Every search check mattered. I remember the final room of the house was completely empty except for a chest. In the chest we actually found a large bulk of the fortune. We were about to leave when my little 12 year old brain had a thought. I said, "I push the chest, aside and search UNDER it." Sure enough I found a loose brick. And UNDER that loose brick I found ONE copper piece. We then booked it to the exit counting every square on the map as we went. We got out with something like 5 *rounds* to spare.

    Given everything we did in that adventure there's no reason you couldn't package the whole thing up and sell it as a board game. "Crazy Old McPhere's WILL" Can you locate your uncle's inheritance before time runs out? Search for traps! Fight Monsters! But watch the clock and count your pennies. Miss a single coin and it's GAME OVER. The exciting new board game from Parker Brothers.

    \n Confusion of levels. A group deciding, together, to play a game in which you kill everything and take their stuff without anyone ever considering the implications of such things if they really happened in a really real world is an ethical decision. Afterwards, while playing the game, killing something and taking its stuff without ever considering the implications of such an act if it happened in a really real world is not. Maybe it points back at the original ethical decision, but at that point it has nothing to do with ethics. And if there never was an original decision, then the in-the-moment killing can have ethical overtones if a) the group is answering the original question as "yes, we want to consider implications", or b) the killing helps answer the original question. But if the question's been answered (such as when playing Descent the Board Game), then each individual killing decision has no ethical overtones. You can ask meta-questions which do, like "how can they stand to make those decisions without considering the implications? OMG!" but that doesn't push the ethics down to the killing decision.

    Just like when you're robbing a bank, the decision to bring your gun up now rather than two seconds later has no ethical overtones. It's the broader decisions, not that one.

We got out with something like 5 *rounds* to spare.

"Crazy Old McPhere's WILL" Can you locate your uncle's inheritance before time runs out? Search for traps! Fight Monsters! But watch the clock and count your pennies.     Posted By: ClintonThere's no point to collecting XP if played like a board game, as there's no winner.
    \nI dispute this. There's a huge point to collecting XP. XP leads to characters increasing in power. Which leads to being able to have more tactical options during play. And it also purposefully leads to facing more difficult tactical opposition, and thus increases the variety in play. Gathering XP is like slowly taking the training wheels off your bike, or slowly earning back your handicapping stones in Go. Without gathering of XP, the group would be playing 1st level characters for a very long time: there is opportunity for variety in play there, as well, and honing of tactical skills, but not much in the way of leading to facing more difficult opposition because at some point the opposition becomes powerful enough that your group of 1st level characters have little hope of survival.

    PC survival and gathering of XP after a session can represent a "win", but not all games have a zero-sum win as their ending (or pausing) condition.
