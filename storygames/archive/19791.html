\n As much as I like the 5e pregens, I think they confuse the way the new D&D works, partly because the way the character sheets are designed.
Want to know your stealth bonus? Look up the tiny little +number somewhere where it says \u201cstealth\u201d. Same goes for spellcasting attack bonus, sword tohit, etc etc and also damage added to weapons.

But a lot of these numbers are just one of the basic six traits, plus sometimes your proficiency bonus.
The game has a clean design where you roll dex to sneak, and if you\u2019re trained, you add your proficiency bonus as well.
(I\u2019m a long-time advocate for baking numbers together instead of re-adding them all the time, but that goes for when you understand what\u2019s going on, I now feel.)

Here is a \u201cLevel Zero Hero\u201d: generate (or pregenerate) the six stats. You don\u2019t have a proficiency bonus and you don\u2019t have any class features. If you\u2019re a demi, you get that stuff as usual (like the hobbit\u2019s \u201cLucky\u201d trait). Weapons do their normal damage, and thrown, finesse etc are still in the game.
Your to-hit bonus is now str for melee weapons and dex for ranged weapons. Everything is one of the six stats.

This idea is for people who are super new to D&D. I mean, I was thinking of people like for example my parents, if I ever was to play with them.

Whenever you want, after a couple of encounters or whenever you want (you don\u2019t need any particular amount of XP, though you collect XP as normal when you\u2019re a level zero hero) you can upgrade to a class level. Your proficiency bonus is now two. Complete the character and start playing D&D rules-as-written.

You\u2019ll easier understand that your \u201cskills\u201d, \u201ctool proficiencies\u201d, \u201cweapon proficiencies\u201d, \u201cspellcasting attack roll\u201d all work the same way.

The fact that the whole skill-list is printed (in ant-sized font) on the default, non-alternate character sheet is a double edged sword. It makes the inner workings of the game harder to really see, the game looks more opaque and more like a \u201cblack box\u201d. I kind of wish they hadn\u2019t done this.

    \n That's a very good reason for not even having a skill list. My own D&D gloss doesn't use a skill list despite having very similar skill proficiency rules, specifically because I don't even pretend to approach a comprehensive, objective, always-optimal skills breakdown; the most useful approach to skills that I've ever seen is to have them be opt-in, so that individual characters may possess some specific proficiencies without implying anything about the rest of the campaign setting. This way, if I ever need to have a character with a skill in say Skiing, I can, and that doesn't cause weird knock-on effects on the skill lists of other unconnected characters.

    I guess that an explicit and detailed skill list is popular in WotC D&D because it allows them to organize very specific write-ups about how to resolve various situations, and use the skill list as headers for that list. So instead of listing "This is how you resolve climbing challenges", they can write "Climbing: how to use this skill". This is, I guess, sort of more clear than saying that "you climb with Dexterity checks, and apply suitable proficiencies such as Climbing, Mountaineering, Cat-thievery, where applicable". After all, how will you know when and which proficiencies apply to situations if the book doesn't list what each proficiency is used for explicitly, duh

    I guess that I'm arguing for a skill listless character sheet with spontaneous skill definition, if I ever end up running 5th edition. Not like the game fundamentally cares about the skill list as this whole, entire collection - all the rules reference individual skills (or "all skills" when it comes to that Bard class feature), and it's not exactly beyond my powers to be flexible about which skills apply to which situations, if non-standard skills players invent should cause confusion.

That lowers the chance of supremely powerful characters via attributes. Might have to put in some bonus points for when you hit 1st finally, depending on what kind of stats your people are used to seeing.

    In our version for instance what you roll depends on if you're going to start in the Novice block (basically 0 level classless for 6 levels) or the Apprentice block (equivalent to 1st level in a class in previous editions). Novices roll 3d6, but earn a bonus attribute point (with restrictions) each level until Apprentice, whereas starting at Apprentice let's you roll 4d6D1. The latter creates characters with an average of 4.5 more attribute points, but people who don't like 0 level play tend to prefer higher attributes anyway, so it works well.

    Well, it depends on what your goal is. If the campaign is going to transition into standard 5E progression with 4d6 drop the lowest, I don't see anything wrong with the 0th level characters starting the same.

    As it is, I always prefer point buy. (Actually, I prefer pick one of these three stat lines.) You know that guy that manages to roll at least one 18 per character? I'm the guy who rolls a 5 on 4d6 drop the lowest to help balance out the universe.
    \nWell let's say your story is a bunch of 12-16yr old kids just starting out with no classes yet. 4d6D1 (or the standard array, or average point buy) is going to usually create some pretty high stats...higher than might be rational for that age/experience bracket. What's more, the point of such games is often to explore (as a part of the game) the sudden, rapid growth that takes place just prior to, and at the early stages of, a career/path. Starting with full stats doesn't offer that simulation, whereas starting with reduced stats (via rolling or point buy) with quick bonuses gained along the way could. That allows you to play the full, normal scope of the game, but add the low power/youth/average element to the early parts.

    One other point is that our group is firmly pre-3rd with regards to what attributes mean. At our tables, 20 is absolute natural human max in an attribute (barring magic), and 10.5 is the worldwide average. We also prefer the more representative distribution curve (as presented in pre-3rd roughly), as opposed to the flatter one obtained using 3rd+ attribute bonuses. That means that in our games exceptional (16+) attributes are TRULY exceptional (just like someone with an Int equivalent `160IQ would be in our world). 18+ are PHENOMENALLY rare, even at higher levels (which is what someone who benches ~511lbs due to Str should be). That lets us abide the lower stats generated by a 3d6 or reduced point system.
