    \n We're an extreme slice of the gaming pie, having already bought into the culture of following the design process, news, and works of designers online.

    We do laugh at this sort of thing sometimes. In one of the "Stuff to Watch" threads there was this cute video of bears arguing over RPGs:
    http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Rb_R1q8PEnM

    But when it comes down to it, all that marketing speak that we see everywhere in our niches about "the long tail", "the new economy", and greater openness, visibility, and creating a connection between authors and the audience...

    ...that sword cuts both ways.

    If the author pulls all the right levers and turns all the right knobs and I follow their blog or whatever, chances are that I'll have a connection with that author (even if it's ephemeral: Hey, it's how humans work emotionally. Just ask ELIZA). If that author seems like a nice person (regardless of political views or whatever; in my case the "highest virtue" that I look for is Empathy with others), then I'll be more inclined to check out their works. I'll look at them with rose-colored glasses, finding less flaws in their works (which may be riddled with them, see the 2002 version of Dread: Pandemonium for example).

    If they're an asshole constantly, then I'll avoid their works. I might browse them (at the store, or on PDF or whatever), but there's so many games out there that I can easily "shut off" interest in an otherwise okay-looking game in order to play dozens of others. If I am presented with their work, I'll likely find the flaws all too easily.

    If they were an asshole in the past, but show some redeeming qualities, I'll give them more consideration. People change, and I like that.

    If they were a great person in the past, but turned into a bitter asshole, then I'll start to watch my wallet and find the flaws faster. Again, people change.

    If I opened the book and found something awesome in it, chances are I'll start to reciprocally find things to respect in that author. Rarely happens, but it does happen sometimes. And if I end up actually liking the work or using it, I buy it, no question. I won't steal, even from people I don't like. I have bought works in this regard.

    As much as I'd like to say "the work stands divorced from the creator of the work", in communities where I'm active it's not really the case. Again, it's that "using the internet/media to create a relationship between the creator and the audience" factor, and how it can be a double-edged sword if you're act like a piece of shit.

      \n Looking at this from the creator's point of view, it does make life in the Internet a bit insecure for anybody worried about their sales that there are so many people who very consciously connect the creator's personal behaviour with their work and apparently keep a running count of who's worthy of purchase. Seems like one should play it safe and not be real to avoid causing offense, you know? This seems even more sad when you consider the utterly frivolous nature of Internet offense: people get a burr up their nose from what would barely register as a rigorous coffee table debate in any civilized company I've frequented in the real world. It's a hyper-correct culture we have here. Add the fact that most often this act of not buying comes up as a self-made boycott declaration from somebody who might or might not have had any buying intent anyway, and the whole idea starts to look like a petty rhetorical stunt that only has meaning as an ultimate condemnation of somebody; it seems like this personal boycott is not for yourself, but for the purpose of making the poor guy really feel sorry for pissing in your morning cereal.

      My own stand on this sort of thing is that humility entails respect, and respect entails engagement. In civilized society I can sit down with somebody I disagree with, and I can enjoy the message he delivers when it is done skillfully. There is nothing wrong in enjoying Dave Sim's Cerebus, for example, even if you disagree with some of its many themes. This is doubly true even if you disagree with Dave Sim's personal views expressed outside the work: are those views really so awful that you feel a need to take away basic human courtesy from the man by treating him like a pariah? My rough experience is that it's more socially acceptable to display this sort of extreme close-mindedness (moral indignation one might term it) in the USA than it is here, which might account for why I see too much of this in the Internet. For my own part somebody like Sim is not even close to losing my basic respect for him as a thinking individual, and while I disagree with his worldview, I can still respect his work as worthy opposition and enjoy it as a window to the world and experiences of somebody who thinks differently from my own convictions; this is how civilization works.

      (Dave Sim is a good example here because I consider him one of the greatest comics-creators ever even while having a basically opposite worldview to his. The narrative of Sim's descent from liberal humanism to Abrahamic, paternalistic fundamentalism is as chilling as it is engaging. My attitude towards the man and his works is not dissimilar to the way I view C.S. Lewis - as a believer in free society and culture engendered by free exchange of ideas I can appreciate the clarity and passion these men put into their art while still disagreeing vehemently with their beliefs and conclusions.)

      This discussion concerns many different impulses, of course. One that I find sort of understandable is the idea that if you really dislike somebody, that dislike might rub off and affect your view of their work, too. That's just psychology, not much that can be done about it if it happens. I find that I can usually forgive people when I look at their art, though, so this is not a big deal for me. Even the most annoying game designers are at their best in work created with honesty and passion.

      Another strand aside from simple distaste here is the idea of economical support for jerks. I don't buy into this argument - I'm not paying Sim for his views, I'm paying him for the delivery. In fact, what I'm paying him for is the wonderful service he does to an open cultural environment by giving a strong argument for the things he argues for in his art. Whether I agree or disagree is besides the point. Speaking as a customer of the culture industry, I'm not in it to pay people to agree with me. That's just a childish attitude, and one that leads to cliquish, partisan thinking instead of true communication. And I specifically don't want anybody to feel like they have to agree with me or I'll go off in a huff and refuse to consider their work ever again. Keep engaging the culture in meaningful and powerful ways and I'll keep listening, no matter what your opinion. With this attitude I foster a low-tension conversation culture where people can talk with me without having to worry about offending me accidentally in some extreme I-never-want-to-see-you-again manner.

      (In respect to Brett's cross-post and because people get burrs up their nose in the Internet, let me state that a careful reading of the above should make it clear that I am not trying to make any guesses about anybody's intelligence or virtue, even should they disagree with me. I probably come closest in the part where I say that only buying art that agrees with you is childish; I think that this is a fair opinion to hold in the abstract, without trying to particularly pin this childishness on anybody.)

      \n Honesty is very important (for me).

      No matter what you do, no matter how nice you are, millions of people are not going to like you (I'm using you as a placeholder for you or your product). The more vanilla you are, the more likely you will be ignored.

      You have to stand out to make an impact, which means for a lot of people to like you, there will also be a lot of people that don't like you. If that's the case, why be dishonest? It's a lot of extra work for probably nothing. At worst, if you really think being honest about a specific topic is going to be that much of a problem, then just don't say anything about that topic. But lying is just going to get you into trouble in the long run, it's a lot of work, will probably drain you emotionally, and it sucks.

      Ultimately you aren't guaranteed success. I would rather be honest and fail, than be something I'm not with a tiny chance of success. I'm not advocating being stupid in your communications. You can be sensitive and polite while still being honest. But I don't see the benefit in lying.
