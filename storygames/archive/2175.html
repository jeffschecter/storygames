    Our DM presented us with one during our high-school AD&D2 campaign. Predictably, one of the players just HAD to press his luck and keep drawing until one of the bad things happened to him. We just used a deck comprised of standard poker cards. I was always excited by the prospect of building one out of Tarot cards, since at that age such a thing was still slightly taboo.

    It's Open Gaming Content now, so it occurred to me that it would be even cooler if you could actually have the cards as described, with original art and such.

    In case anyone's unfamiliar with the cards, here's the list.

    There's another item in D&D like this, the Deck of Illusions, but it's not quite as cool.

    In a similar vein, WotC bundled a set of Olidammara's Dice with pre-orders of the Epic Level Handbook. As far as I can tell, these were just normal pipped d6's with a custom symbol printed on the "One" face.

    I've maintained that AD&D1 is really just a collection of minigames. (Combat game. Thieving game. Lordship game. Pseudo-medieval simulator.) These items are some of the more imaginative examples. Games within the game, so to speak.

    Yeah, but the Wand of Wonder is just a table roll. There's no prop you can make to generate the random effect instead of making a table roll. I think that's what I like best about the aforementioned items: you, the player, actually handle a prop similar to the magic item in the game, and that prop has a functional randomizing effect. They were designed to be real-world items from the ground up.

    If this is just going to devolve into geeking out about D&D trivia, I'll mention there was an item called "Wand of A Wonder" in Temple of Elemental Evil, which had a 1% chance of firing a death ray, with the rest of the table being bad or annoyingly useless effects. Presumably, the fun was getting the players' hopes up for having found a Wand of Wonder, then savoring the schadenfreude of ensuing use.
