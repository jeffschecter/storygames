\n In my discussion, "Wars we've fought," I talked about using historical wars as a backdrop for roleplay. I briefly mentioned how my early '80s gaming group chose the Vietnam War as our war of choice because it was still so close to our experience; my father and uncle both fought there.

But what if you want to get at the heart of an historical war by ahistorical means? Getting back to the Vietnam example, what do you think of depicting that sort of low-intensity guerrilla warfare in an SF or fantasy setting? I'm thinking about the Misty Waters section of the PS2 game, Killzone, in which the heroes head into a mangrove swamp and basically do a three-man sweep-and-clear operation against the Helghast. It's a Vietnam scenario in all but name.

Do you think it cheapens the original conflict to approach it in this way? The more I think about Vietnam gaming I suspect more and more that it would have to be dealt with allegorically rather than straight, mostly because simply saying the phrase "Vietnam game" is going to turn off a significant portion of the gaming population. There's just something about that war that turns people off, or maybe it's idea of playing any historical conflict where real people died. You saw some of that blowback when Afghanistan d20 was released.

So is an allegorical method more conducive to successful gaming, divorced as it is from issues of propriety? Or are we better off simply having our wars as they were?

    \n There's an argument that I really like that but whose source I can't recall to the effect that the 70s dungeon-crawling D&D was a way of gaming Vietnam from the perspective of an 18-year-old G.I.: a trap-filled, ambush-laden free-fire zone from which one occasionally was allowed to withdraw for R&R "in town."

    But to speak to your larger question: allegorical play lets you frame the historical event the way you want, rather than bringing in the complexities and ambiguities of real-life events. To the extent that the parallel is obvious, it can be regarded as kind of tendentious: you can be read as arguing that "this is how it was in the real world" without having the courage to actually come out and say it, because if you're called on it you'll just go, "Oh, but this is fiction."

    I think if your intention is to re-create a historical war--or a historical situation of any sort, really--you should have the courage to play it straight. J.R.R. Tolkien is on record as having a "cordial dislike" for "allegory in all its manifestations." Fantasy or sf shouldn't be a thinly veiled real-world situation; the fictional situation should emerge from the postulates and what-ifs you've established about the fictional world you're trying to create.

    \n Well, there's also the practical problem of History Phobia that seems common among RPGers.

    With passive entertainment ( books, films) someone has done all of the work of deciding what and which aspects of history to use and show to the audience. With RPGs, the players will all be interacting with that stuff, which implies a certain basic level of familiarity with at least the broad strokes of the period.

    And, when I say basic, I mean really, really basic. A lot of folks don't have even that.

    \nYes, it can be, definitely.

    I told you about Forbidden City, right? It's a game about the Opium Wars. The British sent gunboats and soldiers into China, because they wouldn't buy our opium. There were various atrocities: particularly nasty was the sacking of the Summer Palace, in which we destroyed a beautiful palace, and the treasures it contained, to assert our superiority. (I'm simplifying these events slightly, but that's not a bad summary).

    Now, you couldn't just write a game about being British soldiers in China, committing atrocities. It'd be horrible. So, instead, Forbidden City takes place in a allegorical dreamworld, called the Smoke-Filled Land. There are references to Chinese myth. And you go through this beautiful land with a gun, which lets you destroy things.

    It's not a perfect game, by a long shot, but it does do that allegorical thing you're talking about. I'm proud of it, in a sort of oblique way.

    \n I think the war through allegory is going to depend on what your objective is. On one hand no one's uncle died fighting swamp elves so they're going to enter into that game without the emotional baggage they would bring to a game about fighting the Viet Cong. Considering this is done for entertainment, this might allow the players to explore different roles than they would fill comfortable with otherwise. If I play a grunt in the king's army, and I throw an exploding potion into a swamp elf hut, I as a player, am going to be more comfortable than if I'm playing a private using a flamethrower to light up some poor smuck's home.
    \nHowever, if your objective is to examine the effects off this particular conflict than you really need to be willing to face it head on. A game about vietnam and the consequences should be set in vietnam not on the planet Mantiev.
    \nIn conclusion if you want to explore a specific element of history, be it D-Day, the Tet offensive, or the Chosin Reservoir play that, but if your goal is to explore something like asymmetric warfare vs. a superior opponent or uppity natives vs. imperialistic oppressors I believe these could just as easily be explored in some sort of allegory without the potential drawbacks of using real history.
    \nOne last point is that there is a world of difference between a game set in the Iraq War and during the 100 years war. Realistically while people might have connections and patriotic opinions of the latter they are going to so much less than the former it might as well be a fantasy setting.

    \n I think a lot depends on what you mean by "getting to the heart of" the war.

    I wouldn't bother using allegory as a way of getting past issues of "propriety" (like Jason, I'm reluctant to take the horror out of war - I like much more the reaction of the narrator in _The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao_, who gets beat up and swears never to write another fight scene). That said...

    1. It can be a way of getting past knee-jerk reactions. If you say, "let's do Vietnam," people have pre-conceived notions. When I ran my thinly-veiled Rwandan genocide in my Eberron game it was in part a way of breaking past the fact that everyone thinks they already know what should have been done, and getting them to think about how to react. Somewhat to my surprise, it worked so well that the PCs mostly ignored cries for help and one even consciously participated in part of the slaughter.

    2. It can be a way of focusing on what's interesting to you without loading people down with detail, especially people who aren't experts on the game. My Liberia peacekeepers Unsung scenario is set in Equatorial Kundu. This lets me run the scenario without risking it turning into a class where I teach people about Liberia, beyond the elements I want to focus on. In other words, it can make it easier to "get to the heart," if you've decided what the "heart" is.

    3. It can let you combine elements of different conflicts in interesting ways, or (the converse of 1, in some ways) bring a war "home" in a way it might not be. My favorite example of this is Brian Wood's comic book, DMZ - it's the Iraq war (basically), but it asks the reader, "what if this were happening in Manhattan?"

    4. Similar to 2, it can give you the ability to flesh out a totally fictional war in a way that has some versimilitude. I use what I know about African conflicts a lot when running games that involve wars just because filing the numbers off the CNDP gives me a rebel group when I need one that's more complex and interesting than a random one I might make up.

    One problem with the propriety thing - and, though I've used it, with use 1 - is that it can risk rubbing people's noses in things, hoodwinking them a bit. If someone doesn't want to play Vietnam because they feel strongly about it, and you throw them at swamp elves, it may be a bad scene.

    \n I want to make a distinction between "allegory" and what I'll call "homology" (i.e., structural similarity) in the relationships between historical and fictional worlds. In allegory, the logic of the situation is that of a fable, in which characters are purely figurative--they represent something else. So Animal Farm isn't a speculative fiction exploration of what if barnyard animals tried to govern themselves, and no one takes it as such. They recognize it as allegorical, in which the pigs stand for something else--in this case, the revolutionary elite who upon achieving power arrogate unto themselves its perquisites: "Some animals," we learn, "are more equal than others," and in that hypocritical epigram is the moral of the story.

    Homology, on the other hand, emerges in situations where an author imagines how the operation of social, political, economic, and other forces affects conditions in the fictional world; to the extent that those forces parallel those in operation in particular historical circumstances, resemblances emerge. This can be done self-consciously for exactly this homological effect. So Asimov creates a Galactic Empire that in some ways resembles the Roman Empire because of its centralization of authority and so forth, but it's not an exact parallel, and it's not the case that we are meant to read the Foundation as the Roman Catholic Church or the Royal Society or any such thing.

    So allegory has its place, but I'd argue that it's bad news for fantasy & science fiction, along the lines of Tolkien's argument, which as I understand it distinguishes between allegory as the author's attempt to impose meaning upon a narrative and interpretation as the reader's right to make sense of it on his or her own. So the negativity with which some of us respond to a hypothetical proposal to base a campaign around hurling fireballs at elusive swamp elves in which it is made clear--perhaps by conventions of naming or details of culture--that they are stand-ins for the Viet Cong may be a product of that attempt at imposing a specific meaning on the situation, rather than allowing an interpretation to emerge organically from the specific details of the situation.
