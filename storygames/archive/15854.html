\n Was talking about this over on G+ and thought I'd share.

I'm in a Monsterhearts game now - there's 4 of us, and we only play if all 4 can make it. (A lot of indie rpg's, including MH and AW, want a quorum of 4, I think, because you want a triangle of PC relationships and a GM.) Assuming any given one of us can make it 3 out of 4 sessions - which seems pretty good to me, with our busy schedules and who knows when someone's going to get sick or have to work late or go off to a conference or what - that gives us a .75^4 chance of playing on a given night, which is only 31%, which kind of sucks. Particularly if we let ourselves start feeling bad about it - "Hey, why aren't people showing up, do they not like me or my game?" - "Oh, I feel so guilty for having to flake *again*" etc.

So let's say I invite someone else to play and they join on board - now there's 5 - and I just gave myself a refresher course in binomial distributions so I could teach a class, so I can use Excel -
you invite 5 (1 GM+4 players) but you'll still play if only 4 show, using Excel's BINOM.DIST(1,5,0.25,TRUE) that's still only a 63% chance to play.

I don't want to invite 6, because I think games start to suck with 6. But let's look at the math: 6 with a quorum of 4 - BINOM.DIST(2,6,0.25,TRUE), 83% chance of playing, but a (0.75^6)=18% chance of everybody showing up and not having a very good time because of the too-low-participation problem.

Still, I'm kind of liking those odds, I think I'd be happy with 1 out of 5 nights being kind of crappy so that I could play on 4 out of 5.

    \n Not that I want to piss on your thread. Hope it didn't come off that way. Just wanting to mention why my people-math isn't quite that clean.

    And the results I see don't bear out the probabilities. Not even close. I was running a game with 8 people, including GM. I was way more likely to see 8 show up than 7. If 1 person couldn't make it, chances are that 2-3 others would say "well, if we don't meet this time that's actually good for me cause I have some ____ to catch up on anyway" and the session would get dropped.

    Part of what made it work well was scheduling it every 2 weeks instead of every week. That way, game time was much less impact on everyone's schedule. Plus, the sessions were irregular enough to increase the longing to meet up again. So everyone was more invested in sticking to the schedule if possible. Nobody wanted to be the reason that the group didn't meet, unless they really couldn't make it for a compelling reason.

    \n So here's one I did in 2002-2005, when I ran a long-standing D&D3 game.

    I said "This campaign has all the continuity of one of those video games where you put a coin in and press '2P Start'. You can give an explanation for where your guy has been and why they are here now, or not. I really don't give a damn." It worked marvelously. The narrative, "team of adventurers seeks fame and fortune and hot dudes/chicks" was not interrupted by people being missing because the narrative wasn't about any single one of them. A lot of time we just assumed they were off being wined and dined by some googly eyed nobleman/woman who wanted to get them into bed, and that had priority over punching elbow-deep into a gelatinous cube. Honeys before mummies.

    The game where there are four players but we will play with just two is Nobilis. In Nobilis it is really easy to finesse people in and out of situations.

    The game I ran last year was an experiment in several ways. It was ment to be a travelogue, basically, of disparate people all headed to the same distant city on the other side of the mega-continent. Somewhat Vancian mode. They would meet time and again on the road. It worked otherwise fine, except I found it to be really exhausting to run. Normally my energy is up after a game, but something about the setup just wrung me dry. (Since I'm naming games, it was running on Eero's Solar System.) Anyways, the idea was that they would be in a new place every time, with stuff developing between the player characters, and to lesser degree with a few NPCs: the continuing narrative would basically have been the drama the characters cooked up between them across the variable backdrop. I still think the concept is solid, but I'm not sure how to make it work.

    The game I'm starting now is an old-schooly dungeon crawl using ACKS, with the westmarchian meta-rule that either everyone ends up back in civilization at the end of a session, or everyone can schedule a continuation within 7 days, or we roll on a table (inspired by the Jeff Rients' one) to see who makes it back alive and in what shape. Everyone knows that the purpose of the meta-rule is to allow a different bunch to play the next time.

    The game that runs more slowly is Trail of Cthulhu, going on maybe 20th (15th? 25th?) session of the Armitage Files. It is not amenable to these strategies so easily, unfortunately. Still, the players are keen, so sometimes we manage two games a month. ...but sometimes there are long nasty breaks.

    \n Anecdote time:
    \n
    \nI once ran a V:tM game, Sabbat side. We had 3 fairly hard-core players, plus myself. We also had a couple that were less consistent. This was probably the most successful game I've ever run in terms of consecutive sessions played and length of campaign. Due to the fact that it was also my *first* moderately successful campaign, I had a big problem saying no to people who were interested in the game, and as some players drifted out, others came in. At one point, I had- I shit you not- 12 player characters.
    \n
    \nOne night, almost all of them showed up.
    \n
    \nMan, that was a crazy night. It was also, looking back through the haze of time and memory, a pretty good session. I don't necessarily think that I was at the top of my GMing game back then, but I was definitely at the height of my rules-mastery, I'd learned to deal with having my plots shredded by crazy PCs, and I'd learned to improvise like a mother. Long-standing wall-flowers comfortably backed the plays of the main players, occasional wing-nut players threw their monkeywrenches, and new players got thrown right into the thick of the mix, with the full support of the rest of the players. Powerful NPCs were thwarted, millions of dollars of property damage were caused, and I walked out of there with a story to tell for years.
    \n
    \nWhy did it work? I've never really thought that much about it, but I'll try now. First, the above-mentioned fighting trim I was in was a vital component. Secondly, though it's part of the first, is a complete unwillingness to back down from play, no matter what happened. Third was definitely that my hardcores (and I) were, well, hardcore. We were all young and mostly single, with no kids and no major demands on our evenings. The peripherals drifted in and out, but so long as the core was there 9 weeks out of 10, it worked.
    \n
    \nFourth, and possibly the most important, was a decision I made very early on in the campaign, and almost never (once that I recall) deviated from: Each session was one night of in-game time. Each session began at dusk, and ended at dawn. Each period of play was bookended, and the players often went their separate ways, to their individual havens, at the end of each night. The biggest benefit of this was that there was never any reason to handwave someone out of or into a scene based on shifting attendance from week to week. This allowed me to make it work when more than half of my player base was gone for 2-3 weeks in a row, leaving me with 2 players who were ALL fired up to play.
    \n
    \nBasically, I guess it boils down to: an ability to adapt to changes, a commitment to play, and a low barrier to entry and exit.
