    John, as the guy responsible for starting that sidestep, I think it's totally a red herring.

    First of all, the distinction between Freelancers and Publishers is really fuzzy. Sure, in any given instance of writing/designing/publishing, you're either one or the other, but when we're talking about people, it gets weird. Is Ken Hite "just a freelancer"? Was John Wick a freelancer or a publisher before he struck out into indie territory? Loads and loads of folks who we'd recognize as "the big guys" now started off as freelancers. Some folks -- like Brand who forgot to mention it -- are both freelancers for others and self-publishers at the same time.

    Then the distinction between Mainstream and Indie when talking about people is also fuzzy. Again I point at Brand. Look at any of the "mainstream publishers" who started off as just them and maybe a couple friends -- definitely indie -- and are now recognized as part of the "industry." Or look at it the other way around: Shock: is Joshua's book but it's got some Ben Lehman fiction in it. Matt Snyder has done layout for how many "indie" titles, most recently Mutual Decision? Were Ben and Matt freelancers? Were Joshua and Ron "mainstream"? With the notable exceptions of Wizards and White Wolf, most "mainstream" game companies do not have a central office, do not have more than a handful of fulltimers (and the fulltimers are usually the founders/owners), and look a whole lot like indie publishers.

    Lastly, we're not really talking about a lot of people. "Freelancers for Mainstream Games" is, assuming we mean folks who feed themselves and their families off of their earnings, is... probably less than 100 people total? I'll be generous and say it's 250. And that number includes Ken Hite and Robin D Laws. It includes people who are also self-publishing as well. With such a small population that overlaps with other populations so much, it's hard to extract any clean data or make any meaningful conclusion about the body as a whole.

    I'd really rather consider everybody involved in publishing RPGs to be in the same boat. The products that we make are different, sure, and can be categorized and scrutinized, but the people... I find it easier to deal with them as people, one at a time.

    This whole thread seems strange to me. We're too small a community to have "groups." We all know each other, so let's just consider each other individuals.

    Consider this scenario. Ten years from now, some upstart starts giving his games away for free, and saying that anyone who isn't doing so is "sold out" and that their games can't be good. He says, "those guys at The Forge, they're just corrupt capitalists, and only free RPGS are ever going to be any good!" What's more, he has a good game of his own, it's bitten off a chunk of players who are really into it, and he's convinced a number of talented designers that it's the right way to go. A "freebie games" movement starts, based on this one guy, and those who best understand his ideas, surrounded by lot of other people who at least buy into them.

    Now, here I am, still making "indie" games at that point, and I look at this community and say, "know what? Maybe they have some good ideas. I'll check it out." But I get there, and I don't understand their jargon, and don't really want to take the time to learn it (after all, I already have my jargon that's always worked for me), and some of the things they're saying seem very strange. After all, isn't selling a game a good way to distribute it? I'm accused of just not getting it.

    I might find this new movement annoying. Depends. But I'd like to think that I could remain rational about it. I mean, I'd be pretty petty to rant against it simply because I felt that my "edgy" status was being drained away, wouldn't I? Isn't there always going to be some new movement?

    OK, of course I'm talking about Ron Edwards and Bruce Baugh here. Or other parallels I could come up with. Note that it's very easy to say that Bruce isn't buying into it, because we're not bowing down to him as some sort of authority...but that wouldn't match my perception of his interaction with The Forge. We could say that The Forgies went out of their way to alienate Bruce...but that's not what I saw. Oh, sure, there were some folks who probably interacted with him based on the fact that they'd had awful experiences with game systems from WW with whom he was associated when they were produced. But for his part, Bruce always seemed to take it in stride (especially considering his personality). He had read Ron's essays that, yes, use WOD games as the whipping boy because they are emblematic of some things that he sees as a problem, but he still came and discussed things despite.

    Was Bruce at The Forge being disingenuous? Trying to look like he was giving the ideas there a fair shake, and all the time intending to storm off? Not the impression that I got. And I don't recall him storming off, in any case. Oh, yeah, he and Malcolm are prone to tirades, but, then, I'm no saint either (and neither is John Wick, for instance). That's just personality.

    Oh, and BTW, check out Bruce's Gamma World for the community rules. Amongst other cool things he's come up with. He's certainly not some sort of dinosaur who hasn't learned anything from newer games. Adventure! even has metagame player empowerment.

    Have I missed him going off on The Forge or something lately on RPG.net, that his name is even being brought up here? If he has, do I care?

    I think that it's interesting that we're looking for an enemy where there simply isn't one. Oh, there are folks that disagree with some things about the "indie" movement. But that, at least in theory, has always been desired, no? Or has Ron only using rhetoric with his exhortations that all rational debate should be heard out? Does Ron sometimes come across as heavy-handed in dealing with what might be an opinion in his view instead of debate? Sure, but with reason. Do some people see him as a tyrant for it? Yeah, but they've never talked to him in person. In any case, again, personality.

    You know, if Indie Games or White Wolf were Islamo-fascist terroritsts, I might see the need to discuss who is on what side. But they're just guys producing TT RPGs by slightly different methods. And I've yet to find an actual publisher that I don't like (the FATAL guys being the exception that proves the rule). So I don't know what y'all are on about.

    What was the topic of this thread again? Perhaps it was John's motive all along to point out the lack of any real divide between the "indie crowd" and those other guys who, before Ron, used to be the indie crowd? If so, all I can say is that, here's one, at least, who doesn't buy the hype or need any more drama about such insubstantial issues.

    I think there might be some small minorities who feel that they have to defend the "indie" side of things, and another for the "WoD" side of things or "Traditional" side, because they think there's some kinda open warfare going on. I think those people envision Ron Edwards and Bruce Baugh meeting at GenCon, and throwing down in Aisle 1200. I think those people need to get real lives. Fortunately they're actually rare.

    Mike

    To wit:
    1. Trad/Mainstream Game Writer (TMGW) comes into Forge.
    2. TMGW tries to learn the jargon and start conversations.
    3. TMGW asks questions previously discussed or ruled dead (which was unknown because, hey, new to the community).
    4. Forge community suggests TMGW do his homework of previously discussed/dead threads (in varying levels of politeness).
    5. TMGW attempts to do so.
    6. TMGW restates question or isolates specific unit for discussion according to forum culture.
    7. Either misuse of jargon or tripping over another revious/dead thread leads to repeat of #3.
    8. Repeat of #4 (lesser levels of politeness, in general; didn't we tell him to RTFM???).
    9. Repeat of #5 (decreasing levels of interest in doing homework).
    10. TMGW restates question again.
    11. Either:
    11A. Question dismissed out of hand as violating some precept of the community or misunderstanding some element of established community theory.
    11B. Community asks for further refinement of question. (TMGW complies. Repeat #11B.)
    11C. Discussion ensues.
    12. Community member mentions something in response that TMGW responds to involving his trad/mainstream experiences.
    13. Either:
    13A. Response disregarded because indie is seemingly thought of being completely the opposite of trad/mainstream.
    13B. Voluable lack of comprehension of why trad/mainstream experience worked that way.
    13C. Lack of interest in response.
    13D. Utter, vocal disregard of years of game design/writing by TMGW.

    Ultimately, what I -- I'm not trying to speak for Bruce here, just personally -- often feel when I post something at the Forge is that while I've made the effort to learn the lingo, the effort is often unreturned to learn my background or lingo.

    Also, while I don't expect anyone to "bow down" to my experiences as an authority, I -- and Bruce, too -- do have some experience in game writing and publishing -- in some cases, significantly more experience than community respondents. To have that ignored, disregarded, or actively belittled is frustrating, to say the least. A modicum of "well, that's interesting" goes pretty far.

    In my case, the sense that I was putting in a whole lot more work to understand the community than the community spent in being open and interested in my experiences, plus the disregard or brushing aside of what I have learned in game design (and tried to express to community members), plus overly abstruse jargon, plus a difference in design work-process (I don't design in the open), plus what I see as a lopsided focus on design without equal balance for production, publishing, and marketing, limits my participation at the Forge to a monthly lurk or occasional post (usually in the Publishing forum).

    But that's just me and my impressions. I'm not saying the Forge is eeee-vil or wrong or anything; just that the board seems to have a very insular culture, unsuited to this designer who designs in isolation.

      I think that is also has to do with the phenomena that adding a new (vociferous) member to a group changes that group. People resist, because they don't like change. I see it all the time.

      At work when someone new with ideas joins the team. The old hands get sniffy because he starts pointing out the same old problems. The old hands feel he is suggesting they did not know what they were doing and won't ask questions to find out why they made those choices, or they don't want to explain again. The new guy feels the old hands are shutting him out. In gaming it happens when someone new joins the group. When Mike started posting over on the HeroQuest yahoo groups a few people reacted negatively to him. Here's this new guy we don't know telling us he has a different way of doing some of these things. I seem to recall he and Mark Galeotti had a few run ins. Now Mike's 'one of the gang' and has a credit in Mythic Russia.

      It's the same lessons we learned as kids. Joining the gang can be hard. Rejection sucks. Outsiders don't get it.

      Most of the time folks work it out, sometimes they don't. Off-line its easier, you all go for a beer after work and the old timers clue the new guy in. And a lot of folks have clued into that here with talk about how the Forge works best after F2F meetings. Off-line it still harder to do. Continents and time-zones divide us. Written communication has no tone or inflection. Cultural differences are unknown.

      But this is nothing new.
