\n Hey all, this is something that I was thinking about after talking to Eric and Lisa Provost a couple weeks back. The conversation went something like this:

Eric: I want to run a genuine 3-session The Mountain Witch game again. Not a 1-session one. You need 3 sessions, I think, for it to be the best game it can be.
Eric and Lisa begin to talk about a 2-year game of MAGE that Eric ran and Lisa and others played in: The game lasted two years (1-2 sessions a week). They had some really gripping moments. Lots of attachment to characters, both their own and other NPCs. However, they also realized that there was a lot of BS in those 2 years that didn't need to be there.

My question, that Eric was thinking about: "Is it possible to (after ejecting "BS", whatever that BS means) run say a mini-campaign for a few months (say 6-8+ sessions) where you can get that same level of attachment as the 1-2 year games?"

Now, when I say attachment, I'm thinking of games with a heavy social component. Basically, World of Darkness stuff and games played out like that (AMBER, etc). Not just all "4 guys who adventure together and get minimal face-time".

Eric's answer was: "Yes. But I'll have to think about why". I'm hoping he'll jump in later with some ideas.

What are your ideas? Tricks (system independent) to get more attachment to the game without playing for 2 years?

-Andy

    \n Here's my small idea, which kind of worked for a 6-7 session PTA game that I ran (and if I knew then what I know now, I would have aimed for 10 sessions):

    Make a relationship map. Have about 6 major NPCs, including protagonists (4-5) and antagonists (1-2 : And these are "rivals", not "must kill them at every opportunity"). Through the early game, tie each player to 1-2 of them. If more NPCs need to be created, do so sparingly.

    Finally, let the Players sometimes take the roles of the NPCs if their PC is not in the scene.

    This helped create a situation where the NPCs felt important and really took on a history of their own. The players became attached to them. Then, kinda like a feedback loop, their attachment to their own players grew because of what they did with/to the NPCs. I feel like this condensed a lot of time into more juicy, poignant roleplaying moments.

    Perhaps the "let players roleplay the parts of the NPCs sometimes" was key, even when the GM had specific plans for those NPCs. Sort of that "my character" feel, but it extends to NPCs?

    \n I guess one question to start off with is: how often do you play? My group plays every other week, so a 6-8 month campaign is much different from, say, a two year campaign with a group that meets weekly.

    With the above game, that's around, what, at least 100 sessions? My group's typical campaign length ranges from four sessions for on GM to eight sessions for me. I'm not sure what I can do to make my game feel like it has the investment of a game that's at least twelve times longer.

    * A GURPS Black Ops game where I asked the players to write up After-Action Reviews. (Which I later wrote about for Pyramid)

    \n Very good topic. This is something that I've been thinking about a lot and is somewhat topical to our gaming group. We don't really play short games per se and when we do aim for more concise stuff our shortest tends to be around half a year, once every week. We like it and it works well, so there's no real problem, but I wouldn't mind trying some really short term games.

    I think framing straight to the point is key and making the characters intimate with the premise. Very often these long games start by meandering about for long times, looking to create connection to characters and to hook in the characters to the premise. I think both of these should just be assumed. When we started framing tighter, I found - perhaps not surprisingly - the campaigns got shorter from the norm, with nothing much lost in the character department.

    \n My attempt at this is laid out in my latest Pentasystem design journal entry. To save you clicking:\n

    1. Pull out the coolmap and decide what themes on it engage you (you-the-group). Add in others, by all means.
    2. Find which entities connect to those themes, and divide up ownership of them among the players.
    3. Create main characters and give them motivating attributes that link to the themes and entities.
    4. Create supporting characters that are connected to the main characters and that put a face on the entities. Probably do this with an R-map. Make sure you build in some conflict (\xe2\u20ac\x9dfamily is important to me, I hate heretics, my brother-in-law is a heretic\xe2\u20ac\x9d).
    5. Add motivating attributes to the main characters which connect them to the supporting characters.
    6. Create some situations, as gateways, which will act as \xe2\u20ac\u0153inciting incidents\xe2\u20ac\x9d to the main characters you have - features of the world-as-it-is that they can\xe2\u20ac\u2122t ignore but will need to try to change, just because of who they are. Make sure, as well, that the things that can bring about change are things the characters are uniquely equipped to do.
    7. Engaging, conflict-filled gameplay ensues. Or such would be my assumption.


    \nI've actually started to wonder, given JBR's situation creation thread, whether 3 and 4 should be reversed.

    Also, from the Initiate me into Dogs initiations thread, I've added a step between 5 and 6: an "Exposition" scene for each character, where you play through a conflict and end up with a goal or dilemma for the character to take forward into play.

    Posted By: Paul T.So, just how can you "make sure this kind of exchange is happening at least every five minutes"? What techniques, mechanics, etc, have you found useful? Andwhomakes sure it is happening?
    One rules-build that works pretty well is this: If you use one of your own dice for your own purposes (succeeding at a narration, usually) then it goes into a discard pile. From that discard pile, it can never go back into your own hand ... you can only hand it out to someone else by expressing an opinion about their character. When that person then uses your die, it doesn't go to their discard pile, it comes back to you. PLUS, you get some input into their narration if they succeed.

    The little circle of that economy (Yours -> Discard -> Opinion -> Yours) tends to get people expressing opinions pretty quickly, of necessity. Otherwise they just plain run out of dice.

    There are other ways to do it, motivated through different channels (opportunity vs. necessity, for instance) but this is a nice, simple one that gets the point across quickly.
