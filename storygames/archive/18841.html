\n In the early days, when man first designed roleplaying games, the characters went down into dungeons and hacked monster and took their loot. From this, you got XP and gp, so you could go down in other dungeons and hack bigger monsters for bigger loot.

This kind of reward system has somehow rubbed itself off on everything. It seems to me like a standard solution is to throw a reward on something to make it more appealing. Now why should you do that? Why should you give a +2 bonus for a skillful attempt to roleplay a seduction? Why should you give 1 XP for following your key? Why should you get a die when someone thinks you did something good?

The thing is, getting XP for using the game mechanics by rolling dice is sound. It makes sense that: if you do something, you can then do some more.

Getting XP for following a key isn't. I didn't use any game mechanics - I just created a situation. Yet I still getting XP. That's a bribe.

I'm getting FATE points for using negative aspects. That's a bribe.

If the game wants me to do something, it should make me do it because I want to. Otherwise, I should play another game. A game shouldn't have to bribe me. A game should tell me what to do. Negative aspects should be as fun to activate as positive ones without getting negative response from the system. Keys should be followed up in new scenes with similar themes.

    \nI feel like adding an additional wrinkle to this:

    Sometimes players make characters who are exceptionally good at a particular thing not because they want that thing, but because they think that thing will happen no matter what, and they want to get it over with quickly and cleanly.

    Imagine a Western game in a system where a master gunslinger can end a fight before it even begins: if you think that gunfights will be inevitable AND you don't want to spend a lot of actual, real-life time playing out gunfights, you might choose to play a master gunslinger in order to minimize your play's focus on shooting people. In that case, it's less of a flag saying "I want everything to be about guns," and more a flag that says, "If we have to have guns, I want to make it an automatic victory for me so that we can move on to more interesting things right away." Likewise, someone who really wanted to spend a lot of time in gunfights might keep their shootin' skills in a low-to-medium range, so that the fights will last much longer and give them more to do.

    I guess what I'm saying is, it pays to ask a lot of questions about why a character is good at something, and to not just assume that +10 Gunplay means a player wants a steady diet of high-noon showdowns and a monomaniacal emphasis on guns. We've all misread the situation before: a high skill can sometimes mean "I don't care about this," and a low skill can sometimes mean "I want to have to deal with this all the time," and vice versa. If you're not 100% sure which is which for that person and that character and that game, then it's not a very useful flag, is it?

    \n Since this thread is about rewards, I thought I'd share this video about motivational theory: (about 10 minutes, and well presented). It's about research designed to motivate workers, so they talk about money as the reward, but it's also really fascinating how counterintuitive the outcomes were:
    - for tasks that don't require cognition, eg manual labour, increasing the reward increases the productivity
    - for tasks that do require cognition, even a little bit, increasing the reward decreases the productivity.
    And that's across the board, from well heeled to low heeled, and in different cultures. So it's possible that focusing a game too much on reward systems might actually decrease player abilities to nut through a logic/strategy puzzle like winning a combat. (But so long as everyone's having fun, there's no problem with that.)

    What they do recommend is that for high level thinking activities the strategy of offering more and more money isn't as helpful as offering enough so that the person doesn't have to worry about it and giving them less tangible incentives, like a pleasant workspace, a good team dynamic, and opportunities to be creative and autonomous. I know for me that the latter strategy is true - my main goal for straight money rewards is to have enough so I can get what I need and some of what I want, and any extra goes in my rainy day account so I won't have to worry if something random happens in the future.       \n I agree with JDCorley in this, I hacked fate points for another game and lately we've been playing PF with hero points. In the hack, players started to make meta-jokes about putting their characters in trouble and claiming a fate point for it. It kinda defeated the purpose of the mechanic and some roleplaying moments which would otherwise be cool, became a joke that broke the inmersion a bit. Yet, in PF, all players care was about making good scenes or drop one-liners here and there to create amazing roleplaying moments; and then the same currency was used to create more of those moments by twisting the rules a bit and allowing us to do cool stuff beyond the system limitations, because it made the scene really cool.

      In conclussion: It all depends on how you see it. Think of it as a bribe, it will feel as a bribe and you'll start to treat it like that and spread that sensation to the other players. If you treat it like applause, give it proper respect and link it to awesome moments of the game, it will start to shine and acquire a value of it's own.

      Another viewpoint about the same issue: some people (like me) feel a bit uncomfortable with praise, either giving or receiving it. This mechanic actually makes it more comfortable to give praise, in the same way a roll on a social trait feels more comfortable than roleplaying the entire interaction, for a player with poor social skills. If your group is totally comfortable with praise, it's easier to see that you'll probably see it as something totally unnecessary that will create more problems than solutions.
