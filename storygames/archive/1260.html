So this weekend I participated in a weekend LARP. It is a group I have larped with more or less annually. It's usually a good time especially since it's in the PA mountains and I could bring my dogs to the wilderness with me.

The game always has a homebrew changeling aspect to it. This year it was about pirates in the Caribbean, invited by a local governor whose offering amnesty at a high price.

Here's the point:
There was a lot of emphasis put on the GM's characters (where I thought this could tie into many of the games we talk about here). Now in a LARP there is an enormous amount of player power since there is no real narrative involved in the process. The narrative is replaced by direct conversations and direction (go over there and talk to so-and-so).

Brief plot situation: One character needed a few items scattered amongst the crews to open a gate to Arcadia (which has been closed). Many PCs didn't even know about the gate or it's possibility (I guessed it before we played but that was based on games I have played with these folk before).

The main conflict was between three GMs-run-characters (unbeknownst to anyone in the game). One was manipulating another and the third was a foil to the other two. They obviously worked out the ending in what one NPC would react to another. And in the end the main bad guy triumphed over all. As it turned out almost no one knew the effects of what was going on and I felt a sense of "aren't we clever, I manipulated you all in my ploy for power."

I thought this was a bit dis-satisfying but I remained silent unless asked for criticism. So I ask you, does this sound like a viable means to unviel plot? What would you have changed? Sure, I know this is all pretty vague but I didn't see going into details as crucial for the crux of the discussion. If you really want to know details let me know and I can elucidate.

Personally my thought was that the GM's characters should have been PCs and not NPCs. I think the players needed to be more involved. I think this game would have had a far greater impact if moe information was given out and if secrets were doled out to the players to be discovered by the characters. However, I am uncertain how this would be achieved in a LARP situation where the reveal seems to drive players a little more than tabletop.

It is akin to a game of Luke's Inheritance (Luke's games tend to be very LARPish) Fred and I played and Fred was bummed that the coolest things going on were in his head

- Don

    Well, in my experience running several weekend LARPs (after having played in dozens before-hand) I will tell you two things:

    First: It is absolutely imperative that the players be given the keys to the kingdom, plot-wise. If the NPCs have a terrible scheme that they wish to hatch then that terrible scheme must absolutely require the assistance of as many PCs as they can possibly manage, and must hinge completely upon the actions and choices of at least a few. People can smell where they aren't necessary with freakish accuracy, and will wander away from a dramatic scene in progress when they figure out that there's not going to be any place for them.
    I was NPCing at a diplomatic treaty game. Two sides were deciding the fate of a little scrap of land. I was supposed to be a monkey-wrench: a native of the little scrap, with legitimate claims but no power at all to offer the PCs anything that would make them take me seriously. So I asked the GM "Hey ... how convincing do you want me to be? Is there going to be trouble if every single PC sides with me?" She looked at me, rolled her eyes and said "Just be as convincing as you can Tony, and let me worry about the story." I still think that the coalition we formed (yes, every single PC eventually sided with me, leaving the powerful, story-centric NPCs out in the cold) would have made for a great story ... but instead the GM had me spontaneously develop a severe case of "dead by fiat" and told everyone to quit jacking around and get back to power politics.
    Second: The PCs are on crack. They will do absolutely, monumentally, unbelievably STUPID things with no logic and no remorse. As a group they cannot be trusted to put together even the simplest of ideas into a coherent plan or story.
    My friend Anthony made this awesome spider costume that had him (alone) wielding eight scything, swinging, rebounding boffer weapons. He was an avatar of death. The PCs got wind of him before the game, and did not want to take him on, even at 40-to-1 odds. We were gleeful. We placed the McGuffin in the spider's cave and said "Hey, the spider sleeps at night ... and on sundown at saturday the world will be destroyed if you don't have the McGuffin. If you guys get your act together and get out there sometime friday night then you'll never have to face him ... otherwise you're gonna have to fight." They didn't get out there friday. By mid-day saturday they knew the score, and were kicking themselves. But none of them moved toward the cave. I sent someone out to ask what their plan was. The plan, they said, was to wait until nightfall, when the spider would be asleep. I sent my courier back out. He discovered that, yes, they knew that the world was ending at sundown. They felt strongly that their best shot was to wait until full dark, get the item, then run back to camp really fast in hopes of getting there before the sun went down.
    So there you've got the dilemma: In order to make a game work properly the GMs need to put the power into the hands of the PCs. But, I kid you not, the PCs make some really mind-bogglingly bone-headed decisions. It is impossible to fool-proof a story, no matter how simple, from such ingenious fools. So, honestly, I think there's always going to be a balancing act. You put enough power into the hands of the PCs, but keep some active, driving NPCs in the mix to provide a structure around which PC action can gel.

    Now it sounds as if your GMs went way the hell over on one end of that spectrum, and ran a pretty questionable game as a result. But I gotta tell you, I've got a world of sympathy for them.

    I have a theory about LARPs. It's something I have been saying for years and the idea constantly faces resistance.

    In my opinion a LARP is simply chatting in character. To take that further it's the responsibility of all involved to find engaing topics to chat about (or instigate topics) but ultimately there is nothing more to it. Now some LARP types are different, particularly when you get into boffer LARPs with mad props and NPC mooks you can accomplish a little more. However I don't think any LARP should just be one scene after another of action. This is not a cinematic medium like tabletop can be. GM narraration should be kept to a minimium. Players should suss out their own drama and try to attain their goals socially - and consequences for acting in an asocial manner should be as harsh as they are in the real world.

    All the information the characters need should be in their backgrounds, doled out throughout the game through NPCs or added background or found in well placed props. That information should lead them to their social goals.

    With some very difficult and dynamic exceptions a LARP should be driven by social goals. How do I get info from this person? How do I convince them of an action? How do I rally their support? How do I get others to rally against them? Once they have the social goals then there should be some sort of measurement for their success in game (a vote off the island, appointment of a new post, a lynching, a tbale full of gathered components). And I think most importantly I think people should be talking about it. We should be talking about what we should wear, what we should be bringing, how we should or shouldn't act and commenting on how others act. In my opinion that is the heartbeat of a LARP.

    Too often I see games where the GMs stand amidst a group of LARPers and narrate what they see and what situation they are in then ask the players to elucidate on what their PCs are doing. Whispered meeting happen as players corner GMs with their laundry lists of secret actions and how they are interacting with the outside world to get their goals met. Players wander off in hiding until the need for what they have stolen has passed yet wander around "out of game" chatting with their compatriots. It is often forgotten that what makes a LARP is not the cool things you accomplished but the conversations you had in your persuit.

    If players and GMs keep this in mind I think a significant amount of frustration can be avoided.
