    \n Mark, this reminds me of doing layout for Mortal Coil Revised. One of the biggest challenges there is that it's not just Rules + Example, but Rule + Example + Diagram, because Brennan's made these sweet diagrams that show how to move tokens around. What I ended up with was something like:

    RULES
    X | D

    The rules text was at the top of the page and then, below it, I put the Example text on the left, then a vertical rule, and finally the example Diagram on the right. This didn't always work perfectly, since I also had to get the rest of the text to flow between pages, but that was my intent on every page.

    \n Man, that was tough to get JUST right. :}

    One thing to consider about all of these basic layouts: some are FAR easier to update and extend than others, and each requires its own techniques for "proper" document structure:
    * Continuous alternating (NOT on facing pages), one or multiple topics per page - This is nearly trivial to manage and extend, as its just done with paragraph styles.
    * One topic per spread, example on facing page - Almost as trivial as the above, as one can force page breaks with paragraph styles, so it's like the above but with a LOT more whitespace. HOWEVER, centering the content could be a tricky proposition, in some layout programs (some don't let you specify vertical and horizontal orientation of a paragraph style, only space before and after and leading and indenting).
    * The last four - These are nightmares of layout, and require a page-layout editor rather than a word processor or book-layout editor (those the latter usually have tools to kludge together a page-layout approach, it's not their strong suit). You have to deal with text boxes, floating (or page-fixed) anchors, and do it for EVERY chunk of content--no resorting to a master page, here (hmmm... except MAYBE in FrameMaker....I'd have to play around a bit). In essence, it's the same layout challenge as a magazine or a densely-packed catalog (like Campmor catalogs, not Lego or REI). And God save you from your layout artist if you "just need to add a few paragraphs to section 12" because it will fuck up the entire, manually-aligned page flow every page after the one you 'tweak.' Which, by the way, makes the "one topic per page/spread" approach advantageous... UNLESS you do something like make the Examples align to the gutter or in the gutter-side column and the Body Text in the outside column: THEN, you'd at the least want to do two text flow (Body and Examples) and have each of their paragraphs/headings do a page break. AS LONG AS you have the EXACT same number of Body and Example headings/breaking paragraph styles, then you can insert one and let the Text Flow A and B sort itself out, reflowing all the subsequent pages. Not too tricky, really.

    But those callout examples are fucking bat-shit. AWESOME, but batshit. You'd BETTER be damned sure you are done with content additions or expansions before you start to layout THAT bitch kitty. And as a quick aside... think about how cool it would be to ALTERNATE FACING of those two callout styles (Examples in callout v Body in callout)! So you could have the Block of Rules on the left pages, with basic Examples of each in the callouts... then on the right pages, you'd have an *extended* example, with the Rules in callouts, showing the math or the applicable rule or what-not. GREAT way to engage two different modes of thinking, to help folks memorize a game's rules!

    Damn... I wish GLASS could be laid out like that. But I have other demands. ASCII @HACK, however, could definitely be done that way! it's small enough (and approaching static in design) to be manageable with a nearly total manual page modification process.

    \n Textbook layout is some of the most complex layout in the world. And most of the time, it's overdone. It's driven by Sales needing to be able to point out the books' features to school administrators quickly and easily. Textbooks are still primarily sold face-to-face in very short meetings. After those meetings, there are adoption processes where a handful of administrators (occasionally teachers and parents) sit in a room surrounded by very very structurally complex textbook samples and having to pick one or two for their district or state. So yeah, the more things that grab their eyes as they turn pages, the more they feel the textbook offers, which is not necessarily the case.

    Textbook publishing is just a nightmare top to bottom, designed to fail in every metric but profit.

    (And David, they're all moving or trying to move into XML archiving. It's such a glorious headache...

    Which is to say: it's easy to design layouts for grabbing attention and looking impressive, but it's a whole nother world to design a layout (and a product) that facilitates use. And then it's a whole nother galaxy to design a layout that grabs attention on the shelf and is also easy to use...
