\n I have a worry about GenCon 2008, which I wanted to share, in a self-reflective Knife Fight stylee.

Firstly, I'd like to come to GenCon 2008. I'm thinking how I might do that.

Also, I have a game. It's called A Reason To Murder, it's being playtested, and it's early days, but it's looking good.

So my worry is: next GenCon, should I be thinking about The Ashcan Collective or the Forge Booth? (It's a rhetorical worry, not a question to be answered, you understand.)

(Of course, neither of those booths may exist next year. But let's assume, for the moment, that they will, and that they'd both have me.)

If I'm honest, I'd be most at home at The Ashcan Collective. I'm greatly in favour of games that are playtested to death: so, if I'm acting with integrity, A Reason To Murder should be tested to death. I'd playtest it through next year, then present it as an ashcan at GenCon.

Having said that...

The Forge Booth is where it all happens. I'd learn a hell of a lot from joining The Forge Booth. Again, I'd playtest it through next year and, indeed, many designers playtest their games in that period of time. I'd polish it up, get artwork, and present it as a finished product.

I'm not particularly looking for an answer, here, or advice. But I think it's fascinating. And I imagine it's something that other people wonder about too.

Note that pressure to present it as a finished product. And, it must be said, much of that pressure comes because the Forge booth is there. I'd love to be a part of it. But, to do that, I need a finished product. And aaaargh.

Graham

    \n You don't have to decide today, Graham. Shooting for a Gen Con release in any form is not wise, trust me. Ashcan Front games are supposed to be almost finished, so there is an expectation there just as solid in my mind as the Forge booth. The bar is pretty high, based on the quality of this year's ashcans. Spend the next six months refining your game and playtesting as hard as you can, then re-assess - that's my advice. By then you'll have a much better idea whether 90% or 100% is a more appropriate goal.

    Also, I'm not convinced that the Forge booth is "where it all happens" any more. It's happening all over the place.

    \n Wow, sorry to threadjack by proxy, Graham.

    As Andy said, I have been working on Do for about six months now. I've run a couple playtests and am currently revising v. 1.2 of the playtest document. I plan to run many more, refining and re-writing after each test.

    I set this year's GenCon as a promotional deadline to spread the word about the game and drive traffic to the site. Fred Hicks had a cool guerrilla marketing scheme that I implemented, handing out promotional materials to all the cool, talkative folks in the indie scene and to a connection I had at the GM HQ.     Like you, I'm setting next year's GenCon as a launchdate. Three to six more months of rigorous alpha playtesting, followed by three months of public beta playtesting, followed by three months of production time, with promotional support throughout the year seems is a loose and manageable schedule for me.

    At the end of it all, I hope to have my game suitable for sale at a Forge/IPR/Indie-Collective booth. If the aforementioned schedule gets derailed, such as by an impassable mechanical dilemma that isn't resolved by beta playtesting, I'll shift my focus to the Ashcan Front.

    I hope that gives you some idea of one way to go about it. I have no idea if it'll be successful. :P

    \nWell, yes and no. They certainly could revoke your badge for selling "offsite" if they found out about it. It has happened in the past (or at least been threatened). Also, to sell outside the exhibit hall, you'd need a street-vendor's license from Indianapolis, as well as whatever state permits are required. Selling in the hotel is almost certainly expressly fordbidden, unless you get explicit permission, and could get you in trouble--maybe even booted from your room. And still requires an IN state permit. And something as big as Gen Con, or the convention center itself, might've bought out any rights to sales immediately in front of the convention center, making that illegal outright.

    Different management, but we nearly got in trouble for selling 2 copies (and we'd only brought 2 with us to the convention, so it's not like they merely caught us quickly) of the ashcan of Four Colors al Fresco at Origins, a few years back. And, at least on paper, they have virtually identical rules about such things.

    And, yes, it *is* intended to stop small-scale commerce. That's why you can't generally sell M:tG cards on-site (other than in designated areas/to designated people). Also, 20-40 copies of a new game isn't "small scale"--that's respectible sales for a new indie game at a booth at GenCon. All of which isn't to say you'd get caught, just that no one should have the illusion that even if they're caught, nothing could possibly come of it.

    But, i see that it has been clarified that what i initially read Andy as saying is correct: no actual sales occurred, just hooking people so they'd go buy it elsewhere (and, in this case, once it was available).
