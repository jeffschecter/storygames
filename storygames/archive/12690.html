\n We started a new game on Sunday night at the Embassy Suites, based on a half-remembered and half-communicated scene the previous night at the convention center. This eventually came to be known as SAGEFIGHT because Paul Riddle would keep time by saying "1, 2, SAGE!" This is how to SageFight:

Rule 1: You have to tell everyone about SageFight. More people make funner.
Rule 2: Everyone gets around in a circle, at arm's length from each other.
Rule 3: Take your most awesome kung fu stance. Do not move.
Rule 4: A time keeper will give a signal at regular intervals, either by calling it out or clapping. Think of it like a metronome.
Rule 5: On the signal, you switch to another stance, but must keep your balance and must not move after switching your stance. When you switch your stance, you can move your arms, torso, head and/or one foot. The other foot remains rooted in place.

Goal: Try to tap the back of another person's hand with the palm-side of your hand. If your hand is hit, you are out. If you are the last person standing, you win SAGEFIGHT!

What happens is something between improvisational dance and kung fu larp. It is fun to do, fun to watch and easy to teach to new players. We'll post videos as they pop up on youtube.

    This is a two-player fencing version called Masters Without Swords.

    Rules:

    * Fencers take stances along a straight line. We used a line of tiles on the lobby floor.
    * Begin by touching arms in a mannerly fashion, like crossing swords.
    * The timekeeper will tell the fencers to break. Then the fencers take their most awesome fencing pose.
    * The timekeeper will start keeping a rhythmic beat, just like in regular SAGEFIGHT.
    * Score one point by touching your opponent's torso with the palm-side of your hand.
    * If you step off the line or lose your balance, you're out and your opponent scores one point.

    Goal: Be the first to score best of 3 points.

    There is a lot of jostling and crossing "swords." You feel very much like a jedi knight.

      \nOkay, people. If we're really going to make this a thing, we gotta set down some basic forms and stances and give them some awesome names. Imagine these as the chess pieces in a Sagefighter's repertoire. The improvised flow between stances is what allows the growth from amateur to master.

      Flamenco Defense
      Sensei Sage's favorite defensive posture. One hand close to the body, one hand high above his head. This allows for an easy transition into an upward stroke with his lower hand or a downward stroke with his upper hand.

      \n I have developed a technique that may prove useful in Sagefight.

      Claws of the Mantis
      While feeling out your opponent's posture and strategy, keep your hands turned in close to your body. This way, even if your arm is extended, you won't spend precious microseconds pulling your hand away. At worst, your opponent may only come in contact with your forearm.

      Mantis Trap
      This is s difficult, but potent gambit. Leave your hand extended, broad and vulnerable. When your opponent reaches for it, remain still. Simply drop your hand at the wrist, let your opponent's hand pass by and a microsecond later you can tap your opponent's hand while it is still moving.

      \n I think the degree of disclaimer is probably dependent on the audience of the book. For example, my mom used to teach preschool. She had a number of books on outdoor activities for children - these had only the barest of disclaimers/health warnings. On the other side, boffer LARPs have always had very explicit rules on physical contact, binding and holding, head-blows.

      I think one way to minimize the need for disclaimers is to incorporate mechanics that penalize you for unintentional touchs and body checks from the wrist up (in much the same way that boffer LARPs often apply damage done in head strikes back to the person who swung the weapon). This way, the game will maintain a focus on avoiding unwanted touches while maintaining the appropriate range to only make hand-on-hand touches.

      \n Two opponents face off. You have two wooden spoons each. One or both of them have a lemon on it. You can only knock your opponent's lemons off with your spoons. If you loose one lemon, you must put that hand behind your back. If you lose all lemons, you are out.

      They also played Ninja! Everyone stands in a circle, shouts Ninja! And strikes a pose. Everyone must stand still. You start with one player. He/she attempts to hit the back of the hand of another player who is allowed to defend themselves (dodge or block). If the attack sucedes, the defender is out. Anyone involved in the attack who is still in stays frozen in the position they were in at the end of the attack. The next player going round the circle is the new attacker. Continue until one player remains. A variant is that the last two players face off, bow, strike poses (Ninja!) and proceed from there. There is a photograph of Ninja in Actual Play.
