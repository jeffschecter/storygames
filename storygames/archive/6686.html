Posted By: Brand_RobinsPosted By: jenskotPosted By: Jonathan WaltonChris Pramas and Nicole Lindroos (of Green Ronin) talk to Erik Mona (of Paizo) about open gaming and their reactions to all the confusion going on over at WOTC...for an hour and a half. Lots of interesting thoughts, though.
\n[1] They mention that RPGs are one of the most commonly stolen books from general book stores. This isn't the first time I've heard this. I know quite a few people at Barnes and Nobles and Borders that claim the same thing. Crazy!

[2] The librarians for the TPL say the same. Only the reference libraries with closed reading rooms keep RPGs here, partly because otherwise they get stolen like mad.

Why is this?

Anyone have any additional information?

I just spoke to a friend today who works at B&Ns and he indicated this was one of the reasons why RPGs are placed where they are in the store.

    Posted By: Alvin FrewerBut the value of RPGs is usually in the playing of it, not usually the reading.
    \nI don't know if that's entirely true. Most gamers I know have a significant number of games on their shelves that they have never played, and would probably admit they will likely not play. I think to a certain extent there is a strong "enamoured expectation of happy play" that prompts the sale that may not connect very well with the practical reality of actually playing.

    I also know several people who make a distinction between games books they know they bought just because they like to read them, for one reason or another (background, to see how the rules are explained, to marvel at the layouts and artwork, etc, etc) and the game books they know they bought because they were actually going to use them in play.

    Posted By: viktor_haagBecause the target market for RPGs represents a perfect storm combining poor impulse control, youth, a skewed sense of disaffected entitlement, and just-gotta-have-it collector neurosis?
    \nAWESOME answer!

    I wonder if any of this correlates to pirating digital copies of books. A few years ago I ran events locally in public spaces and I met many people who had huge print outs of their favorite RPGs. When I asked them why they didn't buy them, the general answers was, "why should I when I can get them for free?" In general I stopped playing with people who derived rewards from other people's work without permission and without giving back someway (I'm willing to define giving back fairly loosely). To be fair, many of these people were between the ages of 18-25. But then again, they were also NYU and Columbia students (very expensive schools). I actually grew up fairly poor and in high school I would skip several lunches to raise my share of the cost of an RPG book that I would split with several of my friends. But then again, we all do stupid things when we are young. Crazy!

    \n As one of those absconders (as a youth), I can vouch first-hand: A not-insignificant fraction of my game collection in my early teens was pilfered. I bought the occassional paperback (including the Dragonlance series), but mostly I got my reads from the local library because I grew up pretty poor. I received the 1st Ed PHB for my birthday, but then the mid-80s scare and the Red Devil cover meant that parental investment was out of the question. $30 was a lot of money, and the people working at Waldenbooks during the day were incredibly lax. I wanted it like it would kill me if I didn't get it, I had minimal legit options, I was 13 and it turned out I was pretty good at it.

    Since then I've put a few thousand dollars into publisher's hands. I'm not saying it evens it out, but at least it makes me feel better about it.

    As a sideline, this was my primary means of gaining access to nudie books.

    Take from that what you will.

    \n i bought WotG in pdf format and printed it out. and just stuck it in binders. it had less to do with the price and more to do with instant gratification and the fact that i was able to print it out on the laser printers at my school's computer lab where i worked.
    by and large, i think i would rather have a real book now,
    EDIT: WotG especially sucks in this format because that's a huge book. print it out one sided and it doubles in page count. jeez. i can practically lug around my whole collection of WoD books easier than the binders with that and the companion. printed .pdfs of small indie games don't bother me, though for the most part i always prefer a physical book.

    but there are some advantages. i just punched holes and put it in binders, and i was able to divide it up, putting, for example, all the things that players would need to make characters into one binder, stuff primarily for the gm in another, rules for combat in another.
    i also didn't mind writing in it to correct errata, whereas i really hate the idea of defacing a "real" book that way.
    but that isn't the point of this thread. i'm sorry.

    i've never stolen an rpg book.

    i'm not surprised a lot of people do, though. they is pricey.
