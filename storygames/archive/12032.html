    \n I'd say the analogy is flawed, that there is necessarily middle-ground between Clock and Cloud, wherein a system is partly governed by chance and partly by order. RPGs are an example that would lie as close to halfway between the two, in the main. On the one hand, we have the randomness of the dice, governing the final outcome regardless of our careful planning. On the other we have solid base numbers that never change, our statistics and skills (or whatever - generalising broadly here) that we can use to insert a certain degree of predictability into the chaos. On one hand we have the rules, stern adjudicators of fairness and guidance for the direction the game will take, then on the other we have players and GMs who are frequently unpredictable and frequently bend the rules to suit the situation they are in.

    As they say, All Generalisations are Dangerous (including this one).

    As for my own games, I'd say they err on the side of orderliness, but only slightly.

    -Ash

    \n So I guess any system (and here I mean system in the science! sense, not the gaming sense) can be described as clocky or cloudy, depending on the process of interest. In a grandfather clock, the motion of the minute hand is clocky; the flow of heat through the system is cloudy. In a cloud, the shape of the cloud's boundary is cloudy; the motion of any given droplet of water vapor is clocky.
    \n
    \nDon't confuse cloudy with random; stochastic processes can be perfectly clocky, as long as you understand the probability distributions involved. I'd say it's more about chaotic systems, where minor distances in initial conditions lead to large differences in the system's final state, or the way the dynamics of the system unfold.
    \n
    \nAny system can be decomposed into subsystems, and interactions between those subsystems. Some of those subsystems will be clocky, and some cloudy. At each point of interaction, either the clockiness or the cloudiness will dominate.
    \n
    \nHere's an example. Anyone remember Silmenume's posts about his group's Middle Earth game on the Forge, around 2006 or so? If you don't, it was a heavy sim game with a strong GM. The main mechanic seemed to be: roll a d20, if the result is middling the character performs on his normal level, a low or high result means he performs better or worse, a 1 or 20 means that the scene changes unexpectedly somehow. So that's a clocky system, the perfectly understood roll of a die, interacting with a cloudy system, the GM's synthesis of the situation at hand and the die's result. It's pretty clear here that, in this interaction, cloudiness dominates.
    \n
    \nHere's another example: Choosing what power to use on whom in D&D4e.; That's another interaction of a cloudy process, the player's choice, and a clocky process, the combat system and specific power rules. I'm tempted to say that in this interaction, clockiness wins, as the result of the interaction (who takes how much damage and what status effects) is input to another rather clocky subsystem, the larger set of combat rules.
    \n
    \nIt should be possible to formulate a precise mathematical definition of clockiness or cloudiness. Something like, given a system s with possible states S, a function F(i) that maps initial conditions to final conditions (deterministically or stochastically), and a probability distribution P over possible initial conditions i, clockiness is the integral over P of the mutual information between F(i) and F(i') for all possible i', where i' is a "neighboring" initial condition of i according to some formalism.
