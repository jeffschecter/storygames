\n Last night I ran a game of Trail of Cthulhu in which the characters were introduced to each other as fellow passengers on a train. Then forces intervened and they woke up in an asylum (some might call this railroading or perhaps a bang). I could have started them off in the asylum but I wanted to make it all the more confusing.

In a previous incarnation as a Man in Black, I wrote a Hellboy scenario which started in the middle of a fight, and then the players found out why they were fighting. It seemed to me to fit the comic idiom. SJG didn't like this and I was asked to rewrite it in chronological order, which I did.

And of course, there's John Tynes' brilliant Cthulhu game In Media Res in which 4 amnesiacs find themselves in a house with a dead body (we played it as a LARP but you can tabletop it too).

In Creative Writing, especially where short stories are concerned, we've been told to cut to the chase. Indeed Checkov said something along the lines that a short story is all middle.

So I wondered what the SG view was on this issue. Do you prefer to start scenarios in the thick of the action, with not necessarily a full understanding of the facts, or do you want character development and then on with the show?

    \n Depends, obviously and always, on the game and the situation.

    I have a generalized dislike of in media res for games because the middle of your story may not be the middle of my story, and so whoever is framing "to the point" may have the totally wrong point. In fact, this happens more often than not. I also have had a lot of bad experiences in the past with folks going to the middle skipping the stuff that actually makes the middle matter, killing all the context and emotional continuity that makes the story work. This happens a lot, because many gamers are tone deaf.

    (A good author doesn't generally have either of these problems in a short story, which is one of the places where the different arts really are different, I guess. The tangle of group narratives that form stories and the ongoing emotional situation in a game just really don't map out to the classical short story model here.)

    However, that said, when imr is used for a good reason, with understanding of the affect, it can make me happy. The OP example of "meet on a train, wake up in an asylum" would probably make me happy, because its a rather CoC thing to have happen. There is a congruity between player and character that it introduces, which while it isn't always my cuppa, sure can work wonders in CoC. And, frankly, I'd rather just be framed into the asylum than play scene after scene where I attempt to escape but can't, and so end up there anyway.

    \n Christian,

    As usual I agree with you.

    However, I do sometimes use an IMR trick in long term games. The way it gets referred to is "body and a gun" -- because my group are all clever monkeys, and noted that structurally it almost always starts a session where one of the PCs is standing in a scene out of context from the last session, with something bad or strange going on. A dead body on the floor and the PC standing over them with a smoking gun in their hand is the classic example.

    From there we often play some crazy thing happening (cops come in!) and then flash back and play out everything that went into making up the moment we saw. Sometimes we find out what we saw isn`t what we thought it was ("it wasn't me, it was the one armed man!") sometimes we find out its worse. Those sessions everyone has a goal and we all play towards it, which is fun. (In PTA it works well when no one has a high spotlight episode, so the structure of the plot can dominate a little.)

    So yea, that's a little IMR trick that I've had good luck with, even in a group of immersives who like to know what their character knows in every scene.

Railroading is when you retain control of what the game is about once it starts.

It worked great, really well, the game kicked off with a bang (using that in the general rather than technical sense) and was huge fun.

    So, why don't I use the technique more often? I tend to chicken out. Some players do get lost without context, they need to know the situation before they feel they can respond in character, but the people I play with are cool, I just tend to lose my nerve and go for the easier option of slow buildup. But that can lead to a loss of tension, to the early part of the session, crucial for creating mood, being kind of flat.

    We're currently playing Swords and Wizardry on a rotating GM basis, each GM gets one night for their scenario then it's on to the next guy. Next time it's my slot, I'm starting in media res, because you've reminded me how well it can work and when I only have one night I need to cut to the chase (it's why I'm liking the rotating GM thing for a bit actually, it really makes you focus). Next game, we open with the pirate vessel sinking as the Hessallion navy bombard it and the crew look expectantly to the PCs, or something along those lines.

    Cool thread. I do think, as noted by Graham, that it's a technique best used sparingly or it loses its impact, but then that's true of so many techniques and used sparingly does not mean not used at all.

    \nYeah, this is how I like to use it as well. Warm up to the Real Thing. Also, in scenario or campaign beginnings, to give the folks a sense of who their characters are, and to play up what their characters do for the others.

    Eric Provost did this thing for The Mountain Witch, which I now use pretty much all the time now in adventure games. Basically, the game starts in the middle of a fight, "while the credits roll". There are enemies attacking. There are two "combat rounds". NO DICE ARE ROLLED. You simply describe your character, and what they're doing. You succeed at whatever you're doing, basically. No rules, just description. When round two is over, the fight ends, people say their one liners, and the Real Adventure begins.

    \nDanger Patrol always starts with action. The GM says what crazy action set-piece you're involved in (The burning magna-zeppelin is hurtling down the Rocket City central power core, headed for the atomic generators at the bottom. There are screaming passengers everywhere!). Then you do a round of "Previously, on Danger Patrol..." where each player describes a few shots from the previous episode (which you didn't play) that leads up to the current set-piece. This lets them color up the action and include stuff they want to see in the game. Like Clinton's infamous line, "Enchantra! It's you! You swore never to return after you betrayed the Patrol and broke my heart!"

    So it's in media res but with some input from everyone on the details.

    \nI'm a fan of starting a game with an In Media Res fight scene, especially if the campaign is with characters that are higher than starting level. There's nothing that gets the players more into really understanding their characters than a fast combat, and realizing three rounds in that they weren't using one of their powers/items/abilities to their full extent.
    \n
    \nI prefer to have this first combat either completely unrelated to the main arc of the story, or only tangentally related, so that decisions in this particular fight won't set the tone for the whole campaign.
    \n
    \nI also really like this technique for games like Call of Cthulhu where fighting the monsters = death, but a little fight with some mobsters can let the players blow off steam and get their yayas out so they can settle down into serious studying of ancient texts!
    \n
    \nAll that goodness said, it is very easily overused, especially if it stresses some of your players.
