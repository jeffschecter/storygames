\n When I was a much younger man it was the height of the Cold War, the Reagan era when we were really laying it heavy on the Soviet Union and forcing them toward inevitable collapse. Consequently our Top Secret and Top Secret S/I games involved a lot of Cold War machinations, and with a heavy dose of Gold Eagle Books being the norm, we killed an awful lot of Commies. Mostly KGB agents and their minions, but Commies nonetheless.

Anyway, I was thinking about those relatively simple games and comparing them to what seems to be the standard of gaming here at Story Games. Just a cursory examination of the bite-sized AP thread reveals a lot of sophisticated storytelling going on. There doesn't seem to be a lot of room for the kinds of games where you just do a lot of Commie killing.

Now, feel free to substitute your cannon fodder of choice here. Maybe it's orcs or the faceless minions of Fu Manchu or whatever. The point is that they exist primarily to be killed by the PCs, not engaged in a sophisticated battle of wits.

I wonder if we have the time or the inclination to play the kind of games I'm talking about. Games that aren't full of angst and moral dilemmas and the like. Which isn't to say that sophisticated gaming is necessary bad. I don't mean to imply that at all. I'm simply wondering if maybe we've lost touch with a more basic, cathartic mode of play.

I'm a bit skeptical if there is such a thing as simple roleplaying - to me it seems that people only characterize their roleplaying as simple and unconceited (as if anything you do freely and for fun could be conceited) when they want to take an anti-intellectual tack on the whole endeavour. When you dig beneath the surface, I haven't really encountered gaming styles or traditions that would truly be "simple"; there's always a lot of nuance and skill involved in roleplaying, and many goals that can be pushed quite far.

    I think I believe that "simple" is something that happens when you want clear systems and a back to basics approach with quick payoffs. That is not particularly attached to adventure games or story games or any other specific set of goals and methods, I think. In fact, my game Zombie Cinema is to my eyes very much a "simple" approach to story games. Is it truly any more complex or multi-layered or serious than any Commie-killing game?

    W.R. Irwin, The Game of the Impossible: A Rhetoric of Fantasy. University of Illinois Press, 1976Tolkien . . . defends and praises fantasy more than he describes it, but from his evaluating comments understanding may be inferred.
