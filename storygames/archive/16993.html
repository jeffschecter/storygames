\n Looking at Greece's TEM virtual currency and the mind-popping FavaBank in the UK, is it time Indie skillsets turned to one another for services?

I'm looking for some ideas about how to (in)formalise a currency for story games designers.

In the simplest form, users would advertise their wares, come to an arrangement and spend virtual currency on services via an independant broker (me, for example). The virtual currency would be recorded publicly. This could achieved manually with a simple forum but would benefit from some automation. Please let me know how this has been handled before, the practical considerations and ideas for implementation.

And whether you feel you would be able to contribute toward it as a user or developer.

    In other situations in which people pay by exchange of tasks (like: a plumber pays the tailor for his shirts with his plumber work), they use some kind of cheques in which you write the time spent on service.
    So you may use these cheques as a currency with some other third professional, actually using time units (hours or half of an hour) as "money".

    \n Why not use money? This is exactly the use case Kroisos designed it for, after all. It's worked fine for me, at least. Instead of public and centralized tracking I could just pay you $100 to design my cover, and then you could maybe pay me or somebody else $100 or $80 or whatever to do what needs doing.

    Alternatively, if there's not sufficient money in the system, try out Ripple for a protocol that enables the participants to credit each other with some. Seems like a very effective tool for monetizing community trust, even if it is yet highly experimental.

    What you're suggesting is a local exchange trading system. I'm not much of a fan (even central bank money is more fair, usually), but even if I were, I'm not entirely clear on the need. Would it be just for fun? Do you think that the pricing of these services needs revamping, and a currency innovation is the way to get there? I could understand the lark motivation, it's fun to mess about with systems design. As long as it's not too serious, no reason not to do it.

    \n When you take away money, services are traded. In a design culture (where practice makes better), earning currency by employing skills reaps a double reward.

    Furthermore, a barter system using virtual currency would reduce the hard margin. By hard margin, I mean cash margin (as opposed to man-hour margin). Paying an artist to do a cover for your Lady Blackbird hack is different to playtesting a few systems and using earned currency to commission the artwork.

    This investigation comes off the back of conversation with a friend. He's an artist and I'm a graphic designer. He just blew a load of cash on a bad layout artist and didn't have the capital to invest in another try. I wanted to offer my help but my time is precious. If he could write me an IOU that I could spend later on his work, or on the work of another artist, that'd be just fine.

    I've just signed up to Villages which uses the ripple system. It seems you offer hearts (each representing one hour of work) in response to favours rendered. I don't understand it very well.

    \n Hmm, I should probably elucidate that I've been considering introducing the Ripple protocol (the idea behind Villages) to the indie rpg scene for a few years now, as it could streamline and reduce the expenses I acrue in buying and selling games overseas. Also, the community has plentiful trust thanks to years of joint artistic endeavours, so there'll probably more currency in the system than we would ever need. The only major stumbling block that's been discouraging me here is the fact that gift economies, including the indie scene, are low in volatility, which means that most of the time a given person will have only little utility from messing with the system. Not much motivation in setting up a system that comes up once in a blue moon; for it to be truly useful for e.g. myself it'd need to be active and robust enough to facilitate game sales (my most common international economic transaction by far). As long as we're talking about helping out somebody every few months, it's easier to just do it instead of messing about with an accounting system.

    This is to say, I'm all for a solution like Villages, it's an interesting experiment that won't hurt anybody even if it fizzles.

    \n@rafu Your scene-specific paradigm makes a hugely important distinction. This is an industry where success can't be measured by wealth. There's just not enough cash in the flow. The thing this community excels at, for me, is the aggregation of ideas and experience, not the accumulation of luxury. Designers share. The distinction from the common marketplace, where services are often given reluctantly for the sake of living, is the hobbism, the passion for which has yet been unquantifiable. When I help you, or listen to you, or think about your work, I'm helping myself. So to think about this scene, as you put it, and how it is already served by donation and volunteerism, is important.

    @Tazio The advantage of the digital baretering system is in the social part. Good relationships are rewarded publicly. Not so good relationships are, necessarily but implicitly, punished.
