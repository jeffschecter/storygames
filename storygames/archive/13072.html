    \n Tyler, you're walking, perhaps naively, into a conversation that has been ongoing at least since 2000, and swinging a big stick around without knowing who the participants in this conversation are, where the lines are drawn, and what the current issues of contention are. That's not the best way to start a conversation, especially on a topic as contentious as "what does 'indie' mean" has become.

    Some of the background is like this (and I only know a piece of it):

    The definition of "indie" used by the Forge -- from which SG and large portion of this particular indie scene descends (though not the Evil Hat guys, directly, though they use IPR and have some significant crossover) -- was based around creator ownership, period. There's some complex baggage that goes along with that statement, like the tradition of paying dirt-cheap prices for art because you're only licensing the publication rights from artists (when they may or may not be interested in any of the rights) and the Forge giving rise to a series of autuer-style self-publishers, such that models where multiple creators were involved were only sometimes experimented with (Universalis, etc.)... but that's the simple version: creator ownership.

    Currently, different members of the indie community feel more or less strongly about creator ownership being the key thing that unites us. Ben and Joshua are probably among the most vocal, hardcore advocates of creator ownership being the most important thing. If you ask other folks, you'll get some more wishy-washy answers that are much more flexible regarding what the point of the community is.

    Evil Hat had been doing Fate on their own, as a Fudge hack, and had a big internet following before they ever did Spirit of the Century. They had some connections to the post-Forge or pan-Forge community and published SOTC using a similar model and distributed it through IPR and then Fred got closedly involved in the IPR/Forge/Adept Press booth at GenCon and eventually started doing some work for IPR. So Evil Hat went from being kinda doing their own thing to being at the heart of this particular indie community, at least in terms of publishing (I keep qualifying that because there are other indie publishers who have little or no connection with this scene). A lot of us liked SOTC a lot, so this was cool. But there were also a bunch of discussions, which drove Fred nuts, about whether SOTC was indie, etc. etc. And there was some other resentment about other stuff, including the direction that IPR was going (which may not have had anything to do with Evil Hat), so... as things stand now, Evil Hat -- as far as I can tell -- doesn't go out of the way to call themselves indie or try to claim that label. Plus, with licensing Dresden, you clearly have a not-indie game, though nobody gives Luke any shit about Burning Empires or Mouse Guard (and Luke says that Mouse Guard isn't indie), so there's totally a double standard going on there, but that's a whole separate issue.

    As far as I can tell, the weird feelings about SOTC and Evil Hat are mostly not about their runaway success or their attempt to appeal to mainstream gamers. Those are things that most indie publishers aspire to, Matt Snyder's opinions notwithstanding. Luke certainly has runaway success and aims for a mainstream gamer audience. Same with Vincent, at least for most of his games. There's a bunch of other weird thorny stuff that's behind the whole "SOTC/Evil Hat isn't indie anymore/ever" meme that's been going around. I was just talking to Ryan Macklin about it when he was visiting and he had a couple of insights, but I'm still not sure what the whole story is. I would like to track down what the truth of the matter is and get the involved parties to sit down and talk to each other, because, honestly, it's kinda dumb and counterproductive for all of us. But I don't know how to make that happen at this point.

    \n I use the term indie as a descriptive term, as in the Forge definition of creator owned and managed. The term is also understood to imply certain design aesthetics and philosophies within this community, which is why it's often used as shorthand, but those qualities are not actually part of the definition I use. A game made off the OGL by a small press, creator owned and managed company is indie, even if it doesn't possess the design aesthetics and philosophies implied by the term.
    \n
    \nSo, Burning Wheel is indie, because it's creator owned and managed. Mouse Guard is not because it's owned and (presumably) managed by someone other than the game's creator. It has the same design philosophies and aesthetics (at least some of them) as BW, but that's not part of the definition.
    \n
    \nHence the reason why indie is generally considered to be a useless term, because no one really knows what you mean by it until you define it, in which case, it's generally easier just to spell out the definition you're using. That said, I still use it as shorthand when talking to gamers who aren't into the indie/small press/story-game community. When I use it there, I'm specifically using it as "Not WOTC, White Wolf, etc." It's easier when you're talking in generalities. Occasionally, I'll use the term small press instead. I basically never use the term story-game, unless I'm referring to this site/community.

    \n Between publishers and the public, "indie" is just a label. It has a dozen meanings, all colloquially intermixed and fun. "I like indie games! They have such very weird dice mechanics!" or whatever. "Check out my new game! It's my most trad yet!" All good, all fun. If it's a little bit confusing, that's just life, right?

    Between publishers and designers though, independence establishes the fundamental nature of the relationship.

    "Hello Vincent, I'm pleased to meet you. I've designed a game that I was wondering if you'd take a look at, to publish." "Cool! No, I won't! I'm the indie hardcore, I don't believe in selling your game to a publisher. I'd be thrilled to help you publish it yourself, though."

    This is why Evil Hat isn't indie. It has nothing to do with the quality, popularity or success of their games, it's all about who makes business decisions about, and profits from, the creator's work.

    -Vincent

      \n How are RPG publishers currently exploiting creators?

      This is an honest question. I know how they can... I just don't know how they are. And I don't mean the edge cases. How are they on the average?

      I have many friends who freelance for RPG publishers. They make about as much money as they would if they self published the same content and sold around 300-500 copies themselves. Although freelancing has given them easily 10 times more exposure which gives them more opportunities moving forward.

      But if they were able to sell more than 500 copies on their own without doing much more work than what they were being paid to do as a freelancer, freelancing becomes less profitable. Usually you do have to do a lot more work and possibly work that isn't fun, so for some people they would have to sell closer to 1,000 copies before self publishing became worth it (assuming publishers would alternatively pay them a decently for equivalent work). For some people, they need to freelance first (or give products or services away for free) before they can start selling 1,000 copies on their own. And the average Paizo freelancer I know sells much less when they self publish.

      Ways I believe creators can be exploited:
      - low pay
      - late or no pay
      - no royalty options on work that is virtually guaranteed to sell well specifically because of your contributions
      - non-competition agreements when pay isn't enough to live on
      - full time work without full time benefits
      - not paying for creation of Intellectual Property
      - undefined contract terms (example: scalable expenses that deduct pay with no limit or proof needed)
      - creators pay for expenses up front and are / may be reimbursed later
      - publisher gains rights over content they haven't paid for
      - publisher limits creators rights without paying for that privilege (example: you can't include your work in your portfolio or sell original artwork)
      - no credit
      - and I'm sure much more!

      Although you could argue that it isn't exploitation if all these details are upfront, clearly understood, the creator doesn't need the work to survive and is ok with these terms.

      Also, creators that can sell 1,000+ copies on their own need publishers less but are also in a position of power to negotiate the terms of their publishing contracts. A lot of this come down to leverage. If your work sell poorly on its own, then freelancing may be a huge benefit. But if your work sells well on its own, you can self publish or negotiate to make freelancing worth it.

      It all comes down to the details. I know no one is saying publishers, middlemen, and/or self publishing is automatically good or bad... It really just depends.

      \n Jason, the only people who say anything about the Forge "inventing indie games" are people who hold it up as a straw man to try to make the point that the whole thing is ridiculous.

      Of course indie games predate the Forge. Major Wesley was publishing indie war games in the 60s. Moreover, the very first article posted at the Forge, The Nuked Apple Cart(dated as it may now be), was speaking to other indie game designers.

      In the late 1990s, Print on demand was still coming into being, and it would be years before it could produce anything of acceptable quality.

      Digital distribution meant you snailmail me $5 and I'll email you a text file.

      And the conventional wisdom was that to be successful in RPGs, if you couldn't sell your game to a publisher, you needed to print no less than 20,000 copies of your game and have at least 6 supplements in the can. Otherwise, no distributor will touch you. Without a distributor, no retailer will carry your product. And since retail is the only way to get your product into the hands of the customer, you can forget about success unless you follow the conventional wisdom.

      Every year, we tour the floor at Origins and Gen Con, and without fail find somebody, even in 2010, who has bought into this conventional wisdom. They have spent their inheritance or taken out a second mortgage on their house. And they're not going to see that money again.

      Ron planted his flag at the Forge by stating that it didn't have to be that way. It seems obvious now, but it wasn't back then. Sure, there were many other indie designers out there, but many of them were taking enormous risks.

      The Forge cam together as a mutual support society for indie designers who were ready to throw conventional wisdom out the window and blaze their own trail. And because it was a new trail, everyone taught each other and learned from each other. Everyone learned about digital distribution and marketing together, tested the PoD waters together, etc.

      Design and content were and always have been secondary to that one overriding idea. As Joshua has pointed out, if indie games do different things in design, that is an emergent property of the emphasis on creator-owned and controlled publishing. But in itself, design, innovative or not, has no bearing on whether something is indie or not.

      That's why all those different and varied games fit into the same bucket. Indie is not a content category, design category or popularity category. It's a publishing category.

      Should a game's indie-ness matter to you as a player of games? I don't see why it should. It doesn't to me. I'll buy and play a good game whether it's published by Vincent or WotC.

      At the same time, I don't want anyone involved in making games to lose their shirt doing so. I don't care if something is indie or non-indie, so long as everyone involved is treated fairly. But it is important to me that people know the indie option is there, that if they choose, they can do it themselves in their own way.

And since retail is the only way to get your product into the hands of the customer, you can forget about success unless you follow the conventional wisdom.

      Every year, we tour the floor at Origins and Gen Con, and without fail find somebody, even in 2010, who has bought into this conventional wisdom. They have spent their inheritance or taken out a second mortgage on their house. And they're not going to see that money again.

      Ron planted his flag at the Forge by stating that it didn't have to be that way. It seems obvious now, but it wasn't back then. Sure, there were many other indie designers out there, but many of them were taking enormous risks.

      The Forge cam together as a mutual support society for indie designers who were ready to throw conventional wisdom out the window and blaze their own trail. And because it was a new trail, everyone taught each other and learned from each other. Everyone learned about digital distribution and marketing together, tested the PoD waters together, etc.

      Design and content were and always have been secondary to that one overriding idea. As Joshua has pointed out, if indie games do different things in design, that is an emergent property of the emphasis on creator-owned and controlled publishing. But in itself, design, innovative or not, has no bearing on whether something is indie or not.

      That's why all those different and varied games fit into the same bucket. Indie is not a content category, design category or popularity category. It's a publishing category.

      Should a game's indie-ness matter to you as a player of games? I don't see why it should. It doesn't to me. I'll buy and play a good game whether it's published by Vincent or WotC.

      At the same time, I don't want anyone involved in making games to lose their shirt doing so. I don't care if something is indie or non-indie, so long as everyone involved is treated fairly. But it is important to me that people know the indie option is there, that if they choose, they can do it themselves in their own way.

      Posted By: Simon RogersOnce you are having to fight for your definition of a word, or attack people for casual use of a word which does not match your definition, you know you are losing. I've nearly given up on "less" vs "fewer." I think indie has gone the same way
      \nIt's almost impossible to control the definition of a word outside of a given context. So whilst you can say "on SG we mean creator-owned" as your audience widens its very hard to maintain that specific a definition. This is especially true where the term has in itself been appropriated from music and film, where the usage of the term indie predates that in gaming, and means something different (an independent label does not have the same definition as an independent games company).

      I have immense sympathy with all the people who created something on the Forge (and other places) through sweat and commitment, who would like to retain ownership of the term indie to describe work in alignment with the aims that they outlined there. This is especially true because media and film have given the term 'indie' a 'cool' factor that publishers would love to be associated with.

      To the point of the original poster though, I think the door swings both ways. Any publicity for the work happening outside the mainstream is good, not bad.
