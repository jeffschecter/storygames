\n So in another thread I mentioned that I thought the IIEE issue was related to but not the same thing as the Storyboarding issue. Ralph asked me to break that down. So it here goes, concrete examples and everything!

A group of Mouse Guard players get into a heated argument with the mayor of the town. We go into an Argument Conflict (i.e. MG version of Dual of Wits). We each say what we want. The guard mice want the mayor to recognize their authority in the situation at hand. The mayor wants to put the mice in their place since they technically have no jurisdiction inside of his town. Great.

We execute the Conflict procedures. We script things. Each action we reveal what we\u2019re doing. We roll dice. We add or subtract disposition. Helping dice are passed around. Narration is limited to, \u201cOkay, this action I attack with my deceiver skill\u201d and \u201cYeah, I\u2019m helping with Politian-wise because I know how this stuff works!\u201d and so on. In other words we have only the vaguest sense of what\u2019s happening moment to moment.

In the end, things resolve the mayor is either cowed or has asserted his dominance and which ever side lost adjust their next action accordingly and play proceeds. This is IIEE w/o Teeth. We jumped from \u201cthe Guard and Mayor are fighting over authority\u201d to \u201cthe Mayor is cowed and submits to the Guard\u201d with no real idea how that was accomplished. *How* did the Guard mice cow the Mayor is completely lost except for some vague notions about \u201cthey argued.\u201d In Mouse Guard maintaining those details requires the discipline Vincent talks about.

This, however, isn\u2019t Storyboarding because we\u2019re still in the realm of the characters want x and the force that opposes them wants y and here is which gets what and to what degree. We\u2019re losing the details but narrative motion and creative input authority are maintained. Storyboarding (as I mean it) requires one player reaching over and moving around bits that don\u2019t belong to him in order to establish a plot point of their personal preference.

Example: \u201cOkay, so Joe and I are finally in that dual we\u2019ve been threatening to have happen when Mary walks in the middle of it and I accidentally stab her!\u201d What I\u2019ve done here is completely walked all over whatever Joe may have wanted out of having a duel with my character in the first place in favor of jumping to some other point of drama. Not only have we lost the details of the duel, we\u2019ve lost the whole duel and any emotional content it may have had. Joe is now put in the position of having to socially defend the creative validity of the duel as its own thing.

Where I\u2019ve seen this most often are players over stating the Agenda part of scene requesting in Primetime Adventures. For example even this milder form, \u201cThe Agenda is Joe and I are having a duel when Mary walks in on us\u201d shifts focus away from the duel an on to Mary\u2019s discovery of the duel in progress again losing all the emotional content of the duel.

Does that make the distinction clear?

    \n I'm still not 100% sure what everyone means with the term "storyboarding", but here's my take:

    Normal RPG *play* has a certain "linear" or "sequential" nature (even if the *story* jumps back and forth in time). This is the essence of improvisation. You say something, then I add what happens next, then you declare the next event, etc. (RPGs certainly allow for open negotiation, but the point is that normal RPG play doesn't revisit an event after it's been established.) This is why the entire concept of IIEE---a sequence outlining how events are established---exists.

    Story-boarding (in my mind) represents those times when the players abandon the "sequential" process and just start brainstorming or negotiating events.

    So Jesse's "IIEE w/o teeth" example isn't story-boarding because the IIEE process was maintained, even if details were left out. His later examples are story-boarding because the IIEE process was basically thrown out the window.

Its like Dogs' "Even Now?" paradigm cranked up to 11. You can right now...right this very instant...take the most emotionally powerful, sob threatening, heart wrenching dillema you can think of based on everything you know about how to push the buttons of the player across the table from you...and hit it...hard and repeatedly.     \n
    \nPTA you have different mechanics but the same situation.
    \n
    \n
    \nI have a theory that suggests that one of the reasons for opposition to storyboarding techniques in play is that its not safe. It puts your character image at risk. Not just his stats, or his hit points, or his mechanical effectiveness, but who the character is down deep in their soul in the hands of the table, and the end result is you as a player may be forced to reconcile who you thought your character was as a person, with who the character was revealed to be after the table got done putting him through the crucible. Playing the fiction moment to moment is safer. You can see the direction events are going, things are forshadowed in a way that gives you a greater option to backout or find another path, it leaves more of the choices of your character in your hands and to an extent allows you to be the final keeper of that character's soul.
    \n
    \nWhen you go to more story boarding and direct story manipulation play...you are automatically, almost by definition, increasing the vulnerability of your character. Its no longer just up to you to decide who your character is, but the entire table now gets a say in who your character is, by what suggestions they make for stakes, or what dillemas they put your character in etc. etc. In the end, play becomes as much about discovering who the other players perceive your character to be as who you are trying to portray him to be. I REALLY REALLY love that. And story boarding techniques is one technique that is particularly good at delivering that sort of play.
    \n
    \nI'm reminded of our recent game of IaWA. There's not an overt story board driver in IaWA the way there is in Polaris or PTA...but there is a covert driver in the group brainstorming of oracle interpretations and Best Interests at the beginning of play, how that feeds into the types of scenes the GM sets during play and, most powerfully, in how you wield the Stick. The Stick is one of the greatest tools of this nature since "But only if". Its your chance to force the other player to accept a truth...a truth that you select...if they don't want to lose mechanical effectiveness. Man. I remember my primary character struggling throughout play to be the ruthless but noble Cardinal Richelieu-esque character I'd envisioned against others attempts to make him more smarmy and questionably motivated. I largely won that struggle in the end. Keith's primary character however...pretty much became our ongoing villain...sick, twisted, and creepy...despite Keith's best efforts to portray him as noble and honestly in love. That struggle made the moments of redemption so much more powerful, and made Keith's character one of the most memorable I've ever seen portrayed.
    \n
    \n
    \nHave you played Showdown Jesse? In that game the entire object of play is to claim your opponent's character, rip out its soul, step on it, and hand it back to them broken...having earned with a die roll the right to ruin the other players image of their character as a hero and instead reveal as proven fact in the fiction that they are...whatever you think they will find most distasteful. Its very liberating as a roleplaying exercise to try to create the coolest most amazing "I love this character" character you can, one who holds to the archetypical ideals you find yourself playing most frequently...and then experience that horrible knot in the pit of your stomach as play reveals him to be an absolute heel, of exactly the sort your friend knows you abhor...and there is very little you can do to stop it...except accept death gracefully and let your hated rival win. And so the game becomes this ingenious test of how badly do you want to win...and therefor how much are you willing to risk your character being destroyed in the process. Its so much more effective if you start with a character you actually love.
    \n
    \nStoryboarding isn't the only technique that gives other players this level of input into your character...but its one of the most effective ways of getting reliably to the point of being able to ask player to player "Even Now?" and know that you're pushing exactly the right buttons to make that a hard question to answer...especially when both answers say something about your character you're not sure you want to admit about them.
    \n
    \nRight technique for every game design? Of course not...but highly effective, extremely powerful tool as every bit as legitimate and worthy of concideration as any other design tool we have available...absofragginlutely.

    \n The problem is that there's no recognized definition of "story-boarding", we're all just working off of our own ideas. And as you might guess, that's causing a lot of confusion and miscommunication.

    Now, I have to say that I find the whole "story-boarding is just another technique" line to be, uh... unsatisfying. The term was originally conceived to describe unfun play, right? So any proposed definition has to explain why the technique(s) is generating unfun play.

    Furthermore, the term also implies a certain, umm... "emotional distance" between the player and the fiction, right? That's the purpose of comparing play to a boardgame, right? So we need a way to explain that phenomenon as well.
    ___________________

    Jesse's definition involves players crossing borders of authority.

    That would certainly explain unfun play, but you would think that if that was the issue, people would be complaining about player-to-player tension, not "emotional distance" from the fiction.
    ___________________

    Ralph's definition---if I understand him---involves "direct story manipulation".

    Since some players have long complained that "meta play" pulls them out of the fiction, Ralph's explanation is certainly plausible. But personally, I'm not sold on that idea. IME, the ability to "meta think" is a learned skill. Sure, it feel weird at first, but after a while it just becomes the way you operate. Personally, I've internalized the skill so much that I can't even see a distinction any more.
    ___________________

    My definition involves a shift in the way IIEE and the general procedural nature of play works. This is going to take some explaining:

    I believe that improvisation and "composition" (aka "normal" authorship) represents two distinct modes of the creation. As such, each mode presents its own set of advantages and disadvantages. Traditional RPG play is inherently improvisational. In fact, it seems structured to maximize the strengths and minimize the weaknesses of improvisation.

    Now, I would argue that the immediacy of the fiction that we experience when role-playing---stuff like the heightened sense of character identification---is largely a byproduct of improvisational play. This is where my understanding of "story-boarding" comes from.

    Normal play is built "sequentially" or "procedurally", one event on top of the last. I would call story-boarding those times when players step off to the side and develop multiple events all in unison, and then pop them back into the sequence. In other words, they hit the "improvisational pause" button, and begin engaging in "compositional" play.

    In the big picture, this is a perfectly fine technique, but there is a trade-off that players must be aware of. The players gain the advantages of "composition", but they lose the advantages of improvisation. And since the "immediacy" of the fiction is one of those things gained by improvisation, it's one of the things players lose by switching modes.

    And that seems to explain what people are complaining about with "story-boarding".
