Dramatically\u2026 lose more now, win more later

\n
\n
\n
\n\njenskot \n\n\n
\n
\n\nApril 2011 \n edited April 2011 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I've been reading lots of Movie Screen Writing books, talking to actors, producers, writers and a pattern I often see in dramatic works\u2026

If you are a major protagonist, the more you lose on your journey to attain your main goal\u2026 the more likely you will win in the end. Drama comes from contrast.

Although you don't want:

- to be predictable
- the losses not to matter
- your protagonist to lose so often they seem incompetent and you no longer care if they win

But there are no strict rules, and if the audience is so used to certain forms of storytelling\u2026 if their expectations of specific patterns are so strong\u2026 breaking the rules is a great way to create contrast, to create drama.

You don't need to lose most of the time. It's about quality\u2026 not quantity. One credible loss with significant consequences before the end is enough to make winning dramatic.

But in RPGs, this can feel counterintuitive. Drama may be the goal but your characters are honestly trying to overcome obstacles and gathering advantages to increase their chances to win. But dramatically, the more likely their tactics are sound\u2026 the more dramatic it would be for them to lose. At the same time, if you play your characters with the knowledge that you can lose your way to victory\u2026 it feels empty. It undermines the drama. The drama needs characters to be risking everything on their way to success.

Pro Wrestling often uses this formula poorly. Characters talk about winning their matches before a PPV to build momentum\u2026 to gain advantages\u2026 but most of the time the person who wins a match before a PPV is the person who is going to lose at that PPV.

The conflicts your characters face, even within a dramatic structure, have to matter. You may be able to lose your way to overall success but those losses need to hurt. Losing these conflicts means sacrifice, losing things or people you care about and paying terrible costs.

Now RPGs aren't stories. I've seen many RPGs feel lackluster when story became more important than playing. At the same time, RPGs aren't quite games in the traditional sense. I've also seen plenty of RPGs fall flat due to complete randomness. I suspect there is a treasure trove somewhere in the middle.

    The drama needs characters to be risking everything on their way to success.
    \n
    \nA lot of the times, risking the loss of *something* is enough to get some of this effect. You want the thing you risk losing to be of a different kind from what the character ends up winning. It's basically a way for the protagonist to say my end goal (the big win) is more important to me than (this thing I'm risking losing.) This lets you set up for the potential that you'll decide what you have (or improving something that you would have to risk) is more important than what you thought you wanted at first.
    \n
    \nSometimes you need to lose for the risk to be plausible. This is easier to do in a game than in a movie. In a game, you can see the 50/50 chance that your girlfriend is going to leave you. In a movie, they usually have to follow through (at some point at least) for the audience to take it as a credible risk (and thus have emotional/dramatic impact.)

Then, you can offer compels not just on the PC's aspects, but on the aspects for, say, the scene. Second, you have to pay a fate point to refuse a compel, so depending on how many fate points you currently have, you may find yourself in no position to say no. Third, you can use compels to encourage players to act on their aspects, or to bring in complications because of their aspects. I often use it to push a player towards a goal.

Admittedly, I tend not to like the kind of well-defined approach that David outlines. To me, the fate point economy seems much more organic, while still in keeping with that general pattern. It reinforces a satisfying dramatic arc, without enforcing it.

    \n Agreed that its best that whatever system is used, it isn't predictable. Predictable takes away from drama.

    One way we did this in D&D:

    - link together a string of drastically escalating difficult conflicts (Encounter Level-2, EL, EL+2, EL+4)
    - reward failed rolls with points that can be cashed in later to increase (but not guarantee) the chance of success

    It wasn't perfect but if you succeeded most of the early conflicts, later conflicts were harder. If you failed most of the early conflicts, later conflicts were easier (but not "easy"). We also added stunt mechanics that let players make more difficult rolls in exchange for greater effects. This was also a way to allow players to increase their risk of failure as desired.

    \n I've tried a version of this called token. You lose, you get a token. If you want to win pay one or more tokens, depending on the difficulty. If you know the scale of the final task, it's easy to work out how many times you need to lose.

    However, whilst this structure works fine for movies, it's not quite so satisfying for games. Partly, I think because you know what you need to do to win, and partly because in the movie, even though the plot is fixed (and you're pretty sure the good guys will win), it's what you expect from this medium (also there's so much going on that immersion can mean you don't worry too much about the plot). In games, you like to feel in control but at the same time not know what's going to happen. This is harder to do if you make the resolution as simple as the token system.

    You can introduce a random element which makes it a bit more exciting. For every token, roll 1d6, subtract from your total if it's a loss, add if it's a win. The immediate roll gives the scale of the win in that scene and the overall total gives the progress of plot. On average you'll end up where you started, a negative final total is a bitter sweet victory, a positive total something better. If you want to make it more complicated, keep a different running total for each protagonist.

    \nAlso, in a movie, you can be pretty sure that the good guys will win in the end and can still believe that they are trying as hard as they can to win in their earlier conflicts, too. It's not like John McClane is deliberately getting his feet carved up by broken glass or falling down an airshaft to make things more difficult for himself, he's legitimately doing his very best to stop the terrorists at all times. Luke Skywalker isn't going into Bespin thinking, "Okay, so I'll let Darth Vader kick my ass for a little while, that way I'll be able to win against the Emperor later on."

    When you're playing a game where you know that you have to lose a certain number of times to collect tokens in order to win, it's harder to believe that of the characters. At some point, you as a player have to step back and say, "Yeah, okay, this character doesn't want to lose, but if I put everything he's got into this conflict, I won't get a token, and I need the token for the only conflict that's going to actually matter." And now whatever conflict you're in right now is diminished in several ways: it's not the important conflict, and you know that you can't have the character make a genuine effort to win it.


    I wonder if it might be easier to mimic the "lose now, win later" dramatic arc with some mechanic that's outside the player's control, something that just stacks the odds against them early on in the game but gradually shifts until it favors them. Like, the opposition has a massive pool of bonuses available, making it easy for them to outmatch the PCs in any conflict...but as they use those bonuses, they go into a pool that the PCs can use. When the PCs use those bonuses, they go away. That way, at the start of the game the opposition keeps winning, but all they're doing is handing valuable resources over to the PCs, who can then turn around in the second half of the game and use them to beat the snot out of the opposition.

    Posted By: stefoidTo generalize, dont you want the opposition to become firmer as the stakes get higher?
    \nDo you really want that, though? It's not very much in keeping with the dramatic trope of the good guys losing important conflicts and suffering real consequences at the beginning of the play/movie/novel despite their best efforts, but managing to pull it all together at the end and win their ultimate battle. Those stories tend to have the opposition either remaining stable (the Empire doesn't gain in power in any significant way over the course of the movie) or actually falling apart a little (the terrorists in Die Hard have lost men, and one of them is obsessed with revenge for his brother to the point of no longer being a reliable second-in-command). Meanwhile, the heroes are bloodied and bruised from their past setbacks, but somehow manage to find hidden reserves of strength.

    I guess it all comes down to what you're trying to emulate, and whether you made those earlier losses and their consequences count. If you're not trying for genre emulation and a heroic story, then the characters might as well lose and keep losing while their enemies keep getting more powerful and harder to defeat, until at the very end they're fighting the hardest fight they possibly can, and maybe losing that one, too. There's absolutely nothing wrong with that kind of game, it drove all kinds of D&D modules back in the day. It's challenging when the stakes and the forces against you keep escalating, and that's one of the things that a lot of people really love in a game.

    Or if you're aiming to emulate a heroic story in that vein, but make the consequences of their early failures weak and/or easily-fixed, then there's no payoff for winning the (much easier) fight at the end: basically, the heroes get slapped around in a meaningless way early on, then stomp the crap out of their opponents at the end. I guess that could be fun, too, but it's kind of missing the mark set in the first post: the characters are failing at the beginning, but they're not really losing: the contrast between those failures and their success won't be very much, and that last fight won't be satisfying at all.

    But imagine a game where the opposition is really, genuinely, 100% tougher than the PCs at the beginning of the game, and make that hurt. The PCs suffer huge setbacks, lose things that cannot be replaced, and have to deal with long-term consequences. And it happens more than once, but slowly the balance starts to shift: the advantage that the opposition had over them diminishes, meanwhile they're acquiring new resources. By the time that they can step in and force a showdown with their enemies, they're practically equals, or maybe even have a slight advantage. That last fight isn't about "How will we win against these terrible odds?" but rather about getting the catharsis of finally, at long last, winning. It feels like a hard-won victory, because everyone is still thinking about all the grief they had to go through just to get here.

    \n I'm in agreement that "predictable" takes away from "drama".

    But I don't think the use of railroad in this context is correct. Although I can understand why it happens.

    Railroading is a pejorative term that describes a very specific form of dysfunctional gaming. In the larger RPG world, railroading is a style of gaming where there is one predetermined plot line and those involved are forced to follow it even if they don't want to.

    A game mechanic that has a predictable effect that you choose to use is not a railroad.

    I think what's happening is the emotion one feels when being railroaded may be similar to some of the emotions they feel when using a tool set with overly predictable results. That doesn't make it railroading. There may be overlapping feelings but that doesn't make it the same thing.

    It's tempting to use terms like railroading because of the baggage / impact / attention they bring to a conversation. It happens in RPG conversations all the time. "That's a railroad" starts taking the place of "something I don't like." This often happens unintentionally and isn't malicious at all. But its use can distract from having a productive conversation. Instead, let's be specific.

    \n John, great comments about "railroading". I'm in total agreement!

    However, there is another side to that whole issue, and that is how players react to the game. I feel that the more predictable a game's outcome, the easier or more likely it is for the players to go into a scene (or entire game!) with a pre-conceived outcome. When you do that, the whole game suffers: you're no longer open to other possibilities.

    When it's openly communicated, and everyone's on the same page, it can be quite functional, I'm sure.

    But when it's not entirely in the open, it can cause dysfunction at the gaming table.

    Not just GMs do this: I once had a player in a pretty "trad" game who came to the table with a desired ending point for his character. He hoped to confront his character's son and then do something else (I don't know the details: he never told me, or any of the other players what he was planning).

    It made the game much more difficult because he wouldn't engage with anything in play that didn't seem to be heading in that direction. It's not that he wasn't interested: he was just really looking forward to this moment he'd imagined (but not communicated to us), so he wouldn't want to focus on scenes or characters or issues that weren't taking the story in that direction. Once I realized what was going on and we talked, he was perfectly happy to engage with the very same elements he'd previously ignored: he was interested in them, but was just trying to postpone dealing with them because he had this whole story arc planned out.

    It's like when a GM blocks interesting developments from the players because she's anxious to get to her next planned scene, already. If you told her, "Hey, wouldn't it actually be interesting if we did see what would happen if the party requested an audience with the Goblin King?", she might respond, "Oh, yeah, that would be cool, I suppose! Let's try it." It just didn't cross her mind before, as she was so focused on this other planned thing.

    I would imagine that a game like D&D 4E (which I haven't played, so I could be totally wrong) might encourage this kind of thought process:

    "The encounters are balanced so that the PCs [almost?] always win. So, I'm planning this fight. When they succeed, we'll move on to scene G4."

    I know this doesn't have to be dysfunctional, it can be workable and fun too. But I think certain rules can make it easier to slip in that direction without even realizing it.

    For example, given a system which gives players more and more "bennies/bonuses" as they go along, and a scenario about some big villain, it's quite natural to start considering, "What will happen once the heroes have defeated the villain?" This is just as true for players as for GMs. But I think that when you come to the table interested in some plan which will take effect once the villain is defeated, you could be ignoring or shutting down other possible directions for play, and, worse yet, you might be bored with the actual drama of the struggle with the villain. Because you feel like you can already look ahead to something else.

    I don't know if this makes sense. But I'm fairly convinced that some kind of phenomenon along these lines exists. That's what I was trying to say earlier.
