    \n Ralph, I think you're missing the windmills at which folks are tilting.

    * Amazon could have done it more above-board. Doing it as a hidden process makes people feel vulnerable ("What ELSE can they do without me knowing?"). Sure it may be the right thing for Amazon to do (more to that below), but is it equally "right" to attempt to hide the fact by not informing folks? Suppose my purchase information is out of date, because I used a debit card that I've subsequently lost? (I imagine they figure that out and try to email folks, to be sure they get refunds. But still...)

    * On a related note, if Amazon missed that for 1984 and Animal Farm (not exactly "unknown" works), then how many other pirate downloads are lurking out there? Is this the first of many "clean ups"? Am I, as a publisher, now beholden to check Amazon regularly for an illegal sale, or do I rely on them to inform me? Or would they even inform me, or just kill the pirate downloads and do refunds? (Maybe I'd LIKE to know that umtpy-fifty folks DLed a pirate copy, so i could pursue a legal sale with them?)

    * Which reminds me... as of press time, the two books in question were not released by the actual copyright holder in e-reader format. So if I, say, HAD to finish it by an afternoon class today, and I woke up to find it's totally gone... well, let's hope the bookstore is open and stocked or I am well and truly screwed.

    * And, thus, it's nothing like what would happen for you with Universalis, because you're going to release Universalis on Amazon in their AZW format, right? Right?

    * Oh, uh, not-so-finally, there's a reasonable argument that these books should be in the public domain: 1984 is published in 1949, Orwell dies in 1950, it's been 59 years since that date, and copyright law (used to?) protect for "author's life plus fifty years" according to the Berne Convention. Ah, wait, "50 years minimum," so I suppose whoever holds his estate ATM has more than 59 years on the books.

    Which is why stacks and stacks of them sell every year around Back To School time, instead of Guternberg.org having it for free download to nearly any device.

    So anyhow, I don't think it's an "uproar" for the average joe who probably forgot that he had it on his Kindle (and, thus, I reassert that it's DUMB for the estate holders to pull them rather than just offer their version and let Amazon do transfer. Publicly, not hidden, so folks could take the transfer OR get their money back--win, win, win, win). There *is* a bit of uproar for folks who appreciate transparency when their "purchased" data is tampered with, even for the "best" of reasons (protecting a 59 year old IP that's considered Standard Reading in our democracy and many others).
    -----
    It's probably another thread, but I can get a netbook that SMOKES the Kindle for $300, including having a better keyboard and other input devices, larger and higher resolution screen, and FAR more functionality. And my choice of OS. And freedom from being silently reconfigured anytime I'm connected online. All a Kindle wins on is weight and battery life, which I will readily concede is an issue for some folks doing a lot of digital reading (just not for me--I tend to hold my laptop like a large graphic novel, when reading... and I could use the exercise, anyway).

    Glad you dig it, Ralph. I'm sure it's handy for some folks, especially folks who already have netbooks or laptops or for folks who JUST want a reader and want it to work without any need (or, truly, any ability) to configure it. But I'd be looking for it to cost nearly nothing, if I were going to buy just to then only be able to buy content (though, if it surfs the web well enough, that's a non-issue; especially if I can go to Gutenberg or my Googel Docs or favorite blogs).

    But, again, "Is the Kindle worth the money" isn't the subtext of how I (tongue-in-cheek--recognize the allusion to DitV?) title this thread. It's more the entire digital distribution model established by Amazon, and their lack of transparency, and their attempt (it seems) to try to sell devices in an age when services are soon to be king (AKA Web 3.0). I wouldn't try to start a "Kindle-only" distribution model, these days, for instance....

    While I don't like what Amazon / Kindle are doing, I would at least like to point out that the Kindle (and other superior e-book readers that use the same technology) isn't an underpowered netbook. It's designed for a completely different reason and the two devices shouldn't be compared. If you try and use it as a netbook of course you're going to be disappointed. E-books are for reading, plain and simple. Some do other things too (mine can be used as a notepad) but their principle purpose is to read. For this purpose, a netbook is not so well suited. Reading on a backlit screen is uncomfortable, causes eye strain, and is difficult to read in bright daylight. A modern e-book uses e-ink technology in its display. While this only allows for greyscale images to be displayed, the screen does not need to be lit and is viewable from any angle, providing a paper-like display. This doesn't hurt the eyes to read from, even at great length. Also the batteries last much, much longer because the display only draws power when the page is turned.

    Anyway, I don't have a Kindle, thank goodness. I have an iRex iLiad. Even more expensive, but a much larger screen and a lot of extras including reading PDFs properly, not some cut-down proprietary poor man's equivalent Not that I'm a tech-snob or anything.

    Posted By: ValamirAmazon screwed up by offering for sale a digitial copy of a book without ensuring that the seller of the book actually had the rights to sell it. When that error was pointed out to them they 100% rectified their mistake by a) recovering all of the copies that had been sold and shouldn't have been, and b) refunding all of the money.
    \n
    io9's article says:Amazon's action flies in the face of what people expect when they purchase a book. Under the "right of first sale" in the U.S., people can do whatever they like with a book after purchasing it, including giving it to a friend or reselling it. There is no option for a bookseller to take that book back once it's sold.
    \nThis, by the way, is what the uproar is about. Those people who bought 1984 for their Kindle were not engaged in piracy: they purchased what they assumed was a legitimate copy from Amazon. Amazon in turn was selling what it assumed was a legitimate copy from MobileReference. When MobileReference turned out to be pirates, Amazon's duty was to stop selling that version of 1984, MobileReference's duty was to stop pirating the book, and the duty of the actual holders of the copyright was to pursue whatever legal course they wanted to against MobileReference and/or Amazon.

    But the people who bought the book? They aren't pirates. They aren't running off copies of their MobileReference 1984 and passing them out to other people (if that's even possible to do with a Kindle), they're just people who bought a book in good faith. And again, if it were a pirate edition of a paperback, Amazon wouldn't be entitled to take those copies back from those good-faith buyers. They could ask the buyers to return the books for a refund, I suppose, but they wouldn't be able to demand it. They certainly wouldn't be able to sneak into people's libraries and steal the book back, even if they left the owners a refund.

    Then again, these aren't paperbacks. Hell if I know whether they're allowed to delete them remotely or not. Given the way the DMCA was set up, they probably are allowed to, and everything everyone ever does on a Kindle is actually illegal and punishable by huge fines anyway.


    I think the Kindle is all kinds of awesome, myself, but this incident does drive home an important point: if you want the security of genuinely owning something that cannot be taken away from you at any moment for reasons you probably aren't even entitled to know, you need to have it in hardcopy. Electronic versions can be taken away from you at any time, and you won't be able to do anything about it besides stomp your feet and complain. But man, when the laws applying to print were written, citizens actually had rights, and somehow those rights haven't been taken away yet. If it's important to you to be able to read a particular book whenever you want to, buy it in paperback, too.

    \nAnd its a trend that makes complete sense really. Back in the industrial age we valued things. Tangible things you can hold in your hand. We made things and we sold things and we owned things.
    \n
    \nBooks were a thing.
    \n
    \nToday...we don't make things. We offer services, and we create Intellectual Property. Things are old fashioned. Nobody cares about Things. We care about ideas and activities.
    \n
    \nWhen you pay for a service you get to use that service...for as long as you pay for it. When you stop paying you stop getting access to the service. When you pay to have your hair cut you pay for that one instance of getting your hair cut. You don't pay for the right to have your hair perpetually short. If you stop paying your accountant to do your taxes the accountant stops doing your taxes.
    \n
    \nPaying for services is a fundamentally different thing than paying for things. When you pay for a thing you expect to own it forever. When you pay for a service you own it for the duration of that service. When that service includes IP you have a whole different hook. The service you are paying for is no longer an activity like cutting your hair or doing your taxes. The service you are paying for is access to the IP.
    \n
    \nThis has been the direction the western world has been moving in since we transitioned to a service based economy in the 70s and 80s. The web / internet has simply created the technology that makes it possible to turn access into a controllable service.
    \n
    \nEVERYTHING has been moving in this direction for some time now. Businesses are all motivated to figure out a way to turn selling you a product into selling you a service.
    \n
    \nWhen you pay for your car in cash you've bought a thing. When you finance your car you've bought a service. Same with your house. They might pretend that you "own" your house. But if you have a mortgage all you really own is the right to have your debt payments serviced for you. And if you think that paying off your mortgage means you actually own your house...try not paying your property tax and see what you really own. What you own, is the right to live in your house...as long as you pay your taxes...and how is that different from a subscription fee or membership dues, really?
    \n
    \nAnd if you belong to a home owners association, or a condo association then you have restrictions on you. And even if you don't you probably live in an area with Zoning laws and municipal Code Enforcement workers who can tresspass on your property and fine you if they don't like how long your grass is or how much your paint is peeling. EULA's are nothing more than businesses attempting to make everything like that.
    \n
    \nSo yeah, this is the natural extension of moving from a thing based manufacturing society to an idea based service society.
    \n
    \nIn a thing society what you are buying is a book. A physical object.
    \n
    \nIn an idea society what you are buying is access to the author's words.
    \n
    \nIn a thing society when you give a book to someone else nothing is lost. Before there was 1 thing, now there is 1 thing.
    \n
    \nIn an idea society when you give access to the author's words to someone else, something is lost. Somebody got access to those words for free.
    \n
    \nThe logical culmination of living in an idea society is that letting someone else read your book equals stealing access to the author's words. You paid for the right to read those words...they didn't.
    \n
    \nThe logical culmination of living in an idea society is that all access to things will be converted into services.
    \n
    \nYou will not have a car. You will have access to a service which provides transportation.
    \n
    \nYou will not have a house. You will have access to a service which provides living space.
    \n
    \nYou will not have a pantry full of food. You will have access to a service which provides meals
    \n
    \nYou will not have a book. You will have access to a service that lets you read words.
    \n

So copyright law is, ultimately, a law about POSSESSION, not about purchase or sale, in this parallel with pot. (And, for at a time, in Oregon, I could have had my Ugandan pot with me because possession wasn't illegal, just sale and purchase--the "Miracle Ounce" defense that was so popular there at the time: "I found it, your Honor.")

    Since this thread started, there's more uproar, too. And I really, REALLY don't think the issue comes down to not knowing the EULA, teh good v. service paradigm shift, or even copyright violation and who's responsible (the middle man, the pirate) and who should pay (the ignorant consumers via refunds, the pirates via law suit). I think the issue is, simply, OMFG, they can DO that?! But, but... where does it all END?!? *begin picking through the EULA with a fine tooth comb; dismiss Amazon as a worthwhile source and the Kindle as a attractive device*

    And... hehe. I mean, it's fucking 1984! Does ANYONE in Amazon's legal department have a sense of irony? Does anyone in their marketing department understand the notions of public perception and trust (and, more so, how they are central themes of the novel)?

    (And, yeah, for me it's a bit of a "fuck you" to the estate holders, for owning a property--no, wait, TWO properties--that are required reading in most middle schools in America. Apparently, anything written by an author who died later than 1938 is, de facto, a financial drain on either parents or the education system, if it is to be a subject of study. Or maybe they get away with non-royalty printing of them under Fair Use? I'm not clear on that, akchully....)
