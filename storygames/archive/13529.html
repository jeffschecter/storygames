\n
\n
\n
\n\nSuna \n\n\n

\n
\n Lately I've been working like mad on my current project, Beyond the Mirror (you can find mention of it in the Praxis forums, here). Refining some structures within the game, simplifying whenever I found something superfluous during the recent playtests, etc.

However, sometimes I do have the feeling of being a tiny ant standing on the shoulders of giants. Whatever solution I try to come up with, like, say, determining narration authorities in my game, feels like something already seen, so even if the main crunch structure has a certain novelty, it's thanks to what lies in the past that I may be creating what i'm creating right now. Had there not been Polaris, Beyond the Mirror would probably not even be a masterless game.
How do you cope with that? Do you ever feel like your game lacks of originality even when playtesters give you enthusiastic feedback?

    \n Think of all those writers who realized that the plot of their novel is the same as the plot from some work of Shakespear, an episode of Gilmore Girls, and the comercial for Lucky Charms. Worry less about your originality than making the game you want to make. If it isn't a word for word copy of Dogs in the Vinyard, Vincent has no real grounds to be unhappy. Your game will be yours no matter its influences and if those influences don't work together it will show in playtesting and you will fix it.

    Be just and fear not; do not let your feelings about this be a reason to stop creating.

    \n I know exactly how you feel. My GameChef entry, which I'm still working on, had vast tracts of territory which were inspired almost whole cloth from How We Came To Live Here, which I had recently played when I made the game. It felt odd, knowing that, seeing that, even though at the time of design, I hadn't been thinking about it; it was only in retrospect that I saw those similarities. Brennan Taylor saw them, too, but ultimately, he was kind enough to say that he liked the game, thought I should keep working on it, and perhaps most importantly, that I shouldn't be trying to reinvent the wheel. If I recall correctly, he likened it to computer code: you don't redesign a whole new code every single time you want to write a program, because it's okay to use the code that came before. It's there, it's a useful tool, and it's a waste of your time and energy not to use it if it's appropriate. That was a big help to me.

    I think I mostly get past this by not realizing what I'm doing when I'm designing, only seeing it on retrospect, and then telling myself that it's okay to have built on the tremendous work of others.

    (Which is why I will be publishing, on Thor's suggestion, a new game called "Cogs in the Junkyard," about robots who enforce the will of the Factory, the divine source of all true life in the Junkyard. They wander around from wreck to wreck, ensuring that no sinful breaches of programming take place.)

    (Goddammit, I meant that as a joke, and now I actually kind of like it.)

    \n It was Shock's list of influences that first delivered my epiphany on this subject. I mean, sure, Ron always touted Sorcerer as a descendant of Over the Edge, but it was easy for me to dismiss that, like "sure, I can see the similarity but OtE is so primitive and Sorcerer is so brilliant. Whereas Shock: has a really focused bibliography that celebrates its influences in detail, and contained a lot of designs that I respect, so I was open to the insight.

    The phrase that did it for me is, "In many ways, Shock: is a purpose-build modification of PTA."

    When I read that, something clicked and I went "oh my God it SO IS!" And my world was never the same.

    I realized that designers are "stealing" from each other all the time, and it's more about the particular arrangement of parts than about inventing all your own parts, which is impossible anyway. This helped me to break into design myself.

    When I entered game Chef 2008, I ended up building my game Spectre of the Beast (still in development, now called "Mortar of Utopia") largely on the chassis of Shock:. In many ways it's a similar game--you play Protagonists in an invented world (though a past one, rather than future) whose pursuit of their goals have a profound effect on society, and play revolves around the attendant issues. So I took Shock's Protagoniost/Antagonist roles, series of scenes each with a conflict, and based Ambitions roughly on Story goals. I added a Hope mechanic somewhat like that in Contenders (which also apportions protagonism and antagonism similar to Shock:). But on top of that chassis I arranged pieces in such a way that they would work a certain way in play, which is different than the way they work in Shock:.

    Joshua's approach to presenting his influences gave me an implicit permission to do that. To look at previous ingenious applications of tools and suss out my very own application, similar yet distinct, without feeling that I had to start from scratch. Now, I do still get the feeling sometimes that "all the ground has been covered," and certainly as more and more niches get filled it will be trickier and trickier to find a distinctive statement to make. I believe it's valuable to ask, for any design idea (or any art, really), "could this thing be done well with existing works?" Or put another way, "What do I want to say with this design, distinct from what others are saying?"

    Notice I say "distinct" instead of "new." The key isn't to find what hasn't been done, the key is to find your own voice. that voice will be in chorus with a hundred others, and will owe its inspiration to thousands more. But if you focus on finding that, on saying the thing that's welling up in you and DEMANDING that it be said, you'll do well.

    Peace,
    -Joel

    \n Having dealt with design problems for which there was no available prior work, my advice is do what best suits your design goals - don't hamper yourself by either the need to innovate or the need to borrow. Novel ideas tend to be shaky and you'll need to run them through their paces. At the same time, just because something worked for a different game, doesn't mean it'll translate to yours.

    That being said, deep innovation likely undermines a game's potential for popularity and frequent play - so if that is part of your goals then you should be happy about the resemblance to familiar games.

    everything is a reaction to something else.

    our ideas are the reactions of long and short term memory with current experience.

    someone with a background in board games is going to play prime time adventures and bounce off of it at a completely diffident angle then someone who has a background in rpgs or video games

    originality is more or less how far your reaction bounces from the source.

    i think it's a very nice complement include your direct and sources in your product, but really i think something like Bach's "Soli Deo Gloria" covers all your bases. in other words fighting your ego and sense of ownership helps.

    \n Not to talk about art again...

    I used to hate Picasso. Like, hate. I thought his paintings were ugly, the color palettes jarring, his brushstrokes a pompous statement about how he was so talented he didn't have to try like the rest of us to properly use paint.

    My junior year in art school I took an illustration class. Already far into my education, I had a good handle on the basics, and so I was kinda insulted when our teacher asked us to do what we did in jr high art class "copy Guernica". Really? I was soooo beyond that. I understood abstract art, what it was about, and I hated Picasso. Begrudgingly, I started the project.

    And... I couldn't do it. It was fucking hard to copy. My rendition was like a 6 year old took some crayons and macaroni and tried to create a masterpiece.

    That's when I started liking Picasso.

    All this to say... it takes a lot of work to be a master. But that doesn't mean you can't work at getting there. I know that I'll be working on being as good as Picasso for the rest of my life (and really, that's not a goal I would like to achieve... what makes me happy about creating is not the same thing that made Picasso happy about creating), but I really enjoy putting myself to the challenge. Have confidence in what you create, learn from what came before you, and know that, while you're not Picasso, you're making something beautiful and unique because it came from you.

    You get better at designing things by going through the process of designing them. Keep at it!

    \n After reading the thread, I think here's an angle that's been hinted at but not directly stated:

    I don't worry about stealing, I celebrate it. That's the point. Doing anything even vaguely creative often means you're part of a creative community, and that includes building on the work of others in that community. Imitation is the sincerest form of flattery, etc. Creation done right is a community process, and the myth of the lonely creator is just that--mythical, and potentially harmful as well. Renaissance Florence and the explosion of genius? Try Renaissance Florence and a tightly-knit system of apprenticeship and training. With few exceptions, great creators are great because they interact with other creators, and stealing is part and parcel with that creative community.

    Of course there are lonely aspects of creation, too, hard work that has to be done on one's own, and that unique individual viewpoint will make the work distinct, maybe even innovative. I don't think I can put it better than it already was:\n
    Posted By: anansigirlHave confidence in what you create, learn from what came before you, and know that, while you're not Picasso, you're making something beautiful and unique because it came from you.
    \nFurthermore, most innovation is incremental rather than in leaps and bounds. You take something someone else made and do something different with it, maybe improve it a little. Once in a while you have a quantum leap like say, Dogs in the Vineyard, Primetime Adventures, or Polaris, but even those giant leaps sprang from the accumulated little steps taken as a community while everyone used other people's works and ideas. Much like Cam's story, these cobbled-together Frankenstein's monsters will most likely be cannibalized for someone else's monster, and maybe lead to better, stronger monsters. It will be the designer's hard work that makes it possible, of course, but it's the community that makes such innovations happen faster, makes them better, and makes the process more social and fun than any creative hermitage could ever be.
