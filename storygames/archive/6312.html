    \n Red Box only had 3 levels for characters in it, so technically BFRPG or LL don't emulate the game exactly, because both BFRPG and LL have information that would have been from Blue Box and whatever colors followed those. You could buy a copy of the Rules Cyclopedia, which includes all the rules from Red Box, Blue Box, and the two that followed. It's $4 from paizo. If you're into D&D, then it's worth tracking down a hardback copy.

    Red Box was expanded upon by the many text and adventure modules that came out to support the game, plus the boxes that followed. I suspect that these texts are what LL is referring to in the intro. I'm not sure you'll find simplicity or clarity in Red Box. I seem to remember a bit of confusion over a good part of the game. An example is alignments. The characters were suppose to act according to their alignment or else the DM would assign a penalty. There weren't really guidelines about the penalties. Some of the spells were broken -- we hardly played with magic-users, but I remember a few cases where we were left wondering, what the heck? You'll find wonderful advice, like the DM is god in how the rules are used, thrown away or followed.

    \n Both versions have some substantial differences from Blue Book Basic from Holmes (no relation), however.     \nWell... one of the points I've been trying to make is that, in fact, Red Box is actually a rather late version of D&D. I recall it's appearance, and thinking, "That's odd... now that we have AD&D, why do we need another version of Basic?" Even if it is much more nicely edited.
    \n
    \nSo as for the analogy, Red Box is more like GURPS Lite. http://www.sjgames.com/gurps/Lite/
    \n
    \nWhat's fascinating is how people talk about making RPGs that are more approachable. This is what the Red Box is along with the associated series (Expert, Master, Immortal, etc). An attempt to cut D&D up into more easily absorbed chunks so that it's less daunting than AD&D. (Without commenting on the politics that were behind each edition, this is the reasoning for the presentation).
