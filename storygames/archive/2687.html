\n From Kitten's First Full Moon
\n
\n
It was Kitten's first full moon.
\nWhen she saw it, she thought,
\nThere's a little bowl of milk in the sky.
\nAnd she wanted it.
\n
\n
\nI think that characters are the most fun when they have goals that they cannot even begin to directly accomplish. I also think that characters are the most fun when they are making progress and having an impact on the world.
\n
\nI've been thinking that the right way to do this is to give people two goals: The crazy, unattainable, true goal and the workable, challenging sane goal that they think (mistakenly!) will lead directly to their true goal.
\n
\nSo, in Kitten's First Full Moon, Kitten has a true goal of "Get the bowl of milk in the sky" and a sane goal of "Run around like a freakin' little maniac, trying to stretch, jump or climb as high as you can." What makes it a wonderful story is both that she makes such spectacular successes at her sane goals and that her true goal remains forever beyond her reach. "Ah, but a man's reach should exceed his grasp, or what's a heaven for?"
\n
\nIn Firefly, Malcolm Reynolds has a true goal of "Restore freedom to the universe" and a sane goal of "Keep on flying, no matter what." Again, part of what makes it a wonderful story is both that he has such spectacular successes at his sane goal and that his true goal remains forever beyond his reach.
\n
\nThis gap is both what keeps a character driving and what leaves a place in the story for acts of grace and faith by other people: Kitten returns, dejected, to her home to find that someone has set a big bowl of milk out, just for her. Lucky Kitten!
\n
\nHow can you sell this idea to players, so that they are as excited about creating a wonderfully unattainable True Goal as they are about planning out the steps for their Sane Goal?

    \n One way to sell it is with fantastic examples, two of which you've provided here. When I read about those characters, I go, yeah, I want to play someone like that.

    Another selling point is control. When I play Mal, and I know I ain't bringin' freedom to the universe, then I have a lot of authorial control when I set a scene that revolves around Mal trying to bring freedom to the universe. I know he's not gonna get it, so I can play to that angle with supreme confidence. The exact outcome of the scene will be in doubt, but since I know for sure that Mal's goal is too big and crazy, I'm free to explore the drama, comedy, or tragedy within it. It's a safe space I've carved out in the narrative for myself.

    PTA does this with Issues. Your issue isn't going away anytime soon (even after your spotlight, probably) so you can do this same kind of safe-space making around it.

    \n Hi! (I still feel myself newby here.)

    I think it is a very interesting, if not crucial point, what if the player doesn't want to resolve the Q-goal. I think this is important, because the player him/herself decided in the first place that this goal is not attainable. The player sais: "I have this and this as unattainable goal.", then once like lightning from the bright sky, comes the GM-fiat and tells the player: "No, it is attainable from now on. You only have to ask for it. No, you don't have to thank me. Anytime. Bye." If the player him/herself said, (s)he wanted it as an unattainable gole, why should it be resolved in-game? I think this is a fully metagame issue.

    If we look at these unattainable goals what are they for? They strengthen the character, make him/her able to something. The kitten to climb etc. I think that it would give a mechanical advantage is not only a sideeffect of a background, but it is the only purpose of it. (I think it is called motivation in the filmindustry.)

    What if it would not only give simple mechanical advantages, but the only role of it would be to give the player one mighty advantage that cannot be measured? For example my character has "I want to fully eliminate the crime in Gotham." What does that mean? This Q-goal gives my character (Batman) the ability to be the best crimefighter in freakin' Gotham. It's not only +3 dexterity and such. How should this work mechanically? I think that the Q-goal should give the player GM-powers. The player should get permission to overrule the other players on the field of the Q-goal. For example if my character is Batman, and I am investigating the footprints, I can tell the other players instead of the GM what those mean. Maybe it is a bit too awkward example, but something like this.

    And in this case noone would ever consider to resolve the Q-goals except if the player is bored and would like to have another instead of the old one.

    \nI'm gonna lobby for a distinction here. In story theory, there's the Goal of the story, then there's what has to be done in order to reach the goal. I would argue that one of the steps Kitten has to take towards succeeding or failing in his goal to get the moon is run around try to jump or stretch to it. We also have to admit that the outcome of a goal can be either success or failure -- both put resolution on the aspiration.
    \n
    \nThe distinction between Goal and Requirement is useful for RPG play because the Goal is what the character aims for, but the Requirement is what he or she spends most time doing. In PTA, the player is arbiter of when the character has done enough of the Requireiment to achieve his Goal -- this is the moment when the player changes the character's Issue.
    \n
    \nSorcerer is interesting because, instead of focusing on the goal, the Kicker mechanic generally focuses on the Consequence -- what will happen if the character doesn't do something. Sorcerer allows the group to decide when enough Requirements have been followed for the Consequence to be squeleched or guaranteed.

    \n Hi Tony,
    \n
    \nWell, "Requirement" is a term from a theory of story I like, so it assumes a pre-determined goal. That part doesn't apply to story games, as the goal is often determined only through play.
    \n
    \nHowever, I think it suggests a useful distinctions between the goal and the efforts made to reach it.
    \n
    \nWhat you say about not necessarily "doing" happens to fall right in line with this theory. It posits four realms of conflict in a story: external situation, external activity, psychological landscape, and psychological activity. The theory says that for a story to feel complete and satisfying it needs to explore all four of these somehow.
    \n
    \nIn a typical Firefly episode--the Train Job--everybody is involved in the external activity of acquiring the drugs on the train. The requirement for resolving that would be understanding (by acquiring external information) who deserves the drug. Meanwhile, Mal has a personal external situation -- he's captain, responsible for the mess he's gotten his people into and for his contract with Mr. Niska. This is only a one hour episode so it doesn't get to fully develop the story, but it does hint at the other two realms -- Crow is fixed on pursuing Mal (psychological landscape) and Mal and Crow try to persuade/intimidate each other (psychological activity).
