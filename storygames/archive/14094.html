Perspective: Roleplaying is simply a tool for portraying fiction

\n I was thinking about how you can turn any situation with characters into roleplaying. If I'm playing Sims Online and an interesting situation occurs between my character and another person's character, that's a story. It doesn't have to be recorded anywhere, the story of the events that transcribed simply exists as a narrative in my mind and the other person's mind*. And, I may or may not apply the lens of fiction to this story. For example, I might think the story is about "Jake trips over a baby toy and Melinda laughs at him". Or, I might think "I was playing Sims the other night and I made a character Jake. I had Jake trip over a toy, and then Megan made her character laugh at Jake". There are of course more examples. I might describe it in first person terms, thinking about the character as myself. I can leave these thoughts to myself as a narrative that exists in my memory. Whether or not I acknowledge any significance about the fact that the events are a narrative doesn't really matter at this point.

But if I do imagine the story as fiction, I can take it a step further and portray such a narrative to others. In an online game environment, I might use text chat to share pieces of a fictional story I imagine; drawn from what I perceive. Again, the perspective I use for this portrayal probably isn't too significant. I could chat as if I'm Jake speaking to Melinda, or write as a narrator with a God's eye view. Personally, I think the environment (online game) is less important than the medium (written text). In a table-top environment, I would use speech to verbally portray my pieces of the story**. I guess it's interesting to note how online roleplaying lends itself to literary techniques (writing) while tabletop/PnP roleplaying lends itself to acting and storytelling techniques (verbal, somantic).

So what's really interesting to me is finding out where this line is. Assuming the above process, when have you crossed from "definitely not roleplaying" to "definitely roleplaying"? Does it require deliberation to roleplay? Is a minimum amount of skill required in the portrayal? Does roleplaying require a back and forth between multiple people, or can one person "roleplay to" another, pure spectator person? Can a single person roleplay by herself?

I would be interested to hear what other people think about this particular view of roleplaying: it's a tool for creating fiction.

-- John M.

* -- I'm not going take the tangent to discuss the phenomenon of a shared story taking place in multiple peoples' minds.
** -- Hm, ironic that it was dubbed "Pen and Paper" while the roleplaying itself is verbal

    \n You can role-play over anything. Scrabble, Monopoly can be role-playing games. As long as some fiction is created in the exchange, some story results.

    \n One way of defining story (not the only way, but it's one) is that you start with a conclusion - preferably an interesting one. From there, you trace back to the most salient causes that lead to that conclusion. You frame those causes into a narrative, which you present within a medium of expression (literature, audio, motion picture, etc.). And, once you have done so, you have a story.

    From that perspective, stories happen any time that something interesting happens and someone takes the time to trace back what lead to that interesting thing happening.

    In your Sims Online example, you could start with the interesting thing being "Jake felt embarrassed". From there you trace back to the causes - Jake tripped. Melinda laughed. Those things resulted in Jake feeling embarrassed. And bam, you have a little story.

    Looked at that way, the act of roleplaying is the act of participating in a shared environment that is designed to to lead to interesting events with traceable lines of causation -- to create a space in which stories are likely to happen.

    There are lots of possibilities for how to go about creating that space. The only one that is strongly implied in the name is the idea of taking on a role - acting as some sort of fictional character (even if that fictional character is only barely differentiated from the "nonfictional self" -- such as a portrayal of the self in an alternate reality). Other than that, there are tons of variables.

    So, yes, chatting in-character while playing Sims Online certainly falls well under the umbrella of the act of roleplaying. Lots of things do.

    Posted By: OrlyPerspective:RoleplayingLife is simply a tool for portraying fiction
    \nAfter thinking about this statement, I believe I disagree. I can see a distinction between experiencing a story and portraying one. For example: I go to the market and purchase some Dr. Pepper. Later I think about the fact that I went to the market and grabbed some Dr. Pepper. I realize that's a story. If I do nothing with that, then I've "lived" the story, or you might say "experienced" it, or maybe you only say that I was "aware" of it. However, I could also decide to portray that story in some fashion. Lets' say that I tell someone about my adventure to the market. Now I have not only experienced the story, but I've also portrayed it.

    So, I don't think life is a tool for portraying stories. I think it's a tool for living them, but you need to make the decision yourself to use other tools for the portrayal. Books, films, plays, roleplaying games would be examples of those tools. You are probably winking as I miss your point, but I think it's useful to draw the distinction. Life is full of story potential, but it's quite possible for it to be untapped potential.
