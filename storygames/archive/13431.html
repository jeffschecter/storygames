    \nI think this is the stuff that's as-yet less explored. Backstory has been explored a bit with flash-back mechanics, but secrets, campaigns, and epic GM plots are all pretty ripe for digging.

    I think secrets could be used to push further into PCs-as-enemies/frenemies territory.
    The whole campaign thing, actually, was kind of covered in Sorcerer&Sword by the concept of Fate - but a cool way to expand on S&S in this regard might be to establish up-front what the ultimate fate of the PCs will be, and the point of play is to see how they get there.

    It might be kinda neat to mess with the Epic Plot stuff by experimenting with giving players many, many characters to portray - in each thread of the overall story, maybe each player has a major character? Nestling multiple BIG plot-lines together into something that's ridiculously big and complex could be really cool, although admittedly it could be one hell of an undertaking.
    But to have something *really* be epic, it needs a Lazy Susan-style model for protagonism, rather than just one or a few main characters all assembled in a party. A Song of Ice and Fire comes to mind, but I suppose things went the way they did because the Stark family got scattered across the realm.

    Maybe the old maxim of "splitting the party" could become a feature, not a problem, by turning over NPCs to the players to make into their secondary protagonists, when it makes sense.
    \nAlso, Levi,
    I think what you're describing is also wielded effectively by Apocalypse World - "acting under fire" is open to interpretation, for instance, despite its battle-ready implications.
    This is actually a game element that hugely contrasted 0e and later editions of D&D - to me, 3rd edition spells are nothing *but* combat tools, while OSR games tend to have somewhat florid, open-ended descriptions of effects, the kind of thing you could build a truly awesome scheme around to really impress the GM.
    Overall, I think there's a balancing act to such descriptions, and it can be crucial to decide what constitutes conflict, what constitutes actual fighting, and when you can skirt around the procedures. For instance, if being damaged by a sentient/living/self-ambulatory entity initiates combat between the damager and the damagee, then knowing that "Telekinesis can move objects as fast as their falling speed" (and falling does damage) means that, while a pit trap may not initiate combat between the trap and its victim, using my telekinesis to slam you against a wall will most certainly start a fight.
    That could be a neat experiment - find "loopholes" that allow you to apply kewl powerz to fightin', but don't make any special abilities (bonus points - any at all) have textually explicit combat applications.
    Haste also gives you more Dexterity, which in turn affects combat initiative.
    Goblin Mask gives a huge bonus to intimidation in certain conditions (such as surprise in combat).
    I shall meditate on this - it's kind of like a deeper version of designing a game specifically to be hack-able, only now there are "tricks" to figure out, applications, as it were, for them system-mastery fans. Cool!
    [edit: major bonus points awarded for designing such a game in which combat is actually quite dangerous. Or is that a weird multi-(dis)incentive? At least you're not short on options if you're creative!]

    Troy,
    I think a lot of story games that are "tight" (i.e. the premise is super-focused) can provide a lot of fodder also. Consider something like My Life With Master, or Polaris (both fairly constrained), on down to something like Before We Sink (my god, that's specific!).
    There were some neat experimental games back in the day that focused on this kind of thing - bonus points to anybody who can remember the game where you were an awkward white-collar office guy locked in a fallout shelter with the girl of his dreams, and the goal is to fumble and fail to get her to go out with you in your last few hours of existence.
    Vincent made an interesting observation about narration-rights once, when discussing Otherkind - he mentioned that, when people were excited about an in-game situation, everyone was too excited not to contribute! But, well, if your play procedures are really tight and deliberate (as opposed to just using narration-rights as a carrot), you might need to shoot them down, or at least insist that the Peanut Gallery funnel all suggestions through the appropriate contributor(s).

    What Zac and Levi are saying above is very close to what I was thinking about in my "imaginary space" thread (but never finished thinking about). OD&D is fundamentally two things: a combat mechanic and an elaborate play pretend exercise.

    The people who "got" the play pretend exercise were all about getting the treasure in clever wasy, with out-of-the box thinking and manipulation of the imaginary world (through the judgement of the GM/group) and avoiding combat (which was quite merciless). The people that were displeased with the combat (which is actually almost the only thing there were hard rulesfor) wanted more balanced combat, more tactical fiddly bits, more resources, more combos, more complexity, more "fairness" etc. becaue, hey, this game has rules for combat, what else are we supposed to do...this branch of evolution ultimately brings us to 4E.

    Immersion is an iffy word. I'm extremely wary of it. Every person means something else by it. It's hard to talk about it.

    Hit points...I don't really know what their specific merit is. They're a pacing mechanic, an abstraction. You can use them for anything. They're a tool.

    Classes are making a comeback. I was pretty sure they were dead a few years ago, but obviously not. They're just getting redefined. Apocalypse World has a very traditional version of them, but some version of "templates" or "careers" never realy went away. Archetypes are useful. IT gives the player an immediate sense what their character is about or what they're capable of or what their role is. In more blunt "tv show"-type game the classes could be something like "the protagonist", "the love interest", "the sidekick" or whatever.

    Modules we've got in some forms. Dogs towns, Storming villages, Lady Hackbird setups, Burning Wheel "adventure" situations (like The Sword). I bet ten different groups played The Sword ten different ways. I think we probably need more stuff like this. The problem is these situations are usually tailored to specific players and characters, are in fact built around characters, so pregens are sometimes a must.

    Campaigns...this is something that I'm wondering about. What constitutes a "campaign"?

    \n For some versions of "old school" you might consider these to be features:

    1. Very quickly thrown-together instant characters, with little attachment from the player's side ("roll up a new character!"). In this type of old-school gaming, things like personality, etc, are emergent in play. You miss your first Dexterity roll and now you're known as "John the Clumsy", etc.

    2. On the other side of the coin, dealing with lovingly-crafted backstory. Especially for players who like to know everything about their character (e.g. "How would I know how my character would react to this situation if I don't know what kind of childhood he had?"). For a lot of people, this is what "immersion" is: knowing the character's psyche inside and out, as much as possible.

    I'd love to see more games that take advantage of either of these playstyles!

    In a Wicked Age... makes me think a little of the first category, especially when I watch GMs writing up NPCs for a chapter.

    \n Paul,

    In the closest game I've played to OSR, (b) was a major issue.

    We spent two whole sessions theorizing about how we might open a dwarven puzzle door, because there was nothing else to do except bail on the entire dungeon and try another one. The players weren't willing to do that, despite the fact that the fun theorizing turned boring about an hour in. If there's some OSR-style game out there that tells the group what to do in that situation, I haven't seen it. Something like "GM, accompany tricky puzzles with some sort of time-pressure element" might be all that's required... I dunno...

    I guess what it comes down to is that, in challenge-based play, engineering good challenges is vitally important and not that easy. In an effort to calibrate difficulty optimally, a lot of games and GMs define challenges and solutions narrowly, and wind up ill-equipped to deal with impromptu whale-torching.

    So, if I'm GMing, and I've got this puzzle door prepped, and the whale's just supposed to be an obstacle that forces the PCs to deal with the puzzle door... but then the PCs deal with the whale instead... how do I respond?

    A lot of GMs, I think, will instinctively go, "That doesn't work. Now deal with the thing I prepped." It's not an attempt to be mean, it's an attempt to stay safe and keep things under control and predictable. The GM knows what will and won't work on the door. He has no idea about the whale, and it's very hard for him to arbitrate that impartially (as challenge-based play demands).

    I know you're familiar with my solutions to these issues in Delve, but Delve doesn't have these expectations about challenge. If obstacles are too easy or too vague in Delve, the players won't feel gypped out of a good test. Not so in some OSR games.

    \n Re: the a,b,c problem, impassable dwarven door and all that, I think there are two variant solutions here.

    One is the sandbox old school thing, which probably hasn't been codified anywhere but in the minds of good DMs. It is twofold:
    -good adventure design doesn't have bottlenecking: there is always more than one way into a place, dungeons should be non-linear, branching etc.
    -if you can't pass the door, then you don't pass the door. Who cares. Maybe you come back later when you're smarter or more powerful or get a clever idea, maybe you don't. The door is there, it doesn't care, the DM doesn't care. This is very much the Western Marches way of playing. The world is uncaring, alive, independent, "real".

    The second is functional "gamist" school, which dips into the first half of the old-school answer, but approaches it from a different angle. We're playing to win, so getting deeper into that dungeon is where the game is at. This also seems to me to be how Jason's group plays LotFP. There's this challenge and we're there to beat it. Doing other stuff is bullshit. The solution is to dictate to the DM/GM that they shouldn't plan for outcomes or solutions, only create challenges. It's about posing the question to the players: "What are you going to do about this, huh?" There's no puzzle with one (or even multiple) solutions, no pixelbitching or anything. Here's a threat, deal with it. If you do, congratulations, if you die, better luck next time, insert coin.
    There's also a degree of nebulous "good GMing" involved here, namely, making the judgement (often a game should have tools to help you with this) of what is dangerous enough or too dangerous or not dangerous enough for the party involved. That's why CR was invented. Storming helps here by not penalizing you for failing. You don't die, merely lose the fight, and you can come back later when you're stronger.

    Both of these aproaches are viable and they're not mutually exclusive I think. It's entirely possible to use both. It's kinda how I'm planning my Pathfinder game these days.

    \n A guy I play with longs for the meaning that comes from a long campaign. Both that the time allows people's brains to build connections between events, and that the shared past gives more meat for the role play itself. In conversation with him, I'm realizing that some of the New Skool techniques are trying to short-cut what time was able to give in those Old Skool games he's longing for.

    Rather than finding what bits players are interested in by playing with them over time, we ask them to put up flags of some type. Rather than mining a year of play history to be the back "conversation" for the most recent choice, we ask people to assume (and share) what they want to presume. Which makes sense, we aren't in high school or college any more, we probably have less time to wait for involvement to build up slowly.

    The two Old Skool techniques that I think we can't recreate though is time and inexperience. We can try to build understanding and technique to get to the action faster (story now), but we can't really recreate the "I've been playing _this_game_ for as long as I was able to play RPGs" feeling nor the wonder of the first time we ran into dinosaur riding goblins worshiping an alien computer from a crashed spaceship.

    Which isn't trying to be depressing. Avoiding the tedious parts and working on recovering our sense of wonder though collaborative create are great! I wonder whether time and first encounters were a bit part of old school though.
