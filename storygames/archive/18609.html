    \n Howdy, Drakon! I think we're in similar boats--I've only really become interested in pursuing more story-game solutions to my RPGing in the last year. This was partly interest, and partly because two years of running Pathfinder burnt me out on high-prep, heavily crunchy rules where I had to fight the system to get player agency. And I ran a low-prep Pathfinder game! ;-)

    As a bridge into story gaming (and one of the finest games ever written), I can't recommend Lady Blackbird enough. It's a graduate-level seminar on roleplaying games in 17 nifty pages! And immense fun to play and "run".

    I've only read Apocalypse World, but it's bloody brilliant and will very likely change the way you look at RPGing, GMing...oh, everything. Including the sky. You. Will. Want. To. Run. It.

    GUMSHOE games (Trail of Cthulhu, The Esoterrorists, Night's Black Agents and a bunch of others) make, IMHO, a nice bridge between older-style, crunchier games and more player-driven, lighter rules story gaming.

    If you want to read the ongoing saga of this old dog trying to learn new tricks, check out my Actual Play thread wherein I try to run the classic Masks of Nyarlathotep under Trail of Cthulhu, meanwhile taking a crowbar to major sections of the module: The Post-Modern Masks of Nyarlathotep.

    Fiasco is super-fun, and can be useful as a springboard to other systems--I used a Fiasco playset to kick off my Nyarlathotep campaign. If you want to gently introduce folks to RPGing, you'll not go wrong with Fiasco.

Earl misplaced a trunk full of gold and he drinks so much he couldn't remember what he did with it, Leroy his partner was really mad; Earl thought he misplaced the trunk in town so Leroy went to find it, meanwhile the sheriff found it and told the deputy to hide it, Leroy finds the sheriff and asks if he saw Earl lately sheriff says he hadn't so Leroy tells him about the chest and if he finds it he will cut Earl out of his share and give it to the sheriff. Meanwhile the deputy and Earl are old friends so the deputy goes to Earl and offers to give the chest back to Earl if he will split it with him and take him back east.

    then the tilt happened and it was all about settling scores, Earl wasn't even listening to the deputy anymore and set off to find Leroy. Leroy met the sheriff at boot hill who told him that he had the gold but he wanted a 70-30 split because he was going to cut his deputy out. Leroy got mad and told the sheriff he better watch his back and went in to town looking for the deputy. Earl meanwhile had found the gold buried at boothill and took the gold and rigged the chest with dynamite and was hiding away off with the plunger waiting for the first person to try and retrieve it. the sheriff got the drop on earl and they fought, Earl got the upper hand and tied the sheriff to the trunk(lol) but tripped on some ropes and had the plunger underneath him and couldn't move without setting it off. Leroy found the deputy and asked him if he knew where the gold was, that he knew the sheriff had him hide it. the deputy acted dumb so earl punched him grabbed his gun and locked him in a cell, and went to find the sheriff. the deputy got free grabbed a billyclub out of his desk and went after Leroy.     \n Darkon,
    InSpectres is a great Story Game that is ghostbusters themed. The fun of that is that the players get to describe whet happens if they roll well and the GM gets to describe what happens when they roll poorly. Every game is a mystery, but because the players have so much creative input, no one knows how the mystery will be solved until the mystery is solved.

If anyone is uncomfortable with the wild-west paladin theme, you can re-skin it as Jedi with the path to the dark side replacing the Sin Progression pretty easily and maintains the original integrity of the design.

    1. It has a defined end.
    2. Audience participation.

    4. Gameplay split up into scenes and acts.
    5. An experience system that directly rewards players chasing after their goals, dramatic roleplaying, and being badass.

    \n It depends on what you mean by preparation time.

    As for approaching the game for the first time as a GM, you have quite a bit to read: a rulebook of 450 pages and a setting book of 250 pages. But don't despair, since that is a lot less than you think since the book isn't of traditional A4/Letter size, but rather smaller (and you don't even need to read the whole thing, I've storytelled TBZ a couple of times in the past, and I've started to read the setting book cover to cover just a week ago). For the rulebook itsef, around 80 pages or so (maybe even less) are actual rules, the rest is just explanations of each "power" subsystem (shinto rituals, yoroi armours, samurais, etc), gives you playable archetypes, pregens and other extra content you don't actually need to read if it's not going to appear on your game.

    For a player it's a lot easier: make them read the manga at the start of the setting book (30 pages), and they'll know eveything they need to select characters and play. The rules are also sumarized by a series of mangas with examples (around 10 pages), and that's all they need to start as well, you can explain everything else on the fly as it becomes relevant. Since the book comes with pregens, you don't need to invest time making character (although that only takes around 5 to 10 minutes since it's just stacking archetypes, not a big deal). But the pregens are very cool, so you can use them over and over again just fine.

    The only thing that you can't probably make on the fly is the basic framework for the scenario (how an adventure is called in TBZ), since it's important that the GM has at least some idea of what's going to happen on each Act (a scenario usually has 3 or 4 Acts), particularly to roleplay the Zero Act (a small prelude for each character, where you asign each character his Destiny for the scenario). Other than that, you can improvise just fine (I know I have =P).


    And seriously, if you enjoy anime, give it a shot, it's one of the best game I've played in years (and I don't even like anime that much, but the game is amazing). You can purchase the PDFs for just $14, and if you want to grab the physical copy later (which comes with the PDFs for free), I think Andy is discounting the PDF price from the physical purchase, so it's a great deal, IMHO.
