    \n It's theory-talk for a GMless game that nevertheless distributes GM-like tasks between the players.

    For example, a game where the players take turns framing scenes (this is quite common) is "GMfull" in the sense that it still relies on the GMing "technology", even if the players are taking turns in the driver's seat. In this context a true "GMless" game would be one that didn't require players to exercise GM-like powers at all.

    (Whether such a roleplaying game - one that doesn't exercise GM powers - actually exists is questionable in itself, but we can say with confidence that some games are closer to the ideal of GMlessness than others, so it's not an useless term even if one concludes that all GMless games are actually GMful.)

    It's one of those terms that doesn't necessarily withstand rigorous scrutiny, but that's nevertheless useful for casual purposes. Sort of like "story game" in that regard [grin].

    \n Collaborative GMless game =
    one player frames the scene but all players can introduce NPCs, world building, plot elements etc...they all play characters and the role traditionally played by the GM at the same time.

    GMfull game =
    players take turns playing the GM role. The GM role goes around the table, while a player plays the GM role they are responsible for all the things a traditional GM is reasonable for: NPCs, World building, plot elements, etc. when it is not the other players turn to GM they just play their characters, just how they would in a traditional GMed game.

    \n Nothing in particular to add, except that Ron Edwards didn't coin "story game". There are a few different origin accounts, but probably the most pertinent and well attested one is that Andy needed a name for this forum, chose "Story Games" (probably inspired by one of the other obscure antecedents), and from there the term was adopted into a vague appellation for "the kinds of roleplaying games that we like". Because this forum was basically started as a more relaxed alternative to Forge (alternative as in an alternate environment for the same people, not as in replacing the functions of the Forge), this "the games we like" originally pretty much meant the sorts of games that were in the vogue at the Forge.

    For the longest time it was common to call "the games we like" (or don't like, in the case of people who disliked the design trends in question) with the appellation "indie game" despite the constant reminders from all sorts of annoyingly staid community figures that "indie" does not, in fact, tell you anything about the creative content of the game. After the Forge was closed down it's become more popular to say "story game", probably because this forum became the de facto highest-profile trademark for this subscene.

    Due to this historical background, the term "story game" tends to be more of a cultural shibboleth than a term of analysis, most of the time when people use it: you call something a "story game" to say that it belongs in a certain subcultural tradition. Substance doesn't really come to it, and while people try to derive a surface meaning from the idea of "story", that doesn't really get you far - pretty much all rpgs have "story".

    I don't mind appropriating the term for a more precise purpose, of course. You like the sound of it, go ride that horse.

    \n Ok, now I will say something potentially rather controversial:

    * What you're calling GMful design is tricky.

    Personally, it's something I love very much (we should play Land of Nodd together sometime, it's more or less my main foray into that). But making it work right is fairly tricky, indeed; there aren't all that many great models. (Polaris is the main one, I'd argue.)

    * Collaborative GMless play is quite "en vogue" right now, but, in many cases, it is a little bit of a "cheat", much like Rule Zero-style GMing in traditional games.

    If you're a designer, it's a lot easier to write a workable and inspiring "collaborative GMless" game, because designs doing that tend to simply offload most of the difficulties of play into the social layer.

    For example, is Fiasco GMful or GMless? Arguably, it's GMless, but many people play it GMful (at least as far as the player portraying their character is concerned). The game itself is fairly silent on the matter, and expects you, as a group, to negotiate your own way to a functional mode of play.

    There's nothing wrong with this... but it is a very convenient way to avoid a lot of hard design questions: "Just make stuff up together and agree on it. Be nice to each other."

    It's sort of the modern version of the traditional games which said to us: "Hey, here are the rules, but sometimes they won't work. If you don't like them, change them, or let the GM just decide what happens!" (aka Rule Zero)

    In other words, you don't need to design a lot of these interactions in this currently-fashionable style of play: you can just ask the players to do it themselves, at the table.

    This is no particular crime (although it has driven a lot of people away from this kind of play, as well), and I think we will continue to get better and better at writing, designing, and explaining this kind of gaming. However, I would also guess that it is at least partially responsible for the current "fashion" of designing such freeform, collaborative games - it's much easier to do than more procedural design, and so we see a real wave of these games coming out. It sets a low bar for designers, and so more games written in this way reach completion and get published.

    Just a guess on my part, of course.

    Jeff,

    I feel pretty confident that I understand Fiasco and how it works quite well. However, I always welcome an opportunity to learn more, and this could be fruitful.

    For instance, I've reviewed the rules text, which was interesting. (Your re-frame of die choice as being about character success, for example, is, I suspect, a very good way to approach Fiasco play. However, the text suggests the contrary - in one example, it specifies that a white die "means the character gets what they want only if it is a positive outcome for the character" (not verbatim, and emphasis mine). I like learning and discussing these little tweaks; sometimes they can be quite useful or insightful.)

    Some brief answers:

    1. If I think of models for a Fiasco game, the first two that come to mind are Lock, Stock, and Two Smoking Barrels (a very Fiasco movie) and the Fiasco episode of Tabletop.

    I'd expect a story which puts more-or-less ordinary, relatable people getting in well over their heads - most likely because they make poor choices (due to poor impulse control and oversized ambitions) but possibly also because of desperation or because they get embroiled in events beyond their control. (Although I think the archetypal Fiasco character gets into trouble very much of their own volition; these are the exception.)

    I'd expect things to develop fairly quickly and escalate beyond a typical situation of that sort, getting messier and messier with each new development.

    The ending would be some mixture of dramatically appropriate and totally unexpected, as the Aftermath tables provide quite nicely - sometimes the people you cheer for find tragic ends, and both the underdogs and the extremely lucky tend to fare better.

    I think a Fiasco ending is rarely all neat and tied up; instead, we get a variety of situations, with surprises and twists thrown in, and likely a good number of loose ends. It's not emotionally satisfying or predictable the way that, say, an epic fantasy story might be: there are shades of gray and both bad people who get what they deserve and good people who get thrown under the bus. There's probably a fair bit of cringing about these characters' fates.

    2. I'm not sure what you mean by this question. According to whom? From what perspective? Are you talking about game mechanics?

    3. I think that there's lots of room for playing against formula in a Fiasco story, but the archetypal format would be one which begins with an unstable situation, and a mix of characters who have oversized ambitions. In the first act, they make plans or jump on opportunities and start creating a big mess.

    In the second act, the situation grows more complex and the characters are likely to resort to more dramatic or desperate means to get what they want: their plans don't go exactly as planned and things start to snowball out of control.

    At the end, we see who comes out of it ahead and who drowns before they can escape from the mess.

    Throughout, I would hope or expect to see characters making decisions which make sense from their perspective (keeping in mind their poor impulse control!), but are likely to end in disaster. ("Harold is going to go buy a gun and try to shoot his ex-wife at the Republican convention? Of course... he would totally do that. But, oh, man, there's no way that's going to end well for him!")

    This is quite enjoyable as an audience, because, in an ideal Fiasco, we can both sympathize with the characters and hope to see them do well but also cringe in anticipation at how poorly this is all probably going to turn out. That dramatic juxtaposition makes for a lot of the fun in the genre.

      \n To me, one of the fun things about a rotating GM setup is how scenes or storylines CAN have a different vibe, style, or feel, and still be coherent.

      There are some books and films that do it well. One silly example - but one which might be familiar to you, Jeff - is the movie American Pie. Whatever you might think of it as a film, one thing it does which is interesting is how each character's storyline is arguably told in a different genre, while retaining coherence with the others.

      I don't remember the names of the characters, but one is clearly living in a raunchy teen sex comedy, another in a romantic comedy (complete with ridiculously happy ending), the third in a very realistic kind of coming of age story (with a bittersweet ending), and the fourth a rather fantastic/surreal story.

      A Rotating GM setup can do this kind of thing really well, without even trying!

      Aside from that, I've seen two or three general approaches taken:

      1. Let's spend time brainstorming and combining ideas until a coherent picture emerges, and make sure each player gets input into everything on the table. We count on the sensitivity of the players to do this well, but also (ideally) give them tools to do so. (Many of my games try to do this.)

      2. Each player's input is distinct, a personal expression, and we trust that the whole will create something new and fun. (Games like Microscope.) the individuality of the inputs is part of the fun here.

      3. One player (or the game itself) brings the vibe and style to the table and tries to make it so vivid that it's immediately obvious to the others which genre and style we are playing in; it's the job of the other players to try to match that as best they can.

      Evocative imagery and strong themes are important here. (Fiasco, Polaris)
