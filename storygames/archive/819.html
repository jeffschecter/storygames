Exquisite Corpse Game?

Jasper
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
In the story chain thread rossum (Mischa Krilov) mentions the Exquisite Corpse method. If poems, stories and even a doom level can be created thus, why not a role-playing game of some kind? Certainly, the results could be no worse than that of a computer algorithm.

Maybe we could break down the game into identifiable (admittedly old-school) components, like "stats" and "resolution." Or perhaps each player could write a semi-modular "rule block" introducing a new idea and explaining its use; the next person would maybe see the title of that block, and the last sentance, but no more.

Might be fun, if silly and unplayable.

    If we're actually generating rules (which interesting because it's harder), I think we'd need either an initial outline -- describing what areas need to be covered -- or maybe some kind of "template" for each rules chunk, which would put resitrctions and requirements on the rules. For instance, each rule can introduce no more than one resource.

    Oh, here's another possibility. Each player write a rules chunk. He is careful to give names to everything he uses. He titles his section semi-descriptively. He passes three pieces of information onto the next player:

    1. the title of his section
    2. a list of the terms he's used, especially any he's invented
    3. a list of questions his section raises but doesn't address.

    An example for number 3: the player's section might include something like "If you have zero hit points, you die." Possibly unadressed questions would be "How do you get to zero HP?" "What happens if you have negative HP?" "What does it mean to 'die'?"

    The next player can then answer one or more of the questions -- though doesn't have to. Seeing all previous players' terms and questions would probably be good; not just your immediate predecessor's.

    I've just started reading Rules of Play: Game Design Fundamentals and they have an Exquisite Corpse Game Game exercise in the book.

    In a group of three the first person writes down two game rules for a game, each rule on a separate line of a sheet of paper. The top rule is covered up and the second is left visible. The second person does the same based off of the visible rule. The third person writes a final rule and a winning condition. The rules are then revealed and the group has to fashion a game out of the set of rules. For more people they recommend each person only writing one rule.
