  • Rob Donoghue
    July 2006
    Ok, so at Dexcon I got a chance to play in Shawn's CUPS (Soon to be "One night") game. It was pretty awesome, but more importantly, it illustrates this point pretty clearly.

    In the system, the stakes are broken down into three possibilities, the good outcome, the not so good outcome, and the not so good outcome. So, given that, my character ends up in a situation where Titania (who's unrequited love for my character was one of the major threads) offers to rescue him from the pursuing Jabberwock. We talked it out, and the outcomes were as follows:
    Good: Declines, and manages to use Titania as a distraction for the beast, and escapes by diving back into the hedge maze. Titania adds a Scorned descriptor.
    Not so good: Accept, and am indebted to Titania.
    Bad: Accept, and as a price, Titiania makes me sacrifice my cold iron sword for one made of glass which will mark me as working with fairy's to mortals, and be recognizable as Titania's to other fairies, including Oberon.

    You would not believe how much I swore when I won that roll. I woul dhave been totally jazzed with the Bad, but the dice betrayed me. THis became a recurring theme over the course of the game, as I could not lose a throw, no matter how hard I tried, so I missed numerous potential complication.

    We discussed this problem in the post mortem, specifically the possibility of making the win into the thing the _player_ wants most, but despite my frustrations, I strongly disagreed for a simple reason. What is good and bad for my character is a far simpler rubric to use than sorting them by the outcome _I_ want. It turns what might be an overly complex weighing of factors into a simple list, bing bang boom, good to go. There is to omuch risk in stakes-setting games of time being spent in the metagame portion of play as is, so anything that makes the decision process go more smoothly is a welcome addition for me.

    Now, shawn's tweaked the system in other ways, so my outcomes are less likely now, and I think it's a big improvement, but at the time, it really crystalized the practicality of using the character to frame these things. It also illustrated the importance of making sure that winning is as interesting as losing, which is counter-counterintuitive, but critical.

    -Rob D.

    This is a phenomenon frequently commented-upon by authors. It's entirely true. I had a minor character in my novel who I was thinking of as kind of a villain or at least unsympathetic, but at the end he turned out really likeable, a bluff, merry chap completely different from the sinister revolutionary leader I'd been picturing. And it happens by itself.

    If I was Jungian I could go on about elements of the collective unconscious having their own independent existence, but I'm not, so I won't.

    People - even imaginary people - have their own internal logic that we understand intuitively, even when it surprises us at a conscious level. There's an idea around that having a "theory of mind" - a mental picture of how other people think and feel and respond - is part of being well-adapted social beings and was a significant evolutionary step (hints of it are seen in apes). Sociopaths are missing bits of it; autistics are missing different bits.

    When your character seems to take on an independent existence, this is because your theory of mind is operating well.
