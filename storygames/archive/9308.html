    Posted By: Adam DrayThey have connectors, so you can snap an arrow to a box and expect it to stay there, even as you rearrange the fucker.
    \nThat's what a "smart connector" is, by the way.
    [tangent]
    The HOTNESS about Visio is that any object can be coded to interface with just about anything. Take the example of an office building map:
    It can help you audit computer assets and spot what's up or down (e.g. shared printers)
    It can provide you with a route from your desk to someone else's, with a click.
    It can start a phone call or chat or email to said person (or a group, if you rubber-band their cubicles).
    It can run electrical automation, like turning off lights automatically once everyone in an area is logged off their PCs.

    And don't even get me STARTED about how you can use it to rapid-prototype computer code or design electronic circuits and actually RUN them, to test, or use it to price out the materials costs of a construction project.

    Anyhow... smart connectors are the LEAST of it's phatness. I tend to chuckle at businesses that buy Visio just to do flowcharts or network diagrams, when a free OO can do just the same things. Of course, one's private home use is another matter--not a whole lot to automate or audit, in the average house. (That said, I did make a Visio doc once to optimize the free floor space for my furniture, letting it shuffle the pieces until it found a maxima. Just to see if I had a grip on its auto-arrange and boundary controls.)

    ALTHOUGH, that does remind me that Visio will take a mess of a flowchart--one just slapped together, with lines in a spaghetti pile and boxes all out of order--and "bubble" it into as clean a configuration as possible, according to rules that you set (e.g. how many line a given line may overlap). THAT could help you, if your system has a lot of wonky interrelations or conditions (not many RPGs really NEED such, though).
    [/tangent]

    Posted By: Adam Dray[Visio] is the only sane way to draw boxy diagrams of any complexity.
    \nNo, it's not. It's one way that works quite well on Windows, but is platform locked. There are lots and lots of other very useful diagramming tools.

    OmniGraffle on OSX is another, but again, it's platform locked.

    These are two, fine, general purpose diagramming tools. There are other, quite interesting diagramming tools that are useful depending upon the kind of diagrams you're trying to produce. I found that the graphviz suite of diagramming tools is quite useful for graph-like diagrams -- I personally found it quite useful for doing relationship maps. OmniGraphSketcher looks like another tool that can be quite useful for graph-type diagrams of a different sort (data plotting).

    I've found that UML and FMC are quite useful ways of modelling certain kinds of information, some of which might be useful to the gaming hobby. I prefer FMC's Dynamic Structure diagrams (derived from petri nets) to traditional flow charts, because they do an excellent job of separating agency from state, and supporting processes that involve more than one agent -- this might prove useful at the gaming table (where a process happens between several agents, sometimes two, but sometimes more than two).
