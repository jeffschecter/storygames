    I only ever read Tabletop RPG. There's probably good stuff I'm missing, but hey ... you can't drink the ocean.

    I always watch the "Photoshop RPG Motivational Posters" thread, because it's funny.

    Then I scan for the little ticks that tell me I've responded to a thread. I try to keep up with stuff I've contributed to.

    And while I'm doing that, I let my eye wander over the titles of threads. If something looks interesting then I check (a) its first page and (b) its most recent page. If it's more than five pages (fifty posts) in already then it's a "read-only" for me ... in most cases I won't want to spend the energy to catch up on the entirety of the thread in order to post knowledgeably, and I make it a policy not to post on a thread I don't entirely understand.

    But in the rare case where I find something interesting in its infancy, I get a post in, and then the next time I come back it will have one of those little tick-marks, and I'll catch up on it.

    There's an art to it ... drinking from the firehose without getting the full brunt.

    My tactics are similar to what most people have already said: don't look past page 2 of the forum, never post a reply (or continue reading) after a thread goes pear-shaped and turns into an idiotic argument, and so on. Those are basic skills for reading any forum with more than a few dozen posters.

    I also don't bother reading over 90% of the threads there, if not more. To some extent, this is easy, because most threads are clearly labeled as being uninteresting/useless to me (e.g., anything that starts with "[Exalted]"), and the rest can be weeded out by just hovering my mouse over the subject and reading the first-paragraph popup. When I find a thread I'm actually interested in, I skim it quickly first, and then read the stuff that caught my eye. I also cut my losses: when a thread isn't delivering anything useful, entertaining, or interesting anymore, I stop reading it.

    Adjust the percentages to match your own interests/free time/patience, and you're set. The key to enjoying participating in a thriving discussion forum with lots of members is learning to be a vicious and uncompromising editor: know what you don't want, and brutally remove those things from your field of view. You may throw out a few gems with the garbage, but those are acceptable losses.


    Oh, and avoid Tangency like you would any other open sewer. But that's just common sense.

    Posted By: Graham WalmsleyOh, sure, tell me anything you've got.
    Well, the rough sketch then:
    • Things you can do: You can get some people to think of you as a person who says useful things. That doesn't have a lot of spread outside of the type of conversations they, in turn, frequent ... but if you choose a topic of interest then you can get a little sub-community in the board that gives a sense of continuity. If you've got a .sig (as Joshua rightly points out) that sense of "this is a good person" has been known to translate into sales, so that's a nice thing.
    • Things you cannot do: You cannot make yourself known to the entire board, and more than you can speak directly to every person in America. That means that you can never expect to get any better treatment than anyone else, simply because the vast majority of your readers will be reading you for the first time. There is no distinction between you and "everyone else."
    Within those limits, calm, productive, overwhelmingly positive posting is a great means to an end. Yes, some people will attack you (I'm convinced that on the internet someone can always be found to hate any proposition) and if you stay positive then their opinion will not matter, except inasmuch as their posting style draws attention to how cool you are. Somebody out there is gonna get jazzed about what you're jazzed about ... even if they don't actually post. So that's lonely, but good.

    Am I making any sense?

      I have a great deal of sympathy for the RPG.Net mods, as I do for most people who are doing something entirely quixotic. They're trying to do a noble thing. They want to make a place that's welcoming. Surely everyone is for that, in theory.

      On the other hand, I also know that there are predictable feedbacks from their choices, for themselves and their community, and that it's going to bite them in the ass, HARD.

      Policing based on hurt feelings rather than on objective principles of what is right and what is wrong has a predictable feedback effect: The more people expect to be protected from having their feelings hurt, the easier it becomes to hurt their feelings. Any new offense has an effect that is conflated by your sense of outrage that your right to not be offended has been betrayed. More and more things become offensive. Less and less things become permissible. Eventually, if the practice is followed with blind and unwavering conviction, everything is offensive and nothing is permissible.

      The mods will stop somewhere before then, and given time the community will rebound just fine. I just worry that each step they take down their current path is going to make their eventual change of heart harder on them and nastier for the community. I do hate to see people hurt for trying to do the right thing.
