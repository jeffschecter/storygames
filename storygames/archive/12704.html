    \n I don't know if I can offer advice, but I can offer an example. When I demo Happy Birthday Robot for people, I don't bring out the dice or anything. Here's the script I follow:\n
    "Okay, I'm going to start a sentence and you're going to add three words. Ready? 'Robot wakes up outside AND..."

    (Wait for response.)

    "Great! Now (person's companion or nearby listener) would add four more words."

    (Turn towards other person.)

    "Ready? 'Robot wakes up outside and it is raining, BUT..."

    (Wait for response. Turn back to original person.)

    "Excellent! Now that completes a turn. The game continues with you starting a sentence, me doing the AND part and him doing the BUT part. The dice tell you how many words you can use. You also get coins you can share to give other people free words. Over time, we tell a complete story about Robot's birthday. "
    \nBy this point, I can tell whether they're interested. If not, I bid a friendly farewell. If so, I hand them the book and show off the cute pictures. Either way, I didn't spend more than two minutes demoing the core interaction of the game.

    \n I said this in one of those link dump links:

    "In fifteen minutes, you can basically do the following if you've practiced and are prepared:

    1. Give a short overview of the game and what it is about.
    2. Explain the most compelling and necessary mechanic.
    3. Throw the demo-er into a situation where they get to make one major choice.
    4. Determine the outcome of that choice and illustrate why the game makes that cool.
    5. Explain what's tasty about the full game that you had to leave out of the demo."

    I think many people like focused attention, and like to get a taste of what the game's potential is, and they really really like meeting people who sincerely fucking love their creations. Even if your 15 minutes (or 7.5 minutes each, if you have two people, which you should) doesn't lead to a sale, you are still playing your game in public, and other people are watching, listening, and maybe getting interested. And if you do it right, no one will leave your demo with anything but a friendly impression of you as a person and your game as a thing. That will ripple out.

    \nIf chargen is the most fun part of play, make it a part of the demo. Not the complete chargen, but enough to give them a taste. Give them a partial pregen and have the player make one or two chargen choices. Then play a scene of the game, and have the short scenario draw in their chargen choices somehow.

    \n One thing I learned from Ron Edwards that struck a chord - you don't need to demo the game, you need to demo the experience of the game. So my Roach demo completely communicates the game's feel and fun, but has little to do with the actual rules - just a few cool bits, not the entire structure.
