\n To the Diplomacy players here:

I'm wondering about playing Diplomacy, and the variations that exist, particularly for faster play and fewer participants. I know of a bunch of good options for six players, and I'm familiar with the suggested variations in the basic rules, but those seem clunky.

I can't find any reliable resource for well-tested variants of Diplomacy, and it sounds like they're not easy to design. Eero posted an interesting account of the Five Italies map, and how it didn't work too well: which makes sense, a certain asymmetry makes the game much more interesting.

So, I have two questions:

1. Can you get a shorter game simply by reducing the number of SCs that must be owned by the winner? I don't really see why not, but surely someone's tried that and actually knows whether it works or not.

2. It seems to me that you could develop some variations simply by cutting off chunks of the map. It takes careful thinking, but doesn't seem impossible or anything.

    \n Reusing the Calhamer map in different ways is a pretty common way of making off-the-cuff variants when you absolutely have to have something to play with however many people.     \n I'd say that balance in Diplomacy is a whole-board phenomenon, so taking just a part of the board doesn't preserve it by itself. Taking a part of the board is useful in preserving tactical relationships, though; a common weakness of variant maps is when the map is not locally interesting - the local relationships between territories are not concominant of tactically interesting and heterogenous play situations. That's the sort of thing you get help for by using a pre-existing map as a template.

    How many players are you considering? One reason for why variants are challenging is that seven players is actually a pretty nice number for the game insofar as the diplomacy is concerned, so a variant will have to account for the lessened diplomatic depth if there are fewer players. Three players, for example, is probably not playable as more than a tactical exercise (that is, the players have to agree in advance to limited diplomacy or entirely relegate diplomacy outside the sphere of the game, or otherwise the game won't resolve due to the vested interest everybody has in not allowing the strongest opponent to win).

    \n Diplomacy is quite commonly played in short form in tournaments and such by setting a turns deadline. Play until the end of 1908, for example, and then count the SCs, with the largest Power winning the game. That probably works better than setting a low winning SC count, as it doesn't change the end-game logic as dramatically.

    I'm not too fond of the artificially shortened game, as the truncated end-game tends to reflect on the nature of the whole enterprise. It's not that far-fetched to say that the relatively common over-aggressive game-style we have here in Finland has largely developed because a majority of the games is played in tournament form; blindly attacking the weakest point in enemy lines makes only too much sense when you know that the game's going to end in four rounds, after which the player with the most SCs will win, no matter their strategic or diplomatic position.

    \n Sure, but it has its own weirdnesses. I do admit that my approach towards the game is pretty staid and serious, so what is a weakness to a variant in my book might be just what somebody else would like to have.

    As a point of interest, in case you didn't know: the theoretical justification for the otherwise arbitrary winning SC count in the game is that 18 SCs represents a majority of the available territory on the Calhamer board. The logic is that anybody able to dominate that much of the board would, given time, be able to conquer the rest as well. Thus the usual, often unsaid variant victory requirement is always half of whatever is the given board's SC count. Not an argument against alternative victory conditions, but a sort of aesthetic game design justification for the default rule.

    \n Merely guessing at it, I'd expect good players to play much more conservatively if the SC limit was lower. Consequently individual Powers would not be eliminated as easily, and players would be more inclined to drop whatever they were doing to turn against a dominant Power. End-game concerns would not exist, as the end-game itself would not exist; victory would be determined locally, as a given player would have the ability to achieve the winning SC count without having to go to a full-board end-game. You could basically lose the game without ever having an opportunity to challenge the winner if you happened to play on different parts of the board. Mid-game strategy would likely be much more aggressive, as you wouldn't have to worry about the board positions during end-game if you could just finish the game by attacking and conquering one of your neighbours.

    Nothing on that list outright ruins the game, so I guess I wouldn't be averse to trying out a lower SC limit as a lark, although I would rather play with the normal limit if given the choice. Considering the Calhamer map, an interesting experimental limit could be 10 SCs - it'd be high enough to not be achieved entirely accidentally, but so low that it can be achieved in middle-game easily enough. I suspect that the overall game experience would be less loose and more paranoid, with everybody watching each other's moves carefully; whoever managed to get a decisive upper hand against a neighbour would be immediately on the brink of overall victory. Interesting as a curiousity, surely.
