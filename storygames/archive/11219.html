    \nGoodman Games overview of the adventure tabletop RPG market over the last few years...\n
    \n
    2001-2004: 80%+ of RPG'ers were playing 3E.
    \n
    \n2004-2005: Some gamers fell off between 3E and 3.5, but still 75% playing 3.5.
    \n
    \n2005-2007: D20 variants multiply. Mutants & Masterminds, Iron Heroes, Arcana Evolved, Castles & Crusades, Conan, others. Most of the market is playing some version of 3E, but it's no longer all D&D.; Various d20 publishers begin to release their own stand-alone RPG's (e.g., Runequest).
    \n
    \n2008: Most, but not all, of the RPG market converts to 4E. Market is now split between 4E and many varieties of 3E holdouts. Other systems proliferate, including Hackmaster Basic and the 1E retro-clones. "Old-school" goes mainstream. Goodman Games remains the only "d20 company" still primarily supporting WotC D&D.;
    \n
    \n2009: Pathfinder releases. Fantasy RPG market is now split between 4E and Pathfinder, with another big chunk split to the other stand-alone RPG's (Castles & Crusades, Runequest, Fantasycraft, upcoming Dragon Age, etc.), and another chunk shopping online in the retro-clone market (which I personally have a fondness for).
    \n
    \n2010: What's a module publisher to do?
    \n
    \nMy primary love remains adventures, but the market is so fragmented that the customers who played DCC modules in 2004 are now playing 6 different systems.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    What if a DCC were written in "native 4E" but there were downloads to support other systems? Or...what if the DCC had generic stats ("Orc, 6 hp, axe, chainmail")...and ALL detailed stats were available as a download? So if you play 4E you download the 4E stats PDF...if you play Pathfinder you download the Pathfinder stats PDF...etc.

    \n Interesting discussion (which quickly turns into an internet fight) over on RPGNet about reselling PDFs here:
    http://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?t=491951

    So while everyone's scrambling to dish out their armchair lawyer info or take a "strong internet angry-man stance", I gotta say that on a small scale (that is, a personal level, not through a website and not on eBay) I would totally support the buying and selling of used PDFs to friends and acquaintances. I've got PDFs that I know I'll never use again, that I might as well delete. I don't mind giving my one copy to a friend, or selling my one copy to one friend for a few bucks (or like a Taco Combo from the taco place on 9th street in Durham). I won't download it again even though I Have The Right, and I won't keep a copy even though I totally could. Likewise, I'd trust the person I'm buying a secondhand PDF from to do the same.

    On a small, personal scale, where you know who you're selling to and they know you, I see no problem with this. On a larger scale, where you're taking product to eBay, or selling indiscriminately through your website, that's more problematic (and for these two cases I'm still using the example of where the owner says "I will not look at it again, I will delete it on my PC, etc" but you don't know them and they don't know you. I'm NOT talking a situation where oen dude is selling multiple copies of one product, keeping it, using it, etc).

    Anyway...

    \n[used PDFs 2]

    From a consumer end, if I can't resell something I've bought but I end up not using, the price I'm willing to pay for it drops a ton. Sellers of $1-10 dollar pdfs probably have nothing to worry about. John Wick won't ever see me wanting to dump my pdf copy of Curse of the Yellow Sign Part 1 for $2 bucks. I will very likely never again buy a pdf for more than $10 though. I mean, hell, $10 is a movie ticket. I can't transfer that experience either by way of sale.     Given what I saw in that thread, I decidely don't want hard copy or even disk copy to go away. Those at least I can resell or trade legitimately.

    Has anyone even done any research into the final money to creators if pdf resales were allowed legitimately? I'm not talking about crazy copy and distribute tons of times, just one-to-one sales.

    I feel like the PDF providers are falling down a bit on this one. I'm low tech. I think of a PDF I've purchased just as book in a funny format. I have zero interest inmaking a bunch of copies and reselling them and gutting the creator's profits. On the other hand, I do have an interest in sharing the thing or reselling my copy when I'm done with it. Lack of ability to do either means I'm just not as interested in buying experimentally on impulse, which I do a fair bit.

      \nEvery statement in this, except for "born at MIT", is wrong, and it doesn't take much research to find that:
      (1) There were earlier computer games, even graphical ones (a predecessor of Pong was built in 1958.)
      (2) As far as I know, Spacewar predates any ARPA involvement in MIT (earliest project I can find is MAC starting in 1963.)
      (3) The screen was a DEC "Type 30" display peripheral. I'm sure it shares an ancestry with radar screens, but so do all CRTs.
      (4) The game was an explicit homage to the "Lensman" SF novels by E.E. "Doc" Smith, written decades before the space race (in the 1930s I believe).

      Not to mention that the MIT hackers were pretty far away from the military-industrial mindset. Just like the wargame/RPG culture of the early '70s, which they of course overlap heavily with, they were a weird mixture of nerd/proto-hippie/anarchist/fandom.

      I'm not even going to go read the website it links to. Anyone who can use the words "collectivist" or "revolutionary" with a straight face today is deep into some alternate cranio-anal dimension that I have no desire to experience.
