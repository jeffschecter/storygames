Posted By: SweeneyToddFor any game out there, there are folks whose experiences with it were so personally unsatisfying that they develop a desire to "save" people from it. D&D is certainly one of those.
I would not call it "personally unsatisfying", but I have had some experiences with D&D which has made me think: why is the quality of scenarios and gameplay so low on that game, repeatedly, compared to other classical games I have participated in, both on conventions and in private? Why do D&D repeatedly produce substandard gameplay?

It took me some years, and some game-sessions, but the conclusion is simple; the game itself is flawed. It is an anachronism. The game is full of tidbits that don't connect. The GM is hard pressed to make good gaming out of it, and very often she will not succeed. The game is too rules heavy to translate into effective gameplay, without serious tweaking on the part of the GM. Most of that tweaking is the GM shouldering the responsibility of the game-smiths. Why should we expect the GM to rebuild the rules made by professional game-smiths? They are the professionals, and she is the amateur! Why?

Nothing personal about it, really, other than the fact that I resent thousands of players being robbed the best chance to experience great gameplay. I know for a fact that thousands of D&D-players have sessions full of frustration due to the flaws of this game. As there are a lot of D&D-players, that makes for a lot of frustration.

Please, all you wizards out there; give them a GOOD edition of the game!

Or please help me convince them that other RPGs may give them a better chance of having THAT GREAT RPG-FEELING THEY ONCE HAD! (or never had)

    \nThe first trick is to not tell people that the game they enjoy is flawed. That despite their experience to the contrary, it produces bad games. No matter how many anecdotes you can tell about people you've talked with, played with at cons, or experiences that you've had, they'll never trump the personal experience of thse who enjoy it. I used to be like you. And I hate D&D 3rd edition or AD&D either edition. Although I'm excited about 4th edition, and still have fond memories of basic D&D, I'm not a D&D fan. I think its label as a roleplaying game is questionable. But stop telling people their game is broken.

    How do I convince people that other RPGs might give them some fun? I play with them. If they are playing D&D, so be it. As long as it's not some unending campaign where one person will be GM forever, I'll have a chance after some number of sessions to say, "I'll run something". I'll have learned what they like, what they don't. Most importantly, I might discover that we just don't mesh well and we'll part ways. But I'll try to have fun. I'll try to make friends. Those two things are my bigger goal. My goal isn't to get them to stop playing D&D. It's only partially to get them to see other games. My goal is to find more people to play games with.

    But stop being that guy. Stop being that guy that thinks he knows what is fun for everyone else. Stop being that guy who tells people their fun is wrong. Being a force of negativity will only strengthen people's negativity. People who agree without will shout "Yeah, he's right! D&D sucks!" People who disagree will go "Wow, he's wrong. He sucks!" And people with a broad perspective will say, "Wow, he's angry and bitter. That sucks." It's all negative.

    Instead, be that guy that knows what is fun for himself. Be that guy that tells people what he finds fun. Be a force of positivity. Make people go "Man, that guy's having fun. And that's awesome!" And if that never happens with D&D, fine. You don't need to tell us. If that always happens with some other games, that's great. Tell us about it.

    I think you have a mistaken impression that Story Games is about indie games vs traditional. But its really about having fun. And fun isn't defined by rules. Its by how you play the game.

Hack'n slash is great in it's way, but for most people it is plain stupid, and for a lot of players it lose its magic after a while. then comes the phase when they try to patch the game, to do things like intrigue or peaceful conflict-resolution, or realistic cultures, or whatever they perceive could be really cool to interact on. And then they run into tons of frustration, awakening the wrath of co-players, awakening the sleeping limitations of the game itself, and generally making a mess of it.

    I dare say that almost any classical RPG out there have better possibilities for diverse gameplay and thematics than D&D.

    I am criticizing D&D for the way it tries to make roleplaying. Some players are in love with that way of doing it. The reasons for their love are manifold, and I would be stupid not to believe some great gaming-experiences to be at the hearth of it. Still I maintain that the game itself has great flaws. The largely unspoken limitations of the game is one. To call it a "roleplaying game", when it is more and more like a boardgame, is another one, creating expectations the game never can justify. But the multitude of rules, and exceptions to rules, is by far the worse, in my view. All these flaws makes the game produce crappy gameplay far too often.

    As far as I have discerned by the writings on 4E, it too seem to be ridden with rules and rules-exceptions in abundance. What I consider ridiculously heavy patching is already at play, both in this forum and others. It leaves me with the impression that the game has kept its drama-crushing behemothness. If my impression turns out to be right, it actually makes me sad. So lets hope for a better (fantasy)world, in sympathy with thousands of players out there.


    Sidenote 1:
    I am under the impression that D&D has an even more dominating position in the US than in other roleplaying countries. Is that correct? Are other games being played over there? (not talking indie games here, but mainstream classical RPGs)

    Sidenote 2:
    I believe D&D does a "great" job of frightening people from taking up roleplaying. The dominating position of this one game, makes a lot of people believe that roleplaying is a silly combat-fantasy had by dumb boys. Discussing games in forums like this, we often loose sight of what RPGs really are: a fringe-activity in the society. I believe dialogue-based roleplaying could engage far more people, if they had the chance to see other games than D&D on the scene. Sad fact, in my view. If you think this makes me "hate" the game, you are wrong, but it makes me strive both to lessen the import of this game, and to hold out other games for people to play. In the long term RPGs in general will be better off without the monopoly-like dominance of D&D.

    \n Alas, it looks as if Thomas is going to fall just short of completing the standard line of argument here (that all those people who 'enjoy' D&D are either mouth-breathers or deluded ... or both). But for those who've come recently to the Story-Games community, this thread is almost like a performance-art rendition of the worst parts of indie-scene in 2005 and before (not that it's changed entirely: there are some who are still behaving in that fashion).

    For people taking notes: there are two basic dysfunctional trends in the RPG-theory dialog and this thread beautifully illustrates both of them.
    1. Superiority or "I game better than you" (where "you" is the traditional gamer--or maybe just the D&D gamer)
    2. Victimization or "I was (or, in some formulations, "people are") injured by traditional game text"

    Here we see both! Fabula (et. al) are just objectively better games--it's a statement of fact! I have seen indie games compared to a Ferrari while traditional games were a Honda civic (and the poster with a straight face expected me to believe he was not saying indie games were better--the Honda was ... more reliable!).

    The real drama, however, is in victimization. Even without claiming that the games fundamentally damage the player, we can see here that people--traditional people--need to be saved from them. In this case the indie theorist / game designer is freakin' public servant!

    Where Does This Usually Go?
    These formulations have a single basic problem: the 'bad games' tend to be much more popular than the 'good games.' Occam's Razor suggests that if something reliably created a dysfunctional environment and caused misery and strife that it wouldn't spread--that superior products could replace it--and that it would be widely recognized as a bad thing. I am sure there will be posters here who will be able to enumerate at length why some game or other is better than the mainstream comparisons. Things like network effects, an early head start, and difficulty in advertising will be cited. Clearly the big-break in RPG marketing is just around the corner waiting to explode. In this mind-set there's no such thing as a "mainstream RPG" because the mainstream doesn't play it ... yet!

    But all of that's dull (and, you know, not overly objectionable). The chocolaty-goodness comes when the dysfunctional theorist decides that the masses who play do so because they are inferior--or victims (see above).

    There are three major trends:
    1. People continue to play D&D because they are hooked. D&D players return again and again to a dysfunctional environment because the experience is (perhaps literally, depending on who is talking) addictive. They get a "glimpse" of what gaming can be and then, by the same psychological mechanism that makes slot-machines so enticing, they keep coming back, chasing the dream!
    2. The people do not even actually enjoy the game at all. Reports to the contrary are self-justification and rationalization (like, and oh man, I've actually seen this, a woman in an abusive
    marriage). These people delusionally make up stories after the fact to re-create an enjoyable game that never happened (I'm reminded of the memory-implant vacations from Total Recall).
    3. Some large amount of people like inferior things ... because they are inferior! Look at McDonalds and Top-40 hits! How else could you explain them? Hey? Hello? NASCAR? C'mon people--aren't we better than that?

    I have to honestly say I'm very happy to see that this approach isn't getting traction here. That makes it more comedy than really problematic. Maybe it's a sign of how much things have changed since 2005. I also need to add for the record that I'm aware that (a) people do have profoundly negative experiences with some RPGs and I'm not minimizing the hurt there (I don't think it's correct or healthy, however, to blame external, non-acting agencies like rule-books for continuing self-destructive behavior). Also that (b) there are plenty of RPGs with a lot of room for improvement (including your favorite one, unless it is Dogs in the Vineyard which, in its present edition is clearly the tightest RPG ever written ... if you think I am being anything more than slightly hyperbolic, you are mistaken!).

    But this thread--these posts--are everything I could ask for illustrating toxic RPG dialog so I've got to take my hat off to Thomas for giving us this thread.

    -Marco
