\n Starting to get into geocaching, and it occurred to me a great thing to leave in those ammo cans would be a a tiny RPG for beginners, to hopefully convert people, one geocacher at a time.

So I'm thinking:
- short and compact, so it's cheap for me to print out and would fold up neatly and fit inside a geocache box or ammo can
- free / open, so I'm not violating any copyrights doing this
- user-friendly / good for rookies rpg stars waiting to be born - that is, hard to screw up the play of
- likely to convert people - after playing they'll want to play more (at the bottom of the page, I'm imagining "if you liked this, go to this website" which would have a bunch of links to Blackbird, Geiger Counter, etc.)

Which already might narrow the field down to nothing - Cthulhu Dark and Ghost/Echo and the like assume veteran players; and there's a ton of one-page games (like from the 5-minute thread) that are short and but unplaytested and also likely to not deliver in the hands of rookies. (Blank page syndrome, etc.)

And even still, there's more I'd like the game to do:
- be something you can play while walking through the woods

Which makes me think of Vincent Baker's creatures of the forest game, which I don't remember the exact name of and can't find a link to, but even that I'm not sure will deliver for a group of rookies.

What y'all got?

    \n Jamie,

    you were thinking of Otherkind. And I wouldn't recommend that game, because it has zero GM advice. It's just a set of "Player's Handbook"-rules and some implied setting. It needs a drama-sensitive and/or fantasy-accustomed GM. I believe Vincent wrote Otherkind to prove a point, not to provide us with a rookie-friendly game. Don't get me wrong, Otherkind is one of the best-written and clearest set of rules known to mankind, but it doesn't "play itself".

    I'll second Microcosm. It has one of the best basic explanations I've seen in a long time, plus situation-generation, R-maps and other Indie goodness.

    Oh, and there's also The Puddle.
