I'm very pleased to say that Beowulf is headed for press tomorrow! Preorders are $14+$5 s&h and will probably ship a few days before Gen Con.

\n

The book includes the full translated text of the poem, essays and exegesis by medievalist Dr. Michael Drout, the full Anthology Engine rules by Vincent Baker, all sewn together by me, including the Three Wyrds, the epic oracles spinning fate and tragedy, eagerly foretelling blood in the snow and eyes peering in the wild darkness.

\n

As it's an ashcan, I'm looking forward to getting feedback from folks. I'm particularly interested in some of the long-term play effects of the Wyrds.

    Here's the deal with Beowulf: I've wanted to publish and edition of the poem for years. When Vincent started working on In a Wicked Age, we were talking about the rules, and I said, "Holy carp (sic.)! These rules would be perfect for Beowulf! Can I put these rules in the back of the book?" He said, "Yeah, I'm thinking that I'm going to name the system so I can license it to people who want to use it for appropriate stuff." Then he told me he was actually going to name the system, which was interesting, and he was going to call it "CAVBAGS", or the Coerceion And Violence Based Adventure Gaming System: Perfect For Any Setting". I suggested something punchier, like "Anthology Engine" but he said he was afraid of alienating traditional gamers with a title like that. Our subsequent feud raged across generations, but finally, my great-grandson, borne by sea monsters and Yankee Jews, convinced his great-grandson, proud Geat-Utahns to change the name. The feud was resolved as our great-grandsons married each other to seal the deal with a political \u2014 but not wholly dispassionate \u2014\xa0political marriage.

    \n

    (Admittedly, everything from CAVBAGS on is largely unsupported because of the lacun\xe6 in the text, but it seems reasonable to assume, given similar texts from the time, that this is how it happened.)

    \n

    So that simple little idea is now kind of a big book: 244 pages (and I just realized I'm missing a piece, so it'll grow a couple of pages today). In that volume are:
    \n* The full Grummere translation of the poem with verse summaries. One of the things I want feedback on is the quality of this translation \u2014 if it's evocative and easy enough to understand.
    \n* Beowulf Basics by Dr. Michael Drout, an exegesis of the events of the poem including family trees and discussion of life in 6th century Northern Europe.
    \n* Dating Beowulf by Dr. Michael Drout, an essay describing not only how Beowulf's been dated in various ways, but the sociopolitical ramifications of those various dates. I include this because, as players, we're creating improvised fiction based on a 21st century publication of a 19th century translation, using 16th century syntax, of a tenth century text of a ~sixth century poem. The ways we interpret it are inescapably tied to our own current perception of things, which is what the game is about at its core: what it means when President Violent Monkey calls people "heroes".
    \n* The full adaptation of the Anthology Engine rules.
    \n* The Wyrds Three, an oracle in order
    \n* A quickie rundown for people who have played IaWA before.

    \n

    So, yeah, Rob's right. There are some tweaks to the Anthology Engine rules. Being an ashcan, those rules are marked so people can pay attention to them and give me feedback.

    \n

    The Wyrds Three are divided into three sections: The Hero Discovered, The Hero Defined, The Hero Destroyed. You have to play them in order and you have to spend a certain amount of time in each of the acts before moving to the next Wyrd.

    \n

    In the first Wyrd, you play just a bunch of characters. At least one of them is the hero's parent. More of them might also be the hero's parent. Hard to know! You have to take the first opportunity for pregnancy. That pregnant character will bear the hero. It might take a couple of episodes of pregnancy before the hero's born, but once the hero's born and you've played a couple of episodes in The Hero Discovered with the hero being alive, you can move on. The hero is mentioned explicitly and implicitly in the Wyrds, which means that they'll often have advantage dice to spare since they won't have to buy themselves into the story as often.

    \n

    The consequences of action are that you negotiate out ("How about, OK, the king submits and you appease Queen Medb with his blood?"), or you can Maim or Debase the loser. That is, if you're familiar with IaWA, there's no "exhaustion". If you're really taking the hits, you're being left with fighting for myself and with violence. When ring-breaking fails and word-hoards are spent, there's nothing but blood on the snow to make the world be the way you want.

    \n

    The deal with the book is, both covers are the "front". You flip it over to read the poem or play the game. One side has a human eye on the cover, the other a monster's eye. They are afraid of each other.

    Seamus Heaney's translation, I'm sad to say, is a much better read by itself. Significantly, he owns it and didn't write back when I inquired about using it. Grummere's is most interesting in the form that it adds to the poem.

    \n

    Like, check this out. From the first review there:

    \n
    \n

    Why, though, should anyone read the Gummere translation?...The Gummere translation excels it at capturing the feel of the Old English, and particularly the alliterations which pervade the original Anglo-Saxon text.

    \n
    \n

    OK, while I agree with the reviewer's recognition of the alliteration (something Heaney uses, too), the "feel of Old English" is preserved by using Shakespeare-like language! The amount of time between Shakespeare and the events in Beowulf is the same amount of time between us, now, and the height of the Byzantine Empire. Modern English is closer by far to Shakespearean English.

    \n

    What's particularly interesting about that to me is that it highlights the place the reader (and even moreso, a modern creator, such as you, when you're playing it) has in looking at texts like these. Like, check out the movie Beowulf & Grendel \u2014 the creators had to do a lot to make Beowulf not just be a violent braggart. Grendel is sympathetic and even honorable. They added female characters who, you know do stuff. They even have a poet at the end clearly modifying events to make the poem more fun. The Christianity that is purely subtextual (and probably an addition to the poem by a tenth-century scribe) is criticized openly.

    \n

    It's that kind of re-envisioning that I'm expecting from Beowulf players. It's certainly been the case with every game I've played so far, and I think the Grummere translation gives that kind of liberty.

    Edit:
    \nOh, also, Fnord: the influence of Beowulf is actually pretty weird. The book sat, unread, for centuries in a library that burned down in the 19th century. It was the only book that survived (and it got some damage, itself.) So literature pretty much went on without it for hundreds of years. Then, when it was discovered, it gave us this window into a previous incarnation of our language and the (largely illiterate) culture that spawned it. That said, it's part of a very influential body of work. Beowulf (my edition) draws on tellings of King Arthur (which probably took place around the same time), the Kalevala, the Nibelungenlied, and other stuff in that vein, and those are clearly influential on our view of the period and the ethical grounds on which it's based. The mediography in the book also counts Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, which is a 20th century English interpretation of a 14th century French poem, imposing 14th century French morals on 6th century English events (with a healthy dose of surreal fairy stuff).

    \n

    The proof of a tale is in the telling, and Beowulf, like Shock:, is not designed to reproduce a type of fiction, but to give you the tools to create your own. These are your own questions about honor and heroism, your own tales of monsters and those that wear human faces. It's about what happens when you face the world with only the tools of coercion.

    Matthijs, I've made a few editorial decisions here and there, including a more detailed table of contents, but it's fundamentally unchanged, except for a couple of things I thought could be stated more strongly. The examples are rewritten in a way that I hope is clear and helpful, too.

    \n

    That said, this is an ashcan and, as I get feedback from folks, I'll be modifying the text to clarify. Honestly, I don't see what the big deal is, but then, it comes from my local culture of play, so that's not surprising. I think the way IaWA is written is a really neat experiment and I'm not surprised that some people had a hard time with it. Such is the nature of experiment. I find it very interesting that people less familiar with our indie gaming subculture have an easier time with it than the rest of us, too. I think it's because your narrative authority as a player (not the GM) is restricted to concrete stuff your character does and suggestions to other people.

    \n

    Writing for strangers, or even people you've played with once or twice, is really hard. I'm looking forward to getting some feedback on this. I suspect Vincent will be listening, too.

    \n

    John, I wish I liked the movie more for the sake of a good debate about art. Sadly, I didn't love it that much. I liked the moral and ethical shift from the original because it says what I'm trying to say with this game, but I just didn't love it enough to fight for it.

    \n Re: the Beowulf game...its one of my favorite boardgames ever.
    \n
    \nAt its core its Just Another Auction Game. But after a few plays...its really much more than that. The game consists of a series of auctions represented by wheels on the game board. Each wheel represents a different part of the Beowulf story...so you are actually playing through Beowulf's legend as his thanes starting at Hygelic's hall until he dies.
    \n
    \nEach auction your bidding different combinations of resources (cards) drinking horns for commaraderie, ships for traveling, etc. So based on the cards you have you have to strategize which upcoming auctions you think you can win. Each auction has a number of different prizes ranging from healing, to points, to powerful items, to more cards, to secret points, to money. Whoever stays in the auction the longest (wins) gets first pick, so part of the strategy is how much should you spend to get the best thing on the wheel vs. something else. Most of the time paying less for 3rd place is better than paying a ton for 1st...but if everyone is aiming for third place than you pick up 2nd or 1st cheap...and on it goes. There are sometimes harmful prizes on the wheel as well (like damage) so if you drop out first you can get smoked...so you're not only competing to come in first but also to not come in last. There are also a couple different kinds of auctions, some hidden bid, some open. And finally there are places where you get stuff for free...like more cards. Players who managed their hands effectively might not need more cards and so can choose one of the other options (like points) while players who blew through their cards need more. Towards the end there are special opportunities to discard certain combinations of things for additional bonuses so planning ahead so you know what you want to have available when you get there is also a strategy.
    \n
    \nIts one of those games that can be played effectively with very little mental effort...but then as you get experienced with it and start to see some of the subtleties theres a depth there thats unexpected.
    \n
    \nIf you're a fan of of the psychology of auction games...its one of the very best. And if you pay attention to the theme rather than just going through the mechanics...you can tell the story of beowulf while you play.
