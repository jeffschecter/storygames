    I was gonna play nice, but then you dorks got all OED on this motherfucker, so: I call bullshit. (In a nice way!)

    If you said just making something available online = publishing, can you name for me some game you considered published, real, entirely out there that isn't in the form of a fancy PDF / POD / for sale? I myself catch myself thinking that being available for sale in some form is how a game is ligitimately out and published. I'm sure for almost all gamers out there, having the book available in some bound, real form - even POD - is a necessary condition.

    However, that's not a good idea. One shouldn't need one's game ideas to be legitimized by commerce and expense in order to be considered quality. (There is, I think, a factor where putting a potential price on something increases the "buy in" of the purchaser and their belief in the product, but that's a whole lot of handwavium.)

    So, ideally, here's what I think are the criteria for a "published" game:

    • available to people you don't know, online or through some real-world outlet
    • the text is edited and clean, and presented pleasantly. (It doesn't have to be a fancy or artistic medium, but it needs to be clean, rather than raw.)
    • has been playtested

    The third one is a little bit more about completeness than publishedness, but they ought to mesh together.

    From my point of view, you're not "published," unless you're in print, on physical media, for sale.

    It's a personal definition. Physical print publication, whether it's on granite tablets or paper, has a tangible, immutable aspect to it that sets it apart from electronic publishing.

    And it's important for your publication to be for sale. It's easy to stand at a corner and pass out flyers, or leave your script lying on a table somewhere. It's a lot harder to get people to actually buy your publication.

    I might be able to see CD-ROM dissemination of electronic files as publication, but I initially don't like the idea.

    Publishing physically *requires* some sort of tangible investment. It's too easy for any joe to simply write and disseminate something electronically. I mean, heck, I've literally just published this post.

    So I choose to personally restrict the definition.

    Shit, if we're gonna require playtesting for "published" status, that rules out some commercial, "traditional" games.

    It feels like there's "sorta published," meaning people can go download it and play it, and "really published," which means it's done and stuff.

    Publication represents the end of the process -- at least till the next revision. The "sorta published" term is really just a synonym for "posted" or "printed," which could mean that you dumped a copy to your printer and gave it to someone or put it on Story Games, and then kept working on it. Without the "end of the process" bit, it's not "really published."

    To sum, a published game:
    1. is in a finished state (as in, you stopped working on it), and
    2. is available to the public
    It doesn't have to be playtested or even playable. It doesn't have to be "complete." You just have to be done with it and stick it where people can get it.

    I think it's also worth noting that, the world being what it is, "published" is a power word. How you define it has to do with who you want to give, or take, power from.

    Consider this as an example:

    Long, long ago, before I was all Forge cool and Marxist enlightened, I was at a very small local game con in So Cal up near Bakersfield. I had just gotten an award for a book I'd worked on, physically published by a game company with a name big enough to have released video games based on their RPGs. In the course of the night, I talked to several different people about being published.

    The first guy had put a game, a nifty one (that I can't remember for the life of me), on the net. It was in Word format, IIRC, and was nicely enough laid out and a lot of fun to play. When he got nervous about hanging out with the "published" authors, we assured him he was published -- he had a game out that other people he did not know were playing. He was one of us, and could move into the group as a peer.

    The second guy had never put out a game, and had a really fucking big mouth. Typical con know-it-all. I got tired of him fast, and so rather than talking to him like a human being I told him something along the lines of "Come back and talk to me once you've actually published something." He got defensive and quickly left, which is just what I'd wanted. I'd pulled the elitest power barrier down in his face -- I was a published king, he a lowly pleeb.

    So, "published" divides me from you, and us from them. But it also gets more interesting.

    Once upon a time I published my own RPG book in .pdf format under a free liscence from another company. It got very high reviews, made me a small bit of money, and got me a lot of experience and knowledge. I also sold it on CD at cons -- making me "published" again -- this time self published.

    Then last night I'm talking to Ben, and saying how I'm not a real Forgie yet because I haven't yet "published" my own game. By which I mean, I haven't self published a game of my own design based upon nothing but my own work. At this point I was using "published" in a very exclusionary Indie sceen sense in order to exclude myself. (Ben, btw, had none of that and smacked me upside the head.)

    The point of all this being that the games we give the title of "published" to and the games we will not give the title of "published" to sometimes has to do with platonic ideals of "the public" -- but just as often has to do with whom we want to give or take prestige from.
