\n Here's a completely unstudied (as far as I know) wild guess, but... does it make sense?

1. Games like Fiasco, Baron Munchausen, A Penny for Your Thoughts, etc, they look like they can really "put you on the spot".
"Here, be creative, right now! Lest ye be judged!"

This is scary. I'm not saying whether or not it really is that difficult to create on the spot. Maybe it's not, maybe it is. But. It looks difficult and it looks like you'll be judged and found inadequate.

And the normal people who like games like Trivial Pursuit and Scrabble, they're like "uh... no thanks, I'm... busy that day", they see this creativity as something that is reserved for creative types and artists and "those others" with their paint stained overalls and untuned guitars, not us normal people with cars and a couple of hours over for Candy Crush.

2. So then the target audience is for those hippies, those drama majors?
Sure, here we have a greater niche but here the scare-factor is "Oh, it's too nerdy, I tried once and it was so complicated with opportunity attacks and proficiency bonus and procedural scenes and tilt tables and and..."



In short: a mainstream game needs to consider:
1. Creative demands that can scale. That allow your creative contributions to shine when you've got some ideas, and let you to just sit back and go along when you don't have any ideas. (B/X and BECMI did this well. Fiasco actually also does ok, you can just say "I want to resolve", as long as someone is creative around the table. APFyT is awful in this regard though.)

2. Simple and deep rules. Nerds, wait, make that all game designers, always overestimate how difficult learning and understanding their rules are for new players.

eh.

    \n I was investigating a bit about #1 just yesterday, and found this.

    So basically, if players know their creativity is going to be evaluated, or in the case of these games, it's going to compete against other's players creativity it's way harder to become creative. This study alone justifies my aversion to play like, 80% of story games I've seen through all the time I've been lurking SG. It's not that they were bad games, it's just that a lot of them are too upfront demanding in creative terms that I often feel like, even being a creative person, that I can't make quality material on the spot in the same amount and consistence the game requires.

    About #2 (and a bit of #1 too) I've been analyzing my group and my own skillset, and how these engage and relate with different games. There are people who can literally hear numbers talk, they can see an excel spreadsheet and feel a living town in it. But they have problems if everything is about relating words and/or spatial imagination.

    If you tell me to throw a handfull of sand to the air in my imagination, stop it in the air and then imagine all those sandgrains rotate 180 around an imaginary line, shaping the same amount of circles as grains, I can easily do so, rotate that shape in my imagination and draw it from any point of view. This paragraph alone is enough to give my number-oriented pal a headache. Literally.

    But then again show me that spreadsheet and it won't be nothing but boring number to me, and while through some effort I can understand the information, I'd find it more useful if it was a list of words, a map or a drawing.

    So, when my pal designed a game, it wasn't a surprise for me that it used a d100+attributes+skill+ specialization bonuses besides items, virtues and flaws that modified that, along with situational modifiers of +/-20 and +/-50, while insisting the game was insanely simple (well, it is for his standards and I get most of the game, but I wouldn't offer it to a complete newbie as his first RPG. I couldn't be able to GM it properly anyway. A lot of people can, but there's also a lot of people like me who can't)

    When I designed my game "Offline" after a lot of trial, error and further research of different mechanics hacked from both trad and dirty hippie games, I gave it a good thought about what I've been calling Accessibility in various threads recently, which comes down to lower your design minimum requirements so people with different skillsets can engage with it completely and then add those skillsets to increase their own fun and the fun of the people around them.

    At some points it means just to explain things briefly more than once in more than one section, and cross reference as well, because people with memory issues will certainly appreciate this. Examples, Actual play reports, Visual aids like flowcharts and Comics, Video tutorials, every little thing counts and is crucial to help players learn the proper way to play the game. It doesn't matter if they deconstruct it later and make their own way of playing the game, as designers we have to be responsible because there are lots of pieces in our games that are potential problems for gameplay, and it's our duty to identify them and either smooth them out or prevent players against them. Check this out.

    At other points it passes for making good playing advice into rules. You need to be that assertive because when players read a rulebook they are looking for clear procedures to get things going in the best way possible. By offering them as advice instead of rules, you come off as insecure, like, you don't know if this good advice actually works or not, otherwise you would have included it in the rules. Of course, you still need to be careful about what of that advice you turn into rules and how do you express them, but rest assured, nobody follows rules they don't like nor blames the author for putting those there. As a designer, no matter if you write "You must do X in this game" you're not forcing people to do X, they will easily discern by themselves if it's a good/necessary part of the procedure or not. How many of you uses encumbrance rules because the book told you so? Check this out.

    Then at other moments it passes for offering optional rules for people to add their skillset to the fullest. The basic system doesn't need too much numbers, but you can easily offer an alternate character sheet that does consider several more variables, and a explanation of how to use them. You can give alternate rules about grid and minis use as well, or even just suggest how can the game be otherwise modified to suit different tastes.

    On the end Accessibility means: Designer, put on the reader's shoes, don't assume they have your skillset nor your problems with certain mechanics, nor your knowledge about RPGs, nor your hate/love for some type of games, etc. The more different kind of readers you can make your game for, the better.

    In short: a mainstream game needs to consider:

    1. Creative demands that can scale. That allow your creative contributions to shine when you've got some ideas, and let you to just sit back and go along when you don't have any ideas.

    2. Simple and deep rules. Nerds, wait, make that all game designers, always overestimate how difficult learning and understanding their rules are for new players.
    \nI completely agree with your analysis and I've been messing around with a simple diceless system that may serve as a backbone for a shy-people-friendly game:

    - The role of Narrator is rotative and as a Narrator you have complete freedom (some limits/guidelines may be required in a final ruleset).
    - After a stipulated period of time (30 sec or 5 sentences or as soon as any other player raises his hand) the Narrator must stop and the other players can suggest possible continuations for the story (if they want). Then the current Narrator must "buy" the continuation that he finds more inspiring to the player that suggested it by paying a token.
    - Once the Narrator runs out of tokens his role is passed to the player that has most tokens in that moment and the story continues.

    For an even more shy-friendly enviroment you could try the following optional rule:

    - Instead of proposing a new continuation you can extend the continuation suggested by another player (adding a twist or a detail that you consider interesting), but you must pay a token to that player in advance, so you are going to lose a token unless your extended version is selected by the current Narrator.

    This system (I call it Distributed Storytelling System) was meant to be played online (whith the Narrator writing a blog-post once a day and the players suggesting continuations in the comments) but, when played around a table, it allows shy players to sit in silence for as long as they wish and contribute only when they feel prepared. It even allow people to enter or leave the game at any moment, so you only need 3 engaged players to make it work at any time.

    In that sense, and taking into account that this is just a draft, it satisfies your demands of simplicity and shy-players friendlyness.

    For example, another optional rule that I'm planning to include is that anybody can give anybody else a token every time he considers that the other person has done something great.

    The idea is to use the tokens to express mechanically things that usually are just implicit (from "Ei! That was a great idea!" to "I want to hear your version of this").

    At the same time, the "please, give me ideas to continue the story" mechanic may serve as a lifesaver to shy people once "in the spot". They can just say a pair of obvious sentences and ask for creative help as soon as they feel stuck.

    The final objective is to help everyone to overcome their fear by giving them the option (instead of the obligation) to participate. The biggest risk is to get shy players overwhelmed by the creativity of less shy players (and this is why the "you can extend another players continuation" was created).

    \n There's nothing to create in karaoke, you're not demanded to make up a song or a melody, and it's expected that people will suck at it, otherwise it won't be fun. Besides that, how many people would be willing to do karaoke with total strangers without alcohol?

    Anyway, all those obstacles (Expected reward, competition, surveillance, time limits, and evaluation) can be overcome if presented in a less demanding way, if used sparingly or just in different moments of the game, as most of us have proven in different games.

    We've been playing for ages using XP, loot and more as rewards, we've been having fun betraying each other in Paranoia, we've been playing while being observed in conventions and faced time limits there and even in our own houses. And it's not like Story Games aren't played at all. I've tried my some of own designs around player created material and found out that it's all about how do you ask the players for the material, when, how much do you ask and your reaction to their input.

    -You can ask for background stories between sessions ans thus eliminate time pressure, often you will find players making pages and pages of historical background around their characters.

    -Right after one player has given an input, the next will feel reassured with this cue and give their own input. Each input gives more clues to the next player about how to present information and the limits of their agency on the fiction.

    -React positively by not blocking the first player input and inmediately building over it, this acts as an incredible reward and builds trust instantly. You will see players autoedit themselves later and if you're often consequent with whatever was stated from the start of the game, things can go wild but never gonzo.

    -Avoid Gonzo by blocking but based only on consistency. This means that players can keep coming up with strange ideas, but only as long as these don't contradict/negate the existance of ideas previously established. If it makes sense for the world they are creating, embrace it. If it can't be because anything you established before, offer a different suggestion.

    I've got more of this tricks that now I consider as rules for whenever I play, but right now I don't have enough time to list them all, my boss is about to come. I'm talking here about tried, and tested stuff, to which I've just found some studies that support and explain why.

      "X is happening; how does your character react?" mostly relies on the existing fiction as a guide for the answer, and if in doubt, a player can draw on their own life experiences and their experiences from consuming other fiction to help them answer the question. People make decisions all the time. People see other people \u2013 fictional or not \u2013 make decisions all the time, and usually empathetically understand the basis for that decision-making.       This doesn't mean that a question of one type will necessarily be harder than a question of the other type \u2013 for certain people, each might be easy. But they call on different skills, so they're not going to be equivalently difficult by default.

      \n Very briefly (don't have time for a full post), but two things:

      1. I'm beginning to think, more and more, that the important skill of a roleplaying/story/gamer is not imagination and creativity (I've yet to a see person fail on this front, unless given a total blank page-type situation, which is not what most good games do). Rather, it's the ability to *limit* your creative contributions in a way which suits the game being played.

      Anyone can invent something new: "Ok, how about a robot? Robots are cool. And it's red." Limiting yourself to only contributing in certain, specific ways (who gets to say what about which topics, what are your "narrative authorities", and what suits the tone of the game), that's the tricky part, and not at all natural.

      "Be obvious" is an example of this. It says to limit yourself to your first instinct, and not to try to overreach. Not a natural way to do things, but effective when grasped. (In my experience, gamers who think they're "not creative enough" don't lack creativity. Instead, they have set for themselves - in their minds - an improbably high bar for "creative" means. In most RPGs, saying "I swing my sword", or "it's a giant monster! with three eyes!" is more than enough. But these players tie themselves in knots thinking that that's not good enough, and then they freeze up. When I've been able to explain this to someone - and they actually listen - they usually relax and become awesome, creative gamers within minutes.)


      2. I think that positive feedback is really, really key here.

      David Berg once told me that he thinks the most fundamental successful act of roleplaying is this (I'm paraphrasing what he said very loosely):

      * Player A makes up something, makes some kind of statement about a fictional reality.

      * Other players respond with: "Heck, yeah! That totally happens!/That's exactly how it is!"

      The positive feedback there creates an engagement which is tremendously exciting and really, really fun.

      If you see two people going:

      A - "There's a big monster!"
      B - "Oh no! Does it have big teeth?"
      A - "Yeah! It has big teeth, like THIS! And really sharp!"
      B - "Oh, no! That's scary!"
      A - "Yeah, it sure is! Now it growls and acts scary!"
      B - "Yikes!"
      A - "So what are you going to do!"
      B - "I draw my sword and I chop its head off, like THIS!"
      A - "Yeah! That's exactly what happens! Its head flies off and it's dead!"
      B - "Wow! Yeay! Awesome!"

      That exchange will be enough to leave them both smiling and laughing, because each is engaging fully and enthusiastically with everything the other is saying. It's a positive feedback loop which is at the core of successful roleplaying, and, I think, totally fulfilling on its own.

      Everything else get layered on top of that, where we go from simple play pretend to more nuanced forms of play where we can create different layers of engagement (challenge, discovery, risk, etc).

      \n Indeed. There comes a point where simple agreement is boring, and where denying certain additions as accurate starts to reinforce the feeling of it being real.

      When you say that you chop three-quarters through the monster's neck and it's still unfazed, you are, first and foremost, accepting the reality of what the other player just said: they are struggling with the monster and they have delivered a powerful blow with their sword.       \n I'm talking about something far more basic. It's not about whether you accept the full reality of what the other person is saying, a la "say yes, and" in an improv context. It's that you see their creative input as valid and meaningful. They've added something to the fiction - in a way appropriate to the game being played (could be in-character, out-of-character, or whatever other way) - and you respond to that, by engaging with it fully.

      In a traditional "I say what my character does, the GM describes the effects" kind of game, we know implicitly that the player's statement is one of action/intent: my character *tries* to do this, right now. If you say, "I chop his head off!", you know that's crossing the line, and so you're not surprised when there's a little retcon ("Actually, not all the way...").

      Where it would break down is if the other party didn't recognize the legitimacy of your statement at all. For instance, I respond with, "Nah. Actually you didn't hit him hard. It was kind a weak, half-assed swing."

      What makes the game work is that you've just said something made-up ("My character swings his sword as hard as he can, trying to chop off his head!") and I react by acknowledging that ("Yes! You swing the sword as hard as you can! And you're strong, so, yeah, you totally do that! And it definitely *could* chop off someone's head."). I don't try to undermine your ability to make that statement, but engage with it fully.

      It's weird to talk about, because it's so *natural* to us ("how else would you do it?"), but, if you think about it, that's rather a very weird thing. Usually, in everyday life, when people make things up, we *don't* play along. In agreeing to play a roleplaying game, we are quite intentionally engaging in a strange and different way of relating to each other. We are agreeing to deal with untrue, fictional statements as though they had *weight*.
