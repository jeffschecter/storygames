\n What are the physical totems that in your experience indicate "roleplaying is happening!"

There's research that suggests addiction is triggered or intensified by three main factors:

A familiar environment associated with your addiction,
The presence of friends and acquaintances who are also addicts,
And the presence of paraphernalia associated with your addiction.

So, for gaming, environment is pretty obvious, and "fellow addicts" is pretty obvious. Let's enumerate a list of paraphernalia!

Here's my list so far:


Character sheets
Dice
Cards
Minifigs
Maps
Illustrations/Drawings/Diagrams
Photos
Props
R-maps
Story maps
Counters/tokens
Books
A Board

Obviously, not all need to be present, but all can reinforce the experience.

    \n The Table.

    Orie (my wife) and I always eat our food Japanese-style: Around the kotatsu (think "large coffee table") in our living room. When guests come over, we eat there too.

    My dining room may ostensibly be used maybe 1-2 times a year for (only) dining, but in all other respects it is absolutely a Dojo for Gaming. Even my wife corrected me when looking for a new table:

    Me: Hmmm. Not sure if this would work as a good dining table...
    Orie: Dining? You mean GAMING table, right?
    Me: Ahhhhhh. Heh.

    The center of that gaming dojo sits The Table. Upon Which We Game.

    This was the table we used for years: https://picasaweb.google.com/106430054160848801055/CreativeEleganceTable
    It sang to me when I saw it; I got it and used it for years.

    A few months ago, I had an inspiration to make the room a better place, a better dojo: And indeed that's what happened. I got a new table: https://twitter.com/#!/diamondsutra/status/154675334959218688/photo/1

    I cycle through dice, as my Favorites change every few years (although I totally think of them as totems of the table). I think the dice cup is a fetish as well.

    But the most fetishized thing is the table: The altar in the middle of the dojo of gaming that takes up about 1/5 of the space on the first floor of my home.

    -Andy

    \n Since I mentioned...\n
    An MP3 player pre-loaded with roughly 12,000 sound effects and atmospheric music pieces
    Decent set of battery operated (or rechargeable) speakers for said MP3 player
    \n...I'll elaborate.

    Most of the sounds and music I use in my games are orchestral soundtracks for movies or atmospheric pieces. I typically have them divided into playlists based on the game genre we're playing. The music sits in the background typically playing at a level below the conversation. I often try to choose music without distracting lyrics (or any lyrics at all). Maybe 6000 of the files fit this criteria.

    As someone who used to buy sound equipment and MP3 players for a major retail chain a few yeas back, I still have a dozen or so MP3 players lying around. When I want to get fancy, I might throw together a player with a half-dozen simple sound effects; "gunshots", the clang of sword on armour, a melee in the distance, the eerie cry of a night predator. I predetermine a bunch of effects that I think will be useful in the upcoming game, and with only a few files to sort through, I can pretty much instantly get the effect I need whenever I need it. If I don't have the file, I don't stop play until I find it; we just move on.

    Sometimes, If I know that a game will last 3 hours, I'll go to the effort of specifically programming a play-list that builds up tension over the course of that 3 hours. But most times, the music runs on random play.

    Another use for music draws from the soundtrack of a Battletech computer game launched in the mid 90s...I've lost the game, but I've still got the sound files that I ripped from the CD. The game included a series of heartbeat pulse sounds in a variety of speeds, these are sorted into speed order on the music device being used. In a few game I've had these on "single track repeat" through the entire game. When adrenaline is starting to pump, the track is moved slightly above resting pulse (and repeats at that level), when action is thick we move up to a cycle of a fast heartbeat...when he action is over we gradually work back to the resting pulse...when a character dies, we get the ECG flatline pulse.
