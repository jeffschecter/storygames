When Gamers Rule the Earth

Josh Roby
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
The world will be so much nicer when we're in charge.

On a lazy Sunday afternoon, when you have nothing to do and no desire to be productive, you can stroll on down to the community gaming center and find a pick-up game from among the many that spontaneously start up at all hours.

The municipal library will have games shelved by genre, a section in the kid's department, and a copious roleplaying theory section.

People holding PhDs in game design -- "game doctors" -- will be seen as an essential part of any community, much like doctors and clergymen.

Television as we know it today will be replaced by interactive video conferencing, allowing anyone, anywhere, to play Primetime Adventures and create their own television shows.

    Heh. Actual play cohousing. That's a riot.

    I live in a graduate student co-op in Eugene, Oregon (currently visiting family and friends in B-town Indiana for the summer), with the Student's Cooperative Association of Eugene.

    Here's my experiences with co-op living, writ small.

    1) Dishes, kitchens, food and bathrooms are the #1 concerns with a co-operative living situation. People doing their own damn dishes is a constant battle. Splitting cooking duties works really well, especially when you have 2x as many people as nights when you have shared meals. Division of labor means that each person doesn't need to be doing more than 5-10 hours of housework per week, and this number goes down a bit as you get more people.

    2) As for social contract, we have the advantage of all being graduate students/college grads who are very studious. Last year, we had one person working on her second B.A. (so she could go into grad school), a 30-something in continuing studies, and towards the end of the year, a Live-in SO of one of the students. But through nearly-weekly meetings, we were able to bring up issues on all levels, from dishes to maintenance to planning of food buying, and it worked remarkably well, especially considering the absurd amount of busy most all of us were as 1st year graduate students.

    If folks have other questions about co-op living, just lemme know. My experience wouldn't be representative of an all-gamer or almost-all-gamer cooperative housing situation, but I'm more than glad to toss in more 'Actual Play' anecdotes and notes.

    The more redundancy in the living space, the better, I think. Seriously, I think it'd be worthwhile to look into existing rental spaces. Buy a small apartment building with, like, 10 apartments, and you have at least ten kitchens, probably 15-20 bedrooms (depending on the ratio of bedrooms per apartment), at least ten full baths and probably more. Knock out some walls in the central living areas to make them bigger and to make it more a "communal living space" than just "a bunch of apartments where gamers happen to live". I'd suggest maybe buying one of those converted factories with the massive lofts, cause they're probably the easiest to "open up" between the various bedrooms.

    This way, huge communal cooking meals are easy, while making sure that people who might just not want to share that particular meal have a space to cook their own. ("hey," I might say, "I don't feel like Lasagna tonight. I'll cook my meal over here, then still come eat with y'all").

    Just from living with roommates it's obvious that one needs good rules about washing dishes (generally speaking: wash your own when cooking for yourself, everyone pitch in to wash when someone's cooked a meal for everyone) and other household chores. The bathroom duties could probably be handled by the occupants of adjacent bedrooms. Etc. etc.
