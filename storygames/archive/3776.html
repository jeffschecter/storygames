    \n Judd,

    Absolutely. And many of us have had pretty nasty shit said about us. Most of us, I think, have dealt with it without going to booze and hookers to block out the pain.

    However, I think we need to make an honesty mark in the area of "inside/outside" and think about if there is a difference between the two.

    If random internet shitcock says Dictionary of Mu is for shit, do you care much? If I post a 20 page long review of how Dictionary of Mu is shit and how no one who wants RPGs to exist as a medium should buy it, do you care much? If Ron Edwards comes out and says that he's sorry he ever allowed Dictionary of Mu to be published for Sorcerer, do you care much?

    As always, I use hyperbole. But really, I think for a lot of us there is a difference in weight between what someone who is "inside" or "part of the community" says, and what those outside it say. If Clinton harshes your game up one side and down the other, is it the same thing as if the RPG Pundit does it? If someone on ENWorld reviews your game to an audience who probably wouldn't have bought it anyway, is it the same as if one of the big ten post to Storygames and the Forge about how it shows a lazy and uninspiring ethic with no vision?

    I strongly think there are two different conversations to be had there. One is "dealing with random shit heads" or even "dealing with public reaction to a published work" and the other is "dealing with it when members of a peer or support community react negatively to your work, especially in a way that may affect sales or community respect."

    Cause I find it much easier to talk shit about, say Artesia (which is an indie game) than I do about Nine Worlds, because I know Matt and like the bastard, where Mark is just some dude. When I go to talk shit about Scion, I know some of the guys who wrote for it, but I've no real sense of community with them. When I go to talk shit about TSOY, otoh, I see Clinton's face looking like I just slapped him in the balls and chicken out.

    Of course, chances are that Matt and Clinton would probably deal with my criticism just fine. But the fact that they're in a community with them, that I like and respect them and want them to like and respect me does influence how I do or do not talk about things. It doesn't cripple me or anything, but there is a social situation there that is different than the one I face when I rip Changeling: The Lost limb from limb.

    And the truth is, I have seen some really bad reactions to criticism in the community. Some of them have been outright negative, some of them have just been overly defensive and/or dismissive, and some of them have been whiny and hurt or smile about it in public and then bitch about it in private to people who know both involved parties. So much as Matt and Clinton may deal with it, I don't know for sure, and I do have a good feeling that there are others who would not deal with it well at all.

    \n Sorry... can't hold back any longer! What a horribly loaded term 'negative criticism' is!
    \n
    \nHow about good old 'Constructive Feedback'?
    \n
    \nFor ten years now I have written shared universe fanfic. As a writer, there have been two things I have learned to hate. The first is the adoring fanboy ('OhmiGOD! That was great. I've given up food as I sit next to my PC for the next installment and I am starting a letter writing campaign to have you installed as Writer for Life on Batman. I bow to your awesome!!') and the second is the bile-filled lunatic ('OhmiGOD! That was horrible. If I have to read another word of your putrid drivel I will gouge my eyes out with a rusty nail. I have sent a hitman out to eliminate your family from the gene pool for crimes against literature. Fucktard!'). Neither are helpful.
    \n
    \nFeedback that seeks to inform the writer of what you liked about the story, what you didn't like about the story, what you found confusing about the story - thats where the meat of the review is and thats what spurs me on to do better things. The feedback can be mostly positive, mostly negative or somewhere inbetween but as long as it is CONSTRUCTIVE it works.
    \n
    \nThere are even nice little rules for giving constructive feedback:
    \n
    \n- Read and understand what the designer has aimed for ('Game X has too narrow a setting focus' vs 'I understand the narrow setting focus of Game X but I wish it could have been easier to adapt elsewhere')
    \n- Own your feedback ('EVERYONE thinks that Game X is unplayable' vs 'I think Game X is unplayable')
    \n- Target the game and not the person ('Designer Y knows nothing about what makes superheroes click' vs 'This game doesn't seem to address what makes superheroes click')
    \n- Say what you like about something first, creating an atmosphere that shows you are not a bile merchant.
    \n- Be constructive in your negative comments - ('Game X's combat system really blows chunks' vs ' I found the combat system of Game X to be counter-intuitive and at odds with the rest of the system. Particularly A, B and C....')
    \n- Give working examples of the stuff that doesn't work for you and highlight where your disconnect happens.
    \n- Accept that the designer may read your feedback, but isn't contratually bound to agree or change their game because of it. They write, you buy, you comment, they evaluate - end of.
    \n
    \nThats got to be a good way of being honest and yet not offensive to someone over something that is essentially a labour of love? I know I would exchange one long, well written piece of feedback on the good and the bad of what I do than one million fanboy explosions. They serve no purpose apart from to give me a momentary warm feeling.
    \n
