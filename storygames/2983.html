What's a better name for the "The Mountain Witch Trick"?

Andy
May 2007 edited May 2007 in Story Games
Basically, what I'm referring to is this (I used it a lot, glaringly obviously so, in my "The Mountain Witch - Tenra Bansho Crossover" this last weekend):

GM: "There's a mural on the wall here. It disturbs you (points at a player). What is the mural, and why do you find it disturbing?"

GM: "As you pass the graveyard, a gravestone marker attracts your attention (points at player). Whose is it?"

GM: "The ghost mouths something to you. What is it saying?"

That sort of thing. This is something I refer to as "The Mountain Witch trick", since it's used a lot in that game. I use it in a lot of games, particularly when I'm stuck and need a player-nudge for Something Cool to happen soon.

I used to refer to it as... the word I've forgotten*. Basically the counselling trick where you turn the person's statement back into a question at them, ala ELIZA (the computer program):

Person: I like bananas.
Counsellor: Why do you feel that you like bananas?

But I'm trying to think of a better name. Not necessarily "technical term in the consulting world", but if there is such a word I'd love to hear it.

Thoughts?

-Andy

  • Graham
    May 2007 edited May 2007
    Oooh, hold on, again.

    Matthew's right to suggest the term offer to describe this. But it's not "offer" as in "offering" it to the players.

    An offer in improv is any addition to the scene that other players can build on. Roughly translated into game terms, an offer would be anything added to the shared fiction for players to build on:

    GM: There's a mural on the wall, depicting people dying in battle.

    Now, there's another improv concept: the blind offer. In a blind offer, you add something to the scene, but you only partially define it, leaving it to someone else to define.

    Translated into game terms, it's pretty much what Andy suggests...

    GM [makes blind offer]: There's a mural on the wall. The paintings on it are disturbing.
    Player [defines blind offer]: Yeah, they're scenes of people dying in battle.

    Except, in Andy's original post, the GM is framing part of the blind offer as a question. But that's fine. It's still a blind offer.

    Graham

      Ten years have passed. Over one fourth of my life. (27% of it.)

      I think over the years this has still been called mountainwitching or the Mountain Witch technique.

      Some thoughts: "prompting" isn't good since that's much wider. Even just "What do you do?" is prompting.

      Any references to Eliza or Rogerian therapy are misleading as well, I think. (As one-time implementor of an Eliza clone (it's one of the exercises in PAIP).)

      As for the technique it's good. I started using it very tamely in 2001 at first with very save things like "You're in your own apartment, what does it look like?" and then more extreme, like "You've swapped brains with the BBEG, what was his plan?"

      There are some games (not TMW obv) where I deliberately abstain from mountainwitching throughout the campaign. (I mean, "what does your apartment look like" doesn't count, but otherwise I like a clear role separation PC-portrayer/world-portrayer for those particular games.)

      Also, another stray, OT thought: I've seen some GMs answer player questions by "I dunno, you tell me". I'd rather them ask another player. Now I don't mean for "The mural disturbs you, why?" GM-originated questions but for more procedural questions generated player-side. Martha (player): "What's behind that door?" [GM turns from Martha to Alice] "What does she see?"

      We were experimenting with this in Fiasco last Friday. One character was rummaging through another players secret lair.
    • Paul_T

      I think that using moments of hesitation or uncertainty to "pass the buck" to another player is an incredibly powerful tool for collaborative story gaming. There's always *someone* at the table who has a ready idea, and passing it around means that everything that develops and everything that happens has multiple people getting input into it. This gives a game tremendous forward momentum and feels really exhilarating in practice.

      I even put together a game which operates on this as its only mechanic. It's surprisingly powerful and effective. (As the Worm Turns)

      For what it's worth, I like the term "fishing", simply because "prompting", while perhaps more accurate, could mean so many other things.

      Vincent's "ask provocative questions" is also good.
