Do you use gender neutral language in your RPG text?

If yes, how?

Say someone wrote: "I wonder who my GM at the con will be. I hope he is friendly."

Methods I've seen to make "he" gender neutral in this example:
1. "I wonder who my GM at the con will be. I hope the GM is friendly."
2. "I wonder who my GM at the con will be. I hope he or she is friendly."
3. "I wonder who my GM at the con will be. I hope they are friendly."
4. "I wonder who my GM at the con will be. Whom I hope is friendly."

I generally prefer #3, using "they", as it sounds very natural and not as wordy as "he or she". But many people consider this improper as GM is singular and "they" is plural.

What do you prefer?

    But I think it's important to make an exception to grammatical orthodoxy in the particular case of this hobby, to counter the latent "boy's club" assumptions. Some gaming texts have adopted the convention of simply alternating from masculine to feminine pronouns between paragraphs, or similarly, arbitrarily assigning feminine pronouns to "the GM" and masculine pronouns to "the players." These seem to be a fine compromise, because they simultaneously read like proper English while also implying that women can and should play this game.

    If I had my druthers, you could use a gender-neutral neologism, but you can probably only get away with this if you're writing a science fiction game.

    Gender-neutral pronouns are not actually a big deal. That is, they are really, really easy to avoid.

    Now technically, and as has already been pointed out, using 'he' as the default is correct. Using 'they' instead is incorrect, and it's not only technically incorrect (with all the prissy and it-doesn't-matter bullshit emphasis you want to put on it), but using 'they' occludes and confuses meaning. They means more than one person. Don't use it to mean one person, because readers will get confused and think you mean more than one person. Not all the time, no, but enough that it's a bad choice when there are much better alternatives. Alternating pronouns also does this, to a slightly less degree, but has the added bonus of making you sound like a schizophrenic.

    There is a grammatically correct alternative in "one". As in "One doesn't do such things." Unfortunately, it's not commonly used or familiar for most audiences, especially in the US. It's a pity, cause it's the most elegant and hassle-free solution.

    There are gender-neutral neologisms as have already been suggested. They are less familiar than using "one" and therefore, in my mind, less useful.

    It's relatively trivial, however, to turn around sentence structure so that the default pronoun isn't used. Instead of, "I wonder who my GM at the con will be, I hope he is friendly." just write, "I wonder if my GM will be friendly." When you're going the distance, do as Shreyas suggests and just give your hypothetical people names and then use the appropriately gendered pronoun. And specificity is one of the keys of good writing, anyway. It's a good habit to, uh, engender.

    Then there's the option of intentionally breaking the he-default rule. If you break a rule unintentionally, it's a mistake; if you break a rule intentionally, it's a statement. In Full Light, Full Steam I assign female pronouns to all ungendered persons of authority. I don't point it out, I don't screen it with names for the hypothetical people, it's just there and it's a subtle jibe at established gender power structures.

    So that's my two cents -- either write so it's not necessary, or make a decision for a reason and stick with it.

    February 2007 edited February 2007
    Oh, and Brand's right that it depends what kind of book you're writing. "They" gives a modern tone:

    "The Active Player plays a card. They then narrate an advantage to their character."

    He/she sounds rather technical:

    "The Active Player plays a card. He/she then narrates an advantage to his/her character."

    Ze sounds otherworldly:

    "The Active Player plays a card. Ze then narrates an advantage to zir character."

    One sounds formal and old-fashioned:

    "If one is the Active Player, one plays a card, then narrates an advantage to one's character."

    So Ze, for example, sounds great in Shock: (sci-fi) but jarring in The Hours (a horror thing set in a mansion). So it depends on the voice you want to use for the text.

    Larry, Joshua BR, you guys are killin' me. You have got to stop with the "it's technically incorrect" thing. You're killin' me.

    Folks, don't let them snow you with confident, authoritative pronouncements. I'm sure they're nice guys, and they even have fine ideas about what to do if you're trying to avoid the singular they. But it is not technically incorrect. It is socially incorrect (admittedly, in some often important circles). Singular they is grammatically correct by every standard important to people who study language seriously and technically (usage, historical attestation, distribution, comprehensibility, internal consistency within the linguistic system, etc.). Even among linguists who disagree about very fundamental issues about language (such as whether we're born with some sort of generative grammar or not), there is simply no controversy about singular they. None. It doesn't matter whether you cut the cake historically or synchronically, you're going to get the same result. There is worldwide consensus among linguists on this, and there has been for a very long time. Do you know how rare a good consensus is among scholars? We get paid to prove each other wrong!

    Please don't believe this old bit of folklore. There are good reasons to avoid singular they. Most of them have to do with the popularity of this bit of folklore and some of its tiresome ilk among social gatekeepers. But don't avoid using singular they just because you've been tricked or bullied into believing it is grammatically incorrect. Avoid it for a good reason, like maybe you really want that job, and it's really not a big deal to avoid they. Avoid it for the same reason you get a nice, new haircut before you go to the interview.

    I side with Joshua and with Rich. Bugaboos about singular they are like the bugaboos about double negatives and ending a sentence with a preposition. However, you can do some simple things to avoid even raising a question, like... using plural they where possible. Compare:

    • The GM should surprise his players.
    • GMs should surprise their players.

    There are cases where you can't avoid a singular, so you adopt another technique, like using one, specifying an imaginary person's gender, switching to the imperative (surprise your players!) or whatever's appropriate.

    The one ironclad rule is: Don't write a disclaimer about the use of language. If you're going with the "GM is female, PCs are male" approach, don't explain it, just do it. Disclaimers are whiney and a waste of space. Only exception I can think of is if you feel the need to use a neologism, you should have a one-sentence explanation of it up front. But personally, I would avoid the neologisms, because they never last; what happened to "E" and "Co"? That's what's going to happen to "Ze".

    Ryan, it probably wasn't any sort of dyslexia, just the normal reading process getting mucked up. The gender-neutral pronouns in Shock: (sie? I forget which it is) probably just aren't one of your sight words -- words that you recognize on sight without recognizing and decoding the individual letters. Generally, your brain sort of expects anything four (or five) letters or smaller to be a sight word, and focuses its letter recognition and decoding processes on longer words. Your eyeball literally flicks right over the little words with your brain assuming that that flick is enough to recognize it easily. (Sie is actually one of the less disruptive neologisms since it can be easily mistaken in that flick for 'she' -- which kind of totally defeats the purpose of using the word.) The 'missed' words start piling up until you can't decode the meaning of whole sentences. You have to go back and ploddingly go word to word, making sure you read each and every one, forcing your brain and eye to get every single letter. It's like reading when you're in kindergarten (or whenever you learned to read) -- exhausting. In a parallel point, it's very much like games that use attribute abbreviations throughout the rules text -- very hard for new readers to follow, but once you get used to it (ie, add ST, DX, and PS to your sight words) you barely notice it any more.

      I know it's going back a way in the thread, but I wanted to address something.

      Use of the second person to avoid this works, but is not some surprising technique. That is, it's a major choice to decide whether you want to use the intimate tone of "you and me" or to use the formal tone of "The GM and the author." Most importantly, don't ever switch between these modes in the middle of your text. Definitely don't use this just to avoid having to use one of the other methods, and then switch back to a formal tone elsewhere.

      People report that when the tone changes back and forth between formal and informal that this makes reading really hard. Possibly worse, it makes the writing seem amaturish (I'm not making a judgment here, I'm just saying what many people find to be true). Vincent made the decision to go with informal and intimate all the way through, and that works for him. I really like the formal mode myself to the extent that people have commented that my texts are unpleasant to read like textbooks in some cases.

      I like textbooks...

      Anyhow, yes, in terms of ease of writing, this argues in favor of using the informal tone on this one point. For myself, my writing tends to be formal enough that it's completely consistent with my tone to use "They", "The player," or even "One." I know it probably sounds archaic, but I like that.

      So, how would I write the challenge text?

      "The Leader can choose their own character if they like. One must explain - but never defend - one's decision."

      But in many cases, the problem is that this is lazy - you're just converting the sentence that popped into your head. If my sentence looks odd, it's because it's a conversion of one that works best with "he" in it. Better writing is to come up with an entirely diferent way of stating the idea.

      "The Leader selects any character, and then explains - but does not defend - the decision."

      Much mo betta.

      Mike

      P.S. I learned this lesson (not lazily converting, but rewriting) from Rich Forrest above, if I recall correctly, when he was editing Universalis or something for me. In all things gramatical, I defer to Rich. His point above isn't being given enough due. It's not "correct/incorrect" that really matters, it's what works.

      I guess I should drop in here since Shock: has been mentioned twice already.

      Under the Bed uses "they" because it's colloquial, which is appropriate in a game about children.

      Shock: uses sie/hir because it's a neologism that gets to the core of one's cultural assumptions, which is what the game's about. Obviously, it makes it hard to read. That's because it's not saying exactly what you think it's saying.

      I always use a gendered pronoun when the subject has a gender.

      God never gets a pronoun (which is a separate, but maybe related, issue).

      So, yes, I use gender neutral language because gender isn't at the core of what I'm discussing, though the question of gender might be. If I write a game like Bliss Stage, I'll give genders.

      Incidentally, I think Fred's Fudge standard is really good. It personalizes those jobs, to make the GM "she" and the players "he". It makes it so they're people, not just positions around a table. It could be willfully misread, but I think most are willing to see what that's saying.
