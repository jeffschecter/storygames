\n Looking to see if there is a game system that does this:

Your character has six ability scores, each corresponds to a die.

D4, D6, D8, D10, D12, D20. Weakest is D4, Best is D20.

You need to roll equal or over TN to succeed. Rolling the highest number on the die explodes (you get to roll againand add to total). As you get better at you can increase the chance for the die to explode (example, advance spend a point to let the D4 explode on a 3-4).

Using D&D as an example, lets say you are a Magic-User and your abilities are:

STR = D4
CON = D8
DEX = D12
WIS = D6
INT = D20
CHA = D10

You need to break down a door using STR, the target number is 7. You\u2019ve got to roll a 4 on the D4 and then a 3-4 to succeed.

But for a Fighter who\u2019s STR is a D20, the chance for success is much higher.

Anything like this?

    \n Dice sizes as attribute/skill/whatever scores has of course been done several times. However, most iterations I remember off the top of my head (including the aforementioned Dogs in the Vineyard and, a bit more closely, In a Wicked Age) only use that for "contested" rolls - i.e. die-vs-die, rather than a fixed target number.

    Relevant here is an old @Jason_Morningstar freebie called Dungeon Squad, which is close enough to what you describe, except dice aren't assigned to "STR, INT, WIS, etc." but to "Fighter/Rogue/Mage" descriptors - which is quite reminiscent of "clich\xe9s" in Risus (a 1990s game). Dungeon Squad is also one of the pillars upon which the excellent On Mighty Thews, by @Simon_C arryer, is built.

    On Mighty Thews is definitely a game you might want to check out. Characters are described by the six die types, d4-d20, assigned to: Warrior, Explorer, Sorcerer, 2 freeform/player-defined traits and 1 alignment/personality trait. Your d20 always corresponds with the latter, which is also the least often used - kind of a "special move". On Mighty Thews has contested rolls too (PC-vs-PC or vs-NPC), but most of the time you roll against a fixed target number of "4", which works excellently with the d4-d12 probability distribution. Margins of success matter a lot, though, so that while a 4 is guaranteed to give you what you wanted (a bare bones version of it) most of the time you really hope for a 6 or 8 at least. Trait synergy is represented by rolling multiple dice at the same time and only keeping the highest-rolling. Re-roll tokens are relatively common (you basically collect 1 per scene you didn't roll your d20).

    \n I don't remember what game it was, but an elegant variation I have seen is the opposite -- where smaller dice are better and you have to roll under a target number. Then you always have a chance of rolling what you need without any exploding dice mechanic (because you can always roll a 1).

    Of course exploding dice are fun. But they can also make the math a little strange (e.g., the odds of rolling a 5 on an exploding d4 is the same as rolling a 4; and the odds of smaller dice exploding is much larger than larger dice, which is perhaps counterintuitive), so you have to be sure you want that in your resolution system.
