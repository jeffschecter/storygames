Awesomify...Titan?

Malcolm Craig
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
I'm one of those for whom the first experience of anything approaching roleplaying was the Fighting Fantasy books that became popular in the 1980s. I remember the glee I felt when 'Titan', guide to the Fighting Fantasy world came out! A setting book produced by a mass-market publisher that sold in fairly big number to eager kids such as myself.

And it's still on my booksgellf. It's battered, coffee-stained and slightly foxed but beneath the cheesy stock fantasy tropes, I think there's the potential for a pretty damn good game world in there. So, for those of us who remember The Warlock of Firetop Mountain, The Island of the Lizard King and the brain-metling Sorcery quadrilogy, let's awesomify Titan!

For a start off, it has some great (if redolent of a certain amount of cheese) villains already built in to the setting: Lord Azzur of Blacksand, Shareela the Snow Witch and so on. So I'd say lets make them even better. Port Blacksand should not just be powerful and scary as a piratical city state, it should have vast, well-organised navy in addition to freebooters. And they have developed primitive cannon and harnesed ancient demons to power their most heavily armed and armoured warships.

And a set of mechanics that offer the same childhood thrill that FF offered but with the cool stuff that we also want as adults.

Anyone else with any ideas?

Cheers
Malcolm

    I am hungry for an awesomified Titan. However, since the actual source books are in my mum's attic I will withold from elaborating on the setting qua setting, and instead focus on what I want from a game derived from those terrific solo-game books.

    System-wise, it ought to emphasise the meat of the gamebooks - traps, puzzles, and fights - without just falling back into standard D&Dism. Avoid magic users: PCs should be warriors, rogues or a bit of both (hence my thoughts about doing it with Iron Heroes). Conflicts should be centred round actual objectives, rather than looting + leveling (hence my thoughts in the previously linked thread about Burning Wheel). I think the reward/experience system should be something like Wyrds.

    The game should be anti-hoarding; rather, any special items accumulated should be used up or given away fairly soon, and the mechanics should encourage that. (A straightfoward way would be to tie items, or their effects, to Keys or Beliefs).

    The fighting fantasy books were always fairly lethal, if you ignore the use of that analogue version of 'save points' (using your fingers to preserve the page with your last decision point). I think you would want to preserve some of that. I think you would also be well served by fairly small groups, to retain that lonely, desperate feeling. In the books, you often met an ally who accompanied you part of the way, but was slain before the end. In a tabletop session, each player would be hoping that they would be the protagonist, rather than the avenged ally. (Essentially the Band of Brothers effect). And of course, some of these allies would turn out to be traitors, a common occurrence on Titan.

    How about this: players can play either Protagonist character (PC) or Additional characters (AC). Everyone begins with a PC, but no new ones can be introduced within an adventure.

    When one dies, the player is offered an AC to play -this could be pregenerated by the GM, or thrown together on the fly by the player, but with certain GM-proscribed details. One possibility might be drawing cards that each say "Traitor" or "Ally" and have adventure-specific Wryds/goals already laid out on them.

    After the end of the adventure, anyone playing an AC may convert them into a PC, or dump em and generate a new PC (in which case, the AC may enter into the GM's stable of NPCs, or return at a later date). There should either be reward implications of playing PCs - perhaps only they can achieve goals which produce Legends (adventure-scale Wyrds or resolution of Beliefs) - or some purely social-level recognition: "Man, we totally owned Deathtrap Dungeon!" "Ahem. Sander the crafty owned that dungeon - with a little help from his allies, of course." "...grumble..."

    I'm focusing on this a as a reasonably lightweight game. That's because (a) that's what I like and (b) anything more serious has a vibe of "Grim, Fin-Headed Arserape" as the comic people call it.*

    So I'm looking at the "Making a difference and kicking some arse" angle, rather than the "choose wrong and you're dead" part. If a game is deadly and frustrating, I don't see why I would play it for fun.

    Based on that Savage Worlds would work quite well. Combat can be a lot of fun for example, because players have Bennies they can spend to reduce damage or re-roll if they do badly. So people tend to try crazy stuff ("Only a minus 4? Sure I'll aim for his head!").

    It has a magic system, but to be honest there's no harm in just not using it. As in, a lot of the settings have no magic using PCs. Traditionally, the characters in these books were not overt magic users. You sometimes found a magic sword if you were lucky, but that was about it.

    Hoarding... Hmmm. I'd probably be thinking of this as a series of games set in the same setting. that is, there's a goal ("Kill the Warlock" or "Escape the Deathtrap Dungeon" for example) and that's a session of play or adventure. After it's over, you throw away your characters and create new ones for the next game. Those characters' stories are over.

    * I don't know where this term originates, but it was probably Warren Ellis. It's where people "modernise" classic superheroes by making them "edgy" "dark" and giving them issues. Like MiracleMan turning out to be Hitler's love midget in the Alan Moore series, or Ultimate Giant Man beating his wife because she's mutant scum, but her loving him anyway. Basically it's taking something people used to love for it's childlike innocence as a kid and making it into something fairly unpleasant, then expecting them to like it because hey, it's good 'ol Spidey!

    I actually quite like that type of comic, because I hate superheroes.
