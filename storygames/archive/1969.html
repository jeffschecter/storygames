James_Nostack
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
So there's a thread about "Exploring the Rules through Gameplay" http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=1962&page=1#Item_12

and I'm wondering: are there any games where characters are created through play? Like, in traditional RPG's, you build your dude, and then you play. Sometimes it's wicked fast (like with Capes click-n-locks), but I'm wondering about games where that doesn't exist at all. (I imagine they do, because I'm not up on this stuff.)

Like, Jonathan Walton's KKKKK comes to mind, where one player must be Kublai Khan, and the other player must be his assassin, and the details about their personalities show up through flashbacks. But, in a way, that seems like a cheat because (to a very small extent) those characters are handed to you, rather than you building it entirely from scratch.

  • Josh Roby
    December 2006
    Hey, Tony.

    As for the first question, I do find that players need a "seed" to start off from, much like a seed crystal. On the other hand, the simple requirements of roleplaying also dictate that each character will need to be defined in terms of how they appear to the other characters. Cause your character steps into a scene, right, and you need to tell everybody else what they see. I find that the initial appearance of a character works just fine as a seed. Even though I don't like the character, Cordelia from Buffy the Vampire Slayer is a prime example; she stepped onto screen and you knew what she was all about, and then character development grew that out and later subverted it.

    For an AP example, when we playtested Sailing for Atlantis, which is character generation throughout play, my character started out as "Dressed in a military uniform and introducing himself as Astor." And I knew from the outset that he most certainly was not just a simple soldier -- I riffed off of that "who is he really, and why is he pretending to be a soldier" for the first half of the game or so, which built up enough character momentum that when I had an opportunity for him to reveal his true nature, it went off perfectly: oh, he's part of a vast international conspiracy to dupe the Queen of the Underworld and engineer a new Golden Age. Which all developed organically out of "why's he pretending to be a soldier?"

    Regarding the second question, the Deus Ex Machina thing was entirely possible in Sailing for Atlantis, but it never came up. Now, this may be because of the game itself and its ridiculous scope. At one point I basically bought the Flying Dutchman in its freakiest incarnation possible and the rest of the table nodded and went, "Yeah, okay." I don't know if character-generation-through-play will work very well in a more tactical game, since tactics is often shepherding resources carefully, and giving everybody a deck full of jokers throws tactics out the window.

    I'm designing a game called, "Silence Keeps Me A Victim." In that game I am using a playable character creation. The characters aren't blank slates although they have the same starting attributes. The Attributes are Mask, Voice, Shame, and perhaps Pride. Characters start with a Mask rating that decreases and gain in Voice and Shame. I'm not sure how Pride figures in yet, it just sounds like it needs to be involved. Anyway the changing of attributes suggests things about the characters but doesn't really say things. What speaks loudly is that characters will have traits that define them similiar to Dogs, that they gain through game play. Anyway....

    So I think to have playable character creation you need something to play with to start and this beginning thing shouldn't be designed as physics like strength, stamina, etc, but should be things that allow the effecting of fiction while leaving room for the explanation of them being strong,quick, clever, well connected, etc. I think it would be really interesting to have a game where those descriptions narrowed your opprotunities to describe anything but strengthened your ability to use those descriptions. For instance you kick the door open, maybe you get more "dice" now for describing solving problems with strength and less for other descriptions. This is not what I'm doing however, it's just a thought.

    I then think in creating a playable Character Creation, you need to gain things that define the character, rather than gain things that define the characters ability. I think this may help alleviate but not completely prevent Deus Ex Machina. In my game the traits you gain are actually assigned by someone else which I think totally prevents D.E.M. but has other problems it can instill.
