    \n I've not had any trouble with the first scene. You crash, you are placed in immediate danger, with more danger just over the horizon. It's the scenes immediately after that's been resolved where things tend to need direction for me - "OK, we're out of the crash and temporarily safe, now what?"

    Some advice on applying immediate, compelling pressure that can't be ignored would be good, but starting with a crash already puts the players in the right frame of mind, I think.

    \n My experience was similar to Jason's. Although, there was a moment right at the beginning when the players were kind of like, "Ok, so we crashed..." so I had a black helicopter appear on the horizon. That got them moving.

    Oh, and I had one of the agents survive the crash. I stuck him under some burning wreckage that was about to explode. One of the Psis tried to help him with her powers but instead blew everything up and killed him. It was pretty great.

    Scene two was weak. "I guess we keep on running," was what they had to say, so I turned the firehose of adversity on them. Some tips for doing that well in early scenes would be a good thing.

    \n Hey Chris, I think you really do need focused advice and strong examples of play. Stuff like:

    When uncertain about what you should be doing, try to answer a question - yours or another character's.

    Of your brainstormy suggestions, that's #1 written more actively. #2 is lame. #3 ought to happen by default, although there's neither a dis-incentive or mechanical problem with "splitting the party".
