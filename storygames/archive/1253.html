Designing for the 25-35 Demographic

komradebob
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
So, over in the Threads to watch thread, the discussion came up regarding designs that fit with the lifestyle of a mid-range age group of gamers. I'm 35, been playing pgs since around 1980 or so and fit in that bracket. the discussion was spurred by an rpgnet post about a player who was leaving the hobby for a variety of reasons.

I think that gaming in that 25-35 age bracket is hard. I don't think that many games are designed with that age bracket in mind, although there is certainly a body of articles that have been written about the issue.

Anyway, here are some of my thoughts on issues to consider about this age group. I'll leave it to your Gamist wiles to figure out approaches that deal with these.

1) This age bracket has out of gaming responsibilities.
In terms of money, career, and time, this age group is likely to have other considerations that take precedence over gaming, no matter how much they love it. Further, this age bracket is likely to have peers working under similar constraints.

2) Going along with #1, this age bracket is likely to be interacting with non-gamers even more regularly than gamers.
Hrm? Okay, what I mean is this: For me, at a younger age, I could find gamers all around me. Once I hit this age bracket, the situations that allowed me to connect with other gamers my own age were lessened. No college/school. Thus, I'm looking at gaming, but now I'm looking at potential players that have little/no rpg experience, and are themselves working under the constarints of situation #1.

3) I have disposable income, better than I had at an earlier age, but I need to be more selective with it.
This came up in the Spirit of the Century thread ( by Vaxalon, I believe). Price _is_ important. I can spend $50 on a game, or I can spend $50 on a movie or beer. I _know_ I'm going to drink the beer or see the movie. I'm not as surre about the game, no matter what I'd like to have happen.

4)If I can get 4 people together for 4 hours, 2 of them are going to be kibbitzing. It also might only happen once a month.
If we end up playing a game, something had better happen. 4 hours of chargen? I think not. 4 hours of a single combat? It'd better be an epic battle, not two rooms and a couple weedy goblins.

5) Replay value.
Boardgames have replay value. RPGs promise it. I can pick up Monopoly and play it any old time. I can play it with a different group composition. This does not tend to be true with rpgs.

6) I need to not have to spend a bunch of pre-game time that may not come into play.

7) Conversely, there is an important solo-hobby aspect that keeps me interested.
Both minis games and ccgs have aspects of non-active play that form part of the hobby aspect. How can this be encouraged in a system for an rpg?

Okay, that's what I've got. Feel frre to add or grill me as appropriate.

Best regards,
Robert

    4. I play every week, but I think for many this is a real issue. A game should be capable of satisfying play in an evening, much as a boardgame or card game would be.

    7. This doesn't really resonate with me. I want to spend my gaming time gaming, I have a huge number of other things to do when not gaming and do not really want a solo play aspect.

    So, what could help?

    Rules need not be light, but they need be intuitive and easily houseruled. If I have only a day a month to play in I do not want play stopped while I look up rules, but nor do I want something too rules light particularly if I am playing with people more accustomed to board and card games where the rules are reasonably clear. Thus I want flexible and intuitive rules that are easily amended/kludged without breaking the game.

    Anything that gets me playing faster is good. I want rapid chargen, I want pregens, I want prestatted opponents that I can simply plug and play. Pendragon has stats for a variety of standard NPCs, that is part of why I am currently running Pendragon, of course there are other ways of getting the same end but I do not want to spend an afternoon that I do not have prepping NPCs.

    Chargen cannot be a session to itself. Lots of games now assume we have a session creating characters, I then go away and prep a game based on those characters, then we play. Quite simply, that takes too long. I want pregens or better yet to swiftly create the characters on the night and be playing within half an hour. nor do I want to spend much time deciding what the setting is or anything like that.

    Mechanisms to encourage replay help. That's why Savage Worlds has levels by the way, it is explicitly designed to create an incentive to keep playing, to see your guy level up. Other things may also help, but I prefer ongoing games to one shots and so do many other gamers, thus anything which helps me run an ongoing game is good. By contrast, if a game is for one-shots then I want it to be playable out of the box. Both techniques have their place, and produce different results. For ongoing games, I want fast chargen, mechanics to reward continued play, quick bursts of gaming goodness - I do not want to play for a month before I get to the good stuff whatever that happens to be. For one off games I want to open the box and play, so pregens are good, a predefined setting is good, prestatted opponents or some way of getting to the same end, all these things help me.

    Thoughts?

    Eh. Some of these problems don't really hold up as generalizations, and I don't think there's much in design that can help with them. I think that some of it boils down to matters of taste and or dedication - choices, in other words. Sure, your time is more limited as you get older, and sure, it's not as easy to meet other gamers, and sure, there isn't as much time to prepare stuff. A lot of games already cater to these needs - see PTA for the big one, see DitV with its "get to the cool stuff right now" aesthetic, see Spirit of the Century which was specifically designed to address some of these issues.

    For example, your #3? I don't follow. It sounds like you're blaming the game for your lifestyle choices. I have quite a few DVDs I've bought but haven't watched yet, just as I've got a couple of RPGs recently that I haven't played yet. I could say I just haven't had the time to sit down and watch those movies, but the reality is I haven't chosen to use what time I do have in that fashion. Plain and simple. And ultimately, most other entertainments are fleeting, whereas RPGs have a potentially unlimited shelf life. So I don't think that, other than moment-to-moment economic concerns, price really enters into it as much as you're proposing.

    Same thing with #4 - having two hours of kibbitzing vs. two hours of playing is purely a social concern. When I get my group together (all people in the demographic in question, two with wife and kids), there's an understanding that anything not focused toward play is to be kept to a minimum. Period. And even within that, we use aggressive scene framing to make sure that the most stuff gets done that can - no dwelling on the shopping trips and whatnot, no chewing the scenery needlessly with NPCs unless everyone's really into it. Often, we only get to play for two and a half hours or so, and we get a ton done. It's just a matter of setting up expectations properly. Can you put guidelines to that effect in a rulebook? Sure, but it's already been done.

    #5 - I can't pick up Monopoly and play it any old time. It'd be way harder for me to do that than to run a one-shot of some random RPG. Again, choice and priorities.

    #6 - Agreed. Tons of indie games are already very low-prep.

    #7 - If your time is limited as per your #1, how does this aspect necessarily help you? Deliberately setting aside the free time to play is often difficult enough without also spending time with painting minis, sorting cards, or whatnot.

    Bottom line is, most of the obstacles that adult living brings to gaming can be overcome with simple dedication, communication, and focus. Don't know any gamers? Start talking about it in other social venues with the intent of recruiting friends. Don't have much time? Push for definite schedules and take a hard stance on dithering during the session. For everything else, there's already a ton out there which supports your needs.

    I'm in this demographic, and I play more now than I ever did before. Not because I don't have more responsibilities, I do have more.     Max, regarding the "lightness" of the system, it's really not about light or heavy, it's about Points of Contact. Meaning how much interface with the system do you have to have in order for play to proceed forward. Your assessment is quite correct in that "freeform" games actually have amongst the highest amounts of POC, because you're constantly negotiating exactly how things get created in-game. With a strong set of rules, that's made much simpler.

    That said, they don't have to be voluminous rules, they simply have to be designed intent on effectively driving the action forward. This is why, for instance, Dogs in the Vinyard propells play so well clearly while having a simple rule set. Note also that when you have such a system the need for houseruling and such goes down, because the game already provides exactly what it needs to go forward. So it saves time here as well.

    Since I'm in my 40s, I share a lot of these concerns; they are what I want to fix with my own designs. Some of the concerns, the social networking side, are going to be difficult if not impossible to solve through game design, although a game that would appeal to those currently outside the hobby (easier to grasp, restrained lasersharking, less fetishy) would certainly help. Cost is fixable by keeping everything in one book and minimizing accessories (paper, pencils, dice, or standard card decks are OK, anything else is pushing it.)

    Time is probably the biggest concern. Things like replay value, duration of chargen, learning curve, and the like are all really questions about time investment. Here are a few of my thoughts on games for the middle-aged gamer:

    LEARNING CURVE: The rules should be condenseable to 1 or 2 pages. They should be one or two core mechanics and a few simple variations of these for specific purposes. The rules book can elaborate these mechanics, provide numerous examples and plenty of inspiration, but you should be able to play the game using a rules cheatsheet, with no need to refer to the book at all.

    CHARGEN: This should take five minutes, tops. You should only need to make 3 to 4 decisions during chargen and be able to detail your character with just a few descriptors. Details about background, relationships, skill specialties, and the like can exist, but should emerge as a result of play instead of being required before play begins. NO PERSONALITY ATTRIBUTES, except those that are central to the concept behind the game, like Sanity or Rage or Love (but not all three.) Personality should emerge through play as well.

    EQUIPMENT: Avoid price lists. Equipment choices should be treated like other choices during chargen: they are cues to the other players about what you'd like play to include. Some games hande this with rolls against a Wealth attribute to see if you could have purchased that item, but why have a roll at all, for most things?

    Posted By: BalbinusOther than that though, am I right that essentially you think we should not try to consider what would appeal more to this demographic as it is our own fault we are struggling to find time to play?
    Not entirely right, no. Mainly, I want to advocate infusing the discussion with a healthy dose of self-perspective on two levels, is all. First, I'm not sure how many of us can really speak for the "demographic", as it were - all we can really talk about are personal experiences, and what elements of design can aid us personally, with the hope that other folks like us can benefit from it in the same way. It's more honest to do it that way, I think, and eliminates the possibility of false objectivity.

    Second, there's a limit at which game design can realistically overcome the obstacles of personal, real-life priorities and choices, or lack of priorities and choices. I don't think that's a controversial observation, and I'm not trying to be abrasive by pointing it out - if you'd rather be a father than a gamer, when you have to choose between the two, that pretty much is what it is. (Not that I personally know anyone who'd choose the latter instead.)

    With those two points firmly in mind, I think a lot of game designs out there *already have* addressed a lot of Robert's stated issues in a direct, focused way - the approaches are out there. Primetime Adventures is relatively cheap, has a small learning curve for newbie gamers, requires minimal prep or pre-game time during the week, and has a low enough POC to get a ton done in two hours. That's most of what he wanted.

    But! Lemme be helpful, in the way that Robert instructed:

    1.) I don't have as much spare time these days. -- In my experience, overt flagging mechanics (Fate's aspects, TSOY's Keys, Sorcerer's Kickers, PTA's Issues, etc.) go a long, long way toward minimizing prep. They immediately give clear suggestions for session content that you can often improvise from in play. Also, having a firm core story in place is helpful, a concept that Primetime Adventures calls "franchise" - a sense of what to expect, what the patterns are that get broken and reincorporated most often during play. Like, in a Star Trek game, you know that if you show up to play with a new planet to explore and some problem threatening the natives, no one will flip. You get that mainly from pre-game discussion, which doesn't necessarily have to take place in the session. Deliberate franchising mechanics might be an awesome way to handle this.

    2.) I can only really play with newbies. -- Minimize your points of contact with the rules and eliminate any rules that rely on dogmatic RPG conventions if they have no purpose in the action of play, like a separate, detailed combat system, shopping lists and currency, etc. Frankly, I vastly prefer playing with newbies these days - they carry very few assumptions with them into play overall, and can very quickly get into the mode of an elegant game system with minimal instruction.

    3.) I'm selective about my cash. -- I stand by my initial comments on this point, but you can minimize this by finding one game and sticking with it if it solves most of your needs, or splitting the cost for the gamebook amongst the group and treating it as a community text. If your friends will all shell out for bowling shoes, they'll probably shell out for a gamebook. The harder, but ultimately more satisfying route, is to design the game yourself, which makes it free.

    4.) My group kibbitzes a lot. -- Have explicit rules regarding scene framing and pacing, either in your local social contract or as a mechanic in the game. PTA does this extremely well. Consider the core action of your session, what the main arenas of player investment are, and "cut the fat" from your scening to emphasize that stuff.

    5.) Replay value. -- I'm having the hardest time with this point, because I think it really does boil down to a matter of taste among the individuals and encouragement on your end. The design factors already mentioned above tend to contribute, IMO, to the game's replay value. Check out Tim Kleinert's The Mountain Witch for a design that gets closest to the "board game" aesthetic I think you're talking about.

    6.) I don't want wasted prep. -- See #1.

    7.) Out of game, solo stuff to invest in. -- I'm stretching for this one, again, because you didn't respond to my question of how this helps you in the face of time constraints. But, if you want, you can introduce a between session activity that has some mechanical value in play - obvious example of this is the old house rules where you get extra XP if you write a journal entry about the session or whatnot. Neel K.'s Lexicon can be used as a between-session activity like this, and Malcolm Sheppard's new game Codex incorporates writing game fiction into the process of play.

    Let me point out first that I preceeded that statement with a comment about restricting personality chargen to elements that are essential to the core concept of the game. Also, all of those examples were focusing on what it takes to cut down the time investment. Even if a player has a preference or at least a tolerance for longer chargen processes, what *sells* a game to an older gamer is the ability start a game on the spur of the moment, whenever everyone present just happens to have a few hours to spare and feels in the mood for an RPG.

    In that context, game prep should focus on the essentials. Many elements of a character, like clothing worn, behavioral quirks, and general temperment can be evolved during play. If that's the case, why do it before play? It's almost always easier to refine your character concept in the context of a typical scenario than it is in a vacuum. Quicker, too. People often have "blank page terror" or writer's block when faced with too many choices, anyways. So, move anything that can be developed during play *into* play, to minimize prep time and make it easier to be creative.

    Old D&D did this. There were no personality decisions before play at all, yet players easily developed characters with personalities. Compare that to GURPS, where all personality details have point values and are part of normal chargen. In my experience, GURPS chargen got bogged down when players tried to decide what disadvantages to take to get the skills and advantages they thought they would need. The result tended to be weird emotional cripples that didn't feel like real people. Oh, and half the time, they had the wrong advantages for the challenges they faced, anyways. The Quirks system was another failure in GURPS. It seemed like a good idea: encourage players to differentiate their characters by offering them character points. However, most Quirks wound up sounding pretty flat and uninteresting ("Likes to wear blue") and wasted more prep time, sometime ten or fifteen minutes, than they were really worth.

    The title of this thread confused me at first because my initial response was, "Isn't that what we've been doing this entire time?" I very much see RPGs slowly gentrifying, with the target market swinging from teens to post-collegiates. Seriously: Twilight? Roach? Dogs in the Vineyard? These are games that primarily appeal to folks past college, already sunk in the midst of a developing lifestyle. Even Exalted has an older qualified-cynicism thing going on in its depiction of the world (contrast between "everyone is trying to do the right thing but they're blinded by their situation" with older WoD-styled "people are fucking up the world cause they're stupid and mean").

    I don't think the 25-35 demographic has less time, I think they have different time. People have already mentioned that the slow-boil idle thoughts development of a campaign is possible in school and difficult out of it, and I think this is a good example. But when you get down to the numbers of it, I play way the hell more these days than I ever did in school. This is because of a few things, all part of the 25-35 demographic: I'm more mobile and willing to 'commute' to a game, I have more options in terms of money and connections in getting a game space to play in (which sounds terrible -- simply, in one instance, one player's access lets us play in a boardroom after business hours; another player owns a house), I have better time and resource organizational skills that allow me to put together games, and perhaps most importantly, I have "adult" conflict-negotiation and community-building skills that allow me to find and build a playgroup in way more conscious methods than I ever would have when I was 22. So sure, I have less time -- but I'm able to use that time way more efficiently, and in way more structured means, than when I had all the free time in the world.

    Illustrative example: in high school and college, we'd organize a game and order pizza. Nowadays, we organize a game-and-dinner night.

    Now, while I do think that a lot of modern games are trying to appeal to an older demographic, I think a lot of them are still being designed for teenagers. Exalted, for instance, assumes the never-ending campaign as a default structure, with most of the character development happening away from the table in daydreamland. Its rules guide combat first, resources second, travel third, and social conflicts a distant, distant fourth. It enshrines a GM as an absolute and beneficial authority and the players as supplicants and dependents. These are the rule structures that teens need. Adults can handle more complex and nuanced social interaction at the table, and probably desire nuanced social interaction in the game. They're more capable of developing character in play and in crisis.

    I've written Full Light, Full Steam with the assumption of postcollegiate players. It has decentralized authority, emphasis on characterization over questing, deals with complex themes (identity, patriotism, imperialism, economics, oppression), and has no default structure but guidelines for creating your own. If all goes according to plan, it will give mature players what they need and not require of them what they don't have. It's flexible and assumes that the players are capable of switching things around to fit their life, rather than assuming that they subscribe to the "gamer lifestyle" I enjoyed when I was 20.
